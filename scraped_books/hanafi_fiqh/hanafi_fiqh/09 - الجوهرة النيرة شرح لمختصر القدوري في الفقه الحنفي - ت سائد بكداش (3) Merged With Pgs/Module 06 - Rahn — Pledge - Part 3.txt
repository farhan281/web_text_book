Ruling on Destruction of the Pledged Property
States liability and consequences when pawn is destroyed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a pawn is destroyed by the pledgor or a third party, and explain who is the proper litigant and how the value is handled.

Core Idea
Pledgor replaces destroyed pawn; third party compensates the creditor, who alone sues.

When property is pledged as collateral, its destruction raises questions of liability. The Hanafi school provides clear rules: if the pledgor destroys it, he must replace it; if a third party destroys it, the creditor (mortgagee) is the one to claim compensation. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical consequences, including how values are reconciled when they differ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two destruction scenarios
Read the passage and note that it distinguishes between destruction by the pledgor (الراهن) and by a third party (أجنبي). Each has a different legal consequence.
2
Determine the hukm for each scenario
For the pledgor: he must guarantee (يضمن) i.e., provide a replacement. For the third party: the creditor is the litigant (الخصم) and takes the value, which becomes a new pawn.
The hukm level is wajib (obligatory) for both.
3
Analyze the value calculation
The passage explains that the third party pays the value on the day of destruction. If that value differs from the value at the time of pledging, the difference is treated like a loss from a calamity, affecting the debt proportionally.
The key principle: value is assessed on the day of possession (قبض), not earlier.
4
Understand the procedure when debt is deferred or due
If the debt is deferred (مؤجل), the compensation becomes the new pawn. If the debt is due (حال) and the compensation is of the same type, the creditor may take it and any surplus goes to the pledgor; if different type, the creditor can demand the debt or sell the compensation.
This shows the flexibility in handling the compensation.
5
Note the variant reading in some copies
The footnote mentions a textual variant: 'in some copies: by all the value' instead of 'sells by the value'. This does not change the core ruling but shows textual transmission nuances.
Such variants are common in fiqh manuscripts.

When a Third Party Destroys the Pledge: The Lender as Plaintiff and Substitute Pledge
Ruling on third-party destruction of pledge and its replacement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a third party destroys a pledged item, identify the proper plaintiff, and explain how the compensation value replaces the original pledge.

Core Idea
The lender sues for compensation; the value becomes a new pledge in his hand.

This passage addresses a scenario where a third party destroys the pledged item. The Hanafi school gives the lender standing to seek compensation because of his possessory interest. The compensation received is treated as a substitute pledge, preserving the security for the debt. We will examine the details of valuation, surplus, and the treatment of the substitute when the debt is deferred or due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wrongdoer and the act
The third party (ajnabi) consumes (istihlak) or destroys the pledged item. This triggers liability.
Consumption includes any act that ends the item's existence.
2
Determine the proper plaintiff
The lender (murtahin) is the litigant (khasm) because he holds the pledge as security (qabd istifa'). The pawner does not have standing.
The lender's possession gives him a direct interest.
3
Understand the compensation basis
The third party must pay the value (qimah) on the day of destruction (yawm halak), not the value on the day of pledge or any other date.
If the value dropped, the lower amount is paid; the difference is treated as lost by an act of God.
4
Apply the surplus principle
If the compensation is less than the original pledge's value, the shortage is not recoverable from the third party. Instead, it is as if the pledge perished by a calamity (so the lender bears that loss proportionally).
E.g., pledge worth 1000, destroyed when worth 500, third party pays 500; the extra 500 is lost from the pledge's security.
5
Handle the substitute pledge
If the debt is deferred, the compensation becomes a new pledge in the lender's hand. If the debt is due and the compensation is of the same type (e.g., cash), the lender may take it; any surplus goes to the pawner. If different type, the lender may demand the debt or sell the compensation for its value.
This preserves the security arrangement.

Ruling stating pawner's liability for injuring the pledge with rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that the pawner is liable for injuring the pledge and explain the legal reason (illa) for this liability.

Core Idea
The pawner is liable for any injury he causes to the pledged item because he violates the lender's possessory right.

In this lesson, we examine a specific rule from the chapter of pledge (rahn): if the pawner himself damages the pledged property, he must compensate. While the pawner owns the item, the lender's security interest creates a right that the pawner cannot infringe. The text gives a concise ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and subject
The act is 'jinaaya' (injury) by the pawner upon the pledge. The pledge is in the lender's possession as collateral.
The injury can be physical harm to a slave or damage to an item.
2
State the ruling
The injury is 'مضمونة' (guaranteed/liable), meaning the pawner must compensate for the damage.
Compensation is typically the reduction in the pledge's value.
3
Explain the rationale
The commentary gives the illa (legal reason): because the injury removes the lender's hand (i.e., possessory right) from the injured part. The lender's right to hold the pledge as security is violated.
This is a violation of the lender's interest, not ownership.
4
Apply the ruling
If the pawner cuts the hand of a pledged slave, he must pay damages. The pledge contract continues on the remaining value.
If the entire pledge is destroyed, the debt may become due or the pawner must substitute.

Lender's Injury to Pledge: Debt Reduction
Ruling on lender's injury to pledge with condition on compensation type.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a lender's injury to a pledge reduces the debt by the amount of injury, and explain the condition regarding the type of compensation.

Core Idea
Injuring a pledge reduces your debt, but only if the compensation matches the debt type.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a crucial ruling in Hanafi pledge (rahn) law: if the lender (murtahin) damages the pledged item, his debt is reduced by the amount of the injury. However, as the commentary explains, this automatic reduction only applies when the compensation is of the same type as the debt. Understanding this rule requires appreciating the lender's dual role as creditor and custodian, and the consequences of his wrongful act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the matn: 'And the injury of the lender upon it reduces from the debt by its amount.' This is the core ruling: the debt decreases by the value of the injury.
2
Analyze the Condition
The commentary states: 'if the guarantee is of the same type as the debt; but if it is of a different type, then mutual consent is required.' Determine what 'same type' means (e.g., both money, both gold).
If the debt is gold and the compensation is silver, that is different type.
3
Trace the Legal Logic
The commentary explains the underlying reason: the lender becomes a usurper (ghasib) of the value. As a ghasib, he must pay the full value of the injury. Then he can set off (muqaasah) his debt from that payment. If the injury exceeds the debt, he receives the surplus.
This mirrors general Hanafi rules of ghasb: full liability plus offset of mutual obligations.
4
Apply the Ruling
Consider examples: Debt=100, injury=30. Debt reduces to 70. If injury=150, the lender pays 50 surplus to pawner. If compensation is in a different type (e.g., debt is cash, injury compensation is a cow), reduction requires mutual consent.
Without consent, the lender must claim the compensation separately.

Injury Caused by the Pledge: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the liability of a pledged slave's torts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on injuries caused by a pledged slave and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding injury to the lender.

Core Idea
A pledged slave's injury to its master is void for monetary claims but valid for retaliation; to the lender, it is void under Abu Hanifa if no surplus.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses torts committed by a pledged slave (rahn). The general principle is that such injuries are 'hadar' (void of liability), but the rule splits depending on the victim. A critical intra-Hanafi disagreement arises when the slave injures the lender and its value does not exceed the debt. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply and distinguish between the views of the founding imams.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule and its scope
Identify the base ruling: the injury of the pledge upon the pawner, lender, or their property is hadar. Note the exceptions and conditions mentioned in the matn.
The rule is not absolute; it differentiates between monetary claims and retaliation.
2
Analyze injury to the pawner (owner)
Distinguish between monetary injury (void by consensus) and injury requiring retaliation (qawad) where the slave is treated as a stranger and liability is established.
Consensus (ijma') applies only to monetary injury against the owner.
3
Understand injury to the lender and the condition of surplus
Focus on the condition: 'if there is no surplus in its value over the debt'. Abu Hanifa rules it is hadar. Grasp his reasoning: circularity because the lender would ultimately pay himself.
The surplus is the slave's value minus the debt.
4
Contrast with the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the injury is established in the slave's neck regardless of surplus. The pawner may cancel the pledge and surrender the slave.
The text from the lesson only presents the opposition; no rebuttal is given.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the lender cannot claim compensation when no surplus. Under Sahibayn, the lender can, and the pawner has an option.
This difference affects real-world transactions involving collateral.

Liability for Injuries Involving the Pledged Property (Jinaayah al-Rahn)
Explains three rulings on injuries involving the pledged property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of injury liability in a pledge (rahn) and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding injury by the pledged slave on the pledgee.

Core Idea
If a pledged slave injures the pledgee with no surplus value, Abu Hanifa says it's void; the Sahibayn establish it.

A pledge (rahn) is security for a debt, but who bears liability if someone is injured by or on the pledged property? This lesson examines three distinct scenarios derived from the Mukhtasar of al-Quduri and its commentary. The most nuanced case involves the pledged slave injuring the pledgee, where the Hanafi masters differ on whether compensation is due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three persons causing injury
List the pledgor (raahin), pledgee (murtahin), and the pledged property (rahn) itself. Note the general ruling for each: pledgor's injury is guaranteed (madmoonah), pledgee's injury reduces debt (tusqitu min al-dayn), and injury by the pledged property is void (hadar) on all parties.
These are the foundational statements from the matn.
2
Understand the reasoning for pledgor and pledgee
The pledgor's injury is guaranteed because he removes the pledgee's possession. The pledgee's injury reduces his debt by the amount of injury because he is a usurper (ghasib) and must guarantee; he may set off the debt from the value.
This reasoning is rooted in the concept of possession and guarantee.
3
Focus on injury by the pledged property on the pledgor
When the pledged property (slave) injures the pledgor or his property with monetary liability, it is unanimously void because a master cannot owe money to his own slave. However, if retaliation (qawad) is due, the slave is taken.
This is clear and uncontested.
4
Analyze the dispute on injury by the pledged slave on the pledgee
When the pledged slave injures the pledgee with monetary liability and there is no surplus over the debt, Abu Hanifa rules it is void (hadar) because establishing liability would require cancellation (the ultimate guarantor is the pledgee). The Sahibayn rule it is established on the slave's person, giving options to the parties.
This is the core ikhtilaf of the lesson. Note the two narrations from Abu Hanifa when surplus exists.
5
Note the rulings when surplus value exists
If the slave's value exceeds the debt, Abu Hanifa has two narrations: (1) injury is established to the extent of the trust (amanah) portion, (2) not established. The passage also covers injury to the pledgee's property with surplus.
The example with goods worth 2000 and debt 1000 illustrates the calculation.

The Two Riwayat on Pawn Injury and Release (Fakaak)
Commentary on pawn injury and release

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two riwāyāt of Abū Ḥanīfa regarding injury by a pawn when surplus exists, and apply the rules of compensation and release to a practical case.

Core Idea
A pawn's injury liability hinges on surplus: two conflicting narrations treat surplus as trust or as pledge.

This lesson unpacks a complex area of Hanafi pawn law: what happens when the pawned property (e.g., a slave) causes injury to the pledgee or his property? The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents two riwāyāt from Abū Ḥanīfa on whether liability attaches to the surplus value. It then works through a concrete example of damage by the pledgor, the process of fakaak (release), and additional rules on expenses and retaliation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to handle ikhtilāf within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two riwāyāt
Locate the two narrations from Abū Ḥanīfa when the pawn has surplus value over the debt. One riwāya establishes injury liability in the amount of the trust (amānah); the other does not because the trust portion is held as pledge.
The first riwāya analogizes to a deposit (wadīʿa) slave; the second stresses the nature of rahn.
2
Understand the rationale behind each riwāya
For the first: the surplus is not in the pledgee's liability (ḍamān), so it is like a trust. For the second: the entire pawn, including surplus, is in the pledgee's hand by way of pledge, so no separate amānah exists.
The dispute is purely about the legal characterization of the surplus portion.
3

Work through the case: pawn worth 2000, debt 1000. Pledgor damages goods worth 2000. The pledgee demands the pawn. The pledgor may pay half (1000) to release the pawn, or the pawn is sold and proceeds split.
Half payment corresponds to the deficient liability portion; the trust portion is already covered by the pawn's value.
4
Understand the fakaak process and expenses
If the pledgor chooses to pay, he pays half; afterward the pawn is free from injury claim and remains pledged. Also note expenses: house rent on pledgee, shepherd and maintenance on pledgor.
The expenses section is inserted here but belongs to a separate ruling on custody costs.
5
Examine the retaliation rule
If the injury requires qiṣāṣ, the pledgee gets retaliation and the debt drops because the pawn has been destroyed by a cause in his hand (the pledgee's own claim).
This is a special case where the pawn's destruction leads to debt remission.

Allocating Pawn Expenses: Pledgee vs Pledgor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish which pawn expenses fall on the pledgee and which on the pledgor by applying the Hanafi criterion of liability (ḍamān) versus benefit.

Core Idea
Housing and guard of a pawn are on the pledgee because he holds it as a liability; shepherding and maintenance are on the pledgor because they benefit the pawn's growth.

In a pawn (rahn) contract, the pledgee holds the pledged item as a trust but with liability (ḍamān). This chapter clarifies who bears various expenses. The Hanafi school distinguishes between costs of preserving the pawn (which fall on the pledgee) and costs that benefit the pawn's increase (which fall on the pledgor). The pledgee's liability (ḍamān) for the pawn is the basis for requiring him to pay for preservation costs like housing and guard.
Liability makes him responsible for the item's safety.

Redemption of Offspring When Original Pawn is Destroyed
Explains rulings on offspring of pawned item after original is destroyed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the share of the offspring of a pawned item that must be redeemed when the original is destroyed, using the Hanafi method of valuation at possession and redemption.

Core Idea
Offspring of a pawn is not automatically included; it must be redeemed proportionally based on values at different times.

In this lesson, we examine a key rule regarding pawn (rahn): what happens when the pawned item is destroyed but its offspring remains? The Hanafi school holds that the offspring is separate and must be redeemed by paying a proportionate share of the debt. The calculation depends on the value of the original at the time of possession and the value of the offspring at the time of redemption. We will walk through the legal reasoning and work through numerical examples from the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the essential elements
List the pawned item (original), its offspring, the debt amount, and their values at two critical times: the day the pawn was taken into possession (value of original) and the day of redemption (value of offspring).
2
Understand the valuation rule
The original's value is fixed at possession because it entered the mortgagee's guarantee then. The offspring's value is considered at redemption because it becomes guaranteed only at that point.
3
Apply the proportional division
Divide the total debt by the sum of the two values (as if they were parts of a whole). This gives the share of debt attributable to each.
Example: debt=10, original value=10, offspring value=10 → total value=20, each share=10/20=0.5 → mother's share=5, offspring's share=5.
4
Cancel the original's share
The share of debt corresponding to the original is cancelled—it is as if that portion of the debt is paid by the destruction of the pawn.
5
Calculate redemption amount
The share corresponding to the offspring must be paid by the pawnor to redeem the offspring. This is the amount due.
6
Handle value changes
If the offspring's value changes after the original's destruction, you must recalculate: the new value at redemption replaces the earlier one, and the division is redone retroactively.
This is shown in the passage with the sheep example: if offspring value rises to 20, the mother's share becomes 3.33 instead of 5.

Increase in Pledge: Ruling and Calculation
Explains ruling on increase (birth) in pledge and debt division calculations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the proportionate division of debt between an original pledge and its increase, and explain the Hanafi position that increase is part of the pledge.

Core Idea
Increase in a pledge is part of the pledge; debt is divided proportionally according to values at possession.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah explains the ruling on increase in a pledge. Through numerical examples, the author demonstrates how the debt is divided between the original pledge and its increase when values change. The passage also addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute: while the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) hold that increase is permissible and part of the pledge, Zufar disagreed. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly calculating redemption amounts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
A pledged item produces an increase (e.g., offspring) while in the creditor's possession. Both the original and the increase are subject to the debt.
The increase is considered pledged from the moment of its birth/generation.
2
Divide the debt based on values at possession
The debt is divided proportionally between the original and the increase using their respective values on the day the increase was taken into possession (qabd).
The creditor's liability (daman) attached at possession, so that value is controlling.
3
Adjust for subsequent value changes
If the increase's value changes after the original's destruction, the division is recalculated. The share of the original is determined by the value of the increase at the time of its possession, not later fluctuations.
The text gives examples: if the increase's value rises to 20 after the mother's death, the mother's share becomes 3.33.
4
Apply the rule of permissibility
The author states 'Tajuz al-ziyadah fi al-rahn' – increase in the pledge is permissible. This is the operative Hanafi position (mufta bihi).
Zufar's opposing view is noted but not adopted.

Commentary
This passage is from a Hanafi commentary (sharh) on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, addressing the ruling on increase (al-ziyadah) in a pledge (rahn). The author first presents illustrative cases (surah al-mas'alah). He explains that when a pledged item produces an increase (like offspring), that increase is considered part of the pledge and shares proportionally in covering the debt. The debt is divided between the original and the increase based on their respective values at the time of possession (qabd). The examples show how changes in the increase's value after the original's destruction affect the allocation of the debt. The author then states the general rule: 'Increase in the pledge is permissible' (tajuz al-ziyadah fi al-rahn). He immediately notes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Zufar (a prominent student of Abu Hanifa) held it impermissible, but the relied-upon position of our school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is permissibility. The practical consequence is that the increase is treated as part of the pledge, and the debt is divided proportionally. The author then explains the method: the debt is divided based on the values at the time of possession, not future fluctuations, because liability (daman) is established at possession. If the original decreases in value in the pledgee's hand, the debt is reduced accordingly. If the pledgor later adds another pledge after a decrease, the remaining debt is divided proportionally between the remaining original and the new increase.

Increase in the Pledge: Permissibility and Proportional Debt Division
Hanafi ruling on increase in pledge with intra-madhhab dispute and calculation method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on increasing a pledge, state the opposing view of Zufar, and calculate the proportional division of debt among multiple pledges based on possession values.

Core Idea
Adding collateral is permissible; debt is divided among pledges proportionally by possession value.

In this passage, the Hanafi school permits the pledgor to add an additional item as collateral after the original contract. This ruling is contested by Zufar b. al-Hudhayl, who holds that such an increase is invalid. The text then provides a detailed method for dividing the debt among multiple pledges based on their values at the time of possession (qabd), not at redemption or any later time. You will learn the key principle that the liability is tied to possession, fixing the valuation date.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Dispute
Recognize that the act in question is increasing the pledge (adding a new item of collateral to an existing debt after the original contract). Note the intra-madhhab dispute: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani consider it permissible (ja'iz), while Zufar considers it invalid.
This is not about increasing the debt; that is a separate ruling.
2
Understand the Valuation Principle
The key principle is that liability (daman) is tied to possession (qabd). Therefore, the value of each pledge is fixed at the moment the creditor takes possession of it. Later changes in value (except total loss) do not affect the proportional division of the debt.
This differs from valuation at contract formation or redemption.
3
Apply the Calculation Method
When multiple pledges secure a single debt, the debt is divided proportionally according to the value of each pledge at the time of its possession. For example, if original pledge value = 1000, additional pledge value = 500 at possession, and total debt = 1000, then the original secures 2/3 (666.67) and the additional secures 1/3 (333.33).
The sum of the fractional shares equals the full debt.
4
Consider Special Cases
The text also covers scenarios where the original pledge decreases in value after possession (e.g., due to defect or birth of offspring). In such cases, the loss is borne by the creditor proportionally; the debt is reduced by the amount of the decrease. If the pledgor later adds another pledge after the decrease, the remaining debt is divided between the remaining value of the original and the new pledge.
The birth of offspring counts as a decrease because the value of the mother may drop, but the offspring compensates.

Increase in the Debt: The Hanafi Internal Dispute
Hanafi internal dispute on increasing the debt after the pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on increasing the debt after a pledge, and identify the opinions of Abu Yusuf and Zufar within the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad forbid increasing the debt; Abu Yusuf permits it, but the school's relied-upon view is the majority.

In the Hanafi school, the permissibility of increasing the debt after a pledge has been given is a matter of internal disagreement. The majority of the early Hanafi masters, including Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that it is not permissible, while Abu Yusuf permits it. This lesson examines the positions and the practical consequences for pledge contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act in question
Understand what 'ziyada fi al-dayn' means: adding an additional loan amount after the pledge has already been given.
2
State the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They hold that it is not permissible (la yajuz). The existing pledge does not secure the additional amount.
3
State the position of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf permits it, equating increase in debt with increase in pledge.
4
State the position of Zufar
Zufar (and al-Shafi'i) forbid both increase in pledge and increase in debt.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon fatwa follows the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: increase in debt is not permissible.

Core Idea
Pledging one item to two creditors is valid because the pledge applies to the whole, avoiding the shuyu' that invalidates a gift.

This lesson examines a practical scenario in muamalat: can a debtor pledge the same asset to two different creditors? The Hanafi school permits it, but with important conditions. We'll explore the reasoning behind this ruling and how it differs from the rules of gift (hibah).

Liability in Rahn: Transgression and Return to Pledgor
Rulings on pledgee's transgression and lending the pledge to the pledgor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when a pledgee becomes liable as a usurper and when liability ceases due to lending the pledge back to the pledgor.

Core Idea
Transgression makes the pledgee a usurper; returning possession to the pledgor ends liability.

In a pledge (rahn) contract, the pledgee holds the collateral as a trust (amana) for preservation. If he uses it without permission, he transgresses and becomes liable as a usurper (ghasib) for the full value. However, if he returns the pledge to the pledgor by lending it to him, the liability shifts or disappears. This lesson unpacks these two interrelated rulings with practical examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage first addresses transgression (ta'addi) by the pledgee. Determine what constitutes transgression and the legal consequence: full liability as a usurper (daman al-ghasb).
Transgression includes any use beyond preservation, e.g., wearing a pledged ring or garment.
2
Understand the ta'lil (reasoning)
The commentary explains that transgression removes the permission-based holding, making the pledgee akin to a usurper. Also, the value exceeding the debt is a trust, and trusts become liable upon transgression.
The analogy (qiyas) to ghasb is explicit: 'ṣāra ka-annahu akhadhahu bi-ghayri idhnihi'.
3
Apply the distinction: use vs. preservation
Work through the examples: customary wearing (ring on little finger, garment worn normally) = transgression; non-customary placement (ring on other fingers, garment on shoulder) = preservation, no liability.
Custom depends on the pledgor's habit, e.g., wearing two rings as adornment vs. not.
4
Analyze the second ruling: loan to pledgor
When the pledgee lends the pledge back to the pledgor and he takes possession, the pledgee's liability ends. If it perishes thereafter, no one is liable because the pledgor is holding his own property.
The possession that created liability is removed ('azāla al-qabḍ al-mūjib li-al-ḍamān').
5
Trace the underlying principle
Liability in rahn is tied to possession with a duty to preserve. Transgression breaches that duty, triggering usurper liability. Returning possession to the owner restores the trust nature and ends liability.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle that amana is only guaranteed in cases of ta'addi or negligence.

Core Idea

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explores a critical intersection of two contracts: mortgage (rahn) and lease (ijarah). According to Imam al-Karakhi, if the mortgagor leases the pledged item to the mortgagee, the mortgage is terminated. But why? The underlying reasoning reveals a key legal principle about the incompatibility of usufruct rights with the restrictive nature of a pledge.

The option is his: 'إن شاء ضمن المرتهن... وإن شاء ضمن المستأجر'.
3
Trace the liability chain between parties
If the lessee pays, he can recover from the mortgagee because the mortgagee deceived him. If the mortgagee pays, he can recover only the value of the usufruct from the lessee, not the full value.
Note the asymmetry: the mortgagee does not recover the full compensation from the lessee.
4
Apply the rule for leasing with permission or ratification
When both parties consent (either initially or by later ratification), the lease is valid, the mortgage is voided, and rent belongs to the mortgagor.
Reinforces the core from lesson 1.
5
Analyze the death scenario
If the mortgagor dies, his executor sells the pledge to pay the debt. If no executor and heirs are minors, the judge appoints an executor. Adult heirs take charge themselves.
The text distinguishes between minor and adult heirs.
6
Note the prohibition on re-pledging
The mortgagor cannot pledge the same property again while the first mortgage exists. If he does and the first mortgagee ratifies, the first mortgage is voided.
This prevents double pledging.

Commentary

Rulings on Mortgagor Death and Lease of Mortgaged Property
States rulings after mortgagor's death and leasing effects on mortgage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings when a mortgagor dies, explain the effect of leasing mortgaged property, and distinguish when a mortgage is voided or revived.

Core Idea
Leasing a mortgaged property voids the mortgage; it does not automatically revive.

In this passage, we learn the practical consequences of a mortgage (rahn) after the mortgagor's death and when the property is leased. The Hanafi school holds that the mortgaged property is held in restraint (habs), and any transaction that transfers usufruct may invalidate the mortgage. These rules protect both the mortgagee's security and the mortgagor's ownership rights.

Commentary
This passage contains several rulings from the chapter on mortgage (rahn). First ruling: upon the death of the mortgagor, his executor (wasi) must sell the mortgaged property and use the proceeds to repay the debt secured by the mortgage. If the mortgagor died without appointing an executor, the judge appoints one. However, if the heirs are adults, they themselves are responsible to redeem the property. Second ruling: if the mortgagee takes back possession of the property (e.g., after the debt is paid), it returns to its original mortgaged status. Third ruling: leasing the mortgaged property with mutual permission voids the mortgage because the property leaves the state of being pledged; the rent belongs to the mortgagor as owner, but the right to collect it belongs to the party that made the lease contract. Once the lease ends, the mortgage does not automatically revive; it must be renewed. Fourth ruling: the mortgagor cannot re-mortgage the same property to another person; if he does and the first mortgagee ratifies it, the first mortgage is void and the second takes effect. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi principle that the mortgaged property is in a state of legal restraint (habs), and any transaction inconsistent with that restraint may nullify the mortgage.
