Pre-emption for Non-Monetary Consideration
Rulings on pre-emption for non-monetary considerations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for pre-emption when the sale price is a commodity, a standard good, or another property, by determining whether the pre-emptor pays value (qimah) or equivalent (mithl).

Core Idea
In pre-emption, the payment method matches the fungibility of the sale price: value for non-fungibles, equivalent for fungibles.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) allows a co-owner or neighbor to step into the buyer's place when a property is sold. But what if the buyer paid with a commodity, wheat, or even another property? The classical Hanafi text provides clear rules: the pre-emptor does not simply pay cash, but must give the same kind of consideration as the original buyer. This lesson unpacks those rules and the reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sale consideration
First, classify what the buyer gave in exchange for the property: is it a general commodity ('arad), a measured/weighed good (makil/mawzun), or another piece of real estate?
Commodities are non-fungible; measured/weighed goods are fungible; real estate is also non-fungible but has unique value.
2
Determine the category: thawat al-qimah or thawat al-amthal
Commodities and real estate belong to 'thawat al-qimah' (things valued individually). Measured/weighed goods belong to 'thawat al-amthal' (things that can be replaced by like quantity).
This categorization drives the payment method.
3
Apply the payment rule
For thawat al-qimah, the pre-emptor pays the market value (qimah) of the consideration. For thawat al-amthal, he pays the exact same type and quantity (mithl).
Value is assessed at the time of pre-emption, not the original sale.
4
Handle the real estate barter scenario
If the sale was a barter of two properties, and the pre-emptor has right to both, he must pay the value of each to the respective buyer. If he has right to only one, he pays the value of the other property.
This ensures the pre-emptor does not gain an unfair advantage.

Commentary
This passage is from Kitab al-Shuf'a (Book of Pre-emption) and presents three rulings with their ta'lil (legal reasoning). The first ruling: when a property is sold in exchange for a commodity (e.g., furniture, animals), the pre-emptor may exercise his right by paying the market value (qimah) of that commodity, because commodities are non-fungible and vary in value. The second ruling: when the sale price is a measured (makil) or weighed (mawzun) item (e.g., wheat, oil), the pre-emptor takes the property by paying the same type and quantity (mithl), because such items are fungible and have standard equivalents. The third ruling: when the sale involves a barter of two properties, and the pre-emptor is entitled to both (being co-owner or neighbor to both), he must pay the value of each property to the respective buyer. If he is only entitled to one, he pays the value of the other property. These rulings are based on the nature of the consideration: commodities are valued, standard goods are replaced by equivalents, and real estate is valued. No explicit Quranic or Hadith proof is cited; the reasoning is rational (ta'lil).

مكيل (makil): Good sold by volume, e.g., wheat, barely.
موزون (mawzun): Good sold by weight, e.g., oil, dates.
قيمة (qimah): Market value in monetary terms.
مثل (mithl): Equivalent in type and quantity.

Pre-emption When the Pre-emptor is Misinformed
Rulings on invalid surrender due to deception and handling multiple pre-emptors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a surrender of pre-emption is invalid due to misinformation and distinguish the Hanafi position on price and type differences.

Core Idea
Surrender of pre-emption is only valid with full knowledge; misinformation voids it.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah details several scenarios where a pre-emptor's surrender is invalid because the information he received was false or incomplete. Understanding these rules ensures that the right of pre-emption is not lost through deception, and that surrender is binding only when made with true knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify invalid surrender due to misreported price
If the pre-emptor is told the price is 1000 and surrenders, but later learns it was less, or paid in goods (wheat/barley) worth 1000 or more, his surrender is void. Focus on the condition: the reported sum is higher than the actual price or the actual payment is in a different form (goods) that he may not be able to pay.
This applies even if the goods are worth the same or more, because the ability to pay in goods is different from paying cash.
2
Analyze the rationale of gharar and ability to pay
The commentary explains that there is deception (gharar) because the pre-emptor would not have surrendered had he known the true price or payment type. Moreover, he can pay the lower amount or the goods but not the reported 1000. This rational basis (istihsan) supports the invalidity.
This is a juristic preference over strict adherence to the apparent surrender.
3
Distinguish the case of dinars (gold coins)
If the reported price was 1000 dirhams (silver) but the actual sale was for dinars (gold) worth 1000 or more, the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) hold that the surrender is valid; Zufar disagrees. Examine the reasoning: different genus vs. value equivalence.
The thamarah al-khilaf: if dinars are worth less than 1000, surrender is void by consensus (because value is lower).
4
Recognize misidentification of the buyer
If the pre-emptor is told the buyer is X and surrenders because he is comfortable with X as a neighbor, but the buyer is actually Y, his surrender is void. Similarly, if the buyer is X and Y, his surrender for X is valid but he can take Y's share. This shows that surrender is specific to the person and based on personal preferences.
Also, if he is told only half the property was sold but the whole was sold, his surrender is void due to misrepresentation of the extent.
5
Apply the rule for two pre-emptors
When there are two pre-emptors and one surrenders, the other must take the entire property or none at all. The right is indivisible; the remaining pre-emptor cannot take only a share.
This prevents fragmentation and hardship for the buyer.

Commentary
This passage presents several rulings regarding when a pre-emptor's surrender of his right is invalid due to misinformation. The first ruling: if the pre-emptor is told the sale price is 1000 (dinars or dirhams) and surrenders, but later discovers the price was lower or that the price was wheat/barley worth 1000 or more, his surrender is void. The reason is that he was deceived (gharar) and he might afford the lower price or the goods but not the full 1000. The second ruling: if the price was actually dinars worth 1000, his surrender is valid, but if worth less, it is not. Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) dissents, saying he retains the right in both cases because dinars are a different genus. The third ruling: if the pre-emptor is told the buyer is a specific person (e.g., Zaid) and he surrenders because he accepts that neighbor, but the buyer is actually someone else, his surrender is invalid because his consent was based on the specific individual. Similarly, if he is told the buyer is Zaid but actually it is Zaid and Amr together, his surrender for Zaid is valid but he may still take Amr's share because the surrender did not apply to Amr. Finally, if he is told only half the property was sold and surrenders, but later learns the whole was sold, his surrender is invalid. The overall principle is that surrender based on incorrect or incomplete information is not a valid relinquishment of the right of pre-emption. The commentary uses rational arguments (ability to pay, deception, personal considerations) to support these rulings.

Agent as Opponent in Pre-emption
Explains who stands as opponent when an agent buys for another

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify who is the opponent (khasm) in a shuf'ah claim when an agent purchases property, and state the condition under which the principal becomes the opponent.

Core Idea
The agent who contracts the sale is the opponent until he hands over possession to the principal.

This short but precise passage from the matn clarifies a critical procedural point in the law of pre-emption: who should the pre-emptor name as the defendant when the property was bought by an agent? The rule hinges on who is the contracting party and who retains possession. The commentary expands the matn, explaining the reasoning and the important exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and its Exception
Read the matn: 'Whoever buys a house for another: he is the opponent in pre-emption, unless he hands it over to the principal.' Identify the general rule (the agent is the opponent) and the exception (if he hands over, the principal becomes the opponent).
The exception is introduced by 'illa (unless).
2
Locate the 'Illah (Reason)
The commentary states: 'because he is the contracting party.' This is the legal reason ('illah) for making the agent the opponent. Understand that the shuf'ah claim is against the one who actually made the contract and currently holds the property.
The 'illah is explicit: al-'aqd (the contract).
3
Derive the Legal Consequences
From the rule, deduce that the pre-emptor can take the property from the agent's hand, pay the price to the agent, and the liability for defects ('uhdah) remains on the agent until he hands over.
This protects the pre-emptor and the principal.
4
Apply the Exception
If the agent has already handed over the property to the principal, the agent no longer has possession, so the opponent shifts to the principal. The rule emphasizes possession as a key factor alongside the contract.
The exception is conditional on actual handover (taslim).

Commentary
This passage addresses who the rightful opponent (khasm) in a shuf'ah claim is. The matn establishes that when an agent buys a property for his principal, the agent himself is the opponent in the shuf'ah lawsuit. The reason is that he is the actual contracting party ('aqid). The pre-emptor (shafi') can take the property from the agent's possession, pay the price to him, and the liability ('uhdah) for defects remains on the agent. However, if the agent hands over the property to the principal, then the agent no longer has possession, and the principal becomes the opponent. This is because the opponent is defined by possession of the property, and the agent has relinquished that.

Basis of Pre-emption Price When Substitute Is Given
Ruling and proof on price calculation for pre-emption when substitute given

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on pre-emption when a substitute is given for the price and explain the legal rationale.

Core Idea
Pre-emption attaches to the original price, not to any substitute given later.

Imagine a buyer purchases a property for a set price, but later the seller agrees to accept a different item (like a garment) instead of the cash. If a pre-emptor now wants to exercise their right, what should they pay? This lesson clarifies that the pre-emptor must pay the original price, because pre-emption is built on the consideration of the original sale contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The buyer originally purchases the property for a price (e.g., cash). Later, the buyer and seller agree to substitute that price with a different item (e.g., a garment). This substitution is a separate transaction.
The substitute is not part of the original sale contract.
2
State the ruling
When the pre-emptor (shafi') wishes to take the property, they must pay the original price, not the substitute garment. The substitute is irrelevant for pre-emption.
This is the relied-upon Hanafi position (zahir al-riwayah).
3
Trace the legal reasoning
The reason (illa) is that pre-emption is established only on the consideration ('iwad) that was the subject of the original contract. The garment was acquired through a second, separate contract and is not tied to the pre-emptor's right.
This is a clear qiyas jaliy (manifest analogy).

Legal Tricks to Avoid Pre-emption: The Hanafi Timeline
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hila for shuf'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on using legal tricks to avoid pre-emption and distinguish between the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad based on timing.

Core Idea
Legal tricks are not disliked before obligation but are disliked after.

Legal tricks (hila) in Hanafi fiqh are not always prohibited. When it comes to avoiding obligations like pre-emption, the scholars differed. This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, focusing on the timing of the trick relative to the obligation. You will learn how the fatwa changes before and after the right of pre-emption arises, and how this principle extends to zakat and hajj.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State clearly what each imam holds: Abu Yusuf says no dislike; Muhammad says disliked.
Both are within the Hanafi school.
2
Examine the proof of each
Abu Yusuf: It is merely abstaining from imposing a right on oneself. Muhammad: Pre-emption is to remove harm; tricks perpetuate harm.
The difference is in the underlying objective of the obligation.
3
Apply the fatwa timing distinction
Before the sale (obligation not yet arisen), follow Abu Yusuf; after the sale (obligation arisen), follow Muhammad.
This reconciles the two views.
4
Extend to other obligations
The same dispute applies to zakat and hajj, with fatwa on Muhammad's view after obligation. Also, there is consensus that skipping a sajdah ayah to avoid sajdah is disliked.
This shows consistency in the school's approach.

Ruling on pre-emptor's options when buyer has built or planted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the pre-emptor's options when the buyer has built or planted before the court award, and distinguish the majority Hanafi view from Abu Yusuf's view.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor can force the buyer to remove improvements or take them at removal value — his right precedes the buyer's.

When a buyer builds or plants on land subject to pre-emption before the court decides, a conflict arises between the pre-emptor's right and the buyer's investment. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi ruling, the intra-school disagreement, and the rationale that the pre-emptor's stronger right precedes the buyer's ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The passage begins with the case: buyer builds or plants, then court awards pre-emption. The pre-emptor has a choice (khayar) between two options.
The ruling applies when the improvements are made before the court's decision.
2
State the majority Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Zufar hold that the pre-emptor may either take the land at the original price plus the value of the building or plants as if removed (maqlu'an), or compel the buyer to remove them.

The text argues that the buyer built without authorization from the right-holder, and the pre-emptor's right is stronger because it precedes the buyer's ownership (hence the buyer's sale and gift are void).

If the buyer builds a mosque, the pre-emptor can still take the land and order the mosque demolished. Abu Yusuf dissents, analogizing to a void sale where manumission cannot be undone. The majority responds that the pre-emptor's right was established before the mosque existed.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A pre-emptor recovers only the price, not improvement costs, because recovery requires deception, which is absent in pre-emption.

This lesson examines a key ruling in pre-emption (shuf'ah). When a pre-emptor takes property, improves it, and then a third party proves rightful ownership, the pre-emptor can only recover the purchase price—not the value of buildings or plants. Guided Path

Understand the Scenario

The matn rules: the pre-emptor recovers only the price (thaman) he paid, not the value of improvements (qimat al-bina wa al-ghars).
This is the mufta bihi position with no intra-Hanafi dispute.

The commentary explains that recovery of improvement value is tied to deception (ghurur). The seller who sells someone else's property deceives the buyer. In pre-emption, the pre-emptor takes by right, not through a sale from the claimant, so no deception exists.
The passage cites consensus (ijma') that an ordinary buyer deceived by the seller recovers improvement value; this proves the rule by contrast.
4

An ordinary buyer who builds or plants and then the property is claimed recovers improvement value from the seller because the seller deceived him by selling and delivering property not his own. The pre-emptor has no such claim against the seller or original buyer because they did not grant him ownership.
This contrast highlights the 'illah (effective cause): deception.
5

The practical takeaway: pre-emptors should be cautious about making significant improvements to property taken through shuf'ah, as they risk losing improvement costs if the property is later claimed. There is no comeback against the seller for improvements.

Effect of Rescission on the Pre-emptor's Right in Shuf'ah
Ruling on pre-emption after rescission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether pre-emption (shufʿah) survives after a rescission of sale, distinguishing between mutual rescission (taqāyul) and court-ordered rescission due to an option or defect.

Core Idea
Mutual rescission preserves pre-emption; court-ordered rescission extinguishes it — the nature of the rescission determines the right.

This passage examines a subtle point in the law of pre-emption: what happens to the pre-emptor's right when the sale is later rescinded? The answer depends on how the rescission occurs. Al-Karkhi and al-Hidayah offer complementary rulings that hinge on whether the rescission is a new sale or a complete dissolution. By tracing their reasoning, you will see how Hanafi jurists carefully classify legal acts to protect rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize that the passage discusses two types of rescission: mutual rescission (taqāyul) and court-ordered rescission (faskh bi qaḍā) due to an option or defect.
Taqāyul is by agreement; judicial rescission is by court order.
2
State al-Karkhi's ruling on taqāyul
Al-Karkhi holds that regardless of whether possession has taken place, the pre-emptor retains the right because taqāyul is a new sale, not a pure dissolution.

Al-Hidayah rules that if the buyer returns the property due to an option (sight or condition) or a defect, and a judge decrees the return, the rescission is a complete dissolution (faskh min kulli wajh) and eliminates the pre-emptor's right.
No distinction between before and after possession.

Examine the middle case: defect without qaḍā
If the buyer returns the property due to a defect without a court order, the pre-emptor's right survives only if the rescission occurs after possession. Before possession, it is a dissolution from the root (faskh min al-aṣl) and the right is lost.
This is the distinctive position of al-Hidayah not mentioned by al-Karkhi.

Apply the rule
Given a scenario, determine whether a rescission preserves or extinguishes the pre-emptor's right by classifying it as a new sale (taqāyul), judicial faskh, or defect-based non-judicial faskh after possession.
The key is whether the rescission is consensual (new sale) or a judicial dissolution.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The agent alone has the right to demand the price; the principal cannot unless the agent permits.

Agency (wakalah) in sale transfers the right to collect the price to the agent, not the principal. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the procedural rules: the principal cannot demand payment directly, but the agent can authorize him. Even if the agent forbids the principal from collecting, any payment made to the principal discharges the buyer by istihsan.

The passage involves the principal (al-muwakkil), the agent (al-wakil), and the buyer. Understand their roles: the principal owns the goods, the agent is appointed to sell, and the buyer purchases.

The default ruling: the principal has no right to demand the price. Only the agent has that right, as the direct party to the sale.

Explains requirements for specifying the object in a purchase agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for specifying the subject matter in a purchase agency and apply them to determine whether a given agency is valid.

Core Idea
In a purchase agency, the subject matter must be sufficiently specified to avoid excessive ignorance (jahalah fahishah).

When someone appoints an agent to buy something, the object must be known clearly. This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains the minimum specifications required: either the genus and type, or the genus and price. The key is to eliminate uncertainty so the agent understands exactly what to purchase.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The principal must specify either the genus (jins) and type (sifah/naw‘), or the genus and the price. This makes the authorized action known to the agent.
Genus: broad category (e.g., slave). Type: specific kind (e.g., Abyssinian). Price: amount in currency.
2
Distinguish Between Genera and Types
Some words cover multiple distinct genera (e.g., 'riding animal' includes horses, donkeys, mules). Others cover multiple types under one genus (e.g., 'slave' includes various ethnicities). The rule differs: for multiple genera, both genus and type are required even if price is specified; for multiple types, price or type suffices.
'Type' here refers to the specific description within the genus.
3
Apply to Terms Covering Multiple Genera
If the word includes different genera (like 'garment', 'house'), specifying the price alone is insufficient. The type must also be mentioned. Example: 'Buy me a house for 500 dirhams' is invalid without specifying the type of house.
The reason is that with a price, items from every genus exist, causing excessive uncertainty (jahalah fahishah).
4
Apply to Terms Covering Multiple Types Under One Genus
If the word covers types within a single genus (like 'slave' or 'female slave'), then specifying either the price or the type is sufficient. For example, 'Buy me a slave for 100 dirhams' is valid because the price indicates the type available in that range.
Mentioning the type also reduces uncertainty enough.
5
Determine Validity Based on Specification
If the principal fails to specify genus, type, or price appropriately, the agency is invalid due to excessive ignorance. Use the rules above to test a given scenario.
Always check if the term spans multiple genera or multiple types first.

Agency in Sarf and Salam: Possession and Separation
Ruling with proof on agency in sarf and salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency in sarf and salam, identify the condition of possession before separation, and distinguish between agent and messenger.

Core Idea
In sarf and salam, agency voids if the agent separates before possession; the messenger cannot perfect the contract.

The text from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the nuanced rules of agency in two special sale contracts: currency exchange (sarf) and advance payment sale (salam). Understanding these rules prevents common errors in transactions involving immediate exchange and future delivery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling on agency
Recognize that agency (wakalah) is permissible for any contract the principal can perform themselves. This is the baseline rule for sarf and salam.

2

Agency in salam is only valid from the buyer (rabb al-salam). The seller cannot appoint an agent to accept salam because that would involve selling on credit with the price for a third party.
The text explicitly forbids agency from the seller's side.

In both sarf and salam, the agent (contracting party) must take possession before parting from the other party. If the agent separates before possession, the contract becomes void (batil).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A contract by an agent in the principal's absence needs ratification; but a purchase made without permission binds the agent, not the principal.

The commentator then deepens the rule: this applies only to sales; an unauthorized purchase by the agent is binding on the agent himself. Dismissal of an Agent: Rules and Notification
Detailed ruling on dismissal of an agent: methods, notification, and constructive dismissal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid dismissal of an agent and distinguish between actual and constructive dismissal.

Core Idea
Dismissal requires the agent's knowledge, except in automatic cases like death or loss of the subject matter.

Dismissing an agent is a right of the principal, but it is not always effective immediately. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains when a dismissal actually terminates the agency and when it does not, covering written notice, messengers, witnesses, and even automatic dismissal. Understanding this distinction is crucial to determine the validity of an agent's actions after a purported dismissal.

1
Identify the general rule and its exception
The text begins with the principle that a principal may dismiss the agent anytime because agency is his right. However, an exception exists when a third party's right is attached, such as in a pledge where the agent's authority is part of the pledge contract.

Examine the methods of effective notification
Dismissal must reach the agent. This can be through a letter that he reads, a messenger of any description, or two witnesses (or one trustworthy) who inform him. If the agent does not receive the news, he remains an agent and his actions are valid.
The text emphasizes that even if the principal takes witnesses but does not inform the agent, it is ineffective.

Certain events automatically terminate the agency without requiring the agent's knowledge: death of the principal, loss of ownership of the subject matter (e.g., selling the slave or setting him free). If the subject matter returns by rescission, the agency revives; if by new acquisition, it does not.
This is an exception to the general requirement of notification.

And they (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: It is permissible for him to sell to them at the like of the value, except for his slave and his mukatib. Chapter of Agency. Likewise, whoever's testimony is not accepted for him (the principal); because the agent is trusted, so if he sells to these persons, suspicion attaches to him because the benefits between him and these persons are connected. Ijarah and Sarf are upon this difference. He said: (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: It is permissible for him to sell to them at the like of the value, except for his slave and his mukatib) because the authorization is absolute, and there is no suspicion because the properties are distinct. Contrary to the slave, because it is a sale from himself, for what is in the slave's hand belongs to the master. Likewise, the master has a right in the earnings of the mukatib, and he becomes a (slave) by inability. In his statement: 'at the like of the value' is an indication that it is not permissible in their view also in slight fraud (ghubn yasir), otherwise there would be no benefit in the specification. This is in al-Nihayah. But it is mentioned in al-Dhakhirah that selling to them with slight fraud is permissible in their view. He said in al-Dhakhirah: The agent for sale, if he sells to one whose testimony is not accepted for him: if it is for more than the value, it is permissible without dispute. If it is for less with gross fraud: it is not permissible, without dispute. If it is with slight fraud: it is not permissible according to Abu Hanifah, and in their view it is permissible. If it is at the like of the value: from Abu Hanifah there are two narrations. If the principal ordered him to sell to these persons, or said: 'Sell to whomever it is permissible to sell to from these persons,' it is permissible by consensus, except that he sells to himself, or to his minor child, or to his slave who has no debt: then it is absolutely not permissible even if the principal explicitly authorized him to do so. In al-Mabsut, it is bound to the slave who has no debt, as if there is an indication that if he is in debt, selling to him is permissible when the will is general. Likewise is the ruling for the agent for purchase if he buys from these persons. If he authorized him to marry him a woman, and the agent married him his daughter: if she is a minor, it is not permissible by consensus. If she is an adult, likewise it is not permissible according to Abu Hanifah, and in their view it is permissible. Similarly, if the agent married him his slave woman, or one whose testimony is not accepted for him, it is upon this difference. If he married him his sister or one whose testimony is accepted for him, it is permissible by consensus. He said: (The agent for sale is allowed to sell for little or much according to Abu Hanifah). Likewise with goods, because his order to sell is general, and the rule of wording is to apply it generally, and this is according to Abu Hanifah. The dispute is in the absolute agency; but if he said: 'Sell it for one hundred or a thousand,' it is not permissible to decrease by consensus.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah, a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, discusses the ruling of an agent (wakil) who sells to persons whose testimony is not accepted for the principal (such as close relatives, since their testimony would be rejected due to interest). The text begins by stating that the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) permit the agent to sell to such persons provided it is at the fair market value (like value), except selling to his own slave or his mukatib (a slave under contract of manumission). The reason given is that the agency is absolute (mutlaq) and there is no suspicion of favoritism because the properties are distinct. The exception for the slave is because it is effectively a sale to himself, and for the mukatib because the master has a right in his earnings and may become a slave again if unable to pay. The text then discusses the issue of ghubn (fraud or undervaluation). According to al-Nihayah, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad also do not allow sale with slight fraud (ghubn yasir) under this ruling, but al-Dhakhirah mentions that they allow slight fraud. The dispute is clearly outlined: if the agent sells for more than value, it is unanimously permitted; if for less with gross fraud, it is unanimously prohibited; with slight fraud, Abu Hanifah prohibits while the two companions allow; at like value, there are two narrations from Abu Hanifah. Next, the passage covers the case where the principal explicitly orders the agent to sell to such persons: it is permissible by consensus, except when the agent sells to himself, his minor child, or his slave without debt; these are absolutely prohibited even if authorized. A nuance from al-Mabsut: if the slave has debts, selling to him may be permissible under a general authorization. The same rulings apply when the agent buys from such persons. The passage then transitions to agency in marriage: if the agent is authorized to marry a woman to the principal, and he marries him his own daughter, if she is a minor, it is invalid by consensus; if adult, it is invalid according to Abu Hanifah but valid according to the two companions. The same disagreement applies if the agent marries him his slave woman or a woman whose testimony is not accepted. Marriage to the agent's sister or a woman whose testimony is accepted is valid by consensus. Finally, the text returns to sale agency, stating that according to Abu Hanifah, the agent may sell for little or much, while the two companions require like value. This is because the order to sell is general, and the principle is that a general wording applies to all its individuals. This only applies to absolute agency; if the principal specifies a price, the agent cannot decrease from it by consensus.

Key Terms
الوكيل (al-wakīl): The agent appointed by the principal (الموكل) to perform a legal act (here, selling) on his behalf.
الغبن (al-ghubn): Fraud or deception in price; selling for less than the market value (or buying for more). It is classified as fāḥish (gross) or yasīr (slight).
المكاتب (al-mukātib): A slave who has entered into a contract of kitābah with his master to earn and pay for his freedom in installments.

Key Takeaway
The agent's authority under general authorization is broad according to Abu Hanifah, but the Sahibayn restrict it to protect the principal from potential fraud; both views are respected within the Hanafi school.
