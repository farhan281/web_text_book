Ownership During Option: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Explains ownership dispute, maintenance, and disposal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on ownership of the sold item during khiyar al-shart and explain the practical consequences.

Core Idea
Ownership of the sold item during option is contested: Abu Hanifa says no, Sahibayn says yes, affecting liability for loss.

This lesson examines the foundational dispute within the Hanafi school on whether the buyer owns the sold item during the option period. The discussion reveals how legal reasoning (istidlal) can diverge even within the same school, with concrete effects on who bears the risk of destruction and who is entitled to increases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Pinpoint that the disagreement is between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) on the buyer's ownership of the sold item during khiyar al-shart.
The option is for the buyer.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa: the sold item does not enter the buyer's ownership. His proof: the price remains in the buyer's ownership; owning both countervalues is invalid in a contract of exchange.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli).
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
The Sahibayn: the sold item does enter the buyer's ownership. Their proof: if the buyer does not own it, the item would be ownerless, which has no precedent in Shari'a.
They argue that the item must have an owner.
4
Identify the consensus points
Both sides agree that maintenance is on the buyer and the buyer may dispose of the item, such disposal being acceptance.
This shows practical agreement despite theoretical disagreement.
5
Recognize the practical difference
If the item is destroyed during the option period, Abu Hanifa places loss on the seller; the Sahibayn place it on the buyer.
This thamarah al-khilaf is critical for liability.

عند أبي حنيفة: لا يدخل، وعندهما يدخل، وتجب نفقته على المشتري، بالإجماع، إذا كان الخيار له؛ لأنه قد خرج من ملك البائع. والثمن لا يخرج من ملك المشتري بالإجماع. وإنما لم يدخل المبيعُ في ملك المشتري عند أبي حنيفة؛ لأن الثمن باق على ملكه، فلو ملك المبيع لاجتمع في ملكه العوضان، وهذا لا يصح. وهما يقولان: المبيعُ قد خرج من ملك البائع، فلو لم يملكه المشتري يكون زائلاً لا إلى مالك، ولا عهد لنا به في الشرع.
ولو تصرف المشتري في المبيع في مدة الخيار، والخيار له: جاز تصرفه، إجماعاً، ويكون إجازة منه.
- ثم إذا كان الخيار للمشتري: فنفوذ البيع بأربعة معان: أحدها: أن يقول: أجزتُ، سواء كان البائع حاضراً أو غائباً. والثاني: أن يموت المشتري في مدة الخيار، فيبطل خياره بموته، وينفذ عقده، ولا تقوم الورثة مقامه، ولا يكون موروثاً عنه. والثالث: أن تمضي مدة الخيار من غير فسخ ممن له الخيار. والرابع: أن يصير المبيع في يد المشتري إلى حال لا يملك المشتري فسخه، مثل أن يهلك المبيع، أو ينتقص في يد المشتري نقصاناً يسيراً أو فاحشاً بفعل المشتري، أو بفعل البائع، أو بآفة سماوية، أو بفعل الأجنبي، أو بفعل المعقود عليه فإنه يبطل خياره، وينفذ البيع.
- وإذا زاد المبيع في مدة الخيار في قبض المشتري زيادة متصلة متولدة من الأصل، كالسِّمَن والبُرء من المرض: منعت الرد والفسخ، وبطل خياره، ونفذ البيع عندهما، كالنقصان. وعند محمد: لا يمنع الردَّ، وهو على خياره. وإن كانت متصلةً غير متولدة منه، كالصَّبغ والخياطة، ولت السويق، أو كانت أرضاً فبنى فيها، أو غرس: منعت الرد، إجماعاً، وينفذ البيع. فإن كانت منفصلةً متولدة منه، كالولد واللبن والثمر والأرش والعقر: منعت الرد أيضاً، وبطل خياره، ونفذ البيع. وإن كانت منفصلةً غير متولدة منه كالكسب والهبة والغلة: لا يمنع الرد، وهو على خياره، إلا أنه إذا اختار البيع: فالزيادة له إجماعاً، وإن اختار الفسخ يردُّ الأصل مع الزيادة عند أبي حنيفة. وقال أبو يوسف ومحمد: يرد الأصل لا غير، والزيادة للمشتري؛ لأن من مذهبهما أن المبيع يدخل في ملكه، وعند أبي حنيفة لا يدخل في ملكه، فتكون الزوائد حاصلةً في ملك البائع، فيلزمه ردُّها إليه. وأما فسخه إذا كان الخيار للمشتري: فهو بأحد أمرين: إما بالقول أو بالفعل، فبالقول: لا يصح إلا بحضرة البائع عندهما. وقال أبو يوسف: يصح بغير حضوره.
Chapter of Option of Stipulation. According to Abu Hanifa: it does not enter (the ownership of the buyer), and according to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) it does enter, and its maintenance is obligatory upon the buyer, by consensus, when the option is his; because it has left the ownership of the seller. And the price does not leave the ownership of the buyer by consensus. The sold item did not enter the ownership of the buyer according to Abu Hanifa only because the price remains in his ownership; if he owned the sold item, both countervalues would be in his ownership, and this is not valid. And they (the two) say: the sold item has left the ownership of the seller; if the buyer does not own it, it would be ownerless, and we have no precedent for that in the Shari'a. If the buyer disposes of the sold item during the option period, and the option is his, his disposal is valid by consensus, and it is considered acceptance. Then if the option is for the buyer, the binding of the sale occurs in four ways: first, that he says 'I accept,' whether the seller is present or absent. Second, that the buyer dies during the option period; his option is voided by his death, and his contract becomes binding, and the heirs do not stand in his place, nor is it inherited from him. Third, that the option period passes without rescission by the one who has the option. Fourth, that the sold item comes into the hands of the buyer in a state where the buyer cannot rescind, such as the sold item is destroyed or suffers a deficiency in the buyer's hands, whether slight or great, by the buyer's act, the seller's act, a natural disaster, the act of a third party, or the act of the subject matter itself; then his option is voided and the sale is binding. And if the sold item increases during the option period while in the buyer's possession, with an increase that is connected and originates from the original, such as fatness or recovery from illness, it prevents return and rescission, and his option is voided, and the sale is binding according to them (the two), like decrease. According to Muhammad, it does not prevent return, and he remains on his option. If it is connected but not originating from it, such as dyeing, tailoring, grinding of meal, or if it is land and he builds or plants, it prevents return by consensus, and the sale becomes binding. If it is separate and originating from it, such as offspring, milk, fruit, compensation for injury, and dower, it also prevents return, and his option is voided, and the sale is binding. If it is separate and not originating from it, such as earnings, gift, and rent, it does not prevent return, and he remains on his option, except that if he chooses the sale, the increase is for him by consensus; and if he chooses rescission, he returns the original with the increase according to Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: he returns only the original, and the increase is for the buyer; because according to their doctrine, the sold item enters his ownership, whereas according to Abu Hanifa it does not enter his ownership, so the increases occur in the ownership of the seller, so he must return them to him. As for his rescission when the option is for the buyer, it is by one of two things: either by statement or by action. By statement: it is not valid except in the presence of the seller according to them (the two). Abu Yusuf said: it is valid in his absence.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on Option of Stipulation (Khiyar al-Shart) in sales, as explained by the commentator (likely Ibn Nujaym? Actually it's from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira), covers several key rulings. The passage opens with a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the ownership of the sold item during the option period when the option is granted to the buyer. Abu Hanifa holds that the sold item does not enter the buyer's ownership, because if it did, both the sold item and the price would be in the buyer's ownership simultaneously, which is not permissible in a contract of exchange. The Sahibayn argue that the sold item has left the seller's ownership, and if the buyer does not own it, it would become ownerless (mubāḥ), which has no precedent in Shari'a. The commentator notes that maintenance of the sold item during this period is the buyer's obligation by consensus, because it has left the seller's ownership regardless of whether the buyer owns it. The passage then states unanimously that the buyer may dispose of the sold item if the option is his, and such disposition is considered acceptance (ijāzah) of the sale. Next, four ways are enumerated in which the sale becomes binding (nāfidh) when the option is for the buyer: (1) explicit verbal acceptance; (2) death of the buyer during the option period (the option is not inherited); (3) expiration of the option period without rescission; (4) occurrence of a situation that prevents rescission, such as destruction or any decrease (even slight) in the sold item by any cause. The discussion then turns to increases in the sold item during the option period. Increases are categorized based on whether they are connected or separate from the original, and whether they originate from it or not. For connected increases originating from the original (e.g., fatness or recovery), the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule that they prevent rescission and the sale becomes binding, while Muhammad holds a distinct view that they do not prevent rescission. Connected increases not originating from the original (e.g., dyeing or tailoring) prevent rescission by consensus. Separate increases originating from the original (e.g., offspring, milk, fruit) also prevent rescission by consensus. Separate increases not originating from the original (e.g., earnings, gift, rent) do not prevent rescission; the buyer retains his option. However, if the buyer chooses to complete the sale, the increase is his by consensus. If he chooses rescission, a dispute arises: Abu Hanifa requires returning the original with the increase, because he holds that the increase occurs in the seller's ownership (since the sold item was not owned by the buyer). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad require returning only the original, with the increase belonging to the buyer, because they hold that the sold item was owned by the buyer. Finally, the passage addresses rescission by the buyer when the option is his: it can be by statement (in the presence of the seller according to the Sahibayn; Abu Yusuf permits it in the seller's absence) or by action (not detailed here).

Key Terms
خيار الشرط (khiyār al-sharṭ): A stipulated option in a contract of sale allowing one or both parties to rescind within a specified period.

Four Ways the Sale Becomes Binding
Lists the four binding events.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four ways a sale becomes binding when the option is for the buyer and apply them to scenarios.

Core Idea
The sale becomes binding by acceptance, death, expiry, or any situation that prevents rescission.

Once the buyer has the option, the sale remains suspended until one of four events occur. This lesson enumerates those events, showing how the contract moves from revocable to binding. Understanding these helps practitioners know when the option is extinguished.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four ways
The text lists four ways: (1) explicit acceptance, (2) death of the buyer, (3) expiry of the period, (4) impossibility of return (e.g., destruction or deficiency).
2
Analyze acceptance
The buyer says 'I accept' – valid whether the seller is present or absent. This is a verbal ijabah.
This is the most straightforward.
3
Analyze death
Death of the buyer voids his option; the contract becomes binding. The option is not inherited.
The heirs cannot exercise the option.
4
Analyze expiry
If the period passes without rescission, the sale becomes binding.
Silence implies acceptance.
5
Analyze impossibility of return
If the item is destroyed or suffers any deficiency (by any cause), the option is voided and the sale binding.
Even slight deficiency counts.

Increases During Option: Categories and Effects
Categorizes increases and their effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to categorize increases (ziyadat) during the option period and state whether they prevent rescission according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The effect of an increase on the option depends on whether it is connected/separate and whether it originates from the original item.

During the option period, the sold item may increase in value or produce offspring, fruits, etc. This lesson categorizes increases and explains which ones prevent the buyer from rescinding. The categorization is based on two criteria: connectedness to the original and origin (whether it arises from the original or not). Understanding this is crucial for knowing if the option remains.

Guided Path
1
Understand the categories
Increases are divided by two dichotomies: muttaṣil/munfaṣil (connected/separate) and mutawallid min al-aṣl/ghayr mutawallid (originating from the original or not).
This yields four categories.
2
Connected originating increases
e.g., fatness, recovery from illness. According to Sahibayn: prevents rescission; contract binding. According to Muhammad: does not prevent.
Intra-Hanafi dispute.
3
Connected non-originating increases
e.g., dyeing, tailoring, building. They prevent rescission by consensus.
Unanimous.
4
Separate originating increases
e.g., offspring, milk, fruit. They also prevent rescission by consensus.
Unanimous.
5
Separate non-originating increases
e.g., earnings, gift, rent. They do not prevent rescission; buyer retains option.
But if buyer chooses sale, increase is his; if rescission, dispute on returning increase.

This passage from the chapter on Option of Stipulation (Khiyar al-Shart) in sales, as explained by the commentator (likely Ibn Nujaym? Actually it's from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira), covers several key rulings. The Sahibayn argue that the sold item has left the seller's ownership, and if the buyer does not own it, it would become ownerless (mubāḥ), which has no precedent in Shari'a. The commentator notes that maintenance of the sold item during this period is the buyer's obligation by consensus, because it has left the seller's ownership regardless of whether the buyer owns it. The passage then states unanimously that the buyer may dispose of the sold item if the option is his, and such disposition is considered acceptance (ijāzah) of the sale. Next, four ways are enumerated in which the sale becomes binding (nāfidh) when the option is for the buyer: (1) explicit verbal acceptance; (2) death of the buyer during the option period (the option is not inherited); (3) expiration of the option period without rescission; (4) occurrence of a situation that prevents rescission, such as destruction or any decrease (even slight) in the sold item by any cause. Increases are categorized based on whether they are connected or separate from the original, and whether they originate from it or not. Connected increases not originating from the original (e.g., dyeing or tailoring) prevent rescission by consensus. Separate increases originating from the original (e.g., offspring, milk, fruit) also prevent rescission by consensus. Separate increases not originating from the original (e.g., earnings, gift, rent) do not prevent rescission; the buyer retains his option. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Rescission can be by statement or action; but by statement, the Sahibayn require the seller's presence, while Abu Yusuf allows it in absence.

The final part of the passage discusses how the buyer can rescind the contract if he has the option. The text mentions two methods: by statement (qawl) and by action (fiʿl). Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ownership During Khiyar al-Shart
Explains khiyar al-shart annulment methods and dispute on buyer's ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on buyer ownership during khiyar al-shart, explain the Sahibayn's counter-argument, and apply the dispute to two practical cases.

Core Idea
The buyer does not own the object during khiyar al-shart according to Abu Hanifa, because both substitutes cannot be in one ownership.

In this lesson, we explore a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: does the buyer own the purchased object during the option period? Abu Hanifa and his two companions disagree, and the practical consequences affect issues like freeing of relatives and validity of marriages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Pinpoint the exact legal question: whether the buyer acquires ownership of the purchased object during the khiyar al-shart period.
The contract is valid but ownership is suspended; the disagreement is on the status during the option.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's dalil
Abu Hanifa argues that since the price remains in the buyer's ownership, the object cannot also enter his ownership—that would unite both substitutes in one person, which is contrary to the nature of exchange.
The principle: 'al-mu'awada taqtadi al-musawa' (exchange demands separation).
3
Examine the Sahibayn's dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that because the object left the seller's ownership, it must enter the buyer's; otherwise it would be ownerless, which is not permissible.
Their reasoning: 'za'ilan la ila malik' is invalid.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify practical consequences: (1) buying a mahram relative—does he become free? (2) buying one's wife—is the marriage invalidated? Abu Hanifa says no to both; the Sahibayn say yes.
These cases illustrate how the ownership dispute directly affects other rulings.
5
Note the agreed case
All scholars agree that if someone says to a slave 'If I buy you, you are free' and buys him with khiyar, the slave becomes free and the option is void. Understand Abu Hanifa's explanation: conditional emancipation is like immediate upon condition fulfillment.
This shows that even in disagreement, there is common ground.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar covers the topic of khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation) in sales. It begins by explaining how the contract is annulled by the seller's action when the price is a specific object: if he acts upon it like an owner, the contract is annulled. Then it details the three ways the seller can validate the sale during the option period: (1) explicit ratification by saying 'I have ratified,' (2) the seller's death during the period, which makes the option void and the contract binding, and (3) the expiry of the period without annulment or ratification. The seller can annul the contract by speech or by action. If he annuls in the buyer's presence, it is effective without need for court or consent. If in the buyer's absence, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the contract becomes valid if the buyer does not learn of the annulment until after the period; Abu Yusuf holds the annulment is valid regardless. There is consensus that ratification in the buyer's absence is valid. Ownership During Khiyar al-Shart: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Dispute on ownership during khiyar al-shart

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on ownership during khiyar al-shart and identify the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf) of the dispute.

Core Idea
In khiyar al-shart, ownership is suspended — Abu Hanifa blocks both counter-values in one hand; the Sahibayn block ownerless property.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about the moment ownership transfers in a sale with an option period (khiyar al-shart). Both Abu Hanifa and his two major disciples agree the contract is valid, but they differ on whether the buyer owns the item during the option period. Their disagreement stems from two different rational principles: one avoids concentration of both counter-values in one person, the other avoids ownerless property. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to trace how fundamental principles generate divergent rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa holds that the buyer does not own the purchased item during the option period. The sale is valid, but ownership is suspended until the option is confirmed or expires.
The matn explicitly says 'la yamlikuhu' (he does not own it).
2
Identify the Sahibayn position and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the buyer does own the item during the option period. Their proof: the item has left the seller's ownership; if it does not enter the buyer's ownership, it would be ownerless property (zā'ilan lā ilā mālik), which is impermissible in Shari'ah.
The text says 'li'annahu lammā kharaja 'an mulki al-bā'i' dakhala fī mulki al-mushtarī'.
3
Analyse Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that since the price (thaman) has not left the buyer's ownership (because it is still due or held by the buyer), if the sold item also entered his ownership, both counter-values ('iwadayn) would be in the possession of one person. This violates the principle of equality (musāwāh) required in commutative contracts (mu'āwaḍah).
The text says 'law ijtama'a al-badalāni fī mulki rajulin wāhid, lā aṣla lahu fī al-shar'.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference affects manumission of relatives, dissolution of marriage, and istibrā' of slave-girls during the option period. For example, if the buyer manumits the purchased slave during the option, it is valid only if he owns it (Sahibayn); Abu Hanifa says it is not valid because he does not own.
These consequences are mentioned in the source's madhhab dispute map.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The passage does not give a tarjih, but later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn's view in commercial transactions because it avoids ownerless property and aligns with market practice.
Authority weight: both views are from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Destruction During Khiyar – Obligation to Pay the Agreed Price
Explains liability for destroyed goods during khiyar and defines thaman vs. qeemah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on destruction of goods during khiyar and distinguish between thaman and qeemah in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
If goods perish during khiyar, the buyer must pay the agreed price, not the market value.

This lesson focuses on a key ruling in sale contracts: when a purchased item is destroyed while in the buyer's possession during the option period (khiyar), the buyer becomes liable to pay the agreed price. The commentator clarifies the difference between the contractual price (thaman) and the market value (qeemah), a distinction essential for understanding Hanafi sales law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the perishing (halak) of the sold item in the buyer's hand while the buyer has khiyar. The ruling is that the buyer must pay the thaman (agreed price), because he is unable to return the item.
The key phrase: 'فإن هلك في يده : هلك بالثمن' – if it perishes in his hand, it perishes with the price.
2
Distinguish thaman from qeemah
Thaman is the price mutually agreed upon by the parties, whether above or below market value. Qeemah is the objective market valuation, used as a standard. The distinction matters because the obligation here is based on thaman, not qeemah.
Hanafi law respects the parties' agreement over market fluctuations.
3
Contrast with destruction in seller's hand
If the item perishes with the seller before the buyer takes possession, the sale is void (batal) – no payment is due.
This shows that possession (qabd) transfers risk.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Buyer A purchases a car for $20,000 with a 3-day inspection option. On day 2, the car is destroyed in A's garage. A must pay $20,000, even if the market value has dropped to $18,000.

Matn ruling on umm walad with khiyar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on when a slave-girl purchased with khiyar becomes umm walad and explain the practical consequence (thamarah) of the dispute.

Core Idea
A slave-girl who bears her buyer's child becomes umm walad only after he confirms the sale according to Abu Hanifa, but immediately upon purchase according to the Sahibayn.

This passage presents two rulings related to khiyar al-shart in the sale of slave-girls. The first, on which there is consensus, requires the buyer to wait for one menstrual period (istibra') after confirming the sale. The second is a point of disagreement within the Hanafi school: when does a slave-girl who has already borne the buyer's child become umm walad if purchased with a stipulation of option? Under Abu Hanifa, the buyer can still reject the sale during the option period. Under the Sahibayn, the sale is immediately binding, so the buyer cannot return her and must pay the price. Additionally, the slave-girl is freed upon the master's death in both views, but the timing differs.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the item perishes in your hand during the option period, you must pay the agreed price, not the market value.

When you buy something with a stipulation of option (khiyar al-shart), the item is in your possession but not yet final. What happens if it perishes or gets a defect? The Hanafi school has clear rules that tie liability to the inability to return. Identify the scenario

Apply the rule for perishing
If the item perishes in the buyer's hand while the buyer has the option, the buyer must pay the agreed price (thaman) because he is unable to return it.

If the option period expires with the defect still present, the sale becomes binding.

Actions Indicating Acceptance in Khiyar al-Shart
Commentary on actions that nullify the option by showing consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which actions with desire nullify the buyer's option in khiyar al-shart and explain the Hanafi proof for why consent is inferred from such actions.

Core Idea
Any action with desire on the sold item constitutes acceptance and voids the option.

When a buyer stipulates an option of cancellation (khiyar al-shart), he must avoid any action that implies acceptance of the sale. The Hanafi school defines specific actions—intercourse, kissing, touching, or looking with desire—as implicit ratification. Even actions performed by a slave-girl, if done with desire and confirmed, are considered consent according to the majority. This lesson unpacks these actions, the definition of desire, and the intra-madhhab dispute regarding the slave-girl's conduct.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Buyer's Actions and the Condition of Desire
List the examples given: intercourse, kissing, touching, and looking at private parts. Note that each action must be accompanied by desire (shahwa) to count as acceptance.
Without desire, such as looking without desire, it is not acceptance.
2
Define Desire (Shahwa)
Understand the two definitions: physical arousal (erection or increase) or inner inclination without physical signs.
The first definition is more objective; the second allows for subjective states.
3
Distinguish Between Buyer's Direct Actions and the Slave-Girl's Actions
The buyer's direct actions with desire are acceptance. The slave-girl's actions (kissing, touching, looking) with desire are also acceptance according to Abu Hanifa, provided she confirms the desire.
Muhammad disagrees on the slave-girl's actions.
4
Examine the Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Compare Abu Hanifa's view (slave-girl's actions = acceptance) with Muhammad's view (no, because the buyer himself has not shown consent).
The dispute is over whether the slave-girl's actions can be attributed to the buyer.
5
Recognize the Stronger Actions
Actions like full intercourse, lying beside, or direct contact (without barrier) void the option even if the buyer is coerced, because they are more than a kiss. This is proven by qiyas: if a kiss nullifies, more intense actions do so a fortiori.
Abu Hanifa holds this view for both buyer and slave-girl actions.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah, a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, discusses the rules regarding actions that indicate acceptance (rida) and thereby invalidate the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart) in a sale. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Istihsan prevails over qiyas when practical necessity overcomes strict analogy.

In Hanafi sales law, certain actions by the buyer during the option period clearly indicate acceptance of the sale. However, a borderline case arises when the buyer rides the animal solely to water it or return it to the seller. While strict analogy (qiyas) suggests this is acceptance, juristic preference (istihsan) rules otherwise based on practical difficulty. Istihsan gives weight to practical reality over strict analogy.

The Hanafi school relies on istihsan. Thus, riding to water or return the animal does not end the option period. This protects the buyer from unintentionally losing the right of return.

When Partial Inspection Cancels the Option of Inspection
Explains when inspection of part negates the option of rescission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on when partial sight of a purchased item cancels the option of inspection and apply them to different categories of goods.

Core Idea
Seeing the essential part of an item cancels the option; what is essential varies by item.

In Hanafi sales law, the buyer has the right to rescind the contract after seeing the purchased item if it is not as desired. This lesson explores a key nuance: when inspecting only a part of the item is sufficient to remove that right. The source passage, from the sharh of al-Quduri, specifies which parts of common items are considered 'essential' and why, and also addresses a critical intra-madhhab dispute regarding houses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the matn: seeing the face of a pile, outer of a garment, face of a slave, or face and hindquarters of an animal removes the option. Note that the ruling applies primarily to sales for a price (cash); in barter, both parties have the option.
The barter exception is important but ancillary.
2
Determine the 'illa (effective cause) for each item
For each item, the commentary explains why the part seen is sufficient: pile uniformity, garment outer indicating inner, face being the essential part of a human, face and hindquarters being the essential parts of a dābba. The 'illa is that the seen part represents the whole in terms of the purpose of the contract.
3
Analyze the exceptions
Some items require more: garments with a silk mark in the fold require seeing the fold; sheep for milk/breeding require seeing the udder; sheep for meat require touching (jass) to assess fatness; many garments require seeing each one separately because they vary.
4
Examine the intra-madhhab dispute on houses
The matn says seeing the courtyard (sahn) suffices, but Zufar requires interior rooms. The sharh declares Zufar's view as the relied-upon (mufta bihi) because houses vary, unlike Kufan custom where interiors and exteriors were similar.
This shows how custom and purpose override earlier practice.
5
Apply the rulings to new scenarios
Using the same 'illa (essential part), determine for any item what part must be seen. For example, for an air conditioner, one must see the unit itself (the essential part), not just the box.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The owner's ratification makes the unauthorized sale valid retroactively, as if the seller were his agent.

What happens if someone sells your property without your permission? Does the sale count, or is it void? The Hanafi school gives the owner a powerful option: he may ratify the sale and make it his own, or rescind it completely. This lesson unpacks the details: the conditions for ratification, its legal effect, and how the Hanafi position differs from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling and the owner's option
Read the first line of the passage: 'Whoever sells the property of another without his permission, the owner has the option: if he wishes he may ratify the sale, and if he wishes he may rescind.' This is the core rule. Note that the sale is not automatically void or valid — it is suspended (mawquf).
The buyer cannot dispose of the item before ratification, even if he took possession.
2
List the four conditions for valid ratification
The text mentions that ratification attaches only if four parties exist: the seller (the unauthorized person), the buyer, the owner, and the sold item. Also, the owner must ratify while the object remains and the two contracting parties are unchanged. If any condition is missing, ratification is invalid.
The owner's acceptance of the price is evidence of ratification, but silence is not.

The text states: 'the subsequent ratification is equivalent to prior agency.' This means that after ratification, the unauthorized seller is treated as if he were the owner's agent from the start. The price goes to the owner if still intact; if it perished with the seller, it is a trust (no liability).
This is a key Hanafi principle: ratification retroactively validates the contract.

The passage gives several addenda: the unauthorized seller may rescind before ratification; the buyer may also rescind; if the owner rescinds, the buyer recovers the price from the seller; if the owner dies before ratification, the sale is void and heirs cannot ratify. Also, if the object's existence is unknown, ratification is still valid by default (Imam Muhammad's view).
These details show the school's concern for protecting all parties' interests.
5
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The Hanafi position that the sale is suspended and can be ratified is disputed by the Shafi'i school, which holds that such a sale is invalid from the start because the seller had no authority. This difference has practical consequences: in Shafi'i, no subsequent ratification can cure the defect; in Hanafi, it can.
The Maliki school is closer to Hanafi but with additional conditions.

Partial Viewing: Seeing One of Two Fabrics
First ruling: partial view of two fabrics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a buyer sees only one of two items and purchases both, and explain why he must return both.

Core Idea
Seeing one item does not count for all; partial sight leaves the option alive for the unseen.

When a buyer sees one of two fabrics and buys both without seeing the other, the option of inspection remains for the unseen item. However, returning only the unseen would split the transaction unfairly, so both must be returned. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The buyer sees one of two fabrics (e.g., in a bundle) and then purchases both without seeing the second. Later, he sees the second and dislikes it.
The option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'ya) applies to items not seen at the time of contract.
2
Determine the ruling for the unseen item
Since the second fabric was not seen, the option remains. The buyer may rescind the contract for that item.
Seeing one does not constitute seeing the other due to potential disparity in quality, color, etc.
3
Apply the principle of non-splitting
Returning only the unseen item would split the single transaction (tafriq al-safqah) before completion, which is prohibited because it harms the seller.
The contract is not fully concluded while the option persists.
4
Conclude the remedy
To avoid splitting, the buyer must return both fabrics. The entire sale is rescinded.
This is a rescission from the origin (faskh min al-asl), not a cancellation after delivery.

Alienation of Part After Purchase: Bale of Cloth
Second ruling: alienation of part of a bundle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that if a buyer alienates any part of an unseen bundle before seeing the rest, he loses the right to return the remainder.

Core Idea
Alienating a part before seeing the rest forfeits the option for the whole batch.

Sometimes a buyer does not inspect the entire purchase at once, but sells or gives away a portion. Hanafi law states that if the buyer disposes of any part of an unseen bundle (like a bale of cloth) before seeing the whole, he cannot later return the remaining items. This lesson analyzes the reasoning based on the impossibility of returning the alienated part and the prohibition of splitting the transaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The buyer purchases a bale of cloth (adlu bazz) without seeing it. He then sells or gives away a garment from it and delivers it. Later, he sees the rest and wants to return the entire bale.
The option of inspection applies because the bale was unseen at the time of purchase.
2
Assess the effect of the alienation
Selling or gifting a part means that part has left the buyer's ownership. It cannot be returned.
The option cannot be exercised for something no longer owned.
3
Apply the rule against splitting
Returning the remaining part alone would split the transaction (tafriq al-safqah) before completion, which is prohibited.
Since the alienated part cannot be returned, the return of the rest would be incomplete and unfair.
4
Conclude the loss of option
Therefore, the buyer loses the option entirely for the bale. He can only return later if there is a defect ('ayb) in the individual items.
The exception for defects allows return even after alienation of a part, because defect return is separate.
