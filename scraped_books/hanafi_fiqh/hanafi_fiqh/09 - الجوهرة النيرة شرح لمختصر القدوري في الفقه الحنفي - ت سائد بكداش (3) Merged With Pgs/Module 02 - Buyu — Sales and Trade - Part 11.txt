Interpretation of Confession: Invalid Transaction Cause
Ruling on interpreting confession as retraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on interpreting a confession of debt from an invalid sale and apply the rule of connected vs disconnected explanation.

Core Idea
A confession explained as arising from an invalid transaction may be a retraction; the ruling depends on whether the explanation is connected.

When someone confesses to a debt and then clarifies that it originates from a prohibited sale (like wine or pig), the law must decide whether that clarification nullifies the confession. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement: Abu Hanifa treats any such clarification as an unacceptable retraction, while the Sahibayn distinguish based on whether the clarification is connected to the confession. This lesson unpacks that dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Confession and Explanation
The person confesses to a debt (e.g., 'I owe you 100 dirhams') and then says 'it was from the price of wine or pig.' This explanation specifies an invalid cause for the debt.
2
Understand the General Ruling
The base text states that such an explanation is a retraction (ruju') of the confession because the price of wine/pig is not a binding obligation. Thus, the debt is not due.
This is the starting point; the dispute concerns how to treat the retraction.
3
Contrast Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa holds that the explanation is not accepted as a valid interpretation, whether connected or disconnected, because it is a retraction. Therefore, the original confession stands and the debt is binding.
Abu Hanifa views any explanation after an unambiguous confession as a separate retraction that does not void the original confession.
4
Contrast the Sahibayn's Position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani differentiate: if the explanation is connected (immediately following the confession without a break), it is considered part of the confession, making the confession invalid from the start – so no debt. If disconnected, the explanation is not accepted, and the debt is binding.
The key variable is connectivity (ittisal).
5
Determine the Practical Difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: In the connected scenario, Abu Hanifa says debt is due; Sahibayn say no debt. In the disconnected scenario, both agree the explanation is not accepted, so debt is due. The mufta bihi in later practice follows Sahibayn for the connected case.
Thus, connectivity is the decisive factor in actual fatwa.

Confession of Debt with Bad Dirhams
Intra-Hanafi dispute on debtor's specification of bad currency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi positions on when a debtor's specification of bad currency is accepted, and apply the rule to connected vs. disconnected confessions.

Core Idea
The debtor's claim of bad currency is only accepted if the confession is disconnected from the transaction or no transaction is mentioned.

When someone confesses a debt, the currency quality can become a point of dispute. This passage examines an intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Hanifa generally holds that the debtor's claim of bad dirhams is not credible, while the Sahibayn distinguish based on whether the specification was part of the same utterance. Understanding this distinction is crucial for advising clients in financial disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The debtor confesses a debt arising from a sale (or loan) and specifies the dirhams are bad. The creditor disputes, asserting they are good. Two sub-scenarios: the specification is connected (same sentence) or disconnected (separate utterance).
The source also covers analogous cases: lead, copper, base coins, and loan confessions.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the debtor's specification of bad currency is not accepted in any case when a transaction is mentioned. He must pay good dirhams. The default assumption is that contracts involve good currency.
Abu Hanifa views the specification as a retraction of the original confession, which is not credible without proof.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's distinction
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differentiate based on connectedness: if the bad quality is stated in the same sentence (connected), it is part of the original truth, so the debtor is believed. If stated separately (disconnected), it is a new claim not accepted.
This uses the principle that connected speech is a single unit; disconnection indicates a separate claim.
4
Apply to analogous cases
The same disagreement applies if the debtor says 'lead' (suttuqah) or 'copper' instead of 'counterfeit', and also to loan confessions where the debtor later specifies the currency was bad.
The rule extends to any specification of substandard currency.
5
Identify the consensus exception
If the debtor confesses a debt without mentioning any transaction (e.g., 'I owe you a thousand dirhams, and they are counterfeit'), the debtor's claim is accepted by consensus. The term 'dirhams' includes bad dirhams as a type.
This exception shows the importance of the word 'dirhams' encompassing both good and bad.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, describing coins as worn-out in an acknowledgment is accepted only if connected in speech — or rejected outright.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding an acknowledgment of debt from a sale price where the confessor specifies the coins as worn-out (ziyuf) but the claimant demands good (jiyad). Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the confessor must pay good coins regardless, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani distinguish based on whether the description is mentioned connectedly or separately. Quality Description in Ghasb and Wadi'ah: Disconnected Statements
Rulings on quality description in ghasb and wadi'ah with dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for disconnected quality descriptions in ghasb and wadi'ah and explain the disagreement with Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
In ghasb and wadi'ah, the confessor is believed even if the description is disconnected.

This lesson examines a key distinction: in non-contractual takings like usurpation or deposit, there is no customary expectation of good quality coins. Thus, a later claim that the coins were worn-out or counterfeit is accepted even if made separately. We will explore the rationale and the minority view of Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and context
Determine whether the confession relates to a loan, sale, ghasb (usurpation), or wadi'ah (deposit). The ruling depends on this context.
Loan and sale are contractual; ghasb and wadi'ah are non-contractual takings.
2
Note the initial confession and later description
The person first confesses to taking a specific amount, then later adds a description of the coins (e.g., worn-out or counterfeit).
3
Determine if the description is connected or disconnected
A connected description follows immediately without significant break; a disconnected one comes later, possibly in a separate statement.
4
State the widespread Hanafi ruling
In ghasb and wadi'ah, the confessor is believed even if the description is disconnected. The rationale is that there is no custom or dealing in standard quality for such takings.
5
Compare with Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf requires connection, analogizing to loan, where a disconnected description is not accepted. This is a legitimate intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The thamarah (practical difference) is that Abu Yusuf would reject the description if disconnected.

Description vs Exclusion and the Necessity Exception
Distinction between description and exclusion, with necessity exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a description (wasf) and an exclusion (istithna) regarding the validity of disconnection, and apply the necessity exception.

Core Idea
A description can be valid when disconnected; an exclusion of quantity must be connected, unless separation is due to necessity.

This lesson refines the rules of additional statements in acknowledgments. Not all qualifiers are treated equally: describing the quality of coins is different from excluding an amount. Moreover, if the speaker is forced to pause (e.g., coughing or interruption), the separation is excused. Understanding these nuances prevents misapplication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nature of the additional statement
Determine if it is a description (wasf) of quality (e.g., worn-out, counterfeit) or an exclusion (istithna) of quantity (e.g., 'except it is less').
The text explicitly contrasts 'الزيافة' (description) with 'استثناء المقدار' (exclusion of amount).
2
Apply the ruling based on type
For a description, the ruling depends on context: in ghasb/wadi'ah, it is valid even if disconnected; in loan/sale, it requires connection. For an exclusion of quantity, it must be connected in all contexts.
3
Check for necessity
If the separation was due to a necessity (ضرورة) like coughing, speech interruption, or other unavoidable pause, the statement is considered connected (واصل).
The text says: 'فإن كان الفصل ضرورة انقطاع الكلام: فهو واصل'.
4
Determine the ruling in the given scenario
Combine the above steps: first identify the type, then check connection, then check necessity if disconnected.

This lesson addresses a critical protection of inheritance shares: a person in terminal illness cannot favor an heir through an acknowledgment of debt, gift, or bequest without the consent of the other heirs. Shuf'ah: Definition and Priority of Pre-emptors
Hanafi definition and priority of shuf'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shuf'ah, identify its three-tier priority structure, and explain why the right is wajib (established) but not a duty.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is a right to substitute in a sale, prioritized by closeness: property partner, right partner, then neighbor.

Shuf'ah (pre-emption) is a distinctive institution in Hanafi fiqh that protects co-owners and neighbours from the harm of an unwanted new owner. The term itself is derived from 'shaf'' meaning joining, reflecting how the pre-emptor joins the sold property to his own. In this passage, the author defines shuf'ah, clarifies its legal nature, and establishes a clear hierarchy of claimants. A key point of contention with the Shafi'i school arises regarding partners in appurtenant rights, such as water shares or pathways.

Guided Path
1
Trace the linguistic root
The term shuf'ah is derived from al-shaf' (joining), the opposite of witt (oddness). Understand that the concept linguistically implies pairing or annexation.
2
Identify the legal definition
Legally, shuf'ah is the right of a co-owner or neighbour to substitute for the buyer in a sale, transferring the sold property to their ownership to prevent harm.
3
List the three-tier priority
First: the partner in the very property sold (khalīṭ fī nafs al-mabīʿ). Second: the partner in an appurtenant right (e.g., water share, pathway). Third: the adjoining neighbour. This order is sequential; the first tier takes precedence over the second, etc.
The second tier is contested by Imam al-Shafi'i.
4
Analyze the legal nature
Shuf'ah is described as 'wajib'—meaning it is an established right (thābit), not an obligation (fard). The pre-emptor may choose to exercise or waive it without sin. The basis is preventing permanent harm (al-adhā 'alā al-dawām).
5
Examine the inter-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that shuf'ah applies only to co-ownership in the property itself, not to separate rights. The Hanafi response relies on analogy and istihsan, arguing that the harm to a right-holder is equally permanent.
This difference affects real-world property transactions; Hanafi judges will grant shuf'ah to water-share partners, Shafi'i judges will not.

Core Idea
Pre-emptor owns only by surrender or judgment; buyer may dispose freely until then.

One of the most confusing aspects of shuf'a is timing. Students often assume that the moment a pre-emptor makes his demand, the property becomes his. But the Hanafi school insists that ownership only transfers by actual surrender (taslim) or a court decree (hukm). This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira elaborates the practical consequences of this principle, including the buyer's full freedom to dispose of the property – even demolishing or renting it – until a judge rules otherwise.

The commentary explains the rationale: the buyer's ownership is complete, so it does not transfer to the pre-emptor except by a new cause (mutual consent or judicial decree). This underpins all the subsequent rules.

If the pre-emptor dies after making the two demands but before taking possession (by surrender or judgment), his right is not inherited because he never owned the property.

The right depends on continued ownership of the adjacent land.

The buyer may sell, demolish, rent, or consume fruits of the property. His actions are valid until a judge rules in favor of the pre-emptor. The pre-emptor can only take the property as it stands at the time of judgment.

The Two Demands in Pre-emption: Prompt and Entitlement
Definition of prompt demand and entitlement demand with requirements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two required demands for pre-emption, explain their sequence, and differentiate the majority Hanafi view from Imam Muhammad's view on lapse.

Core Idea
Two distinct demands establish pre-emption forever; one demand is insufficient.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) is a right to take over a sold property from the buyer. This right is not automatic; it must be actively claimed through a specific procedure. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira outlines the two demands required: the prompt demand (talab al-muthawaba) immediately upon learning of the sale, and the entitlement demand (talab al-istihqaq) made before the buyer, seller, or at the property. Understanding this procedure is crucial because failure to execute it correctly may forfeit the right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two demands
The text states that there are two demands: prompt demand (talab al-muthawaba) and entitlement demand (talab al-istihqaq). Note that they are distinct and sequential.
2
Understand the role of witnesses
Witnessing (ishhad) is recommended but not obligatory for the prompt demand; it serves to prevent denial. For the entitlement demand, the text says 'he has witnesses made to it' – though not explicitly required, it is strongly recommended for proof.
Witnesses are not a condition for validity of the demand, but practically necessary to prove the claim.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and the apparent narration) holds that after the two demands, the right remains forever even if the pre-emptor does not follow up. Imam Muhammad holds that if a month passes without renewing the demand, the right lapses.
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the majority view, as explicitly stated in the text as 'zahir al-riwaya'.
4
Determine the practical consequence
Under the majority view, the pre-emptor can take the property at any time after the two demands, even after years. Under Muhammad's view, he must make a new demand within a month or lose the right.

This passage from the Book of Pre-emption (Kitab al-Shuf'a) explains the procedure for claiming the right of pre-emption (shuf'a). Shuf'a: Scope and the Ship Dispute with Imam Malik
Defines the scope of shuf'a and rebuts Malik's view on ships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi scope of shuf'a (preemption) and explain why ships are excluded, rebutting the Maliki counter-argument.

Core Idea
Shuf'a applies only to real estate; ships are movables, so no preemption.

This passage defines the boundaries of the right of preemption (shuf'a) in Hanafi law. The author clarifies that shuf'a is wajib only in real estate, even if indivisible, and does not extend to movables, buildings without land, or ships. A key dispute with Imam Malik arises over ships, which he considers habitable like real estate. The Hanafi response rests on the Prophetic hadith restricting shuf'a to homesteads and gardens, and on the logical argument that movables lack the permanence of real estate.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the basic ruling: shuf'a is wajib only in real estate ('iqqār). It does not apply to movables (manqūl) such as chattels or ships.
Also note the exception: buildings or palm trees sold without land are treated as movables.
2
Identify the opposing view
Imam Malik holds that shuf'a applies to ships because they are inhabited (tuskan) like real estate.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in different definitions of the effective cause.
3
Follow the Hanafi proof
Two proofs: (1) The hadith 'La shuf'a illa fi rab' aw ha'it' (No preemption except in a homestead or a garden) uses a restrictive (hasr) construction. (2) Qiyas: ships are movable like chattels, and ownership in movables does not have the permanence of real estate.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut but its meaning is decisive (qati' al-dalala) due to the hasr.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds to Malik by arguing that the 'illa (effective cause) of shuf'a is not merely inhabitation but the permanence (istiqrār) of real estate, which ships lack. The hadith explicitly restricts shuf'a to immovable property.
The Hanafi position is consistent with the general principle that shuf'a is a right to prevent harm from a new partner entering co-owned immovable property.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In Hanafi law, a co-owner cannot claim shuf'a when a ship is sold to a third party. In Maliki law, such a claim is valid. This difference has practical implications for maritime commerce and co-ownership.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school is the exclusion of ships, as confirmed by al-Hidayah and other later authorities.

Pre-emption for a Minor: Guardian's Authority and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on equality and guardian's role in minor's pre-emption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on whether a guardian's failure to claim or explicit surrender of a minor's pre-emption right drops that right permanently, and explain the proofs and counter-proofs.

Core Idea
A guardian's surrender of a minor's pre-emption right is valid only if it provides compensation — pure harm is not allowed.

The matn establishes that all persons — Muslim, dhimmi, male, female, minor, adult — are equal in the right of pre-emption (shuf'a). The commentator then turns to a practical question: who acts for a minor, and what happens if the guardian fails to claim or actively surrenders the right? This triggers an important intra-Hanafi dispute between the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and another transmission from Muhammad and Zufar. Understanding this dispute illuminates the delicate balance between protecting the minor's interest and respecting the guardian's authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their positions
The passage first lists who may validly claim pre-emption for a minor: father, executor, grandfather, judge, or his appointee. Then it presents the dispute: the Sahibayn hold that failure to claim or surrender drops the right permanently; Muhammad and Zufar hold that it does not drop and the minor can claim after puberty.
Note that Muhammad al-Shaybani appears in both camps via different riwayat (transmissions).
2
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
They argue that whoever has the right to take the property also has the right to surrender it. Moreover, if the guardian takes it and then sells it for the same price, it is permissible. Surrendering achieves the same result — the minor retains the price and is relieved from liability for defects (ḍamān al-darak). Thus surrender is beneficial.
This is a double argument: authority over the right and a specific benefit.
3
Examine Muhammad and Zufar's proof
They analogize surrender to immunity from debt (barā'a min al-dayn) or pardon for qisas (al-afw 'an al-qisas), which a guardian cannot do because they harm the minor without compensation. Surrendering pre-emption also harms the minor by losing a valuable right.
The analogy is based on harm (ḍarar).
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's response
The Sahibayn distinguish their case: in debt and qisas, the right is relinquished without any compensation; here, the minor receives compensation in the form of retaining the price and avoiding potential loss. Therefore, the analogy fails.
The distinguishing factor is compensation ('iwaḍ).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text does not explicitly give a tarjih, but the Sahibayn's view is generally relied upon in the school. Thus, if a guardian fails to claim or surrenders, the right drops permanently.
However, if no guardian exists, the minor's right remains until puberty.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is triggered by any acquisition of real estate for monetary consideration, not only by sale.

In this brief but precise passage, the commentator explains a key terminological choice in the matn. Al-Quduri deliberately says 'if one owns real estate with a consideration' rather than 'if one purchases'. This distinction reveals that the right of pre-emption is tied to the presence of monetary consideration, not to the specific contract of sale. This is a Hanafi position.

This passage is an expansion (sharh) of the matn from Mukhtasar al-Quduri on the book of shuf'a (pre-emption). It explains the judicial procedure when a pre-emptor (shafi') approaches the judge claiming that a property has been sold and he has the right of pre-emption. Contesting Preemption Without the Price
Permissibility of contesting preemption without bringing the price to court

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a preemptor may contest without bringing the price and explain the legal reasoning (ta'lil) behind it.

Core Idea
The preemptor may claim preemption without paying first; the price is due only after ownership transfers.

This lesson addresses a practical question in preemption (shuf'a): must the preemptor bring the purchase price to court when he files his claim? The classical Hanafi ruling is clear: he may contest without the price. The reasoning is based on the sequence of legal events — the obligation to pay arises only after the property actually vests in him, which requires either the buyer's consent or a court order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of contesting preemption (al-munaza'ah fi al-shuf'ah). The ruling: it is permissible (ja'iz) even if the preemptor does not bring the price to the judge's court.
The text uses 'tajuz' indicating permissibility, not obligation.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
Why is it permitted? Because the price (thaman) becomes obligatory only after the property transfers to the preemptor. Transfer (intiqal) occurs only through one of two means: (1) the buyer's voluntary consent (rida) or (2) the qadi's binding judgment (qada). Since the claim alone does not transfer ownership, demanding the price upfront would be premature.
The reasoning is a rational proof (dalil 'aqli) based on the sequence of legal obligations.
3
Apply the principle to a scenario
Consider a preemptor who hears of a sale and goes to court immediately without bringing cash. According to the Hanafi school, his claim is procedurally valid. The judge will hear the case; if he rules in the preemptor's favor, only then must the preemptor pay the price. If the judge rules against him, no payment is due.
This differs from some other schools that may require deposit or guarantee.

The Void Effect of Settling Pre-Emption for Compensation
Ruling on effect of accepting compensation for pre‑emption right

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on settling pre‑emption for compensation and explain why the consideration must be returned.

Core Idea
Accepting compensation to abandon pre‑emption voids the right and the payment must be returned.

Can a person who has the right of pre‑emption (shuf‘a) sell that right to the buyer? Hanafi law treats pre‑emption as a right meant to prevent hardship from an unwanted co‑owner, not as a commodity. This lesson examines what happens when the pre‑emptor agrees to drop his claim in exchange for payment—a contract that the law considers void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is the pre‑emptor (shafi‘) settling (sulh) with the buyer (or seller) to abandon his right of pre‑emption in exchange for a consideration (e.g., money or goods).
2
State the ruling
The pre‑emption right is voided (batalat al‑shuf‘a) and the consideration must be returned (yaruddu al‑‘iwad).
The right is not transferable.
3
Understand the rationale
By accepting compensation, the pre‑emptor indicates he is turning away from the right (i‘raadan ‘anha). Since the right cannot be sold or traded for worldly gain, the consideration is unlawful (ghayr mubah).
4
Apply the similar case
If the buyer tells the pre‑emptor 'Purchase the property from me and drop the lawsuit,' and he agrees to buy, the ruling is the same: pre‑emption is void.
This reinforces the principle.

Death of Pre-emptor and Buyer: Effect on Pre-emption
Ruling with rational proof on death of parties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether pre-emption survives the death of the pre-emptor or the buyer, and explain the rationale underpinning each ruling.

Core Idea
Pre-emption lapses with the pre-emptor's death but survives the buyer's death.

Pre-emption (shuf'ah) is a powerful right that allows a co-owner or neighbour to step into a sale. But what happens if the person entitled to it dies before exercising it? Or if the buyer dies while the right is still pending? This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies two symmetrical rules: the pre-emptor's death extinguishes the right, while the buyer's death leaves it intact. The reasoning hinges on whether the right is personal and contingent on a direct connection to the property at the time of sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the ruling carefully: it presents two separate cases — death of the pre-emptor and death of the buyer. Note the contrasting outcomes.
The ruling uses opposite wording: 'batalat' (void) vs. 'lam tasqut' (not dropped).
2
Understand the key distinction
Why is pre-emption not inherited? The commentary explains: the heir had no ownership at the moment of sale. Pre-emption is a personal right tied to the pre-emptor's connection at the contract time.
Contrast this with the buyer's death: the pre-emptor's right persists because the entitled person (the pre-emptor) still exists.
3
Apply the timing nuance
Note the exception: if the pre-emptor dies after a judgment in his favor but before payment, the right becomes binding on his heirs. Distinguish 'before judgment' from 'after judgment'.
This shows that after judicial confirmation, the right transforms into a property interest that can be inherited.
4
Analyze the priority over debts and bequests
The passage explicitly states that the pre-emptor's right cannot be defeated by the buyer's debts or bequests. The pre-emptor can annul any sale or bequest made by the buyer's executor or judge.
This reinforces that pre-emption, once attached, takes precedence as a prior right (taqaddum haqqihi).

Pre-emptor Selling Property Before Decree
Ruling with proof: effect of pre-emptor's sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a pre-emptor selling his connecting property before judgment and identify when the sale voids or preserves his pre-emption right.

Core Idea
A definitive sale voids pre-emption; a sale with option preserves if rescinded.

This passage from the Jawhara al-Nayira clarifies how a pre-emptor's own actions affect his claim. The right of pre-emption depends on a property connection that must exist until judgment. If the pre-emptor sells his connecting property definitively, that connection is severed and his right is lost. However, if he sells with a retained option (khiyar), ownership remains with him, preserving the connection—provided he ultimately rescinds the sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is the pre-emptor selling his own property (the one that gives him connection) before the judge decrees pre-emption. The default ruling: pre-emption becomes void (batil).
This applies regardless of whether the pre-emptor knew about the purchase of the pre-empted property.
2
Specify the condition: definitive sale
The voidance occurs only if the sale is definitive (bay' batt) – a sale without any option (khiyar) that immediately transfers ownership.
A definitive sale severs the connection because ownership passes to the buyer.
3
Understand the rationale
The cause (sabab) of pre-emption entitlement is connection (ittisal) to the pre-empted property. If the pre-emptor sells that connecting property, the cause is removed before he acquires ownership of the pre-empted property, so the right cannot vest.
This is a logical reasoning (dalil 'aqli).
4
Identify the exception: sale with option
If the pre-emptor sells with a condition of option (khiyar) for himself, his pre-emption does NOT become void. Because the option prevents the transfer of ownership; the seller remains owner during the option period, thus the connection continues.
Exception applies only if the option is for the seller (khiyar al-shart).
5
Apply the condition for the exception
The pre-emption is preserved only if the pre-emptor eventually chooses to rescind the sale. If he does not rescind (e.g., option lapses or he confirms), the sale becomes definitive and pre-emption is void.
The text explicitly says 'if he chooses to rescind the sale'.

Tricks to Nullify Pre-emption: Long-term Lease and Lifetime Loan
Lists two tricks that nullify pre-emption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two specific contractual arrangements that nullify the right of pre-emption in the Hanafi school and explain the legal reasoning behind them.

Core Idea
Accepting a long-term lease or a lifetime loan for use voids the pre-emptor's right — the usufruct separation defeats ownership claim.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra presents two specific legal devices (hiyal) that nullify the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah). The Hanafi school recognizes that if the pre-emptor agrees to such arrangements, he implicitly waives his right to take the property in full ownership. These are considered valid contracts but their effect on pre-emption is unique.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracts
Read the text and note the two specific contracts mentioned: a hundred-year lease (ijarah) for a dirham, and a lifetime loan for use ('ariyah).
These are not exhaustive; they are examples of tricks that work.
2
Understand the triggering condition
The pre-emption becomes void only when the pre-emptor 'seeks that' (i.e., accepts or agrees to the lease/loan). Acceptance is key.
If the pre-emptor rejects the offer, pre-emption remains.
3
Grasp the legal reasoning
By accepting a long-term lease or lifetime loan, the pre-emptor separates the usufruct from ownership for an extended period, making it impossible to take full ownership. Thus his right is forfeited.
The lease/loan must be for a duration that effectively defeats ownership.
4
Recognize the device as a trick (hilah)
The text explicitly calls these 'hail' (tricks). The Hanafi school permits such legal tools if they comply with contract formalities, even if the intent is to circumvent pre-emption.
Other schools may not recognize these tricks as valid.
5
Note cross-madhhab disagreement
The Shafi'i school generally does not recognize such tricks; they would hold that pre-emption remains unless expressly waived.
This is practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Agent as Pre-emptor: Seller's Agent vs Buyer's Agent
Explains when an agent who is also a pre-emptor retains or loses the right

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios of an agent who is a pre-emptor and explain the Hanafi reasoning for each ruling.

Core Idea
Seller's agent loses pre-emption due to delivery obligation; buyer's agent retains it as completion of contract.

In Hanafi fiqh, an agent who is also a pre-emptor faces different rulings depending on which party he represents. The key factor is whether his role creates an obligation that conflicts with the right of pre-emption. This lesson unpacks the rulings for the seller's agent and the buyer's agent, along with the rationale and a procedural question about execution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios and their rulings
Read the passage and note the three distinct cases: (1) seller's agent sells while being pre-emptor – no pre-emption; (2) seller's agent guarantees vendee's recourse – no pre-emption; (3) buyer's agent buys while being pre-emptor – pre-emption is valid.
Each ruling is accompanied by a short proof.
2
Understand the rationale for the seller's agent
The sale contract obliges the seller's agent to deliver the property to the buyer. This delivery obligation is incompatible with the pre-emptor's right to take the property, thus voiding the pre-emption right.
The obligation arises from the agent's role as seller.
3
Understand the rationale for guaranteeing vendee's recourse
Guaranteeing the vendee's recourse (daman al-darak) validates the sale. Claiming pre-emption would effectively rescind the sale, contradicting the guarantee. Hence, pre-emption is invalid.
The guarantee solidifies the sale, making pre-emption inconsistent.
4
Understand the rationale for the buyer's agent
The sale is concluded for the principal (the buyer), not the agent. Pre-emption arises after the sale and is lost only by delivery or silence. Neither occurred, so the agent retains the right. Taking by pre-emption completes the contract, not rescinds it.
The agent's own role does not create a delivery obligation.
5
Analyze the procedural execution
The passage addresses how judgment is executed: if the principal is present, the agent is ordered to take the property for himself and his recourse is against the seller. If the principal is absent, the agent takes possession for the principal first, then exercises pre-emption, with recourse on the agent.
This clarifies the practical enforcement of the ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the effect of seller's option and buyer's option on the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) and state the ruling for each case, including the timing of demand.

Core Idea
Seller's option blocks pre-emption; buyer's option does not—ownership transfer is key.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) is a right to take over a sale, but it only arises once the sale is final. When a sale includes an option (khiyar), ownership may or may not transfer during the option period depending on who holds the option. This lesson explains the Hanafi rulings on shuf'a in both scenarios, clarifying when the shafi' may act and the consequences of taking the property within the option period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two option scenarios
The passage treats two cases: a sale with seller's option and a sale with buyer's option. Recognize which party holds the khiyar.
Seller's option: property stays with seller. Buyer's option: property transfers to buyer subject to return.
2
Determine effect on ownership
For seller's option, the sold item remains in seller's ownership during the option period. For buyer's option, ownership transfers immediately to the buyer, though the buyer can rescind later.
Ownership transfer is the key factor for shuf'a.
3
Apply the pre-emption rule
Shuf'a requires a completed sale (ownership transfer). Therefore, no shuf'a during seller's option; shuf'a is due immediately with buyer's option (by consensus).
In seller's option, the sale is not final, so no pre-emption.
4
Note the timing of demand for seller's option
When the seller drops the option, the sale becomes binding and shuf'a becomes due. The shafi' must demand pre-emption at that moment (not earlier).
Demand is conditioned on the dropping of the option – this is the correct view (al-sahih).
5
Understand the effect of shafi' taking within option period
If the shafi' takes the property during the buyer's option period (within three days), the sale becomes binding on the buyer; the buyer can no longer return the goods, and the shafi' does not inherit the buyer's option.
The option is a personal right of the buyer, not transferable to the shafi'.

Core Idea
An invalid sale does not produce shuf'a; a shafi' with an option can claim pre-emption if he chooses the sale.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents two distinct shuf'a scenarios. First, when a property is bought through a fāsid contract, no pre-emption arises because ownership has not effectively transferred. Second, if the buyer stipulates an option for a third party who happens to be a pre-emptor, that third party may exercise shuf'a by accepting the sale. Why is there no shuf'a? Because an invalid sale does not transfer ownership — a key condition for shuf'a is absent.
Ownership remains with the seller; the buyer has only possession with a duty to return.

If the buyer stipulates an option for a third party, and that third party is a shafi', then if the shafi' chooses to confirm the sale, he acquires the right of shuf'a.
The khiyar for a third party is valid and binds the contracting parties.

Explains that no shuf'ah exists in an invalid sale, but it becomes due if the right to rescind is lost.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shuf'ah in a fasid sale and explain the condition that triggers its obligation (loss of right to rescind).

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is not due in a fasid sale while the right to rescind remains; once that right vanishes, shuf'ah becomes wajib.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains a nuanced Hanafi ruling: although a fasid (invalid) sale does not initially give rise to pre-emption rights, it can do so if the seller's right to rescind is lost. Understanding why requires grasping the Hanafi view that a fasid sale can transfer ownership upon possession, but the lingering right of rescission prevents shuf'ah from being established.

Guided Path

The base text states: 'No shuf'ah in a fasid purchase.' Identify that this is the default ruling.

The commentator explains this is due to the possibility of rescission.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The pre-emptor's right is fixed at contract; payment status and remission wording determine the price he pays.

In pre-emption (shuf'a), disputes may arise between buyer and seller about the actual price. The Hanafi school provides clear rules to protect the pre-emptor's right without allowing collusion. This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyirah explains the rulings based on whether the price has been paid and how any remission affects the pre-emptor's obligation.

Unpaid: seller's claim; paid: buyer's claim.

If unpaid, the pre-emptor takes at the seller's lower price because the contract is not fully concluded. If paid, the pre-emptor takes at the buyer's higher price because the seller becomes like a third party.
The rationale is based on the completion of the contract.

Partial remission benefits the pre-emptor, so the reduced price applies. Full remission does not benefit the pre-emptor; he still pays the original price. Remission after taking and similar acts like acquittal follow the same rule.
The pre-emptor's obligation is independent of the buyer's.

Multiple expressions may allow the pre-emptor to benefit from a final reduction.

The pre-emptor's right is established at the contract; subsequent acts between buyer and seller cannot prejudice it, but partial reductions pass through because they mirror the buyer's obligation.

Distribution of Shuf'a Among Multiple Pre-emptors
Ruling that shuf'a is divided per heads, not per shares, with Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on dividing shuf'a among multiple pre-emptors and apply the Hanafi rule to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Shuf'a is divided per heads equally, not per shares, because the right is personal and equal for all co-owners.

This lesson examines a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding how the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) is distributed when multiple co-owners have the right to pre-empt a sold share. The Hanafi school holds that the right is divided equally among the pre-emptors by counting individuals (heads), regardless of their respective ownership percentages. The Shafi'i school, in contrast, allocates the right in proportion to each pre-emptor's share. Understanding this difference is essential for correctly applying the rules of shuf'a in joint ownership scenarios.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling in the passage: the right of shuf'a among multiple pre-emptors is divided equally according to the number of persons (heads), not according to the sizes of their shares.
The passage uses the phrase 'على عدد رؤوسهم' (according to the count of their heads).
2
Identify the Shafi'i counterposition
Note the opposing view: al-Shafi'i holds that the division is according to the measures of shares (على مقادير الأنصباء).
3
Analyze the rationales
Consider the underlying reasoning: Hanafi views shuf'a as a personal right granted equally to each co-owner to prevent harm from a new partner; the harm is the same regardless of share size. Shafi'i views shuf'a as a right derived from the owned share, hence proportional.
The Hanafi rationale is that the right is established to prevent harm, and the harm is equal for all partners.
4
Apply to the given example
Work through the example: a house owned by three: A (1/2), B (1/3), C (1/6). A sells his half. According to Hanafi, B and C each get half of the sold half (i.e., 1/4 each). According to Shafi'i, B gets 2/3 of the sold half (1/3) and C gets 1/3 of the sold half (1/6).
The example concretizes the dispute: per heads gives equal shares; per shares gives proportional shares.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Recognize that the dispute affects the actual distribution of the pre-empted property. In the example, the difference is substantial: one-quarter each for B and C under Hanafi, versus one-third and one-sixth under Shafi'i.
This thamarah al-khilaf is critical for fatwa and court rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on how a buyer's increase or seller's reduction of price after sale affects the pre-emptor's right, and apply the condition of 'one phrase vs multiple phrases' for reductions.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor's right locks at the contract price; later changes by parties do not bind him unless the seller reduces in one phrase.

Pre-emption (shuf'ah) gives a co-owner or neighbor the right to take a sold property at the price agreed in the contract. But what happens if the buyer and seller change the price after the sale? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position: the pre-emptor is protected from post-contract increases, but may benefit from a genuine reduction made by the seller in a single statement.

The pre-emptor's right was established at the time of the contract, at the price then agreed. Any subsequent change is a private agreement between buyer and seller that cannot affect a third party's vested right.

If the seller reduces the entire price in one phrase, it is as if that was the true price; if multiple phrases, the last phrase prevails because each new statement supersedes the previous.
This condition prevents manipulation by the seller to the pre-emptor's disadvantage.

Summarize the legal principle
The pre-emptor's right is fixed at the contract price. Increases do not affect him; reductions benefit him only if made in one decisive phrase.
The principle protects the pre-emptor from post-contract collusion while allowing genuine adjustments.

Distribution of Pre-emption Among Multiple Pre-emptors
Hanafi ruling and Shafi'i dispute on apportioning pre-emption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule for dividing pre-emption among multiple pre-emptors and contrast it with the Shafi'i position, identifying the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Pre-emption is divided by heads not shares.

When two or more partners have the right of pre-emption, how is the sold share apportioned? The Hanafi school adopts a simple equal division by the number of pre-emptors, regardless of their ownership shares. This lesson examines the ruling, the illustrative example, and the Shafi'i counter-position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: pre-emption is divided equally by the number of pre-emptors, not by their ownership shares.
The matn states 'by number of their heads'.
2
Understand the example
Consider a house owned by three: one has half, another a third, another a sixth. The half-owner sells his share; the other two claim pre-emption.
The example concretizes the rule.
3
Compute the Hanafi outcome
Since there are two pre-emptors, the sold share is divided equally: each gets half.
Ownership shares (1/3 and 1/6) are ignored.
4
Contrast with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i rules proportional to existing shares: the owner of 1/3 gets 2/3 of the sold share, the owner of 1/6 gets 1/3.
The difference stems from different views on the nature of pre-emption.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical effect: different division of the same property. Hanafi: half each; Shafi'i: two-thirds and one-third.
This clarifies why the dispute matters in real cases.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute. The commentator illustrates with an example: three partners own half, third, and sixth. The half-owner sells his share. The dispute is based on different interpretations of the nature of pre-emption: Hanafi considers it a right attached to the person (hence equal shares), while Shafi'i considers it a right attached to the property (hence proportional to ownership).

The Present Pre-emptor's Right to the Whole Property
Scenarios of partial presence and priority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one pre-emptor appears first and another later, and when the buyer is a pre-emptor.

Core Idea
A present pre-emptor takes the whole property initially; later pre-emptors share, but priority matters.

This lesson explores the initial scenario in pre-emption: when some pre-emptors are absent, the present one can claim the entire property. The rulings also address what happens if the buyer himself is a pre-emptor and a better claimant appears.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
One pre-emptor is present while others are absent. He claims his right.
The absent pre-emptor may or may not later claim.
2
Determine the initial ruling
The judge awards the entire property to the present pre-emptor, because the absent one's claim is uncertain.
The present right is not destroyed by doubt.
3
Handle the later arrival
If another pre-emptor of equal priority appears later and proves his right, he gets half.
The later claimant shares equally with the first.
4
Apply the buyer-as-pre-emptor case
If the buyer himself is a pre-emptor and a better pre-emptor appears, the buyer loses the property; if an equal appears, they share; if an inferior appears, no effect.
Priority determines the outcome.

Transfer of Pre-emption Right is Invalid
Ruling on transferring pre-emption right

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when one pre-emptor tries to transfer his right to another pre-emptor.

A pre-emptor cannot sell or give his pre-emption right to another; doing so forfeits his own right.

The final ruling in the passage addresses whether a pre-emptor can transfer his right to another pre-emptor. The answer is no: such a transfer is invalid, and the giver loses his right, which then reverts to the remaining pre-emptors.
