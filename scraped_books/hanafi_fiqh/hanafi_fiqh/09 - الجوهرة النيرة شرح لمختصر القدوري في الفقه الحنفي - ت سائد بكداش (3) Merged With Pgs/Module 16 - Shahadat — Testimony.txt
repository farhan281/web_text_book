Interpretation of Unspecified Property in Iqrar
Ruling on referring description of unspecified property to confessor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who interprets unspecified property in an iqrar and apply the exception for amounts less than a dirham based on custom.

Core Idea
When a confessor leaves property unspecified, he alone defines it — but custom excludes trifling amounts.

When someone confesses a debt without specifying the amount, the law must determine whose description prevails. The Hanafi school gives authority to the confessor because his admission was on unknown property; he is best placed to clarify his intention. However, there is a customary threshold: amounts less than one dirham are not considered property and thus are not accepted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the case involves an iqrar (confession) where the confessor (muqarr) says 'he has a right over me of property' (mal) without specifying the quantity or type.
The property is 'majhul' (unknown) at the time of confession.
2
Apply the default rule
The default Hanafi rule: the reference for describing the property is the confessor himself. His statement is accepted whether he claims the amount is little or much.
The legal reasoning (ta'lil) is that the confession was on a majhul object, so the confessor is the most knowledgeable.
3
Apply the customary exception
The confessor is not believed if he interprets the property as less than one dirham, because by custom ('urf) such a small amount is not considered property (mal).
Custom determines the minimum threshold for what counts as mal in this context.
4
Consider disparaging descriptions
If the confessor uses words like 'contemptible', 'little', or 'scanty', the same rule applies: his interpretation is accepted for both little and much, as long as it is at least one dirham.
These adjectives do not affect the general principle.

Admission of Debt from an Unspecified Slave: Ruling and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Non-Receipt
Ruling and commentary on the slave case and the dispute on non-receipt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on admission of debt from an unspecified slave and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the debtor's claim of non-receipt.

Core Idea
The debtor's claim of non-receipt is accepted by Abu Hanifa unconditionally; the Sahibayn require it to be stated immediately.

When a debtor says 'I owe you a thousand from the price of a slave' without specifying the slave, the debt is established. But if the debtor adds 'but I did not receive it,' a crucial intra-Hanafi dispute arises. Abu Hanifa accepts the claim unconditionally, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) accept it only if stated in the same breath. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning and practical consequences of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the admission and its effect
The debtor says 'I owe a thousand from the price of a slave' without specifying the slave. Under Abu Hanifa, the debtor is liable for the thousand immediately.
2
Analyze the claim of non-receipt
If the debtor adds 'but I did not receive it,' Abu Hanifa accepts his claim, so the debt is not due. The Sahibayn differentiate: if the claim is stated consecutively (wasala), it is accepted; if separated (fasala), it is not.
Wasala means connected in the same utterance; fasala means stated later.
3
Examine the reasoning behind the Sahibayn's view
The Sahibayn argue that the admission itself is a proof of debt. A later denial of receipt is a retraction, which is not allowed. An immediate denial can be seen as part of the admission, clarifying that no delivery occurred.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Hanifa, a debtor can escape liability by claiming non-receipt even if he mentions it later. Under the Sahibayn, he must mention it at the same time to avoid liability.
In practice, judges often follow the Sahibayn's view for caution.

Commentary
This lesson, taken from the sharh of al-Jawhara an-Nayyira, expounds on two rulings from al-Quduri's matn regarding admissions of debt from the price of a slave (when the slave is unspecified) and from the price of prohibited items (wine and swine). First ruling: if a person says 'I owe him a thousand from the price of a slave' without specifying the slave, Abu Hanifa rules that the debtor is liable for the thousand. However, if the debtor adds 'but I did not receive it,' Abu Hanifa accepts his claim of non-receipt, and thus the debt is not due, because he is informing about a condition that prevents payment. The creditor may argue that the slave belongs to the debtor or was sent by someone else, but if the debtor insists he never received the slave, the debt falls. The Sahibayn differentiate: if the debtor stated the non-receipt immediately (connected/wasala), his word is accepted; if he said it later (separate/fasala), it is not. The second ruling: if the admission is for the price of wine or swine, the debtor is liable for the thousand regardless, and his explanation that it is from such a transaction is not accepted, because the admission itself creates a debt in his liability (dhimma), and the prohibited nature does not negate the obligation—he must pay, though the transaction itself is invalid. The commentary explains why the debtor's interpretation is rejected: the word 'upon me' entails an absolute obligation. Admission of Debt from Price of Wine or Swine: Binding Ruling Despite Prohibited Transaction
Ruling on admission of debt from price of wine/swine and rejection of interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an admission of debt from the price of wine or swine is binding and explain why the debtor's interpretation of the cause is not accepted.

Core Idea
An admission of debt from an illegal transaction is still binding; the debtor cannot escape by explaining the prohibited source.

If a debtor says 'I owe a thousand dirhams from the price of wine or swine,' the Hanafi school holds him liable for the full amount, even though the underlying sale is haram. The debtor's attempt to interpret the debt as arising from a prohibited transaction—hoping to avoid payment—is rejected. This lesson explores the reasoning: the admission itself creates a debt in the liability (dhimma), separate from the validity of the original contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the debtor's statement
The debtor says 'I owe a thousand dirhams from the price of wine or swine.' This is an admission of debt with a specified cause (thaman).
2
Apply the ruling: immediate liability
The debtor is liable for the thousand dirhams regardless of the prohibited nature of the transaction. The admission itself is binding.
3
Examine the rejection of the debtor's interpretation
The debtor might later claim that the debt is from a haram sale, thereby attempting to negate the obligation. The court does not accept this interpretation because the debtor's statement 'upon me' is an absolute proof of debt.
The reason: the word 'upon me' ('ala) establishes the debt in the dhimma; the cause is irrelevant.
4
Understand the rationale
Admission (iqrar) is a proof that stands on its own; allowing the debtor to negate it by referencing the illegal cause would undermine the reliability of admissions. The underlying transaction's invalidity does not affect the admission's validity.

Evidence Preference in Pre-emption Price Disputes
Ruling on whose evidence is accepted when price is disputed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default evidentiary rule in a price dispute between pre-emptor and buyer and identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement when both bring evidence.

Core Idea
Default: buyer's word with oath; exception: pre-emptor's evidence alone; if both: majority prefers pre-emptor's evidence, Abu Yusuf prefers buyer's.

In a shuf'a dispute over the price, the default rule gives precedence to the buyer's statement, taken with an oath. However, if the pre-emptor brings valid evidence, that evidence is accepted. This lesson delves into the intra-Hanafi disagreement on which party's evidence should be preferred when both produce proof. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for a judge or mufti handling pre-emption claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
State the general rule when the pre-emptor and buyer disagree on price and neither brings evidence.
The text: 'فالقول قول المشتري مع يمينه' (the statement is the statement of the buyer with his oath).
2
Recognize the exception for pre-emptor's evidence
Note that if the pre-emptor alone provides evidence, his evidence is accepted.
The text: 'فإن أقام الشفيع بينة: قضي بها' (if the pre-emptor produces evidence, it is decided by it).
3
Pinpoint the intra-Hanafi conflict
Identify the disagreement when both parties present evidence.
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say pre-emptor's evidence; Abu Yusuf says buyer's evidence.
4
Analyze Abu Yusuf's reasoning
Understand Abu Yusuf's justification: the buyer's evidence is 'more confirmatory' (akthar ithbātan).
Consider possible reasons: buyer is the original party with stronger documentation?
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Conclude that the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is the relied-upon position.
The text does not explicitly resolve, but the majority is given as the primary view.

General Permissibility of Agency in Litigation and Execution
General ruling on agency for litigation and execution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the general Hanafi ruling on agency for litigation and execution and IDENTIFY the exceptions for hudud and qisas.

Core Idea
Agency is generally valid in all rights except hudud and qisas, which require personal presence.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Consent and Excuses for Agency
Intra-Hanafi dispute on consent condition and its exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to EXPLAIN Abu Hanifa's requirement of opponent's consent for agency in litigation and IDENTIFY the exceptions (sickness, travel, secluded woman, menstruation).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires opponent's consent unless the principal has a valid excuse preventing court attendance.

Now we dive into the dispute. The general rule above was the view of the two disciples. Abu Hanifa differed sharply, demanding the opponent's consent for agency in litigation—unless the principal is sick, traveling, or otherwise unable to attend. Later Hanafis added more excuses. Let's examine this position and its practical impact.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa says agency for litigation is invalid without the opponent's consent ('لا يجوز التوكيل بالخصومة إلا برضا الخصم'). This is his default ruling.
He treats litigation as a personal matter requiring the opponent to face the actual party.
2
List the explicit exceptions
The exceptions are: (1) the principal is sick with an illness that prevents attendance, (2) the principal is absent on a journey of three days or more. The illness must physically prevent coming to court; a minor sickness does not qualify.
Travel less than three days is treated as present—no exception.
3
Explore expanded exceptions from later scholars
Later Hanafi jurists (mutakaddimun) used istihsan to add the case of a secluded woman (mukhaddara) who is not accustomed to appearing before men. If she would be too shy to present her case, she may appoint an agent without consent.
Exception applies only if she is not used to mixing with men; otherwise, she is like a man.
4
Consider the menstruation excuse
If the judge sits in the mosque and a woman is menstruating (unable to enter mosque), she may appoint an agent without consent if she is the claimant. If she is the defendant, the claimant may insist on her attendance unless the court moves.
This is a conditional excuse depending on her role.

Payment to an Alleged Agent: Debtor's Liability and Recourse
Ruling on debtor paying a claimed agent and subsequent recourse when principal returns

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a debtor pays a claimed agent and explain the debtor's recourse if the principal denies the agency.

Core Idea
A debtor who pays an alleged agent without verification pays at his own risk; only the principal's confirmation or the debtor's own admission protects him.

When someone claims to be the agent of an absent creditor to collect a debt, the debtor must decide whether to pay. The Hanafi school provides detailed rules on the debtor's liability and subsequent recourse. This lesson examines the key scenarios: when the debtor verifies the agent, when he pays without verification, and what happens if the principal later appears and denies the agency. We also explore a related intra-Hanafi dispute on the testimony of a former agent about the right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actors and their statements
Identify the debtor (gharīm), the alleged agent (wakīl), and the absent principal (ghā'ib). Note the debtor's response: verification (taṣdīq), denial, or silence.
2
Apply the rule for compelling payment
If the debtor verifies the agent, the judge compels payment. If he denies or remains silent, he is not compelled, but voluntary payment still binds him.
Verification is an admission against the debtor's own interest, establishing the agency legally.
3
Analyze the principal's appearance and recourse
When the principal appears, if he confirms the agency, the payment stands. If he denies, the debtor must pay the principal again but has recourse against the agent if the money remains in the agent's hand.
The debtor's payment does not discharge the debt unless the agency is proven by the principal.
4
Examine the former agent's testimony
The passage also discusses whether a former agent can testify about the right. The sound opinion (al-aṣaḥḥ) distinguishes based on whether he litigated before a judge.
This involves an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf differs from the Sahibayn.

فإن رجع صاحب المال على الغريم: رجع الغريم على الوكيل؛ لأنه لم يُصدقه على الوكالة، وإنما دفعه إليه على رجاء الإجازة، فإذا انقطع رجاؤه: رجع عليه.
وفي الوجوه كلها : ليس له أن يسترد المدفوع حتى يحضر الغائب؛ لأن المؤدى صار حقاً للغائب، إما ظاهراً أو محتملاً.
- قال الخجندي: إذا جاء الموكَّل : إن أقر بالوكالة: مضى الأمر على وجهه، وإن أنكرها : أخذ دينه من الغريم ثانياً، والغريم يرجع على الوكيل إن كان باقياً في يده، وإن استهلكه: ضمنه مثله.
وإن هلك في يده من غير تعد: إن كان صدقه: لا يرجع عليه، وإن صدقه، وشرط عليه الضمان، أو كذبه، أو سكت: رجع عليه.
ثم إذا رجع الموكل على الغريم، وأراد الغريم أن يُحلَّفه : ما وكَّلتُ: كان له ذلك إن كان دفع إلى الوكيل عن تصديق، وإن كان عن سكوت: ليس له أن يُحلفه، إلا إذا عاد إلى التصديق.
Book of Agency. Whoever claims that he is the agent of an absent person to collect his debt, and the debtor verifies him: he is ordered to deliver the debt to him. Then if the absent person appears and verifies him: it is valid, otherwise: the debtor pays the debt to him a second time. And regarding that for which he was appointed agent: if he testifies before or after revocation, and he has litigated regarding it: it is not accepted due to suspicion. If it is after [revocation] and he has not litigated: it is accepted, according to the most sound opinion. He said in al-Mustafa: If the agent for litigation is removed before he litigates: his testimony is not accepted according to Abu Yusuf, contrary to the two [Abu Hanifa and Muhammad]. If he litigated: it is not accepted by consensus. In al-Yanabi': If he appointed him for litigation, and he litigated, then removed him, then the agent testified regarding that right: if the litigation was before the judge: his testimony is not accepted, if it was before other than the judge: it is accepted according to the two. And Abu Yusuf said: His testimony is not accepted after the agency, whether he litigated or not. * His statement: (Whoever claims that he is the agent of the absent person to collect his debt, and the debtor verifies him: he is ordered to deliver the debt to him) meaning he is compelled to do so; because the agency has become apparent through verification, and because his verification is an admission against himself. Then if he pays him: he has no right to retrieve it afterwards. It is restricted to verification; because if he remains silent or denies him: he is not compelled to pay him, but if he paid him: he has no right to retrieve it. * His statement: (If the absent person appears and verifies him: it is valid, otherwise: the debtor pays the debt to him a second time); because the receipt has not been confirmed, since he denied the agency, and the statement regarding that is his statement with his oath. * His statement: (And he [the debtor] has recourse against the agent if it remains in his hand). It is conditioned on its remaining; because if it is lost in his hand or destroyed without transgression: no liability on him; because by his verification he acknowledged that he was rightful in taking, and he is oppressed in this taking, and the oppressed should not oppress others. Even if the debtor did not verify him regarding the agency, but paid him based on his claim. If the owner of the money has recourse against the debtor, the debtor has recourse against the agent; because he did not verify him regarding the agency, but paid him in hope of ratification, and when his hope is cut off, he has recourse against him. In all cases: he cannot recover the paid amount until the absent person appears; because the paid amount has become a right of the absent person, either apparent or possible. - Al-Khujandi said: When the principal comes: if he acknowledges the agency: the matter proceeds as is; if he denies it: he takes his debt from the debtor a second time, and the debtor has recourse against the agent if it remains in his hand; if he consumed it, he guarantees its equivalent. If it is destroyed in his hand without transgression: if he [the agent] was verified [by the debtor]: he has no recourse; if he was verified and he [the debtor] stipulated liability, or he [the debtor] denied him, or remained silent: he has recourse. Then when the principal has recourse against the debtor, and the debtor wishes to make him take an oath: 'I did not appoint an agent': he has that right if he paid the agent upon verification; if it was upon silence: he cannot make him take an oath unless he returns to verification.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Agency in al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains the ruling when someone claims to be an agent of an absent principal to collect a debt. The author expands on the matn of al-Quduri. The passage is a series of rulings with underlying legal reasoning. First, if the debtor verifies the alleged agent, the judge compels him to pay because the verification itself establishes the agency as a proof (since the debtor's admission is evidence against himself). Once paid, the debtor cannot retrieve the money. If the debtor remains silent or denies, he is not compelled to pay, but voluntary payment still binds him. Second, if the absent principal later appears: if he confirms the agency, the payment is valid; if he denies, the debtor must pay the principal again. This is because the debtor's payment to the alleged agent did not legally discharge the debt since the agency was not proven. The debtor then has recourse against the agent if the money is still in the agent's hand. If the agent lost the money without negligence, the debtor cannot recover because by verifying the agent he implicitly admitted the agent's right to take, and the agent is considered 'oppressed' — having taken the money mistakenly, not wrongfully. The passage also discusses a secondary issue: can the former agent testify about the right? If he was appointed for litigation and litigated, his testimony is rejected due to suspicion of self-interest. If he did not litigate, his testimony is accepted according to the sound opinion. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf holds that after any agency, the agent's testimony is never accepted, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad differentiate based on whether he litigated before a judge or elsewhere. The commentary ends with Al-Khujandi's summary and additional detail on oaths: the debtor can demand an oath from the principal only if he paid upon verification, not if he paid in silence without verification.

Key Terms
الوكالة (al-wikālah): A contract of representation where one person (the principal) appoints another (the agent) to act on his behalf in a specific matter, such as collecting a debt.
الغريم (al-gharīm): The debtor; the person who owes the debt that the agent claims to collect.
الوكيل (al-wakīl): The agent; the person appointed to act on behalf of the principal.
الغائب (al-ghā'ib): The absent principal; the creditor who is not present to confirm or deny the agency.
التصديق (al-taṣdīq): Verification or admission by the debtor that the claimant is indeed the agent of the absent principal.
الخصومة (al-khuṣūmah): Litigation; the act of disputing or defending a right before a judge or other authority.

Key Takeaway
A debtor who pays an alleged agent of an absent creditor does so at his own risk; if the principal denies the agency, the debtor must pay again but can seek recourse from the agent if the money remains.
