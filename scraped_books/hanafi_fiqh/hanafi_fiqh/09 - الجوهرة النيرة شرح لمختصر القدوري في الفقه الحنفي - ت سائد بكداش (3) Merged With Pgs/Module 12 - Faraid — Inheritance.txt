Puberty in Hanafi Fiqh: Signs, Age, and Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on bulugh conditions and age limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the primary signs of puberty (bulugh) for males and females, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the maximum age, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Puberty is established by physical signs; if absent, age limits vary within the Hanafi school.

In Hanafi fiqh, legal majority (bulugh) triggers full capacity for worship, contracts, and liabilities. This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah lays out the primary signs for males and females, then presents a key dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples (Sahibayn) regarding the maximum age when no signs appear. We will analyse these positions and see how later Hanafi practice has settled on one view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary signs
Find in the passage the explicit signs for males (ihtilam, inzal, ihbal) and females (hayd, ihtilam, hibl). Note that wet dream requires emission and desire.
These are the only signs that trigger immediate ruling of puberty.
2
Set the fallback age limits
When no primary sign appears, determine the age at which majority is assumed. Note the difference: Abu Hanifa sets 18 for males and 17 for females; Sahibayn set 15 for both.
The age of 'ashudd' (full strength) is the basis for Abu Hanifa's limit.
3
Compare the two positions
Understand the evidence for each view: Abu Hanifa relies on the Quranic verse (6:152) and Ibn Abbas' interpretation; Sahibayn rely on custom and practicality.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
4
Examine secondary signs
The passage lists pubic hair, breast development, armpit hair, etc. Note which are considered unreliable (zaghab, voice change) and which have ikhtilaf (pubic hair coarse growth accepted by Abu Yusuf).
In the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah), secondary signs are not sufficient.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Determine which view is relied upon for fatwa. According to later scholars, the Sahibayn's view (15 years for both) is preferred due to practical ease and broad acceptance.
Tarjih is based on convenience and prevailing custom.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah, a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the age of majority (bulugh) for males and females. The author first states the standard Hanafi position: puberty is determined by physical signs. For a male, these signs are wet dream (ihtilam) with emission, ejaculation in wakefulness, or impregnation if he engages in intercourse. For a female, signs are menstruation, wet dream, or pregnancy. If none of these signs appear, then according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the male is considered to have reached majority at eighteen years (Shamsi) and the female at seventeen years. The proof is the Quranic verse 'until he reaches his full strength' (ashudd), which Ibn Abbas interpreted as eighteen years. Since this is the minimum age reported for ashudd, the ruling is based on it to remain cautious.

Then the commentator mentions the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): both male and female reach majority upon completing fifteen years. This is a well-known ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school on the maximum age in the absence of signs.

Finally, the passage discusses other potential signs of puberty: pubic hair (nabat al-anah), coarse growth of which is considered by Abu Yusuf but not in the relied-upon view; breast development (nuhud al-thady) – not considered in the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) but some later scholars accept it; armpit and mustache hair – difference of opinion; soft down and voice change – not considered at all. The mufta bihi position is that these secondary signs are unreliable; only the primary signs (wet dream, ejaculation, menstruation, pregnancy) and age limits according to the preferred view (Sahibayn’s fifteen years is often relied upon in later fatwa) determine majority.

Defining Dangerous Illness in Hanafi Fiqh: Examples and Special Cases
Definitional list with a derived ruling for childbirth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi examples of dangerous and non-dangerous illnesses and explain why a woman in labor is treated analogously for the one-third estate rule.

Core Idea
Only life-threatening illnesses count as marad al-mawt; childbirth analogously restricts gratuitous acts to one-third.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the crucial distinction between dangerous (mukhif) and non-dangerous illnesses in Hanafi law. The classification determines whether a sick person's acts (like gifts or iqrar) are limited to one-third of his estate — a key rule of inheritance. The text lists specific examples and adds a special ruling for a woman in labor, whose condition is treated analogously to dangerous illness with a unique outcome depending on survival.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The text categorizes illnesses into two groups: 'marad mukhif' and 'ghayr mukhif'. List each example and note which group it falls under.
Dangerous: plague, colic, pleurisy, persistent nosebleed, continuous fever, frequent diarrhea, hemorrhage, terminal tuberculosis. Non-dangerous: scabies, toothache, ophthalmia, capillary hemorrhage.
2
Understand the basis for classification
Dangerous illnesses are all potentially fatal; recovery is not expected. Non-dangerous ones are minor ailments that do not threaten life.
The criterion is 'khawf' (fear of death) as determined by custom (urf).
3
Analyze the legal consequence
During a dangerous illness, any gratuitous act (gift, iqrar, charity) is limited to one-third of the estate. If the act exceeds one-third, the excess is invalid unless heirs approve.
This is the same rule as marad al-mawt (deathbed illness).
4
Apply the special ruling for labor
A woman in labor is treated as being in a dangerous state. However, if she survives, her acts become fully valid (no one-third limit). If she dies, the limit applies.
The text explicitly says: 'if she is saved, all that she did is valid.'
5
Distinguish from other schools
These examples are specific to Hanafi fiqh. Other schools may define dangerous illness more broadly. Custom (urf) can also expand or contract the list.

Multiple Claimants for Paternity: Hanafi Rulings and Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Rulings when two or three persons each claim a child, and inheritance consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings when two or three claimants each prove paternity of a child, and apply the inheritance consequences for the two-claimant case.

Core Idea
Two claimants with evidence make the child a son of both, inheriting one combined father's share; three claimants trigger a dispute: Abu Yusuf denies, Muhammad accepts lineage from up to three.

This passage from the Jawhara al-Nayira explores a rare scenario where multiple claimants each bring valid evidence of paternity for the same child. The Hanafi school addresses two-claimant and three-claimant cases distinctly, with the two-claimant case having explicit inheritance rules and the three-claimant case revealing an intra-madhhab disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Understanding these rulings clarifies how the school balances evidentiary proof with the practical limits of paternity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two separate cases: one with two claimants and another with three claimants. For each, note the ruling on lineage establishment and any inheritance implications.
Focus on the difference in number of claimants and the resulting judgment.
2
Analyze the two-claimant inheritance rule
When the child dies leaving children, the two fathers collectively inherit the share of one father, which is one-sixth (1/6). If one of the fathers dies, the surviving father takes the full one-sixth. Understand why the share is combined: they are treated as a single father in inheritance.
This prevents double inheritance of the father's share.
3
Examine the three-claimant dispute
Abu Yusuf holds that lineage cannot be established from three claimants; Muhammad holds that it can be established from three, but not from more than three. The passage does not give preference, leaving it as an internal ikhtilaf.
Later Hanafi works may incline toward Muhammad's view due to the strength of multiple evidences.
4
Consider the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Yusuf, the child has no recognized father from among the three; under Muhammad, the child has three fathers, which affects inheritance and other legal relationships.
This highlights the importance of knowing which position is mufta bihi in your jurisdiction.

Inheritance When Two Men Claim Paternity with Equal Evidence
Ruling on dual paternity inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on inheritance shares when two men present equal evidence of paternity.

Core Idea
Two valid proofs of paternity yield one father's inheritance share, not two.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a unique scenario where two men each present valid evidence that a child is their son. The Hanafi school resolves the conflict by recognizing dual paternity but limiting inheritance to a single father's share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claimants and Their Proof
Both men bring valid bayyinah (evidence) that the child is their son. This creates a conflict of equal proofs.
Bayyinah in Hanafi law typically requires two male witnesses or one male and two females.
2
Recognize the Legal Consequence of Equal Proofs
Since neither proof can be preferred, the child is legally considered the son of both men.
This is a departure from the norm of exclusive paternity.
3
Apply the Inheritance Rule on Child's Death
When the child dies, the two fathers collectively inherit only the share of a single father: one-sixth if the child leaves children (i.e., descendants).
They do not each get one-sixth; they share one-sixth.
4
Apply the Rule on Death of a Claimant Father
If one of the two fathers dies, the surviving father inherits the full one-sixth share, as if he were the sole father.
This follows from the principle that the surviving father now has undivided paternity.

Acknowledgment of Lineage from Non-Parents: Effect on Lineage and Inheritance
Ruling on acknowledgment of lineage and its inheritance effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of lineage from non-parents and apply the conditions under which the acknowledged person inherits.

Core Idea
Acknowledging lineage from non-parents does not establish lineage but may grant inheritance if no known heir exists.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a person acknowledges someone as a brother, uncle, or other relative who is not a parent or child. The Hanafi school distinguishes sharply between establishing lineage (which requires evidence such as birth) and creating inheritance rights through acknowledgment. The key principle is that one cannot impose lineage on another (ḥaml al-nasab ʿalā al-ghayr), but one can dispose of his property as he wishes when there is no known heir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that if a person acknowledges a lineage from someone other than parents or children (e.g., brother, uncle), the acknowledgment is not accepted for establishing lineage. This is the default ruling.
The Arabic term: 'lam yuqbal iqrāruhu fī al-nasab'.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is that acknowledging lineage from non-parents entails imposing lineage on another person (ḥaml al-nasab ʿalā al-ghayr), which is not permissible without proper evidence. The commentary provides this rational justification.
Note the distinction from acknowledging one's own lineage, which is accepted.
3
Distinguish the inheritance effect
Although lineage is not established, the acknowledged person may still inherit. This occurs only if the acknowledger has no known heir. The passage states: 'if he has a known heir, near or far, that heir is more entitled to the inheritance'. If no heir exists, the acknowledged person takes the entire inheritance.
4
Apply to a specific case: acknowledgment of a brother after father's death
The passage gives a specific example: if a person whose father has died acknowledges a brother, the brother's lineage from him is not established, but he still shares in the inheritance. This is because the acknowledgment implies two things: imposing lineage (which is not accepted) and conferring a right to inheritance (which is accepted if no other heir).
Important: the brother does not become a real heir; he takes the property akin to a bequest.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi variation
The passage mentions a narration from al-Hasan ibn Ziyād that Abu Hanīfa held that lineage can be established from up to five witnesses, but not more. This is a weaker, non-operative view (nawādir). The operative position (zāhir al-riwāya) is that the acknowledgment is not accepted for lineage at all.

Ruling on acknowledgment of kinship with proofs and examples

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of a relative gives inheritance but not lineage — you cannot establish another's birth; you can only share your property.

In the Hanafi school, when a person acknowledges another as a relative (e.g., a brother), this acknowledgment does not create a legal blood relationship. However, it does affect inheritance: the acknowledged person receives a share of the estate if there is no known heir. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this rule, including the analogy to a buyer acknowledging emancipation, and the practical consequences for inheritance shares.

The author uses an analogy: if a buyer acknowledges that the seller emancipated a slave, the emancipation is valid (since it's the seller's action), but the buyer cannot reclaim the price (since he has no authority over the seller).
This qiyas shows that acknowledgment can be accepted in one aspect and rejected in another.

When a son acknowledges a relative of his deceased father—such as his father's wife—the relative gains inheritance rights only from the son's own share. The woman gets 2/9 of the acknowledging son's half (which is 2/18 of the whole), the acknowledging son gets 7/9 of his half (7/18 of the whole). The denier keeps his whole half (9/18). So total shares: denier 9, woman 2, acknowledging son 7, sum 18. This illustrates the key Hanafi principle: an acknowledgment only binds the muqirr's own share, not the shares of others, so the denying son suffers no loss.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): A formal acknowledgment or admission by a person of a right belonging to another, which becomes binding on the acknowledging person alone.
مقر (muqirr): The person who makes the acknowledgment (iqrar). In this passage, it refers to the son who acknowledges a relative.
منكر (munkir): The person who denies or rejects the acknowledgment made by another. Here, the other son who denies the woman's status.
