Riba and the Half-Sa' Threshold: Ruling on Small Amounts
Elaborates on riba for small amounts and weight-based items, with a dispute on nasi'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on riba when one countervalue is below half sa' and the other reaches or exceeds it, and explain the rationale based on measurement as the cause.

Core Idea
An exchange of the same genus is only ribawi if one countervalue reaches the specified measure; below that, small amounts are negligible.

In this lesson, we examine a key nuance in Hanafi riba law: the treatment of small quantities. The Hanafi school establishes a minimum threshold—half a sa'—for measured items. Exchanges where both amounts are below this threshold are permissible even with disparity. However, when one amount reaches or exceeds the threshold, the sale becomes prohibited. This ruling demonstrates how the Shariah draws precise boundaries to prevent riba while allowing ordinary transactions of trivial amounts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the threshold
Half a sa' is the minimum measure for riba in grain-like items according to the Hanafi school.
A sa' is a volume measure; half sa' is the threshold.
2
Compare the two countervalues
Determine whether each countervalue reaches half sa' or not.
If both are below, the sale is permissible; if one is below and the other equal or above, it is prohibited.
3
Apply the rule
If both amounts are below half sa', exchange is permissible even if unequal. If one reaches half sa' and the other does not, the sale is invalid due to riba.
The presence of measurement in the larger amount triggers the prohibition.
4
Understand the cause
The cause (illa) of riba in this context is measurement (kayl) combined with genus. When one countervalue is measured (reaches half sa') and the other is not, the cause is present in the larger amount, making the transaction ribawi.
The smaller amount is treated as negligible (like a handful).

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on riba, focusing on practical applications. The Hanafi school, citing al-Hidayah, rules that amounts less than half a sa' are treated like handfuls (hafna): they have no fixed Shariah measure, so exchanging unequal handfuls of the same genus is permissible, provided neither reaches half a sa'. However, selling a handful for a qafiz (which is larger) is prohibited because one countervalue reaches the threshold of measurement while the other does not. This is due to the presence of the cause (measurement) in the larger amount. The same principle applies to weighed items like iron and lead; riba is established because weight and genus exist. The Shafi'i school disagrees, as these items lack edibility or currency status. Finally, a new dispute arises: according to Hanafis, selling the same genus for the same genus but with deferred payment (nasi'ah) is prohibited, even without excess, because the cause (genus) alone suffices for the prohibition of nasi'ah. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that genus alone does not prohibit nasi'ah; additional conditions are needed.

Key Terms
نصف صاع (niṣf ṣāʿ): A fixed Shariah measure; half a sa' is the minimum threshold for riba in measured items.
حفنة (ḥafnah): A handful; an indefinite small amount not reaching half a sa'.
النساء (al-nasīʾah): Riba due to deferment (delay) in exchange of same genus items.

Riba in Weighed Items and the Dispute over Nasi'ah
Elaborates on riba for small amounts and weight-based items, with a dispute on nasi'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position that riba applies to all weighed items (like iron and lead) and that mere genus similarity prohibits nasi'ah, and contrast it with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Weight and genus alone establish riba; nasi'ah is prohibited by genus alone in the Hanafi school.

This lesson extends the discussion of riba beyond grain to weighed items such as iron and lead. The Hanafi school holds that riba applies to any commodity that is sold by weight, regardless of edibility or monetary status. Furthermore, a new dimension is introduced: the prohibition of nasi'ah (deferred payment) when the genus is identical, even without excess. This position is directly challenged by Imam al-Shafi'i, who restricts riba to edibles and currency and does not consider genus alone sufficient for nasi'ah prohibition. We will analyze the difference and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Hanafi cause for riba in weighed items
The cause (illa) of riba in weighed items is quantity (weight) combined with genus. Thus, any item sold by weight, like iron, is ribawi.
The Shafi'i school requires edibility (tu'm) or currency (thimaniyya) as additional causes.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that iron and lead are not edible and not currency, so riba does not apply.
This limits ribawi items to staple foods and money.
3
Analyze the nasi'ah dispute
Hanafi: Selling wheat for wheat with deferred payment is prohibited even if the quantity is equal, because the genus alone prohibits nasi'ah. Shafi'i: For nasi'ah to be prohibited, additional conditions like measurement or weight are needed; genus alone is insufficient.
Nasi'ah is one of the two types of riba (riba al-nasi'ah).
4
Apply the rulings to a transaction
Practice: Determine whether exchanging 1 kg of iron for 1 kg of iron on the spot is permissible. Then consider deferred payment.
Hanafi: permissible if equal and immediate; prohibited if deferred. Shafi'i: permissible in all cases because iron is not ribawi.

Riba in Countable and Weighable Items: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on the 'illa of riba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits selling a watermelon for two watermelons while the Shafi'i prohibits it, and apply the Hanafi view on the effective cause of riba to new cases.

Core Idea
Riba applies only to standard measure or weight; count items are safe.

This passage from the Chapter of Riba presents the Hanafi position that the effective cause of riba is standard measure (kayl) or weight (wazn), not taste (ta'm). The Shafi'i school disagrees, arguing that taste is the cause. Through examples involving watermelons, eggs, and handfuls, the Hanafi school demonstrates that items sold by count or by amount less than half a sa' are not subject to riba. The passage also clarifies rulings for weighable metals like iron and lead. Understanding this dispute is crucial for distinguishing valid from invalid exchange contracts across different types of goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling and its basis
Note which transactions the Hanafi school permits (e.g., watermelon for two, handful for two) and which it prohibits (handful for qafiz). The key principle: items not sold by standard measure (kayl) or weight (wazn) are not subject to riba.
The effective cause is kayl/wazn, not taste.
2
Identify the Shafi'i ruling and its basis
The Shafi'i school prohibits selling watermelon for two because of shared taste (ta'm). They consider taste as the effective cause, so any exchange of like-for-like food items (even if by count) with excess or delay is riba.
Shafi'i relies on the hadith of six commodities and extends to all food with taste.
3
Locate the specific examples and their implications
The Hanafi school provides examples: watermelon (count), egg (count), handful (amount less than half sa') – these are permissible. However, a handful (less than half sa') exchanged for a qafiz (more than half sa') is prohibited because one side reaches the threshold of standard measure.
The threshold of half sa' is important: transactions below it are treated like handfuls.
4
Trace the logical extension to weighable items
The Hanafi school extends riba to iron and lead based on weight (wazn) and genus (jins). The Shafi'i rejects this because iron and lead lack taste and monetary value (thamaniyya).
This shows the practical difference: Hanafi prohibits riba in metals; Shafi'i permits it.

Genus Alone Prohibits Delay: The Hanafi Rule on Like-for-Like Sales
Ruling on selling like-for-like with delay and proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school prohibits delay (nasi'ah) in exchanging items of the same genus, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
In the Hanafi school, selling a sheep for a sheep on credit is invalid — genus alone prohibits delay, even if equal.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the Hanafi position on when a like-for-like exchange becomes riba. While both schools agree that excess (tafadul) is prohibited, they differ on whether delay (nasi'ah) alone, when quantities are equal, invalidates the contract. The commentator uses concrete examples — dates, sheep, slaves — to illustrate the reasoning rooted in the hadith of six commodities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
Determine the ruling for selling a measured or weighed item for its own genus, like for like. The passage states it is permissible if equal and hand-to-hand, but not if there is excess or delay.
The basic rule: equality + hand-to-hand = valid; any excess or delay = invalid.
2
Recognize the dispute
Find the point of ikhtilaf: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i on whether delay alone (nasi'ah) invalidates when quantities are equal. Hanafi says yes, Shafi'i says no for items other than gold and silver.
The dispute is about the effective cause (illah) of the prohibition.
3
Trace the proof
Examine the hadith cited (wheat, barley, salt, dates, gold, silver) and how Hanafi derives that genus alone prohibits delay. Understand the qiyas from gold/silver to all measured/weighed items.
Hanafi argues that the condition 'hand to hand' in the hadith applies to all six commodities, indicating that genus is the cause.
4
Apply to new cases
Work through the examples: Harawi/Marwi dates, sheep, slave. Confirm that Hanafi prohibits delayed exchange in each case because genus alone suffices.
The thamarah al-khilaf: a sheep-for-sheep credit sale is invalid in Hanafi but valid in Shafi'i.

The basic principle: if sold like for like (equal amount) and hand-to-hand, it is permissible; if there is excess or delay, it is prohibited. Selling Like-for-Like of Same Genus: Ruling and Proof
Ruling with proof from hadith and comparison with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling same-genus items like-for-like, derive the prohibition of excess and credit from the hadith of six ribawi items, and explain the point of difference with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
When selling same-genus ribawi items, equality in amount and hand-to-hand delivery are required; excess or credit invalidates.

This passage from the sharh of al-Quduri’s Mukhtasar lays down the foundational rule for riba in exchange contracts. You will learn the precise conditions for a valid sale of measured or weighed items of the same kind, and how the Hanafi school extends the prohibition of credit beyond the explicit text. The proof is the well-known hadith listing six commodities, which the Hanafis interpret to include genus as a cause for prohibiting delay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its conditions
Read the opening sentence: selling a measured or weighed item for its own kind like-for-like is permissible; with excess it is not. Note that equality in quantity is a condition.
The ruling applies to both makil (measured) and mawzun (weighed) items.
2
Locate the dalil from hadith
The author cites the hadith of the six ribawi items (wheat, barley, salt, dates, gold, silver) with the command to exchange like-for-like and hand-to-hand. This is the textual proof.
The hadith is ahad but accepted as evidence in muamalat.
3
Extract the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'like for like' indicates equality is required; the prohibition of excess shows any increase is riba. 'Hand to hand' indicates immediate delivery, so delay is also prohibited when genus is present.
Hanafi usul: al-amr yufidu al-wujub; the prohibition implies fasād.
4
Analyze the ikhtilaf with Shafi'i
Shafi'i holds that credit is allowed in items not measured/weighed (e.g., animals, slaves) because the cause of riba is measure/weight or food. Hanafi says genus alone suffices, as seen in gold and silver in the hadith.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both schools are valid.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The Hanafi position (credit prohibited whenever genus is present) is the relied-upon view in the madhhab, as affirmed by the author.
Follow this ruling for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

Selling by Estimation: Zufar's Disagreement
Intra-Hanafi dispute on selling by estimation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi majority position from Zufar's opinion on selling by estimation (mujazafah) for ribawi items, and apply the condition that knowledge of equality must exist at the time of contract.

Core Idea
Knowledge of equality must exist at contract time, not after, because riba requires certainty.

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute over exchanging two heaps of the same food by estimation (mujazafah). The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) holds the contract invalid even if later found equal, while Zufar argues that actual equality suffices. The key is whether knowledge of equality (al-ilm bi al-musawah) is a condition at the moment of contract.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi majority position
Identify the ruling of the majority (Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn) on this sale: it is fasid (invalid) because the condition of known equality at contract time is not met.
The contract is fasid, not batil, because it can be corrected if rescinded.
2
Identify Zufar's opinion and his proof
Zufar argues the contract is valid because actual equality (mumathalah) is found after measurement. His proof: the condition of equality is fulfilled in reality.
Zufar focuses on actual outcome rather than knowledge at contract.
3
Follow the Hanafi response
The majority responds that ribawi transactions require strict precaution; uncertainty at contract time (even if later resolved) invalidates. Knowledge of equality is a condition, not merely actual equality.
The principle is: 'al-mumathalah mashrutah bil-ilm' (equality is conditioned on knowledge).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Hanafi majority: contract is void and must be rescinded. Zufar: contract is valid and binding. Practical difference: in Zufar's view, no action needed; in majority, the sale is fasid and the parties must separate the goods.
Thamarah al-khilaf: the practical consequence in real transactions.
5
Determine mufta bihi
The majority view (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school. Zufar's opinion is considered a minority view.
Tarjih authority: Zahir al-Riwayah supports the majority.

Selling Good Quality for Bad Quality in Ribawi Items: Quantity, Not Quality
Ruling on quality disparity and conditions for riba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when selling ribawi items of the same genus, any difference in quality does not justify inequality in quantity; only equal quantity makes the sale valid.

Core Idea
In riba, quality has no value; only quantity by weight or measure counts.

When you exchange wheat for wheat, or silver for silver, the law of riba prohibits any excess, even if one is higher quality. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position: quality is legally irrelevant—what matters is that the quantities are equal, measured by the appropriate standard (kayl or wazn). We'll also see when excess and credit become permissible: when the genus or the measuring standard differs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage states: 'It is not permissible to sell good quality for poor quality from that which involves riba except like for like.' Identify the act (sale of ribawi items of same genus) and the prohibition (excess).
The prohibition is about disparity in quantity, not quality.
2
Understand why quality is ignored
The author explains that quality has no legal value when meeting its genus in ribawi items. The effective cause (illa) of riba is genus plus the attached meaning (al-ma'na al-madmun ilayh), which is the means of quantitation (kayl for staples, wazn for metals). Quality does not affect this cause.
3
Define 'al-ma'na al-madmun ilayh'
The passage defines it as the measure (kayl) in wheat and the weight (wazn) in silver. It is the standard by which the item is typically quantified in trade.
This is key: for riba to apply, both parties must be exchanging items from the same genus AND quantified by the same medium.
4
Apply the two scenarios
When both genus and attached meaning are absent (e.g., walnuts for eggs), excess and credit are permissible. When both are present (e.g., wheat for wheat), excess and credit are prohibited. Work through the examples: Herawi dates for Marwi dates (if considered different species?) and walnuts for eggs.

If you want to exchange one sack of high-quality wheat for one sack of low-quality wheat, it is valid because the quantity is equal. But if you try to give 1 kg of high-quality for 1.2 kg of low-quality, it is prohibited due to riba.
Credit (nasa') is also prohibited when both genus and measure are the same.

Conditions for Riba in Sales: Genus, Measure, and Knowledge of Equality
Sharh explaining conditions for riba and genus classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules of riba to determine whether excess (tafadul) and credit (nasa') are permissible in a given exchange of ribawi items.

Core Idea
In ribawi sales, genus and measure together prohibit both excess and credit; differing either allows excess but not credit.

This commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar tackles the precise conditions that trigger the prohibition of riba in exchanges of specific commodities. You will learn how to classify items by genus and measure, and understand why knowledge of equality at contract time is essential. The passage also clarifies a key intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar and contrasts the Hanafi position with Malik's on genus classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key descriptors
The passage introduces two descriptors that govern riba: genus (al-jins) and the standard measure (al-ma'na al-madmum ilayh), which is either volume (kayl) or weight (wazn). Understand that each ribawi item has a fixed measure (e.g., wheat is measured by volume, silver by weight).
The measure is attached to the genus; it defines the quantity that must be equal.
2
Apply the rule to the four scenarios
The passage presents four scenarios based on presence or absence of these descriptors: (1) both absent → excess and credit allowed; (2) both present → both forbidden; (3) one present and one absent → excess allowed but credit forbidden; (4) equality unknown at contract → Hanafi majority forbids even if equality later confirmed.
Use the examples: wheat/barley (different genus, same volume) → excess allowed, credit forbidden; silver/gold (different genus, same weight) → same ruling.
3
Analyze the Zufar dispute
Zufar argues that if equality exists, the sale is valid even without knowledge at contract time. The Hanafi majority, citing caution (ihtiyat), requires knowledge of equality at the time of contract. Identify the practical consequence: if two parties exchange wheat for wheat without knowing exact equality, the contract is void in the majority view.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is the majority view.
4
Understand genus classification
The passage classifies many items: all dates are one genus, all grapes one genus, all wheat one genus, etc. But wheat and barley are separate genera. Contrast with Malik, who deems wheat and barley one genus, thus prohibiting excess.
Genus is based on common name and usage, not botanical family.
5
Apply to a multi-variable scenario
Combine the rules: dates (genus dates, measure volume) with raisins (genus raisins, measure volume) → both different genera but same measure → excess allowed, credit forbidden. If selling dates for dates (same genus, same measure) → both excess and credit forbidden.
Always check both genus and measure to determine the ruling.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri addresses rulings on riba (usury/interest) in the exchange of grains and their derivatives. The passage opens with the general rule: it is not permissible to sell wheat for flour or for saweeq (parched grain meal), whether equal or unequal, because they belong to the same genus. The commentator expands this by comparing it to the exchange of like items (wheat for wheat, barley for barley) where hand-to-hand delivery in the sitting is not required—unlike currency exchange (sarf). He then discusses cases where one item is a debt (dāʾin) and the other is a specific good (ʿayn). If the specific good is the sold item (mabīʿ), it is permissible but the debt must be delivered in the sitting before separation. If the debt is the sold item, it is not permissible because one would be selling what one does not possess. The commentator notes that knowing which is the price (thaman) and which is the sold item is determined by the preposition 'bāʾ' (ب) entering upon it. The commentator then explains the reasoning: wheat, flour, and saweeq are considered one genus because flour is implicitly present in wheat; grinding just separates it. Thus, exchanging wheat for flour is akin to exchanging flour for flour with an increase (because flour is more voluminous). He analogizes this to fried wheat vs. unfried wheat. The core of the passage is the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding selling flour for saweeq. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, it is not permissible because there is implied homoloty (mujānasa) between wheat, flour, and saweeq; they all derive from the same grain, so exchanging any two of them is like exchanging the same genus with potential increase. They argue that even though nourishment is common, the difference in primary use creates a distinction. They support their view by analogizing to selling good wheat for worm-eaten wheat, which is permissible despite one being superior. The text does not explicitly resolve the dispute, so both views are presented from the authoritative sources.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prohibits flour-sawiq sale due to hidden homogeneity; Sahibayn allow it due to different purposes.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on whether flour (دقيق) and sawiq (parched barley flour) are considered the same genus for riba purposes. Abu Hanifa holds that they are homogeneous because both derive from the same grain, while the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) consider them different genera due to their distinct intended uses. Understanding this disagreement sharpens your ability to apply genus homogeneality (مُجانَسَة) in riba transactions.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa permits and the Sahibayn prohibit selling fresh dates for dried dates when equal in weight, and identify the practical consequence of this ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
Fresh dates are legally dates according to the Prophet's words, but drying reduces weight—hence the split.

This passage addresses a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on whether fresh dates (rutab) can be exchanged for dried dates (tamr) in equal weight. The disagreement turns on how to reconcile two hadiths and whether the decrease upon drying triggers riba concerns. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to weigh textual evidence and practical reasoning in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
The base text (Quduri) states that selling fresh dates for dried dates like-for-like is permissible. Note that this is a general statement without qualification.
The matn simplifies; the commentary expands.
2
Trace Abu Hanifa's evidence
Abu Hanifa relies on the hadith where the Prophet called fresh dates 'tamr'. Therefore, they are the same species, and equal exchange is valid per riba rules.
The key phrase: 'أو كل تمر خيبر هكذا'
3
Trace the Sahibayn's evidence
They cite another hadith: the Prophet forbade selling fresh dates for dried dates when informed that fresh dates lose weight upon drying. They see this as a general prohibition to prevent riba.
The hadith: 'أينقص إذا جف؟ قال: فلا إذا'
4
Analyse Abu Hanifa's response
He interprets the prohibition as specific to a guardian of an orphan, out of concern for the orphan's interest, not as a general ruling invalidating the contract.
This is a takhsīs al-ʿilla (specification of the effective cause).
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa considers the contract valid; the Sahibayn deem it invalid (prohibited and void).
This affects fatwa and ḥiyal (legal stratagems) for avoiding riba.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on riba discusses the ruling on selling fresh dates (rutab) for dried dates (tamr) and grapes (anab) for raisins (zabib). The base matn from Mukhtasar al-Quduri states that selling fresh dates for dried dates is permissible when the exchange is equal in weight (mithlan bi mithl). The commentator then explains that this ruling is according to Abu Hanifa, who considers fresh dates to be a type of date based on the hadith where the Prophet ﷺ referred to fresh dates from Khaybar as 'tamr'. For Abu Hanifa, since fresh and dried dates are the same species, an equal exchange is valid. However, Abu Hanifa's two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that such a sale is not permissible—even if equal—because fresh dates shrink upon drying, leading to an eventual disparity in a ribawi transaction. They cite the hadith where the Prophet ﷺ asked whether fresh dates decrease when dried, and upon receiving an affirmative answer, forbade the sale. The commentator reconciles the two hadith by citing al-Nihayah: the prohibition was specific to a guardian of an orphan, out of concern for the orphan's interest, not as a general rule. Thus, the hadith does not invalidate the contract. The same dispute applies to the sale of fresh dates for fresh dates and unripe dates (busr) for fresh dates. For grapes and raisins, the ruling follows the same pattern: Abu Hanifa permits equal exchange, while the two students prohibit it. However, one opinion holds that it is impermissible by consensus, drawing an analogy to fried and unfried wheat. Abu Hanifa distinguishes the two cases: for dates, the hadith explicitly calls fresh dates 'tamr', so they share the same legal identity; for grapes and raisins, no such textual identification exists, so the analogy does not automatically apply. The passage illustrates an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf based on different textual evidence and interpretive methods.

Key Terms
الربا (al-ribā): Usury or interest, prohibited in Islamic law; includes riba al-fadl (excess in exchange of same species) and riba al-nasi'ah (deferred increase).
الرطب (al-rutab): Fresh, moist dates (fresh fruit).
التمر (al-tamr): Dried dates.
مثلاً بمثل (mithlan bi mithl): Like for like; equal in weight or measure for the same species.

Barter of Fresh and Dried Dates: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on riba al-fadl for dates and grapes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on bartering fresh dates for dried dates and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa permits exchanging fresh dates for dried dates if equal; the Sahibayn prohibit it.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of bartering fresh dates (rutab) for dried dates (tamr) and grapes for raisins. The disagreement hinges on whether fresh and dried forms of the same fruit are considered the same genus for riba al-fadl. Abu Hanifa allows the exchange when equal by weight, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), prohibit it. Understanding this ikhtilaf deepens your appreciation of how custom and textual evidence shape Hanafi rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the legal question? Is bartering fresh dates for dried dates (or grapes for raisins) subject to riba al-fadl?
2
State the Hanafi position of Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa permits the exchange if equal by weight. He bases this on custom (urf) and a specific hadith where the Prophet called fresh dates 'tamr' only in that context.
3
State the opposing position of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad prohibit the exchange. They argue that fresh dates are essentially the same as dried dates, so any unequal exchange constitutes riba.
4
Analyze the evidence for each view
Abu Hanifa relies on custom and the limited scope of the hadith. The Sahibayn rely on qiyas (analogy) treating fresh and dried as the same genus.
5
Determine the practical outcome (thamarah al-khilaf)
Following Abu Hanifa, one may exchange fresh for dried dates if equal. Following the Sahibayn, such exchanges are prohibited regardless of equality.
Later Hanafi practice often follows Abu Hanifa, but some muftis prefer the Sahibayn's view for caution.

Ruling on Barter of Olives with Oil and Similar Items
Rulings on exchanging natural products with their processed extracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for bartering natural products with their processed extracts and apply the condition of excess to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
Bartering olives for oil is only permissible if the oil given exceeds the oil in the olives – the excess compensates for the pressed residue.

This passage from the chapter on riba addresses a common transaction: exchanging a natural product for its processed derivative. The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes between riba and permissible trade by requiring a specific condition to account for the by-product. Understanding this ruling helps avoid prohibited uncertainty (jahalah) and riba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited base transaction
Recognize that exchanging olives for oil, sesame for sesame oil, etc., is generally prohibited unless a specific condition is met.
The prohibition stems from potential riba due to same genus and inequality.
2
Recognize the condition that makes it permissible
The oil given must be more than the oil content in the original commodity. The excess oil is considered payment for the pressed residue (thajir), a separate commodity.
The amount of oil in the original must be known or approximated to avoid jahalah.
3
Understand the rationale of thajir
The pressed residue is a distinct commodity, so the excess oil is not riba but a legitimate exchange for that residue.
This concept is unique to Hanafi fiqh and enables the transaction.
4
Apply to analogous cases
The same rule applies to walnuts with oil, milk with butter, grapes with juice, and dates with date syrup.
In each case, the processed extract is paired with the residue.
5
Distinguish the cotton-yarn case
For cotton with yarn, equality is required because spinning reduces weight without producing a separate commodity. Selling yarn for cloth is always permissible.
The consensus on canvas for cotton further illustrates different commodity status.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on riba outlines rulings for bartering natural products with their processed derivatives. The general rule is that if a product (e.g., olives) contains a certain amount of oil, selling it for its own oil is not permissible unless the oil given in exchange exceeds the amount of oil that would be extracted from the olives. This is because the excess oil is compensation for the pressed residue (thajir) which is a separate commodity. Thus, one must give more oil so that the oil equal to the oil content of the olives is exchanged for the oil itself without increase (which would be riba), and the additional oil is payment for the residue. This ruling applies also to sesame with sesame oil (shiraj), walnuts with their oil, milk with butter, grapes with grape juice, and dates with date syrup (dibs). In all these cases, if the amount of oil or extract in the original item is unknown, the transaction is prohibited because it may involve riba due to uncertainty. The passage then addresses the case of cotton and yarn: according to al-Dhakhira, exchanging cotton yarn for cotton is only permissible if equal, because spinning reduces the weight and it is like wheat and flour. Fatawa Qadi Khan also requires equality. However, exchanging yarn for cloth is always permissible because cloth is a different manufactured product. Finally, it is unanimously permissible to exchange canvas (karbas) for cotton in any quantity, as they are different commodities.

No riba when master and slave are effectively one owner; but indebtedness breaks unity, even within Hanafi school.

The general rule in Hanafi fiqh is that transactions between a master and his slave are free from riba because the slave's property belongs to the master. However, this rule has important conditions—the slave must be authorized to trade (ma'dhun) and must not be indebted. When the slave is indebted, the unity of ownership is compromised, and a well-known disagreement arises between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) on the status of the slave's property. Understand the General Ruling
The passage states 'No riba between the master and his slave.' The reason given is that the slave and what he possesses are the property of the master. This means a master can exchange ribawi items with his slave without riba because it is like dealing with himself.
This is based on the principle that riba requires two distinct parties.

The ruling applies only when the slave is ma'dhun (permitted to trade) and is not indebted. If the slave is not ma'dhun or is indebted, the unity of ownership is affected, and riba may occur. The text explicitly says 'if he is ma'dhun and not indebted.'

When the slave is indebted, Abu Hanifa holds that the property in the slave's hand belongs to the slave (but goes to creditors), so the master and slave become separate parties and riba applies. The practical difference may arise in other rulings (e.g., if the slave's property is used to pay off debts).

The passage concludes that the same ruling applies between a master and his mukatab (slave on contract of manumission). The mukatab is considered a separate party in financial matters, so riba can occur between them just as with an indebted ma'dhun slave.

Commentary
This passage is from the sharh (commentary) on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, expanding on the chapter of istibra'. The author explains that istibra' (a waiting period to verify the womb is empty) is of two types: recommended (mustahabb) for the seller, and obligatory (wajib) for the buyer.

For the seller: if he owns a slave girl with whom he has had intercourse and intends to transfer ownership (by sale, gift, marriage, etc.), it is recommended that he wait for one menstruation to ensure she is not pregnant from him. This is to avoid selling his own child unknowingly. However, if he does not do so, and the new owner (husband or buyer) has intercourse with her, no further istibra' is required of the new owner.

For the buyer: when a person acquires a slave girl (by any means—sale, gift, inheritance, etc.), it is obligatory for him to wait before having intercourse with her. The proof is the hadith of the Prophet (SAW) regarding the captives of Awtas: "No pregnant woman is to be approached until she gives birth, nor a non-pregnant woman until she is made to wait for one menstruation." This obligation applies regardless of how he obtained her, even if she is a virgin, because the cause is the acquisition of ownership. The method of istibra' depends on her condition: if she menstruates, one menstruation; if she does not menstruate (due to age or illness), one month; if she is pregnant, until delivery.

A key point: the menstruation that occurs before taking possession (e.g., after the contract but before the buyer physically receives her) does not count, because the ruling (istibra') cannot precede its cause (ownership + possession). However, Imam Abu Yusuf disagreed: he held that in cases of purchase, inheritance, and bequest, a menstruation occurring after the contract but before possession is sufficient. The relied-upon position according to the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa (and Muhammad): the menstruation must occur after possession. This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.

Rulings on istibra' after a fasid purchase

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A fasid sale transfers ownership upon possession, but the buyer cannot have intercourse; if the sale is rescinded, the seller must perform istibra'.

A fasid (invalid) sale in Hanafi fiqh is not completely void; it transfers ownership upon possession, though with defects. This leads to specific rules regarding istibra' (the waiting period to ensure a slave girl is not pregnant). Understanding these rules is crucial for correct practice in slave transactions, and the reasoning reveals the Hanafi concern for preventing lineage confusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage presents three distinct situations: (1) initial fasid purchase with possession; (2) later valid purchase after menstruation during fasid possession; (3) rescission of fasid sale by a judge and return to seller. Recognize each scenario and the specific ruling attached.
Each scenario has a different ruling on istibra'.
2
Analyze the buyer's prohibition
Even if the slave girl menstruates while in the buyer's possession under a fasid contract, he may not have intercourse. Understand that this is because the prohibition is a religious right (haqq Allah), not merely a contract right. The menstruation does not remove the prohibition.
The buyer's ownership is imperfect (fasid) so intercourse remains forbidden.
3
Understand why previous menstruation does not count
If later the buyer purchases her with a valid contract, the menstruation that occurred during the fasid possession is not counted for istibra'. This is because that period was not a valid ownership domain for the purpose of istibra'.
Istibra' must be performed during a period of valid ownership (sahih).
4

When the judge rescinds the fasid sale and she returns to the seller, the seller must perform istibra' before having intercourse. Reason: ownership during fasid sale was effective (upon possession), so there is a possibility of pregnancy from the buyer. The seller must clear that possibility.
This is analogous to the case of a foster-relative, where the seller must also do istibra' after return.

Return for defect is a second sale: the seller must perform a new istibra' even if she already menstruated at the buyer's.

The ruling highlights the Hanafi principle that return for defect is legally analogous to a new sale or rescission (iqala), necessitating a fresh istibra' to protect lineage and ownership rights.

The seller must not approach her until she menstruates once after the return. This applies whether the return is by court order or mutual consent.

The ruling is unconditional: the seller must wait for a fresh menstruation regardless of how the return was effected (judicial or mutual).
This ensures no gap in the protection of lineage.

Waiting Period for Istibra' When Slave-Girl Does Not Menstruate
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the waiting periods for istibra' according to the Hanafi imams and apply the principle that the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is relied upon for fatwa.

Core Idea
When a purchased slave-girl does not menstruate, the Hanafi majority requires a three-month waiting period; other imams differ.

In this lesson, we examine a key dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the waiting period (istibra') for a purchased slave-girl who does not menstruate. The imams differ based on the time it takes for pregnancy to become apparent. Understanding this dispute helps you see how legal reasoning (ijtihad) operates within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
A buyer purchases a slave-girl of menstruating age who does not menstruate. The question is: when may he have intercourse with her?
Istibra' is wajib to avoid mixing lineages.
2
State the positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: three months and more; Muhammad: two months and five days; Zufar: two years.
These are all within the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the proofs
The majority rely on observable signs of pregnancy within three months. Muhammad considers the minimum period for pregnancy detection. Zufar considers maximum gestation.
The proof is based on experience (aql) and analogy to iddah.
4
Determine the tarjih
The accepted view is the majority (three months) as per the author of Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, citing Al-Hidayah.
Tarjih is done based on consistency with other rulings and practicality.
5
Apply the thamarah
The practical difference: buyer may have intercourse after two months according to Muhammad, but must wait three months according to the mufta bihi.
The buyer should follow the mufta bihi unless he follows Muhammad's ijtihad.
