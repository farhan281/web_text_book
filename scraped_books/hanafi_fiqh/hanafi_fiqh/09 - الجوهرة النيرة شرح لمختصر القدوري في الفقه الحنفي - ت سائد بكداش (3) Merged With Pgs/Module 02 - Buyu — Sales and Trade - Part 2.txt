Core Idea
Trees permanently attached to land are included in its sale, just like a building.

In Hanafi sale law, when land is sold, anything permanently attached to it is included automatically. This lesson explores the ruling regarding trees and date palms, and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path

2
Extract the primary proof
The text gives a reason: 'because they are attached to it for permanence'. This is the illa (cause) for inclusion.
3
Analyze the qiyas
The text analogizes trees to a building: a building is attached to land for permanence, so it is included. Trees have the same attribute.

4

Apply the rule
In practice, when drafting a sale contract for land in a Hanafi context, you must explicitly exclude trees if you want them separate.

Core Idea
When you sell a house or land, the building and trees are automatically included—they are attributes of the thing sold.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri explains a key principle of Hanafi sales law: the default inclusion of permanently attached items. Understanding this helps you know what is and is not included in a sale contract without explicit mention, based on customary usage and legal analogy.

The commentator gives two reasons: the name 'house' customarily includes the yard and building; and the building is an attribute of the house, so it follows the sale.
Custom ('urf) is a recognized source of law in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the reasoning for the trees
Trees are included because they are permanently attached to the land (ittisal qarar), like a building. They remain in the land indefinitely, so they are part of the land.

When a house is sold, all that is within its four boundaries enters the sale: rooms, chambers, upper and lower parts, kitchen, well, latrine, etc.
This shows the definition of 'house' encompasses all permanent fixtures.

Recognize the ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require explicit mention for trees, but Hanafi holds automatic inclusion. This difference stems from different weighting of custom and analogy.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi within the school.

Fruit on Trees Sold with Land: Intra-Hanafi Disputes on Option and Price Deduction
Intra-Hanafi dispute on option and price deduction when fruit is consumed before delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on buyer's option when fruit perishes after sale, and calculate the correct price deduction according to each view.

Core Idea
When fruit on sold trees is consumed before delivery, the buyer's right to cancel and the deduction from price vary among the Hanafi imams.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explores a classic dispute: what happens when land with fruit-bearing trees is sold, and the fruit perishes or is eaten before the buyer takes delivery? The three main Hanafi authorities—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani—offer different answers about whether the buyer can cancel the sale (khiyar) and how much to deduct from the price. Understanding these nuances is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to khiyar al-tafarril (option of separation) and price apportionment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main scenarios
The passage treats two distinct cases: (1) fruit exists at the time of contract and is stipulated for the buyer; (2) fruit does not exist at contract but appears before delivery. The ruling and dispute differ based on timing.
Scenario 1 has consensus on price division (thirds). Scenario 2 generates the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
2
Understand the consensus positions
When fruit is present at contract and stipulated, price is divided equally among land, trees, and fruit (three equal shares). If the fruit perishes by an act of God (heavenly calamity), nothing is deducted and the buyer has no option—this is agreed upon. Also, if the seller consumes the fruit in scenario 1, the buyer has an option (by consensus) and a deduction of one-third of the price.
Consensus exists on these points, as stated in the text: 'إجماعاً' and 'في قولهم جميعاً'.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on option in scenario 2
When fruit appears after contract and the seller eats it before delivery, Abu Hanifa holds that the buyer has no option (لا خيار له). Muhammad gives the buyer the option. Abu Yusuf also gives the option but with a different deduction calculation. This difference stems from how they view the fruit in relation to the contract.
The text: 'ولا خيار له عند أبي حنيفة خاصة، وعند محمد : له الخيار. وقال أبو يوسف: ... وله الخيار'.
4
Distinguish the price deduction calculations
In scenario 2, if the seller eats the fruit, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (according to the initial 'عندهما') deduct one-third of the price. However, Abu Yusuf deducts one-quarter, reasoning that the price is divided first between land and palms (halves), then within the palms the fruit gets half of the palms' share, resulting in a one-quarter deduction for the fruit.
Calculate: land 1/2, palms 1/2; then palms 1/2 split into palm (1/4) and fruit (1/4). So fruit's share is 1/4. Abu Yusuf's view is that the deduction should be 1/4, not 1/3.
5
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Consider a case where land is valued at 400, trees at 400, and fruit at 200 (total 1000). If the seller eats the fruit after contract but before delivery, compute the deduction under each imam's view: Abu Hanifa/Muhammad: deduct 1/3 of total price (333.33) – buyer pays 666.67; Abu Yusuf: deduct 1/4 (250) – buyer pays 750. Also note that option rights differ.
The text does not specify a mufta bihi (operative opinion). In practice, later Hanafi authorities may choose one view; consult reliable fatwa sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding standing crops in a land sale and identify when crops are included.

Core Idea
Crops are not part of the land; they remain the seller's unless explicitly named in the sale.

This passage from the matn establishes a key distinction in Hanafi sales law: what is included automatically in a land sale. While trees are considered part of the land because of their permanent attachment, crops are temporary and thus not included unless specified. Understanding this rule prevents disputes over ownership after the sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The matn states a clear default: crops do not enter the sale of land. Identify the act (selling land) and the ruling (crops excluded).
2
Understand the exception
The rule ends with 'except by naming'. This means if the buyer wants the crops, he must explicitly stipulate them in the contract.
3
Distinguish crops from trees
Trees are permanently attached to the land, so they are included automatically. Crops are seasonal and will be harvested, so they are considered movable property.
This distinction is based on the Hanafi view of permanence (istiqrar).
4
Apply the rule
In a real sale, if the contract is silent, the seller retains ownership of the crops. The buyer only acquires the land. If the crops are already harvested, they are definitely not part of the land.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Fruit belongs to the buyer as growth, but how it values and whether option exists splits Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

This passage dives into an intra-Hanafi dispute about fruit that appears on sold land and palm trees after the contract but before the buyer takes delivery. While all agree the fruit belongs to the buyer, they diverge on how to value it as an 'increase' (ziyada) and whether the buyer can rescind the sale if the seller consumes the fruit. Guided Path
1
Locate the core ruling

Consider two exceptions: (a) heavenly calamity – no deduction nor option unanimously; (b) when separate prices are named for land and trees – fruit is unanimously increase on trees only, reaffirming the same deduction and option disagreement.
Heavenly calamity eliminates compensation because fruit was not delivered and not consumed.
5
Synthesize the thamarah al-khilaf
Summarize the practical differences: deduction fraction (1/3 vs 1/4) and whether buyer has option to rescind. Recognize that no single mufta bihi is given, but later Hanafi practice often adopts the Sahibayn or Abu Hanifa depending on the issue.

Sale of Fruit and the Condition of Leaving on the Tree
Explains ruling on fruit sale and condition of leaving

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the sale of fruit on trees, explain when the sale becomes invalid due to a condition of leaving, and apply the rules of profit allocation in different scenarios.

Core Idea
A fruit sale is valid even if unripe, but the buyer must cut immediately; a condition to leave it invalidates the sale.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position on selling fruit that remains on the tree. The law distinguishes between the validity of the sale itself and the duty to remove the fruit promptly. We explore the consequences of stipulating a delay, as well as the rules governing profit when the fruit is left on the tree with or without permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State the rule: selling fruit (ripe or unripe) is valid, and the buyer must cut it immediately to vacate the seller's ownership.
This is the default ruling for an absolute sale.
2
Analyze the invalidating condition
If the buyer stipulates leaving the fruit on the tree, the sale becomes fasid (invalid). Why? Because it is a condition not required by the contract, it occupies the seller's property without right, and it combines two transactions in one (sale + loan/rental).
This is a condition that benefits the buyer beyond the contract.
3
Distinguish permission scenarios
If the buyer leaves the fruit with the seller's permission, the profit is lawful. Without permission, the increase in value must be given in charity. If the fruit has reached full size, no charity is needed as the change is only in state.
Profit lawfulness depends on the basis of retention.
4
Examine rental cases
If the buyer rents the tree until ripening, the profit is lawful because the invalid rental does not negate permission. But if he buys green crops and rents the land, the rental is void due to ignorance of harvest time, and he must pay fair rent and only keep profit up to the price and rent.
The distinction hinges on the object rented (tree vs. land).
5
Note the method of reasoning
The Hanafi school uses legal reasoning (ta'lil) based on principles: conditions not required by contract, occupation of another's property, and combining transactions.
This shows how fiqh rulings are derived from core usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a valid exception (istithna) and an invalid conditional clause ('ala an li) in a sale of fruit, and state the Hanafi ruling for each.

Core Idea
A true exception excludes from the start; a condition includes then removes with unknown price share, causing gharar.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains a key nuance in Hanafi sales law: the difference between excluding a known quantity from a sale and adding a condition that the seller retains a part. The ruling hinges on whether the excluded item is initially part of the sale or not. Understanding this distinction prevents invalid contracts due to gharar (uncertainty).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage contrasts two phrasings: 'I sell you this flock except this sheep' (istithna) versus 'I sell you this flock on condition that I have this sheep.' Recognize that the first is a true exception and the second is a conditional retention.
The same distinction applies to fruit: 'except one known pound' vs. 'on condition that I have one pound.'
2
Analyze the effect on the sale
In a true exception, the excluded item never enters the contract, so the price covers only the remainder. In the conditional phrase, the item first enters the sale, then exits with an unknown portion of the price, creating gharar.
The unknown portion is the key to invalidity.
3
Examine the analogy with the slave example
The passage analogizes: selling a slave except a tenth is valid because the tenth is excluded from the start. But selling the slave 'on condition that I have a tenth' is invalid because the tenth is initially included and then removed with an unknown share.
This reinforces the principle.
4
Apply to the fruit scenario
Selling fruit on the tree except a known single pound is valid because the pound is clearly excluded. But if the exception is plural (pounds), it is invalid due to uncertainty about the exact quantity on the tree. However, if the fruit is already cut, selling all except a known amount (e.g., one sa') is valid because the quantity is known.
The condition 'on condition that I have…' is always invalid regardless of the quantity.
5
State the final ruling
For a valid exception: the exception must be known and specific (single unit on tree, or any known amount if cut). Using the phrase 'on condition that I have…' renders the entire sale fasid (corrupt) due to gharar.

Selling Grains in Husks: Conditions and Limitations
Conditions for selling grains in husks and related rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions for valid sale of grains in husks and explain why same-type exchange is prohibited to avoid riba.

Core Idea
Selling wheat in ear is valid only if exchanged for a different type; same-type sale risks riba.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah comments on Al-Quduri's ruling on selling wheat in its ear and beans in their pod. The key condition is that the exchange commodity must be of a different type; otherwise, uncertainty in quantity creates riba. The seller must thresh the grain when selling by measure. Selling straw before it exists is void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is selling grains (e.g., wheat, beans, sesame, rice) while still in their husks (ear or pod). The general ruling is that it is permissible (sahih), but subject to conditions.

2
Condition of exchange type
The sale is valid only if the exchange is for a different type (bikhilaf jinsi). If the exchange is for the same type (e.g., wheat for wheat), it is impermissible due to potential riba from unknown quantity.
This applies even if the same grain is in different forms (e.g., ear vs threshed).
3
Reason for prohibition on same-type exchange
When exchanging like commodities, equality of quantity is required. The unknown quantity in the husk creates gharar and risk of riba al-fadl.
Hanafi usul: riba is prohibited in six commodities; grains are among them.
4
Responsibility for threshing
When the sale is by measure (mukayyalah), the seller must thresh the grain to deliver the agreed quantity. This is the seller's duty.
If the sale is by count of ears (not measure), threshing may not be required.
5
Invalid case: selling non-existent straw
Selling wheat straw before it is actually straw (i.e., before threshing) is invalid because it does not yet exist as straw; it is a sale of a non-existent object.
After threshing, the straw exists and can be sold.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses the option of condition (khayar al-shart) in sale. The matn states that khayar al-shart is valid for up to three days, either for the seller, the buyer, or both. The commentary begins by noting that this option prevents the immediate transfer of ownership (the initial effect of the sale) and is established for annulment (faskh) rather than ratification (ijazah) according to the Hanafis. Thus, if the option period expires without the option-holder annulling, the contract becomes binding. Imam Malik held the opposite view: the option is for ratification, so if the period passes without ratification, the contract is annulled. The commentary clarifies that the author's restriction to sale excludes options in divorce or manumission. The phrase 'and for both' may mean each individually or both jointly. A major intra-Hanafi dispute concerns the maximum duration: Abu Hanifa and Zafar hold that three days is the maximum; exceeding it voids the contract. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad permit any specified period beyond three days. The text then provides consequences: if the option period is exceeded, the sale is void ab initio according to Abu Hanifa and Zafar. However, if within three days the option-holder ratifies, dies, or the sold item is destroyed, the sale becomes valid and binding according to Abu Hanifa (Zafar disagrees, saying it remains void). Finally, a conditional sale where non-payment within three days voids the sale is valid; extending to four days is invalid per Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, but Muhammad allows it. If payment is made within three days, the sale is valid by consensus. The passage emphasizes the Hanafi position that the option is for faskh and that the three-day limit is a strict condition.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Abrogating an Unspecified Khiyar al-Shart
Ruling on sale with unspecified option period and effect of abrogation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi positions on whether abrogating an unspecified khiyar al-shart can validate a void sale, and identify the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Abrogating a khiyar without a time limit can save the sale before three days, but after three days the imams split.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the effect of abrogating a stipulated option (khiyar al-shart) when the option period was not specified. By consensus, the sale is void (fasid) due to the indefinite option. However, the imams differ on whether subsequent abrogation can validate the contract. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to track how different usul assumptions lead to different furu' outcomes, even within the same madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
First, recall the consensus: if a seller or buyer stipulates a khiyar without specifying a time, the sale is void (fasid) by consensus. This is because the option period is indefinite, which introduces uncertainty (gharar).
This is the starting point — the contract is void, not just invalid or suspended.
2
Analyze the first scenario: abrogation before three days
The passage examines what happens if the party with the option (صاحب الخيار) abrogates his option after taking possession but before three days have passed, and before the contract is rescinded. The three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) say the sale becomes valid (jā'iz). Zufar says it does not become valid.
The three imams see the abrogation as a cure for the voidness, while Zufar sees the voidness as irremediable.
3
Analyze the second scenario: abrogation after three days
If the option is abrogated after three days have passed, Abu Hanifa and Zufar hold that the sale does not become valid. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that it does become valid. Note the shift: Abu Hanifa joins Zufar in the second scenario, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad maintain validity.
The split is not between a majority and minority; it's a 2-2 split with Zufar siding with Abu Hanifa in the second scenario.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: In a court or fatwa context, a judge who follows Abu Hanifa (or Zufar) would rule the sale void if the option was abrogated after three days. A judge who follows Abu Yusuf or Muhammad would enforce the sale. Before three days, the majority view (three imams) would validate the sale; Zufar would not.
This disagreement affects real contracts where the parties later agree to waive the option.
5
Consider the underlying rationale
While the passage does not cite the dalil, the difference likely stems from whether the indefinite time renders the contract inherently void (batil) or merely voidable (fasid) with potential for cure. The three imams (before three days) seem to view the voidness as curable by abrogation within the customary maximum (three days). Abu Hanifa's switch after three days suggests he considers the indefinite time as a permanent defect if not cured within the recognized period.
This is a usul-level question: can an act (abrogation) retroactively validate a contract that was void due to an invalid condition?

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ownership During Option Period
Commentary expanding on matn with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on ownership of the sold item during the option period and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) each side uses.

Core Idea
During the option period, ownership of the sold item is disputed: Abu Hanifa says seller's; Sahibayn says buyer's.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ownership of a sold item while the buyer's option (khiyar al-shart) is still active. The ruling on compensation for destruction and the validity of disposal hinge on this underlying ownership question. We will walk through the arguments of Abu Hanifa and the Two Companions (Sahibayn) and see how their different premises lead to different practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on destruction during option
The matn states that if the buyer takes possession and the item perishes, he must guarantee its value (if qimi) or its like (if mithli). This is agreed upon, but the reasoning differs based on ownership.
This ruling is independent of the ownership dispute for the buyer's liability.
2
Recognize the dispute on ownership
The commentator clarifies that while the item leaves the seller's ownership by consensus, there is disagreement on whether it enters the buyer's ownership. Abu Hanifa says no; Sahibayn say yes.
This is the core ikhtilaf in the passage.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's rational proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the price remains in the buyer's ownership; if he also owned the sold item, two countervalues would be owned by the same person, which is invalid in a sale contract.
This is a qiyas based on the principle that a sale involves exchange of countervalues.
4
Analyze Sahibayn's rational proof
The Sahibayn argue that the item has left the seller's ownership; if it does not enter the buyer's ownership, it would be ownerless, which is not recognized in Islamic law.
They emphasize continuity of ownership; a thing cannot be without an owner.
5
Apply to scenarios of disposal and liability
Despite the dispute, the practical ruling on the buyer's disposal during the option (if the option is for him) is agreed: it is valid and considered acceptance (ijazah). The dispute mainly affects other issues like the permissibility of the buyer's disposal without acceptance (Abu Hanifa says invalid), but the text notes consensus on validity when the disposal is made.
The thamarah al-khilaf is limited in this specific scenario because the disposal itself acts as acceptance, settling ownership.

This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ownership of the sold item during the option period and related rulings. The commentator (sharh) expands on the matn of Mukhtasar al-Quduri.

Four Ways the Buyer's Option Terminates
Enumeration of option-terminating events

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four events that terminate the buyer's option and apply them to determine when a sale becomes binding.

Core Idea
The buyer's option ends by acceptance, death, expiry, or loss of the item — the sale then becomes binding.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah spells out four specific events that bring the buyer's khiyar al-shart to an end, making the sale irrevocable. Understanding these categories is essential for knowing when a buyer loses the right to rescind.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first way: explicit acceptance
The buyer says 'I accept' (أجزتُ). This terminates the option immediately, even if the seller is absent.
Acceptance can be explicit by word or by action that implies acceptance.
2
Identify the second way: death of the buyer
If the buyer dies during the option period, his option ends and the sale becomes binding. The right is not inherited.
This is because the option is a personal right that is not transferable.
3
Identify the third way: expiry of the option period
If the specified period passes without rescission, the sale becomes binding automatically.
The period must be clearly defined in the contract.
4
Identify the fourth way: loss or diminishment of the item
If the sold item is destroyed or significantly diminished (even by a slight decrease caused by any party), the option is lost.
This includes actions by the buyer, seller, natural disaster, or a third party.
5
Apply the categories to scenarios
Given a scenario, determine which of the four causes applies and conclude whether the sale is binding.
Remember the key principle: the option is personal and is lost upon any event that makes rescission impossible.
