Authority of Agent in Sale: Absolute or Customary?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on agent's pricing and payment authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the agent's authority to sell at any price and explain the proof based on general command, contrasting it with the Sahibayn's restriction to customary price and terms.

Core Idea
General agency gives absolute pricing authority per Abu Hanifa; custom restricts it per the two companions.

When a principal authorizes an agent to sell without specifying price or terms, how much discretion does the agent have? This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). We'll examine their proofs based on usul al-fiqh and see how the difference affects the validity of the sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Abu Hanifa holds the agent can sell for a little or a lot (any price). The two companions restrict: the agent may not sell at a reduction that people would not normally overlook (ghubn fahish).
This is the central ikhtilaf in the passage.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
The command to sell is absolute ('amm). In Hanafi usul, general wording is carried on its generality. Sale is defined as exchange of property for property, which occurs at any price.
Abu Hanifa relies on literal interpretation of the command.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
An unrestricted command is restricted by custom ('urf). The customary sale is at market price; an excessively low price resembles a gift. Also, they require payment in cash (dirhams/dinars) unless otherwise agreed.
They use custom as a restricting factor.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the sale is valid and binding. Under Sahibayn, the sale is invalid unless the principal ratifies it. The same applies to credit terms: Abu Hanifa permits any term; Sahibayn restrict to customary term.
The practical difference is whether the buyer gets the property or the principal can void.
5
Apply the principle to related cases
If the agent marries his daughter to the principal, Abu Hanifa disallows it due to conflict of interest, but Sahibayn allow if she is adult. Similarly, purchase agent may buy at a premium that people overlook.
Consistency: the dispute extends to other agency transactions.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, discusses the scope of an agent's authority in sale and purchase. The core issue is whether a general authorization (wakala mutlaqa) permits the agent to deviate from market norms in price or payment terms. The matn states that according to Abu Hanifa, an agent authorized to sell may sell for a little or a lot (i.e., any price). The commentator elaborates: the command to sell is general ('amm), and general wording is carried on its generality. This is a usul principle. However, the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) restrict this: the agent may not sell at a reduction that people would not normally overlook (i.e., ghubn fahish, excessive loss). Their reasoning is that unrestricted command is restricted by custom; the customary sale is at market price, and an excessively low price resembles a gift. Abu Hanifa responds that 'sale' is an exchange of property for property, and that exists at any price. The same principle applies to purchase: the agent may buy at a slight premium but not at an excessive one (opinion of Sahibayn). The passage also covers payment terms: Abu Hanifa includes cash and any deferral, while the two restrict to customary terms. If there is a dispute about instructions, the principal's word is taken. Additionally, the passage addresses special cases like the agent marrying someone to the principal. The consensus is that the agent cannot marry his own minor daughter or his own nephew? Actually, the text: if the agent marries his own daughter to the principal, it is invalid by consensus if she is minor (due to lack of capacity?). If adult, Abu Hanifa says invalid, the two say valid. The reasoning relates to the conflict of interest and the agent's testimonial qualification. The key takeaway: in general agency, Abu Hanifa is more permissive, while the two companions impose customary restrictions. The relied-upon position for fatwa may vary; this text is from a Hanafi commentary that presents the positions.

Key Terms

الغبن الفاحش (al-ghubn al-fāḥish): An excessive loss in price that people would ordinarily not overlook; considered a form of gift from one perspective.
المحاباة (al-muḥābāh): Selling at a favorable price (below market) as a favor; still considered a sale.

Agent's Sale Authority: Ghabn Fahish and Payment Terms
Dispute on selling at a loss and terms of payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding an agent's authority to sell with a price deficiency and on credit, and apply the ruling in a case of dispute.

Core Idea
An agent's absolute sale authority is restricted by custom; Sahibayn require market price and currency, Abu Hanifa allows any price and terms.

In Hanafi agency law, a principal may appoint an agent to sell property. A key question arises: how far does the agent's authority extend? This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on whether the agent can sell at a loss and for goods or on credit. Understanding this dispute clarifies the role of custom (urf) in restricting absolute commands and highlights the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) for valid sales.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the ruling of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding selling with excessive loss (ghabn fahish) and the currency of payment.
Abu Hanifa permits any price and goods; Sahibayn require market price and currency.
2
Trace the proofs
For each position, identify the dalil used – for Sahibayn, custom restricts the absolute command and ghabn fahish resembles a gift; for Abu Hanifa, the literal meaning of bay' (exchange of property) includes goods and any price.
Both use the same absolute command (mutlaq amr) but differ on its restriction.
3
Examine the usul principle
The Sahibayn apply the principle that an absolute command is restricted by customary practice (urf). Abu Hanifa holds the command remains unrestricted unless explicitly qualified.
This is a classic usul al-fiqh issue on the effect of urf.
4
Apply to thamarah al-khilaf
Understand the practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the sale is valid and binding on the principal; under Sahibayn, it is invalid without the principal's subsequent approval.
The dispute directly affects the validity of the agent's transaction.
5
Extend to payment terms
The same logic applies to deferred payment: Abu Hanifa allows any term; Sahibayn restrict to a customary term. Also, if a dispute arises between principal and agent on the terms, the principal's word is taken.
By consensus, an agent may sell on credit if not specified.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the authority of an agent appointed to sell. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that the agent cannot sell at a price that people would not ordinarily tolerate (ghabn fahish), and that the sale must be for currency (dirhams or dinars), not goods (urud). Their reasoning is that an absolute command is restricted by custom, and a sale with excessive loss is effectively a gift. Abu Hanifa, however, maintains that the agent is commanded to sell in an absolute sense, and that 'sale' (bay') is an exchange of property for property, which can be with goods as well as currency. The passage then addresses related issues: a sale with favoritism (muhabah) is still a sale (as proven by an oath), and the absolute command includes both cash and credit to any term according to Abu Hanifa, while the Sahibayn restrict it to a customary term. In case of dispute between principal and agent about the terms, the principal's word is taken. Finally, by consensus, an agent appointed to sell without specification of cash or credit may sell on credit.

الغبن الفاحش (al-ghabn al-fāhish): Excessive deficiency in price that people would not normally tolerate; considered a form of gift.
المحاباة (al-muḥābāh): A sale with favoritism where the seller grants a price concession to the buyer, which is still a valid sale.
المتعارف (al-mutaʿārif): What is customary or conventional in commercial practice, used to restrict an absolute command.

Purchase Agent's Price Authority: General Rule and Exception
Ruling on permissible price range for purchase agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule for a purchase agent's authority regarding price and apply Imam Khujandi's exception for known-price goods.

Core Idea
An agent may buy at market value or with customary excess, but for goods with a known price, any extra does not bind the principal.

When you appoint someone to buy for you, can they overpay? The Hanafi school sets a clear boundary: the agent may only pay up to market value plus what people normally tolerate. However, for staples like bread and meat with a known price, any overpayment—no matter how small—falls on the agent, not you. This lesson unpacks that rule and its crucial exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its classification
Identify the act under discussion: a purchase agent (wakil bil-shira') buying goods for the principal (muwakkil). The ruling concerns the agent's authority to set the price.
This is a wakalah contract, not a sale itself.
2
Define the key terms: thaman al-mithl and al-taghābul
Define thaman al-mithl (market value) and al-taghābul (mutual toleration of a small price difference). Understand that ghabn yasir (slight overpayment) is permissible, while ghabn fahish (excessive overpayment) is not.
This distinction depends on custom (urf).
3
State the general rule from the matn
The agent may buy at market value (thaman al-mithl) or with an increase that people ordinarily tolerate. Any increase beyond that is not permitted.
The base matn does not differentiate between goods.
4
Apply Imam Khujandi's exception
For goods with a known market price (e.g., bread, meat), any overpayment, little or much, does not bind the principal. The agent bears the excess.
This is a later refinement from Khujandi, found in 'Shahan'.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The operative position is the qualified rule: for unknown-price goods, the agent can pay up to market value plus customary tolerance; for known-price goods, any excess is not binding.
This combines the general rule and the exception.

Commentary

This ruling is also cited from the work 'Shahan'.

Purchase Agent Restrictions and Classification of Agents
Ruling on prohibited sellers and agent classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on purchase agent restrictions based on testimony and apply al-Khujandi's five-category classification to identify which agents are bound by fair terms.

Core Idea
An agent's authority is limited by potential conflicts and by the agent's own legal capacity.

Not all sellers are equal in the eyes of a purchase agent. Abu Hanifa and his two disciples disagree on whether an agent may buy from someone whose testimony would be rejected for the agent. This lesson unpacks that dispute and then presents al-Khujandi's practical classification of empowered agents, distinguishing those who must transact fairly from those who may deviate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute on prohibited sellers
Read the first sentence: the agent may not buy from those whose testimony is not acceptable for him according to Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn permit it at market price with tolerable ghabn. Note the reason (potential conflict of interest).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
2
Understand the consensus exception and principal's authorization
Next, the agent cannot buy from his own slave or mukatab by consensus. But if the principal orders it, it becomes permissible. However, buying from the agent's minor child, slave, or mukatab even with authorization has special rules (not detailed here).
Consensus (ijma') strengthens the rule.
3
Learn Khujandi's classification framework
Al-Khujandi divides those who act by empowerment into five categories. The text gives two: (1) those who can only transact on fair terms (father, grandfather, wasi), and (2) those who can transact on fair or unfair terms (mukatab, ma'dhun) – but with a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
The remaining three categories are not given in this passage.
4
Apply the classification to the mukatab and ma'dhun
According to Abu Hanifa, the mukatab and ma'dhun may sell something worth 1000 for 1 dirham (extreme loss) or buy something worth 1 for 1000 (extreme gain). The Sahibayn restrict them to fair terms. This is another intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf within the classification.
Ghabn fahish (gross negligence) is the key concept.

Core Idea
A guardian may transact with an orphan only if it is clearly beneficial—the stricter view is not relied upon.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: may a guardian (wasi) sell his own property to the orphan under his care, or buy the orphan's property? However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad in another transmission permit the transaction provided it is beneficial (khayr) for the orphan. Understanding this disagreement and its resolution is essential for correctly applying Hanafi contract law in fiduciary contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actor and the action
The actor is the guardian (wasi) of an orphan. The action is either selling his own property to the orphan or buying property from the orphan. Note that the guardian is acting on behalf of the orphan in one capacity and for himself in the other.

Imam Muhammad holds: 'لا يجوز بحال' (not permissible at all). The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad in another transmission) hold: 'إن كان خيراً لليتيم جاز' (if it is beneficial for the orphan, it is permissible).

Muhammad's absolute prohibition is based on the strong suspicion of self-dealing (tuhma) and the need to protect the orphan's property from any potential exploitation. The two's position considers the maslahah (benefit) of the orphan: if the transaction is clearly advantageous (e.g., above-market price for the orphan's sale), the benefit outweighs the suspicion.
The key difference is how strictly conflict of interest is weighed against practical benefit.

Under Muhammad's view, the transaction is void (batil) regardless of price. Under the two's view, if the transaction is genuinely beneficial (khayr), it is valid and enforceable; if not, it is void.
The condition 'khayr' is determined by market value and the orphan's overall welfare.

The relied-upon position is that of the two: permissibility if beneficial. This is because it balances protection of the orphan with practical necessity, allowing beneficial transactions to proceed.
This is the position adopted by later Hanafi authorities such as al-Marghinani and al-Haskafi.

If so-and-so says, 'I did not authorize him,' then he is not an agent for him, because the acknowledgment is repudiated by his rejection, unless the buyer hands him over, in which case it is a sale by mutual giving (ta'āṭī), and he (the seller) bears liability. Commentary
This passage deals with two related issues in the law of agency concerning sales. First, it presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the attribution of a purchase made by an agent when the contract is absolute (i.e., the agent does not specify whether he is buying for himself or for the principal). Imam Muhammad holds that the purchase belongs to the contracting party (the agent himself), based on the general principle that each person acts for himself. Imam Abu Yusuf, however, holds that the decisive factor is which money—the agent's or the principal's—is actually paid. If the agent pays from the principal's money, the purchase is for the principal; if from his own, for himself. The commentator clarifies that the author's phrase 'or he buys it with the principal's money' refers not to actual payment but to attributing the contract to the principal's dirhams. When the contract is explicitly attributed to the principal's money, there is consensus that the purchase is for the principal, regardless of which money is paid. Second, the passage discusses a scenario where someone tells a person to sell a slave to a third party, the person sells, but then the third party denies having authorized the sale. The ruling is that the third party can claim the slave because the original statement constituted an acknowledgment of agency, which cannot be undone by later denial. However, if the third party also denies authorization, then the seller is not an agent, unless the buyer (the original seller) hands over the slave—in which case it becomes a valid sale by mutual giving (ta'āṭī). The commentator notes that this case establishes that delivery on the basis of sale suffices for ta'āṭī, even without payment, for both valuable and worthless items.

Conditions for a Valid Sale by Mutual Dealing (Bay' al-Ta'ati)
Ruling on requirement of immediate payment and delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for the validity of a sale by mutual dealing (bay' al-ta'ati) in Hanafi fiqh and apply them to determine if a given transaction qualifies as a valid sale.

Core Idea
A sale by mutual dealing requires immediate cash payment and delivery with sale intent to be valid.

This passage from the Hanafi work al-Waqi'at sets out the necessary conditions for a sale by mutual dealing (bay' al-ta'ati), where offer and acceptance are implied through actions rather than verbal statements. While some schools may require explicit verbal contracts, the Hanafi school validates such transactions provided the price is paid in cash and the goods are delivered immediately, both done with the clear intention of a sale. Understanding these conditions is essential for distinguishing a valid sale from a mere gift or invalid exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act being ruled upon
Bay' al-ta'ati is a sale where mutual delivery of goods and payment takes place without verbal offer and acceptance. The ruling specifies its validity conditions.
This type of sale is common in everyday transactions like buying small items from a shop.
2
Locate the necessary conditions
The text states two conditions: (1) immediate cash payment (naqd al-thaman) and (2) delivery of the goods (taslim) done on the basis of a sale (ala wajh al-bay').
Implicit in 'ala wajh al-bay' is the mutual intention to conclude a sale, not a gift or loan.
3
Apply the conditions to a scenario
For example, if A hands B money and B gives A an item without words, it is a valid sale if both intend a sale and the transfer is immediate. If payment or delivery is deferred, it does not qualify as bay' al-ta'ati.
Deferred payment or delivery requires explicit agreement and may fall under other contract types.
