Definition and Legal Nature of Rahn (Pledge)
Introduction to rahn with definitions and distinctions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definitions of rahn, distinguish it from kafalah, and identify its valid scope regarding debts and punishments.

Core Idea
Rahn is a contract of security with property, not on liability, and is valid only for financial rights collectible from the pledge.

This passage opens the chapter on Rahn (pledge/mortgage) by providing both its linguistic and legal definitions. It clarifies that rahn is a contract that places property as security for a debt, distinct from suretyship and from the seller's retention right. The passage also highlights its merits and delineates its proper scope—only for financial claims, not for hudud or qisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
Find the literal definition of 'rahn' in Arabic. The passage states it means 'detention' (habs).
Linguistic meaning is broader than legal.
2
Identify the legal (shar‘i) definition
Locate the technical definition: a contract of security with property.
This definition is used to derive rulings.
3
Understand the Quranic proof
Examine the verse (74:38) and how the word 'rahinah' supports the concept of detention as security.
The verse shows the root meaning but is metaphorical.
4
Distinguish from kafalah and other security forms
Note the differences: rahn secures with property, kafalah secures on the dhimmah; the seller's retention is not a contract.
These exclusions (ihtiraz) are critical for precise application.
5
Recognize the scope of valid rahn
Understand that rahn is only valid for financial rights (like debts) that can be collected from the pledge; not for hudud or qisas.
This limits the contract to recoverable monetary obligations.

The Book of Pledge. "Rahn" linguistically is: detention, i.e., detaining a thing for any reason, property or non-property. Allah Almighty said: "Every soul is held in pledge for what it has earned" (al-Muddaththir 38), i.e., detained because of the evil consequences of what it earned of sins. And in sacred law: it is an expression for a contract of security with property. As a precaution against suretyship (kafalah), for it is a contract of security in the liability (dhimmah). And also as a precaution against the sold item in the seller's hand, for it is a security but not a contract upon a security document. And it is said: it is in sacred law: making a thing detained for a right that can be collected from the pledge, like debts, such that rahn is not valid for prescribed punishments (hudud) and retaliation (qisas), nor is the pledge of a mudir (a manager/slave). And among the merits of rahn is that it considers both sides: the side of the pledger (rahin) and the side of the pledgee (murtahin).

Commentary
This passage introduces the chapter on Rahn (pledge/mortgage) with its linguistic and legal definitions, a Quranic proof, and distinctions from similar concepts. Linguistically, rahn means detention of something physically (not necessarily property). The Quranic verse (74:38) provides a metaphorical basis: the soul is detained by its deeds. Legally, rahn is a contract that places property as security for a debt, distinguishing it from kafalah (suretyship) which secures an obligation on the creditworthiness (dhimmah) of a person, and from the seller's right to retain sold goods until payment (which is a security but not a contract). The passage also notes that rahn is only valid for financial rights that can be collected from the pledged property, so it does not apply to hudud (fixed punishments) or qisas (retaliation) as these are rights of Allah or the state, not collectible debts. The term 'mudir' likely refers to a mudabbar (a slave whose freedom is contingent on the master's death) or a manager; the text says a mudir cannot give a pledge, possibly because his property rights are limited. Finally, the merit of rahn is highlighted: it balances the interests of both pledger and pledgee.

Key Terms
الرَّهْن (ar-rahn): A contract of security with property, where a thing is detained to secure a right that can be collected from it, like a debt.
الكَفَالَة (al-kafālah): A contract of suretyship on the liability (dhimmah) of a person, securing an obligation.
الوَثِيقَة (al-wathīqah): A security document or arrangement that ensures fulfillment of an obligation.

Definition of Rahn: Linguistic and Legal Meaning
Defines rahn linguistically and legally with proofs and wisdom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definitions of rahn, distinguish it from similar contracts, and explain the wisdom behind its legislation.

Core Idea
Rahn is a contract of security with property, legislated to protect both debtor and creditor.

This lesson explores the meaning of rahn from its Quranic root ('detention') to its precise fiqh definition as a contract of security with property. We will also highlight the wisdom behind its legislation, showing how it serves both the pledgor and the pledgee.

Guided Path
1
Trace the linguistic origin
Understand that 'rahn' linguistically means detention. The Quran uses 'raheenah' in Surah al-Muddaththir: every soul is detained for its deeds. This gives the root concept.
The linguistic meaning is broader than the legal one.
2
Identify the legal definition
Legally, rahn is a 'contract of security with property' (عقد وثيقة بمال). The author distinguishes it from kafalah (suretyship on liability) and from the seller's retaining possession as security (which is not a separate contract).
Focus on the two safeguards (احتراز) mentioned.
3
Understand the distinctions
Know the differences: Kafalah secures the person's liability, not a specific property. A seller's retention of the sold item is a security but not a contract; rahn is a dedicated contract.
4
Appreciate the wisdom
The passage explains that rahn benefits both parties: for the pledgor, it tempers the creditor's harshness; for the pledgee, it protects the debt from risks like waste, denial, or insolvency.
This demonstrates the Lawgiver's consideration of both sides.

Rahn linguistically is: detention, i.e., detaining a thing for any reason, property or non-property. Allah Most High said: {Every soul, for what it has earned, is retained} (al-Muddaththir 38), i.e., detained by the evil consequence of what it committed of sins. Legally: it is an expression for a contract of security with property. As a safeguard against kafalah (suretyship), for it is a contract of security on the liability (dhimmah). And also as a safeguard against the sold item in the hand of the seller, for it is a security, but not a contract of security. And it is said: legally it is: making a thing detained for a right that can be exacted from the pledge, such as debts, such that pledge is not valid for hudud and qisas, nor for the pledge of the mudir. Among the virtues of rahn is that it considers both sides: the side of the pledgor and the side of the pledgee. As for the side of the pledgor: the pledgee may be a severe adversary, especially if he finds a license from the Lawgiver by clear statement, i.e., his saying, peace be upon him: 'The creditor has the hand and the tongue.' So perhaps he increases in harshness such that he does not let the pledgor eat nor let him sleep. So Allah Most High had mercy on him, and legislated rahn to ease his matter, expand his heart, until he is able to collect what he can pay his debt with at his leisure, and protect his honor during his respite. And as for the side of the pledgee: his debt is exposed to loss and destruction because the pledgor may waste his property by extravagance, or other creditors may come to take his property, or he may deny while the pledgee has no evidence, or he may die insolvent without a specific surety. So the Lawgiver considered the pledgee and legislated rahn so that he may reach his debt by the strongest means and the most secure things, to the extent that if he does not acknowledge the debt, he wins what is equivalent to his debt from the pledge. He (the author), may Allah have mercy on him, said: (Rahn is concluded by offer and acceptance). The offer is the pillar of rahn alone – that the pledgor says: 'I pledge this thing to you for your debt that you have against me.' The pillar is made solely the offer without acceptance because rahn is a contract of security.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Rahn (Pledge) and serves a dual purpose: defining the term and establishing the fundamental rule of its contract formation. The author begins with the linguistic definition – al-rajn meaning detention or confinement – and then cites Quranic evidence from Surah al-Muddaththir where every soul is 'raheenah' (pledged) for its deeds, illustrating the basic idea of being held accountable. He then moves to the legal (shar‘i) definition: rahn is a contract that secures a debt or other financial right with property, distinguishing it from kafalah (suretyship) which secures a liability (dhimmah) rather than a specific item, and from the situation where a seller retains possession of the sold item as security, which is not a separate contract. The author explains the wisdom behind the legislation of rahn: it benefits both parties. For the pledgor, it softens the creditor's harshness (who might otherwise press him relentlessly), giving him time and ease to repay. For the pledgee, it protects his debt from risks such as the pledgor's wastefulness, other creditors, denial, or insolvency. The passage then transitions to the ruling on formation: rahn is concluded by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). However, the author – following the Hanafi school – clarifies that the actual pillar (rukn) is only the offer from the pledgor, because rahn is a contract that inherently involves giving possession to the pledgee; acceptance is implied by taking possession or is not a formal requirement. This is a distinctive Hanafi position.

الكفالة (al-kafalah): Suretyship, a contract of security on the liability (dhimmah), not on specific property.

The Pillar of Rahn: Why Only Offer (Ijab)?
States the rule that the pillar of rahn is the offer alone, with the reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the pillar of the rahn contract according to the Hanafi school, explain why acceptance is not a pillar, and compare this with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the pillar of rahn is the offer (ijab); acceptance is a condition, not a pillar.

The passage states that the pillar of rahn is the offer alone, because rahn is a contract of security. This lesson unpacks that ruling, explores the intra-madhhab reasoning, and contrasts it with the Shafi'i view that acceptance is also a pillar.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The pillar (rukn) of rahn is the offer (ijab) from the pledgor. The author explicitly says: 'الإيجاب: ركن الرهن بمجرده' (The offer is the pillar of rahn alone).
Contrast with the general rule that most contracts require both offer and acceptance as pillars.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that acceptance (qabul) is also a pillar. For him, a contract cannot be concluded without mutual consent expressed through both ijab and qabul.
This is a well-known ikhtilaf in usul al-fiqh regarding the nature of accessory contracts.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i school relies on the general principle that contracts require mutual consent (taradi), and the Quranic verse 'An taradin minkum'. They analogize rahn to sale, where both offer and acceptance are pillars.
Though not in the passage, this is standard Shafi'i usul.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The passage gives the reason: 'لأن الرهن عقد وثيقة' (because rahn is a contract of security). The Hanafi school distinguishes security contracts from commutative contracts; the essential element is the act of the pledgor making the property security. Acceptance is implied by possession or explicit words but is not constitutive.
This is a qiyas based on the nature of wathiqah contracts.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: According to Hanafi, if the pledgor says 'I pledge this' and hands over the item without the pledgee's verbal acceptance, the contract is valid if the pledgee takes possession. According to Shafi'i, express acceptance is required; absence would render the contract void.
This affects how security agreements are documented and validated.

The Pillar of Rahn: Offer Alone
States the ruling and proofs for the pillar of rahn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the pillar (rukn) of rahn, explain why the offer alone suffices, and distinguish rahn from sale.

Core Idea
In rahn, only the pledgor's offer is the pillar; acceptance is not a pillar because rahn is gratuitous, like a gift.

In this passage, the author of Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the essential pillar of the contract of rahn (pledge). He clarifies that while rahn is concluded by offer and acceptance, the foundational element (rukn) is only the offer from the pledgor. This distinction arises from the nature of rahn as a gratuitous contract (tabarru'). We will examine the reasoning, the analogy to hiba, and the textual proofs from Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on the pillar of rahn
The text states that the offer (ijab) is the rukn of rahn. Locate the exact statement: 'الإيجاب: ركن الرهن بمجرده'.
2
Understand the reason: rahn is a gratuitous contract
The author argues that because the pledgor does not receive any return from the pledgee, rahn is classified as tabarru'. In such contracts, the pillar is only the offer.
Contrast with exchange contracts (mu'awada) like sale.
3
Apply the analogy to hiba
Rahn is compared to gifts (hiba) and charity (sadaqah). Just as in hiba the offer alone constitutes the pillar, similarly in rahn the offer is the pillar. The practical implication for oaths is shown: if one swears not to give a gift and makes an offer without acceptance, the oath is broken.
The text uses the oath example to illustrate the existence of the contract by offer alone.
4
Distinguish from sale
Sale is an exchange contract where both parties give and receive. Therefore, both offer and acceptance are pillars. The text contrasts the oath effect: if one swears not to sell and makes an offer without acceptance, the oath is not broken.
This highlights the difference between gratuitous and exchange contracts.

Rahn is a gratuitous contract: only the offer (ijab) is the pillar, and it is valid for any valuable item, even in residence.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, establishes the nature of the contract of rahn (pledge) as a gratuitous contract (tabarru'), distinguishing it from sale (bay'). The commentator explains that in rahn, the pledger gives the pledgee possession of the pledged item without receiving anything in return from the pledgee, making it akin to a gift (hibah) or charity (sadaqah). Consequently, the pillar (rukn) of rahn is merely the offer (ijab) from the pledger, without requiring acceptance (qabul) from the pledgee. This is evidenced by the legal consequence: if a person swears not to give a gift and then makes an offer to give, even if the other does not accept, the oath is broken. In contrast, in a sale (which is a commutative contract, mu'awadah), both offer and acceptance are pillars; if one swears not to sell and merely makes an offer without acceptance, the oath is not broken. The passage then provides the primary scriptural proof: the Quranic verse (al-Baqarah 2:283) which permits a 'pledge held in hand' (farhun maqbudah), and the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) pledging his armor to a Jew for food. The scholars extract two rulings from this hadith. First, rahn is permissible for any valuable item, even items used for acts of worship ('ibadah), such as a copy of the Qur'an, because the Prophet's armor was used for jihad. This refutes the view of some ascetics (al-mutaqashshifah) who prohibit pledging such items. Second, rahn is permissible both at home and on a journey, since the Prophet's pledge occurred in Medina. This refutes the Zahirites (ashab al-zawahir) who restrict rahn to travel based on the conditional phrase in the verse. The commentator argues that the verse does not impose a condition, but describes the common practice of resorting to rahn when documentation is unavailable, which typically happens during travel. Therefore, rahn is valid in all circumstances.

Possession: Condition for Bindingness, Not Validity in Pledge
Commentary on possession as a condition for bindingness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the roles of possession (qabd) in pledge validity versus bindingness, and apply the Hanafi position that possession is a condition for luzum, not jawaz.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a pledge is valid without possession but becomes binding only upon delivery.

The pledge contract (rahn) occupies a unique position in Hanafi muamalat: it is valid before possession but not binding. This lesson unpacks the key ruling from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah, which distinguishes between in'iqad (validity) and luzum (bindingness). Through the source text and its proof from the continuous practice of the early community, you will understand why possession is a condition for bindingness, not permissibility, and how this affects scenarios such as the pledgor's death before delivery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the proof
The matn states 'wa yatimmu bil-qabd' (it is completed by possession). The commentator provides the proof: the continuous practice (al-mu'amalah al-zahirah) from the Prophet's time to the present of pledging in residence and travel, which demonstrates the permissibility of the contract itself.
This is a dalil from tawatur 'amali (continuous practice), which is qat'i in thubut and dalalah.
2
Clarify the legal distinction
The commentator explains that qabd is not a condition for in'iqad (validity) but for luzum (bindingness). He analogizes to the condition of option (khiyar) in sale: a sale with an option is valid but not binding until the option expires. Similarly, a pledge is valid without qabd but becomes binding only upon delivery, like a gift (hiba).
This distinction is central to Hanafi contract theory: validity (sihha) is separate from bindingness (luzum).
3
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Muhammad holds that qabd is a condition for jawaz (permissibility/validity), meaning the pledge is not valid without possession. The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and the author's preference from Khwaharzadeh) hold that qabd is only for luzum.
This is a rare intra-Hanafi divergence; the mufta bihi is the majority view.
4
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
If the pledgor dies before delivering the pledged item to the pledgee, the heirs are not compelled to deliver because the contract was not binding. This is unlike a sale, where the contract is binding immediately and the heirs must deliver.
The analogy to gift is key: a gift is not binding until possession is taken.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that qabd is a condition for luzum, not jawaz. This is the view of the majority, supported by Khwaharzadeh and preferred by the author of Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah.
Students should memorize this as the operative ruling.

Rulings on achieving valid possession of pawned item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that possession (qabd) of a pawn is validly achieved by removing obstacles (takhliya), explain the qiyas on sale, and distinguish the Hanafi majority view from Abu Yusuf's requirement of physical transfer for moveables.

Core Idea
In pawn, removing obstacles suffices for possession — analogized to sale, but Abu Yusuf requires actual handover for moveables.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah sets out the Hanafi position that possession of a pawned item is achieved not by physical seizure but by the pawnor vacating the property (takhliya). The author grounds this in qiyas on sale and notes an intra-madhab disagreement with Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Examine the pre-conditions for the pawn before possession
The matn requires the pawn to be mahuz (already possessed separately), mufarrigh (emptied of other rights), and mumayyaz (distinguishable). These ensure the pawn is capable of being possessed.
These conditions apply to the pawn itself before the mortgagee takes possession.
2
Understand the rule: takhliya suffices for qabd
The commentator states that takhliya (removing obstacles) is sufficient; this is the relied-upon transmission (zahir al-riwaya).
Takhliya means the pawnor lifts any barrier preventing the mortgagee from taking control.
3
Analyze the proof: qiyas on sale
Since pawn is a legitimate contract, its possession is analogous to possession in sale, where takhliya also suffices. The illa is 'possession by virtue of a legitimate contract'.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi opposition
Abu Yusuf requires physical transfer (naql) for moveables (manqul), while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad accept takhliya. The text prefers the majority view (al-awwal asahh).
The differing view is recorded but not relied upon for fatwa.
5
Consider the rulings on agent and invalid agreement
The mortgagee's agent can take possession; if parties agree to leave the pawn with the pawnor, the contract is invalid, and later seizure is not possible.
This reinforces that qabd is essential for a valid binding pawn.

The Obligation of Continued Possession in Pawn

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Holding onto the pawn is obligatory in Hanafi; the pawnor may not use it — but Shafi'i allows the owner's benefit.

Three Conditions for a Valid Pawned Item
Explains required attributes of pawn and their legal effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three required attributes of a pawned item at the time of possession and explain why their absence renders the contract fasid rather than batil.

Core Idea
A pawn must be mahuz, mufarragh, and mumayyaz at possession; missing any makes it defective but correctable.

In a pawn contract (rahn), the item used as security must meet specific attributes at the moment the mortgagee takes possession. This lesson clarifies those attributes—mahuz, mufarragh, mumayyaz—and the legal consequence if they are absent. Understanding these conditions is essential for ensuring a valid pawn contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three attributes
The commentator lists three conditions: mahuz (separately possessed), mufarragh (empty of other property), mumayyaz (identified/distinct). Understand what each means.
2
Understand the timing
These conditions are required at the time of possession, not contract formation. The contract is valid but incomplete until possession.
3
Apply the distinction between fasid and batil
If these conditions are missing at possession, the contract is fasid (defective), not batil (void). A void contract is legally nonexistent; a defective one can be corrected.
4
Work through examples
For each attribute, consider the example given: fruit without tree (not mahuz), trees without fruit or land without crop (not mufarragh), undivided share (not mumayyaz).
These examples show the practical application.

The Pawnor's Right to Cancel Before Possession
Explains pawnor's option before delivery and binding effect of possession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the pawnor has the option to cancel before delivery and describe when the contract becomes binding.

Core Idea
The pawn contract is non-binding until possession; only upon delivery does it become binding and the mortgagee's guarantee begins.

In a pawn contract, the pawnor retains a choice: he may either deliver the pawn or cancel the agreement. This is because the contract's purpose—securing the debt—cannot be fulfilled without control over the asset. The commentator provides a logical proof comparing it to actual recovery of a debt. Understanding this option is vital for both parties in a pawn transaction.

Identify the pawnor's option

Once the pawnor delivers the pawn and the mortgagee takes possession, the pawn enters the mortgagee's guarantee (daman). The mortgagee is then liable for its safekeeping.

Ruling on Undelivered Pledge and Conditions for Validity

Core Idea
A pledge is not binding until delivery; the pledgor may withdraw, and the property must be distinct and free of inseparable attachments.

This lesson examines the Hanafi view on the pledge (rahn) contract, focusing on the moment it becomes binding and the specific conditions the pledged property must meet. Understanding these rules is essential for differentiating between sound (sahih) and defective (fasid) pledges in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling before delivery
Read the first sentence: 'وما لم يقبضه : فالراهن بالخيار'. The pledgor has the option to deliver or withdraw. The contract is not binding.
2
Identify the ruling after delivery
Read the second sentence: 'فإذا سلَّمه إليه، وقبضه : دخل في ضمانه'. Once delivered and taken possession, the pledge enters the pledgee's guarantee (daman). The contract becomes binding.
The pledgee is now liable for the property; if it perishes, the debt is not reduced.
3
Distinguish between fasid and batil
The commentary explains that if the pledged property lacks a required description (mawsuf) at the time of delivery, the contract is fasid (defective) but not batil (void). Fasid has the essence but lacks an attribute; batil lacks both.
This distinction affects whether the contract can be rectified.
4
List the specific conditions
The text states three conditions: muhawwaran (harvested), mufarraghan (emptied), and mumayyazan (distinct). These prevent common invalid scenarios like pledging fruit on the tree without the tree, or an undivided share.
These conditions ensure the pledged property is clearly identified and separable.
5
Understand the rationale
The final part explains why bindingness depends on possession: the purpose of a pledge (security) cannot be achieved without possession, as the pledge is a virtual satisfaction of the debt, akin to actual satisfaction requiring receipt.
This is a rational (ma'qul) proof for the ruling.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah comments on al-Quduri's matn on rahn (pledge/mortgage). Rahn Only Valid for Guaranteed Debt; Rahn bi al-Darak Void
States that rahn is only valid for a guaranteed debt and distinguishes rahn bi al-darak.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for a valid rahn and distinguish between rahn bi al-darak (void) and rahn bi al-dayn al-maw'ud (valid by istihsan).

Core Idea
A pledge is only valid for an actual debt; a pledge for a future uncertain liability is void.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah clarifies a fundamental condition for rahn: the debt must be maddmun (guaranteed and actually due). The shuruh explains why rahn cannot secure a contingent liability, unlike kafalah, and introduces an exceptional case (promised loan) by istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Note that rahn is valid only for a guaranteed debt (dayn maddmun). The commentary says the term is emphatic because all debts are guaranteed; it serves to exclude rahn for contingent debts.
The condition is in the opening statement.
2
Distinguish future debt types
Two types of future debt: rahn bi al-darak (void) – securing a potential future liability like a future sale price; and rahn bi al-dayn al-maw'ud (valid by istihsan) – securing a promised loan that will be given.
The difference lies in whether the debt is specific and intended to be created immediately (promised loan) or merely contingent (darak).
3
Trace the proof
The proof for voidness of rahn bi al-darak is that rahn involves an exchange (giving and receiving) like sale, so it cannot tolerate uncertainty. Kafalah, on the other hand, is a commitment to demand payment and can be attached to risk due to custom.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) comparing contract types.
4
Apply the ruling to a void case
If rahn bi al-darak is taken and the pledged property perishes, it perishes as a trust (amanah) because there is no valid contract.
No liability on pledgee; the pledgor bears the loss.
5
Note the exception
Rahn bi al-dayn al-maw'ud is valid by istihsan due to necessity (need for security before loan is given). If the pledge perishes before the loan, compensation is based on the lesser of its value or the promised loan amount.
This shows flexibility in the Hanafi school for customary transactions.

The Condition of a Guaranteed Debt in Pledge (Rahn)
Explaining the condition that a pledge is only valid for a guaranteed debt, distinguishing from Shafi'i, and clarifying exceptions for darak and promised debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for a valid pledge, explain why it differs from the Shafi'i view, and apply the ruling to contingent debts like darak and promised debts.

Core Idea
A pledge is valid only for an existing, guaranteed debt; it functions as an exchange, not a trust.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies a foundational condition of the pledge contract (rahn): it is only valid when securing a debt that is already guaranteed (mamdum). This ruling is contrasted with Imam al-Shafi'i's view that the pledge is a trust, leading to different consequences if the pledged property is destroyed. The passage also explores two borderline cases—pledge for recourse (daman al-darak) and pledge for a promised loan—to illustrate the scope and rationale of the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core condition and its exceptions
Start with the base ruling: 'A pledge is only valid for a guaranteed debt.' Understand that 'guaranteed' means the debt is existing and unconditional. Then note two key exceptions: (1) Pledge for a contingent debt like darak is void. (2) Pledge for a promised debt (loan not yet given) is valid by treating the promised debt as existing.
The word 'guaranteed' is an emphasis, as all debts are guaranteed, but the exception for darak shows it excludes contingent liabilities.
2
Analyze the Shafi'i opposition
Al-Shafi'i holds the pledge is a trust (amanah), so destruction does not reduce the debt. Contrast with Hanafi view that the pledge is a guarantee (daman). Identify the thamarah al-khilaf: different liability on the pledgee.
The Hanafi proof uses the exchange element (payment and receipt) to analogize to sale.
3
Work through the analogy to purchase
Examine the qiyas: Taking possession on the basis of a pledge is like taking possession on a purchase offer (sawm al-bay'), both impose liability. The illa is the intention to ultimately own/recover the value. Apply this to the promised debt case.
The analogy is jaliyy (clear) and uses tahqiq al-manat.
4
Distinguish kafalah from rahn
Understand why kafalah (suretyship) is valid for darak but rahn is not. Because kafalah is an undertaking to pursue payment, which can be conditional on uncertain events due to custom. Rahn involves immediate exchange, so it requires an existing debt.
This distinction reinforces the swap-like nature of rahn.
5
Apply to practical scenarios
Given a new scenario (e.g., pledge for a future sale price, pledge for a loan that will be given later), classify it as valid or invalid based on whether the debt is guaranteed. Use the rule: existing debt = valid; contingent = invalid (except promised debt by custom).
The promised debt exception is based on need (hajah) and is treated by qiyas to purchase offer.

However, the author clarifies that it excludes 'guarantee of recourse' (daman al-darak), which is a contingent liability where one guarantees a debt that has not yet arisen. For example, if someone says, 'I will be responsible for the price of what you buy from so-and-so,' and before any purchase takes a pledge, this is invalid because the debt is not yet guaranteed. The ruling is derived from al-Hidayah: a pledge for such a contingent liability is void, but a suretyship (kafalah) for the same is valid because suretyship can be attached to risk due to customary practice. The reason for the distinction is that a pledge involves 'payment' or 'satisfaction' (ifa') and 'receipt' (istifa'), resembling a sale, whereas suretyship is merely an undertaking to pursue payment and can be conditional on uncertain events. If someone nevertheless takes a pledge for recourse and it is destroyed, it is destroyed as a trust because the underlying contract is void. In contrast, a pledge for a promised debt—where someone says 'I pledge this to you for a loan of ten dirhams' and the pledge is destroyed before the loan is given—is treated as a guarantee. The promised debt is considered existing for the sake of necessity (hajah), and the possession is akin to taking something on the basis of a purchase offer (sawm al-bay'), which imposes liability. This ruling is supported by analogy (qiyas): just as goods taken on a purchase basis are guaranteed, so too are goods taken on a pledge basis. The author cites al-Nihayah for a scenario where a buyer, fearing a claim of ownership, takes a pledge from the seller for the price: this is invalid, and the pledgee cannot retain the pledge nor is he liable for its destruction because the debt is not yet obligatory. The passage concludes by defining a 'guaranteed debt' as one that is not extinguished except by payment or release, emphasizing that a valid pledge requires an existing, non-contingent obligation.

Key Terms
رهن (rahn): A contract where property is given as security for a debt, allowing the creditor to recover the debt from the property's value if the debt is not paid.
دين مضمون (dayn mamdum): A debt that is guaranteed, meaning it is existing and unconditional, not contingent or promised, and is extinguished only by payment or release.
ضمان الدرك (daman al-darak): A guarantee against a claim of ownership on a sold item, i.e., the seller's liability if the buyer loses the property to a third party claiming ownership.
الدين الموعود (al-dayn al-maw'ud): A promised debt, e.g., a pledge given as security for a loan that has not yet been given; Hanafis treat it as existing for guarantee purposes due to need.

Rahn for Inherently Guaranteed Items: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Rahn is valid for inherently guaranteed items because the primary obligation is value, not the specific object — they are effectively debts.

This passage examines a critique of al-Quduri's rule that rahn is only valid for a guaranteed debt. It defends the validity of rahn for items like mahr, khul' compensation, and usurped property, which are not debts in form but are treated as debts by the majority of Hanafi scholars due to the obligation of value. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi methodology of legal interpretation (ta'lil) and the concept of makhlas (a way of release).

Guided Path

Identify the objection

The passage answers that the majority of mashayikh hold that the wajib (obligation) in these cases is the value (qimah). Returning the specific 'ayn is only a makhlas (way of release), not the primary duty. Therefore, these are considered debts, and rahn for them is valid.

When the Pledge Perishes: Legal Consequences in Hanafi Fiqh
States the ruling when the pledge perishes and its value equals the debt

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the pledge perishes and equals the debt, the debt is deemed paid – a legal construction, not actual collection.

When a pledged property (rahn) perishes while in the custody of the pledgee (murtahin), a central question arises: does the debt remain or is it considered satisfied? This lesson examines that ruling, its key concept of 'deemed collection' (mustawfi hukman), and how it applies in various scenarios, including destruction by the parties or a third party, and special cases where the pledge secures a specific object rather than a debt. Guided Path
1
Identify the main scenario and ruling
The core scenario: the pledge perishes in the pledgee's hands, and its value equals the debt. The ruling: the pledgee is deemed to have collected his debt (mustawfi li-daynihi hukman). This is a hukm wad'i (legal construction), not an actual receipt of payment.
If the value is less than the debt, the pledgee remains owed the balance; if more, the pledgor may claim the surplus.

If the pledgee destroys the pledge, he guarantees its full value. If the pledgor destroys it, he guarantees its value, and that guaranteed sum becomes a new pledge in the pledgee's hands. If a third party destroys it, that party guarantees the value, which substitutes the original pledge.

Commentary
This passage provides the ruling on a corrupt pledge (rahn fasid) – a pledge that is defective in some aspect (e.g., possession not taken properly). It presents two opinions. Attached Items Must Enter the Pledge
Rulings on inclusion of attached items in a pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that attached items (muttaṣil) automatically enter a pledge of land or trees, and identify when a pledge is void due to partial possession.

Core Idea
In a pledge, everything attached by nature must be included; otherwise, it resembles an undivided share and is void.

Pledge (rahn) requires the creditor to take exclusive possession of the pledged property. When that property is connected to other items—like fruit on a tree or crops in the ground—the law must decide whether those items are separate. The Hanafi school holds that attachment forces inclusion to make possession possible. This lesson unpacks that principle and its contrast with sale, where attachments do not automatically enter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pledged property and its attachments
Determine what the pledgor explicitly pledges (e.g., land, palm trees) and what is physically connected to it (e.g., fruit, crops).
Attachment here means natural connection (خلقة), not placement.
2
Apply the rule of automatic inclusion for attachments
If something is attached by nature to the pledged item, it must enter the pledge automatically. Otherwise, the pledge would be like pledging an undivided share (شائع), which is invalid because exclusive possession is impossible.
The rule is tied to the possibility of exclusive qabḍ.
3
Contrast with the rule in sale (bayʿ)
In sale, attached items do not automatically enter; they must be explicitly named and priced. Sale is valid without them, so the contract is not jeopardized.
The difference arises because sale does not require taking possession of the attached items.
4
Examine exceptions: movable items inside a container or house
Furniture in a house or goods in a sack are not naturally attached; they can be pledged separately because they are not 'occupied' by the container.
Similarly, an animal's load can be pledged alone if the load is handed over.
5
Check delivery conditions for animals with loads
When pledging a riding animal, the load must be removed for delivery to be valid; but pledging the load alone is valid because the animal is not occupied by it.
The obstruction principle: anything that prevents exclusive possession invalidates delivery.

Expansion of rahn classifications with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given pledge (rahn) as sahih, fasid, or batil based on the nature of the obligation it secures and state whether liability (daman) attaches.

Core Idea
A pledge's validity hinges on what it secures: debt (sahih), impermissible property (fasid), or trusts (batil)—liability only for sahih and fasid.

In this passage, the author of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on the classification of pledges (rahn) according to the Hanafi school. Understanding these three categories—sahih, fasid, and batil—is essential for determining when a creditor can claim compensation if the pledged item is lost. The classification depends entirely on the underlying obligation: whether it is a guaranteed debt, an impermissible commodity, or a mere trust.

Guided Path
1
Identify the underlying obligation
Determine what the pledge is securing. Is it a debt (dayn), a tangible asset that is guaranteed by itself (like a sale item), a forbidden substance (wine, pig), a guarantee of defect (darak), or a trust (amanat like deposits, loans)?
The nature of the obligation determines the validity of the pledge.
2

This disagreement illustrates the Hanafi intra-madhhab ikhtilaf on whether a pawn continues for a substituted obligation.

Custody and Liability of the Adl in Rahn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid placement with an adl in rahn, and determine the liability of the adl when he delivers the pledge to one party without the other's consent.

Core Idea
The adl is a dual trustee: delivering to one party breaches the other's right and makes the adl liable.

In a pledge contract (rahn), the parties may agree to deposit the pledged item with an impartial third party called the adl. The Hanafi school validates this arrangement on the basis that possession (qabd) is a right of the pledgee, which he may delegate. However, the adl holds the item for both parties: for the pledgor in terms of ownership and custody, and for the pledgee in terms of security. Therefore, the adl must not deliver the pledge to either party without the consent of the other; otherwise, he becomes liable for the value.

Guided Path

Identify the ruling and its basis
The passage states that placement with adl is permissible (ja'iz). The basis is qiyas: taking possession is a right of the pledgee, so he may delegate it, just as he may exercise other rights himself.
The proof is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
2
Analyze the dual relationship
The adl is a depositary (mudi') for both parties: for the pledgor regarding the physical object (al-'ayn) and for the pledgee regarding the monetary value (al-maliyah). Each party has a distinct right that the adl must respect.
This dual nature is key to understanding liability.
3
Understand the liability rule
If the adl delivers the pledge to either party without the other's consent, he becomes liable (damin) because he has breached his duty to the other party. Delivery to one is considered handing over to a stranger with respect to the other's right.
The adl's liability arises from breach of trust, not negligence.
4

If the pledge perishes in the adl's hand, it is from the pledgee's guarantee (daman al-murtahin) because the adl's hand is considered an extension of the pledgee's hand.

Commentary
This commentary expands on the matn ruling that placing the pledge with an adl is permissible. The reason given is that taking possession (qabd) is a right of the pledgee, so he may exercise it himself or delegate it, analogous to all his rights. The condition of the pledgor's consent is required because he retains ownership of the pledge. The commentary then clarifies a crucial rule: neither the pledgor nor the pledgee may take the pledge from the adl without the other's consent, because the pledgor has a right to the adl's custody (hifz) and the pledgee has a right to eventual collection (istifa'). If the adl delivers the pledge to either party, he becomes liable (damin) because he breaches his duty: he is a trustee for the pledgor regarding the physical object and a trustee for the pledgee regarding the monetary value, and delivering to one is delivering to a stranger regarding the other's right. Finally, if the pledge perishes in the adl's hand, it is from the pledgee's guarantee because the adl's hand is considered the hand of the pledgee, as he stands in his place. This establishes the adl as a deputy of the pledgee for liability purposes.

Authority of the Adl to Sell the Pledge
Sharh explaining the adl's authority and types of authorization

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of authorization (taslit) for the adl and state the legal consequences of each.

Core Idea
Authorization for the adl to sell can be revocable or irrevocable—only when stipulated in the contract is it irrevocable.

A trustee (adl) holds a pledge but does not automatically have the right to sell it. Only with specific authorization can he act. But not all authorizations are equal: the timing and form determine whether the parties can revoke it later. This lesson unpacks the default rule and the two faces of authorization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
Note that the adl cannot sell the pledge unless authorized. This is the baseline: no inherent authority.
2
Recognize the two types of authorization
Authorization can be either stipulated in the original pledge contract OR given separately afterward. The passage only elaborates the first type.
The second type is implied to have different rules, likely revocable.
3
Analyze the effect of contractual authorization
If authorization is part of the pledge contract, the adl cannot be dismissed by either party, nor is he dismissed by death of either party. He may sell without the heirs' presence.
This makes the authorization perpetual and binding.
4
Contrast with separate authorization
Though not detailed, separate authorization is likely revocable and may be affected by death. This contrast is essential to understanding the rule.
Think: why would the law treat them differently? Because contractual authorization is a condition of the contract itself.

Commentary
