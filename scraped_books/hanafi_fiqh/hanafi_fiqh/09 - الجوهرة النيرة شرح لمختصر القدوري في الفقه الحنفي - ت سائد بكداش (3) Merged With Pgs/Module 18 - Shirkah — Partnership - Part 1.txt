Ruling on Partnership and 'Ariyah in Acknowledgment
Two specific acknowledgment phrases and their rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that the phrase 'في دراهمي' indicates partnership and 'عارية' is treated as a loan.

Core Idea
In acknowledgment, 'في دراهمي' means partnership; 'عارية' means loan, not trust.

This lesson explains two specific expressions used in acknowledgments (iqrar). When someone says 'He has a thousand in these dirhams of mine,' it is legally interpreted as an admission of partnership. When someone says 'He has a thousand dirhams with me as a loan ('ariyah),' it is treated as a loan (qard), even though 'ariyah is ordinarily a loan for use. The same rule applies to all measurable and weighable goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its literal meaning
Read the two expressions: 'له في دراهمي هذه ألف' and 'له عندي ألف درهم عارية'. Understand their plain language: the first claims the other has a share in specific dirhams; the second claims the dirhams are held as a loan for use.
The literal meaning guides the legal classification.
2
Determine the legal effect of each phrase
Apply the Hanafi rule: the first phrase creates a partnership (shirkah) in that amount of money; the second creates a loan (qard), not a trust or bailment.
For dirhams, 'ariyah is effectively qard because they are consumed.
3
Extend the rule to all measurable/weighable goods
Note that the passage adds 'and likewise everything that is measured or weighed' — the rule for 'ariyah applies to all such goods.
This extension prevents evasion of loan rules through terminology.

Commentary
This passage gives two additional rules in acknowledgments. First, if someone says 'له في دراهمي هذه ألف' (He has a thousand in these dirhams of mine), this is an acknowledgment of partnership (shirkah) in that specific amount of money. The speaker admits that the other person shares ownership in the dirhams. Second, if someone says 'له عندي ألف درهم عارية' (He has with me a thousand dirhams as a loan), it is treated as a qard (loan), even though 'عارية' literally means a loan for use. This is because 'عارية' of fungible goods like dirhams is effectively a loan of consumption, making the holder liable. The phrase 'وكذا كل ما يُكال ويوزن' extends this rule to all measurable and weighable goods.

Key Terms
شركة (shirkah): Partnership; joint ownership of property or business.
عارية (ʿāriyah): A loan of an item for use without charge, with liability for the item.
قرض (qarḍ): Loan of fungible goods that are consumed and repaid in kind.

Shirkat al-Amlāk: Definition and Ruling on Disposition
Definition of shirkat al-amlāk and its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shirkat al-amlāk and shirkat al-‘uqūd, and state the ruling on disposing of a partner's share without permission, citing the underlying principle.

Core Idea
In co-ownership, each partner is a stranger to the other's share; permission is required for any disposition.

Partnership in Islamic law is not a single concept; it spans co-ownership and contractual ventures. This passage introduces the first type—shirkat al-amlāk—where two or more people own a specific asset together. The key legal insight is that such co-ownership does not confer mutual agency; each owner remains a complete stranger to the other's portion. Understanding this distinction is essential before studying contractual partnerships, where agency and profit-sharing are central.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of partnership
Locate where the author divides partnership into 'shirkat al-amlāk' and 'shirkat al-‘uqūd'. Note that this is the foundational classification for all partnership discussions in Hanafi fiqh.
The text says: 'الشركة على ضربين : شركة أملاك، وشركة عقود.'
2
Define shirkat al-amlāk
Find the definition: 'العين التي يَرِثُها رجلان، أو يشتريانها'. Recognize that co-ownership arises from inheritance, joint purchase, gift, bequest, or indistinguishable mixing—not from a contract of partnership.
The sharh expands these causes (gift, bequest, mixing).
3
State the ruling on disposition
Identify the legal ruling: 'لا يجوز لأحدهما أن يتصرف في نصيب الآخر إلا بإذنه'. Understand that any act of disposition (sale, gift, use) of the other's share is impermissible and invalid without permission.
The ruling uses 'لا يجوز', indicating prohibition (haram).
4
Explain the underlying principle
Note the reason given: each partner is like a stranger (كالأجنبي) regarding the other's share. The general rule is that using another's property is not allowed except with permission or legal authority (ولاية).
This principle also applies to other areas of fiqh, e.g., guardianship.
5
Apply the principle to a scenario
Imagine two brothers inherit a house. Under shirkat al-amlāk, brother A cannot sell brother B's half without B's explicit permission. If he does, the sale is invalid (bāṭil).
This distinguishes co-ownership from contractual partnership, where partners have mutual agency.

Two Types of Partnership and the Ruling on Property Partnership
Defines two types of partnership and ruling on property partnership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish shirkat al-amlak from shirkat al-uqud and state the ruling that a co-owner may not dispose of the other's share without permission.

Core Idea
In Shirkat al-Amwal, each co-owner is a stranger to the other's share—permission is required for any disposal.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar introduces the chapter on partnership (shirkah) by defining its two main types: partnership in ownership (shirkat al-amlak) and partnership by contract (shirkat al-uqud). The focus here is on the first type, where joint ownership arises through inheritance, purchase, gift, or bequest. The key ruling is that no co-owner may act regarding the other's share without explicit permission, as each is considered a stranger (ajnabi) to that portion.

Understand the Definition of Shirkat al-Amwal
The text defines shirkat al-amlak as joint ownership of an 'ayn (asset) through inheritance, purchase, gift, or bequest. Recognize these as causes of shared ownership.

The text clearly states: 'la yajuzu li-ahadihima an yatasarrafa fi naseeb al-akhar illa bi-idhnihi'. Each partner is like a stranger (ajnabi) regarding the other's share.
4
Apply the Stranger Analogy
The commentator explains the rationale: a person cannot act on another's property without permission or legal authority (wilayah). In co-ownership, neither partner has such authority over the other's share.

Types of Contractual Partnership in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition and classification of contractual partnership types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four types of shirkat al-uqud and distinguish each by its defining characteristics.

Core Idea
Contractual partnerships are either unlimited (mufawadah) or limited (inan), with special forms for craftsmen and credit.

This lesson introduces the four standard types of contractual partnerships (shirkat al-uqud) as classified in the Hanafi school. Understanding these categories is essential for determining the rights and obligations of partners in commercial ventures.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching category
Recognize that shirkat al-uqud is the second type of partnership (after shirkat al-milk). All four subtypes fall under contractual partnerships formed by offer and acceptance.
The passage begins by stating 'And the second type: Partnership of Contracts'.
2
Name the four types
Memorize the four names: Mufawadah, Inan, Shirkat al-Sanai' (partnership of craftsmen), and Shirkat al-Wujuh (credit partnership).
The text uses the term 'وجوه' (wujuh) meaning faces/reputations.
3
Distinguish Mufawadah from Inan
Understand that Mufawadah requires full equality in capital, profit, loss, and authority; Inan allows variation. All other types (craftsmen, credit) are subtypes of either Mufawadah or Inan.
The alternative classification from al-Khujandi emphasizes this binary.
4
Note the alternative classification
Al-Khujandi groups partnerships by contribution: capital, labor, or credit. Each group then splits into Mufawadah or Inan. This shows flexibility in Hanafi categorization.
The commentator cites this to show that the four-type division is not the only valid one.

The Pillar of Partnership Formation: Ijab wa Qabul
Definition of the pillar for partnership contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why ijab wa qabul is the rukn of shirkat al-uqud and apply it to determine if a partnership contract is validly formed.

Core Idea
A partnership contract is formed only by explicit offer and acceptance; the rukn is verbal expression, not written confirmation.

This lesson focuses on the fundamental requirement for creating any contractual partnership: the exchange of offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). The Hanafi school insists on explicit verbal expressions to avoid ambiguity and ensure mutual consent. Without this rukn, the contract is void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rukn as ijab wa qabul
The commentary explicitly states: 'Its pillar: offer and acceptance.' This means the contract's existence depends on this exchange.
The rukn is what makes the contract legally exist; without it, there is no contract.
2
Understand the required wording
The text gives a sample: 'I partner with you in such and such' and 'I accept.' The wording must indicate mutual consent and the scope of partnership.
Hanafi law allows flexible wording as long as it clearly shows agreement.
3
Distinguish rukn from conditions
While ijab wa qabul is the rukn (pillar), there are external conditions (shurut) for validity, such as capacity of the parties and lawful subject matter.
The rukn is internal to the contract; conditions are external prerequisites.
4
Apply to partnership types
All four types of shirkat al-uqud require this rukn. Whether it is Mufawadah or Inan, the formation is the same: offer and acceptance.
The rukn does not change with the type; only the terms (equality, profit shares) differ.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Shirkat al-Mufawadah and apply its conditions to determine whether a proposed partnership is valid.

Core Idea
Mufawadah requires absolute equality in capital, authority, and liability between two free, Muslim, adult, sane partners.

Partnership in Islamic law takes many forms, and one of the most detailed is Shirkat al-Mufawadah. This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah defines it precisely, highlighting the strict equality required. Understanding these conditions will allow you to distinguish a valid Mufawadah from an invalid one.

Partnership with Bullion: The Role of Custom (Urf) in Hanafi Fiqh
Explaining the conditional validity of partnership with uncoined metals based on custom.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Partnership with bullion is valid only if custom treats it as money – custom converts a commodity into a currency.

This lesson unpacks a nuanced ruling from the matn: partnership is invalid with assets other than the three standard currencies (dirham, dinar, fulus) unless local custom ('urf) accepts them as a medium of exchange. The commentator explains that tibr (gold dust) and nuqrah (silver ingots) have a dual nature: they are like commodities (not standard prices) but also like currency (exchange is considered sarf). Thus, custom decides their status. This showcases Hanafi reliance on 'urf as a legal source.

Guided Path

Identify the general rule for partnership capital
Recall that in Hanafi law, valid partnership capital must be one of the three standard currencies: dirhams, dinars, or fulus. Any other asset makes the partnership invalid unless an exception applies.

Recognise the exceptional class: tibr and nuqrah

The commentator argues that tibr and nuqrah have a dual resemblance (shabah): they are like commodities because they are not fixed prices, and like currency because transactions involving them are considered ṣarf (exchange of precious metals). This dual nature prevents a simple classification.

Custom is determined by widespread practice, not individual agreement.

The ruling is conditional: partnership with tibr/nuqrah is ṣaḥīḥ (valid) if and only if custom treats them as currency. This demonstrates the Hanafi principle that 'urf can modify the legal status of assets in contractual contexts.
This ruling is part of the Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah and is muftā bihī.
