Slave's Confession to Hadd or Qisas: Binding Immediately
Commentary on ruling of slave's confession and jinaayah cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a slave's confession to hadd or qisas and explain the master's liability in cases of the slave's jinaayah, distinguishing between intentional and accidental acts.

Core Idea
A slave's confession to hadd or qisas binds immediately because it is against his own interest.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah discusses a key distinction: a slave’s admission to a hadd punishment or qisas retaliation is effective at once, as it is against his own self. The commentator then extends the discussion to cases where a slave kills or injures someone, clarifying when qisas applies and when the master must pay compensation or hand over the slave. Understanding these distinctions is essential for grasping the Hanafi treatment of slave liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling
Locate the core statement: a slave's confession to a hadd or qisas is binding immediately. Note the reason given: because it is against himself and not suspected.
The reason is aqli (rational).
2
Distinguish intentional vs. accidental killing
The passage distinguishes between intentional murder (amd) and accidental killing (khata) or injuries. Intentional murder requires qisas on the slave; other cases give the master options.
Master chooses between handing over the slave or paying arsh.
3
Examine the case of manumission
If the master manumits the slave after intentional murder but before execution, the manumission is valid and the slave is executed as a free person. The master owes nothing.
The slave becomes a free person subject to qisas.
4
Analyze the scenario with multiple guardians
If there are two guardians (walis) of the victim and one pardons, the other gets a claim for money. He may require the slave to work (istis'a) for half the slave's value. The master owes nothing.
This applies after manumission; the claim becomes monetary.
5
Summarize the master's liability
The master is liable for unintentional acts (handing over or paying arsh) but not for intentional acts that warrant qisas. The slave is directly liable for hadd and qisas upon confession.
Confession to hadd/qisas is always binding on the slave.

Slave's Confession to Accidental Killing: Two Rival Narrations
Comparison of Khujandi's and Karkhi's rulings on slave's confession to accidental killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of al-Khujandi and al-Karkhi on a slave's confession to accidental killing and explain the legal reasoning behind al-Karkhi's ruling.

Core Idea
A slave's confession to accidental killing does not bind his master, but whether it binds the slave after freedom is disputed within the Hanafi school.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute over the validity of a slave's confession to unintentional homicide. Two authoritative later Hanafi works—al-Khujandi and al-Karkhi—offer conflicting rulings. By analyzing their reasoning, you will see how the slave's legal capacity (permitted vs. interdicted) shapes the outcome.

Guided Path
1
State the common ground
Both sources agree that the master is not liable for the slave's confession to accidental killing. The liability, if any, is in the slave's dhimma (liability).
This is consistent with the general Hanafi principle that a master is not liable for his slave's torts beyond the slave's value (or not at all for confessions).
2
Identify al-Khujandi's position
According to al-Khujandi, the confession is valid and the slave will be held accountable after gaining freedom. The blood money becomes a debt on the slave.
This position treats the slave as having full capacity for future liability, similar to a free person's confession.
3
Identify al-Karkhi's position
Al-Karkhi holds that the confession is void for both a permitted (ma'dhun) and an interdicted (mahjur) slave. If the slave is later freed, he is not pursued for anything.
This position emphasizes the slave's lack of capacity to incur obligations outside his master's authorization.
4
Analyze al-Karkhi's reasoning
For the mahjur: confessing to a property obligation (blood money) is like confessing to a debt—void because he has no authority over property. For the ma'dhun: his permission is limited to trade debts; a tort is not within his authorization, so his confession is likewise void.
The key distinction is between debts arising from trade (which the ma'dhun is permitted for) and obligations arising from torts (which he is not).
5
Synthesize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under al-Karkhi's view, a freed slave cannot be held liable for the accidental killing based solely on his confession; under al-Khujandi, he can be pursued.
This difference affects whether the victim's family can claim blood money from the freed slave.

Ruling on Retracting a Confession of Theft and Its Consequences
Ruling on retracting confession of theft and its effect on liability and hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on retraction of confession in theft to distinguish between its effect on hadd punishment and financial liability.

Core Idea
A retraction of confession is accepted for hadd but not for financial liability; the confessor pays the first victim but is amputated for a new confession.

This passage examines a specific case where a thief confesses to stealing from one person, then retracts and confesses to stealing from another. Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him) provides a nuanced ruling that separates financial liability from the hadd punishment. This demonstrates how the Hanafi school treats retraction differently in matters of rights of Allah (hadd) and rights of humans (mal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two confessions
The person first says 'I stole ten dirhams from this one', then says 'No, rather I stole ten dirhams from this one'. Recognize that 'la bal' indicates a retraction of the first and an assertion of the second.
2
Determine the effect of retraction on hadd
Apply the principle: retraction is accepted in hadd matters. Therefore, the first confession (now retracted) does not lead to amputation.
3
Determine the effect of retraction on financial liability
Apply the principle: retraction is not accepted in financial obligations. Therefore, the confessor must still pay the ten dirhams to the first person.
This is because financial rights belong to individuals and cannot be nullified by retraction without proof.
4
Analyze the second confession
The statement after retraction constitutes a new confession of theft against the second person. Since it is a fresh confession, it is accepted, and the hadd becomes applicable.
5
Synthesize the final outcome
Combine the above: the confessor pays the first person (financial liability remains) and is amputated for stealing from the second (hadd is applied).
This shows the separation between financial and criminal consequences.

The Validity of Iqrar: Tasdiq and the Effect of Retraction
Ruling on condition of affirmation and effect of retraction in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of tasdiq for the validity of iqrar and apply the rule that retraction is accepted in hudud but not in financial obligations, using the example of theft.

Core Idea
Tasdiq by the muqarr lahu is a condition for iqrar; retraction is accepted in hadd but not in mal, preserving financial rights.

In Hanafi law, a confession (iqrar) is a declaration that establishes rights and liabilities. However, its validity depends on the affirmation of the person in whose favor it is made. Moreover, the confessor may retract, but that retraction has different effects depending on whether the case involves a fixed punishment (hadd) or a financial right (mal). This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains these principles with a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for validity
The muqarr lahu must affirm (tasdiq) the confession. If he denies, the iqrar is void. Later affirmation does not revive it; a new iqrar is required.
2
Determine the effect of retraction by the confessor
The confessor's retraction is accepted in matters of hadd (fixed punishments) but not in financial obligations (mal). This means the punishment is dropped, but the financial liability remains.
The example: confessor says 'I stole from A' then retracts. He must still pay A but is not cut off for theft from A.
3
Apply the rule to the new confession
When the confessor then states 'No, rather I stole from B,' this is a new iqrar. It is valid for both hadd and mal, so he is cut off for theft from B and presumably must also pay B.
The text only mentions the hadd for the second; the financial liability for B is implied.
4
State the underlying rationale
Hadd punishments are strict and retraction is encouraged to avoid them; financial rights are owed to individuals and cannot be negated by retraction alone.
This distinction is a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh in iqrar.

Ten Dirhams as Great Wealth: Abu Hanifa vs. al-Sarakhsi
Intra-Hanafi dispute and refinement on interpreting 'great wealth' in confession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi difference between Abu Hanifa and al-Sarakhsi on the interpretation of ten dirhams as 'great wealth' and apply the mufta bihi view to a confession scenario.

Core Idea
A confessor's claim of 'great wealth' is judged by his personal financial state, not a fixed nisab.

This passage presents a refinement within the Hanafi school. While Abu Hanifa initially considered ten dirhams as 'great' due to it being the nisab for theft, al-Sarakhsi introduced a more nuanced view based on the individual's poverty or wealth. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the correct ruling in iqrar (confession) cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
What legal question is being debated? The central issue is whether a confessor's statement 'I owe him great wealth' can be interpreted as referring to ten dirhams.
The passage deals with iqrar bi al-mal (confession of a debt). The key term is 'great wealth' (mal 'azim).
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa holds that ten dirhams is a 'great' amount because it is the nisab for theft hadd, for which the hand is cut. Thus, a confessor saying 'from dirhams' (meaning ten dirhams) is believed.
The dalil is the legal significance of ten dirhams in criminal law.
3
Present al-Sarakhsi's Refinement
Al-Sarakhsi argues that the correct view is to consider the confessor's personal financial state (poverty or wealth). He notes conflicting standards: 200 dirhams is 'great' for zakat, 10 for theft. The reference should be to the individual's situation.
This is a classic example of talil (reasoning) leading to a context-sensitive ruling.
4
Analyze the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: For a rich confessor, ten dirhams is not great, so his interpretation may be rejected. For a poor confessor, it is accepted.
This affects the validity of the iqrar and the consequent obligation to pay.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi
Al-Sarakhsi's view is considered the most correct (al-asahh) and is the operative position for fatwa.
The Hanafi school thus adopts a flexible standard based on 'urf (custom) and personal circumstances.

The Hanafi position, as stated by Imam al-Quduri, places the financial burden on the thief himself. The reasoning is a straightforward application of causality (sabab al-wujub): the theft is the cause that made the punishment necessary, so the thief bears its expenses.
