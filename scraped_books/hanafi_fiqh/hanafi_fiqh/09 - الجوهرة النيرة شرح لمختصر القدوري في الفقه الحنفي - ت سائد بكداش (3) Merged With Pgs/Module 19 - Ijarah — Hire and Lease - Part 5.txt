Sale of Leased Property: Validity and Effects
Ruling on sale of leased property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on selling a leased property and explain the rights of the tenant, buyer, and seller.

Core Idea
Sale of leased property is valid; tenant's lease continues, buyer must wait until term ends.

A common scenario: a landlord sells a property that is currently leased. Does the sale affect the lease? The Hanafi school rules that the sale is valid, but the buyer cannot take possession until the lease expires. The tenant may also consent to cancel the lease. This lesson explains the rules.

Guided Path
1
Establish the general ruling
The text states: if a lessor sells the house before the lease term ends, the sale is valid. The buyer must accept possession when the lease ends.
The lease does not invalidate the sale.
2
Consider the tenant's options
The tenant may either: (a) permit the sale, which validates the sale and voids the lease for the remaining term, or (b) rescind (the lease), but this does not affect the sale.
Tenant's consent ends his right to occupy.
3
Analyze the buyer's knowledge
If the buyer knew of the lease at purchase, he cannot demand delivery from the seller until the lease expires. If he did not know, he has an option: annul the sale as defective (khiyar al-'ayb) or confirm it.
These are buyer protections.
4
Synthesize the scenario
Combine all: sale valid, tenant holds rights, buyer's knowledge determines his options. The lease and sale coexist until term ends or tenant consents to cancel.
The main principle: lease binds the buyer.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, now addressing the effect of death and sale on a lease contract. The commentator first presents the ruling: if a party dies and the lease was contracted for his own benefit, the lease terminates. However, if it was contracted on behalf of another (e.g., a parent for a child, a guardian, an administrator), the lease remains in effect after the agent's death. The commentator clarifies that the phrase 'is terminated' in the matn means the tenant has the right to rescind, not automatic dissolution. Then a new ruling is introduced: if the lessor sells the leased property during the lease term, the sale is valid. When the lease expires, the buyer must take possession and cannot refuse. If the tenant consents to the sale, the sale is effective and the lease is void for the remaining period. If the tenant rescinds (i.e., the lease), the sale is not affected. The buyer's knowledge matters: if he knew of the lease at purchase, he cannot demand delivery until the lease ends; if he was unaware, he has an option (khiyar al-'ayb) to cancel the sale or confirm it. The commentator then provides the rational proof (ta'lil) for the death ruling: the lease contract implies entitlement to rent from the tenant's property; if the tenant dies, the rent would be taken from his heirs' property (which they are not obliged to give), so the contract ends. Similarly, if the lessor dies, the benefits (usufruct) would be taken from his heirs' property without their consent, which is impermissible. Examples of contracts for others that survive the contracting party's death include a father leasing for his minor child, a guardian, a waqf administrator, or an agent — because the benefit belongs to the principal, not the deceased.

Death of a Party in Ijarah: Self vs. Others
Ruling with proof on death in ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of death in ijarah, distinguish between contracts made for oneself and for another, and apply the rule to cases involving agents and guardians.

Core Idea
Death of a party dissolves ijarah if for oneself, not if for another; the lessee has an option to rescind.

In this lesson, we examine a specific rule from the Hanafi school regarding the impact of death on an ijarah (lease) contract. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah clarifies when the contract dissolves and when it remains, and provides the underlying rationale. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying ijarah in estate and agency contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the death of a party in an ijarah contract. The ruling: if contracted for oneself, the ijarah is dissolved; if contracted for another, it remains.
The commentator clarifies that 'dissolved' means the lessee (or heirs) have the option to rescind, not automatic nullification.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is qiyas (analogical reasoning): the contract cannot impose obligations on the property of heirs without their consent.
The rationale is given: for lessee's death, rent would be taken from heir's property; for lessor's death, benefits would be taken from heir's property.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The contract of ijarah is an exchange of benefits for rent. Death transfers ownership to heirs, so continuing the contract would take value from the heirs' property, which is not permissible without their agreement.
This reasoning is specific to contracts made for oneself; when made for another, the contracting party is not the owner.
4
Apply the exception
If the contract was made for another (e.g., agent, guardian, waqf administrator), it remains valid because the benefits belong to the principal (e.g., minor, waqf), not the deceased's estate.
The text gives examples: the agent (wakil), executor (wasi), father leasing for his minor son, and waqf administrator.

Selling Leased Property Before Term Expiry
Ruling on sale of leased asset during lease term

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling leased property before the lease term expires, distinguish the buyer's rights based on knowledge of the lease, and apply the option of defect (khiyar al-ayb) in this context.

Core Idea
A lease does not prevent sale; the sale is valid but possession is deferred, and the uninformed buyer can rescind for the hidden defect.

In this lesson, we examine a common practical scenario: the owner of a leased property sells it to a third party before the tenant's lease ends. The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling that protects both the buyer's ownership rights and the tenant's possessory rights. We will see how knowledge of the lease affects the buyer's options, and how the option of defect (khiyar al-ayb) applies to an existing lease.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction and timing
Recognize that the owner sells a property that is currently leased, and the sale occurs before the lease term ends.
The timing is critical: the sale predates the end of the ijarah.
2
State the default ruling
The sale is ja'iz (valid) and becomes lazim (binding) after the lease expires. The buyer cannot refuse possession.
Ja'iz here means the contract is effective, but it is not immediately binding; it becomes binding upon term expiry.
3
Analyze the effect of the lessee's consent
If the lessee authorizes the sale, the ijarah terminates for the remainder, but the sale remains valid even if the lessee later rescinds.
The lessee's rescission does not undo the sale; the sale is independent.
4
Determine the buyer's rights based on knowledge
If the buyer knew of the lease at purchase, he must wait until the lease ends. If unaware, he has the option to annul the sale due to defect (the hidden lease) or confirm it.
This is khiyar al-ayb: the lease is considered a defect that affects the buyer's interest.
5
Identify the underlying principle
The lease is a prior right to possession that the buyer must respect; the sale transfers ownership subject to that right, and nondisclosure of the lease gives the buyer an option.
The principle balances ownership transfer with existing contractual obligations.

Validity of Khiyar al-Shart in Ijarah
Ruling on khiyar in ijarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on stipulating an option in ijarah and explain when the option period begins.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-shart is valid in ijarah as in sale; the period starts at contract formation.

Khiyar al-shart (option to rescind) is a well-known feature in sales, but does it apply to leases? The Hanafi school extends it to ijarah contracts, following the principle that both are commutative exchanges. This lesson examines the ruling and a key detail: when does the option period actually begin?

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
The text states: 'It is valid to stipulate an option in ijarah as in sale.' This is the core ruling: khiyar al-shart is sahih (valid) in ijarah contracts.
The ruling is categorical; no exceptions are mentioned.
2
Compare to sale (bay')
The phrase 'as in sale' indicates that the same rules for khiyar al-shart in sale apply to ijarah. In Hanafi fiqh, khiyar al-shart is permitted in all contracts of exchange ('aqd al-mu'awadah).
This distinguishes the Hanafi view from schools that restrict options to sale only.
3
Determine the start of the option period
The commentator adds: 'the beginning of the option period is considered from the time of the ijarah contract.' This means the period runs from the moment the contract is concluded, not from possession or payment.
This is crucial for calculating the duration (e.g., three days default).
4
Recognize cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih: Shafi'is hold that khiyar al-shart is invalid in ijarah because it delays the immediate transfer of benefits. Understanding this highlights the practical consequence.
The disagreement stems from the nature of ijarah: transfer of benefits vs. transfer of ownership.

Faskh (Rescission) of Ijarah Due to Excuses
Ruling on judicial rescission of ijarah due to supervening excuses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for judicial rescission of ijarah and explain why marriage is not rescinded for similar excuses.

Core Idea
Ijarah can be rescinded by a judge due to hardship; marriage cannot.

In Hanafi fiqh, ijarah (lease) is a contract of exchange for benefits. Unlike sale, it can be rescinded by a judge when a supervening excuse makes continuation impossible or severely burdensome. This lesson examines the ruling, its conditions, and a key comparison with marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that ijarah is rescinded due to excuses. The ruling is that judicial rescission (faskh) is permissible (ja'iz) when a valid excuse exists.
The excuses include loss of capital for the lessee and insolvency for the lessor.
2
Apply the condition of judicial involvement
The text emphasizes that rescission requires a judge's decree (qada'). This is confirmed by reference to al-Ziyadat.
The judge also sells the leased property if needed to satisfy debts.
3
Analyze the examples
Two scenarios: a lessee who loses his capital (cannot pay rent) and a lessor who becomes insolvent (needs to sell the leased asset). Both are valid excuses for faskh.
The judge's role is to verify the excuse and order rescission.
4
Contrast with marriage
Marriage is not rescinded for similar excuses because it is a contract of right of enjoyment (istibaha), not of benefits. The lessor retaining the right to enjoy is not like the lessee paying rent.
This highlights the unique nature of ijarah as a financial contract.
5
Note the authority
The ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah, specifically mentioned in al-Ziyadat by Imam Muhammad.
The Hanafi position is consistent across major texts.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Ijarah (Hiring/Leasing) presents various scenarios where the hirer (musta'jir/mukhtari) or lessor (mu'jir/mukari) may rescind the contract due to an excuse ('udhr). The rulings are based on established principles in the Hanafi school, primarily from al-Jami' al-Saghir and other authoritative works. The passage also discusses the role of the judge: when a lessor sells the property after leasing, the judge ratifies the sale and the lease is rescinded consequently; the judge does not intentionally rescind the lease to avoid harming the lessee's right. The passage ends mid-sentence on the illness of the camel-driver (jammal). The commentary serves as a practical guide for students: the ability to rescind depends on genuine hardship, not mere desire for better terms.

Two agents must act jointly when discretion is needed; for specific acts one may act alone.

Delegation in Wakalah: When Can an Agent Appoint a Sub-Agent?
Rulings on delegation by the agent to a sub-agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that an agent cannot delegate his authority without the principal's permission, and apply the exceptions of express permission and absolute delegation.

Core Idea
An agent may not delegate unless the principal permits; the sub-agent then becomes the principal's agent.

The second part of the passage addresses a crucial issue: can an agent (wakil) appoint another agent to perform the task? The Hanafi school generally prohibits this unless the principal explicitly allows it or gives absolute discretion. This lesson explains the rationale and the legal consequences when delegation is permitted.

Guided Path
1
State the general prohibition
The text opens with 'The agent may not delegate regarding what he was appointed for, unless the principal permits him or says: 'Act according to your opinion.' So delegation without permission is invalid.
Reason: the principal trusted his judgment, not another's.
2
Identify the exceptions
Two exceptions: (1) explicit permission, (2) absolute delegation (tafwid) such as 'act according to your opinion'.
3
Consequences of permitted delegation
If delegation is permitted, the second agent becomes the agent of the principal, not the first agent. The first agent cannot remove him. Both agents are removed by the death of the original principal.
The text from al-Hidayah explains this.
4
Note additional fatwa
According to al-Fatawa, if the principal delegates absolutely, the first agent can validly delegate and also retain the power to remove the sub-agent.
This is a nuance: the first agent retains removal power.
