Taking the Pledge from the Custodian and Liability for Loss
Ruling on who can take the pledged item and who bears loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules that neither party may take the pledged item from the custodian without consent and that the pledgee bears the loss if the item perishes in the custodian's hand, and explain the rationale based on the dual rights theory.

Core Idea
Neither party may unilaterally take the pledge from the neutral custodian; the pledgee bears the loss if it perishes in custody.

In a pledge (rahn) contract, the pledged item is often held by a trusted third party (al-‘adl) to protect the rights of both debtor and creditor. But what happens if one of the parties tries to take the item back, or if the item is lost while in custody? This passage from the matn lays down two fundamental rules: a prohibition on taking the item without mutual consent, and a rule that the pledgee bears the financial loss if the item perishes in the custodian's hands. The commentary unpacks the reasoning, showing how the custodian's role and the parties' rights interact. By understanding these rules, you will grasp the delicate balance of interests in Hanafi law of pledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key actors and their rights
Recognize the three parties: pledgor (owner), pledgee (creditor), and custodian (neutral holder). The pledgor has a right of ownership, the pledgee has a right to collect from the value, and the custodian is a trustee for both.
The custodian's hand represents the pledgee's interest in the financial value.
2
Understand the prohibition on taking
The text states that neither the pledgor nor the pledgee may take the pledged item from the custodian. The commentary explains that each has a right that would be nullified by unilateral action: the pledgor's right to preservation and the pledgee's right to secure the debt. Taking the item without the other's consent violates that right.
This prohibition is not about ownership but about possession; the item cannot be removed from the trusted location.
3
Examine the custodian's liability for wrongful delivery
If the custodian delivers the item to either party without the other's consent, he becomes liable (damān) for its value. This is because he is a depositary for both: for the substance on behalf of the pledgor, and for the financial value on behalf of the pledgee. Delivering to one party is like delivering to a stranger from the other's perspective.
This liability protects the deprived party's right.
4
Analyze the rule on loss in the custodian's hands
If the pledged item perishes while in the custodian's hands, the loss is from the guarantee (damān) of the pledgee, not the custodian. The commentary explains that the custodian's hand is considered the hand of the pledgee, since the custodian stands in his place for possession.
The practical effect: the debt remains due, but the pledgee loses the security; he cannot demand a replacement from the pledgor.

Commentary

Types of Authorization for the Custodian to Sell the Pledge
Rulings on the two types of authorization for the custodian to sell the pledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of authorization for the custodian to sell the pledge and state the legal effects of each.

Core Idea
Authorization in the contract is binding; authorization after the contract is revocable like a normal agency.

In pledge (rahn) transactions, the custodian (ʿadl) holds the pledged item but cannot sell it unless authorized. This passage clarifies that authorization is not monolithic—it can be given in the pledge contract itself or afterwards. Each type carries different consequences regarding revocability, effect of death, and the custodian's obligations. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rules of pledge and agency in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
Start by noting the general principle: the custodian has no right to sell the pledge without authorization. This is the baseline.
2
Recognize the two types of authorization
The passage divides taslīṭ (authorization) into two: (1) stipulated in the original pledge contract, and (2) given after the contract. Each has different rules.
This classification is unique to Hanafi fiqh and has practical implications.
3
Analyze the effects of type 1 (in-contract authorization)
For authorization in the contract: the custodian cannot be dismissed by either party; the authorization survives the death of pledgor or pledgee; if the custodian dies, the agency ends and his heir/executor cannot sell (except per Abu Yusuf); if the custodian refuses, he is compelled.
Note the exception from Abu Yusuf regarding the executor.
4
Analyze the effects of type 2 (post-contract authorization)
For authorization after the contract: the pledgor can dismiss the custodian at any time; the authorization ends upon the pledgor's death; the custodian may refuse to sell and is not compelled—this follows the general rules of agency.
This type is more flexible and less binding.
5
Apply the rules to a hypothetical scenario
Practice: If the custodian is authorized in the contract and the pledgor dies, can the custodian still sell? (Yes, as per the passage.) If the authorization was after the contract, can the custodian be compelled? (No, he may refuse.)
This step consolidates the distinction.

Authorization is of two types: authorization stipulated in the pledge contract, and authorization (stipulated) after (the contract). 1. Commentary
This passage explains the conditions under which the custodian (عدل) may sell the pledged item. The default is that he cannot sell unless authorized. Authorization is of two types: (1) stipulated in the original pledge contract, and (2) given after the contract. The Custodian's Sale Price: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the permissible sale price by the authorized custodian.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the price at which the custodian may sell the pledge, and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa gives the custodian absolute discretion on price; the Sahibayn require market value or slight variation.

When the custodian is authorized to both sell and pay off the debt, a further question arises: at what price may he sell? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement. Abu Hanifa treats the custodian like an absolute agent with full discretion, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) impose a restriction to protect the pledgor from significant loss. This lesson examines their proofs and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the precondition for the dispute
The ikhtilaf arises when the custodian is authorized to sell and pay the debt (مسلّط على البيع وإيفاء الدين). Without such authorization, the earlier rules apply.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa allows the custodian to sell at any price, high or low, like an absolute agent (كالوكيل بالبيع المطلق). The custodian is trusted to use his judgment.
This gives maximum flexibility.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad restrict the sale to cash at the item's value or slightly less, within the range of what is normally overlooked (بقدر ما يتغابن فيه). They aim to prevent exploitation of the pledgor.
This protects the pledgor from gross undervaluation.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the custodian can sell at any price, even far below market. Under the Sahibayn, the sale must be close to market value. The practical consequence is the degree of risk the pledgor bears.
5
Apply the rule to a scenario
If the custodian sells a pledged car worth $10,000 for $1,000: Abu Hanifa would uphold the sale if authorized absolutely; the Sahibayn would consider it invalid beyond the normal range of variation.
This illustrates the importance of the dispute.

Death of the Trustee in a Pledge: Agency Dissolved, Not Inherited
Ruling on trustee agency after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the trustee's agency after the death of the pawnbroker or the trustee, and explain why the agency does not pass to heirs or executors.

Core Idea
The trustee's agency ends with his death because consent is personal; it does not transfer to heirs.

In a pledge (rahn) contract, a trustee (adl) is often appointed to hold the pawn. What happens if the trustee dies? Does his agency pass to his heir or executor? The Hanafi school rules that the agency dissolves irrevocably, based on the principle of personal consent. This lesson unpacks that ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
List the pawner (rahin), pawnbroker (murtahin), and trustee (adl). Understand that the trustee is an agent for both parties to sell the pawn if needed.
The trustee's agency is a form of wakalah.
2
Determine the effect of pawnbroker's death
The text states that if the pawnbroker dies, the trustee remains in his agency. Why? Because the pledge contract itself is not invalidated by death; only the agency relation may be affected.
The contract of rahn is binding and continues.
3
Determine the effect of trustee's death
Upon the trustee's death, his agency is dissolved. Neither his heir nor his executor can replace him. The reason: the principal (the pawner and pawnbroker) consented to the trustee's personal judgment, not to that of any other person.
This is based on the principle that agency (wakalah) is a contract built on personal trust.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference (Abu Yusuf)
Abu Yusuf narrates that the trustee's executor may sell the pawn. However, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that no one can replace the trustee, and the empowerment ends.
Al-Hidayah mentions both views but the main text supports the termination.
5
Distinguish timing of empowerment
If the trustee's empowerment to sell was given after the pledge contract, the pawner can dismiss him at will, and the trustee himself is not compelled to sell – like any other agent.
This contrasts with the initial appointment where the trustee might be compelled to sell if he refuses.

Pawning Fungibles: Same-Type Destruction and Compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on pawning fungible items and apply the rule of discharge by weight/measure when a pawn of the same genus is destroyed.

Core Idea
When a pawn of the same genus is destroyed, the debt is discharged by weight, not value — quality is irrelevant.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra explains the permissibility of pawning fungible items such as dirhams, dinars, and goods sold by measure or weight. The key ruling concerns what happens if the pawn is destroyed: if the pawn and debt are of the same type, the debt is discharged by returning an equivalent weight or measure, regardless of differences in quality. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa, while his two disciples differ when the pawn is of a different type. Understanding this distinction helps avoid riba and clarifies the rights of the pawnbroker and trustee.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is pawning fungible items (mithliyat). The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz). The reason given is that satisfaction of the debt can be realized from the pawn itself.
Fungible items are those that can be replaced by equivalent units.
2

This is based on Abu Hanifa's view; considering value would lead to riba.
3

Core Idea
Sale of pledged property is suspended, not void; it becomes effective by ratification or debt payment.

A pledgor owns the property but the mortgagee has a right to detain it until the debt is paid. If the pledgor sells the property without permission, the sale is not automatically void but suspended. Emancipation of a Pledged Slave: Pledgor's Authority vs. Pledgee's Right
Ruling on pledgor's emancipation of pledged slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a pledgor emancipating a pledged slave, derive it using qiyas, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument with the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Ownership trumps possession: the pledgor's emancipation is effective, and the pledgee's right shifts to compensation.

When a debtor pledges a slave as collateral, the slave remains the debtor's property. But what happens if the debtor then frees that slave without the creditor's permission? The Hanafi school rules that the emancipation is valid and the slave is free—because ownership gives full authority. The Shafi'i school disagrees, distinguishing between solvent and insolvent debtors. This lesson unpacks the proof and practical consequences of this dispute.

Guided Path
1

Identify the exact ruling: the emancipation of a pledged slave by the pledgor without the pledgee's permission is effective (nafidh).
The slave exits the pledge and becomes free.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position
Contrast: al-Shafi'i holds it is ineffective if the pledgor is insolvent (mu'sir), but effective if solvent (muyassir), requiring a new pledge of value.
The key variable is solvency.
3
Trace the Hanafi proof (qiyas)
The Hanafi dalil: analogy to emancipation of a purchased slave before delivery, and to slaves who are married, under kitaba, or leased. In all these cases, ownership allows emancipation.
The 'illah (effective cause) is absolute ownership of the slave, which the pledge does not remove.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to Shafi'i
The Hanafi rebuttal: ownership of the corpus is stronger than the pledgee's right of possession. Since the stronger (ownership) is not blocked by the weaker (possession), the emancipation is effective a fortiori (bi-tariq al-awla).
The Shafi'i distinction based on solvency is rejected because ownership is unconditional.

Practical consequence: In Hanafi school, the slave is freed even if the pledgor is insolvent; the pledgee loses the security unless the debt is due (then demands payment) or deferred (gets the value as new pledge). In Shafi'i school, an insolvent pledgor's emancipation is void, so the slave remains pledged.
This difference has direct implications for the rights of the parties.

In the Hanafi school, the mortgage (rahn) creates a debt attachment to the slave's person. Sa'yah depends on master's insolvency at manumission; slave's value at that time sets the maximum, but prior pawning value may impose a cap if higher.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah concerning a pawned slave who is manumitted by the pawner. The key is that the freed slave must work to pay off the master's debt (sa'yah) only if the master was insolvent at the moment of manumission. The text also discusses how changes in the slave's market value between pawning and manumission affect the required amount, as well as the case where the debt is less than the slave's value. Tadbir of a Pawned Slave: Effects and Comparison with Manumission
Explains effect of tadbir on pawned slave and contrasts with itq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the effects of tadbir on a pawn contract and distinguish them from those of immediate manumission (itq).

Core Idea
Tadbir invalidates the pawn but gives the pledgee a choice to claim the slave or the pawner, unlike itq where the pledgee's claim is limited.

When a pawner declares tadbir (postponed manumission) on a pawned slave, the pawn contract is dissolved. However, the pledgee's rights are not nullified; he can choose to claim the slave's value or demand payment from the pawner. This lesson unpacks the rulings from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah and contrasts them with immediate manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the effect of tadbir on the pawn contract
The passage states that tadbir is valid and the pawn is invalidated, just like in itq. This means the slave leaves the pawn's possession.
The pawn contract ends immediately upon tadbir.
2
Analyze the pledgee's options after tadbir
The pledgee may claim his debt either from the slave (by taking his value through sa'ayah) or from the pawner, regardless of the pawner's solvency.
The pledgee's choice is not limited to the slave's value in tadbir.
3
Understand the rationale behind the pledgee's choice
Since the mudabbar's earnings belong to the master, the pledgee can pursue the master directly. The slave's acquisitions are considered part of the master's assets.
This is why the option exists regardless of the master's financial status.
4
Contrast tadbir with itq in two points
First, in itq, the slave's sa'ayah is limited to the least of three values; in tadbir, it is for the entire debt. Second, in itq the slave can seek reimbursement; in tadbir he cannot.
The difference stems from ownership of earnings: in tadbir, earnings belong to the master; in itq, to the freed slave.
5
Apply the ruling to a hypothetical
Example: Zayd pawns his slave Amr for 100 dirhams. Then Zayd declares tadbir. Amr's value is 80. The pledgee can demand 100 from Zayd or take Amr's value (80) by sa'ayah. If itq instead, the pledgee could only take min(100,80, value at itq) and Amr could seek reimbursement.
The sa'yah amount and reimbursement possibility differ.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A pawned slave-girl bearing her pawner’s child invalidates the pawn and she must work to repay the debt, with no reimbursement.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in pawn transactions (rahn): what happens when the pawner (the debtor who pledges a slave-girl) fathers a child with that slave-girl. The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that the act of istilad (begetting a child) is valid and automatically cancels the pawn contract. The slave-girl, now an umm walad, must then earn money to pay off the entire debt, but she does so for her master's benefit without right to compensation.

The act is istilad (fathering a child) by the pawner upon the pawned slave-girl. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih).
2

The valid istilad invalidates the pawn contract (batala al-rahn). The pawn is no longer in effect.
This is analogous to tadbir (promising freedom after death) which also voids the pawn.

The slave-girl must acquire (tusā‘ī) in order to pay off the entire debt, just like a mudabbar slave. 'Sa'yah' means she works and earns money that goes to pay the debt.
She is not free until the debt is paid, but she becomes free upon the master's death (as umm walad).
4
Explain why no reimbursement is due
The slave-girl cannot claim reimbursement from her master for the money she earns to pay the debt, because her earnings belong to the master (kashuhā mālun li-l-mawlā). She is merely using the master's own property to satisfy his debt.
This aligns with the principle that an umm walad's earnings are her master's during his lifetime.

Ruling on the Mudabbar Slave in Pawn and Comparison with Emancipation
Explains creditor's right against mudabbar slave and two differences from emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a mudabbar slave in pawn and explain the two key differences between tadbir and full emancipation.

Core Idea
A mudabbar's earnings belong to the master, so the creditor can claim from either the slave or the master, and the slave works for the full debt without reclaim.

This passage from Sharh al-Quduri explains how a mudabbar slave (promised freedom after the master's death) is treated when pawned. The creditor may claim the debt from either the slave or the pledgor, regardless of solvency. The text then highlights two critical differences from a fully freed slave: the amount the slave must work and the right of reclaim. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correct application in pawn contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The pawn of a mudabbar slave. What is the creditor's right? He may take from either the slave or the pledgor, irrespective of solvency.
2
Trace the reasoning (talil)
Why does the creditor have this option? Because the mudabbar's earnings belong to the master (the pledgor). Hence, the claim is against the master's assets, and the slave's earnings are part of those assets.
The key principle: 'Aksab al-mudabbar li-mawlahu' (earnings of mudabbar belong to the master).
3
Compare with full emancipation
How is tadbir different from 'itq? Note two differences: (a) amount of sa'aya – in tadbir the slave works for the full debt, in 'itq only the minimum of three values; (b) right of reclaim – in tadbir the slave does not reclaim, in 'itq he does.
The difference arises because the freed slave's earnings are his own, while the mudabbar's are not.
4
Apply to the umm walad case
If the pawned slave is female and the master impregnates her, the pawn is voided, and she works for the full debt like a mudabbar, with no right of reclaim. This extends the same principle.
The istilad cancels the pawn but the obligation to work remains.
5
Determine the operative ruling (mufta bihi)
The text states the ruling clearly: creditor may choose, slave works full debt, no reclaim. This is the zahir al-riwayah position and the mufta bihi.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is mentioned; the ruling is relied upon.
