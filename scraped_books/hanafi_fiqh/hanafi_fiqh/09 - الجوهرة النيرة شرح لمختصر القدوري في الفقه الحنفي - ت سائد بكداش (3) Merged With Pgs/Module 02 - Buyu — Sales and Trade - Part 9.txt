For gold and silver, weight equality is mandated by text, not custom; craftsmanship does not change the ruling.

This lesson focuses on a key condition for valid exchange of ribawi items: equality in weight for gold and silver. Custom can override the general rule for base metals because their ribawi status is established by custom, not text.

Explaining rulings of gold-silver exchange with proofs from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings of a sarf contract and explain why hand-to-hand possession is a condition for validity, not formation.

Core Idea
In sarf, disparity allowed but hand-to-hand mandatory; separation before receipt voids the contract.

This lesson unpacks the core rulings of sarf (exchange of gold and silver) as explained in the commentary on al-Quduri. You will learn when disparity in quantity is permissible, why immediate hand-to-hand possession is required, and what happens if the parties separate before completing the exchange. The proof is drawn from the Prophet's ﷺ hadith: 'Gold for silver is riba unless it is take and take.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its three rulings
The passage presents three connected rulings for a sarf contract: (1) disparity in quantity is permissible, (2) hand-to-hand possession is obligatory, (3) separation before full receipt voids the contract. Also, disposal of the price before receipt is prohibited.
These rulings apply only when the two items are of different genera (gold vs. silver).
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'الذهب بالورق رباً، إلا هاء وهاء' (Gold for silver is riba, except take and take). This is the textual proof for the obligation of hand-to-hand possession.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud (3341) and is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'إلا هاء وهاء' (except hand-to-hand) excludes the transaction from being riba only if immediate exchange occurs. The imperative implies obligation in Hanafi usul: the command to 'take and take' requires immediate delivery.
4
Understand the legal consequence: condition of validity vs. formation
The commentary clarifies that hand-to-hand possession is a condition of validity (shart al-jawāz), not a condition of formation (shart al-in‘iqād). The contract is initially valid, but if the parties separate before full receipt, it becomes void (bāṭil). This is deduced from the matn's wording 'بطل العقد' (the contract becomes void) – voidance only occurs after a valid contract.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position; other schools may treat it as a pillar.
5
Apply the ruling: prohibition of disposal before receipt
If one sells a dinar for ten dirhams, he cannot use those dirhams to buy another item before actually receiving them. If he does, the second sale is invalid (fāsid) and does not affect the original sarf contract.
The prohibition prevents riba by ensuring the exchange is completed before any further disposal.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of al-Quduri regarding the chapter of sarf (exchange of gold and silver). It presents three connected rulings: (1) in a sale of gold for silver, disparity in quantity is permissible because they are different genera, but hand-to-hand possession (taqabud) is obligatory to avoid riba; (2) if the parties separate physically before both have taken possession of their respective counterparts, the contract becomes void (batal); (3) before receiving the price in a sarf contract, it is not allowed to dispose of that price in another transaction. Additional narrations from Ibn ‘Umar and ‘Umar illustrate that separation must be physical; walking together or sleeping in the same session does not constitute separation. The practical consequence is that if someone buys gold for silver and then, before receiving the silver, buys another item with that same silver, the second purchase is invalid, and the original exchange remains pending until proper possession is taken.

Separation Before Full Delivery Voids the Contract
Ruling on separation before taqabud and its fiqh implications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for separation before taqabud in a sarf contract and explain why subsequent delivery does not validate it.

Core Idea
Taqabud in sarf is a condition for the contract's continuation — its failure voids the contract, and later delivery cannot revive it.

The sarf contract requires immediate hand-to-hand delivery of both countervalues. This lesson examines what happens if the parties separate before that delivery is completed. The commentator explains the subtle distinction: taqabud is a condition for the contract's permissibility and continuation, not its formation. Understanding this distinction is key to applying the ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the hukm when the parties separate before full taqabud in a sarf contract: the contract becomes void (batil).
The matn explicitly says 'batal al-aqd'.
2
Understand the conceptual distinction
The commentator clarifies that taqabud is a condition for permissibility/continuation (shart al-jawaz), not for formation (shart al-in'iqaad). This is why voidness (butlan) applies only after valid formation.
The quote from al-Nihayah reinforces this: taqabud is a condition for the contract's survival.
3
Apply the ruling to post-separation delivery
If one party takes possession after separating, the contract does not become valid. The voidness is not curable.
The text says 'la yanqalibu ja'izan'.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position contrasts with the Shafi'i view, where taqabud is considered a condition for validity (sihha) and the contract becomes fasid (not batil), curable by later delivery.
This ikhtilaf hinges on the classification of taqabud.

No Disposing of the Price Before Receipt in Sarf
Ruling forbidding disposal of price before receipt in currency exchange

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on disposing of the price before receipt in a sarf contract and identify the consequence of such disposal.

Core Idea
In sarf, the price is not owned until received – disposal before receipt is haram and invalidates the second transaction.

Currency exchange (sarf) has special rules because both items are ribawi. One key rule is that neither party can use the money they are to receive until they actually take possession. This lesson explains that prohibition and its practical effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act being ruled is 'disposing of the price (thaman al-sarf) before receiving it.' The ruling is that it is impermissible (haram).
2
Analyze the given example
The commentator illustrates: if someone sells a dinar for ten dirhams, then before receiving the dirhams uses them to buy a garment, that second sale is fasid (invalid).
The original sarf contract remains valid; he still must receive the dirhams.
3
Extract the condition
For any disposal of the price (e.g., sale, gift, pledge) to be valid, the price must first be physically received (qabd).
This condition arises because ownership of ribawi items in a contract is not complete without possession.
4
Determine the consequence of violation
If the price is disposed of before receipt, that second transaction is fasid (defective/invalid). The original sarf, however, is unaffected.
The remedy is to still complete the sarf by receiving the price; the second transaction is not cured.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Forgiveness or gift of the price before receipt in sarf is invalid; acceptance voids the contract.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in currency exchange (sarf): what happens if one party attempts to forgive or gift the price before physically receiving it. The Hanafi school holds that such disposal is impermissible because ownership in sarf requires possession (qabd). The passage also clarifies two possible outcomes based on acceptance and includes an expansion by al-Karkhi regarding compulsion to receive. Understanding this helps prevent nullification of exchange contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
Recognize that forgiving (ibra') or gifting (hiba) the price in a sarf contract before receipt is the act under discussion. State the ruling: it is not permissible (ghayr ja'iz).
The ruling is based on the principle that one cannot dispose of what one does not yet own (ownership requires receipt in sarf).
2

The text says 'because it is a disposal before receipt.' Internalize that in Hanafi fiqh, ownership of the price in sarf is contingent upon physical possession (qabd). Any attempt to transfer, forgive, or gift before that is invalid.
This is specific to sarf due to the Prophetic command for hand-to-hand exchange.
3
Distinguish Outcomes Based on Acceptance
If the counterparty accepts the forgiveness or gift, the original exchange contract becomes void (batil). If not accepted, the contract remains valid and unaffected.
The key variable is the acceptance of the ibra'/hiba by the debtor (the one whose debt is forgiven).
4

When the giver (seller) attempts to gift the price back and the buyer refuses, but the seller then refuses to take the price, the buyer is compelled to receive. This prevents the seller from unilaterally terminating the contract.
Al-Karkhi's ruling emphasizes that the buyer's right to complete the contract overrides the seller's attempt to rescind.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partial payment in a sarf contract, identify the condition for its applicability, and explain why the voidness is incidental and does not spread.

Core Idea
In a sarf contract, partial payment before separation leads to joint ownership; voidness is confined to the unpaid portion.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from the Chapter of Sarf: when a silver vessel is sold and the parties separate after partial payment, the contract is valid for the paid portion and void for the unpaid, making the vessel jointly owned. The passage also discusses the crucial condition regarding the vessel's silver content and the buyer's option if part of the vessel is usurped. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the concept of incidental corruption (fasad tari') and its distinction from the sword case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is selling a silver vessel for currency. The ruling: if the parties separate after partial payment, the contract is valid for the paid portion and void for the unpaid portion.
The entire transaction is treated as sarf (currency exchange), requiring immediate possession (qabd) of both countervalues.
2
Locate the condition for the ruling
The ruling applies only when the unmounted silver (pure silver content) exceeds the ornamentation value. If equal, less, or unknown, the sale is invalid from the start.
This condition prevents buying silver with unknown equivalence.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The contract is considered entirely as sarf, so each unit of silver must be exchanged hand-to-hand. Partial possession validates that portion; missing possession voids the unpaid portion. The voidness is incidental (tari') — it does not spread to the valid part.
'Incidental voidness' means the contract was initially valid but becomes void due to a subsequent factor (separation).
4
Distinguish from the sword case
In the sword case (non-ribawi items), if parties separate before full payment, the entire contract becomes void because the item is not divisible by weight for riba purposes. In sarf, the voidness is partial.
The commentary emphasizes that the sarf ruling is a caution against applying the sword case analogically.
5
Apply the ruling to partial usurpation
If part of the vessel is later claimed by a third party (istihqaq), the buyer has an option: take the remainder at a proportional price or return it. This option exists because the harm (cutting the vessel) did not come from the buyer.
This is unlike the initial separation, where the buyer caused the division and thus had no option.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the chapter of sarf. The matn gives the ruling: if someone sells a silver vessel and the parties separate after receiving part of the price, the contract is void for the unpaid portion and valid for the paid portion, making the vessel joint property. If later part of the vessel is usurped (claimed by a third party), the buyer has the option to either take the remaining part at its proportional price or return it. However, this only applies when the unmounted silver (the pure silver content) is greater than the ornamentation value; if it is equal, less, or unknown, the sale is invalid from the start. The commentary explains the reasoning: the entire transaction is treated as sarf (currency exchange), so each part must be exchanged hand-to-hand. Since partial payment was made, the contract is valid for the part paid and void for the unpaid, with the voidness being incidental (does not spread to the valid part). This is different from the sword case (where the contract becomes entirely void if separation occurs before full payment) because in that case, the items were not purely ribawi. The commentary also clarifies that the vessel becoming shared creates a partnership, and no option (khiyar) exists for either party regarding that partnership because the separation without full possession was due to the buyer's action (he paid only part and then separated). However, if part of the vessel is later usurped, the buyer gets an option because he did not cause that harm.

In silver exchange, partial receipt and separation voids the unpaid part but does not spread to the paid part.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in currency exchange (sarf): selling a silver vessel where the parties separate after receiving only part of the price. The Hanafi school rules that the contract is valid for the paid portion and void for the unpaid portion, making the vessel jointly owned. The commentary explains why the corruption is temporary and does not pervade the entire contract, unlike the case of a sword. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a currency exchange sale becomes valid upon ratification by the entitled person (mustahiq) and explain the agency relationship that results.

Core Idea
Ratification by the entitled person cures the defect in a currency exchange sale, but only if the parties have not yet separated.

In Hanafi sales law, when a sold item is later claimed by a third party (mustahiq), the sale is initially suspended. This lesson examines the ruling if the mustahiq ratifies the sale before the court establishes his entitlement. The key is that ratification can validate the contract, but only if the buyer and seller have not separated after the ratification. Additionally, the contracting parties become agents for the ratifier, so contractual rights and obligations are attached to them, not the ratifier.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the ratification (ijaza) by the entitled person. The ruling: the contract becomes valid (sahih) and the price belongs to the ratifier.
2
Identify the conditions for validity
The ratification must occur before judgment of entitlement. Also, the buyer and seller must not have parted after the ratification. If they separate before ratification, the contract is void.
Note: If the entitled person himself leaves the session before ratifying, but the contracting parties remain, the contract remains valid.
3
Understand the agency relationship
Upon ratification, the original contracting parties become agents (wakeel) of the ratifier. All rights and obligations of the contract are attached to the agents, not the ratifier.
4
Note the boundary cases
If the parties separate before ratification → contract void. If the entitlement claimant leaves before ratifying but the parties stay → contract still valid (can be ratified later).

Validity of Mixed Ribawi Sales and the Division Methods
Ruling on mixed ribawi currency sales with division methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of dividing mixed currency sales to determine validity and distinguish between qismat al-i'tibar and qismat al-mukhalafah.

Core Idea
A mixed sale does not automatically entail riba if each genus is set against its own kind.

In the chapter of currency exchange (sarf), the classical scholars faced a challenge: how to rule on sales where a bundle of dirhams and dinars is exchanged for another bundle with different proportions. At first glance, such transactions may seem to involve riba because like currencies are not equal. This passage provides the Hanafi solution: the contract is construed validly by dividing it into two separate exchanges, one for each genus. The reasoning is rooted in a powerful usul principle that when a contract has both a valid and invalid aspect, it is upheld on the valid one. We will also explore the two division methods used and the intra-Hanafi disagreement with Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
The passage begins: 'If he sells two dirhams and a dinar for two dinars and a dirham: the sale is permissible.' State the hukm (permissible) and note that it applies when the genera can be set against each other.
2
Locate and understand the supporting dalil
The author cites a general principle: 'if a contract has two aspects, one validating it and the other invalidating it, it is construed upon what validates it.' This is the proof. Consider how this principle applies here: the contract could be seen as ribawi if the dirhams are not equal, but it is validated by dividing the exchange.
This is a talil (reasoning) based on the maxim of preferring validity.
3
Analyse the two division methods
The passage introduces qismat al-i'tibar (division of consideration) and qismat al-mukhalafah (division of opposites). Work through the examples: in the first, one side has a surplus of the same genus; in the second, the genera are swapped. Understand how each ensures equality within the same genus.
Focus on the arithmetic: e.g., 10 dirhams + 1 dinar for 12 dirhams → 10 dirhams against 10 dirhams, dinar against 2 dirhams.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl disagrees, holding such sales invalid. His reasoning is that the exact exchange rate between the two genera cannot be determined without riba. The Hanafi response is that the division method resolves this uncertainty.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
5
State the mufta bihi and its rationale
The relied-upon position is the majority view (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) that the sale is valid. The rationale is that the contract can be interpreted in a riba-free manner, and Islamic law favours validity in transactions.
The matn of al-Quduri itself indicates this ruling.

Basic Ruling on Heterogeneous Currency Exchange (Sarf)
Exposition of a basic sarf ruling with rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling eleven dirhams for ten dirhams and one dinar, and explain how the allocation ensures validity under sarf rules.

Core Idea
In sarf, different types are exchanged freely, but same-type exchanges must be equal in weight.

The first ruling in this passage presents a common scenario: selling eleven dirhams for ten dirhams and a dinar. At first glance, it seems imbalanced, but the Hanafi school rules it valid by splitting the transaction into two separate exchanges. This lesson unpacks the allocation method and its underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction
Recognize the two types: dirhams (silver) and dinars (gold). The seller gives 11 dirhams and receives 10 dirhams + 1 dinar.
Total dirhams given (11) exceed dirhams received (10).
2
Apply the sarf rule for same type
Same-type exchanges (dirham with dirham) must be equal in weight. The 10 dirhams received are matched with 10 of the dirhams given.
This avoids riba al-fadl within the same genus.
3
Account for the surplus
The remaining dirham (the 11th) is considered exchanged for the dinar, a different type. Since different types have no requirement of equality, this is permissible.
The dinar compensates the extra dirham.
4
Conclude the ruling
The sale is valid (jā'iz) because the transaction is decomposed into two valid sarf exchanges: 10 dirhams for 10 dirhams (like for like) and 1 dinar for 1 dirham (different types).
This allocation is the basis for many complex sarf transactions.

Qismat al-Mukhalafah: Exchanging Two Types with Disparity
Exposition of a second permissible sarf structure with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qismat al-mukhalafah and apply it to a multi-currency exchange scene.

Core Idea
When two types of ribawi goods are traded for two other types, each type is matched with its counterpart, permitting disparity.

The passage introduces a second type of sarf transaction called qismat al-mukhalafah, where two types (e.g., dirhams and dinars) are exchanged for two other types with an apparent disparity. The Hanafi ruling deems it valid because each type is placed in exchange for the other type, ensuring no riba within the same genus. This lesson explores the definition and application with examples from al-Asl.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scenario
Two types of ribawi goods (e.g., dirhams and dinars) are sold for two other types (also dirhams and dinars but in different quantities).
Example: 2 dirhams + 1 dinar for 2 dinars + 1 dirham.
2
Understand the contrast
There is disparity overall: e.g., dirham side has 2 dirhams and 1 dinar, the other side has 1 dirham and 2 dinars. The numbers are not equal per type.
But Hanafi does not require overall equality because each type is dealt with separately.
3
Apply matching rule
Each type is placed in exchange for its counterpart: dirhams with dirhams, dinars with dinars. The surplus in one type is offset by the surplus in the other.
This avoids riba within the same genus (jins).
4
Conclude permissibility
The transaction is permissible (jā'iz ʿindanā) according to Hanafi school. The text emphasizes 'according to us', indicating disagreement with some other schools.
The proof is the matching principle, which prevents riba.

Gift of Extra Weight in Sarf and the Role of Divisibility
Application of hibah musha' rules to a sarf transaction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the validity of gifting a surplus daniq in a sarf transaction when the dirhams are sound versus broken.

Core Idea
A gift of a common share in an indivisible property is valid, but if the property is divisible, the gift requires physical division.

This lesson examines a nuanced case: buying ten dirhams for ten dirhams, where one party gives an extra daniq as a gift outside the sale. The ruling depends on whether the dirhams are sound (whole) or broken. The passage explains that for sound dirhams, the gift of the daniq is valid as a hibah musha' (gift of a common share) in something indivisible. For broken coins, the daniq can be separated, making the gift invalid without division. This illustrates the Hanafi principle of hibah musha'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base transaction
Ten dirhams are exchanged for ten dirhams of equal weight. One party then gifts a daniq (a small fraction) extra, outside the sale.
The daniq is not part of the sale price.
2
Check the condition of the dirhams
Determine whether the dirhams are sound whole coins (sihah) or broken pieces (mukassarah/ghallah).
Sound coins are indivisible in practice; broken coins are divisible.
3
Apply hibah musha' rules
If sound: the daniq is an undivided share in the whole coin, which is indivisible. Gift of a common share in indivisible property is valid. If broken: the daniq is a separate piece, so the gift is of a common share in divisible property, which is invalid without physical division.
The gift is a separate act from the sale.
4
Determine the overall ruling
If sound: sale and gift both valid. If broken: gift invalid, and consequently the sale becomes invalid because the extra weight was not compensated (the buyer ends up with more weight without proper exchange).
The text says: 'the sale is not permissible' in the broken case.

Ruling on Coins by Predominant Metal and Exchange (Sarf)
Commentary on al-Quduri's ruling for coins with predominant silver/gold vs. alloy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'al-hukm li al-ghalib' to classify coins as currency or merchandise and determine the permissibility of exchanging them with inequality (tafadul).

Core Idea
A coin's legal status follows its majority: silver/gold = ribawi; alloy = merchandise, allowing differential exchange.

In Hanafi fiqh, the exchange of coins (sarf) is governed by the principle that the majority component determines the coin's legal classification. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains that if the majority of a dirham or dinar is pure silver or gold, it retains its status as ribawi currency, requiring equal weight and immediate delivery. If the majority is alloy (ghish), the coin is treated as merchandise ('urud), permitting inequality in exchange. This distinction is crucial for merchants and jurists to avoid riba while allowing practical trade in mixed-metal coinage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of coins based on majority composition
The passage distinguishes between coins where the majority is silver/gold and coins where the majority is alloy (ghish). This classification determines the legal ruling.
Also note the subcategory of ghalla (broken coins) which are treated based on weight equality.
2
Determine the ruling for each category in sarf exchange
For majority silver/gold: ribawi rules apply—no inequality, must be equal by weight, and hand-to-hand delivery. For majority alloy: treated as merchandise, so inequality and deferment are allowed.
The passage explicitly permits selling alloy-dominant coins for their kind with inequality.
3
Analyze the exception for coins that can be purified from alloy
If the precious metal can be extracted (takhallus), the coin is not fully consumed and is treated as a mixture. Selling such a coin for pure silver requires careful consideration to avoid riba.
The passage gives an example: selling a coin with separable alloy is like selling copper and silver together.
4
Understand the requirement of hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) for alloy coins with silver content
Even if the majority is alloy, as long as both sides contain some silver, the transaction remains sarf, necessitating immediate delivery. This is cited from al-Hidayah.
This prevents deferment and ensures the exchange is spot.
5
Apply the principle 'al-hukm li al-ghalib' to other contexts
This maxim is a general legal tool in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding its application here helps in other areas where predominance determines ruling (e.g., purity of water, ingredients of food).
The principle is rooted in ease and practicality.

Fulūs Obsolescence: Sale Void and Loan Dispute
States a ruling and an intra-Hanafi dispute on fulūs obsolescence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on a sale where fulūs become obsolete before delivery, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on repaying a loan of obsolete fulūs.

Core Idea
If fulūs become obsolete before delivery, the sale is void; for a loan, Abu Hanifa demands like, Sahibayn demand value.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira addresses two distinct scenarios involving obsolete fulūs (currency coins). First, if fulūs that were current at the time of sale become worthless before physical delivery, Abu Hanifa rules the sale void. Second, if a loan of fulūs is made and the currency later becomes obsolete, there is a well-known intra-Hanafi disagreement: Abu Hanifa insists on returning the same type, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue for paying the value, differing on the valuation date. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish between a contract's essential elements (price) and post-contractual changes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sale ruling
Read the first part: a sale with current fulūs that become obsolete before qabḍ (delivery). State the ruling: the sale becomes void (bāṭil) according to Abu Hanifa. This is because the price (fulūs) is no longer a valid medium of exchange.
The ruling is clear and undisputed within the passage.
2
Analyze the loan scenario
Now read the second scenario: a loan (qarḍ) of fulūs, then obsolescence. Abu Hanifa says the borrower must return the like (mithl), because a loan is a tamlīk contract that obligates returning the same thing in meaning.
Abu Hanifa treats the loan as an exchange of like for like, not value.
3
Contrast the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue for payment of value (qīma) instead of the like. They differ on the valuation date: Abu Yusuf says value on the day of receipt, Muhammad says on the day of obsolescence.
The Sahibayn analogize this to the case of adulterated dirhams (maghshūsha).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, if the fulūs become worthless, the borrower must still obtain and return the same type (which may be costly or impossible if obsolete). Under the Sahibayn, the borrower pays cash value, but the amount depends on the date chosen.
This affects the borrower’s liability significantly.
5
Relate to broader Hanafi principles
Note the underlying principle: Abu Hanifa emphasizes the essence of the contract (returning the same kind), while the Sahibayn look to practical consequences (value). This mirrors Hanafi debates on tamlīk vs. damān contracts.
Think about how this connects to the concept of mithl vs. qīma in Hanafi fiqh.

Buying for Half Dirham of Fulūs
States the permissibility and conditions of a sale using fulūs as price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a sale using fulūs as payment and identify the three key provisions: obligation to deliver value, option to substitute, and contract not rescinded by destruction.

Core Idea
A sale with fulūs is valid; the buyer owes the equivalent value, can substitute coins, and the contract endures even if the fulūs are destroyed.

This short passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar establishes a foundational rule for transactions using fulūs (copper coins) as currency. Understanding this ruling clarifies that fulūs are treated as a measure of value (dayn) rather than specific items, which has important practical consequences for the contract's validity and obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on the sale itself
The matn states 'jāza al-bayʿ' meaning the sale is valid and permissible. Fulūs are recognized as legitimate currency for transactions.
In Hanafi fiqh, 'jāza' in contracts indicates legal validity and enforceability.
2
Determine the buyer's obligation
The buyer owes the amount of fulūs that can be bought for half a dirham at the time of the contract. This is a debt (dayn) measured by value, not specific coins.
The obligation is defined by market value, not particular fulūs.
3
Analyze the two ancillary provisions
First, the buyer may substitute other fulūs of equal value. Second, if the fulūs are destroyed, the contract is not rescinded because the obligation is a debt, not a specific item.
These provisions emphasize that fulūs function as currency (thaman) rather than specific goods (muthamman).
