Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Management of the Safeeh's Wealth for Zakat and Maintenance
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the safeeh's wealth management and the obligations of zakat and nafaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two disciples' ruling on the safeeh's wealth and explain how the obligations of zakat and nafaqah are fulfilled under their position.

Core Idea
A safeeh's wealth is never handed over until prudence, but divine and personal obligations like zakat and nafaqah must still be fulfilled.

This passage presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute: what happens when a person reaches adulthood but remains financially imprudent (safeeh)? The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) adopt a strict position — the wealth is never relinquished until prudence is proven. Yet, they affirm that the safeeh's wealth is subject to obligatory payments: zakat as a divine right and maintenance for dependents as personal rights. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical mechanism for fulfilling these obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on wealth management
The two disciples state that the safeeh's wealth is never handed over and his transactions are invalid. Contrast this with Abu Hanifa's likely more lenient view.
The text emphasizes 'لا يدفع إليه ماله أبداً' — never handed over.
2
Analyze the obligation of zakat
Zakat must be paid from the safeeh's wealth because it is a divine command, like prayer and fasting. It is taken out with his permission; for grazing livestock, some say without permission.
The rule is that divine obligations supersede the prohibitions on his management.
3
Examine the Hidayah's practical mechanism
The Hidayah suggests the judge gives the zakat amount to the safeeh to distribute himself (to fulfill the intention requirement), but sends a trustee to ensure proper disbursement.
This balances the need for the safeeh's niyyah and the protection of the wealth.
4
Understand the obligation of nafaqah
Spending on dependents (children, wife, needy relatives) is mandatory as these are rights of people, not affected by the safeeh's lack of prudence.
Human rights (huquq al-'ibad) are not nullified by financial interdiction.

Zakat and Maintenance from the Safih's Wealth
Ruling with proof, explaining extraction of zakat and nafaqah from safih's wealth.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why zakat and maintenance are taken from the safih's wealth, describe the procedure for distributing zakat with a trustee, and identify the intra-Hanafi difference regarding livestock.

Core Idea
The safih’s interdiction does not suspend his religious or interpersonal duties—both zakat and nafaqah are extracted from his wealth.

This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira clarifies that being declared safih (prodigal) does not exempt a person from fundamental financial obligations. Zakat, a divine command, and maintenance for dependents, a right of others, must still be fulfilled. The judge oversees these payments, ensuring proper intention for zakat and direct provision for nafaqah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two obligations
The passage discusses zakat (obligatory alms) and nafaqah (maintenance for dependents). Note that both are taken from the safih's wealth despite his interdiction.
Zakat is an act of worship; nafaqah is a right of others.
2
Understand the rationale for each
Zakat is required because it is commanded by Allah, similar to prayer and fasting. Nafaqah is required because it is a right of the dependents that cannot be nullified by the safih's status.
3
Analyse the procedure for zakat
The judge gives the zakat amount to the safih so that he can distribute it himself (to fulfill the intention requirement). However, a trustee is sent to ensure the money is spent properly. There is also a view that for grazing livestock, zakat can be taken without permission.
Al-Hidayah: 'the judge gives the amount to him ... but sends a trustee with him'
4
Analyse the procedure for nafaqah
Maintenance is simply spent from his wealth on his children, wife, and eligible relatives. No trustee is mentioned because it is a right of the recipients, not a devotional act requiring intention.
The safih's interdiction does not affect these rights.

Second, nafaqah (maintenance) for his dependents: these are rights owed to others, and the safih's condition does not invalidate such rights. Therefore, the judge ensures these payments are made.

Quantifying Vague Descriptions in Iqrar
Rulings on interpreting ambiguous descriptors in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that vague property descriptors in iqrar are quantified using established benchmarks (e.g., 10 dirhams for 'precious', and zakat nisab for species-specific 'great').

Core Idea
Vague descriptors in iqrar are binding based on fixed legal benchmarks, not subjective opinion.

When someone confesses to possessing or usurping property described only as 'precious' or 'great,' the law needs a concrete measure. This passage shows how the Hanafi school uses well-known thresholds—like the nisab for theft or the zakat nisab for livestock—to determine the minimum liability. Understanding this principle prevents disputes over ambiguous language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of descriptors
The passage distinguishes between vague value terms (nafis, khatir, kathir) and species-specific size terms ('azim for camels, cows, sheep). Recognize that each category has its own benchmark.
2
Determine the benchmark for vague value terms
For terms like 'precious', the minimum liability is 10 dirhams. This is the nisab of theft, a well-known legal standard. Ask: why 10? Because it is the threshold for punishment and thus signifies significance.
3
Determine the benchmark for species-specific 'great'
For 'great camels', the number is 25; for cows, 30; for sheep, 40. These are exactly the zakat nisab for each species. The descriptor 'great' triggers the zakat threshold, not a lower amount.
4
Confirm the underlying legal reasoning (talil)
The choice of these numbers is not arbitrary: they are already established in shariah for other rulings (theft nisab, zakat nisab). This shows the legal system prefers objective, known quantities over subjective estimation.

Commentary
This passage presents two rulings. First, if a confessor uses descriptors like 'precious' (nafis), 'serious' (khatir), or 'much' (kathir) without specifying an amount, the minimum that becomes binding on him is ten dirhams, according to Abu Hanifa. This is presumably because these terms imply a significant amount, and ten dirhams is the nisab for theft, which is a recognized benchmark. Second, if the confessor says 'I usurped great camels' (ibil 'azimah), etc., then specific numbers are binding: for camels, 25; for cows, 30; for sheep, 40. These numbers correspond to the nisab of zakat for each animal type (25 camels, 30 cows, 40 sheep). The reasoning is that 'great' is defined by the zakat nisab for that species.

Key Terms
نفيس (nafis): Precious; a descriptor implying significant value, quantified as at least 10 dirhams by Abu Hanifa.
غصب (ghasb): Usurpation; wrongful appropriation of another's property, distinct from theft.
إبل عظيمة (ibil 'azimah): Great camels; the term 'great' is quantified by the zakat nisab of 25.
خطير (khatir): Serious or important property; same ruling as nafis: 10 dirhams.

Liability of the Second Zakat Payer in a Partnership Dispute
Resolves liability when two partners each pay zakat on the same wealth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on liability when two partners pay zakat successively, and apply the concept of 'azl hukmi to determine compensation.

Core Idea
If two partners pay zakat successively, the second is liable even if unaware—according to Abu Hanifa.

Zakat is a sacred obligation, and when partners authorize each other to pay, the risk of double payment arises. The Hanafi school debates whether the second payer must compensate the principal if the first already discharged the duty. This lesson unpacks the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, focusing on the agent’s liability and the doctrine of legal dismissal (ʿazl ḥukmī).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling: the second payer is liable whether he knew of the first payment or not. This is because his payment did not count as zakat, so he acted contrary to the permission.
The key principle: the agent's action must achieve the principal's goal (discharging the obligation).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that the second payer is not liable if he was unaware of the first payment. They argue that he fulfilled his order to transfer ownership.
They analogize to the case of slaughtering a sacrifice after the need is gone.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Sahibayn rely on the analogy: an agent ordered to slaughter a sacrifice for ihsar does so after the confinement is lifted; he is not liable because he did what was in his capacity. Similarly, the payer transfers ownership, which is within his power.
They emphasize that the agent's duty is the act, not ensuring it counts as zakat.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: the principal's purpose is to discharge his obligation, and once the first payment achieved that, the second payment is void. The agent is legally dismissed (ʿazl ḥukmī) because his action no longer serves the goal, regardless of his knowledge.
This is a 'legal dismissal'—automatic, not dependent on awareness.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if two partners pay successively, the second must compensate the principal according to Abu Hanifa, unless they paid simultaneously (where each compensates the other's portion). The Sahibayn exempt the unaware second payer.
The text does not specify a mufta bihi; both positions are transmitted in the school.

Commentary
This passage discusses a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding a scenario where two partners each pay zakat on the same wealth successively. The Sahibayn say he is not liable if he did not know. If they pay simultaneously, each is liable for the other's portion. The text then extends the same ruling to a similar case: a principal ordered an agent to pay zakat, and after the principal paid himself, the agent gave charity. The Sahibayn argue the agent is not liable because he fulfilled the order (transfer ownership), even though it didn't count as zakat. Abu Hanifa argues the agent is liable because his payment did not achieve the goal of discharging the principal's obligation, so he contradicted his orders. The reasoning is based on the concept of 'azl hukmi (legal dismissal).

Key Terms

زكاة (zakah): Obligatory alms; a fixed portion of wealth given to the poor.
تمليك (tamlik): Transfer of ownership; the essential element of zakat payment.
