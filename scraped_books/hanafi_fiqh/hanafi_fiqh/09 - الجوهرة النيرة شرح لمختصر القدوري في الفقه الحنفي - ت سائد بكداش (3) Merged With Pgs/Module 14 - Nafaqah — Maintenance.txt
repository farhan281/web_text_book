Maintenance Priority for the Bankrupt and His Dependents
Ruling on maintenance priority over creditors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance priority for a bankrupt and his dependents, and identify which dependents are entitled to such priority.

Core Idea
Essential need of the bankrupt and his family trumps creditor claims.

This passage from al-Quduri establishes a critical priority rule in bankruptcy: before creditors can collect, the bankrupt and his essential dependents must be provided for. Understanding this hierarchy is key to applying Hanafi nafaqah law in insolvency cases.

Guided Path
1
Extract the core ruling
Identify the main legal statement: the bankrupt's own maintenance and that of his dependents must be taken from his assets first, before paying creditors.
The phrase 'يُنفق على المفلس من ماله' directly states this priority.
2
Identify the beneficiaries
List the categories of dependents entitled to this priority: the bankrupt himself, his wife, his minor children, and his mahram relatives (those with whom marriage is permanently prohibited).
Mahram relatives include mother, sister, daughter, but not cousins or other non-mahram.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning
Understand why maintenance takes precedence: the essential need (al-hājah al-aṣliyyah) of the bankrupt and his dependents outweighs the rights of creditors, analogous to the bankrupt's own necessity.
This is a principle of human dignity and survival over financial claims.
4
Determine the limitation on relatives
Note that only mahram relatives are included, not all blood relatives. Non-mahram relatives like paternal cousins are excluded because their maintenance is not obligatory on the bankrupt in normal circumstances.
The commentary explicitly restricts 'ذوي أرحامه' to 'ذوي الرَّحِم المحرم'.
5
Consider the practical effect
Apply the ruling: in a bankruptcy case, the judge will first allocate sufficient funds for the bankrupt and his dependents' basic needs over a reasonable period, and only the remainder goes to creditors.
If assets are insufficient, creditors receive nothing after maintenance is secured.

Commentary
This passage establishes that the bankrupt's own maintenance and that of his dependents are given priority over the claims of creditors. The judge is to allocate from the bankrupt's assets sufficient funds to cover basic needs. The commentator clarifies that 'relatives' (dhawī arhāmihi) refers specifically to mahram relatives (those with whom marriage is permanently prohibited, such as mother, sister, daughter), because their maintenance is obligatory upon the bankrupt in normal circumstances. The reasoning is that their primary need (al-hājah al-aṣliyyah) is deemed superior to the creditors' rights, analogous to the bankrupt's own maintenance. This ensures that the bankrupt and his essential family do not become destitute while creditors are paid.

Key Terms
ذوي أرحامه (dhawī arḥāmihi): Relatives of prohibited degrees (mahram), for whom maintenance is obligatory.
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Maintenance covering food, clothing, shelter, and basic necessaries.

Imprisonment for Wife's Maintenance
Ruling that a man is imprisoned for failing to pay his wife's maintenance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on imprisoning a husband for non-payment of his wife's maintenance and distinguish between the underlying obligation (nafaqah) and the enforcement mechanism (imprisonment).

Core Idea
A husband who can pay but refuses may be imprisoned—nafaqah is a debt with teeth.

This brief passage from a Hanafi legal text establishes that a man can be imprisoned for failing to provide his wife's maintenance (nafaqah). At first glance, it seems straightforward, but it raises important questions: Is nafaqah a debt? What conditions must be met for imprisonment? In this lesson, we will unpack this ruling and understand its place in Hanafi family law.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the legal consequence stated in the passage—what happens to the husband?
The passage says: 'A man is imprisoned for the maintenance of his wife.'
2
Define the underlying obligation
What is nafaqah? Who owes it and under what circumstances?
Nafaqah is the financial support a husband must provide; it is wajib upon marriage.
3
Identify the legal nature of imprisonment
Is it a penalty, a debt collection tool, or both?
Imprisonment is coercive (to enforce payment), not punitive.
4
Determine conditions for imprisonment
What must be true about the husband for imprisonment to apply?
The husband must have the means to pay but refuse; an indigent husband is not imprisoned.
5
Note cross-madhhab perspective
Is there any disagreement among madhahib on this ruling?
The principle is agreed upon, but procedures (e.g., need for court order) differ.

Commentary
This passage is a brief ruling statement that a husband can be imprisoned for failing to provide maintenance (nafaqah) to his wife. This is part of the chapter on imprisonment for debt (hajr). The rule is that just as a debtor is imprisoned for financial obligations, a husband who neglects his wife's maintenance is also subject to imprisonment. This is because nafaqah is a financial obligation (dayn) akin to a debt. The imprisonment serves as a means to pressure him to fulfill his duty. No further details are given in this snippet, but it establishes the principle.

Key Terms
نفقة زوجته (nafaqah zawjathi): The financial support a husband must provide to his wife, including food, clothing, and shelter.

Agent Spending Own Money: Istihsan Overrides Qiyas
Ruling on agent reimbursement by istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when an agent spends his own money on the principal's family and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas in this case.

Core Idea
An agent who spends his own money on the principal's family is entitled to reimbursement by istihsan, not a volunteer.

This passage tackles a practical situation: a person gives money to an agent to spend on his family, but the agent uses his own funds instead. What happens to the original money? The text explains that by default analogy (qiyas) the agent would be a volunteer, but Hanafi juristic preference (istihsan) treats him as entitled to reimbursement, likening his role to an agent for purchase. Understanding this ruling clarifies how Hanafi fiqh balances strict analogy with practical needs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
The principal gives 10 dirhams to an agent to spend on his family. The agent spends 10 dirhams from his own pocket instead. What is the ruling on the original 10 dirhams?
The agent did not use the given money; he used his own.
2
State the qiyas ruling
By strict analogy (qiyas), the agent is considered a volunteer (mutabarri‘). He has no right to the given money; it remains the principal's property.
Qiyas is the default method in Hanafi usul.
3
State the istihsan ruling
By juristic preference (istihsan), the agent is entitled to take the given money as reimbursement. The text says: 'the ten are against the ten' meaning the given money compensates his own spending.
Istihsan overrides qiyas here for practical ease and custom.
4
Understand the reasoning
The agent for spending is also an agent for purchasing; when he spends his own money, it is as if he bought provisions with the principal's money and then the principal's money becomes payment to the agent.
This avoids hardship and encourages agents to act flexibly.
5
Apply the analogous case
The text cites Al-Karkhi: if an agent is given money to pay a debt and pays from his own, he can then collect the given money, just like a purchase agent who buys with his own money and takes compensation.
The same principle applies to debt payment and spending on family.

Commentary
This passage discusses a case where a person gives money to an agent to spend on his (the principal's) family. If the agent instead spends his own money on the family, the original money is considered as reimbursement. The explanation: the agent for spending is also an agent for purchasing (i.e., buying provisions), so when he spends his own money, it is as if he purchased with the principal's money, and then the principal's money becomes payment to the agent. This ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference). By qiyas (analogy), the agent would be a volunteer (tabarru‘) making him a donor, but istihsan overrides that. A similar ruling is cited from Al-Karkhi: if an agent is given money to pay a debt and he pays from his own money and then collects the given money, it is valid, analogous to an agent appointed to purchase who buys with his own money and then takes the purchase money.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsān): Juristic preference that permits departing from qiyas for a stronger reason.
قياس (qiyās): Analogical reasoning used as a default method.
