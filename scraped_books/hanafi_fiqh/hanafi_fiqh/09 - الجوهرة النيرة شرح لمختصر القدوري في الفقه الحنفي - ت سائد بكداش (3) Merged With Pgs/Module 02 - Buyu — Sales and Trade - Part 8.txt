Legal Devices (Hiyal) to Avoid Istibra': Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute on legal devices

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi positions on legal devices (hiyal) to avoid istibra' and apply the distinction between when Abu Yusuf's view is taken and when Muhammad's view is taken.

Core Idea
Legal devices to avoid istibra' are permitted by Abu Yusuf but disliked by Muhammad; the mufta bihi depends on whether the seller approached the slave-girl.

This lesson addresses the controversial topic of using legal devices (hiyal) to circumvent the requirement of istibra'. Abu Yusuf permits such devices, while Muhammad considers them disliked. The text provides a nuanced ruling that depends on whether the seller had intercourse with the slave-girl during her pure period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Abu Yusuf allows hiyal to drop istibra'; Muhammad dislikes them.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference.
2
Understand the accepted view
The accepted view is: if the seller did not approach her in that tuhr, Abu Yusuf's view is taken (allowed); if he did approach her, Muhammad's view is taken (disliked).
The distinction is based on the likelihood of pregnancy.
3
Examine the first device
If the buyer has no free wife: he may marry the slave-girl before purchase, consummate, then buy her.
After consummation, she is his wife, so no istibra' needed upon purchase.
4
Examine the second device
If the buyer has a free wife: the seller may marry her to a trustworthy person before sale, or the buyer does so before taking possession, then buys her, then the husband divorces.
At the time of ownership, she is married, so no istibra'.
5
Apply the principle
Istibra' is not required if, at the time of acquiring ownership (with possession), her private part was not lawful for the buyer.
The device ensures that she is already married at the critical moment.

Chapter of Istibra'. If (a slave-girl) returns to the seller after suckling (i.e., the child she nursed returns?), then istibra' is required upon him, such is this. If one buys a slave-girl who is of menstruating age but does not menstruate, then according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, he may not have intercourse with her until a period passes during which if she were pregnant the pregnancy would have become apparent, which is three months and more; because if a pregnant woman passes this time, the signs of her pregnancy appear by swelling of her belly or by the flow of her milk, and if this time passes and no pregnancy becomes apparent, then the apparent state is that she is not pregnant, so it becomes as if he performed istibra' with one menstruation. Muhammad said: He may not have intercourse with her until two months and five days pass. Zufar said: Until two years pass. If one buys a slave-girl who has a husband, takes possession of her, and her husband divorces her before consummation, then there is no istibra' upon him. If the slave-girl menstruates while with the buyer, then he finds a defect in her and returns her to the seller, the seller may not approach her until she menstruates once, whether the return is by court order or by consent; because the return due to defect is legally considered a second sale, like rescission (iqālah), and if he rescinds, it is not permissible for him to have intercourse with her until he performs istibra'. Likewise this. There is no harm in using a legal device (ḥīla) to drop istibra' according to Abu Yusuf. Muhammad said: It is disliked (makrūh). The accepted view is the statement of Abu Yusuf when he knows that the seller did not approach her in that ṭuhr (pure period), and the statement of Muhammad when he did approach her. The device when the buyer has no free wife is that he marries her before purchase and then buys her. Imam Ẓahīr al-Dīn said: He marries her and consummates, then buys her; if he buys her before consummation, then no. If he has a free wife, then the device is that the seller marries her before the purchase, or the buyer marries her before taking possession to a trustworthy person, then buys her and takes possession, then the husband divorces; because when the cause exists, which is acquisition of ownership confirmed by possession, if her private part was not lawful for him, istibra' is not required even if it becomes lawful afterwards; because consideration is given to the time of the cause, which is acquisition of ownership. Thus in Al-Hidāyah. In Al-Khujandī: The device in that is that the seller marries her before the sale to a man who sells her and delivers her to the buyer, then the husband divorces her before consummation, so she becomes lawful for the buyer without istibra'. And if the husband divorces her before possession, then the buyer takes possession, she is not lawful for him until he performs istibra'.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah discusses the rulings on istibra' (a waiting period to ensure a purchased slave-girl is not pregnant). The commentator expands on the matn of Al-Qudūrī. The first ruling states that if a slave-girl returns to the seller after having been suckled (or after the child she nursed returns? text unclear), istibra' is required. Then a major dispute among Hanafi imams: if a buyer purchases a menstruating-age slave-girl who does not menstruate, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf require a three-month waiting period (similar to the ʿiddah of a divorced woman), reasoning that pregnancy signs appear within that time. Muhammad requires two months and five days (approximately the minimum period of pregnancy becoming apparent). Zufar requires two full years, likely based on the maximum possible gestation. The author follows the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Next, a ruling on buying a married slave-girl: if the husband divorces her before consummation, no istibra' is needed because she was not sexually available to the buyer initially. Then, if a slave-girl is returned due to a defect after menstruating with the buyer, the seller must wait for one menstruation before intercourse, as the return is like a new sale. Finally, the passage discusses legal devices (ḥiyal) to avoid istibra'. Abu Yusuf permits them; Muhammad dislikes them. The accepted view distinguishes: if the seller did not approach her in that ṭuhr, Abu Yusuf's view is taken; if he did approach her, Muhammad's view applies. Two devices are given: if the buyer has no free wife, he may marry the slave-girl before purchase and consummate, then buy her. If he has a free wife, the seller marries her to a trustworthy person before sale, or the buyer does so before taking possession, then buys her and the husband divorces. The key principle: istibra' is required at the time of acquiring ownership (with possession). If at that time her private part is not lawful (i.e., she is already sexually prohibited to the buyer due to marriage), then istibra' is not required even if she becomes lawful later.

Key Terms
الحيلة (al-ḥīla): A legal device to avoid a ruling by means of a technically permissible action.

The Permissibility of Salam and Its Subject Matter
Definition and permissibility of salam with conditions on subject matter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of salam, list the three categories of goods permissible for salam, and explain why gold and silver are excluded.

Core Idea
Salam is allowed only for homogeneous commodities, not for currencies like gold and silver.

In this passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, we learn that salam (advance payment sale) is a special contract where the price is paid immediately but the goods are delivered later. The author outlines which types of goods can be the subject matter of salam: those measured by volume, by weight (excluding gold and silver), and homogeneous countable items. Understanding these categories is essential to ensuring the validity of salam contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Recognize that salam is a sale where immediate payment is made for future delivery of a commodity. It is distinguished from sarf (currency exchange) where both payments are immediate.
Salam is a form of bai' (sale) with deferred delivery.
2
List the permissible goods
From the text, extract the three categories: makilat (volume-measured), mawzunat (weighed items excluding gold/silver), and ma'dudat that do not vary (homogeneous countable items).
Examples: grains, oil, walnuts, eggs.
3
Note the exclusion of gold and silver
The commentary clarifies that mawzunat excludes gold and silver coins because they are considered prices (thaman), not commodities (muthman). The muslam fih must be a commodity.
This prevents salam from being used for currency speculation.
4
Understand the rationale for excluding gold/silver
Gold and silver serve as standard currencies; allowing salam for them would lead to ambiguity and potential exploitation, as their value is not precisely determinable at delivery.
Hanafi fiqh strictly separates thaman and muthman.

Commentary
This opening passage of the chapter on Salam (advance payment sale) sets forth the definition and conditions of permissibility. The commentator first explains why the author placed salam after previous sales: previous chapters discussed sales where neither receipt nor delivery is required on the spot; salam requires immediate receipt of the price but deferred delivery of the commodity; sarf (currency exchange) requires both. Salam is presented before sarf as progression from lesser to greater. The commentary adds a critical clarification: mawzunat here excludes gold and silver coins (naqd), because those are considered prices (thaman) and cannot be the subject matter of salam; the muslam fih (the item to be delivered) must always be a commodity (muthman).

Key Terms
السَّلَم (al-salam): A sale where the price is paid immediately in the session, and the commodity is delivered later on a specified date. Also called bai' al-salam or bai' al-salaf.
المكيلات (al-makilat): Goods that are customarily sold by volume measure (kayl), such as grains and dates.
الموزونات (al-mawzunat): Goods customarily sold by weight (wazn), excluding gold and silver coins (which are currencies).
المعدودات التي لا تتفاوت (al-ma'dudat allati la tatafawat): Countable items that do not vary significantly between individual units, so they can be precisely described by count (e.g., walnuts, eggs).
المثمن (al-muthman): A commodity that is not used as a standard currency; the subject matter of a sale that is not a price (thaman). In salam, the muslam fih must be a muthman.

Rulings of Permissible Subject Matter in Salam Contracts
Commentary expanding on permissible items for salam contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of items permissible for salam and identify which items are excluded due to uncertainty, applying the Hanafi principle of custom (urf).

Core Idea
Salam is valid only in precisely describable, non-varying items; animals and highly variable items are forbidden.

This lesson unpacks the conditions for valid salam sales, focusing on which goods qualify. Salam requires immediate payment and deferred delivery, so the goods must be precisely specified to avoid gharar (uncertainty). The commentator expands on the matn, listing four permissible categories and addressing a key intra-Hanafi dispute over walnuts and eggs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type and its requirement
Salam (also called salaf) is a sale where the price is paid immediately in the session and the goods are delivered later. This distinguishes it from sarf (money exchange) where both countervalues must be exchanged immediately.
The author first situates salam among sales before listing categories.
2
List the four permissible categories
Salam is valid in: (1) makilat – things sold by volume (e.g., wheat, barley); (2) mawzunat – things sold by weight, excluding gold and silver which are prices (e.g., iron, saffron); (3) madhru'at – things measured by length, like cloth; and (4) ma'dudat that do not vary greatly – countable items where custom ignores minor variation (e.g., walnuts, eggs).
Mawzunat excludes prices (thaman) because the subject matter must be a commodity (muthman).
3
Distinguish between ma'dudat that do and do not vary
Walnuts and eggs are permissible because people do not price each unit separately; the small and large are treated equally by custom. Watermelon, cucumber, and pomegranate are prohibited because individual units vary significantly, leading to uncertainty.
The text uses the analogy: 'It is not said: this egg is for such-and-such and this is for such-and-such.'
4
Note the prohibition on animals and their limbs
Salam is not valid in animals (e.g., heads, trotters) due to extreme variation in fatness, age, type, and size, which cannot be precisely described.
This is a categorical ban based on unavoidable gharar.
5
Explore the intra-Hanafi dispute
Zufar forbids salam in walnuts and eggs, arguing they can vary. A weak narration from Abu Hanifa forbids ostrich eggs. The majority (mufta bihi) permits walnuts and eggs based on custom that disregards minor variation.
The commentator affirms the majority view, and this is the operative position for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the chapter of salam from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It begins by situating salam among sales: there are two special contracts requiring immediate payment (salam and sarf). Salam requires payment of the price in the session, while delivery of the goods is delayed. The commentator then explains the matn: salam is valid in three categories: things measured by volume (makilat), by weight (mawzunat), and countable items that do not vary significantly (ma'dudat) — like walnuts and eggs. The key condition is that the subject matter must be capable of precise description to avoid uncertainty (gharar). The commentator clarifies that 'mawzunat' excludes gold and silver because they are prices, not commodities. He then adds items measured by length (madhru'at, e.g., cloth). Salam is prohibited in animals and their parts due to extreme variation (e.g., fatness, age). The text notes an intra-Hanafi difference: Zufar forbids salam in walnuts and eggs, but the majority view (and the mufta bihi) allows it based on custom that ignores minor variation. Ostrich eggs are an exception (per narration from Abu Hanifa) because of significant size variation. The commentary emphasizes the need to specify all relevant descriptions (length, width, weight) for validity.

Key Terms
السلم (as-salam): A sale where the price is paid immediately in the session, and the goods are delivered later; also called salaf.
المكيلات (al-makīlāt): Items sold by volume measure, such as grains (wheat, barley, rice).
الموزونات (al-mawzūnāt): Items sold by weight, excluding gold and silver (which are prices).
المعدودات التي لا تتفاوت (al-ma'dūdāt allatī lā tatafāwatu): Countable items where individual variation is negligible by custom, e.g., walnuts, eggs.
التفاوت (at-tafāwut): Significant variation between individual units of the same type, which invalidates salam due to uncertainty.

Salam in Animals, Countables, and Specific Commodities
Commentary on salam restrictions for various goods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which goods are valid for salam according to the Hanafi school and explain the rationale based on certainty (gharar) and custom (urf).

Core Idea
Salam requires certainty: animals are too variable, but eggs are fine because custom ignores their minor differences.

The salam contract allows forward sale with immediate payment, but only if the goods are precisely defined. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains which goods qualify: animals and their parts are out due to hidden variation, while countable items like eggs and walnuts are in because people treat them as equal by custom. Cloth, skins, and firewood have their own conditions. Let's explore the reasoning behind these rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle for valid salam
Identify that the subject matter must be known (ma'lum) to avoid gharar. The Hanafi school uses this as the core criterion.
This principle underlies all the specific rulings in the passage.
2
Categorize goods into valid and invalid
From the text, list which goods are valid (mikyal, mawzun, non-varying countables like eggs/walnuts) and which are invalid (animals, parts, skins by number, firewood by bundles, fresh fodder by handfuls).
Note that cloth (madhru'at) is valid if described properly.
3
Explain the rationale for each invalid category
For animals: hidden variation in fatness, age, speed, etc. For skins: cannot be controlled by description, varying sizes. For bundles/handfuls: imprecise and unknown.
All invalid due to inability to achieve certainty.
4
Explain how certainty is achieved for valid categories
For mikyal/mawzun: weight or capacity is known. For countables: custom disregards minor variation. For cloth: description of length, width, weight if applicable.
Each valid good has a method to eliminate gharar.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on eggs and walnuts
Imam Zufar disallows salam in eggs and walnuts, while the school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) allows it based on custom. Abu Hanifa reportedly disallows in ostrich eggs due to greater variation.
The mufta bihi is the position allowing it.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the rulings of salam (forward sale with immediate payment) various types of goods. The author explains that salam is not allowed in animals or their parts (heads, trotters) due to significant variation in intrinsic qualities (fatness, age, speed, etc.) that cannot be captured by description. This prohibition applies to humans as well—slaves of same age and description can differ drastically in intellect and character. However, salam is allowed in goods measured by capacity or weight if the quantity is known, and in countable items that do not vary (like eggs and walnuts) according to the Hanafi school, because people customarily disregard minor size differences. Imam Zufar disagrees, disallowing salam in eggs and walnuts, while Abu Hanifa reportedly disallows it in ostrich eggs due to their variation. For cloth (madhru'at), description of length, width, and measure is required; if sold by weight like silk, weight must be specified. For skins sold by number, salam is invalid unless the skin is described for a known purpose like a mus-haf. Similarly, paper requires specification of type, length, width, and quality. Firewood sold in bundles is invalid unless the bundle size is defined by the rope length. Fresh fodder sold by handfuls is invalid because it is imprecise. All these rulings are based on the principle that salam requires certainty of the subject matter to avoid gharar (uncertainty).

Salam on Variable Items: Leather, Firewood, and Fodder
Ruling and commentary on salam in non-standard items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on salam in leather by count, firewood by bundle, and fresh fodder by handful, and identify the conditions that make such salam valid.

Core Idea
Salam requires precise specification; items sold by variable count or measure are invalid unless fully described.

Salam is a forward sale where the price is paid upfront. For it to be valid, the subject matter must be precisely known. This lesson examines three items that are typically sold in ways that introduce uncertainty—leather by count, firewood by bundle, and fresh fodder by handful. We will see why these are invalid and what specifications can salvage the contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for salam subject matter
Salam requires the commodity to be precisely specifiable by description (wazn, kayl, or exact dimensions). If sold by count of variable items, it is invalid unless each piece is individually described.
The core condition is that the subject must be known with certainty to avoid gharar.
2
Examine the ruling for leathers
Leathers sold by count are invalid because they vary in size and quality. However, if the buyer specifies a known piece suitable for a mushaf and states its length, width, and quality, it becomes valid.
The exception requires three specifications: length, width, and quality.
3
Examine the ruling for firewood
Firewood sold by bundles (huzam) is invalid because the bundle size is unknown. But if the length of the tying rope is specified (e.g., one or two cubits), the bundle becomes standardized and salam is allowed.
The rope length is the key measure to remove uncertainty.
4
Examine the ruling for fresh fodder
Fresh fodder sold by handfuls (jurzan) is invalid because a handful is not a standard measure and varies. The text does not mention an exception, implying it remains invalid.
The commentator clarifies the lexical meaning but no remedy is provided.
5
Apply the principle to paper
Similarly, salam in paper is invalid unless the type, length, width, and quality are specified. This shows the same principle extends to other variable items.
Paper is analogous to leather: needs exact dimensions and quality.

Condition of existence of salam commodity from contract to delivery
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute on existence requirement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school requires continuous market availability of the salam commodity from contract to delivery, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Salam requires continuous market availability — ability to deliver depends on ability to acquire at any moment.

This passage addresses a key condition in salam contracts: the subject matter must be available in the market from the time of the contract until delivery. The Hanafi school argues that this is necessary because delivery is contingent on acquisition, and the seller might die at any moment, making immediate delivery necessary. The Shafi'i school holds that availability at delivery time is sufficient. We will examine the proofs and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the salam commodity must be present in the market throughout the entire period from contract to delivery.
This is a condition for validity (shart al-sihha).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Shafi'i school holds that it is sufficient if the commodity is available at the time of delivery only.
They argue that ability to deliver is required only when the obligation arises.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Shafi'i's proof: the obligation to deliver arises at the delivery date, so that is the relevant time for checking ability.
This is based on the principle that obligations are only binding at their due time.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafi responds with two arguments: (1) ability to deliver is through acquisition (tahsil), which requires time; (2) the seller's death could make any moment a delivery date, so continuous existence is needed.
The first argument is rational, the second is a precaution against uncertainty.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if the commodity is unavailable at contract but available at delivery, Hanafi deems the contract invalid (fasid), while Shafi'i upholds it.
This illustrates the impact of the differing conditions on real transactions.

The Condition of Continuous Existence in Salam: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Ruling with proof and Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on continuous existence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on continuous existence of the subject matter in salam, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and apply the thamarah al-khilaf to a real scenario.

Core Idea
Salam requires the subject matter to exist from contract to delivery — mere availability at the due date is insufficient under Hanafi law.

In a salam contract, the buyer pays upfront for goods to be delivered later. But what if the goods disappear from the market before the due date? The Hanafi school insists the subject matter must exist continuously from the time of contract to the time of delivery. The Shafi'i school disagrees, holding that availability at the due date is enough. This lesson unpacks the proofs on both sides and explains why this difference matters in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling
Read the opening sentence of the matn: 'Salam is not valid unless the subject matter exists from the time of the contract until the time of delivery.' Note that this is the Hanafi position.
The definition of 'existence' (wujud) is being found in the market, not merely in private homes.
2
Understand the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i holds that existence at the time of delivery is sufficient because the obligation to deliver arises only then.
This relies on the principle that ability to perform is required at the time of performance.
3
Follow the Hanafi proof (dalil aqli)
The Hanafi response: ability to deliver is through acquisition (tahsil), which requires the item to be continuously available so the seller can acquire it at any time before delivery. Also, any moment in the term (e.g., the seller's death) could become the actual delivery date.

The Seven Conditions of Salam Sale
Explaining the seven conditions of salam with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven conditions of salam required by Abū Ḥanīfa and explain why each condition is necessary to avoid gharar.

Core Idea
Salam is valid only when seven specific conditions remove uncertainty in every essential element.

The chapter on salam covers a transaction where the buyer pays now for future delivery. To prevent prohibited uncertainty (gharar), Abū Ḥanīfa prescribed seven conditions that must be explicitly stated in the contract. This lesson unpacks each condition and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Salam is a sale of a deferred commodity for an immediate price (raʾs al-māl). Understand its unique nature as an exception to the prohibition of selling what one does not possess.
Salam is permitted by the Qurʾān (2:282) and Sunnah.
2
List the seven conditions
Recite the seven conditions in order: known genus, type, quality, quantity, term, capital amount (if measured/weighed/counted), and place of delivery (if transport cost exists).
These are all required by Abū Ḥanīfa; the Ṣāḥibayn differ on the last two.
3
Understand each condition's purpose
For each condition, ask: what uncertainty does it remove? For example, specifying genus prevents confusion about the subject matter; specifying the term prevents dispute about delivery time.
Conditions 1–5 are agreed upon; 6–7 are disputed.
4
Note the additional condition
The capital (raʾs al-māl) must be handed over before the parties separate. This is a unanimous requirement to prevent the contract from becoming a sale of debt for debt.
This is stated separately in the text: 'ولا يصح السلم حتى يقبض رأس المال قبل أن يفارقه'.

Intra-Hanafī Dispute on Salam Conditions: Capital Quantity and Delivery Place
Detailed exposition of the intra-Hanafi dispute on two salam conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on conditions 6 and 7 of salam, identify their proofs, and apply the rulings to practical scenarios.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires precise quantification of capital and specification of delivery place; the Sahibayn deem pointing sufficient for capital and delivery at contract location.

While the first five conditions of salam are uncontested, Abū Ḥanīfa and his two main disciples disagreed on whether the capital’s quantity must be known when it is a specific item, and whether the place of delivery must be named if transport costs exist. This lesson explores the reasoning behind each view and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the disputed issues
Identify the two conditions that are contested: (1) knowledge of capital amount when capital is a specific identified item, and (2) specification of delivery place when transport cost exists.
These are conditions 6 and 7 of the seven conditions.
2
Present Abu Hanifa’s position and proof
Abu Hanifa requires both conditions to eliminate uncertainty (gharar). His proof: ignorance of capital amount leads to ignorance of what the seller receives; delivery place uncertainty leads to disputes over location.
He uses rational argument (dalīl aqlī) based on avoiding gharar.
3
Present the Sahibayn’s position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that if the capital is a specific identifiable item (e.g., a particular cloth), pointing suffices, analogous to price in a normal sale. For place, they say delivery is at the contract location because possession occurred there.
They use qiyās to the price in ordinary sales and to the principle that possession follows location.
4
Analyse Abu Hanifa’s response
Abu Hanifa refutes the analogy: in salam, the capital is the subject of the contract and must be quantified; a garment’s quantity is not what is contracted upon, so the analogy fails. For place, transport cost creates the need for specification.
The response distinguishes between cases where quantity matters and where it does not.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamara)
Identify how the disagreement affects real contracts. For example, if capital is a specific camel, Abu Hanifa requires stating its age/weight; Sahibayn accept the camel as is. For delivery, if transport is costly, Abu Hanifa requires naming the city; Sahibayn accept the contract location.
The muftā bihī is Abu Hanifa’s view.

Specifying the Place of Delivery in Salam: Dispute Between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on place of delivery in salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on specifying the place of delivery in salam, identify the disagreement of the Sahibayn, and apply the distinction based on transport cost.

Core Idea
In salam, the place of delivery is a condition only for goods with transport cost according to Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn disagree.

The commentator Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains a point from Al-Quduri's matn about specifying the place of delivery in a salam contract. Abu Hanifa requires it only when the goods have carriage expenses (haml wa mu'nah), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider it unnecessary. This lesson clarifies the intra-Hanafi dispute and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Recognize that the passage discusses whether specifying the place of delivery is a condition (shart) for the validity of a salam contract. The matn states the ruling, and the commentary explains the dispute.
Focus on the phrase 'إذا كان له حَمْلٌ ومُؤنة' as the key to the distinction.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa requires specification of the place only if the goods have transport cost and effort. If not specified, the contract may be invalid or deficient. For goods without such cost, delivery can be wherever the seller meets the buyer.
For light goods like musk, 'يسلّمه إليه حيث لقيه'.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that specifying the place is not a condition at all. The seller must deliver at the place where the contract was made, regardless of transport cost.
They say 'ليس ذلك بشرط'.
4
Analyze the distinction based on transport cost
For goods with transport cost, Abu Hanifa conditions specification; the Sahibayn do not. For goods without transport cost, Abu Hanifa allows delivery wherever met; the Sahibayn still require delivery at contract place.
This shows a consistent difference in their approach to default delivery location.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if a salam contract for heavy goods lacks a specified place, Abu Hanifa would deem the contract defective (fasid) or incomplete, while the Sahibayn would consider it valid with delivery at the contract location. For light goods, Abu Hanifa gives flexibility; the Sahibayn are stricter.
This affects enforcement and potential disputes.

Specification of Capital in Salam When Determinate
Statement of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on not needing to specify capital if determinate, with reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on specifying capital in salam when it is determinate, and explain the reasoning for not needing to name it.

Core Idea
If the capital is a specific item, naming its quantity is unnecessary.

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the conditions of a valid salam contract. While Abu Hanifa required the capital to be specified even if determinate, his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, held that if the capital is a determinate object, its identity alone suffices. We will explore their reasoning and the practical consequence for the place of delivery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The disputed condition: whether the quantity of the capital (ra's al-mal) must be named when the capital is a specific, determinate item (mu'ayyan).
This applies only when the capital is a concrete object, not unspecific currency.
2
State the two positions within the Hanafi school
Abu Hanifa: the quantity must be named even if the item is determinate. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: no need to name it.
Both positions are authoritative within the school.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad reason that a determinate item itself removes ambiguity; describing its quantity is redundant because the capital is identified by its physical identity.
The commentary adds: the buyer took possession (malak) in that place, so delivery is there.
4
Apply the rule to place of delivery
They also hold that the place of delivery need not be specified; it defaults to the place of contract, because possession of the capital was taken there.
This ensures correspondence between where the capital is given and where the deferred goods are delivered.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practically, if the capital is a specific car, under Abu Yusuf and Muhammad you need not state its value; the car itself defines the capital. Under Abu Hanifa, you must state its quantity (e.g., 'one car' suffices, but no extra description).
The difference is minimal but illustrates methodology.

Core Idea
In salam, the capital must be taken before separation; the session is preserved by clear water, sleep, or walking together.

The validity of a salam contract hinges on the seller taking possession of the price before the parties leave the meeting place. This lesson explains the basic condition and clarifies what counts as 'remaining in the session'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for validity
What is required in salam regarding the capital? The matn states: the capital must be taken before separation.
2
Understand the concept of 'session' (majlis)
The contract session is where the parties transact; certain actions keep them in the session, others break it.
The examples help define the boundary.
3
Apply to scenarios: water, sleep, fainting, walking
Determine which actions preserve the session. Clear water = no break; cloudy water = break. Sleep, fainting, and walking together = no break.
The rationale is whether the parties remain in the same place and can still complete the exchange.
4
Note the same ruling for sarf
The commentator states that the same rules apply to currency exchange (sarf).
Sarf also requires immediate exchange in the session.

Commentary
This lengthy passage explains the condition for validity of salam: the seller must take possession of the capital (price) before the parties leave the session (majlis). The commentator then examines various scenarios that arise if the parties are still in the session but something happens. Entering clear water does not end the session, so the contract is valid; entering cloudy water does end it if the water obstructs view? Invalidity of Khiyar al-Shart in Salam
Ruling with proof: the invalidation of khiyar al-shart in salam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why khiyar al-shart invalidates salam and state the ruling when it is dropped before separation.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-shart prevents complete qabd, so it invalidates salam; dropping it before separation saves the contract.

The option of condition (khiyar al-shart) gives a party the right to cancel the contract within a set period. However, in salam, such an option contradicts the requirement of immediate and complete taking of possession. This lesson explores the reasoning and an exception.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling: salam is invalid if khiyar al-shart is present.
The matn states 'ولا يصح السلم إذا كان فيه خيار الشرط'.
2
Identify the reason: because it prevents complete qabd.
The ta'lil (cause) is given: 'لأنه يمنع تمام القبض'.
Complete qabd is essential in salam.
3
Consider the exception: if the option is dropped before separation, the contract becomes valid.
Provided the capital is still extant (قائم).
Dropping the option removes the obstacle.
4
Note the disagreement with Zufar.
Zufar holds that even if dropped, the contract is not valid.
Zufar's view is that the initial invalidity cannot be cured.

Ruling on Counterfeit Capital in Salam After Parting
Ruling with proof on the consequences of counterfeit capital in salam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on discovering counterfeit capital after separation in salam, including the intra-Hanafi differences.

Core Idea
If the capital is found counterfeit, accepting it validates salam; exchanging it voids the contract under Abu Hanifa, unless the exchange is of a small amount.

After the parties have separated and the seller has taken the capital, if the capital turns out to be counterfeit, the salam may be affected depending on how the seller responds. This lesson details the views of Abu Hanifa and his two companions on partial and full counterfeit.

Guided Path

Distinguish the scenarios: full counterfeit vs partial.
The passage covers two cases: entire capital is counterfeit, or only part.
2
Abu Hanifa's view: any exchange of full counterfeit voids the contract.
If the seller exchanges it, the salam becomes invalid.
Accepting it (without exchange) validates.
3
Companions' view: exchange in the same session does not void.
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that if the exchange happens in the council of rejection (مجلس الرد), the contract remains valid.
The session is considered a continuation of the original majlis.
4
Partial counterfeit: thresholds matter.
Muhammad: less than half is 'little'; half or more invalidates that portion. Abu Yusuf's narration from Abu Hanifa: up to one-third does not invalidate.
The difference is in defining 'little'.
5
Consensus on different genus (tin/lead): contract void.
If the capital is of a completely different substance, the contract is void by consensus because the agreed genus was not delivered.

Salam in Various Commodities and the Dispute on Bread
Explaining permissibility of salam in various commodities and intra-Hanafi dispute on bread

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi condition for valid salam and explain why the mufta bihi allows salam in bread by weight despite Abu Hanifa's prohibition.

Core Idea
Salam is valid only for precisely describable items; bread is disputed but the fatwa allows it by weight.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the key condition for a valid salam contract: the commodity must be precisely describable and measurable. It then navigates an intra-Hanafi dispute over salam in bread, showing how later scholars preferred Abu Yusuf's view to meet public need. Understanding this dispute illustrates the Hanafi method of balancing textual evidence with practical necessity (istihsan).

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for valid salam
Find the sentence that states the principle: salam is permissible for any item whose description can be precisely defined and whose quantity can be known. Note this is the core condition to avoid gharar (uncertainty).
The rule is: 'كُلُّ مَا أَمْكَنَ ضَبْطُ صِفَتِهِ وَمَعْرِفَةُ مِقْدَارِهِ جَازَ السَّلَمُ فِيهِ'.
2
Examine the examples given
The text lists permissible items (milk, bricks, small pearls by weight) and impermissible ones (pomegranates, watermelons, etc.). For each, identify why it fits or fails the condition. This reinforces how the rule is applied in practice.
Bricks require specifying length, width, thickness; pearls are known by weight; fruits vary in size.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute on bread
Read the section where the author quotes al-Mabsut and al-Dhakhirah. Abu Hanifa prohibits salam in bread because bread varies in ripeness (doneness). Abu Yusuf allows it by weight. Understand both positions and their rationales.
The dispute is not about the general rule but whether bread can be standardized despite baking variation.
4
Analyze the tarjih (preference) and its basis
The mashayikh (later scholars) chose Abu Yusuf's view. The text cites 'al-Nihayah' and mentions 'need of the people'. This illustrates istihsan (juristic preference) overriding a strict analogy. The mufta bihi is thus permissibility by weight.
The conditions of salam must still be fulfilled: known weight, quality, delivery time.
5
Summarize the practical implication
Conclude that for a mufti today, salam in bread is valid if sold by weight with clear specifications. The initial ruling of Abu Hanifa is not followed due to later consensus on public interest.
This dispute is a classic example of how the school evolves through istihsan.

Conditions for Valid Salam Sale: Precision of Description and Quantity
Ruling on invalidity of salam for unspecified commodity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two essential conditions for a valid salam sale and explain why their absence renders the contract invalid.

Core Idea
A salam sale is invalid unless the commodity is precisely described and its quantity known — ignorance leads to voiding dispute.

This passage from the chapter on Salam (forward sale) establishes a fundamental principle of contract law: certainty in the subject matter. The Hanafi school holds that for a salam contract to be valid, the commodity must be specified in both description and quantity. Without these, the contract is void due to the risk of dispute. Let's examine the ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the legal ruling: salam is not permissible (la yajuz) for commodities that lack precise description or known quantity.
The ruling is absolute; it applies to any commodity that fails either condition.
2
List the required conditions
Extract the two conditions from the text: dabt al-sifah (precise description) and ma'rifat al-miqdar (knowledge of quantity).
These are separate conditions; both must be fulfilled.
3
Understand the rationale
The reason given is that ignorance (jahl) leads to dispute (munaza'ah). The Hanafi school aims to avoid uncertainty in contracts.
The text literally says 'does not lead to dispute', but the intended meaning is the opposite: uncertainty leads to dispute.
4
Recognize the legal consequence
If either condition is missing, the contract is invalid (batil). No legal effects arise from it.
This differs from a fasid (irregular) contract, which may have some effects.

In Hanafi fiqh, animals are permissible to sell if they provide lawful benefit; the elephant dispute hinges on whether it is like a predator or like a pig.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses miscellaneous sale rulings for animals. You may be surprised that selling dogs is permissible in the Hanafi school, unlike some others. The passage also reveals an internal Hanafi dispute on the elephant: is it akin to a pure predator or an impure pig? The answer determines whether its bones and skin can be traded.

Selling cats is unanimously permissible, providing a clear example of an animal with clear benefit (pest control).

Muhammad al-Shaybani treats the elephant as impure like a pig, while Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf treat it like a predator. This disagreement affects whether its bones and skin can be sold and used.
The difference stems from the elephant's essential impurity (najis al-ayn) versus its similarity to pure predatory animals.

Determine the mufta bihi

2

For Muhammad, the elephant's hide is not purified by tanning, bones are impure, so no sale. For Abu Hanifa/Yusuf, it is like a beast of prey, so hide can be tanned and bones sold.
This affects not only sale but also ritual purity.

The conflict is over the prevailing benefit.

If a monkey is sold for its hide, it would be permissible under Abu Hanifa; if sold as a pet, it might not be permissible under Abu Yusuf. There is no clear tarjih in the text.
