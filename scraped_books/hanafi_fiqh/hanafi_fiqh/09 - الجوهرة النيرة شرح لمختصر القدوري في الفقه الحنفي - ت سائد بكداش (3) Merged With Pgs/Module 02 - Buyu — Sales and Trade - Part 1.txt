Placement of Sales in Fiqh: Universal Need and Livelihood
Rationale for chapter placement in a fiqh work.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rationale for placing the chapter on sales after worship and before marriage in Hanafi fiqh works, and compare alternative arrangements.

Core Idea
Need for sales is universal and livelihood depends on it, justifying placement after worship but before marriage.

In organizing a fiqh text, the author of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains his choice to place the Book of Sales after acts of worship and before the chapter on marriage. This ordering reflects a deeper understanding of human needs: sales support subsistence, which is more immediate than the institution of marriage. Some scholars disagree, placing marriage first because it is a form of worship leading to tawhid. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's placement
The author explicitly states that he placed sales after worship and before marriage. Identify this in the Arabic text.
Look at the beginning: 'إنما عقب الشيخ البيع بالعبادات، وأخر النكاح'
2
Understand the reasoning from universality
The author argues that people's need for sales is more general (أعم) than for marriage because it encompasses everyone regardless of age or gender.
Connect this to the principle of 'عموم الحاجة' (universal need).
3
Recognize the reasoning from subsistence
Subsistence (البقاء) through sales is stronger than through marriage because livelihood supports the body's well-being.
The author says: 'والبقاء بالبيع أقوى من البقاء بالنكاح'.
4
Analyze the alternative arrangement
Some authors, like the author of al-Hidayah, prioritize marriage because it is an act of worship that leads to tawhid through offspring.
The author acknowledges both are valid: 'وكل منهم مصيب في مقصده'.

Definition of Bay' (Sale): Linguistic and Legal with Mutual Consent
Definition of a key concept in fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of bay' and explain why mutual consent is a necessary condition in the legal definition.

Core Idea
Bay' legally is the exchange of property with mutual consent to prevent coercion and corruption.

The passage gives a concise definition of sale (bay'): linguistically it is the exchange of property for another property, and legally it is the same but with the added condition of mutual consent. This condition is crucial because it prevents the corruption that arises from coercion. Understanding this definition is foundational for all transactions in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic definition
The author states that linguistically, bay' is 'تمليك مال بمال آخر' — transfer of ownership of one property for another property.
This is the basic verbal meaning.
2
Recognize the legal definition
Legally, it is the same, but with the addition of mutual consent (قيد التراضي).
The author says: 'وكذا في الشرع، لكن زيد فيه قيد التراضي'.
3
Understand the reason for adding mutual consent
Mutual consent is added because of the corruption that results from coercion (التغالب). The author cites the Quranic principle: 'والله لا يحب الفساد' (Allah does not love corruption).
Coercion invalidates contracts because it leads to injustice.

Definition of Sale and the Ruling on Ta'ati
Introduction to Kitab al-Buyu' with definitions and intra-Hanafi dispute on ta'ati

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of sale, distinguish between the Iraqi and Khurasani definitions, and apply the correct view on ta'ati for valuable versus cheap items.

Core Idea
The validity of ta'ati hinges on whether sale is defined by verbal formula or by consensual exchange.

This lesson introduces the Book of Sales from al-Jawharah al-Nayrah, beginning with the placement of sales after worship and before marriage due to their universal necessity. The core is the definition of sale (bay‘) and a key intra-Hanafi dispute about whether mutual delivery (ta‘ātī) suffices for valuable items. Understanding this definition reveals the school's reliance on mutual consent as the essence of the contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic and legal definitions
Notice that the author first gives a linguistic definition (exchange of property for property) and then adds the legal condition of mutual consent to prevent coercion.
This addition is crucial: it frames sale as a consensual act.
2
Distinguish the Iraqi and Khurasani definitions
The Iraqi definition emphasizes explicit offer and acceptance (ījāb wa qabūl) in two properties without donation. The Khurasani definition focuses on exchange of property not in the manner of donation, omitting the verbal formula requirement.
These are not contradictory; they focus on different aspects of the contract.
3
Understand the practical difference: ta‘ātī for valuable items
The real-world consequence of the definitional difference is whether a sale by mutual delivery (handing goods and money without words) is valid for valuable items. The Khurasani view validates it; the Iraqi view does not.
For cheap items (e.g., vegetables), there is consensus that ta‘ātī is valid.
4
Recognize the tarjīḥ and its basis
The author declares the Khurasani view as correct because 'the consideration is mutual consent' (al-‘ibrah li-l-tarāḍī). This is the relied-upon position.
This tarjīḥ is from later authorities, not the classical Zahir al-Riwayah.

Definition of Sale and Its Formation by Offer and Acceptance
Defines sale and discusses conditions for its formation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of sale and explain the conditions for its formation by offer and acceptance, including the intra-school dispute regarding mutual exchange (ta'ati).

Core Idea
Sale is an exchange of property without donation, concluded by offer and acceptance; mutual exchange suffices for low-value items by consensus, and for high-value items per the correct view.

The sale contract (bay') is the most common commercial transaction in Islamic law. This lesson presents its definition according to the Hanafi school and clarifies how it is formed through offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). A unique feature of Hanafi law is the recognition of mutual exchange (ta'ati) as valid without spoken words, which is the subject of a well-known intra-school debate between Iraqi and Khurasani scholars. Understanding this debate will equip you to apply the correct ruling in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of sale
Read the two definitions: the Iraqi definition (offer and acceptance regarding two properties without donation) and the Khurasani definition (exchange of property for property without donation). Note that both exclude donation (tabarru').
The difference is subtle but leads to a practical dispute about ta'ati.
2
Understand the legal effect on ta'ati
The Khurasani definition—focusing on the exchange element—allows sale to be concluded by mutual exchange (ta'ati) for all items. The Iraqi definition—emphasizing verbal expression—requires explicit ijab and qabul for valuable items.
For low-value items, both agree that ta'ati is valid by consensus.
3
Recognize the conditions for offer and acceptance
Sale is valid when both ijab and qabul are expressed in the past tense (e.g., 'I sold,' 'I bought'). If the imperative is used, a third utterance is needed to complete the contract.
This differs from marriage (nikah), where one utterance may be in the future tense.
4
Apply the rules to examples
Consider scenarios: buying bread by handing over money (ta'ati valid by consensus), buying a car by handing over money (ta'ati valid per correct view). Also, if the buyer says 'I bought this for 100' and the seller says 'I sold,' the sale is valid regardless of who spoke first.
Mutual consent ('ibrat li al-taradi) is the underlying basis.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Acceptance must occur in the same session; any disconnection voids the offer.

In Hanafi contract law, a sale is not concluded until the offeree accepts within the same meeting (majlis). This lesson explores the khiyar al-qabul—the offeree's right to accept or reject—and the precise rules that define when a session is considered continuous. This lesson focuses on the Hanafi ruling on deferred payment (thaman mu'ajjal). The school permits deferring the price but not the sold object. Distinguishing Mabi' and Thaman: Disposal and Destruction Before Possession
Comparing legal effects of mabi' and thaman

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mabi' is a specific object bound by possession; thaman is a debt in dhimma, free and resilient.

In sale contracts, the object (mabi') and price (thaman) appear symmetrical but carry distinct legal consequences. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira highlights two critical differences: the permissibility of disposal before possession, and the effect of destruction before delivery. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correctly applying Hanafi sale law and avoiding invalid transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal issues

The mabi' is a specific physical item; its loss makes performance impossible. The thaman is a fungible debt; the contract is on the dhimma, so the specific coins are not essential.
This distinction ensures sales are not voided by incidental loss of currency.

Selling a Pile of Food per Unit: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Uncertainty
Intra-Hanafi legal dispute with proofs and resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the sale of an unmeasured pile per unit, and apply the rule of khiyar al-tafarrur al-safqah.

Core Idea
Uncertainty that can be removed later does not necessarily invalidate a sale according to the Sahibayn.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: a person sells a pile of grain saying 'each qafiz for a dirham' without specifying the total number of qafizes. The issue is uncertainty (jahalah) about the quantity and total price. Abu Hanifa holds that only one qafiz is validly sold, while the Sahibayn hold the entire sale valid. The disagreement hinges on whether removable ignorance prevents the validity of a sale. We will explore the proofs and the practical consequence: the buyer's option (khiyar) when the sale is split.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
The sale involves a pile (subrah) of food sold at a fixed unit price (each qafiz for a dirham) without stating the total number of units. This creates uncertainty about the overall object and price.
The uncertainty is inherent at the time of contract because the exact quantity is unknown.
2
State the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa rules that only one qafiz is validly sold; the rest is invalid unless the total is specified or measured. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule that the entire sale is valid, whether or not the total is mentioned.
Both positions are within the Hanafi school; this is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze the proofs (dalil) for each
Abu Hanifa argues that applying the contract to the whole pile is impossible due to ignorance of the precise object and total price; thus it is confined to the minimum known unit. The Sahibayn argue that the ignorance can be removed by measuring later, so it does not prevent validity.
The core difference is whether jahalah (ignorance) that is removable is a bar to validity.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah)
Under Abu Hanifa, because only one unit is valid, the transaction is split (tafarruq al-safqah), giving the buyer an option (khiyar) to take or leave that single unit. Under the Sahibayn, the entire pile is valid, so no such option arises from this defect.
Even if the pile is later measured, Abu Hanifa still gives the buyer an option because he only learns the quantity at that moment.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The text does not specify which view is relied upon, but later Hanafi works often adopt the Sahibayn's position because it facilitates commerce by reducing barriers.
In contemporary fatwa, the Sahibayn's view is generally preferred for transactions involving bulk goods.

Core Idea
When the total quantity is unknown, Abu Hanifa limits the sale to one unit; the Sahibayn accept curable ignorance.

This passage addresses a common practical scenario: selling a heap of grain where only the unit price is given. The Hanafi school divides on whether such a contract is valid for the entire heap or only one unit. This disagreement hinges on the nature of ignorance (jahalah) in the contract and whether it is curable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the positions
Who is selling what? The seller sells a pile of grain (sibrah) with the price set per qafiz but without stating the total number of qafiz. Abu Hanifa holds the sale is valid only for one qafiz; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold the entire sale is valid.
The matn from al-Quduri presents Abu Hanifa's view as the default.
2

Abu Hanifa: ignorance (jahalah) of the total quantity and price makes the contract impossible to execute for the whole, so it is limited to the minimum known. The Sahibayn: this ignorance is removable by the parties (by specifying total or measuring) and therefore does not invalidate.

3

Under Abu Hanifa, only one qafiz is validly sold, and the buyer has an option to take it or leave due to splitting of the transaction. Under the Sahibayn, the entire batch is sold validly with no option.
The buyer's option arises from tafarruq al-ṣafqah (splitting of the contract).
4

If measurement occurs in the council, ignorance is removed, but Abu Hanifa still gives the buyer an option because knowledge was acquired after contract formation. If measurement occurs after separation, the contract has already become fasid (invalid) and cannot be rectified without a new contract.
Measurement after separation does not cure the initial defect according to Abu Hanifa.
5
Consider the broader principle
This case illustrates the Hanafi approach to jahalah fahishah (gross ignorance) vs. minor curable ignorance. The Sahibayn's position shows that if the ignorance can be easily removed, the contract may be upheld.
This principle applies to other sales where quantity or price is initially uncertain.

This passage from the commentary (al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah) explains the ruling on khiyar (option) when a purchased quantity—such as a heap of grain—is found to be less or more than the specified amount. The ruling is based on the legal concepts of tafarruq al-safqah (splitting of the bargain) and the nature of quantity as an essential part (asl) rather than a description (wasf).

Core Idea
Length is a description; its deficiency grants only full-price or cancel, not a reduction.

When you buy a cloth or land described by a specific length (e.g., 10 cubits), does a shortfall allow a price reduction? Sale of House Includes Building Even if Not Specified
Ruling that building is included in sale of house by default

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the inclusion of the building in the sale of a house and identify the two proofs (custom and attribute) that support it.

Core Idea
The sale of a house automatically includes its building and all fixtures because the term 'house' in custom encompasses land and structure, and attributes follow the sold item.

When someone buys a house, does the sale include the building itself or just the land? In Hanafi fiqh, the default rule is that the building is included even if not explicitly mentioned. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this rule, based on customary usage and the principle that attributes of a sold item are automatically transferred.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being sold? A house (dar). What is the ruling? The building (bina) enters into the sale even if not explicitly mentioned.
The ruling is stated as a default rule, not requiring explicit stipulation.
2

The text states that the term 'house' in common usage encompasses both the plot (arsah) and the building. This is a proof based on customary understanding.
Customary usage is a recognized source of law in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Locate the second proof: attribute (sifah)
The building is considered an attribute of the house, and attributes of a sold item are automatically included in the sale.
This is a general principle: attributes follow the essence.
4

The inclusion is not limited to the building alone but extends to all permanent fixtures and structures within the property boundaries.
