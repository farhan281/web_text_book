Rent for a Hired House: Obligation Even Without Occupancy
Ruling on rent obligation for a hired house

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which rent becomes due in a house rental contract and identify the exception that relieves the tenant from payment.

Core Idea
Rent is due upon delivery of keys and passage of time, even if the tenant never moves in, unless a legal impediment prevents occupancy.

Many people assume that if they rent a property but never actually live there, they don't have to pay the rent. Hanafi law, however, takes a different view. This lesson clarifies the general rule and the specific exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its parties
Recognize that this is a hiring contract (ijara) where the tenant hires a house to live in. The landlord is the lessor; the tenant is the lessee.
2
Note the trigger for rent obligation
Rent becomes obligatory when two conditions are met: the keys are handed over (delivery of possession) and the rental period passes. These are conditions for the rent to be due.
Delivery of keys symbolises giving access to the benefit.
3
Understand the default ruling
Once the conditions are satisfied, the tenant must pay the full rent regardless of whether he actually lived in the house or used it. The rent is for the availability of the benefit, not its actual consumption.
4
Recognize the exception
If an external prevention (mani') – such as a ruler's order or legal obstacle – prevents the tenant from using the house, the rent is not due. The tenant must prove that the prevention was real and not self-imposed.
Temporary prevention may not fully cancel the rent; permanent prevention does.

Core Idea

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah addresses a specific scenario in hire (ijarah): what happens when a hirer violates the terms of the contract but later returns to proper conduct? Breach of Hire: Liability and Rent in Hanafi Fiqh
Scenarios of breach: liability and rent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules to determine when a hirer is liable for damage and when rent is owed after a breach.

Core Idea
A hirer who breaches the contract may owe rent even without using the item, unless a legitimate impediment excuses non-use.

When a hire contract is breached, the consequences hinge on whether the hirer exceeded authority (ta‘addi) and whether the owner was deprived of the item's benefit. The passage explores several scenarios—from detaining the animal without use to taking a different route—to clarify when daman (liability) applies and when the specified rent (al-ujrah al-musamma) remains due. Understanding these distinctions ensures correct application in real-world hire disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hired item and the breach
For each scenario, first note what was hired (animal or house) and exactly how the hirer deviated from the contract terms.
Common breaches: not going to the agreed place, taking a different route, not using the item, or not returning on time.
2
Determine if the hirer became a violator (mukhalif)
If the hirer acted without permission or beyond the agreed scope, he is considered a violator. This triggers liability for damage and may affect rent.
Going via a usual road does not make him a violator; an unusual road does.
3
Check for damage and its cause
If the hired item perished, liability (daman) requires causation. The passage shows that detaining without authorization or going to an unauthorized place makes the hirer responsible even without direct fault.
No need to prove negligence—breach itself gives rise to daman.
4
Apply the rule for rent (ujrah)
Rent is generally due if the hirer received possession (even without using) and no legitimate excuse exists. Exceptions: if the hirer immediately abandoned the contract without taking benefit, or if a preventer (mani‘) like a ruler's order blocked occupation.
For animals: rent is due if the animal reached the destination and was returned safely, or if the hirer simply took it without riding. For houses: once keys are handed over, rent is due for the full term unless a preventer arose.
5
Consider exceptions (istithna')
Review the exception for houses: if a legitimate impediment (e.g., ruler's order) prevented the hirer from using the house, rent is not due despite possession.
The burden of proof for the impediment lies on the hirer.

Hired Item as Amanah and Usage Restrictions
States amanah principle and usage restrictions for hired items.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the amanah principle in ijarah and state the usage restrictions according to Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The hired item is a trust; the hirer is not liable for accidental loss but must use it exactly as specified and according to custom.

This lesson covers two interconnected rulings: the general principle that the hired item is an amanah (trust) in the hirer's hands, protecting him from liability for accidental loss, and the specific restrictions on how he must use the item – according to the contract and custom. Neglecting these restrictions constitutes transgression (ta‘addī) and removes the amanah protection.

Guided Path
1
State the amanah principle
The text says the hired item is a trust (amanah) in the hirer's hand, whether the contract is valid or invalid. Therefore, if it perishes without transgression, dispute, or third-party crime, the hirer is not liable.
This is a foundational rule in Hanafi ijarah.
2
Identify exceptions to the amanah
The hirer becomes liable if there is ta‘addī (transgression), khilāf (dispute?), or jināyah (third-party crime). The condition 'khilāf' here likely means a dispute arising from the hirer's wrongdoing.
The passage lists three exceptions; ta‘addī is the most common.
3
List the usage restrictions
The hirer must use the animal exactly as agreed: bareback if hired bareback, with saddle if hired with saddle; cannot ride if hired for loading; cannot load if hired for riding; cannot lie down or lean; rather, he rides according to custom.
These are examples of proper use; any deviation is ta‘addī.
4
Apply the custom rule
The hirer must ride according to ‘urf (custom) and ‘ādah (usage). Custom defines the proper riding posture and manner.
‘Urf is a secondary source in Hanafi fiqh.

Classification of Employees: Common vs. Private and Liability
Definition and liability classification of employees in ijarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ajir mushtarak and ajir khas based on the Hanafi definition, and state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding liability of the common worker for lost goods.

Core Idea
Common worker holds goods as trust (amana) and does not guarantee loss unless negligent, per Abu Hanifa.

In Hanafi fiqh, the classification of hired workers determines their liability for goods in their possession. This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah distinguishes between the common worker (ajir mushtarak) who serves multiple clients and does not earn wages until completion, and the private worker (ajir khas) who is hired exclusively. The central legal issue is whether the goods are held as a trust (amana) or a guarantee (daman). Abu Hanifa and Zufar hold the former view for the common worker, while the Sahibayn advocate the latter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
Read the opening sentence: 'والأجراء على ضربين: أجير مشترك، وأجير خاص'. Recognize that the passage is establishing a binary classification of employees.
The distinction is fundamental to liability rules.
2
Define the common worker (ajir mushtarak)
The text states: 'المشترك: من لا يستحق الأجرة حتى يعمل' – a common worker is one who does not become entitled to wages until he actually performs the work. Examples include dyers and washermen. This implies the worker is hired for a specific task, not a period.
He works for multiple hirers, hence 'common'.
3
Understand the trust status of goods
The text says: 'والمتاعُ أمانةٌ في يده'. Goods in his hand are a trust. Under Abu Hanifa's view, if the goods perish without his fault, he is not liable. This is based on the principle that the worker is a trustee (amin).
Contrast with private worker who is a guarantor.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The text then presents a dispute: 'عند أبي حنيفة... وقالا: يضمنه'. Abu Hanifa and Zufar say no guarantee; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the goods are guaranteed (madhmun) upon taking possession, unless the loss is from an overwhelming calamity like a major fire.
This ikhtilaf has practical implications for contracts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the common worker (ajir mushtarak) and specific worker (ajir khas), and state the intra-Hanafi dispute on liability with the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The common worker is not liable for loss according to Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn impose liability to protect people's wealth.

In Hanafi fiqh, workers are classified into two types: the common worker who works for multiple hirers and does not receive wages until completion, and the specific worker hired for a period. The most significant dispute concerns the liability of the common worker for items in his possession—a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two eminent disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of workers
Identify the characteristics that distinguish ajir mushtarak from ajir khas. The common worker is not exclusive to one hirer and does not deserve wages until work is done (e.g., dyer, washer). The specific worker is hired for a period and deserves wages for that time regardless of output (e.g., servant).
The definition directly affects the liability ruling.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
According to Abu Hanifa (and Zufar), the item in the worker's hand is an amanah (trust). If it perishes without negligence, the worker does not guarantee it.
The basis is that possession was with the owner's permission.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the worker guarantees the item upon receipt (mazmun), except if the loss is due to an overwhelming calamity (like a fire that engulfs the shop or an overpowering enemy).
This is a precaution to protect people's property and encourage carefulness.
4
Examine the proofs
Abu Hanifa's proof: Qiyas on a deposit (wadi'ah) – receipt with permission implies trust. The Sahibayn's proof: Istihsan based on maslahah – deterrence prevents carelessness.
Both are valid ijtihad within the school.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: If the item is lost or damaged, under Abu Hanifa the owner bears the loss unless he proves negligence. Under the Sahibayn, the worker must compensate unless the loss was unavoidable.
This affects real disputes between customers and service providers.
6

Later scholars (mutakharin) often recommend sulh (settlement for half the value) to resolve the disagreement. Abu al-Layth stated the fatwa is on Abu Hanifa's view.
Thus, the mufta bihi is generally non-liability, but practical caution may suggest sulh.

Definition and ruling on the special hired worker

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of a special employee (ajīr khāṣṣ) and explain why wages are owed even without work.

Core Idea
A special employee earns wages by being available, not by working.

In Hanafi fiqh, hired workers are classified into two types: the special employee (ajīr khāṣṣ) and the common worker (ajīr mushtarak). This lesson defines the first type and establishes its unique ruling: wages become obligatory merely by making oneself available for the entire hired period, regardless of actual work performed. This principle has important implications for contractual obligations.

Guided Path

2
Understand the condition for entitlement
The text states entitlement is by 'surrendering himself for the duration' (بتسليم نفسه في المدة). This means the employee must make himself available and ready to work.
3
Recognize the core ruling
Wages are wajib (obligatory) even if the employee does not actually perform any work, because the basis is availability, not output.
4
Note the reason for the name 'special'
The employee is called 'special' because he is exclusive to this employer; he cannot work for others during the contract period.
5
Differentiate from the common worker
Contrast with ajīr mushtarak who is paid per task and can work for multiple employers.

Liability of Cupper and Phlebotomist
Ruling on liability for cupper and phlebotomist

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for liability of a cupper or phlebotomist and apply them to scenarios with and without permission.

Core Idea
A medical practitioner is not liable for harm if he acts with permission and within customary limits.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on liability for injuries caused by cupping and phlebotomy. The key distinction is whether the practitioner exceeded the customary procedure and whether the patient's permission was given. Understanding this helps differentiate between permissible medical practice and actionable harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
Recognize that the ruling involves a cupper (bazzāgh) or phlebotomist (fassād) performing a procedure on an animal (or person) with the owner's permission.
The text mentions 'صاحب الدابة' (owner of the animal).
2
Apply the default rule
If the practitioner does not exceed the customary place, there is no liability for any harm that occurs.
Customary place refers to the usual site and manner of the procedure.
3
Identify the exception for exceeding custom
If the practitioner exceeds the customary place, he becomes liable because he acted beyond the permission granted.
The phrase 'لم يُؤذن له في ذلك' (he was not permitted for that) is key.
4
Apply the no-permission case
If the procedure was done without the owner's permission, the practitioner is liable regardless of whether he exceeded the customary place.
This emphasizes that permission is a separate necessary condition.

Liability of Circumciser for Injury to Glans
Ruling on circumciser liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a circumciser's liability when he accidentally cuts the glans, distinguishing between death and recovery.

Core Idea
If a circumciser cuts the glans, he owes half the blood-money if the boy dies, and full blood-money if he survives.

This lesson covers a specific case of medical liability: a circumciser who mistakenly cuts off the glans. The ruling depends on whether the boy dies or recovers, because the act is a combination of permitted and unpermitted actions. Understanding this distinction helps clarify how Islamic law apportions liability when multiple causes contribute to harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two actions involved
Circumcision involves cutting the foreskin (permitted) and accidentally cutting the glans (not permitted).
The text distinguishes between 'قطع الجلدة' (cutting the skin) and 'قطع الحشفة' (cutting the glans).
2
Apply the reasoning for death
When the boy dies, both actions contributed: one permitted (foreskin) and one not (glans). Therefore, the circumciser is responsible for only half the blood-money, because two causes shared the outcome.
The principle is that if death results from two causes, one lawful and one unlawful, liability is divided.
3
Apply the reasoning for recovery
When the boy recovers, the permitted action (skin cutting) is considered null, and the only remaining cause is the unpermitted severance of the glans. Hence, full blood-money is owed.
The recovery eliminates the contribution of the permitted act, leaving the unlawful act as the sole cause.
4
State the final ruling
Thus, half diya for death, full diya for survival.
Diya is the blood-money for the loss of the glans.

The Special Employee: Definition and Liability
Definition of ajir khas and liability rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the special employee (ajir khas) and state the rule for his liability as a trustee.

Core Idea
The special employee is a trustee (amin) — not liable for loss unless he transgresses.

In Hanafi fiqh, the special employee (ajir khas) is a central figure in the contract of hire. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira defines who a special employee is and clarifies his liability: he is entrusted with the employer's property and is not responsible for accidental loss, but only for harm caused by his own transgression. Understanding this distinction is key to correctly applying the rules of liability in hire contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Locate the definition of ajir khas: who is entitled to wages by making himself available, even without working, for a specified period.
Example: hiring a man for a month for service or grazing.
2
Explain the reason for the name
Understand why he is called 'special': because his work is exclusively for that employer during the contract period.
Cannot work for others during that time.
3
State the general liability rule
The special employee is a trustee (amin): no liability for loss of items in his hand or from his work unless he transgresses.
Example: stolen goods, broken pot from customary work.
4
Distinguish transgression from customary work
Identify acts that constitute transgression (e.g., striking a sheep and blinding it) versus acts within customary work (e.g., pounding cloth causing tear).
Transgression triggers liability.
5
Apply specific rulings for shepherds
Note additional rules: no liability for natural deaths, wolf attacks, drowning while watering; full wages even if most sheep die as long as some are grazed; prohibition of unauthorized mating; no liability for the shepherd if a male mounts on its own, or if a sheep wanders and chasing would risk the rest.
Shepherd's word is accepted with oath as trustee.

Non-Liability of the Special Employee (Ajir Khas) and Transgression
Expanding on the rule of non-liability for ajir khas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling for the ajir khas regarding loss and identify the condition that triggers liability.

Core Idea
The ajir khas is a trustee — no liability for loss unless he transgresses.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on the liability of the special employee (ajir khas). The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira establishes that the ajir khas, as a trustee, is not liable for property lost in his possession or from his work. However, if he commits ta'addi (transgression) by doing work in an abnormal manner, he becomes liable. Understanding this distinction is crucial for contracts of hire and the rights of employers and employees.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The ajir khas is not liable for any loss of the entrusted property, whether it occurs in his possession (e.g., theft) or from his work (e.g., breakage). This is because he is an amin (trustee).
2
Recognize the two categories of loss
Losses are of two types: loss in hand (like theft or usurpation) and loss from work (like a pot breaking or a garment tearing during normal work). Both are covered by the default non-liability.
3
Understand the condition for liability
The exception is transgression (ta'addi). If the employee performs work in an unusual or customary manner that causes damage, he becomes liable. The work must be customary and conventional for non-liability to apply.
Example: striking a sheep and gouging its eye is transgression; watering sheep that drown is not.
4
Apply the condition to examples
In the passage, if the employee does something abnormal like striking a sheep hard enough to cause injury, he is a transgressor and liable. But if a sheep drowns while being watered normally, he is not liable.
5
Note the evidentiary rule
Because the ajir khas is an amin, his word is accepted with oath regarding the loss. This means if he claims the loss occurred without transgression, the employer must accept his statement unless there is evidence to the contrary.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the validity of hiring a camel to carry a mahmil and two riders in the Hanafi school and identify when the contract becomes void.

Core Idea
A void hire if the riders are unspecified — specificity or delegation of choice is required.

Hiring a camel for a specific purpose, such as carrying a mahmil (litter) and two riders, is a common transaction in classical Hanafi fiqh. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the conditions that make such a contract valid, and the consequences of leaving key terms uncertain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hiring a camel to carry a mahmil (a litter) and two riders to Mecca. The ruling stated is that it is permissible (jā'iz), i.e., the contract is valid.
The matn uses 'jāz' meaning the contract is sound, subject to conditions.
2
Determine the conditions for validity
Two conditions are highlighted: (1) the mahmil must be the customary type (al-maḥmil al-mu'tād), and (2) the riders must be specified, or the lessor must say 'on condition that I ride whomever I wish.' Without specification or delegation, the contract is void.
The specification avoids gharar (uncertainty) which invalidates the contract.
3
Analyze the consequence of missing a condition
If the lessor simply says 'I hired for riding' (istāʾjart ʿalā al-rukūb) without specifying riders or giving a choice, the hire is declared void (fāsidah).
This is because the object of hire (the service of carrying) is too vague.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference on journey scope
The matn states that the hire is specifically for the outward journey (dhahāb) only. However, al-Ghāyah mentions that it covers both going and returning (dhahāb wa majīʾ).
This difference affects the duration of the contract but not the core conditions.
5
Connect to modern scenarios
Today, the same principle applies when hiring a bus or taxi to carry passengers and luggage to a specific destination: the number of passengers and luggage must be clear, or the hirer must have the right to decide later.
The underlying cause (ʿillah) is removal of uncertainty.

Freed Slave's Option in Prepaid Rent Ijara
Ruling on freed slave's option in prepaid rent ijara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a freed slave's right to cancel does not affect the lessor's ownership of prepaid rent, and state who keeps the rent if the slave continues the contract.

Core Idea
Prepaid rent belongs to the master even if the slave is freed; the slave's option is only to cancel, not to claim rent.

Imagine you rent a slave for a year and pay the full rent upfront. Mid-year, the master frees the slave. Can the slave walk away and demand a refund? This Hanafi ruling clarifies that the prepaid rent is already owned by the master, but the freed slave has the right to continue or cancel the contract. The key is understanding that the right of rescission (faskh) does not retroactively undo the master's ownership of the advance payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The ruling concerns a hire contract (ijara) for a slave where the master collects the full rent in advance, then frees the slave. The slave chooses to continue the contract.

The text says 'فالأجرة كلُّها للمولى' meaning all the rent belongs to the master. The reason given: 'لأنه قد ملكها بالتعجيل' (because he owned it by advancing).
The key principle: ownership of the prepaid amount is established at the time of payment.
3
Explain the slave's right of rescission
The slave has the right to faskh (cancellation), but if he does not exercise it, the rent remains with the master. The right to cancel does not retroactively affect the master's ownership.
The text: 'ويثبت حق الفسخ للعبد، فإذا لم يفسخ: استحقت الأجرة على الوجه الذي اقتضاه القبض.'
4

The ruling is attributed to 'al-Karkhi', indicating it is a relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.
Mention of 'في الكرخي' shows transmission from a major Hanafi authority.

Lessor's Delivery of Equipment and Hirer's Liability
Ruling on lessor's delivery obligations and hirer's liability for equipment loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the equipment a lessor must deliver for camels, horses, and donkeys, and state the rule that the hirer is not liable for loss of that equipment.

Core Idea
The lessor must provide specific tack; if it is lost in the hirer's hands, the hirer does not compensate, just as with the animal itself.

When you rent an animal for transport, what equipment must the owner provide? And who bears the risk if that equipment is lost? This passage lists the mandatory items for camels (girth, saddle, nose ring), horses (bridle), and donkeys (packsaddle), and clarifies that the hirer is not liable for loss of these items—similar to the rule that the hirer is not liable if the animal itself dies without his fault.

The list is exhaustive for these animals.

State the liability rule

The equipment is considered an accessory to the animal. Since the hirer is not liable for the animal's accidental death or loss (without fault), the same applies to the equipment.
This aligns with the general principle in ijara that the hirer is not liable for destruction of the leased object unless there is negligence.

Rulings on Inspection, Eating Provisions, and Lessor's Duties in Ijara
Classical Hanafi rulings on camel hire obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on inspecting the mahmil, replacing consumed provisions, and the lessor's obligations for mounting/dismounting and leading the animal, and identify when rent becomes due.

Core Idea
The rent is not due by contract alone; it is due after service, and the lessor must assist with mounting/dismounting and obligatory prayers.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira discusses several practical rulings in a camel hire contract (ijara). It covers what is recommended for the lessor before the journey, what happens if the hirer consumes the provisions, when the rent becomes payable, and the specific duties of the lessor regarding the animal and riders. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correctly applying Hanafi fiqh in lease agreements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
Go through the passage and list each act mentioned: inspecting the mahmil, eating provisions, theft of provisions, timing of rent, provision of mahmil and cover, lessor's duties (lifting, lowering, driving, leading, dismounting for prayers, kneeling for certain riders). For each, state the ruling (recommended, permissible, obligatory, not obligatory).
This step helps you see the structure of the passage.
2
Understand the inspection rule
The text says inspecting the mahmil is 'ajwad' (better). This is a sunnah or mustahabb. Why? Because it removes ignorance (jahala) about the condition of the mahmil, which could otherwise lead to dispute.
Inspection eliminates gharar (uncertainty) in the subject matter.
3
Analyze the provision consumption rule
If the hirer eats from the provisions he hired the camel to carry, he must replace the equivalent. The same applies if the provisions are stolen. The Hidayah extends this to any measurable goods (makil/mawzun).
This is because the hirer is in possession as an amana (trust), but consumption constitutes use, so he becomes liable.
4
Distinguish lessor's vs hirer's responsibilities
The mahmil and its cover (ghita') are the hirer's property/responsibility. The lessor must perform certain actions: lifting/lowering the mahmil, driving/leading the camel, letting riders dismount for obligatory prayer and purification (but not for eating or nafl prayer), and making the camel kneel for women, the sick, and the elderly.
These duties are wajib on the lessor as part of the service.
5
Note the timing of rent
The rent (ujrah) is not due by the contract alone. It becomes due either by taking possession (qabd) of the leased asset or after the service is performed. This is a key Hanafi principle distinguishing ijara from sale.
Contrast with the view that rent is due immediately upon contract (as in some other schools).

When is Hire Due? Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Explains when hire becomes due and contrasts with Shafi'i view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on when the hire becomes due, identify the three triggers, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Hire is not due by contract alone; gradual exchange requires payment only upon receipt, stipulation, or advance payment.

In this passage, the author explains a core difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the hire contract (ijara). While both agree that hire is a valid exchange, they disagree on when the lessor becomes entitled to the payment. The Hanafi position is built on the idea that the contract is concluded gradually as benefits are received, requiring equality in exchange. The Shafi'i view analogizes hire to sale, where ownership of the price transfers immediately upon contract. Understanding this dispute clarifies a key practical difference: what happens if the lessor frees the slave (the hire) before the hirer receives any benefit?

Guided Path

Imam al-Shafi'i holds that ownership of the hire transfers immediately upon contract, making the hire due instantly.
This is analogous to a sale where the price is due at contract.

This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school argues that hire differs from sale because benefits are delivered gradually; equality in exchange requires that payment correspond to benefit received.

For stipulation of immediate payment, the lessor has the right to withhold the hired property until he receives the wage, analogized to the seller's right to withhold the sold item until payment. For immediate payment without stipulation, if the lessee pays early, the lessor owns it and if he then manumits the slave (if that is the wage), it is valid. If the contract later rescinds, the lessor may still withhold the property for the wage but does not guarantee it if it perishes. If the property is destroyed or reclaimed, or if one party dies, the manumitter must pay the slave's value because the delivery of the hired property is frustrated. The text also notes that if the lessee manumits after delivery to the lessor, it is invalid because the lessor now owns the slave. For the third cause, receipt of benefit, the text explains that when the lessee actually enjoys the usufruct, ownership of the wage is established in return. Finally, a condition to defer payment until the end of the term or after completion is permissible as it is consistent with the contract's requirements.

Discharging the Hire Fee Before Benefit: Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ibra'/hiba of hire fee before benefit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the validity of discharging or gifting the hire fee before benefit, and apply the principle that ownership of the fee is a prerequisite for its valid discharge.

Core Idea
A discharge or gift of the hire fee is invalid if the fee is not yet owned—timing of ownership matters.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani regarding the validity of a lessor discharging or gifting the hire fee before the lessee has taken any benefit. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi principle that ownership of the fee (ujrah) accrues with benefit, not merely by contract. The two Imams differ on whether the fee can be considered owned before benefit is taken, leading to different rulings on the validity of ibra' and hiba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and context
The act is the lessor discharging (ibra') or gifting (hiba) the hire fee to the lessee after the contract is concluded but before the lessee takes any benefit. The fee may be a specific good ('ayn) or a debt (dayn).
Prompt payment not stipulated; asset not yet received.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and his reasoning
Abu Yusuf holds that the discharge or gift is invalid regardless of whether the fee is 'ayn or dayn. Reason: the fee is not owned by the contract alone; it becomes due only as benefit is taken, so discharging a right not yet due is void. The contract remains valid because the discharge is void.
Analogy to deferred debt is rejected.
3

Muhammad distinguishes: if the fee is a debt, the discharge or gift is valid because the lessor owns the debt (it is a right in the dhimmah). If the fee is a specific good and the lessor gifts it, the contract becomes void if the lessee accepts the gift (since the good is now owned by the lessee); if rejected, the contract stands.
Gift of 'ayn transfers ownership immediately upon acceptance, extinguishing the fee obligation.
4

Under Abu Yusuf, the contract remains valid and the fee must still be paid as benefit is taken. Under Muhammad, if fee is debt, contract continues and lessee is freed; if fee is 'ayn and gift accepted, contract is void ab initio.
Practical difference: whether the lessee can be sued for the fee later.
5
Extract the underlying principle
The core principle: ownership of the fee (ujrah) is a prerequisite for valid ibra' or hiba. Hanafis differ on when that ownership arises—whether at contract (Muhammad for debt) or only with benefit (Abu Yusuf).
This relates to the nature of 'ujrah in ijara.
