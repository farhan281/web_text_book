Death of the Option Holder: Option Does Not Pass to Heirs
Third ruling: death of the option holder and intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khiyar al-ru'ya after the death of the option holder and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute with Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
The option of inspection dies with the buyer; it does not pass to heirs.

When a buyer dies while still holding the option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'ya), does the right pass to his heirs? The Hanafi school, following Abu Hanifa, says the option becomes void. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing it passes if the heirs know of it. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the default rule
A person has the option of inspection (i.e., he bought an unseen item). He dies before seeing it. The question is whether his heirs can exercise the option.
The option is personal to the buyer, linked to his right to choose upon sight.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
The option becomes void (batala) upon death and does not transfer to heirs. This is analogous to khiyar al-shart (option of stipulation) which also terminates on death.
The analogy (qiyas) is the main evidence.
3
State Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf holds that the option is not automatically void; it can be exercised by the heirs if they have knowledge of its existence at the time of confirmation (ijazah).
Doubt (shakk) about the heir's knowledge prevents automatic validity.
4
Identify the thamarah (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, heirs have no right to return the goods. Under Abu Yusuf, they can confirm the contract and then return if they choose.
The dispute is about the nature of the option: is it a personal right that ends with death, or a property right that can pass?

Khiyar al-Ru'yah: Disputes over Change and Sight
Rulings on khiyar when goods seen before purchase and disputes about change and sight

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving disputes in khiyar al-ru'yah regarding change of the object and whether the buyer saw it.

Core Idea
In khiyar al-ru'yah, the seller's word prevails for change in short periods; the buyer's word for sight claims.

After a buyer sees an item and later purchases it, two common disputes arise: (1) the buyer claims the item changed; (2) the buyer denies having seen it at all. This passage from the Jawharah al-Nayirah clarifies how the Hanafi school resolves these disputes, shifting the burden of proof based on custom and legal principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disputes
Note the two separate issues: dispute about change (taghayyur) and dispute about sight (ru'yah).
Each has its own rule.
2
Analyse the change dispute rule
For change, the general rule is that the seller's word is taken with an oath. Determine the exception: if the period is long (a month or more), the buyer's word is taken.
The rational proof: change is an accident; the original sight is the cause of binding. Over a long period, custom indicates likely change.
3
Define 'long' and 'short' periods
The passage defines 'long' as a month or more, 'short' as less than a month. This is a customary (urf) definition.
Memorise this benchmark.
4
Analyse the sight dispute rule
If the dispute is about whether the buyer saw the item at or before the contract, the buyer's word is always taken with an oath.
Because the seller is claiming an event (sight) that the buyer denies.

Khiyar al-Ru'yah: Ruling, Dispute on Change, and Defining the Long Period
States ruling on option of sight and discusses disputes on change and duration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khiyar al-ru'yah, apply the rule for burden of proof when buyer and seller dispute change, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on defining the 'long period'.

Core Idea
A buyer who sees an item then purchases it later has an option if it changed; if they dispute change, the seller's word is taken unless the period is long.

When a buyer sees a product and later buys it, Islamic law grants him an option if the product has changed from what he saw. But what happens if they disagree about whether it changed? The Hanafi school applies a default rule based on the principle that change is a new occurrence, but makes an exception for a long period. This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri explains the ruling and reveals a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute about how long 'long' actually is.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a buyer seeing an item then purchasing after a period. The ruling: if unchanged, no option; if changed, buyer has an option to cancel or accept.
This is the core of khiyar al-ru'yah.
2
Analyze the dispute on change
If they differ on whether the item changed, the seller's word is taken with oath by default, because change is a new occurrence and the default is continuity of the original state.
Al-asl 'adam al-taghayyur: the original state is no change.
3
Identify the exception for a long period
When the period between sight and purchase is long, the buyer's word is taken because it is likely the item changed over time. The example of a slave girl after twenty years illustrates this.
The rationale is that 'things change over long time' (al-ashya' tataghayyar bi-tul al-zaman).
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on duration
Al-Hidayah mentions the exception without specifying the length. Later scholars (some say 'قيل') define 'long' as a month or more, 'short' as less than a month.
This is an example of later jurists refining a general principle for practical application.
5
Apply the dispute to a case
If the period is exactly one month, the later view treats it as long (buyer's word), while the earlier view leaves it open. Also consider the separate dispute: if they differ on whether the buyer saw the item at all, the buyer's word is taken with oath.
For the sight dispute, the buyer's word is always taken because sight is a new occurrence claimed by the seller.

Option of Defect: Definition and Ruling
Defines defect and states buyer's option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a defect in Hanafi fiqh and state the buyer's option to return defective goods, including the procedural difference before and after possession.

Core Idea
A buyer may return defective goods if the defect existed at sale, and before possession no consent needed.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi option of defect (khiyar al-'ayb), a key consumer protection in Islamic sales. The passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah defines what constitutes a defect and outlines the buyer's automatic right to return the item or keep it at full price. Understanding the distinction between pre-possession and post-possession procedures is crucial for correct application.

Guided Path
1
Define the defect
Identify the Hanafi definition from the passage: a defect is what the original sound nature (fitrah salimah) is free from — i.e., an imperfection that deviates from the norm.
This is a functional definition tied to custom (urf).
2
State the basic ruling
The buyer has the option: keep the item with full price or return it. This option is established by default without being stipulated.
The option is a right (mubah), not an obligation.
3
Identify the conditions for the option to exist
The defect must have been present at the time of sale and unknown to the buyer at contract and possession. If the buyer saw the defect, it implies acceptance.
The option is inherited by heirs and does not require a condition.
4
Distinguish the procedure before and after possession
Before possession: buyer can return simply by saying 'I return it' without seller's consent or judge's ruling. After possession: return requires seller's consent or a judicial decision.
The distinction protects both parties' interests at different stages.

Option of Defect: Definition and Basic Ruling
Defines defect and states buyer's option to keep or return defective goods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a defect in a sale and explain the buyer's option, including the two permissible choices, the conditions for the option, and the prohibition against keeping the item and claiming a price reduction.

Core Idea
A defect is whatever decreases value per merchant custom; the buyer must take the item fully or return it, never keep it for a discount.

In this lesson, we examine the buyer's option when a defect is discovered in a purchased item. The Hanafi school defines a defect based on merchant custom and grants the buyer a choice: keep the item with the full price or return it. This lesson will walk you through the definition, the option's features, and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the buyer discovering a defect in the purchased item. The ruling: the buyer has an option (khiyar) – he may choose to keep the item for the full price or return it.
The option is established without a condition, is not suspended, and is inheritable.
2
Define the defect
The defect is defined by custom of merchants: whatever decreases the price according to trade practice is a defect. This includes both major and minor flaws as long as trade professionals consider it a defect.
The text quotes al-Khujandi: 'al-‘ayb ma naqasa al-thaman ‘inda al-tujjar'.
3
Determine the conditions
The defect must have existed with the seller at the time of contract, and the buyer must not have been aware of it at the time of sale or taking possession. If the buyer knew, it is considered acceptance.
If the defect arose after the contract but before possession, the option still applies.
4
Apply the procedural rules
If the defect is discovered before taking possession, the buyer can rescind simply by declaring 'I return it' without needing the seller's consent or a court. If after possession, rescission requires the seller's consent or a judge's ruling.
The rescission is a faskh between the parties but a new sale regarding third parties if done with consent.
5
Note the prohibition
The buyer may not keep the item and demand a reduction in price. This is because the seller only agreed to part with the item for the full price, and attributes do not correspond to a portion of the price.
Exception: if the seller voluntarily agrees to a reduction, it is permissible.

Core Idea
The ruling changes because the cause changes: childhood weakness vs adult vice.

This lesson delves into the reasoning behind the Hanafi rule. Why does the same action—bedwetting, theft, or running away—have different implications before and after puberty? The answer lies in the underlying cause. Understanding this cause-based approach is essential for correctly applying the option of defect in sales.

Guided Path
1
Identify the action and the two age phases: minor and adult
The actions are bedwetting, running away (ibaq), and theft. The two phases are before puberty (minor) and after puberty (adult).

Bedwetting in a child is due to weakness of the bladder (da'f al-mathana). Running away is due to love of play (hubb al-la'ib). Theft is due to lack of care (qillat al-mubalah).
These causes are not blameworthy character flaws; they are natural immaturity.
3
State the cause for each action in adults
Bedwetting in an adult is due to internal disease (da' fi al-batin). Running away is due to malice in the heart (khubth fi al-qalb). Theft is also due to malice (khubth fi al-batin).
Adults are held accountable; these causes indicate a bad character.
4
Connect to the ruling on return
If the defect appears in the same phase as at sale (i.e., the cause is the same), the buyer can return. If the phase changed, the cause changed, so the defect is considered new and not returnable.
Example: If bedwetting occurred in childhood with the seller and in childhood with the buyer, it is the same defect. If it only occurs after puberty with the buyer, it is new.
5
Apply to theft with the food exception
Theft for food from master is not a defect because it is driven by hunger, not bad character. But theft for sale indicates greed, so it is a defect.
This exception applies only to minors and only from master's house.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A defect recurring after puberty is a new defect if the cause differs.

When you buy a slave (or any object) and a defect appears that had occurred earlier, you might assume it's the same defect. But Hanafi law looks deeper: it examines the cause. A child wetting the bed is due to a weak bladder; an adult doing so is due to illness. These are legally different defects. This lesson unpacks that crucial distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defect and its time of occurrence

2
Determine the cause based on age
According to the text, the same behavioral defect has different causes in childhood vs. adulthood: bed-wetting (weak bladder vs. internal disease), running away (love of play vs. malice), theft (lack of caution vs. malice). Use these prescribed causes.
3
Compare causes of the two occurrences
If the causes are identical (both in childhood or both in adulthood), the defect is the same ('ayn al-ayb). If the causes differ (one childhood, one adulthood), it is a new defect.
4
Apply the ruling on return
If the defect is the same, the buyer may return the slave. If it is a new defect, return is not allowed because the defect present at sale is considered to have disappeared.
5
Handle the insanity exception
Insanity is treated differently: if it occurred in childhood, it is considered a permanent defect. Even if it recurs after puberty, it is the same defect because the cause (insanity) is presumed continuous. So return is always allowed.

Criteria for determining defects in slaves and animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi criterion for a defect and apply it to distinguish between defects in humans and animals.

Core Idea
A defect undermines the intended purpose; context matters—pregnancy is a defect in women, not in animals.

This lesson explores the foundational principle behind the Hanafi definition of a defect: any condition that diminishes the value or prevents the intended purpose of the sold item. The text clarifies that context is key—what is a defect in one category may not be in another. We will see how this applies to male slaves, female slaves, and animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the intended purpose
Determine the primary purpose for which the item is typically purchased. For a male slave, it is service (istikhdām); for a female slave, intercourse or marriage; for an animal, productivity.
The purpose may vary in different contexts, but the Hanafi school sets these defaults.
2
Assess impact on purpose
Examine whether the condition in question prevents or significantly hinders the intended purpose. If it does, it is a defect; if it does not, it is not a defect.
E.g., a cold does not prevent service, so it is not a defect.
3
Apply to key examples
Work through the examples: pregnancy in women prevents intercourse/marriage, so it is a defect; pregnancy in animals is an increase (increase of value), so not a defect. Habitual fornication in a male slave distracts from service, so it becomes a defect.
The text also mentions cessation of menses and istihadah as defects because they indicate infertility or disease.
4
Note exceptions
Occasional fornication is not a defect; only when habitual (more than twice) does it impair service. Similarly, a common cold is temporary and does not indicate a chronic defect.
The principle is that the defect must be inherent and lasting.

Comprehensive List of Defects and Post-Sale Discovery Rule
Detailed list of defects and rule for post-sale discovery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the listed defects and apply the rule when a pre-existing defect is discovered after the buyer has caused a new defect.

Core Idea
Many physical and moral defects are explicitly listed; if a buyer discovers a pre-existing defect after altering the item, he cannot return it but can claim compensation.

This lesson covers the detailed enumeration of defects in the Hanafi school, including physical conditions like blindness, leprosy, and paralysis, as well as moral defects in slaves such as abandoning prayer. It also addresses a practical scenario: if the buyer causes a new defect and then discovers a pre-existing one, the remedy is limited to compensation, not return.

Guided Path
1
Review the explicit list of physical defects
Read through the list: madness, leprosy, white spots, blindness, being one-eyed, squint, paralysis, deafness, muteness, extra/missing fingers, sores, diseases, swelling of testicles (ādir), impotence (ʿinnīn), castration (khaṣī), effeminacy (takhnīth).
These are defects because they reduce the value or prevent the intended purpose.
2
Note moral defects specific to slaves
Abandoning prayer (tark al-ṣalāh), backbiting (namīmah), and lying (kidhb) are defects in slaves because they harm the owner's property and reflect poor character.
Moral defects are specific to human subjects; they do not apply to animals.
3
Understand the post-sale discovery rule
If a buyer discovers a pre-existing defect after having caused a new defect himself (e.g., breaking an arm), he cannot return the item because return would harm the seller by imposing a declining value. Instead, he claims the difference in value caused by the pre-existing defect.
This rule prevents abuse: the buyer cannot benefit from his own damage.
4
Apply to the special case of buying as castrated
If a slave is bought on the condition that he is castrated, but he is found uncastrated, no option exists because the buyer got what he paid for (a non-castrated slave is more valuable). The defect is the absence of an expected condition, but the ruling protects the seller.
This is an exception: the buyer assumed a lower value, so no complaint.

Recourse for Deficiency When New Defect Occurs
Ruling and calculation for price reduction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of deficiency recourse when a new defect arises and calculate the proportional claim.

Core Idea
When a new defect occurs, the buyer cannot return the item but claims a proportional refund based on value loss.

In this passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, the commentator explains a crucial exception in sales: if a buyer discovers a pre-existing defect after a new defect has occurred, returning the item would harm the seller. The Hanafi school therefore permits only a claim for the proportional deficiency. The passage provides a clear calculation method using market appraisal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its rationale
State the central ruling: the buyer cannot return the item but can claim the deficiency. Understand the rationale: returning would harm the seller because the item left sound and returns with a new defect.
This is based on the principle of preventing harm (la darar).
2
Understand the appraisal method (taqwim)
Learn how to determine the deficiency proportion: appraise the item without the old defect, then with it. Find the difference in value, then calculate its ratio to the sound value.
The appraisal uses market value, not purchase price.
3
Apply the proportion to the purchase price
Multiply the purchase price (al-thaman) by the deficiency ratio to get the claim amount.
The claim is a fraction of the price paid, not of the market value.
4
Work through the example
Verify understanding with the given example: cloth bought for 10 dirhams, market value 100, defect reduces value by 10 (10% loss), claim = 10% of 10 = 1 dirham.

Core Idea
After gratuitous freeing or death, buyer claims arsh; after compensated freeing, no claim.

When a purchased slave changes status before the buyer discovers a defect, returning the slave becomes impossible or inequitable. The Hanafi school provides a remedy: the buyer may claim compensation (arsh) for the defect. However, this remedy is not available when the freeing was compensated, because the buyer has already received financial benefit. This lesson explains the rule and its exception.

The buyer purchases a slave and then either frees him gratuitously or the slave dies, then a defect is discovered. The ruling: the buyer claims compensation (nuqsan) for the reduction in value due to the defect. The same applies to making the slave a mudabbar (freedom upon master's death) or making a female slave an umm walad (mother of master's child).
These cases all involve a change in the slave's legal status that prevents return.
2
Understand why return is not possible
In these cases, the slave's status has changed: freed, dead, mudabbar (future freedom), or umm walad (cannot be sold). Return would be impractical or legally impossible. Therefore, compensation is the only remedy.
This is similar to khiyar al-'ayb where the item is consumed or transformed.

The key distinction is whether the buyer received a payment or not.

Ruling on Defect After Buyer Modification: Freeing, Dyeing, Cutting
Expansion on matn rulings for defect after buyer modification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when a buyer introduces an increase, return is replaced by compensation (arsh), and identify the dispute on black dye as an increase or decrease.

Core Idea
If the buyer modifies the item before discovering a defect, he cannot return it but claims compensation for the defect's decrease.

We have seen that the option of defect allows the buyer to return defective goods. But what if the buyer alters the item before discovering the defect? Can he still return it? This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi ruling: the buyer loses the right to return if he introduced an increase (like freeing a slave). Instead, he claims the decrease in value (arsh). The commentary expands on nuances such as dyeing and cutting cloth, and an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether black dye is an increase or decrease.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The buyer performs an act (freeing, dyeing, cutting) on the purchased item before discovering a pre-existing defect. The ruling is that if the act constitutes an increase, return is impossible and arsh is wajib.
The increase must be something that has market value.
2
Locate the governing principle
The key principle: 'Because he introduced an increase for which wealth is given, so he cannot take it with that increase. When return is impossible, compensation (arsh) is obligatory.' This is a rational (aqli) proof based on preventing unjust enrichment.
This principle is applied in all subcases.
3
Analyze the subcases of dyeing
Dyeing red is explicitly an increase. Dyeing black is disputed: Abu Hanifa considers it a decrease (damage), while the Sahibayn consider it an increase. The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) follows the Sahibayn.
The thamarah al-khilaf: if black is increase, buyer claims arsh; if decrease, buyer cannot claim.
4
Analyze the subcases of cutting cloth
Cutting without stitching and then transacting with knowledge of defect: no claim. Cutting and stitching (creating a shirt): claim arsh. Cutting and selling before stitching: no claim because seller accepts defective item.
The seller's argument: 'I accept it defective' bars claim if the buyer didn't complete an increase.
5
Note the exception for freeing with payment
If the buyer frees a slave for a payment (on money) or through kitaba, he does not claim arsh because he held the substitute price (badal). Only gratuitous freeing triggers arsh.
This prevents double recovery.

Buyer's Destruction of Purchased Item: Effect on Defect Claim
Commentary on rulings when buyer kills or eats the purchased item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a buyer destroys the purchased item before discovering a defect, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a third party's killing.

Core Idea
If the buyer destroys the item, he forfeits the right to claim compensation for defects - his own act bars the claim.

In this lesson, we examine a specific scenario in the law of options (khiyar al-ayb): what happens when the buyer himself destroys the purchased item (kills a slave or eats food) and later discovers a defect. The Hanafi school's position, based on Abu Hanifa, is clear: the buyer recovers nothing because the destruction was his own doing. We will also explore a nuanced intra-Hanafi difference when a third party kills the slave and the buyer takes its value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The buyer kills the slave or eats the food, then discovers a defect. According to Abu Hanifa, he recovers nothing (lam yarja' bi shay').
This is the core ruling: buyer's own destructive act bars arsh.
2
Distinguish from cases not caused by buyer
If the slave dies naturally, ownership ends and arsh is allowed. If the buyer emancipates, by istihsan he can claim arsh because emancipation, like death, ends ownership.
Natural death and emancipation do not involve the buyer's destruction, so arsh is preserved.
3
Analyze the qiyas and istihsan
By qiyas (analogy), emancipation would be like killing (no arsh) because the prevention is by the buyer's action. But istihsan (juristic preference) overrides: emancipation ends ownership like death, so arsh is allowed.
This shows the Hanafi method of preferring istihsan over qiyas.
4
Understand the third-party killing case
If someone else kills the slave, the buyer takes its value (qima). Abu Hanifa holds that taking qima is like selling, so no arsh. Abu Yusuf holds that the buyer can still claim arsh because the defect existed before the killing.
This intra-Hanafi dispute hinges on whether taking value negates the defect claim.

Ruling on returning goods to original seller after subsequent sale

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A return accepted by a judge's ruling is a rescission ab initio; without it, it is a new sale that bars recourse to the original seller.

When goods are sold multiple times and a defect is discovered, the ability to return to the original seller depends critically on how the first buyer (the middleman) handled the return from the second buyer. The Hanafi school, as explained in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, draws a clear line: acceptance by a judge's ruling creates a retroactive rescission, while acceptance without it is treated as a new sale. Understanding this distinction is vital for any merchant or legal practitioner dealing with multi-party transactions.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
Identify the parties: original seller (A), first buyer (B), second buyer (C). The slave (or good) is sold from A to B, then from B to C. C discovers a defect and returns it to B.
2
Identify the two possible outcomes
The text presents two cases: B accepts the return from C either by a judge's ruling or without it. The ruling on whether B can then return the good to A depends on this distinction.
3
Understand the legal effect of judge's ruling
When B accepts the return by a judge's ruling, the original contract between A and B is rescinded ab initio (from the start), as if the sale never occurred. Thus, B can return the good to A.

The text says 'it is a new sale in relation to the third party'.

In real transactions, the middleman must ensure that any acceptance of a return is done with a judge's ruling if they wish to preserve the right to recourse against the original seller.

The void sale never transfers ownership, even with possession and permission. The suspended sale requires the owner's permission to be effective.

The reason is that 'voidable' is a broader category: every void sale is also voidable, but not vice versa. The voidable is the lesser prohibition, so it encompasses both types.

Distinguishing Batil and Fasid in Sales of Impure Items
Expansion on invalid sales and batil/fasid distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between batil and fasid sales in Hanafi fiqh and state the rulings for sales of impure items and free persons.

Core Idea
A sale that lacks exchange of property (mal) is batil; a sale of prohibited but ownable items is fasid.

In Hanafi law, not all invalid sales are equal. The commentator expands on the matn's general term 'fasid' to show that some sales are batil (void ab initio) while others are fasid (voidable). The key lies in whether the subject matter is considered property (mal). This lesson walks through the distinctions and their practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of invalid sales
The matn says 'the sale is fasid' and lists dead animals, blood, wine, pig, and free persons. The commentator clarifies that this covers both batil (dead animals, blood, free persons) and fasid (wine, pig).
Distinguish between the term 'fasid' in the matn (general) and the technical categories.
2
Determine whether the item is considered property (mal)
If the item is not considered mal by any scholar (dead animals, blood, free persons), the sale is batil because the pillar of exchange of property is absent. If it is considered mal by some scholars (wine, pig), the sale is fasid because the exchange of property exists but the item is ritually impure.
The opinion that wine and pig are property is attributed to 'some mashayikh' as per al-Hidayah.
3
Apply the ruling: batil vs fasid consequences
Batil sale confers no ownership; if the item perishes in the buyer's hands, according to Abu Hanifa it is an amanah (trust, no liability), while according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad it is madmun (guaranteed). Fasid sale: the buyer owns the item but has the option to return it.
Intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn on liability.
4
Special rule for wine and pig sold for currency
If wine or pig is sold for dirhams/dinars, the sale is batil. If sold for other goods, it is fasid. This is because the sale of an impure item for pure currency is considered an exchange of property for property, but the impurity makes it void; selling for goods makes it voidable.
This nuance is specific to Hanafi fiqh.
5
Free persons: batil due to lack of ownership
A free person is not ownable property, so selling them is batil regardless of price. The buyer cannot take possession, so the contract is void.
This is consistent with the rule that the subject matter must be owned and deliverable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on selling umm walad, mudabbar, and mkatab, and explain why the mkatab's consent can validate the sale.

Core Idea
Selling slaves with a right to future freedom is void; but a mkatab can consent, rescinding his kitaba, to make the sale valid.

This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyirah clarifies that selling a slave who has a special right to freedom—whether as umm walad, mudabbar, or mkatab—is fundamentally invalid (batil). However, for the mkatab, if he freely consents to the sale, his consent effectively rescinds his contract of manumission (kitaba), thus removing the obstacle and allowing the sale to proceed. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the nuanced intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the mkatab's consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The matn states that selling umm walad, mudabbar, and mkatab is fasid. The commentary clarifies that 'fasid' here means 'batil' (void). Recognize that the ruling is absolute voidness, not merely voidable.
2
Understand the reason for voidness
Each of these slaves has a right to eventual freedom (umm walad upon master's death, mudabbar upon tadbir, mkatab upon paying kitaba). Selling them would extinguish that right without a valid cause, so the sale is void.

The passage from Al-Hidayah introduces an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: if the mkatab consents to being sold, there are two narrations. The more apparent (azhar) is permissibility. The reasoning is that his consent implies he rescinds the kitaba before the sale, thus removing the barrier and making the sale valid.

Distinguish the case of sale without consent
If the mkatab is sold without his consent and later ratifies the sale, the contract is not valid by a single narration (i.e., unanimously). Post-hoc ratification does not cure the defect because the consent must precede the contract to rescind the kitaba.
This highlights that the timing of consent is crucial.
5
Apply the ruling to practice
In a real scenario: a master sells his mkatab with the mkatab's expressed consent. According to the stronger Hanafi view, this sale is valid. If the mkatab later claims he did not consent, the burden of proof is on the buyer to prove consent.
This shows the fatwa hierarchy: the azhar view becomes mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A sale is fasid if delivery requires harming the remainder; cutting the item before rescission removes the defect and makes it sahih.

Selling an unspecified portion of a divisible object—like a cubit from a specific cloth or a beam from a given ceiling—raises a problem: the seller cannot deliver the exact portion without damaging the remaining property. The Hanafi school rules such a sale as fasid (defective) due to this inability to deliver without harm. However, if the seller physically separates the item (cuts the cloth or removes the beam) before the buyer cancels the transaction, the contract becomes valid. This principle is contrasted with cases where the sold item is not a tangible existing object (e.g., seeds inside fruit) or where delivery inevitably causes harm (e.g., wool on a live animal).

Understand that if the seller physically separates the sold item (cuts the cloth or removes the beam) before the buyer rescinds (faskh), the sale becomes sahih (valid) because the invalidating cause (mufsid) is removed.

بخلاف ما إذا باع النوى في التمر، أو البزر في البطيخ، حيث لا ينقلب صحيحاً وإن شقهما وأخرج المبيع؛ لأن في وجودهما احتمالاً. أما الجذع عين موجودة، بخلاف الصوف، فإنه لا ينقلب صحيحاً بالتسليم أيضاً؛ لأنه لا يخلو إما أن يكون تسليمه بالنتف، أو بالجز، فبالنتف: لا يجوز؛ لأن فيه ضرراً بالحيوان وبالجز: لا يمكن استيفاؤه وقد بقي منه شيء، فيحتاج إلى نتفه، وفيه ضرر بالحيوان.
And the sale of Muzabanah is not (valid), which is selling fruit on top of palm trees by estimation (kharṣ) as dried dates. Because (the seller) cannot deliver except with harm. So if the seller cuts the branch or uproots the trunk before the buyer cancels: it returns to being valid due to the removal of the corrupting element. Contrary to the case when he sells pits in dates or seeds in watermelon, where it does not become valid even if he splits them and removes the sold item, because there is probability in their existence. As for the trunk, it is an existing entity. Contrary to wool: it does not become valid by delivery either, because its delivery is either by plucking or by shearing. By plucking: not permissible because it harms the animal. By shearing: it cannot be fully taken while something remains, so it needs plucking, and that harms the animal.

Commentary
This passage explains the ruling of Muzabanah sale (selling fresh dates on trees by estimation for dried dates). The reason for prohibition is that delivery of the fruit involves cutting or harming the tree, which is not permissible. However, if the seller cuts the branch or uproots the trunk before the buyer cancels, the sale becomes valid because the cause of harm is removed. This is contrasted with selling pits in dates or seeds in watermelon, where even after extraction the sale remains invalid because the existence of the pits/seeds is uncertain. Similarly, wool cannot be delivered without some harm to the animal, so the sale remains invalid. The key point is that the validity depends on whether the corrupting element (harm/haram delivery) can be completely eliminated.

Key Terms
المزابنة (al-muzābanah): Sale of fresh fruit on trees by estimation for dried fruit of the same kind.
الخرص (al-kharṣ): Estimation of the amount of crop still on the tree.
الضرر (al-ḍarar): Harm, here referring to necessary harm in delivering the fruit.
الذّراع (al-dhirāʿ): Branch (of a palm tree); cutting it removes the harmful delivery.
الجذع (al-jadʿ): Trunk or base of a palm tree; uprooting it removes the harm.
الصوف (al-ṣūf): Wool; its delivery involves unavoidable harm, so the sale remains fasid.

Key Takeaway
A fasid sale caused by harm in delivery can become valid if the seller eliminates the harm before the buyer cancels, provided the harm is completely removable without residual damage.
