Releasing a Sick Debtor: Balancing Creditor Rights and Preservation of Life
Ruling on releasing a sick debtor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on releasing a sick debtor from prison, apply the qiyas used as proof, and distinguish the cases based on availability of a servant.

Core Idea
Preserving life overrides the creditor's right to imprison the debtor when death is feared.

This lesson examines a crucial intersection of debt law and the sanctity of life. The Hanafi school carefully balances the creditor's right to detain a debtor with the imperative to save life. The passage presents a clear ruling: a sick debtor without a servant may be released if death is feared, supported by a powerful analogy to the permissibility of taking another's property to prevent starvation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the debtor's situation
The passage distinguishes two scenarios: the debtor has a servant, or does not have one and death is feared. Note the ruling for each.
The presence of a servant changes the ruling because the servant can provide care, so release is not needed.
2
Extract the ruling and its proof
State the ruling: release is permissible (mubah) only when there is no servant and death is feared. Then locate the proof: a qiyas (analogy) based on the permissibility of using another's property to ward off death.
The proof is a fortiori argument: if taking property is allowed to save life, then releasing a debtor is even more justified.
3
Analyze the qiyas structure
Identify the asl (original case), far' (new case), and illa (effective cause). The asl is a starving person who may take another's property. The far' is the sick debtor facing death from imprisonment. The illa is the necessity to preserve life.
The illa is the need to save life, which is a higher objective (maqsad) of the Shari'ah.
4
Apply the ruling to modern analogues
Consider modern scenarios such as a debtor with a serious medical condition requiring outside treatment not available in prison, or a debtor who is a primary caregiver.
The principle of necessity (darurah) overrides financial claims.

وإذا مَرِضَ وأضناه المرض إن كان له خادم: لا يخرج ليزداد ضجراً، فيسارع بالقضاء، ولا يخرج للمداواة، ويُداوى في السجن. وإن لم يكن له خادمٌ وخُشي عليه الموت: فإنه يخرج؛ لأنه إذا خشي على نفسه الموت من الجوع كان له أن يدفعه بمال الغير، فكيف يجوز إهلاكه لأجل مال الغير؟! وإن احتاج إلى الجماع : فلا بأس أن تُدخل عليه امرأته أو جاريته فيطأها حيث لا يطلع عليه أحد. وفي النهاية»: إذا طلب المحبوس امرأته أو أمته إلى فراشه في الحبس: لم يُمنع إن كان في الحبس موضع خال، فإن امتنعت الزوجة: لم تُجبر، وإن امتنعت الأمة: أُجبرت. وإنما كان للزوجة الحرة أن تمتنع ؛ لأنه لا يصلح للسكنى، والزوجة الأمة تُجبر إذا رضي سيدها.
And when he is ill and the illness has weakened him: if he has a servant, he is not to be released so that he becomes more distressed, thus hastening to settle the debt; and he is not released for medical treatment, but is treated in prison. And if he has no servant and it is feared that he might die, then he is released; because if he fears death from hunger, he is allowed to ward it off with the property of another, so how can it be permissible to let him perish because of another's property? And if he needs intercourse, there is no harm in having his wife or slave-girl brought in to him for him to have intercourse with her where no one sees them. And in al-Nihayah: If the imprisoned person asks for his wife or his slave-girl to his bed in prison, he is not prevented if there is a private place in prison; if the wife refuses, she is not compelled; and if the slave-girl refuses, she is compelled. And the free wife may only refuse because the place is not suitable for dwelling, whereas the slave wife is compelled if her master consents.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof discussing the treatment of a debtor who falls ill while imprisoned. The passage presents a distinction based on whether the debtor has a servant. If he has a servant, he is not released to prevent him from becoming too comfortable; the distress of illness combined with imprisonment is intended to pressure him to pay his debt quickly. He is also not released for medical treatment; treatment is provided within the prison. However, if he has no servant and his illness is life-threatening, he is released. The reasoning is analogical (qiyas): just as a person facing starvation may lawfully take another's property to save his life, it is even more fitting to release him so he does not die due to the debt. The passage then addresses conjugal needs: it is permissible (la ba's) for the prisoner's wife or slave-girl to be brought to him in private for intercourse. The author cites al-Nihayah, which elaborates: the prisoner is not prevented if a private spot exists. The free wife may refuse because the prison is not a proper dwelling, but a slave-girl is compelled if her master (the prisoner's owner, not the prisoner himself) agrees. This shows that the free wife retains her right to refuse cohabitation in an unsuitable place, while the slave-girl has less autonomy.

Key Terms
أضناه المرض (adnāhu al-maraḍ): The illness has worn him out and made him weak to the point of being bedridden or close to death.
لا بأس (lā ba's): No harm; it is permissible (mubah) without being recommended or obligatory.

Conjugal Rights of an Imprisoned Husband: Free Wife vs Slave Wife
Ruling on conjugal visits for an imprisoned husband

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on permitting conjugal visits for an imprisoned husband, and distinguish the legal treatment of a free wife and a slave wife regarding compulsion.

Core Idea
A free wife may refuse conjugal visits in prison due to unsuitable dwelling; a slave wife can be compelled if her master agrees.

This lesson addresses the intimate needs of an imprisoned husband. The Hanafi school permits his wife or slave-girl to be brought to him in private. However, a critical distinction emerges: a free wife retains the right to refuse because the prison is not a proper marital dwelling, while a slave wife has less autonomy and may be compelled. This reflects the broader Hanafi approach to marital rights and the status of slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the permissibility and condition
Note that conjugal visits are permitted (la ba's) as long as privacy is ensured (no one sees them).
The permission is conditional on the availability of a private place in prison.
2
Distinguish free wife and slave wife
The passage from al-Nihayah states: if the wife is free, she may refuse and cannot be compelled; if she is a slave (umm walad or married slave), she may be compelled if her master (owner) consents.
The free wife's right to refuse is based on the prison not being a suitable dwelling (sukna).
3
Understand the legal basis for the distinction
A free wife has full marital rights, including the right to a proper home. A slave wife's rights are limited; her master's authority overrides her consent in certain matters.
This distinction is consistent with Hanafi slave law where a slave's marriage is subject to the master's permission.
4
Apply to scenarios
Consider different combinations: prisoner is a slave, or the wife is a free convert. Determine who has the right to refuse.
The ruling only addresses the wife's side; the husband's right to demand conjugal visits is not absolute if there is no privacy.
