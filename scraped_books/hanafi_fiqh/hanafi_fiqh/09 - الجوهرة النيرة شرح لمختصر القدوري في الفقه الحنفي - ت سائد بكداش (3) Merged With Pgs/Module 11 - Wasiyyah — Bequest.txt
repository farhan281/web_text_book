Bequeathing Charity in Sickness: Valid Within One-Third
Ruling on the interdicted person's will for charity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wills for charity by an interdicted sick person and distinguish between qurb and abwab al-khair.

Core Idea
A sick person's will for charity is valid within one-third because it is commanded and brings closeness to Allah.

In this passage, al-Quduri presents a key ruling about the financial capacity of an interdicted person in terminal illness. The author asserts that such a person may validly bequeath for charitable purposes up to one-third of his estate. The proof is based on the general command to make wills and the benefit of drawing closer to Allah. Additionally, the commentary clarifies the difference between 'qurb' (acts of worship via beneficial structures) and 'abwab al-khair' (all good deeds, including guarantees).

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject
The ruling applies to a person who is interdicted (محجور) due to terminal illness. Despite interdiction, the person can make a will for charitable purposes.
Interdiction normally prevents financial transactions, but the will for charity is an exception.
2
Determine the scope of the valid bequest
The bequest is valid only within one-third of the estate. Any excess is void unless heirs consent.
This is the standard one-third rule for wills in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the proof (dalil)
The proof is twofold: (1) The will is commanded by Allah (Quran 2:180 implies obligation to make bequests), so one should not be prevented; (2) The will brings closeness to Allah, so it serves the testator's spiritual benefit.
The proof is aqli (rational) supported by naqli (Quranic command).
4
Differentiate 'qurb' from 'abwab al-khair'
Qurb are acts that become worship indirectly, like building mosques or bridges. Abwab al-khair is more general, including qurb and other good deeds like financial guarantees (kafala, daman).
Understanding this distinction clarifies the scope of the ruling: any good deed falls under abwab al-khair.

Rulings on the Mahjur's Will and Dhihar with Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Commentary on mahjur's will and dhihar with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a mahjur's will and dhihar, and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the method of expiation for dhihar.

Core Idea
A mahjur's will and dhihar are valid, but expiation for dhihar depends on solvency—Abu Hanifa requires emancipation, Sahibayn allow fasting.

This lesson examines two related rulings: the validity of bequests (wasiyyah) by a person under interdiction (mahjur) and the validity of dhihar pronounced by such a person. The commentary from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains not only the basic rulings but also an important intra-Hanafi disagreement between the founder Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on whether fasting suffices for expiation when the mahjur is insolvent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its basic ruling
First, note that the mahjur’s will for charitable purposes is valid within one-third of his wealth, and his dhihar is valid and cannot be revoked. These are the foundational rulings.
2
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on expiation
The commentary states that the entire subsequent branching (about whether fasting suffices) is according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Abu Hanifa holds a different view: he treats the mahjur like a non-mahjur, so emancipation is required if possible.
This is a legitimate difference of opinion within the Hanafi school.
3
Follow the Sahibayn's reasoning
According to the Sahibayn, the mahjur is effectively insolvent because he cannot use his wealth. Therefore, fasting is initially sufficient for expiation. If he later becomes solvent, then emancipation becomes necessary.
The analogy: like a poor person who fasts and then acquires wealth.
4
Follow Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that the mahjur is not necessarily insolvent; the interdiction is a temporary restriction. Therefore, if he has wealth (even if withheld), he must free a slave. Fasting is only for those truly unable to free a slave.
This view emphasizes the actual ability rather than the legal restriction.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The key difference: under the Sahibayn, a mahjur who fasts and later becomes solvent must still free a slave because the initial fasting is only valid due to the impediment. Under Abu Hanifa, if the mahjur had the means at the time of dhihar, he must free immediately; if he truly had no means, fasting suffices.
The practical difference lies in the treatment of subsequent solvency.

Iqrar of a Sick Person to an Heir: Invalid Unless Ratified
States the ruling on iqrar of sick to heir with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on iqrar of a sick person to an heir and apply the exception of ratification by other heirs.

Core Idea
A sick person's iqrar to an heir is void unless other heirs agree — it is treated like a prohibited bequest.

In Hanafi law, a person in marad al-mawt (deathly illness) cannot validly acknowledge a right (iqrar) in favor of his own heir without the consent of the other heirs. This is because such an iqrar is analogous to a bequest to an heir, which is explicitly prohibited by the hadith. The same rule applies to gifts and bequests. Understanding this principle protects the rights of all heirs from potential fraud and ensures equitable distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The iqrar (acknowledgment) of a person in marad al-mawt (fatal illness) to his own heir. The ruling is that it is batil (void).
The act must be performed during a death-connected illness; iqrar in health is valid regardless of the recipient.
2
State the exception
The iqrar becomes valid if the rest of the heirs (who would be affected) confirm (saddaqa) it. Their consent lifts the prohibition.
The same exception applies to hibah (gift) and wasiyyah (bequest) to an heir.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The primary evidence is the hadith: 'La wasiyya li-warith' (No bequest to an heir). This is a hadith ahad, transmitted via multiple chains, with zanni thubut but qati dalala.
The hadith directly prohibits bequests to heirs; iqrar is included by qiyas (analogy) because it similarly deprives other heirs.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal reasoning)
The phrase 'la wasiyya' is a negation of permissibility. By analogy (qiyas), iqrar and hibah during death illness are treated like wasiyyah because all three involve transferring wealth to an heir, potentially harming other heirs.
The illa (effective cause) is the potential deprivation of other heirs' rightful shares.
5
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly difference)
The Hanafi position is that iqrar to an heir is invalid without ratification. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that iqrar is valid even without ratification, as it is an acknowledgment of a pre-existing right, not a new transfer.
The practical difference: under Shafi'i law, no consent needed; under Hanafi, consent is required.

Timing of Heir Status: Iqrar vs Wasiyyah
Defines when heir status is considered for iqrar and wasiyyah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when heir status is considered for iqrar and wasiyyah and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Heir status is determined at the time of iqrar for acknowledgments, but at death for bequests.

In Hanafi inheritance law, the timing for determining whether a person is an heir depends on the legal act involved. This distinction is crucial because an iqrar (acknowledgment) to an heir is invalid unless ratified by other heirs, while a bequest to an heir is invalid outright. The text from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira establishes that for iqrar, heir status is assessed at the moment of acknowledgment; for wasiyyah, it is assessed at the time of death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act
First, determine whether the act is an iqrar (acknowledgment) or a wasiyyah (bequest). This determines which rule applies.
Iqrar typically occurs during death illness (marad al-mawt) but can be general.
2
Recall the timing rule for that act
For iqrar: consider heir status at the time the iqrar is made. For wasiyyah: consider heir status at the time of death.
These are fixed rules; no other timing is considered.
3
Apply to a concrete scenario
Take a scenario: A dying man acknowledges a debt to his brother, who is his heir at that moment. The brother later renounces inheritance. Since heir status is judged at iqrar time, the iqrar remains subject to the rules of iqrar to an heir (requires ratification). Conversely, if a bequest is made to the same brother, his heir status at death is decisive—if he is an heir at death, the bequest is invalid.
This illustrates the practical effect of the rule.

This affects validity: if a person acknowledges a debt to someone who is his heir at the time of iqrar but later ceases to be an heir (e.g., due to renunciation), the iqrar is still invalid for lack of ratification. Similarly, a bequest to someone who is an heir at death is invalid even if they were not an heir when the bequest was made.

Iqrar to a Non-Heir during Death Illness
Ruling on iqrar to a non-heir during death illness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on iqrar to a non-heir during death illness and explain why it is valid even if it consumes the entire estate.

Core Idea
Iqrar to a non-heir is valid without limit because it confirms a pre-existing debt, not a new gift.

This lesson focuses on a specific ruling from Al-Hidayah: a person in death illness may acknowledge a debt to a non-heir, and it is valid even if it consumes all his wealth. This contrasts with gratuitous acts, which are limited. Understand why iqrar is treated differently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act as iqrar
Iqrar (acknowledgment) is a confirmation of a pre-existing right, not a gratuitous transfer. Confirm that the act is indeed an iqrar (e.g., 'I owe you $100') and not a gift.
Iqrar must be clear and unequivocal.
2
Verify the recipient is a non-heir
The recipient must be ajnabi (not an heir). Iqrar to an heir is invalid unless accepted by other heirs.
An heir is someone who would inherit from the sick person at the time of death.
3
Apply the ruling
The iqrar is valid (ja'iz) even if it encompasses the entire estate. There is no limit because it does not deplete the estate; it merely confirms an existing liability.
4
Understand the rationale
Iqrar does not harm heirs because it only recognizes a debt that was already owed. Heirs cannot inherit property that was never owned by the deceased; debts take priority over inheritance.

Ibra and the One-Third Rule for Tabarru'at
Rulings on ibra attributed to health and the one-third rule for tabarru'at.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a claimed past ibra during death illness is invalid and apply the one-third rule to various gratuitous acts.

Core Idea
Any gratuitous act during death illness, including ibra, counts against the one-third limit; a claim of past discharge is treated as a present ibra.

This lesson examines two interconnected rulings from Al-Hidayah: first, the invalidity of a sick person's claim to have discharged a debtor in the past; second, the general rule that all gratuitous acts (tabarru'at) during death illness are limited to one-third of the estate. Learn how the law prevents fraud and protects heirs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the claim of past ibra
A sick person says, 'I discharged so-and-so from his debt during my health.' This claim is not accepted because the sick person does not own the discharge at the present time and cannot unilaterally create a past ibra.
The claim is based solely on his word; there is no evidence.
2
Treat it as a present ibra
We rule that the ibra exists in the present moment (during death illness) and therefore it is subject to the one-third rule as a gratuitous act.
3
Apply the one-third rule to all tabarru'at
The text states that all tabarru'at during death illness are considered from the one-third share. This includes gifts, manumissions, tadbir, favor in sales, and ibra.
Tabarru'at are acts that transfer a benefit without compensation.
4
Recognize exceptions
Iqrar (acknowledgment) to a non-heir is not a tabarru' and thus not limited. Also, tabarru'at can be validated if the heirs consent to the excess.

Joint vs Separate Appointment in Agency
Ruling on two agents appointed jointly cannot act alone; exception if appointed separately.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between joint and separate appointment in agency and apply the ruling to determine when an agent may act individually.

Core Idea
Two agents appointed together must act jointly; if appointed separately, each may act alone.

In Hanafi fiqh, the power of an agent depends on the wording of their appointment. This lesson examines a key distinction: when two agents are appointed in a single statement, they form a joint authority and cannot act independently. However, if the principal appoints them in separate statements, each gains independent authority. The rule changes for executors (wasiyyan), who must always act jointly, even if appointed separately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the method of appointment
Read the principal's words: Were the two agents appointed in one sentence (e.g., 'I appoint you both to sell...') or in two separate sentences?
The key is the unity or separation of the appointing statement.
2
Determine the ruling for agency
If appointment is in one statement, neither agent may act alone; both must act together. If appointment is in separate statements, any one agent may act individually.
The exception applies only to agency (wakalah), not to executors (wasiyyah).
3
Compare with executors
For executors (wasiyyan), even if the testator appoints each separately in the will, they must still act jointly. This is because the will takes effect at death, and at that moment they become a single joint entity.
The underlying rationale is that the testator's death creates a unified executorship.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a situation, determine whether the agents or executors can act alone. Check the wording: single vs. multiple statements, and whether it's agency or executorship.
If the text says 'wasiyyan' (executors), the rule is stricter: always joint action.
