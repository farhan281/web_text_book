Types of Istibra': Mustahabb for the Seller, Wajib for the Buyer
Defines istibra' and distinguishes types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istibra', distinguish between the seller's (mustahabb) and buyer's (wajib) istibra', and state the hadith evidence for the obligation.

Core Idea
Istibra' is wajib for the buyer to ensure the womb is empty, but only mustahabb for the seller.

The concept of istibra' is a crucial legal mechanism in Hanafi fiqh to prevent confusion of lineage when a female slave is transferred. This passage, though not originally in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, is added by the commentator as indispensable. It clearly divides istibra' into two categories based on who performs it: the seller or the buyer. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rulings in transactions involving slave women.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of istibra'
Read the passage carefully. Istibra' is a waiting period of one menstrual cycle for a slave woman after separation from her owner, to verify that she is not pregnant.
The term literally means 'seeking to be free of pregnancy'.
2
Identify the two types based on who performs it
The author states istibra' is of two types: mustahabb (recommended) for the seller and wajib (obligatory) for the buyer. Note the reasoning: the seller performs it to avoid selling his own offspring; the buyer performs it to ensure he does not have intercourse with a pregnant woman.
The seller's istibra' is not required for validity of the sale, but it is highly recommended.
3
Locate the evidence for the buyer's obligation
The passage quotes the Prophet's (ﷺ) statement regarding the captives of Awtas: 'Beware! Do not have intercourse with a pregnant woman until she delivers, nor with a non-pregnant woman until...' This hadith is the basis for the wajib ruling.
The hadith is ahad (singular transmission) and its indication is zanni, but it is accepted as evidence in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Analyze the exception for marriage
If the owner marries off his slave girl without istibra', the husband may have intercourse without waiting. This exception shows that the obligation to wait is specific to purchase; marriage grants immediate right to intercourse.
The reasoning is that in marriage, the risk of mixing lineage is mitigated by other factors (e.g., iddah after divorce).

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of istibra', which is a waiting period imposed on a slave woman after being transferred to a new owner, to ascertain that she is not pregnant from the previous owner. The author divides istibra' into two categories: mustahabb (recommended) for the seller — meaning it is not obligatory but meritorious for the original owner to wait before concluding the sale or marriage; and wajib (obligatory) for the buyer — the new owner must wait one menstrual period before having intercourse. The hadith from the captives of Awtas (a battle) is quoted as evidence for the obligation. The seller's istibra' is recommended to avoid the risk of selling one's own offspring unknowingly. The buyer's istibra' is obligatory to ensure the womb is empty. Importantly, if the seller does not perform istibra' and the buyer takes possession, the buyer still must perform wajib istibra' before intercourse; the buyer does not rely on the seller's actions. The passage also notes that if the owner marries off his slave girl without istibra', the husband may have intercourse without needing to wait, because in marriage the right of intercourse is immediate and the risk of mixing lineage is less of a concern (or perhaps the istibra' is already assumed by waiting after divorce? But the text says 'لا استبراء' for the husband). This is a specific ruling.

Key Terms
استبراء (istibra'): A waiting period of one menstrual cycle for a slave woman after transfer to a new owner to ensure she is not pregnant from the previous owner.
مدبرة (mudabbara): A slave woman who has been promised freedom upon her master's death.
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who has borne a child to her master, becoming free upon his death.

The Obligation of Istibra' and Its Rulings
Rulings on istibra' for newly acquired female slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of istibra' for a newly acquired female slave, identify its cause and period, and apply the condition that possession must precede the counting of a menstrual cycle.

Core Idea
Istibra' is wajib upon acquiring a female slave; a menstruation before taking possession does not count.

When a person acquires a female slave, the Hanafi school requires a waiting period called istibra' to ensure she is not pregnant from a previous owner. This lesson explains the obligation, the method based on her condition, and the critical timing of possession. We will also examine why a menstruation that occurs before taking physical delivery does not fulfill the requirement, according to the majority of the Hanafi imams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Here it is 'istibra'' when a person acquires a female slave. The ruling is 'wajib' (obligatory). The text states 'فوجب على كل من ملك جارية أن لا يقربها حتى يستبرئها'.
The obligation is established by analogy to 'iddah or by ahad hadith, according to Hanafi usul.
2
Determine the method of istibra' based on the slave's condition
The text specifies three scenarios: if she menstruates → one menstrual period (haydah); if she does not menstruate (e.g., young or old) → one month; if she is pregnant → until delivery. Note that the period is uniform regardless of the method of acquisition (sale, gift, inheritance, etc.).
This is the standard Hanafi position.
3
Analyze the condition of possession (qabd)
The cause (sabab) of istibra' is the acquisition of both ownership (milk) and physical possession (yad). Therefore, a menstruation that occurs before taking possession does not count. This is a key conditional element. The text explicitly states that the ruling cannot precede the cause.
Abu Yusuf disagrees, holding that ownership alone suffices in purchase, inheritance, and bequest. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4
Identify the prohibitions during the istibra' period
During istibra', the master is prohibited from kissing, touching with desire, looking at the private parts with desire, and embracing. These are incitements (dawa'i) to intercourse, and when the act itself is prohibited, its incitements are also prohibited. The text analogizes to zihar and 'iddah.
This is based on the principle 'ما حرم فعله حرم فعله وحرم دواعيه'.
5
Apply the rules to special cases
The text covers partial ownership (half then remaining half), fasid (invalid) sale, and return to seller after fasid sale. In each case, the previous menstruation is not counted if it occurred before full possession or under a voidable ownership. The prohibition during istibra' is a right of Allah, so even after return, the original seller must perform istibra' again.
These cases illustrate the strict requirement that the cause be complete.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement on the obligation of istibra' when a person acquires a female slave. The author begins by stating that it is obligatory (wajib) for anyone who owns a female slave not to have intercourse with her until he performs istibra' with one menstrual cycle. This applies regardless of the method of acquisition—sale, gift, charity, bequest, inheritance, khul', kitabah, or due to a crime—and regardless of whether the seller is a woman or a minor. The reason given is that the cause (sabab) of istibra' is the acquisition of new ownership. The method of istibra' depends on the slave's condition: if she menstruates, one menstrual period; if she does not (e.g., due to age), one month; if pregnant, until delivery. The author then clarifies that a menstruation occurring before taking possession (qabd) does not count, except according to Abu Yusuf, who considers it sufficient in purchase, inheritance, and bequest. During the istibra' period, the master is prohibited from kissing, touching with desire, looking at the private parts with desire, or embracing, because these are incitements to intercourse—when intercourse is prohibited, its incitements are also prohibited. This is analogized to the prohibition of enjoyment during zihar and 'iddah. The passage then covers special cases: if someone owns half a slave, she menstruates, then he owns the other half, he must start a new istibra'; if istibra' was by delivery, he may enjoy her (except intercourse) during nifas; if a slave is bought with a fasid (invalid) sale and possession taken, intercourse is prohibited even if she menstruates, and later a valid purchase does not count the previous menstruation; if the judge rescinds the fasid sale and returns her to the seller, the seller must perform istibra' again because the prohibition was for Allah's right.

Key Terms
الاستبراء (al-istibra'): A waiting period imposed upon a person who acquires a female slave to ensure that she is not pregnant from a previous owner.
حيضة (haydah): One menstrual period, used as the standard period for istibra' for a menstruating slave girl.
قبض (qabd): Taking physical possession of the purchased item, which is a condition for ownership in a fasid sale and for the cause of istibra'.
استحداث الملك (istihdath al-milk): The acquisition of new ownership, which is the primary cause (sabab) of istibra'.
دواعي الجماع (dawa'i al-jima'): Incitements to intercourse, such as kissing, touching with desire, and embracing, which are prohibited during the istibra' period.

Marriage and Mahr of the Mahjur (Legally Restricted Person)
Rulings on validity of marriage and mahr by a legally restricted person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of marriage and mahr for a mahjur, and explain the limits based on mahr al-mithl and the prohibition on marrying off others.

Core Idea
A mahjur may marry himself and set a mahr, but only up to mahr al-mithl; excess is void, and he cannot marry off his daughter or sister.

We have discussed the mahjur's general financial restrictions. But what about marriage? Marriage is a primary need and not a purely financial transaction. The Hanafi school balances the mahjur's need for companionship with the protection of his wealth and the rights of others. This lesson dissects the rulings on the validity of his marriage and the limits on his dower specification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a mahjur (legally restricted person) entering a marriage contract. The ruling is: the marriage is valid (sahih). Confirm that validity applies even if he specifies a dower.
The text explicitly says: 'جاز نكاحه' (his marriage is valid).
2
Locate the dalil (proof) for validity
The proof cited from al-Hidayah: marriage is not affected by jest (الهزل) and it is among his primary needs (حوائجه الأصلية). This establishes that the mahjur's capacity for marriage is separate from his financial interdiction.
The argument type is talil (reasoning), not a direct textual proof.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on marrying off others
Note that Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the mahjur may marry himself but cannot give his daughter or sister in marriage. This is the mufta bihi position. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf are silent here, but the text relies on Muhammad's view.
The reason: he is under interdiction regarding the rights of others (محجور عليه في حق غيره).
4
Examine the mahr ruling
If the mahjur specifies a mahr above mahr al-mithl (proper dower), only the amount equal to mahr al-mithl is valid; the excess is void. This is the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn). The reasoning: the vulva (right to intercourse) is compensable, and mahr al-mithl is the proper exchange. The mahjur's authority over wealth is limited.
If he divorces before consummation, half of the named mahr is due, but capped at mahr al-mithl.
5
Apply to the mufsida (corrupt woman) case
If a woman under interdiction due to her own corruption (mufsida) marries a suitable man with a mahr equal to mahr al-mithl or with a negligible disparity (taghabun), the marriage is valid. This confirms that marriage is not voided by interdiction as long as the financial terms are fair.

Marriage of an Interdicted Person (Mahjur) and Dowry Limit
Ruling on marriage and dowry for a person under financial interdiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of marriage of a mahjur and explain why the excess dowry is void.

Core Idea
An interdicted person's marriage is valid, but any dowry above mahr al-mithl is void.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common question: can a person under financial interdiction (mahjur) marry? The answer is yes, but with a key limitation on the dowry. Understanding this ruling reveals the Hanafi distinction between financial and personal capacities, and the role of istihsan in deriving fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The marriage contract of a mahjur. What is the ruling? 'Jaza nikaahuhu' – his marriage is valid (sahih).
Also note he may marry up to four women.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The author cites two reasons: (1) jest (hazl) does not affect the validity of marriage, and (2) marriage is among his original necessities (hawa'ij asliyya). This is an istihsan reasoning.
This shows that interdiction (hajr) is financial, not personal.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the mahjur may marry himself but may not marry his daughter or sister because he is interdicted regarding others. The main position allows him to marry himself but with limitation on dowry.
This is an ikhtilaf within the school; the mufta bihi follows Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
4
Identify the dowry ruling
If a mahjur specifies a dowry, the amount up to mahr al-mithl (proper dowry) is valid, and any excess (fadl) is batil (void).
This is the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (qawlahuma).
5
Trace the reasoning for the dowry limit
The excess is void because the vulva is valued, and mahr al-mithl is the proper substitute. The divorce ruling: if divorced before consummation, half the specified amount (within limit) is due.
The specification is valid up to mahr al-mithl, so half of that amount is owed.

Marriage of an Interdicted Woman and Dowry Conditions
Rulings on a woman under interdiction marrying with proper or inadequate dowry

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the marriage of an interdicted woman (mufsida) and explain the consequences of deficient dowry and non-kufu marriage.

Core Idea
An interdicted woman can marry a compatible man, but dowry deficiencies may require completion or separation.

This passage from Al-Hidayah examines the marriage of a woman under financial interdiction (mufsida). The Hanafi school confirms that interdiction does not invalidate marriage. However, the dowry must be proper or the deficiency must be trivial. If the deficiency is significant, the husband gets an option before consummation. After consummation, he must pay the proper dowry. If she marries a non-compatible man, the judge can annul to protect the family's honor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Determine that the woman is mufsida (interdicted due to wasteful behavior). Her marital capacity is intact but subject to conditions.
The husband may also be interdicted, which affects the dowry treatment.
2
Assess compatibility and dowry
Check if the husband is kufu (compatible in lineage, religion, etc.) and if the dowry is mahr al-mithl or a deficiency customarily overlooked (yataghāban).
If the deficiency is within yataghāban, the marriage is valid without further remedy.
3
Determine consequences for deficient dowry
If the deficiency is not trivial and not consummated, the husband may complete the dowry or separate. If consummated, he must complete the proper dowry.
The husband's option is a form of remedial justice, not automatic annulment.
4
Handle the case of both spouses interdicted
If both are interdicted, the same rules apply: excess over proper dowry is void, deficiency requires completion or separation.
The interdiction does not affect the logic of dowry adjustment.
5
Apply the rule for non-kufu marriage
If the woman marries a non-compatible man, the judge may annul the marriage to protect the guardians' honor, even if the dowry is proper.
This is to prevent harm (shain) to the family.

Khul' of an Interdicted Woman: Validity and Effect
Ruling on khul' initiated by a woman under interdiction and its legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khul' of an interdicted woman and distinguish between the effects of talaq vs khul' wording.

Core Idea
Khul' by an interdicted woman is valid, but the compensation is a void donation — the wording determines if the divorce is raj'i or ba'in.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a specific scenario: a woman under financial interdiction (mufsida) initiates khul' by offering compensation to her husband. The ruling separates the validity of the divorce from the enforceability of the financial condition, and further explains how the wording used affects the revocability of the divorce. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying khul' in cases where one party has limited legal capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its core ruling
Focus on the first sentence: 'ولو أنها اختلعت من زوجها بمال: جاز الخلع، ولم يلزمها المال'. The act is khul' initiated by an interdicted woman. The ruling has two parts: the khul' itself is permitted (valid), but the financial compensation is not binding on her.
2
Analyze the reasoning for invalid compensation
The text explains: 'لأن خروج البضع من ملك الزوج لا قيمة له، فصارت ببذل المال متبرعةً، وتبرعها : لا يجوز.' The wife's 'bidding' (vulva) has no market value; by paying money she is making a donation (tabarru'), which is invalid due to her interdiction. The compensation is thus void.
The invalidity of donation by a mahjur is a standard Hanafi rule.
3
Understand the validity of khul' itself
The khul' is valid because the husband suspends the divorce on her acceptance (ta'liq al-talaq bi qabuliha). This is analogous to suspending divorce on entering a house. The condition (acceptance) occurred, so the divorce takes effect regardless of the financial condition.
The condition is qabul (acceptance), not the payment. The payment is a separate invalid condition.
4
Distinguish between wording: talaq vs khul'
If the husband used the word 'talaq' (one divorce), the divorce is raj'i because only the pure divorce remains after the void condition. If he used the word 'khul'', it is ba'in because khul' inherently implies a ba'in divorce. Contrast with a female slave: her divorce is ba'in even with talaq wording because she is interdicted differently.
This distinction is unique to the interdicted woman; a normal woman's khul' with talaq wording may also be ba'in if the compensation is valid. Here, because compensation is void, the wording is decisive.

A woman under interdiction's khul' is valid as divorce, but she is not liable for the property because she cannot donate.

No Pre-emption in Dower or Khul' Houses
Ruling excluding houses from pre-emption in dower and khul'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that pre-emption (shuf'ah) does not apply to houses given as dower or in khul', and explain the legal reason based on the condition of property exchange.

Core Idea
Pre-emption requires property-for-property exchange; dower and khul' are not property, so no shuf'ah.

Pre-emption (shuf'ah) is a right to take over a sold property from a co-owner. However, this right does not extend to every transfer of property; it only applies to commutative property exchanges. In this lesson, we examine why houses given as dower in marriage or as compensation in khul' divorce are not subject to pre-emption. We also explore a branch case where a cash condition is attached, clarifying that the subsidiary sale element does not trigger shuf'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transactions
The passage lists two transactions: a house given as dower (mahr) in marriage, and a house given as consideration in khul' divorce. Note that both originate from marital/family law, not sales.
2
Locate the underlying principle (talil)
The text states the reason: 'because pre-emption is only obligatory in exchange of property for property'. This is the key condition for shuf'ah to exist.
This principle is derived from the definition of shuf'ah.
3
Analyze why dower and khul' are not property exchanges
Dower is a marital obligation, not a price; khul' compensation is for separation, not a sale. They are not 'mal' (wealth/property) in the sense of a commutative contract.
The Hanafi school emphasizes the nature of the transaction.
4
Examine the branch case with conditional cash return
If a dower includes a condition that the wife returns cash, there is a sale-like element, but it is subsidiary (tabi') to the marriage. Since the original contract does not carry shuf'ah, the subsidiary also does not.
Abu Hanifa's view: the subsidiary follows the original (al-tabi' tabi').
5
Conclude the Hanafi ruling
No pre-emption in any of these scenarios. The reason is consistent: the transaction lacks the essential property-for-property exchange.
This is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.

Nature of 'Inan Partnership and Profit Distribution
Explains 'inan partnership as agency-based and addresses profit-sharing disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the basis of 'inan partnership (agency) and apply the Hanafi ruling that profit sharing may be unequal even if capital is equal.

Core Idea
Profit can be earned by work and skill, not just capital; so unequal profit shares are valid.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the foundational nature of 'inan partnership. Unlike mufawadah, which requires equal capital and mutual suretyship, 'inan is built on agency. This simple basis allows flexibility in capital and profit sharing. The text addresses a key dispute: can partners who contribute equal capital agree to share profits unequally? The Hanafi school answers yes, while Zufar and al-Shafi'i disagree. Understanding this ruling clarifies how Islamic partnerships can accommodate varying levels of work and skill.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal basis of 'inan partnership
The text states 'inan is concluded on the basis of agency (wakalah), not suretyship (kafalah). Each partner acts as an agent for the other in partnership transactions.
This means even a minor or slave permitted to trade can enter an 'inan partnership because they can give agency, even if they cannot guarantee debts.
2
Understand the two-step formation process
The partnership requires first a partnership of ownership (by mixing property) and then a contractual partnership to establish agency. The example from Al-Hidayah shows how unequal capitals are adjusted before contracting.
The need for the second contract ensures each partner is explicitly an agent for the other.
3
State the Hanafi position on profit distribution when capital is equal
The text rules that partners may be equal in capital but differ in profit share. This is permissible.
4
Analyze the proof against the opposing view
The Hanafi proof: profit can be earned by capital or by work, as shown in mudarabah. Since each alone justifies profit, combining both also justifies profit. Additionally, one partner may be more skilled, so equality would be unfair.
This is a qiyas to mudarabah, a known partnership form where the worker's effort is compensated with a share of profit.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario where only one partner works
Even if only one partner works on the entire capital, profit is divided as agreed, as if both worked. The stipulation governs, not actual work.
This reinforces that profit sharing is based on agreement, not on actual labor contribution.

Agent as Mere Intermediary: Rights Attach to the Principal
Rulings on agency in marriage, khul', and blood money: the agent is a safīr mahḍ.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that in contracts of marriage, khul', and sulh over blood money, the agent is a safīr maḥḍ and all rights and liabilities attach to the principal, and distinguish scenarios where the agent may become personally liable.

Core Idea
In certain contracts, the agent is only a messenger – all rights and duties belong to the principal alone.

When a person appoints an agent to conclude a marriage or khul' divorce, is that agent personally responsible for the dower or compensation? The Hanafi school answers with a clear principle: the agent is a pure intermediary (safīr mahḍ), so rights and obligations flow only to the principal. However, there are nuances when the agent gives a personal guarantee or when the wording of the contract is ambiguous.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contracts where the agent is a safīr mahḍ
From the text: marriage (nikāḥ), khul', and conciliation regarding deliberate bloodshed (ṣulḥ ʿan dam al-ʿamd). For these contracts, the agent must explicitly attribute the act to the principal.
The list is not exhaustive – it covers contracts where identity of the principal matters.
2
State the legal consequence of safīr mahḍ
All rights (e.g., the dower, the right to delivery of the wife) and obligations (e.g., payment of blood money) belong to the principal, not to the agent.
The agent cannot be sued for the dower or forced to hand over the wife.
3
Explain the reasoning: comparison to a messenger (rasūl)
If the agent attributed the contract to himself, it would be his own. Since he cannot dispense with attributing to the principal, he is like a mere messenger.
The ḥikma (wisdom) is to prevent confusion and protect the agent's liability.
4
Analyse the exception: father marrying off his minor son
When a father (acting as guardian) accepts an offer made directly to his son, the marriage is valid for the son even if the father does not explicitly say 'for my son'. The offer's wording ('to your son') determines the result.
This is not a case of agency (wakāla) but of guardianship (wilāya).
5
Distinguish the agent's liability in khul' with a guarantee
If the agent of the woman guarantees (ḍamān) the compensation, he becomes liable on the guarantee, not on the contract itself. The contract remains between the spouses.
The agent's liability here is secondary – it arises from the guarantee, not the agency.
