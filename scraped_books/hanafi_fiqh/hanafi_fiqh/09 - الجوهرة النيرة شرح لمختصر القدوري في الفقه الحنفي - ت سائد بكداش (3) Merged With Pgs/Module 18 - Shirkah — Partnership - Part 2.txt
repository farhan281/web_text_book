Invalidity of Partnership in Collecting Mubahat
Ruling and proof for invalidity of partnership in mubahat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partnership in collecting mubahat and explain why agency over mubah is invalid.

Core Idea
Partnership in collecting free goods is void because one cannot appoint an agent over what he does not own.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: partnership in gathering firewood, grass, or hunting is invalid. The reasoning goes to the heart of Hanafi contract law—partnership implies agency, and agency over ownerless things is legally impossible. We will unpack the proof and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is partnership (shirkah) in collecting mubahat (firewood, grass, hunting). The ruling is 'la tajuz' (invalid).
The text explicitly states 'لا تجوز الشركة في الاحتطاب والاصطياد والاحتشاش'.
2
Understand the legal nature of partnership
In Hanafi law, partnership entails mutual agency (wakalah). Each partner acts as an agent for the other regarding the partnership property.
This is a foundational principle: al-shirkah tataḍammanu maʿnā al-wakālah.
3
Analyze agency over mubah
Agency requires the principal to have ownership or authority over the subject matter. Mubahat are ownerless; before taking possession, the principal has no valid command. Hence, appointing an agent to collect mubah is void.
The text says 'التوكيل في أخذ المباح باطل'.
4
Apply the analogy (qiyas)
Since partnership includes agency, and agency over mubah is void, partnership in collecting mubah is void by logical extension.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
5
Determine the consequence
Each person owns what he collects individually. If they mix the collected items, it becomes co-ownership by mixing (not partnership). The price is divided according to weight/volume or value.
The sharh elaborates on the mixing scenario and rules for division.

Compensation for Assisting in Mubahat and Joint Hunting with a Dog
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compensation for assistance in mubahat and joint dog hunting rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the compensation rule for assisting in collecting mubahat and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the wage cap, and apply the ruling on joint dog release.

Core Idea
Assistance in mubahat earns wage of the like; Abu Yusuf caps at half value, Muhammad allows full wage.

This lesson examines rulings on compensation when one partner assists another in collecting mubahat (ownerless goods like firewood). The text presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between the two leading students of Abu Hanifa: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding their differing views on whether the assistant's wage should be capped at half the value of the collected items is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi partnership law. The lesson also clarifies a separate rule about joint hunting with a dog.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two subcases
Note the scenario: one partner works (e.g., cuts wood) and the other assists (ties bundles). Also the case where assistance is setting a net but nothing is caught.
2
State the compensation rule and the dispute
The assistant is entitled to ujr al-mithl (market wage). Abu Yusuf caps this at half the value of the collected items; Muhammad allows full wage without limit.
Intra-Hanafi dispute: both are from the same school.
3
Identify the consensus case
When the net catches nothing of value, both scholars agree the assistant gets full wage.
Consensus (ijma') within the Hanafi school.
4
Derive the rule for joint dog release
When both partners release a dog together, the catch belongs exclusively to the dog's owner because the non-owner's release is ineffective when the owner also releases.
This reinforces the principle that ownership in mubahat is based on one's own action.
5
Evaluate the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the collected wood sells for 100 and the market wage for assistance is 60, Abu Yusuf would limit the assistant to 50, while Muhammad would give the full 60.
The mufta bihi position is Muhammad's view in later Hanafi works.

Core Idea
The owner's release is the only valid cause of acquisition; the non-owner's release is legally irrelevant.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in partnership (shirkah): one partner owns a hunting dog, but both partners release it together on game. Who owns the catch? The text provides a clear ruling based on the principle that only the owner's release has legal effect, drawing an analogy (qiyas) to other cases where a non-owner's participation is disregarded.

The reasoning given is: 'because the release by a non-owner is not considered alongside the owner's release.' This is a qiyas (analogy): just as in other areas where a non-owner's action is ineffective when the owner acts, here the non-owner's release is irrelevant.

Since the non-owner's release has no legal weight, the dog's action is attributed solely to the owner. Thus, the catch is the owner's property.
This applies even if the non-owner physically released the dog first or simultaneously.

If the dog were jointly owned, the ruling might differ, but that scenario is not covered here. This ruling is specific to sole ownership.
The principle is that ownership of the means of hunting determines ownership of the game.

Invalid Partnership for Mubah Property
Explains why partnership for mubah is invalid and the compensation rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for partnership involving mubah property, explain the reason for invalidity via qiyas, and determine the compensation owed for use of a partner's asset under a void contract.

Core Idea
Partnership to acquire mubah is invalid; earnings belong to the worker, but market hire is owed for using the partner's property.

This lesson examines a specific partnership case from the Jawahirah al-Nayirah sharh: two partners contribute a mule and a water-carrying animal to draw water from a free source and share the earnings. The text rules the partnership invalid and outlines the compensation owed. We will unpack the reasoning (talil) behind both the invalidity and the wage obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a partnership where each partner contributes an animal to draw water from a mubah source (like a river), sharing earnings. The ruling: the partnership is invalid (fasidah).

2
Locate the dalil for invalidity
The commentator states the invalidity is because the contract is 'contracted on acquiring mubah (free property), which is water.' This is a qiyas: the asl is that partnership requires the subject to be a valid owned commodity; water before acquisition is not owned.
The illa: the subject is mubah, not owned by either partner at time of contract.
3
Apply the rule: compensation for use of partner's property
The worker uses the other partner's animal without a valid contract. Since the partnership is void, the use is a violation of property rights. Therefore, the worker must pay the market hire (ajr mithl) for the animal's usufruct.
Who owes whom depends on who performed the work.
4
Determine the compensation direction
If the mule owner drew the water, he owes the hire of the water-carrying beast. If the beast owner drew, he owes the hire of the mule. This ensures no unjust enrichment.
Compensation is for the specific animal used.
5
Summarize the legal consequences
The partnership is invalid, so no profit sharing. The worker owns the water (mubah) upon acquisition, but must compensate the other for the use of his property under a void contract.
Principle: void contract does not transfer ownership, but requires restitution.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the second agent is only liable if he knew of the prior payment; if unaware, he is not liable because he performed the task he was ordered (transferring ownership to the poor). For simultaneous payments, both agents are liable for the other's share (each must compensate the other). The same ruling applies to a person ordered to pay zakat who gives charity to a poor person after the orderer has already paid zakat himself. The Sahibayn analogize to the case of a person ordered to slaughter a sacrifice for ihsar (being prevented from completing Hajj): if the sacrifice is performed after the ihsar has been lifted and the orderer has already performed Hajj, the agent is not liable because he did what he could. Abu Hanifa argues that the agent's action did not achieve the required zakat (due to the principal's prior payment), so the agent becomes a wrongdoer (mukhalif) and must guarantee the amount; he is considered constructively dismissed (azl hukmi).

Key Terms
ضمان (daman): Liability to compensate or guarantee a loss; used here for the second agent's responsibility to repay the amount paid.
عزل حكمي (azl hukmi): Constructive dismissal: the agent is considered dismissed from his agency because his action failed to fulfill the purpose of the order, even without explicit revocation.
مأمور (ma'mur): The person ordered to perform an act, here specifically the agent appointed to pay zakat or slaughter a sacrifice.

The Legal Effect of Guarantee (Daman) in Delegated Mudarabah
Ruling on guarantee in delegated mudarabah and purity of profit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the validity of mudarabah when the first or second mudarib guarantees the capital, and explain why the first mudarib's profit is not pure (tayyib).

Core Idea
If the first mudarib guarantees the capital, the delegated mudarabah becomes valid as if he owned it, but his profit is tainted by the guarantee.

This passage continues the discussion of delegated mudarabah by examining the consequences of a guarantee (daman). It distinguishes between the first mudarib and the second mudarib providing the guarantee, and introduces the concept of legal impurity (khubth) affecting the purity of profit. This distinction is crucial for determining lawful earnings and zakat obligations.

Mudarib Prohibited from Marrying Slaves: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Commentary on mudarib's restrictions: marriage and kitabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a mudarib marrying a slave from the capital and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A mudarib cannot use capital for marriage — marriage is not trade.

In this passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, we find a clear illustration of the limits of a mudarib's authority. While the mudarib can exercise judgment in trade, he cannot divert capital to non-commercial acts. The case of marrying a female slave sparks an intra-Hanafi dispute that reveals the school's rigorous definition of trade.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited acts
The passage lists three acts the mudarib cannot perform from the capital: marrying a male slave, marrying a female slave, and writing a kitabah contract. Determine which act has a dispute.
The dispute centers on the female slave.
2
State the majority Hanafi view
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the mudarib cannot marry the female slave. Their evidence: a permitted slave girl (ma'dhunah) cannot marry herself, showing marriage is not a commercial act.
The ma'dhunah is a slave allowed to trade, yet she cannot marry without permission.
3
Contrast with Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf permits marrying the female slave because marriage brings dower (compensation) and relieves the owner from maintenance, making it akin to a sale.
Abu Yusuf sees the dower as a price and maintenance saving as a benefit.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The majority responds that the dower is not a true price and that marriage is a personal relationship, not commerce. Maintenance is not a debt on the capital.
The distinction is between trade and personal acts.
5
Determine the practical consequence
If the mudarib marries the slave despite the prohibition, the marriage is valid but the dower is from his own pocket, not the capital.
The thamarah al-khilaf: liability falls on the mudarib personally.

Khujandi's Classification of Five Types of Authorized Agents
Classifies five types of agents with different rules for sale and purchase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the five categories of authorized agents in Hanafi law and apply the differing rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding sales at extreme prices.

Core Idea
Authorization to trade does not grant unlimited pricing discretion; Hanafi law classifies agents into five types, each with distinct rules for extreme price sales.

This lesson presents a classic Hanafi classification by al-Khujandi, organizing all types of agents into five categories based on their authority to sell and buy at prices differing from market value. You will learn the default rule for each category and the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the permissible range of prices. Understanding this classification is essential for determining the validity of sales conducted by various representatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five categories
List the five categories of agents as presented by al-Khujandi: (1) father, grandfather, executor; (2) mukatib and ma'dhun; (3) mudarib, partners, and absolute agent; (4) a person in death-illness with debt; (5) executor after death (subcase).
2
Determine the default rule for each category
For each category, note what is permitted regarding selling and buying prices. For example, category 1 sells at customary prices, category 2 may or may not sell at extreme prices depending on the imam, etc.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
For categories 2 and 3, Abu Hanifa allows selling at any price (even extreme), while the Sahibayn restrict to customary prices. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
Also note category 4: a person in death-illness may not give even a small favor if debt is overwhelming.
4
Understand the underlying reasoning
Abu Hanifa views agents in categories 2 and 3 as having broad authority similar to a free person, while the Sahibayn prioritize protecting the principal or creditors from potential harm.
5
Apply to a scenario
Given an agent type and a transaction with extreme price difference, determine validity according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. For example, a mudarib selling a 1000-dirham item for 500 dirhams: valid under Abu Hanifa, invalid under Sahibayn.
