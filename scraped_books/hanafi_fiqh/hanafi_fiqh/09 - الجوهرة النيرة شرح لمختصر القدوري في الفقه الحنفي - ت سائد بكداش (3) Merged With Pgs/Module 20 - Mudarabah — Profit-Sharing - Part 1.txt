Hanafi Definition of Mudarabah (Qirad)
Definition of mudarabah and its essential contract elements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mudarabah, identify its pillars (offer and acceptance), and list the key conditions for a valid mudarabah contract.

Core Idea
Mudarabah: capital from one, labor from the other, profit shared — a trust-based partnership.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi legal definition of mudarabah (qirad), a foundational contract in Islamic finance. You will learn the linguistic root, the precise legal elements, and the essential pillars that make this contract valid. Understanding this definition sets the stage for all subsequent rules governing mudarabah.

Guided Path
1
Linguistic derivation
Understand that 'mudarabah' comes from 'darb fi al-ard' meaning travel for trade, as supported by the Quranic verse.
The term highlights the traveler/worker aspect of the mudarib.
2
Legal definition
Grasp the two-party structure: capital from one party (rabb al-mal), labor from the other (mudarib), profit shared as agreed.
Loss is solely on the capital owner unless negligence.
3
Pillars of the contract
Identify offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) as the essential pillars; sample wordings are provided.
Without clear offer and acceptance, the contract is batil (void).
4
Conditions for validity
Note that capital must be known, profit share specified, and parties competent.
Unknown profit share leads to fasid (corrupt) contract.
5
Partnership in profit only
Recognize that the partnership is specifically in profit, not in capital or loss—this distinguishes mudarabah from a loan.
The mudarib is an agent, not a debtor.

Commentary
This passage is a definition (definition_ruling) of mudarabah (known as qirad in other schools) from the Hanafi perspective. It begins with the linguistic meaning: 'mudarabah' is derived from 'darb fi al-ard' (striking/traveling the earth), as traders travel for business. The Quranic verse provided supports this. The legal definition (shar'an) states that mudarabah is a contract between two parties: one provides the capital (rabb al-mal) and the other provides labor/entrepreneurship (mudarib) to trade with that capital; the resulting profit is shared between them according to an agreed ratio (here mentioned as half). Loss is borne solely by the capital owner unless the mudarib is negligent. The pillars (rukn) of the contract are offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). Several valid offer wordings are given: 'I gave you this capital as mudarabah/mu'amalah' or 'Take this capital and work with it as mudarabah.' The acceptance by the mudarib can be 'qabiltu' (I accept), 'akhathtu' (I took), or 'raditu' (I am satisfied). The profit share must be specified, e.g., half. The author then quotes the matn (original text) stating that mudarabah is a contract of partnership (shirkah) with capital from one and labor from the other. The commentator clarifies that this partnership is specifically in profit (shirkah fi al-ribh), not in the capital or losses. This is a fundamental Hanafi definition distinguishing mudarabah from other partnerships.

Key Terms
مضاربة (mudarabah): A contract where one party provides capital and the other provides labor for trade, with profit sharing as agreed.
رب المال (rabb al-mal): The capital provider in a mudarabah contract.
مضارب (mudarib): The working partner who manages the capital in a mudarabah.

Definition and Pillar of Mudarabah
Defines mudarabah linguistically and legally with its pillar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mudarabah linguistically and legally, and state its single pillar (rukn) with the required phrases for offer and acceptance.

Core Idea
Mudarabah is a partnership in profit, not capital; its sole pillar is offer and acceptance.

This lesson opens the Book of Mudarabah. The commentator first explains the linguistic root and then provides the Shar'i definition. Crucially, he pinpoints the pillar (rukn) of the contract, distinguishing it from mere conditions. Understanding this foundation is essential for all subsequent rulings on mudarabah.

Distinguish linguistic vs. legal definition
The passage first gives the linguistic origin (ضرب في الأرض – travel) and then the legal definition. Note how the Shar'i meaning builds on the linguistic but is narrower and technical.

The contract is valid with any wording that indicates the meaning.

Clarify the matn's nuance
The author (al-Quduri) says 'عقد على الشركة بمال من أحد الشريكين وعمل من الآخر' – partnership in capital and labor. The commentator clarifies: 'مراده الشركة في الربح' – he means partnership in profit, not in the capital itself. This is a critical refinement.

The Mudarib's Five Ranks and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Profit Purity
Expansion of mudarib statuses and an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to (1) state the five legal statuses of the mudarib with their triggering conditions, and (2) explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether profit from violating conditions is halal (tayyib).

Core Idea
The mudarib's status shifts with each action; when he violates conditions, profit is his but not halal according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira outlines the five distinct legal statuses a mudarib can assume, from trustee to usurper. It then records an intra-Hanafi dispute on a critical point: if the mudarib violates the owner's conditions, does the profit become legally pure (halal) for him? The answer affects both liability and personal consumption.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate the five scenarios
List each scenario with its trigger: (a) holding capital → trustee (amin), (b) trading → agent (wakil), (c) profit appears → partner (sharik), (d) corrupt contract → employee (ajir), (e) violation of conditions → usurper (ghasib).
Each status carries different rules on liability and profit.
2
Examine the violation case in detail
Note that both sides agree the profit belongs to the mudarib. The dispute is over its purity (tayyib).
The profit is owned, but is it consumable without reservation?
3
Identify the positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say profit is not tayyib (halal). Abu Yusuf says it is tayyib.
The text uses 'عندهما' (according to the two) indicating the majority view.
4
Understand the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the mudarib follows Abu Yusuf, he can consume the profit; if he follows the majority, he must avoid it or seek tazkiyah.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.
5
Review the legal stratagem (hila)
The capital owner can make the capital guaranteed by a loan-then-mudarabah arrangement. This protects the capital from loss.
The hila is a sophisticated device used in Hanafi fiqh.

The Five Ranks of the Mudarib and the Hilah for Capital Guarantee
Lists five legal statuses of a mudarib and a legal trick for capital guarantee.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five legal statuses of a mudarib, explain the triggering action for each, and apply the hilah that transforms the capital from amanah to dhaman.

Core Idea
The mudarib's liability shifts with his actions: from trustee to agent, partner, hireling, or usurper.

Mudarabah is a foundational contract for Islamic investment, but the mudarib's legal relationship to the capital is dynamic. This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah provides a clear taxonomy of five distinct statuses a mudarib may occupy, along with a clever legal device (hilah) that the owner can use to secure the capital. Understanding these ranks is essential for correctly assessing liability and profit entitlement in mudarabah contracts.

Guided Path
1
List the five ranks in order
Memorize the sequence: amin (trustee), wakil (agent), sharik (partner), ajir (hireling), ghasib (usurper). Each corresponds to a specific action or event.
The order is chronological based on the mudarib's actions.
2
Identify the trigger for each rank
For each rank, note the event that causes the shift: receiving capital, purchasing, earning profit, contract becoming fasid, violating a condition.
These triggers are cumulative; later ranks override earlier ones.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on profit in violation
When the mudarib violates a condition, the profit belongs to him but its lawfulness is debated: the two (Abu Yusuf & Muhammad) say it is not pure (la yatibu), while Abu Yusuf alone says it is pure (yatibu).
The mufta bihi position is that of the two (majority).
4
Explain the legal trick (hilah)
The owner lends the capital to the mudarib as a qard (loan), takes witnesses, then the mudarib returns it as mudarabah capital. This makes the capital a debt (dhaman) before it becomes amanah.
This trick ensures the mudarib is liable for loss even before any violation.
5
Apply the framework to a scenario
Given a simple fact pattern, trace the mudarib's statuses step-by-step and determine the ruling on capital and profit.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, enumerates the five different legal statuses a mudarib (investment partner) can occupy in a mudarabah (silent partnership). The author begins by stating that mudarabah comprises various rulings. First, when the mudarib merely receives the capital upon the owner's order, it is an amanah (trust) like a wadi'ah (deposit) until he starts working with it, because he has taken possession with permission. Second, when he purchases with the capital, it becomes wakalah (agency) because he is transacting in another's property by permission. Third, if a profit results, he becomes a sharik (partner) in that profit. Fourth, if the mudarabah becomes fasid (invalid) due to a defect in the contract, it transforms into ijarah (hire) and the mudarib is entitled to ujr al-mithl (market wage) for his work. Fifth, if the mudarib violates a condition stipulated by the owner, he is like a ghasib (usurper) and the capital is maddmun (guaranteed) upon him, meaning he is liable for any loss. Regarding the profit in this violation scenario, the text notes a disagreement: according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), the profit is not pure (la yatibu) for the mudarib, but Abu Yusuf alone holds it is pure (yatibu). The passage then provides a legal trick (hilah) for the owner who wishes to make the capital guaranteed on the mudarib from the outset: the owner lends the capital to the mudarib (outright loan), takes witnesses, then the mudarib returns it as a mudarabah investment. This way, if the capital perishes in the mudarib's hands, it is a loan liability; if profit accrues, it is shared. Finally, the author summarizes the five ranks: amin, wakil, sharik, ajir, ghasib.

Key Terms
أمانة (amānah): A property held in trust by a person without liability for loss except in cases of negligence or violation.
الخجندي (al-Khujandī): A reference to a Hanafi jurist or his work, likely 'al-Mukhtar' or similar, cited for the legal trick.

Mudarabah with Debt: Two Scenarios and the Ruling
Two scenarios of mudarabah with debt and the Abu Hanifa-Sahibayn dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between two scenarios of mudarabah involving debt and state the Hanafi rulings for each, including the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Mudarabah with a debt owed by a third party is valid after collection; mudarabah with the debt the mudarib himself owes is invalid according to Abu Hanifa.

The passage presents two cases of mudarabah where the capital originates from a debt. The first case is unproblematic: the capital owner instructs the mudarib to collect a debt from a third party and then use that money as capital. The second case is more controversial: the capital owner tells his own debtor to use the debt as capital. Here, Abu Hanifa rules it invalid, while the two students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it valid. This lesson unpacks the reasoning for each view and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first scenario: collection from a third party
The capital owner (rabb al-mal) says to the mudarib: 'Take my debt owed by so-and-so, collect it, and then use it as mudarabah capital.' The text rules this valid once the debt is collected, because the collected money becomes a trust (amanah) in the mudarib's hand, which is consistent with mudarabah.
The key is that the capital becomes actually possessed by the mudarib.
2
Analyze the second scenario: debtor as mudarib
The capital owner says to his debtor: 'Work with the debt you owe me as mudarabah capital.' The text states that according to Abu Hanifa, this is invalid. The mudarib (debtor) uses the debt as if it were capital, but he never actually receives it; the debt remains a liability (dhaman) on him. Any purchases he makes with that 'capital' are his own, profits and losses are his, and he is not freed from the original debt.
The reasoning: mudarabah requires capital to be a trust (amanah), but a debt is a guaranteed liability (madhmūn). They are incompatible.
3
Consider the Sahibayn's opposing view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the mudarabah is valid and the mudarib is freed from the debt because the contract effectively converts the debt into capital. They consider the mutual agreement as sufficient to transform the liability into a trust.
Their view facilitates transactions, as it allows debtors to invest the debt without actually receiving cash.
4
Evaluate the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the contract is invalid (Abu Hanifa), any profit from the mudarib's work belongs to him alone, and he bears any loss; the debt remains payable. If the contract is valid (Sahibayn), the profit is shared according to the contract, and the debt is considered settled, so the mudarib no longer owes the original amount.
This is a significant financial difference.
5
Determine the preferred view
Later Hanafi works (like al-Hidayah and subsequent fatwa collections) adopt the view of the Sahibayn as mufta bihi because it is more practical and avoids unnecessary hardship for debtors.
This is an example of istihsan (juristic preference) overriding analogy.

Profit Share Condition: Musha' and Consequences of Fixed Stipulation
Condition of profit sharing and consequences of void mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the condition that profit must be a common share (musha'), describe what happens when a fixed amount is stipulated, and state the intra-Hanafi disputes on the mudarib's wage and liability in a void mudarabah.

Core Idea
Profit must be an unspecified share, not a fixed sum; otherwise the mudarabah becomes void and the mudarib is entitled to ujr al-mithl.

The second key condition in the passage is that the profit must be shared as a common, unspecified fraction (musha'). If either party is promised a fixed number of dirhams, the contract is void (fasid). The commentary then dives into the consequences: the mudarib gets the equivalent wage (ujr al-mithl), not profit. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether the wage is capped by the named amount and whether a wage is due if no profit is realized. The passage also addresses liability for the capital in a void mudarabah, again with a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether the mudarib is liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition from the matn
The matn states: 'One of its conditions: that the profit be shared between them as a common share (musha‘), neither of them being entitled to named dirhams from it.' Understand the meaning of musha‘: it must be a fraction like half, third, or one-fifth, not a fixed amount like 100 dirhams.
This is to ensure uncertainty (gharar) is avoided.
2
Apply the condition to an example
The sharh gives an example: if the capital owner says, 'Whatever Allah provides, the mudarib gets 100 dirhams,' the mudarabah is fasid. Because it is possible that the profit is less than 100 dirhams, which would cut into the capital? Actually, the reasoning is that such a stipulation severs the partnership (ju'z al-sharika) because it deprives one party of profit.
The void contract does not make the work invalid; the mudarib is still entitled to compensation.
3
Determine the compensation for the mudarib in a void mudarabah
The text rules that the mudarib gets ujr al-mithl (equivalent wage), not profit, because the work was done under a void contract. This is analogous to void hire (ijarah fasidah). The wage is determined by the market rate for similar work.
The wage is calculated based on time and effort, not the outcome.
4
Examine the dispute on the amount of wage
Abu Yusuf (in one narration) says the wage cannot exceed the named amount (100 dirhams) and if no profit, no wage because a void contract cannot be stronger than a valid one. Muhammad says the full ujr al-mithl is always due, whether profit or not, because a void mudarabah becomes a hire contract, which always obligates wage.
The relied-upon view is Muhammad's.
5
Analyze the liability for capital in void mudarabah
In a valid mudarabah, capital is a trust (amanah). In the void one, the text says the capital is not guaranteed (by analogy). But the dispute: Abu Hanifa holds no liability (following his principle that a common hired worker is not liable), while the Sahibayn hold the mudarib liable (common hired worker is liable in their view). This is a branch of the general dispute on ajir mushtarak.
This shows how the classification affects liability.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Invalid Mudarabah with Fixed Wage
Intra-Hanafi dispute on invalid mudarabah with fixed wage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an invalid mudarabah with a fixed wage and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the mudarib's entitlement to ujr al-mithl.

Core Idea
An invalid mudarabah transforms into a lease: the mudarib is entitled to the equivalent wage (ujr al-mithl) for work done, even if no profit is made.

This lesson explores a nuanced ruling in Hanafi muamalat: when a mudarabah contract is invalid due to a fixed profit share, the mudarib is not left empty-handed. Instead, the contract becomes analogous to a lease, granting the mudarib a market-rate wage. However, the two leading disciples of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, disagree on the details, particularly when no profit is earned. Understanding their arguments sharpens our ability to apply qiyas in contract law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defective condition and the ruling on the contract
The text begins with a scenario: a mudarabah where the profit share is fixed as a lump sum (e.g., 100 dirhams). Recognize that this violates the requirement that profit must be a percentage of actual profit, making the contract fasid (corrupt). The ruling is that the mudarabah is invalid, but not void; some effects remain.
In Hanafi law, a fasid contract is one that is defective in its conditions but can still produce partial legal effects.
2
Recognize the fallback to ujr al-mithl
The author states that regardless of profit, the mudarib gets ujr al-mithl (equivalent wage), not a share of profit. The reasoning is that the work was performed under an invalid contract for a promised consideration; since the consideration is not validly owed, the mudarib reverts to the market wage, analogous to a void lease.
This is the agreed position in the Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Understand Abu Yusuf's position on the wage cap and the no-profit scenario
Abu Yusuf has two views: (a) the ujr al-mithl cannot exceed the named amount (the fixed wage), and (b) if no profit is made, the mudarib gets nothing. His argument: an invalid contract cannot be stronger than a valid one; in a valid mudarabah with no profit, the mudarib gets nothing, so in an invalid one it is even more so.
The second view (no profit = no wage) is arguably stricter.
4
Understand Muhammad's position and his qiyas to ijara
Muhammad holds that the ujr al-mithl is uncapped, and the mudarib is entitled to it whether profit is made or not. His proof: the invalid mudarabah becomes a lease (ijara); in a lease, the wage is due for work performed, regardless of the employer's profit. Profit is irrelevant.
Muhammad's analogy (qiyas) to ijara is based on the illa of work performed under a defective contract for compensation.
5
Note the practical difference and the mufta bihi position
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is clear: in a loss-making venture, Abu Yusuf (in one narration) would give nothing, while Muhammad gives the full market wage. Later Hanafi fatwa authority generally adopts Muhammad's view as more equitable and consistent with the transformation of the contract into a lease.
The text also notes a secondary dispute on liability (daman) for loss of capital, but this is not the focus of the main ruling.

It then explains a ruling from the matn: if the owner stipulates that he himself will work, the contract is invalid because this condition prevents the mudarib from having exclusive control (خلوص يد) over the capital, as the owner's involvement would interfere with the mudarib's discretion in trade. The commentary then provides exceptions: a father or guardian may stipulate their own work when giving an orphan's wealth as mudarabah, because they are not owners; they are like strangers (كالأجنبيين) since each of them may act as mudarib with the orphan's wealth. However, if they stipulate the orphan's work (the minor owner), the mudarabah becomes invalid because the orphan is the owner and cannot be controlled. Similarly, a mukatab (a slave under contract of emancipation) may stipulate his master's work without invalidating the contract, because the master does not own the mukatab's earnings (he only has right to the kitabah payment). Thus, the master in this context is like a stranger. The key principle is that the mudarib must have complete control over the capital; any condition that undermines this exclusivity invalidates the contract, with specific exceptions for non-owner parties.

Time-Limited Mudarabah and Interpreting Restrictions
Ruling on time-limited mudarabah and interpretation of restrictions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on time-limited mudarabah and apply the Hanafi rule for interpreting restrictions in a mudarabah contract.

Core Idea
Time-limited mudarabah is valid; restrictions are interpreted broadly unless explicitly narrowed.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two practical issues in mudarabah: whether a time limit is permissible, and how to interpret conditions placed on the mudarib. Understanding these rules is essential for structuring valid mudarabah contracts and resolving disputes between capital owner and manager.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on time-limited mudarabah
State that setting a specific known time for mudarabah is permissible and the contract automatically terminates upon expiry. Note the evidence: mudarabah is a form of agency, and agency can be time-bound.
The ruling is based on qiyas (analogy) to wakalah.
2
Recognize the default rule for interpreting restrictions
When the capital owner imposes a restriction (e.g., 'work in the market of Kufa'), the default is that it applies generally. If the mudarib works in Kufa but outside the market, it is still permissible because the restriction is understood broadly.
This default protects the mudarib's actions unless explicitly narrowed.
3
Distinguish explicit prohibitions from general restrictions
If the owner says 'do not work except in the market of Kufa', this is an explicit prohibition. Working elsewhere makes the mudarib a violator, and the purchased goods belong to him (no profit share).
The contrast shows that the language 'except' creates a specific restriction.
4
Apply the dispute rule on generality and specificity
If the parties differ on whether the restriction was general or specific, the one claiming generality (usually the mudarib) is believed. The default is that restrictions do not limit beyond their apparent meaning.
This rule resolves conflicts in favor of the mudarib's broader interpretation.
5
Understand restrictions tied to a specific person
A condition to buy from or sell to a named person is valid and must be followed, as it is based on trust. The mudarib cannot exceed this limitation.
Such restrictions are binding because they are explicitly stated and beneficial.

Manumission of Slaves in Mudarabah: Profit and Liability
Detailed rulings on slave purchase with profit and manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for manumission of slaves purchased with mudarabah capital when there is profit, no profit, or multiple slaves, and determine the compensation due.

Core Idea
Mudarib's manumission effective only in his profit share; capital owner must compensate if he destroys the profit share.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayira details how manumission rights are distributed when a mudarib or capital owner frees slaves purchased with mudarabah funds. The key is whether the slave's value exceeds the capital, creating a profit share for the mudarib. We will walk through three scenarios to understand the interplay of profit, liability, and manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the profit scenario
First, determine if the slave's value exceeds the capital. This determines whether the mudarib has a profit share.
E.g., capital 1000, slave worth 2000: excess exists.
2
Calculate the mudarib's share in the slave
When capital is 1000 and slave worth 2000, profit is 1000. Mudarib gets half the profit (500), which is a quarter of the slave's value. So his share is 1/4 of the slave.
Profit share is half of profit unless otherwise agreed.
3
Apply manumission by each party
If mudarib manumits, it frees only his share (1/4). If capital owner manumits, it frees his share (3/4) plus the mudarib's share? No, capital owner's manumission frees only his own portion; the mudarib's share remains unless compensation is paid.
Manumission by a partner only affects his own undivided share.
4
Handle the two-slave scenario
When two slaves are bought each worth the capital, mudarib has no profit share in either. Manumission by mudarib does nothing. Capital owner's manumission of both together frees them but he must compensate mudarib for lost profit (500). If separate, first slave covers capital completely, second is for profit and half is freed.
Compensation is strict liability (ḍamān itlāf).
5
Synthesize the rule
The mudarib's power of manumission is limited to his profit share. Capital owner can manumit fully but must pay for the mudarib's lost share.

Sub-Contracting Mudarabah Without Permission: Guarantee Rulings
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mudarib liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on the liability of a mudarib who sub-contracts without permission and explain which is the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
The mudarib's liability for sub-contracting without permission depends on profit, work, or delivery — three distinct Hanafi views.

In this lesson, we examine an important intra-Hanafi dispute: when a mudarib gives the capital to another mudarib without the owner's permission, at what point does the first mudarib become liable for the capital? The scholars differ on whether liability attaches upon delivery, upon work by the second mudarib, or only after profit is realized. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for determining financial responsibility in mudarabah contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the core question
The mudarib (first agent) gives the capital to a second mudarib without the owner's permission. The core question: when does the first mudarib become liable (daman) for the capital?
2
State the three Hanafi positions
List the positions: (1) Hasan narration from Abu Hanifa: liable only after profit. (2) Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya): liable as soon as the second mudarib works, regardless of profit. (3) Zufar: liable immediately upon delivery.
3
Identify the key differences in liability triggers
Compare the triggers: profit (Hasan), work (Sahibayn), delivery (Zufar). Note that each trigger reflects a different assessment of risk and control.
4
Understand the second mudarib's liability and the owner's option
The text reports that at Abu Hanifa, the second mudarib is not liable (unlike the case of a depositary), while at the Sahibayn he is liable. The well-known (mashhur) view is that the owner has the option to hold either mudarib liable, which harmonizes the positions.
5
Apply the distinction from the depositary case
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: the second depositary takes possession for the first's benefit, so no liability. The second mudarib works for his own benefit, so liability is possible. This explains why the second mudarib can be liable in mudarabah but not in deposit.

Validity of Changing Profit Ratio in Sub-Contracting Mudarabah
Ruling with distribution and liability analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the validity and profit distribution when a mudarib sub-contracts with a different profit ratio under general permission, and state the ruling on the taint (khubth) of the first mudarib's profit.

Core Idea
Changing the sub-contract profit ratio is valid under general permission; the first mudarib's profit is tainted (khubth) because it arises from liability, not work.

In this passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah, the author examines a nuanced scenario in mudarabah: the first mudarib, with the owner's permission to sub-contract, gives the capital to a second mudarib but changes the profit-sharing ratio from one-half to one-third. The text rules it valid and explains the resulting profit distribution, as well as the taint (khubth) in the first mudarib's profit. Understanding this ruling clarifies the limits of agency and liability in mudarabah.

Guided Path
1
Identify Parties and Agreements
Recognize the three parties: owner (rabb al-mal), first mudarib (who receives capital with permission to sub-contract), and second mudarib. The owner's profit share is one-half; the first mudarib changes the sub-contract ratio to one-third.
2
Determine Initial Validity
The sub-contract is valid (jāz) because the owner gave general permission for sub-contracting, and the change in ratio does not exceed that authority.
3
Calculate Profit Distribution
Apply the rule: the owner's share is fixed per his condition (one-half). The second mudarib gets his contracted share (one-third of total). The first mudarib receives the remainder (one-sixth).
4
Analyse Liability Scenarios
Understand that the owner may hold either mudarib liable for the capital. If the first is held liable, his ownership is retroactively confirmed, preserving the sub-contract. If the second is held liable, he has recourse against the first.
5
Evaluate Taint (Khubth) in Profit
The second mudarib's profit is tayyib (pure) because earned by work. The first mudarib's profit has khubth because it arises from ownership via guarantee, not direct work, and is therefore not completely pure.
In Hanafi fiqh, khubth makes the profit undesirable but not necessarily unlawful; it may require tazkiyah (purification).

Profit Distribution and Liability When a Mudarib Appoints a Second Mudarib
Ruling on profit shares and guarantee in multi-level mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the profit shares of the capital owner, first mudarib, and second mudarib in a multi-level mudarabah, and determine when the first mudarib must compensate the second from his own wealth.

Core Idea
A mudarib cannot give away profit that belongs to the capital owner – promises beyond his own share must be fulfilled from his own pocket.

In mudarabah, profit distribution is governed by the initial contract and the owner's rights. When a first mudarib appoints a second mudarib, the shares must respect the owner's prior entitlement. This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira explains the precise calculation and the principle of guarantee when the first mudarib overpromises the second.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their contractual shares
List the capital owner (rabb al-mal), first mudarib, and second mudarib. Note the first mudarib's stipulation regarding his own share (e.g., half of all profit) and the share offered to the second mudarib.
The first mudarib's own share is defined by his initial condition, which also determines the owner's share as the remainder.
2
Determine the owner's guaranteed share
The owner is always entitled to his share as per the original mudarabah contract. In the given case, the first mudarib's condition 'half for me' implies the owner gets the other half. This share cannot be reduced by the first mudarib's subsequent promises.
The owner's share is fixed at the outset and takes priority.
3
Compute the second mudarib's actual share
The second mudarib can only receive profit from the remainder after the owner's share is taken. If his stipulated share exceeds this remainder, it is not enforceable against the owner.
The second mudarib's actual share is capped at the available profit after the owner's share.
4
Calculate the first mudarib's liability
If the second mudarib's stipulated share is greater than the actual share received, the first mudarib must compensate the difference from his own wealth, because his promise to the second was valid and binding upon him.
The first mudarib's guarantee (daman) arises from a valid promise that cannot be fulfilled due to the owner's prior right.

Stipulating Excess Profit for a Second Mudarib
Ruling on profit share for second mudarib with compensation

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Stipulations cannot override the owner's profit right; excess promised is binding on the promisor personally.

In a mudarabah, the profit share of the capital owner is protected against agreements between the mudaribs. When the first mudarib hires a second mudarib and promises him a share larger than what the owner allows, the owner still gets his agreed share. The second mudarib takes what remains, and the first mudarib must compensate any shortfall from his own wealth. This lesson examines the exact calculation and legal reasoning behind this ruling.
