Imprisonment of the Debtor and Sale of Property by the Judge
Ruling on imprisonment and sale by judge with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on imprisoning a debtor with property, explain the intra-Hanafi difference regarding the judge's sale, and identify the proofs from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma.

Core Idea
A debtor with property is imprisoned until he sells; the judge does not sell during his life.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra clarifies the Hanafi approach to debt enforcement. A critical question arises: if a debtor has property but refuses to pay, what can the judge do? The Hanafi position distinguishes between the debtor's lifetime and after death. During life, the judge's role is to imprison, not to sell. After death, the judge sells all assets. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proofs, and the intra-madhhab differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage rules that if a debtor has property, the judge does not dispose of it during life but imprisons him until he sells it himself. This is the Hanafi position.
The ruling is specific to when the debtor has property; if no property, different rules apply.
2
Locate the proofs
The passage cites three proofs: Quran (5:33 interpreted as imprisonment), Sunnah (the Prophet imprisoned a man for freeing a share of a slave), and Ijma (Ali's prison building with Companion approval).
Each proof supports the permissibility and legitimacy of imprisonment to enforce debt.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The Quranic verse 'or be exiled' is interpreted as imprisonment because exile from all earth is impossible. The hadith shows direct prophetic action. The ijma shows Companion consensus.
The Hanafi school uses these proofs to establish imprisonment as a valid coercive measure.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds that the judge only imprisons during life; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow the judge to sell if the debtor is absent or refuses. The passage follows Abu Hanifa.
This difference affects the judge's immediate authority.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the debtor retains control over sale but is imprisoned. Under the Sahibayn, the judge can expedite payment by selling directly.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view in this context.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra, a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, addresses the ruling on a debtor who has property but refuses to pay his debts. It begins by stating that if the debtor has property, the judge does not directly dispose of it during the debtor's lifetime – this is the position of Abu Hanifa. Instead, the judge imprisons him until he sells the property himself to pay off the debt. The commentary explains that this applies only during the debtor's life; if the debtor dies, the judge sells all his property, whether movable or immovable, to satisfy the debts. The liability for any defects in the sold property falls on the creditors (who receive the proceeds), not on the judge or his trustee. Analogously, if the judge sells the estate for a legatee or for a minor, the liability falls on the legatee or minor respectively. The purpose of imprisonment is to enforce payment and protect the rights of creditors. The passage then provides proofs for imprisonment from the Quran (verse 5:33 interpreted as imprisonment), the Sunnah (the Prophet imprisoned a man for freeing a share of a slave until he sold booty), and consensus (the actions of Caliph Ali in building prisons with the Companions' approval). Finally, the passage discusses the case when the debt is of one currency and the debtor has another. If the debt is in dirhams and the debtor has dirhams, the judge may simply pay the debt from the debtor's property without his permission, by consensus. If the debt is in dirhams but the debtor has dinars (or vice versa), the judge sells the currency to convert it into the debt type. This ruling is specific to Abu Hanifa and is based on istihsan (juristic preference), treating dirhams and dinars as a single genus in certain contexts.

Key Terms
الحجر (al-hajr): Legal restriction on a person's capacity to dispose of property, typically due to debt or insolvency.
الحبس (al-habs): Imprisonment of a debtor who has property but refuses to pay, as a coercive measure.
العهدة (al-uhdah): Liability for defects in a sold item; who bears the responsibility if the property sold by the judge turns out to have a defect.

Interdiction Upon Creditors' Request
Ruling on judge imposing interdiction at creditors' request

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a judge imposes interdiction on a bankrupt debtor and explain why sale at fair price is not prevented.

Core Idea
Interdiction protects creditors; it only restricts sales below fair price.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on when a judge must intervene to protect creditors from a bankrupt debtor. The ruling balances the debtor's freedom with creditor rights, using istihsan to override a strict qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Trigger
The ruling: the judge imposes interdiction (hajr) when the creditors request it. The trigger is the creditors' formal request, not the bankruptcy alone. Identify the act: the judge's imposition of hajr and prohibition of transactions.
Hajr means preventing the bankrupt from disposing of his property.
2
Understand the Prohibition's Scope
The judge prevents the bankrupt from selling, transacting, and acknowledging new debts. But there is a key qualification: this prevention only applies if the bankrupt sells at less than the fair market price (thaman al-mithl). If he sells at fair price, he is not prevented.
The 'thaman al-mithl' is the standard market value.
3
Analyze the Judge's Sale Authority
If the bankrupt refuses to sell his assets voluntarily to pay debts, the judge himself sells the property. This is an exception to the general rule (qiyas) that a judge should not sell and a creditor cannot seize property. The school adopts istihsan to permit this for creditors' benefit.
Qiyas: 'لا يبيعه' – analogy would say no sale. Istihsan allows sale to prevent harm.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Position
This is the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. No disagreement from Abu Hanifa is recorded here, so it is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) in the madhhab.
The matn does not mention Abu Hanifa's opinion; the two imams' view stands.

Acknowledgment of Debt During Interdiction
Ruling on validity of acknowledgment of debt while under interdiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment during interdiction and identify when the acknowledgment is immediately binding versus suspended.

Core Idea
A bankrupt's acknowledgment of a new debt is valid but suspended until prior debts are paid, unless it arises from consumption or new property.

When a person is interdicted (hajr) due to bankruptcy, does their acknowledgment of a new debt affect the property already claimed by creditors? The Hanafi school, according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, protects the rights of existing creditors by suspending the effect of such acknowledgments. However, exceptions exist for claims based on consumption (istihlak) and for property acquired after interdiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the bankrupt person's acknowledgment of a new debt while under judicial interdiction. The ruling: the acknowledgment is valid (sahih) but its effect is suspended until all existing debts are paid.
The suspension protects prior creditors' rights that have already attached to the bankrupt's property.
2
Locate the evidence
The evidence is a rational proof (dalil aqli): the prior creditors' rights are attached to the property, so the bankrupt cannot nullify those rights by acknowledging new debts. This is the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Sahibayn).
The proof is explicit: 'because the right of the earlier creditors has become attached to this property'.
3
Identify the exceptions
Two exceptions are given: (1) consumption (istihlak) of another's property creates immediate liability because it is witnessed; (2) acknowledgment in property acquired after interdiction is immediately effective because prior creditors' rights do not attach to it.
These exceptions show the underlying principle: attachment of rights determines suspension.
4
Apply to cases
Given a scenario, determine whether the acknowledgment is suspended or immediate. For example, if the bankrupt destroys goods (istihlak), the liability is immediate; if he acknowledges a gift debt from before interdiction, it is suspended.
The distinction hinges on whether the right of prior creditors attaches to the property in question.

Commentary
This passage addresses the effect of a bankrupt person's acknowledgment of a new debt while under judicial interdiction. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, such an acknowledgment is not immediately binding; it becomes binding only after all existing debts are fully paid. The reasoning is that the existing creditors' rights have already attached to the bankrupt's property (the assets at the time of interdiction). The bankrupt cannot undermine those rights by acknowledging additional creditors and thereby reducing the share of the original creditors. However, if the bankrupt consumes (استهلاك) someone else's property during interdiction (e.g., destroys goods), that liability is enforceable immediately because the harm is witnessed and cannot be denied. Additionally, if the bankrupt acquires new property after interdiction (e.g., by gift or inheritance), his acknowledgment in that new property is effective, as the original creditors' rights did not attach to it. This reflects the principle that interdiction only affects the property existing at its imposition.

Distinguishing Debt and Trust in Acknowledgment
Contrasting phrases for debt vs trust and their sub-rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish debt acknowledgment from trust acknowledgment based on the phrases 'علي'/'قبلي' vs 'عندي'/'معي', and apply the Hanafi rule on connected vs. disconnected claims of deposit.

Core Idea
The phrase 'علي' signals debt, while 'عندي' signals trust—but a connected claim of trust can override the default.

This passage from the Quduri matn and its commentary establishes a crucial distinction in the law of acknowledgment (iqrar). The specific wording used determines whether the property is presumed to be a debt (diyanah) or a trust (amanah). We will also examine how an acknowledger can claim it is a deposit if he connects it properly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main acknowledgment phrases
Note that 'له علي' and 'له قبلي' are used for debt, while 'له عندي' and 'له معي' are used for trust. Also note the extension to 'في بيتي', 'في صندوقي', etc.
2
Understand the reasoning for each phrase
Explain why 'علي' is a formula of obligation (صيغة إيجاب) and 'قبلي' implies guarantee (ضمان). Conversely, 'عندي' indicates mere possession without liability.
Al-Jawhara explains that 'قبلي' derives from 'قبالة' which is synonym for guarantee like 'كفالة'.
3
Analyze the sub-case of deposit claim
If the acknowledger says the debt is actually a deposit, his claim is accepted if he connects it (وصل) to his acknowledgment (e.g., 'له علي وديعة'), but rejected if he disconnects (فصل) his statement.
The reason is that when connected, the speech is taken as a whole, and 'علي' is interpreted metaphorically as obligation to preserve and deliver.
4
Understand the proof for the connected/disconnected rule
The proof is linguistic custom (urf) and the principle that speech is interpreted as a coherent unit. The default of 'علي' is debt, but a connected exception changes the meaning.
Al-Jawhara states that if disconnected, the apparent meaning prevails because the speech has settled (الكلام لم يستقر).
5
Apply the distinction in practice
When the claimant asserts a different characterization (e.g., loan instead of deposit), the burden of proof shifts. For 'عندي', the default is trust, so the claimant must provide evidence to assert liability.

Agent for Litigation is Agent for Receipt
Ruling that litigation agent has authority to receive payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions regarding whether an agent appointed for litigation (wakīl bi al-khuṣūmah) is automatically authorized to receive payment, and identify which view is the mufta bihi and why.

Core Idea
A litigation agent's authority to receive payment is disputed; the later fatwa requires explicit authorization to prevent abuse.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses a key issue in Hanafi agency law: does appointing someone to litigate automatically give them the right to collect the claimed property? The three founding imams of the school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) held yes, but Imam Zufar dissented. Remarkably, later scholars adopted Zufar's view as the operative fatwa due to changing circumstances. Understanding this shift illustrates how Hanafi fiqh evolves through 'urf and maṣlaḥa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
What is the specific matter of disagreement? It is whether an agent appointed solely for litigation (خصومة) is also authorized to receive payment (قبض).
The principal's intention is key.
2
State the majority view and its proof
Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad hold that the agent is authorized for receipt. Their proof: whoever controls a matter controls its completion, and litigation ends with receipt.
This is a qiyas (analogy) based on the logic of completion.
3
State Zufar's view and his proof
Zufar argues that the principal only consented to litigation, not to receipt, which is a separate act. Receipt requires explicit authorization.
Zufar's evidence is based on the limits of consent.
4
Identify the mufta bihi and the reason for the shift
Later scholars adopted Zufar's view due to widespread dishonesty among agents. The fatwa now requires explicit mention of receipt.
The tarjih is based on istihsan (juristic preference) and practical necessity.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider: a principal appoints an agent to sue for a debt without mentioning receipt. The agent wins and collects the money. According to the mufta bihi, the receipt is invalid because explicit authorization was missing.
The debtor may still owe the principal.
