Interpreting Ambiguous Numbers in Acknowledgment
A sequence of rulings on ambiguous numerical expressions in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Hanafi linguistic rules to interpret ambiguous numerical expressions in iqrar and determine the minimum obligatory amount.

Core Idea
Hanafi fiqh uses linguistic principles to fix a minimum debt from ambiguous expressions like 'kadhā kadhā' and 'ghayr'.

When someone acknowledges a debt using vague terms like 'kadhā kadhā dirhams', the law must determine a definite obligation. The Hanafi school employs rules of Arabic grammar and usage to extract a minimum amount. This lesson walks through several common ambiguous phrases, showing how each is resolved to a specific number.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous expression
First, note the exact phrasing: does it use repeated 'kadhā' without conjunction, or words like 'aḍʿāf' (multiples) or 'ghayr' (other than)?
The phrase 'kadhā kadhā' without 'waw' is key.
2
Recall the Hanafi interpretive rule
Each expression has a standard interpretation. For 'kadhā kadhā' without 'waw', the two numbers refer to consecutive numbers from 11 to 19. For 'aḍʿāf', it means the number is multiplied three times. For 'ghayr', it implies the opposite number of the same kind.
These rules are based on linguistic convention and established in Hanafi texts.
3
Apply the rule to find the minimum
Calculate the minimum debt using the rule. For 'kadhā kadhā', the minimum is 11. For 'aḍʿāf', multiply the base number three times. For 'ghayr', double the stated number.
The minimum is taken because it is the least certain obligation.
4
Handle combined terms like 'mudaʿʿaf' and 'aḍʿāf'
If both 'mudaʿʿaf' (doubled) and 'aḍʿāf' (multiples) appear, apply sequentially: first multiply according to 'mudaʿʿaf', then apply 'aḍʿāf'.
For example, 'dirhams mudaʿʿafa aḍʿāfan' yields 6 (base 3 doubled) then tripled to 18.
5
State the obligatory amount
Conclude with the precise number the debtor must pay. This is the mufta bihi position.
The rulings are consistent across Hanafi sources like al-Karkhi and al-Hidayah.

Rulings on 'Kadhā wa Kadhā' and Other Expressions with Waw
Rulings on ambiguous expressions with 'kadhā' in acknowledgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the specific amount owed for each common ambiguous expression with 'kadhā' in Hanafi law and explain the linguistic rationale behind the ruling.

Core Idea
The grammatical ending and presence of 'waw' in 'kadhā' expressions determine the exact amount owed in acknowledgments.

In this passage, we continue exploring ambiguous expressions in acknowledgments (iqrār). The word 'kadhā' is inherently vague, but the Hanafi school has established clear rules based on its grammatical form and whether it is repeated with the conjunction 'waw'. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for determining the minimum amount a debtor must pay when he uses such ambiguous language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expression and its grammatical case
Locate each instance of 'kadhā' in the passage and note its grammatical ending (nasb, khafd, raf‘, or sukun) and whether it is followed by a singular or plural noun or another 'kadhā' with 'waw'.
For example, 'kadhā wa kadhā dirhaman' has two 'kadhā's with 'waw' and the noun in accusative.
2
State the ruling for each variant
For each expression, write down the amount owed as stated in the text. For instance, 'kadhā dirham' (genitive) = 100 dirhams.
The rulings are based on established linguistic convention (urf) and the principle of certainty (muta’ayyan).
3
Explain the linguistic rationale (talil)
For each ruling, identify the reason given in the text. For example, two 'kadhā's with 'waw' are interpreted as two separate clauses, yielding numbers 21-99, with the minimum (21) binding.
The grammatical case of the following noun is key: khafd indicates 'hundred', raf‘/sukun indicates 'one' (because it interprets the ambiguous), nasb after a single 'kadhā' indicates '20', etc.
4
Apply the principle of tafsīr al-mubham
When 'kadhā' is followed by a noun in the nominative or sukun, it is interpreted as 'one' because it is an 'interpretation of the ambiguous' (tafsīr al-mubham). This means the ambiguous word is given its simplest meaning.
Similarly, when the noun is plural ('dirāhīm'), it indicates a minimum of three.
5
Address compound expressions
Note how multiple 'kadhā's with 'waw' (e.g., kadhā wa kadhā wa kadhā) are handled: the minimum amounts are summed, yielding 121 dirhams (1+20+100 or a structured interpretation).
Also note the special case of 'thuluth kadhā' without waw, which is interpreted as 11 based on al-Hidayah.

Types and Conditions of Exception (Istithnā') in Acknowledgments
Explains types and conditions of exception in acknowledgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Istithnā' Ta'tīl and Istithnā' Tahsīl and state the conditions for a valid exception in an acknowledgment.

Core Idea
A valid exception must be connected and leave something remaining; nullifying exceptions cancel the entire acknowledgment.

In Hanafi law, when a person makes an acknowledgment (iqrār), they may exclude part of what they owe using an exception (istithnā'). Not all exceptions are valid. The jurists distinguish between types—some void the entire statement, others partially reduce it. You need to know the conditions for each type to correctly determine what is owed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of exception
Listen for words like 'if Allah wills' (nullifying) or 'except' (achieving). Nullifying exceptions void the whole statement; achieving exceptions partially exclude.
Istithnā' Ta'tīl uses ta'liq phrasing; Istithnā' Tahsīl uses illā, ghayr, or sawā.
2
Check connectivity
If the exception is separated from the acknowledgment (mufassal), it is invalid regardless of type. It must be connected (mawsūl).
Connectivity means no significant pause or intervening speech.
3
If same genus, check remaining amount
When the exception is from the same genus (e.g., dirhams except dirhams), something must remain after the exception. If it consumes all, it is a retraction and void.
Example: 'Ten except nine' leaves one; 'Ten except ten' is void.
4
If different genus, exception can exhaust
If the exception is from a different genus (e.g., dirhams except wheat), it is valid even if it covers the entire principal, as long as it is connected.
Example: 'My women are divorced except these' is valid even if these are all his women.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
According to the Sahibayn, excepting the majority is valid; Abu Yusuf holds it invalid. Multiple exceptions are resolved by alternating negation and affirmation.

In the previous lesson, we learned the basic rules of exception. Now we dive into a key internal Hanafi dispute: can a person validly except the majority of what they acknowledge? Abu Yusuf argues that excepting the majority is effectively a retraction, but the two senior imams (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) maintain it is valid. Also, when multiple exceptions are made in a single statement, a precise arithmetic method is needed to determine the final amount.

1

The base text says 'it is the same whether he excepts the lesser or the greater'—this is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Then note Abu Yusuf's objection: excepting the majority is void.

Acknowledgment of an Item in a Container: Literal Interpretation of Speech
Ruling on acknowledgment of an item within a container or property, with internal Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that acknowledgment is taken literally to determine whether a container or real estate is included in an acknowledgment.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment is speech: only what is explicitly said is owed, not the container or property.

When someone acknowledges an item located 'in' something else—like a beast in a stable or dates in a basket—does that acknowledgment extend to the container? The Hanafi school answers with a clear principle: acknowledgment follows the literal words used. This lesson unpacks that rule, examines its limits, and explores the internal debate over real estate and ghasb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its literal scope
Read the acknowledgment carefully: what exactly did the person say? Only the words uttered create liability. For example, 'I owe a beast in a stable' means only the beast.
2
Analyze the language: 'in' vs. 'from'
The particle matters. 'In' (في) does not transfer ownership of the container; 'from' (من) indicates removal and may imply the container is not included. Distinguish between 'food in a sack' (both owed, because sack is necessary for access) and 'dates from a basket' (only dates owed).
The exception is when the container is essential to take the item (e.g., a handkerchief for a garment), then the container is included.
3
Apply to real estate: the ghasb distinction
Real estate (like a stable or house) cannot be the object of ghasb according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Therefore, acknowledging a beast 'in a stable' does not require returning the stable. Muhammad disagrees, arguing real estate can be guaranteed by ghasb.
This ikhtilaf turns on whether ghasb applies to immovables.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: only the movable item is owed, not the container or real estate, unless the container is necessary for access.
The author of al-Jawharah al-Nayirah presents this as the established view.

Dispute on Acknowledging 'a Garment in Ten Garments'
An intra-Hanafi dispute on the interpretation of an acknowledgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that only one garment is owed when someone acknowledges 'a garment in ten garments' and evaluate Imam Muhammad's counter-argument.

Core Idea
Custom and the default of non-liability limit acknowledgment to one garment, despite literal possibility.

This ruling involves an acknowledgment where the wording creates ambiguity. The Hanafi majority and Imam Muhammad disagree on whether the ten garments are included. The dispute turns on custom, the meaning of the particle 'fi', and the default principle of bara'at al-dhimmah.

Guided Path
1
State the acknowledgment wording
The debtor says 'I owe a garment in ten garments.' This creates ambiguity about whether the ten are included.
2
Identify the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold only one garment is owed. Muhammad al-Shaybani holds eleven are owed.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Examine the proof for the majority
Customarily, ten garments cannot be a container for one. Also, Abu Yusuf argues that 'fi' can mean 'among', creating doubt; the default is no liability.
The Quranic verse 89:29 is cited to show 'fi' meaning 'among'.
4
Examine Muhammad's proof
Muhammad argues it is possible to wrap a valuable garment in ten others, so all eleven may be owed.
Possibility vs. custom.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: only one garment is owed. The thamarah al-khilaf is the number of garments owed.

Commentary
This passage addresses two final rulings in the chapter. First, a dispute regarding acknowledgment of 'a garment in ten garments': Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that only one garment is owed because ten garments cannot serve as a container for a single garment customarily. Imam Muhammad argues that eleven garments could be owed because one might wrap a precious garment in ten others. Abu Yusuf further points out that the particle 'fi' can mean 'among' (as in Quran 89:29 'Enter among My servants'), creating ambiguity; the default principle is that liability is not established (bara'at al-dhimmah). The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Second, if someone acknowledges usurping a garment and later brings a defective one, his statement (that this is the garment) is accepted upon oath because ghasb does not require the item to be flawless. The burden of proof lies on the claimant if he disputes the defectiveness.

Ruling on Returning a Defective Usurped Garment
A ruling on the validity of returning a defective usurped property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on an acknowledgment of usurping a defective garment and explain the reason.

Core Idea
The obligatory return of a usurped item is not conditional on its soundness; a defective return is accepted.

The second ruling deals with a situation where someone acknowledges usurping a garment but later produces a defective one. The sharh clarifies that the acknowledgment is valid and the defendant's statement is accepted upon oath because ghasb does not require perfection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The usurper acknowledges taking a garment and brings a defective one. The claimant may dispute the defect or claim the garment was sound.
2
State the ruling
The defendant's statement that this is the garment is accepted with his oath.
3
Examine the reason
Usurpation (ghasb) is not specific to sound items; the obligation is to return the exact item taken, even if defective.
4
Contrast with sales
In sales, a defect gives the buyer a right to return; in ghasb, the return of the same item fulfills the obligation.
5
Apply to practice
If the claimant wants compensation for the defect, he must prove the defect existed at the time of usurpation.

Definition and General Rule of Agency (Wakalah)
Definition and general rule of agency with exceptions and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of agency, apply the general rule that any contract you can do yourself can be delegated, and identify the exceptions for litigation and execution of hudud and qisas, including the intra-Hanafi dispute on consent.

Core Idea
You can delegate any contract you can do yourself, but hudud and qisas cannot be delegated when the principal is absent.

This lesson opens the chapter on agency (wakalah) from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. We begin with the linguistic and technical definition, then explore the foundational principle: what you can do yourself, you can delegate. However, not every act falls under this rule, and specific exceptions exist. We will also examine an important intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding litigation agency and the role of the opponent's consent.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of wakalah
Learn the linguistic (preservation) and technical (appointing another for a known disposition) definitions. Understand that 'wakil' means protector.
The term is derived from the Qur'anic phrase 'wa kafa billahi wakila'.
2
State the general rule and its rationale
The rule: every contract a person may conclude for himself, he may appoint an agent for. The rationale: need due to inability or absence.
Notice the text specifies 'contracts' not 'acts'.
3
Distinguish contracts from mere acts
Acts like executing qisas (retaliation) one can do personally but cannot delegate when absent. This shows the scope is limited to contractual dispositions.
This is a key restriction.
4
Analyze the exceptions for litigation and execution
Agency for litigation (khusumah) is generally allowed, and for execution (istifa') except hudud and qisas when principal absent. Learn the reasons.
Abu Yusuf added li'an to the exceptions.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on litigation consent
Abu Hanifa requires opponent's consent unless principal is sick or absent 3+ days. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allow it generally. The mufta bihi follows the Sahibayn.
This is a practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the agent cannot act without opponent's consent unless the principal is excused.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on al-wakalah (agency) from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The author first gives the linguistic and technical definition: wakalah in language means preservation (hifz), derived from 'wakil' meaning protector; technically, it is the appointment of another to act on one's behalf in a legally recognized disposition (tasarruf ma'lum). He then states the general principle: any contract that one may validly conclude for oneself, one may also delegate to an agent. The rationale is that a person may be unable to act directly, needing an agent. The author clarifies that the principle only applies to contracts, not to mere acts like execution of qisas (retaliation), which one may do oneself but cannot delegate when the principal is absent. Agency requires explicit wording, such as 'I appoint you as agent to sell my slave' or 'to buy such-and-such.' The text then addresses specific cases: (1) It is permissible to appoint an agent for litigation (khusumah) – i.e., to sue or defend – in all types of rights, and also for proving those rights through evidence. (2) It is permissible to appoint an agent for execution (istifa') of rights, except for hudud (fixed corporal punishments) and qisas, where agency is invalid if the principal is absent from the session (i.e., the place where the punishment is carried out). This exception applies to the person who was slandered (maqdhuuf), the victim of theft, and the guardian of retaliation. An intra-Hanafi dispute is then mentioned: Abu Yusuf's reported view that certain expressions (e.g., 'I love that you sell,' 'I am pleased,' 'I wish,' 'I intend') constitute a valid agency, while saying 'I do not forbid you' does not. Separately, Abu Hanifa holds that agency in litigation requires the opponent's consent, except when the principal is sick or absent for three days journey or more. The passage thus presents the core definition, general rule, and key exceptions and disagreements within the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
الوكالة (al-wakālah): In this text, al-wakalah is defined as the appointment of another to act on one's behalf in a known disposition (tasarruf ma‘lum).
التوكيل (al-tawkīl): The act of appointing an agent (wakil) to perform a task on one's behalf.
الخصومة (al-khuṣūmah): Litigation or legal dispute; here it means the act of suing or defending a claim in court.
الاستيفاء (al-istīfā’): Execution or collection of a right, such as taking retaliation or extracting a debt. In this text it specifically refers to executing hadd punishments or qisas.

Agency Nullified by Death and Continuous Insanity
Ruling and commentary on nullification by death and continuous insanity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three nullifiers of agency and identify the Hanafi definition of continuous insanity (mutbiq) and its intra-madhhab variations.

Core Idea
Only continuous insanity (mutbiq) voids agency; temporary fainting does not.

The agency contract is built on the principal's command. When the principal loses capacity, the command ceases. This lesson examines how death and continuous insanity nullify agency, focusing on the intra-Hanafi dispute over the minimum duration of mutbiq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nullifying events
Read the matn: agency is invalidated by death of principal, continuous insanity (junun mutbiq), and apostasy with joining dar al-harb. Focus on death and insanity first.
Apostasy is treated separately in the next lesson.
2
Understand the reasoning
The agent acts by command (amr). Death and insanity void the capacity to command, so acting without command is invalid. If the principal recovers, agency returns.
This is based on logical necessity (dalil aqli).
3
Define mutbiq (continuous insanity)
Continuous insanity is distinguished from temporary fainting/illness. The text gives three views: Abu Yusuf says one month (based on fasting) or more than a day and night (based on prayers); Muhammad says a full year (based on missing all obligations); Abu Hanifa adopts one month.
Al-Karkhi reports Abu Hanifa's view as one month.
4
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is one month (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf's first view). This is more cautious and aligns with majority practice.
Muhammad's view is recorded but not preferred.
5
Apply the ruling
If the principal becomes insane for less than a month intermittently, agency continues. If the insanity is continuous for a month or more, agency is void.
The agent must track duration to know authority.

Effect of Apostasy on the Agency Contract
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of principal's apostasy on agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the effect of the principal's apostasy on the agency contract and evaluate the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa suspends agency upon apostasy; Sahibayn wait until death or judgment.

Apostasy places the principal in a state of suspended legal capacity. Does this immediately affect the agency contract? The Hanafi school has a notable intra-madhhab ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa holds that the agency is suspended, while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that it remains valid until the apostate dies or is judicially declared to have joined dar al-harb. This lesson explores the proofs and practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Read the passage: 'هذا قول أبي حنيفة لأن تصرف المرتد موقوف عنده، وكذا وكالته'. Abu Hanifa views the apostate's transactions as suspended (mawquf). Hence the agency is also suspended.
Suspension means transactions are not immediately void but await final disposition.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
The passage: 'وأما عندهما: فتصرفاته نافذةٌ، فلا تبطل وكالته، إلا أن يموت أو يُقتل على ردته، أو يُحكم بلحاقه'. The Sahibayn consider the apostate's transactions effective, so agency remains valid until death, killing, or a judicial ruling of joining dar al-harb.
Judgment (hukm) is required for joining dar al-harb, not just physical relocation.
3
Examine each proof
Abu Hanifa's proof: apostate lacks full capacity (ahliyyat al-ada') for new transactions; existing contracts are suspended. Sahibayn's proof: apostate's property rights remain, so his contracts are effective during the istitaba period.
No direct textual evidence is quoted; it's rational argument.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, any sale made by the agent during apostasy is void if the principal dies or joins dar al-harb; under Sahibayn, it is valid regardless of the outcome.
This affects third parties dealing with the agent.
5
Apply to scenarios
Consider cases: apostasy of male vs female principal, and return before/after judgment. The passage gives specific rulings.
A female apostate does not affect agency until death or judgment because her apostasy does not void her contracts.
