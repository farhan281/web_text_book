No Profit: Mudarib Not Compelled to Collect Debts
Ruling when no profit: mudarib not forced to collect debts but told to appoint agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mudarib's duty to collect debts when there is no profit, and explain the reason based on the mudarib's status as a volunteer.

Core Idea
Without profit, the mudarib is a volunteer; volunteers are not compelled to fulfill their promises.

In a mudarabah partnership, the mudarib works with the capital owner's money. But what happens if the mudarabah ends with no profit and there are still unpaid debts? Is the mudarib forced to collect them? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling rooted in the nature of the mudarib's role.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Recognize the specific condition: no profit in the mudarabah when separation occurs. The outcome: the mudarib is not obligated to collect debts.
The ruling is contrary to what some might expect – that the mudarib must complete the collection.
2
Analyze the first rationale
The mudarib is a pure agent (wakil mahd) and a volunteer. A volunteer cannot be forced to complete what he volunteered for.
This is based on qiyas: volunteers in any contract are not compelled.
3
Analyze the second rationale
The debts belong solely to the capital owner (rabb al-mal). The mudarib has no share in them without profit, so he cannot be forced to act on someone else's property.
This reinforces the idea that obligation follows ownership of benefit.
4
Understand the recommended action
Although not compelled, the mudarib is told to appoint the capital owner as his agent to collect the debts, so that the capital owner's right is not lost.
The mudarib retains the rights of the contract delegation is necessary.
5
Note the variant wording in al-Jami' al-Saghir
Imam Muhammad uses 'ahil' (make a transfer) instead of 'wakkil' (appoint as agent), but the meaning is agency due to the similarity between hawalah and agency.
This shows intra-Hanafi nuance in expression, not in ruling.

Loss of Mudarabah Capital Is Charged to Profit
Ruling that mudarabah loss is offset against profit first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for handling losses in mudarabah and explain the qiyas reasoning that supports it.

Core Idea
Losses in mudarabah are first deducted from profit because profit is subsidiary to capital.

This passage establishes a fundamental principle in mudarabah (silent partnership): when assets are lost, the loss is first offset against any profit that has been earned, not against the original capital. The author supports this with a clear qiyas (analogy) to the treatment of loss in zakat, where loss is attributed to the exempt portion before the taxable nisab. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly determining profit distribution and protecting the capital owner's principal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal rule stated in the text
Read the Arabic: 'وما هلك من مال المضاربة فهو من الربح، دون رأس المال'. The rule is explicit: any loss from mudarabah assets is taken from profit, not from capital.
This rule applies regardless of the cause of loss (theft, damage, market decline) unless negligence is involved.
2
Extract the underlying rationale (ta'lil)
The author explains: 'because profit is subsidiary (tabi') to capital'. This is the legal cause (illa). The subsidiary should bear loss before the principal.
The Hanafi maxim 'al-tabi' tabi'' (the subsidiary follows the main) is at work here.
3
Analyze the qiyas (analogy) to zakat
The author draws an analogy: just as in zakat, loss is directed to the 'afw (the exempt portion below nisab) before affecting the principal, so in mudarabah loss is directed to profit (the subsidiary) before capital.
The 'afw in zakat is like profit in mudarabah: both are subsidiary and absorb loss first.
4
Apply the rule to a practical scenario
Consider a mudarabah with capital of $10,000 and profit of $2,000. If $500 of goods are damaged, the $500 loss is subtracted from the $2,000 profit, leaving $1,500 net profit to be shared. Capital remains intact at $10,000.
If loss exceeds profit (e.g., $3,000 loss), the profit is wiped out and the remaining loss ($1,000) then reduces capital.

Loss Allocation in Mudarabah: Profit First, No Liability
Ruling on loss allocation with proof by analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that loss in mudarabah is deducted from profit before capital, explain the proof by analogy to zakah and deposit, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on the mudarib's liability in an invalid (fasid) mudarabah.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, loss eats profit first, not capital; the mudarib is a trustee, not a guarantor.

This passage from the canonical Hanafi text establishes the foundational rule on how loss is allocated in mudarabah. It clarifies the mudarib's status as an amin (trustee), drawing analogies to zakah and deposit law, and notes an important intra-madhhab difference when the contract is invalid. By working through this lesson, you will understand not only the ruling but also the legal reasoning that supports it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling
State the two main rules: (1) any loss in mudarabah property is deducted from profit, not from capital; (2) if loss exceeds profit, the mudarib is not liable for the excess.
This sets the foundation: profit is subsidiary to capital, so loss strikes the subsidiary first.
2
Understand the proof (dalil) by analogy
Analyze the two analogies: (a) like zakah, where loss is attributed to the surplus (al-‘afw) before the principal; (b) like a deposit (wadi‘ah), where the holder is a trustee and his word is accepted regarding loss.
The proof is based on qiyas jaliy: the commonality is that profit is subsidiary and the mudarib holds as a trust.
3
Note the mudarib's status as trustee
Explain that the mudarabah property is amanah (trust) in the mudarib's hands. He is not liable for loss unless negligence is proven. His word is accepted regarding the loss, just as in a deposit.
This principle is decisive for the no-liability rule.
4
Examine the special case of prior profit distribution
If profit has already been distributed and then a loss occurs while the mudarabah continues, the distributed profit must be returned to restore the capital first. Only after full recovery can any remainder be divided as profit.
This shows the priority of capital preservation; profit distribution is provisional until the contract ends.

Owner Restriction on Mudarib's Sales (Cash or Credit)
Ruling on restrictions in mudarabah sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when an owner restricts a mudarib to cash-only or credit-only sales and apply the analogy to agency.

Core Idea
A mudarib must obey a cash-only restriction, but a credit-only restriction does not bar cash sales.

In mudarabah, the owner may specify how the mudarib conducts sales. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that such specifications are binding in one direction but not the other. Understanding these rules ensures the mudarib knows the limits of his authority and can maximize the owner's benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two restrictions
The owner may say 'sell only for cash' or 'sell only on credit'. Note that these are different: one prohibits credit, the other commands credit.
The text explicitly mentions both scenarios.
2
Determine the mudarib's obligation for each
For cash-only: the mudarib must obey. For credit-only: the mudarib may still sell for cash because it is more beneficial.
The ruling is not symmetrical: cash restriction is binding, credit restriction is not.
3
Understand the reasoning and analogy
The passage explains that cash saves hastens the owner's money and is intrinsically better. The analogy with an agent authorized to sell at a minimum price supports the idea that acting more beneficially is allowed.
The underlying principle is maximizing the owner's benefit.

Mudarib Cannot Marry Slaves or Make Kitabah: The Limits of Authority
Ruling on Mudarib's Authority: Marriage and Kitabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a mudarib cannot marry slaves or make kitabah from mudarabah capital, and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the female slave.

Core Idea
The mudarib's authority is strictly limited to trade; marriage and manumission are not commercial activities.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the limits of a mudarib's authority. While the mudarib is authorized to trade, he may not use mudarabah funds to marry a slave or enter into a kitabah contract. However, there is a subtle dispute among the Hanafi imams regarding a female slave: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad prohibit it, while Abu Yusuf permits it. Understanding this difference hinges on whether marriage is considered a commercial transaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three prohibited acts
List the three acts the mudarib cannot perform with mudarabah capital: marrying a male slave, marrying a female slave, and making a kitabah contract.
There is agreement on male slave and kitabah; dispute only on female slave.
2
Analyze the rationale for the male slave
The reason given is that marriage creates a debt (the dowry) that attaches to the mudarabah without any compensation return.
This is a clear case of burden without benefit.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on the female slave
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that marriage is not trade, analogizing to the fact that a ma'dhunah (authorized slave-girl) cannot marry herself. Abu Yusuf argues that marrying a female slave yields the dowry (compensation) and saves maintenance costs, making it akin to a sale.
The core issue: Is marriage purely transactional?
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Abu Yusuf, if the mudarib marries the female slave, the marriage is valid and the dowry benefits the mudarabah. According to the majority, the marriage is invalid and the mudarib must compensate the mudarabah for any loss.
The difference affects liability and validity.
5
Apply the same principle to kitabah
Kitabah is a manumission contract, not a trade, so it is unanimously prohibited for the mudarib.
This reinforces the rule: only trade is within scope.
