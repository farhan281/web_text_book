Imprisonment for Debt: The Father, the Mukatab, and the Guardian
Rulings on imprisoning father, mukatab, and guardian for debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on imprisoning a father for his son's debt and for maintenance, apply the ta'lil to the mukatab case, and identify when a guardian may be imprisoned for a minor's debt.

Core Idea
A father is not imprisoned for his son's debt, but a guardian is — the key is whether non-payment constitutes oppression.

This passage from al-Quduri presents three distinct cases concerning imprisonment for debt, each with its own rationale. While a father is exempt from imprisonment for his son's debt, he can be imprisoned for withholding maintenance. Similarly, a mukatab is not imprisoned for his kitabah debt because the debt does not arise from oppression. In contrast, a guardian who refuses to pay a minor's debts from the minor's wealth can be imprisoned. Understanding the underlying ta'lil (reason) helps distinguish these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three cases
List the three parties mentioned: father (for son's debt), mukatab (for kitabah debt), guardian (for minor's debt).
2
Determine the ruling for each
For each case, state whether imprisonment is allowed or not: father not imprisoned for debt but imprisoned for maintenance; mukatab not imprisoned; guardian imprisoned if he refuses.
3
Extract the ta'lil for the mukatab
The passage explicitly gives the reason: 'because he does not become an oppressor by that, and imprisonment is the penalty for oppression.'
4
Identify the condition for the guardian
Note the prerequisites: the minor has wealth, the guardian is permitted to pay the debt, and he refuses. Only then does the judge imprison him.
5
Synthesize the general principle
Imprisonment for debt is a penalty for oppression (zulm), not for mere inability or contractual non-payment. Thus, the father and mukatab are not imprisoned because their non-payment is not oppressive.
The guardian is imprisoned because his refusal is a form of oppression.

A father, a mukatab, and a minor’s guardian are exempt from imprisonment for debt, but for different reasons.

The general rule in Hanafi fiqh is that a debtor who is able to pay but refuses may be imprisoned as a coercive measure. However, there are exceptions. This lesson presents three specific cases from the matn of al-Quduri: the father, the mukatab, and the minor debtor's guardian. Understanding these exceptions reveals how the law considers personal status and the nature of the obligation.

Identify the condition that triggers imprisonment

Imprisonment for Debt and Proof of Insolvency
Commentary on imprisonment for debt and evidence of insolvency

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Evidence of insolvency is accepted only after imprisonment; imprisonment itself proves the debtor's distress.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira expounds the fiqh of debt imprisonment and proof of insolvency. It clarifies that imprisonment is a coercive measure, not a punishment, and that the acceptance of testimony about a debtor's insolvency depends on whether it occurs before or after imprisonment. The text also highlights an internal Hanafi difference on the matter and provides the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Recognize that the passage addresses two main questions: (1) the procedure for imprisoning a debtor, and (2) when testimony of insolvency is accepted.
The text is a commentary on al-Quduri's matn, expanding the ruling.
2
Extract the ruling on imprisonment duration
Note that the imprisonment period is not fixed; it depends on the judge's discretion based on the debtor's likely distress. The source mentions two to three months as a common range but emphasizes individual circumstances.
The period is left to the judge's opinion (ra'y al-hakim).
3
Understand the proof of insolvency
Distinguish between evidence before and after imprisonment. Before imprisonment, testimony of insolvency is not accepted because witnesses cannot know of hidden wealth. After imprisonment, the imprisonment itself is circumstantial proof, so testimony is accepted.
This is based on the principle that negative testimony is generally not reliable without supporting evidence.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Recognize that two narrations exist regarding acceptance of evidence before imprisonment: one accepts, one does not. The majority (عامة المشايخ) and al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah prefer non-acceptance.
The mufta bihi position is non-acceptance before imprisonment.
5
Apply the ruling after release
After release, the debtor cannot be confined to one place. Creditors may accompany him and go around with him but cannot prevent him from traveling or transacting.
This limits the creditors' remedy to monitoring, not restraint.

Creditors' Rights: Taking Surplus Earnings and Mulazamah
Ruling on taking surplus income and accompaniment for debt collection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking surplus earnings and accompaniment (mulāzamah) for debt collection, and apply the exceptions regarding female debtors and harm.

Core Idea
Creditors may take surplus earnings and accompany the debtor, but never use violence.

This lesson examines the Hanafi school's permissibility of two debt collection methods: taking the debtor's surplus income beyond basic needs, and physically accompanying the debtor (mulāzamah). These rulings are grounded in a prophetic hadith and are subject to important exceptions that protect the debtor's dignity and privacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two collection methods
The passage gives creditors two tools: taking surplus earnings (above family expenses) and accompanying the debtor. Note that taking surplus is divided proportionally among multiple creditors.
2
Examine the primary proof

Hadith is zanni al-thubut but its meaning is clear (qati al-dalala).

If the debtor is a woman, a male creditor cannot accompany her due to seclusion (khalwa). Instead, a trustworthy female agent is sent to accompany her.
This exception prioritizes the prohibition of khalwa over debt collection.

Resolve a conflict between creditor and debtor preferences
If the debtor prefers imprisonment and the creditor prefers mulāzamah, the creditor's choice prevails because it is more effective. However, if mulāzamah would cause clear harm to the debtor (e.g., preventing entry to his home), the judge may order imprisonment to prevent harm.
The judge has discretion to prevent abuse.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Bankruptcy Declaration (Iflas)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether iflas declaration prevents creditor accompaniment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on the effect of a judge's iflas declaration and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
For Abu Hanifa, the judge's declaration of bankruptcy is only apparent, so the creditor's right to accompany the debtor remains.

This passage dives into an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a judge's declaration of bankruptcy (iflas) truly protects the debtor from being followed by creditors. Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples—Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani—disagree on the epistemological weight of such a declaration. The dispute turns on whether the declaration can conclusively establish the debtor's hardship (usrah), entitling him to respite.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue in dispute
What is the exact legal question? Here, it is whether the judge's iflas declaration prevents creditors from accompanying the debtor.
2
State the position of the Sahibayn
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the declaration is valid and establishes hardship (usrah), so the debtor deserves respite; creditors cannot follow him into his house and must wait until he comes out.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that iflas cannot be conclusively proven because sustenance is variable and witnesses can only attest to apparent poverty, so the declaration does not invalidate the creditor's right of accompaniment.
4
Compare the epistemological basis
The Sahibayn treat the judicial declaration as establishing a legal fact (hardship), while Abu Hanifa treats it as only apparent—sufficient for repelling immediate pursuit but not for canceling an established right.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under the Sahibayn, the debtor is protected from accompaniment after iflas; under Abu Hanifa, the creditor may continue to accompany the debtor even after the declaration.

The creditors cannot follow him into his house if he enters out of necessity; they must wait. Abu Hanifa disagrees. He argues that bankruptcy cannot be conclusively proven because sustenance from Allah is ever-changing, and witnesses can only attest to the apparent lack of wealth, not to absolute poverty. Therefore, the declaration only serves to repel immediate pursuit, but does not cancel the creditor's right to accompaniment (mulazamah). In essence, the debtor is not fully protected. The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi, but the Hanafi school often gives precedence to Abu Hanifa's view, though in practice later scholars may follow the Sahibayn in certain contexts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Iqrar informs of a past right; it does not create a new one.

Confession (iqrar) is a foundational concept in Hanafi fiqh, often misunderstood as a transaction that creates rights. Guided Path

First, note that linguistically iqrar derives from 'qarra' (to be fixed). Legally, it is 'ikhbar'an kain sabaq' — information about a past occurrence. Contrast this with 'insha'' (creation).

The text gives examples: confessing that property in Zayd's hand belongs to Amr, or that a slave in another's hand is free. These confirm pre-existing rights; they do not transfer ownership or grant freedom instantly. If the confessor later acquires the property, he must hand it over.

The author discusses a false confession (kadhiban) where the confessee knows it is false. The confession is valid in court (qada'an) but haram for the confessee to accept (diyanatan). If the confessee voluntarily gives it away, it becomes permissible.
The diyanatan/qada'an distinction is crucial.

The text lists two conditions: (1) consent and free will (al-rida wa al-taw') — coercion invalidates; (2) sanity and puberty (al-aql wa al-bulugh) — the insane and minor's confession is invalid.
These are shurut for validity.
5

Combine the definition and conditions: iqrar is a voluntary, conscious acknowledgment of a past right. It is not a gift or sale, and its effect is to confirm, not create.

How Ambiguity Affects the Validity of an Iqrar
Distinguishes between permissible and impermissible ambiguity in iqrar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ambiguity in the thing acknowledged (which does not invalidate) and ambiguity in the person of the confessor or confesee (which invalidates), and apply this distinction to practical scenarios.

Core Idea
Ambiguity in the thing acknowledged is overlooked in iqrar, but ambiguity in who is involved voids it.

The previous lesson established who can make a valid iqrar. Now we turn to what can be acknowledged. The Hanafi school takes a remarkably flexible stance: you can acknowledge a right even if you do not know its exact amount. However, this flexibility stops at the door of personal identity. If the parties— either the one making the acknowledgment or the one receiving it—are unknown, the iqrar fails. This lesson unpacks the rationale behind this distinction and how it applies in real cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of jahalah (ambiguity)
The text distinguishes between jahalah in the thing acknowledged (al-muqarr bihi) and jahalah in the persons (al-muqarr lahu and al-muqarr). The first is permitted; the second is not.
2
Understand why ambiguity in the thing is allowed
The commentator gives examples: destroying property of unknown value, inflicting a wound with unknown compensation, or having an unsettled account. In each case, the right exists even if the exact amount is unknown, so acknowledging it is valid.
This is supported by istihsan (juristic preference) to facilitate acknowledgment of debts and torts.
3
Understand why ambiguity in the person invalidates
If the claimant (muqarr lahu) is unknown, e.g., 'I owe one of you 100 dirhams,' the iqrar is invalid because the unknown cannot be a claimant (mustahiqq). Similarly, if the confessor (muqarr) is unknown, e.g., 'You have 100 against one of us,' the iqrar is invalid because the defendant is unknown.
The right must vest in a known person and the obligation must attach to a known individual.
4
Apply the distinction to a new scenario
Consider: A person says 'I owe Zayd 100 dirhams, but I am not sure if it is 100 or 200.' This is ambiguity in the thing (amount) and is valid. If he says 'I owe either Zayd or Amr 100 dirhams,' that is ambiguity in the claimant and invalid.

Locate the phrase 'yushtarat li-sihhat al-iqrar: tasdiq al-muqarr lahu' – this tells you that tasdiq is a condition (shart) for a valid iqrar. Understand that without it, the iqrar is void ab initio.
The Arabic verb 'yushtarat' explicitly signals a condition, not a mere recommendation.
2
Understand the effect of denial
The passage says 'hatta law kadhdhabahu lam yasihh' – if the confesse denies (kadhdhaba), the iqrar is not valid. Note that denial here means explicitly rejecting the truth of the statement.
3
Trace the consequence of later acceptance
If the confesse later accepts, the original iqrar does not become valid; a new iqrar is required. This underscores the requirement of concurrent (simultaneous) acceptance.
The ruling prevents retroactive validation, protecting the confessor from unintended binding effects.
4
Recognize the confessor's right to retract
While the confesse is denying, the confessor may revoke his statement. This is because no binding legal obligation has yet formed; the confession is still conditional.
This right ends once the confesse accepts; after that, the confession becomes binding.
5
Appreciate the mutual nature
The three rules together show that iqrar is a bilateral act: it requires both the confessor's declaration and the confesse's affirmation. The ruling ensures that no one is bound by a statement that the other party disputes.
This reflects the Hanafi concern for protecting individuals from false or coerced confessions.

The minimum for 'great wealth' is the zakat nisab of the specified type.

The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt described as 'great wealth' (mal 'azim). The description must be given effect; if the confessor later claims a smaller amount, he is not believed.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The plural 'amwal' means at least three properties, each at least nisab, so the minimum is three times the nisab.

When a confession refers to 'great properties', the Arabic plural 'amwal' indicates a minimum of three. Each property must be at least the nisab for zakat (200 dirhams or 20 dinars), resulting in a total of 600 dirhams or 60 dinars. This lesson unpacks the reasoning.

The plural form is key.

Apply the rule for Arabic plurals
The minimum number for a plural in Arabic is three.
This is a grammatical rule: أقل الجمع ثلاثة.
3
Determine the minimum value per property
Each property must be at least the nisab for wealth: 200 dirhams of silver or 20 dinars of gold.
Nisab is the threshold for zakat.
4
Calculate the total minimum
Multiply the number of properties (3) by the nisab: 3 × 200 = 600 dirhams; 3 × 20 = 60 dinars.
5
Conclude the ruling
The confessor is not believed if he claims less than 600 dirhams or 60 dinars.
This gives a definite amount.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the minimum amount for 'many dirhams' and explain the reasoning behind the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
For 'many dirhams', Abu Hanifa sets the minimum at 10 based on language; the Sahibayn set it at 200 based on custom.

When someone acknowledges a debt as 'many dirhams', the amount owed is ambiguous. The Hanafi school has a well-known internal dispute on this point, rooted in different methods of interpretation—one linguistic, the other customary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the rule
The act is an acknowledgment of a debt described as 'many dirhams'. The rule is that the acknowledgee is not believed for less than a certain minimum.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that the minimum is 10 dirhams. His dalil is linguistic: the plural (jam') covers up to ten; numbers above ten are expressed with 'eleven' etc., so 'many' means more than ten.
He argues that ten is the endpoint of the simple plural form.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the minimum is 200 dirhams. Their dalil is customary: 'many' is what lifts a person from poverty to sufficiency, which is the zakat nisab of 200 dirhams.
They rely on common usage ('urf) rather than linguistic technicality.
4
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference directly affects the amount the acknowledgee must pay: either 10 or 200 dirhams.
This dispute has significant financial implications.
5
Consider the tarjih
The matn of al-Quduri presents Abu Hanifa's view as the default wording, but later Hanafi works may prefer the Sahibayn's view in some contexts.
The commentary does not explicitly choose one; it records both.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default number of dirhams owed when a person says 'kadha' with accusative, genitive, nominative/sukun, or plural forms.

Core Idea
The grammatical case of 'kadha' determines the number: nasb=20, khafd=100, raf/sukun=1, jama'=3.

In Hanafi fiqh, ambiguous expressions in confessions are resolved by linguistic defaults. The word 'kadha' (such-and-such) takes different meanings based on its case ending. This lesson covers the four basic forms, each yielding a fixed number of dirhams.

Guided Path

Identify the grammatical form of 'kadha'
Look at the word after 'kadha' and its case ending: accusative (dirhaman), genitive (dirhim), nominative/sukun (dirhamun/dirham), or plural (darahim).
The accusative is marked by fatha/tanwin, genitive by kasra/tanwin, nominative by dammah/tanwin, and plural by the pattern.
2
Apply the default rule for each case
For accusative: 20. For genitive: 100. For nominative or sukun: 1. For plural: 3.
These defaults are from the Zahir al-Riwayah and are relied upon in Hanafi fatwa.
3
Confirm the ruling
The person confessing is obligated to pay that exact number of dirhams, as the expression is interpreted by the court.
The rule is based on resolving ambiguity in favor of the most common usage.

Distinguishing Debt from Trust in Confessions: 'Ali' and 'Qibali' vs. 'Indi' and 'Ma'a'
Ruling on expressions indicating debt vs. trust in confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule to classify a confession as either a debt or a trust based on the expression used, and state the ruling when the confessor claims a deposit.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, 'ali' and 'qibali' indicate debt; 'indi' and 'ma'a' indicate trust.

In Islamic legal practice, confessions often hinge on specific wording. The Hanafi school has precise rules for interpreting common phrases like 'upon me' (ali) and 'with me' (indi). This passage explains these distinctions and an important nuance: what happens when a person who said 'ali' later claims it was a deposit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expression
Note the exact Arabic word used in the confession: 'ali', 'qibali', 'indi', or 'ma'a'.
2
Classify as debt or trust
Apply the rule: 'ali' and 'qibali' = debt; 'indi' and 'ma'a' = trust.
This classification is based on the apparent meaning (zahir) of each word.
3
Understand the linguistic basis
Recognize that 'ali' is a form of obligation (ijab), and 'qibali' derives from 'qabalah' meaning guarantee (daman). 'Indi' and 'ma'a' denote possession without obligation.
This reasoning is talil (giving the 'illa).
4
Check the deposit claim exception
If the confessor later claims it was a deposit (wadi'ah), determine if the claim was connected (same sentence) or separate (different statement).
Connected: he is believed (metaphorical interpretation). Separate: he is not believed.
5
Apply the connected rule
If connected, the word 'ali' is interpreted as 'the responsibility to preserve and deliver', so the confession is of a trust, not a debt.
This is an exception based on the possibility of a metaphorical meaning.

Interpreting Ambiguous Confessions of Possession
Ruling on interpreting confession as trust

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of establishing the lesser liability (aqalluhuma) when a confession of possession is ambiguous.

Core Idea
When possession is ambiguous, the lesser liability (trust) is presumed over guarantee.

When someone confesses that an item is in his possession, the nature of that possession may be unclear. Is he a trustee or a guarantor? The Hanafi school resolves this ambiguity by presuming the lesser obligation—trust. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this rule and its application in judicial settings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act of confession
Determine what the person said (e.g., 'it is in my house', 'in my box', 'in my bag'). This is a confession of possession (iqrar bi al-yad).
The confession must be clear that the item is physically in his control.
2
Classify the type of possession
Possession can be either as a guarantor (madhmunah, e.g., loan, deposit for guarantee) or as a trustee (amanah, e.g., trust, wadi'ah). The confession does not specify which.
3
Apply the rule of lesser liability
Since the confession is ambiguous, the Hanafi rule is to establish the lesser liability (aqalluhuma). Compare the obligations: a guarantor is liable for loss, a trustee is not. The lesser is trust.
This is based on the principle of favoring the defendant when evidence is unclear.
4
Handle the claimant's counterclaim
If the claimant asserts it is a loan (guarantee), his claim is not accepted without evidence (bayyinah). The default remains trust.
The burden of proof is on the claimant.

Core Idea
The wording of a confession decides: gift needs delivery, debt is immediate, partnership is joint ownership, loan is fungible repayment.

In Hanafi fiqh, the precise wording of a person's statement determines whether it is a gift, a debt confession, a partnership acknowledgment, or a loan. This lesson unpacks four key examples from the matn, showing how the word 'min' (from) signals a gift, while explicit negation of right signals a debt. Understanding these distinctions prevents misapplication in legal practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance
Read the exact phrase uttered. Notice key words like 'min', 'la haqq' (no right), 'fi' (in), and 'ariyah' (loan).
Each keyword triggers a different legal category.
2
Classify the legal category
Determine whether the phrase indicates a gift (hibah), confession of debt (iqrar), partnership (shirkah), or loan (qard).
'Min' signals beginning of ownership → gift; 'la haqq' removes the donor’s right → debt; 'fi' implies co-ownership → partnership; 'ariyah' treated as loan.
3
Apply the condition for validity
For gifts, check if delivery (qabd) occurred. For other categories, the utterance itself creates the legal effect without additional condition.
If the gift offer is not delivered, it is invalid. Confession, partnership, and loan are binding upon utterance.
4
Extend to similar items
The rule for loan (qard) applies to all items sold by measure or weight (makil/mawzun), not just dirhams.
This is a general principle: any fungible item lent is governed by qard rules.

The Hanafi position adheres to the precise linguistic usage, holding that 'na'am' in response to a negative question does not necessarily affirm the negative but rather the positive? Actually the detail: In Arabic, 'bala' is used to contradict a negative statement, affirming the positive content. 'Na'am' is used to agree with a positive statement. For a negative question like 'Do I not owe you?', the correct affirmation is 'bala' (yes, I owe you). If one says 'na'am', it might be interpreted as 'yes, you don't owe me'? That is the Hanafi argument. The opposing view considers 'urf' (custom) over linguistic precision. The relied-upon Hanafi position is that 'na'am' is not acknowledgment, though some later Hanafi jurists may take a different view. Students should understand the linguistic distinction as the basis of the Hanafi ruling.

Ruling on Exception in Acknowledgment and Its Types
Ruling on exception in acknowledgment and classification of exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid exception (istithnā') in a debt acknowledgment and apply them to determine whether the exception is valid or void.

Core Idea
A connected exception in acknowledgment is valid only if it leaves something remaining; covering everything is a void retraction.

In Hanafi fiqh, when someone acknowledges a debt but immediately excludes part of it, the ruling depends on several conditions. This lesson examines the types of exception—nullifying and specifying—and explains when an exception is valid and when it is considered a retraction. Mastery of these rules is essential for understanding the binding nature of acknowledgments and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is acknowledging a debt with an exception. The default ruling: exception is valid (sahih) if conditions are met, and the remainder is binding upon the acknowledger.
2
Distinguish the two types of exception
Exception is either nullifying (ta'tīl) or specifying (taḥṣīl). Nullifying voids the entire statement (e.g., 'in shā' Allāh' in vows). Specifying excludes part of the acknowledgment and is the type relevant here.
Only specifying exception applies to acknowledgments.
3
List the conditions for a valid specifying exception
The exception must be connected (muttasil) to the acknowledgment, must leave at least something remaining after the exception, and if from the same genus, cannot cover the entire amount (otherwise it is a retraction). If from a different genus, it is valid even if it covers all named.
Disconnected exception is invalid regardless.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on exception of majority
Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly held that exception of more than half is invalid, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allowed any amount as long as something remains. The later school follows the Sahibayn.
The mufta bihi position is that exception of any amount (lesser or greater) is valid.

Core Idea
In consecutive exceptions, the first negates, the second affirms — they alternate until the end.

This lesson explains the Hanafi method for calculating multiple exceptions in a single confession. Understanding this method is essential for judges and muftis when evaluating ambiguous statements of debt.

Guided Path

Exception in Compound Acknowledgment: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on exception in compound acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the validity of an exception in a compound acknowledgment according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah) of their disagreement.

Core Idea
An exception that takes out the entire subject (the whole kurr) is void and breaks the continuity of the exception.

In Hanafi fiqh, the validity of an exception (istithna) depends on the continuity of speech and whether the excepted item is part of the subject. This lesson examines a classic intra-madhhab dispute where a compound acknowledgment contains an exception that removes the entire class of one item. Understanding this case sharpens your ability to apply legal linguistics to financial acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acknowledgment and the exception
The person says: 'I owe so-and-so a kurr of wheat and a kurr of barley, except a kurr of wheat and a qafiz of barley.' Note that excepting a kurr of wheat takes out the entire wheat debt, and excepting a qafiz of barley takes out a part of the barley debt.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules the entire exception invalid. Reasoning: excepting the whole (istithna al-jumlah) is linguistically void; therefore, the statement 'except a kurr of wheat' is a void insertion that breaks the continuity. The subsequent exception of a qafiz of barley is treated as if said after a silence, so it is also invalid.
Key concept: 'inqata'a al-istithna' (the exception is broken)
3

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold the exception from barley valid, but from wheat invalid. Reasoning: the speech is continuous (al-kalam muttasil) and the exception from barley is connected; the void part does not break the continuity. They analogize to saying 'I owe you ten, O Zayd, except nine dirhams' where the vocative does not break continuity.
Analogy: 'as if he said: I owe so-and-so ten, O so-and-so, except nine dirhams'
4
Determine the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa: the acknowledger owes two full kurrs (wheat and barley). Under the Sahibayn: he owes one kurr wheat and 39 qafiz barley (since 1 kurr = 40 qafiz). Note also the nuance in Abu Hanifa's view: if the person called (vocative) is the acknowledgee, the exception is valid; otherwise not.

Interpreting Mixed Acknowledgments: Qiyas versus Istihsan
Series of rulings on ambiguous acknowledgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for interpreting acknowledgments combining numbers and units, distinguishing between qiyas and istihsan, and state the mufta bihi ruling for each scenario.

Core Idea
When a number and a unit are combined ambiguously, istihsan merges them, while qiyas separates them—except when three or more items are mentioned.

In Islamic law, acknowledgments (iqrar) must be interpreted to determine the exact liability. When someone says 'I owe you ten and a dirham,' is it eleven dirhams or ten dirhams and a separate dirham? The Hanafi school addresses this through a nuanced interplay of qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference). This lesson unpacks these rulings, showing how the number of items mentioned and the type of unit affect the outcome, and why the Hanafi position ultimately relies on istihsan to avoid confusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acknowledgment pattern
Listen to the wording: 'a hundred and a dirham,' 'ten and a dirham,' 'ten and three dirhams,' etc. Note whether the second item is a single unit, two units, or three or more units.
The pattern determines which rule applies.
2
Apply the default: qiyas vs istihsan
For a single unit after a number (e.g., 'ten and a dirham'), qiyas treats it as two separate liabilities: ten (ambiguous what type) and one dirham. Istihsan combines them into eleven dirhams. For two units, qiyas gives ten and two dirhams; istihsan gives twelve dirhams. For three or more, both agree: the number before 'and' modifies the unit, so 'ten and three dirhams' is thirteen dirhams.
The turning point is when the second number is three or more.
3
Understand the role of 'hundred'
When the number is 'a hundred,' the word 'hundred' itself is ambiguous (could be dirhams, dinars, or garments). The ruling: if followed by a single unit like 'dirham,' the 'hundred' is interpreted as that unit (dirhams). If followed by a different type like 'garment,' only one garment is owed, and the interpretation of 'hundred' is left to the acknowledger (e.g., a hundred dirhams).
The principle is that the unit after 'and' specifies the preceding number only if they are the same genus.
4
Extend to dinars and other measures
The same rules apply to dinars, weighed items, and measured items. For example, 'ten and a dinar' is eleven dinars by istihsan; 'ten and two dinars' is twelve dinars; 'ten and three dinars' is thirteen dinars by both methods.
The type of unit does not change the rule.
5
Apply the principle to multiple garments
If someone says 'a hundred and two garments,' he owes two garments, and the 'hundred' is interpreted by him. If he says 'a hundred and three garments,' all are garments (i.e., 103 garments).
This follows the same pattern: three or more items mean everything is that item.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the debtor's explanation is accepted for ambiguous terms in iqrar, and apply it to the terms 'نيف' and unspecified units.

Core Idea
In ambiguous iqrar, the debtor defines the meaning; his word is law, except where language sets a floor.

When someone says 'I owe you ten' without specifying dirhams or dinars, or adds a vague 'nif', the law presumes ambiguity. Hanafi fiqh resolves this by giving the debtor the right to clarify, as long as the clarification does not contradict linguistic norms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous term
Locate the word or phrase in the iqrar that is indefinite, such as 'شيء' (something), 'نيف' (surplus), or an omitted unit after a number.
The ambiguity can be in the quantity (nif) or in the category (unspecified dirham/dinar).
2
Apply the rule of debtor's explanation
The debtor is asked to clarify what he meant, and his statement is accepted without oath, unlike in the case of denial.
This is based on the principle that the debtor is the one who knows his own liability best.
3
Check linguistic constraints
Ensure the debtor's clarification does not violate the meaning of the term. For 'نيف', any increase is permissible, even less than one. For an unspecified 'ten', the debtor must pick a valid type (e.g., dirhams).
No lower limit for 'نيف'; but for 'بضع' or 'قريب' there are floors (covered in next lesson).
4
Determine the owed amount
Based on the debtor's explanation, the amount is fixed. If the debtor says 'ten dirhams', he owes ten dirhams. If he says 'ten and a nif of one dirham', he owes eleven dirhams.
The creditor may object, but the debtor's word prevails.

And if he says: 'He has ten on me', then the explanation is to him.
So if he says: 'dirhams, or dinars, or fils, or walnuts', then his word is accepted, just as if he said: 'He has something on me', then the explanation is to him.
And if he says: 'I have on him ten thousand dirhams and a nif (some over), or ten dirhams and a nif', then the ruling regarding the nif: what he says, either a dirham or more.
And he may make it less than a dirham, because nif is what increases, whether little or much. And if he says: 'bidth (some) and fifty dirhams', then bidth is three dirhams and above, and he is not allowed to decrease from three.

And if he says: 'He has on me close to a thousand, or most of a thousand, or about a thousand, or the bulk of a thousand', then he owes five hundred and something, and his word is accepted concerning the increase, and he is not believed regarding half or less.

Commentary
This passage covers several rulings on ambiguous expressions in iqrar. First, if someone says 'I owe ten' without specifying the unit, the debtor is asked to clarify, and his clarification is accepted. If he then says 'dirhams', 'dinars', etc., his word is taken. Similarly, if he says 'a nif' (an unspecified excess) over a number, he can define it as any amount, even less than one dirham. However, the word 'bidth' (a small number) has a minimum of three, so he cannot claim less than three. For phrases like 'close to a thousand', the minimum is five hundred plus something; the debtor's statement about the excess is accepted, but he cannot claim it is half or less of the total. These rules ensure that the iqrar remains valid while protecting the debtor from excessive claims.

Key Terms
نيف (nif): An indefinite surplus or excess over a round number.
بضع (bidth): A small number between three and nine inclusive; it has a minimum of three.
قريب من (qarib min): 'Close to' a number, which legally means more than half but not the whole.
