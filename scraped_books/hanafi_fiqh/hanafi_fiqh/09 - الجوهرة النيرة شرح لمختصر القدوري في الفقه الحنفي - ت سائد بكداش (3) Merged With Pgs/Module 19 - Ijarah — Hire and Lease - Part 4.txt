Hiring and Transactions of Undivided Share (Musha')
Dispute on hiring musha' and its extension to rahn, hiba, waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on hiring, pledging, gifting, and endowing an undivided share, and distinguish the rulings of Abu Hanifa, the Sahibayn, and Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Delivery of musha' is the crux: Abu Hanifa denies it, Sahibayn affirm it via takhliyah or rotation.

The permissibility of transactions involving undivided property (musha') hinges on the feasibility of delivery. Abu Hanifa maintained that hiring musha' is invalid because delivery is impossible, except from a co-owner. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argued that delivery can be achieved through vacating the property or rotating usage. This lesson explores that dispute and extends it to other contracts like pledge, gift, and endowment.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on hiring musha'
Identify the two views: Abu Hanifa prohibits hiring musha' except from the co-owner; the Sahibayn permit it.
The text begins with Abu Hanifa's view, then gives the Sahibayn's.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's argument
They base permissibility on delivery via takhliyah (vacating) or tahaayu' (rotation), analogizing to sale and hiring from a co-owner.
This is a clear qiyas (analogy).
3
Extend to other transactions
Learn the rulings for rahn, hiba, and waqf: rahn invalid in all cases; hiba valid only for indivisible items; waqf disputed between Abu Yusuf (valid) and Muhammad (invalid).
Cross-madhhab: Shafi'i allows all these.
4
Apply the rule on subsequent shuyu'
If a hiring contract was valid initially and later the property becomes musha' (e.g., death of a co-lessor), the contract remains valid.
This is illustrated by the examples of hiring from two people.

Ijarah of Joint Property: The Sahibayn's View
States Hanafi ruling on hiring joint property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on ijarah of musha' according to the Sahibayn and explain their proof (takhliyah/tahayu').

Core Idea
Joint property can be leased if delivery is possible through vacating or mutual arrangement.

In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of leasing undivided shares of joint property (musha'). While Imam Abu Hanifa held such contracts invalid due to the impossibility of delivering a physical share alone, his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argued that delivery can be achieved through constructive means. Their reasoning is a classic example of rational proof (dalil aqli) within Hanafi legal methodology.

Guided Path
1
State the default position (Abu Hanifa's view)
The base text likely states that ijarah of musha' is not allowed because delivery of an undivided share alone is impossible.
This is implied; the passage focuses on the Sahibayn's counter-argument.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's ruling and their key proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that ijarah of musha' is permissible. Their proof: delivery (taslim) is possible through takhliyah (vacating) or tahayu' (mutual arrangement).
Their argument is based on the feasibility of delivery, not on textual evidence.
3
Explain how takhliyah/tahayu' enables delivery
Takhliyah means the lessor physically vacates the premises, giving the lessee effective control. Tahayu' is a mutual partition or arrangement between co-owners allocating exclusive use.
These methods make possession of the undivided share practically achievable.
4
Compare to analogous contracts
The Sahibayn analogize to leasing to a partner (where delivery is also partial) and to sale of joint property (which is valid).
Qiyas: if sale of musha' is valid, leasing should be similarly valid.
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If one follows Abu Hanifa, the contract is fasid (invalid); if one follows the Sahibayn, it is sahih (valid) and binding.
The mufta bihi in later Hanafi works is typically the Sahibayn's view.

Rahn of Joint Property (Musha') – Hanafi vs Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on rahn of joint property, identify the Shafi'i position, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A mortgage of joint property is invalid in Hanafi law because delivery of an undivided share is impossible.

What happens when you try to mortgage a share of a house you own jointly with someone else? The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools part ways on this question. In this lesson, we examine the short but decisive text from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah that states the Hanafi prohibition and notes the Shafi'i allowance. Understanding the basis of this difference sharpens your grasp of the conditions of a valid rahn contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the Hanafi ruling
The passage states that rahn of musha' (common property) is not permissible according to Hanafis, whether the property can be divided or not. This is a general prohibition covering both divisible and indivisible shares.
Note the phrase 'indo' (according to us) marks the Hanafi position.
2
Identify the opposing school and its ruling
Imam al-Shafi'i is quoted as saying it is permissible (yajuz). This is the opposite ruling.
The Shafi'i argument, not elaborated here, often relies on the possibility of constructive delivery.
3
Understand the Hanafi rationale
The Hanafi ruling is based on the requirement that the mortgaged item must be fully and exclusively delivered to the mortgagee. An undivided share cannot be physically handed over because it is shared, so the condition of delivery (qabd) is not fulfilled.
For divisible property, the share could theoretically be separated, but Hanafis still forbid it because the contract was concluded before division.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: a Hanafi judge or mufti would declare a mortgage on joint property null and void (batil). A Shafi'i would uphold it as valid.
This difference can affect property transactions in mixed-madhhab contexts.

Hiba of Joint Property (Musha')
Contrasts Hanafi and Shafi'i rulings on hiba of musha'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiba of musha' based on divisibility, explain the condition for validity, and compare it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
A gift of joint property is valid in Hanafi law only if the property is indivisible — otherwise it requires prior partition.

Gifting a share in a property you co-own seems straightforward, but Hanafi fiqh draws a critical line: can the property be divided or not? This lesson unpacks the ruling on hiba of musha' (joint property) and why the school distinguishes between divisible and indivisible assets. You’ll also see how Imam al-Shafi'i differs and what that means in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
The Hanafi ruling hinges on whether the property 'can be divided' (yahtamil al-qisma) or not. Determine what makes a property divisible in a practical sense.
Divisibility here means partition without significant loss of value or utility.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each case
If the property is indivisible, hiba is permissible (ja'iz). If divisible, hiba is not permissible (la tujuz). Memorise this binary: indivisible → valid; divisible → invalid.
'Indivisible' examples: a small house, a single room. 'Divisible' examples: a large plot of land, a building with multiple units.
3
State the Shafi'i ruling
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that hiba of musha' is permissible in all cases, regardless of divisibility. No condition of partition is needed.
This is based on the view that constructive delivery (takhliya) suffices for validity.
4
Recognise the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi law, a gift of a share in a divisible property is invalid; the donor must first partition the property, then gift the separate portion. Under Shafi'i law, the gift is valid even without partition.
This affects estate planning, partnerships, and family gifts.
5
Note authority weight
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view in the school. Although the passage does not detail the proof, the later fuqaha have upheld this distinction.
The evidence is based on the requirement of complete delivery (qabd) for hiba, which is only possible for indivisible property when co-ownership remains.

Effect of Subsequent Joint Ownership on Ijarah
Ruling on ijarah when joint ownership arises after contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of an ijarah contract when joint ownership arises after the contract, and apply the rule to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
A valid ijarah contract is not invalidated by subsequent joint ownership; it remains effective for the share of the surviving party.

Ijarah contracts are resilient to changes in ownership that occur after the contract is concluded. In Hanafi fiqh, once a lease is validly executed on non-shared property, later events such as the death of a lessor or lessee do not automatically void the entire contract. Instead, the contract continues for the remaining party's share, while being voided only for the deceased's portion. This lesson examines the two illustrative cases from the matn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is ijarah (lease) on non-common property. The ruling is that it is valid, and subsequent joint ownership does not invalidate it.
2
Analyze the first scenario
When a lessor dies leaving heirs, the contract remains valid for the share of the surviving lessor, even though the property becomes jointly owned.
Focus on the phrase 'لا تنتقض الإجارة في حصة الحي'
3
Analyze the second scenario
When a lessee dies, the contract is void only for the deceased lessee and remains valid for the surviving lessee.
Focus on 'انتقضت الإجارة في حقه، وبقي في حق الحي جائزاً'
4
Extract the underlying principle
The contract is not retroactively invalidated by partial changes; it continues for the unaffected parties. Subsequent events do not invalidate a contract that was valid at inception, except for the specific part affected.
This is a specific application of the general Hanafi rule that contracts are preserved as much as possible.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A craftsman may detain the object until paid, but liability for loss splits the Hanafi school.

This lesson dives into an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a craftsman's right to detain the object of his work until payment and what happens if the detained object is lost or destroyed. While all Hanafi imams agree on the right to detain, they differ sharply on liability and the fate of the fee. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi methodology of qiyas and the interplay between trust and guarantee.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed ruling
All Hanafi imams agree that a craftsman whose work leaves an effect (e.g., washer, dyer, tailor) has the right to detain the object after completing work until he receives his fee.
This is the starting point; the disagreement is on what follows if the object perishes during detention.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the craftsman is not liable for the loss and also receives no fee. His reasoning: the detention is not transgressive (the craftsman has a right to detain), so he remains a trustee (ameen). The loss of the object before delivery means the contract cannot be completed, so the fee is forfeited.
Key concept: no transgression (ta'addi) → no liability as a usurper.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the craftsman is liable and give him an option. Their reasoning: the object was already in his possession with liability (daman) before detention (he was responsible for it during work), so detention only reinforces that liability. The craftsman may choose to pay the value of the object unworked (forfeiting his fee) or pay the value worked (keeping the fee).
Note the shift: pre-detention possession is already guaranteed, so detention does not create a new trust status.
4
Analyze the qiyas (analogy) used
Abu Hanifa analogizes the craftsman to a trustee (ameen) who is not liable for accidental loss. The Sahibayn analogize to a possessor with guarantee (like a borrower or lessee) who is liable. The key difference is how they classify the nature of possession before and after detention.
The illa (effective cause) is the nature of custody: benefit to the holder vs. benefit to the owner.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and practical consequence
Later Hanafi scholars generally adopt the Sahibayn's view as the operative position for fatwa, as it better protects the owner's property. Practically, a craftsman who detains an object after work assumes liability for its safety; if it perishes, he must compensate the owner.
The option given by the Sahibayn allows the craftsman to choose between paying the unworked or worked value.

Artisans with Trace: Retention and Liability
Ruling on artisans with trace: retention and liability dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on an artisan's right to retain a work object and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding liability for loss.

Core Idea
Artisans whose work leaves a trace may retain the object, but liability differs: Abu Hanifa says no, Sahibayn say yes with an option.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah expands on a key ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: the right of artisans (e.g., dyers, washers, tailors) to hold onto a customer's object until paid. The central legal question is what happens if the retained object is destroyed – who bears the loss? The commentary presents the classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, with distinct rationales for each view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule and Its Condition
State the general rule: every artisan whose work leaves a trace (athar fi al-'ayn) may retain (yabsis) the object after finishing until payment. Then note the condition: the trace must be physical, like dyeing, washing, or tailoring that alters the object itself.
The passage explicitly mentions 'athar fi al-'ayn' as the distinguishing factor; later it adds a nuance from al-Dhakhira regarding the washer's materials.
2
Analyze the Case of Loss: Abu Hanifa's View
If the artisan retains and the object is lost, Abu Hanifa says: no liability (la daman) and no wage (la ajr). The rationale is that the artisan is not transgressing (ghair muta'addi) by retaining rightfully, and the object perished before delivery, so the contract is frustrated.
The phrase 'ghair muta'addi' is key: retention is a lawful act, so it does not trigger liability.
3
Analyze the Case of Loss: Sahibayn's View
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold the artisan liable (yadman). Their reasoning: the object was already in the artisan's possession as a trust (amanah) before retention, and retention only strengthens the guarantee. However, they grant an option: the artisan may either compensate the value without the work done (and get no wage) or with the work done (and get the wage).
This option is a compromise protecting both parties.
4
Examine the Nuance from al-Dhakhira
The commentary adds that if a washer uses additional materials like starch and eggs (which leave a trace), he has the right of retention; if he only whitens without such materials, he does not. This refines the scope of 'athar fi al-'ayn'.
This shows that the 'trace' must be a substantive addition, not mere cleaning.

Artisans Without Trace: No Right of Retention and Liability as Usurpers
Ruling that artisans like porters cannot retain object and become guarantors if they do

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for artisans whose work leaves no trace, explain the reasoning behind the prohibition of retention, and apply the liability consequences when retention occurs.

Core Idea
An artisan without trace cannot retain; if he does, he becomes a usurper with full liability.

The previous lesson covered artisans whose work leaves a trace, granting them a right of retention. Now we turn to those whose work leaves no trace—like porters and boatmen. They have no such right, and any retention makes them usurpers. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and consequences, including the special case of returning a runaway slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of artisan
Determine which artisans have work that leaves no trace on the object. Examples from the passage: porter (hammal), boatman (mallah), and washer (ghasl al-thawb). These are ajir mushtarak (shared workers).
Contrast with tailors or dyers whose work alters the object.
2
State the ruling and its basis
The ruling is that such artisans may not retain the object for the wage. The reason: the contract is for the work itself, which does not subsist in the object. Retention is therefore inconceivable—you cannot retain what is not materially related to the work.
The Arabic phrasing: 'لأن المعقود عليه نفس العمل، وهو غير قائم في العين، فلا يتصور حبسه'.
3
Recognize the liability if retention occurs
If the artisan retains the object, he becomes a usurper (ghasib). The object was a trust (amanah) in his hands; retention for debt converts it to usurpation. The owner has a choice: claim full value as carried (including wage) or value as not carried (no wage). The artisan remains liable for the full value.
This is similar to a deposit: a deposit cannot be retained for a debt.
4
Note the exception of the runaway slave
The returner of a runaway slave may retain the slave for the reward, even though his work leaves no trace. This is because the slave was on the verge of loss, and the returner revived him, akin to a sale. This is an istihsan exception.
The principle: special need (saving from loss) justifies retention.

Obligation in Invalid Ijarah: Fair Wage Not Exceeding Named
Ruling on consideration in void lease contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation in an invalid ijarah, distinguish the cap when the named wage is known, and explain the difference between valid and invalid ijarah regarding when the wage becomes due.

Core Idea
In invalid ijarah, the fair wage is due but capped at the named wage if known.

This lesson tackles a common scenario: a lease contract is invalid (fasid), yet the tenant has used the property. What compensation is owed? The Hanafi school balances protecting parties from unjust enrichment while respecting the named wage the parties themselves agreed upon. We'll also see an intra-Hanafi disagreement and a key distinction from valid leases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
First, confirm the lease is invalid (fasid). This could be due to uncertainty in the subject matter, invalid condition, or missing a condition of validity. The ruling only applies once the contract is identified as fasid.
Invalid ijarah does not transfer ownership of usufruct but if benefit is taken, compensation is due.
2
State the general ruling
The general rule: the lessor/worker is entitled to ujr al-mithl (the fair market wage) but this is capped by the named wage (al-musamma) if the named wage was known.
This cap is to prevent the lessee from being forced to pay more than they agreed, even if the fair wage is higher.
3
Identify Zufar's exception
Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) opposes the cap. He holds that the fair wage is due in full, even if it exceeds the named wage. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
Zufar's view is known but the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the majority view with the cap.
4
Apply the consensus exception
If the named wage was unknown (majhul) or no wage was named, then by consensus the fair wage is due without any cap. Examples: hiring a house for unspecified repairs, or hiring a worker without fixing a wage.
Uncertainty in the named wage leads to the contract being fasid; here the fair wage is fully due to avoid injustice.
5
Distinguish valid vs invalid ijarah
In a valid ijarah, the wage becomes due upon mere delivery of possession (takhliyah) of the property, whether the tenant uses it or not. In an invalid ijarah, the wage only becomes due upon actual utilization (haqiqat al-intifa').
This protects the lessee in an invalid contract—they only pay if they actually benefited.

Lessee's Option for Defect: Classification and Right to Rescind
Defect option in lease

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify defects that affect benefit and identify the lessee's right to rescind without court intervention.

Core Idea
The lessee has the right to rescind if the defect harms the intended benefit, and can do so without court.

This lesson addresses the lessee's khiyar (option) upon discovering a defect in the leased property. The key criterion is whether the defect harms the intended benefit (manafi'), not just the property's value. The passage classifies defects into two categories: those that do not affect benefit (no option) and those that do (option). It also discusses rescission without court, multi-property contracts, and the effect of repair.

Guided Path
1
Identify the criterion for option
The option exists if the defect 'يضر بالسكنى' (harms the residence) or generally 'يؤثر في المنافع' (affects the benefits). The lessee cannot benefit except with harm.
2
Classify defects into two types
Type 1: Defects that do not affect the benefit (e.g., loss of one eye of a slave if service unaffected, collapse of a non-essential wall) → no option. Type 2: Defects that affect benefit (e.g., illness of a slave, injury to an animal, structural collapse of a house) → option exists.
3
Understand the rescission process
The lessee may rescind alone (ينفرد بالفسخ) without needing a court judgment (لا يحتاج إلى القضاء). This is an independent right.
4
Apply rules for multi-property contracts
If two properties are leased in one contract (صفقة واحدة) and a defect occurs in one, the lessee may rescind both together. This prevents partial performance.
5
Note the effect of repair
If the lessor repairs the defect, the option is extinguished because the defect is removed. The lessee no longer has grounds to rescind.

Lessee's Right to Cancel for Defects and Automatic Annulment
Ruling on lessee's option for defects and automatic annulment of lease.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a lessee may cancel a lease for a defect and distinguish between automatic annulment (infisakh) and contractual cancellation (faskh) in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
A harmful defect gives the lessee an option; total loss of benefit automatically annuls the lease.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rules on defects in leased property. While some defects merely allow the lessee to cancel, others, like complete destruction, automatically terminate the contract. The text from al-Quduri, explained by the commentator, draws key distinctions based on the severity of the defect and its impact on the intended benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is finding a harmful defect in the leased property. The ruling is that the lessee has an option (khiyar) to cancel the lease (faskh) if the defect harms the intended benefit.
The option exists only if the defect is harmful; minor defects do not give this right.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): the lessee cannot benefit from the property except with harm. This harm justifies the option as a removal of hardship.
The proof is based on the principle of 'la darar wa la dirar' (no harm).
3
Classify the types of defects
Defects are of two types: those that do not affect the primary benefit (e.g., loss of a slave's eye, non-essential wall collapse) – no option; and those that do affect benefit (e.g., illness, partial collapse, galled back) – option established.
The classification hinges on whether the defect impairs the core purpose of the lease.
4
Differentiate between faskh and infisakh
For partial defects, the lessee must exercise faskh (cancellation) unilaterally. For total destruction or loss of essential benefit (e.g., house ruined, water cut), the lease undergoes infisakh (automatic annulment) without need for action.
The commentator states the correct view (sahih) is automatic annulment.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Some Hanafi scholars, including Imam Muhammad in one narration, hold that the lease does not annul automatically; it remains and the lessee merely has an option to cancel. The relied-upon view is automatic annulment.
The practical consequence: under the variant view, the lessee must cancel formally to stop rent; under the correct view, no action is needed.

Lease Termination Upon Loss of Benefit: Automatic vs. Rescission
Ruling and intra-madhhab dispute on lease termination when benefit is lost.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between automatic termination and rescission in Hanafi Ijarah law, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
When the leased property loses its benefit, the lease automatically ends—no need for formal rescission.

This passage from Sharh al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a practical issue in Ijarah: what happens when the leased property is destroyed or loses its essential benefit? The commentator clarifies an important internal Hanafi dispute about whether the contract dissolves automatically or requires an active rescission. Understanding this ruling protects both tenant and landlord from unfair rent obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify trigger events and legal consequence
Note the three events: destruction of house, loss of irrigation water, or loss of water for mill. The matn states 'the lease is terminated' (انفسخت الإجارة).
These events make the subject matter of the lease unusable.
2
Understand financial implication
The tenant is liable only for rent proportional to the period he actually used the property. No future rent is owed.
This prevents unjust enrichment of the lessor.
3
Owner's duty and tenant's option
The owner is not forced to repair; the tenant may leave without penalty if repairs are not made. Repairs of wells/cesspits caused by tenant's use are not forced on owner.
The tenant's right to exit is a remedy, not an obligation.
4
Analyse the intra-Hanafi dispute
The commentator notes that 'انفسخت' can mean the right to rescind, but the correct view (الصحيح) is that no rescission is needed. Some Hanafis (including a view from Muhammad) hold the contract does not terminate automatically.
Muhammad's narration: if the lessor rebuilds, the tenant cannot prevent him, implying the contract was not dissolved.
5
Identify mufta bihi
The automatic termination view is preferred (the sound position). The tenant may leave immediately without formal faskh.
This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

If the house is ruined, or the water supply of the estate is cut off, or the water from the mill is cut off: the lease is terminated, and he is liable for what he inhabited or used the mill. He is not forced to [pay for the repair]; because the owner is not forced to repair his property, and the tenant has the right to leave if the lessor does not repair that. Also, the repair of the well, the cesspit, and the sewer is upon the owner, and he is not forced to [do it] if it became full due to the tenant's action. If the term expires and there is dust from the tenant's sweeping or ashes in the house: he must remove it, because it happened by his action, so it is like goods he placed there. If the tenant repairs any crack in the house: he is a volunteer, no compensation is given to him. His statement: (If the house is ruined, or the water supply of the estate is cut off, or the water from the mill is cut off: the lease is terminated) means he has the right to rescind it. And in it is an indication that there is no need for rescission, and this is correct. Some of our companions said that the contract is not terminated. Commentary
This passage from Sharh al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses the ruling on lease termination when the property loses its intended benefit. The commentator expands on the matn of al-Quduri. The passage opens with a ruling statement: if the house is ruined, or the water supply of an estate is cut, or the mill ceases to have water, the lease terminates automatically, and the tenant is only liable for rent in proportion to the period he actually used the property. The owner is not compelled to repair his own property, and the tenant may exit the lease if the owner fails to restore the benefit. The same applies to wells and cesspits: their maintenance is the owner's responsibility, but if they became full due to the tenant's own actions, the owner is not forced to clean them. Upon the expiry of the term, if the tenant left debris such as dust or ashes, he must remove them as they are his own creation, akin to property he placed there. If the tenant voluntarily makes any repairs to the house, he does so without claim for compensation. The commentator then explains the matn's phrase 'the lease is terminated' — meaning the tenant has the right to rescind, but there is a subtle indication that no formal rescission is needed; the contract dissolves by itself. This is the sound position. However, some Hanafi scholars hold that the contract does not automatically terminate. There is a transmission from Imam Muhammad that if the lessor rebuilds the house, neither party can prevent the other from re-entering, which implies the contract was not automatically dissolved. Thus, the operative view is that termination requires active rescission by the tenant, and the narration from Muhammad supports the non-automatic view.

Key Terms
انفسخت الإجارة (infaskhat al-ijarah): The lease contract is terminated/dissolved automatically.
شرب الضيعة (shirb al-day'ah): The water supply/irrigation right attached to an agricultural estate.
رَحَى (raha): Mill (watermill) leased for grinding.

Termination of Lease upon Death of a Party
Ruling with proof on termination upon death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between cases where a lease terminates upon death of a party and where it continues, based on whether the contract was for personal benefit or for another.

Core Idea
Lease terminates on death only if contracted for one's own benefit.

A lease is a contract of mutual exchange: rent for usufruct. Death of a party upsets this exchange because the deceased's property is now owned by heirs. The Hanafi school distinguishes: if the deceased contracted for himself, the lease ends; if for another, it continues. This lesson unpacks the rationale and application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contracting capacity
Determine whether the deceased entered the lease for his own benefit or as an agent for another (e.g., father for son, guardian, waqf administrator).
The text uses 'li nafsihi' vs 'li ghayrihi'.
2
Apply the ruling for personal contract
If for himself, the lease is terminated upon his death. Commentators clarify this means the surviving party has the right to rescind (faskh).
Automatic dissolution vs right to rescind is a nuance from the commentary.
3
Apply the ruling for agency contract
If the deceased was acting for another, the lease continues after his death. Examples: father leasing for minor child, guardian, executor, waqf administrator.
The principal's interest remains, so the contract survives.
4
Analyze the rationale (ta'lil)
For personal contract: after death, enforcing the lease would take rent or usufruct from the deceased's heirs without their consent, which is impermissible. For agency, the benefit belongs to the principal, not the agent.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on justice.

وإذا مات أحد المتعاقدين وقد عَقَدَ الإجارة لنفسه : انفسخت الإجارة. وإن كان عقدها لغيره : لم تنفسخ الإجارة . ف يكون معنى قول الشيخ :انفسخت أي للمستأجر أن يفسخ وإذا آجر داره، ثم باعها قبل انقضاء المدة: فالبيع جائز، حتى إن المدة إذا انقضت : كان البيع لازماً للمشتري، وليس له أن يمتنع عن الأخذ. ولو أن المستأجر أجاز البيع جاز البيع، وبطلت الإجارة فيما بقي من المدة، ولو فسخ : فإنه لا ينفسخ البيع. فإن كان المشتري عالماً وقت الشراء بعقد الإجارة: فليس له أن يطالب البائع بالتسليم إلى أن تمضي الإجارة، وإن لم يكن عالماً وقت الشراء: فهو بالخيار : إن شاء نقضه بالعيب، وإن شاء أمضاه. * قوله: (وإذا مات أحد المتعاقدين وقد عقد الإجارة لنفسه: العقد). انفسخ أما موت المستأجر : فلأن العقد اقتضى استحقاق الأجرة من ماله، فلو بقينا الإجارة بعد موته: استحقت الأجرة من ملك الغير. وإن كان المؤجر هو الذي مات فلو بقينا الإجارة بعد موته: استوفيت المنافع من ملك غيره، وهذا لا يجوز. * قوله: (وإن كان عَقَدَها لغيره لم تنفسخ، مثل الوكيل، والوصي، والأب إذا آجر لابنه الصغير والمتولي في الوقف إذا عقد، ثم مات.
If one of the two contracting parties dies and he contracted the lease for himself: the lease is terminated. If he contracted it for another: the lease is not terminated. So the meaning of the shaykh's statement: 'is terminated' means that the tenant may rescind. If he leases his house, then sells it before the expiry of the term: the sale is valid, to the extent that when the term expires, the sale is binding upon the buyer, and he cannot refrain from taking [possession]. If the tenant permits the sale, the sale is valid and the lease is void for the remaining term; and if he rescinds, the sale is not rescinded. If the buyer knew at the time of purchase about the lease contract, then he has no right to demand delivery from the seller until the lease expires; if he did not know at the time of purchase, then he has the option: if he wishes, he can annul it due to defect, or if he wishes, he can confirm it. His statement: (If one of the two contracting parties dies and he contracted the lease for himself: the contract is terminated.) As for the death of the tenant: because the contract required the entitlement of rent from his property; if we sustained the lease after his death, the rent would be taken from the property of another. And if the lessor is the one who died: if we sustained the lease after his death, the benefits would be taken from the property of another, and this is not permissible. His statement: (If he contracted it for another, it is not terminated), such as the agent, executor, father if he leases for his minor son, and the administrator of a waqf if he contracts, then dies.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, now addressing the effect of death and sale on a lease contract. The commentator first presents the ruling: if a party dies and the lease was contracted for his own benefit, the lease terminates. However, if it was contracted on behalf of another (e.g., a parent for a child, a guardian, an administrator), the lease remains in effect after the agent's death. The commentator clarifies that the phrase 'is terminated' in the matn means the tenant has the right to rescind, not automatic dissolution. Then a new ruling is introduced: if the lessor sells the leased property during the lease term, the sale is valid. When the lease expires, the buyer must take possession and cannot refuse. If the tenant consents to the sale, the sale is effective and the lease is void for the remaining period. If the tenant rescinds (i.e., the lease), the sale is not affected. The buyer's knowledge matters: if he knew of the lease at purchase, he cannot demand delivery until the lease ends; if he was unaware, he has an option (khiyar al-'ayb) to cancel the sale or confirm it. The commentator then provides the rational proof (ta'lil) for the death ruling: the lease contract implies entitlement to rent from the tenant's property; if the tenant dies, the rent would be taken from his heirs' property (which they are not obliged to give), so the contract ends. Similarly, if the lessor dies, the benefits (usufruct) would be taken from his heirs' property without their consent, which is impermissible. Examples of contracts for others that survive the contracting party's death include a father leasing for his minor child, a guardian, a waqf administrator, or an agent — because the benefit belongs to the principal, not the deceased.

Key Terms
المتعاقدين (al-muta'aqidayn): The two contracting parties: lessor and lessee.
فسخ (faskh): Rescission of the contract by one party.
وكيل (wakil): Agent who contracts lease on behalf of a principal.
