The Hunter's Throw Sale: Gharar in Transactions
Definition and ruling of an uncertain sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the hunter's throw sale and identify the elements of gharar that make it invalid.

Core Idea
A sale with unknown outcome is invalid — gharar voids the contract.

This lesson examines a specific type of prohibited sale known as ḍarbat al-qāniṣ. The sale is invalid because the subject matter is entirely uncertain: the buyer pays for whatever (if anything) the seller catches in a single throw of his net. The Hanafi school classes this as fasid due to jahalah and gharar. Understanding this ruling clarifies the broader prohibition on uncertain transactions.

3

The sale is fasid (invalid) and not permissible.

Conditions in sales are either implied (valid), beneficial (invalid), or meaningless/harmful (valid sale, void condition).

When you add a condition to a sale, Hanafi jurists do not treat all conditions equally. Some are perfectly valid because they clarify what the contract already requires. Others invalidate the entire transaction because they give one party an unfair extra benefit. And some are simply ignored—the sale stands, the condition is void. This lesson unpacks these three categories with concrete examples and examines a key dispute within the Hanafi school itself.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the three categories
Read the opening lines: 'then this is of three types'. Your task is to identify the essential feature that distinguishes each category: whether the condition is implied by the contract (muqtada al-aqd), provides benefit to a party or subject, or has no benefit (or causes harm).
The first category has both elements valid; the second has both invalid; the third has valid sale but void condition.
2
Analyze the first category: conditions implied by the contract
Look at the examples: describing the price as cash/deferred or describing the commodity as a cook/scribe. The author says 'because these are conditions implied by the contract'. Your task is to understand that anything necessary for clarifying the thing sold or the price is essentially part of the contract itself, so it is valid.
This category covers typical descriptions that define the subject matter.
3
Analyze the second category: conditions with benefit
Identify conditions that give extra benefit to the buyer (tailoring, carrying), seller (retaining use), or subject of contract (manumission). Note the key phrase: 'not implied by the contract' and 'benefit for one of the contracting parties or for the subject of the contract, and it is among those capable of litigation'. The sale is fasid because it imposes an extra obligation.
Benefit to the subject only matters if the subject is a person (ahl al-khusumah); animals or objects are not litigants, so conditions benefiting them may be treated differently.
4
Analyze the third category: conditions with no benefit or harm
Examine the examples: 'not eat food', 'not sell animal', or harmful stipulations like tearing cloth, avoiding intercourse, demolishing house. Here the condition provides no genuine benefit, so it is void. But the sale remains valid unless the condition is harmful—in which case Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ.
Abu Yusuf invalidates the sale for harmful conditions; Muhammad holds the sale valid and condition void.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on manumission
Focus on the manumission example: the sale is initially fasid, but if the buyer frees the slave, Abu Hanifa rules that the sale becomes valid and the named price is due (istihsan), while the Sahibayn maintain it stays fasid and only market value is due. Your task is to understand the basis: Istihsan vs. qiyas with tadbir.
This dispute shows how the same factual scenario can lead to different outcomes depending on the juristic method.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the classification of conditions in sales according to the Hanafi school. The author, Ibn al-Hummam, expands on the concise statement from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar that conditions in sales fall into three categories. First: conditions that are implied by the contract (muqtada al-ʿaqd), such as describing the price as cash or deferred, or describing the commodity as a cook or scribe. These conditions are valid because they merely clarify what is already part of the contract; the sale is valid and the condition is binding. Second: conditions that are not implied by the contract and provide a benefit to one party or the subject of the contract (which is a person capable of litigation) and are not customary. Examples include tailoring cloth, carrying wheat, or cutting dates for the buyer; or the seller retaining use of a house, land, or animal after sale. Also, conditions benefiting the subject of the contract, like manumission, tadbīr, or istīlād (for a slave-girl). These invalidate both the sale and the condition because they introduce an extra obligation not part of the original contract and go against the principle that sales should be free of such encumbrances. A significant intra-Hanafi dispute arises in the case of buying a slave with the condition of manumission. Abu Hanifa holds that if the buyer frees him, the sale becomes valid (by istihsan) and the agreed price is due, as the condition transforms the invalidity into validity. The two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the sale remains invalid and the buyer owes the market value, analogous to the condition of tadbīr where no such transformation occurs. Third: conditions with no benefit, or even harmful conditions. If the condition provides no benefit at all (e.g., not to eat food or not to sell an animal), the sale is valid and the condition is void. If the condition causes harm (e.g., tearing cloth, avoiding intercourse, demolishing a house), there is a dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Abu Yusuf considers such a harmful condition sufficient to invalidate the entire sale, because it indicates a frivolous intent. Muhammad, however, considers that such a condition is simply void, but the sale remains valid, as the condition is not beneficial to either party.

Key Terms
البيع الفاسد (al-bayʿ al-fāsid): A sale that is legally invalid due to a defect in a condition or element, but may be rectified or becomes valid upon fulfillment of the condition.
الشرط (al-sharṭ): A stipulation added to the sale contract that imposes an extra obligation beyond the exchange of goods and price.
مقتضى العقد (muqtaḍā al-ʿaqd): What the contract inherently requires, such as delivery of the commodity and payment of the price, including necessary descriptions.
أهل الخصومة (ahl al-khuṣūmah): A person capable of litigation, i.e., a free person or slave who can sue and be sued, as opposed to animals or inanimate objects.

Conditions That Invalidate a Sale: The Hanafi Framework and Intra-School Disputes
Explaining types of conditions in sales and intra-Hanafi disagreements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conditions that invalidate a sale (making it fasid) and those that are void but do not affect validity, and state the intra-Hanafi positions on disputed conditions such as emancipation and harm.

Core Idea
A condition providing extra benefit to a party and not inherent to the contract invalidates the sale; conditions of no benefit or inherent rights are void without affecting validity.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah explains the critical Hanafi distinction between conditions that make a sale fasid and those that are simply void. The author presents numerous examples and then dives into intra-Hanafi disagreements among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on specific conditions like emancipation and causing harm. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying the law of sales in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of condition
Determine whether the condition benefits the buyer, the seller, the object of sale, has no benefit, or causes harm. Each type leads to a different ruling.
Conditions benefiting a party or the object are problematic; conditions without benefit are not.
2
State the general rule
The general rule is that a condition which provides extra benefit to a party and is not inherent to the contract makes the sale fasid (invalid in description).
This is based on the hadith prohibiting 'two transactions in one' and the principle that a contract should not impose extra obligations.
3
Apply the rule to examples
Work through the given examples: tailoring (buyer benefit), renting back (seller benefit), emancipation (object benefit) – all make the sale fasid. Conditions like 'no eating' have no benefit, so the sale is valid.
Examples from the text include buying cloth with tailoring, or a house with the seller residing.
4
Spot the intra-Hanafi disputes
Note where Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ from Abu Hanifa: on conditions of emancipation (price after emancipation) and conditions of harm (e.g., tearing cloth, no intercourse).
Abu Hanifa uses istihsan for emancipation; Muhammad holds the sale valid for harm conditions.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Understand the practical consequences: for emancipation, Abu Hanifa says named price; Sahibayn say market value. For harm, Abu Yusuf says fasid; Muhammad says valid.
This shows that the same scenario can yield different legal outcomes depending on which imam's view is followed.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on invalid sales (al-bay' al-fasid) explains various conditions that can affect the validity of a sale. The author first lists conditions that benefit the buyer: tailoring a cloth, carrying wheat, cutting fruit – these are services not inherent to the sale, so the sale becomes fasid. Similarly, conditions benefiting the seller: renting a house back, letting the seller ride an animal, wearing a cloth, or a loan – all invalidate the sale because they impose extra obligations. Conditions benefiting the object (e.g., emancipation of a slave) also make the sale fasid. The passage then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: Al-Karkhi reports that if a slave is bought with condition of emancipation, the sale is fasid; if the buyer then emancipates, Abu Hanifa (by istihsan) says he must pay the named price, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say he must pay the market value because it's a fasid sale. Abu Hanifa's reasoning is that the contract is initially fasid but becomes valid upon emancipation. Next, conditions with no benefit (e.g., prohibiting eating or selling) are void but the sale remains valid because they don't affect the contract. When the condition causes harm (tearing cloth, no intercourse, demolishing house), Abu Yusuf rules the sale fasid, while Muhammad rules it valid with void condition. The commentary then addresses a specific scenario: selling a slave with service or a house with residency for the seller – such conditions make the sale fasid because they combine a sale with a rental or loan, which the hadith prohibits as two transactions in one. The passage concludes with a dispute on conditions regarding intercourse with a slave girl: unanimously valid if condition is for intercourse (inherent right of ownership), but Abu Hanifa invalidates both conditions of intercourse and no-intercourse (according to Al-Khujandi), Muhammad validates both, and Abu Yusuf differentiates. This demonstrates the nuanced Hanafi approach to conditions in sales, emphasizing the principle that a condition not required by the contract that benefits a party invalidates the sale.

Key Terms
بيع فاسد (bay' fasid): A sale that is invalid in its description (wasf) but has the potential to become valid if the defect is removed, e.g., by satisfying the condition or repeating the contract.
شرط لا يقتضيه العقد (shart la yaqtadihi al-'aqd): A condition that is not required by the nature of the contract and imposes an extra obligation on one party.
استحسان (istihsan): A method of juristic preference departing from strict analogy (qiyas) for a more equitable result, used by Abu Hanifa in this passage.

Invalid Conditions in Sale: The Hanafi Ruling

Core Idea
A sale with a condition that benefits only the seller is fasid because it combines two contracts in one, which is prohibited.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents a key principle in Hanafi mu'amalat: extraneous conditions that benefit one party render a sale invalid. The author provides concrete examples and grounds the ruling in the Prophetic prohibition of 'two transactions in one'. Understanding this principle helps you distinguish valid conditions from invalid ones in contracts.

This is the core legal analysis.

The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear (qat'i al-dalala).

This step develops legal reasoning.

Prohibited Sales: Sale with Condition and Two Conditions
Explains hadith prohibitions on conditional and ambiguous sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a sale with a condition (bay' wa-shart) and two conditions in a sale (shartayn fi bay'), explain why they are prohibited in the Hanafi school, and apply the ruling to new scenarios.

Core Idea
A sale is fasid if it includes a condition benefiting one party or if the price is ambiguous.

The Prophet (PBUH) forbade several types of sales to eliminate uncertainty and unfair advantage. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lists six prohibitions, then expands on two: a sale with a condition beneficial to one party, and a sale with two conditions that create price ambiguity. The Hanafi school classifies these as fasid (defective) because they introduce gharar (uncertainty) or an extraneous obligation that conflicts with the contract's purpose. Understanding these rulings helps you structure valid contracts in daily transactions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the hadith prohibition
The passage begins by listing six sales prohibited by the Prophet. The first two are the focus: bay' wa-shart and shartayn fi bay'. Recognize that the prohibition is based on preventing gharar and injustice.
The full hadith is not quoted, but the prohibition is well-known.
2
Define 'sale with a condition'
Bay' wa-shart is a sale with a condition that benefits one contracting party (e.g., seller stipulates that buyer must lend him money). Identify that the condition is extraneous to the sale and benefits only one side.
Hanafis allow conditions that are part of the contract's purpose, e.g., stipulating delivery.
3
Define 'two conditions in a sale'
Shartayn fi bay' refers to a sale where the price is left ambiguous by offering two alternatives without fixing one. Examples: different prices for cash vs. deferred payment, or paying in different commodities. The price is unknown at the time of contract.
The ambiguity must be resolved at the time of contract; otherwise the sale is fasid.
4
Identify the defect and ruling
Both types are invalid due to either an extraneous condition (bay' wa-shart) or jahalah (price uncertainty). The Hanafi ruling is that the sale is fasid, meaning it can be rectified by removing the defect. If not rectified, the contract is void.
Fasid differs from batil: fasid can be corrected; batil cannot.

Such a condition renders the sale invalid because it introduces an extraneous obligation that conflicts with the purpose of the contract. The second prohibition, 'two conditions in a sale' (shartayn fi bay'), refers to sales in which the price is made contingent on two unspecified alternatives, creating ambiguity. Two Transactions in One (Safqatan fi Safqa) and Sale & Loan
Explanation of prohibited combined transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify examples of 'two transactions in one' and 'sale with loan' and explain why they are invalid.

Core Idea
Combining a sale with another exchange or a loan creates ambiguity and is prohibited.

This section covers two interrelated prohibitions: 'two transactions in one' and 'sale combined with a loan.' The first includes both conditional exchanges and mixing a sale with a service (like tailoring). The second is selling on condition that the buyer also gives a loan or gift. Both are invalid because they combine contracts in a way that creates uncertainty or extra obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first interpretation of safqatan fi safqa
The text gives: 'I sell you this slave for 1000 on condition that you sell me this horse for 1000.' This is a conditional exchange where one sale is contingent on another.
This creates a reciprocal dependency that makes the price ambiguous.
2
Identify the second interpretation
Selling cloth with the condition of tailoring, or wheat with condition of carriage to home. Here the price covers both the item and the service, mixing sale (bay') and hire (ijarah).
This combines two different contracts in one, which is prohibited.
3
Understand the ruling on sale with loan
Selling with the condition that the buyer gives a loan or a gift to the seller is invalid. This is a separate hadith prohibition.
The condition attaches an extra obligation beyond the price.
4
Recognize the underlying principle
Both prohibitions stem from the same idea: the contract must be clear and free from conditions that alter its essence or create ambiguity.
Hanafi fiqh strictly separates contracts to avoid riba and gharar.

Profit on What Is Not Guaranteed (Ribh Ma Lam Yudman)
Explanation of prohibited profit from items not yet taken possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the principle 'profit on what is not guaranteed' and apply it to cases where a buyer receives profits before taking possession.

Core Idea
Profit from an item is not lawful until the buyer bears the risk of loss through possession.

This section explains the prophetic prohibition of 'profit on what is not guaranteed.' The scenario: a buyer purchases a slave, and before taking physical possession (qabd), the slave receives a gift or earns something. The buyer cannot lawfully keep those accretions because he did not yet bear the risk of loss. The principle is that profit must be accompanied by liability (daman).

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The buyer buys a slave, but before taking possession, the slave is given a gift or earns income (kasb). The buyer then takes possession of the slave along with these accretions.
Possession (qabd) is physical taking of the item.
2
Understand the ruling
The accretions (gift or earnings) do not become lawful for the buyer; they are 'profit on what is not guaranteed' because the buyer did not bear the risk of loss before possession.
The ruling is that the buyer cannot keep the accretions; they belong to the seller or original owner.
3
Understand the principle
The principle is 'ribh ma lam yudman': one cannot profit from an item unless one bears its risk (if it were destroyed, the buyer would suffer loss). Until possession, the risk is still on the seller.
This principle is derived from the hadith and is part of Hanafi fiqh.
4
Note the scope
This applies to movable goods (manqulat). The accretions could be of the same type as the price or different—it does not matter.
It does not apply to real estate or cases where risk has transferred before possession? Actually, risk transfers with possession; until then, risk is with seller.

Selling Before Possession and Selling What One Does Not Own
Rulings on selling before possession and selling without ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which items cannot be sold before possession and explain the exception for salam.

Core Idea
Movable goods cannot be sold before taking physical possession; one cannot sell what one does not own except in salam.

The final part of the passage covers two related prohibitions: selling movable goods before taking possession (bay' ma lam yuqbad) and selling what one does not own (bay' ma laysa 'indahu). The first applies to manqulat (movables); the second is prohibited in all cases except for the salam (forward sale) contract, which has a special concession. These rules prevent speculation and ensure that the seller can deliver the item.

Identify the first prohibition
The passage states: 'As for his prohibition of selling what has not been taken possession of: it means regarding movable goods.'
This is based on a hadith: 'He who buys food, let him not sell it until he takes possession of it.' Hanafis extend it to all movables.
2
Identify the second prohibition
Selling what is not in one's ownership (or not in one's physical possession/control) is generally prohibited, with the exception of salam (forward sale).
The exception is that salam is allowed despite the seller not possessing the goods at the time of contract.
3
Understand the rationale
These prohibitions prevent selling goods one cannot deliver, which would cause gharar and potential disputes.
Salam is allowed by special textual permission because it fulfills economic needs.
4
Note the scope
The first rule applies to movables; real estate may have different rules. The second rule applies to all goods except salam.
In Hanafi, selling before possession is prohibited for manqulat, but for real estate, it may be permissible after registration? Actually, possession of real estate can be constructive.

Invalid Sale with Condition of Delayed Delivery
Ruling on sale with condition of deferred delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling a commodity with a condition of deferred delivery and explain why the contract is fasid.

Core Idea
A sale is invalid (fasid) if the seller stipulates a delay in delivery that negates the immediate obligation.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Mukhtasar al-Quduri: selling a tangible commodity ('ayn) with a condition that delivery will not occur until the end of the month. The Hanafi school deems such a sale fasid (invalid) because the condition contradicts the inherent requirement of immediate delivery. We will unpack why this condition is considered corrupt and what it means for sale contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is selling an 'ayn (tangible commodity) with a condition that delivery will not take place until the end of the month. The ruling is fasid (invalid).
The condition is attached by the seller, not the buyer.
2
Understand the reason for invalidity
The condition negates the immediate delivery (taslim) that the contract inherently requires. The seller has no benefit from deferring delivery; it only benefits the buyer by delaying payment or receipt.
The commentary states: 'because there is no benefit for the seller in deferring the sold item, and it involves a condition negating the delivery required by the contract.'
3
Distinguish between permissible deferment and corrupt condition
Deferring delivery is permissible only if it benefits the buyer or is agreed for a valid reason. Here, the condition is imposed by the seller and contradicts the seller's duty to deliver immediately.
If the buyer requests a delay, the ruling may differ (valid as a service).
4
Recognize the Hanafi position as mufta bihi
This ruling is from Mukhtasar al-Quduri, which belongs to Zahir al-Riwayah. Other schools may allow such a condition, but in Hanafi fiqh it is fasid and the relied-upon position is that the sale is invalid.
Cross-madhhab: Shafi'i may consider it valid as a stipulated term.

Three Categories of Exception of the Unborn
Commentary on ruling and three categories of exceptions in womb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the three Hanafi categories of contracts involving exception of the unborn young (istithna' ma fi al-batin), determining the validity of the contract and the exception.

Core Idea
Exception of unborn voids only contracts that are invalidated by any invalid condition; in others, only the exception is void.

The Hanafi school treats an exception excluding the unborn from a sale as a fasid (invalid) condition that corrupts the entire sale. However, this same exception does not always invalidate the entire contract. The commentator on Mukhtasar al-Quduri classifies contracts into three groups based on how they respond to such an invalid condition. Understanding this taxonomy is essential for knowing when the unborn remains with the mother or passes separately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Base Ruling
Start with the matn: a sale (or similar contract) with an exception of the unborn is fasid (invalid). This is because the exception is deemed an invalid condition (shart fasid), and sale is a contract voided by such conditions.
2
Understand the Key Principle
Not all contracts are voided by invalid conditions. Some contracts, like gift and marriage, remain valid even if an invalid condition is attached; only the condition is void. The classification depends on the nature of the contract.
3
List the Three Categories
Category 1: Both contract and exception are invalid (sale, hire, kitaba, pledge). Category 2: Contract valid, exception invalid (gift, charity, marriage, khul', compromise over intentional homicide, manumission). Category 3: Both contract and exception are valid (bequest only). Memorize these categories and the entry rules.
4
Apply the Rules to New Scenarios
Given a contract type, determine which category it falls into. For example, if someone gives a female slave as a gift with exception of her unborn, the gift is valid and both mother and child go to the donee. If someone makes a bequest of a slave with the same exception, the bequest is valid and the unborn goes to the heirs.
5
Note the Hanafi Specificity
This classification is specific to the Hanafi school. The underlying principle is that some contracts are 'tolerate' invalid conditions (like gifts where the object is the corpus) while others (like sale which requires precise exchange) are voided. This distinction is a key feature of Hanafi fiqh.

Invalid Sale with Tailoring Condition
Ruling on buying cloth conditional on tailoring

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a sale is combined with a tailoring condition and explain why the sale is fasid.

Core Idea
A sale combined with a service without price separation is fasid.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in commercial transactions: buying cloth with the condition that the seller also tailors it into a garment. The Hanafi school holds that such a contract is invalid (fasid) because it conflates a sale (of the cloth) with a hire (for tailoring) without specifying the price for each, creating uncertainty (gharar). Understanding this ruling helps you structure contracts correctly to avoid nullity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Condition
Read the text: a person buys cloth on the condition that the seller cuts and sews it into a shirt, cloak, or sandal (including shaping or lacing). Recognize that the contract includes both a transfer of ownership (sale) and a service (tailoring).
2
Determine the Legal Classification
The text states 'the sale is invalid' (fasid). In Hanafi fiqh, fasid means the contract is defective but can be corrected by removing the defect. Identify the hukm level as fasid.
Fasid is distinguished from batil (void) in that a fasid contract may become valid if the defect is eliminated.
3
Analyze the Reason for Invalidity
The invalidity arises because the price is not allocated between the cloth (commodity) and the tailoring (service). This creates uncertainty (gharar) in the consideration, which is prohibited in sales. The contract mixes a sale (bay') with a hire (ijara) without clear separation.
The Hanafi principle: a single contract cannot combine a sale and a hire unless the price for each is specified separately.
4
Apply the Ruling to Correct the Contract
To make the transaction valid, the buyer should either (a) buy the cloth and then separately hire the seller for tailoring, with distinct prices, or (b) buy the finished garment at a single price without splitting the cost of cloth and labor, provided no uncertainty exists.
If the tailoring is offered as a gift (without extra charge), the sale is valid because there is no additional consideration for the service.

Sale Conditioned on Seller's Work is Fasid
States ruling for sale conditioned on seller's work

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a sale conditioned on the seller performing additional work and explain why it is classified as fasid.

Core Idea
A sale conditioned on extra work by the seller is fasid because the condition benefits one party and is not essential to the contract.

In this passage, the author of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains that if a buyer purchases a garment on the condition that the seller cuts and sews it into a shirt or cloak, the sale is invalid (fasid) in the Hanafi school. This is because the condition brings a benefit to one of the parties and is not a necessary requirement of the sale contract. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish between valid conditions and those that corrupt a contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and condition
Examine the scenario: buying a garment with the condition that the seller cuts and sews it into a specific garment. Recognize that this condition requires additional work from the seller beyond the delivery of the item.
The condition is an extra act not intrinsic to the sale.
2
Classify the condition
Determine whether the condition is required by the contract (muqtada al-'aqd) or brings benefit to one party. In this case, the condition benefits the buyer (saves cost of tailor) and is not part of the essential terms of a sale.
In Hanafi fiqh, a condition that brings benefit to one party without being essential to the contract is considered fāsid (invalid).
3
Apply the ruling
According to the Hanafi school, a sale with such a condition is fasid (corrupt) because invalid conditions nullify the contract. The sale is not completely void but defective; it can be rectified if the condition is removed.
The ruling is fasid, not bāṭil, meaning it is temporarily invalid and can become valid if the corrupt condition is dropped.
4
Consider the rationale
The commentator explains: 'because this is a condition not required by the contract and it contains a benefit for one of them.' This talil (reasoning) is the basis for the ruling.
The illah is the presence of a non-essential benefit-condition.

Three Positions on Invalid Exceptions in Contracts
Explains three positions on invalid exceptions in contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Hanafi positions on the effect of an invalid exception (istithna) in contracts and apply the correct position to sale, lease, gift, marriage, bequest, and other contracts.

Core Idea
An invalid exception (e.g., excepting the unborn child) may invalidate both the contract and the exception, only the exception, or neither, depending on the contract type.

After establishing the ruling on invalid conditions, the commentator of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah expands on the effect of an invalid exception (istithna) in various contracts. Using the example of buying a pregnant slave-girl and excepting her unborn child, he presents three positions within the Hanafi school: (1) both the contract and the exception are invalid in contracts like sale, lease, kitabah, and rahn; (2) the contract is valid but the exception is invalid in contracts like gift, charity, marriage, khul', sulh over premeditated murder, and emancipation; (3) both are valid only in bequest (wasiyyah). Understanding this classification is crucial for applying the correct ruling when an invalid exception occurs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of invalid exception (istithna)
An example: buying a pregnant slave-girl and stipulating that the unborn child is excluded from the sale. This is an invalid exception because the unborn child is part of the mother and cannot be separated in a sale.
The exception is considered an invalid condition (shart fāsid) in the contract.
2
List the three positions and their contract categories
From the text: Position 1: both contract and exception invalid for sale (bay'), lease (ijārah), kitabah (contract of manumission), and rahn (pledge). Position 2: contract valid, exception invalid for gift (hibah), charity (ṣadaqah), marriage (nikāḥ), khul' (divorce by redemption), sulh over premeditated murder (ṣulḥ 'an dam al-'amd), and emancipation ('itq). Position 3: both valid only for bequest (waṣiyyah).
Memorize these categories as they are standard.
3
Identify the underlying principle
The key is whether the contract type is invalidated by invalid conditions. Sale, lease, kitabah, and rahn are invalidated by a bad condition; hence both are fasid. Gift, charity, etc., are not invalidated by bad conditions, so only the exception fails. Bequest is treated specially: the exception is valid, and both are upheld.
This principle is explicitly stated: 'because these contracts are invalidated by invalid conditions' vs. 'these contracts are not invalidated by invalid conditions'.
4
Apply the classification to new cases
Given a contract and an invalid exception, determine which position applies. E.g., if someone gives a pregnant slave-girl as a gift and excepts the child, the gift is valid but the exception is invalid; the child goes with the gift.
The text provides examples: 'the gift is valid, and both mother and child are included'.

Uncertain Time Terms in Sale vs. Guaranty (Kafalah)

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A sale with a time term unknown in existence is invalid; slight ignorance in earliness/lateness is tolerated only in kafalah and post-contract deferment.

Identify the ruling and its conditions

This lesson addresses a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: not all invalid sales are the same. Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the nature of ownership in an invalid sale where the price is unspecified or uncertain. The chapter is about invalid sales (al-bay' al-fasid). The author begins by narrating from Muhammad (al-Shaybani) through Ibn Sama'a: if a person sells something saying 'I sell to you for what my camels graze in your land' or 'for what you drink from my well,' the buyer owns the sold item upon taking possession because a valuable consideration has been named. The analogy is drawn to selling grass or water that one has collected, which is valid; hence the contract includes two valuable considerations (the item and the uncertain benefit). Abu Yusuf extends this: if the seller is silent about the price, the sale implies a price, so the market value is established. However, if the seller explicitly says 'for no price,' it is not a sale at all. Then the passage focuses on the meaning of 'owing the sold item' (malaka al-mabī'). Some scholars (the people of Iraq) say the buyer does not own the actual corpus (al-'ayn) but only the right to dispose (al-tasarruf). The scholars of Balkh say he owns the corpus, and that is the preferred view (al-mukhtar) because Muhammad explicitly stated that he owns the corpus (al-raqaba). Evidence for the Balkh view includes: if the buyer frees the slave, the wala' (patronage) belongs to him, not the seller; if the buyer sells the item, the price is his and he owes the value to the original seller; pre-emption rights arise for the buyer if adjacent property is sold, and the seller's later freeing of the slave is ineffective. These indicate ownership of the corpus. The Iraqi view is based on practical prohibitions: food cannot be eaten, a slave girl cannot be touched, and pre-emption is not required. Al-Khujandi refutes this by noting that permissibility is not a function of ownership; one can own something without being allowed to use it (e.g., a slave who is a close relative). Pre-emption is not required because the seller's right has not been severed; pre-emption requires the seller's right to be cut off, not merely the buyer's ownership. Additional benefit: if the seller steals the item after delivery, his hand is cut off because the buyer is considered the owner. Finally, the obligation to pay value (qima) or like-kind (mithl) is explained: if the item is of the category that has a market value (like non-standard items), the buyer pays its value on the day of possession; if it is of a standardized category (like grain), he pays like-kind because it is guaranteed by possession, similar to usurpation. The buyer's statement regarding the amount (with oath) is accepted because he is the one liable for the guarantee.

Ownership in an Invalid Sale: The Hanafi Refutation of the Iraqi School
Refutation of Iraqi school on ownership in fasid sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that the buyer acquires ownership in a fasid sale and evaluate the Iraqi school's counter-argument.

Core Idea
Ownership and permissibility are separate — prohibition of use does not negate ownership.

In the Hanafi school, an invalid sale (bayʿ fāsid) still transfers ownership of the sold item to the buyer, but the buyer's use is prohibited until the defect is resolved. The Iraqi school objected: if the buyer truly owned it, why can't he use it? Al-Khujandi's commentary clarifies the distinction between milk (ownership) and ibāḥah (permissibility), using powerful analogies from Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Clearly state that in a fasid sale, the buyer acquires ownership (milk) of the sold item, as stated in al-Quduri's matn: 'mālaka al-mabīʿ'.
This is a core Hanafi principle that distinguishes fasid from bāṭil.
2
Identify the opponent and their objection
The Iraqi school argued that if the buyer owned the item, it would be permissible to use it (eat, have intercourse, etc.) and pre-emption would apply, but neither is the case.
Their objection is based on conflating ownership with unrestricted use.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response: ownership ≠ permissibility
Al-Khujandi provides two examples: (1) profit from something not yet guaranteed is owned but not permissible; (2) owning a mahram slave-girl gives ownership but not permissibility of intercourse.
These examples prove that prohibition of use can coexist with ownership.
4
Examine the pre-emption issue specifically
The lack of pre-emption (shufʿa) is not due to lack of buyer's ownership, but because the seller's right has not been severed. Pre-emption is triggered by the severance of the seller's right, not by the buyer's ownership.
This is supported by the case of acknowledgement (iqrār) where pre-emption applies even if the buyer denies the sale.
5
Identify the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Hanafis, the buyer is considered owner for certain rulings: e.g., if the seller steals the item after delivery, his hand is cut off (ḥadd of theft). The Iraqi view would not impose ḥadd because ownership did not transfer.
This difference has real-world implications in criminal liability and rescission rights.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An increase that is inseparable and not derived from the buyer cuts off the right to rescind; other increases do not.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

A person buys an item through a defective contract (bay' fasid), then sells it at a profit. What should he do with that profit? The Hanafi school distinguishes between the initial profit and profit earned after reinvesting the proceeds. Charity here means sadaqah, not returning to seller.

The ruling hinges on the transfer of ownership being valid in itself, not on the original obligation.

The underlying principle: profit from a fasid contract must be purified because the contract itself is defective; but once ownership over the price is established (by executing a valid subsequent transaction), the taint is removed.

The Prohibition of Najsh and Bidding Over Another's Bid
Explains two prohibited practices in sales with Hanafi qualifications.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for najsh and sawm, distinguish prohibited from permissible cases, and apply the conditions to concrete scenarios.

Core Idea
Najsh is prohibited when it deceives a fair-paying buyer; it is permitted to correct an underpriced offer.

The Prophet (peace be upon him) prohibited two sales practices: najsh (artificially inflating price without intent to buy) and bidding over another's bid when the deal is nearly done. The Hanafi school, however, examines the buyer's initial offer: if the buyer is offering a fair price, interference is harmful and forbidden; if the buyer is lowballing, a third party may bid up to market value even without real purchase intent.
