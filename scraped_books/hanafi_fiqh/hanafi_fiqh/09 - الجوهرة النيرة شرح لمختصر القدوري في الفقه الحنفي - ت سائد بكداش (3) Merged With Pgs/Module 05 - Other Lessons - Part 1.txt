Legal Stratagems (Hiyal) to Avoid Istibra'
Ruling on hiyal to avoid istibra' in two scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal stratagems to avoid istibra' and explain the underlying principle that istibra' is only required when ownership coincides with lawful access to the vulva.

Core Idea
Istibra' is avoided if at the moment of ownership the vulva is not lawful — the cause is absent.

The obligation of istibra' ensures a purchased slave-girl's womb is free of another's child. But Hanafi jurisprudence permits lawful stratagems to avoid this obligation by altering the timing of ownership relative to marital status. This passage from the chapter on istibra' explains two scenarios — with and without a free wife — and the key principle that the moment of acquisition determines the rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation in question
Recognize that istibra' is a waiting period wajib upon purchasing a slave-girl when her vulva becomes lawful to the buyer through ownership.
Istibra' is wajib in Hanafi school.
2
Note the two scenarios
The text provides separate hiyal for a buyer who has no free wife and for a buyer who has a free wife (and thus cannot marry the slave-girl).
Scenario 1: buyer without free wife. Scenario 2: buyer with free wife.
3
Understand the hila for each case
For buyer without free wife: marry her, consummate, then purchase. For buyer with free wife: have seller or a trusted third party marry her, then purchase and take possession, then the third party divorces.
Consummation is required in scenario 1; in scenario 2, the marriage must be valid at time of possession.
4
Extract the underlying 'illa (cause)
The effective cause for istibra' is the acquisition of ownership (istihdath al-milk). If at the moment of acquisition the vulva is not lawful (due to marriage), the cause is absent and istibra' is not required.
Timing is critical: the status at the moment of ownership determines the ruling.
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi difference
Al-Hidayah requires that the marriage be in effect at the time of purchase and possession. Al-Khujandi allows divorce to occur before consummation but after possession, though divorce before possession invalidates the hila.
The mufta bihi is the position of al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on legal stratagems (hiyal) to avoid the obligation of istibra' when purchasing a slave-girl. Istibra' is a waiting period imposed to ensure the womb is free of another's child. The text gives two scenarios. First, if the buyer has no free wife, he may marry the slave-girl, consummate, then buy her—because at the moment he acquires ownership, her womb is already lawful to him via marriage, so no istibra' is needed. The text records a difference between al-Hidayah and al-Khujandi: in al-Khujandi, the divorce can occur before consummation (and before possession?) and she becomes lawful without istibra'; but if divorce occurs before possession, then possession alone requires istibra'. All authorities agree on the underlying principle: the time of acquisition determines the ruling.

Selling Gold for Silver Without Exact Measure
Author rules on selling gold for silver without equality of weight

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling gold for silver without exact measure (mujazafatan) and explain the reasoning based on the permissibility of tafadul between different genera.

Core Idea
Selling gold for silver by estimation is permissible because inequality (tafadul) between different metals is allowed, but immediate hand-to-hand exchange remains required.

In this passage, the commentator expands on a ruling from the matn concerning the sale of gold for silver without weighing or measuring. The key insight is that such a transaction only introduces tafadul (inequality), which is already permissible between different ribawi genera. However, the condition of immediate hand-to-hand exchange (qabd) is reaffirmed to prevent riba. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the essential condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The sale of gold for silver without exact measurement (mujazafatan). The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz).
Note the term 'mujazafatan' — selling by estimation without precise weighing.
2
Locate the dalil (reasoning)
The commentator provides a concise proof: 'because there is nothing in mujazafatan more than tafadul, and tafadul between gold and silver is permissible.' This is an analogical argument (qiyas).
The basis is the already established permissibility of inequality in exchange between different genera.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does this reasoning prove the ruling? Mujazafatan is nothing but tafadul (inequality) in the amount exchanged. Since inequality is allowed between gold and silver, the lack of exact measure is not an additional prohibition.
The only concern in such a sale is the inequality, which is already ruled permissible.
4
Note the condition (qabd)
Despite the permissibility, the contract is conditioned upon immediate hand-to-hand exchange (qabd) in the sitting. Without qabd, the contract becomes fasid (voidable).
This condition is common to all sarf (currency exchange) contracts to avoid riba al-nasi'ah.
5
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
The Hanafi position differs from the Shafi'i school, which requires equality even between different metals. The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that Hanafis allow estimation, while Shafi'is require exact weighing.
The difference arises from the definition of ribawi items and the role of uncertainty (jahalah).

Invalidity of Ibra' and Hiba Before Qabd in Sarf
Ruling on ibra' and hiba before qabd in sarf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ibra' and hiba of the price before qabd in sarf, distinguish the effects of acceptance vs non-acceptance, and apply the compulsion rule.

Core Idea
Disposal of the price before hand-to-hand exchange is prohibited; acceptance of such disposal voids the sarf contract.

Sarf (currency exchange) requires hand-to-hand exchange (qabd) to avoid the risk of riba. The text addresses attempts to circumvent this requirement by remitting (ibra') or gifting (hiba) the price before qabd. The ruling affirms that such acts are invalid as they constitute disposal before possession. The response depends on whether the counterparty accepts the remission or gift, and a party trying to rescind the contract by refusing qabd may be compelled to complete it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited acts
The text names two specific acts: ibra' (remission of the price debt) and hiba (gifting the price). Determine that these are forms of disposal of the price before physical possession (qabd).
The prohibition is not absolute but conditional on qabd not having occurred.
2
Apply the general principle
The reason given is 'because it is a disposal before taking possession.' Understand that in Hanafi sarf, no disposition of the counter-values is allowed until hand-to-hand exchange is complete. This principle prevents riba.
Disposal includes any transfer of ownership, even if unilateral.
3
Analyze the effect of acceptance
If the buyer accepts the ibra' or hiba, the sarf contract becomes void (batal). If the buyer does not accept, the contract remains valid. This shows that the invalidity is not automatic but contingent on acceptance.
Acceptance effectively waives the requirement of qabd, which is essential for the contract's validity.
4
Examine the compulsion rule
The text cites al-Karkhi: if the seller gifts the price, the buyer refuses, and the seller then refuses to take back the money, the seller is compelled to take possession. This prevents the seller from using the attempted gift as a way to rescind the contract.
The compulsion ensures the other party's right to complete the contract is protected.
5
Note cross-madhhab distinction
The Hanafi position is that ibra' before qabd is invalid. Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may allow ibra' as a separate act not tied to the sarf contract. Recognize that this difference stems from how the link between the act and the original contract is viewed.
The practical consequence: in the Hanafi school, such an ibra' does not discharge the debt.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on what nullifies a sarf contract. The general principle: until hand-to-hand exchange (qabd) occurs, neither party may dispose of the counter-value. Thus, if the seller remits (ibra') the price or gives it as a gift (hiba) to the buyer before qabd, such action is invalid (la yajuz) because it is a disposal before possession. If the buyer accepts the remission or gift, the original sarf contract becomes void because the price has been effectively waived without qabd. If the buyer does not accept, the contract remains valid. The author then cites al-Karkhi: if the seller gives the price as a gift and the buyer declines, but the seller then refuses to take back the offered money, the seller is compelled to accept the price (i.e., complete the qabd) because his refusal is an attempt to rescind the contract unilaterally, which is not allowed since the other party has a right to the completion of the contract.

Invalidity of Pawning Jointly-Owned and Connected Property
Inter-madhhab dispute on pawning jointly-owned property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on pawning jointly-owned property and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the concept of continuous possession.

Core Idea
Joint ownership destroys continuous possession, which is the essence of pawn.

This passage from Al-Jawharat al-Nayyirah addresses a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on the validity of pawning a shared property. The Hanafi school holds that such a pledge is invalid because pawn requires continuous exclusive detention, which is impossible with joint ownership due to necessary alternation. The Shafi'i school permits it by analogy with sale. The discussion also extends to connected property like fruit without trees.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: pawning jointly-owned property (mushā') is invalid (lā yajūz), whether the property is divisible or not, and whether pledged to a stranger or partner. The reason is that ishā'ah prevents continuous possession.
The ruling applies to both divisible and indivisible property.
2
Identify the opposing school and their proof
Imam al-Shafi'i holds it is permissible, drawing an analogy to sale: just as jointly-owned property can be sold, it can be pledged.
Shafi'i uses qiyās on bay'.
3
Follow the Hanafi response
The Hanafi rebuttal: sale transfers ownership (which accepts sharing), but pawn requires physical detention (ḥabs dā'im), which cannot be shared. The analogy fails because the effective cause ('illa) differs.
The text says: 'It becomes as if he said: I pledge it to you one day, and one day not.'
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: In the Hanafi school, one cannot use a share in a jointly-owned house as collateral; in the Shafi'i school, it is valid. This affects financial transactions and security.
5
Extend to connected property
The same invalidity applies to property connected to something else, like pledging palm trees without fruit, or land without crops. The connection creates similar issues of continuous detention.
The text includes this as an extension of the same principle.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary Al-Jawharat al-Nayyirah on the chapter of pledge (rahn) from Mukhtasar al-Quduri. It begins by establishing that pawning jointly-owned (mushā') property is invalid, whether the property is divisible or not, and whether pledged to a stranger or a partner. The reason given is that commingling (ishā'ah) prevents continuous possession (istidāmat al-qabd), because alternation (muhāyāt) is necessary in jointly-owned property. The passage then presents the Shafi'i view, which analogizes to sale, holding that jointly-owned property can be pledged just as it can be sold. The Hanafi response is that pawn (rahn) differs fundamentally from sale: the object of pawn is continuous detention (habs dā'im), which is legislated only in a possessed state by the text (i.e., the requirement of possession in the pawn verse). If joint property were allowed, the necessary continuity would be lost due to required alternation, analogous to saying 'I pawn it to you one day, not the next.' Therefore, invalid.

Destruction of the Pawn Properly Pawned
Ruling on destruction of pawn in lender's hand when properly pawned

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the pawner's liability when a properly pawned item perishes and explain the reasoning behind the limitation to the amount of debt discharged.

Core Idea
Liability for destroyed pawn is limited to the amount of debt discharged; the owner volunteers the excess.

This lesson clarifies the legal consequences when a pawned item perishes in the lender's hands, provided the pawning was done properly. The ruling establishes a precise limit on the pawner's liability: only the amount of debt that was cleared by the destruction. Understanding this rule prevents confusion about who bears the loss of excess value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key parties and the act
The pawner (borrower) pawns an item to the lender, and the item perishes in the lender's hands. The pawning must be done properly—without violating the borrowing terms.
Proper pawning means the borrower used the item exactly as permitted by the owner.
2
State the ruling
The pawner is liable to the owner only for the amount of debt that was discharged due to the destruction. For example, if the item covered a debt of 100, the owner can claim 100, not the full value.
The ruling applies only if the pawning was proper.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The borrower paid his debt using the owner's property by the owner's own command. Therefore, he has recourse against the owner only for what he paid, i.e., the debt amount. The owner is considered a volunteer (mutṭawwiʿ) for any excess value.
The owner's consent is key: he knew the item would be used to pay the debt.
4
Recognize the excess rule
If the item's value exceeds the debt, the owner bears the loss of the surplus. The owner cannot claim more than the debt from the pawner.
This prevents unjust enrichment: the pawner should not profit from the destruction.

Owner Redeeming the Pawn: Limited Recovery
Ruling on owner redeeming pawn when borrower cannot

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the owner redeeming a pawn when the borrower cannot, and apply the analogy (qiyas) that limits recovery to the item's value.

Core Idea
Owner recovers only item's value, not full debt, when redeeming pawn for borrower.

In pawn transactions, if the borrower cannot redeem the pawn, the owner may do so. However, the owner's right to recover from the borrower is limited to the market value of the item at the time of pawning, not the full amount paid to redeem. This rule is derived by analogy to the case where the pawn perishes in the creditor's hands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
The owner lends an item to the borrower, who then pawns it to a third-party creditor for a sum exceeding the item's value. The borrower cannot redeem.
The borrower is the musta'ir (borrower of the item), the owner is the mu'ir (lender of the item), and the creditor is the murtahin (pawn-holder).
2
State the ruling
The text states: 'الرهن يهلك به، ولا يرجع بأكثر ذلك' – the pawn perishes with him (the owner), and he does not recover more than that. This means the owner bears the loss of the item (it is considered as if it perished) and can only recover from the borrower an amount equal to the item's value.
The phrase 'يهلك به' is a legal fiction: the owner's act of redemption is treated as if the item had perished, limiting his recourse.
3
Understand the example and rationale
Example: Item worth 100, pawned for 200. Owner redeems for 200. He recovers only 100 from the borrower. Rationale: If the item had perished in the creditor's hand, the borrower's liability would be limited to the item's value (100), so the same applies when the owner redeems.
The analogy is based on the common cause: the borrower cannot be made to pay more than the item's value.
4
Trace the qiyas (analogy)
The original case (asl): item perishes in creditor's hand → borrower owes only item's value. The new case (far'): owner redeems the item → by analogy, borrower owes only item's value. The effective cause (illa) is the discharge of the debt through the item's value.
This qiyas is a clear analogy (qiyas jali) because the ruling in the asl is established by necessity.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
For any similar transaction, the owner's recovery is capped at the fair market value of the item at the time of pawning, regardless of the debt amount.
The valuation is based on the time of pawning, not redemption, to prevent manipulation.

This prevents the owner from profiting from his own redemption.

Renting the Pawn to the Lender Exits the Pledge
Ruling on pawner renting the pawn to the lender

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on renting a pawned item to the lender and explain why the pledge is annulled.

Core Idea
A valid lease on a pawned item annuls the pledge; mutual consent to lease implies rescission of the original contract.

This passage from al-Karkhi addresses a specific scenario where the pawner (debtor) rents the pawned item back to the lender (creditor). The ruling is clear: the pledge ends and does not revive automatically. The reasoning is rooted in the nature of lease as a contract that transfers entitlement to usufruct, which is incompatible with the pledge's retention of possession. The passage also covers cases involving a third party, showing the principle applies broadly whenever both parties consent to the lease.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and parties
Recognize the scenario: the pawner (debtor) leases the pawned item to the lender (creditor). This is the primary case discussed.
Also consider the subsidiary cases where a third party is involved with mutual consent.
2
State the ruling
The text says the pawned item 'exits the pledge' and does not return without a new contract. The pledge is annulled, not suspended.
The ruling is the same in all three variants: direct lease to lender, pawner to third party with lender's consent, and lender to third party with pawner's consent.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil)
The reasoning is that a lease transfers full entitlement to the usufruct, which conflicts with the pawn's nature. Both parties' consent to the lease implies they agree to rescind the pawn (tafāshukh).
The analogy 'as if they rescinded' shows the mutual consent is the key factor.
4
Apply the principle to modern contexts
This ruling applies to any pawned item (e.g., car, jewelry) where a lease is agreed upon with the lender's consent. The pawn is voided permanently.
If the lease is invalid, the pawn may remain valid. A new pawn contract is required to reinstate the pledge.

The reasoning is that both parties' consent to the lease implies rescission (tafāshukh) of the original pledge.

Ruling on lender renting the pawn without pawner's permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a lender renting a pawn without permission and identify the pawner's options if the pawn is destroyed.

Core Idea
Lender renting pawn without permission is haram; pawner chooses liability between lender and renter.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a lender who rents out a pawned item without the pawner's consent. The text from al-Jundi clarifies the prohibition and provides a detailed remedy if the item is destroyed. Understanding this scenario helps you apply rules of unauthorized benefit and constructive liability in pledge contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Prohibition
Read the opening statement: 'It is not permissible for the lender to rent the pawn.' Recognize that this is a clear prohibition (haram).
2
Note the Missing Condition
The text specifies the act is done 'without the permission of the pawner.' Identify permission as the essential condition that is violated.
3
Trace the Consequences of Destruction
The text outlines two scenarios: if the pawn perishes while with the renter, the pawner has a choice. Follow each path and note the liabilities.

The renter can recover from the lender because the lender 'deceived him' (gharrah). This is a talil (reasoning) showing that the lender induced the renter into an invalid contract.
5

The text concludes that any compensation received by the lender is not lawful for him (la yatib). This underscores that the lender cannot benefit from his unauthorized act.

Causes and Definition of Legal Interdiction

Hajr prevents a person from transacting to protect their rights; three causes: minority, slavery, insanity.

Legal interdiction (hajr) is a protective mechanism in Hanafi fiqh that restricts a person's capacity to perform certain transactions due to a deficiency. This lesson establishes the foundational definition and enumerates the three causes recognized by Imam Quduri. Understanding these causes is essential before studying the specific rulings for each category.

Identify the linguistic meaning

This emphasizes substitution by guardian or representative.

The text explicitly lists three causes: minority (sughar), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junun).
These are the only causes mentioned in this passage.

Interdiction Affects Utterances, Not Actions: Legal Capacity of Minors and the Insane
Commentary on the legal capacity of minors and insane regarding statements vs. deeds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that interdiction for minors and insane persons affects utterances but not actions, and apply it to cases of acknowledgment, istilad, ownership, and pure benefit.

Core Idea
Utterances require legal capacity; actions are valid because they are tangible and cannot be undone.

In Hanafi fiqh, minors and the insane are under interdiction (hajr) to protect them. However, this interdiction does not apply uniformly to all their legal acts. The key distinction lies between utterances (aqwaal) – such as contracts and acknowledgments – and physical actions (af'aal) – such as taking possession or causing harm. This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains the rationale and provides specific examples to help you navigate this critical principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central rule
The passage states: 'These three meanings necessitate interdiction in utterances, not in actions.' This is the core principle: the three causes of interdiction (minority, insanity, slavery) restrict verbal legal acts but not physical acts.
2
Examine the rational proof
Actions cannot be undone because they exist physically and are witnessed. Utterances, on the other hand, rely on legal recognition and intent. Therefore, actions are valid even from those without full capacity, while utterances require capacity.
3
Identify exceptions and nuances
A discerning authorized minor is held accountable for utterances concerning wealth. Additionally, pure benefit (naf' mahd) such as accepting a gift is valid. The principle of istihsan also validates a minor's hire contract once the work is completed.
4
Apply the rule to specific examples
The passage gives four key applications: (1) An insane person's act of istilad is valid, but his verbal acknowledgment of it is not. (2) If a minor or insane person owns a mahram relative, that relative becomes free automatically because ownership is an action. (3) Verbal manumission by them is invalid. (4) A minor's acknowledgment of debt is valid only if he is a discerning authorized minor.
5
Define the scope of pure benefit
Pure benefit (accepting gifts, charity) is valid from minors and insane because it only confers advantage and requires no reciprocal obligation. This shows that actions of pure benefit are treated like other actions.

This passage clarifies the limited legal capacity of a slave. While a slave can be held liable for his actions (torts, crimes), his statements—including contracts and confessions—are restricted because his property belongs to his master. The key distinction: if he is permitted to trade (ma'dhun), his trade-related statements are effective, but a confession of debt still only binds after freedom.

The commentary defends this: preventing him from taking possession of his wealth does not invalidate his transactions, just as a buyer may be prevented from taking possession before payment but his manumission of a slave is valid. The rationale for the 25-year limit is disciplinary; after that age, further discipline is generally unnecessary as he could become a grandfather, and extended interdiction would be absurd. The commentary from al-Yanabi' illustrates this with a hypothetical: a boy reaches puberty at 12, marries, has a child after 6 months; the child reaches puberty at 12, marries, and has a child after 6 months; the original boy is then 25 and a grandfather. At that point, preventing him from managing his wealth would be repugnant. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ from Abu Hanifa: they hold that the spendthrift (safīh) is interdicted (yuḥjar ʿalayhi) and prevented from transacting. They further differ between themselves: Abu Yusuf requires a court order for interdiction and release, while Muhammad holds that the spendthriftness itself (safah) effects interdiction, and rectification of his finances lifts it.

مبذر (mubadhdhir): A squanderer who wastes wealth without purpose or benefit, but his transactions are valid according to Abu Hanifa.

Manumission by the Interdicted Prodigal: Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Dispute with Shafi'i on manumission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on manumission by a safih under hajr and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Manumission by a safih under hajr is valid because it is immune to jest; the Shafi'i analogy to slavery fails.

This passage focuses on a specific point of contention between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: can a prodigal (safih) under legal interdiction (hajr) validly manumit a slave? The Hanafis, represented by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, argue that manumission is not affected by jest (hazl), and thus remains valid despite the interdiction. Al-Shafi'i counters by analogizing the safih to a slave, for whom all transactions except divorce are invalid. The passage also explains why the slave must work to pay his value, as a form of compensation that does not undo the manumission.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on manumission
The text affirms that manumission is executed (nafadha) and the slave must work to pay his value. This is the established Hanafi position.
The rationale: manumission cannot be rescinded once it occurs.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i holds that manumission by a safih under hajr is not executed. His ruling is based on analogizing the safih to a slave.
For a slave, all financial transactions and manumissions are invalid.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof (al-asl)
The Hanafi principle: every transaction affected by jest (hazl) is affected by interdiction; manumission is not affected by jest, so it is valid. The safih is like a jester whose speech is not taken seriously, but manumission is a devotional act immune to jest.
This principle is attributed to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
4
Examine the Shafi'i proof and the Hanafi response
The Shafi'i analogy (qiyas) to slavery is rejected because a slave lacks ownership capacity, while a safih has capacity but is restricted for his own protection. The basis of hajr for prodigality is different from that of slavery.
The thamarah (practical consequence): in Hanafi, manumission takes effect and slave pays value; in Shafi'i, manumission is void and slave remains owned.
5
Understand the slave's payment (sua fi qimathi)
The slave must work to pay his value because the purpose of interdiction (consideration for the safih's interest) would require annulling the manumission, but that is impossible, so compensation is imposed.
This is a unique Hanafi solution balancing the validity of the act and the protection of the safih's property.

Puberty Signs and Age Limits: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Defining puberty signs and age limits for girls and boys

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definitive signs of puberty for girls according to the Hanafi school and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the age of majority in the absence of such signs, including the legal reasoning (ta'lil) and the treatment of secondary signs.

Core Idea
Puberty is determined by clear signs; in their absence, age limits differ: 17 years for girls (Abu Hanifa) vs 15 years for both (Sahibayn).

This passage from Kitab al-Hajr in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira defines when a minor reaches legal majority. It lists the definitive signs for girls—menstruation, wet dream, and pregnancy—and then presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute on the age limit when these signs are absent. The commentary by al-Haddadi expands on secondary signs such as pubic hair, breast development, armpit hair, mustache, soft downy hair, and voice change, clarifying which are legally considered signs of puberty. The reasoning (ta'lil) underpinning Abu Hanifa's position is that females develop faster, so their age limit is set lower relative to males.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitive signs of puberty for a girl
From the matn, list the three definitive signs: menstruation (al-ḥayḍ), wet dream (al-iḥtilām), and pregnancy (al-ḥabl). These establish puberty conclusively.
These are physical signs that indicate the onset of reproductive capacity, which triggers full legal capacity (al-ahliyya al-wājiba).
2
State the age limit in the absence of signs according to Abu Hanifa
When no definitive signs appear, Abu Hanifa holds that a girl reaches puberty upon completing 17 lunar years. The commentary justifies this by noting that females mature faster, so their age limit is set one year earlier than males (who would be 18 in the absence of signs).
The passage does not explicitly state the boys' age, but the reasoning implies it as per standard Hanafi doctrine.
3
Contrast with the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybānī hold that both boys and girls reach puberty upon completing 15 lunar years, regardless of signs. Their position is based on a general principle that 15 years is the maximum age for majority, as supported by other evidence (e.g., the ḥadīth about Ibn 'Umar's participation in battle).
This is a legitimate ikhtilāf within the Ḥanafī school, and both views are transmitted in the ẓāhir al-riwāyah.
4
Analyze the status of secondary signs
The commentary discusses various bodily changes: pubic hair (naba al- 'ānih) is generally disregarded, though one transmission from Abu Yusuf considers coarse hair requiring shaving as a sign. Breast development (nuhūd al-thady) is not a sign in ẓāhir al-riwāyah, but some scholars consider it. Armpit hair and mustache follow the same disagreement as pubic hair. Soft downy hair (zaghb) and voice deepening are not considered.
These secondary signs are not definitive; they may vary and are thus not reliable for establishing puberty.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The dispute affects when a girl gains full legal capacity to manage property, marry without a guardian’s permission (in certain contexts), and be subject to penal obligations. Under Abu Hanifa, capacity is delayed until 17; under the Sahibayn, capacity begins at 15. This has significant implications for financial transactions and personal status.
The muftā bihī (operative position) in later Ḥanafī fatwā tends to follow the Sahibayn for practical ease, but both positions remain viable.

When a person is declared bankrupt and placed under judicial interdiction (ḥajr), he loses the capacity to dispose of his property. But what if he acknowledges a new debt to another person? Commentary
This passage details the treatment of a debtor imprisoned for debt. The overarching principle is to create hardship to compel payment, but with accommodations for dire necessities. The debtor is not released for religious festivities or obligations because these are personal duties that can be fulfilled later; the priority is to pressure repayment. For funerals, there is an intra-Hanafi difference: Muhammad holds that the debtor leaves only if no one else can handle the washing and shrouding; another opinion allows release with a guarantor for close relatives (parents, grandparents, children), and this is the relied-upon position. The prison itself should be harsh: no furnishings to cause distress. However, if the debtor falls ill and has no servant, he is released to prevent death, as preservation of life takes precedence over the creditor's right. Conjugal rights are accommodated: the wife or slave-girl may be brought to him privately. The free wife may refuse due to the unsuitability of the prison; the slave-girl may be compelled by her master. Family and neighbors may visit for consultation, but not for long stays. The debtor is not allowed to practice his craft, to maintain distress. Finally, a man is imprisoned for non-payment of his wife's maintenance, as that is a necessary obligation.

Release of Imprisoned Debtor: Period and Proof of Insolvency
Ruling on releasing a debtor after a period or upon proving insolvency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi procedure for releasing an imprisoned debtor and state the rationale for not accepting evidence of insolvency before imprisonment.

Core Idea
Imprisonment of a debtor is a tool to uncover hidden wealth, not a punishment; release is required when insolvency becomes apparent.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira explains the Hanafi ruling on when a debtor who cannot pay should be released from prison. The matn states that after two or three months of imprisonment, if no wealth is found, the judge must release him. The commentary expands on the discretionary nature of the period and the crucial principle that evidence of insolvency presented before imprisonment is not accepted. This is because hidden wealth may exist, and imprisonment serves to induce distress that might bring it to light.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on release
State the two scenarios when a debtor is released: after a period of imprisonment (2-3 months or as determined by the judge) and upon proving insolvency after imprisonment.
The ruling is that release is obligatory (wajib) once absence of wealth is confirmed.
2
Examine the rationale for pre-release imprisonment
Understand why witnesses' testimony of insolvency before imprisonment is not accepted: witnesses cannot know hidden wealth. Imprisonment is necessary to force disclosure through distress.
This is a qiyas-based proof: the illa is the possibility of hidden wealth.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the period
Some narrations state 4-6 months, but the relied-upon view is that it is not a fixed period; it depends on the debtor's endurance and the judge's discretion.
The commentary emphasizes: 'This is not a fixed estimate; rather it depends on the condition of the prisoner.'
4
Apply the mufta bihi position
The operative ruling: the judge investigates after a reasonable period (2-3 months or less if the debtor is easily distressed) and releases if no wealth appears. Evidence after imprisonment is accepted.
This is the standard Zahir al-Riwayah position.
