The Definition and Proof of Ijarah: Overriding Qiyas with Sunnah
Opens the Book of Ijarah with definition and Sunnah proof of permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijarah, explain why qiyas would prohibit it, and cite the hadith proof that establishes its permissibility.

Core Idea
Ijarah is permitted by explicit hadith, overriding the qiyas that would invalidate it for contracting non-existent usufructs.

This lesson opens the Book of Ijarah with a precise definition and a key legal puzzle: by strict analogy, hiring should be invalid because it contracts for future benefits that do not yet exist. Yet the Hanafi school affirms its validity, relying on explicit Prophetic commands. Understanding how ijarah overrides qiyas is essential to grasping the role of istihsan in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of ijarah
Read the definition: 'A contract on usufructs in exchange for financial compensation, renewing as the usufructs occur.' Note that the subject matter is usufructs (manafi'), not the object itself, and the contract is continuously renewed.
This is unique; other contracts are executed once.
2
Understand the qiyas objection
Analytical reasoning (qiyas) would prohibit ijarah because one cannot contract for something not yet created or not owned. Identify why this analogy might seem logical: a future benefit is non-existent at the time of contract.
This objection applies to all contracts for future services or rentals.
3
Identify the hadith proof
The author cites two hadiths: (1) 'Give the hired worker his wages before his sweat dries' – a direct command showing validity; (2) The Prophet will oppose those who hire and do not pay – implying the hiring itself is valid. Extract the legal implication from these texts.
The hadiths are from al-Bukhari, Ibn Majah, and al-Bayhaqi.
4
Recognize the operation of istihsan
The permissibility of ijarah is an application of istihsan (juristic preference): an exception to qiyas based on stronger evidence (Sunnah). This demonstrates the Hanafi hierarchy of proofs.
Istihsan is a key Hanafi usul principle.

Definition and Validity Conditions of Ijarah
Opening of Kitab al-Ijarah with definition, proof, and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Ijarah, explain why it is permitted despite qiyas, state the two essential conditions for its validity, and distinguish it from other contracts.

Core Idea
Ijarah is a contract on benefits, permitted by hadith despite qiyas, but only valid if benefits and wage are known.

Ijarah (hiring/leasing) is a vital contract in daily life. The Hanafi school defines it as a contract on benefits in exchange for compensation. This lesson explores its definition, the analogical objection, the prophetic override, and the core conditions that make it valid.

Guided Path
1
Define Ijarah
Identify the core definition: a contract on benefits (manafi') in exchange for monetary compensation (ajr). Note that it renews as benefits occur.
Benefits are treated as a property right in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Understand the qiyas objection
Recognize that analogical reasoning (qiyas) suggests Ijarah is invalid because it contracts on something not yet created (future benefits) and not owned at contract time. This is the default ruling of qiyas.
Qiyas would make Ijarah batil (void) if applied strictly.
3
Identify the prophetic proof
Locate the two hadiths quoted: the command to pay wages promptly and the warning against withholding wages. These hadiths override the qiyas, making Ijarah permissible (ja'iz). This is an application of istihsan (juristic preference).
The imperative in the hadith indicates obligation, confirming validity.
4
State the conditions of validity
Extract the two essential conditions: the benefits (manafi') must be known (ma'luma) and the wage (ujrah) must be known (ma'luma). Ignorance of either leads to dispute and invalidates the contract.
Knowledge includes specification of amount, duration, and currency.
5
Distinguish from other contracts
Compare Ijarah with sale (transfer of entity for compensation) and marriage (contract on permissibility). In Ijarah, if benefits cannot be delivered, compensation is not due; in marriage, the dowry is due upon contract regardless.
This distinction helps clarify the nature of Ijarah as a contract on benefits.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and legal analysis of Ijarah (hiring/leasing) from the Hanafi school. The author opens by defining Ijarah as a contract on benefits for monetary compensation, whose validity renews as benefits occur. He then presents the qiyas (analogical reasoning) that would deem Ijarah invalid because it involves something not yet created (the benefits) and not owned by the lessor at the time of contract. The author then states the essential conditions for validity: both the benefits and the wage must be known to avoid dispute. Conditions for Valid Ijarah: Knowledge of Benefit and Rent
Definition and conditions of ijarah with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid ijarah contract and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the place of delivery for rent items.

Core Idea
Ijarah requires both benefit and rent to be known; ignorance invalidates the contract.

In this passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, the commentator explains the fundamental nature of ijarah as a contract on usufruct (manafi') for compensation. He then details the essential condition that both the benefit and the rent must be known (ma'lum), and provides specific rules for different types of rent. An important intra-Hanafi difference arises regarding whether the place of delivery must be specified when the rent is a heavy item requiring transport. This lesson will guide you through the logical structure of these conditions and the practical consequences of their omission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its nature
The passage defines ijarah as a contract on benefits. Note the talil: if the benefit is not delivered (e.g., house collapses), no rent is due. This distinguishes ijarah from nikah where the dower is binding regardless of delivery.
Compare ijarah with sale: ijarah transfers usufruct, not corpus.
2
State the general condition of knowledge
Both the benefit (manfa'ah) and the rent (ujrah) must be known (ma'lum). The reason given is that ignorance leads to dispute. This parallels the requirement in sale that the price and object must be known.
The condition applies to all ijarah contracts.
3
Apply specific rules for different rent types
The passage details requirements for various forms of rent: dirhams (specify amount, default to local currency), weighed/measured/counted items (specify quantity), goods/garments (specify quantity, description, and term like salam), slaves/animals (specific identification by pointing), and benefits as rent (specify if different genus).
Follow the text's order: dirhams, then weighed items, then goods, then slaves/animals, then benefit-as-rent.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on place of delivery
For rent items with transportation burden (e.g., heavy goods), Abu Hanifa requires specification of the place of delivery. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) do not require it; the renter delivers at the rented premises. Note the thamarah: if unspecified, the contract may be invalid according to Abu Hanifa but valid according to them.
This dispute is within the Hanafi school, not with other madhhabs.
5
Evaluate the ruling on same-genus rent
If the rent is a benefit of the same genus as the leased benefit (e.g., house for house), the ijarah is fasid (invalid) because of riba. This is based on the principle that exchanging like items for more or less is riba, even if the subject matter is usufruct.
Distinguish from cross-genus benefits where ijarah is valid.

Commentary
This passage from 'al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah' is a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It begins by defining ijarah as a contract on benefits (manafi') in exchange for compensation ('iwad). The commentator explains that if delivery of the benefit is prevented by any external factor, the lessee is not liable for the rent, proving that the contract is on the benefit itself. He distinguishes ijarah from nikah (marriage), where the dower is binding immediately because the contract is on istibaha (right to enjoy), not on actual delivery. The author then classifies transfers (tamlikat) into two categories: transfer of corpus ('ayn) and of usufruct (manfa'ah). Corpus transfers are either for compensation (sale) or gratuitous (gift). Usufruct transfers are also either for compensation (ijarah) or gratuitous ('ariyah or bequest). The main body of the passage details the condition that both the benefit and the rent must be known (ma'lum). The reasoning is that ignorance leads to dispute. For rent in dirhams, the amount must be specified; it defaults to the local currency; if currencies vary, the contract is invalid unless one is specified. The rent may also require description (sifah). For measured, weighed, or counted items, the quantity must be specified. A key intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa requires specifying the place of delivery for items with transport burden; the Sahibayn do not, allowing delivery at the rented premises. For goods ('urud) and garments, the conditions of salam (forward sale) apply: quantity, description, and term must be specified. For slaves and animals, specific identification is required. Finally, if the rent is itself a benefit (e.g., service), it is permissible if it is of a different genus (e.g., dwelling for riding). However, if it is of the same genus (e.g., house for house), the ijarah is invalid (fasid) due to the prohibition of riba in exchange of like items.

Key Terms
الإجارة (al-ijārah): A contract on usufruct (manafi') in exchange for compensation ('iwad).
المنافع (al-manāfi'): The usufruct or benefits derived from an asset (e.g., dwelling in a house, riding an animal).
الأجرة (al-ujrah): The rent or compensation paid in exchange for the benefit. It can be money, goods, or services.
العارية (al-'āriyah): A gratuitous transfer of the benefit of a property, i.e., a loan for use without compensation.
نقد البلد (naqd al-balad): The currency prevalent in the locality if the rent is in dirhams and the type is not specified.

Prohibition of Exchanging Same-Type Usufructs in Ijarah (Riba al-Nisa)
Explains the ruling on exchanging usufructs in ijarah: valid if different types, fasid if same type, with disputes with Shafi'i and intra-Hanafi differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why exchanging same-type usufructs in ijarah is invalid (fasid) in the Hanafi school, and apply the principle to related scenarios.

Core Idea
Exchanging usufructs of the same kind triggers riba al-nisa, making the ijarah fasid.

The Hanafi school extends the prohibition of riba al-nisa (usury of delay) to exchanges of usufructs when they are of the same genus. This means renting a house in exchange for the use of another house is invalid, even if both are specified. The Shafi'i school disagrees, limiting riba to tangible commodities. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi reasoning, the intra-madhhab dispute on partial performance, and a case study on co-ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ujrah as usufructs
In ijarah, both the leased object and the payment can be usufructs. Determine if they are of the same kind (jins) or different kinds.
Examples: sakan (residence) is a usufruct of property; rukub (riding) is a usufruct of an animal.
2
Apply the riba al-nisa rule
If both usufructs are of the same genus (e.g., residence for residence), the contract is fasid because the same genus alone (الجنس بانفراده) prohibits riba al-nisa. This is analogous to exchanging one dirham for two dirhams.
The Hanafi principle: usufructs are considered mal (wealth) and are subject to riba rules when exchanged for their own kind.
3
Contrast with different genera
If the usufructs are of different kinds (e.g., residence for riding), the contract is valid because no riba al-nisa arises.
The passage gives examples: sakan for rukub, or renting a house for a slave's service.
4
Analyze partial performance
If one party performs but the other does not, Muhammad says ujrat al-mithl is owed; Abu Yusuf says nothing is owed. The mufta bihi is Muhammad's view (wa huwa al-zahir).
The text explicitly states the tarjih for Muhammad's view.
5
Examine the co-ownership case
Two co-owners of a slave cannot exchange shares for different work because the shares are identical in quality. It would only be valid if the work were in two different slaves (different genus).
This applies the same principle: identical genus triggers riba al-nisa.

If one party performs but not the other, Imam Muhammad says the fair rent (ujrat al-mithl) is due for the performed service; Abu Yusuf says nothing is due because the contract was void. Another example involves a slave owned by two partners: one rents his share to the other for sewing in exchange for goldsmithing in the next month. This is invalid because both shares in the same slave are identical; to be valid, the tasks must be different and on two separate slaves. The commentary explains the ruling: rent is the price of utility, so it is measured by the price of sold goods. However, the ruling is not perfectly reversible: items like animals cannot be a price in sale but can be rent.

When Is a Porter's Hire Due? The Ruling on Lowering Goods
States that no hire is due if goods are not lowered from head

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a hired porter must fully complete the service—including lowering the goods—to be entitled to payment, and explain the legal reasoning (talil) behind it.

Core Idea
The hire is not earned until the work is fully completed — partial performance does not deserve payment.

This short but powerful ruling from the Hanafi scholar al-Karkhi clarifies a common point of dispute in hire contracts (ijarah). A porter who carries goods on his head but does not lower them at the destination has not finished the job. The reasoning is simple: lowering is part of the completion of the work. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its implications for understanding when a hired worker's right to payment crystalizes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Ruling
What is the specific act under discussion? The act is 'al-hatt' — lowering the goods from the porter's head. The ruling is that if he does not perform this act, the hire (al-ujrah) is not obligatory (lā tajibu).
The ruling is negative: no hire due, not that the contract is invalid.
2
Locate the Daleel (Proof)
The text gives the reason directly: 'because lowering is part of the completion of the work' (li-anna al-hatta min tamām al-ʿamal). This is the daleel — it is a talīl (causal explanation) that establishes the link between incompleteness and non-entitlement.
The daleel is not a Quranic verse or hadith but a clear legal principle articulated by a Hanafi authority.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah (How the Proof Proves)
The phrase 'min tamām al-ʿamal' means that lowering is an integral part of the work. Since the hire is owed only for the completed work, omitting this part means the work is incomplete, so the hire is not due. The wajh is that completion is a condition for payment.
This is a straightforward linguistic-legal inference from the term 'tamām'.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf (Scholarly Difference)
The Hanafi position is that no hire is due for partial performance. Other schools (e.g., the Shafiʿi school) may allow proportionate payment for the work done. This difference is acknowledged in the Hanafi books as a valid ikhtilaf.
The practical consequence (thamarāt al-khilāf) is whether the porter gets anything if he carries but does not lower.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih (Preferred View)
Although this ruling is from al-Karkhi (not from the six authoritative books of Zahir al-Riwaya), it is accepted as the muftā bihi position in the Hanafi school. The logic is sound and consistent with general Hanafi principles on ijarah.
The authority weight is fatawa mutaʾakhkhirin — later fatwa literature.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Subleasing before possession is prohibited for movables but allowed for immovables per the Sahibayn.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah clarifies an important distinction in Hanafi lease law. The tenant's right to sublease before taking physical possession depends on the nature of the property. Identify the act and its context
The act in question is subleasing (i.e., the tenant renting the property to another) before the tenant has taken physical possession of the property. This is a derivative of the general prohibition on selling before possession (bay' al-mabi' qabla al-qabd).

Under the Sahibayn's view, a tenant of a house can sublease it immediately after signing the lease, before moving in. Under Muhammad's view, the tenant must wait until taking possession.

A hired animal may only be loaded with goods equal or lesser in harm than specified; greater harm makes the hirer liable as a usurper.

In Hanafi Ijara, when a lessee specifies the type and quantity of cargo, the lessor's consent is limited to that specific load. Understanding this rule is crucial to determine liability in case of damage.

Rulings on liability for violation of load specifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Ḥanafī rulings when a hirer violates load specifications in ijarah and apply the principles of ḍamān to different violation scenarios.

Core Idea
Violating the agreed load transforms the hirer into a usurper, making him liable for the beast's value if it perishes.

When a person hires a beast of burden, the contract specifies the type and quantity of load. The Ḥanafī school places great emphasis on adhering to these terms, as deviations can cause unforeseen harm. This lesson explores the consequences of such violations, focusing on when the hirer becomes liable (ḍamān) and how the liability is calculated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the violation
Determine what was agreed (type, weight, volume) and what was actually loaded. For example, agreed to carry wheat but loaded iron.

2
Assess the nature of the load
Classify whether the substituted load is more harmful by nature (e.g., iron vs cotton) or merely heavier (e.g., wheat vs barley). The Ḥanafī school considers both weight and harmfulness.

3
Determine the cause of death

If the load exceeds the beast's customary capacity, the hirer is liable for the full value regardless of permission.
This is because no permission is deemed given for an impossible load.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for liability when a hirer exceeds the agreed terms in an animal lease (ijarah) for riding, load, or destination, and distinguish between proportional and full liability.

Core Idea
Exceeding the agreed terms in ijarah makes the hirer a violator (mukhalif), liable for damages proportional to the excess.

When you hire an animal for riding or carrying goods, the contract specifies the terms. If you exceed those terms—taking an extra rider, overloading, or going beyond the agreed destination—you become a violator. The Hanafi school has detailed rules for liability in such cases, which this passage explains. Understanding these rules is essential for anyone dealing with lease contracts.

Guided Path

Identify the violation
Determine exactly what term was exceeded: taking an extra rider (irdaf), carrying extra load, or exceeding the destination.
The hirer becomes a 'mukhalif' (violator).
2
Assess the animal's capacity
For extra rider or load, check if the animal can bear the total (rider+hirer or total load). If yes, liability is proportional; if no, full liability.
Capacity refers to the animal's normal ability, not the specific animal's condition.
3
Apply the ruling for extra rider
If the animal can bear both, the hirer is liable for half the value. If not, full value. The weight of riders is not considered because skill matters.
The half-value rule is unique to riding; it does not apply to load.
4
Apply the ruling for extra load
If the total load is bearable, liability is for the proportion of damage caused by the excess load (e.g., if excess is 1/5 of total, liability is 1/5 of value). If the total is unbearable, full liability.
Liability is apportioned because the damage is caused by both permitted and unpermitted loads.
5

If the hirer goes beyond the agreed place and the animal dies, full liability. If returned safely, rent is due only for the permitted outbound journey; no rent for return because rent and liability cannot combine.
The same rule applies to borrowed items ('ariyah).

In hire contracts, the agreed terms define the limits of permissible use. If the hirer violates the agreed destination, he becomes a 'mukhalif' (violator) and assumes liability for the hired item. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Safe return after deviation still obligates rent; harm alone voids it.

In Hanafi Ijarah law, a hirer who deviates from the agreed route normally forfeits the rent and becomes liable for damages. This lesson presents a critical exception: if the animal reaches the specified destination and is returned safely to its owner, the original rent remains due. The ruling balances the owner's right to compensation for use against the hirer's breach, showing that liability hinges on actual harm rather than mere violation.

Rent Due Upon Possession in Ijara
Ruling when hirer fails to ride but takes the animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that rent becomes wajib (obligatory) upon taking possession of a hired object, regardless of actual use, and apply this principle to similar ijara scenarios.

Core Idea
Rent is due upon taking possession—the hirer's right to use is what matters, not actual use.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from the Hanafi school: when a person hires an animal to ride to a known place, takes it, but never rides it or loads anything on it. Does the hirer still have to pay rent? The answer is yes. This ruling highlights a key principle in Hanafi ijara law: rent is owed for the availability of the benefit (al-manfa‘ah), not necessarily its utilization. The hirer, by taking possession, has prevented the owner from using or hiring the animal to someone else, and thus the obligation is triggered.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contractual act and the missing performance
The hirer entered a contract to ride the animal to a known place (e.g., a city). He took the animal (possession) but did not ride nor load anything. Note what he did (possession) and what he did not do (use).
The text says 'fa dhahaba biha wa lam yarkabha' — he went off with it and did not ride it.
2
State the ruling and the operative factor
The ruling is 'rent is obligatory upon him' (wajib). The operative factor is taking possession (taqabbud). The Hanafi school views possession as the moment when the hirer gains the ability to use the object, which is the essence of the contract.
Contrast with a case where the hirer never takes possession (e.g., stays home) — then rent would not be due.
3
Explore the underlying rationale
Why does possession alone obligate rent? Because the hirer has prevented the owner from earning from the animal during the hire period. The benefit (manfa‘ah) is considered delivered when the hirer can access it, even if not exercised. This is a Hanafi principle in ijara.
This differs from a sale where risk passes after possession; here, the obligation is triggered by the hirer's control.
