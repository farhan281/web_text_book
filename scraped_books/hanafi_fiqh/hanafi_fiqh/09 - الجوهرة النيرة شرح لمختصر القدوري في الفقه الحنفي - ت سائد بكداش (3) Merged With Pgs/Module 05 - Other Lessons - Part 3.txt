Terms with Fixed Minimums: 'بضع' and 'قريب من'
Rulings on 'بضع' and 'قريب من' in iqrar: fixed minima and debtor's word on excess.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between terms with a fixed minimum (bidth=3) and terms with a proportional minimum (qarib=half+1), and calculate the owed amount in iqrar involving these terms.

Core Idea
Some ambiguous numbers have linguistic floors that protect the creditor from undervaluation.

While 'nif' gives the debtor full freedom, the words 'bidth' (بضع) and 'qarib' (قريب) impose a lower boundary. 'Bidth' means at least three, so the debtor cannot claim less. 'Qarib' means more than half, so the minimum is half plus one. These floors balance the debtor's right to clarify with the creditor's right to a meaningful amount.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the ambiguous phrase
Identify if the iqrar uses 'بضع' (a few) or 'قريب من' (close to) or synonyms like 'جل' (most), 'زهاء' (about), 'عظم' (bulk).
These terms have legally defined minima.
2
Apply the linguistic minimum
For 'بضع', the minimum is three. For 'قريب' and its synonyms, the minimum is more than half of the number mentioned, i.e., half plus at least one unit.
The minimum is part of the 'hadd' (limit) of the term.
3
Accept debtor's word for the excess
Above the floor, the debtor's clarification of the exact amount is accepted, because the excess is ambiguous and the debtor is best informed.
The debtor cannot go below the floor.
4
Reject claims below the floor
If the debtor claims an amount that is half or less of the total, he is not believed. For 'qarib', claiming half or less contradicts the meaning of 'close to'.
The text explicitly says 'لا يُصدق في النصف وما دونه'.

Interpreting Ambiguous Numbers in Confession: Hanafi Rules
Rulings on ambiguous numbers in confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Hanafi rules to determine the minimum liability when a confession uses ambiguous numerical expressions like 'nayf', 'bid'', or 'jull'.

Core Idea
The confessor's word is accepted in ambiguous terms, but linguistic norms set minimums for words like bid' and jull.

When someone confesses a debt using numbers and ambiguous qualifiers, how do we determine what he owes? The Hanafi school balances the confessor's right to clarify with the objective meaning of words. This lesson walks you through the key expressions: compound numbers, 'nayf', 'bid'', 'close to', and 'most of'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of ambiguous expression
The passage covers several categories: (1) a number with a different item (e.g., 'hundred and two sheep'), (2) an unspecified number alone (e.g., 'ten'), (3) 'nayf' (an additional unspecified amount), (4) 'bid'' (a few), and (5) 'close to' / 'most of' / 'around' / 'bulk of'.
Each category has its own rule.
2
Determine if the confessor clarifies the ambiguous part
For compound numbers like 'ten and a slave', the confessor owes only the separate item (slave) and can explain the number as he wishes. For an unspecified number like 'ten', he must specify the type (dirhams, dinars, etc.). If he uses a generic word like 'dirhams', his word is accepted.
The key principle: ambiguity is resolved by the confessor's explanation, unless the term has a fixed linguistic minimum.
3
Apply the specific rules for 'nayf', 'bid'', and 'close to'
- 'Nayf' (an addition): The confessor can set it as any amount, even less than one dirham. - 'Bid'' (a few): Fixed at minimum three (e.g., 50+3).- 'Close to a thousand' or similar: Minimum is 500 plus something (so at least 501). The confessor's word is accepted for the excess, but he cannot claim less than half.
These minimums prevent the confessor from exploiting ambiguity to owe nothing.
4
Conclude the owed amount
Combine the base number and the interpreted ambiguous part to find the minimum liability. For example, 'bid' and 50 dirhams' = at least 53 dirhams.
The confessor may owe more if his explanation is greater, but not less than the minimum.

Ruling on Saying 'In sha Allah' in Confessions
Ruling and commentary on conditional and suspended confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between istithna', ta'liq, and ta'jil in confessions, and state the Hanafi ruling for each scenario.

Core Idea
Saying 'in sha Allah' connected to a confession voids it entirely; only genuine deferment (ta'jil) is valid.

This passage addresses a common but legally significant phrase: 'in sha Allah' when used in a confession. The Hanafi school treats it as voiding the confession, distinguishing it from legitimate deferment and invalid suspension. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly applying rulings on confessions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is making a confession (iqrar) with the phrase 'in sha Allah' connected directly. The Hanafi ruling is that such a confession is not binding (batil).
The phrase must be connected; if separated, it may not nullify.
2
Analyze the reasoning
The jurists reason that the exception either nullifies the statement entirely or suspends it on a future condition. Both are incompatible with the binding and immediate nature of a confession.
The nullification is based on the principle that the exception contradicts the purpose of confession.
3
Distinguish deferment (ta'jil)
If the confession includes a term like 'when I die' or 'at the beginning of the month', it is considered ta'jil (deferment), not ta'liq. The confession is valid, and if the term is unknown, the debt becomes immediately due.
Deferment specifies a time for payment; it does not suspend the existence of the debt.
4
Recognize invalid suspension (ta'liq)
Suspending the confession on a condition that does not specify a term, such as 'if so-and-so wills' or 'if I enter the house', makes the confession invalid, even if the condition occurs.
Such conditions are uncertain (gharar) and inconsistent with the seriousness of confession.

Iqrār with Option (Khiyār)
Ruling on iqrār with option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on iqrār with a stipulated option and explain why the option is void.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment is binding immediately; an option clause is void because iqrār is not a contract.

In Hanafi fiqh, iqrār is a unilateral binding declaration. Unlike contracts, it does not admit conditions or options. This lesson explains the ruling when someone acknowledges a right and stipulates an option for themselves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine that the act is an acknowledgment (iqrār) with a stipulated option. The ruling is that the acknowledgment is binding (lāzim) and the option is void (bāṭil).
2
Understand the scenario
The scenario includes acknowledging a debt, usurpation, deposit, or loan, with an option period (e.g., three days). The option has no legal effect.
3
Analyze the reasoning
The reason is that iqrār is a confession, not a contract. An option (khiyār) is a right to rescind, but a confession once made is binding and cannot be rescinded.
This distinguishes iqrār from sales, where khiyār is valid.
4
Distinguish from contracts
Contracts can include options because they are bilateral and subject to negotiation. Iqrār is unilateral and binding immediately.

Ruling on conditional iqrār and deferral

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between valid deferral (ta'jīl) and invalid suspension (ta'līq) in iqrār, and identify which conditions are void.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment cannot be suspended; conditions like 'if I die' are treated as deferral, but conditions on uncertain events like 'if so-and-so wills' are void.

The Hanafi school strictly requires iqrār to be unconditional. However, some phrases that appear conditional are actually valid as deferral of payment. This lesson examines the distinction between ta'līq (suspension) and ta'jīl (deferral) using examples like 'if I die' vs 'if so-and-so wills'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Iqrār cannot be suspended (ta'līq) on a future uncertain event. Any condition that truly suspends the acknowledgment makes it void.
2
Analyze the exception: deferral
Statements like 'if I die' or 'if the month begins' are considered specifying a term (ta'jīl), not suspension. They are valid.
The key is whether the condition denotes a term or a pure risk.
3
Understand the consequence of deferral
If the acknowledgee denies the term, the debt becomes immediately due. The term is treated as an agreement on payment time.
4
Recognize invalid conditions
Conditions based on another's will or uncertain events (e.g., 'if so-and-so wills', 'if you enter a house') are invalid because they are risks (khaṭar).
Even if the condition occurs later, the acknowledgment remains void.
5
Apply to the case 'if I die'

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between how the Hanafi school treats confession involving immovable property vs movable containers and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
Whether a confession to seizing property includes the container depends on whether the container is immovable or movable.

This lesson explores a key distinction in Hanafi law regarding confessions involving property located inside larger structures. The classic example is confessing to an animal in a stable: does that include the stable itself? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on this point, and the answer depends on whether the 'container' is real estate or a movable object like a handkerchief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the conflicting rulings
Recognize that the confession is about taking something inside something else. Determine which items are in dispute – the contained item and the container.
In the stable example, the dispute is whether the stable is also owed.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
According to them, only the movable item (animal or wheat) is owed; the immovable (stable or house) is not. They consider real estate not subject to ghasb.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
3
State the position of Muhammad
Muhammad holds that both the movable and the immovable are owed, because real estate is guaranteed by ghasb.
This is a minority view within the Hanafi school.
4
Analyze the reasoning for each side
Examine the legal principle: Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf base their view on the nature of real estate being immune to ghasb, while Muhammad extends ghasb to real estate.
The reasoning is aqli (rational) based on the definition of ghasb.
5
Distinguish the movable container case
When the container is movable (e.g., handkerchief), all three imams agree that both the contained and the container are owed, because movables are uniformly subject to ghasb.
This is the case of 'thawban fi mindeel'.

Interpreting 'Fi' in Confessions: Containment and Ambiguity
Internal Hanafi dispute on the interpretation of 'fi' in confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the number of items owed in a Hanafi confession using 'fi' when the phrase implies literal containment versus when it introduces uncertainty, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
The literal 'fi' means inside; but doubt about metaphorical use invokes bara'at al-dhimmah, reducing liability to one item.

When someone says 'I owe a cloth in a cloth,' the preposition 'fi' is understood literally: the cloth is inside another cloth, so both are owed. But when the container is impossibly large or the word 'fi' could mean 'among,' the Hanafi school splits. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that only the first item is owed, while Muhammad al-Shaybani sees a possibility that all items are intended. In this lesson, we trace the linguistic and legal reasoning behind these positions.

Guided Path
1
Step 1: Establish the literal rule
Read the first ruling: 'If he says: I owe a cloth in a cloth, both are binding upon him.' Recognize that the Hanafi position takes 'fi' at face value as physical containment. The cloth is inside another, so both are owed.
This assumes the context of ghasb (usurpation).
2
Step 2: Identify the dispute for 'thawb fi 'asharat athwab'
Read the second ruling: 'If he says: I owe a cloth in ten cloths, only one cloth is owed according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf; Muhammad says eleven.' Note the disagreement over whether ten cloths can be a container.
Muhammad considers the possibility of a valuable cloth wrapped in ten others.
3
Step 3: Follow Abu Yusuf's dalil from the Quran
Abu Yusuf argues using the verse 'Enter among My servants' where 'fi' means 'among' (not 'inside'). This demonstrates that 'fi' can be ambiguous. Since the confession is doubtful, the principle of bara'at al-dhimmah (presumption of innocence) means only one item is obligated.
The dalil is a single verse; its interpretation is zanni.
4
Step 4: Apply the ruling
Conclude: In a confession with 'fi,' if the container could be literal, both items owed; if not (e.g., ten cloths for one cloth), only one item owed. The Hanafi school follows the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
The mufta bihi is the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Confession of Usurpation: Accepting the Defendant's Claim of Defect
A specific ruling within confessions: handling defect claims in ghasb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that in a confession of ghasb, if the defendant produces a defective item, his statement that this was the usurped item is accepted with his oath.

Core Idea
A confession of ghasb does not imply the item was perfect; the defendant's claim of defect is credible.

When someone confesses to usurping a cloth but presents a defective one, can the plaintiff demand a sound one? The Hanafi ruling states that the defendant's statement about the defect is accepted with his oath. This is because the act of usurpation (ghasb) does not require the property to be in perfect condition. In this lesson, we examine the rationale behind this lenient treatment and its application.

Guided Path
1
Step 1: Understand the confession of ghasb
The defendant says: 'I usurped a cloth' but later produces a defective cloth. The key is that he admits liability, but disputes the condition of the item.
2
Step 2: Identify the ruling
The text states: 'f-al-qawl qawluhu fihi ma'a yaminih' – his statement is accepted with his oath. The burden of proof shifts: the plaintiff must prove the item was sound if he challenges the defect.
This ruling applies specifically when the defendant does not deny the ghasb but only disputes the defect.
3
Step 3: Grasp the rationale
The commentary explains: 'because ghasb is not specific to sound goods.' Usurpation is the unlawful taking, regardless of the item's condition. Therefore, the defendant's claim that the taken item was defective is plausible.
This is based on the default assumption that the defendant's word is accepted (tasdiq) in such matters, especially when he confesses to a liability.

Confessions of Counterfeit Dirhams and Numerical Phrases
Expansion on confessions with counterfeitness and numerical ambiguities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on confessions involving counterfeit dirhams and interpret ambiguous numerical phrases like 'five in five', and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa from the two disciples.

Core Idea
A confession is believed when consistent; claiming counterfeitness after a sale is contradictory, and 'five in five' means only one five unless addition is intended.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar regarding confessions with qualifications. The first ruling addresses whether a debtor who confesses to dirhams but then claims they were counterfeit is believed. The second ruling clarifies the interpretation of the phrase 'five in five' when the confessor intends multiplication or addition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the confession and its qualification
Recognize that the debtor has confessed to owing dirhams but adds that they are counterfeit (ziyuf). The ruling depends on whether this qualification is connected (wasl) or separated (fasl) from the confession, and whether the confession is linked to a sale or loan.
Connection means explicitly linking the qualification to the same transaction.
2
Apply the rule for confessions not linked to a sale/loan
If the confession is not tied to a specific transaction (e.g., general debt), the debtor is believed both when connecting and separating the qualification, according to the relied-upon view.
The text says 'he is believed, whether he connects or separates' for the absolute case.
3
Distinguish Abu Hanifa's position for sale-linked confessions
When the confession is linked to a sale or loan, Abu Hanifa rules that the debtor is not believed at all, because a valid sale implies the price was sound, and claiming it was counterfeit is a contradiction.
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: the sale contract's absolute nature requires the price to be valid.
4
Apply the position of the two disciples
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that if the debtor connects the counterfeitness claim to the sale (e.g., 'the dirhams from that sale were counterfeit'), he is believed; if he separates, he is not.
The two disciples give weight to consistency in the statement.
5
Interpret numerical phrases 'five in five' and 'five with five'
When someone says 'five in five' intending multiplication, only one five is owed (5 x 1 = 5) because multiplication does not create multiple physical sums. If he says 'five with five' intending addition, ten is owed.
Zufar and al-Hasan hold that twenty-five is owed for 'five in five', but the majority view is one five.

Capacity of the Confessor: Sleep and Insanity
Rulings on iqrar during sleep and insanity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on an iqrar (acknowledgment) made while asleep or insane, and apply the original presumption of sanity to determine liability.

Core Idea
An iqrar during sleep is invalid; an iqrar during insanity is invalid only if the insanity is known.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies when an acknowledgment (iqrar) is binding. The key principle is that capacity matters: while an adult's denial is accepted with oath, claims of sleep or insanity must be assessed against the original presumption of sanity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline ruling for an adult
When the confessor is an adult and denies the acknowledgment, his statement (with oath) is accepted, and nothing is due. This establishes the default: the confessor's word is taken unless proven otherwise.
This is the general principle for an adult confessor.
2
Analyze the claim of sleep
If the confessor says he made the acknowledgment while asleep, his statement is accepted, meaning the iqrar is not binding. Sleep suspends legal capacity.
Sleep is a state that eliminates capacity for voluntary acts.
3
Analyze the claim of insanity (junun or barsam)
If the confessor claims insanity or pleurisy, we distinguish: if it is known that he suffered from that condition, his statement is accepted (iqrar invalid); if it is not known, the iqrar is binding because the original state (al-asl) is sanity.
The burden of proof is on the confessor to establish the known condition.
4
Apply the legal maxim 'al-asl al-salamah'
The original presumption is that a person is sane and capable. Therefore, without evidence of insanity, the acknowledgment stands as valid.
This presumption is a key usul principle.

Liability of Minors and Insane Persons for Taking Property
Ruling on liability of minor/insane for taking property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a minor or insane person is liable (damin) for taking money, even though they lack contractual capacity.

Core Idea
Minors and insane persons are liable for tortious takings because their actions causing harm are valid.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a specific case: when a minor or insane person confesses to taking money. Unlike their general incapacity for contracts, their actions causing harm are recognized in Hanafi law, making them liable for compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
The act is a statement: 'I took a thousand from you.' The speaker is either a minor (below puberty) or an insane person (majnun).
This is not an iqrar of debt but an admission of a tortious taking.
2
Determine the ruling
The text says the person is 'daminan' (liable). This means he must compensate the amount taken.
Liability (daman) is a legal obligation to pay or restore.
3
Understand the rationale
The reason given is 'because their actions are valid.' In Hanafi fiqh, the actions of minors and insane persons are considered valid (sahih) for purposes of causing harm (itlaf) and incurring liability, even though they lack capacity for contracts and other voluntary acts.
This is a key exception to the general rule that minors and insane persons have no legal effect from their statements.
4
Contrast with iqrar
If a minor or insane person makes an acknowledgment of debt (iqrar) without a taking, it is not binding. But here, because the taking is an actual physical act, liability arises.
The distinction is between mere words (iqrar) and actions causing harm.

Ruling on Acknowledgment of Defective Coins after Usurpation or Deposit
States ruling on accepting acknowledgment of defective coins, with ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of defective coins after usurpation or deposit and explain why the claim is accepted even when disconnected, and identify the exception for tin/lead.

Core Idea
In usurpation and deposit, the acknowledger is believed even if the claim of defectiveness is disconnected — no presumption of good coins.

When someone acknowledges that they usurped or received a deposit of a sum of money, and later describes the coins as counterfeit or debased, should they be taken at their word? This passage from Kitab al-Iqrar clarifies the Hanafi position: the person is believed regardless of whether the description is given in the same breath or separately, because there is no presumption that the coins were sound. We will examine the reasoning, the intra-Hanafi difference, and a special case involving tin or lead.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Identify what is being acknowledged (usurpation or deposit) and the subsequent claim (coins are counterfeit/debased). The ruling: the acknowledger is believed (musaddaq), and the acknowledgment is valid (sahih).
The key is that the claim about the coins does not contradict the acknowledgment; it qualifies it.
2
Locate the dalil (reasoning)
The proof given is rational: a person may usurp whatever they find and deposit whatever they own, so there is no requirement for good coins. Thus, the subsequent description of defectiveness is consistent with the original act.
This is a talil (causal reasoning) rather than a textual proof.
3
Note the ikhtilaf
There is an intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Yusuf holds that if the description is disconnected, he is not believed, by analogy to a loan (qard). Also a 'qila' view says he is never believed because acknowledgments default to contracts, not prohibited consumption.
The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) reject the analogy to loan because usurpation/deposit do not create a debt of sound coins.
4
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that he is believed in both connected and disconnected cases, as stated by Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. This is the zahir al-riwayah.
The special case of tin/lead (stooq/rasas) is treated like an exception (istithna): believed only if connected.
5
Apply the distinction for tin/lead
If the claim is that the coins are made of tin or lead, it is treated differently: if connected, he is believed; if disconnected, he is not. This is because tin/lead are entirely different substances, not just defective silver.
This follows the rule of istithna (exception), which requires connection.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Iqrar discusses the ruling on an acknowledgment of taking a thousand dirhams by way of usurpation (ghasb) or deposit (wadi'ah), where the acknowledger later claims that the dirhams were counterfeit (ziyuf) or debased (nabahrajah). The general rule is that such an acknowledgment is accepted and the person is believed, whether his statement about the coins is connected (wassal) to the acknowledgment or disconnected (fasal). The reason is that a person may usurp or receive as deposit whatever coins he finds, including defective ones, so there is no presumption that the coins must be of good quality. This ruling is contrasted with a minority view (qila) that says the acknowledgment should not be believed because acknowledgments default to valid contracts, not to forbidden consumption (istihlak muharram) which would be the case if the coins were defective. There is also an intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Yusuf holds that if the claim of defectiveness is disconnected (fasal), he is not believed, by analogy to a loan (qard) where the qard creates a debt of sound coins. Imam Muhammad and Abu Hanifa, however, maintain that the ruling stands even if disconnected because the deposit and usurpation do not imply soundness. The passage also includes a variant: if after acknowledging usurpation or deposit, he says the coins are made of tin (stooq) or lead (rasas), he is believed if connected; if disconnected, he is not believed. This shows that the description of material is like an exception (istithna) which is only valid when connected.

نبهرجة (nabahrajah): Debased coins, similar to ziyuf.
وصل/فصل (wassal/fasal): Connected or disconnected speech; whether the description is uttered in the same breath or separately.

Disconnected Exception of Quantity in Acknowledgment is Invalid
States ruling on disconnected quantity exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on disconnected exception of quantity in acknowledgment and identify when a disconnection is excused.

Core Idea
An exception of quantity in an acknowledgment is only valid if connected, unless the disconnection was unavoidable.

Imagine someone acknowledges a debt but later says 'except that it is less by some amount.' In Hanafi law, such an exception is only accepted if uttered in the same breath. This lesson explains the ruling, the reason, and the exception for necessary interruptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement and its parts
Recognize that the acknowledgment includes a quantity and an exception ('except that it is deficient by such'). Determine whether the exception was stated consecutively (connected) or in a separate utterance (disconnected).
The key variable is connection (ittisal) vs. disconnection (fasl).
2
Apply the default rule
If the exception is disconnected, the exception is not accepted (the acknowledgment stands as the full quantity). The reason: this is an exception of quantity (istithna al-miqdar), and in Hanafi law exceptions are only valid when connected.
The rule applies specifically to quantity exceptions, not to descriptions.
3
Distinguish between exception and description
Notice the contrast with describing coins as counterfeit (ziyafah). A description is valid even if disconnected, because it is a wasf (attribute) not an istithna (exception).
This distinction is crucial for understanding the scope of the rule.
4
Check for the necessity exception
If the disconnection was due to an unavoidable interruption (e.g., coughing, someone interrupting), the person is considered to have connected. The law recognizes human limitations.
The necessity must be genuine; not every pause justifies the exception.

An unborn child can be a creditor only if the debt arises from inheritance or bequest.

Acknowledgment of debt (iqrar) is a powerful legal tool that can create obligations even for an unborn child. The validity depends on linking the debt to a legal cause: either bequest (wasiyyah) from someone, or inheritance (irth) from the father.
The words 'أوصى له به فلان' (bequeathed) and 'مات أبوه فورثه' (father died and he inherited) are the operative phrases.

An unborn child can own property through inheritance or bequest because these are direct transfers of existing rights. Contracts (like sale) require active capacity, which a fetus lacks.
This is consistent with the Hanafi view that haml has limited legal personality.
4
Apply the ruling to new scenarios

The passage does not explicitly cover the absence of a cause, but the implication is clear.

What Does Acknowledging a Composite Item Include?
Ruling on scope of acknowledgment for composite items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that acknowledgment of a composite item transfers ownership of all customary parts.

Core Idea
The name of a composite item in custom includes all its parts; acknowledgment transfers them all.

A simple acknowledgment can have far-reaching consequences. This lesson explores a key Hanafi principle: when you acknowledge that a composite item belongs to another, the name of that item in customary usage determines what is included. The text from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah gives examples with a ring, sword, and tent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the item and its parts
Read the acknowledgment: what item is named? List the constituent parts mentioned in the text or implied by custom.
The text explicitly lists parts for ring (band, setting), sword (blade, scabbard, strap), tent (poles, covering).
2
Recognize the customary name includes all customary parts
The Hanafi rule is that the name of a composite item in common usage (urf) encompasses all its essential components. This is based on the Arabic linguistic custom.
The text says: 'because the name includes all.'
3
Apply the rule to the given examples
For each item, confirm that the acknowledged item's name includes all listed parts. Even explicit exclusion of a part (e.g., setting) is ineffective because the name already includes it.
The ring example: even if the acknowledger says 'the setting is for me,' the entire ring goes to the other person.
4
Generalize to other composite items
The same principle applies to any composite item. In Hanafi law, the default ruling is that acknowledging an item by its customary name transfers ownership of all its customary parts.
Custom may vary; if local usage differs, that custom would govern.

Core Idea
Debts proven by external causes are trusted; mere acknowledgments in terminal illness are suspect.

When a person on their deathbed acknowledges debts, not all debts are treated equally. Hanafi law distinguishes between debts that arise from clear, verifiable transactions and those that are merely acknowledged. This lesson explores the rationale behind giving priority to 'debts known by causes' and health debts over acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of debts
Debts from health (dayn al-siha), debts from illness with known causes (al-dayn al-ma'ruf bi al-asbab), and debts merely acknowledged during illness (iqrar).
2
State the ruling on precedence
Health debts and debts with known causes are equal and given precedence over mere acknowledgments. This is because there is no suspicion (tuhma) in debts established by known causes.
The text says: 'fadayn al-siha, wa al-dayn al-ma'ruf bi al-asbab muqaddam.'
3

The reasoning is 'la tuhma fi thubut al-ma'ruf bi al-asbab; idh al-mu'ayan la madda lahu' – there is no suspicion in what is established by causes because the obvious cannot be denied.
Examples: price of property owned or consumed, marriage dowry, expenses for medicine.
4

If the patient actually paid a debt (e.g., repaid a loan or paid for a purchase) during illness and it is proven by evidence (bayyina), that payment is valid and not subject to the same prioritization rules.
The text says: 'illa idha qada ma istaqradahu fi maradihi, aw naqada thaman ma ishtarahu fi maradihi, wa qad u'lima bi al-bayyina.'
5

All creditors share equally in the remaining estate after prioritized debts.

Defining Marad al-Mawt: The Hanafi Criteria
Presents multiple Hanafi definitions of death-illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the various Hanafi definitions of marad al-mawt, identify the mufta bihi opinion (Abu al-Layth), and apply it to determine when donations are limited to one-third.

Core Idea
The relied-upon definition of marad al-mawt is the inability to pray standing — a clear, practical criterion.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah reviews multiple Hanafi definitions of 'marad al-mawt' (death-illness), a legal condition that restricts a person's donations and acknowledgments to one-third of the estate. The commentator surveys several opinions and selects the view of Abu al-Layth as the most appropriate. Understanding this definition is essential for correctly applying the rules of bequests and gifts during a final illness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal context
Recognize that marad al-mawt is a condition that triggers special rules for donations and acknowledgments. If a person is in a death-illness, their gratuitous transfers (e.g., gifts, bequests) are limited to one-third of the estate unless the heirs approve.
This rule protects heirs' rights and prevents evasion of inheritance laws.
2
List the initial definitions
Note the three definitions given: (1) inability to stand without help, (2) being bedridden even if able to stand, (3) inability to walk without support. Each provides a different threshold.
These are all valid opinions within the Hanafi school, but not all are considered equally strong.
3
Identify Abu al-Layth's definition
Abu al-Layth defines it as inability to pray standing. The commentator explicitly prefers this view ('أحب') and adopts it ('به نأخذ'). This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
This definition is considered most appropriate because it is easy to apply and captures the essence of incapacity.
4
Examine al-Khujandi's definition
Al-Khujandi adds another criterion: inability to stand for one's needs, with permission to pray sitting, and the illness must be life-threatening. This clarifies that the condition is both physical incapacity and medical danger.
Al-Khujandi's definition reinforces that the illness must be 'makhuf' (feared).
5
Determine the practical consequence
The difference between definitions affects when the death-illness is considered to begin. For example, if a person cannot stand for prayer but can walk, their donations are limited. If they can pray sitting but cannot stand for needs, also limited. The mufta bihi (Abu al-Layth) provides the clearest benchmark.
In all cases, once marad al-mawt is established, donations beyond one-third are invalid unless heirs consent.

Acknowledging a debt to an heir is valid; giving a gift to an heir is invalid.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah presents two contrasting cases involving transactions with heirs during death illness. The key distinction is between acknowledging a liability and making a gift.

Acknowledging Dower in Sickness: The Mahr al-Mithl Limit
Ruling on acknowledging dower to wife in terminal illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a husband's acknowledgment of dower during terminal illness, identify the proper dower as the limit, and explain the rationale of preventing fraud on heirs.

Core Idea
In terminal illness, acknowledgment of dower is trusted only up to the proper dower; excess is invalid to protect heirs.

In Hanafi law, an iqrar (acknowledgment) by a terminally ill person is subject to special scrutiny to protect the rights of heirs. When the acknowledgment is of a dower (mahr) to his wife, who is also an heir, the law balances her claim with the interest of other heirs by limiting the accepted amount to mahr al-mithl (the proper dower). This lesson unpacks the exact ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and context
Recognize that the act is an iqrar (acknowledgment) by a husband of a mahr (dower) owed to his wife, occurring during his terminal illness (marad al-mawt). The wife is his legal heir.
Terminal illness is defined as an illness from which death is expected and which actually leads to death.
2
State the ruling
The husband is believed (his acknowledgment is valid and binding) if the amount he acknowledges is equal to or less than her proper dower (mahr al-mithl). He is not believed for any amount exceeding the proper dower; the excess is invalid.
3
Understand the limit: mahr al-mithl
Mahr al-mithl is the dower typically given to women similar to her in family, status, and locality. It serves as an objective benchmark to prevent the husband from making an inflated acknowledgment that disadvantages other heirs.
4
Explain the rationale
Since the wife is an heir, an acknowledgment of mahr above the norm is treated like a bequest to an heir, which is not permitted in terminal illness (unless other heirs consent, but here the default is that it is not binding). The limit protects the inheritance rights of other heirs.
This is analogous to the one-third rule for bequests to non-heirs, but here the rule is stricter to prevent disguised gifts to heirs.
5
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may accept the full acknowledgment if no fraud is proven, but the Hanafi school applies this objective cap to prevent abuse.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment or admission of a right or debt owed to another.
أجنبية (ajnabiyyah): A woman who is not a mahram (close relative) and not a wife at the time of acknowledgment.
مرض الموت (marad al-mawt): Deathbed illness, a terminal condition that triggers special rulings on transactions and gifts to protect heirs' rights.

A) Because marriage is more binding

Key Takeaway
An acknowledgment to a stranger remains valid even after marriage because the status at the time of acknowledgment determines validity, not subsequent changes.
