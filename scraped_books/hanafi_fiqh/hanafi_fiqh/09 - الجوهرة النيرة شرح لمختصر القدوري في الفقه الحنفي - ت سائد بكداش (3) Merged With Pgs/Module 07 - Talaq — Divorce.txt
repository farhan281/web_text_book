General Legal Capacity of Minors and the Insane
Ruling on general capacity: contracts, acknowledgments, actions, and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on contracts, acknowledgments, and actions of minors and the insane, and apply the distinction between verbal and physical acts.

Core Idea
Minors and the insane lack valid speech; their contracts and acknowledgments are void, but their physical actions are valid unless involving hudud or qisas.

This lesson explores the foundational Hanafi principle that legal capacity ('aqd, iqrar, shahada) depends on valid speech (qawl). For minors and the insane, their words carry no legal effect, hence contracts and acknowledgments are invalid. However, physical actions (af'al) remain valid, as they do not require speech. Exceptions exist for acts where doubt (shubha) averts punishment, such as hudud and qisas. We will also examine cases of pure benefit (naf' mahd) and agency (wikala).

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for contracts and acknowledgments
The text states: 'الصبي والمجنون لا تصح عقودهما ولا إقرارهما ومشاهدتهما' – their contracts, acknowledgments, and testimonies are invalid because they lack valid speech (qaul). The reason is that legal consideration is based on the Law, and intention (qasd) is a condition.
2
Understand the exception for actions
Actions (af'al) are generally valid from minors and the insane, except where the ruling is averted by doubts (shubhat), such as hudud and qisas. In such cases, lack of intention creates a doubt that prevents punishment, but the act itself remains valid.
3
Analyze the example of istilad (paternity claim) of an insane person
Istilad by physical act (intercourse with a slave-girl) is valid and establishes paternity, while an acknowledgment of istilad by statement is invalid, because his acknowledgment is deficient (naqis). This illustrates the distinction between act and speech.
4
Examine the case of owning a blood relative (mahram)
If a minor or insane person owns a close relative (e.g., parent or child), that relative becomes free automatically (by operation of law), because the act of ownership itself is valid from them. However, if they attempt to manumit by word, it is invalid.
5
Consider the rulings for pure benefit and agency
Pure benefit (naf' mahd) such as accepting a gift or charity is valid directly. Also, if a minor hires himself out and performs work, the wage is due by istihsan. Additionally, a minor's statement regarding another's property, divorce, or manumission is valid if he acts as a deputy (wakil) for a sane principal.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Hajr of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the legal capacity of minors (الصبي) and the insane (المجنون) in various matters. The author first affirms that their contracts (عقود), acknowledgments (إقرارات), and testimonies (مشاهدة) are invalid because they lack valid speech (قول) in the legal sense. However, actions (أفعال) are treated differently; they are generally valid, as they can produce effects from any person. An exception is made for acts whose rulings are averted by doubts, such as hudud and qisas, where lack of intention creates a doubt that prevents punishment. The author then gives specific examples: the istilad (paternity claim) of an insane person by action (actually having intercourse with a slave-girl) is valid, and if the slave-girl gives birth from him, the child is recognized. But if he acknowledges paternity by statement, it is invalid because his acknowledgment is defective. Similarly, if a minor or insane person owns a close blood relative (dhā raḥim maḥram), that relative becomes free automatically due to the ownership by marriage relationship? Actually the text says 'because ownership by marriage is valid from them'? In Hanafi law, if a person owns a close relative (e.g., father, mother, child), that relative is freed by operation of law. This ruling applies here because the act of ownership itself is valid from them. If they attempt to free a slave by verbal manumission (qawl), it is invalid because their words carry no legal effect. The commentary then adds that pure benefit (naf' mahḍ) is valid from them, such as accepting a gift or charity. Also, if a minor hires himself out and performs the work, the wage is due by istihsan, even though the contract is technically invalid because their words are null; the wage is justified by the benefit received. Likewise, a minor's word is valid regarding another's property, divorce, or manumission if he is acting as an agent (wakīl) for a sane principal. The passage then turns to divorce and manumission specifically: even if performed directly, they do not occur because the Prophet ﷺ said: 'Every divorce occurs except the divorce of a minor and the insane.' Manumission is considered pure harm (tamaḥḍuḍa ḍarar) because it is a relinquishment of a right, so it is invalid like a gift (hibah) or release (barā'ah). Unlike other contracts, these are not even suspended upon the guardian's permission because the minor lacks desire (shahwa) to assess benefit, and the guardian cannot assess compatibility due to possible future harmony. So they are absolutely void if done by the minor himself. However, if a sane man appoints a minor as his agent for divorce, the divorce is effective and the principal's wife is divorced, because the minor here is merely a conveyor of the principal's will.

Key Terms
الصبي (al-ṣabī): A minor who has not reached puberty and does not have legal capacity for verbal statements.
المجنون (al-majnūn): An insane person who lacks intellect and legal capacity for any statements.
النفع المحض (al-naf‘ al-maḥḍ): Pure benefit without any accompanying harm or liability; e.g., accepting a gift or charity.
استيلاد (istīlād): The act of a master impregnating his slave-girl, resulting in the child being free and the mother becoming an umm walad.
إسقاط حق (isqāṭ ḥaqq): Relinquishment of a right, such as divorce or manumission, considered a harmful action requiring legal capacity.

Divorce and Manumission by Minors and the Insane
Ruling on divorce and manumission: absolute nullity with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why divorce and manumission by minors and the insane are absolutely void, citing the hadith evidence and the reasoning of isqat haqq.

Core Idea
Divorce and manumission by minors and the insane are void because they are relinquishment of rights (isqat haqq) and are pure harm, and cannot even be suspended upon guardian permission.

The previous lesson established that minors and the insane lack capacity for verbal acts. Here we focus on two specific acts: divorce (talaq) and manumission (itq). The passage provides a hadith that explicitly excludes their divorce from the general validity of divorce. Additionally, these acts are classified as isqat haqq (relinquishment of rights), which is pure harm. Unlike other contracts, they cannot be made conditional on guardian permission because the minor cannot assess benefit due to lack of desire (shahwa), and the guardian cannot determine suitability due to possibility of future harmony. The lesson also covers the exception when a minor acts as a deputy for a sane principal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on divorce and manumission
The text states: 'ولا يقع طلاقهما ولا عناقهما' – their divorce does not occur, nor their manumission. This is an absolute nullity.
2
Analyze the hadith evidence
The Prophet ﷺ said: 'كل طلاق واقع إلا طلاق الصبي والمعتوه.' – Every divorce occurs except the divorce of a minor and the insane. This hadith is cited as the proof (dalil). Its thubut (transmission) is zanni (speculative) but its dalala (meaning) is qati' (certain) in excluding these two categories.
3
Understand the reasoning based on isqat haqq
Divorce and manumission are relinquishment of rights (isqat haqq). They involve pure harm (tamahhud madarrah) because the person loses rights (e.g., marriage bond, ownership). Hence they are invalid from those lacking capacity, similar to gift (hibah) and release (bara'ah).
4
Distinguish from other contracts regarding guardian permission
Unlike other contracts, divorce and manumission are not suspended upon guardian's permission. Reason: the minor lacks desire (shahwa) to assess benefit in divorce; the guardian cannot gauge marital compatibility due to possibility of future harmony. Therefore, these acts are void even with guardian approval.
5
Examine the exception of agency
If a sane man appoints a minor as his deputy (wakil) for divorce, the minor's statement is valid and the principal's wife is divorced. The minor's speech is effective because he acts as a conveyor of the principal's will.

The Invalidity of Divorce and Manumission by a Minor or Insane Person, and Related Exceptions
Explaining invalidity of talaq and 'itaq by minor/insane, exceptions for agency and tort.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on talaq and 'itaq by a minor or insane person, explain the proof (hadith and qiyas), identify exceptions (agency, khul', tort), and apply these rulings to case scenarios.

Core Idea
A minor or insane person cannot divorce or manumit, but can be liable for tort and act as agent.

This lesson examines the legal capacity of minors and the insane in Hanafi fiqh. While their divorce and manumission are invalid due to lack of capacity for isqat, certain acts like accepting khul' compensation or acting as an agent for another's divorce are valid. Additionally, liability for property destruction attaches regardless of capacity. The primary proof is a hadith and analogical reasoning (qiyas) to gift and quittance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Divorce and manumission by a minor or insane person are ruled as batil (invalid).
The act is not effective at all.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites a hadith: 'Every divorce is effective except the divorce of the minor and the insane.' Also, qiyas is made to hibah and bara'ah, which are invalid from such persons.
The hadith is weak but acted upon by the Companions.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit exception in the hadith excludes minor/insane from effectiveness. Qiyas: isqat haqq requires legal capacity; minor/insane lack it.
The illa is isqat haqq (relinquishment of a right).
4
Note the exceptions
Agency: a minor as wakil for another's divorce/manumission is valid because the act is attributed to the principal. Khul': a restricted slave can accept khul' compensation without master's permission as pure benefit. Tort: liability for destruction regardless of capacity.
These exceptions do not contradict the general rule.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response to cross-madhhab views
Some schools differentiate discerning minors; Hanafi holds blanket invalidity based on lack of sexual desire and understanding.
The thamarah al-khilaf: a discerning minor's talaq is valid in Shafi'i but invalid in Hanafi.

Core Idea
In a confession from X to Y, Abu Hanifa excludes the endpoint, the Sahibayn include it, Zufar excludes both—a linguistic dispute over 'ila'.

This lesson unpacks a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about interpreting range expressions in confessions. When someone says 'I owe him from one dirham to ten dirhams,' how many dirhams does he actually owe? The answer reveals deep differences in how the Hanafi masters understood the preposition 'ila' (to) and the scope of the term 'ghaya' (endpoint). Abu Hanifa relies on the usul principle that 'ghaya' (ila) excludes the stated endpoint. The Sahibayn consider customary usage in confessions as inclusive. Zufar takes the most literal reading.
No external dalil is cited; it's pure linguistic interpretation.

Later Hanafi fatwa works prefer the Sahibayn's view as more in line with common understanding.

Triple Divorce in Illness Then Acknowledgment of Debt: The Lesser Rule
Ruling on reduced entitlement due to suspicion of collusion

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Suspicion of collusion reduces a wife's claim to the lesser of debt and inheritance in deathbed transactions.

In Hanafi law, deathbed transactions are scrutinized to protect the interests of heirs. This lesson examines a specific case: a husband divorces his wife three times during his terminal illness and then acknowledges a debt to her. The ruling balances the validity of such actions against the risk of fraud. The key concept is tuhma (suspicion), which restricts the wife's entitlement to the lesser amount. However, the ruling changes if the divorce was without her consent.
