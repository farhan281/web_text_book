Prohibited Acts During Istibra' and Their Proof
States prohibitions during istibra' with reasoning from analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibited acts during istibra' and explain the Hanafi qiyas reasoning that extends the prohibition to incitements to intercourse.

Core Idea
When intercourse is forbidden, its incitements are also forbidden — a rule derived from qiyas on dhihar and 'iddah.

The period of istibra' is a wajib waiting period for the owner of a newly purchased slave woman to ensure she is not pregnant. During this time, not only is intercourse itself prohibited, but also acts that lead to it. This lesson unpacks the specific prohibitions and the logical reasoning (qiyas) that supports them, showing how Hanafi fiqh uses analogy to extend rulings consistently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage lists specific actions (kissing, touching with desire, looking at the private part with desire, embracing) and declares them prohibited (haram) during istibra'.
The ruling is clear: these are all haram.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author provides two analogical proofs: first, a direct qiyas on the wife of a man who has declared dhihar; second, a qiyas on the 'iddah period. Both are cases where intercourse is temporarily forbidden and incitements are also forbidden.
The proof is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the dalil proves the ruling)
The common illa (effective cause) is the temporary prohibition of intercourse. Since incitements lead to that which is forbidden, they themselves become forbidden. This is reinforced by the principle: 'When something is forbidden, its incitements are also forbidden.'
The 'illa is the temporary prohibition of intercourse.
4
Note the distinction from menstruation
The author clarifies that this ruling differs from menstruation, where intercourse is prohibited due to harm (adha). In menstruation, incitements like kissing are not prohibited because the harm is absent. This distinction reinforces that istibra' prohibition is based on the potential to lead to intercourse, not on harm.
This shows the precision of the 'illa.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi position
The Hanafi position is that all incitements to intercourse during istibra' are haram. This is based on clear qiyas and is considered authoritative within the school. Some other schools may not extend the prohibition to all incitements, but Hanafi mufta bihi is strict.
Cross-madhhab: Some schools limit prohibitions to explicit actions.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It states that during istibra' (the waiting period for a newly purchased slave woman to ensure she is not pregnant), the owner is prohibited from kissing, touching with desire, looking at her privates with desire, and embracing. The reason given is that these actions are incitements to intercourse, and if intercourse is forbidden, its incitements are also forbidden. This is supported by analogy: the wife of a man who has declared dhihar is forbidden in both intercourse and enjoyment. Similarly, the prohibition in istibra' is like the 'iddah, where incitements are also forbidden. Menstruation is distinguished because its prohibition is due to harm, which is not present in these incitements.

Key Terms

العدة (al-'iddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of husband before remarriage.

Partial Ownership and Istibra'
Ruling on istibra' with partial ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on istibra' when partial ownership becomes full ownership and explain why a menstruation during partial ownership does not satisfy the requirement.

Core Idea
Istibra' begins only after full ownership – a menstruation under partial ownership does not count.

This concise ruling addresses a specific scenario: what if a man owns only half a slave girl, she menstruates, and then he acquires the other half? The Hanafi school clarifies that the earlier menstruation is not sufficient for istibra'. Instead, he must wait for a new menstruation after full ownership, because istibra' is tied to complete ownership, not partial.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is istibra' (waiting for one menstrual cycle to ensure the womb is empty). The ruling: it is wajib (obligatory) before intercourse with a newly acquired slave girl.
Istibra' is required to avoid mixing lineages.
2
Understand the condition of ownership
Istibra' is triggered only by full ownership. Partial ownership does not create the full right to intercourse, so it does not initiate the waiting period.
Ownership here means complete, not fractional.
3
Apply the condition to the given scenario
The man first owned half (partial). The menstruation occurred under partial ownership, so it does not fulfill the requirement. After acquiring the other half, ownership becomes full, and a new menstruation is needed.
The previous menstruation is irrelevant for istibra'.
4
State the consequence
He must wait for a complete menstruation after full ownership before intercourse. If he does not wait, he sins and may face legal consequences.
This ensures the womb is clear of any previous owner's seed.

Enjoyment During Nifas After Istibra' by Birth
Ruling on istibra' completed by birth and subsequent enjoyment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on permissible forms of enjoyment during nifas after istibra' by birth, and distinguish it from the ruling during menstruation.

Core Idea
After birth, all enjoyment is halal except intercourse — nifas rules mirror hayd in this prohibition.

Istibra' is a waiting period to ensure the womb is empty. When it is completed by childbirth, the woman enters nifas (postnatal bleeding). During this state, a man may enjoy her in all ways except sexual intercourse, exactly as with menstruation. This lesson clarifies that exception and its basis in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage gives a ruling on istibra' completed by birth. The act is 'enjoyment' (istimta') during nifas. The ruling is that it is permissible except for intercourse.
Enjoyment includes kissing, touching, etc.
2
Note the prohibition
Intercourse is explicitly excluded (what siwa al-jima'). This prohibition lasts as long as she is in nifas.
Hanafi: nifas maximum 40 days.
3
Compare with menstruation
The passage states 'as we said regarding the menstruating woman.' The ruling for hayd is the same: all enjoyment except intercourse.
Quran 2:222 prohibits intercourse during menstruation.
4
Understand the legal principle
The istibra' purpose (ensuring no pregnancy) is fulfilled by birth. After that, the only restriction is the existing prohibition of intercourse during bleeding states.
This is a qiyas between nifas and hayd.

This is similar to the rule for a menstruating woman: during menstruation, intercourse is forbidden but other forms of enjoyment are allowed.

Istibra' for a Non-Menstruating Slave Girl: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on istibra' duration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and Zufar on the istibra' period for a non-menstruating slave girl, and evaluate the basis for each view.

Core Idea
The duration of istibra' for a non-menstruating slave girl differs among Hanafi imams based on estimates of when pregnancy signs appear.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: when a buyer acquires a slave girl who normally menstruates but does not menstruate after purchase, how long must he wait before intercourse? The early masters differ based on their assessment of when pregnancy would become evident. The majority view (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) sets a three-month standard, while Muhammad and Zufar propose shorter and longer periods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and legal question
What is the exact scenario? A slave girl of menstruating age does not menstruate after purchase. The question: how long must the owner wait before intercourse to ensure she is not pregnant?
The waiting is called istibra', to clear the womb.
2
State the majority position and its proof
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule: wait three months or more until signs of pregnancy would appear. Their proof: if she were pregnant, signs like belly swelling or milk come within three months; absence of signs indicates no pregnancy.
This is based on common medical observation (tajriba).
3
Identify the opposing views
Muhammad says two months and five days; Zufar says two years. Their proofs are not detailed in the text but imply different estimates of when pregnancy signs appear.
Muhammad's view is closer to the majority; Zufar's is a minority extreme.
4
Analyze the practical consequences
Under Abu Hanifa's view, he can have intercourse after three months; under Muhammad's, earlier; under Zufar's, much later. The difference affects the permissibility of intercourse during that period.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
5
Determine the relied-upon position
The mufta bihi position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (three months), as it is the majority of the early masters.
Later Hanafi works generally adopt this view.

Muhammad says two months and five days. Zufar says two years. The basis for the majority view is that pregnancy signs become clear after three months.

Istibra' Exempted: Divorce Before Consummation
Ruling on istibra' exemption due to divorce before consummation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which istibra' is not required for a purchased slave girl: divorce before consummation.

Core Idea
No istibra' needed if the marriage was not consummated before divorce.

This lesson addresses a specific case: a man buys a married slave girl, takes possession, and then her husband divorces her. The key question is whether the buyer must perform istibra' (wait for one menstrual cycle to clear the womb). The text provides a clear ruling: if the divorce happened before consummation, istibra' is not required. This is because there is no possibility of pregnancy from the husband, so the womb is presumed empty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and transaction
A buyer purchases a slave girl who is already married. He takes possession (qabd) of her.
The slave girl is owned by the seller, and her husband is a separate person.
2
Note the divorce timing
The husband divorces the slave girl before he has consummated the marriage (i.e., before any sexual intercourse).
Consummation is the key event that could lead to pregnancy.
3
Apply the ruling
The text states that in this scenario, the buyer does not have to perform istibra'.
The Arabic: فلا استبراء عليه
4
Understand the rationale
Istibra' is only required when there is a possibility of pregnancy from a previous man. Since the marriage was not consummated, the wife could not be pregnant from her husband, so the womb is clear.
This is based on the principle that istibra' clears the womb from any potential pregnancy.
5
Contrast with other cases
If the marriage was consummated before divorce, or if the slave girl had intercourse with a previous owner, istibra' would be wajib.
This ruling is an exception, not the general rule.

Dispute on using a stratagem to avoid istibra'

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A stratagem to drop istibra' is allowed only when the seller is known to have not approached her in that purity.

This passage addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad regarding using a legal stratagem to eliminate the requirement of istibra'. While Abu Yusuf sees no harm, Muhammad considers it disliked. The text then establishes a nuanced adopted position that depends on the seller's actions.

Identify the act and its legal context
Recognize that the act in question is using a legal stratagem (hila) to avoid the obligation of istibra' when purchasing a slave girl.

2
State the two positions within the Hanafi school
Abu Yusuf says there is no harm in the hila; Muhammad says it is disliked (makruh).

Locate the mufta bihi (adopted ruling)
The text specifies that Abu Yusuf's view is taken when it is known the seller did not have intercourse during that purity period; Muhammad's view is taken when the seller did have intercourse.
The adopted view is conditional on the seller's actions.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a case, determine which imam's view applies based on the available knowledge of the seller's intercourse.
Certainty is required for following Abu Yusuf's view.
