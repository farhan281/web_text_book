Wage Due on Completion for Tailor and Washer
Ruling that a tailor or washer cannot demand wage until work is completed, with an exception for work at own place.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rule that a tailor or washer cannot demand payment until work is completed, and identify the exception when the artisan works from his own premises.

Core Idea
Wage is due on completion unless work is in artisan's own place—then proportional pay.

This short passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah establishes a fundamental rule in Hanafi ijarah: a hired worker whose benefit is indivisible cannot demand wages until the entire task is finished. The commentary adds a crucial exception based on the location of work, reflecting the school's practical approach to divisible benefits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Read the matn: 'It is not for the washer and the tailor to demand the wage until they finish the work.' Note who is bound (washer and tailor) and what is prohibited (demanding wage before completion).
The rule treats the work as a single, indivisible benefit.
2
Understand the rationale
The wage is compensation for the complete benefit. Until the work is finished, the benefit has not been fully delivered. Therefore, no wage is due.
This prevents disputes over partial performance.
3
Locate the exception
From the commentary in al-Mustasfa: if the tailor works in his own house (shop), he is entitled to wage in proportion to what he has sewn. The location changes the nature of delivery from a single to multiple partial deliveries.
The exception applies only when the artisan operates from his own premises, not the customer's.
4
Apply the distinction
Consider a washer working at his own laundry vs. at the customer's home. In the first case, each load washed is a deliverable benefit; in the second, the benefit is integrated until all clothes are returned clean.
Divisibility depends on the control and location of the work.
5
Extend to a new scenario
A carpenter is hired to build a table. He works in the customer’s garage. After completing the tabletop, can he demand partial payment? According to the general rule, no, because the work is still at the customer's premises.
Only if he works from his own shop would the exception apply.

Rent Due Daily for House Hire Unless Specified
Ruling on rent payment timing for house hire

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that rent for a house hire is due daily, identify the exception for contractual stipulation, and distinguish the position of Zufar.

Core Idea
Rent is due daily because benefit is received gradually, unless the contract sets a specific time.

This lesson addresses a fundamental question in ijara (hire) contracts: when does the rent become due? The Hanafi school holds a default position that the lessor can demand rent each day as the benefit is realized. However, the parties can agree on a different schedule. An early Hanafi jurist, Zufar, disagreed, holding that rent is a single obligation due at the end of the term. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the nature of rent as compensation for gradually received benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is hiring a house (istījār dār). The default ruling is that the lessor may demand his rent every day.
This is the general Hanafi position.
2
Note the exception
The default applies unless the contract specifies a time for payment. If a time is stated, that stipulation is followed.
Parties can contract out of the default.
3
Recognize the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Zufar, a Hanafi jurist, held that rent is not due until after the entire lease term ends.
This is a difference within the school.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The Hanafi ruling is based on the concept that manfa'ah (benefit) is realized gradually, so the consideration (ujrah) can be demanded gradually.
This is a rational proof (qiyas or istihsan).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that rent is due daily unless the contract provides otherwise.
This is the position of the majority of Hanafi scholars.

Core Idea
Each stage of travel is an independent benefit, so hire can be demanded per stage.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah clarifies a key point in hire contracts (ijarah): when does the hirer's obligation to pay arise? The text gives the Hanafi ruling with its proof and notes earlier different views within the school.

Guided Path

The act is hiring a camel for a journey to Mecca. The ruling is that the camel driver may demand his hire after each stage (marhalah).
2

The proof is given as 'because the travel of each stage is an intended benefit' (li-anna sayra kulli marhalatin maqsud).
This is a talil (causal reasoning) based on the nature of the benefit.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Each stage of travel is a distinct benefit that the hirer receives incrementally. Since ijarah is an exchange of benefit for payment, payment becomes due as each unit of benefit is delivered.
Thus, per-stage payment is logical.

Abu Hanifa initially held that the entire hire is due only after the journey ends, agreeing with Zufar. Abu Yusuf said it is due upon reaching one-third or one-half of the route.
These are earlier views, not the final relied-upon position.

The matn ruling (per-stage payment) is the mufta bihi, as it is the position of Zahir al-Riwayah and the later school.

Core Idea
An artisan's wage is due only upon full completion; after completion, Abu Hanifa considers the wage due even if goods are destroyed, while the Sahibayn require physical delivery.

This lesson examines a classical Hanafi ruling on the timing of wage entitlement for artisans such as tailors and launderers. The general principle is that wages become due only after the work is fully complete, unless the parties stipulated advance payment. The passage also explores an intra-madhhab dispute: once the work is finished, does the artisan bear liability for the goods until delivery? Understanding this distinction clarifies the relationship between completion, delivery, and risk in hiring contracts.

1
Identify the general ruling and its exception
The text states that tailors and launderers cannot demand wages until they finish the work, unless they stipulate advance payment. This establishes the baseline rule: completion is required.

2
Understand the rationale
The reason is that the benefit (manfa'ah) of the work is only realized upon full completion; partial work provides no independent benefit. Therefore, partial payment is not warranted.
This is based on the nature of the service contract (ijara).

This dispute focuses on the definition of delivery (taslim) in ijara.

The general ruling (no wage before completion) is relied upon. For the liability dispute, later fatwa often prefers the Sahibayn's position for caution.
This is based on tarjih in later Hanafi texts.

Wage only due after removal; if burnt, wage only if compensated with baked value.

The Hanafi school defines the moment when a hired worker's wage becomes due by the completion of the work. The Condition of Stipulating Prompt Payment and the Tailor's Example: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Delivery of Work
Commentary on the condition of stipulating prompt payment with a tailor example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when wages are due in a hire contract when prompt payment is stipulated, and distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on constructive versus physical delivery.

Core Idea
Wages are due after work completion, but a stipulation of prompt payment overrides the default; however, if the object perishes before delivery, Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on whether wages are owed.

This passage from Al-Jawharat al-Nayirah expands on a ruling from Mukhtasar al-Quduri regarding the timing of wage obligation in hire contracts. It first states that unless prompt payment is stipulated, wages are not due until completion. Then it introduces an example from Al-Karkhi involving a tailor who sews a cloth in the owner's house. The passage highlights the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, on whether the completion of work in the owner's house constitutes constructive delivery, making wages due even if the cloth perishes before physical handover.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Rule and the Exception
Understand that the default Hanafi rule is that wages are not due until the work is fully completed. However, if prompt payment is stipulated at the time of contract, the condition is binding and wages become due immediately upon completion of the work, overriding the default.
The condition is valid because it does not contradict the nature of the contract; it merely accelerates the timing of payment.
2
Examine the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
When work is performed in the owner's house (or on the owner's premises), Abu Hanifa holds that the work is considered 'delivered' (musallaman li al-'amal) upon completion, even before the owner physically takes possession. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the object remains under the craftsman's guarantee until actual physical delivery.
This dispute hinges on the nature of the hire: is the craftsman an ajir khas (specific hire) who guarantees the object until delivery?
3
Analyze the Consequence If the Object Perishes
If the cloth perishes after sewing but before handover, under Abu Hanifa, wages are automatically due because the work is considered delivered. Under the Sahibayn, the owner has a choice: claim the value of the unsewn cloth (no wages) or the value of the sewn cloth (wages included).
The difference reflects the concept of daman (liability) – the Sahibayn see the craftsman as still liable until physical delivery.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence is that in the Sahibayn's view, the owner has more flexibility: if the cloth is lost, he can either recover its unsewn value without paying wages, or recover the sewn value (including wages) at his option. Under Abu Hanifa, wages are fixed regardless.
Most later Hanafi muftis follow the Sahibayn due to the clarity of the guarantee rule.
5
Apply to Similar Scenarios
The same principle applies to any craftsman who works on the owner's premises: a carpenter making furniture, a mechanic repairing a car, or a dressmaker sewing in the owner's home. The dispute continues to be relevant in contemporary fatwa.
The key is whether the work is done on the owner's property, giving the craftsman some measure of control.

Leasing an Undivided Share (Mushāʿ): The Partner Exception
Ruling on hiring an undivided share with exception for partners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on leasing an undivided share (mushāʿ) and identify the exception when the lessor is a partner, applying the condition of deliverability.

Core Idea
Leasing an undivided share is invalid because delivery is impossible—except from a partner.

Leasing property that you cannot fully deliver is problematic. In the Hanafi school, an undivided share (mushāʿ) in joint property normally cannot be leased because the lessor cannot hand over exclusive possession. However, the text presents a key exception: if the lessor is a partner (co-owner), the lease is valid. This lesson breaks down the reasoning and scope of this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Ruling
Read the matn: 'ولا تجوز إجارة المشاع عند أبي حنيفة' – Leasing an undivided share is not permissible. Recognize this as the default position (fasid contract).
2
Spot the Exception
Look for 'إلا من الشريك' – except from a partner. This is the sole exception. Note that the exception applies whether the property is divisible (مما يُقسم) or indivisible (مما لا يُقسم).
3
Understand the Legal Cause (ʿIllah)
The text gives the reason: 'لأنه أجر ما لا يقدر على تسليمه' – because he has leased something he cannot deliver. The inability to deliver makes the contract fasid.
The 'illah is lack of qabd (possession) for the share.
4
Distinguish the Partner Exception
A partner can lease his specific share because his ownership is defined, and delivery is possible through the partnership arrangement (e.g., co-owner can allow use of the common property).
This is based on the principle that a partner can grant usufruct of his share.

Commentary

This applies whether the property is divisible (e.g., land) or indivisible (e.g., a small room). The text includes the explanation from the sharh: the contract is invalid because it involves something the lessor cannot deliver.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hiring for qisas on life is invalid per the Sahibayn; for limb it is valid by consensus—the difference is the ability to perform the act.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses the permissibility of hiring someone to execute qisas (retaliation). It distinguishes between cases involving loss of life (qisas fi al-nafs) and those involving only a limb (qisas duna al-nafs), revealing an intra-Hanafi disagreement within the transmissions of Imam Muhammad. Understanding this ruling requires analyzing the 'illa (effective cause) of human ability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage discusses two cases: hiring for qisas on life (qisas fi al-nafs) and hiring for qisas on a limb (qisas duna al-nafs). Separate the rulings for each.
2
Note the positions
For life: the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold it impermissible; a narration from Muhammad permits it. For limb: all agree it is permissible (consensus).
The term 'عندهما' refers to the joint opinion of the two companions.
3
Analyze the 'illa (legal cause)
The text states the 'illa: for limb, the purpose is severing the member (ibānah al-ʿuḍw), which is within human ability. For life, the purpose is ending the soul (ifātah al-rūh), which is beyond human ability—it is ultimately God's act.
This 'illa directly impacts the validity of the contract.
4
Apply the ruling
Based on the 'illa, hiring for qisas on a limb is analogous to hiring for slaughter (dhabh), which is permissible. For life, the contract is invalid (bāṭil) because the essential purpose cannot be achieved by the hired party.
The muftā bihī is the impermissibility for life (Sahibayn's view).

استئجار القاضي رجلاً لضرب الحدود
Ruling on hiring for executing hudūd on a monthly basis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a valid monthly hire contract and an invalid per-act contract for executing hudud, and explain the role of certainty (ma'lum) in the validity of ijara.

Core Idea
Certainty of duration validates a hire contract; uncertainty of work quantity invalidates it.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a ruling by Imam Abū Yūsuf on hiring an executioner for ḥudūd punishments. The key distinction is between a monthly salary contract and a per-strike contract. Understanding this distinction requires applying the general principles of ijārah (hire) to a sensitive public office context. The ruling illustrates that even in penal matters, the law requires contractual certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contract types
Read the passage and note the two types of contracts: monthly (mushāharah) and per-act (ḍarb). The monthly contract is for a fixed period; the per-act contract is for each execution.
2
Determine the ruling for each
According to Abū Yūsuf, the monthly contract is valid (lā ba’s, no harm) and the per-act contract is invalid (fāsid).
3
Analyse the reasoning
The monthly contract is based on a known duration (the month), so the compensation is certain. The per-act contract is invalid because the number of strikes is unknown (majhūl).
The key term is 'jahālah' (uncertainty).
4
Extract the general principle
For an ijārah contract to be valid, the subject matter or duration must be known with certainty. This principle overrides the public necessity of executing ḥudūd.

Commentary

And singing and wailing. The Book of Hiring. And they differed regarding hiring for recitation of the Quran over the grave for a known period: Some said: His statement: (It is not permissible to hire for singing and wailing) is not permissible, and some said: it is permissible, and it is the chosen opinion. If he hires him for the execution (of penalties), then that is unknown, so it is not permissible.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses rulings on hiring for specific acts. It begins by noting the prohibition of hiring for singing and wailing because these are sinful acts (ma'siya). The author then presents a dispute about hiring for recitation of the Quran at a grave for a fixed period; some scholars disallow it (considering it potentially akin to singing), while others allow it, and the latter is chosen. All musical instruments are similarly forbidden. Next, the discussion turns to hiring for retaliation (qisas). According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, hiring for qisas in life is invalid because the hired person cannot guarantee the taking of life—it is beyond his capacity. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, however, allows it. There is consensus that hiring for qisas in less than life is valid, as the hired person can amputate the limb. By analogy (qiyas), hiring for slaughter (dhabh) is also valid, as the intent is to cut the veins, not take life, and that is within the person's ability. Finally, Abu Yusuf gives a ruling on hiring an executioner for fixed penalties (hudud): if hired on a monthly basis, the contract is valid because the period is specified; if hired per execution, it is invalid due to uncertainty (jahalah).

Key Terms
القصاص في النفس (al-qisas fi al-nafs): Retaliation involving death, i.e., capital punishment for murder.
القصاص فيما دون النفس (al-qisas fi-ma dun al-nafs): Retaliation involving bodily harm less than death, such as amputation for injury.
القدرة (al-qudrah): Ability or capacity; in fiqh, the hired person must be able to perform the contracted service.

Hiring for Slaughter (Dhabh): Analogy with Qisas Below Life
Ruling on hiring for dhabh with qiyas to qisas below life

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the permissibility of hiring for dhabh by analogizing it to hiring for qisas below life.

Core Idea
Hiring for dhabh is permissible because the intended action (cutting veins) is within human ability, similar to limb removal in qisas.

This passage extends the reasoning from qisas below life to the case of ritual slaughter (dhabh). By using analogy (qiyas), the author shows that as long as the hired person can perform the required physical act, the contract is valid. Understanding this qiyas strengthens our grasp of the principle of capacity in Hanafi contract law.

Guided Path
1
Recall the ruling for qisas below life
Hiring for qisas below life is valid by consensus because the goal (removing a limb) is achievable by the hired person.
This serves as the original case (asl) in the qiyas.
2
Identify the new case: dhabh
The text states: 'It is permissible to hire for slaughter (dhabh).' The reason given is that the intended outcome is cutting the jugular veins, not taking life.
Dhabh is the ritual slaughter of an animal by cutting the throat.
3
Analyze the analogy
The text explicitly compares: 'it resembles (ashbaha) qisas below life.' The common factor (illa) is that in both cases, the main act (cutting veins or removing a limb) is within human ability, while the ultimate result (death) is not the direct subject of the contract.
The illa is the ability to perform the intended action, not the external outcome.
4
Understand the distinction from qisas in life
In qisas in life, the intended outcome is death itself, which is beyond human ability. In dhabh, the intended outcome is the cut, not death.
This distinction is crucial: the contract's subject matter is the act of cutting, not the consequent death.

Hiring an Executioner for Hudud: Condition of Known Period
Ruling on hiring for hudud with distinction based on contract type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that hiring for hudud is valid only if based on a known period (e.g., monthly), not per act.

Core Idea
Hiring for hudud is valid when the contract is for a known period; hiring per execution is invalid due to uncertainty (jahalah).

This passage from Abu Yusuf addresses a practical scenario: hiring a person to carry out fixed penalties (hudud) under a judge. The key distinction is whether the hire is based on a fixed period (like monthly) or per execution. Understanding the underlying principle of jahalah (uncertainty) in contracts is essential for applying this ruling correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracting methods
The text contrasts two methods: hiring on a monthly basis (mushaharah) and hiring for each execution (on the act of striking).
Mushaharah means a fixed monthly payment for a specified period.
2
State the ruling for each
Monthly hiring is valid (la ba'sa = no harm). Hiring per execution is invalid (fahima = invalid).
The text says: 'la ba'sa an yasta'jira ... mushaharah' and 'fa-in kana ghayr mushaharah fa-al-ijarah fasidah'.
3
Analyze the reasoning
For monthly hiring: the contract is based on the period (muddah), which is known. Whether he works or not, the period is fixed. For per execution hiring: the number of executions is unknown (majhul), leading to uncertainty (jahalah) that invalidates the contract.
Certainty of the subject matter is a condition for validity of ijara.
4
Apply the general principle
This ruling exemplifies the principle that ijara requires the subject (either the work or the period) to be known. Here, knowing the period suffices even if the work quantity is unknown.
In Hanafi law, it is permissible to hire for an unknown amount of work if the time is fixed.
