Divorce Thrice Then Debt Acknowledgment in Terminal Illness
Ruling on divorce thrice then debt acknowledgment in terminal illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule when a terminally ill man divorces his wife thrice and acknowledges a debt, and calculate the wife's entitlement in a given scenario.

Core Idea
To prevent collusion, the wife receives the lesser of the acknowledged debt and her inheritance share.

A dying man's last actions can be motivated by a desire to manipulate inheritance. Hanafi fiqh protects against collusion between the divorcing couple by limiting the wife's recovery when a debt is acknowledged after an irrevocable divorce. This lesson examines the ruling when a man divorces his wife three times during his terminal illness and then acknowledges a debt to her. Understanding this rule safeguards the rights of other heirs and prevents fraud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
Recognize that the scenario involves an irrevocable divorce (talaq thalath) occurring during marad al-mawt (terminal illness), followed by a debt acknowledgment.
Marad al-mawt is a condition that triggers special inheritance rules.
2
Recognize the potential for collusion
Understand that the wife and husband could conspire to increase her share beyond inheritance by fabricating a debt.
Collusion would defraud other heirs, so the law imposes a safeguard.
3
Apply the rule: she gets the lesser amount
The wife takes the lesser of two amounts: the debt she claims or her inheritance share from the estate.
If debt = $10,000 and inheritance = $6,000, she takes $6,000.
4
Note the exception for non-requested divorce
If the divorce occurred without her request (she did not instigate), the acknowledgment is void, and she receives her full inheritance.
This exception prevents the husband from prejudicing her rights.
5
Practice calculating a scenario
Given specific numbers, compute the wife's entitlement following the rule.
Always check if she requested the divorce first.

If she divorces without her request, she gets full inheritance and the acknowledgment is void.

Acknowledgment to a Stranger Then Claiming Paternity
Ruling on when acknowledgment is invalidated by subsequent paternity claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a sick person's acknowledgment to an heir is void to cases where paternity is claimed after an acknowledgment.

Core Idea
Paternity claim transforms a stranger into an heir, voiding a sick person's prior acknowledgment.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi inheritance law: a person in terminal illness acknowledges a right to a stranger, then later claims that stranger is his son. The ruling might seem contradictory—the paternity is accepted, yet the acknowledgment is void. The key is understanding that once paternity is established, the recipient becomes an heir, and the Hanafi rule prohibits a sick person from making enforceable acknowledgments to heirs, preventing fraud against other heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial act
Note that the person is in marad al-mawt (terminal illness) and makes an acknowledgment (iqrar) to a stranger—someone who is not an heir at that time.
The acknowledgment could be a debt or a gift.
2
Recognize the subsequent claim
Later, the same person claims that the recipient is his son. This is a claim of paternity (nasab), which, if accepted, establishes lineage.
In Hanafi law, a paternity claim is generally accepted if plausible and not contradicted.
3
Apply the effect of paternity
Once paternity is established, the recipient becomes a legal heir (son). This status retroactively applies from the moment of the claim? Actually, the ruling treats the situation as if the recipient is now an heir.
The key is that at the time of the acknowledgment, the recipient was a stranger, but after the claim, he becomes an heir.
4
Invoke the general rule
In Hanafi fiqh, an acknowledgment made by a person in terminal illness to an heir is invalid (batil). This is to prevent the sick person from favoring an heir beyond the bounds of inheritance shares.
The rule is based on the principle that a sick person's transactions are subject to suspicion of defrauding other heirs.
5
Derive the final ruling
Therefore, the paternity is valid (sahih) and the acknowledgment is void (batil). The son inherits according to his share, but the acknowledgment has no effect.
The practical consequence: the debt or gift promised in the acknowledgment is not enforceable against the estate.

Marriage Does Not Invalidate Prior Acknowledgment
Ruling that marriage does not invalidate prior acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of an acknowledgment made before marriage and explain the legal distinction between paternity claims and marriage.

Core Idea
Marriage is a future contract; it does not retroactively invalidate a prior acknowledgment.

In the previous passages, we saw that acknowledging a paternal relationship with a son during terminal illness is invalid because the son is already an heir. But what about acknowledging a debt or gift to a woman and then marrying her? The rule is different because marriage, unlike paternity, is not retroactive. Let's examine why the timing of the contract matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is making an acknowledgment (iqrar) to a woman who is a stranger, then later marrying her. The ruling is that the acknowledgment remains valid; it is not voided by the subsequent marriage.
This is in contrast to the paternity case where the acknowledgment is void.
2
Locate the Proof
The proof is the distinction (farq) between paternity claims and marriage. Paternity claims are retroactive to the time of conception, so acknowledging a son means he was always an heir. Marriage, however, takes effect only from the time of the contract.
The commentator explicitly states: 'the claim of paternity goes back to the time of conception... marriage is confined to the time of marriage.'
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The legal cause (illah) is the temporal scope of the contract. At the time of acknowledgment, the woman was a non-heir (ajnabiyyah). The marriage later does not change her status retroactively, so the acknowledgment to a non-heir remains valid.
If the marriage were retroactive, the acknowledgment would be to an heir and thus void during terminal illness.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
Other schools may argue differently. The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih, meaning there is disagreement. However, the Hanafi position is clear based on this temporal analysis.
The ikhtilaf centers on whether marriage makes a woman an heir from the time of the contract only or retroactively.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih
The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi (operative ruling). It is based on the clear principle that contracts have prospective effect unless proven otherwise.
This ruling is found in Zahir al-Riwayah and is the standard relied upon for fatwa.

Conditions for Acknowledgment of Paternity
Ruling on conditions for paternity acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a valid acknowledgment of paternity in Hanafi fiqh and apply them to a given scenario to determine whether lineage is established.

Core Idea
Paternity by acknowledgment requires plausible age gap, no known lineage, and child’s confirmation if adult.

Acknowledgment of paternity is a powerful legal tool in Hanafi fiqh, but it is not unconditional. The law requires that the claim be plausible and that the child has no existing father. This lesson unpacks the three key conditions derived from the classical text and explains the rational proof behind each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a person (man or woman) acknowledging a boy as his/her son. The ruling: lineage (nasab) is established, and the child inherits from the acknowledger, even if the latter was sick at the time of acknowledgment.
2
List the three conditions for validity
Condition 1: The child must be capable of being born to the acknowledger (age possibility: woman at least 9 years older, man at least 12.5 years older). Condition 2: The child must have no known lineage to another father. Condition 3: If the child is adult and sane, he must confirm the acknowledgment; if a minor, confirmation is not needed.
These conditions ensure the acknowledgment is not contradictory in appearance or reality.
3
Understand the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The text argues: because the acknowledgment is a self-imposed commitment (iltizam), it is binding on the acknowledger and does not affect others. Hence, it is accepted unless there is a clear legal impediment (e.g., known lineage of the child).
This is a rational proof, not based on a specific Quranic verse or hadith.
4
Note special cases and extensions
Confirmation after the death of the acknowledger is valid. If the acknowledger is a non-Muslim woman (dhimmi) who does not inherit from Muslims, the acknowledgment is valid from all wealth, and any will (wasiyyah) is limited to one-third.
The text also includes a half-line remnant about age which is already covered.
5
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Consider: A 60-year-old man acknowledges a 30-year-old man as his son. The 30-year-old has no known father and confirms. The age difference (30 years) satisfies the condition. Therefore, lineage is established and the son inherits.
If the 30-year-old had a known father, the acknowledgment would be rejected.

A master's claim of paternity over a slave is conditional, but freedom is always granted when claimed.

This passage from a Hanafi text outlines the nuanced rules when a master claims a slave is his son. The ruling depends on the slave's ability to speak and whether he has an existing lineage. Master's Claim of Fatherhood over a Slave: Need for Confirmation
Ruling on master claiming a slave as his father, requiring slave's confirmation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding a master's claim of fatherhood over a slave, explain the requirement of the slave's confirmation, and distinguish it from a claim of sonship.

Core Idea
Claiming another as your father imposes lineage on him, requiring his consent; claiming him as your son asserts your own right, needing no confirmation.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a key distinction in Hanafi law of acknowledgments (iqrar). When a master claims that his slave is his father, the slave's confirmation is necessary for the lineage to be established. In contrast, if the master claims the slave is his son, no such confirmation is needed. The reasoning lies in the nature of the claim: one imposes lineage on another, while the other asserts possession over what is already under control. This lesson will walk through the ruling, the rationale, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The passage presents two distinct claims: (a) master claims slave is his son, (b) master claims slave is his father. Note the difference in outcomes.
The son-claim is about claiming someone under his possession; the father-claim imposes lineage on another.
2
State the Ruling for Each Scenario
For the son-claim: no confirmation needed. For the father-claim: confirmation required for lineage. In both cases, the slave is freed.
Freedom follows from the implication that acknowledging a close relation implies the slave is free-born.
3
Understand the Rationale (Talil)
The author explains: in the son-claim, the master claims something in his possession for himself with no opponent, so he is believed. In the father-claim, he imposes lineage on another (tahmil al-nasab), so the other's confirmation is needed.
Tahmil al-nasab is the key concept: imposing lineage on a third party.
4
Note the Conditions Specified
The passage mentions that the master must have no known lineage and the slave must be of an age to plausibly be his father. These are prerequisites for the scenario to apply.
If the master had known lineage, his claim would contradict it; if the slave is too young, the claim is improbable.
5
Apply to a Hypothetical Variation
Consider a master claiming a slave is his brother. This is an imposition of lineage on another (like fatherhood), so the same rule applies: confirmation required.
Contrast with claiming the slave is his son, which is treated as a self-regarding claim.

Ujrat al-Mithl in Voidable Lease: The Cap and the Dispute
States rulings and an intra-Hanafi dispute on ujrat al-mithl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ujrat al-mithl in a voidable lease and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa, the Sahibayn, and Zufar regarding the cap at the named rent.

Core Idea
In a voidable lease, the lessee pays the fair rent but never more than the agreed amount — the named rent acts as a ceiling.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah addresses a common question: what happens when a lease contract is voidable? The Hanafi school is known for its nuanced distinction between batil and fasid. Here, the author explains that despite the defect, the lessee must pay the fair market rental value (ujrat al-mithl), but with an important limit: it cannot exceed the rent that was named in the contract. This ruling is not without internal disagreement, as we will see.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling

2
Recognize the cap condition
The cap applies only when the named rent is known. If it is unknown (e.g., leasing an unspecified animal or condition), then ujrat al-mithl is due without limit, by consensus.
The text also mentions hiring a worker without named wage.

Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa held the same ruling by qiyas, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) by istihsan, and Zufar disagreed entirely, holding ujrat al-mithl uncapped. The fatwa is on Muhammad's view.
The dispute is about the justification (qiyas vs istihsan) and the cap itself for Zufar.

In a valid lease, rent is due upon delivery (takhlia) regardless of use; in a void lease, rent is due only upon actual benefit (haqiqat al-intifa'). This affects whether rent is owed if the lessee does not use the property.

The relied-upon position is that ujrat al-mithl is capped at the named rent when known. This is the view of the majority (Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn), with fatwa on Muhammad's saying.

Stability and Ownership of the Right of Pre-emption (Shuf'a)

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The right of pre-emption requires immediate demand and formal witnessing to stabilize; ownership only occurs by taking or its equivalent.

The right of pre-emption is not automatically owned upon the sale of shared property. It must be actively pursued through an immediate demand (talab al-muwathaba) and a second formal demand (ishhad) to stabilize it. The passage cites that shuf'a is established by the sale itself, even before the buyer's ownership is confirmed. This shows the right arises from the contract.
The seller's admission suffices even if the buyer denies.

Ownership is not transferred until one of three events: voluntary surrender by the buyer, a judge's ruling, or actual taking. The matn's expression 'owned by taking' is interpreted to include equivalents.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of al-Quduri regarding the right of pre-emption (shuf'a). It addresses two main points: the stability of the right and its ownership. 

First, the right of pre-emption becomes stable (mustaqirr) only upon making a second formal demand (ishhad), also called talab al-taqrir (demand for confirmation). Without this, the right remains weak and can be lost by mere silence. If the pre-emptor waives it after formal demand, it is void because the waiver encounters an established right. The first demand (talab al-muwathaba) is immediate and mandatory; it is described as a 'weak right' that is lost by negligence. The second demand (ishhad) secures it against future voidance by silence. However, the right can still be lost by explicit verbal waiver or by failure to pay the price within a reasonable time, whereupon the judge may annul it.

Second, ownership of the pre-empted property is obtained only by actual taking possession (akhdh) or its equivalent, such as a judge's ruling or the buyer's voluntary surrender. The author notes that Imam Khwahir Zadeh held that a judge's ruling alone is sufficient to transfer ownership even without physical taking. Hence, the phrase 'owned by taking' is interpreted to include constructive taking. The commentary then illustrates the practical consequence: if the pre-emptor dies after the two demands but before a judge's ruling or voluntary surrender, the right is not inherited because ownership was not yet established. Similarly, selling the pre-emptable property before the judge's ruling voids the right. The text also clarifies that the buyer's ownership is complete; the buyer can dispose of the property (sell, demolish, rent) without restriction until a judge rules in favour of the pre-emptor. If the buyer sells to another, the pre-emptor has the choice to take the property at either the first or second price, depending on which benefit he gains. If the property bears fruit, the pre-emptor must take it at the full price regardless of fruit consumption, since ownership had not yet transferred to him.

The overall ruling is straightforward: the pre-emption right is not owned at the moment of sale; it is merely a potential right that must be activated through proper demands and finalised by taking possession or its legal equivalent.

الإشهاد (al-ishhad): The second formal demand (talab al-taqrir) made in the presence of witnesses to secure the right of pre-emption against voidance by silence.
طلب المواثبة (talab al-muwathaba): The immediate first demand made at the time of learning of the sale; it is necessary to avoid loss of right by negligence.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah demand is immediate; the absent shafi' must act at once or lose the right.

This passage from Al-Karkhi presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether demand for shuf'ah must be immediate (fawr) or can be delayed within the sitting (majlis). Guided Path

If the shafi' is absent from the town when he learns of the sale, he must still take immediate action: either make witnesses (ishhad) or appoint an agent to claim the shuf'ah. If he neglects to do so while able, his right is lost.

Timing of Demand for Pre-emption: Fawr vs. Majlis
Intra-Hanafi dispute on timing of pre-emption demand and conditions of notification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the immediate (fawr) and session (majlis) requirements for pre-emption demand, and explain the practical consequences of each position according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Pre-emption demand must be made immediately upon knowledge, or it lapses – a majority Hanafi rule based on a prophetic analogy.

The claim of pre-emption (shuf‘a) is a special right that must be asserted promptly. This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the timing of the demand: must it be immediate (fawr) or can it be delayed within the same session (majlis)? The dispute has significant practical implications for when the right is lost, as we will explore through the textual evidence and subsequent rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
First, recognize that the debate is over the timing required for the valid demand of pre-emption. The key question: how soon must the pre-emptor act after learning of the sale?
The term 'fawr' means immediate/immediacy; 'majlis' means the session or gathering where the knowledge was received.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the demand must be made immediately (fawr). Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that it may be made within the same session (majlis), as long as the pre-emptor does not stand up or engage in unrelated matters.
The session view is analogized to khiyar al-qabul (option of acceptance) and khiyar al-mukhayyara (option for a bride given choice).
3
Analyze the proofs
The majority (the 'two') cite the hadith: الشفعة كنَشطة عقال ('Pre-emption is like loosening a hobble'), which implies swift action. Muhammad relies on qiyas (analogy) to contractual options that persist throughout the session.
The hadith is ahad and zanni, but the Hanafi school accepts it as evidence. The response is that the text overrides qiyas.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under fawr, any delay – even asking a question or reading a letter to the end – voids the right. Under majlis, the right remains until the pre-emptor leaves the session or occupies himself with other matters.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is the immediate demand, as per the majority of Hanafi scholars.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Pre-emption (Shuf'a) addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: the timing of the demand (talab) and the conditions under which a pre-emptor must act. The passage begins by mentioning the ruling from al-Karkhi: according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the demand must be made immediately (fawr) upon learning of the sale. Imam Muhammad, however, holds that the demand can be made within the same session (majlis), analogous to the option of acceptance (khiyar al-qabul) and the choice given to a mukhayyara (a bride given option of divorce). The two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) support their view with the hadith: 'Pre-emption is like loosening a hobble' – meaning it must be done swiftly, as a hobble is quickly untied to release the animal. The passage then explains the practical consequences: if the demand is on the session, the pre-emptor retains his right as long as he does not stand up or get occupied with other matters. Abu Bakr al-Razi adds that if no witnesses are available, the pre-emptor should verbally declare his claim to preserve it before God. For an absent pre-emptor, if he takes witnesses immediately or appoints an agent, his pre-emption stands; otherwise, it lapses. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that selling the property that qualifies one for pre-emption before judgment voids the right, and identify the exception for sales with option.

Core Idea
Selling the qualifying property before judgment destroys the right, unless the sale includes an option.

The right of pre-emption arises from the pre-emptor's ownership of a connected property (e.g., a share in a common wall or easement). If the pre-emptor sells that qualifying property before the court grants him the pre-empted property, his right becomes void. This is because the link (connection) between his ownership and the pre-empted property is severed. However, if he sells with a stipulation of option (khiyar), the ownership does not immediately transfer, so the connection remains. This lesson explains this nuanced ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pre-emptor's qualifying property
Determine which property the pre-emptor owns that gives him the right of pre-emption (e.g., his share in the common property).
This is 'ما يشفع به' (the property by virtue of which he pre-empts).
2
Determine the timing of the sale
Did the pre-emptor sell that qualifying property before the court judged his pre-emption?
The ruling applies only before judgment.
3
Apply the rule for a final sale
If the sale of the qualifying property is final (battan), the pre-emption right becomes void.
4
Understand the rationale
The cause of entitlement is the connection (ittisal) of the pre-empted property to his ownership. Once the qualifying property is sold, that connection is severed before he obtains ownership.
The right is void because the cause ceases before the effect.
5
Note the exception for sale with option
If the sale is with a right of option (khiyar al-shart), the pre-emptor's ownership does not immediately transfer, so the connection remains. If he later chooses to rescind the sale, his pre-emption right is preserved.
The option prevents the removal of ownership (yalman'u zawal mulkihi).

Pre-emption Rights of an Agent Who Is Also the Shafi'
Ruling on the shuf'a of an agent who is a pre-emptor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the shuf'a rights of a buyer's agent from a seller's agent and state the Hanafi legal reasoning (talil) for each ruling.

Core Idea
An agent’s pre-emption right depends on whether the sale binds him to deliver (seller’s agent) or completes the contract (buyer’s agent).

An agent who is also a pre-emptor (shafi‘) raises a conflict: his role in the sale may contradict his personal right of pre-emption. This lesson examines three scenarios from the text: the seller’s agent, the seller’s agent who guarantees drk, and the buyer’s agent. The reasoning (talil) shows that the agent’s obligation to deliver (seller) nullifies shuf‘a, while the agent’s role as a purchaser (buyer) preserves it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Locate the three rulings: (1) seller's agent sells → no shuf'a; (2) seller's agent guarantees drk → no shuf'a; (3) buyer's agent buys → shuf'a remains. Note each ruling from the matn.
The drk guarantee validates the sale, so claiming shuf'a would contradict it.
2
Analyze the reasoning for the seller's agent
Understand the talil: the contract obligates the seller's agent to deliver the property. Pre-emption would require him to take it back, contradicting that obligation. Hence shuf'a is invalid.
This is based on istihsan: the agent's delivery duty is stronger.
3
Analyze the reasoning for the buyer's agent
The sale is for the principal (the actual buyer). Pre-emption arises after the contract and is lost only by delivery or silence. Neither occurred. Taking by shuf'a perfects the contract, so it is valid.
The agent's possession is first for the principal if absent, then for himself if present.
4
Apply the rulings to a new scenario
Given a situation where a co-owner acts as agent for both seller and buyer, determine whether he retains shuf'a. Use the principles: if he is the seller's agent, he loses; if he is the buyer's agent, he keeps it.
This step practices distinguishing roles.

Multiple Pre-emptors: Present Claimant Takes All Then Shares
Explains pre-emption award when some pre-emptors are absent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on pre-emption when some pre-emptors are absent and apply it to scenarios of equal, superior, and inferior pre-emptors.

Core Idea
The present pre-emptor takes the whole; absent ones share if they later claim.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) can become complicated when multiple entitled persons exist and some are absent. The Hanafi school addresses this by awarding the entire property to the first claimant who appears, but later allows absent pre-emptors to reclaim their share. This lesson unpacks the precise rulings from the classical texts of al-Quduri and its commentaries, including the critical ranking of pre-emptors by priority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Identify who the pre-emptors (shufa'a) are and whether they are present or absent. Note that the property has already been sold to an outsider, triggering the right of pre-emption.
Pre-emptors can be co-owners or neighbors with varying priority.
2
Apply the present pre-emptor rule
The first pre-emptor who appears and proves his right is awarded the entire property by the judge. This is because the absent one's intention is unknown; they may or may not claim, so the present one's right is not suspended due to doubt.
This ensures the present claimant's right is not lost.
3
Handle subsequent claimants
When another pre-emptor appears later, the judge awards half to the new claimant, effectively sharing equally between the two. This applies if the second pre-emptor is of equal rank (misl).
If the second is superior (awla), they take the entire property from the first; if inferior (dun), they get nothing.
4
Understand the priority ranking
The text gives a special case: if the buyer himself is a pre-emptor (e.g., co-owner who buys the outsider's share), then his own pre-emption right is considered. Another pre-emptor of equal rank takes half; a superior takes all; an inferior takes nothing.
This ranking is based on the Hanafi principle that the co-owner is more entitled than the neighbor.
