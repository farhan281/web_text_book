Mixed Exchange with Fulūs and Silver: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling and intra-school dispute on a mixed exchange transaction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a mixed transaction with fulūs and silver coin and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa, the Sahibayn, and Zufar.

Core Idea
The wording of a mixed exchange determines validity—Abu Hanifa invalidates all, the Sahibayn split the transaction.

This passage delves into the intricacies of a specific transaction with a money-changer (ṣayrafī). The customer gives one dirham and requests two items: fulūs (copper coins) and a silver coin weighing half a dirham minus a ḥabbah. The Hanafi school exhibits an internal ijtihādī difference on how to rule such a mixed exchange. Understanding the reasoning behind each position sharpens your ability to apply the principles of sale (bayʿ) and specification (taʿyīn).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Transaction Scenario
Understand the exact request: giving one dirham to a money-changer and asking for fulūs with half and a silver coin (half minus a ḥabbah) with the other half.
This is a composite sale combining two commodities with a single price.
2
State the Positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa rules the entire transaction fāsid (invalid). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the fulūs part is valid, the silver part invalid.
The difference hinges on whether the price is adequately specified for each component.
3
Analyze the Alternative Wording and Its Ruling
If the customer says 'Give me half a dirham of fulūs and a half minus a ḥabbah' (without allocating halves of the dirham), the sale is valid.
This wording avoids the ambiguity of splitting the single dirham.
4
Evaluate Zufar's Objection and the Hanafi Response
Zufar argues that fulūs fluctuate in value, making the price unknown. The response: 'half dirham of fulūs' is a known expression for a known quantity.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle that customary expressions can remove uncertainty (jahāla).
5
Note Muhammad's Qualification
Muhammad restricts the validity to amounts less than one dirham; for one dirham or more, it is not valid.
This shows the limitation of applying the 'known expression' rule beyond small amounts.

Commentary
This passage discusses a specific transaction with a moneychanger (ṣayrafī). Splitting the Price in Sarf: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on price splitting in sarf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on price splitting in sarf and apply the thamarah al-khilaf to similar transactions.

Core Idea
In sarf, splitting the price into valid and invalid parts can void the entire contract or just the invalid part, depending on the imam's view.

Currency exchange (ṣarf) requires precise specification and immediate exchange. This lesson examines a nuanced case where the buyer asks for a combination of fulus and a fractional dirham. The Hanafi masters disagree on whether such a split corrupts the entire deal or only the problematic portion. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to handle complex transactional conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type and disputed condition
This is a ṣarf contract where the buyer gives one dirham and requests: 'half in fulus, and the other half as a dirham minus one ḥabbah.' The dispute is about whether this condition invalidates the whole sale.
Ṣarf requires immediate hand-to-hand exchange and no delay; here the issue is the combination of two different exchanges.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his analogy
Abu Hanifa rules the entire contract void. He analogizes to a sale combining a free person and a slave, where the invalid condition corrupts everything.
The analogy (qiyās) is that an invalid condition in one part taints the whole when the contract is indivisible.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
They hold the fulus part valid, the fractional part void, because specifying the price divides the contract into two independent transactions.
Their principle: 'tafsīl al-thaman yajʿal al-ʿaqd al-wāḥid ka-ʿaqdayn' – specifying the price makes the single contract like two contracts.
4
Note Zufar's third opinion and the author's rejection
Zufar says it is void due to price uncertainty (gharar) because fulus values fluctuate. The author rejects this, arguing the expression is clear and specifies a known quantity.
The author's rebuttal: 'hādhihi ʿibārah maʿlūmah ʿan miqdār maʿlūm' – it is a known expression for a known amount.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical consequence: under the Sahibayn's view, the fulus transaction stands; under Abu Hanifa's, nothing stands. The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view as more practical for commerce.
This illustrates how later Hanafi practice favors facilitating transactions over strict voiding.

Commentary
This passage deals with a specific scenario in currency exchange (sarf) where a person gives a dirham to a money-changer and asks for a combination of fulus (copper coins) and a fractional dirham minus a habba. The ruling hinges on how the price is expressed. If the buyer says 'give me half of this dirham in fulus and half of it in half except a habba,' Abu Hanifa holds the entire contract void because the price is not properly specified—the half dirham is being used as both price and commodity (because the fulus are bought with part of the dirham and the fractional dirham minus a habba is to be given as change, which involves a sale of a dirham for a dirham minus a habba, which is riba). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) however treat the statement as implicitly dividing the contract into two separate transactions: one for fulus (valid) and one for the fractional dirham (void due to the deficiency). They argue that specifying the price in parts makes the single contract like two independent contracts. Abu Hanifa responds that even if considered one contract, the condition of selling half a dirham for half except a habba is invalid, and it is a built-in condition that corrupts the whole. He analogizes to combining a free person and a slave in one sale, which void the whole. Zufar's opinion that such transactions are invalid due to price uncertainty (since fulus values fluctuate) is rejected by the author (the commentator) with the argument that the expression is unambiguous and specifies a known quantity. The passage also notes Muhammad's restriction: if one says 'for a dirham fulus' (i.e., buy one dirham worth of fulus) it is invalid because fulus themselves are not a fixed measure of value; but for less than a dirham it is permissible. The overall authority weight is from the Zahir al-Riwayah as it appears in a commentary on al-Quduri.

Contract Unity in Sarf: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether specifying the price creates separate contracts in sarf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether specifying the price splits a single sarf contract and apply the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
In sarf, detailing the price splits the contract for Sahibayn, but not for Abu Hanifa—invalidity of one part taints the whole.

Consider a currency exchange where part of the payment is for valid coins and part for a coin that is underweight, involving riba. Does the entire transaction become invalid, or can the valid part stand? The answer depends on a fundamental disagreement within the Hanafi school: whether specifying (tafsir) and detailing (tafsil) the price turns a single contract into two. This lesson unpacks that dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction and the problematic condition
Read the scenario: a person offers a dirham in exchange for fulus (copper coins) and a small dirham that is underweight by one grain. The underweight part constitutes riba al-fadl. Note how the price is detailed: half the dirham for fulus, half for the small dirham.
The condition 'except one grain' makes the exchange unequal, triggering riba.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his evidence
Abu Hanifa rules the entire contract invalid. His evidence: the contract is one, so invalidity in any part invalidates the whole. He draws an analogy (qiyas) to selling a free person and a slave together—the free person cannot be sold, so the whole sale is void.
The key principle: unity of the contract.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their evidence
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) rule that the fulus portion is valid and only the dirham portion is invalid. Their evidence: detailing the price splits the single contract into two separate contracts; thus, invalidity in one does not affect the other.
Their principle: tafsir and tafsil create contractual separation.
4
Explain the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the entire transaction is void—both fulus and dirham parts are null. Under the Sahibayn, the fulus part is valid and enforceable, while only the dirham part is void.
This affects whether the parties can salvage any part of the exchange.
5
Note the mufta bihi (operative position)
The passage does not specify which view is relied upon. In later Hanafi practice, the Sahibayn's view is often preferred for practical ease, as it allows valid parts of transactions to stand.
Check later fatwa sources for the established position.

Rahn Guarantee: The Lesser of Value and Debt
States Hanafi ruling on rahn guarantee and disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the guarantee of a pledge and compare it with the positions of Zufar, al-Shafi'i, and Qadi Shurayh, identifying the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
A pledge is guaranteed only by the lesser of its value and the debt — excess goes back to the pledgor.

This passage from the Book of Pledge (Kitab al-Rahn) establishes the core Hanafi principle that a pledge is guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt. It then presents a dispute among scholars: Zufar argues for full value, al-Shafi'i considers it a trust, and Qadi Shurayh discharges the entire debt. Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for correctly handling cases where a pledged item is destroyed.

Legal Interdiction: Slave, Insane Person, and Minor
Explains rulings on transactions of legally interdicted persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on transactions of a slave, a continuously insane person, and a discerning minor, and explain the role of guardian approval and the exception of excessive loss.

Core Idea
A slave needs permission; an insane person's transaction is void; a discerning minor's transaction is suspended on guardian approval.

The chapter on legal interdiction (al-hajr) restricts certain persons from freely disposing of property. This passage explains three categories: slaves, the continuously insane, and minors who understand the contract. Each has a distinct ruling based on capacity and potential harm. We will explore these rulings, their conditions, and the rationale behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories and their basic rulings
Read the opening statements: slave needs permission, insane person's transaction is invalid absolutely, and the minor's transaction is discussed later. Note the ruling for each.
The insane person here is the one whose mind is completely overcome, never lucid.
2
Analyze the exception for the insane: continuous vs intermittent
The text specifies that the rule applies to one who never regains consciousness. If an insane person has lucid intervals, his transactions during those intervals are valid. Distinguish between the two.
The Hanafi school makes this distinction based on the person's capacity at the time of the transaction.
3
Examine the minor's transaction: suspension and guardian's option
The minor who understands the contract (ya'qil al-bay') makes a transaction that is suspended (mawquf). The guardian may ratify it if it is beneficial or annul it. Understand the guardian's discretionary role.
The minor must be discerning (mumayyiz) for suspension; otherwise the transaction is void.
4
Note the limitation: excessive loss prevents ratification
Even if the guardian wants to ratify, if the minor suffered excessive loss (ghubn fahish), the transaction cannot be validated. Slight loss is acceptable. Understand the threshold.
Excessive loss is typically defined as more than one-third deviation from market value.
5
Distinguish between statements and actions
Interdiction (hajr) applies to statements like sales and acknowledgments, but not to actions like destroying property. Actions cannot be undone, so they are binding regardless of interdiction.
This distinction affects liability for damages caused by the interdicted person.

This lesson examines the guardian's power to ratify or rescind a contract entered by the minor, and the critical exception of flagrant fraud (ghubn fahish). Interdiction of the Squanderer and the Non-Rashid Boy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on the squanderer's transaction validity and apply the exception for public harm, and explain the validity of the non-rashid boy's transactions despite property withholding.

Core Idea
A squanderer is not interdicted unless he causes public harm; a non-rashid boy's transactions are valid even if his wealth is withheld.

This passage from the Book of Interdiction presents two key rulings from Imam Abu Hanifa. The first concerns the squanderer (safeeh): he is not interdicted unless his actions cause widespread harm. The second concerns a boy who reaches adulthood but is not yet prudent (ghayr rashid): his wealth is withheld until age 25, yet his transactions remain valid. Understanding these rulings requires grasping the delicate balance between preserving a person's legal capacity and protecting property from misuse.

Conclude that legal capacity (ahliyyah) is not removed by foolishness alone, but the control of property may be delayed for protection. The validity of transactions does not depend on physical possession.

Commentary
This passage is from the Book of Interdiction (al-Hajr) and presents two key rulings from Imam Abu Hanifa. The first ruling: a squanderer (safeeh) who is free, adult, and sane is not subjected to legal interdiction. The overall principle is that capacity (ahliyyah) is not removed due to foolishness alone, but the control of wealth may be delayed for protection until a maximum age.

Core Idea
Testimony of insolvency is accepted when based on circumstantial evidence—imprisonment itself proves lack of wealth.

You may wonder how a negative fact like 'he has no money' can be proven by testimony. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi response: such testimony is not purely negative but rests on observable evidence—the debtor's imprisonment. You'll learn the ruling's nuances and practical implications.

Seller's Priority in Bankruptcy: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on seller's claim to specific goods in bankruptcy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on seller's rights in bankruptcy, explain the thamarah al-khilaf with the Shafi'i school, and identify the exceptional cases where the seller retains priority.

Core Idea
Delivery of goods equals consent to be an ordinary creditor in Hanafi law.

When a buyer becomes bankrupt before paying the price, is the seller entitled to reclaim the specific goods, or must he stand in line with other creditors? This is a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The answer hinges on the legal effect of delivery: does it transfer full ownership and make the seller merely a creditor, or does the seller retain a proprietary interest until payment? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi reasoning and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
The buyer purchases goods, takes possession with the seller's permission, then becomes bankrupt (or dies) before full payment. There are other creditors. What is the seller's position?
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The seller is 'uswat al-ghurama' – equal to other creditors. Why? Because by voluntarily delivering the goods, the seller consented to dropping his right to the specific item and accepted the buyer's personal liability (dhimma).
This is a rational (aqli) proof: delivery implies transfer of ownership and acceptance of countervalue in the buyer's debt.
3
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the seller is 'awla bi-mata'ihi' – has more right to his goods. The proof: ownership is not fully transferred until the price is paid; thus the seller retains a proprietary interest that survives bankruptcy.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: delivery is an act of transferring possession, which in sales indicates transfer of ownership. The consent to deliver implies consent to accept the buyer's liability. Thus the seller has no higher claim than other creditors.
The thamarah al-khilaf: in Hanafi, the seller ranks equally; in Shafi'i, he gets his goods first.
5
Note the exceptional cases where seller is preferred
If the seller did not deliver (e.g., the buyer took goods without permission), the seller retains a right of retention (haqq al-habs) and is like a mortgagee – he is paid first from the price of the goods. Also, if the price was deferred and the buyer dies, the term accelerates and the seller still becomes a creditor.

Exception of Different Genus in Iqrar: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute on exception of different genus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on istithnā' in iqrār to distinguish valid exceptions (items established in the dhimma) from invalid ones (specific items), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-position.

Core Idea
In Hanafi iqrār, exception of a different genus is valid only if the excepted item can itself be established in the dhimma.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents the Hanafi ruling on exceptions in acknowledgments when the excepted item is of a different genus. The discussion reveals a nuanced principle regarding what types of items can be excepted, and highlights a dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and issue
The act is making an exception (istithnā') in an iqrār (acknowledgment of debt). The issue is whether the exception is valid when the excepted item is of a different genus than the acknowledged item.
For example, acknowledging 100 dirhams except a dinar (different genus: gold vs silver).
2
State the Hanafi position
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the exception is valid if the excepted item is something that can be established in the dhimma by itself (like items measured by volume, weight, or similar countable items). It is invalid if the item is not so established (e.g., a specific garment or sheep).
Muhammad and Zufar hold a stricter view: exception of different genus is always invalid.
3
Identify the opposing view
Al-Shafi'i validates the exception in all cases, and requires the debtor to pay the market value of the excepted item.
Thus, in the Shafi'i school, the exception is always effective, but the debtor must compensate with value.
4
Explain the Hanafi principle
The Hanafi principle distinguishes between items that can be owed as debts (established in the dhimma) and specific objects ('ayn). An exception from a debt must be from the same genus or from something that can stand alone in the dhimma. Items like dinars, wheat (kaylī, waznī, 'adadī) can be debts; a specific garment cannot.
This is based on the idea that the exception must be of a nature that can be owed, otherwise it is like an attempt to transfer a specific object from a debt, which is not possible.
5
Apply the principle to examples
In the examples given: acknowledging 100 dirhams except a dinar (gold coin) – since dinars are established in the dhimma, the exception is valid, and the debtor owes 100 dirhams minus the value of the dinar. Similarly for a qafiz of wheat. But if the exception is a garment, the exception is invalid and the full 100 dirhams are owed.
If the value of the excepted item equals the entire acknowledged amount, nothing is owed.

Acknowledgment of Debt from Unspecified Slave: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on acknowledgment of debt from unspecified slave and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of debt from an unspecified price and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding connected vs. disconnected qualifications.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment of debt is binding; a subsequent claim of non-receipt is a retraction unless it was connected to the acknowledgment itself.

When someone acknowledges a debt from a specific transaction, but then claims he never received the consideration, does that void the debt? The Hanafi school addresses this through the rules of iqrar, distinguishing between connected and disconnected statements. This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt from the price of an unspecified slave. The dispute is whether the acknowledger's claim of non-receipt of the slave is accepted to negate the debt.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds the debt is binding; the claim of non-receipt is a retraction (ruju’) of the acknowledgment and is not accepted. The acknowledgment itself creates the obligation.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their condition
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say: if the claim of non-receipt is connected (wasala) – i.e., uttered without interruption after the acknowledgment – it is a qualification, not a retraction, and the debtor is believed. If disconnected (fasala), it is a retraction.
The connection/disconnection distinction is key.
4
Apply the distinction to sub-cases
The passage also discusses cases where the acknowledgee denies the sale or claims the slave was not sold: if the acknowledgee says 'the slave is your slave, you did not sell him,' the debt is still binding because the acknowledgment was made when the slave was sound. If he says 'the slave is my slave, I did not sell him,' the debt is not binding because the acknowledgment was conditional on the sale.
Shows how context affects the ruling.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, the debtor must pay the thousand in all cases. Under the Sahibayn, if the claim of non-receipt is connected, nothing is due; if disconnected, the debt stands.
This affects legal strategy in iqrar cases.

Acknowledgment of Debt from an Invalid Cause
Ruling on acknowledgment of debt from invalid sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of debt from an invalid cause and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the effect of connected versus disconnected qualification.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment of debt is binding; specifying an invalid cause is a retraction, not a valid qualification.

When someone acknowledges a debt and then explains that it arose from the sale of wine or pork, Islamic law treats this explanation with suspicion because those commodities are not valid property. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position: the initial debt stands, and the qualification is rejected as a retraction. We will also explore the nuance introduced by the Sahibayn regarding connected statements.

The dispute hinges on whether the qualification is considered part of the original acknowledgment or a separate retraction.
