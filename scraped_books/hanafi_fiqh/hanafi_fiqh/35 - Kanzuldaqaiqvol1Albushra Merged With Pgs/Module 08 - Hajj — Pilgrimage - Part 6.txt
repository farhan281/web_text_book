Hady: General Rulings and Exceptions
Ruling on hady with proof and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition and minimum of hady, identify the three permissible species, explain the conditions inherited from udhiyah, and apply the special rulings for tawaf al-rukun while junub and intercourse after wuquf.

Core Idea
Hady wajib; minimum sheep; conditions like udhiyah; but tawaf junub and post-wuquf intercourse require a camel.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq explains the rulings of hady—the animal offering in Hajj. It begins with the definition, minimum, and permissible types, then establishes that the same conditions as udhiyah apply. Finally, it details two specific violations that require a camel instead of a sheep: performing tawaf al-rukun while in a state of janabah and engaging in intercourse after standing at Arafah (before shaving and tawaf). The passage also clarifies related issues such as defects occurring at slaughter and the views of different Hanafi imams.

Guided Path
1
Define hady and identify its causes
Hady is an animal offered to the Haram for closeness to Allah, caused by qiran, tamattu', ihsar, jaza' al-sayd, and violations.
The causes are mentioned earlier in the text.
2
State the minimum and permissible species
The minimum is a sheep. Permissible: camels, cows, sheep (both males and females). Camels are highest, then cows, then sheep.
3
List the conditions inherited from udhiyah
Animals must be thani (2-year-old) from camels/cows, jadh'ah (1-year-old) from sheep, free from defects. Defects at time of slaughter are excused.
This is based on the hadith of Ali.
4
Analyze the Quranic evidence and custom
Quran 5:95 uses 'an'am' which includes camels, cows, sheep. Custom from Prophet's time confirms these three.
Imam Muhammad's explicit assignments for game animals further support this.
5
Identify the exceptions requiring a camel
Two cases: tawaf al-rukun while junub and intercourse after wuquf before shaving/tawaf. For these, a camel (badanah) is required. After shaving, a sheep suffices.
Menstruation and postpartum are analogized to janabah.

Commentary
This passage is from the 'Chapter of Hady' in Kanz al-Daqaiq, defining hady and its rulings. It opens with the minimum of hady – a sheep – and states that hady includes camels, cows, and sheep, just like udhiyah. The condition that udhiyah requires animals free from certain defects (e.g., blindness, lameness) applies equally to hady. However, the text notes an exception: a sheep suffices for all cases requiring hady except two specific violations: 1) performing the tawaf al-rukun (tawaf al-ziyarah) while in a state of janabah, and 2) engaging in intercourse after standing at Arafah but before shaving and tawaf. For these two, a camel (badanah) is required. The commentator then explains the linguistic variants for 'hady' (sukun on dal vs. kasr with tashdid). He cites the reason for the chapter's placement: preceding chapters discussed causes (qiran, tamattu', ihsar, etc.) that necessitate hady, so the effect comes after. Evidence for the three types comes from Quran 5:95, where 'compensation' is from 'an'am' (cattle), which camels, cows, and sheep. The passage mentions that Imam Muhammad explicitly sets specific compensations for hunted animals (gazelle → sheep, ostrich → camel, wild donkey → cow), while the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) also accept that compensation can be bought with equivalent value from these types. The conditions for hady mirror udhiyah: animals must be 'thani' (two-year-old) from camels, cows, and sheep, and 'jadh'ah' (one-year-old) from sheep, free from defects. However, if defects arise at the moment of slaughter, it is excusable. Finally, the text details the exceptions: tawaf al-rukun while junub requires a camel, as does intercourse after wuquf but before shaving and tawaf. After shaving, the stronger view is that a sheep suffices. Menstruation and postpartum bleeding are parallel to janabah in this ruling.

Definition, Types, and Conditions of Hady
Definition, types, and conditions of hady

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of hady, identify the permitted types of animals, list the conditions for validity (type, age, freedom from defects), and cite the quranic and sunnah evidence.

Core Idea
Hady must be from camels, cattle, or sheep, at least a sheep, free from major defects, and of the proper age (thani/jadha').

This lesson from Kanz al-Daqaiq defines hady—the sacrificial animal brought to the Haram. It explains why hady is only from three animal types, sets the minimum at a sheep, and establishes conditions for validity including age and freedom from defects. The rulings are supported by the Quran, hadith, and analogy to udhiyah.

Guided Path
1
Define hady
Understand that hady (also pronounced as hiddy or haday) is the animal brought to the Haram for nearness. It is the result of obligations like tamattu', qiran, and violations.
2
Identify the permitted types and minimum
Hady can only be from camels, cattle, or sheep. The best is camels, then cattle, and the minimum is a sheep (or goat). This is established by Quran (al-Ma'idah 5:95), custom, and analogy from hunting compensation.
The text cites Ibn Abbas: 'What is available of hady is a sheep.'
3
List the conditions for validity
Three main conditions: 1) from the three types, 2) age requirement (thani for all animals, jadha' for sheep), 3) freedom from major defects (blindness, lameness, etc.) at the time of slaughter. If a defect occurs during slaughter, it is permissible.
These conditions are identical to those for udhiyah, based on the hadith of Ali.
4
Identify the proofs
The Quranic verse (al-Ma'idah 95) is the primary evidence. The custom of the Prophet and the hadith of Ali (age requirement) provide additional support. Qiyas to udhiyah confirms the conditions.

Two Violations Requiring a Camel in Hajj
Specifies two violations requiring badanah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hajj violations that require a camel (badanah) and explain the reasoning (talil) behind the elevated penalty.

Core Idea
Two specific Hajj violations require a camel, not a sheep, due to their greater severity.

In Hajj, most violations that require a blood sacrifice (dam) are compensated with a sheep. However, there are two exceptions where a camel is obligatory. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq specifies those exceptions and gives the underlying rationale: the severity of major ritual impurity (janabah) and the timing of intercourse relative to key Hajj rites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two violations
State exactly which two acts trigger a camel penalty: 1) performing tawaf al-rukun while junub, 2) having intercourse after standing at Arafah but before shaving (halq) and tawaf.
Note that the text excludes intercourse before wuquf (which requires a sheep) and after halq (where the preferred view is a sheep).
2
Understand the ruling for each
For both violations, the required sacrifice is a camel (badanah). For other violations requiring blood, a sheep suffices.
3
Trace the rationale (talil)
For tawaf while junub: janabah is a major impurity, so the compensation is elevated to show the difference between minor and major shortcomings. For intercourse before halq: it occurs at a critical stage of Hajj, hence more severe. Menstruation/post-childbirth are analogized to janabah.
The text says 'to show the difference between the lesser and greater' – a principle of proportional compensation.
4
Distinguish nuances
Note the intra-madhhab difference: if intercourse occurs after halq, the preponderant (rajih) view requires only a sheep, though another view requires a camel. Also note that women in menstruation or post-childbirth are treated like junub for this rule.
This shows that the timing relative to halq is decisive.
5
Apply the ruling
If a pilgrim commits one of these two violations, they owe a camel. For other violations, a sheep. Know the distinction for fatwa purposes.

The commentary clarifies that the phrase 'after wuquf' is used to exclude intercourse before wuquf, which would require a sheep. The main point is that these two acts require a camel, while other violations requiring blood normally require a sheep.

Ruling on Taking the Hadyi to 'Arafat (Ta'rif)
Clarification that ta'rif is not obligatory but recommended for gratitude sacrifices

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ta'rif and distinguish when it is recommended versus not recommended.

Core Idea
Ta'rif is not obligatory, but it is recommended for gratitude sacrifices; for expiatory sacrifices, concealment is better.

During Hajj, many pilgrims wonder whether they must take their sacrificial animal to 'Arafat. The Hanafi school clarifies that ta'rif (taking the animal to 'Arafat) is not a requirement. The sole obligation is to transport the animal to the Haram for slaughter. However, for gratitude sacrifices (dam shukr) such as the hadyi of tamattu' and qiran, ta'rif is recommended as an expression of thanks. For expiatory sacrifices (dam kaffarah), concealment is more appropriate, so ta'rif is not recommended. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this nuanced ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act under consideration is ta'rif—taking the sacrificial animal to 'Arafat. The matn states that it is not obligatory (wajib). This is the foundational point: the obligation is merely to bring the animal to the Haram, not to a specific location within it.
2
Understand the scope of obligation
The primary obligation of hadyi is fulfilled by transferring the animal to the Haram (the sanctuary of Makkah) and then slaughtering it. Going to 'Arafat is an additional act that goes beyond the required fulfillment.
The Arabic verb 'wa layusa yajibu' clearly negates obligation.
3
Distinguish between types of hadyi
The passage divides hadyi into two categories: dam shukr (gratitude blood, e.g., from tamattu' and qiran) and dam kaffarah (expiatory blood due to violations). For shukr, ta'rif is recommended (mandub) because it enhances the display of gratitude. For kaffarah, it is not recommended because concealment (satr) is more befitting for a transgression.
The key term is 'yandabu inn kana dam shukr'.
4
Consider the practical timing
Expiatory blood may be slaughtered before the Day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah), so there is no need to delay for ta'rif. Gratitude sacrifices, however, are typically slaughtered after the Day of Nahr, making ta'rif feasible.

Charity of Cover and Halter; Prohibition of Butcher's Wage
Rulings on charity of cover/halter and prohibition of butcher's wage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding the charity of the sacrificial animal's cover (jill) and halter (khitam), and explain why the butcher cannot be paid from the animal.

Core Idea
The cover and halter must be given in charity; the butcher's wage cannot come from the animal.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents two key rulings related to the sacrificial animal (hadi). The first concerns the disposal of the animal's cover and halter—they must be given in charity, not retained. The second prohibits paying the butcher from the animal itself, as this would make him a partner in the meat, contradicting the purpose of the sacrifice. The proof is a hadith narrated by 'Ali (ra) that the Prophet (sallallahu 'alayhi wasallam) ordered all parts of his sacrificial animal to be divided and nothing given to the butcher.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The matn states that the cover (jill) and halter (khitam) must be given in charity, and the butcher's wage should not be given from the animal.
Note different rulings: charity is wajib; paying from the animal is haraam.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of 'Ali (ra) that the Prophet (sallallahu 'alayhi wasallam) ordered all parts including covers to be divided and nothing given for slaughter.
The hadith is a Sunnah ahad with zanni thubut but qati dalala regarding the prohibition.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The command to divide 'all its parts' includes the cover and halter, so they must be given away. The prohibition of giving 'anything for its slaughter' directly forbids paying the butcher from the animal.
The phrase 'لا يعطي في جزارتها شيئاً' is the operative part.
4
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
If the butcher is paid from the animal, he becomes a partner in its meat, which defeats the purpose of the sacrifice (the entire animal should be consumed for Allah).
This reasoning prevents waiving the prohibition even if the butcher stipulates it.
5
Note the exception
If the owner voluntarily gives some meat or skin to the butcher as a charitable gift, it is permissible.
This is not payment but sadaqah.

Preferred Method of Slaughter for Sacrificial Animals: Nahr vs Dhabh
Ruling on preferred slaughter method for sacrificial animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between nahr and dhabh, state which method is preferred for camels versus cows and sheep, and cite the Quranic proofs.

Core Idea
Nahr is better for camels; dhabh for cows and sheep, based on Quranic usage.

After discussing the disposal of parts and butcher's wage, the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq turns to the preferred method of slaughter for different sacrificial animals. The passage explains that for camels, the better method is nahr (slaughtering while the animal stands), while for cows and sheep, dhabh (slaughtering while the animal lies down) is superior. The evidence is drawn from Quranic verses where specific verbs (nahr and dhabh) are used for each animal type. Additionally, it is recommended that the owner perform the slaughter himself if he is skilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods of slaughter
Dhabh: slaughtering by cutting the throat while the animal is laid down. Nahr: slaughtering by cutting the throat while the animal is standing.
Both are valid; the difference is preference.
2
State the preferred method for each animal
For camels: nahr is better. For cows and sheep: dhabh is better, and they should be laid down before slaughter.
The text uses 'الأفضل' indicating sunnah or mustahabb.
3
Locate the Quranic proofs
For cows: verse 2:67 uses the word 'تذبحوا' (dhabh). For the ram of Isma'il: verse 37:107 uses 'ذبح' (dhabh). For camels: verse 22:36 mentions 'when their sides fall', implying they are standing (nahr).
The proofs are from the Quran, indicating the preferred method.
4
Understand the reasoning
The Quran uses the specific verb for each animal, showing the Sunnah method. For camels, nahr is associated with standing; for cows/sheep, dhabh is associated with laying down.
This is based on the practice of the Prophet (sallallahu 'alayhi wasallam) and the Companions.
5
Note the recommendation for the owner
It is better (first) for the owner to slaughter the animal himself if he is proficient, to ensure proper method and devotion.
This is an additional adab (etiquette).

Prohibitions on Using the Hadyi and Ruling on a Disabled Animal
Rulings on riding, milking, sprinkling, and disabled animal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on riding, milking, and sprinkling the hadyi, explain the necessity exception, and apply the compensation rule if the animal is injured.

Core Idea
The hadyi is consecrated; personal use is prohibited unless necessity permits, with compensation for loss.

The passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentary outlines the sacred nature of the sacrificial animal (hadyi). It prohibits riding or milking it without necessity, recommends sprinkling water on its udder to stop milk, and details the obligations if the animal becomes disabled before slaughter. These rulings balance respect for the consecrated animal with practical needs, supported by hadith and juristic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify act and default ruling
Determine the default hukm for riding, milking, and using the hadyi. The text states 'do not ride it without necessity', indicating prohibition (haram) unless necessity arises.
Riding is considered an act of disrespect to the consecrated animal.
2
Recognize the necessity exception
Riding is permitted when necessity (darurah) arises, such as being forced to move the animal. The hadith 'Ride it with kindness when forced to it' is the proof.
Necessity must be genuine; the animal remains property and must not be emaciated.
3
Analyze consequence of unauthorized use
If riding causes injury, the rider must compensate for the decrease in value and give compensation in charity to the poor, not the rich.
This reflects the principle that benefiting from the sacred animal requires accountability.
4
Understand milking and sprinkling ruling
Milking is prohibited when slaughter time is near; if far and the udder is painful, milking is allowed and the milk must be given in charity. Sprinkling cold water on the udder is recommended to stop milk flow.
The milk is part of the animal, so it cannot be consumed or sold for private benefit.
5
Define disability ('atab) of the animal
If the obligatory hadyi becomes disabled (near death) before reaching the place of slaughter, or has a major defect (e.g., loss of more than one-third of ear per Abu Hanifa, more than half per the two disciples), a replacement is required.
The definition of major defect differs between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
For wajib hady, substitution; for voluntary, slaughter and mark—the obligation is in the dhimmah vs 'ayn.

The hady animal may become disabled or defective before reaching its place of sacrifice. Islamic law provides distinct rulings based on whether the hady was obligatory (wajib) or voluntary (tatwwu'). This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position, its proof from the Sunnah, and the key difference with the Shafi'i school regarding consumption by the poor among the party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two cases: (1) a wajib hady becomes disabled/defective; (2) a voluntary hady becomes disabled/defective. Note the different required actions.
For wajib: another animal is substituted; for voluntary: slaughter and mark, no substitution.
2
Understand the legal basis
The ruling hinges on the nature of the obligation. For wajib hady, the contract is in the dhimmah (obligation on the person), so if the animal fails, it must be replaced. For voluntary, the obligation is on the specific animal ('ayn), so if it perishes, nothing is owed.
This distinction is fundamental in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Analyze the hadith proof
The hadith of Qabisah and Naji al-Khuzai provides the basis for voluntary hady: slaughter, dip sandal in blood, strike side, and do not eat yourself or your companions. Identify how the Hanafi school uses this proof.
The hadith is from Muslim and Ahmad.
4

The commentary clarifies that substitution for wajib hady is required only if the owner is wealthy. If poor, the defective animal suffices. This is derived from the principle of ability (wus'a).
The term 'wusra' (wealth) is implied in the text.
5
Resolve the Shafi'i dispute
Shafi'i argues from the hadith that no one from the party may eat, but Hanafi restricts this to the rich. The Hanafi response: the companions were rich at the time; the poor may eat because charity is superior.
This is a practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Taqleed: Which Sacrificial Animals Receive a Necklace in Hanafi Fiqh
Specifies which sacrificial animals receive a necklace and which do not

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the types of sacrificial animals that receive taqleed according to the Hanafi school and explain why sheep and expiatory blood are excluded.

Core Idea
Taqleed is a sunnah only for camels of voluntary, tamattu', and qiran offerings – not for sheep or expiatory blood.

The practice of placing a necklace on a sacrificial animal marks it as dedicated to Allah. The Hanafi school restricts this to specific types of camels, based on the nature of the offering and the strength of the evidence. In this lesson, we will examine the ruling and the reasoning behind it, including the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is taqleed? It is placing a necklace on a sacrificial animal. According to the Hanafi school, it is a sunnah muakkadah (emphasized sunnah) for certain types of camels.
The ruling is sunnah, not wajib.
2
List the animals that receive taqleed
The source explicitly mentions camels of tatwwu' (voluntary), tamattu' (the female camel brought as hady for tamattu' Hajj), and qiran (the camel for qiran Hajj). Additionally, according to some like Al-Muhit, a camel offered as a vow (nadhr) is also included because it is sacrificial blood.
Only camels (badanah) – not cows, sheep, or goats.
3
Examine the animals that do NOT receive taqleed
Animals offered for expiations (kaffarah) or for being prevented from completing Hajj (ihsar) are not given a necklace because their cause involves a wrong (jinaayah), and concealment is more appropriate. Also, sheep are not given a necklace due to lack of customary practice.
The principle: sacrificial blood (dam al-nusuk) is displayed; blood of jinaayah and ihsar is concealed.
4
Analyze the dispute with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i held that sheep are also given a necklace based on a hadith of Aisha that the Prophet sent a sheep and gave it a necklace. The Hanafi response is that the hadith is solitary (infarada bihi) and the practice was abandoned by the Prophet and companions, so it is not a sunnah.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa).
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
A pilgrim performing tamattu' has a camel. He should place a necklace on it. If he had a sheep, he would not. For a cow or goat, no taqleed either.
The type of animal and the type of offering determine the ruling.

Defective Hadi and Taqlid in the Hanafi School
Matn with commentary on defective Hadi and taqlid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi rulings for obligatory vs voluntary Hadi when defective, and explain why taqlid is only for camels.

Core Idea
An obligatory Hadi binds the dhimmah; a voluntary Hadi binds the animal. Defects affect them differently.

When a sacrificial animal (hadi) becomes defective or is about to die, the ruling depends on whether the hadi was obligatory (wajib) or voluntary (tatawwu'). This distinction is rooted in where the obligation resides: in the dhimmah or in the specific animal. The passage also addresses the practice of garlanding (taqlid) and a dispute with the Shafi'i school over whether sheep are garlanded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what is being ruled upon: a defective Hadi. For obligatory Hadi (e.g., due to tamattu'), a substitute must be brought (wajib). For voluntary Hadi, no substitute is needed; the animal is slaughtered and marked.
The ruling hinges on whether the obligation is in the dhimmah or attached to the specific animal.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith from Qabisah and Naji al-Khuza'i (narrated by Muslim and Ahmad) is the primary proof. It instructs to slaughter a disabled or dying hadi, mark it, and forbids the owner and wealthy companions from eating it.
The hadith is ahad but accepted as evidence in this context.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command 'slaughter it' and the prohibition from eating indicate that the animal is not wasted; it is given to the poor. For voluntary hadi, the animal is the locus of devotion, so no replacement is needed. For obligatory hadi, the duty is in the dhimmah, so a substitute must be offered.
Al-Shafi'i interprets the hadith differently, arguing that no one should eat it.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Al-Shafi'i holds that a defective or dying voluntary Hadi cannot be eaten even by poor companions; it must be left for beasts. The Hanafi response: the hadith is interpreted for wealthy companions; the poor may eat it.
The practical difference: Hanafis allow distribution to poor companions; Shafi'is do not.
5
Evaluate the second dispute: taqlid of sheep
Hanafis garland only camels (badanah) for tamattu', qiran, and nadhr. Al-Shafi'i extends taqlid to sheep based on a hadith. Hanafi response: the Prophet did it once and then abandoned; the Companions did not practice it; the hadith is solitary.
The effect: Hanafis consider garlanding sheep not mustahabb; Shafi'is consider it mustahabb.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Testimony about Arafah standing is accepted before the day but not after—unless tadaruk is possible; istihsan overrides qiyas to avoid hardship.

In Hanafi fiqh, when pilgrims testify about the day they stood at Arafah, the acceptance of their testimony depends on whether they can rectify (tadaruk) a potential error. This lesson explores the ruling: testimony before the day is accepted with a requirement to repeat the standing, while after the day it is not accepted if tadaruk is impossible. The text justifies this ruling by balancing qiyas (analogy) with istihsan (juridical preference) to avoid undue hardship.

Guided Path

Identify the two scenarios

Understand the role of tadaruk
Tadaruk (rectification) is the possibility to repeat the standing if confusion is resolved in time. Before the day, tadaruk is possible; after the day, it is impossible. This distinction drives the ruling.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Each jamrah is independent; omitting one allows making it up alone without penalty.

During the days of Tashriq, pilgrims throw jamrahs in a specified order. But what if a pilgrim accidentally misses the first? The Hanafi school provides two valid options: rethrow all three in order (better) or only the missed one. This lesson examines the ruling, the proof from analogy with Jamrat al-Aqabah, and the contrasting view of Imam al-Shafi'i.

The pilgrim may either rethrow all three in the recommended order (better) or throw only the missed jamrah. Both are valid.
The better option is to rethrow all in order to observe the sunnah sequence.

The proof is qiyas with Jamrat al-Aqabah, which is valid as a standalone act on the day of Nahr, showing each jamrah is independent.

In practice, the pilgrim makes up the missed jamrah within its time and no penalty (dam) is required.
The ruling applies identically on days 2, 3, and 4.

Core Idea
A vow to walk makes walking obligatory; riding requires a sacrificial animal (dam).

Vowing to perform Hajj on foot is a serious commitment. The Hanafi school holds that once you make such a vow, walking becomes wajib until the essential tawaf. The author did not specify. Some say from the miqat, but the correct view is from his house, because that is the customary understanding.
