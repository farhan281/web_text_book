Witnessing for Fosterage: Hanafi Requirements
Ruling on the number and gender of witnesses required to prove fosterage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi requirement for witnesses in proving fosterage and explain why women alone are insufficient.

Core Idea
Fosterage proof equals property proof—two men or one man and two women.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on the number and gender of witnesses required to prove fosterage (رضاع) in court. The Hanafis analogize fosterage to property claims, requiring either two just men or one man and two women. Women alone are not accepted because establishing the prohibition of marriage due to fosterage involves nullifying marital rights, which is akin to nullifying ownership rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is testifying to establish fosterage. The ruling: such testimony is valid only if it meets the standard for property claims.
2
State the Hanafi witness requirement
Fosterage is proven by two just, sane, adult free men, or one man and two such women.
This is the same as for financial claims (mal).
3
Understand the reasoning
The prohibition of marriage due to fosterage is a consequence of marital ownership (milk al-nikah). Ownership is not nullified by women's testimony alone, so fosterage cannot be proven by women alone.
The analogy (qiyas) to property is the key proof.
4
Note the procedural requirement
Separation of spouses due to fosterage requires a judicial decree from a qadi, because it annuls the husband's right.
The ruling is not self-executing.
5
Compare with other madhahib
Shafi'i accepts four women; Maliki one just woman; Hanbali one wet nurse. The Hanafi view is more stringent.
These differences arise from different analogies and textual interpretations.

By that by which property is established: which is the testimony of two just, sane, adult free men or one man and two women similarly. (Tahtawi) But separation does not occur except by the judge's separation decree because it entails nullifying the right of the servant. Commentary
This passage establishes the procedural requirements for proving fosterage in court. The standard for proof is the same as for financial claims: two male witnesses or one male and two females. This is because fosterage involves the establishment of legal prohibitions that affect marital rights, which are akin to property rights in the sense of 'ownership' (milk al-nikah). The Hanafi school analogizes the proof of fosterage to proof of financial rights (mal), hence the phrase 'by that by which property is established'. This is a qiyas. Importantly, the separation of the spouses (firqah) due to fosterage does not happen automatically; it requires a judicial decree from a qadi because it involves annulling the husband's right (haqq al-'abd). Women alone cannot testify to establish fosterage because the prohibition is a consequence of marital ownership (milk), and ownership cannot be nullified by women's testimony alone. The passage then contrasts the Hanafi position with other schools: al-Shafi'i accepts four women; Malik accepts one trustworthy woman; Ahmad accepts a single wet nurse. The Hanafi rule applies regardless of whether the testimony is given before or after the marriage.

Key Terms
شهادة (shahadah): Testimony in court to establish a fact.
الملك (al-milk): Ownership; here used for marital authority (milk al-nikah).

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, fosterage is proven by the same evidentiary standard as property claims, requiring either two just men or one man and two women; women alone are insufficient because establishing fosterage nullifies marital rights akin to ownership.
