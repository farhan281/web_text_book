Resolving the Apparent Contradiction: Traveler Arriving Home During a Ramadan Day
Reconciling two Hanafi rulings on a traveler's fast when returning home

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a traveler who arrives home during a Ramadan day must make up the fast, and reconcile the apparent contradiction in the Hanafi texts.

Core Idea
A traveler must make up if he arrives after breaking fast or missing intention time; otherwise he may fast validly.

A student of Hanafi fiqh may encounter what appears to be a contradiction: earlier it is said that a traveler who intends to break and then returns home can validly intend to fast for the rest of the day, yet later it says he must make up. This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq, with the commentary of Fath al-Qadir, resolves the tension by specifying the precise conditions that trigger the obligation to make up. By understanding this reconciliation, you will be able to apply the correct ruling to any scenario involving a traveling fasting person upon returning home.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two seemingly conflicting rulings
First, recall the earlier ruling: a traveler who intends to break his fast, then arrives in his city, may validly intend to fast for the remaining part of the day. Second, note the present ruling: a traveler who arrives in part of the day must make up the fast. Recognize the potential contradiction.
2
Recognize the objection (إن قلت)
The text presents a hypothetical objection: 'If you say: requiring him to make up contradicts what preceded...' This frames the apparent conflict as a student's question.
The structure 'فإن قلت... قلت' is a standard dialectical device in post-classical Hanafi texts to answer anticipated objections.
3
State the commentator's resolution (قلت)
The resolution begins with 'I say: there is no contradiction because...' The commentator (Fath al-Qadir) asserts that the two rulings apply to different scenarios.
4
Identify the distinguishing conditions
The text specifies: the present ruling (make-up required) applies when the traveler arrives 'after having used an invalidator' (i.e., already broke his fast) or 'after the time for intention has passed'. The earlier ruling applies when neither condition holds—i.e., he has not broken his fast and the time for intention is still available.
In the Hanafi school, a traveler may validly intend to fast even after dawn as long as he has not yet eaten. 'Time for intention' here likely refers to the period before dawn for non‑travelers; the traveler's window extends until he breaks his fast.
5
Apply the correct ruling to a given scenario
Given a concrete situation (e.g., traveler returns after eating, or returns before eating with intention still possible), determine whether the ruling of qada applies or the ruling of valid fasting. Practice by constructing hypothetical cases.

Commentary
This passage addresses a potential contradiction in Hanafi rulings regarding a traveler. Earlier it was stated that a traveler who intends to break his fast (i.e., decides not to fast) and then arrives in his city during the day can validly change his intention and still fast from that point on. Eating Suhur Mistakenly After Dawn: Qada Without Kaffarah
Ruling on mistaken suhur

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a fasting person who eats suhur mistakenly after dawn and distinguish between qada and kaffarah.

Core Idea
One who eats suhur thinking it is still night must make up the fast but owes no expiation.

This lesson examines a common scenario during Ramadan: eating suhur under the mistaken belief that dawn has not yet arrived. The Hanafi school provides a clear, nuanced ruling that balances intention, mistake, and the inviolability of the fast. Understanding this distinction is essential for both laypersons and those giving fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is eating suhur while fasting. The ruling: if eaten after dawn, the fast is broken. But if done mistakenly, only make-up (qada) is required, not expiation (kaffarah).
Kaffarah only applies to deliberate, intentional violations without a valid excuse.
2
Understand the term 'zann'
Zann here includes hesitation or doubt about whether night remains. It covers both preponderant belief and mere doubt.
This expands the scope of valid excuse.
3
Apply the principle of original assumption
The original assumption is that night remains until certainty of dawn. Doubt does not override this assumption.
If he doubts whether he ate after dawn, no compensation is needed because the default is night.
4
Conclude the ruling
Since he acted based on a valid excuse (zann or reliance on a reliable source), the fast is invalidated but only qada is wajib, not kaffarah.
This is the mufta bihi position.

The Sunnah of Suhur: Timing and Blessings
Sunnah of suhur

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the sunnah acts related to suhur and explain the recommended timings for suhur and iftar based on Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Suhur is a blessed sunnah that distinguishes our fast; delaying it and hastening iftar are recommended.

This lesson highlights the prophetic practices related to the pre-dawn meal (suhur) and breaking the fast (iftar). The Hanafi school emphasizes the recommendation of suhur, its timing, and the virtues cited in authentic hadith. Knowing these sunan enhances one's worship and understanding of the fast's etiquettes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on suhur
Suhur is recommended (mustahabb) according to the Hanafi school, with some scholars considering it sunnah mu'akkadah.
The text mentions both views.
2
Cite the hadith proofs
The Prophet (ﷺ) said: 'Eat suhur, for in suhur there is blessing.' Also: 'The difference between our fast and that of the People of the Book is the suhur meal.' These indicate merit.
These hadith are found in Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Note the recommended timings
Delaying suhur (to just before dawn) and hastening iftar (immediately after sunset) are recommended based on the hadith: 'My ummah will continue to be good as long as they delay suhur and hasten iftar.'
The recommendation is for the optimal practice, not obligation.
4
List the sunnah of breaking fast
It is sunnah to break fast with fresh dates; if not available, with dried dates; if not, with sips of water.
This is derived from the practice of the Prophet (ﷺ) as reported by Anas.

Eating Intentionally After Forgetful Eating: Qada' vs Kaffarah
Explains ruling on one who eats intentionally after having eaten forgetfully

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between qada' and kaffarah when intentional eating follows forgetful eating during Ramadan, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding knowledge of the hadith.

Core Idea
Intentional eating after forgetful eating does not require kaffarah in the relied-upon view, only make-up.

One might think that any intentional eating during Ramadan invalidates the fast and requires kaffarah. However, when the initial eating was forgetful, the ruling becomes nuanced. In this passage, we explore the Hanafi position and the disagreement over whether knowledge of a prophetic hadith triggers kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act sequence
The case is a person who first eats forgetfully (while fasting) and then, thinking his fast is broken, eats intentionally. Determine the legal status of each act.
Forgetful eating does not break the fast by consensus.
2
State the majority Hanafi ruling (zahir al-riwaya)
According to the relied-upon narration, the person must make up the day (qada') but does not have to pay kaffarah. This applies regardless of whether he knew the hadith about forgetful eating.
The text declares this as 'al-sahih' (the correct view).
3
Identify the alternative view from Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
There is a narration from Abu Hanifa that if the person had heard the hadith 'Whoever forgets while fasting... let him complete his fast' and understood it, then kaffarah is required. This is also the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn).
This view makes kaffarah conditional on knowledge of the ruling.
4
Examine the cupping case and further differences
The passage applies similar reasoning to someone who gets cupped, thinks it breaks the fast, and then eats intentionally. Here, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ: Muhammad says no kaffarah because the Prophet's words are stronger, while Abu Yusuf holds the opposite.
This shows the complexity of basing rulings on individual ijtihad vs. transmitted texts.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and the authority weight
The text states 'wa huwa al-sahih' for the view of no kaffarah. This is the operative ruling for fatwa, supported by the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya).
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah, which is the most reliable level.

Forced Intercourse and Fasting: Qada Without Kaffarah
Explains ruling on coerced intercourse while fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a sleeping or insane woman coerced into intercourse while fasting, distinguish it from the forgetting case, and apply the principle that textual exceptions cannot be extended by qiyas.

Core Idea
She must make up the day but no kaffarah – intent matters, but the fast is still broken.

Fiqh balances the invalidation of the fast with the absence of sinful intent. The same intercourse that breaks the fast does not require kaffarah when the woman is asleep, insane, or coerced. But is even the make-up (qada) waived? Zufar and al-Shafi‘i say yes, by analogy with the forgetful person. The Hanafi school says no – the textual exemption for forgetting cannot be extended by qiyas. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the ruling
The passage addresses a sleeping or insane woman who is forced into intercourse while fasting. The Hanafi ruling is: she must make up the day (qada) but does not pay kaffarah.
Kaffarah is only due when the fast is invalidated intentionally and without valid excuse.
2
Locate the opposing position
Zufar and al-Shafi‘i argue that even qada is not required, drawing an analogy (qiyas) to the person who forgetfully eats or drinks while fasting – that person is exempt from both qada and kaffarah by a specific hadith.
The passage explicitly says 'like the forgetful person' (مثل الناسي).
3
Analyse the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafis respond that the exemption for the forgetful person is established by a textual (naqli) exception, so it cannot be extended by analogy. The general principle applies: intercourse invalidates the fast, and qada is required unless a specific text waives it.
This shows the Hanafi methodology: textual exceptions are not freely analogized.
4
Resolve the textual dispute about the word 'majnuna'
The passage reports a debate: some scholars say the original text read 'majbūrah' (compelled woman) rather than 'majnūnah' (insane woman). This is a scribal issue but the ruling applies to both.
Isa ibn Aban and Imam Muhammad discussed this – the ruling covers coerced women as well.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the Hanafi view, a woman in this situation must fast one day later as qada. Under Zufar/Al-Shafi‘i, she does not. This affects a woman who discovers her fast was invalidated by forced intercourse.
The difference is real: one day of qada or not.

No Qada for Vowed Fast on Prohibited Days
Ruling on vow to fast whole year with prohibited days and intra-Hanafi dispute on qada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qada for a vowed fast broken on a prohibited day and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute and the underlying rationale (وجه الفرق).

Core Idea
If you start a vowed fast on a prohibited day, you are commanded to stop — no qada required, because your commencement itself is a violation.

A vow to fast every day of the year is a serious commitment, but what happens when that vow collides with days on which fasting is prohibited? This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi dispute: does the commencement of such a fast obligate you to complete it, and if broken, must you make it up? We will follow the reasoning of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, and understand the key distinction (وجه الفرق) that determines the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Dispute
Recognize the subject: a person vows to fast every day of the year, but some days (Eid and Tashriq) are prohibited for fasting. The person commences the fast on a prohibited day and then breaks it. The dispute is whether qada (making up that day) is obligatory.
The act is a combination of a vow (nadhr) and a prohibited day.
2
State Each Position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds: no qada required. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold: qada is obligatory.
The matn (base text) states 'لا قضاء' which is Abu Hanifa's view.
3
Trace the Proofs (Dalil) of Each Side
Abu Hanifa: The person becomes a perpetrator of the prohibition by commencing the fast, so he is commanded to stop; hence no obligation to complete, and therefore no qada. The Sahibayn: Commencement of a vow creates a binding obligation, analogous to commencing prayer in a disliked time; breaking it necessitates qada.
The Sahibayn use qiyas (analogy) to prayer.
4
Understand the وجه الفرق (Point of Distinction)
The key distinction: In the case of prayer in a disliked time, the act itself (prayer) is not prohibited, only disliked. But in the case of fasting on a prohibited day, the act of fasting is itself haram (forbidden). Therefore, the obligation to complete is nullified from the start.
This distinction is central to Abu Hanifa's reasoning and is the mufta bihi (relied-upon opinion).
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Imam Abu Hanifa: no qada is required. This is the view presented in the matn and supported by the detailed distinction.
Although the Sahibayn have a valid argument, the predominant view is Abu Hanifa's.

The Rulings and Conditions of I'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of i'tikaf, explain its legal ruling, list its conditions and pillars, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute regarding fasting.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is a spiritual retreat in the mosque; its minimum is one hour, and fasting is required only for obligatory i'tikaf.

This lesson unpacks the chapter on i'tikaf from Kanz al-Daqaiq. You will learn its linguistic and legal meaning, the ruling (sunnah muakkadah), the conditions (mosque, intention, etc.), and the critical dispute over whether fasting is a condition. Guided Path
1

The text gives the root 'akafa and Quranic usage; legally it is staying in a mosque with intention.

2
Identify the ruling and its categories
I'tikaf is sunnah muakkadah (emphatically recommended), sunnat kifaya, and is divided into obligatory (if vowed), sunnah (last ten of Ramadan), and mustahabb (other times).
The text mentions it is sunnat kifaya by consensus for the last ten days.
3
List the pillars and conditions
The pillar (rukn) is the stay (lubth). Conditions include: mosque (masjid jama'ah for obligatory, any mosque for nafl), intention (niyyah), sanity, and Islam.

For obligatory i'tikaf, Hanafi holds fasting is a condition (based on hadith); Shafi'i disagrees. For nafl, intra-Hanafi: al-Hasan from Abu Hanifa says fasting is needed (min one day); Sahibayn say no (min one hour). Zahir al-Riwayah follows Sahibayn.

Determine the mufta bihi and practical implications
The relied-upon position is that fasting is not a condition for nafl i'tikaf; minimum is one hour. For obligatory i'tikaf, fasting is required and it must be in a masjid jama'ah.
Best places for i'tikaf: Masjid al-Haram, then Prophet's mosque, then Bayt al-Maqdis, then jami' mosques.

Conditions of Consecutive Fasting in Ẓihār Expiation
Explains the conditions of consecutive fasting for ẓihār expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid consecutive fasting in ẓihār expiation and identify which interruptions (Ramadan, Eid, menses, postpartum bleeding, illness) require restarting or are excused.

Core Idea
Consecutive fasting for ẓihār must be free of prohibited days, but valid excuses like menstruation do not break the sequence.

This passage details the conditions for the fasting option in ẓihār expiation when one cannot free a slave. It explains that the two months must be consecutive and cannot include Ramadan, the two Eids, or the days of Tashreeq. Natural interruptions like menstruation are excused, but postpartum bleeding and illness require restarting the fast. Understanding these conditions ensures the expiation is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
The ruling: if one cannot free a slave, he must fast two consecutive months. The Quranic proof is from Surah al-Nisa (4:92).
2
Understand the condition of consecutiveness
The word 'mutatabi'ayn' implies uninterrupted sequence. Ramadan and prohibited days cannot fall within the two months because they would break the sequence or invalidate the fast.
3
Examine natural interruptions
Menstruation does not break consecutiveness because it is unavoidable. Postpartum bleeding and illness do break it because they are avoidable (one can find two months without them).
4
Determine the duration
Fasting by lunar months suffices even if incomplete; otherwise sixty full days are required. If fifty-nine days are completed and one breaks, restart is needed.
5
Note the exception for ability to free a slave
If during the fast one becomes able to free a slave before sunset of the last day, freeing is obligatory and the fast becomes voluntary.

Effect of Intercourse on Consecutive Fasting for Ẓihār: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether intercourse during the two-month fast requires restarting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) requires restarting the two-month fast if any intercourse occurs during the period, and evaluate the counter-argument of Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Any intercourse during the two-month fast breaks consecutiveness because the Quran requires the fast to be completely free of sexual contact before expiation.

This passage addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: whether nocturnal or forgetful intercourse during the two-month fast for ẓihār requires restarting. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that any intercourse (night or day, intentional or forgetful) breaks the sequence, citing Quran 58:3. Abu Yusuf argues that only intentional daytime intercourse breaks it because the fast itself is not invalidated. Abu Yusuf argues that nocturnal intercourse does not invalidate the fast, so it does not break consecutiveness; the condition only requires the fast to be before the first contact, not to be free of contact during.

The thamarah: under the majority view, any intercourse requires restart; under Abu Yusuf's view, nocturnal intercourse is permitted without restart. The settled position is the majority view.

Commentary
This passage explains the fasting option for ẓihār expiation when one cannot find or afford a slave to free. The Quranic proof is from Surah al-Nisa' (4:92) which prescribes two consecutive months of fasting. The key conditions: 1) Consecutiveness (tataabu‘): the two months must be uninterrupted. Ramadan cannot be included because it is obligated by Allah and cannot serve for ẓihār. Likewise, the days of Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the Days of Tashreeq (11th-13th of Dhul Hijjah) are prohibited for fasting and break consecutiveness. If a woman menstruates during the fast, it does not break consecutiveness because it is unavoidable. However, if she experiences postpartum bleeding or illness, she must restart because she can find two months without them. 2) Intercourse during the fast: if a man has intercourse with his wife during these two months, the sequence is broken. The Hanafi scholarship splits: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that any intercourse (night or day, intentional or forgetful) requires restarting the two months, because the condition is that the fasting be completely before sexual contact (Quran 58:3). Abu Yusuf holds that nocturnal intercourse does not break the sequence because it does not invalidate the fast; only intentional daytime intercourse does. The majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is that the verse 'before they touch' requires all fasting to precede any touching, so any touching during the period breaks the order. If he simply breaks the fast (e.g., eats) without intercourse, he also must restart. The passage also mentions that if after completing two months of fasting the person acquires the means to free a slave before sunset of the last day, he must free a slave and his fasting becomes voluntary (nafl).

Key Terms
تتابع (tataabu‘): Consecutiveness of days in fasting; the two months must be uninterrupted except for valid reasons like menstruation.
أيام منهية (ayyām manhiyya): Prohibited days for fasting: Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the days of Tashreeq (11th-13th of Dhul Hijjah).
استئناف (isti'nāf): Restarting the two-month fast from the beginning due to an interruption.

Conditions of the Fasting Expiation for Zihar
Explains the conditions for the fasting expiation of zihar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the validity of the two-month fast for zihar expiation and identify when the fast must be restarted.

Core Idea
The zihar fast requires two consecutive months free from Ramadan, Eid, and monthly cycles to be valid.

This lesson unpacks the framework for the fasting expiation of zihar. The Qur'an mandates two consecutive months of fasting before marital relations can resume. But not every fast counts—certain days are excluded, and certain interruptions require a full restart. We will trace these conditions through the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation and its sequence
The expiation for zihar is fasting two consecutive months. This is the second option after freeing a slave.
The order is fixed: free slave → fast → feed, but only if unable.
2
List prohibited days that cannot be included
Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the days of Tashriq cannot be used because they are either obligatory or prohibited for fasting.
These days break the consecutiveness if they occur within the two months.
3
Examine exceptions for women's cycles and illness
Menstruation and postpartum bleeding do not break consecutiveness for expiations other than zihar, but for zihar they do, because it is possible to find two clean months.
Illness also requires restart in zihar.
4
Determine the minimum duration: lunar months vs 60 days
Fasting by lunar months suffices even if incomplete (29+29=58 days), but if counting days, 60 full days are required.
Breaking on day 59 requires restart.
5
Identify acts that necessitate restarting
Breaking the fast intentionally or forgetfully, or having intercourse (even at night or forgetfully) requires restarting the two months from scratch.
This is the subject of an intra-madhhab dispute covered in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the rulings for the expiation (kaffarah) of zihar. It opens by establishing the basic rule: the expiation for zihar is fasting two consecutive months. The author immediately addresses exceptions: these two months cannot include Ramadan (which cannot be used for zihar expiation because it is already obligatory), the two Eids, and the days of Tashriq (11th-13th Dhul Hijjah) because fasting on those days is prohibited. However, for zihar, she must restart if she menstruates or becomes ill because it is possible to find two consecutive months free of such impediments. Breaking the fast intentionally or due to forgetful intercourse requires restarting the fast. There is a dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that both breaking the fast and intercourse (even at night) require restarting. Abu Yusuf argues that intercourse does not break consecutiveness because it does not invalidate the fast; only breaking the fast does. For the slave, only fasting is allowed; the master cannot feed or free on his behalf because the slave owns no property. The Intra-Madhhab Dispute on Intercourse During the Zihar Fast
Explains the disagreement on whether intercourse breaks the consecutiveness of the zihar fast.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding intercourse during the zihar fast and justify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The Quranic condition 'before they touch each other' makes the fast contingent on avoiding intercourse entirely, overriding analogy with regular fasting.

This lesson zeroes in on a key point of intra-madhhab ikhtilaf: does intercourse during the two-month fast of zihar require restarting? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say yes; Abu Yusuf says no. We will examine the proofs on both sides and see how the text of the Qur'an settles the matter.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
The issue is whether intercourse (even at night or forgetfully) during the zihar fast breaks the consecutiveness and requires restart.
2
Present the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (at-tarafayn)
They hold that any intercourse during the two months requires restart because the Qur'an says the fast must be 'min qabli an yatamassa' – before they touch each other. This implies the entire fast must be free from intercourse.
Forgetful intercourse during the day also requires restart, unlike in regular fasting.
3
Present the position of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf argues that intercourse does not invalidate the fast itself (since it occurs at night), so the sequence of days is not broken. Only breaking the fast (eating/drinking) should require restart.
Al-Shafi'i agrees with Abu Yusuf.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The text states that the condition is explicit in the Qur'an: the fast must be before and free from intercourse. Since the condition is not met, restart is wajib. Analogy with other fasts is overridden by the specific text.
The key is that the condition is not just about the fast being valid, but about the sequence itself.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: a husband who has intercourse at night during the fast must restart according to Abu Hanifa, but not according to Abu Yusuf.
The mufta bihi is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

There is a dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that both breaking the fast and intercourse (even at night) require restarting. Abu Yusuf argues that intercourse does not break consecutiveness because it does not invalidate the fast; only breaking the fast does. The proof for Abu Hanifa is Quran 58:3 (before they touch each other), which requires the fast to be before and free from intercourse. For the slave, only fasting is allowed; the master cannot feed or free on his behalf because the slave owns no property. The amount of feeding (if unable to fast) is half a sa' of wheat or a sa' of dates/barley per poor person, based on the hadith of Salamah ibn Sakhr al-Bayadi. Value (qimah) is not accepted according to the Hanafi school, contrary to the other schools. However, combining different types of food (e.g., wheat and barley) to complete the amount is permissible. If someone authorizes another to feed on his behalf, it is valid. The passage includes detailed proofs from Quran, hadith, and rational arguments (qiyas, istihsan) and demonstrates the Hanafi method of deriving rulings.

Key Terms
الطرفان (at-tarafayn): Refers to the two Imams: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, who held the same view on this issue.
التتابع (at-tatabu'): Consecutiveness of fasting days; the two months must be fasted continuously without interruption.
المسيس (al-masis): Sexual intercourse; it is a condition for the fast of zihar to be free from it until completion.
