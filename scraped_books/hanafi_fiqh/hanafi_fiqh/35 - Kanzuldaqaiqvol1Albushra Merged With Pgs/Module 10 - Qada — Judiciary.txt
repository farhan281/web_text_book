Effect of a Judge's Ruling Based on Perjured Testimony and Absolute Property Claims
Ruling on a judge's decision based on false witnesses in marriage and its limits.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on permissibility of intercourse when a judge rules marriage based on false testimony, explain the intra-madhhab dispute, and distinguish marriage cases from absolute property claims.

Core Idea
A judge's ruling creates outward validity even if based on false testimony — your certain knowledge does not nullify the external effect.

This passage addresses a critical tension: when external legal proof contradicts inner certainty. A judge rules marriage based on false witnesses; the man knows the truth. The Hanafi school splits on the permissibility of intercourse. The passage also contrasts this with cases of absolute ownership claims, where the judge's ruling has no effect. We will explore the reasoning and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the dispute
Read the passage: a woman claims marriage, man denies, two witnesses lie, judge rules. Understand the conflicting views: Abu Hanifa and early Abu Yusuf permit intercourse outwardly; later Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and Shafi'i forbid it.
Focus on the key phrase: "قيل: حل له وطيها عند أبي حنيفة وأبي يوسف في القول الأول".
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's argument
The judge rules based on what is within his capacity (في وسعه). The witnesses appear truthful to the judge; inner truth is unknowable. The judge is commanded to act on apparent truth. Therefore, the ruling holds outwardly.
Contrast with the case where witnesses are found to be disqualified (e.g., slaves or disbelievers) – then the ruling does not take effect because their discreditation is ascertainable.
3
Analyze the opposing argument
The man has certain knowledge of falsehood. Acting on a known falsehood is impermissible. The judge erred in evidence. Thus, the ruling cannot make the intercourse permissible.
This is the reasoning of Muhammad al-Shaybani and later Abu Yusuf, supported by Imam al-Shafi'i.
4
Examine the condition for validity
The woman must be legally eligible for marriage (not already married, in iddah, or triple divorced). If she is not, the ruling does not take effect even outwardly.
This condition applies to the ruling's effectiveness.
5
Distinguish from absolute property claims (amlak mursala)
In a claim of absolute ownership without specifying a cause (sale, inheritance, etc.), the judge's ruling has no effect inwardly or outwardly. Because the cause is uncertain and cannot be established by the judge's creation unlike marriage.
If the cause is specified and can be created by the judge (like sale), there is ikhtilaf. If it cannot (like inheritance), the ruling never takes effect.

And 'he ruled for her marriage': meaning the judge ruled for the marriage between them. (Inayah) And 'he had not married her': [meaning the situation is that the man had not. (Inayah)] That is, if a woman claims that a man married her and he denies, then she brings two witnesses who testify that he married her, and the judge rules based on their testimony while the man is certain he did not marry her, it is said: it is permissible for him to have intercourse with her according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf in the first opinion. Abu Yusuf in the later opinion, which is the opinion of Muhammad and al-Shafi'i, said: it is not permissible for him to have intercourse with her, because the judge erred in the evidence, since the witnesses are liars, so it is as if they are found to be slaves or disbelievers. And for him (Abu Hanifa) that he ruled based on what was within his capacity, and the witnesses are truthful to him (the judge) even if not with Allah, because ascertaining the truth is impossible, only Allah knows it. The judge is commanded to rule based on testimony that is truthful in his estimation, unlike the case where they are found to be disbelievers, where the ruling does not take effect because ascertaining those matters is possible through signs. Here, he has thoroughly investigated the condition of the witnesses and no negligence is found. The condition is that the woman be fit for initiating marriage; if she is a married woman or in iddah of another or triple divorced, his ruling does not take effect. The issue of the text is the issue of a judge's ruling based on false testimony: it takes effect outwardly and inwardly according to Abu Hanifa. By outward effect is meant the establishment of the ruling such as enablement (tamkin), maintenance, division, etc. By inward effect is meant the establishment of ownership and permissibility between him and Allah, and the sin is on the claimant for bringing a false claim. There is no disagreement among them regarding absolute properties (amlak mursala) that the judge's ruling does not take effect. The meaning of 'mursala' is that he claims absolute ownership without mentioning a cause, saying 'this is mine' and brings evidence and the judge rules accordingly; that ruling does not take effect because there are many possible causes and none is more likely than another. If he mentions a specific cause, then if it can be established by the judge as a creation like sale or lease, then there is ikhtilaf; if it cannot be like inheritance, the ruling does not take effect by agreement. (Inayah, Mustakhlas)

Commentary
This passage discusses a nuanced scenario: a woman claims marriage, the man denies, but two witnesses testify against him, and the judge rules that marriage exists. The man is certain the witnesses are lying. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf in his earlier view) is that it is permissible for the man to have intercourse with her outwardly, because the judge's ruling is binding externally. Abu Yusuf's later view, adopted by Muhammad al-Shaybani and Imam al-Shafi'i, is that it is not permissible, because the judge's ruling cannot override certain knowledge. The passage provides the reasoning: the judge must rely on apparent evidence; he cannot know inner truth. The ruling is valid as long as the witnesses were not proven to be disqualified (e.g., slaves or disbelievers). A condition is that the woman must be legally eligible for marriage (not already married, in iddah, or triple divorced). The text also distinguishes between cases where the judge can rule effectively: in matters of absolute ownership (amlak mursala, mere claim of ownership without cause), the ruling has no effect even outwardly, because the cause is uncertain. This is an important principle: a judge's ruling can create external validity in matters of marriage but not in undetermined property claims.

Key Terms
القضاء بشهادة الزور (al-qada' bi shahadat al-zur): A judge's ruling based on false testimony
الأملاك المرسلة (al-amlak al-mursala): Absolute claims of ownership without specifying a cause (e.g., sale, inheritance)
