Establishing Lineage of a Widow's Child Within Two Years
Ruling with proof on lineage of a widow's child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the maximum period for attributing a child to a deceased husband and explain the istihsan-based proof.

Core Idea
Caution in lineage overrides fixed 'iddah: the two-year maximum for pregnancy preserves parentage.

In Hanafi fiqh, the death of a husband imposes a waiting period ('iddah) normally fixed at four months and ten days. But what if the widow gives birth after that period? This lesson unpacks a ruling where the Hanafi school extends the possibility of establishing lineage up to two years, prioritizing caution (ihtiyat) in matters of parentage. We will examine the proof based on istihsan and contrast it with the view of Imam Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The case: a widow gives birth after the normal 'iddah of death has passed. The question is whether the child's lineage is attributed to the deceased husband.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi position: the child's lineage is established if born within two years of the husband's death, regardless of the 'iddah duration.
This is the mufta bihi view.
3
Consider Zufar's opposing view
Zufar limits the window to ten months and ten days, arguing that beyond that, the 'iddah is complete and pregnancy cannot be presumed.
Zufar is a Hanafi jurist but his view is not adopted.
4
Examine the Hanafi proof
The proof is istihsan: caution in lineage (ihtiyat fi ithbat al-nasab) overrides the apparent completion of 'iddah, because the child may be hidden in the womb for up to two years.
This is a qiyas-istihsan argument.
5
Apply the ruling
If the widow does not confess that her 'iddah ended, she is presumed pregnant, and the child is attributed to the husband.
The 'iddah de facto extends until birth if within two years.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the matn phrase 'wal-mawt li-aqalla minhuma'. The commentator explains the ruling: if a woman who is in 'iddah from her husband's death gives birth within two years from the death, the child's lineage to the deceased husband is established. The condition is that the birth occurs within two years, not more. Zufar's opposing view is presented: he limits the maximum duration of pregnancy to ten months and ten days, after which the 'iddah would have ended and the child cannot be attributed because it would have been conceived after the 'iddah. However, the Hanafi school (lana: our proof) relies on the principle of caution in lineage matters (ihtiyat fi ithbat al-nasab). Since the child can remain in the womb for up to two years (a recognized Hanafi position), if the woman does not admit that her 'iddah is over, she is presumed to be pregnant, and the passage of months does not end her 'iddah. Thus lineage is established within the two-year window.

Key Terms
معتدة الموت (mu'taddah al-mawt): A woman in 'iddah (waiting period) due to her husband's death, which is normally four months and ten days.
نسب (nasab): Lineage or paternity, the legal relationship between a child and his father.
زفر (Zufar): Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl, a major Hanafi jurist from Kufa, a student of Abu Hanifa, often differed on subsidiary issues.

Establishing Lineage through Acknowledgment: Inheritance and the Role of Istihsan
Ruling on paternity acknowledgment and inheritance for mother and child with qiyas vs istihsan analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on inheritance when a man acknowledges paternity and explain the role of istihsan in overriding qiyas.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a child does not automatically acknowledge the mother as a wife; inheritance requires additional proof of her being free and Muslim.

This passage presents a scenario where a man claims a boy as his son and then dies. The boy's mother claims to be his wife. The Hanafi ruling on their inheritance combines two key legal reasoning methods: qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference). Understanding this tension is central to mastering Hanafi methodology in family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the claims
Read the opening scenario: a man says 'he is my son' about a boy, then dies. The boy's mother claims she is the man's wife and the boy is his son from her.
The mother's claim is about both marriage and motherhood.
2
State the ruling for the mother and child
The text rules that both inherit if the mother is known to be free and Muslim. If her freedom is unknown, she does not inherit.
The child inherits regardless of the mother's status because the acknowledgment establishes paternity.
3
Trace the qiyas argument that denies the mother inheritance
Qiyas says: lineage can be established through void marriage or shubha, so acknowledging the child does not imply acknowledging the mother as a wife. Therefore, by analogy to cases where paternity is established without marriage, she should not inherit.
The 'asl' (original case) is acknowledgement without marriage, and the 'far' (new case) is the mother claiming inheritance.
4
Understand the istihsan argument that grants inheritance
Istihsan overrides qiyas here because the mother is known to be free and Muslim, making a valid marriage the normal cause of lineage. The acknowledgment is therefore interpreted as confirming that marriage, unless evidence to the contrary appears.
This shows Hanafi preference for equity (istihsan) over strict analogy.
5
Apply the three-rank system for lineage
The supplement lists three ranks: marriage (automatic, cannot be negated by denial), umm walad (automatic, but can be negated by denial), and slave woman (requires claim in Hanafi, contrary to Shafi'i). This clarifies why the mother's status matters.
The third rank explains the Shafi'i disagreement mentioned in the text.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on establishing lineage presents a complex case of paternity acknowledgment and its implications. The author begins with a scenario: a man says 'he is my son' regarding a boy, then dies. The boy's mother claims she was the man's wife and the boy is his son from her. The ruling is that both the mother and the boy inherit from the dead man, provided the mother is known to be freeborn and Muslim. However, if the mother's freedom is uncertain and an heir claims she was a slave concubine (umm walad) of the dead man's father, then she does not inherit. The text then explains that the boy's lineage is established from the man because he claimed paternity, and that claim is sufficient; the child is identified by the midwife's testimony. The timing is crucial: if she gave birth within six months of the acknowledgment, it's certain, but if later, it's not binding. The text then contrasts qiyas (analogy) with istihsan (equity). Qiyas would deny the mother inheritance because lineage can be established through void marriage or intercourse with shubha, so acknowledgment of the child doesn't imply acknowledgment of marriage. But istihsan favors inheritance because the mother is known to be free and Muslim, and a valid marriage is the usual basis for lineage, so the acknowledgment is interpreted as confirming that marriage unless proven otherwise. The text further clarifies that ignorance of her freedom (not just an heir's statement) is enough to bar inheritance, and that apparent freedom based on the abode (dar al-Islam) can repel slavery but not entitle inheritance. Examples are given: if the heir claims she was Christian or a slave wife, she does not inherit, but she may be entitled to a dower (mahr al-mithl) if intercourse is confirmed. Finally, a summary of three ranks of lineage establishment is provided: (1) marriage – lineage is automatic and not denied by mere negation; (2) umm walad – lineage is automatic but can be negated by denial; (3) slave woman – lineage requires a claim according to the Hanafi school, unlike the Shafi'i view.

Key Terms
ثبوت النسب (thubut al-nasab): The legal establishment of a child's lineage to a father.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child from her master, thereby gaining certain rights and the child's freedom at the master's death.
استحسان (istihsan): A method of juristic preference that abandons strict analogy (qiyas) for a more equitable ruling.
