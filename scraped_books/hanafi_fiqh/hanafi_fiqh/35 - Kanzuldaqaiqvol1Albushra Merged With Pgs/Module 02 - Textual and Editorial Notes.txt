Symbols of the Hanafi Masters: Decoding Abbreviations in Kanz al-Daqa'iq
Commentary on symbols for scholars and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the scholars referenced by the abbreviations in Kanz al-Daqa'iq and explain the function of the letter ط (ta') as a marker for absolute (mutlaq) rulings.

Core Idea
Every letter in the margin marks a master; the letter ط signals a ruling without conditions.

In classical Hanafi texts, abbreviations are not mere shortcuts—they are precise identifiers that link each ruling to its authority. Kanz al-Daqa'iq uses a system of single letters to denote which scholar's opinion is being cited. This lesson unpacks these symbols, focusing on the special use of the letter ط (ta') to indicate absolute (mutlaq) rulings—those stated without qualification. Understanding this code is your first step toward navigating the text independently.

Guided Path
1
List the scholars and their letters
Identify the standard abbreviations: ح (ha) for Abu Hanifa, س (sin) for Abu Yusuf, م (mim) for Muhammad al-Shaybani, ز (zay) for Zufar, ف (fa) for al-Shafi'i, ك (kaf) for Malik, د (dal) for Ahmad, and و (waw) for a narration from Hanafi scholars or a weaker analogy. Note any variations or corrupted symbols in the source text.
The source text may have OCR issues; verify against known standard symbols.
2
Understand the role of ط
The letter ط is added to a ruling to indicate that it is absolute (mutlaq), meaning it is stated without any qualifying conditions or restrictions. This is important for distinguishing between rulings that are unqualified and those that may have hidden restrictions.
This symbol is specific to this book and helps students identify 'unrestricted' positions.
3
Analyze the example given
The passage provides an example: One who wakes up and finds wetness on his clothes. According to the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), ghusl is absolutely required, whether the wetness is mani or madhi. According to Abu Yusuf, no ghusl is needed if it is madhi. This example is marked with ط to show that the book's statement—ghusl required—is presented as absolute, despite the known restriction from Abu Yusuf.
This illustrates how an absolute ruling in the text may still have a qualified variant from another imam.
4
Apply the concept to reading the text
When you encounter a ruling with the letter ط, recognize that the author intended it to be understood without further conditions. If you later see a restriction mentioned elsewhere (e.g., in a commentary or another ruling), you know that the original text presented it as mutlaq. This is crucial for correct understanding and fatwa.
In practice, a ruling marked ط is the primary position stated, but the mufta bihi may differ.

Commentary
This passage is part of the introduction (khutbah) of the book, explaining the symbols used throughout the text. The author describes the system of abbreviations: letters like 'ح' for Abu Hanifa, 'س' for Abu Yusuf, 'م' for Muhammad al-Shaybani, 'ز' for Zufar, 'ف' for al-Shafi'i, 'ك' for Malik, 'د' for Ahmad, and 'و' for a narration from Hanafi scholars or a weaker analogy. The commentator also highlights the use of the letter 'ط' (ta) to denote rulings that are stated without any qualifying conditions or exceptions, i.e., absolute rulings. An example is given: a person who wakes up and finds wetness on his clothes: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, ghusl is necessary regardless of whether the wetness is mani (semen) or madhi (pre-ejaculatory fluid). But according to Abu Yusuf, if it is madhi, no ghusl is required. This illustrates an 'absolute' ruling in the book.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A ruling that is stated without any qualifying condition or restriction in the text.
