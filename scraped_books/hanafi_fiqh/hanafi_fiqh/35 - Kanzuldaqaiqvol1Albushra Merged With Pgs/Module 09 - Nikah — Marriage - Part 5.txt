The Hanafi Hierarchy of Custody (Hadhanah)
Hierarchy of custodians after separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of custodians for a young child in the Hanafi school and identify the conditions that disqualify the mother.

Core Idea
The mother is the primary custodian unless she remarries, apostatizes, or is immoral; then the maternal grandmother takes precedence.

After separation, custody is not automatically awarded to the father. The Hanafi school establishes a detailed hierarchy prioritizing the mother and then relatives through the female line. This lesson examines that hierarchy, its proof from the Prophet's hadith, and an intra-madhhab dispute about the maternal aunt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary custodian and its conditions
State that the mother is the most entitled to custody before and after separation, unless she is apostate, immoral (fajirah) and untrustworthy, or marries a stranger. The last condition is by consensus.
2
Analyze the dalil (proof)
Locate the hadith of the woman who came to the Prophet (PBUH). Identify the operative phrase: 'You are more entitled to him as long as you do not marry.' In Hanafi usul, al-amr yufidu al-wujub, but here it establishes a prior right, not an obligation. Also note the rational argument: mother is more compassionate and capable.
The hadith is dalil naqli; the rational argument is istidlal bi al-ma'qul.
3
Trace the hierarchy after the mother
List in order: maternal grandmother, paternal grandmother, full sister (same parents), consanguine sister (same father), uterine sister (same mother). Explain that the preference follows closeness to the mother's lineage.
The maternal grandmother is preferred over paternal grandmother because custody is traced through the mother's side.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Note that Zufar (and a narration from Muhammad) give the maternal aunt precedence over the paternal grandmother. State the Hanafi tarjih: the paternal grandmother is preferred because she is from 'the mothers' (inherits one-sixth) and has more compassion.
The mufta bihi is the paternal grandmother over the maternal aunt.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its authority
Identify that the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah) is the paternal grandmother first, then the full sister, etc. This is based on Fath and Ayni, and is the operative ruling for fatwa.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on custody (hadanah) outlines the hierarchy of who is most entitled to physically care for a young child after separation of parents. The ruling begins with the mother: she is the primary custodian, whether married or divorced, as long as she does not marry another man (by consensus), and provided she is not an apostate or immoral. The proof is the well-known hadith of the woman who came to the Prophet (peace be upon him) and he ruled, 'You are more entitled to him as long as you do not marry.' Additionally, the mother is considered more compassionate and capable. She is not forced to take custody unless no other close relative exists, whereas the father can be compelled to take the child when the mother is no longer needed. A crucial point: the mother is entitled to payment for custody and breastfeeding from the father, separate from maintenance. Then the text lists the order after the mother: (1) maternal grandmother, (2) paternal grandmother, (3) full sister, (4) consanguine sister (father's side), and then (5) uterine sister (mother's side). There is a dispute: Zufar and in one narration Malik and Muhammad from Abu Hanifa prefer the maternal aunt over the paternal grandmother. However, the text argues for the paternal grandmother because she is a mother (umm) and inherits the mother's share, has more compassion, and is the origin of the child. Practical implication: This hierarchy is used by Hanafi judges to determine custody when the mother is unavailable or disqualified.

Key Terms
الحضانة (al-hadanah): The physical care and upbringing of a young child, including housing, feeding, and daily needs.
أم الأم (umm al-umm): Maternal grandmother; she is the next in line for custody after the mother.
فاجرة (fajirah): A woman who is openly sinful or immoral, rendering her untrustworthy for custody.

Providing a Servant for the Wife
Commentary on the ruling to provide a servant for the wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a husband must provide a servant for his wife and identify the relied-upon Hanafi position on the number of servants.

Core Idea
A husband must provide a servant for his free wife if he is wealthy, but only for her owned servant and in exchange for her service.

This lesson expands on the ruling that a husband may be obliged to provide a servant (khadim) for his wife. The obligation is not absolute; it depends on the wife's freedom, the husband's wealth, the ownership of the servant, and the wife's own performance of household duties. We will explore the conditions, the underlying rationale, and an intra-Hanafi difference regarding how many servants must be provided.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and its Ruling
Recognize that the act is the husband's provision of maintenance for the wife's servant (nafaqat al-khadim). The ruling is wajib under certain conditions.
The text begins by linking this to the wife's maintenance obligation.
2
List the Conditions for Obligation
Note the conditions: wife is free (not a slave), husband is wealthy (musir by nisab of sadaqah immunity), servant is owned by the wife and has no other occupation, and the wife performs household duties (since the servant's maintenance is in exchange for service).
These conditions are derived from the commentary.
3
Distinguish Between Servant's and Wife's Maintenance
Understand that the wife's personal maintenance is for her confinement (habs), while the servant's maintenance is for her service. If the wife refrains from household work, she loses the right to servant's maintenance.
This highlights a key difference in the nature of the two obligations.
4
Analyze the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Number of Servants
Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad hold that one servant suffices, while Abu Yusuf argues for two (indoor and outdoor). Identify the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) as one servant.
The majority view of the two companions is preferred.
5
Apply the Ruling to a Scenario
Given a free wife with an owned servant and a wealthy husband, determine that one servant's maintenance is wajib. If the servant is not owned or the husband is poor, the obligation falls away.
Also note that the husband cannot impose a servant without the wife's consent.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the ruling that a husband must provide a servant for his wife under specific conditions. The commentator clarifies that the obligation is tied to the wife's freedom and the husband's wealth (defined by the nisab of sadaqah immunity, not zakat). The servant's maintenance is compensation for service, so if the wife fails to perform household work, she forfeits it, unlike her personal maintenance which is for her confinement. The ruling applies only when the servant is owned by the wife; if it is hired or borrowed, the Zahir al-Riwayah says no maintenance is owed. The husband cannot impose a servant on her without her consent. If she is a slave, no servant is required. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad hold that one servant suffices, while Abu Yusuf argues for two (one indoor, one outdoor). The relied-upon position is one servant. If the husband is poor, no servant is owed. In case of dispute about wealth, the husband's word is taken unless the wife proves otherwise, based on the legal presumption (al-asl).

No Separation Due to Husband's Inability to Pay
Inter-madhhab dispute on maintenance-based separation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on separation due to inability to pay maintenance and evaluate the Shafi'i objection using Quranic and hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Inability to pay maintenance does not dissolve the marriage — respite, not separation, is the ruling.

This lesson addresses a critical inter-madhhab dispute: should a husband's poverty lead to judicial separation? The Hanafi school firmly holds that it does not, while the Shafi'i school disagrees. Understanding the proofs and reasoning behind these opposing views is essential for appreciating Hanafi methodology.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling: no separation occurs whether the husband is present or absent, and whether the wife demands or not. The wife is instructed to incur debt on the husband's behalf.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Al-Shafi'i holds that if the husband is present and the wife demands, the judge must separate them. Note the condition: presence and demand are triggers.
3
Follow the opponent’s proof
Examine the two hadiths cited: (1) the long hadith of Abu Hurairah including 'feed me or divorce me,' and (2) the report from al-Daraqutni. Understand how they appear to support separation.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Two counter-arguments: (a) Quran 2:280 commands respite for the debtor, and (b) separation (ibṭāl al-milk) is a greater harm than delaying maintenance. Then critique the hadith: one version shows Abu Hurairah did not hear it from the Prophet; the other chain contains a weak narrator.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafi marriage continues, wife can take loans; Shafi'i marriage is dissolved if conditions are met. Recognize which ruling is mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding whether a husband's inability to pay maintenance causes judicial separation (tafriq). The Hanafi position, stated at the outset, is that no separation occurs regardless of the husband's presence or absence, and regardless of whether the wife demands it. The husband is merely ordered to be patient, and the wife is told to incur debt on his behalf. The Shafi'i school holds that if the husband is present and the wife demands either maintenance or divorce, the judge must separate them. Their proof is a hadith narrated by Abu Hurairah, which includes the wife saying 'feed me or divorce me,' and a separate report from al-Daraqutni. The Hanafi response is twofold: first, the Quranic verse (2:280) explicitly commands giving respite to the debtor, which takes priority. Second, separation annuls the marriage contract (ibṭāl al-milk), which is a greater harm than merely delaying the wife's right by ordering her to take loans. The hadith is critiqued on two grounds: one version shows that Abu Hurairah himself said he did not hear it from the Prophet; the other chain is weak due to a disreputable narrator. Hence, the Hanafi view is upheld: the marriage remains intact, and the wife can reclaim the debt from the husband later.

Key Terms
التفريق (al-tafrīq): Judicial separation between spouses due to the husband's inability to provide maintenance.
الاستدانة (al-istidānah): The wife incurring debt on the husband's behalf to cover her maintenance.
إبطال الملك (ibṭāl al-milk): Annulment of the husband's ownership over the marriage contract.

Hiring a Wet Nurse and Mother's Right to Breastfeed
Ruling on father's obligation to hire wet nurse and mother's preference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a mother may claim wages for breastfeeding her own child according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A married mother must breastfeed for free; after divorce she may demand wages unless she is in revocable 'iddah.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq examines the father's obligation to provide a wet nurse and the mother's right to compensation. It distinguishes between a married mother, a mother in 'iddah, and a divorced mother whose 'iddah has expired. Understanding these distinctions is essential for applying the rules of nafaqah and breastfeeding in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The father must hire a wet nurse for his child (wajib), but not the mother if she is married or in revocable 'iddah.
The mother's breastfeeding when married is wajib dianatan.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verse 2:233 (وَالْوَالِدَاتُ يُرْضِعْنَ أَوْلادَهُنَّ) implies obligation when married.
Al-Marghinani cites it as the proof.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative form (al-amr) indicates wujub dianatan for a married mother, so wages are not due.
Performing an obligatory act does not give entitlement to wages.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Two narrations exist on the mother in bain 'iddah: one prohibits wages, another permits. The permissive one is adopted as zahir al-riwayah.
The opposing view argues she already receives maintenance.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The permissive view is accepted because estrangement justifies wages; this is the mufta bihi.
The text states it is the most correct narration.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the father's obligation to provide a wet nurse for his child and the mother's rights. The text first states that the father must hire a wet nurse from those present at the mother's place, but not the mother herself if she is married to him or in 'iddah. The commentary explains that a married mother is religiously obligated to breastfeed per Quran 2:233, so she cannot take wages for it. For a mother in revocable 'iddah, the marriage bond still exists, so the same rule applies. For irrevocable 'iddah, there are two narrations: one prohibits wages because she already receives maintenance, and the other permits it since the husband has estranged her and leniency is not expected; this second narration is the relied-upon position. However, if she is hired to nurse a child from another man, it is permissible. Then the passage states that after the 'iddah ends, the mother has priority for breastfeeding with wages, but only if she accepts the same wage as a stranger. If she demands more, the husband is not forced, and if a stranger offers to do it for less or free, the stranger is preferred. Finally, the commentary clarifies that custody rights are separate; the mother deserves wages for custody, and the breastfeeding wage is not dependent on a formal contract and survives the child's death as a debt.

Key Terms
منكوحة (mankuhah): A woman who is currently married to the man (the father).
معتدة (mu'taddah): A woman in her waiting period after divorce, either revocable or irrevocable.
الإرضاع (al-irda'): The act of breastfeeding a child.
الحضانة (al-hadanah): Custody of the child, which is a separate right from breastfeeding.
