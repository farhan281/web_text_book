Ruling on Entering the Grave from the Qibla Side
Ruling on entering the grave from the qibla side and what to say

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah act of entering a grave from the qibla side and identify the recommended du'a to recite.

Core Idea
Entering the grave from the qibla side and reciting the du'a is a sunnah, not a wajib.

In the Hanafi school, the burial of a deceased includes specific etiquettes derived from the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him). One of these is the direction from which the body is placed into the grave and the words recited at that moment. The passage before us gives a concise ruling with an implied proof from the sunnah, explaining the proper procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is entering the grave from the qibla side and saying the du'a. The ruling is sunnah (recommended), not obligatory.
Sunnah here means a meritorious practice; omitting it does not invalidate the burial.
2
Examine the du'a and its meaning
The du'a is 'Bismillah wa 'ala millati Rasulillah' (In the name of Allah and on the creed of the Messenger of Allah). It is a testimony, not a supplication for forgiveness.
This distinction is important to avoid misunderstanding the purpose of the du'a.
3
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof is the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) as reported in hadith. The commentary from al-Fath indicates that this was the Prophet's practice when placing a deceased.
The proof is a sunnah fi'liyyah (practical sunnah).
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a burial where it is physically difficult to enter from the qibla side. The burial remains valid, but the sunnah is missed. One should enter from the nearest accessible side.
The ruling is not wajib, so ease is considered.

Prohibition of Baked Bricks and Wood, Covering Woman's Grave
Ruling on grave building materials and covering a woman's grave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using baked bricks and wood in the grave and explain the conditions for covering a woman's grave.

Core Idea
Using baked bricks and wood in the grave is makruh tanzih; a woman's grave is covered for modesty.

This lesson from Kanz al-Daqaiq covers two related funeral rulings: the materials used in constructing the grave and the covering of a woman's grave. The matn provides concise prohibitions, which the commentary expands with underlying reasons and exceptions. Understanding the degree of prohibition (makruh vs haram) is critical for correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and materials
The matn mentions baked bricks (al-ājurr), wood (al-khashab), covering the grave of a woman (yusajjā qabrahā), and heaping soil (yuhālu al-turāb).
The prohibition is for placing these in the grave, not for use above ground.
2
State the hukm level
The word 'lā' (not) implies prohibition, but in Hanafi usul it indicates karahah tanzihiyyah (dislike), not haram, due to the presence of a necessity exception.
The ruling is makruh tanzih, not haram.
3
Identify the reason (illa)
Baked bricks involve fire and resemble strong buildings; graves are places of trial (bala'), not permanent construction. Wood is similarly disliked.
The illa is the resemblance to worldly building and unnecessary expense.
4
Recognize the exception
If the soil is loose and requires support, these materials become permissible. Necessity (darurah) lifts the dislike.
The exception is based on the general maxim 'necessity permits the prohibited'.
5
Distinguish between covering woman and man
A woman's grave is covered with a cloth to maintain modesty (sitr); a man's is not covered except for external factors like rain or snow.
The gender distinction is based on the woman's need for concealment.

The Sunnah of Raising the Grave (Tasnim)
Ruling statement on grave shape (tasnim) with proof from practice of the Prophet and caliphs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on grave shape (tasnim) and explain the evidence from the practice of the Prophet and the caliphs.

Core Idea
Raising the grave like a camel hump is the Hanafi sunnah; flat graves are disliked.

The matn from Kanz al-Daqaiq gives a concise command: heap the soil and make the grave raised (tasnim). This brief ruling carries significant legal weight, as it is based on the established practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the Rightly Guided Caliphs. In this lesson, we will unpack the evidence, contrast it with the Shafi'i position, and understand why the Hanafi school insists on a raised grave shape.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on grave shape
The matn states 'yusammam al-qabr' – the grave is made raised. This is the sunnah of tasnim.
Tasnim means raised like a camel's hump.
2
Locate the Hanafi evidence
The Hanafi position relies on reports that the Prophet's own grave was raised, as were the graves of Abu Bakr and 'Umar. This model is the primary dalil.
The evidence is from practice (fi'l) of the Prophet.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school holds that graves should be flat (musattah), citing a hadith that the Prophet made his son Ibrahim's grave flat.
Each school has a proof from prophetic action.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response
Hanafis argue that the flat shape resembles the practice of the People of the Book, and the hadith of Ibrahim's grave may have been for a specific reason or is weaker than the evidence for raising.
The response emphasizes distinguishing Muslim graves.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi
The relied-upon Hanafi position is that tasnim is sunnah, and making graves flat is makruh tanzihan.
This ruling is based on the preponderance of evidence.

Definition and Rulings of the Martyr (Shahid)
Defines the shahid and explains the ruling of ghusl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the shahid according to Hanafi fiqh, state the ruling on ghusl and prayer, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the junub and child martyr.

Core Idea
The martyr of this world is not washed as an honor, but prayer is performed; the martyr of the Hereafter is washed.

In this lesson, we analyze the legal definition of the martyr (shahid) in Hanafi fiqh and the rulings that distinguish him from other deceased. The key question is whether ghusl (ritual washing) is required. We will see that only the shahid of this world—killed unjustly in specific circumstances—is exempt from ghusl. We also explore a major intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether a junub or child martyr is washed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of the shahid
State the Hanafi definition: a Muslim, mukallaf (responsible), pure (tahir) adult killed unjustly by people of war, rebels, highway robbers, or found dead on a battlefield with a wound.
The definition includes conditions that rule out the child and the junub according to Abu Hanifa.
2
Distinguish shahid al-dunya from shahid al-akhira
Note that the shahid of this world is not washed; the shahid of the Hereafter (e.g., one who dies by drowning, fire, etc.) is washed but has the reward of a martyr.
Only shahid al-dunya gets the special burial rite.
3
State the basic ruling on ghusl, prayer, and burial
The shahid of this world: not washed, prayer is performed (contra Shafi'i), buried in his clothes except non-shroud items (e.g., weapons, cap).
The evidence is from the practice of the Prophet ﷺ at Uhud.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on junub and child
Abu Hanifa: wash them (based on Hanzalah hadith). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: do not wash (martyrdom purifies). Identify the evidence and logic for each.
The dispute hinges on whether martyrdom removes existing impurity or only prevents new impurity.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's: the junub and child martyr are washed. This is the fatwa today.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah and other later scholars adopt this view.

The Legal Ruling of the Martyr (Shahid) in the Hanafi School

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The martyr is buried unwashed with his blood, and prayer is performed over him — no one, not even a martyr, is independent of intercession.

In this lesson, we examine the legal definition and rulings of the martyr (shahid) according to the Hanafi school. You will learn the four conditions that must be satisfied for a person to be considered a martyr in the legal sense, and why the Hanafi school insists on performing the funeral prayer even for martyrs, in contrast to the Shafi'i position. The passage from al-Hidayah and its commentaries provides the proof from the Prophet's practice at Uhud.

Guided Path
1

Washing a Martyr Who Was Junub or a Minor: Intra-Hanafi Dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on washing a junub or child martyr, and apply the correct ruling according to the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires washing a junub or child martyr; the Sahibayn exempt them—the matn follows Abu Hanifa.

The matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq states that a martyr who is in a state of major impurity (junub) or a minor must be washed. However, this ruling is contested within the Hanafi school. This lesson explores the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two eminent students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding the rationale and evidence for washing or not washing such martyrs.

2
State the condition for martyrdom according to Abu Hanifa

The Sahibayn argue that martyrdom substitutes for washing just as ritual slaughter (dhabh) replaces washing for an animal's meat. They claim martyrdom removes impurity.

Abu Hanifa cites the Hadith of Hanzalah, whom the angels washed after martyrdom while junub, proving that martyrdom does not remove pre-existing janabah. He also argues that the Uhud martyrs were exempt because they were sinless, but a child has no sin to be purified, so the analogy fails.

Actually, they analogize that martyrdom removes impurity like dhabh removes blood? Their evidence is that it lifts ritual impurity. Abu Hanifa cites the story of Hanzalah who was martyred while junub and the angels washed him, indicating that martyrdom does not remove the need for washing from pre-existing janabah. He also argues that martyrdom prevents the occurrence of post-death impurity but does not remove pre-existing impurity. The passage then extends to menstruating and postpartum women, and to the child. The two companions argue that a child is more deserving of the honor because his being wronged is greater. Abu Hanifa responds that the sword (martyrdom) sufficed for the Uhud martyrs because they were pure from sin, but a child has no sin to purify, so the analogy fails; prophets are washed despite no sin, showing that washing is not based on sin removal. Thus, Abu Hanifa maintains that the child is washed.

جنب (junub): A person in a state of major ritual impurity (janabah) requiring ghusl.
الصبي (al-ṣabī): A minor (child) who is not yet legally responsible (mukallaf).
الذكاة (al-dhakāh): The Islamic method of slaughtering animals; used as analogy for martyrdom replacing washing.

At the heart is the question: does 'ushr require a minimum quantity (nisab) and the ability to store (baqa')? Abu Hanifa's position, which is the relied-upon view in the Hanafi school, holds that 'ushr is due on everything the earth produces, regardless of quantity or storability. Identify the core ruling
The matn states: 'It is obligatory on honey of 'ushr land, and on crops watered by rain and flowing water without condition of nisab and preservation...' Recognize that this is the general ruling according to Abu Hanifa.

The Sahibayn's view aligns with the majority on this point.

The commentator responds: the first hadith refers to zakat of trade, not 'ushr; the second, if authentic, refers to the sadaqah collected by the state tax collector, not the religious 'ushr.
This is a classic example of reconciling conflicting texts.

Under Abu Hanifa, 'ushr is due on small quantities and vegetables. Under the opposing view, 'ushr is only due when nisab is reached and the produce is storable.
This directly affects farmers growing vegetables or small amounts.

Natural growth is exempt from 'ushr unless the land is intentionally used for it.

In this lesson, we explore an important exception to the general rule that 'ushr is due on all produce. The Hanafi school distinguishes between items that grow spontaneously—such as firewood, reeds, and grass—and those that are cultivated. The key factor is the landowner's intention and use of the land. Understanding this exemption is crucial for correctly applying the law of agricultural tithe.

The exemption applies only when the land is not specifically taken or intended for these items. If the landowner does not designate the land for firewood, reeds, or grass, then no 'ushr is due on these items. The reasoning is that with these things, the exploitation of the land is not intended.

If the landowner deliberately turns the land into a reed plantation, firewood plantation, or grass field (i.e., designates it for these items), then 'ushr becomes obligatory. This shows that the exemption is based on the lack of intent to exploit the land for these items.

The commentary clarifies that 'reeds' (qasab) refers to Persian reeds used for pens, which are exempt. Sugarcane and medicinal reeds are not exempt because they are cultivated and have commercial value; 'ushr is due on them.
This shows that the ruling depends on how the plant is used and whether it is cultivated.

He ordered it two days before the eid before zakat on property was made obligatory, and that is correct. Commentary
This passage is an expanded commentary on the chapter of sadaqat al-fitr from Kanz al-Daqaiq. It defines the obligation as wajib (not fard, contra al-Shafi'i), applicable to a free Muslim who possesses nisab beyond basic necessities such as dwelling, clothes, furniture, horse, and weapons used for religious purposes. The hadith of Ibn Abbas provides the basis: it was prescribed to purify the fast and feed the poor. The passage addresses intra-Hanafi conflict: Imam Muhammad holds it is not obligatory on a minor because it is an act of worship, but the majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) see it as having the nature of maintenance (nafaqa) since it is paid on behalf of dependents, akin to spending on relatives. Another dispute with al-Shafi'i concerns the threshold: Shafi'i says it is fard on anyone with surplus beyond one day's food, but Hanafis require zakatable nisab (excess over basic needs). The passage clarifies that items for religious use (house, clothes, books of Islamic knowledge, Quran) are exempt from the nisab calculation. It also discusses that one must pay for himself and for those he supports (poor child, slave), even if the slave is non-Muslim. The amount is half a sa' of wheat or a sa' of barley.

صدقة الفطر (ṣadaqat al-fiṭr): A charitable due paid at the end of Ramadan; wajib upon those with nisab.
نصاب (niṣāb): Minimum wealth threshold for obligation, excluding basic necessities.
فضل عن مسكنه (faḍl ʿan maskanih): Surplus beyond one's dwelling; dwelling is exempt from nisab calculation.

Intention (Niyyah) for Fasting: Hanafi Rulings and Disputes
Matn and commentary on niyyah for fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for when and how intention is required for different types of fasts, and apply them to determine validity in cases of delayed or general intention.

Core Idea
Timing and specificity of intention depend on whether the fast is inherently specified or not.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rulings on intention (niyyah) for fasting, a key condition for validity. The passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq with commentary distinguishes between supererogatory (nafl) fasts and others (Ramadan, qada, kaffarah) in terms of when intention must be made and how specific it must be. We will explore the proofs from Quran and Hadith, and understand why the Hanafi school allows greater flexibility for Ramadan and nafl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of fast
Determine whether the fast is supererogatory (nafl), obligatory (Ramadan), or other obligatory (qada, kaffarah, specified vow). The ruling on intention changes accordingly.
Look for terms like 'النفل', 'رمضان', 'القضاء', 'الكفارات'.
2
Check the intention requirement
For nafl, intention can be from night until before legal midday, and can be absolute or for nafl. For Ramadan and specified vows, intention before noon is sufficient, and a general intention or intention of another type works. For other obligatory fasts, specific intention at night (tabyit) is mandatory.
Tabyit means making intention during the night (after Maghrib before Fajr).
3
Apply the proof
The Hanafi proof for allowing daytime intention for Ramadan is Quran 2:187: 'then complete the fast until night', where 'thumma' indicates delay. The hadith requiring tabyit is interpreted for non-specified fasts or as indicating less reward.
Understand how 'thumma' is used for legal inference (istidlal).
4
Resolve the dispute
Imam Malik requires tabyit for all obligatory fasts; Imam Shafi'i requires it for obligatory but not nafl. The Hanafi response reinterprets the hadith and gives precedence to the Quranic ease for Ramadan.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether a late intention invalidates the fast or not.

Commentary
This passage is a matn (from Kanz al-Daqa'iq) with its commentary, presenting the Hanafi rulings on intention (niyyah) for fasting. The matn begins by distinguishing supererogatory (nafl) fasts from others: for nafl, intention can be made from the night until before midday, and it can be a general intention or specifically for nafl. All other fasts (obligatory, make-up, expiations) require a specific intention made at night (tabyit). The commentary then explains these points with proofs and discussions of disagreements. For the fast of Ramadan, a Hanafi view is that intending before noon is valid, contrary to Imam Malik who requires tabyit, and to Imam Shafi'i who requires tabyit for obligatory but not nafl. The Hanafi proof is the verse 'then complete the fast until night' – the word 'thumma' (then) indicates that the resolve to fast can occur after dawn. The hadith 'no fast for one who does not intend from the night' is explained as either referring to non-specific fasts (e.g., make-up) or as negating full reward. Further, the text discusses intending a nafl fast for a day of Ramadan: according to Hanafis, this is valid and counts as Ramadan because the day is already specified for the obligatory fast, so a specific intention is not needed. If a sick or traveler intends nafl, it does not count as Ramadan because for them the day is not predetermined. The text also covers the need for intention each day (each day's fast being a separate worship) and refutes the Maliki view that one intention suffices for the whole month.

Key Terms
النية (al-niyyah): Intention; the mental resolve to perform an act of worship. In fasting, it is a condition for validity.
التبييت (al-tabyit): Making the intention for fasting during the night, i.e., before dawn (fajr).
نصف النهار (nisf al-nahar): The midpoint of the day according to Islamic law, which is calculated from dawn (fajr) to the time known as al-dahwa al-kubra (the greater forenoon, approximately when the sun fully rises). Intention for nafl is valid until that time.
المطلق (al-mutlaq): Absolute; an intention without specifying the type of fast, e.g., 'I intend to fast' without naming Ramadan or nafl. According to Hanafis, such an intention suffices for Ramadan because it is specified by the day itself.

Qada and Kaffarah for Intentionally Breaking the Fast
Stating ruling and proofs for qada and kaffarah, including disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qada and kaffarah for intentionally breaking the Ramadan fast, distinguish between cases requiring both versus only qada, and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on inzal below the pubic area.

Core Idea
Intentionally breaking the Ramadan fast requires both qada and kaffarah (like zihar), except for inzal below the pubic area where only qada is needed.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq lays out the core Hanafi ruling on expiation for breaking the fast. It also engages in a debate with Imam al-Shafi'i over whether kaffarah applies only to intercourse, and it resolves an internal Hanafi dispute regarding seminal emission without direct genital contact. By studying this passage, you will see how the school uses hadith proof and qiyas (or its limits) to determine the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and its proof
Read the opening statement: 'If one breaks the fast intentionally, he must make it up and pay expiation like the expiation of zihar.' This is the general rule for all intentional breaks. The proof is the hadith of the man who had intercourse and was instructed to free a slave, fast two months, or feed sixty poor. Note that the imperative indicates wujub (obligation).
The kaffarah is sequential: slave, then fasting, then feeding – not a free choice.

The text states: 'There is no expiation for inzal below the pubic area [i.e., between thighs, by hand, etc.].' Here only qada is required. This is the view attributed to Abu Hanifa in one narration.
This exception applies to acts that do not involve direct genital contact.
3
Understand the Shafi'i opposition and Hanafi response
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that kaffarah applies only to intercourse, not to eating or drinking. His reason: the textual proof is specific to intercourse (istimta'), so analogizing other acts (qiyas) is invalid. The Hanafi response uses the general hadith 'Whoever breaks the fast in Ramadan intentionally' and argues that kaffarah is for the sin of breaking the sanctity of the fast, not limited to intercourse.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf): Shafi'i requires kaffarah only for intercourse; Hanafi requires it for all intentional violations.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on anal intercourse and masturbation
There is a difference among the Hanafi imams. According to one narration from Abu Hanifa, inzal below the pubic area (including anal intercourse) requires only qada. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, anal intercourse (and masturbation by hand) requires both qada and kaffarah because it involves direct sexual gratification. The text declares the latter opinion 'the most correct' (al-ashah).
This is an example of an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf being resolved by tarjih (preference) based on strength of evidence.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position for fatwa (mufta bihi) is that of the Sahibayn: kaffarah is required for anal intercourse and for masturbation by hand. For other forms of inzal below the pubic area (e.g., rubbing between thighs), only qada is required. For eating, drinking, etc., both qada and kaffarah are required.
This ruling applies only to Ramadan fasts; for non-Ramadan fasts, only qada is required, no kaffarah.

Breaking Fast through Non-Oral Passages: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explaining rulings and Hanafi disputes for non-oral intake

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether enema, snuff, ear drops, wound medication, urethral drops, and vaginal drops break the fast, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on these rulings.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: any substance entering the body and reaching the stomach breaks the fast; the Sahibayn: only the mouth matters.

This lesson addresses a classic fiqh issue: does the fast break only by eating and drinking through the mouth, or also by substances entering through other passages? The Hanafi school has a well-known debate between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. We will examine the textual evidence and analogical reasoning behind each position, including specific cases like ear drops and wound medication, as well as the controversial rulings on urethral and vaginal drops.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their default ruling per the matn
The matn states that if one has an enema, snuffs medicine, drips oil into the ear, or treats a deep wound with medicine that reaches the stomach or brain, the fast is broken and qada is required without kaffarah. For urethral drops, it says 'la' (does not break).
Note the textual variation: 'aqtara' vs 'qattara' is a grammatical issue, not affecting the ruling.
2
Understand the basis (dalil) cited by the author
The author cites the hadith: 'الفطر مما دخل والوضوء مما خرج' (Breaking the fast is by what enters, and wudu is by what exits) and the reasoning that the arrival of something beneficial to the body into the stomach constitutes the meaning of breaking the fast.
The hadith is mursal and zanni al-thubut, but Abu Hanifa considers it general and applies it to non-oral routes.
3
Trace Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that the criterion for breaking the fast is the arrival of a beneficial substance into the stomach, regardless of the entrance route. This is supported by the literal meaning of the hadith, which does not specify the mouth.
The qiyas is on eating/drinking: the illa (effective cause) is the arrival of benefit to the stomach.
4
Note the Sahibayn's objection
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the fast only breaks by substances entering through the natural and primary passage, i.e., the mouth. Other routes are not analogous to eating and drinking.
They restrict the meaning of 'dakhala' (enters) to the mouth, based on customary understanding.
5
Analyze further disputes on urethral and vaginal drops
For urethral drops: Abu Hanifa says it does not break (whether water or oil), Abu Yusuf says it does, and Muhammad is inconsistent. The correct view is with Abu Hanifa, based on the lack of a direct passage to the stomach. For vaginal drops: the correct view is that it breaks the fast, differing from some opinions.
The dispute on urethra hinges on whether there is a passage between the bladder and the stomach.

Disliked and Permissible Acts for the Fasting Person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which acts are disliked (makruh tanzih) and which are permissible (mubah) for a fasting person according to the Hanafi school, and explain the condition for chewing gum and the evidence for siwak's permissibility.

Core Idea
Tasting and chewing without excuse are disliked; kohl, oil, and siwak are permissible, with siwak allowed at all times.

Fasting requires not only avoiding nullifiers but also avoiding disliked acts. This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq clarifies the boundary between makruh and mubah, including a condition for chewing gum and a disputed ruling on siwak. We will walk through the rulings and the proofs behind them.

Identify the general rule

List the permissible acts

Malik disliked wet siwak due to risk of breaking fast; Shafi'i disliked it in the evening to preserve the 'good smell' of fasting. The Hanafi school rejects both positions, citing the Prophet's frequent siwak use while fasting.
The Hanafi proof is a hadith narrated by Abd ibn Amir.

Synthesize the ruling
The mufta bihi is absolute permissibility of siwak at any time. This is based on explicit prophetic practice (zahir al-riwayah).

Rulings on substances entering the body through non-oral routes
Stating rulings for enema, nose drops, ear drops, wound medicine, and urethral drops with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on enemas, nose drops, ear drops, wound medicine, and urethral drops during fasting, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Any substance reaching the interior invalidates the fast, except via the urethra according to Abu Hanifa.

Fasting is one of the pillars of Islam, and its validity depends on refraining from anything that enters the body in a way that constitutes 'breaking the fast.' The classical Hanafi texts discuss not only the obvious cases of eating and drinking but also medical procedures that introduce substances through non-oral routes. This lesson examines the rulings on enemas, nose drops, ear drops, wound medicine, and urethral drops, highlighting the internal debate among the early Hanafi authorities and the relied-upon position for practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts in question
List the five medical procedures mentioned: enema, sniffing medicine (nose drops), ear drops, wound dressing with medicine reaching the interior, and urethral drops.
Each case involves introducing a substance into the body through a non-oral route.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its proof
Abu Hanifa holds that all these acts break the fast if the substance reaches the interior (jowf) or brain. His proof is the hadith 'al-fitr mimma dakhala' and the rationale that arrival of something that reforms the body constitutes the meaning of breaking the fast.
The criterion is actual arrival, not the specific passage.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and its proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that only substances entering through the mouth break the fast. They argue that the natural passage for food and drink is the mouth; other routes are not analogous.
They make an exception for the mouth alone.
4
Analyze the difference in principle
The dispute hinges on whether the ruling is based on the passage (mouth) or the arrival at the interior. Abu Hanifa emphasizes the latter; the Sahibayn restrict it to the former.
This has implications for all non-oral routes.
5
Determine the mufta bihi for each sub-case
For enema, nose drops, ear drops, and wound medicine, the mufta bihi is with Abu Hanifa (fast broken, qada required, no kaffarah). For urethral drops, the mufta bihi is also with Abu Hanifa (fast not broken).
The commentator (Ramz al-Haqaiq) confirms the soundness of Abu Hanifa's view.
6
Apply to a modern scenario
Consider a fasting patient using a nebulizer for asthma. According to the mufta bihi, the fast is broken because the mist reaches the lungs (interior).
This shows the ongoing relevance of the principle.

Permissible Exemptions: Illness and Travel in Ramadan Fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for breaking the fast due to illness and travel, and distinguish the Hanafi position from that of Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Breaking the fast is permissible only when there is genuine fear of harm based on strong probability, not mere discomfort.

The chapter on 'awāriḍ (impediments) lists eight excuses that permit a Muslim not to fast in Ramadan. This lesson focuses on two: illness and travel. The Hanafi school sets a clear condition: for illness, the fear of increased illness or delayed recovery must be based on ghalabat al-ẓann (overwhelming presumption); for travel, one must have started the journey before dawn. Ghalabat al-ẓann (overwhelming presumption) is required, based on experience, signs, or a reliable Muslim doctor's report.
Not every ailment qualifies.

Hanafi: fear of increased illness or slow recovery suffices. Shafi'i: only fear of death or loss of limb suffices.

Abu Hanifa uses general fear of increase; the two companions add inability to stand in prayer as a sign.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on kissing while fasting based on security from desire and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the hadith of Aisha.

Core Idea
Kissing is permissible if secure from desire; otherwise it is disliked – the Prophet's example shows the condition.

The second part of the passage addresses a common question: can a fasting person kiss his spouse? The Hanafi school makes a precise distinction based on one's ability to control desire. The proof comes from the hadith of Aisha, which shows the Prophet's action but also his qualifying statement. The Shafi'i school permits kissing absolutely, but the Hanafi response uses the hadith's wording to impose a condition. The passage also equates direct skin-to-skin contact (mubashara) to kissing, with a difference from Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on kissing
Kissing is permissible (mubah) if one is secure from desire – i.e., confident it will not lead to intercourse or ejaculation. If not secure, it becomes disliked (makruh).
The ruling applies to both husband and wife.
2
Cite the dalil (proof) and analyze its structure
The hadith from Aisha: 'The Prophet used to kiss while fasting and intimately touch while fasting, but he was the most in control of his desire.' The action shows permissibility, but the qualifying phrase indicates conditionality.
The dalil is ahad (zanni al-thubut) and its meaning (dalala) also zanni, requiring interpretation.
3
Identify the wajh al-dalalah (point of evidence)
The operative phrase is 'he was the most in control of his desire'. This implies that for those who cannot control desire, the action is not recommended even if not absolutely prohibited.
The Hanafi school uses this to differentiate between secure and insecure states.
4
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i permits kissing absolutely, arguing that the Prophet's action without explicit prohibition indicates general permissibility. The Hanafi response is that the qualification shows the ruling is not absolute.
The thamarah al-khilaf: a Hanafi who kisses without security may break fast if arousal leads to ejaculation; Shafi'i sees no issue.
5
Apply to mubashara (direct contact)
The passage states that touching (al-mass) is like kissing, and direct contact (al-mubashara) is like kissing in the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwaya). However, Muhammad al-Shaybani differs, considering mubashara more problematic.
Mubashara is defined as both undressed and the man placing his private part on the woman's private part. The majority of the school still equates it to kissing in ruling.

I'tikaf involves seclusion in the mosque for worship, but daily needs like eating and sleeping naturally arise. The Hanafi school clarifies which acts are permissible and which are disliked, balancing practicality with the sanctity of the mosque. This lesson explores the rulings from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, including the evidence from hadith and differences with other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts in question
List the acts: eating, drinking, sleeping, buying/selling (muba'ah), and bringing the sold item (ihdar al-mabi'). Note that these are considered in the context of the mu'takif performing i'tikaf.
2
State the default ruling for each act
For eating, drinking, sleeping, and necessary buying/selling: they are mubah (permissible) because they do not contradict the purpose of i'tikaf. The Prophet (peace be upon him) ate in the mosque. For buying/selling done for trade, it is makruh tahrimi. Bringing the sold item into the mosque is also makruh tahrimi if it occupies space.
The disliked classification is karahah tahrimiyah, meaning it is close to haram.
3
Distinguish between necessity and trade
Buying food or other necessities is permissible because the mu'takif may need them and cannot easily leave. Buying for profit (tijarah) is disliked because it involves worldly commerce in a place of worship. The hadith 'Keep your mosques away from... your buying and selling' is interpreted as referring to trade, not essential transactions.
If the mu'takif cannot find someone to bring food, he may go out to get it out of necessity without invalidating i'tikaf.
4
Identify the ruling on bringing the sold item
Bringing the sold item (al-mabi') into the mosque is disliked if it occupies space and distracts from worship. Small items like dirhams, dinars, or books that do not occupy space are not disliked. The dislike is tahrimi because it compromises the mosque's purpose.
The text specifies that bringing the item is disliked, but the act of buying/selling itself may be permissible if necessary.
5
Recognize intra- and inter-madhhab differences
Intra-Hanafi: Ibn al-Kamal holds that eating, drinking, and sleeping in the mosque are absolutely not disliked (no karahah). Inter-madhhab: Al-Shafi'i allows the mu'takif to go home to eat, while Hanafi says eating in the mosque is sufficient and leaving invalidates i'tikaf. Malik and Ahmad generally dislike all buying/selling in the mosque, while Hanafi distinguishes necessity from trade.
These differences highlight the role of textual interpretation and principles like istihsan.

Silence and Speech in I'tikaf: Categories and Rulings
Ruling with proof on the prohibition of silence as worship in i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on silence and speech during i'tikaf and apply the distinction between intended worship, obligatory silence from evil, and permissible good speech.

Core Idea
Intentional silence as worship is prohibited in i'tikaf; only silence from evil is obligatory, and good speech is permissible.

In this lesson, we examine a ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq that distinguishes between three types of silence and speech during i'tikaf. You will learn how the Hanafi school differentiates between silence intended as a devotion (which is disliked) and silence from evil (which is obligatory), and how permissible speech is allowed. Understanding this distinction helps avoid innovation in worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of silence/speech
The passage addresses: (1) silence intended as worship, (2) silence from evil, and (3) good speech. Note the ruling for each.
The matn uses 'silence and speech except for good' as a summary.
2
Determine the ruling level for each category
Silence as worship is makruh tahrimi; silence from evil is wajib; good speech is mubah. Check the proofs cited.
The hadith from 'Ali and the Prophet's practice are key.
3
Examine the dalil for prohibition of silence as worship
The hadith from 'Ali indicates that a day of silence is not a valid form of worship, resembling the fast of the People of the Book.
This is a mursal hadith, but relied upon in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Apply the rule to a common scenario
Consider a mu'takif who stays silent all day intending it as worship. The ruling: the silence is makruh tahrimi, though i'tikaf remains valid.
The act is disliked but does not invalidate i'tikaf.

Sexual Intercourse and Its Stimuli in I'tikaf: Hanafi Ruling and Proof
Stating the prohibition of sexual intercourse and its stimuli, and invalidation by intercourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sexual intercourse and its stimuli during i'tikaf, apply the primary dalil (Quran 2:187), and explain why the ruling differs from fasting regarding forgetfulness.

Core Idea
Sexual intercourse and its stimuli invalidate i'tikaf, even if forgetful, because i'tikaf has its own prohibitions distinct from fasting.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on sexual intercourse (wat') and its stimuli (dawa'i) during i'tikaf. The text from Kanz al-Daqa'iq states the prohibition and invalidation, citing the Quranic verse. We will explore the reasoning behind the strict ruling and compare it to the Shafi'i position, focusing on the distinction between prohibitions specific to i'tikaf and those shared with fasting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The matn states that sexual intercourse (wat') is haram during i'tikaf, and it invalidates i'tikaf. Also, its stimuli (dawa'i), like kissing and touching, are prohibited and invalidate if they lead to emission.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the Quranic verse (2:187): 'And do not have intercourse with them while you are in i'tikaf in the mosques'. This is a clear, categorical prohibition.
Dalil is qat'i in thubut and dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'la tubashiruhunna' directly forbids sexual intercourse. The stimuli are included via qiyas because they lead to the prohibited act, similar to Hajj and zihar.
The Hanafi school extends the prohibition to stimuli due to the shared rationale (illa) of preventing the main prohibition.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that forgetful intercourse does not invalidate i'tikaf, and that kissing and touching are not prohibited at all. He distinguishes based on the weakness of the prohibition for forgetfulness.
The Hanafi school rejects this because i'tikaf's prohibitions are absolute and do not depend on intent, unlike fasting.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that any sexual intercourse, intentional or forgetful, invalidates i'tikaf, as does kissing/touching with emission. The Hanafi principle that i'tikaf prohibitions are unaffected by forgetfulness is preferred due to the strength of the textual evidence.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah supports this view.

Inclusion of Nights in a Vow of I'tikaf
Determining whether nights are included when vowing to observe i'tikaf for days.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on inclusion of nights in a vow of i'tikaf for days and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the dual.

Core Idea
When you vow i'tikaf for days, the nights are included; for two days, both nights count – Abu Yusuf dissents.

When you vow to observe i'tikaf for a certain number of days, do the corresponding nights become obligatory as well? The Hanafi school has addressed this question using linguistic principles from Arabic, and a subtle dispute arises when the vow is for exactly two days. In this lesson, we will explore the general rule, the Quranic evidence, and the reasoning behind the majority opinion versus that of Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
1. State the general rule
Identify the rule: if someone vows i'tikaf for days (plural), the nights are included and must be observed consecutively.
The rule applies to any plural number of days.
2
2. Examine the linguistic and Quranic evidence
Understand the proof: in Arabic, the plural of a time period includes its counterpart; e.g., 'three days' in the Quran refers to three nights as well.
The Quran uses 'three days' and 'three nights' interchangeably in the same story.
3
3. Identify the dispute on the dual
Isolate the contested point: Abu Yusuf argues that for two days (dual), only one night should be included, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that both nights are included.
The dispute hinges on whether the dual carries the same inclusive force as the plural.
4
4. Analyze Abu Yusuf's reasoning
Understand his argument: the dual does not need the connective function that necessitated inclusion in the plural, since connection can be achieved without including the first night.
He views the plural rule as a necessity for continuity, which is absent in the dual.
5
5. Conclude with the mufta bihi
Recognize that the majority's view (both nights included) is the relied-upon position for fatwa, as the dual partakes of a plural meaning.
The precautionary principle (ihtiyat) also supports inclusion.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the inclusion of nights when vowing i'tikaf for days. The matn states that nights are included when one vows to observe i'tikaf for days (plural). The commentary explains that in Arabic, the plural of 'days' includes the corresponding nights, as evidenced by the Quranic usage in the story of Zakariyya (AS), where three days and three nights refer to the same event. Therefore, if someone says, 'I vow to observe i'tikaf for three days,' the three nights are included, and the i'tikaf must be consecutive because the unqualified term in i'tikaf implies consecutiveness, unlike fasting. There is a dispute with Abu Yusuf regarding the dual: if one vows two days, Abu Yusuf says only one night is included (the second night), but the majority holds both nights are included because the dual has a plural sense. The practical implementation is explained: for one day, enter before dawn and leave at sunset; for two days, enter before sunset of the first day and leave after sunset of the second day, staying both nights. For many days, enter before sunset of the first day and leave after sunset of the last day. A supplementary point is that a vow must be uttered; a heartfelt intention does not make a binding vow.

Prohibitions in I'tikaf and Nullification by Intercourse
Explaining prohibitions and nullification by intercourse in i'tikaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibitions specific to i'tikaf and explain why forgetful intercourse nullifies i'tikaf according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
In i'tikaf, intercourse and its causes are absolutely prohibited; forgetfulness does not excuse, unlike in fasting.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq explains the prohibitions of i'tikaf and the critical distinction between acts prohibited for i'tikaf itself and those prohibited for fasting. This distinction determines whether forgetfulness can excuse the act. Intercourse is a primary prohibition in i'tikaf, and its nullifying effect is absolute, even if the person forgets.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibitions
List the acts prohibited in i'tikaf: intercourse, its causes (kissing, touching), and leaving the mosque. Note which are specific to i'tikaf and which are shared with fasting.
Intercourse and its causes are specific to i'tikaf; eating/drinking are shared with fasting.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil) for causes
Why are kissing and touching prohibited? Because the prohibition of intercourse extends to its causes in i'tikaf, analogous to hajj and zihar. In fasting, the pillar is self-restraint, so causes are not prohibited to avoid hardship.
The prohibition of intercourse is explicit in the text; causes follow by qiyas.
3
Apply the nullification rule for intercourse
Intercourse in the front or back passage invalidates i'tikaf regardless of intentionality, forgetfulness, night or day, and regardless of ejaculation. Kissing and touching only invalidate if they lead to ejaculation.
This is the Hanafi position: absolute nullification.
4
Contrast with fasting
In fasting, forgetfulness excuses eating and drinking, and the prohibitions vary with intention and time. In i'tikaf, the prohibitions specific to i'tikaf (intercourse, leaving) do not vary; they are absolute.
The principle: what is prohibited for i'tikaf does not differ with state; what is for fasting does differ.
5
Recognize the Shafi'i disagreement
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that causes (kissing, touching) are not prohibited in i'tikaf and that forgetful intercourse does not invalidate. The Hanafi response distinguishes between prohibitions for i'tikaf and for fasting.
The practical consequence: according to Shafi'is, i'tikaf remains valid if intercourse is forgotten.

Inclusion of Nights when Vowing I'tikaf Days and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Dual
Explaining inclusion of nights in vowed i'tikaf and the dispute on the dual.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why nights are included when vowing i'tikaf for days and evaluate the dispute between Abu Yusuf and the two Imams on the inclusion of the first night when vowing two days.

Core Idea
Vowing days in plural requires including nights; for two days, the dual also includes the first night as a precaution.

The second part of this passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq deals with the obligation of nights when a person vows to observe i'tikaf for a number of days. It derives the rule from Quranic usage and discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and the other two founders on whether the dual number includes the corresponding nights. Understanding this shows how linguistic principles affect fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
If someone vows i'tikaf for days (plural), the nights are automatically included because the plural form 'days' linguistically includes the corresponding nights. Similarly, if vowing nights, days are included.
Supported by Quranic verses: three days (Al Imran 3:41) and three nights (Maryam 19:10) refer to the same event.
2
Understand the reasoning
The word 'days' in the plural encompasses both day and night because i'tikaf is a continuous act that spans both. This is unlike fasting, which does not occur at night.
The absolute expression in i'tikaf implies consecutiveness, so vowing days automatically includes nights as part of the continuous period.
3
Apply to the dual case
When vowing two days (dual), Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that both the first and second nights are included. Abu Yusuf argues that the first night is not included because continuity can be achieved without it.
The difference hinges on whether the dual carries the same connotation as the plural.
4
Analyze the evidence for the Hanafi position
The Hanafi view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) relies on the principle that the dual has a meaning of plural, so out of precaution it is attached to the plural. The practical implication is that one must enter the mosque before sunset on the first day and stay until after sunset on the last day.
For a single day vow, one enters before dawn and exits at sunset.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: nights are included in the dual. This is the precautionary view and is adopted for fatwa.
The commentator sides with the 'apparent view' (wajh al-zahir) that the dual has plural meaning.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq on the chapter of I'tikaf. The author first lists the prohibitions: the direct prohibitions and their causes (dawa'i), nullification by intercourse, and the ruling that nights are included when vowing days. The commentary then provides detailed legal reasoning. The key point is the distinction between prohibitions of i'tikaf and those of fasting. In i'tikaf, intercourse is prohibited by explicit text (like in ihram), so its causes (kissing, touching) are also prohibited analogically. In fasting, the pillar is self-restraint, so prohibitions are established implicitly; thus causes are not prohibited to avoid hardship, as fasting is frequent. The commentator cites the Shafi'i opinion that causes are not prohibited. Next, nullification by intercourse is discussed: any intercourse invalidates i'tikaf regardless of intention, forgetfulness, or time, unlike fasting where forgetfulness excuses. This is because the prohibition in i'tikaf is for the act itself, not for the fast. The principle: prohibitions specific to i'tikaf do not vary with forgetfulness or night/day; those shared with fasting do. Then the inclusion of nights is explained: when vowing days, the plural includes corresponding nights, supported by Quranic usage. A dispute with Abu Yusuf arises for the dual number: Abu Yusuf says the first night is not included; the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) says it is, treating the dual as having plural meaning precautionarily. Practical details are given for entering and exiting the mosque based on the number of days vowed.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute regarding 'Umrah. The text begins by stating that 'Umrah is disliked (makruh tahriman) on five days: the Day of Arafah, the Day of Nahr (10th Dhul-Hijjah), and the three Days of Tashriq (11th, 12th, 13th). The reason is that these days are occupied with the actions of Hajj. Some narrations extend the prohibition to two days after the Day of Nahr, totaling five. Abu Yusuf holds that on the Day of Arafah, 'Umrah before noon is not disliked because the main Hajj ritual (wuquf) begins after noon; however, the relied-upon Hanafi position is absolute dislike for all five days. The passage then addresses the legal status of 'Umrah itself. The Hanafi position is that 'Umrah is a sunnah mu'akkadah (confirmed sunna). Some scholars consider it wajib, and some fard kifayah. The Shafi'i school has two views: in the old view it is nafl, in the new view it is fard 'ayn like Hajj. The Shafi'i proofs cited include: the hadith of a man from Banu Amir commanding Hajj and 'Umrah on behalf of his father; the general command in Qur'an 2:196 to complete Hajj and 'Umrah, which they take as obligation; and the saying of 'Ali that 'Umrah is an obligation. The Hanafi response counters that the verse speaks of completion after starting, not initial obligation; the hadith commands on behalf of another, not oneself; and there is explicit hadith from Jabir where the Prophet said 'Umrah is not obligatory. Therefore, the Hanafi view is that 'Umrah is a confirmed sunna, not obligatory. The text ends by saying that the claim of it being fard kifayah is also unsupported. The ruling is that 'Umrah is sunnah mu'akkadah and is disliked on the five mentioned days.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions of khiyar al-bulugh and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding its existence and procedural requirement.

Core Idea
A minor married by a guardian other than father/grandfather can annul the marriage at puberty, but only by judge's decree.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on khiyar al-bulugh — the right of a minor to annul a marriage upon reaching puberty when the marriage was contracted by a guardian other than the father or paternal grandfather. We will explore the conditions required for this option, the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other, and the practical implications for the minor's consent, inheritance, and dower.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qualifying guardians
Understand which guardians trigger khiyar al-bulugh: any guardian except the father and paternal grandfather. This includes the mother, the judge, and other relatives.
The father's father (grandfather) is treated like the father.
2
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad affirm the option for both male and female minors. Abu Yusuf denies it entirely. Examine the rational proofs (dalil aqli) for each view.
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the existence of the option, but with the condition of the judge's decree.
3
Identify the condition of the judge's decree
The option is weak (fi aslihi du'f) and therefore requires a judge's order (bishart al-qada'). The minor cannot simply declare annulment themselves.
This condition distinguishes khiyar al-bulugh from other options like khiyar al-majlis.
4
Distinguish rules for virgin vs. non-virgin and inheritance
A virgin's silence after knowledge voids the option; a non-virgin needs explicit consent. Also, inheritance and dower are due before annulment, unlike suspended or invalid marriages.
These details affect how and when the option is exercised.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling on khiyar al-bulugh (the option at puberty) for minors married by a guardian other than their father or paternal grandfather. The matn states that such minors have the right to annul the marriage upon reaching puberty, but only if a judge decrees the annulment. The commentary clarifies that the father's father (grandfather) is equated with the father. Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad hold that the option exists for both male and female minors married by any guardian except the father and grandfather, while Abu Yusuf denies any option because he considers the marriage binding. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) argue that guardianship is based on consideration (nazariyya), and since the affection of guardians other than the father and grandfather is deficient, the law provides this option to remedy potential harm. The father and grandfather have complete affection, so no option applies. The passage also extends the option to cases where the mother or the judge married the minor, because their consideration is also deficient. However, the most correct view, on which fatwa is given, requires the judge's decree for annulment because the option is weak by itself. The ruling concerning a virgin's silence: if a virgin minor knows of the marriage and remains silent, her silence counts as consent, voiding her option. But if she is a non-virgin (thayyib), her silence does not void the option unless she explicitly consents or indicates consent through actions. The passage also notes that the option for a virgin does not extend beyond the assembly, while for a non-virgin and a boy it remains valid even after leaving. The separation by khiyar al-bulugh is not considered a divorce (talaq) because the female initiates it. Finally, if one spouse dies before the annulment, the other inherits, and the full dower must be paid, unlike in suspended or invalid marriages where there is no inheritance.

Key Terms
خيار البلوغ (khiyar al-bulugh): The legal right of a minor who was married by a guardian other than father/grandfather to annul the marriage upon reaching puberty.
بشرط القضاء (bi-shart al-qada'): The condition that the annulment of the marriage must be decreed by a judge; the minor's own decision is not sufficient due to the weakness of the option.
ثيب (thayyib): A non-virgin woman; in this context, a female who has previously engaged in sexual intercourse, whether before or after marriage.

Fosterage from Unusual Sources: Dead Woman, Man, Goat, Enema, Co-wife
Expands fosterage rulings to unusual milk sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fosterage from milk of a dead woman, a man, a goat, via enema, and from a co-wife, and explain the basis for each ruling.

Core Idea
Only real milk capable of nourishing growth establishes fosterage; enema, man's milk, and goat's milk do not.

This passage extends the rules of fosterage (rida') to borderline cases. The Hanafi school holds that the milk of a dead woman establishes prohibition, while al-Shafi'i disagrees. Similarly, enema does not create fosterage ties, except in a weak narration from Muhammad. The milk of a man or a goat is ineffective. A detailed scenario of co-wife nursing illustrates the practical application of these principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
For each unusual milk source (dead woman, man, goat, enema), state whether it establishes fosterage prohibition (haram) or not.
The act is feeding the milk to an infant under two years.
2
Locate the dalil
Note the proof for the dead woman case: Hanafi argues it is real milk capable of growth; Shafi'i argues no mother exists. For enema, the primary narration says no prohibition; Muhammad analogizes to breaking fast.
The co-wife case uses a hadith: نكاح البنات تحريم الأمهات
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Hanafi position on dead woman's milk relies on the general texts about milk (إطلاق النصوص). The Shafi'i position relies on the absence of a legal 'mother' after death.
For enema, growth is impossible, so prohibition does not arise.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is an inter-madhhab dispute (Hanafi vs Shafi'i) on dead woman's milk, and an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on enema (Muhammad's view vs principal narration).
Also, milk of man and goat is unanimously not effective for fosterage.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) view is that enema does not prohibit (riwayat al-usul), and that dead woman's milk does prohibit (Hanafi position). The co-wife case follows the hadith.
Co-wife nursing before consummation: older wife loses dower; after consummation, both are permanently haram.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of fosterage by addressing borderline cases. First, the milk of a virgin and a dead woman: although it is prohibited to consume (because it is from a dead animal), does it establish fosterage ties? The Hanafi position is that it does, because it is real milk capable of nourishing growth. Al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that a dead woman cannot be a 'mother' for legal purposes. The text provides a rebuttal using examples like tayammum. Next, enema with milk: it does not create prohibition because growth does not occur; similarly, instillation into the urethra or wounds. However, snuff and oral feeding do establish prohibition by consensus. The milk of a man and a goat are not considered 'milk' for fosterage because they do not come from a female human capable of childbirth. Finally, a detailed case: if a man has two wives, an older and a younger, and the older nurses the younger, both become haram to the husband because he would be marrying a mother and daughter through fosterage. The ruling on the older's dower depends on whether the husband had consummated the marriage and whether the separation was her fault. If she nursed voluntarily before consummation, she gets no dower; if forced or insane, she gets half; if consummated, she gets full dower but no maintenance.
