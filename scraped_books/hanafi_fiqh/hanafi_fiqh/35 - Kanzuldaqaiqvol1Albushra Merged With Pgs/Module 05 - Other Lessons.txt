The Four Aspects of Raising Hands for Takbir Tahrimah
Details the four aspects of raising hands in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four aspects of raising hands for takbir tahrimah and identify the supporting dalil.

Core Idea
Raising hands for the opening takbir is sunnah, not wajib; its timing, manner, and place follow the Prophet's practice.

In this lesson, we explore a key sunnah of prayer: raising the hands at the opening takbir. The Hanafi school treats this as a confirmed sunnah (sunnah muakkadah). The author breaks it down into four aspects: the principle that it is sunnah, the time it should be done, the manner of raising, and the place (height). Each aspect is grounded in the Prophetic practice, as reported by Ibn Abbas and Ibn Umar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four aspects
The passage states that the discussion on raising hands has four places: 'fi asl al-raf', fi waqtihi, fi kayfiyyatihi, fi mahallihi' – the principle, time, manner, and place. List them.
These are the four axes of the sunnah ruling.
2
Examine the proof for the principle
The author cites a hadith: 'Do not raise the hands except in seven places,' counting the opening takbir among them. Since this is a hadith ahad, it indicates sunnah, not obligation.
The hadith is narrated by Ibn Abbas and Ibn Umar.
3
Analyze the time condition
The raising must be simultaneous with the takbir because the takbir is meant to inform the deaf person of the start of prayer; simultaneity achieves this purpose.
This is based on the wisdom (hikmah) of the legislation.
4
Describe the manner and place
The hands are raised open (fingers extended, not clenched), fingers pointing to the qibla, palms facing forward. The thumbs align with the earlobes, i.e., hands level with the ears.
This is the described sunnah form.
5
Apply the ruling
Knowing these four aspects, a person can correctly practice this sunnah: raise hands open at the start of the first takbir, simultaneously, thumbs at earlobes, fingers toward qibla.
Omitting it does not invalidate the prayer but leaves a sunnah.

Hanafi vs Shafi'i: Hand Placement in Prayer
Inter-madhhab dispute with dalil naqli

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on hand placement during qiyam, cite the primary dalil, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
The hands are placed under the navel — a confirmed sunnah, not humility but glorification.

This passage addresses a classic inter-madhhab dispute: where should a man place his hands during the standing (qiyam) of prayer? The Hanafi school holds they should be placed under the navel, while the Shafi'i school places them on the chest. We will examine the proofs from both sides and understand the reasoning behind the Hanafi preference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling from the text: a man places his right hand on his left under the navel.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opposing school and their view
The Shafi'i school holds that hands should be placed on the chest. Note the two reasons they give: prophetic practice and humility.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proofs
Examine the two hadiths cited (from Ali and Wa'il ibn Hujr) and the qiyas to placing hands before kings, showing glorification.
The key phrase 'min al-sunnah' indicates it is an established practice, not a wajib.
4
Examine the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i argument
The Hanafi school responds to the humility argument by pointing out that placing hands on the private area over clothes is not disrespectful, and the same principle applies to women who place on chest despite it being 'awrah.
This shows consistency in 'awrah rulings.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference is purely physical posture: under navel vs on chest. No effect on validity of prayer.
Both positions are valid; the dispute is about optimal practice.

The Sunnah Acts of Ruku': Takbir, Tasbih, and Hand Placement
Covers sunan of ruku': takbir, tasbih, hand placement, and the tasbih dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the takbir, tasbih, and hand placement in ruku', explain the evidence, and evaluate the Maliki counter-argument on tasbih.

Core Idea
Takbir, tasbih, and hand placement in ruku' are sunnah, not wajib; the Maliki view is disproven by Hanafi usul.

Ruku' has several recommended acts that enhance the prayer. This lesson identifies the sunnah acts at the moment of bowing: the takbir said when going down, the tasbih recited while bowed, and the manner of gripping the knees. We also examine a key difference with the Maliki school regarding the legal status of the tasbih, and why the Hanafi school holds it to be sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah acts of ruku'
Read the passage and list all actions mentioned for ruku': takbir when bowing, tasbih, and how to hold the knees.
The text also mentions takbir for sujud and raising from it, but focus on ruku' for now.
2
Determine the ruling level for each act
The Hanafi ruling for each is sunnah muakkadah. Understand why it is not wajib or fard.
The distinction between sunnah and wajib has practical consequences for the validity of prayer.
3
Examine the dalil for tasbih
The author cites a hadith: 'When one of you bows, let him say...'. Identify the type of hadith (ahad) and its epistemological weight in Hanafi usul.
The Hanafi principle that a solitary hadith cannot abrogate an unconditional Quranic verse is central.
4
Analyze the dispute with Imam Malik
Imam Malik considered tasbih wajib based on a different reading of the Quranic verse and hadith. Compare the two proofs.
The Hanafi response rests on the absolute nature of the Quranic command 'Bow down' without condition.
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In Hanafi, leaving tasbih does not invalidate prayer; in Maliki, it does. This shows the impact of usul on fiqh.
Also note: reducing tasbih from three is makruh tanzih; lengthening ruku' for a latecomer is makruh tahriman if known.

Ruling on Praying Towards Permissible Objects
Ruling on praying towards specific objects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on praying towards a mushaf, sword, candle, lamp, or carpet with images, and identify the condition that causes dislike.

Core Idea
Praying towards these objects is allowed unless you prostrate on images—then it becomes disliked.

The Hanafi school addresses a practical question: is it disliked to pray while facing objects like a Qur'an, sword, or lamp? The answer is no, unless the prayer mat contains images of living beings and the worshipper prostrates directly on them. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its underlying rationale based on custom and veneration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The passage lists objects: mushaf, suspended sword, candle, lamp, and carpet with images. The default ruling is 'not disliked' (mubah).
The term 'suspended' is customary; the same applies if placed on something.
2
Recognize the exception for prostration on images
The ruling changes if the worshipper prostrates directly on images (tasawir) on the carpet. In that case, it becomes disliked because it is an act of veneration.
Prostration on images is considered ta'zim (veneration), which is prohibited, whereas stepping on them is ihana (disrespect) and permissible.
3
Understand the rationale for lamps and candles
Praying towards a lamp is not disliked because the Magians worship embers, not the flame itself. A candle beside the worshipper (as in Ramadan) is unanimously allowed.
The concern was resemblance to fire-worshippers, but the text distinguishes between embers and kindled fire.
4
Apply the principle to new cases
The key is whether the object is treated with veneration during prayer. Praying towards a sword or mushaf is neutral; only prostrating on images crosses the line.
This ruling is consistent with the Hanafi principle that acts are permissible unless proven otherwise.

The Ruling on Facing the Qibla During Excretion
Ruling statement with proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on facing the qibla during excretion and identify the primary evidence (dalil).

Core Idea
Facing or turning your back to the qibla while relieving yourself is makruh tahrimi.

In this passage, the author moves from discussing disliked acts in prayer to disliked acts outside prayer. The first issue is the etiquette of the toilet: the ruling on facing the qibla. The text states the ruling as 'karih' (disliked), which the commentary specifies as tahriman (close to prohibition). The proof is a direct command from the Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling.
The act is facing the qibla or turning one's back to it while urinating or defecating. The ruling is 'karih' which in Hanafi terminology means makruh tahrimi.
The text uses 'karih' without qualification, but the commentary clarifies it is tahrimi.
2
Locate the dalil.
The proof is the hadith: 'When you go to relieve yourselves, do not face the qibla nor turn your backs to it, but face east or west.' This is a direct command (nahy) from the Prophet.
This hadith is found in Sahih al-Bukhari and others.
3
Understand the scope.
The ruling applies in both open spaces (al-fada') and buildings (al-binyan), as indicated by the general wording of the hadith.
4
Note the nuance of karahah tahrimiyyah.
Makruh tahrimi means the act is sinful if done without a valid excuse, but it is not haram. The Hanafi school distinguishes this from makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked).

Closing the Mosque Door: Ruling and Exception
Details on closing the mosque door with exception for theft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on closing the mosque door and identify the condition that lifts the dislike.

Core Idea
Closing the mosque door is disliked, but fear of theft makes it permissible—and that is the fatwa.

The classical Hanafi position holds that closing the mosque door is disliked because it may prevent people from entering for prayer. However, later jurists recognized a pressing need: security of the mosque’s contents. In this lesson, you will learn the general ruling, the exception, and the specific timings when closing is recommended or avoided.

The act is closing the mosque door. The general ruling is karaha (dislike) because it resembles preventing people from prayer.

2
Locate the exception
The exception is when there is fear for the mosque's goods, especially outside prayer times. The text says: 'no harm if one fears for the mosque's goods in non-prayer time.'

Two timings are given: (1) when prayer times are close (e.g., Asr, Maghrib, Isha) it should not be closed; (2) after Isha until dawn, and from sunrise to midday, it may be closed.
This reconciles the need for security with the need for open access during prayer times.

A real mosque has full sanctity; a funeral prayer area and a house prayer room do not—urinating and intercourse are prohibited in the mosque but permitted in the latter.

The mosque is a sacred space with specific rules: urination, defecation, and sexual activity are strictly forbidden. However, not all places used for prayer share this sanctity. Decorating the Mosque: Permissibility and Conditions
Ruling on mosque decoration with plaster, gold, and other colors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on mosque decoration and identify when the trustee becomes liable.

Core Idea
Decorating the mosque is allowed but not praiseworthy; using waqf funds without structural need incurs liability.

The Prophet warned that ornamentation of mosques is a sign of the Hour. Yet, the Hanafi school permits moderate decoration for veneration, especially from personal wealth. This lesson examines the ruling, the difference of opinion, and the critical condition regarding funding source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling in the base text
The base matn says 'wa la naqshahu bil-jiss wa ma' al-dhahab' (and not decorating it with plaster and gold water). The initial indication is that it is included among disliked acts.
However, the commentary immediately clarifies that it is not disliked.
2
Note the difference of opinion
Some scholars said it is makruh, others said it is a good deed (qurbah). The Hanafi school allows it but does not consider it commendable (lam yastahsinuhu).
This shows the nuanced approach: it is not prohibited but also not recommended.
3
Understand the condition regarding funding
If done from one's own lawful wealth, it is veneration of Allah's house. But if done from waqf (endowment) money, the trustee (mutawalli) is liable (yadman) to compensate, unless it is for structural strengthening.
The text: 'أما من مال الوقف فيضمن' (but from endowment money, he is liable).
4
Recognize the hadith warning
The Prophet counted mosque ornamentation and lengthening of minarets among the signs of the Hour. This discourages excess.
The Hanafis balance this with the principle of venerating the House of Allah.

Method of Washing the Deceased and Rulings
Procedural rulings on washing the dead with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for washing the deceased, apply the rulings on burial perfumes (ḥanūṭ and kāfūr), identify key subsidiary rulings (tayammum, carrying, intention, etc.), and explain the proof from the hadith of Adam and the dispute with al-Shafi'i regarding repeating wudu.

Core Idea
Washing the deceased is a prophetic sunnah from Adam; the procedure and legal details are established by transmitted practice.

In this lesson, we study the Hanafi rulings on ghusl al-mayyit from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The text covers the actual washing method, application of ḥanūṭ and kāfūr, and a host of subsidiary issues such as tayammum, carrying the deceased in prayer, the washer's state, and the treatment of wastewater. The proof for washing as an established practice is the hadith of the angels washing Adam, and the lesson also highlights the Hanafi–Shafi'i difference on whether wudu is repeated after washing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core washing procedure
Read the first few lines describing the gentle stomach wipe, handling excretions, drying, and application of ḥanūṭ and kāfūr. Note that the comment 'lam yu'ad ghusluhu' refers to the wudu not being repeated.
This is the procedural foundation.
2
Analyze the subsidiary rulings (furu')
Go through each furu' listed: tayammum case, carrying deceased in prayer, requirement of intention, purity of washer, hiring, wastewater, children, kissing, husband seeing wife. Understand the reasoning behind each.
These are derived from the established principles.
3
Examine the proof for washing
The text states washing is a 'shari'ah madiyah' citing the hadith of Adam washed by angels. Identify the dalil (mursal hadith) and note its strength (zanni al-thubut).
This establishes the sunnah nature.
4
Compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on wudu
The Hanafi position (from the text) is that wudu is not repeated after washing, while al-Shafi'i holds it should be. Understand the Hanafi argument: death is a stronger state than loss of consciousness, so no need to repeat.
This is a key ikhtilaf.
5
Apply the rulings to practical scenarios
Consider the cases of rain falling on the deceased, a drowned person, and the definition of ḥanūṭ. Confirm that rain does not substitute and that the drowned person still requires washing.
These clarifications prevent common errors.

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq covers the method and rulings of washing the deceased. The text begins by describing the procedure: the washer gently wipes the stomach of the deceased, and any excretions are washed off. The washing is not repeated (i.e., the ghusl of the deceased is not repeated if something comes out; unlike living person whose wudu would break). After washing, the body is dried with a cloth. Hanut (a perfume mixture without wars or saffron for men) is applied to the head and beard, and camphor to the prostration points (forehead, nose, hands, knees, feet). The passage then presents several subsidiary rulings (furu'): If the deceased is given tayammum due to lack of water and then water is found, the washing and prayer are repeated. Carrying a deceased who has not been washed invalidates the prayer of the carrier unless the deceased is a martyr or an animal whose flesh is not impure. Intention (niyyah) is required for washing to discharge the obligation from the living, not to purify the deceased (since the deceased is not legally obligated). The washer should ideally be pure (tahir), and it is disliked to be in a state of major impurity. The nearest relative is preferred to perform the washing. Hiring for washing is discouraged; hiring for sewing the shroud is disputed. The wastewater from washing is impure but is excused on the washer's garment due to general necessity. Young children who have not reached the age of desire have no 'awrah, so they may be washed by either gender. It is permissible to kiss the deceased, and a husband may see his wife (even after death). The basis for washing is the sunnah from the angels washing Adam. Rain or water flowing over the body does not substitute for the ritual ghusl. Even a drowned person must be washed. The commentary clarifies that the initial wipe is gentle to avoid staining the shroud. There is a dispute with al-Shafi'i: he holds that after washing, the deceased should be given wudu (like a living person), but the Hanafi view is that since death removes consciousness completely, wudu is not repeated. The washer dries the body with a cloth (following the Prophet's practice). Hanut is defined and its placement is recommended. Camphor is specified because it repels insects and the prostration points are honored.

The Sunnah Shroud: Composition and Hanafi Proof
Ruling on the sunnah shroud and evidence for including a qamis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the sunnah shroud, identify its three pieces, and explain the tarjih over the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
The sunnah shroud includes a qamis; Hanafis follow the Prophet's explicit action, not just his final shroud.

The shroud (kafan) of a deceased man is a sunnah act. The Hanafi school prescribes three specific pieces: izar, qamis, and lifafah. This differs from the Shafi'i view of three wrappers without a shirt. The dispute is rooted in differing hadith evidence. We will examine the proofs and understand why the Hanafi position is considered stronger.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah shroud pieces
From the text, the shroud consists of izar (lower garment from navel to feet), qamis (shirt from neck to feet without sleeves or opening), and lifafah (wrapper from head to feet). Note that qamis is a distinctive Hanafi requirement.
2
Note the prohibition of grooming
The text states that the deceased's hair and beard should not be combed, and nails and hair should not be cut, as these are acts of adornment for the living. This is based on a narration from Aisha.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
The text cites: the Prophet gave his shirt to shroud Abdullah ibn Ubayy; Ibn Mughaffal was shrouded in his qamis; Ibn Abbas described being shrouded in three garments including a qamis. These show the Prophet's action and Companions' practice.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i school relies on Aisha's narration that the Prophet was shrouded in three Yemeni white garments without a turban or qamis.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the Prophet's direct action (giving his shirt) takes precedence over Aisha's narration, which may describe the outer wrapping only. The Prophet's action is the primary evidence and multiple Companions corroborate it.

Chapter of Funerals, number 171. Method of Shrouding. Do not comb his hair or beard, and do not cut his nails or hair. His shroud as sunnah is: izar, qamis, and lifafah, because that is the adornment of the living. (From the aspect of sunnah) 'Do not comb' etc. from 'tasrih' (sending hair with a comb). From the narration of Aisha that she saw a woman combing a deceased's head with a comb, she said: 'Why do you pull the forelock of your dead?' This is taken from 'nasara' meaning to pull the forelock. She meant that the dead does not need combing, and she expressed it as pulling the forelock to deter. Just as combing is not allowed, cutting any hair (including mustache) is not allowed. Similarly, circumcision is not done by consensus. The Quran is not recited aloud during washing, nor supplications aloud; silent recitation is fine. It is disliked to recite Quran or dhikr in front of the funeral procession; silence is preferred, contrary to al-Shafi'i who says: 'Do with your dead as you do with your bride.' Our proof: this is for adornment, and the dead has no need for it. The narration from Aisha shows disapproval. The hadith quoted by al-Shafi'i is interpreted as preparing with perfume etc. (Fath) Do not cut nails unless broken. The conclusion is that nothing done for adornment is done to the dead. Al-Shafi'i says: cut his mustache, trim his nails, remove hair from armpit and pubic area. (Fath) Branches: From Ayn: A man does not wash his wife nor his umm walad, and they do not wash him in the well-known view from the Imam, except the wife has that right even if she is a dhimmi, provided the marriage still exists at the time of washing. If the deceased woman has only male relatives, her mahram dhu rahim performs tayammum for her. If there is no one, a stranger wraps his hand in a cloth and performs tayammum. If a man has only female relatives, one of his mahram women performs tayammum for him, unless he has a wife who can wash him. If limbs or part of a body are found, they are not washed or prayed over but buried, unless more than half or the head is found, then it is washed and prayed over. If it is unknown whether the deceased is Muslim or disbeliever: if there is a sign of Muslims, it is washed; if not, two narrations. The correct view is that it is washed and prayed over. Signs of Muslims are four: khidab (henna), khitan (circumcision), wearing black, and shaving the pubic area. When dead Muslims and disbelievers are mixed, and Muslims are more numerous, they are all washed and prayed over, and the Muslims intend to pray, and they are buried in Muslim graveyards. It is said to bury them in a separate graveyard as a precaution. (Fath) Izar: that which is worn from the navel to the feet. Qamis: from the base of the neck to the feet, without a pocket, collar, or sleeves. Izar, qamis, and lifafah (which is also wrapped from navel to feet). Contrary to al-Shafi'i, based on Aisha's statement that the Prophet was shrouded in three Yemeni white garments without turban or qamis. Our proof: the hadith of Abdullah ibn Ubayy who asked the Prophet for his shirt to shroud his father, and the Prophet gave it. Also Ibn Mughaffal was shrouded in his qamis. Ibn Abbas said: 'I was shrouded in three garments: his qamis in which he died, and a Najrani hullah, and the hullah is two garments.' Acting upon what we have narrated is preferable because it is the Prophet's action, whereas what they narrated is the action of some Companions. Also it is contradicted by our narrations. The situation of men is more exposed due to their presence, unlike women. (Fath) Branches: If someone bequeaths to be shrouded in two garments, his condition is not observed because it is against sunnah. Similarly, if he bequeaths to be buried with a single man in a grave, it is against sunnah. If he bequeaths that so-and-so should lead his funeral prayer, the chosen view is that the bequest is invalid because it causes estrangement to those who have the right to lead the prayer. Ahmad and Malik say he should be shrouded in three wrappers without a qamis as al-Shafi'i says. (Fath)

Commentary
This section from Kanz al-Daqa'iq covers the sunnah method of shrouding (takfin) and additional rulings related to washing and burial. The text begins by stating that the deceased's hair and beard should not be combed, and nails and hair should not be cut, as these are acts of adornment for the living, and the dead has no need for them. The sunnah shroud for a man consists of three pieces: izar (lower garment from navel to feet), qamis (shirt from neck to feet without sleeves or opening), and lifafah (wrapper from head to feet). The commentary cites the hadith of Aisha disapproving of combing, and notes that cutting the mustache or removing body hair is also not allowed, contrary to al-Shafi'i who permits it based on the hadith 'Do with your dead as you do with your bride.' The Hanafi response is that such acts are for adornment, which the dead does not need. The text then presents a set of subsidiary rulings (furu') attributed to 'Ayn' (likely a gloss or reference). These include: A man does not wash his wife or his umm walad in the well-known view; however, a wife may wash her husband even if she is a dhimmi. If there are no suitable washers, tayammum is performed by a mahram. If only body parts are found, they are not washed or prayed over unless more than half or the head is present. If the religious identity is uncertain, signs of being Muslim (henna, circumcision, black clothing, shaving pubic area) indicate it; if absent, the correct view is to wash and pray over them. When Muslims and disbelievers are mixed, and Muslims are the majority, all are treated as Muslims. The commentary then explains the terms izar, qamis, and lifafah, and presents the Hanafi proof for the three-piece shroud against al-Shafi'i's view of three wrappers without qamis, citing hadiths about the Prophet giving his shirt to shroud Abdullah ibn Ubayy and Ibn Abbas's statement. The text concludes with rulings on bequests: a bequest to be shrouded in two garments or buried with one person is invalid as it contradicts sunnah; a bequest for a specific person to lead the prayer is also invalid to avoid ill feelings.

Key Terms
التيمم (al-tayammum): Dry ablution performed with earth or dust, used for the deceased when washing is not possible.
المحرم (al-mahram): A person whom one cannot marry due to blood relationship, used here for permissible washers.
العلامات (al-ʿalāmāt): The four signs indicating a deceased is Muslim: khidab, khitan, wearing black, shaving pubic area.

Rulings on Shrouding the Deceased (Kafan)
Explanatory chapter on shrouding with proofs and disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on shrouding for men and women, identify the key proofs from sunnah, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i on disputed points.

Core Idea
The shroud is sunnah muakkadah; the minimum is two garments, and the dead must not be groomed for adornment.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq delineates the Hanafi school's rulings on the kafan. It begins with prohibitions on combing and cutting, then prescribes the sunnah shroud for men and women, and addresses minimum sufficiency. The text also engages in inter-madhhab dispute with al-Shafi'i, defending the inclusion of a shirt and two braids.

Guided Path
1
Identify prohibited actions before shrouding
List the actions prohibited: combing hair, cutting nails, mustache, etc. Understand the rationale: these are acts of adornment and the dead has no need.
The prohibition is based on the hadith of Aisha (ra) who disapproved of combing.
2
Distinguish sunnah and kifayah for men
Sunnah shroud: izar, qamis, lifafah. Kifayah (minimum): izar and lifafah. Know that the kifayah is considered wajib if available.
The three-piece set is strongly recommended, but two pieces are sufficient.
3
Compare Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the shroud
Shafi'i uses three wrappers without a shirt. Hanafi includes a shirt based on the Prophet (saw) giving his shirt to shroud Abdullah ibn Ubayy. Evaluate the evidence.
The Hanafi response: Prophetic action takes precedence over companion action.
4
List sunnah and kifayah components for women
Sunnah: dir', izar, khimar, lifafah, and a cloth to bind breasts. Kifayah: izar, lifafah, and khimar. Note the number of braids: two (Hanafi) vs three (Shafi'i).
The braids are placed on the sides, not behind the back, to avoid adornment.
5
Apply subsidiary rulings
Consider wills regarding shroud, rulings for mixed dead, signs of Muslims, and the case of necessity where only partial body is found.
These are practical applications derived from the same principles.

A Muslim may wash and bury a non-Muslim relative, but it is permissible, not obligatory; an apostate is treated without dignity.

When a non-Muslim relative dies, what duties does a Muslim guardian have? Walking Position and Burial Rites
Rulings on walking position and burial rites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on walking behind the funeral, the method of burial using a niche, and the supplication at burial, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-arguments.

Core Idea
Walk behind the funeral, bury in a niche facing qibla, and declare the deceased's faith — these are sunnah practices rooted in prophetic example.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq covers two key funeral rites: the preferred position for mourners and the proper method of burial. It also addresses the supplication recited when placing the body. Each ruling involves a dispute with the Shafi'i school, offering valuable insight into Hanafi proof methodology.

The Sa' Measurement: Reconciling the Intra-Hanafi Dispute

Core Idea
The sa' is eight Baghdadi ratls, which equals five and one-third Madani ratls – an apparent dispute resolved by the difference in ratl definitions.

The sa' is a key measure for Zakat al-Fitr. While the Hanafi matn states it is eight ratls, a well-known narration from Abu Yusuf suggests a different figure. This lesson unpacks the evidence and shows how the school reconciled the apparent conflict.

Guided Path

Read the matn: '...it is eight ratls: each ratl is twenty istars, and the istar is four and a half mithqals.' Recognize that this implicitly refers to the Baghdadi ratl.
The Baghdadi ratl is 20 istars; the Madani ratl is 30 istars.
2
Identify Abu Yusuf's initial view and its proof
Note that Abu Yusuf (and al-Shafi'i) held the sa' is five and one-third ratls, citing the hadith 'Our sa' is the smallest of sa's' and his empirical findings in Madina.

The Hanafi proof is a hadith from Anas: the Prophet performed wudu with a mudd of two ratls and ghusl with a sa' of eight ratls. This directly supports the eight-ratl measure.
The hadith is explicit about the number of ratls.

Commentary
This passage continues from the previous and details the prohibited (mahzurat) acts during i'tikaf. It explains what is permissible and what is disliked or forbidden.

First, the base text lists: eating, drinking, sleeping, and buying/selling 'therein' (i.e., in the mosque) – these are permissible but with conditions. The commentary clarifies that it is permissible for the mu'takif to eat and drink in the mosque, contrary to the view of al-Shafi'i who requires him to leave the mosque to eat. The Hanafi proof: the Prophet ate in the mosque, so there is no necessity to leave. Only if he has no one to bring food may he go out.

Buying and selling (mubaya'ah) is also permissible if needed, but bringing the sold item (al-mabi') into the mosque is disliked (makruh tahrimi). If done for trade, it is also disliked. This is based on hadith. There is an inter-madhhab difference: Malik and Ahmad said buying/selling in mosque is absolutely disliked; Hanafis distinguish between necessary and unnecessary.

Sleeping in the mosque is generally permissible, but the text mentions it as one of the acts that the mu'takif may do. However, there is a mention that the sleep of a stranger (a traveler) is allowed. The commentary notes that Ibn al-Kamal held that eating, drinking, and sleeping in the mosque are not disliked at all. This reflects leniency in the later fatwa.

Silence (samt) that is considered an act of worship is disliked because it resembles the practice of the Magians and the People of the Book. The Prophet prohibited monastic silence. However, if one remains silent without intending worship, it is not disliked. The hadith 'Whoever remains silent is saved' is cited. Speaking except with good is permissible; mu'takif may engage in permissible conversation, as the Prophet did. Speaking evil is forbidden for everyone, let alone a mu'takif.

Sexual intercourse (wat') is haram for the mu'takif, whether in the mosque or outside during an allowed exit. The verse (Quran 2:187) explicitly prohibits it. The commentary explains that the prohibition applies even if one goes out for a natural need, because the state of i'tikaf continues. Also, if the wife is in i'tikaf in her house, intercourse invalidates her i'tikaf. The background: before the revelation, some companions would have intercourse during their i'tikaf exits.

Obligation of the Two Rak'ahs after Tawaf: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Explains the Hanafi proof for the obligation of the two rak'ahs at Maqam Ibrahim and contrasts with Shafi'i view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the two rak'ahs after tawaf, cite the proof from the Prophet's action at Maqam Ibrahim, and explain why the Hanafi school considers it wajib while the Shafi'i school considers it sunnah.

Core Idea
The two rak'ahs after tawaf are wajib because the Prophet followed a divine command — and a command in the Quran indicates obligation.

This lesson focuses on one of the key differences between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools in the rituals of Hajj and Umrah: the ruling on the two rak'ahs prayed after completing tawaf. The source passage, from a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, presents the Hanafi position with its proof and contrasts it with the Shafi'i view. Understanding this dispute will sharpen your ability to analyze dalil and the usul principle that al-amr yufidu al-wujub.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ritual and its location
The ritual is praying two rak'ahs after completing tawaf, preferably at Maqam Ibrahim. The source calls it a conclusion to the tawaf.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi school holds that these two rak'ahs are wajib (obligatory). Al-'Ayni explicitly says 'wa hadhihi al-salat wajibatun 'indana'.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
The Prophet recited the verse 'And take the Maqam of Ibrahim as a place of prayer' (2:125) and then prayed two rak'ahs. The commentary argues that this action was in compliance with the command (amr), and in Hanafi usul, a command indicates obligation (wujub).
The dalil is naqli (transmitted) from Ahmad and Muslim.
4
Consider the opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i considers it sunnah (recommended), arguing that there is no clear evidence of obligation. The verse is permissive, not imperative.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is relied upon (mufta bihi) in the school, as affirmed by the commentator. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that leaving it out requires compensation in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rituals after completing the tawaf. It begins by instructing the pilgrim to kiss the Black Stone (al-Hajar al-Aswad) at the start of each circuit if possible, and to conclude the tawaf by kissing it again. The commentator draws an analogy between the circuits of tawaf and the rak'ahs of prayer: each circuit begins with istilam just as each rak'ah begins with takbir. Thus the Hanafi school maintains it is sunnah.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that tawaf al-qudum is sunnah, distinguish it from the Maliki/Hanbali view that it is wajib, and articulate the Hanafi argument based on the word 'tahiyya'.

Core Idea
Tawaf al-qudum is a voluntary greeting (tahiyya) to the Ka'bah, not an obligation—the term 'greeting' implies voluntary honor, not command.

The source passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents the Hanafi position that it is sunnah, and contrasts it with the view of Malik and Ahmad that it is wajib. The key lies in the linguistic and legal analysis of the Hadith 'Whoever comes to the House, let him greet it with tawaf.' Understanding this will sharpen your skill in applying usul principles to shari'ah texts.

2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi school holds that tawaf al-qudum is sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah) for the non-Makki. It is not wajib.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof is based on the Prophet calling it a 'tahiyya' (greeting). In Arabic, 'tahiyya' refers to voluntary honor, not an obligation. The Quranic obligation to return a greeting (verse 4:86) does not apply because it is about reciprocation, not initiation.
The text also draws an analogy to the greeting of the mosque (tahiyyat al-masjid), which is sunnah for the one entering.
4
Consider the opposing view
Malik and Ahmad hold that tawaf al-qudum is wajib. They cite the Prophet's command: 'Whoever comes to the House, let him greet it with tawaf' – a command (amr) which, in their usul, indicates obligation.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is considered stronger because the word 'tahiyya' itself indicates voluntary honor, and the Prophet's action supports it. The Maliki/Hanbali view relies on the command, but the Hanafi response clarifies that the command is for recommendation (nadb) due to the qarīnah of the word 'tahiyya'.

Completion: Visiting his grave (blessings on him) is the most recommended of recommended acts, close to wajib, because of his statement 'Whoever visits my grave, my intercession becomes obligatory for him'. Then if Hajj is fard on him, he gives precedence to it over the visit, otherwise he may choose. The best is to purely intend visiting the grave; some say to intend visiting the mosque. After visiting him, it is recommended to go to Baqi' al-Gharqad, the cemetery of the people of Medina, to visit the martyrs and shrines, especially the grave of the master of martyrs Hamza (may Allah be pleased with him), and to visit in al-Baqi' the dome of al-Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him), and with him al-Hasan ibn Ali and Zayn al-Abidin and his son Muhammad al-Baqir and his son Ja'far al-Sadiq, and to visit the dome of Amir al-Mu'minin Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) and the dome of Ibrahim (son of the Prophet, peace be upon him) and a group of the wives of the Prophet (may Allah be pleased with them) as well. He also visits the martyrs of Uhud, his aunt Safiyyah, and many of the Companions and Followers. He prays in the mosque of Fatima (may Allah be pleased with her) in al-Baqi'. It is recommended on Thursday to say: 'Peace be upon you for what you patiently endured; how excellent is the final abode! Peace be upon you, abode of believing people, and we, if Allah wills, shall join you.' He recites Ayat al-Kursi and Surah al-Ikhlas. It is recommended to visit the mosque of Quba' on Saturday as has been reported from him (peace be upon him). (Fath)

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on visiting the Prophet's grave (Al-Masjid an-Nabawi). It states that it is the most recommended of recommended acts, close to wajib, based on the hadith 'Whoever visits my grave, my intercession becomes wajib for him.' If one still owes the fard Hajj, he gives precedence to Hajj; otherwise he may choose. The best intention is purely for visiting the grave, though some say to intend visiting the mosque. After visiting the Prophet, it is recommended to visit Baqi' al-Gharqad cemetery, including the graves of Hamza, Al-Abbas, Hasan ibn Ali, Zayn al-Abidin, Muhammad al-Baqir, Ja'far al-Sadiq, Uthman, Ibrahim (the Prophet's son), and the Prophet's wives. Also visit the martyrs of Uhud, his aunt Safiyyah, and other Companions and Followers. He should pray in the mosque of Fatima in Baqi'. On Thursday, he should say the prescribed salutations, recite Ayat al-Kursi and Surah al-Ikhlas. On Saturday, it is recommended to visit Masjid Quba'. The source is Fath (likely Fath al-Qadir or similar).

Key Terms
زيارة قبره (Ziyarat Qabrih): Visiting the grave of the Prophet Muhammad (saw) in Medina.
بقيع الغرقد (Baqi' al-Gharqad): The main cemetery of Medina where many Companions and Ahl al-Bayt are buried.
مسجد قباء (Masjid Quba'): The mosque built by the Prophet (saw) on the outskirts of Medina.
