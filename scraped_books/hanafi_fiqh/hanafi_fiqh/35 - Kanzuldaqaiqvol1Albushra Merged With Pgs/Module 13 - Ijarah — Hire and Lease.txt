Corrective Maintenance in Lease Contracts
Ruling on corrective maintenance obligation in lease contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on corrective maintenance in lease contracts and apply the distinction between major and minor repairs, including the role of custom.

Core Idea
Major repairs burden the lessor; minor repairs follow custom.

In lease contracts, a common question is who bears the cost of repairs. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position: the lessor must ensure the property is usable, so major structural repairs are his obligation. Minor repairs, however, may shift to the lessee if local custom ('urf) so stipulates; otherwise, the lessor remains responsible. We will unpack the legal reasoning (ta'lil) and contrast with other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and ruling
Read the first sentence: if a rented house needs repair that prevents use, it is upon the lessor. Note the hukm level – wajib on the lessor for major repairs.
Major repairs are those without which the contracted benefit cannot be realized.
2
Locate the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The author states: 'because the benefit (manfa'ah) contracted is ownership given to the lessee, and it is not obtained except through maintenance, so it enters the contract necessarily.' This is a qiyas (analogical) proof: maintenance is essential to deliver the object of the contract.
The dalil is aqli (rational), derived from the nature of the contract.
3
Distinguish major vs minor repairs
The text then addresses minor repairs (yasir). If the repair is minor and custom places it on the lessee, it is upon the lessee. If custom does not stipulate, the most correct (asahh) view is that it remains on the lessor.
Hanafi school does not define minor repairs explicitly; they are determined by custom and scale.
4
Assess the role of custom ('urf)
Custom can shift the obligation for minor repairs to the lessee. If custom is silent, the default is lessor's responsibility.
This illustrates Hanafi reliance on 'urf as a subsidiary source.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) treat all repairs as the lessor's responsibility unless otherwise agreed. The Hanafi distinction introduces flexibility but also potential conflict.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is financial: in Hanafi, the lessee may bear minor repair costs if customary.

And among them: the need for maintenance. If the rented house needs repair or fixing that prevents use, then it is upon the lessor, because the benefit contracted is ownership given to the lessee, and it is not obtained except through maintenance, so it enters the contract necessarily. And if the repair is minor, then it is upon the lessee if custom stipulates it. And if it does not stipulate it, the most correct is that it is upon the lessor as well.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the obligation of corrective maintenance in lease contracts. The author establishes that the lessor bears responsibility for major repairs necessary for the leased property to be usable. The reasoning (ta'lil) is that the lessee is entitled to the benefit (manfa'ah) under the contract, and without maintenance the benefit cannot be realized, so the obligation falls on the lessor implicitly. For minor repairs, if custom ('urf) places them on the lessee, that is followed; otherwise, the lessor remains responsible. The ruling is from the Hanafi school, consistent with the principle that the lessor must deliver a usable benefit.

Key Terms
صيانة (siyanah): Maintenance or repair of the leased property to keep it usable.
