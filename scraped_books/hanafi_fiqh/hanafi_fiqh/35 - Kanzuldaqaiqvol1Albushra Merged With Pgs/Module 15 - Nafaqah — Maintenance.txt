Exceptions to Wife's Maintenance: When Nafaqah Drops
Ruling statement listing exceptions to spousal maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the five Hanafi exceptions to the obligation of nafaqah, and apply the underlying principle of ihtibas (confinement) to determine when maintenance is due.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is compensation for the wife's availability; if she is unavailable due to her own action, no maintenance.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq enumerates specific scenarios where a wife loses her right to nafaqah despite a valid marriage. The key principle is that nafaqah is a recompense (jaza') for the wife's confinement (ihtibas) to her husband. When that confinement is absent because of the wife's own doing, the obligation drops. We will walk through each exception and understand the reasoning behind it, including a notable istihsan for the sick wife after consummation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Basis of Nafaqah
Recognize that Hanafi jurists define nafaqah as compensation for the wife's being available (ihtibas) to her husband. It is not simply a right from the marriage contract; it depends on actual or potential benefit.
This is a distinct Hanafi view; other schools may tie nafaqah directly to marriage.
2
Identify the Five Exceptions
From the text, list the five cases: (1) nushuz (rebelliousness), (2) minor wife who cannot be slept with, (3) imprisoned for a debt to a third party, (4) performing hajj with a mahram other than the husband, (5) sick before being brought to the husband's house.
Each case involves the wife preventing or hindering the husband's access, whether physically or legally.
3
Analyze Why Each Exception Forfeits Nafaqah
For each case, note how ihtibas is lost. For nushuz, she leaves without right. For minor, she cannot provide enjoyment. For imprisonment, she is legally unavailable. For hajj, she voluntarily travels away. For sick before transfer, she never entered the husband's custody.
The exception for the sick wife after consummation (by istihsan) shows that if some benefit remains, nafaqah stays.
4
Note Intra-Hanafi Differences
Learn Abu Yusuf's opinions: for a minor who can serve/companion, nafaqah is due; for a sick wife who cannot bear intercourse, no nafaqah unless illness is prolonged.
These opinions are not the mufta bihi but are recorded for completeness.
5
Apply the General Principle
The passage concludes: 'if no benefit at all can be obtained, nafaqah drops; otherwise it remains.' Use this as a test for any new scenario involving availability.
This principle harmonizes all the exceptions and is key to fatwa-giving.

Rebelliousness (nushuz) in the terminology of jurists is: 'the woman leaving her husband's house and preventing herself from him without right,' such as if she has been given her full dower, or it was entirely deferred, or she waived it. In these cases, nafaqah drops due to lack of confinement; because it is recompense for confinement. If the rebellious woman returns to her husband's house, she gets nafaqah. (Mustakhlas, Ayni) 'For the dower': i.e., for the sake of receiving the advanced dower or that which is entirely deferred. (T, A) 'Rebellious': i.e., nafaqah and clothing are not obligatory if she is rebellious, i.e., leaving his house without right, until she returns. (T) 'And a minor not slept with': i.e., nafaqah is also not obligatory if the wife is a minor with whom one does not derive pleasure, whether she is in his house or not; because the prevention of enjoyment is due to a quality in her. Al-Shafi'i said: She has nafaqah, as with a slave possessed by right of ownership; and our (Hanafi) position is that the dower is substitute for (the marriage contract), because according to him nafaqah is substitute for possession (ownership of the wife's sexual organ), so two substitutes cannot be joined for one substituted thing. And because the prevention of enjoyment, which is the purpose of marriage, is from her side, so she becomes like the rebellious woman, unlike the menstruating, postpartum, sick, or retracted woman; because benefit from them is generally obtained in terms of inclinations, or in terms of housekeeping and companionship. Abu Yusuf said: If the minor girl is suitable for service and companionship, she deserves nafaqah from him. If the husband is a minor and she is adult, nafaqah is obligatory on his wealth, like the impotent and the eunuch. If both are minors, she has no nafaqah. (Mustakhlas, Ayni) 'And a woman imprisoned for debt': i.e., nafaqah is also not obligatory if she is imprisoned for a debt owed to someone other than her husband, even if unjustly; because the prevention is from her side. Except if he imprisoned her for a debt, then she gets nafaqah according to the most correct opinion. Similarly, if he can access her in prison. His saying: 'for debt' applies whether she is able to repay it or not; due to loss of confinement, and the fatwa is upon this; because the prevention came from her side through procrastination. If it is not from her (i.e., she is unable to pay), the prevention is not from her side. (Mustakhlas, Ayni) 'And a woman performing hajj with someone other than the husband': i.e., it is also not obligatory if she is performing hajj, even with a mahram, and the fatwa is upon this. (T) He only qualified it with 'hajj' because if she is performing 'umrah or traveling for trade without her husband, they both agree it is not obligatory. He qualified with 'with someone other than the husband' because if she is with her husband, it is obligatory by agreement, meaning specifically residential maintenance (during residence), not travel maintenance or rent. According to Abu Yusuf: If she performs hajj with a mahram after having surrendered herself, she deserves residential maintenance; because performing an obligatory act is an excuse. For the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), her failure of confinement is from her. (And) His saying: 'And a sick woman who has not been brought to the husband's house': i.e., it is also not obligatory if she is sick and has not been transferred to the husband's house due to failure of confinement from her. The three Imams (Abu Hanifah, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad) said: It is obligatory, like one who has been brought. If she falls ill after being brought, she gets nafaqah. The analogy is that it is not obligatory if the illness prevents intercourse due to loss of confinement; the basis for istihsan is that confinement is still present, for he enjoys her company, touches her, and she keeps the house, and the hindrance is temporary, so it resembles menstruation. The conclusion: If no benefit whatsoever can be derived from her, her nafaqah drops; otherwise, not. From Abu Yusuf: She has no nafaqah if she is too sick to bear intercourse; otherwise, she is provided for, unless the illness is prolonged. (Fath, Ayni)

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement enumerating exceptions to the obligation of spousal maintenance. It details cases where, despite a valid marriage, the wife is not entitled to nafaqah because the underlying cause (confinement/availability for the husband) is absent due to her own action or condition. The first exception is nushuz (rebelliousness): if she leaves the house without right. Maintenance resumes upon return. The second is a minor wife with whom intercourse is not possible; the Shafi'i position (she gets nafaqah) is refuted by analogy to a slave: nafaqah is compensation for the husband's right to enjoyment, and a minor cannot fulfill that. Abu Yusuf adds nuance: if she can serve, she gets it. Third: imprisonment for a debt to a third party (not the husband) forfeits maintenance because she is unavailable (unless the husband imprisoned her). Fourth: if she goes on hajj with a mahram other than her husband, she forfeits residential maintenance; if she goes alone or with husband, rules differ. Fifth: a sick woman who has not yet been brought to the husband's house (i.e., before consummation) does not get maintenance; but if illness occurs after consummation, she does (by istihsan) because confinement is still real. The general principle: if no benefit (companionship, service) can be derived, nafaqah drops; otherwise it remains.

Key Terms
النشوز (al-nushuz): Rebelliousness: the woman leaving her husband's house and preventing herself from him without right.
الاحتباس (al-ihtibas): Confinement: the wife being available and confined to the husband, which is the basis for nafaqah.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference: used here to give nafaqah to a sick wife after consummation despite the analogy that would drop it.

Maintenance for the Absent Husband's Dependents
Ruling on judge-ordered maintenance from absent husband's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a judge can impose maintenance from an absent husband's property and identify the mufta bihi position regarding the method of payment.

Core Idea
A judge can order maintenance from the absent husband's property only if the holder acknowledges the property and marriage, and the property is of the same type as the maintenance.

This passage details the Hanafi ruling on how a judge can provide maintenance for the wife, children, and parents of an absent husband when the husband has left property with a third party. It specifies the conditions required for such a ruling, including the acknowledgment of the property and the marriage by the holder, and the type of property. Additionally, it presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether the woman should be given directly from the deposited property or ordered to borrow, with the fatwa following Zufar's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The judge imposing maintenance (nafaqah) for the absent husband's dependents. The ruling is that it is wajib, and the judge can enforce it from the husband's property under certain conditions.
Maintenance is wajib regardless of absence; the judge's role is to enforce it.
2
List the conditions for enforcement
The property must be held by someone who acknowledges both the property and the marriage (or lineage for children). The property must be of the same type as the maintenance (cash, food, clothing). The dependents must be the wife, parents, young children, or older children unable to earn.
If the holder denies, the woman's evidence is not accepted because it would be a ruling against the absent.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
The earlier position (possibly Abu Hanifa) allowed giving from the deposited property. Zufar held that the woman should be ordered to borrow on the husband's credit. The text states the fatwa is on Zufar's view as more convenient.
This is one of six matters where fatwa is given by Zufar's statement.
4
Apply the mufta bihi ruling to a scenario
Given the fatwa follows Zufar, the judge does not directly give property but orders the woman to borrow. She must also provide a guarantor and swear an oath as precautions.
The judge cannot sell property of a different type or impose a value on the absent.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on maintenance for the dependents of an absent husband. The main ruling states that a judge can impose maintenance from the husband's property that is held by a third party who acknowledges both the property and the marriage. The commentary explains that the judge can also act if he has knowledge, even without the holder's acknowledgement. A key point is that the depositary's acknowledgement is binding only on himself, not on the absent husband. The passage then introduces a dispute within the Hanafi school: the earlier view was to give the woman from the deposited property, but Zufar (a prominent Hanafi jurist) held that she should be ordered to borrow on the husband's credit instead, and this is considered more convenient. The fatwa today follows Zufar's view. The property must be of the same type as the maintenance (cash, food, clothing); otherwise, maintenance cannot be imposed because it would require valuation or sale, which is not permissible against an absent person. Gold bullion is treated like dirhams. The passage also specifies that a guarantor and an oath are taken from the woman as precautions. Maintenance from the absent person's property is only for the wife, parents, young children, and older children unable to earn. Finally, the passage contrasts Zufar's view with the earlier one regarding the woman's evidence when the holder does not acknowledge.

Key Terms
الغائب (al-ghā'ib): The husband who is absent and not present to provide for his dependents.
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Maintenance including food, clothing, and housing for dependents.
الكفيل (al-kafīl): A guarantor taken from the woman to protect the absent husband's rights in case the maintenance was already given or she is not entitled.

Maintenance for Divorced Women: Hanafi Position and Shafi'i Dispute
Ruling on maintenance for bā'in and refutation of Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance for a bā'in divorced woman and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the Hanafi refutation.

Core Idea
Maintenance for every divorced woman is wajib; Fatimah's hadith is not generalizable.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position on maintenance (nafaqah) and housing (sakan) for women undergoing 'iddah after divorce. The Hanafi school holds that both are obligatory for every divorced woman, whether revocable or irrevocable, pregnant or not. This directly contradicts the Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali positions, which restrict maintenance to pregnant women only. Our passage presents the evidence, then systematically refutes the opposing proof—the famous hadith of Fatimah bint Qays.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling and its conditions
Identify the core ruling: maintenance and housing are wajib for every divorced woman during 'iddah. Note the three conditions: she must remain in the 'iddah house, the marriage must be valid, and she must be free or a slave permitted to marry.
These conditions are explicitly listed in the passage.
2
Identify the opposing school and their proof
Imam al-Shafi'i (and Malik and Ahmad) argue that a bā'in woman only gets maintenance if pregnant. Their proof is the hadith of Fatimah bint Qays, who said the Prophet ﷺ did not give her housing or maintenance after a triple divorce.
The hadith is widespread but not in Bukhari.
3
Follow the Hanafi response – three refutations
The Hanafi response has three parts: (1) The hadith is munkar (rejected) because senior Companions like 'Umar and 'A'ishah objected to it. (2) It is mudtarib (contradictory) with multiple inconsistent versions. (3) Fatimah was nāshiz due to her sharp tongue, which forfeited her right, as indicated by Quran 65:1.
Each refutation is supported by specific narrations and scholarly opinions.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi fiqh, a bā'in woman receives maintenance and housing; under Shafi'i, she receives neither unless pregnant. This affects the daily life of divorced women and their legal rights.
The dispute has real-world implications for financial support during 'iddah.
5
Apply the ruling to exceptional cases
The passage also clarifies that no maintenance is due for widows (different reason for 'iddah) nor for women whose separation was caused by their own sin (e.g., apostasy), as they are like nāshiz.
These exceptions confirm the general principle: maintenance is tied to the woman being lawfully confined for the husband's right.

Commentary
This passage addresses the maintenance (nafaqah) and housing (sakan) for women undergoing 'iddah after divorce. The Hanafi position is that both are obligatory for every divorced woman, whether the divorce is revocable (raj'ī) or irrevocable (bā'in), and whether she is pregnant or not. Conditions include: she must remain in the 'iddah house; leaving without excuse makes her nāshiz and forfeits maintenance; she must be from a valid marriage (nikah sahih); and she must be free or a slave permitted by her master. The passage then presents a dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, who holds that a bā'in woman only gets maintenance if pregnant. The Hanafis respond with several refutations: 1) Senior Companions like 'Umar and 'A'ishah rejected Fatimah's report; 'Umar said they will not abandon Quran and Sunnah for a woman's statement. 2) The hadith is contradictory (muḍṭarib) in its details. 3) Fatimah was given maintenance initially but lost it due to her insolence (lisān) against her in-laws, making her nāshiz, as indicated by the Quranic verse on 'indecency'. Therefore, the Hanafis maintain that the general rule from the Quran (65:1) and other proofs (including a narration from Tahawi and Daraqutni) establishes maintenance and housing for all divorced women. The passage also notes that Shafi'i himself abandoned the hadith regarding housing. Finally, the passage clarifies that there is no maintenance for widows (because her waiting period is for a different reason) nor for women separated due to their own sin (like apostasy), as they are considered like nāshiz.

Child Maintenance Obligatory on Father
Ruling on father's obligation with proof and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a father's obligation to maintain his child, identify the proof from Quran 2:233, and list the conditions under which this wajib applies.

Core Idea
A father must support his young poor child — the obligation is his alone, even if the child is Muslim and he is a disbeliever.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentaries clarifies that maintenance (nafaqah) of a child is wajib on the father. The ruling is derived from Quran 2:233 and is exclusive to the father. However, important conditions apply: the child must be young, poor, and unable to earn; both father and child must be free.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling level
The act is maintenance (nafaqah) of a child. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) on the father, not on any other relative.
The text explicitly states 'wajib' for the father.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is Quran 2:233, which orders provision for nursing mothers. The Hanafi reasoning (wajh al-dalalah) is that if the father must support the mother for the child's sake, then supporting the child directly is even more obligatory (awla).
The proof is dalil naqli with qati thubut but zanni dalala.
3
List the conditions for obligation
The child must be young (saghir), poor (faqir), and unable to earn ('ajiz 'an al-kasb). The father must be free (hurr) and able to earn. Both child and father must be free (not slaves).
If the child can earn, the father may employ him and spend from that.
4
Note the exceptions and special rules
If the father refuses to spend while able, he is imprisoned — a penalty unique to maintenance. This does not apply to other debts between relatives. Also, the ruling applies even if the child is Muslim and the father is a disbeliever.
Imprisonment is a recourse for the judge.
5
Understand the scope
The text includes both male and female children (wa huwa ya'ummu al-untha wa al-jam'). The obligation is on the father alone, not shared with the mother or grandfather.
This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.

Mother Not Forced to Breastfeed Except in Extremis
Ruling on maternal breastfeeding obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maternal breastfeeding obligation and distinguish between religious duty (diyānan) and legal compulsion (qaḍā'an).

Core Idea
A mother is religiously obliged to breastfeed but the court cannot force her – only if the child refuses all others.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on whether a mother can be forced to breastfeed her child. The distinction between dīyānah (moral duty) and qaḍā' (legal enforcement) is crucial. We will also see the Quranic evidence and the exception when the child has no alternative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is maternal breastfeeding. The ruling: she is not legally compelled (lā tujbir) – it is a religious duty only.
2
Distinguish diyānan and qaḍā'an
Diyānan: obligatory in conscience, sin if omitted. Qaḍā'an: enforceable by a judge. Breastfeeding is only the former.
This distinction is a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh.
3
Analyze the qiyās to household tasks
The text analogizes breastfeeding to sweeping, washing, cooking – all are wajib dīyānan but not compelled. The precedent of the Prophet dividing indoor/outdoor work between Ali and Fatima is cited.
4
Examine the dalīl: Quran 2:233
The verse 'Do not harm a mother on account of her child' prohibits forcing her to breastfeed if it harms her.
Ayat is qaṭ'ī al-thubūt and qaṭ'ī al-dalālah.
5
Note the exception and ikhtilāf
Exception: if the child refuses all other women's breasts, she is compelled by agreement. Ikhtilāf: Mālik compels a non-noble woman, but Hanafī rejects this distinction.
The muftā bihī is the Hanafī position.

Father's Obligation to Maintain His Poor Child
Ruling that father must support his minor poor child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a father must maintain his poor minor child and identify the Quranic proof and its a fortiori reasoning.

Core Idea
Father must maintain his poor minor child only if the child cannot earn and both are free.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq, with detailed commentary, establishes the father's duty to support his young poor child. The ruling is derived from a Quranic verse that obligates provision for the mother due to the child, and by logical priority (awla), the child's own maintenance is even more obligatory. However, the duty is not absolute—it depends on the child's inability to earn and the free status of both father and child. If the father refuses to pay, he may be imprisoned, a sanction unique to maintenance debts.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its key condition
Identify the primary hukm (wajib maintenance) and the critical condition: the child must be poor (faqir) and unable to earn. This condition restricts the absolute wording of the author's phrase 'for his poor child'.
The condition is derived from the commentators' clarification that if the child can earn, the father may give him work.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof
Examine the verse (2:233) which orders the father to provide for the mother 'because of the child'. The reasoning is a fortiori: if the father must support the mother due to the child, then supporting the child directly is even more obligatory.
The verse is quoted partially; the full verse addresses nursing mothers, but the proof is by implication (dalalat al-nass).
3
Identify the scope of the obligation
Note that the obligation applies generally: it includes daughters and multiple children, and even if the child is Muslim and the father is a disbeliever. However, it is restricted to free children and free fathers. A slave child's maintenance falls on the master, and a slave father is not obligated.
This general scope is deduced from the text's absolute wording, qualified by the commentators.
4
Understand enforcement by imprisonment
If the father refuses to spend while able to earn, he can be imprisoned until he pays. This enforcement is specific to maintenance; for other debts, a parent cannot be imprisoned for a child's debt.
The text distinguishes maintenance debts from other financial obligations regarding imprisonment of parents.
5
Distinguish from similar maintenance cases
Contrast this ruling with the maintenance of a wife and child of an absent husband: that obligation is taken from the father's property, whereas this obligation is personal regardless of the father's presence or absence. Thus, there is no repetition.
This distinction answers a potential objection that the ruling is redundant.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a mother's obligation to breastfeed, explain why she is not legally compelled, and compare it with the Maliki view.

Core Idea
A mother is religiously obliged to breastfeed but cannot be forced by a judge, except if no other wet nurse exists.

The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between religious duty (diyanatan) and legal enforceability (qada'an). For breastfeeding, the mother is not compelled by the court even though it is a religious obligation. This ruling is based on the Quranic prohibition of harming the mother and the precedent of the Prophet dividing domestic and external duties between Ali and Fatima. In this lesson, we unpack the evidence and the disagreement with the Maliki school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
The text states 'ولا تجبر' — the mother is not compelled to breastfeed. Understand that this is the default ruling applicable to all mothers, noble or not.
This applies even if she is a noblewoman.
2

Locate the two proofs: (1) the Prophet's division of work between Ali and Fatima, placing inside work on Fatima; (2) Quran 2:233 prohibiting harm to a mother. Connect these to the ruling.

3
Contrast with the Maliki view
The text mentions that Malik compels the mother if she is not noble. Understand the basis: Maliki school considers breastfeeding a direct maternal duty, while Hanafi prioritizes avoiding harm to the mother.
This difference stems from different weighting of the same evidence.
4
Note the exception
The exception: if no other wet nurse can breastfeed the child, the mother is compelled by consensus. This is because the child's life is at stake, overriding the general principle.
This exception is agreed upon by all.
5

The ruling is 'wajib diyanatan' (religiously obligatory) but not enforceable by a judge. This is a key Hanafi concept: the judge does not coerce for a religious duty unless a worldly right is at stake.
This distinction applies to other domestic duties like cooking and cleaning too.

Obligation of Maintenance for Ascendants
Ruling on obligatory maintenance for ascendants if poor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which maintenance of poor parents and grandparents is wajib on a wealthy child, and identify the exceptions for harbi and musta'min parents.

Core Idea
Maintenance of poor ascendants is wajib on a wealthy child, even if they are non-Muslim, but not if they are harbi.

This lesson addresses the obligation of a person to provide for their needy parents, grandparents, and higher ascendants. While the general principle aligns with filial piety, the Hanafi school specifies conditions and exceptions that refine this duty. We will explore the textual proof from the Quran, the key conditions (poverty of ascendants, wealth of the child), and the notable exception of belligerent non-Muslims (harbi).

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligated parties
Determine who must receive maintenance: parents, grandparents, and all ascendants (even upwards). The obligation extends to both paternal and maternal lines, including sinful ascendants.
Grandparents stand in place of parents in inheritance and maintenance.
2
Identify the conditions for obligation
Three conditions: (1) the ascendant must be poor (needy); (2) the child must be wealthy (maysir); (3) the ascendant must not be harbi (belligerent) or musta'min (protected). Note that non-Muslim status (dhimmi) does not negate the obligation.
Poverty is a key distinction from wife's maintenance, which is wajib even if she is wealthy.
3
Locate the evidence
The Quranic verse (Luqman 31:15) commands kind companionship with parents, even disbelieving ones. The Hanafi school interprets 'ma'ruf' to include providing basic needs when parents are in need.
The verse was revealed about parents who were disbelievers, proving the obligation does not require Muslim parents.
4
Note the exception for harbi/musta'min
If the parents are harbi (enemies at war) or musta'min (protected non-Muslims residing in Muslim lands with a pact), there is no obligation. This is because the relationship is not one of mutual protection and loyalty.
This exception prevents supporting enemies of the state.
5
Contrast with wife's maintenance
Unlike ascendant maintenance, wife's maintenance is wajib even if she is wealthy, because it is a compensation for her confinement (habs) to the marriage, analogous to a judge's salary.
This highlights that the rationale for ascendant maintenance is need, not confinement.

Ruling on maintenance and religious difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on maintenance when the parties have different religions, and identify the two exceptions (marriage and lineage).

Core Idea
Difference of religion does not block maintenance for a spouse or direct ancestor/descendant.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq establishes a key principle in Hanafi family law: the duty to provide maintenance (nafaqah) is not automatically invalidated by a difference in religion. However, this duty is limited to two specific relationships: marriage and lineage (parent-child). In this lesson, we unpack the rule and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
Understand the general principle: 'No maintenance for differing religion' means that difference of religion cuts off nafaqah between relatives beyond the exceptions.
2
Recognize the two exceptions
The text states two ties that override the difference: marriage (al-zawjiyyah) and lineage (al-wilad). These are the only relationships where maintenance is owed despite differing religion.
Lineage includes parents, children, grandparents, and grandchildren.

The proof is from Quran 2:233, which links maintenance to inheritance. Since inheritance does not generally occur between Muslims and non-Muslims, maintenance is cut off except where inheritance could potentially occur (spouse and lineage).

4
Apply the rule
A Muslim must support his non-Muslim wife and his non-Muslim parents/children, but not other non-Muslim relatives such as siblings, uncles, or cousins.
The rule assumes the recipient is a dhimmi (non-Muslim citizen), not a harbi (enemy combatant).

Exclusivity of Maintenance Obligation in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on exclusive maintenance obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the exclusivity of maintenance obligation and explain the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
The father alone bears maintenance of his child; the child alone bears maintenance of his parents.

This lesson focuses on a core Hanafi principle in family maintenance (nafaqah): the obligation is exclusive and non-shareable. The father is solely responsible for his young child, and the child is solely responsible for his elderly parents. No other relative, even if wealthy, shares this duty. The Quranic verse 2:233 provides the primary evidence, assigning maintenance to the father alone.

Guided Path

The proof is Quran 2:233, which places maintenance of the child on the father: 'وَعَلَى الْمَوْلُودِ لَهُ رِزْقُهُنَّ'.
The verse addresses the father as the primary provider.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'upon the one to whom the child is born' (ʿalā al-mawlūdi lahu) indicates the father is exclusively addressed, implying sole responsibility.
Hanafi usul holds that addressing one party excludes others by implication.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi narrations (Khasaf, Hasan) hold that for an adult child who cannot earn, the parents share responsibility in thirds. The primary position maintains exclusivity.
Cross-madhhab: Shafiʿi may assign obligation proportionally to inheritance shares.

Maintenance for Poor Mahram Relatives
Ruling that maintenance is due to close mahram relatives who are poor and needy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance for poor mahram relatives, explain the proof from the Quran, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Maintenance for poor mahram relatives is wajib up to their inheritance share.

Maintenance (nafaqah) is not only for wives and children; the Hanafi school extends it to all close relatives who are permanently forbidden in marriage (mahram) when they are poor and unable to earn. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its conditions, and the proof from the Quran and sunna, showing how the Hanafi school derives a broader obligation than some other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is providing maintenance (nafaqah) to certain relatives. The ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) under specific conditions.
Wajib here means obligatory, not merely recommended.
2
Locate the Quranic dalil
The primary proof is Quran 2:233, specifically the phrase 'wa 'ala al-warithi mithlu dhalik' (and upon the heir is the like of that). Also the reading of Ibn Mas'ud adds 'dhi al-rahim al-mahram'.
Ibn Mas'ud's reading is considered a well-known qira'ah, used for tafsir and restriction.
3
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah
The term 'al-warith' is a derived noun (ism mushtaq). In Hanafi usul, when a ruling is attached to a derived noun, the origin of derivation (here, inheritance) is the cause. Thus inheritance is the cause for entitlement to maintenance, and the amount is measured accordingly.
This is a key usul principle: al-hukm yadurru ma'a 'illatihi wujūdan wa 'adaman.
4
Note the conditions
The relative must be: (1) mahram, (2) poor and incapable of earning (e.g., chronically ill or blind), and (3) a potential heir (i.e., would inherit at that moment). The provider must be wealthy (maysir). The maintenance amount is limited to the inheritance share of that relative.
Unlike wife and children, the provider's poverty negates the obligation for these relatives.
5
Examine the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that maintenance is only for direct lineage (walad), not for other mahram relatives. Imam Ahmad holds that it is for any heir relative. The Hanafi position is supported by Ibn Mas'ud's reading and the cause of inheritance.
The practical difference: under Shafi'i, no obligation to support poor siblings; under Hanafi, it is wajib if they are heirs.

Who Bears Nafaqah: Father, Child, and Mahram
Rulings on exclusive maintenance for father/child and extended mahram obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for exclusive maintenance obligations between father and child and for mahram relatives, and apply the condition of wealth and inheritance-based amount.

Core Idea
A father alone supports his child; a child alone supports his parents; mahram relatives are supported to the extent of inheritance if the giver is wealthy.

This passage from the chapter on nafaqah clarifies the boundaries of maintenance obligations among close relatives. While a father bears exclusive responsibility for his child, and a child for his parents, the Hanafi school extends maintenance obligations to mahram relatives under strict conditions. Understanding these rules is essential for determining who must support whom when family members are in need.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two exclusive obligations
The passage states that no one shares with the father in maintaining his child, and no one shares with the child in maintaining his parents. This establishes that the responsibility is exclusive.
This applies to minor children and elderly parents.
2
Understand the basis for exclusivity
For the father, it is his guardianship (wilayah) over the minor child. For the child, it is the hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father' (Sunan Abi Dawud).
The hadith gives the father a special claim on the child's wealth.
3
Define mahram relative and conditions
A mahram relative is any non-lineage relative within prohibited degrees of marriage (e.g., sibling, uncle, aunt). Maintenance is wajib if the relative is poor, unable to earn (e.g., infirm or blind), the giver is wealthy (by Abu Yusuf's definition: owning nisab of sadaqah), and the relative is Muslim.
Shafi'i restricts to lineage; Hanafi extends based on Quran 2:233.
4
Apply the inheritance share
The amount of maintenance is proportional to what the mahram relative would inherit from the giver. This derives from 'And upon the heir is the like of that' (Quran 2:233).
For example, a brother would inherit from his sister; so her maintenance obligation to him is half her estate if no other heirs.

Father's Sale of Son's Movables and Depositor's Liability
Rulings on father's sale for nafaqah and depositor's unauthorized spending

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a father selling his adult son's movable goods for maintenance and identify when a depositor becomes liable for unauthorized spending.

Core Idea
A father may sell his son's movables by istihsan, but not real estate; a depositor who spends without permission is liable.

This lesson examines two rulings from Kanz al-Daqā’iq on maintenance and property rights. First, the case of a poor father selling his adult son's movable goods for his own nafaqah—where Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ. Second, the treatment of a depositor who spends the deposit on the son's parents without authorization, with important exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling for father's sale
What is the act being ruled? Father selling his son's movable goods for his own maintenance. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, it is valid (jaiz) by istihsan. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, it is not valid.
Note the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf.
2
Analyse the conditions and restrictions
The father must be poor, the son must be absent and adult, only movable goods ('arad) can be sold, not real estate, and only for maintenance, not other debts.
Real estate is excluded because it is self-preserving.
3
Examine the proof and istihsan basis
Imam relies on a hadith and the principle of need, allowing sale as a form of preservation (hifz). The Sahibayn apply qiyas: no guardianship over adult son, so no sale.
The argument type is qiyas khafi (hidden analogy).
4
Identify the depositor's ruling
If a depositor (mūdi‘) spends deposited money on the parents of the depositor (i.e., the owner's parents) without permission from the owner or judge, the depositor is liable (daman).
The depositor is a trustee, not an agent.
5
Apply the exceptions
If the parents themselves spend the deposit (if it is in their hands) on their own maintenance, they are not liable because the son owes them maintenance. Also, travel companions who spend on a deceased companion's funeral are not liable by istihsan.
Exceptions are based on legal entitlement or necessity.

Maintenance for Relatives: Duration and Judge's Permission
Ruling on maintenance for relatives with duration and judge's exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when maintenance for relatives drops and explain how a judge's permission can prevent it from falling due.

Core Idea
Maintenance for relatives drops after a month if need is met; judge's permission keeps it alive as a debt.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on maintenance for relatives. Unlike the wife, whose maintenance is unconditional, maintenance for relatives is tied to need. The base text states that if a long period passes, the obligation drops, but with a crucial exception: if the judge permits borrowing, it becomes a debt and remains. Understanding this distinction is key to applying nafaqah correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is providing maintenance (nafaqah) to needy relatives. The default ruling is wajib, but it drops after a long period (more than a month) if the relative is no longer in need.
Maintenance is for need; once need is satisfied, the obligation ceases.
2
Understand the duration condition
The text defines 'long period' as more than a month. If the period is short (less than a month), the obligation does not drop. This is a unique Hanafi detail.
The measure is one month, based on typical need cycles.
3
Contrast with the wife's maintenance
The wife's maintenance does not drop by passage of time even if she is wealthy, because her right is based on marriage, not need.
This highlights the difference between need-based and contract-based obligations.
4
Analyze the judge's exception
If the judge permits the relative to borrow against the future, maintenance becomes a debt in the responsible person's liability and does not drop.
The judge's general authority allows this; it converts future maintenance into a present debt.

Maintenance for Slaves: Obligation and Enforcement
Ruling on maintenance for slaves with proof and enforcement mechanisms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the absolute obligation of maintenance on a master for his slave, cite the hadith proof, and explain the steps if the master refuses.

Core Idea
A master must provide for his slave absolutely; if he refuses, the slave uses his earnings or is sold.

This lesson covers the maintenance of slaves (mamluk). Unlike relatives, the obligation on the master is absolute, irrespective of the slave's age or ability to earn. The proof is a hadith commanding kindness. When the master refuses, the law provides remedies: the slave's earnings are used first, and if none, the master is compelled to sell. Understanding these steps ensures just treatment of slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation and its scope
Maintenance for a slave is wajib on the master, for every slave (male, female, young, old). It is not conditional on need.
This includes food, clothing, and reasonable care.
2
Examine the proof (dalil)
The hadith: 'They are your brothers... let him feed him from what he eats and clothe him from what he wears.' The imperative indicates obligation.
The hadith is considered sound (thubut qati) and the meaning (dalala) is clear.
3
If the master refuses, first option: slave's earnings
If the slave has earnings (e.g., hired out), the maintenance is taken from those earnings, as it serves both the slave's life and the master's property.
This is a compromise that preserves both interests.
4
If no earnings, second option: sell the slave
If the slave cannot earn (disabled, or female not hireable), the master is compelled to sell. The price replaces the slave, so the master's right is not negated.
This differs from a wife, where separation is not allowed because it would be negation without substitute.

Second, the ruling for slaves: maintenance is absolutely obligatory on the master for any slave, male or female, young or old. The proof is the hadith commanding masters to feed and clothe slaves appropriately. If the master refuses, the slave's maintenance is taken from his own earnings if he can earn. If he cannot earn (disabled or similar), the master is compelled to sell the slave, because the slave's value is realized in the price, unlike the wife where separation would be unjust without substitute. Animals are different: they are not considered deserving, so no compulsion, but it is recommended. Abu Yusuf and the other three imams differ, holding that one is compelled to spend on animals due to the prohibition of torture. Finally, for a mudabbar or umm walad, if the master refuses, they earn and eat from earnings; if no earnings, the master is compelled to maintain them. A slave may take from the master's wealth only if incapable of earning.

Maintenance for Animals and Special Slaves: The Dispute and Rulings
Dispute and special rulings for animals, mudabbar, and umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on compelling maintenance for animals, and state the special rulings for mudabbar and umm walad.

Core Idea
Animals are not entitled to maintenance as a right; but Abu Yusuf compels spending to avoid torture.

This lesson addresses the edge cases of nafaqah: animals, mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death), and umm walad (slave mother of master's child). The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on animals: Abu Hanifa says no compulsion, while Abu Yusuf and the majority say compulsion. For mudabbar and umm walad, the rules are stricter: they cannot be sold, so if the master refuses, he is forced to maintain them directly. Understanding these distinctions completes the picture of Hanafi maintenance law.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule for animals
Abu Hanifa holds that maintenance for animals is not legally enforceable; the master is not compelled to spend or sell. However, it is a religious duty (fatwa between him and Allah) to provide or sell.
Animals are not considered 'deserving' (ahl al-istihqaq).
2
Contrast with Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf and the three (Muhammad, Zufar, etc.) hold that the master is compelled to spend on animals or sell them, because torturing animals is prohibited.
This is the majority view among the Sahibayn and later scholars.
3
Apply to jointly owned animals
If an animal is jointly owned and one co-owner refuses to spend, the judge can order him to either sell his share or spend.
This avoids the co-owner free-riding.
4
Special rulings for mudabbar and umm walad
These slaves cannot be sold. If the master refuses to maintain them, they earn and eat from earnings. If no earnings, the master is compelled to spend directly.
Sale is not an option, so the master is forced to maintain.
5
Right of slave to take from master's wealth
If the master refuses, the slave may take from the master's wealth only if the slave is incapable of earning. If capable, he must earn unless the master forbids it.
This is a limited right to prevent starvation.
