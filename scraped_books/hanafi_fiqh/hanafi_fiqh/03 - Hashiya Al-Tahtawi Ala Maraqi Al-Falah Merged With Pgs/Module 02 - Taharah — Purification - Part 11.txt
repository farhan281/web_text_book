Ruling on Writing on Graves and Applying Clay
States rulings and proofs for writing on graves and applying clay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on writing on graves and applying clay, and explain the evidence from the hadith of the Prophet's treatment of Ibrahim's grave.

Core Idea
Writing on graves is disliked; applying clay to exposed graves is permissible.

This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi discusses two related issues: the disliked nature of writing on graves according to Abu Yusuf, and the permissibility of applying clay when graves become exposed. The proof is drawn from the Prophet's action of marking his son's grave with a stone and his command to perfect deeds. A secondary hadith about wind and rain as expiation is also cited, reinforcing the respectful treatment of graves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rulings stated
Find the two rulings: writing on graves is makruh (according to Abu Yusuf), and applying clay to exposed graves is permissible (no harm).
Note that the first ruling is a narration from Abu Yusuf, not necessarily the majority Hanafi view.
2
Examine the first proof
Look at the hadith of the Prophet passing by the grave of his son Ibrahim, seeing a stone, and saying 'Whoever does a deed, let him do it perfectly.' How does this apply?
The stone serves as a marker; the general command implies care for graves.
3
Examine the second proof
Consider the hadith about wind and rain being expiation for the believer's sins. How does this relate to applying clay?
The commentary clarifies wording variations; the meaning supports natural elements being beneficial.
4
Understand the intra-Hanafi difference
Note that Abu Yusuf's dislike is reported; other Hanafis may permit writing without dislike. The relied-upon fatwa conditions permissibility on need (e.g., identification).
The mufta bihi position balances respect and necessity.
5
Apply the ruling to a modern scenario
Consider a cemetery where grave markers are weather-damaged; is it permissible to write names again? Use the principles from this passage.
Apply the condition of need and avoidance of disrespect.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) discussing rulings related to graves. It begins by affirming that the grave of a knowledgeable person is respected and not abused, then reports from Abu Yusuf that he disliked writing on graves. This indicates that writing without necessity is makruh. The matn then states that applying clay to exposed graves is permissible, supported by the hadith of the Prophet marking the grave of his son Ibrahim with a stone and the exhortation to perfect one's work. The hadith about wind and rain as expiation for sins is quoted and linguistically analyzed, confirming that the movement of wind on the grave carries a purifying meaning. The commentary clarifies variants in the hadith's wording.

تطيين (tatyeen): Applying clay or mud to cover exposed graves.
خفق (khafaq): Movement or fluttering, used for wind striking the grave.

Rulings of the Battlefield Martyr: Washing, Shrouding, and Prayer
Explains the legal rulings for the battlefield martyr regarding washing, shrouding, prayer, and removal of items.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on washing, shrouding, and praying over the battlefield martyr, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the martyr in janabah.

Core Idea
The battlefield martyr is not washed but prayed over; only if he died in janabah does Abu Hanifa require washing.

This passage from al-Tahtawi's Hashiya clarifies the unique fiqh of the shahid. While most deceased Muslims require ghusl and kafan, the martyr buried in his blood receives special honor. However, not all categories are identical—let's examine the precise boundaries and the evidence behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
State that the battlefield martyr is not washed, is prayed over, and is buried in his blood and clothes. This is the general rule based on the Prophet's practice at Uhud.
The prayer is fard kifayah; washing is omitted to preserve the blood as a mark of martyrdom.
2
Locate the evidence
Examine the hadith of Ibn Abbas in Abu Dawud: the Prophet ordered removal of iron and leather, burial with blood and clothes. Also the hadith of Hanzala shows the angels washing him due to janabah.
The evidence provides both the general rule and the exception.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Note that Abu Hanifa requires washing for the martyr who died in janabah, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that janabah is removed by death and the sword suffices.
The hadith of Hanzala is the key proof for Abu Hanifa's view, which is mufta bihi.
4
Apply to special cases
Extend the exception to similar cases: child, insane, menstruating, postpartum women follow the same ruling as the junub according to Imam Abu Hanifa. Also, the murtath (carried alive after war) is washed because he regained worldly status.
The ruling for murtath shows that the worldly ruling (washing) returns when the person survives the battle.

Commentary
This passage from the Hashiya of al-Tahtawi on Maraqi al-Falah is a critical refinement of the rulings concerning the battlefield martyr (shahid). The author begins by affirming that the martyr is not washed, as per the Sunnah, citing multiple proofs from the Prophet's practice at Uhud: the Prophet prayed over Hamza and placed an Ansari martyr beside him, praying over him repeatedly, indicating that prayer is offered but washing is omitted. The prayer over the deceased is to honor the believer, and the martyr is most deserving. Next, the shroud is discussed: items not suitable for the shroud (like fur, stuffing, weapons, armor) are removed if something else is available; otherwise, the martyr's own clothes suffice. If his clothes are insufficient, additional cloth is added; if they exceed the shroud of the Sunnah, the excess is removed to preserve for heirs or the Muslim treasury. It is disliked to remove all his clothes so the blood remains as a mark of his martyrdom. A key intra-Hanafi dispute is then presented: According to Imam Abu Hanifa, if the martyr was in a state of major ritual impurity (janabah), he must be washed, based on the hadith that the angels washed Hanzala when he died in janabah. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that he is not washed even then, because death removes janabah. The author supports Abu Hanifa's view by emphasizing that the child and insane—who have no sin—are not washed, so the sword suffices in their case, but the junub is different. The passage continues with extensions: if the martyr is a child or insane, according to Imam, they are not washed because they lack sin; similarly, a menstruating or postpartum woman is treated like the junub—washed according to Imam. Finally, the case of the murtath (one carried from battlefield alive after war ends) is discussed: since he participated in worldly life after the battle, the worldly ruling (washing) applies, so he is washed, though he remains a martyr in the hereafter. The commentary carefully examines each detail, often noting the basis in hadith and the distinctions between the positions of the founders.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahid): One who is killed in battle defending Islam; specific rulings apply: not washed (except in janabah per Abu Hanifa), buried in his blood and clothes, and prayer is offered over him.
مرتث (murtath): One who is carried from the battlefield alive with remaining life after the war ends; he is washed (worldly ruling applies) but is a martyr in the hereafter.
غسل (ghusl): Washing of the deceased body; obligatory for Muslims except the battlefield martyr (with exceptions).

Ruling on Washing the Martyr and Types of Martyrs
Conditions for exemption from ghusl and types of afterlife martyrs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for not washing a martyr and distinguish between a martyr of this world and the afterlife.

Core Idea
Only the battlefield martyr with no worldly engagement is not washed; all others receive ghusl.

The washing of martyrs is a nuanced topic in Hanafi fiqh. While the default for a deceased Muslim is ghusl (washing), shahada (martyrdom) can suspend this obligation under specific conditions. This lesson refines when a martyr is exempt from ghusl and when they are still washed but receive the reward of martyrdom in the Hereafter. Understanding these distinctions is critical for proper janazah practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling and the exception
Start with the general principle: every dead Muslim is washed (sunnah muakkadah). The exception is the shahid (martyr) who dies on the battlefield without worldly engagement after the fight. This passage clarifies the boundaries of that exception.
The exception is not automatic; it depends on actions after the battle.
2
Trace the definition of 'murtath'
A 'murtath' is a wounded soldier carried from the battlefield alive. Such a person loses the exemption because they experienced worldly life after the battle. List the actions that trigger the need for ghusl: eating, drinking, sleeping, treatment, moving for reasons other than danger, making a will (according to the majority), etc.
Even a small act like drinking water nullifies the exemption.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on wills
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani differed on whether a will about Hereafter matters nullifies the exemption. The commentator reconciles by applying Abu Yusuf's view to worldly wills and Muhammad's to religious wills. The preferred view is that any will suggests worldly concern and thus requires ghusl.
This dispute shows the precision of Hanafi legal reasoning.
4
Distinguish martyrs of the world vs. the afterlife
Those who die in battle without post-battle worldly acts are 'martyrs of both' (not washed). Those who are murtath or die from certain causes (plague, drowning, etc.) are 'martyrs of the afterlife' only, so they are washed but receive the reward of shahada.
The worldly ruling (ghusl) is separate from the afterlife reward.

Commentary
This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi is a super-commentary that critically refines the rulings on who is considered a martyr not requiring ghusl (washing) after death, and who is a martyr in the Hereafter. The author begins by discussing the ruling for a menstruating or postpartum woman killed in battle: they are like the junub (impure) and thus do receive ghusl, unlike the martyrs of Uhud. Then he explains the concept of 'murtath' — one who is carried from the battlefield with life remaining. Such a person is washed because he engaged in worldly actions (eating, drinking, treating wounds, etc.) after the war, which removes the special status of not being washed. The text emphasizes that if the murtath performs acts like eating, sleeping, or moving for reasons other than fear of trampling, he loses the worldly ruling of being unwashed. An intra-Hanafi dispute is presented: Abu Yusuf holds that making a will does not nullify the martyr's exemption from ghusl, whether the will concerns worldly or religious matters, while Muhammad al-Shaybani considers that a will about Hereafter matters does nullify it. The commentator clarifies that the apparent view is there is no real dispute, as each addresses different types of will. The passage then lists many categories of martyrs of the Hereafter—those who die from specific diseases (e.g., stomach illness, drowning, plague, pleurisy, tuberculosis), in childbirth, in defense of property/family, in a strange land, etc.—citing rewards and even quoting a hadith about the reward of a martyr for those who uphold the Sunnah during corruption. The commentary stresses that these are martyrs in the afterlife only, so they still receive ghusl in this world. The overall lesson is that the worldly ruling of not being washed is restricted to those who die directly in battle without any post-battle worldly engagement; all others are washed, while many receive the reward of martyrdom in the Hereafter.

This lesson focuses on a specific ruling regarding martyrs (shuhada): whether eating or speaking after being wounded affects their status. Ruling on mixed bodies and dhimmi woman with proof

Core Idea
When bodies mix, majority determines prayer and burial; the dhimmi pregnant woman's case shows prioritising the child's Muslim status.

This lesson addresses two related issues: first, how to handle prayer and burial when the bodies of Muslims and non-Muslims are intermixed, relying on the principle of ghalabah (overwhelming majority); second, the specific case of a dhimmi (non-Muslim protected) woman who dies pregnant with a Muslim child, where the Companions differed. The Hanafi mufta bihi prioritises the child's status, but a compromise of a separate cemetery is also recorded.

State the general rule for mixed bodies
If the dead are mixed and Muslims are the majority, prayer (salat al-janazah) is performed over all, intending the Muslims. The prayer is performed without washing for the slain martyrs, and with washing for other deceased.
2
Identify the exceptions
If Muslims are not the majority (disbelievers are more or equal), no prayer is performed unless a specific individual is known to be Muslim by distinguishing signs (sima: circumcision, henna dyeing, wearing black). Then a separate cemetery is made for them.
The rule 'the prohibited overcomes the permitted' is cited.

Examine the dhimmi pregnant woman case
The Companions differed on whether a dhimmi woman pregnant with a Muslim child should be buried in a Muslim cemetery or a polytheist cemetery. Some prioritised the child (Muslim), others the mother's non-Muslim status. A third view suggested a separate cemetery.
This ikhtilaf is considered the origin of the mixed bodies rule.
4
Determine the Hanafi mufta bihi
The stronger view, transmitted from the majority of Companions, is that she is buried in Muslim cemeteries, prioritising the child's Muslim identity. This is the Hanafi position. The separate cemetery is a compromise when prayer is not performed.
5
Apply the principle of ghalabah
In all cases, the ruling for the majority applies. For the dhimmi woman, the child's Muslim status is given weight because it is the Islamic value to honour.

Commentary
This passage from the Hashiya of al-Tahtawi on Maraqi al-Falah deals with several rulings concerning martyrs (shuhada) and the mixing of Muslim and non-Muslim bodies. The commentary opens by clarifying that the rulings differ based on whether the martyr ate or engaged in worldly activities before or after the cessation of war. If such acts occurred after the war ended, the martyr's status remains unaffected; however, if they occurred before the war ended, the martyr is not considered 'murtath' (one carried from the battlefield still alive) and thus does not receive the full martyr's rulings. The example of Sa'd ibn ar-Rabi' from the Battle of Uhud is cited as proof: despite being severely wounded and speaking, he was still considered a martyr because he spoke after the war had effectively ended (after he was wounded and the battle was over). The passage then addresses the scenario where Muslim and non-Muslim bodies are mixed together. If Muslims are in the majority, prayer is performed over all, intending the Muslims; if disbelievers are in the majority, no prayer is performed unless a specific individual is known to be Muslim. Signs of being Muslim include circumcision, henna dyeing, and wearing black. If the numbers are equal, no prayer is performed because the prohibited overcomes the permitted. A separate cemetery is made for the mixed bodies when prayer is not performed; if prayer is performed, they are buried in Muslim cemeteries. Finally, the passage discusses a dhimmi (non-Muslim protected person) woman who died pregnant with a Muslim child. There was ikhtilaf among the Companions: some preferred the child's status and said she should be buried in Muslim cemeteries, while others considered her as a polytheist. Uqba ibn Amir suggested a separate cemetery. The commentary notes that the root of the ikhtilaf in mixed bodies case originally stems from this issue. The Hanafi position, derived from the majority and the practice of the Companions, is that if Muslims are more, prayer is performed and they are buried with Muslims; otherwise not, but care is taken for identifiable Muslims.

Invalidation of I'tikaf by Intercourse and Its Precursors
Ruling on what invalidates i'tikaf with Quranic proof and analogical reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on what invalidates i'tikaf regarding intercourse and its precursors, explain the Quranic proof (2:187), and distinguish the rule from fasting using the concept of 'hala mudhakkara'.

Core Idea
Intercourse and its precursors invalidate i'tikaf, but not fasting, due to the 'reminding context' of i'tikaf.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling from the chapter on i'tikaf: what invalidates the seclusion. The text provides a Quranic foundation (2:187) and uses analogical reasoning to extend the prohibition to precursors of intercourse. Crucially, the Hanafi school distinguishes i'tikaf from fasting in this regard, based on the nature of the acts. Let's unpack the proof and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is i'tikaf (seclusion in the mosque for worship). The ruling under discussion is that intercourse is prohibited and invalidates i'tikaf.
The text also covers precursors (kissing, touching) and their effect.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is Quran 2:187: 'Do not approach them while you are secluded in the mosques.' This is a direct prohibition.
The verse uses the term 'tubashiruhunna' which implies sexual contact.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative prohibition indicates that intercourse is haram. By analogy (qiyas), its precursors are also prohibited because they lead to the prohibited act.
The text compares with ihram, zihar, and istibra' where precursors are also prohibited.
4
Analyze the distinction from fasting
In fasting, precursors do not invalidate because the essence (rukn) of fasting is abstaining from intercourse, and the prohibition is implicit to protect the rukn. In i'tikaf, there is a 'reminding context' (hala mudhakkara) similar to prayer and Hajj, so the prohibition extends to precursors.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
5
Determine the scope of invalidation
I'tikaf is invalidated by intercourse or by ejaculation resulting from precursors, regardless of intent (deliberate, forgetful, or coerced). However, ejaculation from mere thought or looking does not invalidate.
The text explicitly excludes reflection and looking without physical contact.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling on what invalidates i'tikaf. It begins by discussing the prohibition of speaking without good for a mu'takif, noting that even permissible speech is disliked if the sole purpose is to sit in the mosque for idle talk. Then it focuses on the central issue: sexual intercourse and its precursors. The proof is from the Quranic verse (2:187) which explicitly prohibits approaching one's spouse during i'tikaf. From this, the text derives that touching and kissing are also prohibited by analogy (qiyas). The reasoning is that intercourse is prohibited in i'tikaf, so anything that leads to it (precursors) is also prohibited, just as in the state of ihram, zihar (like saying to one's wife 'You are like my mother's back') and istibra' (seeking purity after menstruation). However, this is distinguished from fasting, where such precursors are not prohibited. The explanation is that in fasting, the essence (rukn) is abstaining from intercourse, and the prohibition of intercourse is only implied to ensure the fulfillment of the rukn; hence it remains limited. In contrast, i'tikaf has a 'reminding context' (hala mudhakkara) similar to prayer and Hajj, where the atmosphere serves as a constant reminder. Therefore, forgiveness does not excuse invalidation by intercourse, unlike in fasting where forgetfulness does not invalidate. The passage concludes that if one ejaculates through thinking or looking without physical contact, i'tikaf is not invalidated.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the Book of Zakat from the Hashiya of al-Tahtawi on Maraqi al-Falah. It begins by defining zakat as the transfer of ownership of specific wealth to a specific person. It states its obligation upon free Muslims who are legally responsible and own the nisab. The passage emphasizes that zakat is obligatory immediately (al-fawr), and delaying without excuse is sinful and causes rejection of testimony. Prophets are exempt because they own nothing; all they possess is a trust. Zakat is a purification from sins, supported by Qur'anic verse 9:103. The text then narrates a hadith about punishment for withholding zakat, including severe curses. The commentary then delves into the wording of the matn, explaining 'hiya' (it is) by affirming the definition of tamlik (transfer of ownership) as the correct view among usul scholars. It clarifies that tamlik excludes ibaha (mere permission), so simply allowing someone to use wealth without ownership transfer does not suffice for zakat. Wealth (mal) is defined as tangible property, not benefits. The nisab is specified as one quarter of a tenth of the capital, or its equivalent from livestock sadaqat. The recipient must be poor and among the eight categories; the giver must cut off all benefit. Conditions of obligation are then enumerated: freedom, Islam, legal responsibility (adult and sane), complete ownership of nisab, free from debt and primary needs, and the passing of one lunar year. The commentary addresses nuances: no zakat on children or the insane if insane a full year; partial sanity requires sanity at start and end of year. Zakat is due on gold and silver even if used for jewelry or adornment because they are created as prices. Debt owed by the owner does not prevent zakat if the debt is not a claim from others; deferred debts are like existing debts. Primary needs (haja asliyya) such as clothing, housing, tools, and books of knowledge for their owners are exempt from zakat. The passage follows the order of the matn, expanding each term with legal precision.

Key Terms
تمليك (tamlīk): Transfer of ownership, excluding mere permission (ibaha).
صدقة (ṣadaqah): Charity; used in the verse to refer to zakat.

Conditions of the Payer (Who Must Pay Zakat)
Conditions of the person obligated to pay zakat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the conditions of the payer and identify who is exempt from zakat.

Core Idea
Zakat is obligatory only on a free, Muslim, adult, sane owner of nisab.

This lesson details the personal conditions that must be present for zakat to be obligatory. The text expands on freedom, Islam, legal responsibility (mukallaf), and complete ownership of nisab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five conditions
The text states: 1) free, 2) Muslim, 3) legally responsible (adult and sane), 4) owner of nisab, 5) complete ownership.
These conditions are derived from the concise matn.
2
Analyze each condition
For freedom: slaves are exempt. For Islam: disbelievers are not addressed. For responsibility: children and the insane are exempt unless partially sane.
The insane: if insane the whole year, no zakat; if partially sane, require sanity at beginning and end.
3
Understand the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
There is a difference regarding the insane. Abu Hanifa requires sanity at start and end; others may differ.
Imam Abu Hanifa's view is preferred in this gloss.
4
Note the ownership condition
The nisab must be owned completely; thus a mukatib (slave making contract) does not fully own what he earns.
Corrupt ownership (e.g., usurped property) may be subject to zakat if no separate property to satisfy debts.

Conditions of the Wealth (Zakatable Assets)
Conditions of the wealth for zakat obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions of the wealth for zakat, including nisab, growth, freedom from debt and primary needs, and the passing of one year.

Core Idea
Zakatable wealth must be nisab, growing, free from debt and primary needs, and have one lunar year pass over it.

This lesson covers the properties of the wealth itself that must be present for zakat to be due. The text expands on nisab, types of wealth, and exclusions like debt and primary needs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wealth conditions
The text lists: nisab (cash, gold/silver, jewelry, vessels, trade goods), free from debt, free from primary needs, growing (even potentially), and passing of one year.
Nisab for gold is 20 mithqal, for silver 200 dirhams.
2
Understand 'free from debt'
Zakat is not due on wealth that is offset by debts owed to others, including deferred debts.
There is a difference: Muhammad said 'arud al-dayn is like destruction; preferred in al-Bahr.
3
Understand 'primary needs'
Items essential for living (clothing, housing, tools, books of knowledge for scholars) are exempt.
If one has cash set aside for these needs, no zakat until the cash is no longer allocated.
4
Understand 'growing'
The wealth must be capable of growth, even if not actually invested; gold and silver are considered growing because they are prices.
A dormant asset like a used car may not be zakatable unless it is trade goods.
5
Year passing
The nisab must be present for the entire lunar year. If it decreases below nisab, the year restarts.
This condition ensures zakah is annual.
