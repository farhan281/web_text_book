The Author's Opening: Basmalah, Hamdalah, and Purpose of the Hashiya
Author's opening: basmalah, hamdalah, and purpose of the book

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three obligatory elements in a classical Hanafi fiqh book opening and explain the rationale for their inclusion and order.

Core Idea
A classical Hanafi fiqh work begins with three obligatory elements: basmalah, hamdalah, and salawat, reflecting Qur'anic style and prophetic tradition.

This passage opens al-Tahtawi's Hashiya on Maraqi al-Falah, a standard Hanafi fiqh commentary. The author explicitly outlines the conventional structure expected of every author: three obligatory openings and four permissible ones. Understanding this structure is essential for navigating classical Hanafi texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three obligatory elements
The author states that it is obligatory (wajib) for every author to include basmalah, hamdalah, and salawat. Locate these in the Arabic text.
The term 'wajib san'atan' means obligatory by custom/profession, not by sharia.
2
Understand why they are obligatory
The author mentions two reasons: the strength of the transmitted hadith and conformity to the style of the Quran. This elevates them above mere custom.
3
Recognize the permissible additions
The author lists four permissible items: praising the subject, stating the motive, naming the book, and explaining its organization. These are optional.
The contrast between 'wajib' and 'ja'iz' is key.
4
Appreciate the order
The basmalah is placed first because of its strong hadith basis and its harmony with the Quranic style. This order is deliberate.
This ordering is common to most Islamic works.

Introduction: Obligatory Elements in Authoring a Book
Author's opening explaining the structure and purpose of the book

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the three obligatory elements that a Hanafi author includes in the preface of a fiqh work and distinguish them from the permissible additions.

Core Idea
Every Hanafi author opens with basmalah, hamdalah, and salawat — these are obligatory in scholarly practice, not fiqh law.

This opening passage from al-Tahtawi’s Hashiya explains the proper structure of a classical Hanafi fiqh book. The author states that every writer must include three sacred formulas, and may add four optional elements. Understanding this structure helps you navigate any Hanafi text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three obligatory elements
Read the line: 'لما كان من الواجب صناعة على كل مصنف ثلاثة أشياء البسملة والحمدلة والصلاة على النبي'. Note that these three formulas are a customary obligation (واجب صناعة) for authors.
This obligation is not a fiqh ruling; it is a scholarly convention.
2
List the four permissible additions
Look for 'ومن الجائز أربعة مدح الفن وذكر الباعث له وتسمية الكتاب وبيان كيفيته من التبويب والتفصيل'. These are optional but recommended.
These additions allow the author to contextualize the work.
3
Understand why the author prioritized them
The text says 'وقدمها على غيرها لقوة حديثها ولموافقة أسلوب القرآن'. The basmalah etc. have strong transmitted support and follow the Quranic style.
This shows the author's reverence for primary sources.

The Ruling of Basmalah in Author’s Opening and at Slaughter
Author introduces his work and clarifies the fiqh ruling of basmalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three obligatory elements of an author’s opening and explain the Hanafi ruling on basmalah at slaughter.

Core Idea
Basmalah is obligatory at slaughter but its specific wording is not required; any sincere dhikr suffices.

This passage comes from the opening of al-Tahtawi's super-commentary on Maraqi al-Falah. It establishes a foundational rule for any Islamic author: one must begin with the basmalah, hamdalah, and salawat. The passage then transitions into a fiqh discussion, demonstrating that even a seemingly simple act like saying 'Bismillah' carries a legal ruling that varies by context—sometimes obligatory, sometimes not. Understanding this will help you appreciate how the five legal categories (ahkam khamsah) apply to everyday actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three obligatory elements for an author
Read the sentence: 'لما كان من الواجب صناعة على كل مصنف ثلاثة أشياء البسملة والحمدلة والصلاة على النبي'. Note that the author considers these three as obligatory (wajib) in the craft of writing.
This is a unique ruling about authorship, not general fiqh.
2
Distinguish between obligatory and permissible additions
The text mentions four optional additions: praising the art, stating the motive, naming the book, and explaining the structure. Recognize that these are permissible (ja'iz), not obligatory.
This shows the flexibility for authors to customize their introductions.
3
Locate the fiqh ruling on basmalah at slaughter
Find the phrase: 'فتارة يكون فرضاً كما عند الذبح'. This explicitly states that basmalah is fard (obligatory) at the time of slaughter.
The context here is dhabh (Islamic slaughter).
4
Understand the required wording for tasmiyah
The text clarifies: 'وإن كان لا يشترط هذا اللفظ بتمامه بل لا يسن، وإنما المنقول باسم الله الله أكبر'. The full phrase 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' is not required; rather, the transmitted formula is 'Bismillah Allahu Akbar', and any sincere remembrance of Allah suffices.
This refines a common misunderstanding.
5
Apply the ruling to the case of forgetting tasmiyah
The text begins to address the issue of one who forgets: 'ولا يرد حل ذبيحة ناسي التسمية لأن الشرع أقام كونه مسلماً من ...' (though cut off). The Hanafi position is that forgetting does not render the meat impermissible.
This is based on the principle that a Muslim's default state is acting according to Sharia.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to his super-commentary (hāshiya) on Marāqī al-Falāḥ, itself a commentary on Nūr al-Īḍāḥ. The author, al-Ṭaḥṭāwī, begins with the basmalah and ḥamdalah, then explains his motivation: he compiled these 'delicate annotations' from the works of earlier scholars, seeking Allah's acceptance. He then quotes the original author (Marāqī al-Falāḥ) who states that every author is obligated to begin with three things: the basmalah, the ḥamdalah (praise of Allah), and the ṣalāt (prayers) upon the Prophet ﷺ. It is also permissible to add four: praising the field of knowledge, stating the motive for writing, naming the book, and explaining its structure. The author placed the basmalah first due to the strength of a ḥadīth (tradition) and to follow the Quranic style. The 'investigators' (al-muḥaqqiqūn) then note that one should discuss the basmalah according to the relevant field. Here, the field is fiqh, which deals with the actions of the mukallaf (legally responsible person) and the five rulings: wujūb (obligation), nadb (recommendation), ibāḥa (permissibility), ḥurma (prohibition), and karāha (dislike). Since uttering the basmalah is an action, it must have a ruling. It can be obligatory, e.g., at the time of slaughter (dhabḥ). However, the specific wording 'Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm' is not required; rather, it is not even recommended (lā yusann). The transmitted formula is 'Bismillāh Allāhu Akbar,' and any remembrance (dhikr) devoted sincerely to Allah suffices. Consequently, the meat of a Muslim who forgets the tasmiya (saying the name of Allah) at slaughter is not rendered impermissible, because the Sharīʿah considers him a Muslim (by default). The text cuts off mid-sentence.

Key Terms
بسملة (basmalah): The phrase 'Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm' (In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful).
حمدلة (ḥamdalah): The phrase 'Al-ḥamdu lillāh' (Praise be to Allah).
الصلاة على النبي (al-ṣalāt 'alā al-nabī): Sending prayers upon the Prophet Muhammad (e.g., 'Allāhumma ṣalli 'alā Muḥammad').
مكلف (mukallaf): A legally responsible person: one who is of sound mind and has reached puberty, subject to the rulings of Sharīʿah.
الأحكام الخمسة (al-aḥkām al-khamsah): The five legal categories: wujūb (obligation), nadb (recommendation), ibāḥa (permissibility), ḥurma (prohibition), karāha (dislike).
تسمية (tasmiyah): Saying the name of Allah (specifically at slaughter).

Core Idea
Uttering the shahada suffices for conversion; order and understanding are not conditions.

This passage from al-Tahtawi's Hashiya addresses a critical fiqh issue: what exactly is required for a non-Muslim to become Muslim? The Hanafi school adopts a notably lenient position, emphasizing that mere utterance of the shahada, even without understanding, is sufficient. This ruling has profound practical implications for da'wah and modern conversion scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is the conversion of a non-Muslim (kafir) to Islam. The ruling is that it is valid by merely uttering the shahada without the need for understanding or order.
This is a fa‘liyah (performative act) that removes the status of kufr.
2
Understand the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school relies on the Hadith that the Prophet said 'I was commanded to fight people until they say La ilaha illa Allah', indicating that the utterance is the criterion.
The proof is from the Sunnah, classified as ahad but operative.
3
Compare with other views
Many hadith scholars (muhaddithun) and the Shafi'i school require understanding the meaning of the shahada. The Hanafis argue that the sahabah accepted people who uttered it without knowing Arabic.
The text mentions that even if someone prays behind a Muslim imam and completes the prayer, they are judged Muslim.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that utterance alone suffices, as stated in al-Qahistani and other later Hanafi sources.
This is a mutakhkhirin fatwa, but it is strong within the school.

Commentary
This passage is the opening sermon (khutbah) of the book. The author begins by discussing the proper pronunciation and understanding of the shahada (لا إله إلا الله). He notes that there is a difference of opinion on whether to elongate the 'alif' in 'لا' or keep it short, with some choosing elongation to emphasize the negation of all other deities, and others choosing shortening to avoid dying before completing the name of Allah. He emphasizes that one must mentally affirm the existence of the One True God while uttering the negation, else the negation alone is disbelief. He then discusses the divine attributes 'الملك' (the King) and 'البر' (the Beneficent), explaining linguistic nuances. Next, he delves into the name 'Muhammad', its etymology, and its superiority over 'Ahmad'. He discusses the Prophet's kunyah (Abu al-Qasim) and the ruling that it is permissible to use it after the initial prohibition was abrogated. Finally, he addresses a significant fiqh issue: the conditions for a non-Muslim to enter Islam. According to the Hanafi school, it is not required to utter both parts of the shahada in order, nor to understand their meaning. If a person affirms the oneness of Allah but rejects the prophethood of Muhammad, they enter Islam by saying 'Muhammad Rasul Allah'. Similarly, if a person prays behind a Muslim imam and completes the prayer, they are judged to be Muslim. The key point is that the mere utterance of the shahada, even without knowledge of its meaning, is sufficient for a person to become Muslim, provided they are not mocking or lying.

Key Terms
لا إله إلا الله (La ilaha illa Allah): The declaration of faith means 'There is no deity but Allah', the fundamental statement of Islamic monotheism.
شهادة (shahadah): The testimony of faith comprising the two parts: لا إله إلا الله and محمد رسول الله.
مرتد (murtadd): Apostate, one who leaves Islam. The text refers to the chapter on apostasy (al-Qahistani) for the ruling on the shahada.

The Opening Sermon: Proper Utterance of the Shahada and Divine Attributes
Opening sermon with linguistic and creedal guidance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the recommended recitation (tajweed) of the shahada and explain the Hanafi perspective on the meaning of the divine attributes 'al-Malik' and 'al-Barr'.

Core Idea
The shahada must be pronounced with careful articulation, and the nuances of Allah's names have profound legal and theological implications.

This lesson introduces the opening sermon of the text, which sets the tone for the entire work. The author begins with the shahada, discussing its proper pronunciation and the importance of elongating certain letters. He then explains the divine names 'al-Malik' and 'al-Barr', highlighting linguistic and theological distinctions. Understanding these preliminaries is essential for mastering the fiqh that follows, as the shahada is the foundation of all acts of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the recitation of the shahada. The ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) for every Muslim to know and utter it correctly, but the proper tajweed (elongation, emphasis) is sunnah or recommended.
The text discusses whether to elongate the alif in 'la' or not, and the preferred view.
2
Analyze the linguistic nuances
The divine name 'al-Malik' (the King) is more specific than 'al-Malik' (the Owner) because it implies sovereignty and authority to command and forbid. 'Al-Barr' (the Beneficent) means the One who does good extensively.
These are not just names; they affect how we understand Allah's actions and our worship.
3
Understand the importance of intention
When uttering the negation 'la ilaha', one must mentally affirm the existence of the One True God; otherwise, the negation alone is disbelief.
This highlights the role of qasd (intention) in acts of worship.
4
Connect to fiqh
The shahada is the key to all ibadat. The proper recitation and understanding of its meaning are prerequisites for valid worship, even though understanding is not required for conversion as seen in the previous lesson.
This lesson is more about adab (etiquette) and precision.

Commentary
This passage is the opening sermon (khutbah) of the book. The author begins by discussing the proper pronunciation and understanding of the shahada (لا إله إلا الله). He notes that there is a difference of opinion on whether to elongate the 'alif' in 'لا' or keep it short, with some choosing elongation to emphasize the negation of all other deities, and others choosing shortening to avoid dying before completing the name of Allah. He emphasizes that one must mentally affirm the existence of the One True God while uttering the negation, else the negation alone is disbelief. He then discusses the divine attributes 'الملك' (the King) and 'البر' (the Beneficent), explaining linguistic nuances. Next, he delves into the name 'Muhammad', its etymology, and its superiority over 'Ahmad'. He discusses the Prophet's kunyah (Abu al-Qasim) and the ruling that it is permissible to use it after the initial prohibition was abrogated. Finally, he addresses a significant fiqh issue: the conditions for a non-Muslim to enter Islam. According to the Hanafi school, it is not required to utter both parts of the shahada in order, nor to understand their meaning. If a person affirms the oneness of Allah but rejects the prophethood of Muhammad, they enter Islam by saying 'Muhammad Rasul Allah'. Similarly, if a person prays behind a Muslim imam and completes the prayer, they are judged to be Muslim. The key point is that the mere utterance of the shahada, even without knowledge of its meaning, is sufficient for a person to become Muslim, provided they are not mocking or lying.

Key Terms
لا إله إلا الله (La ilaha illa Allah): The declaration of faith meaning 'There is no deity but Allah', the fundamental statement of Islamic monotheism.
الملك (al-Malik): The King, the Sovereign; one of Allah's names indicating absolute authority and command.
البر (al-Barr): The Beneficent, the One who does good and is kind.
مد (madd): Prolongation of a vowel sound in recitation.

Hanafi Ruling on Conditions of Entering Islam
Ruling on conversion with Hanafi facilitative approach

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the shahadas for conversion and identify the evidence for non-requirement of order or meaning.

Core Idea
Saying both shahadas in order is not required for Islam; a single shahada suffices.

This passage tackles a practical issue in da'wah and Islamic jurisprudence: what exactly does a non-Muslim need to say to become Muslim? The Hanafi school takes a facilitative approach, focusing on the essence of tawhid and risalah rather than formulaic precision. We will examine the ruling and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage discusses the conditions for a kafir (non-Muslim) to become Muslim. The act is entering Islam, and the ruling is that it is valid without specific wording or order.
The hukm level is not fard/wajib but rather conditions for validity.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites Hanafi texts (al-Qahistani, al-Ashbah) and the example of a person praying in congregation being judged Muslim. The proof is from the practice of the Companions and the principle of facilitating conversion.
No direct Quran/hadith is quoted, but istihsan and practice form the basis.
3
Trace the Hanafi rationale
The Hanafi school holds that the essence of Islam is affirmation of tawhid and risalah; order and understanding are not conditions. This is based on the principle of removing hardship and the fact that the Prophet accepted conversions even from those who could not articulate properly.
Contrast with other schools that require both shahadas in order.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars (especially muhaddithin) require knowledge of the Prophet's name and lineage. The Hanafi school only requires knowing his name (Muhammad) and that he is the seal of prophets.
The passage mentions that knowledge of the father's name is not required.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, a convert can say 'La ilaha illa Allah' alone if they accept tawhid, or 'Muhammad Rasul Allah' alone if they already believe in one God and accept the Prophet. Both are sufficient.
The phrase 'Muhammad Rasul Allah' alone is enough for a monotheist.

Opening exhortation from the book's sermon praising knowledge and urging proper conduct in learning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi view that seeking knowledge is a communal obligation (fard kifayah) and describe the proper attitude of dignity (sakinah) and forbearance (hilm) in seeking and teaching knowledge.

Core Idea
Knowledge surpasses worship; seeking it is an obligation, but it must be pursued with dignity and forbearance.

This opening sermon from Hashiya al-Tahtawi sets the tone for the entire study of fiqh. It is not a ruling on a specific act but an exhortation that establishes the value of sacred knowledge and the character required to seek it. The commentator weaves together prophetic hadith, Quranic verses, and sayings of early scholars to argue for the superiority of knowledge over mere ritual worship and to emphasize that seeking knowledge is a duty upon every Muslim. However, the manner of learning is equally important: the text commands 'learn for it dignity and forbearance,' linking the pursuit of knowledge to inner tranquility (sakinah) and a gentle forbearance (hilm).

Guided Path
1
Identify the central command and its object
The passage begins with 'Learn knowledge, and learn for it dignity and forbearance.' Identify what the author commands: the acquisition of knowledge and simultaneously the cultivation of moral virtues (sakinah and hilm) specifically for that knowledge.
The phrase 'learn for it' implies these qualities are instrumental for effective learning.
2
Locate the proofs cited
Scan the text for direct proofs: the hadith 'knowledge is better than action', the Quranic verse 'Allah witnesses… and the angels, and those of knowledge' (3:18), and other hadith narrations. Identify which of these are used to establish the virtue of knowledge.
The commentator uses both Quran and Sunnah, classifying it as dalil naqli.
3
Analyse the concept of fard kifayah
The hadith 'seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim' is interpreted by the Hanafi school as a communal obligation (fard kifayah), not an individual one. Understand why: the community as a whole must fulfill it, and if some do, the rest are absolved.
This distinguishes Hanafi from views that consider it fard 'ayn for all.
4
Distinguish knowledge from action
The text says 'knowledge is better than action' but also defines the scholar as 'one who acts on his knowledge.' Recognize that knowledge is foundational and more excellent in its transmissible benefit, but action is inseparable from it.
Knowledge benefits others; action benefits only the doer. Hence knowledge's superiority.
5
Explain the role of sakinah and hilm
The command to 'learn for it sakinah and hilm' indicates that proper character is a condition for beneficial learning. Sakinah is outward tranquility and dignity; hilm is internal forbearance. Understand how these qualities affect the learning process and the transmission of knowledge.
Without them, knowledge may be acquired but not properly embodied or taught.

Commentary
This passage is the opening khutbah of the book, containing the author's introduction and several key legal and definitional points. The author, Hasan ibn Ammar al-Tahtawi, begins with praise of Allah and the command to seek knowledge with humility. The Hanafi Source Hierarchy: Zahir al-Riwayah and Nawadir
Author's preface establishing the book's reliance on the most authoritative Hanafi sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, distinguish them from Nawadir, and explain their relative authority in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school's most authoritative rulings come from six books; all other transmissions are secondary.

In the opening of Hashiya al-Tahtawi, the author establishes the credibility of his work by tracing its sources. He explains that the sound rulings in this book are drawn primarily from the six foundational books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, known as Zahir al-Riwayah or al-Usul. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correctly navigating Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path

Some scholars disagree about including the Siyar books.
3
Understand the transmission reliability
Al-Siyar al-Kabir and al-Saghir were Muhammad's last works, considered free from errors. The Mabsut is also called al-Asl. The 'kabir' works are direct from Muhammad from Abu Hanifa; 'saghir' works are via Abu Yusuf.
4
Recognize examples of Nawadir
The passage lists al-Amali, al-Raqqiyat, al-Kaysaniyyat, al-Haruniyyat, and al-Jurjaniyyat. These are less authoritative transmissions.
5
Apply the hierarchy in practice
Rulings from Zahir al-Riwayah take precedence over Nawadir. A mufti must first seek a ruling from these six books before turning to rare transmissions.
This hierarchy is unique to the Hanafi school.

His saying: (al-Shurunbulali) – the author said at the end of his treatise Durr al-Kunuz: this is the common [nisba], but the original is al-Shubrabluli, a nisba to a village opposite Manf al-Ulya in the Minufiyya region in the countryside of Protected Egypt, called Shubra Bulul, and the nisba became famous as al-Shurunbulali. In al-Qamus: Shubri like Sukkari – fifty-three places, all in Egypt, including ten in al-Sharqiyya, five in al-Murtahiyya, six in Jazirat Qusniyya, eleven in al-Gharbiyya, seven in al-Samnudiyya, three in al-Minufiyya, three in Jazirat Bani Nasr, four in al-Buhayra, two in Ramsis, and two in al-Jiziyya. His saying: (May Allah forgive him his sins) – the origin of al-ghufr is concealment; hence the helmet (al-mighfar) is named because it covers the head in war, and forgiveness of sins (ghufran al-dhunub) is their concealment by not taking to account, and it is said it is their complete erasure from the record due to His statement: 'Allah erases what He wills and establishes' [13:39]. His saying: (his sins) meaning his acts of disobedience, both minor and major. His saying: (and conceal his faults) meaning what disfigures and shames him, even if it is not a sin, for example a defect is a fault and not a sin; the conjunction is for difference or general particular. His saying: (and be kind to him) meaning convey to him His goodness and beneficence. His saying: (in his affairs) meaning their great and small. His saying: (what appears of them and what is hidden) – it is possible that the meaning encompasses inward and outward states, i.e., what relates to the heart and what relates to the limbs; or that the inward is what only his close ones know, like matters related to his wife and children, and the outward is what occurs with others like study companions and transactions; and it may encompass both. Allah said: 'And say: My Lord, have mercy on them as they raised me when I was small' [17:24], and it is the key to sustenance. Some hold the minimum supplication for parents is five times daily, as if after each prescribed prayer, because Allah coupled goodness to them with His worship, and the greatest acts of worship after faith are the prayers, and they are five daily. His saying: (and to his teachers) with ya without hamza, plural of shaykh; supplication for them is desirable because they are fathers of souls as parents are fathers of bodies. His saying: (and his offspring) meaning his progeny, from al-dhar' meaning creation, i.e., the group. His saying: (and his lovers) meaning those who love him with a faith-based love, i.e., they love him for his knowledge and obedience, even if the soul has no inclination for that. Narrated by Imam Ahmad and authenticated by al-Hakim. A servant may be prevented from an answer to raise his rank, and may be answered out of dislike for his persistence. Commentary
This passage is the opening of Hashiya al-Tahtawi, a supercommentary on Maraqi al-Falah. It begins with the author invoking blessings upon himself and others, then discusses the significance of supplication and its etiquettes. The core of this section is the classification of the book's sources: the author states that the rulings in this book are predominantly taken from the Zahir al-Riwayah, also called al-Usul, which are the six foundational works of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani: al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Mabsut, and al-Ziyadat. He notes that al-Siyar al-Kabir and al-Saghir were the last of Muhammad's works, hence free from errors. The Mabsut is also known as al-Asl. Some scholars did not count the Siyar among the usul. All other transmissions fall under 'Nawadir' (rare narrations), such as al-Amali, al-Raqqiyat, al-Kaysaniyyat, al-Haruniyyat, and al-Jurjaniyyat. The author distinguishes between 'kabir' and 'saghir' based on whether Muhammad narrated directly from Abu Hanifa or via Abu Yusuf. This classification is crucial for understanding the authority weight of rulings in the Hanafi school: Zahir al-Riwayah carries the highest authority, while Nawadir are secondary. The passage also relates that Imam al-Shafi'i admired and memorized the Mabsut, and that a sage converted to Islam upon studying it. Thus, the book's sound rulings are primarily from the most authoritative sources in the Hanafi madhhab.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (Zahir al-Riwayah): The six foundational books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani that contain the most authoritative Hanafi rulings: al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Siyar al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, al-Mabsut, and al-Ziyadat.
النوادر (al-Nawadir): Rare transmissions that are not part of the six core books, such as al-Amali, al-Raqqiyat, al-Kaysaniyyat, al-Haruniyyat, and al-Jurjaniyyat.
الأصول (al-Usul): Refers to the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, which are the foundational sources of Hanafi fiqh.

Conditions for Du'a Being Answered
Ruling with proof on du'a acceptance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligation of believing in du'a's acceptance and identify the conditions for du'a to be answered according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Du'a is always answered in one of three ways; its conditions ensure it is not wasted.

This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi clarifies a common concern among Muslims: why are some du'as seemingly unanswered? The author affirms that belief in Allah's response is wajib, and then explains that du'a is never lost—it is either granted now, stored for the afterlife, or used to avert harm. He also lists essential conditions for du'a to be effective.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core belief
The passage begins with an obligation: to believe Allah answers du'a. This is a foundational aqidah point.
Note that the belief itself is wajib, while the act of du'a is mustahab.
2
Address the objection
The author anticipates a doubt: why do some supplicants seem unanswered? He responds with two points: (a) du'a has conditions, and (b) response may be delayed.
3
Understand the threefold response
The hadith from Ibn Mas'ud is the proof. It states that du'a is answered in one of three ways: immediate granting, storage for the Hereafter, or warding off an equivalent evil.
This is the key dalil that refutes the notion of unanswered du'a.
4
List the conditions
The author enumerates conditions: sincerity, not hastening, presence of heart, lawful sustenance, certainty, not conditioning with mashiyyah, and not asking for something impossible.
These are conditions for acceptance, not for validity of the du'a itself.
5
Apply the conditions
Recognize that these conditions are in the student's control; fulfilling them maximizes the likelihood of the desired response.
The conditions are both internal (intention, presence of heart) and external (lawful earning).

Commentary
This passage is the author's introduction to his commentary 'Maraqi al-Falah'. He begins by stating the purpose of the book: it corrects the five acts of worship (taharah, salah, sawm, zakat, hajj) with clear expression and evidence from the Quran, Sunnah, and consensus. He then explains that he compiled this work from his larger commentary on the introduction, at the request of eminent scholars, to make it easier for students. The title 'Maraqi al-Falah' means 'Ladders of Success', symbolizing the steps to spiritual success. He names the work after mentioning the source text 'Nur al-Idah'. The author then humbly supplicates to Allah to accept the work, benefit the Ummah, and protect it from those who misuse it. He emphasizes sincerity, warning against riya' (showing off) by citing the hadith of the first three people judged on Judgment Day: the martyr, the scholar, and the reciter of Quran, who all sought worldly praise and were thrown into Hell. He also clarifies that the book is a great blessing for the Ummah and the author, and that it is based on the Hanafi madhhab, though it may benefit others. The commentary explains each phrase of the matn, including the meaning of 'correction of worship', the reason for including taharah as worship, the metaphorical language, and the significance of the title.

Key Terms
مراقي الفلاح (Maraqi al-Falah): Title of the book, meaning 'Ladders of Success' – a commentary on Nur al-Idah.
نور الإيضاح (Nur al-Idah): The base text (matn) by al-Shurunbulali on which Maraqi al-Falah is a commentary.
تصحيح العبادات (Tashih al-Ibadat): Correcting the acts of worship according to proper fiqh rulings.

Sincerity in Knowledge: The Author's Supplication
Author's supplication and warning against insincerity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hadith from Sahih Muslim about the three who enter Hell for riya and explain its implications for the Hanafi student of fiqh.

Core Idea
Knowledge that lacks sincerity brings punishment, not reward.

The author of Maraqi al-Falah opens his book with a heartfelt supplication, asking Allah to make it beneficial, accept it, and guard it from those who misuse it. The commentator expands on this, highlighting the supreme importance of sincerity (ikhlas) in seeking and teaching knowledge. A powerful hadith from Sahih Muslim serves as the anchor: the very first people to be thrown into Hell on the Day of Judgment are a martyr, a scholar, and a Quran reciter who acted for reputation. This lesson explores that warning and its application for every student of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's three requests
Read the opening phrase: the author asks Allah to benefit the nation, accept the book, and protect it from the unworthy. Identify each request and its corresponding line in the Arabic.
These three requests form the structure of the du'aa.
2
Understand the definition of 'benefit'
The commentator defines al-naf' as 'conveying good to others' (إيصال الخير إلى الغير). Relate this to the purpose of fiqh: helping others practice their deen correctly.
3
Analyze the hadith on riya
Read the hadith from Sahih Muslim quoted in the commentary. Note the three categories: martyr, scholar, Quran reciter. Identify the common factor: each acted for praise, not for Allah.
The hadith is a stern warning that even outwardly good deeds can be nullified by bad intention.
4
Connect the hadith to the author's request for acceptance
The author asks Allah to accept the book 'by His bounty,' i.e., make it sincerely for Him. The commentator explains that acceptance (qabul) means Allah is pleased and rewards. Insincere knowledge leads to rejection and punishment.
5
Apply to Hanafi learning
Reflect on how a student of the Hanafi school might fall into riya: learning to debate, to be called a scholar, to attract followers. The warning applies directly to the reader of this book.
The goal is to correct worship (especially salah) out of sincerity.

Commentary
This is the hashiya commentary on the author's khutbah (sermon) of Maraqi al-Falah. The author begins by supplicating to Allah to benefit the entire Muslim nation through his book, to accept it, and to protect it from those who are not worthy. The commentator explains each phrase, emphasizing that the ultimate goal is sincerity (ikhlas). He quotes a hadith from Sahih Muslim reporting that the first three people to be judged on the Day of Resurrection—a martyr, a scholar, and a Quran reciter—are thrown into Hell because their actions were done for show (riya), not for Allah. This serves as a severe warning against seeking fame or worldly gain through religious knowledge. The commentator also notes that the phrase 'by His bounty' refutes the Mu'tazila who held that Allah is obliged to do what is best for creation. Finally, the commentator defines key terms like 'benefit' and 'worthy', grounding them in Arabic lexicons.

النفع (al-naf'u): Defined as 'conveying good to others' (إيصال الخير إلى الغير).

The Prophet's Station and the Gardens of Paradise
Devotional commentary on the Prophet's station

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of al-Wasilah as the highest station in Paradise and state the conditions for attaining the Prophet's intercession based on the Hanafi textual tradition.

Core Idea
The Prophet's station is the root of all gardens; al-Wasilah is the highest rank, and intercession is conditional on faith and submission.

This lesson examines a commentary that reveals the profound connection between the Prophet's station and the structure of Paradise. It clarifies that all gardens branch from his station, with al-Wasilah being the closest to the Divine Throne. The lesson also explores the conditions for receiving the Prophet's intercession, which require sincere acceptance of Allah, Islam, and Muhammad as prophet.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter and colophon of the Hashiya by al-Tahtawi on the book Maraqi al-Falah. It contains the author's introductory supplications, praises, and a detailed account of the completion dates of the original matn (Nur al-Idah), its major commentary (Imdad al-Fattah), and the present abridgment (the Hashiya itself). The author begins by expressing his humility and dependency on Allah, seeking forgiveness and intercession through the Prophet Muhammad. He then describes his work as a selection and abridgment of a larger commentary, done for ease of study. Following the praise, he records the specific dates of authorship: the matn was completed in 1032 AH on a Friday in Jumada al-Ula; the major commentary was drafted in 1045 AH and fair-copied in 1046 AH; and the present abridgment was written between late Jumada al-Akhirah and early Rajab in 1054 AH. The total folios of the major commentary are 360, and of this abridgment 145. The passage also includes a note about the progeny of the Prophet being through Fatima and Ali, and explanations of certain phrases from the earlier text. This colophon is valuable for understanding the chronology and scope of the works in the Hanafi tradition of fiqh commentary.

Key Terms
المتن (al-matn): The original text of the fiqh work (Nur al-Idah) authored by al-Shurunbulali.
الشرح (al-sharh): The major commentary by al-Shurunbulali on his matn, called Imdad al-Fattah.

Navigating a Hanafi Fiqh Text: The Index
Table of contents listing chapters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to navigate a Hanafi fiqh text by identifying its major sections from the index (fihrist).

Core Idea
The index is the roadmap of a fiqh text — mastering it saves hours and reveals the author's logic.

Before diving into the legal rulings, it is essential to understand the structure of the book you are studying. The index (الفهرس) provides an overview of the major topics and their sequence. In Hanafi fiqh works, the organisation often follows a standard pattern: starting with purification (taharah), then prayer (salah), then other pillars. This lesson will teach you to read and use the index effectively, so you can quickly locate any topic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two major books
Look at the index: it begins with 'Kitab al-Taharah' and 'Kitab al-Salah'. These are the first two major sections (books) of the text. Every chapter belongs to one of these books.
2
List chapters under Taharah
Scan the index for chapters under Kitab al-Taharah: topics like wudu, ghusl, tayammum, wipes over khuffs, menstruation, impurities, etc. Note the order and any subdivisions.
For example, 'Fasl fi bayan ahkam al-su'r' (section on leftover water) is a sub-chapter.
3
List chapters under Salah
Similarly, under Kitab al-Salah, you will find chapters on adhan, conditions and pillars, imamate, invalidations, supererogatory prayers, traveller's prayer, etc. The index shows the progression from basic to advanced.
4
Recognize the logical sequence
The index reveals the author's pedagogical logic: purification comes before prayer because prayer requires purity. This sequence is standard in Hanafi fiqh texts. Understanding this helps you predict what comes next.
You can apply this to other chapters like zakat or fasting even if not listed here.
5
Note the absence of later books
This index only covers the first two books. In a full text, there would be books on Zakat, Fasting, Hajj, etc. Recognizing that the index is partial prevents confusion.
Later pages of the index would continue with those books.

Navigating Hashiya al-Tahtawi: Book Structure Overview

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and sequence the major chapters (kutub) of Hashiya al-Tahtawi and use the table of contents to locate specific fiqh topics.

Core Idea
The table of contents is your road map to Hanafi fiqh rulings in Hashiya al-Tahtawi.

This lesson introduces the structure of Hashiya al-Tahtawi, a key commentary on Maraqi al-Falah. The table of contents provides an overview of the book's chapters and sections, helping you navigate the detailed discussions on purification, prayer, zakat, fasting, hajj, and other topics. Mastering this structure will allow you to efficiently reference specific rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the major chapters
Look at the list of chapters (kutub) such as 'كتاب الزكاة' and 'كتاب الحج'. Write them down in order as they appear.
Each chapter covers a major area of fiqh (e.g., Zakat, Hajj).
2
Note the sections within chapters
Under each chapter heading, sections (abwab/fusul) like 'باب المصرف' or 'فصل في كيفية ترتيب أفعال' address specific subtopics. Observe the hierarchy: chapter > section.
Sections break down the chapter into manageable legal issues.
3
Understand the logical sequence
The order reflects typical Hanafi fiqh manuals: acts of worship (ibadat) followed by transactions and penalties. Notice that 'كتاب الزكاة' comes before 'كتاب الحج'.
This sequence aids in memorization and systematic study.
4
Use the index for navigation
When studying a specific issue, first check the table of contents to find the relevant chapter and section. This saves time and provides context.
Common mistake: jumping into a section without understanding its place in the overall structure.
