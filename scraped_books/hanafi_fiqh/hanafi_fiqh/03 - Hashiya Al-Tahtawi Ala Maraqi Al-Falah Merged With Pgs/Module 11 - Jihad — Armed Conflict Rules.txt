The Ruling on the Captive Child Who Dies with a Non-Muslim Parent
Ruling on the janazah of a captive child and the afterlife fate of children of polytheists.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on performing janazah for a captive child of polytheists and explain the principle of taba'iyya and the doctrine of tawaqquf.

Core Idea
A captive child follows the parent in worldly rulings — no janazah; but in the afterlife, the Imam suspends judgment.

This lesson addresses a sensitive fiqh question: what happens to a child or insane person who is captured along with a polytheist parent and dies? Should we pray over them? Are they considered Muslim? The Hanafi school applies the principle of taba'iyya (legal following) for worldly matters but adopts a cautious position (tawaqquf) for the afterlife. We will explore the reasoning and the intra-madhhab difference between Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognise that the passage discusses a child or insane adult captured with a parent from dar al-harb who then dies. The key fact is the parent's non-Muslim status at the time of death.
Only one parent needs to be with the child.
2
Apply taba'iyya to worldly rulings
The child follows the parent in all worldly legal rulings (ahkam al-dunya), including religious status. Therefore, if the parent is non-Muslim, the child is treated as non-Muslim for funeral rites.
Taba'iyya is based on lineage and dependence.
3
Note the tawaqquf of Abu Hanifa
Regarding the child's fate in the afterlife, Abu Hanifa suspends judgment (yuwaqqif) due to conflicting evidences. He neither declares the child to be in Paradise nor in Hell.
Tawaqquf is a principled position of caution.
4
Compare with Imam Muhammad's view
Imam Muhammad holds that Allah does not punish anyone without sin, implying these children are not punished. This is a mercy-based view but does not change the worldly ruling.
The operative fatwa remains the tawaqquf position.

Commentary
This passage from Hashiya Al-Tahtawi addresses the fiqh ruling for a child or an insane adult who is captured (saby) along with one parent from dar al-harb (non-Muslim territory) and then dies. The core issue: how are they treated in terms of funeral rites and their status in the afterlife? The commentator follows the matn of Maraqi al-Falah, which likely states 'like a child or an insane adult captured with one parent, then dies—due to following him in worldly rulings.' The hashia explains that the child or insane person follows the parent in worldly matters (like being considered a non-Muslim for purposes of burial, no janazah, etc.) because of the strong tie of birth (taba'iyya wiladiyya). However, they are not necessarily followers in the afterlife; rather, the Imam (Abu Hanifa) suspends judgment (yuwaqqif) regarding the fate of children of polytheists. A narration from Imam Muhammad states: 'I know that Allah does not punish anyone without a sin,' implying these children are not automatically punished. The condition for a child to be considered Muslim by following a parent is if the parent embraces Islam or if the child himself becomes Muslim ('aqala, i.e., has understanding). The commentary also references Sharh al-Maqdisi: another narration says that if the spirit was breathed into the child (i.e., if it was a live birth), it will be resurrected; otherwise not. This connects to the earlier discussion of the saqt. The hashia emphasizes that the ruling on not performing janazah is because the child is a follower of the parents in worldly ahkam; but in the afterlife, the matter is left to Allah. This lesson clarifies the boundaries of taba'iyya in fiqh and the cautious position on the fate of children.

Key Terms
تبعية (taba'iyya): Legal following: a child or insane person follows his parent in legal rulings pertaining to this world (like religion for funeral, marriage, etc.), but not necessarily in the afterlife.
توقف (tawaqquf): Suspension of judgment: the Imam (Abu Hanifa) refrains from declaring a definitive ruling about the fate of children of polytheists in the afterlife, due to conflicting evidences.

Key Takeaway
