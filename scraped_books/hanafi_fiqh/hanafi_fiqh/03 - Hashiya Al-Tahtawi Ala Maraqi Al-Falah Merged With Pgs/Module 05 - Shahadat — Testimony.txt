Conflicting Testimony: Ruling on Meat and Water
Ruling on conflicting testimony with tahat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of tahaatur to determine the ruling when two reliable witnesses conflict, and state the original ruling for meat versus water.

Core Idea
When witnesses cancel out, the original ruling prevails: haram for meat, pure for water.

This passage addresses a common scenario in testimony law: two upright witnesses contradict each other about the permissibility or purity of a substance. The Hanafi school holds that such testimony mutually cancels, leaving the original ruling in place. This teaches us the importance of legal presumptions—'istishab al-hal'—in resolving evidentiary deadlocks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two 'adl witnesses give contradictory reports: one says the meat was slaughtered by a Magian (haram), the other by a Muslim (halal). Or, one says water is impure, the other says pure.
Ensure both witnesses are 'adl (upright). If one is fasiq, different rules apply.
2
Apply tahaatur (mutual cancellation)
Since both testimonies are equally reliable and contradictory, they cancel each other out. No preference is given to either.
Tahaatur is a default mechanism when evidence is equal.
3
Determine the original ruling
After cancellation, look to the original legal status before the conflict. For meat: the default is haram until lawful slaughter is confirmed. For water: the default is purity.
This is based on the principle 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk'.
4
Derive the final ruling
Meat remains haram (unlawful to eat); water remains pure (allowed to use). The original state is preserved.
The same logic applies to any case where two equally credible witnesses conflict.

Commentary
This passage addresses a case where two reliable ('adl) witnesses give contradictory testimony. For meat: one says it was slaughtered by a Magian (i.e., not lawful), the other says by a Muslim (lawful). Since the reports cancel each other out (tahaatur), the original ruling applies. The original ruling for meat is prohibition (haram) because eating meat requires certainty of lawful slaughter. Therefore, the meat remains unlawful. For water: if one says it is pure and the other impure, they cancel, and water returns to its original ruling of purity. The difference is due to the original state: meat is assumed prohibited until proven otherwise, whereas water is assumed pure. The passage also notes that if the witness is a dhimmi, his testimony is not accepted; if a fasiq, his testimony is taken but taharri is required to prefer one over the other.

Key Terms
تهاتر (tahaatur): Mutual cancellation of evidence when equally reliable.
عدل (adl): Upright, reliable witness.

Conditions for Accepting Crescent Testimony When the Sky Is Cloudy
Ruling with definitions and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for accepting a single witness's testimony for the hilal when the sky is cloudy, define adl and mastur, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the mastur's testimony.

Core Idea
The testifier for hilal when cloudy must be adl; the mastur is accepted in the preferred view.

This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi addresses a practical scenario: when the sky is cloudy, how many witnesses are needed? The Hanafi school allows a single adl witness, but what about a person whose moral state is unknown? The commentator defines 'adl' and 'mastur' and clarifies that the relied-upon view accepts the mastur, contrary to the apparent narration. Understanding these definitions and the dispute is essential for correctly applying the rules of testimony in the context of crescent sighting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and basic ruling
Note that when the sky is cloudy, the qadi accepts the testimony of a single adl witness. This is a relaxation from the usual requirement of two witnesses.
2
Analyze the definitions of adl and mastur
Examine the given definitions: adl is one whose good deeds outweigh his bad, possessing adala and muru'a. Mastur is one whose moral state is unknown. Understand the distinction.
The definition of adl includes both piety and social respectability.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the mastur
The passage mentions that accepting the mastur is the sahih opinion, whereas the zahir al-riwayah rejects it. Determine which is mufta bihi.
The mufta bihi is the sahih opinion.

Commentary
This passage from the hashihya addresses the conditions for accepting testimony (khabar) regarding the sighting of the crescent when the sky is cloudy (or dusty, foggy, etc.). The commentator explains that in such conditions, the judge accepts the testimony of a single upright (adl) witness. The definition of 'adl' is given: a person whose good deeds outweigh his bad deeds, possessing 'adalah' (integrity), which is a habitual state of piety and chivalry (muru'a). Additionally, the testimony of a 'mastur' (a person whose moral state is unknown) is accepted according to the 'sahih' opinion, even though the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya) does not accept it. The commentator then provides lexical explanations for terms like 'dhabab' (fog) and 'nada' (dew), noting that 'nada' is more appropriately rain or wetness, but these do not actually veil the sky. He also clarifies procedural points: the testimony is given in the judge's court (majlis al-qada'), and the witness must be Muslim, sane, and adult. The passage ends with a note on the fasiq (sinner): if the judge follows al-Tahawi's view allowing the fasiq's testimony for hilal, then the fasiq should testify. However, the majority view (al-Diraya, al-Bahr) is that a fasiq's report is not accepted in matters that can be received from upright persons. This passage demonstrates the Hanafi attention to detail in witness qualifications, balancing textual evidence with practical considerations.

Key Terms
عدل (adl): A person whose good deeds outweigh his bad deeds, possessing 'adalah' (integrity) which is a habitual state of piety and chivalry (muru'a).
مستور (mastur): A person of unknown status; no apparent sin nor uprightness has been observed. His testimony for hilal is accepted in the sahih opinion.
مروءة (muru'a): Chivalry or gentlemanliness; part of the definition of 'adl' as a habit that includes both piety and social respectability.
