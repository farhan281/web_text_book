Classification of Knowledge: Fard 'Ayn vs Fard Kifayah
Author classifies knowledge acquisition based on type of obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between fard 'ayn and fard kifayah in the acquisition of knowledge based on the Hanafi classification.

Core Idea
Knowledge needed for a personal duty is fard 'ayn; knowledge needed for a communal duty is fard kifayah.

This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi establishes a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh: the obligation to acquire knowledge depends on its purpose. Not all religious knowledge is individually obligatory; some is communal. Understanding this distinction prevents overwhelming oneself and ensures that essential personal duties are not neglected.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act being classified
The text discusses the acquisition (تحصيل) of knowledge (علم). This is the act under consideration.
The act is not the knowledge itself but the effort to learn it.
2
Distinguish the two categories
The text uses the condition 'by which one reaches (يتوصل به إلى) a fard 'ayn' versus 'fard kifayah'. Identify which condition makes acquisition fard 'ayn and which makes it fard kifayah.
The phrase 'يتوصل به إلى' indicates the knowledge is a means to fulfill an obligation.
3
Apply the principle to examples
Examples given include knowledge of Allah, prayer, zakat, fasting, Hajj, halal and haram. Verify that these are means to fard 'ayn obligations.
Each of these acts is individually obligatory (fard 'ayn).
4
Recognize the communal category
The text contrasts with knowledge that leads to fard kifayah. This includes specialized sciences that only some need to learn for the community.
The text does not list examples but refers to other works.
5
Note the citation of other works
The commentator refers to Al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Ta'lim al-Muta'allim for the complete discussion, showing that this classification is well-established.
This indicates the depth of the subject; the present passage provides the core rule.

And that [knowledge] by which one reaches [fulfillment of] a communal obligation (fard kifāyah), its acquisition is fard kifāyah. The rest of it is in the sermon of Al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Ta'līm al-Muta'allim.
Source pages p. 11

Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling. The commentator (al-Tahtawi) clarifies the author's classification of knowledge. Any knowledge that is essential for performing a personal religious duty (fard 'ayn) becomes obligatory on every individual (fard 'ayn). Examples include knowing Allah, the rulings of prayer, fasting, etc. Knowledge needed for communal obligations (fard kifāyah), such as specialized sciences, is only obligatory on some to suffice the community. The commentator refers to other works for further details.

Key Terms
فرض عين (fard 'ayn): An obligatory act that every individual Muslim must perform; here applied to acquisition of knowledge necessary for personal duties.
فرض كفاية (fard kifāyah): A communal obligation; if some fulfill it, others are excused.

Key Takeaway

The Legal Category of Wājib: Definition, Proof, and Ruling
Defining wājib with evidence hierarchy and prayer rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of wājib, distinguish it from fard based on evidence strength, and apply its ruling in prayer (sajda sahw, repetition).

Core Idea
Wājib is binding in practice but not in belief, because its evidence is probable (zanni), unlike fard's definite (qat'i) proof.

In Hanafi fiqh, not all obligations are equal. The category of wājib sits between fard and sunnah, carrying its own distinct ruling. The passage you will read explains this distinction by grounding it in the certainty of scriptural evidence, then traces the practical consequences in prayer: when a wājib is omitted, the prayer requires prostration of forgetfulness or even repetition. This lesson unpacks that logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition of wājib
Look for the statement that wājib is binding ‘in practice, not in knowledge.’ That is the essence: you must perform it, but you are not required to believe in its obligation as a matter of faith.
This is the key difference from fard.
2
Trace the evidence hierarchy
The passage classifies scriptural evidence into four types by certainty of transmission (thubut) and meaning (dalala). Identify which types establish wājib (the second and third types: qat'i al-thubut zanni al-dalala, and zanni al-thubut qat'i al-dalala).
Wājib is never based on fully qat'i evidence.
3
Examine the rulings of a wājib
Note the three practical consequences: deliberate omission merits punishment (less than for fard) but not takfir; forgetful omission requires sajda sahw; deliberate omission within the prayer time requires repetition, but after the time the prayer is valid yet deficient.
The repetition within time is not obligatory but recommended.
4
Understand the hierarchy of completion
The passage explains that wājib acts complete fard acts, sunnah acts complete wājib, and adab completes sunnah. This shows how the law builds layers of protection around the core obligations.
Preserving the outer layers protects the inner ones.
5
Analyse the cross-madhhab dispute
The text quotes Imam Abu Hanifa: the difference between wājib and fard is like heaven and earth. Contrast this with other schools that do not distinguish them. Understand why the Hanafi school maintains this distinction based on evidence certainty.
This is a matter of usul, not mere terminology.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the nature of wājib (obligatory) in Hanafi fiqh. The author explains that a wājib is a ruling that is binding in practice but not in belief, meaning one must perform it but is not required to believe in its obligation as a matter of faith. This is because the evidence for wājib is zanni (probable), unlike fard (compulsory) which is based on qat'i (definite) evidence. The passage then categorises scriptural evidence into four types based on certainty of transmission and indication, and assigns the various rulings (fard, wājib, sunnah, etc.) accordingly. The purpose of wājib acts is to complete the fard acts; sunnah acts complete wājib acts; and adab (etiquettes) complete sunnah acts, creating a hierarchy of completeness. The ruling of a wājib is that deliberate omission deserves punishment but not excommunication, and forgetful omission requires sajda sahw (prostration of forgetfulness). If one deliberately omits a wājib, the prayer must be repeated within the time; if the time expires, the prayer remains but with deficiency and sin. The passage also notes the difference between wājib and fard, quoting the Imam (Abu Hanifa) that the difference is as vast as between heaven and earth, though some may call wājib 'sunnah' loosely. The commentary then discusses each part of the matn in detail, providing examples of how certain acts (like recitation, tranquility, tashahhud) serve as complements to fard or wājib. It emphasises that preserving the outer levels (adab, sunnah) protects the inner ones (wājib, fard). The final part addresses the consequence of omitting a wājib: if one repeats the prayer within the time, it is recommended but not obligatory, and the first prayer fulfills the fard. This is a key Hanafi position distinguishing wājib from fard.

Key Terms
واجب (wājib): A legal obligation established by zanni (probable) evidence; binding in practice but not requiring belief in its obligation.
فرض (fard): A legal obligation established by qat'i (definite) evidence; binding in both practice and belief.
دليل (dalīl): Scriptural evidence; here classified into four types based on certainty of transmission and meaning.
