Disliked Mourning Practices: Sitting, Mosque, and Food
Critical commentary resolving conflicting hadith on mourning practices.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on three mourning practices and explain how conflicting hadith evidence is reconciled.

Core Idea
Mourning practices are disliked only when they mimic Jahiliyyah; permissible when done without excess.

This lesson examines three actions that are commonly considered makruh during mourning in the Hanafi school: sitting at the door, sitting in the mosque, and the family's provision of food. However, as you will see, these rulings are nuanced by the presence of prohibited elements and the intention behind the action. The Hanafi position reconciles two apparently contradictory hadiths to arrive at a balanced ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three actions and their apparent ruling
The matn states: sitting at the door, sitting in the mosque, and hosting (food) by the family are makruh. Note the initial ruling without conditions.
The matn uses the absolute form 'disliked'.
2
Understand the evidence for dislike
The hadith of Jarir: 'We used to consider gathering at the family of the deceased and their preparing food as part of wailing.' This associates these practices with prohibited wailing.
Wailing (niyahah) is haram by consensus.
3
Understand the counter-evidence
The hadith of the Ansari man: The Prophet attended a funeral and then ate food prepared by the family. This indicates permissibility.
The Prophet's action is a strong proof of permissibility.
4
See how Hanafi scholars reconcile
The dislike is conditional on the presence of prohibited elements (e.g., spreading carpets on the road, excessive display of grief). If no such elements, it is permissible up to three days. Food for the poor is recommended.
Al-Tahtawi resolves the contradiction by restricting the absolute dislike.
5
Apply the distinction in practice
For each action, evaluate whether prohibited elements are present. Sitting at home without display is fine; food prepared for charity is good.
The key is to avoid resembling Jahili practices.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on rulings related to mourning practices. The author discusses three disliked actions: (1) Sitting at the door of the house for mourning, as this was a practice of the Jahiliyyah and the Prophet forbade it. However, some scholars clarify that sitting for up to three days is permissible if no prohibited acts like spreading carpets on the road are involved. (2) Sitting in the mosque for mourning, though sitting elsewhere for three days is allowed. (3) The family of the deceased preparing food for those who come to mourn, as this resembles wailing (niyahah) according to a hadith. However, the author notes a counter-narration showing the Prophet himself ate food prepared by a family of the deceased, indicating permissibility. The dislikedness may be only when done immediately at the time of death. Preparing food for the poor is recommended (hasan). The passage also mentions some exceptions to the questioning in the grave (for certain righteous persons like martyrs, those who die in plague, those who recite Surah al-Mulk, etc.), but the main focus is on the mourning practices.

Key Terms
الجلوس على باب الدار للمصيبة (al-julus 'ala bab al-dar lil-musibah): Sitting at the door of the house as a public display of mourning, a practice of Jahiliyyah.
الضيافة (al-diyafah): Food prepared by the family of the deceased for those who come to offer condolences.
النياحة (al-niyahab): Wailing or loud lamentation for the dead, a forbidden act in Islam.

Exemptions from the Grave Questioning
List of exemptions from the grave questioning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the categories of people who are exempt from the questioning in the grave according to some senior Ahl al-Sunnah.

Core Idea
Certain righteous individuals and special circumstances exempt a believer from the trial of the grave.

The passage mentions a list of people who will not be questioned in the grave, as stated by some senior scholars of Ahl al-Sunnah. This exemption is a mercy from Allah and reflects the high status of these believers. Understanding this encourages believers to strive for these virtues and to appreciate the mercy of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The general rule is that every deceased person is questioned in the grave by the angels Munkar and Nakir about their Lord, religion, and prophet.
This is established by authentic hadith.
2
Memorize the list of exempted categories
The passage lists several groups: the martyr killed in battle against disbelievers, the one guarding the frontier, the one afflicted with plague, those who die during a plague, the one with stomach disease, the insane, the people of the interval (between prophets), the one who dies on Friday night or day, the one who recites Surah al-Mulk every night, and the seeker of knowledge.
Note that some categories overlap (e.g., those who die in plague).
3
Understand the wisdom behind each exemption
Each exemption has a reason: martyrs and guards are in the path of Allah; plague and stomach disease purify the believer; the insane lack responsibility; the interval people never received the message; Friday death is a sign of goodness; reciting Surah al-Mulk protects; seeking knowledge raises one's rank.
The hadith about the seeker of knowledge states that between him and the prophets is only one degree.
4
Recognize the source and reliability
These exemptions are narrated from senior Ahl al-Sunnah and supported by hadith (e.g., Tabarani, Darimi, Ibn Sunni). They are mentioned in reliable books like Jawahir al-Kalam and Takhrij al-Ihya.
The chain is not mutawatir but accepted by many scholars.

Prohibition of 'Aqr and Recommendation of Food for Bereaved
Two rulings: prohibition of grave-slaughter and recommendation of feeding the bereaved.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibition of al-'aqr and explain the recommendation for neighbors to provide food for the bereaved, citing the hadith of Ja'far.

Core Idea
Slaughtering at graves is a Jahili practice forbidden in Islam; supporting the bereaved with food is a sunnah.

In the previous lesson, we learned that the bereaved family should not host gatherings or prepare food. This lesson addresses two related matters: a prohibited practice ('aqr) and a recommended practice (providing food for the family). Both are rooted in the Prophetic teaching to replace pre-Islamic customs with Islamic compassion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and identify that it covers two separate legal topics: the prohibition of 'aqr and the recommendation of tayyib al-ta'am (preparing food for the bereaved).
The first ruling is a negation (no 'aqr), the second is an instruction (istihbab).
2
Analyze the prohibition of 'aqr
Define 'aqr: cutting the hamstring of a camel at the grave. Understand why it was practiced and why it is forbidden: it is a Jahili innovation, not a valid compensation for the deceased's generosity.
The ruling is haram by consensus.
3
Examine the recommendation of food provision
Identify who should provide food (neighbors and distant relatives), for how long (a day and night), and the evidence (hadith of Ja'far). The imperative 'prepare' indicates recommendation (nadb) in this context.
The wisdom: grief preoccupies the family; food sustains them.
4
Understand the wisdom and application
Note that the family themselves should not cook, as they are occupied with grief. Neighbors fulfill a communal duty of care. The one delivering food may console with words provided in the passage.
This prevents the family from falling into weakness.

Ruling on Visiting Graves and the Dispute on Women's Participation
Ruling on grave visitation with proofs and intra-madhhab dispute on women

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on visiting graves for men and women, cite the proofs, and explain the conditions and tarjih regarding women's visitation.

Core Idea
Visiting graves is recommended for men; for women it is permissible without fitna, and recommended for the elderly—contrary to absolute prohibition.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on visiting graves (ziyarat al-qubur). The author establishes that the act is recommended (nadb) for men based on the Prophet's command. He then addresses the controversial question of women's visitation, presenting both the stricter prohibitive views and the more permissive position that is preferred (al-ashah). We will explore the proofs, the sunnah practices, and the conditions that govern women's participation. The goal is to understand the nuanced ruling and avoid the common error of assuming a blanket prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is visiting graves (ziyarat al-qubur). What is its legal classification? The text begins by stating that it is recommended (nadb) – a sunnah, not obligatory. Look for the phrase 'ندب زيارتها'.
2
Cite the primary dalil and analyse the imperative
The author quotes the hadith 'زوروا القبور تذكركم الموت' (Visit graves, they remind you of death). In Hanafi usul, the imperative (amr) indicates recommendation due to the context of lifting a previous prohibition. Identify the operative phrase and how it yields nadb.
The hadith is ahad, so dalala is zanni, reinforcing the recommended rather than obligatory nature.
3
List the sunnah practices during visitation
The text specifies that the sunnah is to stand while visiting, not to step on graves with shoes (prefer barefoot), and to supplicate for the deceased. It also mentions that walking with shoes in graveyards is not disliked in the Hanafi school, contrary to Ahmad's view.
These etiquettes apply to both men and women unless otherwise specified.
4

There is a strong opinion (from al-Tatarkhaniyyah) that women's visitation is forbidden and leads to curse. Another opinion (from Badr al-Ayni) distinguishes between young women (disliked) and elderly women (permitted if no fitna). The text presents both positions with their evidence.
The opposing dalil is the hadith 'لعن الله زائرات القبور' (Allah cursed women who visit graves).
5
Identify the tarjih and its conditions
The text concludes that the most correct view (al-ashah) is that permission is established for both men and women, citing the actions of Fatima and Aisha. However, this permission is conditional: no wailing, no intermingling, and no fitna. For young women it is disliked, for elderly women it is recommended.
The tarjih is based on the general command and the practice of the pious predecessors, and the opposing hadith is interpreted as referring to those who engage in forbidden acts.

Ruling on Cutting Fresh and Dry Grass from Graves
Ruling on removing fresh/dry grass from graves with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting fresh and dry grass from graves and apply the dalil (hadith) to distinguish between the two.

Core Idea
Fresh grass glorifies Allah and brings mercy; cutting it is disliked, while dry grass may be removed.

When visiting graves, it is common to see vegetation. The Hanafi school distinguishes between fresh, moist grass and dry grass. This ruling is rooted in the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ placing a fresh palm frond on a grave, hoping for alleviation of punishment as long as it remains moist. Understanding this ruling helps us respect the sanctity of the cemetery and the spiritual benefit of plants.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of vegetation and their rulings
The passage treats fresh (ratb) and dry (yabis) grass separately. Fresh grass should not be cut without need; dry grass may be removed.
The term 'jashish' refers to dry grass.
2
Locate the primary proof (dalil)
The proof is the hadith where the Prophet ﷺ split a palm frond and placed halves on two graves, hoping for mercy as long as they stayed moist.
This is an ahad hadith from Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah (link between proof and ruling)
Moisture enables the plant to glorify Allah (tasbih), which brings mercy to the deceased. Cutting it removes this benefit, hence the dislike.
The glorification is by 'tongue of state' for plants.
4
Note the extension to all fresh vegetation
The ruling applies not only to palm fronds but to any moist vegetation, as the 'illa (effective cause) is moisture and life.
Even outside a cemetery, cutting fresh grass without need is not recommended.
5
Apply the Quranic verse on tasbih
The verse 'There is not a thing but glorifies His praise' (17:44) supports that all living things glorify Allah, but dry wood or cut stone are considered lifeless.
Scholars differ on whether this includes inanimate objects.

The Appointed Term Is Not Cut Short by the Killer

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage clarifies a fundamental theological principle: although a person is killed, his life ends at the exact moment decreed by Allah. The killer is only a means. The Three (or Four) Conditions for a Binding Vow (Nadhr)
Parses the three conditions for a binding nadhr with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list and explain the three conditions required for a vow to be binding, and identify when a vow is not binding due to these conditions.

Core Idea
A vow is binding only if its type is obligatory in origin, it is intended for itself, and it is not already obligatory by Allah.

Not every vow creates an obligation. The Hanafi school carefully delineates three (or four) conditions that the vowed act must meet. This lesson parses each condition with examples and exceptions, including the added fourth condition of possibility.

Guided Path
1
Condition 1: The type must be obligatory in its origin
The kind of act vowed (e.g., fasting, prayer, charity) must be of a category that has an obligatory instance. Even if a particular instance is prohibited due to an attribute (like fasting on Eid), the type is still 'wajib in origin', so the vow is binding, but you fulfill it on another day.
Example: vowing to fast on the day of Nahr (Eid al-Adha) – the type 'fasting' is obligatory (Ramadan), so the vow binds, but since that day is haram to fast, you fast on another day.
2
Condition 2: The act must be intended for itself (maqsud li-dhatihi)
The vowed act should be an end in itself, not merely a means to something else. For example, wudu is a means to prayer, not an act of devotion sought for its own sake. Vowing to perform wudu is not binding.
Acts like prayer, fasting, i'tikaf, charity – these are maqsud li-dhatihi. Acts like wudu, ghusl, and removing impurities are not.
3
Condition 3: The act must not already be obligatory by Allah
If the act is already fard or wajib (e.g., the five daily prayers, fasting Ramadan, Zakat), vowing to do it is ineffective because it was already required. The vow does not add obligation.
Exception: if the vowed act is already obligatory but with a specific detail that is not required (e.g., praying Zuhr at the earliest time), the vow may not be binding because the act itself is already wajib.
4
Consider the added fourth condition: possibility
The vowed act must be possible to perform. Vowing to fast on a day that has already passed (yesterday) is impossible and not binding. Similarly, vowing something beyond one's ownership is not valid.
This condition is a ziyada (addition) from later scholars; it is rooted in the principle that impossible obligations are not incumbent.

Ruling on Fulfilling a Vow Despite Change of Place or Recipient
Ruling on validity of prayer/charity vow if location/recipient differs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the majority Hanafi position on fulfilling a vow when the place or recipient differs, and explain why the vow remains valid despite the change.

Core Idea
A vow is fulfilled by the essence of the act, not the specific place or person – the majority Hanafi view overrides Zufar's requirement of specification.

In this lesson, we explore a debate within the Hanafi school: if someone vows to pray in Makkah or give charity to a specific poor person, but prays elsewhere or gives to another, is the vow fulfilled? The majority holds that it is, because the core of worship is devotion (qurbah), not the external detail. Imam Zufar dissents, requiring strict adherence to the vow's specification. We will examine the reasoning and the practical implication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The passage rules on valid fulfillment of a vow when the specified place or recipient is changed. The act is a vowed prayer or charity.
2
State the majority Hanafi position
The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) holds that the vow is fulfilled because validity is based on the act of devotion (qurbah), not the specific location or person.
Prayer is exaltation of Allah with the whole body; charity fills a need – both achieved regardless of place/person.
3
Present Zufar's dissenting view
Imam Zufar argues that specification (ta'yin) is required; the act must be done exactly as vowed, otherwise the vow is not fulfilled.
4
Examine the evidence
The majority uses the rationale that the essence of the act is achieved without regard to time, place, or person. Zufar focuses on the literal wording of the vow.
Hadiths about virtue of mosques show preference, not validity.
5
Apply to a real case and determine mufta bihi
A woman vows to pray in the Sacred Mosque but prays in a dark room. According to Zufar she must repeat it; according to the majority she is fine. The mufta bihi is the majority position.
The commentator notes that the ruling is based on qurbah, not reward.

Core Idea
Intercourse nullifies i'tikaf; its prohibition extends to foreplay because the strength of the textual prohibition overcomes hardship considerations.

The Quran explicitly forbids sexual relations during i'tikaf. The Hanafi school derives that this prohibition is so strong that it extends to all intimate acts leading to intercourse, such as kissing and touching with desire. This contrasts with fasting, where such acts are not automatically forbidden due to the nature of the prohibition and frequent occurrence.

Inclusion of Nights in Vowed I'tikaf and Intention for Days Only
Rulings on inclusion of nights and validity of daytime-only intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether nights are included when vowing days of i'tikaf, and explain the exception for intending only days for vows under a month.

Core Idea
When vowing i'tikaf for days, the nights are included unless the vow is for less than a month and one intends only daytime.

This lesson examines the scope of a vowed i'tikaf. When someone vows to perform i'tikaf for a number of days, are the nights automatically included? The Hanafi school has a clear default: the plural 'days' includes the corresponding nights. However, there is a nuanced exception: if the vow is for less than a month and the person specifically intends only the daytime, that intention is given effect. We also look at a minor intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the first night in dual and plural cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
A vowed i'tikaf becomes wajib. The question is what exactly is required: just the days, or the nights as well?
2
Locate the default rule
The plural 'ayyam' (days) includes the corresponding nights by default because i'tikaf is a continuous act covering both day and night, unlike fasting.
Refer to the Quranic usage where 'three days' and 'three nights' describe the same period.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf holds that for dual (e.g., two days) and plural, the first night is not binding because i'tikaf at night is subsidiary to connecting days. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) include it as a precaution.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.
4
Apply the exception for intention
If someone vows i'tikaf for less than a month and intends only the daylight hours, that intention is valid. This is because the literal meaning of 'days' can be restricted to daytime, and the vow is short enough that the default inclusion of nights can be overridden.
For a month or more, the intention would not override the default.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that nights are included for any vowed days, and the exception for intention only applies to vows under a month. Practice accordingly.

Commentary
This passage from the Hashiya deals with the details of a vowed i'tikaf. The main ruling is that when someone vows to perform i'tikaf for a number of days (using the plural 'ayyam'), the corresponding nights are automatically included. Therefore, one enters the mosque before sunset on the first night and exits after sunset on the last day. Similarly, if one vows 'nights', the days are included consecutively. The reason is that in i'tikaf, the act inherently covers both day and night, so the default is continuity (tawatur). This is different from fasting, which only occurs during the day. The passage also addresses a dispute from Imam Abu Yusuf who holds that in the case of a dual (e.g., two days) or plural, the first night is not included because i'tikaf at night is only subsidiary to the need for connection between days, and the first night is not needed for that connection. However, the relied-upon position (al-mu'tamad) is that the nights are included. The text then discusses the validity of intending only the daytime. If one vows i'tikaf for days but specifically intends only the daylight hours, this is valid only if the vow is for less than a month, because the literal meaning of 'days' can include the whole day, but the intention specifies it. This is similar to saying 'I vow i'tikaf for twenty days' and intending only the daytime. The passage concludes with a summary of the various possible combinations of singular, dual, plural, and intentions, referring to the detailed treatment in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Key Terms
نذر الاعتكاف (nadhr al-i'tikaf): A vow to perform i'tikaf, making it obligatory to fulfill as promised.
نية النهار (niyyat al-nahar): Intending only the daytime portion of i'tikaf, excluding nights.
التتابع (al-tatabu'): Consecutiveness; i'tikaf is naturally continuous, so when vowing days, the nights are included consecutively.
