Transporting the Deceased to Another City
Ruling on transporting the deceased to another city

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on transporting a deceased to another city, identify the distance that triggers tahrimi dislike, and evaluate the proofs related to prophets and martyrs.

Core Idea
Transporting a deceased to another city is makruh tahrimi if the distance exceeds two miles, but exceptions exist for prophets and martyrs.

After settling the issue of exhumation, the text addresses a common practical question: may a deceased be transported to another city for burial? This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi explores the ruling, the relevant distance, and the unique treatment of prophets and martyrs, whose bodies remain pure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is transporting the deceased from one town to another. The ruling is makruh tahrimi (prohibition-level dislike) when the distance is more than two miles, because there is no necessity for such transport.
If the distance is two miles or less, there is no dislike.
2
Analyze the distance condition
The text clarifies that 'two miles' is the threshold. A slight increase is not excessive; a significant increase (kathrat fahisha) triggers the dislike.
This is based on the idea that two miles is a distance where burial in the local cemetery is possible, so transport is unnecessary.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some Hanafis (Qadi Khan, based on Sarakhsi) hold that transporting to another city is permissible without any dislike at all. Others (Muhammad al-Shaybani) leave the ruling unrestricted, not conditioned on necessity.
The text indicates that Muhammad's statement is absolute, but the dislike is apparent when distance exceeds two miles.
4
Evaluate the proof from prophets
A proof is cited that Prophet Jacob and Moses transported the body of Joseph from Egypt to Sham. The text rebuts this: the laws of previous nations are not binding on us unless conditions are met, and the bodies of prophets are pure and do not decay, so they are not analogous to ordinary people.
Similarly, martyrs like Sa'd (radiAllahu anhu) are excepted because their bodies are pure.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that transporting a deceased to another city is makruh tahrimi if the distance is more than two miles, unless there is a valid necessity. Prophets and martyrs are exceptions.
This is supported by the sharh and the rebuttal of the Joseph story.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on exhuming a grave after burial. The prohibition is stated: it is not permissible to move a deceased person once the soil has been thrown over him (after the burial is completed). This is by consensus of the Hanafi imams, and the prohibition applies whether the burial was recent or long ago. The rationale is the prohibition of exhumation (نبش), which is haram as a right of Allah. However, an exception is made if the land is usurped or similar, where exhumation becomes necessary. The commentary then discusses a difference of opinion: some authorities (Zayla'i and others) permit moving the body before the final throwing of soil, even after leveling the bricks, while others (Baraziyya and Khulasa) maintain the prohibition. The sharh prefers the former view. The excerpt also includes a lengthy discussion about transporting the deceased to another city, clarifying that the dislike applies to distances exceeding two miles, and exceptions for prophets and martyrs whose bodies do not decay. The passage ends abruptly, likely continuing the discussion.

Key Terms
مكروه تحريما (makruh tahriman): Disliked to the point of prohibition; a ruling that is close to haram but based on zanni evidence.
ميل (mil): A mile; approximately 1.6 km.
شرع من قبلنا (shar' man qablana): The law of previous nations (before Islam).

Rulings on Burial, Exhumation, and Compensation
Compilation of burial and exhumation rulings from Hashiya Al-Tahtawi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which exhumation (nabsh) is permissible or impermissible in Hanafi fiqh and identify the party responsible for compensation.

Core Idea
Exhumation is generally forbidden but permitted by consensus when burial violates ownership rights or causes harm.

This lesson covers the Hanafi legal rulings on burial in various types of land, the right of pre-emption, compensation for digging in another's grave, and the critical exceptions to the prohibition of exhumation. Understanding these rules ensures that the sanctity of the grave is preserved while upholding property rights and preventing harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine whether the act is burial or exhumation. Burial is a fard kifayah; exhumation is generally haram but has exceptions.
2
Determine the nature of the land
Classify the land as owned, usurped, or pre-empted. This affects whether exhumation is required or allowed.
Pre-emption (shuf'ah) gives a co-owner or neighbor the right to take the land, potentially leading to exhumation.
3
Apply the rule on compensation
If buried in a grave dug for another in non-owned land, compensation (value of digging) is wajib from the deceased's estate, or from public treasury/the wealthy.
The guarantor is the heir, or the state.
4
Distinguish the three exhumation cases in al-Mudmarat
Analyze the three categories: (1) permissible by consensus (usurped land or shroud), (2) impermissible by consensus (maternal affection), (3) disputed (water damage).
The relied-upon view allows exhumation for water damage due to harm.
5
Note exceptions for property and misplacement
Exhumation is allowed to retrieve lost property or a usurped shroud. It is not allowed for minor misplacement of the body (e.g., not facing qibla).
The Prophet allowed exhuming Abu Rughal's grave for a golden twig.

[Rulings:] If the land is usurped, the deceased is taken out for the right of its owner if he demands it; if he wishes, he may leave him and benefit from the land for cultivation or other purposes. Or the land is taken by pre-emption: after purchase, the deceased is buried, then the land is taken by pre-emption for the right of the pre-emptor; he may choose as we said. If one buries in a grave dug for another living person in land not owned by anyone, the value of the digging is compensated from his estate, otherwise from the public treasury or the Muslims as we have mentioned. If the cemetery is spacious, that is disliked because the grave's owner is distressed; if the land is narrow, it is permissible without dislike. Abu al-Layth said: because no one knows in which land he will die; this is like one who spreads a mat or prayer rug in a mosque or gathering; if the place is spacious, no one else may pray or sit there; if narrow, another may move the mat and pray or sit there. Whoever digs a grave for himself before his death, there is no harm and he is rewarded; this was the practice of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz and Rabi' ibn Khuthaym and others. And he is not taken out from it. If a woman wishes to exhume her child and move him to her country, that is not permissible for her; the permissibility of some later scholars is not to be considered; exhumation after burial is absolutely prohibited as in al-Fath and others. He said: Except if the land is usurped; in al-Mudmarat, exhumation after burial has three aspects: one agreed permissible, one agreed impermissible, and one disputed. The second: like the mother who wants to see her child's face or move him to another cemetery; it is impermissible by consensus. The third: if water overcomes the grave, it is said permissible to move him; a dream of Salih ibn Ubayd Allah, and Ibn Abbas gave a fatwa for moving; Abu Ja'far permitted it then retracted and forbade. Commentary
This passage from Hashiya Al-Tahtawi is an expansion on the rulings of burial and exhumation from the Hanafi school. Post-Burial Practices: Talqin and the Questioning in the Grave
Post-burial practices from Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on talqin after burial and identify the ruling on gathering at the bereaved family's home.

Core Idea
Talqin after burial is recommended (mustahabb) despite a weak hadith, supported by practice; sitting at the grave for recitation is also recommended.

This lesson covers the recommended practices after burial according to the Hanafi school, including the disliked gathering at the mourners' home, the seated recitation at the grave, and the debated practice of talqin (prompting the deceased). The discussion also touches upon the reality of the grave's questioning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disliked practice
Gathering at the bereaved family's home for condolences before people come is makruh (disliked). After burial, people should disperse and attend to their affairs.
This is based on reports from later Hanafi imams.
2
Understand the recommended practices
Sitting at the grave for the time it takes to slaughter a camel, reciting Quran and supplicating for the deceased, is mustahabb. Also, the Prophet would stand and ask for forgiveness.
It is reported as a practice of Uthman.
3
Analyze the hadith on talqin
The hadith instructs prompting the deceased with the shahada after burial. Despite its weak chain (da'if), it is acted upon because it is supported by the practice of the people of Sham (Amal ahl al-Sham).
Shafi'is recommend it; Hanafis consider it hasan (good) but not obligatory.
4
Learn about the questioning in the grave
The questioning occurs immediately after burial for a permanent grave. If moved due to necessity, questioning happens in the first grave. It is inevitable for every soul, even if in a beast's belly or deep sea.
There is disagreement about prophets and children; the text gives preferred view.
5
Note exceptions and nuances
If a deceased is placed in a coffin to be moved, they are not questioned there. The truth is that everyone is questioned with their tongue, and the angel prompts them.

Reciting Surat Ya-Sin at Graves: Ruling and Intra-Hanafi Difference
Ruling on reciting Ya-Sin at graves with proof and intra-madhhab difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reciting Surat Ya-Sin at graves and explain the intra-madhhab difference between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Reciting Ya-Sin at graves is recommended (mustahabb) in the Hanafi school based on a hadith, though Abu Hanifa considered it disliked.

This lesson covers the sunnah of standing and recitation at graves, focusing on the recommended recitation of Surat Ya-Sin. It presents the hadith proof for this practice and explains the difference of opinion within the Hanafi school between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad. The relied-upon position for fatwa is that it is mustahabb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is reciting Surat Ya-Sin at graves. The ruling in the relied-upon Hanafi position is mustahabb (recommended), not wajib.
The text explicitly states 'wa yustahabbu lil-za'iri qira'atu surati Ya-Sin'.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Anas narrated by Abu Hafs al-'Ukbari: 'Whoever enters the graves and recites Surat Ya-Sin...'
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith promises reward and alleviation of punishment for the deceased. This indicates recommendation (istihbab), not obligation, because the reward is conditional on the act.
Abu Hanifa did not consider this hadith authentic enough to establish even istihbab.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Hanifa considered recitation at graves makruh because he held no authentic reports reached him. Imam Muhammad said it is mustahabb due to supporting traditions. The later relied-upon opinion is Muhammad's view.
The text says 'wa huwa al-madhhab al-mukhtar'.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The chosen position (mufta bihi) is istihbab, as clarified in the book of Istihsan. The majority of later Hanafis follow this view.
The issue remains permissible, and the reward is valid (isal al-thawab is accepted).

Commentary
This passage from the Hashiya details the sunnah actions and recommended practices when visiting graves. It opens with the standard greeting taught by the Prophet (peace be upon him) for visitors to the graveyard. The core of this lesson is the recommended recitation of Surat Ya-Sin for the dead, with the promise that punishment is lightened for the deceased and good deeds for the reader. The proof is the hadith of Anas: ‘Whoever enters the graves and recites Ya-Sin…’ The text also addresses the difference of opinion among Hanafi imams: Imam Abu Hanifa considered recitation at the grave disliked (makruh) due to lack of authentic reports, while Imam Muhammad considered it recommended (mustahabb) due to supporting traditions. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the latter: it is recommended. Additionally, the sunnah of standing while visiting is explained: one should stand facing the deceased, with back to the qibla, and not touch or kiss the grave, as that is a Christian practice. The greeting should be offered, and the visitor should continue in supplication. The lesson reinforces that dedicating reward (isal al-thawab) to the deceased is valid and effective, based on the hadith of charity, Hajj, and supplication.

Key Terms
يس (Ya-Sin): Surah 36 of the Quran, specifically recommended for recitation at graves according to this hadith.
إهداء الثواب (ihda' al-thawab): Gifting the spiritual reward of an act, such as recitation, charity, or Hajj, to the deceased.

Makruhat on Graves: Actions to Avoid and Their Proofs
Listing actions makruh on graves and clarifying the Hanafi stance on sitting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five actions that are makruh on graves in the Hanafi school and explain the proof for each.

Core Idea
Fresh plants on graves glorify Allah; dry ones may be removed—reverence dictates the ruling.

Visiting graves is a sunna, but certain actions diminish the respect due to the deceased. This lesson details the acts the Hanafi school considers disliked (makruh) on graves, ranging from sitting without recitation to uprooting fresh vegetation. Each ruling has a basis in the sunna or in the principle of reverence.

Guided Path
1
List the five disliked actions
Identify each action mentioned as makruh: sitting without recitation, treading with feet, sleeping, relieving oneself, and uprooting fresh plants. Note that relieving oneself is makruh tahrimi, the others are tanzihi.
These are the core rulings in the passage.
2
Note the exception for sitting
The text states that sitting is only disliked if not for recitation. Sitting for recitation or visiting is permissible (mubah).
This exception is the critical refinement that will be explored in the next lesson.
3
Identify the proof for each ruling
For sitting, the hot ember hadith is cited. For treading, the harm of slippers and lack of respect. For fresh plants, the hadith of the palm branch and the concept of tasbih.
Some proofs are explicit, others are implied by the principle of reverence.
4
Apply the hadith of Zayd bin Thabit
This hadith specifies that the prohibition of sitting is only for relieving oneself. It is used to relax the general prohibition implied by the hot ember hadith.
This shows the Hanafi method of specification (takhsis).

Core Idea
The prohibition of sitting on graves is exclusively for relieving oneself; the hot ember hadith is overridden by specific evidence.

While a well-known hadith compares sitting on a grave to sitting on a hot ember, the Hanafi school, relying on the more specific hadith of Zayd bin Thabit and the practice of the Companions, concludes that the prohibition is limited to relieving oneself. Identify the apparent contradiction

Examine the hadith of Zayd bin Thabit
This hadith explicitly says the Prophet only forbade sitting for relieving oneself. It is considered the specific (khass) that limits the general (amm) prohibition.
This hadith is narrated by al-Tahawi with reliable chains.

Consider the actions of the Companions

Their actions are evidence of permissibility.
4
Understand the Hanafi usul principle

This is a standard Hanafi method of resolving apparent conflicts.
5

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wishing for death due to hardship and explain the prophetic guidance.

Core Idea
Wishing for death is disliked, not forbidden, because the Prophet provided a du'a for those who feel compelled.

This passage addresses a common human emotion: the desire for death when faced with severe hardship. The Hanafi school, following the Prophetic guidance, rules that such a wish is makruh tanzihi. The lesson explores the evidence and the proper course of action.

Guided Path

Identify the act and its ruling
The act is tamanni al-mawt (wishing for death) due to affliction. The ruling is makruh tanzihi (lightly disliked).

Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith from Bukhari: 'None of you should wish for death due to an affliction that has befallen him. But if he must do something, let him say: O Allah, keep me alive as long as life is better for me, and cause me to die when death is better for me.'

3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The direct prohibition (لا يتمنين) indicates that the act is disliked. The conditional exception (فإن كان لا بد فاعلاً) allows a du'a, confirming it is not absolutely forbidden.
The permissibility of the du'a shows the act of wishing itself is not haram.
4
Note any ikhtilaf
There is no significant disagreement among schools; the ruling is mutually agreed upon (muttafaq alayh).
5
Apply the ruling
When faced with hardship, one should not wish for death but rather supplicate with the prescribed du'a.
This aligns with patience and trust in Allah's wisdom.

Definition and Ruling of the Martyr
Defining the shahid and burial without washing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the shahid according to Hanafi fiqh, list the conditions that must be met, and state the ruling of burial without washing.

Core Idea
A Hanafi shahid is buried without washing—conditions include unjust killing, adulthood, purity, and no signs of life after battle.

This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi clarifies the legal definition of a martyr in Hanafi fiqh, distinguishing worldly rulings from afterlife reward. It explains the conditions that must be fulfilled for the special burial method and provides the foundational hadith from the Battle of Uhud that commands burying the martyr with his blood and clothes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition of shahid
The text defines the shahid as one killed unjustly by specific categories of people. Note that mere death in war is insufficient; the killer must belong to one of the listed groups.
The definition includes both direct and indirect causation.
2
List the categories of killers
The killers are: warring disbelievers (directly or by causation), rebels (ahl al-baghy), highway robbers (qutta' al-tariq), thieves (at night even with a blunt instrument, or during the day), or any Muslim who kills deliberately with a sharp instrument.
The category 'Muslim who kills unjustly' explicitly includes father or master killing their dependent.
3
Specify the conditions of the killed person
The killed person must be Muslim, adult (baligh), free from ritual impurity (menstruation, postpartum, janabah), and must not be murtath (show signs of life after the battle ends).
Signs of life include eating, drinking, sleeping, or receiving treatment.
4
Understand the term murtath
Murtath refers to a fallen fighter who survives long enough to engage in worldly activities. Such a person is not a shahid in worldly rulings (he is washed and shrouded normally) but is a shahid in the Hereafter.
The term is a specific Hanafi technicality.
5
Apply the hadith to understand the burial ruling
The Prophet ﷺ commanded at Uhud: 'Wrap them with their blood…' This is the primary proof that the shahid is buried without washing and in his blood-stained clothes. The ruling is a sunnah (recommended) act of honor.
The hadith is classified as ahad and zanni al-thubut, but the Hanafi school accepts it for this ruling.

Rulings of the Martyr in Burial and Washing
Defines the martyr and details burial and washing rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a person to be considered a worldly martyr (shahid al-dunya) in the Hanafi school, apply the burial rulings (no washing, prayer, shroud with blood and clothes), and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding washing a martyr who was junub.

Core Idea
A martyr is buried with blood and clothes, unwashed—except when junub, a child, or insane, according to Imam Abu Hanifa.

This passage from Hashiyat al-Tahtawi delineates the Hanafi definition of a martyr (shahid) and the specific burial rites. It distinguishes between the worldly martyr (shahid al-dunya) who is buried without washing and with his blood and clothes, and the otherworldly martyr (shahid al-akhira) who receives normal washing. The ruling is grounded in the Prophet’s command at Uhud and his prayer over the martyrs. A key intra-madhhab difference emerges regarding whether a martyr in a state of janabah should be washed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of worldly martyr
List the persons considered shahid al-dunya: those killed in war against disbelievers, rebels, highway robbers, or killed unjustly by a Muslim with a sharp weapon, intentionally, and meeting other conditions.
Also includes those killed by their father or master.
2
List the conditions for martyrdom
Conditions: Muslim, adult, free from janabah/hayd/nifas, killed intentionally with a sharp weapon, not murtath (no worldly benefit after the war), etc.
A murtath is not a worldly martyr but still gets reward.
3
Understand the burial rulings and their evidence
The martyr is not washed but buried with his blood and clothes. The hadith 'Zammiluhum bi-dima'ihim' (Shroud them with their blood) is the primary proof. The funeral prayer is performed over the martyr.
Weapons and unsuitable items are removed; clothing is adjusted to sunnah shroud.
4
Analyze the exception for the junub martyr
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the martyr is washed if he was in a state of janabah (or is a child or insane). The hadith of Hanzalah (whom the angels washed) supports this.
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree; they hold the general rule applies.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence of the dispute
The thamarah al-khilaf: a junub martyr is washed on Abu Hanifa's view; not washed on the Sahibayn's view. The mufta bihi (operative position) is Abu Hanifa's, based on explicit hadith evidence.
The child and insane martyr are also washed according to Abu Hanifa because they have no sin.

This passage from Hashiyat al-Tahtawi discusses the rulings of the martyr (shahid) in Islamic law. It opens by defining the categories of people who are considered martyrs in the worldly sense (i.e., entitled to special burial rites): those killed in war against disbelievers, rebels, highway robbers, or those killed unjustly by a Muslim, provided certain conditions are met. The killing must be intentional with a sharp weapon, not by accident or with a heavy object (which would impose diyah). It is disliked to remove all his clothes so that the bloodstains remain. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Imam Abu Hanifa in some transmissions, but the passage notes the ikhtilaf.

This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi clarifies what exactly causes the obligation to fast a particular day of Ramadan. While the entire month is sacred, each day's fast is a separate act of worship. The Hanafi school has a nuanced disagreement about whether seeing any part of the month (including the night) suffices, or whether one must witness a part of the day itself. Understanding this sabab affects when a late-day convert or one regaining sanity must fast.

Guided Path

Identify the two opinions on the sabab

The real difference appears when the event occurs after forenoon: Fakhr al-Islam says the obligation does not arise; Shams al-A'imma says it does, because the night was witnessed.
The text highlights that each day is separate: what remains of the month is obligatory, not what passed.

The text states 'وعليه الفتوى' (and upon it is the fatwa), indicating that the view of Fakhr al-Islam is the operative ruling for fatwa.
This tarjih is from later Hanafi authorities.

If a person accepts Islam at 11:00 AM (before forenoon), they must fast the remaining part of that day. If they accept at 2:00 PM, they do not fast that day, but must fast subsequent days.
The suitable part ends at al-duha al-kubra, roughly one quarter of the daylight hours.

Intending Another Wajib Fast During Ramadan or a Specified Nadhr
Ruling on intending another wajib fast during Ramadan or a specified nadhr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on intending another wajib fast during Ramadan for a resident, traveler, and sick person, and distinguish the case of a specified nadhr.

Core Idea
Intending another wajib during Ramadan: for resident it counts as Ramadan; for traveler it counts as intended wajib.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of intending a wajib fast other than Ramadan during the month of Ramadan. The key legal concept is that Ramadan is a 'standard' (mi'yar) determined by the Legislator, which overrides other intentions for a healthy resident. However, for a traveler and a sick person, different narrations exist from the Hanafi imams. Additionally, a specified nadhr (vowed fast) is treated differently, as it is a personal obligation that can be superseded only by an explicit intention of another wajib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of persons
The passage discusses three categories: resident, traveler, and sick. Identify each and note the ruling for intending another wajib during Ramadan.
Also consider the case of a specified nadhr separate from Ramadan.
2
Understand the concept of mi'yar (standard)
Ramadan is designated by the Legislator as the only obligatory fast during its time. When a resident intends another wajib, the standard overrides his intention, making the fast count as Ramadan.
This is based on the hadith: 'When Sha'ban is over, there is no fast except Ramadan.'
3
Analyze the traveler's exception
According to a single narration from Abu Hanifa, if a traveler intends another wajib (e.g., qada), his fast counts for that intended wajib, not Ramadan. The Sahibayn disagree, analogizing to the resident.
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: the traveler, like one in Sha'ban, has a concession to break his fast, so he is not subject to the standard.
4
Examine the sick person's dispute
One narration (from Hasan ibn Ziyad) treats the sick like the traveler; another (preferred by many) says it falls on Ramadan. Later scholars (Ibn al-Kamal, al-Durr) prefer that all except the traveler fall on Ramadan.
The sick person's concession is based on estimated inability, not actual.
5
Apply the ruling to a specified nadhr
If one fasts with the intention of another wajib during the time of a specified nadhr, the nadhr is not fulfilled; the fast counts for the intended wajib and the nadhr remains as a debt. Intending nafl, however, fulfills the nadhr.
This contrasts with Ramadan where even absolute intention suffices for Ramadan.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid vow must be an act of worship intended for itself, not already obligatory, and from a genus that includes an obligatory act.

The Hanafi school provides a rigorous framework for determining when a vow becomes binding. This passage from Hashiyat al-Tahtawi clarifies the obligation of fulfilling vows, outlines three essential conditions for validity, and firmly condemns the common practice of vowing to saints or the dead. Extract from the text: (1) The genus of the vowed act must include something obligatory by origin. (2) The act must be intended for itself (maqsud li dhatihi). (3) The act must not already be obligatory before the vow.

Commentary
This passage from Hashiyat al-Tahtawi is a critical refinement of the chapter on vows. It primarily states that fulfilling a vow is obligatory, supported by the Quranic verse 'And let them fulfill their vows' and the hadith 'Whoever vows to obey Allah, let him obey Him...' The author notes a difference of opinion: some scholars consider it fard (obligatory in the sense of being established by decisive proof), while others say it is wajib (a lesser degree of obligation). The commentator sides with the view that it is fard based on the strongest opinion, but notes that the verse alone establishes wujub, not fardhiyya. The passage then lays down three conditions for a vow to be binding: (1) the genus of the vowed act must include an obligatory act (e.g., fasting, which has obligatory fasting in Ramadan); (2) the act must be intrinsically intended (e.g., prayer, charity) not merely a means to something else (e.g., wudu is for prayer); (3) the act must not already be obligatory before the vow (e.g., one cannot vow to pray the five daily prayers because they are already obligatory). A fourth condition is added: one cannot vow a logically impossible act, like fasting on the day before today. The commentator then branches into specific examples of vows that are not binding: vowing to perform wudu, recite Quran, perform prostration of recitation, or visit the sick in general—because these either fail the second condition (intrinsically intended) or the third condition (already obligatory). There is a transmission from Abu Hanifa that if one vows to visit a specific person, it is not binding because the intention is respect for that person rather than the act itself. The passage concludes with a strong condemnation of common practices of vowing to saints or making offerings at their shrines. Such vows are invalid and unlawful (haram) because they involve directing worship to a creature. The only permissible form of vow that mentions a saint is when the vow is made to Allah and the saint's name indicates the place of distribution to the poor residing there. This aligns with the Hanafi principle that the object of the vow must be a qurba (act of worship) performed for Allah's sake.

Key Terms
النذر (al-nadhr): A commitment to perform an act of worship that is not obligatory by divine command; it becomes wajib upon the one who vows.
المقصود لذاته (al-maqsud li dhatihi): An act intended for its own sake, not merely as a means to something else. For example, charity (sadaqah) is an act of worship in itself, whereas ablution (wudu) is a means to prayer.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Vowing to Visit the Sick
Rulings on invalid vows and the intra-Hanafi dispute on visiting the sick

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school (zahir al-riwaya) rules that vowing to visit a sick person is invalid, and contrast it with the alternative narration of Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
A vow is valid only if the vowed act has a prescribed obligatory counterpart in Shariah; visiting the sick does not.

Vows (nathr) are a serious commitment, but not every act of worship can be made binding by a vow. This lesson explores the Hanafi condition that the vowed act must belong to a category that has a fard or wajib. The classic case of visiting the sick reveals an intra-Hanafi dispute on the matter.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary of al-Tahtawi on Maraqi al-Falah, focusing on the rulings of vows (nathr). It begins by stating that certain acts, when vowed, do not become obligatory. The examples given: making ablution (wudu), reciting Quran, performing the prostration of recitation (sajda al-tilawa), and visiting the sick. The reason is that these acts either have no obligatory counterpart in Shariah or are merely means to other acts. For instance, wudu is a condition for prayer, not an end itself. Visiting the sick, though a virtuous act, does not have an obligatory analogue (such as a fard or wajib of the same kind). The text then mentions an intra-Hanafi dispute: In the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwaya), such vows are invalid. However, a narration from Abu Hanifa states that if one vows to visit a sick person generally (on a specific day), it is valid; but vowing to visit a specific person is invalid because it is not done purely for righteousness but for the person's sake. The hadith 'the visitor of the sick is on the orchards of Paradise' is cited to show the virtue, but legal distinction is based on intention. The commentary then discusses vows made for the deceased (a common practice among laypeople), declaring them invalid and haram. Such vows are considered to be for a creature, and a creature cannot be worshipped. Even if the intention is ultimately for Allah by helping the poor at the shrines, the vow must be explicitly to Allah, and the naming of a saint is only to specify the recipients. The text then returns to valid vows: freeing slaves (because it is analogous to expiatory manumission), i'tikaf (because the last sitting in prayer is obligatory, and i'tikaf shares the element of staying in the mosque), walking Hajj (because walking for the near resident is prescribed), prayer other than fard, fasting, charity, and slaughter (like sacrifice). The principle is that a vow is valid only if the vowed act belongs to a category that has a prescribed obligatory act in Shariah. The passage also notes that a slave or woman may vow i'tikaf, but the master or husband can prevent them; they must make it up later. A mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) cannot be prevented by his master. Finally, it discusses the difference between absolute and conditional vows.

القربة (al-qurba): A righteous act that brings one closer to Allah, such as visiting the sick.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary of al-Tahtawi on Maraqi al-Falah, focusing on the rulings of vows (nathr). It begins by stating that certain acts, when vowed, do not become obligatory. The examples given: making ablution (wudu), reciting Quran, performing the prostration of recitation (sajda al-tilawa), and visiting the sick. The reason is that these acts either have no obligatory counterpart in Shariah or are merely means to other acts. For instance, wudu is a condition for prayer, not an end itself. Visiting the sick, though a virtuous act, does not have an obligatory analogue (such as a fard or wajib of the same kind). The text then mentions an intra-Hanafi dispute: In the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwaya), such vows are invalid. However, a narration from Abu Hanifa states that if one vows to visit a sick person generally (on a specific day), it is valid; but vowing to visit a specific person is invalid because it is not done purely for righteousness but for the person's sake. The hadith 'the visitor of the sick is on the orchards of Paradise' is cited to show the virtue, but legal distinction is based on intention. The commentary then discusses vows made for the deceased (a common practice among laypeople), declaring them invalid and haram. Such vows are considered to be for a creature, and a creature cannot be worshipped. Even if the intention is ultimately for Allah by helping the poor at the shrines, the vow must be explicitly to Allah, and the naming of a saint is only to specify the recipients. The text then returns to valid vows: freeing slaves (because it is analogous to expiatory manumission), i'tikaf (because the last sitting in prayer is obligatory, and i'tikaf shares the element of staying in the mosque), walking Hajj (because walking for the near resident is prescribed), prayer other than fard, fasting, charity, and slaughter (like sacrifice). The principle is that a vow is valid only if the vowed act belongs to a category that has a prescribed obligatory act in Shariah. The passage also notes that a slave or woman may vow i'tikaf, but the master or husband can prevent them; they must make it up later. A mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) cannot be prevented by his master. Finally, it discusses the difference between absolute and conditional vows.

Definition, Conditions, and Types of I'tikaf
Defines i'tikaf linguistically and legally, with proof, conditions, and types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of i'tikaf, identify its conditions, and explain the Hanafi position on the required mosque for its validity.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is a spiritual retreat requiring a congregational mosque for men and intention; fasting is needed only if vowed.

I'tikaf (spiritual retreat) is a significant act of worship, especially during the last ten days of Ramadan. This passage from Hashiya al-Tahtawi defines i'tikaf both linguistically and legally, lists its essential conditions (shurut), and clarifies the types: obligatory (by vow), sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah), and supererogatory (nafl). Special attention is given to the condition of the mosque: for men, the relied-upon Hanafi view requires a mosque where congregational prayer for all five daily prayers is actually held. Understanding these details ensures that one's i'tikaf is valid and rewarding.

Distinguish linguistic vs. legal meaning

The legal definition integrates both concepts: confining oneself from worldly affairs and devoting oneself to Allah.
2
Identify the essential conditions (shurut)
List the conditions for a valid i'tikaf as given in the text: Islam, sanity, intention (niyyah), a designated mosque (for men with congregation, for women her home prayer space), and for obligatory (vowed) i'tikaf: fasting and purity from menstruation/postpartum bleeding.
Puberty and freedom are not conditions; the i'tikaf of a discerning child or a slave is valid.

Examine the ruling that the mosque must be one where congregational prayer for all five prayers is actually held (al-mukhtar), and compare with the views of Abu Yusuf (obligatory only in such mosques, supererogatory in any) and Muhammad al-Shaybani/al-Saruji (valid in any mosque).
The mufta bihi is Imam Abu Hanifa's stricter view, but the dispute is legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.

Classify i'tikaf into: wajib (by vow, immediate or conditional), sunnah mu'akkadah kifayah (last ten days of Ramadan, based on the Prophet's continuous practice), and nafl (supererogatory, any time).
The Prophet never abandoned i'tikaf after entering Madinah, but did not reproach those who omitted it, indicating sunnah kifayah.

Commentary
This passage from Hashiya Al-Tahtawi opens the section on i'tikaf (spiritual retreat) under the Book of Fasting. It first defines i'tikaf linguistically: it can be transitive (confinement, as in the Quranic verse about the sacrificial animal being confined) or intransitive (persistent devotion, as in 'devoting themselves to idols'). The proof is the hadith of 'Ali and Hudhayfah that i'tikaf is only in a congregational mosque, and the rational argument that it entails waiting for prayer in congregation, which is most perfect. Expiations for Killing and Damage in Ihram and Haram
Lists rulings for expiations on harming animals and plants in Haram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the expiation for killing a louse, game, and damaging plants in the Haram, and apply the choices available.

Core Idea
Killing a louse requires half a sa'; game requires a sacrifice, food, or fasting; damaging Haram plants requires value.

The state of ihram and the sanctity of the Haram impose additional restrictions on harming living creatures and plants. This passage from al-Tahtawi's Hashiya details the specific expiations (jaza') for various violations, from killing a tiny louse to cutting a tree. Understanding these gradations reveals the precision of Hanafi fiqh in protecting the sacred precincts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of violation
The passage covers killing small insects (lice/locusts), killing game, damaging game (feathers, hair, limbs, eggs), killing predatory animals, and cutting plants/trees. List each category.
2
Determine the expiation for each category
For lice/locusts: half a sa' per louse (for one), more if many. For game: value appraised; if reaches hady, choose slaughter, feed, or fast. For damage: value of deficiency. For predator: up to one sheep; if attacks, nothing.
3
Apply the options for game
When value reaches hady, the muhrim has three options: slaughter a hady in the Haram, or feed half a sa' per poor person, or fast one day per poor person.
The option to fast is specific to Hanafi school.
4
Analyze the rules for plants in the Haram
Three types of trees are free to cut; one type (self-grown, non-planted) requires value. Grass is haram to graze or cut except idhkhir and truffles.
5
Note the exceptions and nuances
For damage that heals with no trace, no guarantee. For killed egg, value. For halal (non-muhrim) killing in Haram, fasting not accepted.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the Hashiya on Maraqi al-Falah, covering the expiations (jaza') required for various actions in the state of ihram or within the Haram. The main text lists the expiations: for killing a louse or locust, one gives charity of what he wishes, but the commentary explains that the minimum is half a sa' for a single louse, and for more than three the expiation increases. For killing game (which is not permitted in ihram or in the Haram), the game must be appraised by two just men at the place of killing or near it; if its value reaches that of a sacrificial animal (hady), the person has a choice: either buy and slaughter that animal in the Haram, or give food of equivalent value (half a sa' per poor person), or fast one day per poor person. For lesser damage like plucking feathers that prevent flight, cutting hair, or breaking eggs, the value of the deficiency must be paid. Killing a predatory animal (an animal not eaten) does not require expiation beyond one sheep; if it attacks, nothing is due. Fasting is not accepted for a non-muhrim (halal) killing game in the Haram or cutting plants. Trees and grass in the Haram are protected: cutting naturally grown trees that are not of the planted type requires payment of value. The commentator clarifies types of trees: three types are permissible to cut without expiation (planted trees of any kind, and self-grown of planted kind), while one type (self-grown of non-planted kind) requires expiation. Grazing and cutting grass are haram except for idhkhir (a fragrant grass) and truffles. The final note distinguishes this from perfuming someone else's limb or dressing them in sewn garments, where no compensation is due by consensus.
