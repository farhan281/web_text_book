Entering the Prophet's Mosque and Praying the Greeting
Sunan for entering and praying in the Prophet's Mosque

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah acts for entering the Prophet's mosque and explain the significance of praying near the pulpit.

Core Idea
Enter the Prophet's Mosque with humility, pray two rak'ahs near the pulpit, and thank Allah for the blessing.

This lesson covers the recommended etiquette for entering the Prophet's Mosque in Medina. It explains the specific supplications, the posture of humility, and the prayer of greeting (tahiyyat al-masjid) near the pulpit, which is a location of special barakah.

Guided Path
1
Enter with humility and recitation
Enter the mosque with tranquility, observing the majesty of the place, and recite the prescribed duas: 'Bismillah wa 'ala millati Rasul Allah' and 'Rabbi adkhilni mudkhala sidq...'.
The du'a is for an admittance pleasing to Allah and protection.
2
Pray two rak'ahs near the pulpit
After entering, pray two rak'ahs as a greeting to the mosque, standing such that the pulpit's pillar aligns with your right shoulder, as that was the Prophet's station.
This prayer is sunnah muakkadah; the location near the pulpit is recommended.
3
Perform additional prostration of gratitude
After the greeting prayer, perform two additional rak'ahs in gratitude (sajdat al-shukr) for reaching the Prophet's mosque.
This is a separate sunnah to thank Allah for the opportunity.
4
Make personal supplication
After the prayers, make any personal du'as, asking for forgiveness and blessings.
One may ask for whatever they wish.
5
Understand the virtue of the place
Between the pulpit and the Prophet's grave is a garden of Paradise, as per the hadith. This spot is especially blessed.
This encourages reverence and devotion in that area.

With power from You and his provisions, humble with tranquility and dignity, observing the majesty of the place, saying: In the name of Allah and on the religion of the Messenger of Allah, O Lord, admit me with a true admission and bring me out with a true exit, and grant me a helping authority. O Allah, send blessings upon our Master Muhammad and upon the family of Muhammad to the end, and forgive my sins and open for me the doors of Your mercy and bounty. Then he enters the noble mosque and prays its greeting at his pulpit two rak'ahs, and stands such that the pillar of the noble pulpit is opposite his right shoulder, for that is the station of the Prophet, and between his grave and his pulpit is a garden from the gardens of Paradise as he informed, and he said: My pulpit is on my cistern. Then he prostrates in gratitude to Allah Almighty by performing two rak'ahs besides the greeting of the mosque, thanking for what Allah has enabled him and favored him with reaching it. Then he makes whatever supplication he wishes. Then he arises heading towards the noble grave, and stands at a distance of four cubits away from the noble enclosure with utmost manners, turning his back to the qibla, facing the head of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and his noble face, observing his blessed gaze upon you, his hearing of your speech, his responding to your greeting, and his saying Amin to your supplication. And you say: Peace be upon you, O my master, O Messenger of Allah; peace be upon you, O Prophet of Allah; peace be upon you, O Prophet of mercy; peace be upon you, O intercessor of the Ummah; peace be upon you, O master of the messengers; peace be upon you, O Seal of the Prophets; peace be upon you, O Enwrapped; peace be upon you, O Enshrouded; peace be upon you and upon your pure roots and the purified members of your household, from whom Allah has removed impurity and purified them thoroughly. May Allah reward you on our behalf with the best reward He has rewarded any prophet for his people and any messenger for his community. I bear witness that you are the Messenger of Allah, you have conveyed the message, fulfilled the trust, advised the Ummah, clarified the proof, struggled in the way of Allah as He deserves, and established the religion until certainty came to you. May Allah's blessings and peace be upon you and upon the most honored place that is honored by the dwelling of your noble body therein, perpetual blessings and peace from the Lord of the Worlds, as many as what has been and what will be by the knowledge of Allah, a blessing without end. O Messenger of Allah, we are your delegates and visitors of your sanctuary, honored to be in your presence. We have come from distant lands and faraway places, crossing plains and rugged terrain with the purpose of visiting you, to gain your intercession and to look at your monuments and landmarks, to fulfill some of your right, and to seek intercession through you to our Lord. Indeed, sins have broken our backs and burdens have weighed heavily on our shoulders. You are the intercessor whose intercession is accepted, promised the great intercession, the praised station, and the means. And Allah Almighty said: 'And if they, when they wronged themselves, had come to you and asked forgiveness of Allah and the Messenger had asked forgiveness for them, they would have found Allah Forgiving and Merciful.' And we have come wronging ourselves, seeking forgiveness for our sins. So intercede for us with your Lord and ask Him to cause us to die upon your Sunnah, to gather us among your group, to bring us to your cistern, and to give us to drink from your cup, not disgraced nor regretful. Intercession, intercession, intercession, O Messenger of Allah - he says it three times. Our Lord, forgive us and our brothers who preceded us in faith, and do not put in our hearts any rancor towards those who believe. Our Lord, indeed You are Kind, Merciful. And convey it... Satisfied. His words: 'And you fulfilled the trust' - meaning the prayer and other things for which there is reward in doing and punishment in leaving, i.e., you fulfilled that. His words: 'And you clarified the proof' - it is (with damm) the proof (from Qamus). His words: 'His true jihad' - i.e., his jihad that is due, or his greatest jihad. His words: 'Until certainty came to you' - i.e., death. His words: 'By the knowledge of Allah' - attached to 'be' and the one that is omitted of its kind. His words: 'No end to its term' - with fatha on the mim, meaning the limit and end (from Qamus). His words: 'We are your delegates' - i.e., those who come to you and arrive to you. His words: 'Distant' - i.e., far. They say: the home became distant (as a verb) and became far, so it is distant (from Qamus). His words: 'The plain' - it is from the earth, opposite of rough terrain. His words: 'And the rugged' - opposite of plain, like al-wa'r, al-wa'ir, and al-wa'eer. His words: 'To your monuments' - plural of ma'thara, which is a repeated noble deed. His words: 'And your landmarks' - plural of ma'had, the place where something is known. His words: 'Broke' - al-qasm is breaking with separation, or without it. His words: 'Our shoulders' - plural of kahl, the withers, or the upper front of the back near the neck, which is the upper third, containing six vertebrae, and between the shoulder blades, or the joint of the neck to the spine (from Qamus). His words: 'The intercessor whose intercession is accepted' - i.e., whose intercession is accepted. His words: 'And the praised station' - an apposition, synonym. His words: 'And the means' - it is a station in Paradise that is only for him. His words: 'And the Messenger asked forgiveness for them' - there is an address shift to magnify his status. His words: 'Upon your Sunnah' - i.e., upon following your way. His words: 'In your group' - i.e., your troop and company. His words: 'By your cup' - al-ka's is the vessel from which one drinks, or as long as the drink is in it, meaning the cups of your cistern. His words: 'Intercession' - i.e., we ask you for intercession. His words: 'Rancor' - i.e., hatred.

Commentary
This passage from Hashiyat al-Tahtawi describes the sunnah etiquette for visiting the Prophet's mosque and grave in Medina. The text details entering the mosque with humility, reciting specific supplications, praying two rak'ahs as a greeting (tahiyyat al-masjid) near the pulpit, and then proceeding to the grave. The commentator explains key phrases: 'jilalat al-makan' refers to the majesty of the place due to the Prophet and his Companions; 'bismillah wa 'ala millati rasul Allah' indicates entering with intention to follow his religion; the dua 'Rabbi adkhilni mudkhala sidq' is for an admittance pleasing to Allah. The visitor stands about four cubits away from the enclosure, facing the Prophet's head, with the qibla behind them (as is sunnah for visiting graves). The prescribed greeting (salam) is lengthy, acknowledging the Prophet's various titles and seeking his intercession. The commentator clarifies that 'al-muzammil' and 'al-muddaththir' are titles from the Quran referring to the Prophet during revelation. The visitor then testifies to the Prophet's fulfillment of his mission. The commentary also explains that 'al-wasilah' is a station in Paradise unique to the Prophet. The passage emphasizes the belief that the Prophet hears the greeting and responds. This reflects the Hanafi position that visiting the Prophet's grave is a recommended act (mustahabb) with specific adab derived from the practice of the Salaf.

Key Terms
التحية (al-tahiyyah): The greeting prayer (tahiyyat al-masjid) performed upon entering a mosque.
المنبر (al-minbar): The pulpit of the Prophet's Mosque, from which the Prophet delivered sermons.
الروضة (al-rawdah): A garden of Paradise; the area between the Prophet's grave and his pulpit is one such garden.

Visiting the Prophet's Grave and Greeting Him
Sunan for visiting the Prophet's grave and greeting him

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the proper etiquette for greeting the Prophet at his grave and explain the Hanafi belief that the Prophet hears and responds to the greeting.

Core Idea
Stand respectfully at the grave, offer the extended greeting, and seek the Prophet's intercession.

This lesson covers the sunnah manners for visiting the Prophet's grave in Medina. It details the posture, distance, and the specific words of greeting (salam) to be recited, along with testimonies and supplications. The commentator emphasizes that the Prophet is aware of the visitor and responds to the greeting.

Guided Path
1
Approach with reverence
After praying in the mosque, proceed to the grave with utmost respect and humility, standing about four cubits away from the enclosure.
The distance is approximately two meters.
2
Adopt the correct position
Stand facing the head of the Prophet (peace be upon him), with your back to the qibla, observing the blessed gaze upon you and his hearing of your speech.
This is the sunnah for visiting graves.
3
Recite the prescribed greeting
Greet the Prophet with the salaam as taught in the text, addressing him with honorific titles and sending blessings.
The greeting includes 'Peace be upon you, O Messenger of Allah' and other titles like 'O Prophet of Mercy'.
4
Offer testimony and seek intercession
Testify that the Prophet conveyed the message and ask for his intercession, using the specific du'as from the lesson.
The testimony includes praising his struggles and asking for shafa'ah.
5
Understand the virtue and belief
Believe that the Prophet hears the greeting, responds, and says 'Amin' to your supplications. Also, the 'wasilah' is a unique station in Paradise.
This belief is based on hadith and Hanafi doctrine.

Then he enters the noble mosque and prays its greeting at his pulpit two rak'ahs, and stands such that the pillar of the noble pulpit is opposite his right shoulder, for that is the station of the Prophet, and between his grave and his pulpit is a garden from the gardens of Paradise as he informed, and he said: My pulpit is on my cistern. Then he prostrates in gratitude to Allah Almighty by performing two rak'ahs besides the greeting of the mosque, thanking for what Allah has enabled him and favored him with reaching it. Then he makes whatever supplication he wishes. His words: 'No end to its term' - with fatha on the mim, meaning the limit and end (from Qamus). His words: 'We are your delegates' - i.e., those who come to you and arrive to you. His words: 'Distant' - i.e., far. They say: the home became distant (as a verb) and became far, so it is distant (from Qamus). His words: 'The plain' - it is from the earth, opposite of rough terrain. His words: 'And the rugged' - opposite of plain, like al-wa'r, al-wa'ir, and al-wa'eer. His words: 'To your monuments' - plural of ma'thara, which is a repeated noble deed. His words: 'And your landmarks' - plural of ma'had, the place where something is known. Commentary
This passage from Hashiyat al-Tahtawi describes the sunnah etiquette for visiting the Prophet's mosque and grave in Medina. The text details entering the mosque with humility, reciting specific supplications, praying two rak'ahs as a greeting (tahiyyat al-masjid) near the pulpit, and then proceeding to the grave. The commentator explains key phrases: 'jilalat al-makan' refers to the majesty of the place due to the Prophet and his Companions; 'bismillah wa 'ala millati rasul Allah' indicates entering with intention to follow his religion; the dua 'Rabbi adkhilni mudkhala sidq' is for an admittance pleasing to Allah. The visitor stands about four cubits away from the enclosure, facing the Prophet's head, with the qibla behind them (as is sunnah for visiting graves). The prescribed greeting (salam) is lengthy, acknowledging the Prophet's various titles and seeking his intercession. The commentator clarifies that 'al-muzammil' and 'al-muddaththir' are titles from the Quran referring to the Prophet during revelation. The visitor then testifies to the Prophet's fulfillment of his mission. The commentary also explains that 'al-wasilah' is a station in Paradise unique to the Prophet. The passage emphasizes the belief that the Prophet hears the greeting and responds. This reflects the Hanafi position that visiting the Prophet's grave is a recommended act (mustahabb) with specific adab derived from the practice of the Salaf.

الوسيلة (al-wasilah): A station in Paradise exclusive to the Prophet; a means of nearness.

Etiquette of Visiting the Prophet's Grave
Describes the supplications and movements for visiting the Prophet's grave and the two caliphs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the etiquettes of visiting the Prophet's grave, including the specific supplications and movements, and explain why delivering a salam on behalf of another is wajib.

Core Idea
Visiting the Prophet's grave with precise etiquettes is a virtuous act; delivering another's salam is wajib.

This lesson covers the detailed etiquette of visiting the grave of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), as well as those of Abu Bakr and Umar, as described in the Hashiya of al-Tahtawi. You will learn the specific supplications to recite and the movements to perform, along with the legal ruling on conveying greetings from others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of visitation
The visitation consists of three stops: first at the Prophet's grave, then moving a cubit to the right to greet Abu Bakr, then similarly to Umar, and finally stepping back half a cubit to address both.
The movements are precisely measured in cubits.
2
Analyze the supplication to the Prophet
The supplication includes praising the Prophet's conveyance of the message, seeking intercession, and asking for forgiveness. The commentator explains key terms like 'shafa'ah' (intercession) and 'wasilah' (means).
The supplication contains Quranic allusion (4:64).
3
Understand the ruling on delivering salam
The commentator states that delivering the salam on behalf of someone who appointed you is wajib. This is based on the hadith and is considered part of fulfilling the trust.
Some scholars differ; Hanafis hold it as obligatory.
4
Review the greetings to the Caliphs
The greetings praise Abu Bakr for his caliphate, fighting apostasy, and maintaining family ties, and praise Umar for manifesting Islam and strengthening the religion. The commentary refutes misconceptions about enmity between Fatima and Abu Bakr.
These greetings reinforce the virtue of the Companions.
5
Note the critical refinement
The commentary clarifies that Abu Bakr never prostrated to an idol and that the visitor should keep his back to the qibla throughout, which some may consider inappropriate, but is allowed here out of respect.
The refinement addresses potential objections.

Etiquette of Visiting the Prophet's Grave and the Graves of Abu Bakr and Umar
Describes the recommended acts and supplications for ziyarah in Medina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the recommended sequence of actions when visiting the Prophet's Mosque in Medina and recite the key supplications for greeting the Prophet, Abu Bakr, and Umar.

Core Idea
Ziyarah is sunnah: greet the Prophet facing his grave, then Abu Bakr and Umar, then supplicate at the Rawdah and visit the Baqi‘.

This lesson covers the detailed etiquette of visiting the Prophet's Mosque in Medina as described in Hanafi fiqh. You will learn the proper sequence of actions, the specific supplications to recite at the graves of the Prophet, Abu Bakr, and Umar, and the significance of other sacred sites within and around the mosque. This practice is considered a highly recommended sunnah, rooted in the actions of the Salaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal status of ziyarah
Confirm that visiting the Prophet's grave is a highly recommended sunnah (sunnah muakkadah) and not obligatory. The acts that follow are mustahabb (recommended).
Hanafis hold that ziyarah is a virtuous act, unlike some later scholars who consider it innovation.
2
Learn the etiquette of greeting the Prophet
Stand facing the Prophet's grave with your back to the qiblah. Convey any greetings entrusted to you, then send blessings and supplicate as you wish.
السلام عليك يا رسول الله from so-and-so... then صل عليه and supplicate.
3
Visit Abu Bakr al-Siddiq
Move about a cubit to the right until you are opposite the head of Abu Bakr. Recite the prescribed greeting praising his virtues and role as caliph.
The commentary explains the virtues and refutes claims of enmity between Fatima and Abu Bakr.
4
Visit Umar ibn al-Khattab
Move similarly until opposite Umar's head. Recite the greeting acknowledging his contributions to Islam.
Then step back half a cubit to be between the two graves.
5
Return for final supplication and visit other sites
Return to the Prophet's head and recite the verse-based supplication. Then visit the pillar of Abu Lubabah, the Rawdah, the pulpit (touch the pomegranate knob), the weeping pillar, and finally the Baqi‘ cemetery.
The commentary notes that the pomegranate knob no longer exists today.

Commentary
This passage from the Hashiya al-Tahtawi on Maraqi al-Falah details the recommended acts (sunan) for visiting the Prophet's Mosque in Medina. It begins with the etiquette of conveying greetings to the Prophet, emphasizing that it is wajib to deliver the message. The visitor should face the Prophet's grave while facing away from the qiblah, as the qiblah is to be respected. After greeting the Prophet, one moves approximately one cubit to the right to face the grave of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, praising his virtues, then similarly to Umar ibn al-Khattab. Between the two, one stands in the middle. The commentary explains that this sequence is based on the positions of the graves. After these greetings, one returns to the Prophet's head and recites a supplication invoking the Qur'anic verse about seeking forgiveness and intercession. Then follow visits to various sacred spots in the mosque: the pillar of Abu Lubabah (where he tied himself until his repentance was accepted), the Rawdah (the garden), the pulpit where one touches the pomegranate-shaped knob for blessing, and the weeping pillar (where the date-palm trunk that used to lean towards the Prophet during sermons is located). Finally, it is recommended to go to the Baqi‘ cemetery to visit the graves of many Companions, including Hamza, al-Abbas, Hasan ibn Ali, Uthman, and others. The commentary provides linguistic clarifications and notes on the virtues of these figures. The overall ruling is that these acts are mustahabb (recommended) and part of the sunnah of ziyarah.
