Accepting a Judge's Verbal Order for Punishment: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute over accepting judge's order without evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding acceptance of a judge's verbal order for hudud and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A judge's verbal order for punishment is not accepted without verifying the evidence, due to risk of error.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents an internal Hanafi conflict: while the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) allows execution on the judge's word alone, Imam Muhammad later retracted, demanding the executioner witness the evidence. The later scholars (mashayikh) preferred Imam Muhammad's view due to widespread judicial corruption, except for written orders (kitab al-qadi). Let's dissect the reasoning and practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the executioner's decision to carry out a punishment (rajm, qat', darb) on the basis of a judge's verbal order alone. The ruling according to zahir al-riwayah is mubah (permissible), but this is contested.
The act is the executioner's acceptance of the judge's order.
2
State the zahir al-riwayah position and its proof
Zahir al-riwayah holds that the executioner may act on the judge's verbal order. The proof: the judge reports a matter he owns to create (yamluku insha'ah), so his report is free from accusation; also obedience to uli al-amr is obligatory.
This relies on the judge's authority and trustworthiness.
3
State Imam Muhammad's nawadir position and its proof
Imam Muhammad retracted and required the executioner to witness the evidence (shahadah) before acting. Proof: the judge's statement may contain error, and rectification after punishment is impossible.
This is a rare transmission (nawadir) but preferred later.
4
Explain the mashayikh's tarjih and the exception for kitab al-qadi
The later scholars preferred Imam Muhammad's view due to the corrupt state of judges (fasad al-qudat). However, they made an exception for a judge's written order (kitab al-qadi) due to practical necessity.
This tarjih shifts the mufta bihi from zahir al-riwayah to nawadir.
5
Apply the additional classification by Imam Abu Mansur al-Maturidi
Imam Abu Mansur categorized judges: a knowledgeable and just judge's word is accepted; a just but ignorant judge must explain; a corrupt (fasiq) judge's word is not accepted unless the executioner sees the evidence.
This refinement further restricts acceptance based on the judge's qualities.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the acceptance of a judge's verbal order for the execution of criminal punishments (hudud). The base ruling from the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) is that the person appointed to execute the punishment (e.g., stoning, amputation, flogging) is permitted to act upon the judge's verbal order without further verification. The justification is that the judge is reporting a matter that he is authorized to create (yamluku insha'ah), so his report is trustworthy, and obeying those in authority (uli al-amr) is obligatory. However, Imam Muhammad, in a rare transmission (nawadir), retracted this and argued that the executioner must not accept the judge's statement until he sees the evidence for the judgment (i.e., witnesses testify in his presence). This is because the judge's statement may contain error, and once the punishment is executed, rectification is impossible. The later Hanafi scholars (mashayikh) preferred Imam Muhammad's view due to the widespread corruption among judges in later times, except in the case of a written order from the judge (kitab al-qadi) which is deemed necessary. Furthermore, Imam Abu Mansur al-Maturidi categorized judges into four types: (1) a knowledgeable and just judge – his statement is accepted unconditionally; (2) a just but ignorant judge – his statement is accepted only if he provides a sound explanation; (3) an ignorant-fasiq or knowledgeable-fasiq judge – his statement is not accepted unless the executioner personally witnesses the evidence. The text emphasizes that the practical necessity of written orders (kitab al-qadi) is an exception, and the mufta bihi is the more cautious position requiring verification.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The well-known and authoritative transmission within the Hanafi school, primarily from the six books of Imam Muhammad (al-Usul).
نوادر (nawadir): Rare or less-known transmissions from the early Hanafi imams, not part of the six core books.
المشائخ (al-mashayikh): The later Hanafi scholars (usually from the 5th century onwards) who issued fatwas and determined the mufta bihi.

Accepting the Judge's Punishment Order: Zahir al-Riwaya vs. Nawadir
Ruling on whether executor must follow judge's order to punish

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether a judge's order for hadd punishment must be followed and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Zahir al-Riwaya and Imam Muhammad's Nawadir.

Core Idea
A judge's order for hadd is accepted in Zahir al-Riwaya, but Imam Muhammad required witness verification due to risk of error.

This passage addresses a critical question in judicial procedure: must a person appointed to execute a hadd punishment (stoning, amputation, flogging) obey the judge's order without question? The mainstream Hanafi view (Zahir al-Riwaya) permits execution without further inquiry, grounded in the judge's authority as mutawalli and the obligation to obey those in authority. However, a lesser-known narration from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (Nawadir) requires the executor to have witnesses testify in his presence before proceeding, due to the possibility of error and the irreversibility of punishment. The passage further refines this by classifying judges according to their knowledge and justice, as explained by Imam al-Maturidi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the execution of a hadd punishment (r/rejimming, cutting of hand, flogging) based on a judge's order. The ruling in Zahir al-Riwaya is that it is permissible (and required) for the appointed executor to follow the order.
The ruling is about accepting the judge's word as sufficient evidence; the executor does not need to verify independently.
2
State the Zahir al-Riwaya position and its proof
Zahir al-Riwaya holds that the executor must follow the judge's order because the judge is the mutawalli (administrator) capable of creating the ruling, so his report is not suspect. Additionally, obedience to ulu al-amr (those in authority) is wajib. The proof is based on qiyas: the judge's capacity to create the ruling makes his testimony trustworthy.
This is the relied-upon position in the school.
3
State the Nawadir position and its proof
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, in a narration from his Nawadir, retracted his earlier view and required that the executor not accept the judge's order until witnesses testify in his presence. The proof is the possibility of error in the judge's order and the fact that after punishment no remedy is possible.
This is a weaker transmission but was later considered mustahsan by Mashayikh in corrupt times.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the dispute
The author of al-Hidayah notes that Imam Muhammad's narration would also imply rejecting a judge's letter, but the Mashayikh left it due to necessity for the judicial system. However, for cases outside Kitab al-Qadi ila al-Qadi (judge writing to another judge), they considered the Nawadir option preferable because of widespread judicial corruption.
This shows a pragmatic application: in a reliable judiciary, follow the order; in corrupt times, verify.
5
Apply al-Maturidi's classification
Shaykh Abu Mansur al-Maturidi refined the rule based on the judge's qualities: (1) Scholar (mujtahid) and just: order accepted without inquiry; (2) Just but ignorant: order is inquired about, but if explained according to Shariah, must be followed; (3) If judge is sinful (whether ignorant or scholar): order is not accepted unless the executor himself witnesses the cause of the ruling (i.e., the crime).
This classification integrates both knowledge (to avoid error) and justice (to avoid betrayal).

Explanation: In this chapter, it is to explain this principle: whether the word of a single judge, before being dismissed or after being dismissed, is accepted or not. The issue: If a judge said to a person, 'I have ordered stoning on so-and-so, so you stone him,' or 'I have ordered cutting his hand, so you cut his hand,' or 'I have ordered flogging him, so you flog him,' then it is permissible for the one whom the judge gave such an order to do so. This is the Zahir al-Riwaya. And from Imam Muhammad in the Nawadir, there is a narration that he retracted from this statement and said: The one appointed to execute punishment should not accept the judge's order until witnesses testify in his presence. That is, if witnesses testify of the crime in his presence, then it is permissible for him to execute the punishment according to the judge's order; otherwise, he should not accept the judge's order. The proof: There is a possibility of error and mistake in the judge's order, and after punishment, remedy is not possible. Therefore, before executing, he himself should be satisfied, and this is only if the witnesses of the crime testify in his presence. The author of al-Hidayah says: This narration of Imam Muhammad requires that the judge's letter also be not accepted, because there is also a possibility of error. However, the Hanafi Mashayikh have left this narration due to necessity. But apart from Kitab al-Qadi ila al-Qadi, the Hanafi Mashayikh have considered this narration as good and preferable, because in our time, the condition of most judges is corrupt and deteriorated. The reason for Zahir al-Riwaya: The judge has informed of something whose creation he owns and controls, because the judge is the mutawalli (administrator) and the mutawalli is capable of creating the qada. And the one who is capable of creating that which he reports, he is not suspected in the report. So when the judge's order and decision are free from suspicion, his order will be accepted. The second proof: The judge is among the ulu al-amr (those in authority), and obedience to ulu al-amr is obligatory, and since accepting the judge's order is obedience, it will be accepted.
The author of al-Hidayah said: Shaykh Abu Mansur al-Maturidi said: If the judge is a scholar (mujtahid) and just, then his word will be accepted, because being a scholar protects him from error in the decision, and being just saves him from suspicion of betrayal. In this case, there is no need to inquire from the judge by agreement. And if the judge is just but ignorant, then his decision will be inquired about, because due to ignorance, there is suspicion of error. So if the judge explains his decision according to the Shariah and clarifies it, then confirming him is obligatory, and if the judge does not explain according to the Shariah, then confirming him is not obligatory, and his word will not be accepted. And if the judge is ignorant and sinful, or a scholar and sinful, his word will not be accepted, unless the person appointed to execute punishment himself witnesses the cause of the ruling. Because in the case of ignorance and sinfulness, both suspicions of error and betrayal are present; in the case of a scholar and sinful, the suspicion of betrayal is present.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on whether a judge's order to execute a hadd punishment (stoning, cutting of hand, flogging) must be followed by the executor. The author begins by stating the issue: a judge tells a person to execute a punishment. The Zahir al-Riwaya (the well-known transmitted position) is that it is permissible for the executor to follow the order. However, Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani has a narration in the Nawadir (rare transmissions) that he retracted this and said the executor should not accept the judge's order until witnesses testify in his presence. The reasoning is that there is a possibility of error in the judge's order, and after execution, there is no remedy. The author of al-Hidayah notes that Imam Muhammad's narration also implies that a judge's letter should not be accepted, but the Hanafi Mashayikh left this narration due to necessity. However, for cases other than Kitab al-Qadi ila al-Qadi (a judge writing to another judge), they considered this narration praiseworthy given the corruption of judges in their time. The proof for Zahir al-Riwaya is that the judge is the mutawalli (administrator) and is capable of creating what he reports, so his report is not suspicious; also, obedience to ulu al-amr (those in authority) is wajib. Then the passage quotes al-Maturidi's detailed classification: a scholar-just judge's word is accepted without inquiry; a just-ignorant judge's decision requires explanation; a sinful judge's word is not accepted unless the executor witnesses the cause of the ruling. This demonstrates the Hanafi concern for both competence (knowledge) and integrity (justice) in judicial orders.

The Dismissed Judge's Claim: When His Word Prevails
Ruling on the credibility of a dismissed judge's claim regarding past judicial acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a dismissed judge's claim is accepted and explain the role of zahir al-hal in his credibility.

Core Idea
A judge's word is accepted when both parties agree the act happened during his tenure, supported by the presumption that a judge does not rule unjustly.

In Hanafi jurisprudence, the credibility of a public official, such as a judge, is often supported by the principle of 'zahir al-hal' — the apparent state of affairs. This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Hidayah about a dismissed judge who claims he took money from a party as part of a judgment. The opposing party claims the action was unjust. The ruling hinges on the distinction between a claim of injustice and the acknowledgment that the judge acted in his official capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
Note that the judge (now dismissed) claims he took money or cut a hand as part of a lawful judgment during his tenure. The other party claims the action was unjust (zulm).
The judge is not claiming a new right; he is defending his past official act.
2
Determine the condition for acceptance
The judge's word is accepted only if both parties agree (yumuqirru) that the act occurred while the judge was in office. If the other party denies the act entirely or denies it happened during the judgeship, the ruling does not apply.
Mutual agreement on the temporal context is key.
3
Understand the legal presumption (zahir al-hal)
Since a judge is presumed not to rule unjustly, the apparent state (zahir al-hal) becomes a witness for the judge. This presumption tilts the balance in favor of the judge's statement.
This is a form of istihsan based on public policy and trust in the judiciary.
4
Recognize the consequence: no oath on the judge
Because the act is established by mutual agreement, no oath is required from the judge. The burden shifts to the claimant of injustice to prove his claim.
If the other party proves injustice, the judge's claim is rejected and he must compensate.
5
Apply the ruling to analogous cases
The same rule applies if the judge claims he ordered cutting a hand for a crime. As long as the affected person acknowledges the act happened during the judge's tenure, the judge's word prevails.
This consistency shows a general principle for dismissed judges.

The judge was dismissed, he said to a person: 'I took from you a thousand and gave it to so-and-so, in that state I had ruled a thousand dirhams against you.' He said: 'You took these dirhams unjustly.' Then the judge's word will be accepted. And similarly if he said: 'I ruled to cut your hand in the right of this.' This is when the one whose hand was cut and the one from whom the money was taken both acknowledge that he did that while he was a judge. The reason: when they both agreed that he did that in his qada, the apparent state is a witness for him, because the judge does not rule unjustly in the apparent, and there is no oath upon him because his action in his qada is proven by mutual agreement, and there is no oath on the judge.
Note: The scenario is: When a judge has been dismissed, he says to a person, e.g., Khalid: 'During my tenure as judge, I ruled a thousand dirhams for Hamid against you, and I took that thousand from you and gave it to Hamid.' Then Khalid says: 'You took that thousand from me unjustly and wrongfully, so it is obligatory to return them.' In this case, if this person, i.e., Khalid, cannot prove his claim of injustice through proof, then the judge's word is considered reliable. Similarly, if the judge said to someone: 'I ordered your hand to be cut in a matter of right against you,' and that person said: 'You caused my hand to be cut off wrongfully, so I claim compensation.' Then if the person whose hand was cut cannot prove his claim of injustice through proof, the judge's word is considered reliable. In both mentioned cases, the judge's word is considered reliable only when the person whose hand was cut and the person from whom the money was taken both acknowledge that the judge did the act of taking money and cutting the hand during his tenure as judge. The reason: The one from whom money was taken and the judge, or the one whose hand was cut and the judge, have agreed that the judge did this act during his qada. And since the apparent state is that the judge does not rule unjustly, therefore the apparent state becomes a witness for the judge. And when the apparent state is a witness for someone, his statement is considered reliable, so the judge's statement is considered reliable. Also, no oath is imposed on the judge, because his action during his qada is proven by mutual agreement of the judge and the person against whom the ruling was made; that is, by their agreement, it is established that the judge did this act.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on a specific case: a dismissed judge claims that he took money or cut a hand during his tenure as part of a ruling. The recipient claims the action was unjust. The ruling is that if both the judge and the recipient agree that the judge did the act while he was a judge, then the judge's word is accepted. The reasoning is that the apparent state (zahir al-hal) is that a judge does not rule unjustly, so this apparent state serves as a witness for the judge. Therefore, the judge's statement is considered reliable unless the other party proves otherwise. Also, no oath is required from the judge because the action is established by mutual agreement. The commentary clarifies that the judge's word is only accepted if the parties acknowledge the act occurred during the judgeship. The practical application: if the claimant (Khalid) cannot prove his claim of injustice, the judge's word stands.

The Word of a Dismissed Judge Regarding Past Acts
Ruling on accepting a dismissed judge's statement about past acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whose statement is accepted when a dismissed judge's past judicial act is disputed, and apply the three scenarios (act agreed to be during tenure, act claimed to be outside tenure, and liability of the executor).

Core Idea
A dismissed judge's word is accepted when he attributes an act to his tenure, unless the opponent proves otherwise.

When a judge is dismissed, disputes may arise about his past acts. The Hanafi school protects the integrity of judicial authority by presuming a judge acted lawfully during his tenure, unless clear evidence of injustice is presented. This lesson examines the procedural rules governing such claims, including the judge's immunity from oath and the extension of this presumption to his agents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
First, both parties agree the act occurred during the judge's tenure. Second, the affected party claims the act was before or after tenure. Third, the executor (hand-cutter, money-taker) confesses to the act under judicial order. For each, the ruling: judge's word accepted.
2
Analyze the primary reasoning
When both parties agree the act was during judgeship, the apparent state (zahir) that a judge does not rule unjustly becomes a witness for the judge. Hence, his statement prevails without oath.
The oath is not required because the judge is a trustee (amin) and the act is established by mutual acknowledgment.
3
Examine the analogy (qiyas) for disputed timing
If the affected party claims the act was before appointment or after dismissal, the judge's word is still accepted. This is analogous to a person who says 'I divorced while insane' if his insanity is known: he attributes the act to a state that negates liability.
The illa (effective cause) is attributing the act to a known state (madhukah) inconsistent with liability.
4
Understand the immunity of the executor
The one who carried out the judge's order (cut hand, took money) is also not liable, because he acted lawfully under a valid judicial command. This holds even if the transaction was done in the absence of the affected party.
This reinforces the principle that lawful authority protects the agent from liability (daman).

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Adab al-Qadi (The Conduct of the Judge) addresses a procedural question: when a judge is dismissed (azala), and the validity of an act he claims to have performed during his tenure is disputed, whose word is accepted? The text establishes several rulings. First, if a dismissed judge claims that during his judgeship he took money from a person and gave it to another as per a judgment, and the person from whom the money was taken claims the taking was unjust (zulman), the judge's statement is accepted, provided both parties agree that the act occurred while the judge was in office. The same applies if the judge claims he ordered a hand to be cut as a punishment. The reasoning (wajh) is that the apparent state (al-zahir) is that a judge does not rule with injustice; therefore, the apparent state serves as a witness (shahid) for the judge. Additionally, no oath (yamin) is required from the judge because the act is established by the mutual acknowledgment (tasadiq) of both parties. The absence of an oath is also justified by public policy: if judges were required to swear oaths, people would avoid accepting the position, leading to a suspension of people's rights. A second proof is that the judge is considered an amanah (trustee), and the oath is required from the opponent (khasm), not from the trustee. Second, the text rules that if the executor (the one who cut the hand or took the money under the judge's order) also confesses to the act as described by the judge, that executor is not liable (daman) for damages, because the act was done during the judge's tenure and the judge's delivery is valid (sahih). This holds whether the transaction was done in the presence or absence of the affected party. Third, an even more nuanced ruling: if the affected party (the one whose hand was cut or whose money was taken) claims that the judge's act occurred before his appointment (taqlid) or after his dismissal, the judge's statement is still accepted. The text cites the correct (sahih) position, drawing an analogy (qiyas): just as a person who says 'I divorced or freed while I was insane' is believed if insanity was known from him (because he attributes the act to a state inconsistent with liability), similarly the judge attributes his act to his official state which is consistent with lawful authority and inconsistent with liability. This qiyas shows that the judge's word prevails even when the timing is contested.

Key Terms
قاضي (qadi): A judge appointed to settle disputes according to Islamic law; in this text, specifically a former judge after dismissal.
عزل (azl): Dismissal from the office of judgeship; the judge is no longer empowered to hear or decide cases.
ظلم (zulm): Injustice, wrongful taking or harm; in this context, the claim that the judge acted unjustly in taking money or ordering amputation.
ضمان (daman): Legal liability to compensate for loss or damage; in this passage, the question of whether the judge or his agent is liable for acts done under judgment.
يمين (yamin): Oath; a formal statement attesting to the truth of a claim, typically administered to a party in a dispute.
حالة معهودة (hala ma'huda): A known or familiar state; in the analogy, the state of insanity or the state of being a judge.

Non-Liability of Executor Under Judge's Order

Core Idea
A judge's word is accepted when he attributes his action to his tenure, because that state negates liability—like insanity in divorce.

This passage addresses a dispute between a judge and a litigant over when a judicial action (cutting a hand or taking property) occurred. 1

2

The text states: 'because he attributed his action to a known state that negates liability' — this is the core reasoning. Then it cites the analogy to the case of a person who says 'I divorced/freed while I was insane,' with his insanity known.
The proof is a qiyas (analogy) based on a shared illa (effective cause): attribution to a state that nullifies the default consequence.
3

Asl (original case): a person claims to have divorced or freed while insane; his word is accepted and no legal effect follows. Far' (new case): a judge claims his action (cutting hand/taking property) occurred during his tenure; his word is accepted and he incurs no liability.
In both cases, the acting person attributes the act to a state that is incompatible with the normal legal consequence (talaq/itaq or daman).
4

The illa is the 'known state that negates liability' (halat ma'huda munafiya li al-daman). In the asl, the state is insanity (al-junun), which negates legal capacity for talaq/itaq. In the far', the state is judgeship (al-qada), which negates personal liability for official actions performed within tenure.
The illa is explicit: both states are 'known' (ma'hudah) to the public or at least established.
5
Note the condition for the ruling
The ruling is conditioned on the affected party having no evidence. If evidence existed, the judge's word would not be sufficient; the evidence would prevail.
This is implied by the principle that the denial of a claimant who lacks proof is accepted.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Adab al-Qadi explains the ruling when a judge claims that his action (like taking money or cutting a hand) occurred during his tenure as judge, but the affected parties claim it happened before or after. The text presents two subcases. In the first subcase, where only the judge makes the claim, his statement is accepted to deny liability, by analogy to a person who claims to have divorced or freed a slave while insane—since the state (insanity or judgeship) negates the effect, the denier's word is taken. In the second subcase, if the cutter or taker of money admits that the judge's order was given during his tenure, they become liable because they admit the cause of liability, while the judge's statement does not nullify their liability. The distinction is that in the first subcase, all parties mutually affirm the judge's timing, so the judge is exonerated; in the second, the cutter/taker's admission binds them, regardless of the judge's claim.

اقرار (iqrar): Admission or confession, which creates legal liability.
منکر (munkir): The party who denies a claim; their statement is taken as default if the plaintiff has no proof.

Return of Property When the Taker Admits Possession
Ruling when taker admits possession and property is present

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a taker admits receiving property from a judge and explain why the dismissed judge's lone testimony is not evidence for ownership claims.

Core Idea
A taker's admission of possession obligates return; the dismissed judge's word is not proof.

When a judge's decision is later contested, questions arise about the property he distributed. This passage clarifies a key rule: if the person who received the property (the taker) admits the judge gave it to him, the property must be returned to the original owner—regardless of whether the owner claims the judge acted within or outside his tenure. The dismissed judge's statement is not accepted as evidence for ownership, but it may be accepted to deny liability if the property was destroyed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Three parties: the taker (akhidh) who holds the property, the original owner (makhudh minhu al-mal) seeking return, and the dismissed judge (qadi ma'zul) whose action is contested.
2
Understand the owner's possible claims
The owner either confirms that the judge acted during his judgeship or claims he acted outside it. Both scenarios are covered.
The ruling is the same in both cases.
3
Apply the ruling
In both scenarios, the court takes the property from the taker and returns it to the owner.
Reason: the taker admitted the possession originally belonged to the owner.
4
Examine the legal reasoning (talil)
The taker's admission that the hand was for the owner creates a legal obligation to return. His own claim of ownership requires separate proof (hujjah), which he lacks.
The dismissed judge's statement does not constitute hujjah because he is a single witness.
5
Contrast with the destroyed property scenario
If the property was destroyed, the dismissed judge's statement is accepted to deny liability, following the principle that a denier’s word is taken.
This exception is mentioned in the commentary.

Ruling on the Testimony of a Removed Judge Regarding Property Taken During His Term
Ruling on removed qadi's testimony regarding property taken during his term

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a removed judge's testimony regarding property taken during his judgeship is accepted as proof, and explain the reasoning based on acknowledgment and the distinction between the judge as a witness versus a defendant.

Core Idea
A removed judge's testimony is rejected when property is present; accepted when he denies liability as a defendant.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Book of the Conduct of the Judge (Kitab al-Adab al-Qadi) concerning the evidentiary weight of a removed judge's statement about property he took during his tenure. The passage illustrates a key Hanafi principle: the word of a removed judge is not automatically acceptable; its value depends on the role he occupies (witness vs. defendant) and on the presence of stronger evidence such as acknowledgment by the taker. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying rules of evidence in judicial proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
The scenario involves three parties: the original owner (ma'khudh minhu al-mal), the taker (akhidh) who received the property from the judge, and the removed judge (qadi ma'zul). Determine each party's legal interest and whether they are acting as claimant, defendant, or witness.
The original owner is the claimant seeking return of property; the taker is in possession and may claim ownership; the removed judge is now a private individual.
2
Determine the status of the property
The passage considers two cases: property still in the taker's hands (present) or property that has been destroyed (talf). The ruling changes based on this.
When property is present, the taker's acknowledgment is key; when destroyed, the judge's denial of liability becomes relevant.
3
Apply the principle of acknowledgment (iqrar)
The taker's acknowledgment that the property originally belonged to the owner creates a presumption against his claim of ownership. His claim is not accepted without independent proof.
Iqrar is binding on the one who makes it; here it shifts the burden of proof.
4
Evaluate the removed judge's role
When property is present, the removed judge is a mere witness. A single witness's statement is not sufficient to counter the taker's acknowledgment. When property is destroyed, the judge becomes a defendant denying liability, and his word is accepted as a denier's statement (qaul al-munkar).
Hanafi usul: the statement of a defendant is taken prima facie unless there is evidence against him.
5
Derive the ruling and state the Hanafi position
If property is present and the taker acknowledges the judge's action, the property is taken from the taker regardless of the judge's testimony. The removed judge's word is not proof. If property is destroyed, the judge's statement that he acted properly is accepted, and he is not liable for compensation.
The mufta bihi is as stated in the text.

تشریح مسئلہ یہ ہے کہ اگر مال، آخذ مال کے پاس بعینہ موجود ہو اور اس کا اقرار کیا جس کا قاضی نے اقرار کیا ہے۔ یعنی قاضی نے کہا کہ میں نے فیصلہ کا حکم دے کر مال لیکر اس شخص کو دیا ہے اور اس نے کہا کہ ہاں قاضی نے میرے واسطے مال لینے کا حکم دے کر اس سے مال لیکر مجھ کو دیا ہے۔ اب ماخوذ منہ المال اس بارے میں قاضی کی تصدیق کرے کہ اس نے یہ کام بحالت قصا کیا ہے تو دونوں صورتوں میں آخذ سے مال لے لیا جائے گا۔ کیونکہ آخذ اس بات کا تو اقرار کر ہی چکا کہ قبضہ ماخوذ منہ المال کا تھا لیکن ساتھ ساتھ اپنے مالک ہونے کا دعویٰ بھی کرتا ہے۔ تو اس کا یہ دعویٰ بغیر حجت شرعیہ کے معتبر نہ ہوگا۔ اور معزول قاضی چونکہ ایک گواہ ہے اس لئے اس کا قول بھی حجت نہ ہو گا۔ اور آخذ کے پاس سے مال تلف ہونے کی صورت میں قاضی معزول کا قول اس لئے حجت تھا کہ قاضی اس کا تاوان واجب ہونے سے منکر ہے اور منکر کا قول معتبر ہوتا ہے۔
Book of the Conduct of the Judge. And if the property is present in the hand of the taker and he acknowledged what the judge acknowledged, and the one from whom the property was taken confirmed the judge that he did that during his judgeship, or claimed that he did it outside his judgeship, it will be taken from him because he acknowledged that the hand was from the original owner, so his claim of ownership is not believed without proof. And the statement of the removed judge is not proof in this matter. Translation: And if the property is present in the possession of the taker, and he has acknowledged what the judge acknowledged, and the one from whom the property was taken confirmed the judge in that the judge did this act during his tenure of judgment, or claimed that he did it outside the tenure of judgment, then the property will be taken from him. Because the taker has acknowledged that the possession belonged to the one from whom the property was taken, so his claim of ownership will not be believed except with evidence, and the statement of the removed judge is not proof in this matter. Explanation: The issue is that if the property is present with the taker and he has acknowledged what the judge acknowledged, i.e., the judge said, 'I gave a verdict and took the property and gave it to this person,' and he said, 'Yes, the judge ordered me to take the property and took it from him and gave it to me.' Now, if the one from whom the property was taken confirms the judge that he did this during his judgeship, in both cases the property will be taken from the taker. Because the taker has already acknowledged that the possession belonged to the one from whom the property was taken, but at the same time he also claims ownership. So his claim is not valid without a legal proof. And the removed judge, since he is a witness, his statement is also not proof. And in the case where the property is destroyed with the taker, the statement of the removed judge is proof because the judge is denying that he is liable for compensation, and the statement of the denier is considered valid.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of the Conduct of the Judge discusses the evidentiary value of a removed judge's testimony regarding property taken during his judgeship. The ruling: If the property is still in the hands of the person who took it (the taker/آخذ), and he acknowledges that the judge took it and gave it to him, and the original owner (ماخوذ منه المال) confirms the judge's action during his tenure, then the property is taken from the taker. The key point is the taker's acknowledgment that the original owner had possession; this undermines any claim of ownership by the taker unless he brings strong evidence. The removed judge's statement is not accepted as proof in this scenario because he is considered a single witness, and his word is not sufficient to counter the taker's acknowledgment. However, earlier (in a different ruling, implied here) if the property is destroyed in the taker's hands, the removed judge's statement is accepted because he is denying liability (as a defendant), and the denier's word is taken. The author follows Imam al-Marghinani's (author of al-Hidayah) reasoning, focusing on the principles of acknowledgment and the status of a witness.

Key Terms
قاضي (qadi): The judge who issued the original ruling and took the property.
معزول (ma'zul): A judge who has been removed from office.
آخذ (akhidh): The person who took possession of the property from the judge.
ماخوذ منه المال (ma'khudh minhu al-mal): The original owner from whom the property was taken.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi ruling on a removed judge's testimony hinges on the presence of the property: when present, his word is not proof due to the taker's stronger acknowledgment; when destroyed, his word is accepted as a defendant's denial.
