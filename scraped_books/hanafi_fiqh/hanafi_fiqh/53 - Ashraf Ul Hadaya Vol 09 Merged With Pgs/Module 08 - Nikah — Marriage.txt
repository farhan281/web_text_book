Constructive Possession Through Authorized Intercourse in a Pre-Possession Marriage
Ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a buyer marrying off a purchased slave girl before possession, explain the proof based on ownership and authorization, and distinguish between istihsan and qiyas in determining possession.

Core Idea
Marriage before possession is valid; authorized intercourse counts as constructive possession.

This lesson addresses a nuanced scenario where a buyer purchases a slave girl but does not yet take physical possession. Before doing so, the buyer marries her off, and the husband has intercourse. The Hanafi school holds the marriage valid and the intercourse as possession for the buyer, based on ownership and authorization. We'll explore the reasoning, contrast it with a strict analogy (qiyas), and see why other schools differ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Determine the legal act under discussion: a buyer marrying off a purchased slave girl before taking possession, followed by the husband's intercourse. The Hanafi ruling states the marriage is valid, the dower is due, and the intercourse constitutes possession (qabd) for the buyer.
The act is a combination of marriage (nikah) and subsequent intercourse.
2
Locate the Hanafi proof
The proof is based on the buyer's complete ownership (milk al-raqabah) which grants guardianship (wilayah) for marriage, and the husband's intercourse occurring with the buyer's authorization, making it as if the buyer did it himself.
Authorization (taslit) is key: the buyer 'authorized' the husband to have intercourse.
3
Understand the qiyas vs. istihsan reasoning
Strict qiyas would consider mere marriage (without intercourse) as possession because it is a constructive defect (ta'yib hukmi), analogous to a real defect. However, istihsan distinguishes: a real defect involves physical control, which establishes possession; a constructive defect does not. Therefore, without intercourse, no possession.
Istihsan overrides the qiyas in this case.
4
Note the inter-madhhab dispute
Imam Malik, Imam Shafi'i, and Imam Ahmad do not consider the husband's intercourse as possession; they likely require actual physical control. The Hanafi response is that constructive possession through authorization suffices.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is whether the buyer bears the risk and responsibility for defects after possession.
5
Apply the contrast with sale
The text notes that the buyer cannot sell the slave girl before possession because sale requires ability to deliver (qudrah 'ala al-taslim) and avoiding gharar, whereas marriage does not. This distinction reinforces the validity of marriage before possession.
Ability to deliver is a condition for sale but not for marriage.

He said: And whoever buys a slave girl and does not take possession of her until he marries her off, and then the husband has intercourse with her, the marriage is valid because the cause of guardianship exists, namely complete ownership of the person (raqabah). And the dower is obligatory upon the husband. And this [marriage and intercourse] is possession, because the husband's intercourse occurred by authorization from his [the buyer's] side, so his act is like his [the buyer's] act. And if he [the husband] does not have intercourse with her, then it is not possession. The analogy (qiyas) is that he [the buyer] would become possessor because it is a constructive defect (ta'yib hukmi), so it is considered like a real defect. The reason for istihsan is that in a real defect, there is control over the subject matter, and by that he becomes possessor; but not so in a constructive defect, so they differ. Translation: And if someone buys a slave girl and does not take possession of her until he marries her off, and the husband has intercourse with her, this marriage is valid because the cause of guardianship, i.e., complete ownership of the person, is found, and the dower is obligatory upon the husband. And this marriage and the husband's intercourse is possession. Because the husband's intercourse occurred by granting permission from the buyer's side, so the husband's act is like the buyer's act. And if the husband did not have intercourse with her, then merely the marriage is not possession. And the analogy is that (by mere marriage) the buyer would become possessor, because marriage is a constructive defect, so it would be analogized to a real defect. And the reason for istihsan is that in a real defect, there is control over the subject matter, and by that one becomes possessor; but constructive defect is not like that, so they differ. Explanation of the case: A man bought a slave girl but did not take possession of her, and before taking possession, the buyer married her off to a man, and the husband had intercourse with her. This marriage is valid, and the dower is obligatory upon the husband, and the husband's intercourse is counted as possession of the slave girl and the buyer's possession. However, according to Imam Malik, Imam Shafi'i, and Imam Ahmad, the husband's intercourse is not counted as the buyer's possession. Our proof for the validity of the marriage is that the cause of guardianship for the marriage of the slave girl for the master is ownership of the person, and here, due to the sale itself, the buyer has become the owner of the slave girl. So when the buyer becomes the owner of the slave girl through the contract of sale, he also obtains guardianship for her marriage. And when the buyer has guardianship for the marriage of the slave girl, then the marriage he performed is valid and sound. At this point, Allamah Ibn al-Ha'im has written an objection and its answer, which is presented for increase of knowledge. The objection is: Before taking possession of the slave girl, it is permissible for the buyer to marry her off, but it is not permissible to sell her. Why is this? The answer is: A sale is invalidated by uncertainty (gharar) and deception, but marriage is not invalidated by that. Therefore, the buyer's selling would not be permissible, but the marriage would be permissible. The second answer is: For the validity of a sale, it is a condition to have the ability to deliver the sold object, and this ability is obtained after possession. Therefore, before possession, due to the absence of ability to deliver (qudrah 'ala al-taslim), the sale would not be valid. And for the validity of marriage, since ability to deliver is not a condition, marriage before possession is valid. And the buyer's possession is established by the husband's intercourse, because the husband was authorized to have intercourse.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof (dalil aqli) and an inter-madhhab dispute. It discusses the case of a buyer who purchases a slave girl but does not take physical possession; before possession, he marries her off, and the husband has intercourse. The Hanafi ruling is that the marriage is valid, the husband pays the dower, and the husband's intercourse constitutes possession (qabd) for the buyer. The proof is that the buyer has complete ownership of the slave girl (milk al-raqabah), which gives him guardianship (wilayah) to marry her. The husband's intercourse occurs with the buyer's authorization, so it is attributed to the buyer, making him a possessor. The text contrasts this with mere marriage without intercourse: only marriage does not constitute possession. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference): real defects (like causing a physical damage) involve control over the subject matter, thus establishing possession; but a constructive defect (like marriage, which is a legal change) does not. The analogy (qiyas) would suggest that marriage alone should suffice, but istihsan distinguishes. The opposing view from Imam Malik, Imam Shafi'i, and Imam Ahmad holds that the husband's intercourse does not count as possession. The text further addresses an objection raised by Ibn al-Ha'im: why can the buyer marry the slave girl before possession but cannot sell her? The answer: sale requires ability to deliver (qudrah 'ala al-taslim) and avoiding gharar (uncertainty); marriage does not have these conditions. Thus, it is valid to marry but not to sell before possession.

Key Terms
قبض (qabḍ): Possession or taking physical control of the purchased item.
جارية (jāriyah): A female slave (concubine).
نكاح (nikāḥ): Marriage contract.
ولاية (wilāyah): Guardianship or authority to contract marriage.
تعييب حكمي (taʿyīb ḥukmī): Constructive defect; a legal change that does not involve physical alteration.
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference; a method of reasoning that departs from strict analogy for a more suitable ruling.
