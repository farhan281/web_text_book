Creditor Must Accept Debtor's Payment Before Transferee Pays
Question-answer on forcing the creditor to accept the debtor's prepayment in hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the creditor must accept payment from the original debtor before the transferee pays, and explain why the debtor is not considered a volunteer.

Core Idea
The debtor who pays before the transferee is not a volunteer because his payment prevents potential harm to himself.

In a hawala (transfer of debt), the original debtor (muhīl) transfers his debt to a third party (muhtāl ʿalayh). But if the debtor pays the creditor (muhtāl lahu) directly before the transferee pays, must the creditor accept? The Hanafi school answers yes. The reasoning relies on a careful distinction between a gratuitous volunteer and someone whose payment is motivated by self‑interest, even if the debt has technically moved. We will unpack the proof and see why the debtor's payment is not purely a gift.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the original debtor paying the creditor before the transferee pays. The ruling: the creditor is forced to accept (wajib upon the creditor to accept).
This is a practical question that arises after a hawala contract but before the transferee has fulfilled.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The text argues that if the debtor were a pure volunteer (mutabarriʿ), no one could force the creditor to accept. Since the creditor is forced, the debtor cannot be a volunteer. Therefore the debt must still be considered as resting on the debtor in some sense.
The proof is rational (ʿaqli), based on the definition of tabarruʿ.
3
Trace the wajh al‑dalālah
The key is the definition of mutabarriʿ: one who intends only to benefit another, not to remove harm from himself. The debtor's payment removes the potential harm of being pursued for the debt (e.g., imprisonment), so he is not a volunteer.
Even though at the moment of payment the debt has been transferred, the possibility of reversion (e.g., transferee defaults) makes the payment self‑protective.
4
Examine the counterargument and response
Objection: At the time of payment, the debtor is free of debt, so he should be a volunteer. Response: 'Volunteer' is defined by intention, not current obligation. The debtor pays to avoid future harm, so he is not a volunteer.
The text uses a deeper analysis of intention (niyya) to rebut the objection.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the situation where the original debtor (muhil) pays the debt directly to the creditor (muhtal lahu) before the transferee (muhtal alayh) pays. The text first poses a question: if the debtor pays, should the creditor be compelled to accept? The answer is yes. The proof is based on the nature of the debtor's payment: he is not a volunteer because the debt has not been fully discharged by the transfer; there is always a possibility that the transferee will default, causing the debt to revert. Since the debtor's payment removes the potential harm of being pursued for the debt, he is not a volunteer, and therefore the creditor must accept. This ruling underscores that the hawala does not completely absolve the debtor until the transferee actually pays; the debt remains contingent.

The Creditor's Right to Revert After Hawala: The Hanafi Conditional Absolution
Inter-madhhab dispute on hawala and right of recourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the creditor's right to revert after hawala and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position using the concepts of conditional absolution and faskh.

Core Idea
In Hanafi hawala, the debtor's absolution is conditional — if the debt is lost, the hawala is annulled and the creditor reverts.

This lesson addresses a critical ikhtilaf between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding hawala. After a valid hawala, the original debtor is ordinarily freed. But what happens if the transferee fails to pay — can the creditor go back to the debtor? The Hanafi school says yes, based on the principle that the purpose of hawala is safe collection. The Shafi'i school says no, because the debtor's absolution is absolute. The practical consequences are significant, and the Hanafi reasoning involves an interesting analogy to sale.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the basic Hanafi ruling: the creditor can revert to the debtor only if the right is lost (tawa). This loss includes the transferee denying the hawala with an oath or dying insolvent.
According to Abu Hanifa, tawa is limited to these two. The Sahibayn add a third: the judge declaring the transferee bankrupt.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the creditor cannot revert even if the right is lost, because the debtor's absolution (bara'ah) is absolute and unconditional.
Imam Ahmad is also reported to hold this view.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i proof: once the hawala is complete, the debtor is absolutely freed. The debt does not return unless a new cause arises (e.g., the debtor voluntarily assumes the debt again).
This is based on the general principle that absolution is irreversible.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: the absolution is not absolute; it is implicitly conditional upon the safety of the creditor's right, because that is the purpose of hawala. When the purpose fails (right lost), the hawala is annulled (faskh). This is like a sale where the item is destroyed before delivery: the contract is annulled and the price returns.
Some Hanafi scholars hold that the hawala rescinds automatically; others say it requires explicit annulment.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: For Hanafis, if the transferee defaults, the creditor can still claim from the original debtor. For Shafi'is, the creditor can only pursue the transferee and has no recourse against the debtor.
This has significant implications for risk allocation in debt transfers.

Defining Tawa (Loss) in Hawala: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditions of tawa in hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions of tawa according to Abu Hanifa, identify the third condition added by the Sahibayn, and explain the rational basis for the disagreement.

Core Idea
Tawa in hawala is realized by death insolvent or denial with oath (Abu Hanifa); the Sahibayn also accept a judge's ruling of insolvency.

This passage presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute over what exactly constitutes 'tawa' (loss) in a hawala (suretyship) contract. When a transferee (muhtal alayh) fails to pay the creditor, the creditor's right to revert to the original debtor depends on establishing tawa. The passage contrasts the stricter view of Abu Hanifa with the broader view of his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this disagreement is essential for correctly applying the hawala contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
What is the specific legal question being disputed? The text defines the conditions under which a creditor can revert to the original debtor due to the transferee's failure to pay.
The concept of tawa is the trigger for recourse.
2
Extract Abu Hanifa's two conditions
From the passage, list the two events that, according to Abu Hanifa, constitute tawa: (1) the transferee denies the hawala and takes an oath, with no evidence against him; (2) the transferee dies insolvent (no assets, no receivables, no guarantor).
The commentator elaborates that death insolvent means no property left and no one to recover from.
3
Extract the Sahibayn's third condition
The Sahibayn agree with the two conditions but add a third: a judge's ruling of the transferee's insolvency during his lifetime. This is the point of difference.
The judge's ruling is considered authoritative by the Sahibayn.
4
Analyze the underlying rationale
Abu Hanifa's objection: wealth is fluctuating ('comes in the morning and goes in the evening'); a judge's ruling does not permanently establish inability. The Sahibayn consider the ruling binding and thus sufficient.
This is a difference in the epistemological weight given to a judge's decision regarding a person's financial status.

Dispute on Tawiyah Through Judge's Declaration of Insolvency
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tawiyah via iflas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether a judge's iflas ruling constitutes tawiyah in hawalah, and explain the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
A judge's insolvency ruling may or may not end the creditor's right of recourse — the Sahibayn say yes, Imam Abu Hanifa says no, and the school follows the Sahibayn.

In the Hawalah contract, the creditor (muhtal lahu) transfers his right to claim from the original debtor (muheel) to a new debtor (muhtal alayh). If the new debtor becomes insolvent, can the creditor go back to the original debtor? This depends on whether 'tawiyah' (destruction of the right of recourse) has occurred. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on this point, which we will explore.

Guided Path
1
Understand tawiyah
Tawiyah means the creditor's right of recourse against the muheel is destroyed. The Sahibayn recognized two cases where tawiyah occurs: death or disappearance of the muhtal alayh.
These two are agreed upon.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn add a third case: when a judge declares the muhtal alayh insolvent (iflas) during his lifetime. They argue this constitutes tawiyah because the muhtal lahu cannot collect his right.
Their proof is the current inability.
3
Identify Imam's position
Imam Abu Hanifa disagrees. He argues that wealth can change; a person might become wealthy later, so the mere ruling does not destroy the right of recourse permanently.
Imam's proof: 'Wealth comes and goes.'
4
Analyze thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: According to Sahibayn, the muhtal lahu can have recourse against the muheel because tawiyah occurred. According to Imam, recourse is not available unless the muhtal alayh actually dies or disappears.
This determines the creditor's options.
5
Determine mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of the Sahibayn: a judge's iflas ruling does constitute tawiyah.
This is the operative view for fatwa.

Burden of Proof in Hawalah Disputes
Ruling on burden of proof in hawalah disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that the muheel's claim of a prior debt must be proven by evidence, and explain why the muhtal alayh's denial with an oath is accepted.

Core Idea
In hawalah, the denier's word is taken unless the claimant brings proof.

After a hawalah is executed and the muhtal alayh pays, he often seeks reimbursement from the muheel. A common dispute arises when the muheel claims the hawalah was actually to settle a debt the muhtal alayh owed him. How is this conflict resolved? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling, grounded in the universal principle of evidence and oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and Their Claims
First, identify who is claiming what. The muhtal alayh demands reimbursement (ruju'). The muheel responds that he transferred for a prior debt owed by the muhtal alayh. Thus, the muheel becomes the claimant (mudda'i) and the muhtal alayh becomes the denier (munkir).
2
Apply the General Principle of Evidence
Recall the hadith: 'Al-burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier.' The Hanafi school applies this directly. The muheel, as claimant, must produce evidence (baiyyinah); otherwise, his claim is rejected.
This principle is qat'i in thubut but applied via qiyas here.
3
Analyze the Cause of Recourse
The muhtal alayh's right to recourse is established because he paid the debt by the muheel's order. This cause (payment) exists regardless of the muheel's claim. Hence, the muheel is prima facie liable unless he proves his counter-claim.
The existence of the cause is not contested; only the muheel's excuse is.
4
Refute the Argument that Acceptance Implies Admission
One might argue that the muhtal alayh's acceptance of the hawalah is an admission of the prior debt. The text clarifies that hawalah can be done without any underlying debt (e.g., as a favor), so acceptance does not imply admission. This preserves the muhtal alayh's right to deny.
5
Conclude the Ruling
If the muheel has evidence, his claim is accepted and the muhtal alayh loses the right to recourse. If not, the muhtal alayh's denial with an oath is sufficient, and he can recover the amount paid from the muheel.
This ruling is from the zahir al-riwayah and all Hanafi imams agree.

The fact that the muhtal alayh accepted the hawalah does not imply admission of a prior debt, because hawalah can be done without an underlying debt (e.g., as a favor). This ruling protects the muhtal alayh from being forced to pay a debt he denies, while also ensuring the muheel can prove his claim if he has evidence.
