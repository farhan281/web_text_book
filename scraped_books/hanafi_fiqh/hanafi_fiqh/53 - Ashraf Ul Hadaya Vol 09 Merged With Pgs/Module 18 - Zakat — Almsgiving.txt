Scope of a Vow to Give All Wealth as Charity
Ruling on nazr of all wealth and comparison with wasiyyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a vow to give all wealth as sadaqah and explain why it is restricted to zakatable wealth using istihsan.

Core Idea
A vow to give all wealth as sadaqah applies only to zakatable wealth – the servant's obligation mirrors Allah's obligation.

This lesson explores the scope of a nazr (vow) to give all one's wealth in charity. The source text from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents the Hanafi position that such a vow only obligates charity from wealth on which zakat is obligatory, not from every type of property. This ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference), which overrides the apparent generality of the word 'wealth'. We will examine the reasoning, the intra-Hanafi dispute on 'ushri land, and the contrast with a will (wasiyyah), which applies to all types of property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a vow (nazr) where a person says 'my wealth is sadaqah'. The Hanafi ruling is that it becomes wajib to give sadaqah, but only from wealth that is zakatable (gold, silver, livestock, trade goods). Non-zakatable items like houses, household goods, and land (except 'ushri land per Abu Yusuf) are not included.
This ruling is specific to a vow made during life; a will (wasiyyah) is different.
2
Understand the primary evidence (istihsan)
The Hanafi evidence is based on istihsan: the servant's obligation by vow is measured against Allah's obligations. Since Allah has only obligated zakat on specific types of wealth, the servant's vow is understood to apply only to those same types. The word 'wealth' (mal) in the context of a living person is interpreted as surplus wealth (fadil mal), which is zakatable.
This is a departure from qiyas (which would apply the general word to all wealth) because of the context of the Shari'ah's obligations.
3
Trace the dispute with Zufar and Shafi'i
Imam Zufar (Hanafi) and Imam al-Shafi'i argue that the word 'wealth' is general ('amm) and includes all property, so the vow must cover all types. The Hanafi response is that a vow is an act of worship and cannot create an obligation where the Shari'ah did not; the generality is qualified by the context of a living person's needs.
Also, the hadith of Abu Lubaba about one-third is dismissed because it was a statement of repentance, not a vow.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference on 'ushri land
Abu Yusuf includes 'ushri (agricultural) land in the vow because he considers the aspect of charity (sadaqah) in 'ushr predominant. Muhammad excludes it because he sees the aspect of maintenance (mu'nah) as predominant. Both agree that kharaji land is excluded by consensus because it is purely maintenance.
The mufta bihi is often Abu Yusuf's view, but the difference is acknowledged.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
Consider a person who owns gold coins, a house, a car, and a piece of kharaji land. He vows 'my wealth is sadaqah'. According to the Hanafi ruling, he must give in charity the gold coins (if zakatable), but does not have to give the house, car, or kharaji land. If he also owns 'ushri land, then per Abu Yusuf it is included, per Muhammad it is not.
If he also makes a will of one-third, that will applies to all types of property.

As for the will, it is the sister of inheritance, meaning its counterpart. The will is a counterpart of inheritance, and inheritance is not restricted to one type of wealth but applies to all types. Similarly, a will is not restricted to one type of wealth but is executed on all types. The second evidence is that the apparent state of the person making the vow indicates that he intended to obligate charity from his surplus wealth, because a person has many essential needs in life. So it appears that he did not intend to obligate charity from wealth that is for basic needs, but rather from surplus wealth. Surplus wealth is zakatable wealth. Therefore, giving all wealth in charity [is not required].

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses the scope of a vow (nazr) where a person declares 'my wealth is charity for the poor.' The ruling is presented as follows: According to the Hanafi school, such a vow is limited to wealth on which zakat is obligatory (e.g., gold, silver, grazing livestock, trade goods) regardless of whether it reaches the nisab. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference). The passage contrasts this with a will (wasiyyah) of one-third of wealth, which applies to all types of property—zakatable or not—because a will is analogous to inheritance and is executed after death when the person is no longer in need. The text also explains the disagreement among the Hanafi imams: Imam Abu Yusuf includes 'ushri land (land subject to 'ushr tax) in the vow because he considers the aspect of charity predominant, while Imam Muhammad excludes it because he sees the aspect of maintenance as predominant. Both agree that kharaji land (land subject to kharaj tax) is excluded by consensus. The passage then addresses the objections of Imam Zufar and Imam Shafi'i, who argue for the general application of the word 'wealth' to all property. The Hanafi response relies on istihsan: a person's obligation by vow is analogized to Allah's obligations (i.e., the Shari'ah), so it only applies where the Shari'ah has obligated zakat. The hadith of Abu Lubaba is also discussed and dismissed because it was not a vow but a statement of repentance. The overall Hanafi position (mufta bihi) is that the vow applies only to zakatable wealth.

Key Terms
نذر (nazr): A vow by which a person obligates upon himself an act of worship (like charity) that is not originally obligatory.
وصية (wasiyyah): A bequest made by a person during life to take effect after death, up to one-third of the estate.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that deviates from qiyas (analogy) in favor of a more appropriate ruling based on stronger evidence or public interest.

Core Idea
Whether 'ushr is charity or mu'nat decides if land is included in a vow of charity.

This passage explores an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a vow (nathr) to give wealth in charity. The key question is whether 'ushri land—land subject to the 'ushr tithe—is considered zakatable wealth for purposes of the vow. The disagreement hinges on the nature of 'ushr: is it a form of charity (sadaqah) or a service charge (mu'nat)? Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (and Abu Hanifa) differ, leading to distinct legal outcomes. Understanding this issue illuminates how Hanafi jurists analyze the dual nature of levies.

Guided Path
1

2

There are two positions: Imam Abu Yusuf holds that 'ushri land is included in the nathr; Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Hanifa hold it is not included.

The practical difference is: if someone vows to give all his wealth in charity, according to Abu Yusuf he must also give his 'ushri land; according to Muhammad and Abu Hanifa he need not.
The disagreement does not affect kharaji land, which is unanimously excluded.

Core Idea
The vow 'whatever I own is charity' only obligates zakatable wealth surplus to need.

When someone vows to give whatever they own as charity, a key question arises: does this include all property or only zakatable wealth? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on the scope of the word 'milk', but the preferred view equates it with 'mal', restricting the obligation to zakatable wealth surplus to one's basic needs. Additionally, the passage provides guidelines on how much one may retain to meet immediate needs.

Identify the act and ruling

Some scholars argue that 'milk' is more general than 'mal' and thus the vow includes all property, even non-zakatable. The correct view (Sahibayn al-Hidayah) holds they are equal, limiting to zakatable wealth.

If the person has no other wealth besides the property covered by the vow, he may retain his basic sustenance (qawt) until he acquires other wealth, then he gives the retained amount in charity.
The amount retained varies: craftsman one day, produce owner one month, landowner one year, businessman until capital returns.
4

The proof from Quran (9:103) and Hadith uses the word 'amwal', which shari'ah restricts to zakatable wealth. No similar restriction exists for 'milk', but the preferred view applies the same restriction via the principle of surplus to need.
The dalil is cited to justify the restriction to zakatable wealth in the case of 'mal'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a vow using 'milk' versus 'mal', explain the dispute between the mashayikh and the Sahibayn, and apply the rule on retaining enough for basic needs according to income cycle.

Core Idea
When vowing 'what I own is charity,' the Sahibayn limit the obligation to zakatable wealth; need takes precedence over charity.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi dispute about the scope of a vow when someone says 'what I own is charity'. The choice of words—'milk' versus 'mal'—affects what must be given. Additionally, we learn the principle that a person's basic needs take precedence over charity, allowing the vow-maker to retain a portion based on his income cycle.

When vowing 'what I own is charity,' the Sahibayn restrict the obligation to zakatable wealth, and the vow-maker may retain enough for his basic needs according to his income cycle.
