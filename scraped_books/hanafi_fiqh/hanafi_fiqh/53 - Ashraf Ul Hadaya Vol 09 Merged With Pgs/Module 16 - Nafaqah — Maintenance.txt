Why Nafaqah of Children Cannot Be Analogized to Debts
Rational proof (dalil aqli) against analogizing nafaqah to debts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that nafaqah lapses with time and explain why analogizing it to other debts is invalid due to a difference in the effective cause.

Core Idea
Nafaqah falls away with time; debts do not—so they cannot share the same ruling via qiyas.

This passage from the Hanafi classic Ashraf al-Hidayah presents a crucial distinction between the maintenance (nafaqah) of children and ordinary debts. While both involve a financial obligation, their legal nature differs fundamentally: nafaqah is time-sensitive, extinguishing if not claimed in time, whereas debts persist regardless. This difference blocks any attempt to analogize nafaqah to debts using qiyas. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying rulings on child support and avoiding erroneous extensions of debt law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim
The author states that the nafaqah of children cannot be analogized to other debts. Recognize that this is a rejection of a potential qiyas (analogical reasoning).
2
Examine the Proof (Dalil Aqli)
The proof given is that nafaqah falls away (saaqit) with the passage of time, while other debts do not. This contrast in temporal durability is the key distinction.
The Arabic verb 'saqata' indicates the obligation drops; it is not merely unpaid but legally extinguished.
3
Understand the Rationale
Because nafaqah is due day by day as a fresh obligation for each period, it cannot be accumulated and claimed later. In contrast, a debt (dayn) is a single obligation that remains until satisfied.
Hanafi fiqh treats nafaqah as a series of instantaneous obligations, not a single deferred debt.
4
Draw the Conclusion
Since the two cases differ in the 'illah (effective cause) of how time affects them, qiyas is invalid. The ruling for debts cannot be transferred to nafaqah.
In usul al-fiqh, qiyas requires identical 'illah; here the 'illah differs because time extinguishes nafaqah but not debts.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah provides a rational proof (dalil aqli) for why the maintenance (nafaqah) of children is legally distinct from other debts. The author presents a second reason: nafaqah is a right that lapses (saaqit) with the passage of time; once a period of time has passed without payment, the obligation of nafaqah for that period is extinguished and cannot be claimed later. In contrast, ordinary debts (dayn) remain owed regardless of time lapse; they do not fall away simply because time passes. Therefore, the two are fundamentally different, and one cannot apply the same rules to both via analogy (qiyas). This ruling is grounded in Hanafi fiqh and emphasizes the unique nature of nafaqah as a right that is repeatedly due and contingent on the duration of need.

Key Terms
نفقہ (nafaqah): Maintenance, specifically for children in this context—a financial obligation to provide for their needs.
دیون (duyun): Debts—financial obligations that remain binding regardless of the passage of time.
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning in Islamic law; here, the attempt to extend the ruling of one case (other debts) to another (nafaqah).
