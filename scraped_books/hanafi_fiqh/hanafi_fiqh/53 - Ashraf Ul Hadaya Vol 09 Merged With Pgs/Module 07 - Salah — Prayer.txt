Five Agreed Conditions for Salam Contract
Listing and proving five necessary conditions for valid salam agreed by all Hanafi imams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five agreed conditions for a valid salam contract and explain their proof from hadith and rational reasoning.

Core Idea
Salam requires five conditions agreed by all Hanafi imams to prevent uncertainty.

Salam (forward sale) is a contract where payment is made in advance for goods to be delivered later. Because of its deferred nature, Islamic law imposes strict conditions to prevent ambiguity that could lead to disputes. This lesson presents the five conditions that all three senior Hanafi imams—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani—agree are essential for a salam contract to be valid. These conditions are backed by a hadith and a rational argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its peculiarity
Recognize that salam is a sale with forward delivery. Because the subject matter is not immediately present, the law demands extra clarity in its description.
Salam is a type of bay' (sale) but with specific rules due to deferred delivery.
2
List the five agreed conditions
From the passage, extract the five sharait: known genus (jins), known type (nau'), known description (sifah), known quantity (miqdar), and known term (ajal).
These are the conditions on which Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) are unanimous.
3
Understand the textual proof (dalil naqli)
The hadith 'Whoever makes salam, let him make salam with known measure, known weight, and to a known term' explicitly requires quantity and term; the other conditions are derived by implication (dalalah) because without them, the goods remain ambiguous.
The imperative 'فليسلم' indicates obligation in Hanafi usul.
4
Appreciate the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The text states that missing any condition leads to disputes, which breed enmity. Since the Shari'ah aims to prevent harm and conflict, these specifications become necessary.
This is an argument from sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking the means to harm).
5
Distinguish the five agreed conditions from the two disputed ones
Imam Abu Hanifa requires two additional conditions (making seven total), but those are not covered in this lesson. Focus on the five that are uncontested within the Hanafi school.
The disputed conditions are 'knowledge of the place of delivery' and 'indicating the actual market value at the time of contract' (according to some narrations).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hidayah, explaining the necessary conditions for a valid salam (forward sale) contract according to Imam Abu Hanifa. It first states that salam is valid only with seven conditions. The commentator then clarifies that five of these conditions are agreed upon by all Hanafi imams (Imam Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad). These five are: (1) known genus (jins) – specifying the general category like wheat or barley; (2) known type (nau') – specifying the subtype like irrigated or rain-fed; (3) known description (sifah) – specifying quality like good or inferior; (4) known quantity (miqdar) – specifying amount by a standard measure or weight; and (5) known term (ajal) – specifying a clear delivery time. The proof for these conditions is both textual and rational. The textual proof is the hadith: 'Whoever makes salam, let him do so with known measure, known weight, and to a known term.' Although the hadith explicitly mentions only quantity and term, the other conditions are derived by implication (bi al-dalalati). The rational proof is that without these specifications, disputes would arise, leading to enmity, which is detrimental to the contract.

Key Terms
جنس (jins): The general category of the subject matter, e.g., wheat, barley, rice.
نوع (nau'): The subtype within the genus, e.g., irrigated (saqiyya) or rain-fed (bakhtiyya).
صفة (sifah): The quality or description of the goods, e.g., good (jayyid) or inferior (radiyy).
مقدار (miqdar): The precise quantity by a known measure or weight, e.g., 10 kilos by market standard.
اجل (ajal): The fixed term or delivery date, which must be known and specified.

The Disputed Conditions in Salam: Capital and Delivery Place
Intra-madhhab dispute on conditions in salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding specification of capital quantity and delivery place in salam, and evaluate the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Even within a madhhab, conditions can be disputed—the Imam requires specification; the Sahibayn accept indication.

Salam is a forward sale with unique characteristics. Among its conditions, two are debated within the Hanafi school: the specification of the capital amount and the place of delivery. Imam Abu Hanifa requires explicit mention to avoid gharar, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider indication sufficient. This lesson unpacks their proofs and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disputed conditions
Pinpoint the two conditions that are disputed: (1) specification of the capital's quantity when it is measurable/weighable/countable, and (2) specification of the delivery place when the goods are heavy and have transport cost.
2
State the Imam's position and proof
Imam Abu Hanifa requires explicit specification. His proof: without quantity, counterfeit coins cannot be replaced in the session, and if the seller cannot deliver, the capital returned is unknown—leading to loss.
The uncertain (mawhum) is treated as certain due to the unique nature of salam.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
The Sahibayn do not require specification if the capital is specific by indication. Their proof: the objective is achieved by pointing, similar to price/hire and cloth where only description matters.
They analogize to cases where quantity is not the basis (e.g., cloth yards are merely descriptive).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and current fatwa
Practically, per the Imam, omitting quantity makes the contract invalid; per the Sahibayn, it remains valid. Later Hanafi fatwa generally follows the Imam to prevent disputes, though flexibility exists.
Example: a bag of dirhams—per Sahibayn, 'I sell you this bag' suffices; per Imam, the number must be stated.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding two additional conditions for salam: (1) whether the quantity of the capital (ra's al-mal) must be explicitly specified when it is measurable, weighable, or countable, and (2) whether the place of delivery (makan al-taslim) must be specified when the goods are heavy and involve transport cost. Imam Abu Hanifa requires specification in both cases to prevent uncertainty (gharar). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if the capital is specific by indication (e.g., pointing), specification of quantity is unnecessary, and delivery can be at the contract location. Their reasoning: indication suffices, similar to a cloth where only description matters. Imam's counter: unspecified quantity could lead to problems if coins are counterfeit or if the seller cannot procure the goods, requiring return of capital. The text then gives an example from furu': if salam is made in two genera without specifying capital for each, or in two genera without specifying quantity, the ruling follows the Imam's requirement. The passage concludes by noting that other schools (Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali) side with Imam on the first issue.

Key Terms
رأس المال (ra's al-mal): The capital or price paid in advance by the buyer in a salam contract.
مكان التسليم (makan al-taslim): The place where the muslam fihi (goods) are to be delivered.
صاحبین (Sahibayn): The two disciples of Imam Abu Hanifa: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Possession in Ordinary Sale When Buyer Orders Measuring into His Own Bags
Ruling on qabd in shira when buyer orders bagging into his own bags

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shira and salam regarding the validity of the buyer's order to the seller and determine when possession (qabd) is established.

Core Idea
In shira, the buyer owns the goods immediately, so his order is valid and possession occurs; in salam, the buyer does not own until qabd.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in ordinary sale (shira): the buyer orders the seller to measure purchased wheat and fill it into the buyer's own bags, in the buyer's absence. The Hanafi school rules that the buyer becomes possessor (qabid) because the buyer owns the wheat and the bags, and the seller acts as his agent. The passage then contrasts this with a salam contract, where the same order would be invalid because the buyer does not yet own the goods. Understanding this distinction clarifies the role of ownership in determining the validity of instructions and the establishment of possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? The buyer orders the seller to measure and put wheat into the buyer's bags. Ruling: In shira, the buyer becomes possessor; in salam, he does not.
2
Locate the dalil
The text uses the analogy of grinding wheat to illustrate the principle. The dalil is the reasoning (talil) that the order is valid when it meets the buyer's ownership.
The key phrase is 'الا ترى انه لو امره بالطحن'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The connection (wajh) is that the validity of the order depends on ownership. In shira, ownership is immediate; in salam, it is not until qabd.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The contrast between shira and salam is the ikhtilaf. The ruling differs based on the type of contract.
This is not an inter-madhhab dispute but a distinction within Hanafi fiqh.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: in shira, the seller's act suffices for qabd; in salam, the buyer cannot validly order such acts and does not become possessor.

Regarding the purchased wheat, the buyer said to the seller: 'Measure it and fill it into my bags.' The seller measured the wheat and put it into the bags in the buyer's absence. What is the ruling? And if the wheat was purchased (shira) and the case is as it is, he becomes possessor because the order is valid as it met his ownership, for he owns the specific item by sale. Do you not see that if he ordered him to grind it, the flour in salam belongs to the muslim ilayhi, and in shira to the buyer, due to the validity of the order. Similarly, if he ordered him to pour it into the sea, in salam it is destroyed from the wealth of the muslim ilayhi, and in shira from the wealth of the buyer, and the price is obligated upon him for what we said. For this reason, that measurement is deemed sufficient in shira in the correct view, because he is his deputy in measuring, and possession occurs by falling into the buyer's bags. Translation: And if the wheat was purchased, and the rest of the case is as it is, then the buyer becomes possessor. Therefore, the buyer's order became valid because the order met his ownership, for the buyer became owner of this property by sale. Do you not see that if he ordered him not to pour the wheat, in the case of salam the flour belongs to the muslim ilayhi, and in the case of purchase it belongs to the buyer, because the buyer's order is valid. Similarly, if the buyer ordered the seller to throw this property into the sea, in salam this property is wasted from the wealth of the muslim ilayhi, and in purchase it is destroyed from the buyer's wealth, and the price is obligated upon him for the reason we stated. For this reason, in purchase according to the correct saying, reliance is placed on that measurement, because the seller is the buyer's deputy in measuring, and upon filling into the buyer's bags, the buyer becomes possessor. Explanation of the issue: A person purchased specific wheat, and the buyer gave his bags to the seller and said: 'Measure it and fill it into my bags.' Then the seller measured the wheat and filled it into the buyer's bags while the buyer was not present. In this case, the buyer becomes possessor, and the seller is considered the deliverer of the sold item. The proof is that by the mere purchase, the buyer became the owner of the wheat. Hence, the buyer ordered his owned property to be placed into his owned bags. And since ordering one's owned property to be placed into one's owned bags is valid, therefore the buyer's order is also valid. And when the buyer's order is valid, the seller becomes the agent of the buyer for filling the wheat into the bags. And due to the bags being owned by the buyer, they are legally in the buyer's possession. Therefore, the wheat that is filled into them is also in the buyer's possession. Thus it is established that if the seller filled the wheat into the buyer's bags by the buyer's order, the buyer becomes possessor and the seller is discharged. An analogy: The rabb al-salam ordered the muslim ilayhi to grind the muslam fihi, i.e., wheat, and the muslim ilayhi ground it in accordance with the order, then the flour is for the muslim ilayhi, to the extent that if the rabb al-salam took the flour, it would be unlawful, because in that case, before possession of the muslam fihi (wheat), its exchange would be necessary. However, it is not permissible to take something else in exchange for the muslam fihi before possession. And if the wheat was purchased under an absolute sale (not salam), then the buyer ordered the seller to grind it, and the seller ground it accordingly, then the flour is for the buyer, because the buyer's order to grind is valid. So if in the case of salam the flour is destroyed, it is the loss of the muslim ilayhi's wealth, and the debt of the rabb al-salam that was obligatory upon him becomes correct, and it returns as it was. And if in the case of purchase the flour is destroyed, it is the loss of the buyer's wealth, and the price is obligated upon him. Similarly, if the rabb al-salam ordered the muslim ilayhi to throw the muslam fihi into the river, and the muslim ilayhi threw it, then this is the destruction of the muslim ilayhi's wealth, because in salam the order of the rabb al-salam is not valid. And if the buyer ordered the seller to do so, then it is the destruction of the buyer's wealth, and the price is obligated upon him, because the buyer's order is valid. Since the buyer's order to his seller is valid, therefore in the case of purchase, only the seller's measuring is sufficient—this is the correct saying, because the seller became the buyer's deputy in measuring, and by filling into the buyer's bags, the buyer becomes possessor. And since the deputy's act is in reality the act of the principal, the seller's measuring is in reality the buyer's measuring. And when the seller's measuring is in reality the buyer's measuring, then this objection does not arise: that the seller is both the deliverer and the receiver, because now the seller is the deliverer and the buyer is the receiver.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof from Ashraf al-Hidayah on the chapter of Salam. The passage addresses a scenario in an ordinary sale (shira): the buyer orders the seller to measure the purchased wheat and fill it into the buyer's own bags. The seller does so in the buyer's absence. The ruling is that the buyer becomes possessor (qabid). The proof is that the buyer owns the wheat by virtue of the sale, so his order is valid as it pertains to his property. The seller becomes his agent (wakil) for measuring and placing into the bags. Since the bags are owned by the buyer and considered in his legal possession, the wheat placed therein is also in his possession. The text then contrasts this with a salam contract (salam). If in salam the buyer orders the seller to grind the wheat into flour, the flour belongs to the seller (muslim ilayhi) and not the buyer, because the buyer's order is not valid over property he does not own. Similarly, if ordered to throw into the sea, the loss falls on the seller in salam, but on the buyer in shira. The text emphasizes that the measurement done by the seller suffices in shira because he acts as the buyer's deputy. An objection is anticipated: that the seller cannot be both deliverer and receiver. The response is that since the seller acts as the buyer's agent, his measuring is effectively the buyer's measuring, so the buyer is the receiver (mutasallim), not the seller.

Key Terms
قبض (qabd): Taking possession; legal control over an object. In this passage, qabd is established by placing the sold item into the buyer's owned bags, making him possessor.
سلم (salam): A forward contract where payment is made in advance for future delivery of specific goods (muslam fihi).
شرى (shira): Ordinary sale; a contract for immediate exchange of goods for price.

Mixing Salam Goods and Specific Goods in Bags
Intra-Hanafi analysis of mixing order in salam delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a seller mixes salam goods with a specific sold item in the buyer's bags, distinguish the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the qiyas that establishes constructive possession by mixing.

Core Idea
When seller mixes salam goods and specific goods, order matters: first specific gives possession; first debt gives rescission (Abu Hanifa) or option (Sahibayn).

This passage examines a nuanced scenario in salam contracts where the seller is instructed to fill both a debt (muslam fih) and a specific sold item (mabi') into the buyer's bags. The ruling hinges on the order of filling, and within the Hanafi school there is a disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions regarding the legal effect of mixing. By working through this case, you will understand how constructive possession (qabd) can be established through mixing with the buyer's property, and when mixing leads to rescission of the sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The buyer gives his bags to the seller and instructs him to fill both the specific sold item (mabi') and the debt (muslam fih) into them. The seller fills both in the buyer's absence.
Two items: a specific owned item (mabi') and a debt to be delivered (muslam fih).
2
Distinguish the two filling orders
First scenario: seller fills the specific item first, then the debt. Second scenario: seller fills the debt first, then the specific item.
The order is crucial because it determines possession and the validity of the sale.
3
Analyze the first scenario (specific item first)
In the first scenario, the buyer takes possession of both items. The specific item because the seller acts as his agent, and the debt because it mixes with the buyer's owned property, establishing constructive possession by qiyas on a loan of wheat sown in the borrower's land.
The qiyas is based on the illa: mixing the debt with the creditor's property transfers possession.
4
Analyze the second scenario (debt first) – Abu Hanifa's view
Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the sale of the specific item is rescinded (faskh) because the mixing before delivery constitutes destruction (istilak) of the specific item, as it becomes inseparable from the seller's property. The possession of the debt also fails.
The buyer's consent was conditional on a specific order that would give him possession. The disordered mixing violates that condition.
5
Compare the Sahibayn's view on the second scenario
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) give the buyer an option: either rescind the sale or make the seller a partner in the mixed item. They do not consider the mixing as destructive (istilak).
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Abu Hanifa, the sale is void; under Sahibayn, the buyer can choose to continue with a share.

So it is still the property of muslam ilayh, and when it is his property, how can the command of rabb al-salam be correct? And when the command of rabb al-salam is not correct, rabb al-salam will not be considered in possession. So in this case the sale will be rescinded, and when the sale is rescinded, the question of the buyer taking possession does not arise. There remains a question: The mixing of the sold item with the property of muslam ilayh (the muslam fih) occurred with the buyer's permission, so the sale should not be rescinded? The answer is that the buyer was not satisfied with mixing in this manner; rather, he was satisfied with mixing in a manner that would establish his possession, which is that muslam ilayh first fills the specific item into the bags and then fills the debt (muslam fih). Since the mixing did not accord with his consent, the sale is rescinded. And according to the Sahibayn, in this case the buyer has the option: if he wishes he may rescind the sale, and if he wishes he may make muslam ilayh a partner with him in the mixed item, because according to the Sahibayn, the mixing of the sold item (specific property) with the muslam fih (debt property) is not considered istilak (destruction).

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a salam contract where the seller (muslam ilayh) is instructed to fill two types of goods into the buyer's bags: the muslam fih (a debt) and a mabi' (specific sold item). The buyer gives the seller his bags and says: fill both. The seller does so in the buyer's absence. The ruling depends on the order of filling. First scenario: seller fills the specific item first, then the debt. Here, the buyer takes possession of both: the specific item because measurement and filling by the seller (as agent) is like the buyer's own action, and the debt because it mixes with the buyer's specific property, establishing possession by dharurah (necessity analogous to a loan of wheat sown in borrower's land or gold added to a ring). Second scenario: seller fills the debt first, then the specific item. Here, Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the sale of the specific item is rescinded because the mixed item cannot be separated, so it is considered destroyed before delivery. The buyer's possession of the debt also fails because the debt remains in the seller's liability. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) give the buyer an option: either rescind or share the mixed item with the seller, because they do not consider the mixing as destructive (istilak). The passage concludes with a rebuttal of a potential objection: the buyer's permission for mixing does not imply consent to this disordered sequence; he only consented to the order that leads to his possession.

Key Terms
رب السلم (Rabb al-Salam): The buyer in a salam contract, who pays the capital (ra's al-mal) and is owed the muslam fih.
مسلم الیہ (Muslam Ilayh): The seller in a salam contract, who receives the capital and owes the muslam fih.
مسلم فیہ (Muslam Fih): The object of the salam contract, which is a debt (dayn) to be delivered later; it is unspecified at the time of contract.
خلط (Khalt): Mixing of two properties in such a way that they cannot be distinguished or separated.
استبلاک (Istiblaak): The condition where an item is so mixed that it is effectively destroyed (or consumed) in the sense that it cannot be returned intact.

Iqalah after Slave Girl's Death in Salam Contract
Ruling on iqalah in salam after capital perish

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on iqalah in salam when the capital is destroyed and explain the reasoning based on the distinction between ra's al-mal and ma'qud alayh.

Core Idea
In salam, iqalah remains valid even if the capital perishes because the subject matter is the debt, not the price.

This lesson explores a nuanced ruling from Al-Hidayah on the rescission (iqalah) of a salam contract when the capital paid (a slave girl) is destroyed. The ruling demonstrates the Hanafi school's careful distinction between the price (thaman/ra's al-mal) and the subject matter (ma'qud alayh), a distinction that preserves contractual stability even after physical loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognise that the passage presents two cases: (1) iqalah occurs first, then the slave girl dies; (2) the slave girl dies first, then iqalah. Both lead to the same ruling: iqalah is valid.
The 'a fortiori' reasoning (ولى) is key: if valid after death, it is more surely valid when death occurs after iqalah.
2
Distinguish ra's al-mal from ma'qud alayh in salam
In salam, the price (ra's al-mal) is given immediately (here a slave girl), while the subject matter (ma'qud alayh) is a deferred debt of wheat. The passage emphasises that the contract's existence depends on the ma'qud alayh, not the physical capital.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh and critical for the ruling.
3
Apply the principle: iqalah rescinds the contract
Iqalah annuls the contract and requires each party to return what they received. If the capital cannot be returned due to destruction, its value is substituted.
Liability for the value is determined at the time of possession, not at the time of loss.
4
Determine the liability when return is impossible
Since the slave girl is dead and cannot be returned, the muslam ilayh must pay her value on the day he took possession. This transfers the risk of destruction after possession to the holder.
The value is assessed at the time of possession to fix liability.
5
Understand the a fortiori reasoning
If iqalah is allowed after the capital is already destroyed (ikhtiyaran), then surely it remains valid when the capital perishes after iqalah (idhtiraran), because continuity is easier than initiation.
This is a standard Hanafi usul argument: ما جاز ابتداء جاز بقاء بطريق أولى.

Appointing a Deputy for Jumu'ah and Judicial Authority
Expansion on deputyship rules for Jumu'ah and judiciary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Jumu'ah imam may appoint a deputy without sultan's permission while a judge may not, and apply the distinction to analogous cases.

Core Idea
The narrow time of Jumu'ah implies permission to delegate, but judiciary's open time requires explicit sultanic authorization.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a practical question: when an excuse occurs, can the Jumu'ah imam or a judge appoint a substitute? The answer turns on the nature of the act—whether its time is constrained. By examining scenarios and comparing with the law of agency, the Hanafi school derives a nuanced rule that balances authority and necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two offices and their appointment rules
Distinguish the person appointed for Jumu'ah (ma'mūr bi-iqāmati al-jumu'ah) from a judge (qāḍī). Note that both are appointed by the sultan but differ in delegation authority.
The Jumu'ah imam has implicit permission to delegate; the judge does not.
2
Analyze the three Jumu'ah scenarios and the rationale
Work through each scenario: excuse after sermon but before prayer, excuse before sermon, and excuse after prayer has started. For each, determine who delivers the sermon and leads the prayer. The key rationale is that the sermon is a condition for initiating Jumu'ah (shart iftitāh), not for the prayer itself.

3

This is based on the principle of necessity (ḍarūrah).

And when no specific time is fixed, then upon an excuse, the possibility of its being lost due to delay does not remain. Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses the rules concerning the appointment of a substitute (خلیفہ) for the imam who leads Jumu'ah prayer and compares this to the appointment of a deputy judge. The passage is a commentary expanding on the original text of al-Hidayah. It begins by examining three scenarios for Jumu'ah: (1) If the person appointed to lead Jumu'ah (مامور باإقامة الجمعہ) encounters an excuse after the sermon but before starting the prayer, he may appoint a deputy from among the congregation, and that deputy will lead the prayer from where the imam left off. The deputy does not need to have heard the sermon because the sermon is a condition for initiating Jumu'ah (شرط افتتاح), not for the prayer itself. (2) If the excuse occurs before the sermon, the deputy must deliver the sermon and lead the prayer. (3) If the excuse occurs after the prayer has started, the deputy continues the prayer without restarting, regardless of whether he heard the sermon. The key rationale is that Jumu'ah time is narrow (وقت تنگ), so there is no opportunity to seek permission from the sultan. Therefore, the imam is implicitly authorized to appoint a deputy. This contrasts with judicial proceedings (حکم قضاء), which have no fixed time, so a delay due to an excuse does not risk losing the matter. Hence, a judge cannot appoint a deputy without explicit permission from the sultan. The passage then cites the view of صاحب بدائع (likely Imam al-Kasani) that if a judge appoints a deputy without permission but later ratifies the deputy's ruling, it becomes valid, analogous to a lawyer (وکیل) delegating to another without the client's permission but later ratifying the action. Then, citing صاحب ہدایہ (Imam al-Marghinani), it explains that the validity (جواز) of the deputy's ruling depends on the original judge's approval, which supplies the required opinion (رائے). The original judge's ratification brings the ruling into effect. Next, the passage discusses the effect of explicit authorization from the sultan. If the sultan says "ولّ من شئت" (appoint whomever you wish), the judge may appoint a deputy, but that deputy is considered the sultan's appointee; hence, the original judge cannot dismiss him unless explicitly given that power (e.g., by saying "و استبدل من شئت"). Finally, the Chief Judge (قاضی القضاۃ) has full authority to appoint and dismiss subordinate judges by virtue of his office.

Key Terms
خلیفہ (khalīfah): A deputy appointed by the imam of Jumu'ah or by a judge to carry out the duties when an excuse arises.
مامور باإقامة الجمعہ (ma'mūr bi-iqāmati al-jumu'ah): The person appointed by the sultan to lead the Jumu'ah prayer.
قاضی القضاۃ (qādī al-quḍāh): The chief judge who has the authority to appoint and dismiss subordinate judges.
