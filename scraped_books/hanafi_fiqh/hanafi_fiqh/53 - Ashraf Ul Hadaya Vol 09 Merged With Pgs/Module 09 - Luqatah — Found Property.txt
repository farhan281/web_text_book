Ownership of Wild Animal Offspring on Another's Land
Ruling with proof on ownership of wild animal offspring

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that ownership of wild animals and their offspring requires active capture, not mere presence on owned land, and distinguish between mubah produce and land-dependent produce.

Core Idea
Wild animals on your land are not yours until you catch them — only intentional capture or land produce grants ownership.

A bird hatches chicks on your land. Do you own them? The Hanafi school answers: no, unless you prepared the land or actively captured them. This ruling is grounded in the nature of mubah (ownerless) property and a careful distinction between what is a product of the land and what merely passes through. In this lesson, we will unpack the proofs, analogies, and exceptions that shape this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
The passage begins by stating the ruling: if a bird hatches chicks, lays eggs, or a deer makes a lair on someone's land, those belong to the taker. Identify the subject (offspring of wild animals) and the ruling (ownership by capture).
This is the core legal proposition to be proved.
2
Locate the primary proofs
The text gives two proofs: (1) each is mubah (ownerless) and ownership is by prior hand (sabaqat yaduhu), and (2) it is game (sayd) and game belongs to the taker. Identify the key Arabic terms: مبا ح, سَبقَت يَدُه, صيد.
The proof also includes an implicit qiyas on eggs: eggs are the origin of game, hence same ruling.
3
Examine the qiyas and analogies
The text compares the situation to setting a net for drying, game entering a house, or scattered items falling into clothes. In each case, mere presence does not confer ownership unless there is active preparation or restraint. Identify the common illa: the landowner's land is not a tool of capture.
This qiyas reinforces that the landowner does not own by virtue of land alone.
4
Identify the exception (honey)
The text contrasts the above with bees making honey on the land. Honey is considered a produce (hasil) of the land, so the landowner owns it automatically. Note the distinction: honey depends on the land's vegetation, while wild animals are independent.
This exception shows the principle: only things that are direct produce of the land follow land ownership.
5
Apply the principle to new scenarios
Consider: a deer gives birth in your garden; a wild turkey lays eggs in your barn; a stray dog has puppies under your porch. In each case, apply the rule: you do not own unless you actively take possession or had prepared the area for that purpose.
Practice distinguishing between mere presence and active acquisition.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof about ownership of wild animals and their offspring when they appear on someone's property. It begins with a statement (likely from the matn) that if a bird hatches chicks on a man's land, the chicks belong to the one who takes them, not automatically to the landowner. The same applies to eggs laid by a bird and a deer making a lair. The reason given is that these are mubah (ownerless) things, and ownership is acquired by taking possession (sabaqat yaduhu ilayhi). Even if the animal is taken without trickery (bi ghayri hila), it is still game and the game belongs to the taker. Eggs are included as the origin of game; this is proven by the rule that a pilgrim (muhrim) must pay compensation (jaza) for breaking eggs, indicating they are considered game. The landowner does not become owner merely because these things occur on his land, since he did not prepare his land for that purpose (e.g., by setting traps). This is analogous to setting a net for drying clothes – the net does not confer ownership. Similarly, if game enters someone's house, or scattered sugar or coins fall into his clothes, he does not own them unless he actively secures them (by restraining) or had prepared his clothes as a receptacle. However, the rule is different for bees and their honey: if bees make honey on his land, the landowner owns the honey because it is considered a produce (hasil) of his land, like a tree that grows there or soil deposited by water. This is because honey is directly connected to the land's vegetation and is not simply an animal that passes by. The underlying principle is that ownership of land does not automatically confer ownership of wild animals on it; they must be intentionally captured. Only things that are directly a product of the land (like plants) are owned by virtue of land ownership. This ruling is consistent with the Hanafi position on mubahat (ownerless things).

Key Terms
مباح (mubah): Ownerless property that is permissible to take; wild animals fall under this category.
صيد (sayd): Game; wild animals that are lawful to hunt and take.
جزاء (jaza): Penalty or compensation due from a pilgrim (muhrim) for hunting or killing game, including breaking eggs.
تبعا لارضه (taba'an li-ardihi): As a consequence of land ownership; things that naturally grow or accumulate on the land become owned by virtue of land ownership.

Ownership of Birds' Eggs, Young, and Deer on Another's Land
Ruling on ownership of game on another's land with condition of preparation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ownership of game found on another's land and identify when the landowner's preparation creates ownership.

Core Idea
Game on another's land belongs to the first catcher unless the landowner prepared the land for it.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies a common scenario: wild birds nesting or deer residing on someone's property. The key is whether the landowner intentionally prepared the land for that purpose. Understanding the distinction between mubah (unowned) and acquired ownership through preparation is crucial in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Default ruling: mubah and ownership by first catcher
Identify that without preparation, the items are mubah (unowned). According to the hadith, game belongs to the one who catches it. So the first person to take possession becomes owner, regardless of location.
The hadith is a hadith ahad, zanni al-thubut, but qati al-dalalah.
2
Condition for landowner ownership: preparation (i'dad)
The passage states that if the landowner prepared his land specifically for birds to nest or deer to reside, then he owns those items. This is analogous to spreading a net for hunting versus for drying.
Preparation indicates intention and effort, which creates a stronger claim.
3
Proof from hadith and qiyas
The primary proof is the hadith 'al-sayd liman akhadhahu'. The text also uses qiyas: eggs are analogous to game because they are the origin; the penalty for a pilgrim breaking an egg confirms this.
The hadith is the direct textual evidence; qiyas supports the extension to eggs.
4
Analogies in the text
The author provides three analogies: (1) a net spread for drying vs. hunting, (2) a house with an animal that enters knowingly vs. unknowingly, (3) scattered items falling into a garment spread or not spread intentionally.
These analogies illustrate the principle that intention and preparation determine ownership.
5
Conclusion: two scenarios
Summarize: if land not prepared → catcher owns; if land prepared → landowner owns. This is the mufta bihi position.
The landowner's ownership is based on his preparation, not mere possession of the land.

Explanation of the scenario: If a bird hatches young or lays eggs on someone's land, or a deer makes a dwelling on someone's land, and the landowner did not prepare the land for that purpose, then the landowner is not the owner of those items. Rather, whoever catches them first becomes their owner. The proof is that these items are mubah (unowned), they have no owner. So whoever's hand reaches out first to catch them will be their owner. The second proof: the bird's young and the deer are game (shikar), and due to the saying of the Prophet ﷺ 'al-sayd liman akhadhahu' (game belongs to the one who catches it), game belongs to the one who catches it, even if caught without a trick. Not catching by trick does not remove it from being game. For example, if game breaks its leg on someone's land, it belongs to the catcher, not the landowner. As for the egg, its ruling is the same as game, because the egg, although not game in itself, is the origin and root of game. This is why if a pilgrim in ihram breaks an egg or roasts it, he is liable for a penalty just as a hunter would be. So it is known that the egg is also in the ruling of game. The expression of the author of Al-Hidayah: 'wa sahib al-ard lam yu'id ardahu li-dhalik' indicates that if the landowner has prepared his land for the purpose that birds will nest or lay eggs, or deer will live there, then in that case the catcher is not the owner of those items; rather, the landowner is their owner. An example: a person spread his net to dry. By chance an animal got caught in it. Whoever catches it first will be its owner. But if the net owner did not spread it to dry but spread it for hunting, then the animal caught in it belongs not to the catcher but to the net owner. Another example: an animal enters a person's house, and the house owner was unaware, but he closed the door. Then the animal does not belong to the house owner, but whoever catches it first gets it. If the animal entered and the house owner knew, then he closed the door and skylight, then the animal belongs to the house owner. A third example: if someone scattered sweets, dates, or dirhams, and they fell into a person's garment. If he did not spread his garment for that purpose and did not gather the garment after the fall, he is not the owner. Yes, if he spread his garment for that purpose, or he did not spread it for that purpose but gathered the garment after the dates or dirhams fell, then in both cases the garment owner is the owner. It is not permissible for another to take from it. Similarly, if the land is not prepared for birds' eggs, young, and deer, then the landowner is not their owner; whoever catches first becomes owner. If the land was prepared for them, then the landowner is the owner of those items.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding ownership of birds' eggs, young, and deer found on someone's land. The text explains that the default ruling is that such items are mubah (unowned) and belong to the first person to take possession, as they are considered game (sayd) according to the hadith "al-sayd liman akhadhahu". In that case, the landowner owns these items, similar to how if someone spreads a net for hunting, the catch belongs to him. The passage provides several analogies (from nets, houses, and scattered items) to illustrate the difference between intentional preparation and mere coincidence. The text also clarifies that eggs are treated like game in terms of ruling, as they are the origin of game. This is consistent with Hanafi fiqh where the intent and preparation create ownership rights.

Ownership of Honey Collected by Bees on Another's Land
Ruling on ownership of honey found on someone's land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on honey collected by bees on another's land and explain the qiyas used to derive it.

Core Idea
Honey on land belongs to the landowner because it is produce and subordinate (tabi') of the land.

In this passage, the author of Al-Hidayah addresses a specific case: bees collect honey on someone's land. The ruling is compared to the previous case of eggs and deer. The text uses a clear analogy to trees and soil deposits to explain why honey belongs to the landowner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under consideration is bees collecting honey on another person's land. The ruling is that the honey belongs to the landowner, not the finder or the bees' owner.
This differs from the previous case of eggs and deer, which are mobile and not considered produce.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof for this ruling is qiyas (analogy). The author draws an analogy between honey and two types of asl: trees that grow spontaneously on land and soil that accumulates due to water flow. Both become subordinate to the land and belong to its owner.
The asl cases are mentioned explicitly in the text.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The common illa (effective cause) is that honey is a produce/yield (ghallah) of the land that is attached to it. The text argues that honey requires specific food and location, so it is not found everywhere, thus it is attached to the specific land where it is collected.
This distinguishes honey from birds' eggs, which are not attached and can be moved.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other madhhabs may consider honey as ownerless like wild products, but the Hanafi school treats it as attached to the land due to its immobile and site-specific nature.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that the landowner owns the honey. This is consistent with the principle of taba'iyyah and is supported by the clear analogy.
The tarjih authority is Al-Hidayah as interpreted by Ashraf ul Hadaya.

As opposed to honey... The author of Al-Hidayah said: If bees have collected honey on someone's land, the landowner is the owner of that honey. Because honey is something that is counted among the produce and yields of the land. This is because honey is not produced everywhere, nor from every food; rather, it requires specific food and a specific place. So when bees have collected honey on a land, it becomes known that it is the produce of that land. And since honey is the produce of the land, it is subordinate to the land, and by being subordinate to the land, it enters the ownership of whoever owns the land. Just as a tree that grows on land without being planted is subordinate to the land and belongs to the landowner, and just as if soil accumulates on someone's land due to water flow, it is considered subordinate to the land and belongs to the landowner. Similarly, honey is subordinate to the land and is considered to belong to the landowner.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling that honey found on someone's land belongs to the landowner, distinguishing it from the previous case of eggs and deer. The rationale is that honey is considered produce (ghallah) of the land because it requires specific conditions (flowers, location) and is therefore akin to vegetation or soil deposits. The author draws an analogy (qiyas) to trees that grow spontaneously on land and to soil that accumulates due to water flow—both become part of the land and belong to its owner. This is a clear application of the Hanafi principle that anything that attaches to or is produced by land is subordinate to it (taba'iyyah). Unlike birds' eggs which are mobile and not rooted, honey is immobile and therefore follows the land.

Key Terms
پیداوار (paidawar): Produce or yield of the land, including crops, trees, and honey.
تابع (tabi'): Subordinate: something that follows the ruling of the land due to attachment.
