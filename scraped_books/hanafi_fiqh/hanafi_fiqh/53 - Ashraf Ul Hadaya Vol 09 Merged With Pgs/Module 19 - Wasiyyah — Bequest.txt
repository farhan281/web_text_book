Validity of Sale by Uninformed Executor vs Agent
Intra-Hanafi dispute on knowledge requirement for executor vs agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi majority (Zahir al-Riwayah) holds an executor's sale valid without knowledge of appointment, while an agent's sale requires knowledge, and evaluate the reasoning of Imam Abu Yusuf who analogizes the two.

Core Idea
Executorship is succession (khilafah), not deputyship; knowledge is not required.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of a sale conducted by an executor (wasi) who was unaware of his appointment. The Zahir al-Riwayah position upholds the sale, while Imam Abu Yusuf (in Nawadir) rejects it, analogizing to agency. The core distinction hinges on whether executorship is a succession (khilafah) or a deputyship (niyabah), which determines the need for the representative's knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Note the ruling of Zahir al-Riwayah: executor's sale valid without knowledge; agent's sale invalid without knowledge. Then note Abu Yusuf's view: executor's sale also invalid without knowledge.
The text explicitly contrasts the two cases.
2
Understand Abu Yusuf's analogy
Abu Yusuf uses qiyas: executorship is deputyship after death, so it should be treated like agency (deputyship before death); both require knowledge.
Qiyas is the basis for his objection.
3
Recognize the Zahir al-Riwayah distinction
Executorship is attributed to a time when deputyship is invalid (after death), making it a succession (khilafah), not deputyship. Succession does not require knowledge, similar to an heir's transaction.
The key difference: khilafah vs niyabah.
4
Apply the distinction to the rulings
Apply the distinction: executor's transaction valid without knowledge (succession); agent's transaction invalid without knowledge (deputyship). Understand why knowledge is required in agency: the principal is capable, so no interest is lost.
In executorship, interest would be lost because the deceased cannot act.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The Zahir al-Riwayah position (validity of executor's sale) is the relied-upon view for fatwa, despite Abu Yusuf's objection.
Tarjih: Abu Yusuf's view is considered marjuh in the school.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of a sale by an executor (wasi) who was unaware of his appointment. It begins by stating the ruling of Zahir al-Riwayah: the executor's sale is valid even if he had no knowledge of his executorship, whereas an agent's (wakil) sale requires knowledge of the agency. Then it mentions Imam Abu Yusuf's view from Nawadir that the executor's sale is also invalid without knowledge. The text then provides the reasoning: Abu Yusuf analogizes executorship to agency because both are forms of deputyship (niyabah). However, the majority (Zahir al-Riwayah) distinguishes executorship as a succession (khilafah) rather than deputyship, because it takes effect after death when the testator cannot act. Succession does not require the successor's knowledge, similar to an heir who automatically inherits and may transact without knowing of the death. Agency, on the other hand, is a deputyship where the principal is alive and capable; requiring knowledge protects the principal's interest without loss. The text also adds that if someone is merely informed of agency by others, that suffices for valid transactions because it establishes the right without imposing an obligation. The explanation is cut off but reinforces that knowledge is a condition for agency.

Key Terms
وصی (wasi): An executor appointed to manage the deceased's estate after death.
وکیل (wakil): An agent appointed to act on behalf of a living principal.
خلافت (khilafah): Succession that takes effect after the original authority ceases, not requiring the successor's knowledge.
نیابت (niyabah): Deputyship where the principal's authority continues, requiring the deputy's knowledge.

Executorship vs. Agency: Knowledge of Appointment Required?
Distinguishing wasiyyah from wakalah regarding the condition of knowledge for validity of acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school (Zahir al-Riwayah) validates a wasi's transaction without knowledge while requiring knowledge for a wakil, and identify the mufta bihi position in the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
A wasi is a successor (khalifah), not a deputy; successorship does not require knowledge, deputyship does.

This lesson examines an important intra-Hanafi dispute: does an executor (wasi) need to know of his appointment before his actions take legal effect? The Zahir al-Riwayah distinguishes executorship from agency based on the nature of the relationship—successorship versus deputyship. Understanding this distinction is key to applying Hanafi rulings in estate management.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios and the Zahir al-Riwayah ruling
Read the passage and note the two cases: a wasi who sells without knowing and a wakil who sells without knowing. The ruling is clear: wasi's sale valid, wakil's sale invalid. This is the relied-upon transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah).
The Zahir al-Riwayah is the authoritative corpus from Imam Muhammad.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi disagreement
The passage mentions that in Nawadir (uncommon transmissions), Imam Abu Yusuf holds that the wasi's sale is invalid without knowledge, analogizing to the wakil. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
Nawadir are less authoritative than Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Understand the Hanafi foundation: khilafah vs niyabah
The passage explains that the wasi is a khalifah (successor) like an heir, not a na'ib (deputy). Khilafah is attached to a time when the testator has no authority (after death), while niyabah is during life when the principal can act.
This distinction is the core illa (effective cause) for the different rulings.
4
Apply the distinction to the ruling
Since khilafah does not depend on the successor's knowledge, the wasi's transaction is valid without knowledge. For niyabah, knowledge is required because the principal could act himself; if the deputy doesn't know, the principal's interest could be harmed.
The passage emphasizes maslahah (public interest) as a rationale.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its rationale
The Zahir al-Riwayah (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is the operative position for fatwa. The tarjih is based on the weight of the core transmissions and the logical distinction between successorship and deputyship.
Imam Abu Yusuf's view, though respected, is not relied upon here due to the strength of the Zahir al-Riwayah.

ہونا اس بات پر موقوف نہيں ہے كه خليفہ كو اپنے خليفہ ہونے كا علم بھی ہو۔ پس جب خليفہ ہونا اس كے جانے پر موقوف نہيں ہے تو وصى نے اگر اپنے وصى ہونے كو جانے بغير بيچ وغيره كا كوئى تصرف كيا تو يہ تصرف جائز ہوگا۔ جيسا كه اگر وارث نے مورث كے مرنے كے بعد اس كے ترکہ ميں كوئى تصرف كيا مثلاً كوئى چيز فروخت كر دى حالانكہ اس كو مورث كى موت كا علم نہيں ہے تو وارث كا يہ تصرف جائز ہے پس اسی طرح بغير اپنے وصى ہونے كو جانے بغير وصى كا تصرف بھی جائز ہے۔ اور رہا وکيل ہونا تو وہ اپنے مؤكل كا نائب ہوتا ہے۔ كيونكہ وکيل جس كا نائب ہے يعنى موكل وہ بقيد حيات اور صاحب اختيار موجود ہے۔ بہرحال وکالت، انابت يعنى نائب كے ہونا اگر اس كے علم پر موقوف ہو تو مصلحت فوت ہو جائے گى۔ كيونكہ موصى (ميت) بذات خود تصرف سے عاجز ہے۔ اس لئے وکالت كو وکيل كے علم پر موقوف ركھا گيا اور وصاليہ كو وصى كے علم پر موقوف نہيں ركھا گيا۔
Clarification of the ruling: If a person is appointed executor (wasi) and he did not know he was executor, then after the testator's death he sold something from the estate, he is executor and his sale is valid. And if someone appointed an agent (wakil) and he did not know he was agent, then he sold something of the principal's, his sale is not valid. This is the Zahir al-Riwayah. And in Nawadir, it is narrated from Imam Abu Yusuf that in the case of executorship also, the executor's sale before knowledge is not valid. Imam Abu Yusuf analogized executorship to agency, because just as being an executor is being a deputy after death, so being an agent is being a deputy during life. So just as an agent's transaction before knowledge of agency is invalid, similarly an executor's transaction before knowledge of executorship would be invalid. According to Zahir al-Riwayah, the difference between executorship and agency is that being an executor is khilafah (successorship) not niyabah (deputyship). And it is khilafah because executorship is attached to a time when appointing a deputy is invalid—i.e., executorship takes effect after the testator's death, while after death the deceased has absolutely no authority. So it is clear that executorship is khilafah, not niyabah, and khilafah does not depend on knowledge for transactions. That is, being a successor does not depend on the successor knowing he is successor. So if the executor transacted without knowing, it is valid. Just as if an heir transacted with the estate after the death without knowing of the death, the heir's transaction is valid. Similarly, the executor's transaction without knowledge is valid. As for agency, it is deputyship because the principal is alive and has authority. In any case, for the deputy to transact, it is necessary that he know he is deputy. Because if agency depends on the agent's knowledge, no interest is lost since the principal can transact himself. But if executorship depended on the executor's knowledge, interest would be lost because the testator is incapable. Therefore, agency is made dependent on the agent's knowledge, but executorship is not made dependent on the executor's knowledge.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the distinction between executorship (wasiyyah) and agency (wakalah) regarding the requirement of knowledge. The Zahir al-Riwayah (the relied-upon transmission from Imam Muhammad) states that for a wasi, knowledge of appointment is not a condition for the validity of his transactions after the testator's death. If he acts without knowing, his sale is valid. For a wakil, however, knowledge is required because the principal is alive and can act himself. Imam Abu Yusuf, as reported in Nawadir (uncommon transmissions), disagrees and analogizes wasi to wakil, requiring knowledge for both. The author explains the rationale: wasi is a khalifah (successor) like an heir, not a na'ib (deputy). Successorship does not require the successor's awareness because the testator is dead and cannot act. Deputyship does require awareness because the principal can act. This is a nuanced intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, with the Zahir al-Riwayah (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) being the mufta bihi position.

Key Terms
وصي (wasi): A person appointed by a testator to manage the estate after death; executor.
وكيل (wakil): A person authorized by another (principal) to act on his behalf during the principal's lifetime; agent.
خلافت (khilafah): Successorship; one who steps into the legal position of another after the other's death or loss of capacity.
نيابت (niyabah): Deputyship; acting on behalf of a living principal who retains authority.
ظاہر الروايہ (Zahir al-Riwayah): The well-known transmitted rulings from Imam Muhammad's six major works; the primary authoritative source in the Hanafi school.
نوادر (Nawadir): Rare or less widely transmitted rulings; often from Imam Abu Yusuf or others, not in the core six books.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, an executor (wasi) is a successor, not a deputy; therefore, knowledge of appointment is not required for the validity of his transactions.
