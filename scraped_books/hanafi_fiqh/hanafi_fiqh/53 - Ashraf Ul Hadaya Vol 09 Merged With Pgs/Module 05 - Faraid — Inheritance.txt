Trustee's Conflicting Heirship Claims: First Delivery Upheld
Ruling on trust funds when two claims of sonship arise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a trustee makes conflicting claims of heirship after delivering trust property, and explain why the first delivery is upheld.

Core Idea
Once a trustee delivers trust property, a later inconsistent claim does not overturn the first delivery.

This lesson examines a scenario where a trustee initially identifies a sole heir and hands over trust property, but later claims there is another heir. The first heir denies this. The Hanafi ruling provides clarity on who keeps the property, emphasizing the finality of the trustee's initial action. Understanding this helps you grasp how Hanafi law balances trust, authority, and finality in property disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial scenario
The trustee holds 4000 dirhams as a trust. He declares one person to be the sole son and heir, and delivers the property to him.
2
Identify the later claim
The trustee later claims another person is also a son. The first son denies this.
3
State the ruling
The Hanafi ruling: the property remains with the first son; the second claimant cannot take it.
The decision is based on the trustee's initial action.
4
Understand the rationale
The trustee's word is trusted when he acts. A later inconsistent claim without proof does not overturn the already valid distribution.
This protects finality and trust in the trustee's actions.

Credibility of Conversion Claim in Inheritance
Intra-Hanafi dispute on credibility of conversion claim in inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle of taḥkīm al-ḥāl in inheritance disputes and contrast Zufar's attribution to the nearest time.

Core Idea
The present state is used to bar inheritance; Zufar attributes a recent event to the nearest time.

A Christian husband dies, and his widow claims she converted after his death, while the heirs claim she converted before. Whose statement is accepted? The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) holds the heirs' claim credible, applying the present state (she is Muslim) to the past moment of death. Imam Zufar disagrees, arguing that the recent conversion should be attributed to the nearest time – after death – so she inherits. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind each view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and default inheritance
Start by recognizing the baseline: a Christian wife inherits from her Christian husband if she remains Christian at his death. Conversion to Islam either before or after death changes the inheritance outcome due to difference of religion.
Hanafi rule: a Muslim does not inherit from a non-Muslim and vice versa at the time of death.
2
State the conflicting claims
The widow says she converted after death (so she was Christian at death, thus inherits). The heirs say she converted before death (so she was Muslim at death, thus excluded). The dispute is procedural: whose statement is believed?
3
Analyze the Hanafi principle (taḥkīm al-ḥāl)
The majority Hanafi view: the cause of deprivation (being Muslim at death) is established in the present (she is Muslim now), so it is presumed to have existed at the time of death. This is like the water mill analogy: if water flows now, it is presumed to have been flowing earlier.
This principle is used defensively (dafʿ) to prevent inheritance, not to establish entitlement.
4
Contrast Zufar's attribution to the nearest time
Imam Zufar argues that Islam is a recent event (ḥādith), so it should be attributed to the nearest possible time – after death – unless proven otherwise. Thus she was not Muslim at death and inherits.
Zufar's method is also a form of presumption, but it gives weight to the novelty of the event.
5
Determine the practical outcome
Under the majority Hanafi position, the heirs' statement is accepted, and the widow does not inherit unless she provides evidence that she converted after death. Under Zufar's view, she inherits unless the heirs prove the conversion was before death.
The burden of proof shifts depending on the legal presumption applied.

Istishab al-Hal as Defensive Proof in Inheritance Disputes
Explains Hanafi position on istishab al-hal in inheritance cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on istishab al-hal as a defensive proof and apply it to inheritance disputes to determine the validity of the wife's claim.

Core Idea
Istishab al-hal is a defensive proof: it preserves the status quo but does not establish new rights.

This lesson explores the Hanafi perspective on istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) through a classic inheritance dispute involving a non-Muslim spouse who converts after the husband's death. You will learn why the heirs' statement is accepted over the wife's, and how this reveals the school's distinction between defensive and positive proofs. The passage also introduces the opposing views of Imam Zufar and Imam Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Define istishab al-hal and its two directions
Understand the definition: ruling on the existence of something at one time based on its existence at another. Note the two forms: past-to-present and present-to-past.
The watermill example illustrates present-to-past continuity.
2
Identify the two inheritance cases
The text presents two cases: (1) Christian husband dies, wife converts after death, heirs claim earlier conversion; (2) Muslim husband dies, Christian wife converts after death, she claims conversion before death, heirs deny.
Both involve the question of when conversion occurred.
3
State the Hanafi position and the opposing view
Hanafi: heirs' statement is accepted; wife does not inherit. Imam Zufar and Imam Shafi'i: wife's statement accepted in the first case.
The dispute hinges on the nature of istishab al-hal.
4
Analyze the respective proofs
Hanafi proof: current deprivation of inheritance (due to difference of religion) is presumed to have existed at time of death (istishab). Zufar's proof: conversion is a new event attributed to the nearest time (after death), so at death she was Christian, unity of religion allows inheritance.
Note the different applications of istishab and the principle of attribution.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
Practical consequence: under Hanafi law, wife does not inherit; under Zufar/Shafi'i, she inherits in the first case. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi.
The tarjih is based on the principle that istishab is not a positive proof.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the Hanafi position on istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) in the context of inheritance disputes involving a non-Muslim spouse who converts after the husband's death. The passage type is an inter-madhhab dispute, primarily between the Hanafi school and Imam Zufar (with Imam Shafi'i agreeing with Zufar).

The author first defines istishab al-hal: it is a presumption that a state of affairs continues from one time to another unless there is evidence of change. There are two directions: from past to present (e.g., a missing person presumed alive) and from present to past (e.g., watermill flow presumed continuous). The Hanafi position is that istishab al-hal is only a defensive proof (hujjah dafi'ah): it can be used to repel a claim or preserve the status quo, but not to establish a new right (hujjah muthbitah). Imam Zufar and Imam Shafi'i consider it a positive proof.

The author presents two cases:

1. A Christian man dies, and his wife converts after his death. She claims she converted after his death, so at death both were Christian, and she should inherit. The heirs claim she converted before his death, so difference of religion bars inheritance. The Hanafi ruling: the heirs' statement is accepted, and the wife does not inherit. The reasoning: istishab al-hal—currently the wife is excluded from inheritance (due to the heirs' claim of difference of religion), so at the time of death she was also excluded. This supports the heirs' defense. Imam Zufar and Shafi'i argue that the wife's conversion is a new event (hadith) attributed to the nearest time (after death), so she should inherit.

2. A Muslim man dies with a Christian wife. She converts after his death and claims she converted before his death (so at death she was Muslim, unity of religion). The heirs claim she converted after death (difference of religion). Again, the heirs' statement is accepted. The Hanafi reasoning: the wife cannot use istishab al-hal to establish her conversion before death because that would make istishab a positive proof, which Hanafis reject. However, the heirs can use istishab to argue that since she was Christian before death (based on her past state), she remained Christian after death, so at death she was Christian, barring inheritance.

The author emphasizes that the apparent state (zahir al-hal) is not sufficient to establish entitlement; it only suffices for repulsion. The heirs are the repellers (dafi'un), so istishab works for them. The example of the watermill lease illustrates the principle: the current state of water flow determines who has the burden of proof, but it does not create a right—it merely defends the existing claim.

In summary, this passage teaches a key Hanafi usul principle: istishab al-hal preserves the status quo but does not create new obligations or rights. The practical consequence in inheritance law is that a non-Muslim spouse who converts after the husband's death does not inherit from a Muslim husband, and a Christian wife converting after death cannot claim inheritance from a Christian husband if she cannot prove conversion before death. The Hanafi position is relied upon (mufta bihi) in the school.

Key Terms
استصحاب حال (istishab al-hal): Presumption that a state of affairs continues from one time to another unless proven otherwise. In this text, it is applied to present-to-past and past-to-present continuity.
حجت دافعہ (hujjah dafi'ah): A proof used to repel a claim or defend the status quo, not to affirm a new right.
حجت مثبتہ (hujjah muthbitah): A proof that establishes a new legal right or obligation.

This lesson examines a nuanced inheritance case where a Christian wife claims she converted to Islam before her husband's death. The Hanafi school uses two rational proofs—istishab al-hal and attribution of new events to the nearest time—to reject her claim. Understanding why istishab cannot establish entitlement is key to grasping Hanafi usul al-fiqh on this point.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and parties
Identify the case: A Muslim man dies leaving a Christian wife. She later converts and claims to have converted before his death. The heirs claim she converted after. The ruling: the heirs' statement is accepted, and she does not inherit.
The wife must prove her conversion before death to inherit; heirs deny it.
2
Identify the first proof: istishab al-hal
The Hanafi school uses istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). Previously, she was Christian; nothing proves she changed before death. But istishab is only a defensive proof (hujjah dafi'ah), not constructive (hujjah muthbitah). She wants to establish a new right (inheritance), so istishab cannot help her. The heirs are repelling her claim, so istishab works for them.
For the heirs, the presumption is she remained Christian until proven otherwise.
3
Identify the second proof: attribution of new events to nearest time
Her conversion is a new event (hadath). A new event is attributed to the nearest time (aqrab al-awqat), which is after death, not before. So she is deemed to have converted after death. Thus at death she was still Christian, and inheritance requires unity of religion at death.
This principle avoids speculation about earlier times.
4
State the Hanafi position and its basis
The Hanafi ruling is that the wife does not inherit. The proofs are rational: istishab (defensive only) and attribution of new events to nearest time. Other schools may differ on the use of istishab.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.

As for the heirs, they are the repellers, and the apparent occurrence also witnesses for them. Explanation: The scenario of the issue is that a Muslim died, and he had a Christian wife. Then she came as a Muslim after the death of her Muslim husband and claimed, 'I had become Muslim before the death of my Muslim husband,' meaning I was Muslim at the time of his death. Therefore, due to unity of religion, I should inherit. And the heirs of the deceased said, 'You became Muslim after his death,' meaning you were not Muslim at the time of his death. Therefore, due to difference of religion, you should not inherit. So the statement of the heirs will be accepted, meaning the woman will be deprived of her husband's inheritance, and istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) will not be considered a proof in favor of the woman. That is, it will not be said that since this woman is currently Muslim, therefore her being Muslim before the husband's death is also established. And when being Muslim before the husband's death is established, then due to unity of religion, the entitlement to inheritance for her is also established. And this cannot be said because if in this manner by istishab al-hal the woman is declared entitled to inheritance, then istishab al-hal would necessarily be a constructive proof, meaning it would necessitate establishing the entitlement to inheritance by istishab al-hal. While istishab al-hal in our view is not a constructive proof, but rather a defensive proof. So this istishab al-hal will not serve as a proof for the woman to establish the entitlement to inheritance. However, since the heirs are repellers of the woman's entitlement, therefore istishab al-hal becomes a proof for them. And the formulation of istishab al-hal is that this woman was a Christian in the past, i.e., before her husband's death, so after her husband's death she will also be Christian. And when she is Christian after her husband's death, then at the time of death, a fortiori she was Christian. And when she was Christian at the time of death, then due to difference of religion between husband and wife, this Christian woman will be deprived of inheritance from her Muslim husband. And there is a second proof from 'and witnesses for them'. Its gist is that Islam, i.e., the Christian woman's becoming Muslim, is a new event, and a new event is attributed to the nearest time, and the nearest time is after death, not before death. So attributing to the nearest time, it will be said that this woman became Muslim after her husband's death. And when this woman became Muslim after her husband's death, then at the time of death she was certainly Christian. And when she was Christian at the time of her husband's death, then since there was a difference of religion between the husband and wife, therefore this woman will not be entitled to her husband's inheritance.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on inheritance when a Christian wife converts to Islam after her Muslim husband's death and claims she converted before his death. The passage type is a ruling with proof (rational proofs) from the Hanafi school. The ruling: the heirs' statement is accepted, and the wife is deprived of inheritance. The commentator explains two rational proofs. First, istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is a defensive proof, not constructive. The wife wants to establish her entitlement, but istishab cannot do that; it can only defend an existing right. Here, the status quo ante is that she was Christian at the time of death (since she was Christian before and nothing proves she changed), so istishab works in favor of the heirs, who are defending their right against her claim. Second, a new event (her conversion) is attributed to the nearest time, which is after death, not before. Thus, she is deemed to have converted after death, so at death she was Christian. Due to difference of religion (she Christian, he Muslim), she does not inherit. This is the Hanafi position. The text emphasizes that the wife's current Muslim status cannot retroactively establish her Muslim status at death because istishab is not a constructive proof. The heirs' istishab is valid because they are repellers. This ruling aligns with the principle that inheritance requires unity of religion at the time of death.

Key Terms
استصحاب حال (istishab al-hal): Presumption of continuity of a previous state until the contrary is proven. In this text, it is used as a defensive proof, not constructive.
حجة دافعة (hujjah dafi'ah): A proof that repels a claim or defends an existing right. Used to describe istishab in this case.
حجة مثبتة (hujjah muthbitah): A proof that establishes a new right. Istishab is not considered constructive in Hanafi school.
اقرب الاوقات (aqrab al-awqat): The nearest time. Principle that a new event is attributed to the closest possible moment. Here, conversion attributed to after death.
