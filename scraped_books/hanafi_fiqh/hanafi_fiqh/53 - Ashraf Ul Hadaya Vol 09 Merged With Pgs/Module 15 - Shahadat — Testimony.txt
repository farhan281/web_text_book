Permissibility of Prompting a Witness
Distinguishing between permissible and impermissible witness prompting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness prompting and distinguish between permissible (non-suspect) and impermissible (suspect) scenarios.

Core Idea
Prompting a witness is praiseworthy when not suspect, but unanimously forbidden when suspect.

In a court, witnesses may become hesitant due to the awe of the proceedings. The Hanafi school addresses whether a judge may help the witness recall or correct his testimony. This passage clarifies that prompting is not always forbidden; it depends on whether the witness is under suspicion of error or bias.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is the judge prompting a witness (talqin). Note that the passage states it is 'legislated' (mashru‘ah) in the context of the judge's conduct.
The ruling is not absolute; it splits into two cases.
2
Distinguish the two scenarios
Scenario 1: Non-suspect witness – prompting is praiseworthy (mustahsan). Scenario 2: Suspect witness – prompting is unanimously impermissible.
The key criterion is the presence or absence of suspicion (tuhmah).
3
Analyze the rationale for permissibility
Prompting in non-suspect cases revives a human right, similar to other forms of assistance like summoning a party or taking a guarantor.
The analogical reasoning (qiyas) shows it is a form of assistance.
4
Analyze the rationale for impermissibility
In suspect cases, the witness may be mistaken; prompting could lead to false testimony. The example shows a discrepancy that the judge exploits.
The unanimity (bil ittifaq) indicates strong consensus.
5
Apply the distinction
Given a scenario, determine if the witness is suspect by checking for inconsistencies that suggest error or bias.
A suspect case arises when the witness's testimony contradicts the claim in a way that hints at a possible mistake.

It is legislated for the book of the judge's conduct. And sometimes a witness refrains from testifying due to the awe and majesty of the court. So in such a case, prompting the witness is for reviving a human right, like sending someone to summon one of the parties or taking a guarantor from one of the litigants for the other; this assistance is praiseworthy. Similarly, helping a witness by prompting him in a case where he is not suspect is also praiseworthy. An example of a suspect case is: the plaintiff claimed one thousand five hundred dirhams, the defendant denied five hundred dirhams, and the witness testified to one thousand. Then if the judge says, 'Perhaps the plaintiff forgave five hundred dirhams,' the witness becomes aware and corrects his testimony. Such prompting is unanimously impermissible.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on a judge prompting a witness regarding his testimony. It is part of the chapter on the conduct of judges (Adab al-Qadi). The passage distinguishes between two scenarios. First, when the witness is not suspect (ghayr mawdi' tuhmah), prompting is praiseworthy (mustahsan) because it helps revive a rightful claim that the witness might have forgotten due to the intimidating atmosphere of the court. The author gives examples of similarly praiseworthy assistance: summoning a party or taking a guarantor. Second, when the witness is suspect (mawdi' tuhmah), meaning there is a discrepancy that suggests the witness might be mistaken, prompting becomes unanimously impermissible (na jaiz). The example given: the plaintiff claims 1500, the defendant denies 500, and the witness testifies to 1000. If the judge suggests the plaintiff may have forgiven 500, the witness might adjust his testimony. Such prompting is forbidden because it could lead to false testimony or confusion.

Key Terms
تلقین (talqīn): Prompting a witness by the judge to correct or complete his testimony.
موضع تہمت (mawḍi‘ tuhmah): A situation where the witness is suspected of being mistaken or biased due to a discrepancy in the case.
