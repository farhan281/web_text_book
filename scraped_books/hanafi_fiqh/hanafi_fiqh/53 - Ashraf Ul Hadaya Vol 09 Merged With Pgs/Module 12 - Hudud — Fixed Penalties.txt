Compulsion for Surety in Hudud and Qisas: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compulsion for surety

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the legal reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the permissibility of compelling an accused to provide kafalah bi al-nafs in hudud and qisas, and distinguish between hudud that are purely Allah's rights and those that involve human rights.

Core Idea
A hadd involving a human right may allow compulsion for surety; one without does not.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a judge can compel a person accused of a hadd or qisas to provide a surety for his appearance. Abu Hanifa holds that no compulsion is allowed in any hadd or qisas, while the Sahibayn permit it in hadd al-qadhf and qisas because these involve the victim's right. Understanding this dispute requires examining both the general hadith 'La kafalah fi hadd' and the principle of dar' (avoidance) that governs hudud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed act
The dispute is about whether the court can compel the accused to provide a surety for his person (kafalah bi al-nafs) in hudud and qisas cases. It is not about voluntary kafalah.
The term 'kafalah fi hadd' is interpreted as 'compulsion to kafalah'.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa says compulsion is not permissible in any hadd or qisas. His proof is the hadith 'La kafalah fi hadd' without exception, and the principle that hudud are built on dar' (avoidance) – they are to be averted by doubts, so taking security contradicts this.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
The Sahibayn say compulsion is allowed in hadd al-qadhf and qisas because these involve the right of the slave (victim). They analogize to financial claims where compulsion for surety is allowed.
They exclude hudud that are purely Allah's rights like theft and drinking.
4
Analyze the proofs
Abu Hanifa relies on a general text and the spirit of hudud (dar'). The Sahibayn prioritize the victim's right and use qiyas. The hadith is general, but the Sahibayn restrict it by reference to the illah (cause) of human right.
The principle of dar' applies to all hudud, but the Sahibayn argue it is weaker when a human right is at stake.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, an accused of slander or murder cannot be forced to find a surety; if he refuses to appear, the court cannot compel him. Under the Sahibayn, the court can force him, ensuring the victim's right is not lost.
This affects pretrial procedures in Islamic courts.

He said: Kafalah bi al-nafs is not permissible in hudud and qisas according to Abu Hanifa. Its meaning is that one cannot be compelled to provide surety according to him. And the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: One can be compelled in hadd al-qadhf because it involves the right of the slave, and in qisas because it is purely the right of the slave, contrary to the hudud that are purely the right of Allah. And Abu Hanifa's proof is the saying of the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'No kafalah in any hadd, without distinction.' And because the basis of all (hudud) is repelling (dar'), so taking security for them is not obligatory, unlike other rights because they are not repelled by doubts, so taking security for them is appropriate, as in ta'zir.

Translation: According to Imam Abu Hanifa, kafalah bi al-nafs is not permissible in hudud and qisas. Its meaning is that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the person against whom a hadd or qisas claim is made cannot be compelled to provide a surety. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said that in hadd al-qadhf one can be compelled to provide a surety because in hadd al-qadhf there is a right of the slave (the victim), and in qisas one can be compelled because it is purely the right of the slave, unlike the hudud that are purely the right of Allah. And Abu Hanifa's proof is that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said without distinction that there is no kafalah in any hadd, and because all hudud are based on repelling (avoidance), so taking security is not obligatory in them, unlike other rights because they are not repelled by doubts, so taking security for them is appropriate, as in ta'zir.

Commentary: Sahib al-Quduri states that according to Imam Abu Hanifa, kafalah bi al-nafs is not permissible in hudud and qisas. By this statement of Sahib al-Quduri, it is intended that if a person is claimed for hadd or qisas, and the claimant demands a surety for the person from him, i.e., demands a surety who will guarantee to produce him in court to establish the hadd, but the accused refuses to provide a surety, then according to Imam Abu Hanifa he cannot be forced to provide a surety. Thus the meaning of 'la tajuzu al-kafalah bi al-nafs' is 'la yajuzu ijbar al-kafalah' i.e., it is not permissible to compel a person against whom hadd or qisas is due to provide a surety. This is also the view of Imam Ahmad and one view of Imam al-Shafi'i. The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said that a person against whom a claim of hadd al-qadhf is made shall be compelled to provide a surety. That is, if the person refuses to provide a surety, it is permissible to compel him to provide a surety for the person. This is the view of Imam Malik and also one view of Imam al-Shafi'i. The proof of the two is that in hadd al-qadhf, the right of the slave (the slandered person) is to remove the disgrace from himself, and since the purpose of prescribing the hadd is to prevent people from accusation and to clear the world of corruption, hadd al-qadhf is also a right of Allah. Nevertheless, the right of the slave is established in hadd al-qadhf. This is why the claim of the slandered person is a condition for carrying out hadd al-qadhf.

Commentary
This passage presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the permissibility of compelling a person accused of a hadd (Quranic penalty) or qisas (retaliation) to provide a surety for his person (kafalah bi al-nafs). The matn (original text) states the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa that such kafalah is not permissible, meaning the accused cannot be forced to produce a surety. The Sahibayn (Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) differentiate: they permit compulsion in hadd al-qadhf (slander) because it involves a right of the slave (the victim) to clear his reputation, and in qisas because it is purely a personal right. They exclude hudud that are purely rights of Allah (like hadd for theft or drinking), where compulsion is not allowed. Imam Abu Hanifa's proof is a general hadith: 'No kafalah in any hadd' without exception, and the principle that hudud are built on dar' (avoidance) – they are to be averted by doubts, so taking security (which strengthens the claim) contradicts this spirit. The Sahibayn argue that in hadd al-qadhf and qisas, the victim's right outweighs the principle of dar', and since the victim needs the accused present to establish his right, compulsion for suretyship is allowed. The commentator explains that Abu Hanifa's ruling only prohibits compulsion, not voluntary suretyship. The passage also notes that other imams (Ahmad, Malik, al-Shafi'i) have views aligning with either side.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): A fixed Quranic punishment for specific offenses (e.g., theft, zina, slander).
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation for bodily harm or homicide, equal to the injury inflicted.
حد قذف (hadd al-qadhf): The fixed punishment (80 lashes) for false accusation of adultery (zina).
درء (dar'): The principle of repelling or avoiding hudud by doubts; hudud are to be suspended whenever a doubt arises.
إجبار الكفالة (ijbar al-kafalah): Compelling a person to provide a surety (kafil) against his will.
تعزير (ta'zir): Discretionary punishment for offenses that have no fixed hadd or qisas.

Conditions for Imprisoning a Defendant in Hudud and Qisas
Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for imprisoning a defendant in hudud and qisas and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Imprisonment in hudud and qisas is based on suspicion, so one component of complete testimony suffices.

In Hanafi criminal law, imprisonment before conviction serves a preventive purpose based on suspicion (tuhma), not punishment. This lesson unpacks the precise evidentiary threshold required to detain a defendant in hudud and qisas cases, and clarifies a key disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling from the matn
Locate the operative sentence in the Arabic text: 'ولا يحبس فيها حتى يشهد شاهدان مستوران او شاهد عدل يعرفه القاضى'. This is the core ruling.
The pronoun 'ها' refers to hudud, and in some copies 'فيهما' refers to hadd qadhf and qisas.
2
Analyze the condition: mustur witnesses or one adil witness
Understand that 'mustur' means witnesses whose integrity is unknown. The condition allows either two such witnesses or one known adil witness. This is a lower standard than full proof.
This is because the purpose is suspicion, not establishing the hadd itself.
3
Understand the rationale: tuhma and diyanat
The commentator explains that suspicion is established by one component of complete testimony (shahada kāmila) — either number or integrity. Imprisonment to prevent corruption is a religious matter (diyānāt), which requires only one component.
Diyānāt cases have lower evidentiary standards than mu'āmalāt (transactions).
4
Contrast with financial cases
In financial matters, imprisonment is the ultimate punishment (aqsā 'uqūba), so it requires complete proof (two adil witnesses). The text highlights this distinction to show why hudud/qisas are different.
The rationale is that in hudud, the ultimate punishment is the hadd itself, not imprisonment.
5
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
The passage mentions that according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), imprisonment is not allowed on a single witness because security can be obtained through kafalah (suretyship). Imam Abu Hanifa allows it.
The dispute is about whether one adil witness suffices or two (including mustur) are needed.

The gist is that imprisoning the one upon whom the hadd is obligatory is in two situations: either one adil person whose integrity is known to the judge testifies, or two witnesses whose integrity or vice is not apparent testify. The evidence is that imprisonment in hudud and qisas is not for establishing the cause, because establishing the cause requires complete proof. And suspicion is established by one of the two parts of complete testimony. One part of complete testimony is number, i.e., two witnesses, and the other part is integrity, i.e., if the witness is one, he must be adil. And suspicion is established by one part because imprisonment due to suspicion is to prevent corruption, and preventing corruption is from the category of diyanat (religious matters). And to establish diyanat, one part of complete testimony is sufficient, whether it be number or integrity. Therefore, for imprisonment due to suspicion of corruption, the existence of one of the two parts of complete testimony is necessary. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on the conditions for imprisoning a defendant in hudud and qisas. The matn (original text) states: 'He will not be imprisoned therein until two mustur witnesses testify, or one adil witness known to the judge.' The commentator explains that 'mustur' means witnesses whose status (adil or fasiq) is not known. The reason is that imprisonment in hudud and qisas is based on suspicion (tuhma), not on establishing the hadd itself. Suspicion can be established by one part of complete testimony: either the number (two witnesses, even if their integrity is unknown) or the integrity (one adil witness). This is because preventing corruption (suspicion) is a religious matter (diyanat), and for religious matters, one part of complete testimony suffices. The commentator then mentions an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), imprisonment is not based on one witness because security is already obtained through kafalah (suretyship). This implies that the Sahibayn require two witnesses for imprisonment, while Imam Abu Hanifa allows one. The commentator also provides an athar from Ibn Mas'ud and a hadith of the Prophet ﷺ as evidence that imprisonment on suspicion is established. In contrast, for financial cases, imprisonment is the ultimate punishment, so it requires complete proof (two adil witnesses) because it serves as final punishment, not mere suspicion.

Key Terms
مستور (mustur): A witness whose status regarding integrity (adala) or vice (fisq) is unknown/hidden.
تہمت (tuhma): Suspicion that a person may be guilty of a crime; it justifies preventive measures like imprisonment.
شہادت کاملہ (shahada kāmila): Complete testimony consisting of two components: number (two witnesses) and integrity (adala).

Imprisonment in Hudud and Qisas Based on Incomplete Testimony
Ruling on imprisonment in hudud/qisas with incomplete testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a defendant may be imprisoned in hudud/qisas cases according to the Hanafi school, and identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding kafalah.

Core Idea
In hudud/qisas, imprisonment is based on suspicion, not punishment; one just witness or two unknown witnesses suffice.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah expands on a key ruling from al-Hidayah's matn: when can a judge imprison a defendant in hudud and qisas cases? The ruling introduces a uniquely Hanafi concept—that suspicion (tuhmah) can be established by one 'half' of complete testimony, either number or integrity. The commentary also reveals an important intra-Hanafi split: Imam Abu Hanifa allows imprisonment, while the Sahibayn argue that kafalah (suretyship) makes imprisonment unnecessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on imprisoning a defendant in hudud (specifically hadd qadhf) and qisas. The ruling: it is permissible (mubah) for the judge to imprison under certain conditions.
Imprisonment here is a discretionary measure, not the fixed punishment.
2
Extract the conditions for imprisonment
Two conditions are given: (a) two witnesses of concealed character (mastur) testify, OR (b) one just witness known to the judge testifies.
These are alternatives, not cumulative.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
Imprisonment is based on tuhmah (suspicion), which is established by one of the two halves of complete testimony: the number of witnesses (two) or the integrity of a single witness. Removing corruption (suspicion) falls under diyanat (religious matters), where one half suffices.
Contrast with financial cases: there imprisonment is the ultimate punishment, requiring complete proof (two just witnesses).
4
Analyze the evidence cited
The passage cites an athar of Ibn Mas'ud (imprisoning a wine-drinker on one witness) and a hadith that the Prophet ﷺ imprisoned a man on suspicion. These support the principle that imprisonment can be based on incomplete testimony.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut, so it establishes permissibility, not obligation.
5

Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that if kafalah (suretyship for the person) is possible, imprisonment is unnecessary because kafalah provides security. Abu Hanifa disagrees, as he does not accept kafalah in hudud/qisas. The thamarah al-khilaf: under Abu Hanifa, the defendant is imprisoned even if a surety is offered; under Sahibayn, he is released if a surety is provided.
The text from al-Mabsut mentions the Sahibayn view; the mufta bihi is not explicitly stated here.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the ruling on imprisoning a defendant in hudud and qisas cases based on testimony, expanding on the matn of al-Hidayah. The author first quotes the matn: 'He is not imprisoned therein until two mastur witnesses or one just witness known to the judge testify.' The commentary clarifies that the pronoun refers to hudud (limited to hadd qadhf and qisas). The key point is that imprisonment in hudud/qisas is not a punishment but a measure to remove suspicion (tuhmah) against the judge. Suspicion is established by one of the two halves of complete testimony: either the number of witnesses (two) or the integrity of a single witness (one just man known to the judge). This is contrasted with financial cases where imprisonment is the ultimate punishment, thus requiring complete proof (two just witnesses). The evidence includes the practice of Ibn Mas'ud and a hadith of the Prophet (ﷺ) who imprisoned a man on suspicion. An objection is raised that imprisonment on suspicion contradicts the principle of 'ward off hudud by doubts'. The answer is that the suspicion is not against the defendant but against the judge; not imprisoning would make the judge suspect of negligence. The commentary then introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), there is no imprisonment even on the testimony of one just witness because security can be achieved through kafalah (suretyship) in hudud/qisas. Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding imprisonment of the defendant in hadd and qisas cases based on incomplete evidence. The author first establishes a general principle: complete and final punishments require complete evidence (hujja kamila), while minor punishments, such as imprisonment in hadd/qisas, can be proven with incomplete evidence (hujja naqisa). Incomplete evidence here means one part of complete evidence: either two witnesses of concealed probity (mastur al-hal) or one just witness. In property cases, imprisonment is a final punishment, requiring two just witnesses.

An objection is raised: imprisoning on mere suspicion contradicts the hadith 'Avert hadd due to doubts.' The response clarifies that the suspicion here is suspicion against the judge for not acting. The judge imprisons to protect his own reputation for upholding religious matters. The Prophet's imprisonment was to teach permissibility, as he was immune from such suspicion.

The passage then presents the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad), in hadd and qisas cases, even with one just witness or two concealed witnesses, the defendant will NOT be imprisoned, because in these cases kafalah (suretyship for the person) is valid and can be demanded. Since the defendant can be bound by a surety, imprisonment is unnecessary. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, kafalah is not valid in hadd and qisas, so imprisonment is used to secure the defendant. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly given here, but the text aligns with Abu Hanifa. The footnote specifies that 'hadd' in this context means hadd qadhf.

حجت ناقصہ (hujja naqisa): Deficient evidence: either two witnesses of concealed probity (mastur al-hal) or one just witness.
مستور الحال (mastur al-hal): A witness whose probity is not publicly known but is not known to be a liar.
