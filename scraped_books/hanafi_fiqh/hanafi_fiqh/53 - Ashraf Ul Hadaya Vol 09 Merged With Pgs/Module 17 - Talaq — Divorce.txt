Conditional Divorce and the Absent Party: When the Judge Refuses to Pronounce Divorce
Ruling on conditional divorce proof when the condition relates to an absent person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between two cases of conditional divorce involving an absent party and apply the Hanafi rule on qada' 'ala al-gha'ib to determine when a judge accepts evidence of the condition.

Core Idea
A judge does not accept evidence for a condition that nullifies an absent person's right, but does accept if the condition does not affect the absent's rights.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from Al-Hidayah about conditional divorce when the condition involves an absent person. The key principle is qada' 'ala al-gha'ib: a judge cannot issue a judgment that voids the rights of someone not present in court. You will see how the Hanafi school applies this principle to distinguish two seemingly similar cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first scenario (asl) and the judge's ruling
Read the passage to identify the first case: a husband says to his wife, 'If Khalid divorces his wife, you are divorced.' The wife brings evidence that Khalid divorced his wife. What does the judge do?
The judge does not accept the evidence and does not pronounce divorce.
2
Identify the second scenario (far') and the judge's ruling
Now identify the second case: a husband says to his wife, 'If Zayd enters this house, you are divorced.' The wife brings evidence that Zayd entered. What does the judge do?
The judge accepts the evidence and pronounces divorce.
3
Recognize the objection
The text raises an objection: both cases involve a condition about an absent person, so why are the rulings different? Understand the apparent contradiction.
The objection argues that both should be treated the same.
4
Explain the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes based on the effect on the absent person's rights. In the first case, proving the condition (that Khalid divorced his wife) directly nullifies Khalid's right over his wife. In the second case, entering a house does not nullify Zayd's rights.
The first case is qada' 'ala al-gha'ib (judgment against an absent person), which is prohibited; the second case is not.
5
Apply the distinction to a new scenario
Given a new conditional divorce scenario involving an absent party, determine whether the judge should accept evidence by checking whether the condition would nullify the absent's rights.
This step solidifies the concept.

He will not order [the occurrence of] divorce. Because on the absent one, i.e. Khalid, the claim that he divorced his wife is a condition for establishing the right (i.e. occurrence of divorce) against the present one, i.e. the speaker's wife. And it has passed before that in appointing the present as a legal deputy on behalf of the absent, the condition is not considered. Therefore, in this issue too, the condition will not be considered. Here an objection arises: if a man said to his wife, 'If such-and-such person enters this house, then you are divorced,' then the speaker's wife presents evidence that that person entered the house, then her evidence will be accepted and the judge will order that divorce has occurred. Yet this issue is similar to the previous one, i.e. here too, what is claimed against the absent is a condition for proving the right against the present. Therefore, here too, the condition should not have been considered as in the previous issue. The answer is that when entering the house is made a condition, since the right of the absent is not nullified, this does not fall under judgment against the absent. And when it is not of the category of judgment against the absent, then that condition will be considered. And in the previous issue, since the right of the absent, i.e. Khalid, is nullified, the condition will not be considered.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It addresses a specific scenario in the court of a qadi (judge) regarding conditional divorces. The ruling: The judge does not pronounce divorce when the condition involves an absent party and proving it would establish a right against the absent, as this falls under qada' 'ala al-gha'ib (judgment against an absent person) which is not allowed. The proof is that in such cases, the condition (the absent's action) cannot be considered because the present person acts as a legal deputy, and conditions are not considered in that capacity. The passage then raises a potential objection: if a husband says to his wife, 'If Zayd enters this house, you are divorced,' and the wife brings evidence that Zayd entered, the judge does accept the evidence and declares divorce. This seems analogous to the first case because the condition involves an absent person. The answer (Hanafi response) distinguishes the two: In the second case, entering the house does not nullify the absent person's right (since the husband himself set the condition), so it is not qada' 'ala al-gha'ib. In the first case, the claim is that the absent (Khalid) divorced his own wife, which directly nullifies Khalid's right over his wife, making it qada' 'ala al-gha'ib, which is prohibited. Hence the difference.

Key Terms
قضاء علی الغائب (qada' 'ala al-gha'ib): Judgment against an absent person, which is prohibited in Hanafi law when it nullifies the absent's right.
نائب حکمی (na'ib hukmi): A legal deputy appointed by the court to represent an absent party in litigation.
