Ownership of Honey Belongs to Landowner via Qiyas
Ruling with proof: qiyas from trees and silt to honey.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qiyas (analogy) to determine that natural produce like honey belongs to the landowner, and explain the effective cause (illa) of taba‘iyya.

Core Idea
Honey, like a tree or silt, is a natural produce of the land and thus belongs to the owner.

This lesson examines a Hanafi ruling derived by qiyas jali (clear analogy). The source text argues that honey collected by bees on a piece of land becomes the property of the landowner because it is a natural produce physically arising from that land. The author supports this by drawing parallels to two undisputed cases: spontaneously growing trees and silt deposited by water. Understanding this analogy will sharpen your ability to trace how the Hanafi school extends rulings to new cases using shared effective causes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State clearly what is being ruled: honey produced by bees on someone's land belongs to the landowner.
The landowner does not need to plant or tend the source; the honey is automatically owned.
2
Recognize the reasoning method
The passage uses qiyas jali (clear analogy) by comparing honey to two well-established cases: a tree growing spontaneously on land, and silt deposited by water.
Qiyas is a source of law in Hanafi usul when no direct text exists.
3
Extract the effective cause (illa)
Determine why the tree and silt belong to the landowner: because they are natural produce physically arising from or attached to the land. This illa is taba‘iyya (subordination).
The illa must be explicit and restrictable to the new case.
4
Apply the illa to honey
Honey is produced by bees feeding on the land's flora; it is physically collected and stored on that land. Thus it shares the same illa: it is a natural produce of the land.
The bees are not owned; their product follows the land.
5
Confirm the hukm
Since honey shares the illa with the original cases, it acquires the same hukm: ownership by the landowner.
This ruling is consistent with Hanafi principles of property through natural increase (nama').

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof by analogy (qiyas). It states that honey produced by bees on someone's land belongs to the landowner. The reasoning is that honey is a product of the land, as bees derive sustenance from the land's flora. The author presents two analogies: 1) A tree that grows spontaneously on land belongs to the landowner even if he did not plant it. 2) Silt deposited by water on land becomes property of the landowner. Both analogies share the effective cause (illa) that anything physically attached to or arising from the land as its natural produce is subordinate to the land and thus owned by the landowner. This ruling is consistent with Hanafi principles of property acquisition through natural increase and attachment.

Key Terms
تابع (tabi‘): Legally subordinate; property that follows the ownership of the principal object (the land).
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning; extending a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far‘) due to a shared effective cause (illa).

Key Takeaway
Natural produce physically arising from land, such as honey, trees, or silt, is subordinate to the land and owned by the landowner in Hanafi fiqh.
