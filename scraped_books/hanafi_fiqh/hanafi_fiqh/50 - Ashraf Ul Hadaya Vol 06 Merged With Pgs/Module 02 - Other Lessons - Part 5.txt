Oath to Kill a Dead Person: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Knowledge
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath validity with impossible condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath to kill a dead person, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, and apply the qiyas to the case of the pot.

Core Idea
An oath to kill a dead person is valid if the swearer knows; Abu Yusuf extends it to ignorance via the pot analogy.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of an oath conditioned on an impossible act. The issue turns on whether the swearer knows the impossibility. The discussion introduces a key analogy (qiyas) to the well-known case of swearing to drink from an empty pot (mas'alah al-kuuz). Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi methodology for handling impossible conditions in oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath scenario and knowledge statuses
The oath is: 'If I do not kill so-and-so, my wife is divorced.' The person is already dead. Two cases: the swearer knows the death vs. does not know.
The same condition applies: killing a dead person is impossible.
2
State the ruling when the swearer knows the death
If the swearer knows the person is dead, the oath is valid (sahih) and he becomes hanith immediately due to customary incapacity ('ajz 'adi).
The oath is based on a conceivable life (Allah could create life) so it is valid.
3
State the ruling when the swearer does not know
According to the general position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), the oath is not valid and he does not become hanith because the condition is impossible and inconceivable.
The oath was based on an existing life, but no life exists.
4
Explain Imam Abu Yusuf's position and qiyas
Imam Abu Yusuf analogizes to the pot case: swearing to drink from an empty pot is valid and immediately broken. Similarly, here the oath is valid regardless of knowledge.
The pot case ruling applies without detail (tafsil), so knowledge is irrelevant.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (tarjih)
The text states 'there is no detail in that case; this is the correct view,' indicating that Abu Yusuf's position is preferred.
Thus, the oath is always valid and the swearer becomes hanith immediately.

Commentary

By qiyas, in this killing case, Abu Yusuf holds that even if the swearer does not know the person is dead, he becomes hanith. The text implies that the correct view is the one that follows the pot case: the oath is valid and the swearer becomes hanith regardless of knowledge. This highlights the principle that an oath can be based on a hypothetical future event that is impossible in reality, and the swearer is held to the immediate consequence.

Oath to Repay Debt with Defective Coins
Ruling on swearing to repay debt with counterfeit or impure coins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which an oath to repay a debt is fulfilled in Hanafi fiqh, including payment with defective coins and through muqasah.

Core Idea
An oath to repay a debt is fulfilled when the debtor pays with coins of the same genus, even if defective, or when the debt is discharged through muqasah.

This passage from the Hidayah addresses a subtle scenario: what happens when a debtor swears to repay a debt but then pays with coins that are counterfeit or of low quality? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling based on the concept of muqasah (set-off) and the genus of the coins. Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to the conditions of the oath and the nature of the payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an oath to repay a debt. The ruling concerns whether the oath is fulfilled (sahih) or not.
2
Determine the payment method
Consider the coins used: if they are of the same genus as the debt (e.g., silver dirhams), even if defective or impure, the oath is fulfilled. If the coins are of a different genus (e.g., lead), the oath is not fulfilled.
Genus refers to the substance (e.g., silver, gold).
3
Apply muqasah
If the debtor sells an item to the creditor in exchange for the debt, muqasah occurs at the moment of sale, fulfilling the oath even without physical possession.
Muqasah: automatic set-off of mutual debts.
4
Distinguish gift from muqasah
If the creditor forgives the debt (gift), the oath is not fulfilled because repayment requires an act of the debtor, not a waiver by the creditor.
Gift is a unilateral cancellation, not a discharge by payment.
5
Conclude the ruling
The oath is fulfilled only through payment in the same genus or muqasah. Gift or payment in a different genus does not fulfill it.
This is the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Oath Not to Receive Debt in Installments
Ruling on oath not to receive debt piecemeal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an oath not to receive debt in installments is broken and identify the conditions that trigger violation.

Core Idea
Breaking the oath requires receiving the entire debt in separate portions; part alone does not suffice.

This lesson clarifies a common scenario in oaths: swearing not to take a debt repayment piecemeal. The key nuance is that the oath is not broken simply by receiving a partial payment—it only breaks when the entire debt has been collected in separate receipts. Understanding this distinction prevents unnecessary expiation and reflects the Hanafi emphasis on precise legal causation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Oath and the Act
The oath is 'I will not receive my debt dirham by dirham' – that is, in installments. The act in question is receiving part of the debt.
2
Determine When Hanth Occurs
Breaking the oath (hanth) does not occur upon receiving a part; it only occurs when the entire debt is received in separate portions. The reason is that the oath attaches to the entire defined debt.
3
Recognize the Exception for Continuous Weighing
If the creditor receives the debt in two weighings that are only interrupted by the act of weighing itself, this is not considered 'separate receipt' and does not break the oath, because it is often impractical to weigh the whole amount at once.

When Does an Oath Not to Receive Debt in Installments Break?
Ruling on hanth in an oath about receiving debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for hanth in an oath not to receive debt dirham by dirham and apply the exception for weighings.

Core Idea
The oath is not broken by partial receipt; it is broken only when the entire debt is received separately.

This lesson addresses a common situation: a person swears not to accept repayment of a debt in installments. When does such an oath become broken? The Hanafi ruling clarifies that hanth does not occur with partial collection but only when the entire debt is taken in separate portions. There is also a practical exception for weighings done without delay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath's subject
The oath is: 'I will not receive my debt dirham by dirham.' The key is that the debt is defined and owned by the oath-taker.
The debt is 'mu'arraf mudaf ilayh' (defined and added to him).
2
Determine when hanth occurs
Hanth does not occur upon receiving some of the debt; it occurs only when the entire debt is received separately.
The condition is receipt of all (al-kull) with the attribute of separation (tafarruq).
3
Analyze the exception for weighings
If the debt is received in two weighings with no occupation except the act of weighing, that does not count as separation, and no hanth occurs.
This is based on custom (urf): it may be impossible to receive all at once.
4
Understand the linguistic reasoning
The wording 'his debt' (daynahu) refers to the entire debt because it is definite and possessive, so the oath attaches to the whole.
The verb 'aqbada' is added to a defined debt, so it applies to the entirety.

Oaths Regarding Eating Goat and Unripe Dates
Rulings on oaths about eating a goat and unripe dates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle regarding causative attributes in oaths and apply it to determine if an oath is broken when the attribute changes.

Core Idea
An oath limited to a causative attribute ends when that attribute ceases.

This lesson explains a key principle in Hanafi fiqh concerning oaths that mention a specific quality. When someone swears not to eat a certain thing, the oath may be confined to a particular attribute that motivated the oath. Once that attribute no longer exists, the oath no longer applies, so eating the item after the change does not break the oath. Understanding this principle helps avoid unintended oath-breaking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thing and any attribute
Look at what the oath is about: the object (e.g., a goat, dates) and any description attached (e.g., unripe, from this goat).
The attribute may be explicitly stated or implied.
2
Determine if the attribute is a cause
Ask whether the attribute could be the reason for the oath. For example, unripeness may be disliked; goat meat is typical, but milk is not.
Custom and common practice help identify causative attributes.
3
Apply the rule: if causative, oath limited to that state
The oath only applies while the attribute remains. Once the attribute changes (dates ripen, goat becomes old), the oath no longer binds.
This applies whether the noun is definite or indefinite.
4
Check when attribute is not causative
If the attribute is not a cause, then for indefinite nouns the attribute is considered; for definite nouns it is not, meaning the oath applies broadly.
This nuance affects cases like swearing about a 'mad' person.
5
Apply to given case
Use the above steps to decide if a change breaks the oath. For example, swearing not to speak to a madman: madness is a cause, so when he recovers, speaking does not break the oath.
The principle from Al-Muhit confirms this.
