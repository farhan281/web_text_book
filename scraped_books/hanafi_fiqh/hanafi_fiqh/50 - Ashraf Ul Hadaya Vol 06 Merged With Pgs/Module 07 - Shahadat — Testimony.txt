Testimony on Freeing One of Two Slaves vs. Divorcing One of Two Wives
Intra-Hanafi dispute on accepting ambiguous testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on testimony for ambiguous emancipation and distinguish the Sahibayn's analogy to divorce testimony.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa voids ambiguous emancipation testimony; the Sahibayn accept it by analogy to accepted divorce testimony.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of testimony when witnesses claim a man freed one of his two slaves. The ruling hinges on whether a claim (da'wa) from the slave is required and whether ambiguous emancipation resembles divorce in its legal consequences. The Sahibayn analogize emancipation to divorce, while Abu Hanifa distinguishes them based on the prohibition of intercourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage compares testimony on freeing one of two slaves with testimony on divorcing one of two wives. In divorce, there is consensus that the testimony is valid and the husband is compelled to divorce one. In emancipation, there is a dispute.
Both involve ambiguity: neither explicitly identifies which slave or which wife.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
Abu Hanifa rules that testimony on freeing one of two slaves during health is void (batil), unless it is part of a will (by istihsan). The testimony is not accepted because the slave did not bring a claim (da'wa).
The exception for wills shows that istihsan can override the general rule.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and their analogy
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) analogize emancipation to divorce: just as testimony on divorcing one of two wives is valid and compels action, so too should testimony on freeing one of two slaves. They accept the testimony and compel the master to free one slave.
Their qiyas is based on the common element of causing a legal consequence (freedom vs. divorce).
4
Analyze the underlying principle: requirement of da'wa
Abu Hanifa requires a claim from the slave for testimony on freeing a male slave (without prohibition of intercourse). For a female slave, no claim is needed because freedom entails prohibition of intercourse, resembling divorce. Since ambiguous emancipation does not entail such prohibition, it defaults to requiring a claim.
The Sahibayn do not require a claim for any emancipation testimony.
5
Note the exception for wills and tadbir
When the testimony concerns a will (dying declaration) or tadbir (promise of freedom after death), it is accepted by istihsan, even by Abu Hanifa, because these are akin to wills and the da'wa condition is relaxed.
This shows the flexibility of Hanafi fiqh through istihsan.

Two men testified against a man that he freed one of his two slaves. The ruling on testimony. He said: When two men testify against a man that he freed one of his two slaves, the testimony is void according to Abu Hanifa, unless it is in his will, by istihsan, as mentioned in al-'Itaq. And if they testify that he divorced one of his wives, the testimony is valid, and the husband is compelled to divorce one of them, and this is by agreement. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: The testimony in emancipation is similar to that. Translation: And when two men testified about a man that he freed one of his two slaves, according to Imam Abu Hanifa this testimony is void, except in the case of a will, by istihsan. Imam Muhammad mentioned this in Kitab al-'Itaq, stating that according to Abu Hanifa, half of each becomes free. If two witnesses testify that he divorced one of his wives, the testimony is valid, and the man is compelled to divorce one of them. The Sahibayn agreed on this. And the Sahibayn said that testimony in emancipation is like divorce, so the master will be commanded to free one of the two slaves. Commentary: The origin of this is that testimony on freeing a slave is not accepted without a claim from the slave according to Abu Hanifa, but according to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) it is accepted. Testimony on freeing a slave woman and divorcing a wife is accepted without a claim by agreement. This is well-known. And since the claim of the slave is a condition according to him, it cannot be realized in the case of the book because a claim from an unknown person cannot be realized, so testimony is not accepted. According to them, it is not a condition, so testimony is accepted even if no claim exists. As for divorce, the absence of a claim does not cause a defect in testimony because it is not a condition in it. And if they testify that you freed one of your two slave women, it is not accepted according to Abu Hanifa, even if a claim is not a condition in it, because he only does not require a claim because it involves prohibition of intercourse, so it resembles divorce. But ambiguous emancipation does not entail prohibition of intercourse according to him, as we mentioned, so it becomes like testimony on freeing one of two slaves. All this is when they testify during his health that he freed one of his two slaves. But if they testify that he freed one of his two slaves during his death illness, or testify about his tadbir during health or illness, and they give testimony during his death illness or after death, it is accepted by istihsan because tadbir wherever it occurs, occurs as a will, and likewise.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the acceptance of testimony that a man freed one of his two slaves. Imam Abu Hanifa rules that such testimony is void unless it pertains to a will (where istihsan applies). In contrast, testimony that a man divorced one of his two wives is accepted by consensus, and the husband is compelled to divorce one. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) analogize emancipation to divorce, arguing that the master should be compelled to free one slave. The underlying principle: According to Abu Hanifa, testimony on freeing a male slave requires a claim from the slave himself; without a claim, it is not accepted. For a female slave (whose freedom also involves prohibition of intercourse), testimony is accepted without a claim by analogy to divorce. However, ambiguous emancipation (e.g., 'one of two slaves') does not involve prohibition of intercourse, so it defaults to requiring a claim. The Sahibayn do not require a claim for any emancipation testimony. An exception exists for wills and tadbir (declaration of freedom after death), where testimony is accepted by istihsan because such actions are effectively wills.

Key Terms
الشهادة (al-shahāda): Testimony in court; legal evidence given by witnesses.
الدعوى (al-da'wa): A legal claim by a person asserting a right.
استحسان (istihsān): Juristic preference; departing from qiyas for a stronger reason.
عتق ('itq): Emancipation; freeing a slave.
قيل (qiyās): Analogical reasoning in Islamic law.

Testimony on Manumission of One of Two Slaves: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on accepting testimony for ambiguous manumission without a claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding testimony for ambiguous manumission and identify the key condition (da'wa) that distinguishes Abu Hanifa's position from the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires a claim from the slave for manumission testimony; without identity, no claim, hence no testimony.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a dispute within the Hanafi school about the acceptance of testimony when witnesses state that a man freed one of his two slaves, without specifying which one. Abu Hanifa holds such testimony invalid unless it is in a will, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) accept it, drawing an analogy to divorce. The core issue is whether a claim (da'wa) from the slave is required for testimony regarding a male slave's manumission. Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school treats ambiguous legal claims and the role of analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the specific legal scenario: witnesses testify that a man freed one of his two slaves, but do not specify which one. The question is whether this testimony is accepted in court.
The dispute is purely within the Hanafi school; there is no cross-madhhab disagreement here.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and his condition
Abu Hanifa rules the testimony invalid (batil) unless it is in the context of a will (by istihsan). His key condition is that testimony regarding manumission of a male slave requires a claim (da'wa) from the slave himself. Without a claim, the testimony is rejected.
The claim cannot be made when the subject is unknown, so the testimony fails.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position and their analogy
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani accept the testimony as valid (sahih), equating it with testimony for divorce. In divorce, a claim is not required, and the husband is compelled to divorce one of his wives. Similarly, here the owner should be compelled to free one of the two slaves.
They argue by qiyas: manumission and divorce both involve prohibition of the private part (for female slaves and wives), so the ruling should be the same.
4
Trace the distinction
Abu Hanifa responds that the analogy fails because ambiguous manumission of a male slave does not entail prohibition of the private part (since only female manumission does). Therefore, the condition of a claim remains, and the testimony is invalid.
For a female slave, testimony is accepted without a claim by consensus because it entails prohibition of the private part.
5
Note the exceptions
The passage outlines exceptions: if the testimony concerns a will (terminal illness) or tadbir (postponed manumission), it is accepted by istihsan. The opponent is known (the testator or his successor), and the manumission becomes widespread among the slaves after death.
Also, there is a difference of opinion when testimony is given after death about a statement made in health: some say not accepted (not a will), others say accepted due to prevalence.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the acceptance of testimony for the manumission of one of two slaves when the identity is unknown. The matn states Abu Hanifa's position that such testimony is invalid unless it is made in a will (by istihsan). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ, equating it with divorce where testimony is accepted. The commentary then explains the underlying principle: according to Abu Hanifa, testimony regarding a male slave's manumission requires a claim (da'wa) from the slave himself; otherwise it is not accepted. Since in the case of two slaves it is unknown which one is being claimed, no valid claim can exist, so testimony fails. The Sahibayn do not require the claim, so they accept the testimony. For a female slave, the ruling differs because manumission of a female slave implies prohibition of the private part (like divorce), so a claim is not required by consensus. However, Abu Hanifa distinguishes between explicit manumission (which prohibits the private part) and ambiguous manumission (which does not), so he treats the testimony for one of two female slaves the same as for male slaves. The passage then discusses exceptions: if the manumission is in the context of a will (terminal illness) or tadbir, testimony is accepted by istihsan because the opponent (the testator) is known, and after death the manumission spreads to both slaves, making each a specific opponent. Finally, it notes two opinions when testimony occurs after death regarding a statement made in health: one says it is not accepted because it is not a will, another says it is accepted due to prevalence.

Indirect Testimony in Conditional Oaths: The Hajj-Sacrifice Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on indirect testimony in conditional oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether testimony of sacrifice proves performance of Hajj for an oath condition, and identify the mufta bihi position according to Ibn al-Humam.

Core Idea
Testimony about a related act does not necessarily establish the condition if the primary purpose is negative.

This passage examines a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school: if a person swears to free his slave if he does not perform Hajj, then claims he did, can testimony that he performed a sacrifice in Kufa prove that he performed Hajj? Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say no, while Muhammad says yes. The difference turns on how we classify the purpose of the testimony—whether it is essentially negative or positive. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly evaluating indirect evidence in conditional oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the underlying legal problem
The condition is: 'If I do not perform Hajj this year, my slave is free.' The oath-maker claims he performed Hajj. Witnesses testify that he made a sacrifice (udhiyah) in Kufa. Does this testimony prove that he performed Hajj? The answer determines whether the condition is breached.
The primary act conditioned is Hajj, not sacrifice.
2
State the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: the slave is not freed because the testimony is considered negative in purpose. Muhammad: the slave is freed because the testimony is about a known act (sacrifice) that necessarily implies Hajj.
Both positions are from within the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
Muhammad argues that the testimony concerns a specific known act (sacrifice) which is intimately connected to Hajj; proof of the sacrifice makes the occurrence of Hajj nearly certain. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf respond that the testimony's purpose is to negate the non-performance of Hajj (a negative), not to establish the sacrifice itself. Since no one claimed the sacrifice, the testimony functions as a negative proof, which is generally not accepted in Hanafi law.
Key distinction: purpose of testimony vs. content of testimony.
4
Determine the preferred view and its significance
Imam Ibn al-Humam preferred Muhammad's view. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that if the owner later claims fulfillment of the condition, according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf the slave remains enslaved, while according to the preferred view the slave is freed.
The tarjih by Ibn al-Humam makes Muhammad's view the operative position for fatwa.

Retraction of Testimony After Delay: Hanafi Ruling and Proof
Explains ruling on retracting testimony after delay in zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on retracting testimony after delay in zina, explain the Quranic proof, and distinguish this case from retraction after confession (iqrar).

Core Idea
Delay in testimony reveals fisq, making retraction invalid; confession carries no hidden denial, so retraction is allowed.

This lesson examines a critical distinction in Hanafi law: the difference between retracting testimony and retracting a confession. When witnesses delay giving testimony in a hudud case like zina, their later retraction is not accepted because the delay itself taints their credibility. In contrast, a confessor can retract because he has no hidden denial. We will unpack the Quranic proof and the reasoning behind this ruling, and see how it differs from the Shafi'i position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is retracting testimony after a delay in a zina case. The ruling is that such retraction is invalid (batil) because the delay constitutes fisq, and a fasiq's testimony is rejected.
The text explicitly states that retraction after delay is not correct.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is the Quranic verse 'wa aqimu al-shahadata lillah' (establish testimony for Allah). The imperative 'aqimu' indicates obligation to testify promptly. Delay contradicts this command, making the testimony defective.
The verse is general, but the Hanafi interpretation emphasizes promptness.
3
Distinguish from iqrar (confession)
Retraction after confession is valid because no person is an enemy of his own life, and the hadd is a pure right of Allah that can be waived by the confessor. There is no hidden denial in confession, unlike testimony.
The text emphasizes that in testimony, the accused has a hidden denial that is overcome by witnesses; in confession, there is no such denial.
4
Analyze the refutation of Imam al-Shafi'i's analogy
Imam al-Shafi'i analogizes testimony to confession, but the Hanafi school rejects this because testimony involves a hidden denial and delay causes fisq, which does not apply to confession.
The text says 'analogizing this issue to confession is open to consideration' (محل نظر).
5
Synthesize the four factors
The text lists four elements: ta'khir (delay), shahadah (testimony), ruju' (retraction), and iqrar (confession). Understanding their interplay clarifies why testimony and confession differ in retraction rules.
Delay in testimony negates the possibility of valid retraction, whether due to internal corruption or concealment.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof explaining that retraction of testimony after delay in zina cases is invalid, and distinguishing this from retraction after confession (iqrar). The commentator first establishes that retracting testimony after delaying it is considered fisq (sinfulness) because the delay was for concealment or internal corruption, and a fasiq's testimony is rejected. The proof is the Quranic command 'wa aqimu al-shahadata lillah' (establish testimony for Allah), implying that testimony must be given promptly; delay prevents acceptance. In contrast, retraction after a confession (iqrar) is valid because no person harms themselves, and the hadd punishments for zina, drinking, and theft are pure rights of Allah, which can be waived by the person's own admission. The commentator then refutes Imam al-Shafi'i's analogy of this case to iqrar, arguing that the two are fundamentally different. To clarify, the text enumerates four factors: (1) delay, (2) testimony, (3) retraction, (4) confession. The commentator explains that delay, whether due to internal corruption or concealment, invalidates any subsequent retraction. Retraction in rights of Allah invalidates the hadd, while in rights of servants it is not accepted, and if it causes loss, the witnesses are liable. Confession and testimony differ because confession lacks hidden denial; testimony implies hidden denial. Therefore, retraction after testimony-based proof is contradictory and unacceptable, as it indicates either false accusation (tuhma) or fisq.

Key Terms
تاخیر (ta'khir): Delay in giving testimony, whether due to internal corruption (fasad al-batin) or concealment (pardah poshi). In this context, it is a factor that prevents acceptance of retraction.
شہادت (shahadah): Testimony given in court to prove a crime. When based on witnesses, it implies hidden denial by the accused, unlike confession.
رجوع (ruju'): Retraction of testimony after it has been given. In this case, it is invalid if preceded by delay, but valid in iqrar for rights of Allah.
اقرار (iqrar): Confession of a crime by the accused. It lacks hidden denial, so retraction from it is valid for hudud.

Da'wa in Hudud: Zina vs. Theft and the Four Madhahib
Summarizes four schools on testimony/iqrar and distinguishes zina from theft when victim absent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on hadd execution when the victim is absent and explain why testimony after delay is rejected while iqrar is accepted, with reference to the four madhahib.

Core Idea
Da'wa is a condition for theft but not for zina; mere illusion of shubha does not prevent hadd.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses two critical issues in hudud: the effect of the victim's absence on executing hadd, and the acceptance of testimony versus iqrar after a delay. The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between zina and theft based on the requirement of da'wa (claim), and then lays out a comparative analysis of four madhahib on the retraction of testimony and iqrar. Understanding these distinctions is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to procedural justice in criminal cases.

Since there is a difference between zina and theft: in zina, the absence of the woman does not give rise to a claim (da'wa), because da'wa is not a condition in zina. Therefore, after delay, the hadd will not be enforced like in iqrar. If it is doubted that despite the woman's absence, there is a possibility of a claim of shubha (suspicion) on her part, then the remedy is that possibility does not necessitate the ruling of existence of shubha, which would drop the hadd. Rather, here only the illusion (wahm) of shubha exists. This illusion of shubha can be given at most the status of shubha nushuz (lesser doubt), which is of a lower rank than shubha and is not considered. Whereas in theft, da'wa is a condition. Therefore, retraction after confession of theft drops the hadd of theft (amputation of hand), but he is liable for the property to the other. Similarly, the hadd of zina, wine-drinking, and theft are pure rights of Allah. Retraction after confession from them is valid. And delay prevents acceptance of testimony. While the hadd of qadhf and others are rights of servants. Retraction after confession from them is not valid. And delay is not a barrier in rights of servants. Therefore, delay is attributed to absence of da'wa. The ruling depends on the hadd that is purely from rights of Allah. Internal corruption (fasad al-batin) is such a hidden matter that it is not possible to be informed of it, so it is not present in every individual, like the measure of travel in qasr prayer; the existence of hardship is not necessary. Thus, the mentioned evidence (the hidden hardship in qasr prayer) makes it clear that theft is done secretly by deceiving the owner. It is necessary for the witness to inform immediately. By hiding the information of theft, the witness becomes a fasiq. Hence, the testimony of a fasiq is not credible. Therefore, just as from the beginning, change prevents acceptance of testimony, similarly after the judge's verdict, execution of hadd is also a barrier. Ruling on retraction from testimony in hudud with four school positions

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Retraction after testimony is valid for rights of Allah but not for rights of people — the nature of the right determines the ruling.

This passage from the Hidayah commentary addresses a critical distinction in Hanafi fiqh: the treatment of retraction (رجوع) from testimony in hudud cases. The ruling hinges on whether the prescribed punishment is a pure right of Allah (ḥuqūq Allāh) or involves rights of people (ḥuqūq al‑ʿibād). Guided Path

For rights of Allah, retraction from testimony or confession after it has been given is valid (صحیح). For rights of people, retraction is not valid.

Second ruling: unknown woman and the effect of confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why hadd is not imposed when witnesses do not know the woman, and identify the condition under which confession removes the doubt.

Core Idea
If witnesses cannot identify the woman, hadd is averted by doubt that she may be his wife or slave.

The second part of this passage addresses a different scenario: witnesses testify that a man committed zina with a woman they do not know. Identify the scenario

Conflicting Testimonies: Rape vs. Consensual Zina
Ruling on conflicting testimonies: rape vs. consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on conflicting testimonies regarding zina (rape vs. consent) and apply the Hanafi principle of averting hudud due to doubt.

Core Idea
When testimonies conflict on the nature of zina, hadd is averted from both per Abu Hanifa due to doubt, even if the act itself is agreed upon.

This passage delves into an intra-Hanafi dispute where four witnesses agree that zina occurred, but two claim it was rape and two claim it was consensual. The core question: does the hadd punishment apply to the man, the woman, both, or neither? Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi usul principle of 'dara al-hudud bi al-shubuhat'—avert hudud by doubts.

Guided Path
1
State the Scenario
Identify the facts: two witnesses testify that the man forcibly raped the woman (ikrāh), and two others testify that she consented (ṭawāʿiyah). The act of zina is agreed upon, but the nature (coercion vs. consent) is disputed.
The dispute hinges on whether the testimonies conflict on the subject (the act) or on a detail.
2
Identify the Two Positions
Abu Hanifa and Zufar hold that hadd is dropped from both. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Ṣāḥibayn) hold that only the man is subjected to hadd.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilāf.
3
Analyze the Ṣāḥibayn’s Proof
The Ṣāḥibayn argue that all witnesses agree on the obligating cause (al-mūjib) – the act of zina – so the man is liable. The additional claim of rape is just an extra criminal element, not a contradiction. For the woman, her consent is a condition for the hadd, and since witnesses disagree on that condition, it is not proven, so her hadd is dropped.
They treat the testimonies as partially consistent.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa’s Rebuttal
Abu Hanifa rebuts that zina is a single act involving both parties; conflicting descriptions make the entire testimony unreliable. Also, the witnesses of consent become slanderers (qādhifīn) if the woman was actually coerced, and the coercion witnesses become opponents (khaṣmayn) because a coerced woman’s hadd is dropped.
This creates doubt that averts the hadd entirely.
5
Determine the Thamarah and Mufta Bihi
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilāf): under Abu Hanifa’s view, both go free; under Ṣāḥibayn’s view, the man is punished. The muftā bihī (operative position) is Abu Hanifa’s, due to the principle of averting hudūd by doubts.
This is the relied-upon position in fatwa.

Hadd for Zina with Unknown Woman: Doubt and Confession
Ruling on hadd when witnesses do not know the woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that hadd penalties are repelled by doubt (shubha) and distinguish between testimony and confession in zina cases.

Core Idea
When witnesses cannot identify the woman, hadd is not imposed because she might be lawful to him; confession removes all doubt.

This passage from al-Hidayah examines a crucial exception in hudud law: what happens when witnesses testify to zina but cannot identify the woman? The Hanafi school applies the general principle that hadd penalties are dropped due to doubts (al-hudud tudra'u bi al-shubuhat). Here, the doubt arises from the possibility that the woman could be the man's wife or slave girl—relationships in which intercourse is lawful. The ruling clarifies the difference between external testimony and personal confession, where knowledge differs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is testimony (shahada) for zina where witnesses do not know the woman. The ruling is that the hadd penalty is not imposed on the man.
This is a specific application of a general principle.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the maxim 'al-hudud tudra'u bi al-shubuhat' (hadd penalties are repelled by doubts). This is a well-established principle in Hanafi usul.
The doubt here is the possibility (ihtimal) that the woman is lawful for him.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The witnesses' lack of knowledge creates a realistic possibility that the woman is the man's wife or concubine. This doubt is sufficient to prevent the hadd, because certainty is required for corporal punishment.
The text says 'rather that is the apparent [assumption]' – meaning we assume the best of the Muslim man.
4
Note the exception: confession
If the man himself confesses to the zina, the hadd is imposed because he knows his own relationship with the woman. The doubt is removed.
Confession provides certainty (yaqin), while testimony from outsiders does not when the woman is unidentified.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
This ruling is consistent across the Hanafi school; no disagreement is mentioned. It follows the established principle that hudud are suspended by any plausible doubt.
This is a straightforward application, not a contested issue.

Ruling on Testimony with Different Locations for Zina
Ruling when witnesses differ on the place of zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a difference in location among witnesses prevents the hadd for zina and explain the Hanafi position on punishment of the witnesses.

Core Idea
A single act requires four unanimous witnesses; differing locations break the testimony.

In cases of zina, Islamic law demands the highest standard of proof: four eyewitnesses to the act itself. When witnesses agree that zina occurred but give conflicting locations, the necessary unanimity on the specific act is lost. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on such a scenario and the disagreement with Imam Zufar regarding punishment of the witnesses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the testimony presented
Two witnesses testify to zina in Kufa; two others testify to the same couple in Basra. The time is the same but the locations differ.
2
Assess the completeness of the testimony
For hadd to be imposed, four witnesses must agree on a single act. Since zina in Kufa and zina in Basra are considered different acts due to location, neither act has the required four witnesses.
Location is integral to the act in legal consideration.
3
Apply the ruling on hadd
Because the nisaab (plurality) is not met for either act, the hadd punishment is dropped from the accused couple.
Hadd is repelled by any doubt (shubha).
4
Consider the fate of the witnesses

Ruling on retraction after stoning with disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a witness who retracts after the accused has been stoned for zina, contrast it with the Shafi'i and Zufari positions, and justify the Hanafi rebuttal.

Core Idea
A retracting witness in a zina case retrospectively becomes a false accuser, liable for both hadd al-qadhf and compensation for the part of the right he destroyed.

This passage from al-Hidayah deals with a delicate scenario: four witnesses testify against a married man for zina, leading to his execution by stoning. If one of them later retracts, a complex legal problem arises. The Hanafi school imposes a dual punishment — flogging for false accusation (qadhf) and payment of one-quarter of the diyah. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling and the disagreements both from outside and within the school.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two components of the Hanafi ruling: the retractor is flogged (hadd al-qadhf) and must pay a quarter of the diyah. Note the rationale: the remaining three witnesses accounted for three-quarters of the right, so the retractor caused a loss of one-quarter.
The diyah is blood money paid to the heirs of the executed man.
2

Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the retractor should be executed without any financial penalty. His proof is an analogy to witnesses in qisas (retaliation) cases: if they retract after execution, they are executed themselves.
The Hanafi response, promised in the Diyat chapter, distinguishes zina from qisas.
3

Zufar argues that no hadd should be imposed because if the retractor is considered to have accused a living person, the accusation is void by death; if he accused a dead person, the death was by judicial order, creating doubt (shubha).
Zufar's view is within the Hanafi school but rejected by the three senior imams.

The Hanafi majority responds that the retraction effectively converts the testimony into a false accusation (qadhf) of the deceased. Since the original testimony is annulled, the judicial ruling based on it is also annulled, leaving no doubt to block the hadd.
This differs from a third-party accusation after execution, where the ruling stands and the accuser is not considered chaste (muhsin) regarding the deceased.
5

The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf): Hanafi — flogging + diyah; Shafi'i — execution only; Zufar — no punishment. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad: flogging and payment.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and intra-madhhab dispute. It states the Hanafi position that when one of four witnesses in a zina case retracts after the accused has been stoned, the retracting witness is punished with hadd for qadhf (false accusation of zina) and must pay one quarter of the diyah. The diyah payment is because the retraction destroys one quarter of the established right (the blood of the executed person). Imam al-Shafi'i differs, holding that the retracting witness should be executed, not pay diyah, analogizing to witnesses in qisas. Among Hanafis, Imam Zufar argues that no hadd is due, because the accusation either concerned a living person (now dead, so hadd lapses) or a dead person (who was stoned by judicial order, creating doubt). The Hanafi response is that the retraction converts the testimony into qadhf of a dead person, and since the judicial proof (for zina) is nullified, the judge's ruling is also nullified retroactively, removing any doubt. Thus, the retractor is like someone who falsely accuses a muhsin (chaste person), so hadd is due. However, if a third party accuses the executed person, no hadd is due because the judge's ruling remains valid for that third party, making the accused non-muhsin in relation to them.

Retraction Before Stoning: All Witnesses Are Punished
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witness retraction before stoning execution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi majority position on the punishment of witnesses when one retracts before execution, and distinguish it from the minority views of Muhammad and Zufar.

Core Idea
When a zina witness retracts before stoning, all four are punished — their testimony reverts to qadhf.

This passage addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute about a critical moment in hudud cases: after the judge passes the stoning sentence but before it is carried out, one of the four witnesses retracts. The ruling affects both the accused (who is freed) and the witnesses. We will examine the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, who hold that all witnesses are liable, versus the minority opinions of Imam Muhammad and Imam Zufar, who restrict punishment to the retractor alone.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi majority position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two imams referred to as 'lahuma') for this scenario: all four witnesses receive hadd for qadhf, and the accused is freed.
Note that the majority position is the relied-upon view in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the minority views and their reasoning
Imam Muhammad holds that only the retracting witness is punished because the testimony was 'strengthened by the judgement' (qada). Imam Zufar also limits punishment to the retractor, but on the basis that the retractor's statement is not believed against the others.
Both Muhammad and Zufar reach the same outcome but via different legal reasoning.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response to Muhammad
The majority responds that 'execution (imda) is part of the judgement'. Since execution has not occurred, the connection between the ruling and the testimony is incomplete. Therefore, the retraction is similar to a retraction before any ruling, and all witnesses' statements remain qadhf.
Hence, the hadd is dropped from the accused as well.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to Zufar
The majority's proof ('lana') is that the witnesses' speech is originally qadhf and becomes testimony only by being connected to the judge's ruling. Since the connection is incomplete (execution pending), the original qadhf status remains for all, making all liable.
Zufar's view that the retractor is not believed against others is overridden by the principle that the entire testimony was not yet fully established.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical consequence: under the majority, all four witnesses receive hadd (80 lashes) for qadhf; under the minority, only the retracting witness receives hadd, while the others are free.
This distinction underscores the Hanafi emphasis on the procedural completeness of the judicial act.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the scenario where one of four zina witnesses retracts after the judge has passed the ruling of stoning but before it has been executed. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (and the text states 'lahuma', referring to them), all four witnesses must be punished with hadd for qadhf, and the accused is freed from the hadd of zina. Their reasoning is that the judgement ('imda') is part of the judicial process, and since the execution has not occurred, the retraction is considered to have happened before the judgement fully attached, similar to a retraction before any ruling. Therefore, all witnesses are punished because their statements are now considered qadhf. Imam Muhammad disagrees: he says only the retracting witness is punished, because the testimony was already confirmed by the judge's ruling ('bimujarrad al-qada'), so it cannot be nullified except with respect to the retractor himself. He draws an analogy to a retraction after execution, where only the retractor faces consequences. Imam Zufar also holds that only the retractor is punished, but for a different reason: the retractor's statement is not believed against the other witnesses. The Hanafi majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) respond that the witnesses' initial statements are fundamentally qadhf (accusation), and become legally valid testimony only when the judge's ruling is connected to them. Since the ruling was not fully executed, the connection is incomplete, thus the statements remain qadhf, and all four are liable for hadd. This ruling also explains why the accused's hadd is dropped: because the retraction undermines the proof, and the ruling for the accused is retroactively nullified.

شهادة (shahada): Testimony; in this context, it becomes 'testimony' only when the judge's ruling is connected to it.

Liability of Retracting Witnesses in Zina Testimony: The Case of Five Witnesses
Ruling on hadd and diyah when two of five witnesses retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hadd and diyah when two of five witnesses for zina retract, and apply the principle that legal effect depends on the remaining witnesses.

Core Idea
When two of five zina witnesses retract, each incurs hadd and jointly owe one-fourth diyah—the remaining three support three-fourths of the right.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a specific scenario: five witnesses testify to zina, then two retract. The ruling distinguishes between the first retraction (no liability if four remain) and the second (liability arises). The underlying principle is that the legal effect of testimony is measured by what remains, not the act of retraction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the number of witnesses and sequence of retractions
Start with five witnesses. Note that one retracts first, leaving four. Then a second retracts, leaving three.
The first retraction alone does not reduce the number below the required four.
2
Determine the legal effect of the first retraction
With four remaining, the full right (the hadd of the accused) is still proven. Hence, no liability on any witness.
The retractor does not incur hadd or diyah because his retraction does not weaken the proof.
3
Determine the legal effect of the second retraction
Now only three witnesses remain. They support only three-fourths of the right. Thus, the two retractors are liable for the lost one-fourth of the right.
Both retractors are liable, not just the second one, because their combined retractions caused the loss.
4
Apply the rule: liability depends on remaining witnesses
The principle: 'consideration is given to the remaining of the one who remains, not to the retraction of the one who retracted.' The retractors owe hadd (for qadhf) and jointly one-fourth of the diyah (blood money).
Each retractor receives 80 lashes (hadd qadhf) and shares the diyah payment proportionally.

Diyah Liability After Defective Witnesses: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah after execution with defective witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on diyah liability when muqizkin certify incompetent witnesses and evaluate the reasoning based on 'illat al-'illah vs shart.

Core Idea
Tazkiyah as 'illat al-'illah makes muqizkin liable; Sahibayn treat it as shart, placing diyah on bayt al-mal.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute where the practical consequence is substantial: who pays the blood money when an innocent man is executed based on defective witnesses? The disagreement turns on whether tazkiyah (certification of witnesses) is a cause or a condition for the acceptance of testimony. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping Hanafi usul al-fiqh in action.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their positions
Determine who the muqizkin are and which imams (Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold which view regarding diyah liability.
The muqizkin are those who certified the witnesses' uprightness before the Qadi.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's analogy
Understand why they analogize to witnesses of ihsan and conclude that diyah is on the treasury.
Their qiyas: witnesses of ihsan cause stoning but are not liable; similarly, muqizkin cause acceptance but are not liable.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's distinction
Focus on the concept of 'illat al-'illah (cause of the cause) and why tazkiyah is considered a cause, whereas ihsan is a pure condition (shart mahd).
A condition (shart) merely enables, while a cause ('illah) directly produces the effect.
4
Examine the scope of liability
Note that liability only arises if the muqizkin affirmed freedom and Islam; if they merely said 'they are upright' and the witnesses turn out to be slaves, there is no liability because a slave can be upright.
Also note that the witnesses themselves are not liable for diyah nor for hadd al-qadhf after the accused's death.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
State the practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the muqizkin pay diyah personally; under Sahibayn, the state pays from the treasury.
This can affect who is willing to serve as muqizkin.

Commentary
This passage presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding liability for diyah (blood money) when it is discovered after execution that the witnesses in a zina case were legally incompetent (slaves or Majus). The muqizkin are the individuals who verified the witnesses' uprightness ('adalah) before the Qadi. Abu Hanifa holds that the diyah is upon the muqizkin, meaning they must pay it to the victim's heirs, provided they retract their tazkiyah (i.e., admit they were mistaken or lying). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the diyah is upon the public treasury (bayt al-mal). Some say Sahibayn's view applies only if the muqizkin deliberately gave false tazkiyah. The Sahibayn analogize: just as witnesses who testify to the accused's ihsan (being muhsan, a condition for stoning) are not liable if they retract (diyah on treasury), so too the muqizkin are not liable. Abu Hanifa responds: tazkiyah is the very basis (cause of the cause, 'illat al-'illah) for the acceptance of testimony, so the ruling (execution) is attributed to it, unlike ihsan which is merely a condition (shart) not a cause. The passage then clarifies that the muqizkin's liability depends on whether they affirmed freedom and Islam (since those are necessary for witness competence). If they merely said 'they are upright' and the witnesses turn out to be slaves, there is no liability because a slave can be upright. The witnesses themselves are neither liable for diyah nor hadd: their speech did not amount to a valid testimony (since it was nullified), and the hadd of qadhf cannot be applied because the accused died (hadd does not transfer to heirs).

Key Terms
مُزَكِّي (muqizkin): The person(s) who perform tazkiyah, i.e., certify the witnesses' uprightness ('adalah) before the Qadi.
تَزْكِيَة (tazkiyah): The act of attesting to the moral and legal uprightness of witnesses, necessary for their testimony to be accepted.
إِحْصَان (ihsan): The condition of being muhsan (free, adult, Muslim, and having consummated a valid marriage), which is a prerequisite for stoning (rajm) for zina.

Liability for Diyah When Tazkiyah is Retracted: Imam Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah liability with 'illa/shart distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah liability when tazkiyah is retracted, and apply the distinction between 'illa and shart to determine the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The ruling is attributed to the 'illa, not just a condition; deliberate false tazkiyah makes the muzakki liable.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school regarding blood money (diyah) when witnesses for zina are later found disqualified after execution. The key difference is whether the muzakki's tazkiyah is an 'illa (cause) of the testimony's acceptance or merely a condition. This distinction determines whether the diyah falls on the muzakki personally or on the public treasury. We will explore the proofs and why Imam Abu Hanifa's position is considered preponderant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Understand that four witnesses testified for zina, the accused was executed, then the witnesses are found to be Magians or slaves, and the muzakkis (those who declared them upright) retract their tazkiyah.
The retraction is implicit when the witnesses are discovered disqualified.
2
State the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Imam Abu Hanifa: If the muzakki intentionally gave false tazkiyah, he pays diyah. Sahibayn: In all cases of false tazkiyah, diyah is on Bayt al-Mal.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof (qiyas to ihsan witnesses)
They analogize to witnesses who testify that the accused is muhsan (married) and later retract; in that case, diyah is on Bayt al-Mal. They argue that tazkiyah is similar praise.
Sahibayn see tazkiyah as merely a statement of virtue, akin to ihsan testimony.
4
Analyze Imam Abu Hanifa's proof (tazkiyah as 'illa of the 'illa)
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that tazkiyah is the 'illa of the 'illa for accepting testimony; the testimony is accepted because the muzakki declared the witnesses upright. Therefore, the ruling (diyah) is attributed to the muzakki's act, not to a mere condition.
He distinguishes ihsan: ihsan is a shart (condition) for the zina ruling, not an 'illa.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
Practical consequence: If the muzakki intentionally lied, he pays; otherwise, Bayt al-Mal pays. The text concludes that Imam Abu Hanifa's position is preponderant (راج) based on the 'illa/shart distinction.
This distinction is a key usul principle: rulings follow the 'illa, not the shart.

Shubha from Court Order: Qisas or Diyah?
Ruling on liability when shubha from a court order prevents qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of istihsan to determine liability (diyah vs qisas) when a court order creates a shubha, and distinguish the ruling when killing occurs before vs after the order.

Core Idea
A valid-looking court order creates shubha that prevents qisas, so killer pays diyah.

This case illustrates a classic clash between strict analogy (qiyas) and juristic preference (istihsan). Although the killer took a protected life, the apparent validity of the qadi's order at the time of the act introduces a shubha (doubt) that overrides the default qisas ruling. Understanding this interplay is essential for grasping when legal liability shifts from retaliation to compensation in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the default ruling
The killer struck the neck of a person whom the qadi had ordered to be stoned. By qiyas (analogy to a simple homicide), killing a protected soul without right demands qisas (retaliation).
2
Identify the shubha-creating factor
At the moment of killing, the qadi's order was apparently valid – the witnesses' flaw (being slaves) had not yet been discovered. This apparent validity creates a legitimate doubt (shubha) about the lawfulness of the killing.
3
Apply istihsan to override qiyas
The Hanafi rule: shubha prevents the application of hudud and qisas. Here, the shubha from the qadi's order means qisas is dropped, and the killer must pay diyah (blood money) instead.
Istihsan is the juristic preference that gives weight to the shubha over the analogy.
4
Compare with the case without shubha
If the killing occurred before the qadi's order, the testimony had not yet become a proof, so no shubha exists. Then qisas applies. This boundary clarifies when the exception operates.
5
Determine nature and payment of diyah
The diyah is wajib on the killer's own wealth because the killing was intentional ('amd), and the 'aqila (tribal guarantors) do not bear intentional killings. It is spread over three years, as it becomes due by the mere act of killing (not as a deferred penalty).
Payment structure: three annual installments.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Diyah shifts to the state treasury when executioners act under the Imam's command.

This passage addresses a delicate situation: a person is stoned to death under the qadi's order, but later it emerges that the witnesses were slaves (and thus disqualified). The question is who bears the blood money. The Hanafi ruling is clear: the diyah falls on the Bayt al-Mal. The reasoning hinges on legal attribution—since the stoning was carried out in obedience to the imam, the act is ascribed to him. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and distinguishes it from cases where the killer acts independently.

Guided Path
1

2

The text argues that because the stoners obeyed the imam's command, their action is legally attributed to him. This is the key principle: acting under lawful authority transfers liability.
Attribution is a standard Hanafi concept in cases of agency and command.
3
Contrast with the independent killing case
If someone strikes the neck of the accused (not following the stoning order), the act is not attributed to the imam; the killer is personally liable for diyah. This distinction is crucial.
The basis is whether the killer was acting in compliance with the imam's command.
4
Apply the rule to analogous cases
The principle can be extended to any judicially ordered punishment carried out by appointed persons. As long as the executors are obeying a valid order, liability rests with the state treasury.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on state responsibility for judicial errors.

Core Idea
Necessity to bear testimony permits looking at private parts despite general prohibition.

Witnesses in a zina case must be certain of actual penetration, which may require seeing the private parts. This lesson examines a Hanafi ruling that even if witnesses admit to intentionally looking, their testimony remains valid. The legal basis is the principle of darurah (necessity), which allows what is otherwise prohibited to the extent needed for establishing a hadd punishment.

1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is witnesses intentionally looking at private parts during zina testimony. The ruling is that their testimony is accepted (maqbula).

2
Locate the dalil
The dalil is the principle of darurah: necessity permits the prohibited act. The text states 'because looking is permissible for them due to the necessity of bearing testimony.' This is based on the broader shari'ah principle that necessities make prohibitions permissible.
The dalil is not a specific text but a general legal maxim (al-ḍarūrāt tubīḥu al-maḥẓūrāt).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The wajh al-dalalah is that the necessity of establishing hadd (theft, zina, etc.) justifies the otherwise prohibited act of looking. The witnesses' testimony protects the community from false accusations and ensures just punishment, so the need overrides the prohibition.
This applies strictly to the act of looking for testimony; other acts like touching remain prohibited.

Liability for Diyah When Stoning Under Imam's Order
Ruling with proof on diyah liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah when stoning is executed under the imam's order, explain the proof based on transfer of liability, and distinguish it from personal initiative cases.

Core Idea
Execution by imam's order shifts liability to the treasury.

This lesson examines a specific scenario: a person is stoned based on testimony, and later the witnesses are found to be slaves. The question is who bears the diyah. The Hanafi school holds that if the execution was carried out under the authority of the imam, the liability falls on the public treasury, not on the individuals who performed the stoning. This ruling is based on the principle that obeying a valid order transfers the legal attribution of the act to the one who issued the order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is the execution of stoning (rajm) based on testimony. The ruling is that diyah (blood money) is wajib (obligatory) on Bayt al-Mal (public treasury) when the witnesses are later found to be slaves.
This applies only when the execution was ordered by the imam or his deputy.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a rational argument (dalil aqli): the executors complied with the imam's order, so the act is legally attributed to the imam. The text states: 'because he complied with the order of the imam, so his act is transferred to him.'
This is a qiyas (analogy) based on the transfer of liability from the executor to the commander.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause (illa) is the act of carrying out a lawful order. Just as the imam's own act would be on the treasury, the act of his deputies is also on the treasury because they are tools of the imam's authority.
The text reinforces by stating that even if the imam himself stoned, diyah would still be on the treasury.
4
Distinguish the contrary case

The rejection of testimony is a completion of the hadd; if part of the hadd occurs after conversion, the rejection does not attach.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: if a kafir receives one lash of the hadd for qadhf, then converts to Islam, and then receives the remaining lashes, is his testimony rejected? The core revolves around whether the 'rejection of testimony' is a completion (tatimmah) that attaches as a description (sifah) to the entire hadd, and whether partial execution after conversion changes that attachment. Understanding this subtle reasoning is key to appreciating how Hanafi jurists treat punishments that span a change in legal status.

Identify the specific case: a non-Muslim is struck one lash for qadhf, then converts to Islam, then the remaining lashes are administered. The question is whether his testimony is accepted or rejected.
The issue is not about the validity of the hadd itself, but about a collateral consequence (rejection of testimony).
2

Note that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say testimony is accepted; Abu Yusuf says it is rejected. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The text declares the first view as more correct (asahh).

Understand the reasoning: the rejection of testimony is a tatimmah (completion) of the hadd, making it a sifah (description) of that hadd. Since part of the hadd is executed after conversion, the sifah does not fully attach because the hadd is not entirely a pre-Islamic punishment.

Abu Yusuf argues by analogy: the lesser part (one lash before Islam) follows the greater part (remaining lashes after Islam), so the entire hadd is considered post-Islam, and thus the rejection applies.
This uses the principle that the minority follows the majority (al-aqall tabi' li-l-akthar).

This tarjih reflects a cautious approach: do not impose a consequence unless conditions are fully met.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Converting to Islam after hadd al-qadhf restores a non-Muslim's testimony, but freeing a slave after hadd does not — because the non-Muslim had a prior testimony right, the slave did not.

You may wonder whether a person punished for false accusation (qadhf) can ever testify again. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a non-Muslim and a slave: both lose their testimony right after hadd, but conversion reopens it for the non-Muslim, whereas manumission does not for the slave. Guided Path

The passage presents two parallel situations: a non-Muslim punished for qadhf, and a slave punished for qadhf. Note the subsequent events: the non-Muslim converts to Islam, the slave is manumitted.

2

For the non-Muslim: testimony is rejected while he remains a kafir, but accepted after conversion. For the slave: testimony remains rejected even after manumission.

This is a khafiyyah (subtle) effective cause.

The non-Muslim's conversion is a special case because it changes his legal personality.
The Hanafi position on the free Muslim is that his testimony is permanently rejected.

Partial Hadd and Conversion: The Description Principle
Intra-Hanafi dispute on testimony after partial hadd and conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a kafir receives part of the hadd then converts, and explain the reasoning of the majority and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
The rejection of testimony is a description of the complete hadd, so it does not attach when part occurs after conversion.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about a specific scenario: a non-Muslim is punished for qadhf and receives one lash, then converts to Islam, and then receives the remaining lashes. The question is whether his testimony is accepted. The majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani) says yes, while Abu Yusuf says no. We will analyze the reasoning behind each position and the tarjih.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the exact scenario: a kafir receives part of the hadd (qadhf) before conversion and the remainder after. Determine whether his testimony is accepted after conversion.
2
State the majority Hanafi position
The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) hold that the testimony is accepted. Understand their reasoning: the rejection of testimony (radd al-shahada) is a completion (mutammim) of the hadd, making it a description (sifa) of the hadd. Since part of the hadd occurred after Islam, the description does not fully apply to the entire punishment, so the testimony is not rejected.
3
Identify the opposing view
Abu Yusuf holds that the testimony is rejected. His reasoning is based on the maxim 'the lesser follows the greater' (al-qallu yatba‘u al-kathīr). Since the majority of the lashes (79) were administered after conversion, the entire hadd is considered to have occurred after Islam, so the rejection applies.
4
Analyze the tarjih
The text declares the first opinion as more correct (al-ashah). The reason is that the rejection is contingent on the hadd being a single complete punishment; when part occurs before conversion, the description cannot be attached. The maxim of lesser following greater does not override the description principle in this context.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the majority view, the convert's testimony is accepted; under Abu Yusuf's view, it is rejected. Note the consensus that if the entire hadd is given after conversion, testimony is rejected.

If he is struck one lash for false accusation, then he accepts Islam, then the remaining (lashes) are struck, his testimony is permissible, because the rejection of testimony is a completion of the hadd, so it would be a description for it (the hadd). But the part (of the hadd) carried out after Islam is only part of the hadd, so the rejection of testimony is not a description for it. And from Abu Yusuf (it is narrated) that his testimony is rejected, because the lesser follows the greater. And the first (opinion) is more correct. Translation: If a kafir is struck one lash for falsely accusing, then he accepts Islam, then the remaining lashes are given, then his testimony is permissible. Because the rejection of testimony is a completion of the hadd, so it would be a description of the hadd. And the part carried out after Islam is part of the hadd, so the rejection of testimony is not a description for it. And from Imam Abu Yusuf, it is narrated that his testimony is rejected, because the lesser follows the greater. The first opinion is clearer. (And if after becoming Muslim the entire hadd is given, then by consensus the testimony will not be accepted.)

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute about a specific scenario: a non-Muslim who is punished for qadhf receives one lash, then converts to Islam, and then the remaining lashes (79) are administered. The question is whether his testimony is accepted. The first opinion (from Imam Muhammad or the majority) says yes: because the rejection of testimony is a 'description' (sifa) of the hadd, meaning it is a consequence that is attached to the hadd as a whole. If part of the hadd occurs after Islam, then the hadd is not entirely 'described' by the rejection, since the later part belongs to a different context (one who is now Muslim). Therefore, the rejection does not apply. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that the testimony is still rejected because the lesser part (the single lash before Islam) follows the greater part (the 79 lashes after Islam). Since most of the hadd occurred after Islam, the whole hadd is considered to have taken place after conversion, leading to rejection. The text declares the first opinion as more correct (al-ashah). The translation adds that if the entire hadd is given after conversion, then all agree testimony is rejected.

Key Terms
صفة الحد (ṣifat al-ḥadd): A description or attribute of the hadd; the rejection of testimony is considered a characteristic of the hadd punishment itself.
القل يتبع الكثير (al-qallu yatba‘u al-kathīr): A legal maxim meaning the lesser follows the greater; used by Abu Yusuf to argue that the smaller part before Islam is subsumed by the larger part after Islam.

Key Takeaway
When part of the hadd occurs after conversion, the rejection of testimony as a description of the hadd does not attach, so the convert's testimony is accepted – the mufta bihi view.
