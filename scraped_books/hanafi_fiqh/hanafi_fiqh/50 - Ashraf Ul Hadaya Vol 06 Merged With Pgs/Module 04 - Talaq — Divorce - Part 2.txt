Acknowledging Then Denying a Child: Li'an or Hadd?
Ruling on two scenarios of paternity denial and acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for both scenarios of paternity acknowledgment and denial, and explain why li'an becomes wajib or hadd applies.

Core Idea
Denial after acknowledgment triggers li'an; denial before acknowledgment triggers hadd; the child remains his in both.

This passage from Al-Hidayah deals with a husband who first acknowledges a child as his own, then later denies it. The Hanafi school holds that such denial after acknowledgment is an accusation of adultery against the wife, making li'an (mutual cursing) obligatory. Conversely, if he first denies and then acknowledges, he is considered to have contradicted himself, so the original penalty for false accusation (hadd) applies. In both cases, the child's lineage is established.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the order: (1) acknowledge then deny, (2) deny then acknowledge. The ruling differs based on the sequence.
2
Understand the legal effect of iqrar
In Hanafi law, once a man acknowledges paternity (iqrar), the lineage of the child becomes binding upon him. This acknowledgment is a legal admission that cannot be retracted.
Iqrar is a form of evidence; it establishes the fact conclusively.
3
Derive the consequence of denial
If he denies after acknowledgment, he is effectively accusing the mother of zina, making him a qadhif (false accuser). Li'an is prescribed to resolve this accusation. If he denies first and then acknowledges, his denial is a contradiction; the necessity for li'an falls away, and he is punished with hadd for qadhf.
The logic: denial after iqrar = qadhf → li'an; denial before iqrar = self-contradiction → hadd.
4
Note the child's status
In both cases, the child remains his lineage due to his acknowledgment, whether before or after denial. Li'an does not automatically sever lineage; it merely resolves the accusation.
The text emphasizes: 'And the child is his child in both cases because of his acknowledgment earlier or later.'

And whoever acknowledges a child then denies it, then he performs li'an because lineage became binding by his acknowledgment, and by denying after that he becomes an accuser, so he performs li'an. And if he denies it then acknowledges it, he is punished with hadd because when he belied himself, the li'an is void because it was [established] by necessity due to mutual contradiction, and the original ruling in it is hadd for qadhf. So when the contradiction is void, one proceeds to the original. And the child is his child in both cases because of his acknowledgment earlier or later. And li'an is valid without severing lineage just as it is valid without a child.

Commentary
This passage presents two scenarios regarding paternity acknowledgment and its denial. First: A man acknowledges a child as his own, then later denies it. In this case, by his initial acknowledgment, the lineage of the child becomes binding upon him. When he denies it, he effectively accuses the mother of zina, making him a qadhif. Therefore, li'an (mutual cursing) becomes obligatory. Second: He denies the child first, then later acknowledges it. Here, his acknowledgment after denial shows he is lying, so the necessity for li'an is removed. The original ruling, which is hadd for false accusation, applies, and he is punished with 80 lashes. In both cases, however, the child remains his because he affirmed the lineage either first or last. A key point: li'an is valid even without cutting off lineage, just as it is valid without a child being born (i.e., in case of pregnancy alone).

Key Terms
إِقْرَار (iqrar): An admission or acknowledgment of paternity or a fact.
نَفْي (nafy): Denial of paternity after acknowledgment.

Denial of Birth: No Hadd and No Li'an
Ruling when husband denies paternity and maternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband denies both paternity and maternity of a child, and explain why neither hadd nor li'an applies.

Core Idea
Denial of birth is not an accusation of zina; therefore no hadd and no li'an.

In marital disputes, a husband may deny a child's paternity. If he explicitly accuses his wife of adultery, the law prescribes li'an and hadd. But this passage addresses a specific statement: 'Not my son nor your son.' Why does this statement trigger neither punishment? The answer lies in the precise meaning: he denies the child's birth from her altogether, not her chastity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement
Read the husband's statement carefully: 'Not my son nor your son.' Note that he says 'your son' instead of 'your child', but the meaning is clear.
2
State the ruling
The passage gives the ruling: no hadd (fixed punishment for false accusation) and no li'an (mutual imprecation).
This is a straightforward legal outcome.
3
Understand the reasoning
The husband denied the child's birth (الولادة) entirely. He did not accuse his wife of zina. For qadhf (slander) to apply, the accusation must be of zina explicitly or by clear implication. Denying birth does not imply zina because the child could have been adopted, found, etc.
Contrast: if he says 'this child is not mine, she committed zina', that would be different.
4
Apply the distinction
Test your understanding: if a husband says 'I am not the father, you are an adulteress' – that is an accusation and triggers li'an. But if he says 'this child came from nowhere, it is not yours' – that is denial of birth, not accusation.
The key is whether the statement imputes zina to the wife.

And if he says 'Not my son nor your son,' then no hadd and no li'an, because he denied the birth, and by that he does not become an accuser.
Source pages p. 205

Commentary
This is a concise ruling: if a husband says to his wife regarding a child, 'It is not my son nor your son,' he is denying that the child was born from her at all, not accusing her of zina. Since he is not imputing zina to her, he does not become a qadhif. Therefore, neither the fixed punishment (hadd) nor the mutual imprecation (li'an) is applicable. The key point is that for qadhf to apply, the accusation must be of zina specifically; denying birth is not that.

Key Terms
قَاذِف (qadhif): One who accuses a chaste person of zina.
