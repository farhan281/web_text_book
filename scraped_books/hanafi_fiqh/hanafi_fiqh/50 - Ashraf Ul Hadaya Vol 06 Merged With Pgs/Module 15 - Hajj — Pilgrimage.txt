Oath to Walk to the Kaaba: Obligation of Hajj/Umrah on Foot or with Dam
Ruling on oath to walk to the Kaaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when someone swears to walk to the Kaaba and explain the role of custom (urf) and tradition (athar) in overriding analogy.

Core Idea
An oath to walk to the Kaaba obligates Hajj/Umrah on foot, or riding with a dam — based on custom, not qiyas.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: a person declares an oath to walk to the Kaaba. Does such an oath create a binding obligation? The Hanafi school, drawing on a tradition from Ali ibn Abi Talib and prevailing custom, determines that it does — requiring either walking or a compensatory sacrifice. This highlights the school's method of giving weight to customary practice and transmitted precedent even when strict analogy would suggest otherwise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath expression
The key Arabic phrase is 'علي المشي إلى بيت الله' or 'علي المشي إلى الكعبة'. Recognise this as a common vow in the Hanafi context.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The text states: 'فَعَلَيْهِ حَجَّةٌ أَوْ عُمْرَةً مَاشِيًّا' – he must perform one Hajj or one Umrah on foot. An alternative is to ride and sacrifice a dam.
The riding option reflects flexibility but requires compensation.
3
Understand the qiyas counter-argument
Analogy (qiyas) would suggest nothing is binding because walking to the Kaaba is not an inherently obligatory act of worship (قربة واجبة). The text acknowledges this objection.
This sets up the Hanafi preference for istihsan over qiyas.
4
Recognize the Hanafi proofs
The school relies on: (1) an athar (tradition) from Ali ibn Abi Talib, and (2) common custom ('urf) among people who treat this expression as obligating Hajj/Umrah.
Custom makes the act effective in legal consideration, following the principle 'al-ma'ruf 'urfan ka al-mashrut shartan'.

Commentary
This passage begins a new chapter on oaths related to Hajj, prayer, and fasting. The ruling is that if a person, whether inside the Kaaba or elsewhere, says 'Walking to the House of Allah or the Kaaba is obligatory upon me,' then he is obligated to perform one Hajj or one Umrah on foot. However, he has the option to ride instead, but then he must offer a blood sacrifice (dam) as compensation. The text explains that qiyas (analogy) would suggest nothing is binding because walking to the Kaaba is not an inherently obligatory act of worship. However, the Hanafi school relies on a tradition (athar) from Ali ibn Abi Talib and on the common custom ('urf) of people, who use this expression to obligate Hajj or Umrah. Therefore, the oath is considered binding. This is similar to saying 'Visiting the House on foot is upon me,' which would also obligate a walking visit or a sacrifice if riding. The passage highlights istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas, based on transmitted precedent and custom.

Key Terms
عرف (urf): Common custom or practice among people that is considered in legal rulings.
دم (dam): A blood sacrifice (usually a sheep or goat) required as expiation for certain violations in Hajj or for compensation in oaths.
أثر (athar): A tradition or report from a Companion, here specifically from Ali ibn Abi Talib.

Conditional Freeing of Slave on Non-Performance of Hajj
Intra-Hanafi ruling on conditional freeing with testimony of sacrifice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether testimony of sacrifice proves non-performance of Hajj for a conditional freeing, and state the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf versus Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
Testimony of a known act implying a negative is treated as negative testimony, which is not accepted in Hanafi law.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement about the value of circumstantial evidence in a conditional vow. The case involves a person who swears to free his slave if he does not perform Hajj, then later claims the condition is met, bringing witnesses who testify that he sacrificed in Kufa—implying he was not in Mecca. The question is whether such testimony proves the negative condition. The dispute reveals how the Hanafi school treats testimony that aims to establish a negative ruling, a critical principle in judicial evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the condition
Understand the scenario: a man says 'My slave is free if I do not perform Hajj this year.' He later claims his slave is free and brings two witnesses who testify that he sacrificed in Kufa that year.
The testimony is about a positive act (sacrifice) but is used to prove the negative condition (non-Hajj).
2
State the dispute between the Hanafi imams
Imam Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that the slave is not freed because the testimony is essentially negative. Imam Muhammad rules the slave is freed because the testimony is about a known positive act.
The difference hinges on whether the testimony is considered direct proof of the condition or indirect negative proof.
3
Analyze each side's proof
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf argue that the purpose of the testimony is to negate the Hajj, not to establish the sacrifice (no claimant for the sacrifice), so it is like testifying to a pure negative, which is not accepted. Muhammad argues that the sacrifice is a known fact, and from its necessity, the condition (non-Hajj) is realized.
Both sides use qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) to analogize to other cases of negative testimony.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: slave remains in bondage. Under Muhammad: slave is freed.
Ibn al-Humam preferred Muhammad's view, but the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the school is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on a conditional freeing of a slave linked to non-performance of Hajj. The case: A man says, 'If I do not perform Hajj this year, my slave is free.' He then claims his slave is free and brings two witnesses who testify that he sacrificed in Kufa that year. The question is whether this testimony proves the condition (non-Hajj) was fulfilled. Imam Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the slave is not freed because the testimony essentially seeks to prove a negative (that he did not perform Hajj) by circumstantial evidence of sacrifice. They argue that the purpose is to negate the right (i.e., to show Hajj was not performed), not to establish the sacrifice per se, since no one is claiming the sacrifice. Testimony on pure negation is not accepted in Islamic law. Therefore, the condition is not proven, and the slave remains unfreed. Imam Muhammad, however, rules that the slave is freed because the testimony is about a known act (the sacrifice in Kufa) which necessarily implies that he did not perform Hajj (since he was in Kufa for sacrifice, not in Mecca for Hajj). Ibn al-Humam preferred Muhammad's view. The commentary clarifies that the difference hinges on whether the testimony is considered as direct proof of the condition or as indirect negative proof.

شهادة (shahada): Testimony in court; evidence given by witnesses
تضحية (tadhiyah): Sacrifice (offering an animal) usually on Eid al-Adha

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Oaths for Hajj/Umrah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath phrases for Hajj/Umrah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi intra-madhhab dispute on whether saying 'walking towards Haram' obligates Hajj/Umrah, and identify the proofs of each side.

Core Idea
In oaths, customary usage (urf) determines obligation; literal wording alone is insufficient.

This passage from Hidayah presents an internal Hanafi debate between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Sahibayn) regarding the effect of certain oath formulations. The core question: when someone says 'walking towards the Haram is obligatory on me,' does that bind him to perform Hajj or Umrah? The answer hinges on how we interpret the words—by their literal meaning or by customary usage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
The act is an oath or declaration that 'walking towards a specific place is obligatory'. The disputed ruling is whether this makes Hajj or Umrah obligatory.
The places mentioned include the House of Allah, the Haram, Masjid al-Haram, and Safa and Marwah.
2
State the position of Imam Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa holds that none of these phrases obligate Hajj or Umrah. His reasoning is that such wording is not conventionally used to mean entering ihram.
He relies on urf (customary usage) and literal meaning.
3
State the position of Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)
Sahibayn hold that phrases mentioning 'Haram' or 'Masjid al-Haram' do obligate Hajj or Umrah, because these terms include the Kaaba by physical contiguity. However, 'Safa and Marwah' do not because they are separate.
Their proof is based on physical connection (ittisal).
4
Examine the proof of each side
Abu Hanifa: the word 'walking' is not placed for ihram, nor is it customary. Sahibayn: 'Haram' linguistically includes the Kaaba, so the oath applies to Hajj/Umrah.
Both arguments are rational (dalil aqli) based on language and custom.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If someone makes such an oath, according to Abu Hanifa nothing is required; according to Sahibayn, Hajj or Umrah becomes wajib if 'Haram' or 'Masjid al-Haram' is mentioned.
The text does not give a tarjih (preference) between the two.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about oaths related to Hajj/Umrah. The issue: if someone swears or declares that 'going towards the Haram' is obligatory on him, does that make Hajj/Umrah obligatory? Imam Abu Hanifa holds that it does not, because the word 'walking' (chalna) is not conventionally used for ihram; customary usage (urf) is the basis. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that 'Haram' includes the Kaaba due to physical contiguity, so the oath obligates Hajj/Umrah. The author explains the reasoning of each side. Abu Hanifa's position relies on qiyas (analogy) based on the literal meaning not being intended in custom. The dispute shows how Hanafi usul al-fiqh treats urf and haqiqah in oaths.

Key Terms
متعارف (mutārif): Conventionally known, customary usage (urf) in legal contexts.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, customary usage (urf) plays a crucial role in interpreting oaths; merely mentioning 'Haram' does not automatically obligate Hajj/Umrah unless the phrase conventionally implies entering ihram.
