Mahr Waiver in Ambiguous Talaq: Imam Muhammad vs. Sahibayn
Analogy from manumission to mahr in ambiguous talaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the mahr waiver fractions for each wife in ambiguous talaq according to Imam Muhammad and the Sahibayn, and explain the practical difference.

Core Idea
Fractions of mahr waived depend on the wife's position; Imam Muhammad and Sahibayn differ on the incoming wife's share.

In the previous lesson, we learned how ambiguous manumission leads to fractional freedom for slaves. The same analogy applies to divorce (talaq) when a husband ambiguously divorces one of several non-consummated wives and dies before clarifying. This passage from al-Hidayah presents the fractions according to Imam Muhammad and notes a dispute with Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply qiyas and recognize thamarah al-khilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The husband says to his non-consummated wives (ghayr madkhulat) something like 'one of you is divorced' and dies before specifying which one. The mahr (dower) is normally half for a divorced non-consummated wife, but here the ambiguity reduces the claim.
Non-consummation is key; consummated wives would get full mahr.
2
Recall the manumission analogy
In the earlier ambiguous manumission case, if a master said 'one of you is free' to three slaves, the fractions were: outgoing slave 1/2 freedom, standing slave 3/4, incoming slave 1/4 (Imam Muhammad) or 1/2 (Sahibayn). In divorce, the mahr waiver (sacut) follows the same pattern: each wife 'loses' a fraction of her mahr.
The 'loss' corresponds to the portion of freedom the slave would have gained.
3
Apply Imam Muhammad's fractions
Imam Muhammad: outgoing wife loses 1/4 of her mahr, standing wife loses 3/8 (half of her 3/4 freedom? Actually, the text says 'standing loses three-eighths' – this is interpreted as 3/8), incoming wife loses 1/8. These are half of the slave freedom fractions because mahr waiver is half of the freedom proportion (since non-consummated talaq gives half mahr).
Check: outgoing slave freed 1/2 -> wife loses 1/4; standing slave freed 3/4 -> wife loses 3/8; incoming slave freed 1/4 -> wife loses 1/8.
4
Apply the Sahibayn's alternate view
According to Sahibayn, the incoming wife loses 1/4 instead of 1/8. This mirrors their view that the incoming slave gets 1/2 freedom. So the disagreement directly carries over from the manumission case.
The text also mentions a report that Sahibayn agree with Imam Muhammad – requires further verification.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In estate distribution, the incoming wife's mahr claim differs: under Imam Muhammad she gets 7/8 of her mahr (loses 1/8), under Sahibayn she gets 3/4 (loses 1/4). This affects the shares of all heirs and the total estate.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi; both views have textual support, but the mufta bihi is not specified here.

Commentary
This passage extends the earlier principle of ambiguous manumission to the case of divorce. If a husband says to his wives (who are non-consummated, i.e., he has not had intercourse with them) something like 'one of you is divorced,' and he dies before clarifying, then the mahr (dower) of each wife is partially waived. The text gives the specific fractions: the wife who leaves (analogous to the slave who left) loses a quarter of her mahr; the one who remains standing loses three-eighths? (the text says 'خَلَقَةُ الْمَائِهِ' which the Urdu commentary interprets as three-eighths); and the one who enters loses one-eighth. This is according to Imam Muhammad. However, there is a dispute: according to Sahibayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), the incoming wife loses a quarter (not an eighth). The text says there is a difference of opinion on whether this is Imam Muhammad's view exclusively or also the view of the two. The full details are referenced in the book al-Ziyadat.

النابية (al-nabiyah): The wife who remains standing (not leaving, not entering). The text has 'خَلَقَةُ الْمَائِهِ' which is interpreted as 3/8.
الداخلة (al-dakhilah): The wife who enters (incoming) – furthest from the door.

Conditional Manumission: Slave Becomes Ma'dhun, Master Compelled
Ruling with proof on conditional manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional manumission and explain why the master is compelled to accept payment, using the distinction between ta'liq and mu'awadah.

Core Idea
A suspended manumission conditioned on payment is valid; the slave becomes ma'dhun, and the master is compelled to accept the payment at maturity.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a crucial question: when a master tells his slave 'If you pay me 1000 dirhams, you are free,' what is the legal status of the slave before payment, and what happens when he brings the money? The Hanafi school distinguishes between the initial suspension (ta'liq) and the eventual compensation (mu'awadah), leading to unique rulings that balance the interests of both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State the initial ruling: the master's conditional statement is valid, and the slave becomes a ma'dhun (authorized to earn and trade) by implication.
The slave is not a mukatab; this is a unilateral suspension, not a bilateral contract.
2
Understand the effect of ma'dhun status
Explain that becoming ma'dhun permits the slave to engage in trade and acquire property; earnings after the condition belong to him, not the master.
This is crucial for determining ownership of the payment amount.
3
Examine the dispute with Imam Zufar
Imam Zufar held that the master cannot be compelled to accept payment, analogizing it to oaths where no right exists before the condition is fulfilled.
Zufar's view represents a strict qiyas approach.
4
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal using istihsan
The majority Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) uses istihsan: the statement is a compensation in purpose, so at the time of payment it is treated as a sale-like exchange, compelling the master to accept.
This dual nature (ta'liq in wording, mu'awadah in purpose) resolves the conflict.
5
Apply to sub-cases
The text extends the principle: partial payment compels acceptance but does not free the slave until full payment; earnings before the condition belong to the master, earnings after belong to the slave.
These details show the practical operation of the ruling.

He said: If he suspends his manumission upon payment of money, it is valid and he becomes a ma'dhun. That is like him saying: If you pay me one thousand dirhams, you are free. The meaning of his saying 'valid' is that he becomes free at the time of payment without becoming a mukatab, because it is explicit in suspending freedom upon payment, although there is the meaning of compensation in the end, as will be explained if Allah wills. Commentary
This passage from the Hanafi text (al-Hidayah) presents a ruling on manumission conditional upon payment. The passage is a ruling_with_proof, where Imam al-Quduri states that if a master suspends his slave's freedom upon payment of a specific amount, it is valid and the slave becomes a ma'dhun (authorized to engage in trade). The condition is typically expressed as 'If you pay me one thousand dirhams, you are free.' This is not a contract of kitabah (a deferred payment contract for freedom) because it is a conditional suspension, not a binding contract. However, due to the element of exchange (master gets money, slave gets freedom), it resembles compensation in its outcome. The legal reasoning balances both aspects: initially, it is treated as a conditional suspension to protect the master's right to sell the slave and control his earnings before payment; at the moment of payment, it becomes a compensation to protect the slave from deception, compelling the master to accept the money. Imam Zufar disagreed, arguing by analogy to oaths that the master cannot be compelled because no right exists before the condition is fulfilled. But the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and his disciples) relied on istihsan (juristic preference) considering the purpose and the slave's interest. The text further details sub-rulings: if the slave earns money before the suspension, that money belongs to the master and the slave must pay from his own funds; if earned after, the slave keeps it because he is authorized. The word 'in' restricts the offer to the session, while 'idha' makes it ongoing. The analogy with hibah bi-shart al-'iwad (gift with condition of compensation) is drawn.

Key Terms
تعليق العتق (ta'liq al-'itq): Suspending the manumission of a slave upon the occurrence of a future condition, such as payment of money.
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave who is permitted by the master to engage in trade and acquire property, though still owned by the master.
كتابة (kitabah): A contract between master and slave whereby the slave pays a sum in installments and becomes free upon completion, a permanent contract of manumission by compensation.
معاوضة (mu'awadah): Compensation; an exchange of value, e.g., money for freedom, akin to a sale.
إذن دلالة (idhn dalalah): Implied permission; the master's statement indicates permission for the slave to earn and trade, even if not explicit.

Definition and Ruling of Umm Walad
Defines umm walad and states prohibition of sale/gift with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of umm walad, explain the prohibition of selling her, and identify the proof from hadith and the Hanafi reasoning of juz'iyya.

Core Idea
The umm walad cannot be sold: her child's birth establishes a partial connection that necessitates deferred freedom and immediate sale prohibition.

This passage from Ashraf ul Hadaya introduces the concept of umm walad—a slave woman who bears her master's child. The author establishes that she becomes free upon the master's death but is immediately protected from sale or gift. The ruling is supported by a prophetic hadith and a unique Hanafi argument based on the inseparability of fluids (juz'iyya).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is selling an umm walad. The ruling is that it is haram (prohibited). Also, giving her away as a gift is prohibited.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'Her child has freed her' (أَعْتَقَهَا وَلَدُهَا). This is a direct textual proof for the prohibition of sale.
Hadith is reported from Ibn Abbas and others; transmitted in Ibn Majah, Dar Qutni, Hakim.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'a'taqaha' is informative (خبر), indicating that the child effects her emancipation. Emancipation implies that she is no longer a commodity to be sold; thus sale is prohibited. However, freedom is not immediate—only the prohibition is immediate.
The Hanafi usul principle: amr from the Prophet indicates obligation; here it is informative but carries legal effect.
4
Understand the Hanafi logical reasoning (juz'iyya)
The author explains that through the child, a partial connection (juz'iyya) occurs between the master and the slave woman because their fluids mix inseparably. This connection makes sale impermissible, similar to prohibitions due to affinity. After birth, the connection remains legally (hukman) but not physically, so freedom is deferred to the master's death.
This reasoning illustrates the Hanafi method of ta'lil (causation) using a subtle analogy.
5
Apply the ruling to part-ownership
If the master owns only part of the slave woman (e.g., shares), the same prohibition applies because istilad is indivisible—it is a branch of lineage, and lineage cannot be split.
This shows the school's consistency: the effect (prohibition) follows the cause (istilad) even when ownership is partial.

Chapter of Umm Walad. If the slave woman gives birth from her master, she becomes his umm walad. It is not permissible to sell her nor to give her into possession due to his saying peace be upon him: 'Her child has freed her.' He informed of her emancipation, so some of its obligations are established, which is the prohibition of sale. And because a partial connection (juz'iyya) occurs between the man who has intercourse and the woman with whom intercourse is done through the child; the two fluids have mixed such that distinction between them is impossible, as known in the prohibition of affinity. However, after separation, the partial connection remains legally (hukman) not in reality, so the cause becomes weak, thus it necessitates a deferred ruling to after death. The legal continuation of partial connection is with regard to lineage, and it comes from the male side; thus freedom is established for their right, not for women. Even if a free woman owns her husband and has given birth from him, he does not become free upon her death. The establishment of a deferred right of freedom establishes the right of freedom in the present, so selling and removing her from ownership (except to freedom now) is prohibited, but it necessitates her freedom after his death. Similarly, if part of her is owned by him, because istilad is not divisible; it is a derivative of lineage and is considered by its origin.

Commentary
This passage states the core ruling of the Chapter on Istilad: a slave woman who bears a child from her master immediately becomes an 'umm walad' (mother of the master's child). The primary legal consequence is that she can no longer be sold or given away as a gift. The proof is the hadith 'Her child has freed her' (reported from Ibn Abbas and others), indicating that the child causes her eventual freedom. The author then provides a rational explanation: through the child, a partial connection (juz'iyya) is established between the master and the slave woman because their fluids mix inseparably, similar to the prohibition of marriage by affinity. However, this connection weakens after birth, so the freedom is deferred to the master's death. During the master's life, she remains a slave but cannot be transferred. If the master owns only part of her (e.g., a portion of shares), the same ruling applies because istilad is indivisible – it is a branch of lineage, and lineage cannot be split. The commentary also narrates stronger hadiths and reports of ijma' among the Companions that selling an umm walad is prohibited.

Key Terms
ام ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who has borne a child to her master, with the child's lineage established to the master.
استيلاد (istilad): The act of a master having sexual intercourse with his slave woman resulting in a child whose lineage is acknowledged.
جزئية (juz'iyya): A partial connection or union between the master and the slave woman through the child, used as a basis for the prohibition of sale.

Master's Rights Over Umm Walad
Lists permissible actions for the master regarding umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four permissible actions a master can take with his umm walad and state the legal basis for these permissions.

Core Idea
The master's ownership over umm walad remains intact, allowing use, hire, and marriage.

The umm walad enjoys a protected status — she cannot be sold or given away. Yet she remains under the master's ownership. This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies that the master does not lose authority over her. He may still have intimate relations with her, employ her, hire her out to others, or marry her off to another man. Understanding these limits is essential to grasp the unique legal position of the umm walad in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the set of permissible actions
From the passage, list the four actions: wat' (intercourse), istikhdam (service), ijarah (hiring out), and tazwij (marriage).
2
Find the legal basis
The passage states 'because ownership over her remains' (li-anna al-milka fiha qa'im). This is the central reason.
Ownership is the key.

Compare with mudabbara
The master's rights over umm walad are likened to those over a mudabbara. Both cannot be sold but can be used similarly.
4
Note what is excluded
The passage does not mention selling or gifting. Those are prohibited elsewhere.
Selling is not among the rights.

Recognize the unanimity

The Hanafi Requirement of Acknowledgment for Umm Walad's Child Lineage
Dispute on lineage of child from umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school requires the master's acknowledgment for the lineage of an umm walad's child and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Without the master's acknowledgment, the child of an umm walad is not linked to him — ownership creates a barrier to automatic lineage.

This lesson examines a key disagreement between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on the establishment of a child's lineage when born to an umm walad (a slave woman who has borne her master's child). At the core is whether intercourse with a slave automatically attributes the child to the master or whether explicit acknowledgment is required. We will analyze the rationale of each school and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position
Identify the core ruling: the child's lineage is not established unless the master acknowledges the child. Note that this is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position in the Hanafi school.
No acknowledgment = no paternity.
2
Identify the Shafi'i Objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that lineage should be automatically established through intercourse, just as it is through a marriage contract—in fact, intercourse is more directly linked to procreation.
3
Follow the Shafi'i Proof (Qiyas)
The Shafi'i uses analogy: if lineage is established by marriage contract, then by intercourse (which is the very means of procreation) it should be established even more so.

Acquiring Another's Slave-Girl Through Marriage: Hanafi vs Shafi'i on Umm al-Walad Status
Hanafi ruling with proof and Shafi'i disagreement on umm al-walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain and evaluate the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on whether a slave-girl becomes umm al-walad when a man marries her, has a child, then later becomes her owner.

Core Idea
The establishment of nasab through valid marriage creates juz'iyyah, making the slave-girl umm al-walad even if the child was conceived while she was another's property.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute regarding the acquisition of umm al-walad status. The Hanafi school holds that if a man marries another's slave-girl and has a child, then later becomes her owner (e.g., by purchase), she becomes his umm al-walad. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that the child was conceived while the mother was a slave, so the mother cannot become umm al-walad. The dispute hinges on the legal cause ('illah) for umm al-walad status and the role of nasab (lineage).

Guided Path
1

Identify the ruling as given in the text: if a man marries another's slave-girl, has a child, then later owns her, she becomes his umm al-walad. This holds even if he initially owned her and then re-acquired her after a claim.
Note the two scenarios mentioned.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that she does not become umm al-walad in the first scenario, and he has two opinions in the second scenario. His reasoning is based on the child's status at conception.

3

Al-Shafi'i argues that the child was conceived when the mother was a slave (raqiq), so the child was a slave at that moment. Umm al-walad status requires the child to be free at conception because the child is a part of the mother (juz'), and a part cannot differ from the whole. He analogises to a child from zina.
The analogy to zina is central to his argument.
4

The Hanafi response is that the cause for umm al-walad is juz'iyyah (partial-whole relationship) established through nasab. Through valid marriage, nasab is proven, which creates juz'iyyah. This overrides the child's status at conception. Zina is distinguished because no nasab is established; the child is freed only as a direct biological part without legal lineage.
The key point: nasab is the medium for juz'iyyah.
5
Determine the practical difference
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf): In Hanafi law, the woman becomes umm al-walad and is freed upon the master's death. In Shafi'i law, she remains a slave even after being owned by the father of her child.
This affects the woman's status and the child's freedom.

The child is free from conception; father owes son only the maidservant's value, not child's value or 'uqr.

When Two Masters Claim the Same Child: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Dispute on whether both co-owners can claim paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dual paternity of a child born to a jointly-owned slave girl and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument based on physiognomy.

Core Idea
Two co-owners can both be legal fathers of the same child, despite biological impossibility, based on Companion practice.

Imagine you and your business partner jointly own a slave girl. She gives birth to a child. You both claim the child is yours. Biologically, a child can have only one father. Yet the Hanafi school, following the practice of Caliph Umar and other Companions, rules that the child's lineage is established from both of you. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind this apparently paradoxical ruling and contrasts it with the Shafi'i position that requires a physiognomist to decide.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal scenario
Understand the exact facts: a slave girl jointly owned by two partners gives birth; both partners claim paternity. The key condition is that the child was conceived while the slave girl was owned by both.
The ruling only applies when conception occurs during joint ownership; if one partner sells his share after birth, the ruling may differ.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its basis
The Hanafi ruling: the child's lineage is established from both partners, with mutual inheritance rights. The basis is the letter of Umar ibn al-Khattab to the judge Shurayh, which was affirmed by the presence of the Companions, indicating consensus.
The Hanafi school treats this as a legal ruling based on shubhah (plausibility), not biological fact.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i objection and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that since a child cannot be created from two fluids, attributing paternity to two men is impossible. He relies on the practice of physiognomists (qā'ifah) who examine facial features to determine lineage, citing the case of Usamah where the Prophet approved a physiognomist's statement.
Al-Shafi'i views the physiognomist as a decisive tool in ambiguous paternity cases.
4
Examine the Hanafi response to the objection
The Hanafi school responds by pointing to the actions of Umar and Ali. Umar himself summoned three physiognomists; when they agreed the child resembled both partners, he still ruled the child belonged to both, not one. The school argues that the Prophet's approval in Usamah's case was specific to that situation, not a general rule. The Companions' practice establishes a legal principle that overrides biological impossibility.
The Hanafi response demonstrates that shubhah (legal semblance) suffices for lineage, avoiding the impracticality of denying paternity to both.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
The two schools differ in outcome: Hanafi – child inherits from both and both inherit from child; if one owner dies, child becomes solely the son of the survivor. Shafi'i – physiognomist determines single fatherhood; the other partner has no claim.
The Hanafi ruling creates a legal fiction to preserve the child's lineage and avoid statelessness.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding a child born to a slave girl jointly owned by two partners. The Hanafi position, derived from the letter of Umar to Shurayh and the practice of the Companions, is that if both partners claim the child, the child's lineage is established from both of them. Consequently, the child inherits from both and they inherit from him. If one dies, the child becomes exclusively the son of the surviving partner. Imam al-Shafi'i, however, argues that since a child cannot biologically have two fathers, the matter should be referred to a physiognomist (qā'if) who examines facial features to determine resemblance. Al-Shafi'i cites the incident of Usamah ibn Zayd, where the Prophet was pleased with the physiognomist's statement. The Hanafi response includes the precedent of Umar's judgment in a similar case, where after consulting three physiognomists who all found resemblance to both, Umar ruled the child belonged to both. Also, a narration from 'Ali indicates that in a case with three potential fathers, he used lots rather than physiognomy. The Hanafi school prioritizes the practical resolution and the apparent consensus of the Companions, considering that the child's lineage can be legally established from both co-owners despite biological impossibility, based on the principle of istihsan (juristic preference) due to the marital-like relationship.

شُبْهَة (shubhah): Legal plausibility or semblance; in this context, the Hanafi argument that despite biological impossibility, there is sufficient legal semblance to attribute the child to both co-owners.
الاسْتِيلَاد (al-istīlād): The act of a master impregnating his slave girl, making her an umm walad (mother of the child). The chapter deals with rulings related to umm walad.

It is narrated thus: [Arabic] She (Aisha) said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) entered upon me one day happy and said: O Aisha, do you know that Majzaz al-Mudliji entered upon me while Usama ibn Zaid and Zaid were under a blanket covering their heads but their feet were visible, and he said: These feet are from each other. Abu Dawud said: Usama was black and Zaid was white. Lady Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) says: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) came to me one day cheerfully and said: O Aisha! Do you know what Majzaz al-Mudliji said? He just came to me and there Usama and Zaid were sleeping covered with a blanket; their heads were covered and their feet were uncovered. So Majzaz saw and said: These feet are born from one another. This hadith is narrated by the six Imams in their Sahihs. And Abu Dawud said: Zaid was fair-skinned and Usama was dark-skinned. Imam Shafi'i argued from the Messenger of Allah's happiness in this incident that the statement of a Qa'if should be relied upon. If they make the matter clear, then the ruling would be made clear. Therefore this child is the son of both, so he will inherit from both, and both will also inherit from him. And (if the child dies first and one of them is alive) then his entire inheritance will be for the surviving one. And this case came before the group of Companions. And a similar narration is found from Ali. Benefit: Binti narrated from Mubarak ibn Fadalah from Hasan al-Basri from Umar (may Allah be pleased with him). The incident was: Two men had intercourse with a slave woman when she was pure from menses, and she gave birth to a boy. Both claimed him. This case was brought before Umar. He summoned three Qa'ifs. All three agreed that the child resembled both of them. And Umar himself was a Qa'if, so he said: [Arabic/Urdu] ... Then he ruled: This is the son of both, and he will inherit from both, and both will inherit from him. And he will belong to the surviving one. The Rational Proof for Joint Paternity (Qiyas)
Rational legal argument (qiyas) for joint paternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qiyas of equality in cause to derive joint paternity when two claimants are equal.

Core Idea
Equality in cause leads to equality in right; lineage is indivisible but its divisible rulings are shared.

The Hanafi school uses a rational analogy (qiyas) to establish joint paternity when two men have an equal claim. This lesson walks through the logical structure of that argument, showing how the equality in intercourse (sabab al-istiḥqāq) leads to equal attribution, and how the indivisible nature of lineage interacts with divisible rulings like inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Cause of Entitlement
Both men had intercourse (wat') with the slave woman during a period when paternity is possible. This is the sabab al-istiḥqāq (cause of entitlement).
Intercourse during the tuhr period is the key shared act.
2
Establish Equality in Cause and Claim
Since both are equal in the cause of entitlement, they are equal in the claim. The analogy is to two persons each presenting evidence for ownership of one item.
The text explicitly compares this to bayyinah (evidence).
3
Distinguish Divisible and Indivisible Aspects of Lineage
Lineage (nasab) itself is indivisible, but it gives rise to divisible rulings such as inheritance, maintenance, and dowry. Divisible rulings are shared equally; indivisible ones are established fully for each.
This is the core legal subtlety—lineage is not split, but its effects are.
4
Apply the Rule via Qiyas
The qiyas works as follows: the asl (original case) is two persons with equal evidence for ownership, where the item is shared. The illa is equality in cause and claim. The hukm for the far' (paternity claim) is that the child is attributed to both fully for indivisible aspects, and shared for divisible ones.
The qiyas is jaliy (clear) because the illa is explicitly stated.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof (dalil aqli) for the Hanafi ruling when two men equally claim paternity of a child from a slave woman. The key principle is istiwa' (equality) in the cause of entitlement: both men had intercourse with the slave woman during the same tuhr, so their claim is equal. The text explains that although lineage (nasab) is indivisible in itself, it gives rise to divisible rulings such as inheritance, maintenance, etc. The divisible rulings are shared equally, while the indivisible ruling of lineage is established fully for each man—as if each is the sole father. Exceptions are when one claimant is the father of the other (giving him a preponderant right), or one is Muslim and the other a dhimmi (Islam gives preference). The passage also addresses the Prophet's happiness in the Majzaz hadith, refuting the Shafi'i argument by explaining it was due to the refutation of disbelievers' taunts. The conclusion summarizes the ruling: both men are legal fathers; the child inherits from each fully; they inherit from him as one father; the slave woman becomes umm walad for both; and the 'uqr (dower) is offset. The commentary emphasizes that this is a rational proof based on equality and analogy to two persons both presenting evidence (bayyinah).

Key Terms
سبب الاستحقاق (sabab al-istiḥqāq): The cause of entitlement; here, the act of intercourse (wat') with the slave woman during a period when paternity is possible.
عقر ('uqr): The dower (mahr) that becomes due on a man who has intercourse with a slave woman, as compensation for the reduction in her value.

Exceptions and Practical Rulings for Equal Claimants
Discussion of exceptions and detailed rulings following joint paternity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two exceptions to joint paternity and apply the resulting legal consequences (inheritance, umm walad, 'uqr offset).

Core Idea
Joint paternity applies only when claimants are truly equal; a preponderant factor (tā'sīr) breaks the equality.

The Hanafi ruling of joint paternity is not absolute—it has two exceptions: when one claimant is the father of the other, or when one is Muslim and the other a dhimmi. This lesson explains those exceptions and then works through the detailed practical rulings that follow when equality holds: the child inherits fully from each father, the slave woman becomes umm walad for both, and the 'uqr is offset.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Exceptions to Joint Paternity
The text specifies two cases where equality is broken: (1) one claimant is the father of the other, (2) one is Muslim and the other a dhimmi. In each, a preponderant factor gives preference.
The preponderant factor for the father is his right in the son's property; for the Muslim it is Islam itself.
2
Apply the Ruling for the Slave Woman (Umm Walad)
When both men's paternity is established, each has a share in the child, so his share of the mother becomes umm walad (a slave mother of a free child). She becomes free upon their deaths.
This is a consequence of valid paternity claims.
3
Determine the 'Uqr (Dower) Offset
Each man owes half the 'uqr for his intercourse, but since both are equally liable and the amounts are equal, they offset each other—like a set-off in debt.
This avoids double compensation.
4
Calculate Inheritance Between Child and Fathers
The child inherits from each father the full share of a son, because each father acknowledges him as full son. The fathers together inherit from the child as one father (i.e., their combined share equals one father's share).
The acknowledgment is binding against each man individually.

Mukatib's confirmation is needed for master's paternity claim, unlike father's claim from son's slave woman.

This lesson examines a specific case in the Hanafi school: the master’s paternity claim over a child born to his mukatib’s slave woman. Joint Paternity Claim by Co-Owners of a Slave Woman
Ruling with proof on joint paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two co-owners of a slave woman both claim paternity, explain the proof based on equality of cause, identify the exceptions, and assess the inter-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
Two equal co-owners both become full fathers; lineage is indivisible but effects are shared.

This passage presents the Hanafi position on a unique scenario: two men who jointly own a slave woman both claim that a child born to her is theirs. The ruling is based on the principle of equality in the cause of entitlement (ownership). While lineage itself cannot be divided, the legal effects that are divisible are shared proportionally. The passage also outlines exceptions where preponderance (father over son, Muslim over dhimmi) overrides equality, and notes a historical incident involving the Prophet's joy at the qā'if's testimony regarding Usāmah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the simultaneous claim of paternity by two co-owners. The ruling is that both are established as fathers – the child is considered the son of each, with full nasab.
2
Locate the rational proof (dalīl ‘aqlī)
The proof is the equality in the cause (ownership) that leads to equality in the right. The author argues that since both have equal ownership, they have equal entitlement to the child's paternity.
This is a qiyās-based argument: the cause is ownership, so the right is shared.
3
Understand the indivisible vs divisible distinction
Nasab (lineage) is indivisible, so each co-owner is deemed the full father. But effects like inheritance and ‘uqr (compensation) are divisible, so they are split equally between them.
The analogy: like two witnesses establishing a debt – each witness proves the full debt, but the debt is paid once.
4
Note the exceptions to equality
Equality is overridden if one co-owner is the father of the other (father has a prior right over his son's share) or if one is Muslim and the other dhimmi (Islam gives preponderance). In these cases, only the preponderant co-owner is the father.
The narration about Usāmah is cited as support for using a qā’if but is not the general rule.
5
Recognize the inter-madhhab ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi‘i holds that paternity cannot be shared; only one father is possible. The Hanafi response is that indivisible rights can be established in full for each, as with witnesses.
The practical consequence (thamarah): Hanafi child inherits from both fathers; Shafi‘i child inherits from only one.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute and ruling regarding a case where a master (mawlā) has intercourse with the slave woman of his mukātib (a slave under a contract of manumission). The child is born, and the master claims paternity. The ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa (the ‘zāhir’ view) is that the child’s lineage is established to the master only if the mukātib confirms the claim. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that the mukātib’s confirmation is unnecessary, analogizing to a father claiming the child of his son’s slave woman — the son’s confirmation is not needed because the father has authority over the son’s property. Abu Hanifa distinguishes: the master does not have such authority over the mukātib’s earnings; the mukātib’s ownership remains intact. Therefore, the master must pay ‘uqr (compensation) for intercourse and the value of the child (to free him). The child becomes free and his lineage is established to the master, but the slave woman does not become umm walad because the master never owned her. If the mukātib denies, lineage is not established until the master later owns the child, ending the mukātib’s right.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukātib): A slave who has made a contract (kitābah) with his master to earn his freedom by paying installments.
مغرور (maghrūr): One who is deceived, e.g., a man who marries a woman mistakenly thinking she is free, then discovers she is a slave.
عقر ('uqr): Compensation for intercourse with a slave woman without valid ownership.

Oath Not to Speak: Sleeping Person
Ruling on oath not to speak: sleeping person within earshot

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath not to speak to a sleeping person, explain the underlying qiyas, and identify the relied-upon condition from the mashayikh.

Core Idea
An oath not to speak is broken when speech reaches a conscious hearer; sleeping requires waking.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses the intricacies of oaths concerning speech. The key question is: does speaking to a sleeping person who can hear but does not understand break the oath? The Hanafi school examines this through analogies, and the later mashayikh add an important condition that refines the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is speaking to a person you swore not to speak to, while that person is asleep but within earshot. The initial ruling (from al-Quduri's text) is that hanth (oath-breaking) occurs.
Hanth triggers the obligation of kaffarah (expiation).
2
Trace the dalil (qiyas jaliy)
The first analogy (asl) is calling out to a heedless person who can hear but is inattentive. That case causes hanth because speech reaches the ears. By qiyas, speaking to a sleeping person produces the same result.
The illah (effective cause) is that speech reaches the hearing, even if not understood.
3
Identify the ikhtilaf (intra-Hanafi)
Some narrations in al-Mabsut and the majority of later mashayikh (our scholars) condition that the swearer must wake the person up. Without waking, it is like calling from a distance where the voice is not heard.
This second analogy uses a different asl: distant communication that fails to reach the ears.
4
Determine the tarjih (mufta bihi)
The relied-upon (sahih) position is the mashayikh's view: hanth only occurs if the swearer wakes the sleeping person. The initial ruling is considered subject to this condition.
This avoids unnecessary kaffarah.

Ruling on Marrying a Perpetually Forbidden Woman: Shubha and Hadd
A Hanafi legal talil (reasoning) for excluding hadd due to shubha.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa does not apply hadd to a man who marries a mahram and consummates, and contrast the Sahibayn's position.

Core Idea
An invalid marriage with a mahram creates shubha, averting hadd; only tazir applies.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute: whether a man who marries a perpetually forbidden woman (such as his mother or sister) and has intercourse with her is liable for the hadd punishment for zina. The passage from Al-Hidayah presents Imam Abu Hanifa's reasoning that the invalid marriage contract, though void, creates a shubha (legal doubt) because the woman is capable of procreation—the contract's purpose. This shubha prevents the application of hadd, and instead a discretionary punishment (ta'zir) is imposed. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and al-Shafi'i hold that the marriage is completely void and thus no shubha exists, so hadd applies.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa: no hadd, only ta'zir for marrying a mahram and consummating. Identify the ruling from the text.
The ruling is based on the concept of shubha.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's talil
The contract corresponds to its subject (woman is capable of procreation), so it should have been effective, but it fell short of permissibility, creating a shubha. Show how the passage argues this.
The key is that the subject is a potential subject of marriage, unlike a male.
3
Identify the opposing view
The Sahibayn (and al-Shafi'i) hold that the marriage is completely void (like with a male), so no shubha exists; hadd is obligatory.
Their dalil: the contract is batil, so the act is plain zina.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: the woman is a legitimate subject of marriage (capable of procreation), so the contract has some formal validity, creating doubt.
This is the core of the ikhtilaf: what constitutes a shubha?
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, a man who marries his mother and consummates gets ta'zir; under Sahibayn, he gets stoned if muhsan.
This shows the high stakes of the dispute.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling from the chapter on intercourse that obligates or does not obligate hadd. It discusses the case of a man who marries a woman who is perpetually forbidden to him (e.g., a mahram) and then has intercourse with her. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, such a man is not subject to the prescribed hadd punishment (i.e., stoning or lashing for zina) because the marriage contract, though void, creates a shubha (legal doubt) that prevents the application of hadd. The reasoning is that the contract's subject (the woman) is capable of fulfilling the purpose of marriage (procreation), so the contract should have been effective, but since the woman is perpetually forbidden, the contract fails to achieve actual permissibility. This creates a shubha that resembles the truth but is not the truth itself. The man has committed a major crime, but because no specific punishment is determined for this act, he is given a discretionary punishment (ta'zir). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) and Imam al-Shafi'i hold that hadd is obligatory because the marriage is void. The relied-upon position for fatwa is the opinion of the Sahibayn, as mentioned in some sources, though the commentary suggests the opinion of Imam Abu Hanifa is preferable. The distinction is that the contract, though invalid, is not completely void like a marriage with a male; it creates a resemblance to a valid marriage, thereby casting doubt.

Key Terms
شبهة (shubha): A legal doubt or resemblance that prevents the application of prescribed punishments (hudud) because it creates uncertainty about the act's status.
تعزير (ta'zir): A discretionary punishment determined by the judge for crimes that have no fixed hadd or kaffarah.
