Selling a Mudabbar: Hanafi vs Shafi'i Dispute
Refutes Shafi'i using hadith of Jabir to argue mudabbar cannot be sold

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school prohibits selling a mudabbar and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the hadith of Jabir.

Core Idea
Tadbir creates an irrevocable cause of freedom; selling a mudabbar would negate that right.

This lesson examines a famous inter-madhhab dispute over whether a mudabbar slave can be sold before the master's death. The Hanafi school argues that tadbir (the promise of freedom upon death) creates an immediate cause of freedom, making the slave unsellable. The Shafi'i school counters with a hadith where the Prophet himself sold a mudabbar to pay the owner's debt. We will analyze both proofs and the Hanafi response, and understand why the majority of early scholars upheld the Hanafi view.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Hanafi reasoning
Grasp the core principle: when a master declares tadbir, a cause of freedom (sabab al-'itq) arises immediately. This right cannot be undone by subsequent transactions. Therefore, selling or gifting the mudabbar is impermissible because it would nullify that cause.
Note that this is a qiyas (analogical) argument based on the nature of tadbir as a type of will (wasiyyah).
2
Examine the Shafi'i proof
Imam al-Shafi'i relies on the hadith of Jabir (ra) in which the Prophet (saw) sold a mudabbar slave belonging to an Ansari man who had no other wealth. The sale proceeds were used to pay the man's debt. This hadith is authentic (Bukhari, Muslim, etc.).
The Shafi'i position is also held by Imam Ahmad and Ishaq.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the Prophet (saw) did not consider the slave a mudabbar at the time of sale. The man had only intended to make him mudabbar, but the tadbir was not yet executed. Thus the slave was still a regular slave, and the sale was valid. The cause of freedom had not yet been created.
The text acknowledges that this interpretation (ta'wil) has some difficulty (ishkal).
4
Evaluate the tarjih
Despite the difficulty, the Hanafi view is upheld by the majority of the Salaf (early scholars) and by Imam Malik. The text states that the majority of the Salaf hold the view mentioned (the Hanafi view).
Thus the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school is that a mudabbar cannot be sold.

The Master's Rights Over a Mudabbar Slave
States the master's rights to use, hire, marry, and have intercourse with the mudabbar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four specific rights a master retains over a mudabbar slave and state the legal basis: ownership remains.

Core Idea
Ownership of a mudabbar is not extinguished; therefore, all ownership-based rights persist.

Some think that making a mudabbar tadbir grants the slave partial freedom immediately. In fact, the master retains full ownership until death. This passage enumerates four specific actions the master may still perform with the mudabbar, confirming that ownership remains intact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling's subject
The ruling addresses the master's relationship with a mudabbar slave or slave-girl. Understand who is the actor and who is the subject.
Mudabbar: a slave promised freedom upon the master's death.
2
List the four specific rights
Enumerate each right: istikhdam (employing for service), ijar (hiring out), wat' (intercourse if female), and tazwij (marrying off).
Wat' and tazwij apply specifically to a female mudabbara.
3
Understand the legal basis
The text explicitly states: 'li-anna al-milka fihi thabit' — because ownership is established. Authority for these dispositions derives from ownership.
This is a key principle: tadbir does not transfer or diminish ownership.
4
Classify the hukm level
These actions are mubah (permissible) — the master may choose to do them or not. They are not obligatory.
Hukm level: mubah, not wajib or sunnah.
5
Note cross-madhhab considerations
Some other schools might restrict these rights due to the impending freedom, but the Hanafi school maintains full ownership rights.
This is a point of ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is based on ownership continuity.

Mudabbar's Freedom: Third, Debt, and Child Ruling
Provides ruling and proof for mudabbar's freedom conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a mudabbar when the master dies with limited estate or debts, and apply the ijma proof.

Core Idea
Mudabbar freed from one-third; debt must be paid first via slave's work.

When a master who has made a mudabbar slave dies, the tadbir takes effect as a will. The mudabbar is entitled to freedom, but only from one-third of the estate. If there are debts or insufficient property, the slave must work to compensate the heirs or creditors. This lesson explains the precise rules and the consensus of the Companions that supports them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
Note that upon the master's death, the mudabbar is freed from one-third of the estate. This is because tadbir is a will (wasiyyah).
The one-third limitation protects the heirs' shares.
2
Analyze the case with no other property
If the only asset is the mudabbar, he is freed from one-third and must work to pay two-thirds of his value to the heirs.
The slave earns the amount through work (yas'a).
3
Analyze the case with debt
If the master had debt, the mudabbar works to pay his full value because debt precedes the will. He remains enslaved until the debt is settled.
Freedom cannot be revoked, so the slave's earnings substitute for his value.
4
Determine the child's status
The child of a female mudabbar is automatically mudabbar as well, based on consensus.
This follows the child's dependence on the mother.
5
Verify the proof: Ijma of the Companions
The ruling on the child is supported by ijma (consensus) of the Companions, transmitted by prominent narrators.
No contrary report exists, making it a definitive proof.

Conditional Tadbir and the Effect of Uncertain Conditions
Defines conditional tadbir and its ruling, including time-bound conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for conditional tadbir and distinguish between probable and improbable time limits when determining whether the tadbir becomes absolute.

Core Idea
A conditional tadbir is not valid until the condition is met; but if the time limit is impossible, it becomes absolute.

This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling on tadbir that is tied to a specific condition, such as dying from a particular illness or within a certain period. The key legal principle is that uncertainty in the condition delays the effect, unlike absolute tadbir. We will also see how extremely improbable time limits are treated as equivalent to certainty, making the tadbir absolute from the outset.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the legal nature of conditional tadbir. Before the condition is met, the slave is not a mudabbar, and the master may sell him. The ruling is mubah (permissible) to sell.
Conditional tadbir is contrasted with absolute tadbir, where the slave is immediately considered a mudabbar and cannot be sold.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a narration from Hasan from Imam Muhammad (nawadir). This narration provides the distinction between certain and uncertain conditions.
The narration is classified as Sunnah ahad, with zanni thubut and dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The narration shows that when the condition is uncertain (e.g., death from a specific illness), the tadbir is not effective immediately. This contrasts with absolute tadbir where death is certain.
The rationale (talil) is that the cause has not materialized due to the uncertainty.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., the famous view of Imam Malik) hold that conditional tadbir is invalid altogether. The Hanafi school, however, accepts it as valid when the condition occurs.
The Hanafi position here is based on the narration and allows the conditional effect.
5
Apply the principle to time-bound conditions
If the master says 'if I die within a year' the tadbir remains conditional because death within that period is not certain. But if he says 'if I die within 100 years', which is practically impossible for a normal lifespan, the tadbir becomes absolute immediately because death itself is certain.
This distinction is based on the likelihood of the condition occurring.

تشريح .... وَإِنْ عَلَقَ التَّدْبِيرَ بِمَوْتِهِ عَلَى صِفة ... الخ ترجمہ سے مطلب واضح ہے۔
Ruling on suspending tadbir upon death with a condition. And if he suspends tadbir upon his death with a condition, such as saying, 'If I die from this illness, or on this journey, or from such a disease,' then he is not a mudabbar, and selling him is permissible because the cause has not been established immediately due to uncertainty in that condition, unlike the absolute mudabbar, because his freedom is tied to absolute death, which inevitably occurs. But if the master dies under the condition he mentioned, he is freed just as the absolute mudabbar is freed, meaning from the one-third, because the ruling of tadbir is established in the last part of his life due to that condition being realized. Therefore, it is considered from the one-third. And from the conditional types is that he says, 'If I die within a year or ten years,' because of what we mentioned, unlike the case where he says, 'If I die within a hundred years,' and such a person generally does not live that long, so it is like an inevitable occurrence. Benefit: This narration is mentioned in the narration of Hasan from Muhammad, and this is also the famous saying of Imam Malik. Allamah Ayni said likewise. Commentary: And if he suspends tadbir upon his death with a condition... etc. The meaning is clear from the translation.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof for conditional tadbir (tadbir muqayyad). It explains that if the master ties the tadbir to a specific condition (e.g., dying from a particular illness), the slave is not immediately a mudabbar and can be sold. The reason is that the condition is uncertain, so the cause of freedom has not materialized. In contrast, absolute tadbir (tying freedom to death in general) takes effect immediately because death is certain. If the condition is met, the slave becomes free from the one-third at death. The passage also deals with time-bound conditions: if the master says 'if I die within a year or ten years,' it is conditional because death within that period is not certain. However, if he says 'within a hundred years,' since people rarely live that long, it is treated as absolute tadbir—because death itself is certain even if the time limit is improbable. The benefit notes that this ruling is from the narration of Hasan from Imam Muhammad and matches Imam Malik's position.

Key Terms
تدبير مقيد (tadbir muqayyad): Tadbir tied to a specific condition (e.g., dying from a certain illness or within a time).
تعليق (ta'liq): Suspending the tadbir upon a future uncertain event.
