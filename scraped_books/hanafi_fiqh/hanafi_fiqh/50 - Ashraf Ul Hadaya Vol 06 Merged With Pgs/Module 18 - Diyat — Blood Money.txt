Diyah for Death by Strangulation (Abu Hanifa's Ruling)
Ruling on diyah for strangulation by Abu Hanifa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that strangulation is classified as killing with a heavy object in Hanafi fiqh and identify that diyah is wajib on the 'aqila according to Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Strangulation is treated as killing with a heavy object, so diyah is on the 'aqila, not qisas.

This brief passage from Al-Hidayah presents Abu Hanifa's specific ruling on death by strangulation. It classifies the act under the category of 'qatl bil-muthaqqal' (killing with a heavy object), which carries diyah (blood money) payable by the killer's patrilineal relatives (al-‘āqila). Understanding this classification helps distinguish between intentional murder (qatl 'amd) and semi-intentional killing (shibh al-'amd) in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
The act is 'khanaq' (strangulation), which the author explicitly links to 'qatl bil-muthaqqal' (killing with a heavy object). Note that strangulation is not considered pure intentional murder ('amd) but a form of semi-intentional killing.
The term 'muthaqqal' literally means 'heavy'—it refers to using an object that is not typically used for killing.
2
State the ruling (hukm) and who is liable
According to Abu Hanifa, the diyah (blood money) is wajib on the 'āqila (the patrilineal relatives) of the killer. The text uses the phrase 'al-diyatu 'ala 'aqilatihi', meaning 'the diyah is upon his 'āqila'.
This contrasts with intentional murder where qisas (retaliation) is due on the killer themselves.
3
Note the authority and scope
The ruling is attributed specifically to Abu Hanifa. Other Hanafi masters (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) may have different views, and the text promises elaboration in the chapter on diyat.
The passage does not indicate which view is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa).
4
Understand the practical implication
Because strangulation is treated as 'shibh al-'amd' (semi-intentional), the diyah is paid collectively by the 'āqila over three years, and there is no qisas. This protects the killer from retaliation while providing compensation to the victim's family.
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may classify strangulation as intentional if the killer intended death, leading to qisas.

Ruling on Diyah for Strangulation (Khalq)
States the Hanafi position on diyah for strangulation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah for killing by strangulation and identify who bears the liability.

Core Idea
Strangulation killing: diyah is on the 'aqilah, not the killer alone.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, we encounter a specific ruling on homicide by strangulation. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the blood money (diyah) is payable by the killer's 'aqilah, reflecting the principle that certain indirect killings shift liability to the tribe. This lesson clarifies the ruling and its underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the passage is ruling upon: killing by strangulation (al-khalq). The ruling is that diyah is obligatory.
The act is man's act of strangling a person, causing death.
2
Identify the party liable
According to the text, the diyah is 'ala 'aqilatihi (upon his 'aqilah). The 'aqilah is the killer's tribe or group of helpers.
The liability is not on the killer individually but on the group.
3
Note the authority
The ruling is attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa, indicating it is his specific view. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
Other Hanafi scholars may differ; this is the relied-upon position.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the commentary on al-Hidayah. It states that if a person strangles another to death, the diyah (blood money) is payable by the 'aqilah (the tribe or helpers) of the killer, according to Imam Abu Hanifa. This ruling is analogous to killing with a heavy object, as the author notes in the Urdu commentary, and will be elaborated in the chapter on diyat. The 'aqilah bears the diyah because the killing is not direct (by a weapon) but via strangulation, which is considered a form of unintentional killing (shibh 'amd) or similar. The Hanafi position is that the diyah is on the 'aqilah in such cases of indirect killing.

Key Terms
خَلَقَ (khalaqa): To strangle someone; in this context, killing by strangulation.
دِيَة (diyah): Blood money; financial compensation for homicide paid to the victim's heirs.
عَاقِلَة ('aqilah): The tribe or group of helpers (usually paternal male relatives) who pay diyah on behalf of the killer.
