Black Dye and the Role of Custom in Rulings
Nuance within intra-Hanafi dispute: black dye.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling on a thief dyeing a garment black and identify how custom ('urf) can affect legal classifications in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Black dye is treated differently because its value as addition or defect depends on era and custom.

The same Hidayah passage includes a nuance: if the thief dyes the garment black, the ruling changes from that of red dye. The imams differ based on whether black is considered an addition or a defect, and later scholars attribute this to the customary value of colors in different dynasties.

Guided Path
1
Note the specific ruling for black dye
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, if the thief dyes the garment black, the garment is taken from the thief. This differs from the red dye case where the garment is not taken.
2
Understand Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf treats black dye the same as red dye: the garment is not taken from the thief, because he considers black an addition (ziyada) like red.
3
Analyze the rationale of each imam
Abu Hanifa considers black a defect (nuqsan), so the owner's right is not cut off. Muhammad considers black an addition but still subordinate to the owner's right because the dye is tabi'. Abu Yusuf sees black as an addition like red, so the thief's side prevails.
Note that Muhammad's position differs from his view on red dye where he argued for taking the garment.
4
Appreciate the historical note
The commentators explain that the difference may be due to the value of colors in different eras: in the Umayyad period (Abu Hanifa's time), red was valuable and black was a defect; in the Abbasid period (Sahibayn's time), black became valuable.
This shows how custom ('urf) can influence fiqh rulings.
5
Derive the principle
The classification of an alteration as an addition (ziyada) or defect (nuqsan) depends on prevailing custom. When custom changes, the ruling may change accordingly.
This is an application of the legal maxim: 'al-'adah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative).

If a thief steals gold or silver whose value necessitates amputation, then makes them into dirhams or dinars, then his hand is cut and the coins are returned to the owner according to Abu Hanifa. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the owner has no right to take the coins because the thief has become the owner through the transformation. Similarly, if a thief steals a garment and dyes it red, according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf his hand is cut, the garment is not taken from him, and he does not guarantee its value. Muhammad says the garment is taken from him and he is given the increase in value due to the dye, by analogy with usurpation (ghasb). The reasoning is that in theft the owner's right is only in form, not in substance, as the thief is not liable for destruction, so the thief's side is preferred; in ghasb both rights are substantive, so the owner's side is preferred. If the garment is dyed black, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad it is taken from the thief, while Abu Yusuf considers it the same as red dye. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a thief who transforms stolen property before the hadd punishment is carried out. The first issue: a thief steals gold or silver that would necessitate amputation (qat') and then manufactures it into dirhams or dinars. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the thief does not become the owner of the new coins; therefore, his hand is cut and the coins are returned to the original owner. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the thief becomes the owner because the manufacturing is a valuable act that changes the substance, so the owner has no claim to the coins. The second issue involves a thief who steals a garment and dyes it red. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that the hand is cut, the garment is not taken from the thief (i.e., he keeps it), and he is not liable for its value because the owner's right is only formal (surah) not substantive (ma'na)—the thief is not liable if the garment perishes. Muhammad analogizes this to the case of usurpation (ghasb) where a usurper dyes a garment: in ghasb, the garment is returned to the owner with compensation for the increase. Muhammad's qiyas is rejected by Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf who note that in ghasb both parties' rights are substantive, making them equal, so the owner's right prevails. In theft, only the thief has substantive right, so he prevails. The third issue: if the dye is black, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad agree the garment is taken from the thief (since black dye is considered a defect in Abu Hanifa's time, and an addition in Muhammad's time), while Abu Yusuf treats black the same as red. The commentators note that the ikhtilaf reflects the customary value of colors in different eras (Umayyad vs Abbasid).

Key Terms
زيادة (ziyada): Increase in value due to the dye.
نقصان (nuqsan): Defect or decrease in value.
عرف ('urf): Custom that can influence legal rulings.

Listing the four conditions that define highway robbers.

Core Idea
Highway robbery requires four distinct conditions beyond ordinary theft: power, remote location, Dar al-Islam, and nisab.

This passage opens the chapter on highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) by setting out the precise legal conditions that transform a simple theft into the more severe hadd offense of hirabah. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four punishments in Quran 5:33 as explained by Ibn Abbas and apply the Hanafi interpretation of 'nafy' as imprisonment.

Core Idea
Banishment (nafy) in hirabah means imprisonment for Hanafis, not exile — to prevent corruption from spreading.

This passage presents the interpretation of the verse on hirabah (Quran 5:33) by Ibn Abbas, delineating four crimes and corresponding punishments. It then delves into a key inter-madhhab dispute: does 'banishment from the land' mean physical exile (Shafi'i/Hanbali) or imprisonment (Hanafi)? Understanding this difference is crucial for applying the hadd penalty correctly.

Guided Path

This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

The Hanafi school argues that the purpose of the punishment is to protect society. Imprisonment achieves this more effectively than exile, which allows the criminal to continue his crimes in another part of Dar al-Islam.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is the actual method of punishment.

Ruling on early capture of highway robbers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for highway robbers (muharibun) who are captured before taking property or killing, and identify the three conditions that determine this ruling.

Core Idea
If caught before theft or murder, highway robbers face prison until repentance, not hadd.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational Hanafi ruling from Al-Quduri's matn. The passage clarifies that the severe hadd punishments for highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) are reserved for cases where property is actually taken or a person is killed. If the perpetrators are apprehended before either action, the Imam's duty shifts to discretionary imprisonment (ta'zir) until they repent. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying Islamic criminal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage concerns a group or individual who go out with the intention of highway robbery. The ruling is that the Imam must imprison them until they repent.
This is not a hadd punishment but a ta'zir (discretionary penalty).
2
List the conditions for this ruling
Three conditions are required: (1) they must be capable of resistance (imtina'), (2) they must have the intention of highway robbery, and (3) they are caught before taking any property or killing anyone.
The condition of imtina' distinguishes highway robbery from ordinary theft or assault.
3
Understand why hadd does not apply
The Quranic punishment for muharaba (amputation, execution, etc.) applies only when property is taken or life is taken. Since neither occurred, the lesser penalty of imprisonment is used.
This aligns with the verse: 'indeed the penalty for those who wage war...' (Quran 5:33) which specifies punishments for those who 'spread corruption', interpreted as actual harm.
4
Recognize the role of imtina' (ability to resist)
If the person is not capable of resistance (e.g., weak or overpowered easily), they are not considered a muharib and the crime is treated under ordinary theft or assault laws.
This is a distinctive Hanafi condition; other schools may apply hadd more broadly.

Three Cases of Highway Robbery and Their Punishments
First three scenarios of highway robbery and their punishments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios of highway robbery under Hanafi law and identify the punishment prescribed for each scenario.

Core Idea
Highway robbery is punished by four possible penalties; which one applies depends on whether property was taken or killing occurred.

This lesson examines the first three scenarios of highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) as detailed in the Hanafi school. The punishments are derived from the Quranic verse 5:33, which lists four penalties: execution, crucifixion, amputation of opposite limbs, and exile. The Hanafi scholars distribute these penalties according to the nature of the crime, distinguishing between cases where property is taken, killing occurs, or neither. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying the hudud punishments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first scenario
The first scenario is when the robbers set out with intent to loot and fight but are caught before taking property or killing. Their punishment is imprisonment until repentance, plus ta'zir (discretionary punishment) for intimidation.
This is based on interpreting 'exile' in the verse as imprisonment, since it removes their evil from society.
2
Identify the second scenario
The second scenario is when the robbers take property but do not kill. If the property when divided among the group gives each at least ten dirhams (the nisab for theft), the punishment is amputation of the right hand and left foot from opposite sides.
The condition of nisab per person ensures that the hadd punishment is only applied when the property is significant.
3
Identify the third scenario
The third scenario is when the robbers kill but do not take property. Their punishment is execution as a hadd (fixed penalty). The heirs' forgiveness does not waive this punishment because it is a right of Allah.
This is a hadd punishment, not qisas, so it cannot be pardoned by the victim's family.
4
Trace the Quranic basis
The verse 5:33 provides four punishments. The Hanafi school distributes these according to the circumstances. Imprisonment corresponds to exile, amputation for taking property, execution for killing, and crucifixion for both taking property and killing (the fourth scenario, not covered here).
The phrase 'من خلاف' (from opposite sides) indicates amputation of right hand and left foot.
5
Note the additional conditions
The text also lays down conditions: the robbers must be capable of resistance (mumtani'), the property must belong to a Muslim or dhimmi (not a musta'min), and the nisab must be reached for each robber individually.
These conditions ensure that the hadd is only applied when the crime fully qualifies as highway robbery.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Punishment for Highway Robbery: Killing and Taking Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on punishment for fourth case of highway robbery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad on the punishment for highway robbery when both killing and taking property occur, and apply the principle of tadhakhul appropriately.

Core Idea
When killing and theft combine in highway robbery, the Hanafi school splits: a single intensified hadd or an included penalty?

This passage presents the fourth case of highway robbery where the robbers both kill and take property. The Hanafi school internally disagrees on whether the Imam may impose amputation in addition to execution/crucifixion. Imam Muhammad argues that a single crime cannot warrant two hadd penalties, citing the principle of tadhakhul (inclusion) from theft and stoning. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf counter that the combined punishment is a single intensified hadd, not two separate ones. Understanding this dispute clarifies the Hanafi approach to hudud and the scope of judicial discretion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth case and the Imam's options according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
The fourth case is when robbers kill and take property. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the Imam has four choices: amputate then kill, amputate then crucify, kill only, or crucify only.
Notice that amputation is always paired with execution or crucifixion; it is never standalone.
2
Recognize Imam Muhammad's objection and his use of qiyas
Imam Muhammad restricts the Imam to killing or crucifixion alone, without amputation. His proof: a single crime cannot bring two hadd penalties, and the lesser punishment enters into the greater by analogy to theft and stoning.
The analogy: just as a thief who also commits zina is only stoned (not amputated), here the robber who kills is only executed or crucified.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response: why the combined punishment is a single intensified hadd
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf argue that the combined punishment (amputation plus execution/crucifixion) is not two separate hadd penalties but a single intensified punishment due to the aggravated crime (complete disruption of security). Cutting hand and foot together is already treated as one hadd in highway robbery, unlike in theft where they are separate.
This distinction is key: tadhakhul applies only between separate hadd penalties, not within a single intensified hadd.
4
Apply the distinction: tadhakhul applies between separate hadd penalties, not within a single hadd
Tadhakhul (inclusion) means if multiple hadd penalties are due, the lesser is absorbed by the greater. But here, there is only one hadd (the intensified one), so tadhakhul does not operate. Therefore, the Imam may include amputation as part of the same punishment.
This reasoning underscores the Hanafi principle that the severity of the cause justifies a more severe single penalty.

Commentary
This passage addresses the fourth scenario of highway robbery where the robbers both kill and take property. The Hanafi school is divided: according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (as 'lahuma'), the Imam has an option to either amputate then kill or crucify, or just kill, or just crucify. Imam Muhammad holds that only one punishment should be applied—either execution or crucifixion—without amputation, because it is a single crime. His reasoning is based on the principle of tadhakhul (inclusion) in hudud: when multiple hadd penalties arise from a single act, the lesser is absorbed by the greater, analogous to theft and stoning. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf counter that the combined punishment (amputation plus execution/crucifixion) is actually a single intensified penalty due to the aggravated nature of the crime (complete breach of security). They argue that the cutting of hand and foot in this context is a single hadd, not two separate ones, and tadhakhul does not apply within a single hadd. The text further notes that according to Zahir al-Riwayah, the Imam has a choice regarding crucifixion, while Abu Yusuf holds crucifixion is mandatory because it is explicitly mentioned in the Quran. The author (likely Ashraf Ul Hadaya) sides with the option of choice, stating that the deterrent effect is achieved through execution, and exaggeration in crucifixion is optional.

Conditions for Cutting Hand and Foot in Highway Robbery
Explains conditions for cutting punishment in highway robbery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the hadd of cutting hand and foot in highway robbery according to the Hanafi school and apply these conditions to differentiate the punishment from other hudud.

Core Idea
Highway robbery requires a specific punishment: amputation of right hand and left foot, but only if the property is protected and exceeds ten dirhams per robber.

This passage from the chapter on highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) explains the conditions that must be met for the hadd punishment of amputation to apply. It also distinguishes the case where murder is committed, leading to execution as a purely divine right.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its basis
Identify that for highway robbery without murder, the punishment is cruel and unusual (crurifragium) plus hand amputation. The basis is the Quranic verse 5:33.
2
List the conditions stipulated by Quduri
The property must belong to a Muslim or dhimmi, and the value must be at least ten dirhams per robber. This ensures the inviolability of the property and prevents trivializing the hadd.
The first condition from the text: 'the property must belong to a Muslim or dhimmi' (مال مسلم أو ذمي).
3
Identify the specific mode of cutting
The cutting is of the right hand and left foot, so that the robber loses the ability to use opposite limbs, preventing total loss of function.
This is a combined punishment, not two separate ones.
4
Analyze the ruling for murder
If the robbery includes murder, the punishment is execution as a hadd, not left to the victim's family to forgive. This is due to the divine nature of the hadd.
Consensus (ijma') supports this.
5
Apply the conditions to a scenario
Consider a case: a group of bandits rob a Muslim of 5 dirhams each. Since the value is less than 10 dirhams per robber, the hadd of amputation does not apply.
This illustrates the practical effect of the conditions.

Imam's Choice When Robbers Kill and Take Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on punishment for highway robbers who kill and take property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the Imam's options when highway robbers kill and take property, and apply the principle of tadakhul to distinguish between multiple hadd punishments and a single enhanced hadd.

Core Idea
A single severe crime warrants one enhanced hadd, not two separate penalties; tadakhul applies across crimes, not within one.

Highway robbery that results in both murder and theft is a severe crime. The Quran gives the Imam several punishment options, but the Hanafi school debates whether amputation can be combined with execution or if only one penalty suffices. This lesson unpacks the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf against Muhammad al-Shaybani, and the role of tadakhul in hadd punishments.

Guided Path
1
State the Imam's options
From the matn, the Imam has three or four options: cut hands and feet from opposite sides and kill/crucify, kill only, or crucify only. The combined cut+kill/crucify is one option.
The Quranic basis is Surah al-Ma'idah 5:33.
2
Identify Muhammad's objection
Muhammad argues that one crime cannot yield two hadd punishments; the lesser (cutting) is subsumed by the greater (death), analogous to theft combined with zina where only stoning is carried out.
He uses qiyas: lesser enters greater in hudud.
3
Understand Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the combined punishment is a single enhanced hadd, not two separate ones, due to the severity of the cause (destruction of security). Cutting hand and foot in highway robbery is one hadd, unlike in minor theft where they are two.
The severity of the crime justifies the severity of the punishment.
4
Apply the principle of tadakhul
Tadakhul (merging) applies when multiple hadd punishments accrue from separate crimes; it does not apply within a single hadd punishment. Here, the combined cut+death is one hadd, so tadakhul does not merge them.
This refutes the analogy with theft and stoning, which are separate crimes.
5
Analyze the crucifixion sub-dispute
The text then discusses whether the Imam must include crucifixion. The apparent riwayah gives choice, but Abu Yusuf insists on crucifixion because it is explicitly mentioned in the Quran and serves as a deterrent. The author supports the choice view, noting that killing already provides display, and crucifixion adds exaggeration.
This is a secondary ikhtilaf within the same passage.

The Method of Crucifixion: Deterrence vs. Avoiding Mutilation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the method of crucifixion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the method of crucifixion for highway robbers and explain why the view of crucifying alive and piercing the belly is preferred over killing first.

Core Idea
Crucifixion is done alive with a spear thrust to maximize deterrence, as the purpose of hadd is rad'.

In the previous lesson, we learned that for the fourth case of highway robbery (hirabah), the Imam may choose crucifixion. Now we examine the exact method: should the robber be crucified alive or killed first? The Hanafi school has a nuanced internal dispute, resolved by focusing on the purpose of the punishment – deterrence (rad') – which overrides the concern for mutilation (muthlah).

Guided Path
1
State the ruling from the matn
The matn states: 'He is crucified alive, and his belly is pierced with a spear until he dies.' This is the position of al-Quduri and al-Karkhi.
This is the first view.
2
Identify the alternative view
Al-Tahawi reports: kill first, then crucify, to avoid mutilation (muthlah) – i.e., to prevent the prolonged suffering of a live crucifixion.
Second view focuses on avoiding bodily disfigurement.
3
Understand the reasoning behind each view
The first view prioritizes deterrence: the sight of a living robber being pierced is more terrifying and thus more effective. The second view prioritizes the prohibition of mutilation (muthlah), even after death.
Both are valid Hanafi considerations.
4
Examine the tarjih (preference)
The commentator states the first view is 'al-asah' (the more correct) because 'crucifixion in this manner is more effective in deterrence, which is the purpose of it.' The purpose (maqsad) of the hadd is rad' (deterrence), so that overrides the secondary concern.
The tarjih is based on the purpose (talil).
5
Note the implication
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is to crucify alive and pierce the belly. This shows that in Hanafi fiqh, the purpose of the punishment is decisive in resolving internal disputes.
Also, the Zahir al-Riwayah supports this view.

Minor, Insane, or Mahram Among Bandits: Hadd Dropped
Rules about collective liability in hirabah when an incapable or related member is present.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Presence of a minor, insane, or mahram relative voids hadd for all because the crime is one.

This passage tackles a specific exception in the law of highway robbery (hirabah). When a group of bandits includes someone who is either not legally responsible (minor, insane) or a close blood relative (mahram) of the victim, the question arises: does the hadd punishment fall on the remaining sane, unrelated bandits? The Hanafi school is divided, and the reasoning reveals a deep principle about the unity of criminal acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and the Main Ruling
Read the first line: 'If among the bandits there is a minor, insane, or mahram relative... the hadd drops from the rest.' This is the basic rule presented, but it hides an internal dispute.
The key term 'hadd' here refers to the prescribed punishment for hirabah, which includes execution or amputation.
2
Understand the Unitary Crime (Jinaayah Waahidah) Argument
Abu Hanifa and Zufar argue that the crime of hirabah is a single entity performed by the group. If one member's action does not create liability (because of incapacity or mahram status), then the entire crime is defective. Thus, no one can be punished.
They compare it to a group where one acts mistakenly: the intentional actor is also not punished for the combined act.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's Counterargument
Abu Yusuf distinguishes between direct actor (mubaashir) and supporter (rid'). The sane adults are direct actors; their action is fully valid. The defect in the follower does not affect the origin. So the sane ones should still be punished.
Abu Yusuf's view is more individualistic, focusing on personal responsibility.
4
Apply the Analogy of the Mistaken and the Intentional
Abu Hanifa uses the rule: if one person in a group acts mistakenly and another intentionally, the intentional one is not punished for the combined result because the act is considered one. Similarly, here the deficient participant's presence voids the entire group's liability.
This shows how qiyas (analogy) is used to derive the ruling.
5
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf and the Mufta Bihi
The practical difference: if a minor is in the gang, Abu Hanifa drops hadd for all; Abu Yusuf only for the minor. The text states 'al-ashah' (more correct) is the absolute dropping, indicating the preferred view in the school.
For the mahram case, the stronger opinion is that the hadd drops absolutely, even without shared property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the effect of a minor or insane person among highway robbers, explain the reasoning of each, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The presence of a non-liable person among highway robbers drops the hadd for all, because the crime is a single collective act.

In Hanafi criminal law, highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) is treated as a single collective offense. When a minor or insane person participates, the question arises: does the hadd punishment still apply to the sane adults? This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa (supported by Zufar) and Abu Yusuf on this issue, and identifies the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the general ruling
According to the base text, if a minor, insane person, or mahram relative is among the robbers, the hadd is dropped for all. This is the starting point for the discussion.
Note the three categories: minor, insane, and mahram relative. This lesson focuses on the first two.
2
Identify the disputants and their positions
Abu Hanifa and Zufar hold that the hadd drops completely (the stated view). Abu Yusuf holds that if the sane adults directly committed the act (mubashara), they are punished; otherwise the hadd drops.
The dispute is about the effect of the non-liable person on the liability of the sane adults.
3
Examine Abu Yusuf's reasoning
Abu Yusuf distinguishes between the direct perpetrator (asl) and the aider (tabi'). The sane adult's direct commission is sound, so the defect in the follower does not affect the primary. If the minor or insane is the direct perpetrator, then the hadd drops for all.
This is based on qiyas: the primary cause is the direct action.
4
Examine Abu Hanifa's reasoning
The crime is a single collective act (jinaaya wahida) that stands by all participants. If the action of some is not liable (e.g., minor, insane), then the actions of the rest are only part of the cause, and the full ruling (hadd) is not established. They analogize to the case of one intentional killer and one mistaken killer together – the intentional one is not liable for qisas because the act is attributed jointly.
This is an application of istihsan over qiyas, considering the unity of the crime.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text indicates that the correct view is that of Abu Hanifa and Zufar, as the sahih view is 'absolute' for the mahram case, which follows the same principle.
The later Hanafi tradition relies on Abu Hanifa's position.

It should be known that regarding the minor and the insane, the statement of Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar (may Allah have mercy on them) is what has been mentioned. And it is narrated from Imam Abu Yusuf that if the robbery was committed by a sane person (not the minor or insane), then the hadd penalty will be imposed on the rest except the minor and insane, and the same ruling applies to mere theft. His proof is that the person who committed the crime is the principal offender, and those who assist him are his subordinates. And among them, those who have intellect and reason, there is no deficiency in their action. And the subordinate, meaning the minor and insane, their being subordinate is not considered. Therefore, if the hadd is dropped from the subordinate, it will not be dropped from the principals. And if the opposite happens, meaning if there is no deficiency in the subordinate but there is deficiency in the principals, then the hadd will be dropped. And the proof of Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar (may Allah have mercy on them) is that the act of highway robbery is one single crime which was completed and accomplished by all of them together. And it pertains to all of them. So when the action of any one of them does not deserve the hadd penalty, i.e., the complete cause is not present, then the action of the rest remains deficient in cause, and a deficient or partial cause cannot establish the full ruling. Therefore, this situation becomes like a case where one person intentionally joins with another who mistakenly participates. For example, a person shot an arrow at another intentionally, considering him a person, and another person also shot an arrow at him but considering him an animal, and as a result that person died. In this case, the one who shot thinking it was an animal will not be killed in qisas, and simultaneously the other person will also not be killed in qisas, but rather he too will be let go. And in such a situation, if among the highway robbers there is one who is a mahram of the travelers, then regarding that, what Jassas al-Razi (may Allah have mercy on him) says that because of him the hadd is dropped from the rest of the robbers as well. Its interpretation is that if the goods of those who were robbed are jointly owned, then the hadd will be dropped from the robbers. But the correct opinion is that whether their goods are jointly owned or not, in every case the hadd will be dropped. Because the entire act of highway robbery is one single crime which was completed by all the robbers together. Therefore, if from any one of them the hadd is dropped for some reason, it will necessarily be dropped from the rest of the partners as well. In contrast, if among the travelers there is a harbi who entered Dar al-Islam with aman, then regarding him the hadd penalty from the robbers is dropped because there is a deficiency in the inviolability of his blood, and this is specific to that person who came with aman. And in such a place, the hadd is prohibited because there is no complete hirz (protection); rather there is a deficiency in it. Whereas this hirz is not specific to an individual or situation, but rather pertains to the entire caravan collectively, and the entire caravan is one hirz.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the application of the hadd penalty for highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) when among the robbers there is a minor (sabi), an insane person (majnun), or a person who is a mahram (close relative within prohibited degrees) of the victims. The passage first states the majority Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar) that the presence of any such person drops the hadd from all robbers. It then narrates a different view from Imam Abu Yusuf, who distinguishes based on who actually committed the robbery: if the actual robber was a sane adult, then only the minor and insane are exempted, but the sane ones are still punished. The commentary provides two proofs. First, Abu Yusuf's reasoning: the actual criminal is the principal; the others are mere subordinates; deficiency in the subordinate does not affect the principal. Second, Abu Hanifa's reasoning: highway robbery is a single cooperative crime; if one participant's action is deficient in legal cause (e.g., lack of legal capacity), the entire act is deficient, so the hadd falls from all. To illustrate, an analogy is given: two shooters, one intentional and one mistaken, both escape qisas because the killing is one act with mixed causes. Then the passage addresses the case of a mahram among the victims, citing Jassas al-Razi and the correct opinion that the hadd is dropped regardless of joint ownership of goods, because the robbery is one crime. Finally, it contrasts with the case of a harbi (enemy alien) who entered with aman, where the hadd is dropped due to deficiency in the inviolability of his blood, which is specific to that individual, unlike the collective hirz of the caravan.

Learning Objective

Caravan Robbery: One Hirz, No Hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for internal caravan robbery and explain why the hadd for highway robbery is not applied, using the concept of unified protected space (hirz wahid).

Core Idea
The entire caravan is one protected space; internal robbery does not trigger highway hadd.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from the chapter on highway robbery (qat' al-tariq). The text addresses a scenario where robbery occurs within the same caravan. The ruling clarifies that the hadd punishment does not apply because the entire caravan is considered a single protected space (hirz wahid). This highlights a key condition for highway robbery: the robber must be an outsider to the victim's protected space. Understanding this nuance prevents misapplication of hudud penalties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Read the Arabic: 'وَإِذَا قَطَعَ بَعْضُ الْقَافِلَةِ الطَّرِيقَ عَلَى الْبَعْضِ لَمْ يَجِبِ الْحَدُّ'. The act is some members of the caravan cutting the road against others. The ruling is 'lam yajib al-hadd' (the hadd is not obligatory).
Note that the verb 'qata'a al-tariq' is used, but the ruling negates the hadd.
2
Analyze the rationale
The text provides the reason: 'li'anna al-hirza wahid' (because the protected space is one). This indicates that the condition for highway robbery – a separate hirz for each party – is absent.
Hirz is a critical concept in hadd punishments; here it is extended to the caravan as a whole.

The author analogizes the caravan to a single house: 'fasarat al-qafilatu ka-darin wahidatin'. Just as theft within a house by its residents does not meet the conditions for highway robbery (it may be ordinary theft), internal caravan robbery does not meet highway robbery conditions.
This analogy clarifies why the hadd for highway robbery is waived, not the act of robbery itself.

The practical consequence is that the robber is not subject to the hadd of qat' al-tariq (death or crucifixion, etc.). However, they may still be liable for ordinary theft (sariqa) if the conditions for that hadd are met, or for tazir punishment.
The ruling does not absolve the robber from all accountability; it only removes the specific hadd for highway robbery.

Consider: what if a caravan splits into two groups? Would the ruling apply? Based on the principle, if they share the same hirz (e.g., same campsite), likely still one hirz. But if they separate into independent protected spaces, the ruling may differ.
This step encourages extending the ruling to new facts while staying within the Hanafi framework.
