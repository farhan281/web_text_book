Oaths and Agency: When Delegation Does Not Break an Oath
Ruling on oaths regarding transactions when delegation occurs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the Hanafi ruling on whether appointing an agent breaks an oath of personal abstention, and APPLY the distinction between personal and delegated actions to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
An oath not to sell is broken only by your own sale, not by your agent's.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi principle in the chapter of oaths (al-ayman). The source presents a case where a person swears not to engage in a transaction, yet delegates it to an agent. The ruling clarifies that the oath is tied to the individual's own direct action; delegation does not constitute a breach. This underscores the Hanafi approach to literal interpretation of oaths and the separation of legal personalities in agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its subject
Read the passage: the person swears he will not sell, buy, or rent. Note that these are actions he personally undertakes.
2
Recognize the subsequent delegation
The person then appoints a wakil (agent) to perform exactly those prohibited acts.
The agent acts on behalf of the principal but legally the action is attributed to the agent.
3
Apply the legal consequence
According to Hanafi fiqh, the oath is not broken because the action is attributed to the agent, not the principal.
The swearer did not personally perform the prohibited act.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The oath operates on the person's own acts; agency creates a separate legal attribution. Thamarah: the oath remains intact.
This principle is based on the literal interpretation of the oath's wording.

Manumission for a Consideration (العتق على جعل): Ruling and Conditions
Explains ruling on freeing a slave for payment and its legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on freeing a slave for a payment, explain the requirement of acceptance via qiyas with sale, and identify the types of permissible payment and the distinction from kitabah.

Core Idea
Freeing a slave for a payment is like a sale: freedom is immediate upon acceptance, and the payment becomes a debt.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents al-Marghinani's ruling on freeing a slave for a specified payment (ju‘l). The Hanafi school treats this transaction as an exchange (mu‘awada) analogous to sale, requiring the slave's acceptance for immediate effect. The text also clarifies that the payment can be any known genus of property, and distinguishes this from the kitabah contract where slavery continues. Understanding this ruling sharpens your skill in applying qiyas and in distinguishing analogous transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the master doing? What is the legal effect after the slave accepts? The text states that the slave becomes free (عتق) and the payment becomes a debt.
The ruling is sahih (valid) and freedom is immediate.
2
Locate the dalil
The text uses qiyas (analogy) with sale. Identify the original case (asl), the new case (far'), and the effective cause (illa).
The asl is sale, the far' is manumission for payment, and the illa is that both are exchanges (mu‘awada) of property.
3
Analyze the requirement of acceptance
Why must the slave accept? Because it is an exchange, and in exchange contracts, mutual consent is required. The text says 'إنما يعتق بقبوله' (he only becomes free by his acceptance).
Contrast with kitabah where acceptance is also required but the effect is different.
4
Distinguish from kitabah
The text contrasts this payment (ju‘l) with the kitabah payment (badal al-kitabah). In kitabah, slavery persists until full payment, so it is not a pure debt and cannot be guaranteed by a third party.
This distinction is crucial for understanding the legal nature of the debt.
5
Determine the scope of permissible payment
The text states that 'مال' (payment) includes cash, goods, and animals, as long as the genus is known. Slight ignorance of description (like quality) is tolerated.
The payment must be known in genus; otherwise, the transaction becomes fasid.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof stating the Hanafi position on freeing a slave for a consideration (العتق على جعل). The ruling: if a master says to his slave 'You are free upon a thousand dirhams' and the slave accepts, the slave becomes immediately free, and the specified amount becomes a debt owed by the slave. The proof uses qiyas (analogy) with sale: in sale, ownership transfers upon mutual consent; here, freedom transfers upon acceptance because it is an exchange (معاوضة) of property (the freedom) for non-property (the payment). Acceptance is required for immediate effect, like in sale. However, unlike sale, the slave does not own himself, so the payment is analogized to a debt. A key distinction from kitabah (contract of manumission): in kitabah, the payment is not a pure debt because slavery continues until full payment; here, freedom is immediate, so it becomes a pure debt, and can be guaranteed by a third party (كفالة). The text also states that 'payment' (مال) is unqualified and includes cash, goods, and animals, whether specified or not, because the exchange is property for non-property, similar to mahr in marriage, divorce compensation, or blood money settlement. Ignorance of description (جهالة الوصف) is tolerated because it is slight (يسيرة) – the genus must be known, but quality can be median.

عتق على جعل (‘itq ‘ala ju‘l): Manumission of a slave in exchange for a specified payment (ju‘l), where freedom is immediate upon acceptance and payment becomes a debt.
بدل الكتابة (badal al-kitābah): The payment in a kitabah contract, which is not a pure debt because slavery persists until full payment.

Compensation When Service Is Impossible in 'Itq 'ala Ja'l
Ruling on compensation when service is impossible in 'itq 'ala ja'l

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation when service cannot be rendered in 'itq 'ala ja'l and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the nature of service as compensation.

Core Idea
When service is impossible in 'itq 'ala ja'l, the slave's value is paid, not the price of service, because service compensates the slave's inherent value ('ishq).

This lesson examines a specific ruling in 'itq 'ala ja'l: what happens if the service (khidmah) that the slave was to perform becomes impossible? The passage explains that the slave's value must be paid, not a valuation of the service. We will explore the reasoning behind this and an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two shaykhs (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its difficulty
The act under consideration is 'itq 'ala ja'l, where a slave is promised freedom in exchange for service. The difficulty arises when that service becomes impossible (e.g., due to death of slave or master, or entitlement).
2
State the ruling
The author rules that the slave's value (qimah) must be paid to the master, not a price for the unperformed service.
3
Understand Imam Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that service (khidmah) is compensation for the slave's 'ishq (inherent value), which is not considered wealth. Since 'ishq cannot be priced, if service is impossible, we take the slave's value instead of trying to price the service.
'Ishq refers to the intrinsic value of the slave's person, distinct from market price.
4
Understand the Sheikhain's reasoning
The two shaykhs (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) view service as compensation for the slave's person, which is wealth. But if service is impossible, they also agree that the slave's value must be paid because rescission (returning the slave) is not valid in such contracts.
Both positions converge on the same practical outcome.
5
Note the practical convergence
Despite differing on the nature of service, both Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sheikhain conclude that the slave's value is taken. The difference is theoretical, not practical.
This is a key example of intra-madhhab ikhtilaf with identical fatwa.

Ordering Manumission with Marriage Condition, Slave Refuses
Ruling on 'itq with conditional marriage and refusal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ordering manumission with a conditional marriage and explain the legal distinction between 'itq and talaq regarding stipulating compensation on a stranger.

Core Idea
In 'itq, a stranger's promise to pay is not binding, unlike in talaq where it is by analogy with khul'.

This passage presents a detailed case where someone orders another to free a slave-girl with a conditional marriage, but the slave-girl refuses. The ruling is that the manumission is valid and the orderer owes nothing. The Hanafi school bases this on the principle that stipulating compensation on a stranger is invalid in 'itq, contrasting with talaq where it is valid by qiyas with khul'. Let us walk through the legal reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is ordering another (the owner) to free his slave-girl, with the condition that the owner marries her to the orderer. The orderer promises to pay a thousand dirhams to the owner.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle for 'itq
In 'itq, stipulating compensation on a stranger (the orderer) is invalid. Therefore, even if the owner frees the slave-girl, the orderer is not liable for the promised sum.
The text analogizes to the simple case of ordering manumission without a marriage condition, where the orderer also owes nothing.
3
Distinguish from talaq
In talaq, by qiyas with khul', stipulating compensation on a stranger is valid. Thus, if someone orders a divorce with a similar promise, the orderer becomes liable.
This distinction shows the different nature of 'itq and talaq in Hanafi law.

Ordering Manumission 'from me' with Marriage Condition
Ruling on division of price when manumission ordered with 'anni' and marriage condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when someone orders manumission of another's slave-girl with the phrase 'from me' and a condition of marriage, and explain the division of the price between value and dower.

Core Idea
When 'from me' implies purchase, the price splits; only the value portion is due if marriage fails.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced ruling from Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir. The orderer says 'Free your slave-girl from me for a thousand dirhams, provided you marry her to me.' The Hanafi school uses the principle of iqtida' (implication) to treat the phrase 'from me' as including a purchase. The promised sum is then divided between the slave-girl's market value (for the purchase) and her mahr mithl (for the marriage). We will follow the reasoning and the two possible outcomes based on whether the marriage occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and condition
Recognize that the orderer says 'free your slave-girl from me' (anni) and adds a condition to marry her. This is a conditional manumission order with a fixed price.
The phrase 'anni' is critical; without it, the ruling would differ.
2
Apply the principle of iqtida'
The word 'anni' in the context of manumission legally implies a purchase. Because you cannot free someone else's property without owning it, the order is interpreted as an offer to buy first.
This is based on the usul principle that words are taken to imply their necessary concomitants.
3
Divide the amount proportionally
The thousand dirhams is allocated between the slave-girl's market value (purchase price) and her mahr mithl (standard dower). The proportion is based on the ratio of the value to the sum of value and mahr.
If the slave-girl's value is 400 and mahr mithl is 600, then 400 of the promised sum goes to value and 600 to mahr.
4
Determine liability based on marriage outcome
If marriage is refused, the orderer must pay the value portion (because the purchase is effective), but the mahr portion is cancelled. If marriage occurs, the value portion goes to the master and the mahr portion becomes the slave-girl's dower.
The cancellation occurs because the condition (marriage) that formed part of the consideration is not fulfilled.

Division of Payment in Combined Freeing and Marriage
Division of payment in a combined freeing and marriage transaction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of iqtida' (necessary implication) to derive the division of payment in a combined transaction of freeing a slave-girl with a condition of marriage.

Core Idea
When a command implies both purchase and marriage, the payment is divided: the value portion is due, the dower portion is void if marriage fails.

This lesson examines a nuanced transaction where a man instructs another to free his slave-girl for a fee, with the condition that the master marry her to the commander. The payment of one thousand dirhams is not a simple lump sum; it must be allocated between the purchase of the slave-girl and the dower for marriage. Through the Hanafi principle of iqtida' (necessary implication), we uncover how the phrase 'on my behalf' ('anni) triggers a purchase, leading to a fair division of the payment. Understanding this ruling sharpens your ability to analyze combined contracts and the role of implied meanings in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Transaction
The commander says: 'Free your slave-girl on my behalf for one thousand dirhams, with the condition that you marry her to me.' This is a single utterance containing both a freeing and a marriage condition. Your first task is to recognize that this combines two distinct legal acts: purchase (through the implication of freeing on his behalf) and marriage.
The act of freeing on someone's behalf implies that the commander is buying the slave-girl to free her, as he cannot free another's property without ownership.
2
Apply Iqtida' to Reveal Purchase
The phrase 'anni ('on my behalf') is not a direct purchase command, but by necessary implication (iqtida'), it establishes that the commander intends to pay for the slave-girl's value to acquire her. In Hanafi usul, speech may imply what is necessary for its validity: here, freeing on his behalf requires that he first owns her, which necessitates purchase.
This is a key usul principle: iqtida' expands the meaning of an utterance to include its necessary legal accompaniments.
3
Divide the Payment Proportionally
The total fee (1000 dirhams) is split between the slave-girl's market value (qimah) and the dower of a woman of her status (mahr mithl). The portion corresponding to the value is for the purchase (which is delivered through freeing), and the portion for the dower is for the marriage benefit (bud'), which is conditional on the marriage actually taking place.
The division is proportional: if the value is 600 and mahr mithl is 400, then 600 is for purchase and 400 for dower.
4
Determine Delivery and Obligation
The slave-girl's person is delivered (she is freed) – so the value portion becomes wajib on the commander. However, the marriage benefit (bud') is not delivered if she refuses marriage – so the dower portion becomes void (batal) from the commander. If marriage does occur, the dower portion becomes her actual dower.
This follows the rule that payment is only due for what is received.
5
Verify with Qiyas (Analogy)
The ruling is analogized to the sale of a slave and a mudabbar (a slave who will be freed upon master's death) for one price: if the mudabbar cannot be delivered, its portion of the price falls. Here, the combination of purchase and marriage is similar – one benefit (person) is delivered, the other (bud') may not be.
This qiyas strengthens the ruling and shows consistency within Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on a combined transaction. A man says to another: 'Free your slave-girl on my behalf for one thousand dirhams, with the condition that you marry her to me.' The master complies, but the slave-girl refuses marriage after freedom. The payment of one thousand dirhams is divided proportionally between the slave-girl's market value and her like's dower (mahr mithl). The portion allocated to her value is due from the commander because he effectively purchased her and then freed her through the master. The portion allocated to the dower is void because the marriage was not concluded—the benefit of the pudendum was not delivered. This is because the phrase 'on my behalf' ('anni) implies purchase (shira') by necessary implication (iqtida'), as established in Hanafi usul. Thus the commander is treated as having bought the slave-girl (value portion) and contracted marriage (dower portion). If the marriage does occur, the dower portion becomes her dower. Imam Muhammad in al-Jami' al-Saghir also indicates that if the marriage takes place, the dower portion is hers. The analogy is drawn to the sale of a slave and a mudabbar together: the price is divided, and if one part cannot be delivered, its price falls. Here, the slave-girl's person is delivered (as she is freed), but the marriage benefit is not delivered upon her refusal, so that portion is dropped.

Declaring One's Property Haram: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Hanafi ruling with Shafi'i dispute on tahrim al-halal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for tahrim al-halal and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Declaring halal haram is an oath, not a change in divine law – violating it requires kaffarah.

What happens when a person says 'this is haram for me' about something they own? Does it actually become forbidden? The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ sharply. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and shows how the Hanafis treat this statement as a binding oath, while the Shafi'is view it as an invalid act. Understanding this dispute clarifies a key principle: human declarations cannot override Allah's rulings, but they can create personal obligations.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify exactly what the Hanafi school holds: the declaration does not make the thing haram, but it constitutes an oath (yamin). If the person then uses the thing, they violate the oath and must pay kaffarah yamin.
The thing remains halal in itself; the prohibition is only through the oath.
2
Identify the opposing school and its view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that no kaffarah is required because declaring halal haram is an invalid act (reversal of Shari'ah), so no valid oath is formed.
Shafi'is argue that one cannot change what Allah has made halal.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i dalil: tahrim al-halal is qalb al-mashru' (reversal of the prescribed), so a prescribed act (the oath) cannot be concluded from it.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: the wording can be given effect by interpreting it as an oath, preserving the speaker's intent and the linguistic meaning. This is an application of istihsan to avoid making the statement meaningless.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi law, kaffarah is wajib upon violation; under Shafi'i law, no kaffarah is required.
The ruling affects whether someone must feed/clothe the poor or free a slave.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafis and Imam al-Shafi'i. The Hanafi position is that when someone says 'what I own is haram for me,' the thing does not become haram in itself. Instead, this statement is treated as an oath (yamin). The commentary explains that the expiation becomes wajib even for a small amount of the forbidden thing, because once the oath is established, it covers every part.

Manumission of the First Purchased Slave
Ruling on which slave is freed when one says 'the first slave I purchase is free'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath to free the first purchased slave and apply the linguistic conditions of 'awwal' to determine which slave is freed in scenarios of simultaneous and singular purchases.

Core Idea
The word 'awwal' requires both precedence and singularity; without both, no slave is freed.

This lesson examines a common oath in slave trading: 'The first slave I purchase is free.' The Hanafi school interprets this with linguistic precision, demanding that the purchase be both chronologically first and singular. We will see how this works in practice and how adding 'wahdahu' (alone) changes the outcome.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic definition of 'awwal'
Recognize that 'awwal' (first) in Arabic denotes a preceding individual (fard sabiq). This requires both chronological precedence (sabq) and uniqueness/singularity (tafarud) in the context of a purchase.
This is a talil (linguistic reasoning), not a revealed text.
2
Apply to a single purchase
If the person purchases one slave after the oath, that slave is clearly the first and singular, so he is freed. This is the straightforward case.
3
Analyze a simultaneous purchase
If the first purchase includes two slaves together, neither is singularly first (they are purchased concurrently), so neither qualifies as 'awwal'. The condition of singularity is absent.
Even though chronologically they are first, the lack of uniqueness invalidates their claim to being 'the first'.
4
Consider a subsequent purchase
If a third slave is purchased later, he is not chronologically first, so he also does not satisfy the condition. Thus, no slave is freed in this scenario.
5
Add the word 'wahdahu' (alone)
If the oath includes 'wahdahu' (alone), the condition shifts to requiring singularity at the moment of purchase. The first purchase that is made singly (even if not chronologically first overall) will be the one that meets the condition.
Thus, if the first transaction is a pair, that pair is ignored; the first subsequent single purchase becomes the 'first singular purchase' and is freed.

Oath and the Wakeel: When Agent's Action Does Not Cause Hanth
Ruling on breaking an oath when a wakeel performs the prohibited transaction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on breaking an oath when a wakeel performs the prohibited transaction and identify the two exceptions.

Core Idea
The oath is not broken when an agent acts — only the swearer's own deed counts, unless intent or custom dictates otherwise.

Oaths to refrain from transactions are common. What happens if you appoint someone else to do the exact thing you swore not to do? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling based on attribution of the contract. This lesson explores that ruling and its exceptions, sharpening your understanding of how legal responsibility attaches to individual actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Oath and the Agent's Action
Read the source: the oath is not to sell, buy, or lease. The swearer appoints a wakeel (agent) who performs the forbidden act.
The wakeel's role is to execute the contract on behalf of the principal.
2
Determine the Ruling on Hanth
The ruling is that the oath is not broken (lam yahnith). The reason: the contract is executed by the agent, not the swearer.
Hanth means breaking the oath, incurring sin and expiation.
3
Understand the Legal Reasoning (Ta'lil)
The contract is attributed to the person who actually performs it. Rights and obligations (e.g., ownership, liability) fall on the agent. Therefore, the condition for hanth—the act coming from the swearer—is absent.
This attribution is a key Hanafi principle: 'Al-aqd yudaf ila al-aqid'.
4
Recognize the Two Exceptions
Exception 1: If the swearer intended the wakeel's action when swearing, then hanth occurs because he intended the means. Exception 2: If the swearer is a ruler (sultan) who does not personally handle contracts, the wakeel's act is attributed to him by custom.
In the ruler's case, his habitual practice is to act through agents, so the oath is interpreted accordingly.
5
Apply the Ruling to New Scenarios
Given a scenario (e.g., a person swears not to sell, then tells his friend to sell), decide whether hanth occurs, citing the general rule or an exception.
Test your understanding by considering variations in intention and position.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A flaw does not change the genus, so payment with defective silver still fulfills the oath.

Imagine you swear to repay a debt today with silver dirhams. You pay, but later the creditor finds some coins are worn or counterfeit. Is your oath broken? The Hanafi school answers with a clear principle: as long as the payment is of the same genus (silver), the oath stands fulfilled. This lesson unpacks that ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Recognize the scenario: the debtor swore to pay a specific debt 'today' and made the payment. The question is whether the oath is fulfilled (birr) or broken (hanth).

2
Classify the defective coins
The text lists three types: zuhuf (worn/clipped), nabarhajah (counterfeit but same metal), mustahaqqah (legally defective). All share the same genus (silver).
Compare with coins of a different metal: lead (rasas) or copper (sutuqah).

A flaw ('ayb) does not eliminate the genus (jins). Since the defective coins are still silver, the payment satisfies the debt in terms of genus. Accidental acceptance by the creditor solidifies the fulfillment.

4
Determine hanth based on genus match
If the genus matches, no hanth. If the genus differs (different metal), hanth occurs because the oath was to pay with silver dirhams, not with other metals.
The ruling is outcome-based: the oath-taker is not sinful if the genus is preserved.

However, if the coins are made of a different substance (lead or copper), they are not of the same genus as the currency, so the oath is not fulfilled and the debtor becomes sinful (hanith). The reasoning is based on the principle that payment must be in the same genus; flaws within the genus are acceptable, but a different substance changes the genus entirely.

Key Terms
زہوف (zuhuf): A fake or heavily worn coin that is no longer legal tender but still of the same metal.
نبرجہ (nabarhajah): A counterfeit coin that mimics the real currency but is of similar material.
مستحقة (mustahaqqah): A coin that is legally defective or subject to claim by someone else.
جنس (jins): Category or genus; here referring to the type of metal (silver) used for dirhams.

Ruling on Swearing to Repay a Debt Today with Defective or Fake Coins
Ruling on oath fulfillment with defective and fake coins

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between defective coins and fake coins in fulfilling oaths to repay debts, and apply the Hanafi principle that a defect does not negate the genus.

Core Idea
A defect in a coin does not break an oath to repay; only a different genus coin does.

When someone swears to repay a debt today and pays, but the coins are later found to be defective or fake, the key is whether the coins are still of the same genus (dirhams). The Hanafi school uses the principle that a defect ('ayb) does not annihilate the genus (jins). This lesson examines the ruling for various coin qualities and alternative repayment methods like muqasah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its condition
The swearer must repay a debt today using dirhams. Understand the exact terms of the oath.
2
Analyze the coin types
Determine if the coins are defective (zayf, nabhrajah, mustahaqqah) or fake (rasas, sutooqa). Each type has a different effect on the oath.
Defective coins are those rejected by treasury but accepted by merchants (zayfah) or rejected even by merchants (nabhrajah) or claimed by a third party (mustahaqqah). Fake coins are made of lead (rasas) or plated (sutooqa).
3
Apply the genus principle
Defective coins are still dirhams (genus remains), so payment is valid. Fake coins are not dirhams, so payment is void and oath broken.
The principle: 'al-ayb la yu'dimu al-jins'.
4
Consider alternative repayment methods
Selling a slave in lieu of the debt constitutes repayment via muqasah (set-off), fulfilling the oath. Forgiving the debt (hibah) does not, as repayment is the debtor's act.
Muqasah is effective even without physical possession of coins.
5
State the ruling
Oath fulfilled with defective coins; broken with fake coins; fulfilled with muqasah; not fulfilled with hibah.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath to give via gift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oath fulfillment by hibah without acceptance and explain the distinction from sale (bay').

Core Idea
For oath fulfillment, hibah (gift) is completed by the donor alone; acceptance is not required.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether an oath to give something as a gift (hibah) is fulfilled if the recipient does not accept. The majority of Hanafi imams hold that the oath is fulfilled by the donor's act of giving alone, while Imam Zufar disagrees, analogizing hibah to sale. By understanding the legal reasoning, you will grasp how the Hanafi school distinguishes gratuitous contracts (tabarru') from commutative ones (mu'awadah).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: if someone swears to give his slave to so-and-so and then gives it as hibah, but the donee does not accept, the oath is fulfilled. This is the position of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
The ruling is about oath fulfillment, not the validity of the gift itself.
2
Identify the opposing view of Imam Zufar
Imam Zufar holds that the oath is not fulfilled without acceptance because he analogizes hibah to sale (bay') since both are transfers of ownership (tamlik).
Zufar's analogy is qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) based on the common element of ownership transfer.
3
Trace the Hanafi response
The majority rebut by distinguishing hibah as a gratuitous contract (tabarru') whereas sale is commutative (mu'awadah). In tabarru', the purpose is generosity and is achieved by the donor's action alone. They support this with linguistic usage: 'He gave a gift and it was not accepted' is a valid statement.
The key distinction is the nature of the contract: gratuitous vs. exchange.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is that if the donee refuses, according to the majority the oath is still fulfilled; according to Zufar, it is not. This affects whether the oath-swearer is considered sinful or must expiate.
The mufta bihi is the majority position, so in fatwa, the oath is fulfilled.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute between the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) and Imam Zufar regarding an oath to give something as a gift. The ruling: if someone swears to give his slave to a specific person, and then makes a hibah (gift) to that person, but the donee does not accept, the oath is fulfilled. Sale (bay') is a commutative contract requiring reciprocal action (offer and acceptance) from both parties. Thus, the analogy fails. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that the oath is fulfilled by the hibah alone.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In oaths without intention, custom determines the meaning of ambiguous words like 'banafsaj'.

This lesson shows how the Hanafi school relies on custom ('urf) to interpret oaths. When a person swears not to buy 'banafsaj' without specifying intention, the marketplace custom often defines it as violet oil. However, the ruling can vary by region, as illustrated by Faqih Abu al-Layth's differing opinion. Understanding this interplay between literal meaning and custom is crucial for issuing correct fatwas in diverse contexts.

Muftis must ascertain the local 'urf before issuing fatwas.

Custom Distinguishing Male and Female Animals in Oaths
Ruling on custom distinguishing male and female animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that custom (urf) determines the scope of animal names in oaths, distinguishing between male and female animals.

Core Idea
Custom gives distinct names to male and female animals; an oath on one does not cover the other.

In Hanafi fiqh, the scope of an oath is determined by the custom of the swearer's community. This short benefit from Al-Hidayah illustrates that when custom assigns distinct names to male and female animals (e.g., cow vs. bull), an oath sworn on one gender does not include the other. Understanding this principle is crucial for determining whether an oath is broken (hanath).

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its object
Determine exactly what the swearer said: e.g., 'I swear not to eat cow (gai).' Note the specific name used.
The literal wording matters, but custom may override literal generality.
2
Determine the relevant custom
Find the custom of the local community regarding names for male and female animals. Is there a distinct term for each gender?
Custom must be widespread and established, not individual preference.
3
Apply custom to the oath's object
Compare the name used in the oath with the custom. If custom distinguishes, then the oath applies only to the gender covered by that name.
If the name is generic (e.g., 'cattle'), custom may still restrict it if community usage does so.
4
Rule on breach (hanath)
If the swearer does an act that falls outside the custom-defined scope, the oath is not broken. If within, it is broken and expiation may be required.
This analysis assumes no explicit intention to the contrary; intention can override custom if expressed.

Oath Not to Marry: Third Party Intervention and Mode of Consent
Rulings on oath not to marry with third party

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oath-breaking when a third party arranges a marriage, distinguishing between verbal permission and written/implicit consent.

Core Idea
Verbal permission to a third party's marriage arrangement breaks an oath not to marry, but written or implied consent does not.

When a person swears not to marry, but a third party arranges his marriage, the mode of his consent matters. This passage from Ashraf ul Hadaya outlines three scenarios that clarify when an oath is considered broken. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for accurate fatwa and avoiding unnecessary kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three distinct cases
The passage presents three scenarios: (1) oath not to marry, third party arranges marriage, (2) intermediary marries first, then oath, then verbal permission, (3) oath that any wife is divorced, then permission to third party.
Cases 2 and 3 have special rules.
2
Determine the form of consent in each case
For case 1, the form of consent can be verbal, written, or implicit (like paying mahr). For case 2, it is only verbal permission after the marriage. For case 3, it is permission through action to a third party.
The form determines hanath or not.
3
Apply the rule: only direct verbal consent causes hanath in case 1
In case 1, verbal permission makes him hanith; written or implicit does not. This is the fatwa.
Written or implicit actions are not considered direct consent.
4
Note the consensus in case 2 and special ruling in case 3
In case 2, even verbal permission after the oath does not make him hanith by consensus. In case 3, permission through action does not break the oath either.
These are exceptions to the general rule.
