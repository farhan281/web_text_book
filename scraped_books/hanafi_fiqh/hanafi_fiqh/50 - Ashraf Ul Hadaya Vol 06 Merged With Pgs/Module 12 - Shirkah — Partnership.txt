Istilad of Jointly-Owned Maidservant and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on co-owner claiming child from jointly-owned maidservant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on lineage when a co-owner claims a child from a jointly-owned maidservant and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding umm walad status.

Core Idea
Lineage is indivisible — the child belongs entirely to the claiming co-owner; but the umm walad status of the mother splits the Hanafi school.

This passage addresses a specific scenario of joint ownership (shirkah) where one partner impregnates the jointly-owned maidservant and claims the child. We already know from previous lessons that istilad establishes lineage and umm walad status for a sole owner. Here, the complication of joint ownership forces a deeper examination of how lineage and umm walad interact with partial ownership. The Hanafi masters agree on lineage but differ on the mother's status, and we will trace their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal consequence
The act is one co-owner having intercourse with the jointly-owned maidservant, she gives birth, and he claims the child. The first consequence is the establishment of lineage (nasab). The second is the status of the maidservant as umm walad.
Note that lineage is established even though ownership is partial.
2
Analyze the proof for lineage being complete
The text argues that lineage cannot be divided because its cause (insemination) is indivisible — one child cannot be conceived from two separate sources. Since lineage is proven in the part he owns, it necessarily extends to the whole. This is a qiyas based on the indivisibility of the cause.
The proof uses the principle that what cannot be divided when established partly must apply fully.
3
Contrast the positions on umm walad
Here the intra-Hanafi dispute appears: The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that istilad itself is indivisible, so she becomes entirely umm walad immediately. Abu Hanifa holds that only the share he owns becomes umm walad; he must then acquire the partner's share by paying its value, after which she becomes fully umm walad.
The difference hinges on whether the legal effect (umm walad) follows from the act (istilad) or from ownership.
4
Examine the additional obligations
The co-owner must pay half of the 'uqr (compensation) because he had intercourse with a jointly-owned maidservant — the other half being due to the partner. He does not owe the child's value because the child is free from the moment of conception, and the child's lineage traces back to that moment, which is before any potential ownership conflict.
This distinguishes from the father-son case where ownership is a condition that precedes istilad.

Commentary
This passage deals with the case of a jointly-owned maidservant (shirkah) where one of the co-owners has intercourse with her, she bears a child, and he claims the child. The ruling: lineage is established entirely to the claiming co-owner. The reasoning: lineage cannot be partial; since it is established in the half that he owns, it must apply to the whole, because the cause (insemination) is indivisible—one child cannot come from two sperm. Therefore, the child's entire lineage is attributed to him. As for her becoming umm walad, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), she becomes entirely umm walad for him because istilad is indivisible. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, only his share becomes umm walad initially; then he must acquire the partner's share by paying its value, since that share is capable of ownership. After acquisition, the whole maidservant becomes umm walad. Additionally, the claiming co-owner must pay half of her 'uqr (compensation for intercourse with a jointly-owned property). The text distinguishes this from the case of the father with his son's maidservant: there, ownership precedes istilad as a condition (the father does not own the maidservant at the time of intercourse, but later acquires it as a cause for istilad), so the father's case is different—he does not owe the child's value because the child was free from conception (as explained in the previous lesson).

Key Terms
شركة (shirkah): Joint ownership of a maidservant between two partners.
عقر ('uqr): Compensation for intercourse with a jointly-owned maidservant, here half the 'uqr is due because only half was owned.
استيلاد (istilād): Act of impregnating a maidservant owned or partially owned, leading to umm walad status.

Checkpoint
According to the Hanafi school (mufta bihi), what is the status of a jointly-owned maidservant when one co-owner impregnates her and claims the child?
A) She becomes umm walad only for the claiming co-owner's share, and he must buy the other share.
B) She becomes umm walad entirely for the claiming co-owner immediately.
C) She remains jointly owned and the child is free.
D) She becomes free immediately because of the child.

Key Takeaway
In joint ownership, lineage is indivisible and fully attributed to the claiming co-owner, but the umm walad status is disputed; the mufta bihi is that she becomes entirely umm walad.
