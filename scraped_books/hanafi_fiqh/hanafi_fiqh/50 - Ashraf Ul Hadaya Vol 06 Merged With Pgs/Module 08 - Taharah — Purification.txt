Father's Liability for Daughter's Circumcision
Fā'ida on who bears cost of daughter's circumcision

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the father bears the cost of his daughter's circumcision and explain the awlawiyyah (a fortiori) reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
Father must pay for daughter's circumcision by awlawiyyah from stranger's liability.

This short fā'ida (benefit) from the commentary on Al-Hidayah clarifies a practical issue: if a father arranges and pays for his young daughter's circumcision from his own wealth, the cost is binding on him. The reasoning is drawn by analogy (qiyas) from the case of a stranger who would also be liable; if a stranger is obligated, the father—who has a stronger connection—is obligated with greater reason (awlawiyyah). This ruling is a clear application of qiyas jaliy where the 'illa (effective cause) is the undertaking of payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Subject
The passage states a ruling: the cost of a minor daughter's circumcision is binding (lāzim) on the father if he paid from his own wealth. Note that the act of circumcision itself is sunnah, not obligatory.
The ruling is about financial liability, not the obligation to circumcise.
2
Recognize the Method of Derivation: Qiyas
The commentator uses qiyas: the father's case is compared to that of a stranger. The 'illa (reason) is that payment was undertaken. Since a stranger who undertakes payment becomes liable, the father, who has a stronger relationship, is even more liable.
This is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) based on awlawiyyah (a fortiori).
3
Apply the Asl-Far' Structure
Asl (original case): a stranger who pays for circumcision bears the cost. Far' (new case): the father pays for his daughter's circumcision. 'Illa: the act of paying/undertaking payment. Hukm: liability is transferred to the father.
The analogy holds because the 'illa is present in both cases.
4
Note the Consequence: Binding Obligation
The ruling is that the cost becomes binding (wajib) on the father once he undertakes it. This is not a mere voluntary gift; it creates a financial obligation.
This aligns with the general Hanafi principle that the person commissioning a service bears the cost.

Chapter of Istilad. Explanation: The lexical meaning of istilad is to obtain a child. By istilad is meant to have intercourse with one's slave-girl and obtain a child from her. If in reality the lineage of such a child is established to its father (i.e., the master of its mother) and he acknowledges it, then such istilad is proven; otherwise not. Rather, istilad is dependent on the establishment of lineage. Now, if he performed istilad with the slave-girl and a child is born, she becomes his Umm al-Walad (mother of his son). "When the slave-girl gives birth from her master, she becomes Umm al-Walad for him; it is not permissible to sell her nor to make her a gift without compensation, etc." Because of this hadith in which the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, said: "Her child has freed her." Benefit: The mentioned hadith is narrated by Ibn Majah, al-Daraqutni, and al-Hakim from Ibn Abbas, may Allah be pleased with him, that the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, was mentioned the mother of his son Ibrahim, i.e., Mariyah al-Qibtiyyah, and he said: "Her son has freed her." The author has indicated this hadith, but its chain is weak. And Imam Muhammad narrated in Kitab al-Athar from Umar, may Allah be pleased with him, that the Prophet, peace be upon him, used to say from the pulpit in a loud voice: "Selling Umm al-Walad women is forbidden." When the slave-girl gives birth from her master, she becomes free; thereafter she cannot be a mere bondmaid. This chain is authentic. And when the Prophet, peace be upon him, said this on the pulpit in front of all the Companions and no one objected, the consensus of the Companions, may Allah be pleased with them, on this matter is established. This meaning is narrated by Ibn Majah and Imam Ahmad from Ibn Abbas, and by Abu Dawud from Salamah bint Ma'qil. And Imam Malik narrated in al-Muwatta' from Umar, may Allah be pleased with him, that a slave-girl who bears a child from her owner cannot be sold nor given as a gift; rather, he will benefit from her as long as she lives, then when he dies she becomes free. Its chain is authentic. Shaykh Khata'i said that its proof is also that in a sound hadith it is established from the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, that he said: "We, the group of prophets, do not leave heirs, nor does anyone inherit from us. Whatever we leave is charity for Allah." Yet the Prophet, peace be upon him, left Mariyah al-Qibtiyyah, and she was his Umm al-Walad. So if selling Umm al-Walad were permissible, the price of Mariyah would have been given in charity. That did not happen at all.

Umm Walad is Not Valuable Property for Debt and Qisas
Ruling on the legal status of umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the umm walad is not valuable property and apply it to scenarios involving debt and qisas claims.

Core Idea
The umm walad's maternal bond elevates her status, making her immune to seizure for her master's debts.

This passage clarifies a unique aspect of Hanafi law: a slave woman who has borne her master's child (umm walad) is not considered valuable property. Consequently, creditors cannot force her to work to repay debts, nor can she be seized for qisas. The ruling contrasts with the mudabbar slave, who remains valuable property. We'll examine the legal reasoning and practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject
Recognize that the passage discusses the status of the umm walad, not a typical slave. Note her special maternal link.
umm walad literally means 'mother of a child'.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that the umm walad is not ‘muttaqawwim’ (valuable property). Therefore, creditors cannot claim her for repayment of debts, nor can she be taken for qisas.
The same logic extends to other claims like compensation for usurpation (ghasb).
3
Contrast with mudabbar
The passage explicitly contrasts with the mudabbar, who is valuable property. Understand that the mudabbar is a slave promised freedom upon the master's death, but still subject to claims during the master's lifetime.
The distinction hinges on the umm walad's unique status via her child.
4
Identify the evidence
The ruling is based on a hadith from Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib that the umm walad becomes free from the entire estate upon the master's death, indicating her elevated status even during his lifetime.
The full hadith is not quoted, but the citation is sufficient for the legal inference.
5
Apply to scenarios
Given the ruling, if a master has debts, the umm walad cannot be compelled to work to pay them. Similarly, if the master commits qisas, the victim's heirs cannot take the umm walad as compensation.
This protection applies only to the umm walad, not to regular slaves or mudabbars.

Christian's Umm Walad Who Becomes Muslim: Must Work for Freedom
Intra-Hanafi dispute on umm walad's status after conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the majority Hanafi ruling on the Christian's umm walad who converts to Islam, explain Zufar's contrary position, and evaluate the Hanafi response using the qiyas to shared qisas.

Core Idea
A Christian's umm walad who becomes Muslim must work for her value; she is not freed until she pays.

This passage addresses a delicate intra-Hanafi dispute: a Christian's umm walad (slave mother of his child) converts to Islam, but her master remains Christian. The majority holds she must perform sa'yah (work to earn her value) like a mukatabah, while Imam Zufar argues she is freed immediately with a debt. The disagreement turns on how to balance the removal of degradation (dhull) from the convert with the master's property rights. Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the precise case
Identify the scenario: a Christian master's umm walad accepts Islam, and the master refuses to convert. The question is her legal status and path to freedom.
If the master also converts, she remains a slave no change.
2
Identify the majority Hanafi ruling
The matn declares that she must work for her market value (sa'yah) and is like a mukatabah—she is not freed until she pays.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.
3
Identify Zufar's opposing view
Imam Zufar holds she is freed immediately, and the sa'yah becomes a debt on her.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab dispute.
4
Analyse the proofs
Zufar: removing degradation is wajib; sale is impossible, so emancipation is necessary. Majority: making her a mukatabah removes degradation (she becomes free after paying) and protects the master's interests; an analogy to shared qisas shows that even respected persons require compensation when a right is lost.
The majority also warns that immediate freedom while insolvent could cause laziness.
5
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Zufar: she is free immediately and owes a debt. Majority: she remains a slave-like mukatabah until she pays; she does not become free until the money is earned.
The majority view is the mufta bihi (operative for fatwa).

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute between the majority Hanafi position (represented by the matn) and Imam Zufar regarding the Christian's umm walad who converts to Islam. The text states the main ruling: she must work (sa'yah) to pay her market value and becomes like a mukatabah (a slave under contract to purchase freedom). She is not freed until she pays. Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) argues she is freed immediately, with the sa'yah as a debt. The disagreement hinges on the master's refusal of Islam; if he converts, she remains his slave. Zufar's proof: it is wajib to remove degradation (dhull) from her after her Islam, and the only feasible way is emancipation (since sale is impossible as she is not fully owned by the master due to her child). The Hanafi response: making her a mukatabah balances interests—she gains freedom through her own effort, and the master gets compensation. If she were freed while insolvent, she might become lazy. The master considers her valuable, so he should receive compensation. Even if not valuable, she is respected (muhtaramah), and respect suffices for compensation, analogous to shared qisas where one pardon triggers diyah for the remaining guardians. Special cases: if the master dies, she is freed without sa'yah (as her status as umm walad alone suffices). If she becomes incapable during his lifetime, she is not returned to slave status because the cause (her Islam, his remaining a disbeliever) persists, so she remains a mukatabah.

Key Terms
سعایہ (sa'yah): The effort or work a slave does to earn money to pay for her freedom; here, the umm walad must work to pay her market value.
مکاتبہ (mukatabah): A female slave who enters into a contract (kitabah) with her master to purchase her freedom in installments.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master; the child is free, and she has a special status that prevents her sale and grants her freedom upon the master's death.

Umm Walad Through Marriage and Later Ownership
Ruling on umm walad via marriage then ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a man who marries another's slave girl and later owns her makes her his umm walad, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument by identifying the role of lineage (nasab) versus status at conception.

Core Idea
The child's lineage, not the mother's status at conception, establishes umm walad status.

This passage presents a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The core issue is whether a slave girl becomes an umm walad when her owner marries her while she belongs to another, has a child, and then acquires her. The answer hinges on what triggers the umm walad relationship: the child's freedom at conception (Shafi'i) or the mere establishment of lineage (Hanafi). Understanding this distinction is key to grasping Hanafi legal reasoning regarding partiality (juz'iyyah).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the slave girl becomes umm walad upon acquisition after marriage and childbirth.
The text states: 'thumma malakaha sārat umm walad lahu'.
2
Identify Shafi'i's objection
Recognize that al-Shafi'i disagrees: she does not become umm walad because at conception the child was a slave.
Shafi'i argues that the child's slave status at inception prevents partiality.
3
Follow Shafi'i's proof
Examine Shafi'i's analogy: just as a child from zina does not make a fornicator's later ownership result in umm walad status, so here the child was conceived in slavery.
The analogy relies on the absence of free lineage at conception.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi distinction: in marriage, lineage (nasab) is established, which creates partiality (juz'iyyah) between child and parents; in zina, no lineage exists.
Partiality is via lineage, not the mother's status. The child of a deceived person (maghruur) is considered free for lineage.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Note the practical consequence: under Hanafi, she becomes umm walad with all rights; under Shafi'i, she remains a regular slave.
This affects her freedom upon owner's death, etc.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding a man who marries another's slave girl, has a child with her, then later acquires ownership of her. The Hanafi position is that she becomes his umm walad because the child's lineage is established to him, and the partiality (the child being part of both parents) creates the umm walad relationship. Shafi'i argues she does not become umm walad because at conception the child was a slave (since the mother was owned by another), and a slave cannot be considered free in its origin. He analogizes to zina: if a fornicator later owns the child, the child does not become his. The Hanafi response distinguishes: in marriage, lineage is established, and the partiality is via that lineage; in zina, there is no lineage. They also discuss a second scenario: if a man owns a slave girl, gets her pregnant, then she is claimed and taken away, then he re-acquires her, she becomes his umm walad (Hanafi), while Shafi'i has two opinions. The key difference is whether the umm walad status depends on the child being free at conception (Shafi'i) or on the establishment of lineage (Hanafi). The Hanafi view emphasizes that the child is a part of each parent, and once lineage is proven, the partiality is sufficient. The analogy to a child from zina is rejected because no lineage exists there.

Key Terms
جزئیہ (juz'iyyah): The relationship of partiality: the child is considered a part of each parent, which forms the basis for the umm walad status.
ولد المغور (walad al-maghruur): The child born to a man who is deceived about the status of the mother (thinking she is free or his own slave), but is later attributed to him.

Oath on Jewelry: The Pearl Necklace Dispute
Ruling on oath regarding jewelry with intra-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for wearing a silver ring, gold ring, and pearl necklace when under an oath not to wear jewelry, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding plain pearl necklaces.

Core Idea
Oaths follow custom: what counts as jewelry depends on urf, not just the substance.

This passage from Al-Hidayah examines oaths involving jewelry. It distinguishes between items that are legally considered jewelry (like gold rings) and those that are not (like silver rings for men). The key dispute concerns plain pearl necklaces: Abu Hanifa held they are not jewelry unless set, while the Sahibayn considered pearls themselves jewelry. This lesson unpacks the evidence and the fatwa evolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three items and their basic rulings
Read the passage and note that a silver ring does not break the oath, a gold ring does, and a plain pearl necklace is disputed between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
Understand the rationale for silver and gold rings
Silver rings are not 'hily' by custom or Sharia; they are permissible for men. Gold rings are considered hily by both custom and Sharia, so wearing them violates the oath.
This establishes the principle that oaths are interpreted based on custom (urf).
3
Isolate the disputed case: plain pearl necklace
Focus on the disagreement: Abu Hanifa says no hanth, Sahibayn say hanth. Identify their respective proofs.
4
Compare the proofs: custom vs. Quranic usage
Abu Hanifa relies on urf: plain pearls are not worn as adornment in his time. The Sahibayn argue that pearls are called 'hily' in the Quran (Surah al-Zukhruf: 18), so they are inherently adornment.
The teacher notes that the dispute is about which evidence governs oaths.
5
Understand the tarjih and contemporary fatwa
The commentary states that the difference is tied to time and custom. In later times, wearing plain pearls alone became customary, so fatwa now follows the Sahibayn.
This demonstrates how custom changes over time and affects rulings.

Commentary
This passage addresses oaths regarding wearing jewelry (hilya). Oath Not to Smell Rayhan: The Role of Custom in Defining Terms
Definition of rayhan and its application in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on oaths involving 'rayhan' and evaluate the role of custom in interpreting ambiguous terms.

Core Idea
Custom determines the meaning of ambiguous terms in oaths, even overriding technical definitions.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi fiqh regarding oaths: how to interpret an ambiguous term like 'rayhan' when a person swears not to smell it. The Hanafi school presents both a technical definition and a customary approach, with the latter being the operative rule for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
A person swore not to smell rayhan, then smelled rose or jasmine. The ruling is that he does not become hanith (oath-breaker) according to the technical definition.
This is the initial position from the Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is the definition of rayhan as a plant without a stem (saq), which excludes rose and jasmine since they have stems.
The proof is linguistic and based on juristic consensus of earlier Hanafi scholars.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some scholars (Fakhr al-Islam, Sadr al-Shahid) adhere to the technical definition, while Fath al-Qadir prioritizes custom ('urf).
This shows the evolution of the ruling within the school.
4
Apply the custom principle
The mufta bihi is that local custom determines the meaning of rayhan. The oath is broken only if the substance smelled is commonly called rayhan in the locality.
Custom overrides the technical definition for practical application.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: in a region where rose is called rayhan, smelling it would break the oath, whereas under the technical definition it would not.
This shows the flexibility of Hanafi fiqh in accommodating local usage.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The hand is not cut for stealing fruits on trees, uncut crops, or intoxicating drinks because of the hadith and the principle that hudud are repelled by doubts.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on theft of specific items: fruits still on trees, crops not yet harvested, and intoxicating beverages. The Hanafi school, based on a well-known hadith and the principle of repelling hudud with doubts, holds that the hadd amputation does not apply to these cases. کھالینے والی چیزیں یا جیسے گوشت اور تازہ پھل ۔ خواہ وہ کھلیان میں ہو یا کہیں اور ہوں اور گیہوں میں ہاتھ کاٹے جانے پر ایسی صورت میں اجماع ہے کہ قحط سالی نہ ہو۔ کیونکہ قحط سالی اگر ہوتو ہاتھ نہیں کاٹا درختوں پر لگے ہوئے پھل اور لگی ہوئی کھیتی کی چوری میں قطع ید ہے یا نہیں

قَالَ وَلَاقَطعَ فِي الْفَاكِهَةِ عَلَى الشَّجَرِ وَالزَّرْعِ الَّذِى لَمْ يُحْصَدُ لِعَدْمِ الْإِحْرَازِ وَلَا يُقْطَعُ فِي الْأَشْرِبَةِ الْمُطْرِبَةِ لان السَّارِقَ يَتَأَوَّلُ فِي تَنَاوُلِهَا الْإِرَاقَةَ وَلَانَّ بَعْضَهَا لَيْسَ بِمَالٍ وَفِي مَالِيَتِهِ بَعْضِهَا اخْتِلَاتٌ فَيَتَحَقَّقُ شُبُهَةً عَدْمٍ الْمَالِيَةِ

ترجمہ کہاوہ پھل جو درختوں پر لگے ہوں یا کھیتی جو کائی نہ گئی ہو اس کی چوری میں ہاتھ کا نانہیں ہے۔ کیونکہ وہ پورے طور پر محفوظ نہیں ہے اور پینے کی ایسی چیز جس سے نشہ آتا ہو اس کی چوری میں بھی ہاتھ کاٹنا نہیں ہے۔ کیونکہ چور یہ کہے گا کہ میں نے اسے بہا دینے اور برباد کرنے کی نیت سے لیا تھا۔ اور اس لئے بھی کہ پینے کی کچھ چیز جونشہ لاتی ہو۔ مثلاً شراب وغیرہ (بالاتفاق ) مال ہی نہیں ہوتی ہے اور کچھ چیزوں کے مال ہونے کے بارے میں اختلاف علماء ہے۔ اس لئے اس کے مال ہونے کا شبہ ہو گیا۔

تشریح قَالَ وَلَا قَطعَ فِي الْفَاكِهَةِ عَلَى الشَّجَرِ وَالزَّرْعِ الَّذِي لَمْ يُحصد الخ مطلب ترجمہ سے واضح ہے۔
Chapter: What requires amputation and what does not. Ashraf al-Hidayah Urdu commentary says: 'Thamar' means fruits hanging on trees like dates etc., for stealing which the hand is not cut. 'Kathar' means the date palm tree itself, for stealing which the hand is not cut because the hadith states: 'No amputation for fruit nor for the spathe of the palm tree.' And it is said that we accept this statement, and Imam Abu Hanifa also held this view, as mentioned in al-Athar. This hadith is found in Sahih Ibn Hibban, al-Hakim, Musnad Ahmad, Sunan Abi Dawud, Nasa'i, Ibn Majah, Tirmidhi, etc. Ibn Hajar said its chain is authentic; Tahawi said the scholars have accepted it; Ibn al-Arabi said that if anyone disputes it, his objection is not to be considered. In short, this hadith is authentic and well-known. However, an objection is raised: In the hadith of Abdullah ibn Umar, when the Prophet was asked about fruits on trees, he said: 'If a needy person eats some without taking in his garment, there is no penalty; if he takes some away, he must pay double compensation. When the fruits reach the threshing floor (Jarin), if someone steals an amount worth a shield, then amputation is necessary.' This is reported by Abu Dawud and Nasa'i, and similar meanings are in Ahmad and Nasa'i with additions: one who carries away a full garment must pay double and be lashed; one who steals from the threshing floor an amount worth a shield is amputated. According to this, Imam Abu Yusuf, Malik, Shafi'i, and Ahmad permitted amputation for fresh fruits. The author responded: In the threshing floor, only dried dates are taken; in that case, we also cut. But Shaykh Ibn al-Humam said that in al-Mughrib it is mentioned that 'Jarin' is a place where half-ripe dates are collected to dry, so fresh dates are initially taken there. Moreover, the threshing floor is not a secure place; amputation is not obligatory unless there is a specific guard. The best answer is that the hadith 'No amputation for fruit' contradicts it, and in hudud matters, preference is given to the hadith that removes the hadd. Furthermore, the hadith of the threshing floor also mentions double compensation, which none of the scholars accept. It should be clear that amputation for wheat and sugar is established by consensus. Therefore, 'food' (ta'am) or the like must mean something that quickly spoils, like perishables, meat, fresh fruits, whether in a threshing floor or elsewhere. For wheat, amputation is only when there is no famine; if there is famine, the hand is not cut regardless of the type. He said: There is no amputation for fruits on trees nor for crops not harvested due to lack of secure custody; and there is no amputation for intoxicating drinks because the thief will claim he intended to pour them out, and because some are not property, and there is disagreement about the property status of some, thus doubt about property exists. Translation: For fruits on trees or crops not harvested, the hand is not cut because they are not fully secure. For intoxicating beverages, the hand is not cut because the thief will say he took them to spill and waste, and because some intoxicants are not property by consensus, while for others there is disagreement among scholars, creating doubt. Commentary: The meaning of the statement is clear from the translation.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling with proof from the Hanafi school regarding theft of fruits, crops, and intoxicating drinks. The author first cites the hadith 'No amputation for fruit nor for the spathe of the palm tree' as the primary proof. He then addresses an objection from the hadith of Abdullah ibn Umar which suggests amputation for fruits once they reach the threshing floor. The Hanafi response clarifies that the threshing floor (Jarin) is not a secure place (hiraz) and the hadith on amputation is overridden by the principle that hudud are repelled by doubts (shubuhat). The ruling distinguishes between perishable items (like fresh fruits) and non-perishable staples (like wheat), with amputation for the latter only when there is no famine. For intoxicating drinks, the ruling is that the hand is not cut because the thief can claim he intended to waste them, and because the property status of some intoxicants is disputed, creating a legal doubt. The commentary follows the matn order, first explaining the fruits/crops ruling with its proof and objection, then the intoxicants ruling.

Key Terms
ثمر (thamar): Fruits hanging on trees, specifically fresh dates or other fruit still on the tree
كثَر (kathar): The spathe or the date palm tree itself
إحراز (ihraz): Secure custody or protection of property in a place that prevents unauthorized access
شبهة (shubhah): Legal doubt that prevents application of the hadd punishment

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school withholds the hadd amputation for theft of fruits on trees, uncut crops, and intoxicating drinks due to lack of secure custody and the application of doubts, ensuring the penalty is only applied when absolutely clear.
