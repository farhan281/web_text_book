Using the Fihrist: Navigating Volume Six of Ashraf al-Hidayah
Table of contents listing chapter headings and page numbers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate specific fiqh chapters (tadbir, istilad, oaths) in Volume Six using the fihrist.

Core Idea
The fihrist is your map to the volume — know the chapter titles and page numbers to navigate efficiently.

This passage is the table of contents (fihrist) of Volume Six of Ashraf al-Hidayah, an Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. It lists the major chapters and their starting pages. Since the volume covers tadbir (emancipation upon death), istilad (motherhood of a slave woman), and the Book of Oaths, having this list helps you quickly find the discussion you need. In this lesson, we will see how to read and use this fihrist to orient yourself in the volume.

Guided Path
1
Locate the major chapter headings
Identify the three main sections: باب التدبير (Chapter of Tadbir), باب الاستيلاد (Chapter of Istilad), and كتاب الایمان (Book of Oaths). These are clearly labeled in the text.
The headings are in Arabic, while the subtopics are in Urdu.
2
Note the page numbers
For each heading, note the page number given. For example, باب التدبير starts on page 34, باب الاستيلاد on page 49, and كتاب الایمان on page 51. These numbers are approximate due to OCR issues.
Page numbers may not be perfectly accurate; use them as a guide.
3
Understand the subtopics
Within each chapter heading, there are brief descriptions of the rulings covered. Read these to preview the content. For instance, under باب التدبير you see definitions and rulings about the mudabbar slave.
These descriptions are not full rulings but summaries.
4
Use the fihrist for navigation
When studying, refer back to the fihrist to see where you are in the volume's structure. This helps in understanding the flow of topics and locating related discussions.
Keep a bookmark at the fihrist page for quick reference.

Ruling on 'you will do'
Definition of mudabbar
Selling, gifting, and removing mudabbar from ownership is prohibited; what benefits can the master derive from a mudabbar slave or slave girl; if the master dies, the mudabbar is freed from one-third of the estate; ruling on suspending tadbir upon death.
Chapter of Istilad
When does a slave girl become umm walad and the rulings pertaining to her; what authority does the master have over his umm walad; when is the lineage of the child of an umm walad established; what is the ruling if the master denies the child of his umm walad; a man married a woman; the child follows the mother; if the master dies, the umm walad is freed from all of the master's wealth.
If a Christian's umm walad accepts Islam, then it is obligatory on this umm walad to [perform]... and she will be like a mukatabah; she will be free after paying.
Married someone else's slave girl and had a child with her, then became her owner; the slave girl will be umm walad.
A son's slave girl: the patron (wali) had a child with her; he claimed lineage; lineage is established; this slave girl becomes umm walad; and for the son, the father is guarantor of the value.
A jointly owned slave girl gave birth; one claimed lineage; his lineage is established; if both claimed lineage together, lineage is established from both.
Evidence for lineage from both.

Three types of oaths.
Definition of a binding oath (yamin mun'aqida).
Definition of a vain oath (yamin laghw).
Intention, the one who takes an oath, the coerced, and the forgetful are all equal.
Chapter: What constitutes an oath and what does not constitute an oath.
Ruling on oaths by Allah's essential and attributive names; one does not become a swearer by swearing by other than Allah.
Letters of qasam (swearing).
Words 'tum' (you).
Which Persian words form a binding oath.
Ruling on saying 'upon a vow' or 'upon the vow of Allah'.
If I do such, I am a Jew, Christian, or disbeliever; this is an oath.
Issues.
If he said: 'I did such, then upon me is the wrath of Allah or the curse of Allah'; he is not considered a swearer by this.
Expiation of an oath.
Section on expiation.
If unable to do the three things of expiation, then fast three consecutive days.
Preferring expiation over [something].
Ruling on swearing to commit a sin.
If a disbeliever swore while in disbelief, or became a Muslim then violated the oath, no expiation is due. Saying 'whatever I own is forbidden to me' does not make that thing forbidden. If someone said 'all halal is haram for me', it is interpreted as food and drink, or whatever he intended. An absolute vow (nadhr mutlaq) must be fulfilled; same ruling for a specified vow.
If he said 'insha Allah' connected to an oath, he is not a violator.
Chapter: Oaths regarding entering and residing.
If he swore he will not enter a house, then entering the Kaaba, a mosque, or a church does not make him a violator. If he swore he will not enter a house, entering a ruin does not make him a violator.
If he swore 'la yadkhul hadhihi al-dar' (he will not enter this house), then the house became ruined and rebuilt, then he entered, he becomes a violator. If he swore he will not enter this house, and he stayed on its roof, he becomes a violator. If he swore he will not enter this house, and he was inside it, sitting does not make him a violator. If he swore he will not wear this garment while he was wearing it, removed it immediately, he does not become a violator.
If he swore he will not stay in this house, he left himself, but his belongings and family are in it, and he has no intention to return, he becomes a violator.
If he swore he will not be in this city, it is not conditional upon moving children and belongings.

Commentary
This is the fihris (table of contents) of Volume Six of Ashraf al-Hidayah, an Urdu commentary on the Hanafi legal work al-Hidayah. The volume covers several important chapters: first, the chapter on tadbir (the emancipation of a slave upon the master's death) and its rulings; second, the chapter on istilad (the status of a slave woman who bears her master's child, umm walad); and finally, the extensive Book of Oaths (Kitab al-Ayman). The table of contents provides a quick reference to the main topics and their page numbers. Note that the garbled portion ('ة ة تے گئے حالم گے حالم') likely represents a damaged heading or line in the original source. Students should use this fihris for navigation; the actual legal discussions appear on the referenced pages throughout the volume.

Key Terms
فهرست (fihrist): Table of contents listing chapter headings and page numbers for the volume.
تدبير (tadbir): Emancipation of a slave upon the master's death, effective from one-third of the estate.
استيلاد (istilad): The status of a slave woman (umm walad) who bears her master's child, with related rulings.

Navigating the Fihrist for Oaths in Ashraf Ul Hadaya Volume 6

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate and identify the major chapters and topics within the section on oaths (yamin) in Ashraf Ul Hadaya Volume 6 using the fihrist.

Core Idea
The fihrist is your map: it shows you where every ruling on oaths lives in the book.

This passage is the table of contents (fihrist) for Volume 6 of Ashraf Ul Hadaya, an Urdu commentary on Al-Hidayah. It lists the chapters on oaths (yamin), expiation (kafarah), and vows (nadhr) with their page numbers. Because of OCR issues, many page numbers are corrupted, but the structure remains clear. By learning to read this map, you can quickly navigate to any ruling you need.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure
The fihrist is organized by chapters (abwab) and sub-topics. Look for the word 'باب' (chapter) to see the main divisions.
The chapters are listed in order: 'كفارة يمين', 'باب اليمين في الدخول والسكنى', 'باب اليمين في الخروج والاتيان والركوب', 'باب اليمين في الاكل والشرب', 'باب اليمين في الكلام'.
2
Locate the main themes
The section covers: swearing by Allah's names, expiation, vows, oaths regarding entry/exit, eating/drinking, and speech. Each theme is a separate 'باب' with sub-topics.
For example, 'باب اليمين في الدخول والسكنى' covers entering houses, entering ruins, etc.
3
Note the page numbers
Each topic has a page number; even if corrupted, the order is preserved. Use these to jump to the right page in the volume.
For instance, the chapter on expiation starts at page 55 according to the fihrist.
4
Recognize key terms
Words like 'قسم' (oath), 'كفارة' (expiation), 'نذر' (vow), 'حانث' (violator) appear frequently. Knowing these helps you quickly identify the relevant section.
'حانث' is the person who breaks an oath; it appears in many sub-topics.
5
Use the fihrist as a reference
When studying a specific issue, check this index to find its location in the volume. This saves time and helps you see how topics are connected.
For example, to find the ruling on swearing by other than Allah, look for the entry 'غير اللہ کی قسم کھانے سے حالف نہیں ہو گا'.

Key Terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate specific chapters and rulings on oaths (al-yamin) by using the fihrist of Ashraf al-Hidaya, Volume Six.

Core Idea
A fihrist is your roadmap to the Hidayah – memorize its structure to navigate the detailed rulings.

The pages you see are not the beginning of the legal discussion – they are the table of contents (fihrist) for the entire Volume Six of Ashraf al-Hidaya, which covers the extensive topic of oaths (al-aymaan). This fihrist lists each chapter (bāb) and gives a brief summary of key rulings. By learning how to read this index, you will be able to quickly locate the full legal analysis of any oath-related scenario in the volume.

Guided Path

Identify the chapter headings
Look for the word 'باب' (baab) which marks a new chapter. Note the topic (e.g., oaths in emancipation, oaths in sale, etc.).
Each chapter focuses on a specific type of oath-related case.
2
Recognize the summaries
After each chapter heading, there are short summaries of specific rulings. These are condensed statements of the hukm for typical cases.
These summaries are not the full ruling; they serve as memory aids.
3
Use the page numbers
The numbers in the margin (e.g., 95, 96) are page numbers of the original volume. Use them to locate the full discussion.
Page numbers help you navigate to the detailed commentary in the main text.
4
Identify sub-categories
Some chapters have multiple entries; these are sub-issues within the main topic.
For example, under 'باب اليمين في البيع والشراء' there are entries on sale, hire, etc.

Overview of Hudud from the Fihrist of Ashraf al-Hidayah
Table of contents for hudud rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three main categories of hudud (zina, qadhf, shurb) and state the key evidentiary requirements for each based on the fiqh entries in the fihrist.

Core Idea
Hudud are fixed divine punishments; their application hinges on strict evidentiary rules, especially four male witnesses for zina.

This lesson guides you through the table of contents (fihrist) of Volume Six of Ashraf al-Hidayah, a Hanafi commentary. The fihrist summarizes the rulings on hudud (fixed punishments) for zina (fornication/adultery), qadhf (false accusation), and shurb (drinking alcohol). By examining the entries, you will grasp the structure of the text and the core legal principles that govern these punishments in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three hudud topics
Scan the fihrist and note the three main headings: 'حد زنا' (hadd for zina), 'حد شرب' (hadd for drinking), and 'حد قذف' (hadd for slander). Recognize that these are the three types of hudud discussed in this volume.
Each heading is followed by sub-entries and page numbers.
2
Extract witness requirements for zina
From the entries on zina, note that four male witnesses are required. If the number is less than four, the remaining witnesses are punished (for qadhf). Also note conditions: witnesses must not be blind, not previously punished for qadhf, and must be free (not slaves).
See entries: 'چار مردوں نے زنا کی گواہی دی' and 'تعداد شہود چار سے کم ہو تو بقیہ کو حد لگائی جائے'.
3
Compare rulings for different scenarios in zina
Observe how the rulings vary based on the status of witnesses (blind, slaves, transgressors) and the situation (virgin woman, retraction of testimony, etc.). This shows the casuistic method of Hanafi fiqh.
For example, if witnesses are blind or slaves, hadd is still applied to them.
4
Outline the rules for hadd al-shurb
Look at the entries under 'حد شرب'. Note that the hadd is applied only if the smell of alcohol is detected by witnesses or if the person is seen drinking. Confession after the smell disappears does not warrant hadd. Women's testimony is not accepted in this case.
Entry: 'منہ سے بو ختم ہونے کے بعد اقرار کیا تب بھی حد نہیں لگائی جائے گی'.
5
Summarize the rules for hadd al-qadhf
Under 'حد قذف', note that accusing a chaste person of zina or denying lineage incurs hadd. Specific phrases like 'you are not the son of your father' trigger the punishment. The son of the accused can demand punishment if the father is muhsan. If the accuser retracts, the hadd is lifted.
See entries on 'نسب کا انکار' and 'رجوع'.

Commentary
This passage is the table of contents (fihrist) for Volume Six of Ashraf al-Hidayah, an Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah by Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani. It lists topics covered in the book, primarily related to hudud (fixed punishments) for zina (adultery/fornication), shurb al-khamr (drinking alcohol), and qadhf (false accusation of zina). The entries summarize key rulings, such as the requirements for witnesses (four male witnesses, conditions of their qualifications), consequences of retraction, and application of hadd. It also includes sub-chapters (bab) on the specifics of hadd for shurb and qadhf, and various scenarios like accusing someone of illegitimate birth or calling them by insulting terms. The page numbers indicate where each topic is discussed in the main text. This fihrist serves as a navigation aid for the reader.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): Fixed, predetermined punishment prescribed by Islamic law for specific crimes.
زنا (zina): Sexual intercourse between a man and a woman who are not married to each other and not in a valid slave-concubinage.
قذف (qadhf): Accusing a chaste person of zina or denying their lineage.
شرب (shurb): Drinking alcohol (khamr) or any intoxicating substance.
محصن (muhsan): A person who is free, adult, Muslim, and has had valid sexual intercourse in marriage or concubinage.

This is a major section in Hanafi fiqh, reflecting the early Islamic state's engagement with other polities.

Core Idea
A Hanafi scholar's library is as important as his reasoning — knowing the right book is half the fatwa.

This page is a bibliography from Darul Isha'at listing essential Hanafi fiqh works. As a student of the madhhab, you must be able to navigate these references: distinguish between classical fatwa collections like Fatawa Alamgiri, modern compilations like Fatawa Darul Uloom Deoband, and specialized works on women's issues, inheritance, and more. This lesson will help you build a mental map of the Hanafi literary tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify work categories
Scan the list and group the books into categories: fatwa collections (e.g., Fatawa Alamgiri), commentaries/sharh (e.g., Al-Subh al-Nuri), contemporary guides (e.g., Bihishti Zewar), and topical works (e.g., on inheritance, divorce).
This helps you quickly find the right type of source for your research.

Recognize author authority
Note the authors: many are renowned Hanafi jurists (Thanwi, Shafi, Gangohi). Their scholarly pedigree influences the weight of their rulings.
Authority is tied to the scholar's position in the Hanafi hierarchy (e.g., mufti, muhaddith).
3
Understand the scope

Distinguish classical vs contemporary
Classify each work as classical (pre-19th century) or contemporary (19th-20th century). Classical works like Fatawa Alamgiri (17th cent.) are foundational; contemporary works like Fatawa Darul Uloom Deoband (20th cent.) address modern issues.
Fatwa methodology evolves; later works often rely on earlier ones.
5
Apply as reference
Given a specific fiqh question (e.g., organ transplantation), identify which books from this list would be most relevant and justify your choice based on their scope and authority.
This develops practical research skills for fatwa work.

Commentary
This page is a bibliography listing major Hanafi fiqh works, fatwa collections, and contemporary publications from Darul Isha'at. It serves as a reference guide for students of Hanafi fiqh. The list includes classical texts like Fatawa Alamgiri (compiled under Emperor Aurangzeb) and 'Al-Subh al-Nuri Sharh Quduri', as well as modern works by scholars such as Mufti Muhammad Shafi, Maulana Ashraf Ali Thanwi, and Mufti Taqi Usmani. Books on women's issues, inheritance, and Islamic law are also mentioned.
