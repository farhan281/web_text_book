Oath to Inform a Ruler: Limited to Tenure
Ruling on oath tied to ruler's authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath tied to a ruler's authority and explain why it ends with the ruler's death or dismissal.

Core Idea
An oath to inform a ruler is binding only during his tenure; its purpose defines its limit.

This lesson examines a specific oath scenario: a ruler makes a person swear to inform him of any troublemaker entering the city. The key question is: how long does this oath remain binding? The Hanafi school answers by looking at the purpose behind the oath—once the ruler's authority ends, the oath no longer serves its function and thus ceases to be binding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its context
Understand the scenario: a ruler (wali) makes a person swear to inform him of every troublemaker (da'ir) entering the city. The oath is explicitly tied to the ruler's role.
The oath is not general; it is contingent on the ruler's authority.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi school rules that the oath is specific to the period of the ruler's governance (khāṣṣa bi-ḥāli wilāyatihi). It does not extend beyond.
This is the muftā bihī position.
3
Analyze the purpose (maqsūd)
The ruling is based on the purpose: the oath aims to enable the ruler to punish the troublemaker and prevent harm. This can only be achieved while the ruler has authority to act.
The principle: al-umūr bi-maqāṣidihā (matters are judged by their objectives).
4
Determine when authority ends
The ruler's authority ends by death or dismissal (ʿazl). According to the apparent narration (ẓāhir al-riwāya), both events terminate the oath.
Some narrations may differ, but the relied-upon view includes dismissal.
5
Note the ikhtilāf
Other schools might hold that the oath continues unless explicitly limited. The Hanafi position is based on istiḥsān (juristic preference) considering intent.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fīh.

If a ruler made a man swear an oath: 'Whoever troublemaker enters this country, inform me.' How long will this oath remain? And when a governor appoints a man to inform him of every troublemaker entering the city, this (oath) is specific to the period of his governance. Because the purpose is to repel his mischief or that of others by deterring him. So it will not serve its benefit after the removal of his authority. And removal is by death, and likewise by dismissal according to the apparent narration. Translation: If a ruler made a person swear: 'Whoever troublemaker enters this city, inform me.' This oath will remain valid only as long as that ruler remains in power. Because the purpose of this oath is to punish the troublemaker and thus remove his evil or that of others. So after the ruler's authority ends, informing him is of no benefit. This authority ends by his death, and similarly by dismissal according to the apparent ruling.

Commentary
This is a ruling regarding an oath that is contingent on a specific context. The Hanafi school rules that such an oath is limited to the duration of the ruler's authority. The reason (ta'lil) is that the purpose of the oath—preventing harm by punishing the troublemaker—can only be achieved while the ruler has the power to act. Once the ruler dies or is removed from office, the oath no longer binds the person. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) considering the intent. The Arabic quote is from Hidayah, and the commentary explains the reasoning. Note that in the apparent narration (Zahir al-Riwayah), dismissal also ends the oath. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that oaths are interpreted according to their objective (maqsud).

Key Terms
داعِر (da'ir): Troublemaker, mischief-maker; a person who causes disorder.
والی (wali): Governor, ruler, authority figure.
قسم (qasam): Oath, sworn commitment.
مقصد (maqsad): Purpose, objective, intent behind a legal act.
عزل (azl): Dismissal, removal from office.
