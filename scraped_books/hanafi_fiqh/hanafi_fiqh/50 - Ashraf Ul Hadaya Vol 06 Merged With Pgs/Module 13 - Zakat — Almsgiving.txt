Minimum Clothing for Kaffarah and the Proof for Consecutive Fasting
Explains the minimum clothing requirement for kaffarah and the Hanafi proof for three consecutive fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi proof for consecutive fasting in kaffarah, identify the minimum clothing requirement, and explain the substitution by value.

Core Idea
Three fasts must be consecutive; clothing must cover most of the body; value substitution is valid when clothing cannot be given fully.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses two key issues in kaffarah: the requirement of consecutive fasting and the minimum standard for clothing. It also clarifies that when resources are limited, giving the value of food is acceptable. The Hanafi school relies on the recitation of Ibn Mas'ud (RA) to establish consecutiveness, and on custom (urf) to define adequate covering.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two issues in the passage
The passage discusses (1) the proof for consecutive fasting in kaffarah, and (2) the minimum clothing required. Note the inter-madhhab dispute with Shafi'i and the intra-Hanafi difference.
2
Analyze the proof for consecutive fasting
The Hanafi school uses the recitation of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (RA) with the word 'mutatabi'at' as a proof. This recitation is treated as khabar mashhur, allowing an addition to the Quranic text. Compare with the Shafi'i view that fasting can be separate.
The Quranic text is absolute (mutlaq); the addition specifies consecutiveness.
3
Examine the minimum clothing rule
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf require covering most of the body (aammat al-badan); a sarwal alone does not suffice. Imam Muhammad's narrated minimum is not relied upon. The ruling is based on custom (urf).
Urf determines that a person in only a sarwal is called naked.
4
Understand the substitution by value
If one cannot afford the required clothing, giving the monetary value of food (qimah) suffices, but only in the Hanafi school where giving value for food is permissible.
Example: 10 rupees can feed 10 people but not buy sufficient clothing; giving those rupees as food value fulfills the kaffarah for food, not clothing.

Commentary
This passage combines two related rulings from the chapter on kaffarah. First, the Hanafi proof that the three days of fasting for kaffarah must be consecutive (mutatabi'at) is presented. The proof is the recitation of Ibn Mas'ud (RA), which adds the word 'mutatabi'at' to the Quranic verse. This recitation is treated like a famous hadith (khabar mashhur), which according to Hanafi usul can be used to qualify the absolute text. The Shafi'i position that fasting separately is allowed is rejected because the Quduri's statement is based on the Hanafi view. Second, the passage addresses the minimum clothing for kaffarah. There is an intra-Hanafi difference: Imam Muhammad narrates a minimum, while Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the minimum is what covers most of the body (aammat al-badan). The latter view is declared sahih (correct) because custom (urf) considers a person wearing only a sarwal as naked. Thus, a sarwal alone does not suffice for clothing. However, if one's resources are insufficient to provide such clothing, giving food in terms of value (bi i'tibar al-qimah) is permissible, provided the Hanafi position on giving value for food is accepted. The commentary also provides a practical example with rupees.

Absolute and Conditional Vows: Hanafi Rulings and the Undesired Condition Distinction
Hanafi ruling on nadhr with proof and retraction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for fulfilling absolute and conditional vows, distinguishing between desired and undesired conditions, and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A conditional vow with an undesired condition may be fulfilled by the specified act or by kaffarah yamin.

Vows (nadhr) are a serious commitment in Islamic law. The Hanafi school distinguishes between absolute vows, which must be fulfilled exactly, and conditional vows, where the nature of the condition determines the obligation. This lesson unpacks the hadith proof, Abu Hanifa's initial view and his retraction, and the final detailed position that gives flexibility when the condition is undesired.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of nadhr
First, distinguish between an absolute vow (no condition) and a conditional vow (suspended on a condition). The passage gives examples like 'If I do such-and-such, then upon me is Hajj.'
Absolute vows are straightforward: must fulfill.
2
Locate the primary proof
The hadith 'Whoever makes a vow and specifies it, must fulfill what he specified' is cited. Note that it is used for both absolute and conditional vows in the initial Hanafi position.
The hadith is ahad, but its imperative indicates wujub.
3
Trace Abu Hanifa's initial position and retraction
Initially, Abu Hanifa treated conditional vows like absolute ones: must fulfill the specified act. Later, he retracted and introduced the option of kaffarah yamin when the condition is undesired. Imam Muhammad agreed with the retraction.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the later view is considered sahih.
4
Understand the key distinction: desired vs. undesired condition
If the condition is something the person does not desire (e.g., 'If I drink wine'), the vow carries the meaning of an oath (yamin). The person may choose to fulfill the specified act or pay kaffarah yamin. If the condition is desired (e.g., 'If Allah heals my sick'), it is a pure vow and must be fulfilled exactly.
The presence of 'man' (prevention) in the undesired case triggers the oath-like ruling.
5
Confirm the mufta bibi position
The passage explicitly states that the detailed distinction (تفصيل) is the correct view. Thus, the operative fatwa is: for absolute vows, fulfill; for conditional vows, if the condition is undesired, choose between the act or kaffarah; if desired, fulfill the act only.
This is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage covers the rulings on vows (nadhr) in Hanafi fiqh. It begins with absolute vows (without conditions): they must be fulfilled, based on a hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him). Conditional vows (suspended on a condition) are also binding according to Abu Hanifa's initial position, because a conditional vow is treated like an unconditional one. However, the text mentions that Abu Hanifa later retracted (رجوع) and held that if the condition is something the person does not desire (e.g., 'If I drink wine'), then the vow is essentially an oath (yamin), and the person can choose between fulfilling the specified act (Hajj, fasting, charity) or paying kaffarah yamin. This is also the view of Imam Muhammad. On the other hand, if the condition is something the person desires (e.g., 'If Allah heals my sick'), then it is a pure vow and must be fulfilled specifically; kaffarah is not an option, because the element of prevention (oath-like) is absent. This detailed distinction is stated to be the correct view. The text concludes that the person is discharged by any of the two options when the condition is undesired.

Key Terms
نذر (nadhr): A vow to perform an act of worship (like Hajj, fasting, charity) as an obligation upon oneself, either absolutely or conditionally.
شرط لا يريد كونه (shart la yuridu kawnahu): A condition whose occurrence the speaker does not desire; such a vow carries the meaning of an oath (prevention).
كفارة يمين (kaffarah yamin): Expiation for breaking an oath: freeing a slave, feeding/clothing ten poor people, or fasting three days.
