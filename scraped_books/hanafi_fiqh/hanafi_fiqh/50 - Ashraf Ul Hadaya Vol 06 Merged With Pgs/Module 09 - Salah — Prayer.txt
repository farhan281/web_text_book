Definition and First Ruling of Tadbir (Mudabbar)
Defines tadbir and states the prohibition of selling or gifting the mudabbar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tadbir, identify the explicit expressions that establish it, and state the Hanafi ruling that a mudabbar cannot be sold or gifted.

Core Idea
A mudabbar is a slave promised freedom after the master's death and cannot be alienated.

This passage introduces the institution of tadbir, by which a master promises a slave freedom upon his death. The text specifies the explicit words that make a slave mudabbar and then lays down the first ruling: the mudabbar cannot be sold or gifted, because he already has a right to eventual freedom. The Hanafi school compares this to a slave under kitabah, while Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, viewing tadbir as a mere suspension.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the act of tadbir
Identify the master's declaration: using phrases like 'when I die you are free' or 'you are mudabbar'. These are explicit (sarih) and immediately create the mudabbar status.
The passage lists four sample phrases.
2
Understand the legal effect
The mudabbar status means the slave will be free upon the master's death. During the master's life, the slave remains in his ownership but acquires a right that restricts the owner's power of alienation.
This right is analogous to that of a mukatab.
3
State the ruling on sale/gift
The passage says it is not permissible to sell or gift the mudabbar except towards freedom (i.e., immediate manumission). This is analogous to the rule for a mukatab.
The Arabic phrase 'لا يجز بيعـه إلا إلى الحرية' captures the exception.
4
Consider the Shafi'i disagreement
Imam al-Shafi'i considers tadbir a suspension (ta'liq) that does not restrict ownership, so sale is valid. The Hanafi position relies on the analogy with kitabah and a hadith prohibiting the sale of a mudabbar.
This ikhtilaf is based on a usul difference regarding the effect of conditional declarations.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, any sale of a mudabbar by the master is void (batil) in the Hanafi school. The mudabbar remains in the master's ownership but cannot be transferred.
The same applies to gifts and other transfers.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on Tadbir (making a slave mudabbar). The master declares that the slave will be free upon the master's death. The words given are explicit (sarih) and require no intention—the mudabbar status is established immediately. The right of the slave to eventual freedom is like that of a mukatab (slave under kitabah), so the mudabbar cannot be sold, gifted, or transferred out of the master's ownership except to freedom (i.e., if the master frees him immediately or if the master dies). Imam al-Shafi'i disagreed, arguing that tadbir is a mere suspension (ta'liq) and does not affect the owner's right to sell. The Hanafi position is based on the hadith that the Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade the sale of the mudabbar. The analogy with kitabah is used because both involve a right of the slave over the master's property. The commentary notes that during the master's life, the mudabbar remains in his ownership, but alienation is prohibited to protect the slave's expectancy.

Key Terms
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave who has been promised freedom upon the master's death by explicit words.
تدبير (tadbir): The act of making a slave mudabbar; it establishes freedom after death.
كتابة (kitabah): A contract of manumission by which a slave purchases his freedom in installments.

The Prohibition of Selling a Mudabbar: Hanafi Position and Shafi'i Dispute
Defines tadbir and presents the Hanafi ruling with inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the inalienability of a mudabbar, explain the rational distinction between tadbir and other conditional suspensions, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Tadbir establishes an immediate cause of freedom, making the mudabbar unsellable and ungiftable.

This lesson explores the legal institution of tadbir (posthumous manumission) and a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. After defining the act and its explicit expressions, we examine the Hanafi position that a mudabbar cannot be sold or gifted, supported by hadith and a unique rational argument regarding the timing of causation. We then contrast the Shafi'i view that treats tadbir as a mere conditional suspension, and analyze the Hanafi rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
What is tadbir? What expressions create a mudabbar? Recognize that explicit words linking freedom to the master's death effect a mudabbar without need for intent.
The expressions are explicit (sarih); no additional intention is required.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
Once a slave is mudabbar, it is not permissible to sell him, give him as a gift, or remove him from ownership except by immediate emancipation.
This ruling applies to both male and female slaves.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
The hadith 'The mudabbar is not sold, nor given as a gift, nor inherited, and he is free from one-third' forms the primary textual proof, supplemented by rational argument: tadbir is the sole cause of freedom after death, so it must be effective immediately; delaying to after death is impossible because capacity ends at death.
The hadith is narrated by al-Daraqutni with a weak chain, but the Hanafi school accepts it due to supporting reasoning.
4
Present the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that tadbir is a conditional suspension (ta'liq) like any other, and sale/gift are not prevented in such cases; also, tadbir is a will, and a will does not prohibit transactions.
Shafi'i also cites the case of the restricted mudabbar (mudabbar muqayyad) where sale is permitted by consensus.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school distinguishes tadbir from other ta'liq: tadbir is a direct cause (sabab) of freedom, not an oath (yamin). In oaths, prevention is intended and causation can be delayed because capacity remains at the time of condition. In tadbir, the cause must operate immediately because after death no capacity exists. Also, a will is a succession; invalidating its cause is impermissible.
This distinction is the core of the dispute and explains why sale and gift are prohibited.

If the master says to his slave: 'When I die, you are free,' or 'You are free after my death,' or 'You are mudabbar,' or 'I have made you mudabbar,' then he becomes mudabbar, because these expressions are explicit in tadbir, for it is the establishment of freedom after death. Translation: If the master says to his slave: when I die, you are free, or you are free after my death, or you are mudabbar, or I have made you mudabbar, then he becomes mudabbar, whether the slave is male or female, because these words are explicit in making mudabbar. The form of tadbir is to establish freedom after one's death. Benefit: During his life, the slave remains in his master's ownership, and he gave him freedom but specified the time of freedom after his death, so he becomes mudabbar. Since these words are explicit, there is no need to intend making mudabbar. Commentary: 'If the master says to his slave...' etc. The meaning is clear from the translation. The sale, gift, and removal from ownership of the mudabbar are prohibited. Then, it is not permissible to sell him, nor to give him as a gift, nor to remove him from his ownership except to freedom, as in the case of kitabah. Al-Shafi'i said: It is permissible, because it is a suspension of freedom upon a condition, so sale and gift are not prevented by it, as in other suspended matters, and as in the case of the restricted mudabbar, and because tadbir is a will, and a will does not prevent that. Our proof is the saying of the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'The mudabbar is not sold, nor given as a gift, nor inherited, and he is free from one-third.' And because it is a cause of freedom, not that freedom arises after death and there is no other cause. Then making it a cause in the present is more appropriate because it exists in the present and is absent after death. And because after death is a state of nullification of the capacity to act, so it is not possible to delay the causation to a time of nullification of capacity, unlike other suspensions, because the obstacle to causation is present before the condition, for it is an oath, and the oath is an obstacle, and the obstacle is intended, and it contradicts the occurrence of divorce and manumission. And it is possible to delay the causation until the time of the condition because capacity remains at that time, so they differ. And because it is a will, and a will is a succession in the present like inheritance, and invalidating the cause is not permissible, while in sale and similar things, that is necessary. Mixed Date Oaths: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mixed-date oaths with talil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on mixed-date oaths and apply their reasoning to new scenarios.

Core Idea
An oath is broken only if the named item is intended in each bite; mixed attributes split the ruling.

This passage examines a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school: what happens when someone swears not to eat a specific type of date but eats a date that is part ripe and part unripe (mudhannab)? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), differ on whether the oath is broken. Their disagreement hinges on whether the common name of the fruit or the intention in eating each part governs the ruling. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the principles of oaths (al-ayman) to cases where objects have mixed attributes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the oath
First, note the basic situation: a person swears not to eat a specific type of date (e.g., busr or rutab) and then eats a different pure type (no dispute) or a mixed type (mudhannab).
2
State the undisputed rule
If the oath is on busr and the person eats pure rutab, no breaking occurs because the names are different. Similarly, pure busr does not break a rutab oath. This is agreed upon.
3
Analyze the mudhannab case
The mudhannab date has both ripe and unripe portions. Abu Hanifa says the oath is broken because eating it means consuming both, and each is intended in eating. The Sahibayn say no, because the fruit is called by the name of the predominant part (e.g., 'rutab mudhannab' is still called rutab).
4
Contrast the two reasonings
Abu Hanifa emphasizes the act of eating: each piece is intended separately. The Sahibayn emphasize naming: the common name follows the majority, like in purchases.
5
Apply the distinction to buying vs. eating
In buying, the whole object is transacted, so the minor part follows the major. In eating, each bite is a separate intended act. This explains why the rule for oaths on buying (discussed elsewhere) differs.

Oath on Meat: Does It Include Fish, Liver, and Tripe?
Ruling on whether an oath on meat includes fish, liver, and tripe.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on oaths to determine whether eating fish, liver, tripe, pork, or human flesh breaks an oath not to eat meat, and explain the roles of qiyas, istihsan, and custom.

Core Idea
An oath on meat does not include fish, liver, or tripe by default — custom and istihsan override the literal Quranic usage.

In the Hanafi school, the meaning of words in oaths is determined by custom (urf), not just linguistic definitions. This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah illustrates how the school uses istihsan to depart from qiyas in a case where a person swears not to eat meat but then eats fish. It also addresses liver, tripe, pork, and human flesh, showing the interplay of custom, prohibition, and intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Question and Qiyas Argument
The question: Does an oath not to eat meat (lahm) include fish, liver, tripe, etc.? The initial qiyas argument from the Quran would include fish because the Quran calls fish meat.
Qiyas here is based on the literal Quranic usage.
2
Locate the Dalil and Its Wajh al-Dalalah
The Quranic verse (16:14 or 35:12) uses 'lahman' for fish. This seems to prove that fish is meat. However, Hanafi scholars classify this as metaphorical because fish lacks blood, the origin of meat.
The dalil is qat'i in thubut but zanni in dalala (meaning can be interpreted).
3
Apply Istihsan Based on Custom and Blood Origin
Istihsan overrides qiyas because: fish has no blood, custom does not call fish 'meat', and the Quranic usage is figurative. Thus, eating fish does not break the oath by default.
Istihsan is a core Hanafi tool; here it is based on 'urf and a physical reality (blood).
4
Differentiate Purely Prohibited Items
Pork and human flesh, though linguistically meat, are absolutely forbidden (haram qat'i). An oath on meat is meant to prevent eating permissible meat, so eating these does not break the oath.
The oath's purpose is to deter from halal meat; haram is already prohibited.
5
Analyze Liver and Tripe: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Some jurists hold that liver and tripe break the oath because they originate from blood and are used like meat. Others rely on custom: they are not called meat in common usage. The preferred view (mufta bihi) is that they do not break the oath.
The author cites Al-Khulasah al-Muhit and prefers the custom-based view.
6
Consider the Role of Intention
If the swearer specifically intended fish or liver when taking the oath, then eating them breaks the oath (as per Abu Hanifa). This shows that the general rule is subject to the swearer's specific intention.
Intention can override the default custom-based interpretation.

Oath to Drink from an Empty Cup: The Time Limit Distinction
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath to drink from empty cup

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on an oath to drink from an empty cup and apply the distinction between time-bound and absolute oaths to determine when the swearer becomes a sinner.

Core Idea
An oath on a nonexistent subject is invalid unless a time limit makes fulfillment conceivable at the end.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi dispute about an oath to drink water from a cup that is empty at the time of swearing. The positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Abu Yusuf reveal how the school reasons about the necessity of a possible subject for the oath's validity and timing. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly ruling on expiation obligations in such cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Oath
The swearer swears to drink water from a specific cup. Two scenarios: (1) the cup is empty at the time of the oath, (2) the cup initially contains water but the water is later poured away. Also, each scenario can have a time limit (e.g., 'today') or no time limit.
These scenarios are the factual basis for the dispute.
2
State the Positions of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
Without a time limit: the oath is not valid (no sin) because the subject (water) does not exist at the time the oath binds. With a time limit: the oath becomes binding only at the end of the time; if water is absent before that end, sin occurs immediately at that point.
They distinguish based on when the obligation to fulfill arises.
3
State Abu Yusuf's Position
Abu Yusuf holds that the oath binds immediately in both cases. If the cup is empty or the water is removed, the swearer becomes a sinner straightaway.
For Abu Yusuf, the moment of swearing is the moment of obligation.
4
Analyze the Reasoning
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that the possibility of fulfillment must exist at the time the oath becomes binding. Without a time limit, it binds immediately; if the subject is absent, the oath is invalid. With a time limit, it binds at the end, allowing possibility until then. Abu Yusuf argues the oath binds at utterance; if impossible then, sin occurs.
The core difference is when the oath becomes binding (at instant vs. at end of time).
5
Note the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
In the cup scenario, Abu Yusuf requires expiation immediately, while Abu Hanifa and Muhammad do not require it unless a time limit expires without fulfillment.
This difference affects whether the swearer must pay kaffarah.

Oath to Do the Impossible: The Hanafi View on Conceivability
Dispute on oath to do impossible acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that an oath to do something customarily impossible is valid if conceivable in reality, and evaluate Zufr's contrary view.

Core Idea
An oath on a customarily impossible act is valid because it is conceivable in reality; the swearer becomes a sinner immediately.

This lesson addresses an oath to perform an act that is normally impossible, such as climbing to the sky. The Hanafi madhhab holds that such an oath is valid because the act is conceivable in reality (angels climb, God can transform matter), and the oath is binding. Imam Zufr disagrees, arguing that what is customarily impossible is like inherently impossible. The passage provides the Hanafi proof and contrasts this with the cup case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Oath
The swearer swears to climb to the sky or to turn a stone into gold. These actions are customarily impossible for humans.
The key question: Does customary impossibility invalidate the oath?
2
State the Hanafi Position (Abu Hanifa and others)
The oath is valid (mun'aqid) and the swearer becomes a sinner immediately because fulfillment is impossible by custom.
The oath is valid because the act is conceivable in reality.
3
State Zufr's Position
Zufr holds that the oath is invalid (not mun'aqid) because the act is impossible by custom, which is akin to real impossibility. Therefore, the oath does not bind at all.
Zufr equates customary impossibility with inherent impossibility.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Proof
The Hanafi proof (dalil) is that these acts are possible in reality: angels ascend to the sky, and God can turn stone into gold. Therefore, the act is conceivable, so the oath is valid. However, since it does not happen customarily, the swearer fails to fulfill and becomes a sinner immediately, similar to the case of death.
The criterion is real possibility, not customary frequency.
5
Contrast with the Cup Case
The cup case is different because the act (drinking) is possible in principle, but the subject (water) was absent at the time the oath binds. Here, the act itself is possible (in reality) even if not customary, so the oath is valid.
This highlights the distinction between absence of subject and impossibility of act.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding an oath to drink water from a cup. The core issue is whether the oath is valid and when the swearer becomes a sinner (hanith) if the cup is empty. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that if there is no time limit, the oath is not valid because the subject (water) does not exist at the time of the oath. If there is a time limit (e.g., 'today'), the oath becomes valid only at the end of the time, but if the water is removed beforehand, the swearer becomes a sinner immediately. Abu Yusuf differs: for him, the oath is valid immediately regardless of the time limit, and the swearer becomes a sinner as soon as the oath is taken if the cup is empty, because the obligation to fulfill arises instantly. The passage also contrasts this with a case where the cup had water but it was poured away: here all agree that the swearer becomes a sinner because the subject existed at some point. The reasoning is based on the principle that an oath requires the possibility of fulfillment at the time it becomes binding. The discussion then transitions to an oath to do impossible things like climbing the sky, where the Hanafi position (against Zufr) is that such an oath is valid because it is conceivable in reality (angels climb the sky, God can turn stone to gold), so the swearer becomes a sinner immediately due to customary impossibility, similar to the case of the swearer's death.

حانث (ḥānith): One who breaks an oath and becomes sinful, requiring expiation.
مستحيل عادة (mustaḥīl ʿādatan): Customarily impossible; something that does not occur by normal human means but is possible in reality (e.g., by divine intervention).

Oaths to Customarily Impossible Acts: Hanafi Validity
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of oath to customarily impossible acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on oaths to customarily impossible acts and explain the proof against Zufr's objection.

Core Idea
An oath to do something customarily impossible is valid if the act is conceivable in reality.

Can an oath to climb the sky or turn stone to gold be legally binding? Intuitively, one might think such an oath is null because the act is impossible. However, the Hanafi school distinguishes between what is customarily impossible and what is intrinsically impossible. This lesson unpacks the dispute within the Hanafi school itself, focusing on the proof that makes such oaths valid and the immediate consequence of breaking them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the dispute
The case: swearing to climb the sky or turn a stone to gold. The dispute: Imam Zufr argues the oath does not take effect (la tan’aqid) because the act is customarily impossible, analogous to truly impossible acts.
Remember: Zufr's view is a minority within the Hanafi school.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proof (dalil)
The text states: 'Our proof is that fulfillment is conceivable in reality – ascent to the sky is possible in reality; do you not see that angels ascend the sky? Similarly, a stone turning to gold can be effected by Allah.' Thus, the act is not intrinsically impossible.
The proof uses qiyas: analogy to the existence of angels ascending and Allah's omnipotence.
3
Apply the ruling: immediate hanth
Because the oath is valid but the act is customarily impossible, the swearer becomes a sinner (hanith) immediately after the oath. Expiation (kaffarah) is due. This is analogous to the case of the swearer dying: even though resurrection is possible, at death the oath is broken.
Customary impossibility triggers immediate hanth, not invalidity.
4
Differentiate from the cup case
The text contrasts with a case where someone swears to drink water from a cup that is empty. There, the act is not even conceivable at the moment of swearing, so the oath is invalid from the start. Here, the act is conceivable even if not customary.
Key distinction: existence vs. possibility.
5
State the mufta bihi position
The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa) is relied upon: the oath is valid and expiation is required. Zufr's view is not the operative ruling.
Authority weight: Zahir al-Riwayah.

Reciting Quran in Prayer Does Not Break an Oath Not to Speak
Ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute on Quran recitation under oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reciting Quran in prayer under an oath of silence and explain the basis for the difference with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Reciting Quran in prayer does not break an oath of silence because it is not speech by law and custom.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling on oaths: if someone swears not to speak and then recites the Quran during prayer, does the oath break? The answer highlights the role of custom (urf) in Hanafi fiqh, distinguishing Quranic recitation from ordinary speech. We will also explore the Shafi'i position and the evidence each school uses.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: reciting Quran in prayer does not break the oath (la yahnath), but reciting outside prayer does. The same applies to tasbih, tahlil, and takbir.
The default ruling for uttering any words would be breaking the oath; here an exception is made for prayer.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that recitation in prayer also breaks the oath because it is speech in reality (haqiqatan).
This is based on qiyas: all vocal utterances are considered speech.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i proof is qiyas: the literal reality of the words makes them speech, so they should break the oath in any context.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school relies on the hadith: 'This prayer of ours is not suitable for any of the speech of people,' which proves that Quran and dhikr are not considered speech (kalam) in prayer by sacred law (shar') and custom (urf).
The hadith is a dalil naqli that overrides qiyas.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: In the Hanafi school, the oath is not broken and no expiation is needed; in the Shafi'i school, the oath is broken and kaffarah may be required.
This affects divorces and other conditional oaths as well.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling concerning oaths not to speak. The text begins by stating the ruling: if a person who has sworn not to speak recites the Quran during prayer, he does not break his oath (la yahinth). However, if he recites the Quran outside of prayer, he does break the oath. The same ruling applies to saying tasbih (Subhanallah), tahlil (La ilaha illallah), and takbir (Allahu Akbar). The text then presents a qiyas-based argument that would lead to breaking the oath in both cases, which is the position of Imam al-Shafi'i, who considers all of these to be speech in reality (haqiqatan). The Hanafi response is that during prayer, these are not considered speech by custom ('urf) nor by sacred law (shar'). The proof is the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'Indeed this prayer of ours is not suitable for any of the speech of people,' meaning that worldly speech is prohibited, but Quranic recitation and dhikr are not included in that prohibition. The text also mentions a weaker opinion that even outside prayer, one does not break the oath because a reciter or one who glorifies Allah is not called a speaker (mutakallim). However, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that outside prayer, one does break the oath, as stated in the fawaid. The authority weight is from later Hanafi authorities (Fatawa).

Vows to Walk to the Haram: Custom vs. Literal Meaning
Explains rulings on vows to go to Baitullah on foot

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on vows to walk to the Haram and explain how 'urf determines obligation.

Core Idea
Vows are interpreted by custom, not literal wording; walking to the Haram does not imply Hajj.

When someone vows to walk to the House of Allah, custom determines whether Hajj is intended. Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differed on whether mentioning the Haram implies Hajj. This lesson examines the arguments and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Vow
The vow in question is 'Walking to the Haram (or Masjid al-Haram) is obligatory upon me.' The key legal question: Does such a vow obligate Hajj or Umrah?
2
State the Two Hanafi Positions
Imam Abu Hanifa: Nothing is obligatory because the phrase is not customary for Ihram. Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf & Muhammad): Hajj or Umrah becomes obligatory because the Haram encompasses the Kaaba.
3
Examine the Proofs
Sahibayn: The Haram includes the Kaaba by physical connection, so mentioning it is like mentioning the Kaaba itself. Abd Hanifa: Undertaking Ihram with such wording is not customary ('urf); the literal meaning of 'walking' does not imply Ihram.
The Hanafi response: Since the word 'walking' is not placed for Ihram linguistically or customarily, it cannot obligate Hajj.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Imam Abu Hanifa: no obligation arises. This is supported by narrations from Ali and hadiths allowing riding with a sacrifice.
The ruling: If someone vows to walk to the Haram, they are not required to perform Hajj/Umrah, but if they wish to fulfill the vow in a commendable way, they may walk or ride and offer a sacrifice.

Oath Not to Pray: Effect of Incomplete Prayer
Ruling on oath violation with incomplete prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether performing only some parts of prayer (e.g., standing, reciting, bowing) breaks an oath not to pray, and explain the istihsan reasoning that distinguishes prayer from fasting.

Core Idea
Partial prayer does not break an oath — prayer is defined by all its pillars, so incomplete performance is not legally 'prayer'.

Oaths are serious matters in Islamic law, and their violation (hanath) depends on the precise definition of the act sworn about. In this lesson, we examine a key Hanafi ruling: if someone swears not to pray and then performs only part of the prayer—standing, reciting, bowing, but not prostrating—the oath is not broken. This ruling is based on the nature of prayer as a multi-pillar worship and is derived through istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas. Understanding this helps clarify the conditions under which an oath is violated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and the actions performed
Note the exact wording of the oath ('I will not pray') and list the actions: qiyam (standing), qira'ah (recitation), ruku' (bowing). No sajdah (prostration).
The passage also considers a specific oath: 'I will not pray a prayer'.
2
Determine whether the actions constitute a legally valid prayer
Apply the Hanafi definition of salat: it requires completion of all pillars (arkan). The minimum valid prayer includes qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud, and final sitting (though final sitting is wajib, not rukn). Without sajdah, the act is not called prayer.
Hanafi pillars of salah: takbir, qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud, qa'dah akhirah (wajib).
3
Apply the ruling to the oath
Since the oath is 'I will not pray', and the performed actions do not amount to prayer, the oath is not broken (no hanath). If he adds sajdah, then he has completed the essential pillars and the oath is broken, even if he cuts off later.
This is the mufta bihi position from zahir al-riwayah.
4
Distinguish between general and specific oath
For a general oath 'I will not pray', any valid prayer (with sajdah) breaks it. For a specific oath 'I will not pray a prayer', the minimum legally valid prayer is two rak'ahs (due to prohibition of batyara).
Hadith prohibits single-rak'ah prayer (al-batyara).
5
Analyze the qiyas and istihsan reasoning
Qiyas would compare prayer to fasting: beginning the act (takbir for prayer, intention for fasting) should break the oath. But istihsan overrides because prayer consists of multiple distinct pillars; without all, it is not called prayer, whereas fasting is a single continuous act.
This is the 'face of istihsan' (wajh al-istihsan).

Core Idea
An oath of negation requires permanent abandonment of the action.

In this lesson, we examine a key Hanafi principle regarding oaths: when a person swears not to perform a specific act, the negation is absolute and covers all future times. Fulfilling an Affirmative Oath: One Act Suffices
Ruling on affirmative oath completion

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An affirmative oath is fulfilled by a single performance; breach occurs only when the act becomes impossible.

In this passage from Al-Hidayah, Imam al-Marghinani explains the Hanafi position on oaths to perform an action. The key principle is that the obligation is a single, unspecified act. Understanding this helps avoid common mistakes in oath fulfillment.

Identify the act and its ruling
The act is swearing to do something. The ruling is that performing it once fulfills the oath; failing to do it when possible makes one a sinner (hanith).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the nature of the obligation: the text states 'al-multazam fi'lun wahidun ghayru 'ayn' (the obligation is a single, non-specific act).

The phrase 'fi'lun wahidun' indicates that only one occurrence is required. The context of affirmation (ithbat) supports this: it requires existence, not repetition.

Hadd of Drinking Wine: Conditions and Proof
Ruling, conditions, and proof for hadd of drinking wine

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the hadd of drinking wine and explain how the hadith proof is interpreted by the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The hadd for drinking wine is wajib, but execution on the fourth offense only applies to one who considers wine lawful.

This passage introduces the fixed punishment (hadd) for drinking wine. It outlines the conditions under which the penalty is due and provides the primary proof from the Prophet's hadith. However, the Hanafi school interprets the hadith's threat of execution restrictively, confining it to the mustahill (one who deems wine permissible). Understanding these conditions and the proof's interpretation is crucial for correctly applying this penal ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is drinking wine (khamr or other unanimously prohibited intoxicants). The ruling is that the hadd (fixed punishment) becomes wajib upon fulfillment of conditions.
The hadd is not fard because it is a penal sanction subject to evidentiary standards.
2
List the conditions for the hadd
The passage lists conditions: Muslim, sane, adult, not mute, voluntary (no coercion), consumption of a prohibited intoxicant (even a drop), must become intoxicated, must know the prohibition. Also evidentiary condition: the smell must be present or the person brought in intoxicated, or confession with smell present.
Knowledge of prohibition is established by being in Dar al-Islam or having studied the texts.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The Prophet's hadith: 'Whoever drinks wine, flog him; if he returns, flog him; if he returns, flog him; if he returns a fourth time, kill him.' This is a hadith ahad, zanni in transmission and meaning.
Narrated by Ibn Hibban, al-Hakim, al-Shafi'i, al-Darimi, Abu Dawud, al-Nasa'i, and Ibn Majah.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'fajliduh' (flog him) indicates obligation (wujub) per Hanafi principle that al-amr yufidu al-wujub. The mention of execution on the fourth is interpreted as a threat for the mustahill, not as a general command.
Hanafis restrict execution to the mustahill (one who considers wine lawful), carrying it out for apostasy, not as a hadd.
5
Note the ikhtilaf (dispute)
Other schools may require execution on the fourth without istihlal, but Hanafis rely on the interpretation that execution is only for the mustahill, based on the practice of the Companions and later scholarly consensus.
This position is the mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the condition for amputation in theft of a purse from a sleeve. The core issue is whether the purse is in a hiraz (secure place) such that taking it constitutes the required violation (hatk al-hirz). The ruling distinguishes two scenarios: if the purse is outside the sleeve (i.e., the knot is exposed), cutting it does not violate the hiraz because the sleeve is not acting as a secure container—the purse is simply attached. If the thief inserts his hand into the sleeve, the sleeve becomes the hiraz, and taking the purse from inside it is a violation, thus amputation is wajib. The 'illa (cause) is the location of the knot: if the knot is inside the sleeve, the hiraz is breached; if outside, it is not. The ruling also covers the case where the thief unties rather than cuts: if he unties from outside, the hiraz is still not breached? Actually, the text says if he unties instead of cutting, the ruling reverses: untying from outside becomes a violation? This is based on the reversal of the 'illa. The author prefers the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the majority) over that of Abu Yusuf, who held that the hand should be cut in both cases because the purse is always protected either by the sleeve or by the owner. The majority argue that the owner's own protective intent is not sufficient; only the physical hiraz (the sleeve) counts. The commentary concludes that this is analogous to a sack on an animal: if the sack is torn and goods taken from within, amputation applies because the sack is the hiraz; but stealing the whole sack (without tearing) does not entail amputation because the sack is not a secure storage for itself—it is merely a means of transport.

صرة (surrah): A purse or money bag that is tied and placed in a sleeve or waist.
قطع ید (qat' al-yad): Amputation of the hand as a hadd punishment for theft.

Amputation for Theft from a Caravan: The Hiraz Condition
Ruling on amputation for theft from a caravan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on amputation for theft from a caravan and identify the condition of hiraz as the determining factor.

Core Idea
No amputation for theft from a caravan unless a dedicated guard makes it a hiraz.

Amputation (hadd al-sariqa) in Hanafi fiqh requires theft from a proper hiraz (secure place). This lesson examines a classic case: a moving caravan is not considered a hiraz because the attendants focus on travel, not protection. Only if a guard is specifically assigned does the caravan become a hiraz, triggering the hadd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the general ruling
The act is stealing a camel or its load from a caravan (qitar). The default ruling is: no amputation.
This is the base ruling from the text.
2
Locate the underlying 'illa (cause)
The reason (illa) is that the caravan is not muhraz (secured) because the driver, leader, and rider intend travel and transport (qat' al-masafah wa naql al-amti'ah), not protection (hifz).
Intention is key: the people in charge are not acting as guards.
3
Identify the exception
If a dedicated guard (man yashba'uha li al-hifz) is present with the loads, then the caravan becomes a hiraz, and amputation is wajib for theft from that guarded property.
The presence of a guard changes the legal character of the caravan.
4
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Consider a modern equivalent: a moving convoy of trucks with a security escort. The escort's presence would make the convoy a hiraz; theft from a guarded truck would warrant amputation.
The underlying 'illa is the intention to protect, not merely the physical structure.

The Sack as a Hiraz: Conditions for Amputation in Theft
Rulings on amputation for tearing a load and stealing a guarded sack

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios where amputation is wajib for theft involving a sack and explain how the sack functions as a hiraz through its own nature or the owner's guarding.

Core Idea
A sack becomes a hiraz either by its own protective nature or by the owner's active guarding.

This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies two specific cases of theft involving sacks (juwāliq). The central question is: when does the act of taking goods from a sack trigger the hadd punishment of amputation? The Hanafi school answers by analyzing the concept of hiraz (secure place) and extends it to include customary protection. Understanding this reasoning helps you apply the rules of theft beyond obvious locked structures.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two theft scenarios
First scenario: thief tears a tied load (haml) and takes goods from inside. Second scenario: thief steals the entire sack while the owner is guarding it, sleeping on it, or nearby. Both lead to amputation.
Note the distinction: tearing vs. stealing the whole sack; the owner's presence matters in the second.
2
Understand the concept of hiraz
Hiraz is a place or container that secures property. The passage argues that a sack itself can be a hiraz because goods are placed inside for protection, analogous to a sleeve protecting a purse. Alternatively, the owner's active guarding (sitting, sleeping on or near the sack) makes a non-hiraz location (like a road) into a hiraz.
Customary protection (al-hifz al-mu'tad) is the key principle.
3
Analyze the proof via analogy
The proof for the first scenario is qiyas (analogy) to the sleeve. Just as a sleeve protects money placed in it, the sack protects goods placed inside. Therefore, taking from the sack is like taking from a sleeve – both are stealing from a hiraz.
The common illa (effective cause) is the intent to protect by enclosing the goods.
4
Recognize the role of customary protection
For the second scenario, even in a public place like a road, if the owner guards the sack by sitting on it or sleeping on it, that counts as a hiraz. Sleeping nearby is also sufficient according to the chosen view.
This is based on 'ādah (custom) – people commonly protect goods by such actions.

ترجمہ اور اگر چور نے بندھے ہوئے گٹھے یا گون کو پھاڑ کر اس میں سے مال لے لیا تو ہاتھ کاٹا جائے گا۔ کیونکہ جس صورت میں محافظ نہ ہو تو یہ گون ہی حرز و محافظ کا کام دیتی ہے۔ کیونکہ اس گون میں مال رکھنے کا مقصد یہی ہوتا ہے کہ اس کی حفاظت ہو۔ جیسے کہ روپے کی تھیلی کے واسطے آستین کا قسم ہے اس بنا ہو پر حرز میں سے لینا پایا گیا چنانچہ اس کا ہاتھ کا نا جائے گا۔ اور اگر ایسا تھیلا چرایا جس میں سامان ہے اور مال والا
(Juwaliq: with dammah or kasrah on the jim, plural jawaaliq or jawaaleeq. A sack made of wool or hair, like a khurjin used on donkeys or oxen for grain, made from gunny or goat hair etc. - Anwar al-Haq Qasimi)
If the thief tore a tied load and took from it, his hand is cut. Because the sack in such a case is a hiraz, since he intends by placing goods in it to protect them, like the sleeve. So taking from the hiraz is found, and he is cut. And if he steals a sack in which there is goods, while its owner is guarding it or sleeping on it, his hand is cut. Meaning, when the sack is in a place that is not a hiraz, like a road and the like, until it becomes a hiraz by virtue of its owner being watchful of its protection. And because consideration is given to customary protection, and sitting by it or sleeping on it is considered protection by custom, and likewise sleeping near it according to what we have chosen before. And it is mentioned in some copies: 'and its owner is sleeping on it or is protecting it.' And this is in accordance with the chosen view we have presented. Translation: And if the thief tore a tied load or sack and took property from it, his hand will be cut, because in the absence of a guard, the sack itself serves as a hiraz and protector, since the purpose of placing goods in the sack is protection, just like the sleeve for a money purse. Therefore, taking from within a hiraz is established, so his hand is cut. And if he steals a sack containing goods and the owner is guarding it or sleeping on it, his hand is cut.

Commentary
This passage presents two related rulings about sacks (juwaliq). First: If a thief tears a tied load (sack) and takes property from inside it, amputation is wajib. The sack itself is considered a hiraz because the person who placed the goods in it did so with the intent of protection (sayanah). This is analogous to the sleeve protecting a purse. Second: If a thief steals the entire sack while the owner is guarding it (e.g., sitting by it, sleeping on it, or nearby), the owner's presence acts as a hiraz, even if the sack is in an open area like a road. The principle is that customary protection (al-hifz al-mu'tad) is taken into account: sitting by the goods or sleeping on them is considered protective. This the relied-upon view. The passage also notes a textual variant in some manuscripts emphasizing the owner's sleeping on or guarding the sack. The overall lesson is that the sack can be a hiraz either by itself (when it serves as a container for protection) or through the owner's active guarding.

Key Terms
جوالق (juwāliq): A large sack made of wool or hair, used for carrying grain or goods on animals; considered a hiraz for its contents.
حمل (haml): A load or bundle, often tied and placed on an animal.
حرز (hiraz): A secure place or container; in theft, stealing from a hiraz is a condition for amputation.
حفظ معتاد (al-hifz al-mu'tad): Customary protection; the ordinary means people use to safeguard property.

Key Takeaway
Amputation applies when goods are stolen from a hiraz, and the Hanafi school recognizes both the protective nature of a sack and the owner's customary guarding as establishing hiraz.
