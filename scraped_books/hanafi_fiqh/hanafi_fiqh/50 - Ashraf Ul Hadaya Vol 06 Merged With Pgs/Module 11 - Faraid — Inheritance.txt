Proving Lineage of Umm al-Walad's Child: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Dispute on whether master's acknowledgment is needed for child's lineage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on establishing lineage of a slave-girl's child, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and apply the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).

Core Idea
Acknowledgment needed for first child because intercourse with a slave-girl aims at desire, not offspring.

This lesson examines a classical dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over whether a master must acknowledge his slave-girl's child for lineage to be established. The Hanafi school requires acknowledgment for the first child, while Shafi'i holds it is automatic. Understanding the reasoning behind each position deepens your grasp of how intention (qasd) and legal analogy (qiyas) shape rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the core ruling: the child's lineage is not established unless the master acknowledges the child.
This is the opening statement: 'ولا يثبت نسب ولدها إلا أن يعترف به'.
2
Identify the opposing view and its proof
State the Shafi'i position: lineage is established automatically without acknowledgment. Understand their qiyas: since marriage establishes lineage by contract alone, intercourse (more frequent) should do so even more.
Shafi'i uses qiyas awla (more appropriate analogy).
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
Explain why the analogy fails: intercourse with a slave-girl is for desire, not offspring (due to deterrent of reduced value). Ownership without intercourse also requires acknowledgment; thus, the need for acknowledgment is analogous to mere ownership.
Hanafi distinguishes between marriage (offspring intended) and slave-girl (offspring unintended).
4
Examine the exception for subsequent children
If the master acknowledges the first child, subsequent children are automatically attributed. The first acknowledgment establishes that the slave-girl is a firash (legal bed) intended for offspring.
This shows that the requirement is only for the first child; after that, the ruling aligns with marriage.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
In practice, if the master denies the first child, the Hanafi school does not attribute the child to him; the Shafi'i school does. This affects inheritance, guardianship, and maintenance.
The dispute has real-world consequences for the child's status.

When is the lineage of the child of Umm al-Walad established? The lineage of her child is not established unless he acknowledges it. And al-Shafi'i said: His lineage from him is established even if he does not claim, because since lineage is established by contract (nikah), then it is more appropriate that it be established by intercourse, and that is more frequent. Our proof is that intercourse with a slave-girl is intended for fulfilling desire, not for offspring, due to the existence of a deterrent against it; therefore claim is necessary, like ownership of the right hand without intercourse. Unlike contract, because offspring is the intended aim from it, so there is no need for claim. Then if she gives birth after that to a child, his lineage is established without acknowledgment, meaning after his acknowledgment of the first child, because by claiming the first child, the child became the intended aim from her, so she became a bed (firash) like a woman after marriage. Translation: The lineage of the child of this Umm al-Walad from the master will be established only when he himself claims that lineage. And Imam al-Shafi'i said: The lineage will be established even if he does not claim it, because since merely contracting marriage establishes the lineage of the woman's child, then after intercourse it should be established all the more because there is greater facility and ease of intercourse. Our proof is that intercourse with a slave-girl is intended to fulfill one's desire, not for offspring, because there are some obstacles, i.e., if she bears a child, her market value will either be completely lost or at least reduced. Therefore, it is necessary to claim lineage, like a slave-girl who is merely owned and not had intercourse with. Unlike the contract of marriage, because the primary purpose of that is obtaining offspring, so claiming lineage is not necessary for the offspring of a married woman. Then when he acknowledges the lineage of one child of a slave-girl, she becomes Umm al-Walad. Thereafter, if a child is born, the lineage from him will be established without his acknowledgment. That is, when the master acknowledges the first child's lineage, saying "this is my child," then whenever a second child is born, it will be from the lineage of that master, because by claiming the first child it became apparent that bringing the slave-girl to bed was for this purpose. So this slave-girl became his bed-partner like a married woman.

Commentary
This is an inter_madhhab_dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the proof of lineage of a child born to a slave-girl. The Hanafi position, stated first, is that the child's lineage is not established from the master unless the master expressly acknowledges the child. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that lineage is established automatically without acknowledgment, based on an analogy (qiyas): since in marriage, lineage is established by the mere existence of the marriage contract, then with intercourse (which is more frequent and easier) it should be even more so. The Hanafi response (walana) is that the purpose of intercourse with a slave-girl is the fulfillment of sexual desire, not procreation, because there is a deterrent—if she bears a child, her market value decreases. Therefore, acknowledgment is required, similar to a slave-girl who is owned but not had intercourse with. In contrast, in marriage, offspring is the explicit purpose, so acknowledgment is unnecessary. However, if the master acknowledges the first child, then subsequent children born to that slave-girl are automatically attributed to him without further acknowledgment, because the acknowledgment of the first child establishes that the master intended offspring from her, making her a 'firash' (bed) like a married woman. This ruling has practical implications for inheritance and the child's status.

Key Terms
فراش (firash): The legal 'bed' or marital relationship that establishes lineage. For a married woman, the marriage contract creates firash; for a slave-girl, only after acknowledgment of the first child.
اعتراف (i'tiraf): Acknowledgment or confession. In this context, the master's explicit statement that the child is his.
