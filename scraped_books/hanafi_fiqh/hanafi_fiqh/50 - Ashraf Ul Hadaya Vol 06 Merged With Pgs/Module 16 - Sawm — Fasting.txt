Oath Not to Fast: Partial vs. Complete Fasting
Ruling on whether brief fasting violates an oath not to fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a general oath not to fast and a specified oath involving 'a day' or 'a fast', and state whether a brief period of fasting breaks the oath in each case.

Core Idea
A general oath is broken by any true fast; a specified 'day' requires a full, legally valid fast.

This passage examines how legal definitions of fasting affect the interpretation of oaths. The Hanafi school identifies a key distinction: when the oath is general, any instance of fasting counts, but when it specifies a 'day' or 'a fast', it refers to a complete, legally valid fast from dawn to dusk. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining when an oath is broken and whether expiation (kaffarah) is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two oath scenarios
The passage presents two distinct cases: a general oath (e.g., 'I will not fast') and a specified oath (e.g., 'I will not fast a day' or 'I will not fast a fast'). Recognize that the wording changes the ruling.
2
Define the legal meaning of sawm
According to the text, sawm (fasting) is legally defined as abstaining from nullifiers (eating, drinking, marital relations) with the intention of worship. This definition is essential for determining whether the oath is fulfilled.
This is a definitional element used in the proof.
3
Apply the definition to the general oath
For a general oath, any occurrence of valid sawm—even for a moment—satisfies the condition of the oath. Thus, if a person intends fasting and abstains for a short period, the oath is broken (hanith).
The proof is: 'because the condition exists' (li wujud al-shart).
4
Explain why a specified oath requires completion
When the oath specifies 'a day' or 'a fast', the legally valid fast must be completed until the end of the day. A partial fast does not constitute a 'fast' in the legal sense, so the oath is not broken.
The word 'day' is explicit in determining the full duration.
5
Contrast the outcomes
In the general oath, the person becomes hanith (sinner, oath broken). In the specified oath, the person does not become hanith because the condition (a complete fast) is not met.
The practical consequence is whether expiation is due.

Commentary
This passage explains the ruling on oaths related to fasting. Two scenarios are distinguished. First, a general oath 'I will not fast': if the person intentionally fasts even for a short period and then breaks it, he has violated the oath because fasting is defined as abstaining from nullifiers with the intention of worship. The moment he engages in a valid fast, the condition of the oath is met. Second, if the oath specifies 'I will not fast a day' or 'I will not fast a fast', then a partial fast does not violate the oath because the legally valid fast (in the Hanafi school) requires completion until the end of the day without breaking. The word 'day' explicitly denotes the full duration. Therefore, partial fasting is not considered a 'fast' for the purpose of the oath. This shows how legal definitions (the validity of fasting) affect the interpretation of oaths.

Oath Not to Fast: When Partial Fasting Breaks the Oath
States ruling on oath not to fast with partial or complete fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an unqualified and a qualified oath not to fast and apply the Hanafi ruling on when breaking the oath occurs.

Core Idea
An unqualified oath not to fast is broken by any intentional fasting; a qualified oath requires a complete fast.

This passage from a Hanafi fiqh text clarifies how the wording of an oath affects its violation. The definition of fasting—intentional refraining from nullifiers with worship intent—is key to understanding why partial fasting sometimes breaks the oath and sometimes not. Mastering this distinction prevents incorrect application of expiation (kaffarah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two oath wordings
Locate the two different expressions: 'لا أصوم' (unqualified) and 'لا أصوم يوماً' or 'صومًا' (qualified). Notice that the second specifies a unit (day or fast) while the first does not.
2
Recognize the definition of fasting
The passage defines fasting as 'الإمساك عن المفطرات على قصد التقرب' (refraining from nullifiers with intention of worship). This definition is central to the reasoning.
This definition is the 'proof' (dalil) for the first ruling.
3
Apply definition to unqualified oath
Since fasting is defined as the act of refraining with intention, even a partial fast (e.g., one hour) fulfills that definition. Therefore, the unqualified oath is broken (hanth occurs) as soon as any intentional fasting takes place.
The presence of the condition (fasting) triggers the breaking of the oath.
4
Apply definition to qualified oath
When the oath specifies 'a day' or 'a fast', what is intended is the complete, legally valid fast that extends to the end of the day. A partial fast does not constitute that complete act, so the oath is not broken.
The word 'day' (يوم) is explicit in measuring the full duration.
5
Summarize the principle
The ruling hinges on the object of the oath: if the word 'fast' (صوم) is used without qualification, it refers to the generic act; if qualified (e.g., 'a fast' or 'a day'), it refers to the complete Shariah-valid fast.
This is a matter of linguistic interpretation (al-haqiqah al-shar'iyyah vs. al-haqiqah al-lughawiyyah).

Chapter: Oath concerning Hajj, Prayer, and Fasting. Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Hanafi school regarding oaths related to fasting. The first case: a person swears generally 'I will not fast' (لا أصوم) and then intentionally fasts for any portion of the day and breaks it – he breaks his oath because the act of fasting, which is defined as refraining from nullifiers with the intention of worship, has occurred even if only for a short time. The second case: if the oath specifies 'I will not fast a day' (لا أصوم يومًا) or 'a fast' (صومًا), then fasting only part of the day does not break the oath because the intended meaning is the complete, valid fast that lasts until the end of the day. The word 'day' (يوم) is explicit in indicating the full duration. The ruling is based on the literal meaning of the words used in the oath. The commentary in Urdu clarifies the reasoning.

Key Terms
يمين (yamin): Oath; a vow or swearing by Allah to do or not do something.
حنث (hanth): Breaking an oath, sinning by violating the oath.
صوم (sawm): Fasting; refraining from nullifiers (eating, drinking, intercourse) with intention of worship.
إمساك (imsak): Refraining; the act of holding back from things that break the fast.
