Introduction to Usul al-Hidayah and Principle 1 on Ghayah
Book title and list of principles from Kitab al-Taharah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the purpose of Usul al-Hidayah and identify the function of a limiting clause (ghayah) in Hanafi legal texts.

Core Idea
Ghayah clauses define legal scope—understanding them is key to Hanafi textual interpretation.

Welcome to the study of Usul al-Hidayah, a collection of legal maxims extracted from the Hanafi fiqh classic, Al-Hidayah. This opening page presents the book's title, author, and a table of contents listing 227 principles, beginning with those from the Book of Purifications. Our first encounter is with principle 1, which clarifies how limiting clauses (ghayah) operate—a foundational tool for interpreting the boundaries of legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its purpose
Recognize that Usul al-Hidayah is a compilation of principles (usul) derived from the detailed fiqh rulings of Al-Hidayah, meant for memorization and application.
The author, Maulana Muhammad Numan, provides translation and takhrij to aid understanding.
2
Understand the format of the table of contents
Note that each principle is stated concisely and grouped by fiqh chapters; here we see the first 19 principles of Kitab al-Taharah.
The list is for reference; each principle will be explained in detail later.
3
Grasp the first principle on ghayah
Principle 1 distinguishes two types of limiting clauses: one that excludes what is beyond (and thus the excluded part is not subject to the same ruling) and one that explains the ruling (and does not exclude). Memorize this distinction.
This principle is fundamental for interpreting texts with temporal or conditional limits.
4
Scan the remaining principles
Review principles 2–19 to see the range of topics: intention in wudu, repetition in washing vs. wiping, the generality of 'ma', impurities, etc.
Each principle serves as a legal maxim underlying multiple fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This opening window presents the front matter and table of contents of Usul al-Hidayah, a work compiling the principles (usul) extracted from the Hanafi fiqh manual Al-Hidayah. The title page informs us that the book contains 227 principles from the first volume of Al-Hidayah, with translation, commentary, and takhrij (verification of hadith sources) by Maulana Muhammad Numan, a contemporary scholar and teacher of hadith in Karachi. The table of contents lists the principles covered in the Book of Purifications (Kitab al-Taharah). These are legal maxims that underlie specific fiqh rulings. For example, the first principle (qa'idah) addresses how limiting clauses (ghayah) function in legal texts: if a limit is set to exclude what comes after, the excluded part does not fall under the same ruling; but if the limit is for explanation, it does not exclude. This is a fundamental tool for interpreting the scope of commands. The list continues through various topics: the role of intention in wudu, the effect of impurity, the treatment of water, and more. Each principle is stated concisely and is intended for memorization and application. The passage serves as an orientation for the reader, providing a roadmap of the principles to be studied in depth in the subsequent pages.

Key Terms
أصول (usul): Principles or legal maxims extracted from the detailed rulings of Al-Hidayah, used as foundational tools for deriving and understanding fiqh.
غاية (ghayah): A limiting clause or endpoint that defines the scope of a ruling in legal texts.

Understanding the Hanafi Legal Maxims in Usool al-Hidayah
Index of key Hanafi legal maxims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the purpose of the maxim list in Usool al-Hidayah and apply three selected maxims to specific fiqh scenarios.

Core Idea
Legal maxims are the distilled principles that guide Hanafi rulings across chapters.

This passage presents a structured list of legal maxims from Usool al-Hidayah. Each maxim encapsulates a foundational principle of Hanafi fiqh, serving as a memorization anchor for students and a quick reference for jurists. Rather than just a random collection, these maxims are arranged by chapter to show their practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure
Look at the chapter headings (e.g., باب صلاة الجمعة) and the number sequence (133-128). Note that the maxims are grouped by fiqh topic, each introduced by 'إِنَّ' (verily) indicating a firm principle.
The numbering may have irregularities (e.g., 142 appears twice) but the order is intentional.
2
Select key maxims from different chapters
Choose one maxim from the 'Ibadat section (e.g., 136 on taklif) and one from Zakat (e.g., 141 on ownership). Read their Arabic and translations.
Focus on understanding the wording and its legal implication.
3
Analyze the maxim's legal implication
For each selected maxim, ask: What ruling does this principle establish? How does it limit or expand obligations? For example, maxim 136 (obligation revolves around capability) means that duties are conditional on ability.
Consider how the maxim connects to the chapter it belongs to.
4
Apply the maxim to a hypothetical case
Using one maxim, construct a simple fiqh scenario and deduce the ruling. For instance, apply maxim 148 (intention if connected to the action otherwise not) to the case of a person who intends to fast after dawn.
Ensure the application follows the exact wording of the maxim.

Commentary
This passage is a listing of key Hanafi legal maxims (qawāʿid) from the book Usool al-Hidayah, organized by chapters of fiqh. The numbers in parentheses are the maxim numbers used in the original work. The bab headings indicate the chapter where the maxim is applied. Each maxim encapsulates a foundational principle of Hanafi fiqh, such as the importance of intention, the role of custom, and the treatment of debt in zakat. The maxims are memorization anchors for students of the madhhab. The text includes intra-school differences, e.g., in maxim 147 where Abu Hanifa and Muhammad differ on the validity of a judge's declaration of bankruptcy. The listing covers topics from prayer, zakat, hadd penalties, and fasting. Note that some maxim numbers appear out of sequence due to the original indexing.

التكليف (al-Taklīf): Legal obligation imposed by Allah
الزكاة (al-Zakāh): Obligatory almsgiving, one of the five pillars
النِّيَّة (al-Niyyah): Intention required for acts of worship

Biography of Sahib al-Hidayah: Al-Marghinani and His Legacy
Biographical introduction to the author

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the lineage, teachers, and historical significance of Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani, the author of Al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Al-Marghinani's Hidayah is the backbone of Hanafi fiqh in Dars-e-Nizami, and his lineage traces back to Abu Bakr al-Siddiq.

This lesson introduces you to the author of perhaps the most important Hanafi fiqh textbook, Al-Hidayah. Understanding his background, teachers, and the context in which he wrote helps you appreciate why his work is so authoritative in the Hanafi madhhab. We focus on the key biographical details presented in the introduction of Usool al-Hidayah.

Introduction. Al-Hidayah is a widely circulated and well-known book of the Dars-e-Nizami curriculum, included as part of the syllabus in most religious schools of the Indian subcontinent. This book is unparalleled due to its reliability and usefulness. Because of its comprehensive transmitted and rational proofs and its distinctive style, this book holds a special position in Hanafi fiqh. Due to the high status of this book, the author is known in academic circles not by his name 'Burhan al-Din' but by the title 'Sahib al-Hidayah'. His honorable name is Ali, his father's name is Abu Bakr, his grandfather's name is Abd al-Jalil, his kunya is Abu al-Hasan, and he is famous by the title Sahib al-Hidayah. He was born on Monday, 8 Rajab 511 AH, after the Asr prayer. His birthplace and residence is Marghinan, a city in the province of Farghana located in the region of Transoxiana. The river Sayhun (Syr Darya) flows through the Farghana valley, and the city of Marghinan is situated to its east. Sahib al-Hidayah is called Marghinani after this city. He is a descendant of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him). His death occurred on the night of Tuesday, 4 Dhu al-Hijjah 593 AH/1197 CE. Among the teachers in whose service he studied, a few well-known ones are as follows: Imam Najm al-Din Abu Hafs Umar ibn Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi (d. 537 AH) – he wrote a treatise on creed called al-Aqa'id al-Nasafiyyah, which was commented upon by Allamah Taftazani (d. 792 AH) under the name Sharh al-Aqa'id al-Nasafiyyah, which is included in the Dars-e-Nizami curriculum. Imam Husam al-Din Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz ibn Maza (d. 536 AH) – he is known by the title al-Sadr al-Shahid. Imam Abu Umar Uthman ibn Ali al-Makindami (may Allah have mercy on him) – he was a student of Allamah al-Sarakhsi (d. 483 AH). Imam Qawam al-Din Ahmad ibn Abd al-Rashid al-Bukhari (may Allah have mercy on him) – he is the father of the author of the book Khulasat al-Fatawa. Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the introduction (muqaddimah) of the book Usool al-Hidayah. It provides a comprehensive biography of the author, Burhan al-Din Ali ibn Abi Bakr al-Marghinani, famously known as Sahib al-Hidayah. The commentary explains that Al-Hidayah is a central text in the Dars-e-Nizami curriculum across South Asia. The author's lineage traces back to the first Caliph, Abu Bakr al-Siddiq. The author was born in Marghinan, a city in the Farghana valley (present-day Uzbekistan), in 511 AH. He studied under prominent Hanafi scholars such as al-Nasafi, Ibn Maza, al-Makindami, and al-Bukhari. The text also lists references for further biographical information. The teacher should highlight the significance of the Hidayah in the Hanafi school and the scholarly pedigree of its author.

If I were to say, perhaps I could not write even a few lines. Hazrat Maulana Manazir Ahsan Gilani (may Allah have mercy on him) says that Hazrat Anwar Shah Kashmiri (may Allah have mercy on him) used to say: 'Alhamdulillah! I can write something in the specific style of every book, but four books are exempt from this: the Noble Quran, Sahih Bukhari, the Mathnawi, and al-Hidayah.' Those who are aware of the scholarly grandeur of Allamah Kashmiri can appreciate the weight of his statement. This is the reason that although eight centuries have passed since the death of the author of al-Hidayah, al-Hidayah still remains in the curriculum. The reason is that for the purposes for which this book was included in the syllabus, no other book in Hanafi fiqh has yet been authored that could replace it. Shaykh al-Tafsir wa al-Hadith, Hazrat Maulana Muhammad Idris Kandhlawi (may Allah have mercy on him, d. 1384 AH) wrote these words about al-Hidayah at the beginning of Siraj al-Hidayah. This sentence of his indicates a unique characteristic of al-Hidayah: 'Pearls are not obtained by swimming on the surface of the sea. Pearls are obtained by those who have the power to dive deep into the sea.' Among such firmly grounded scholars is Shaykh al-Mirghinani (may Allah have mercy on him), who authored a book named al-Hidayah on the research and precision of the Sharia, i.e., legal rulings. It has no equal in the research and precision of legal rulings and the depth of knowledge. On every issue, he mentioned the statements of the four Imams and for each statement one transmitted proof and one rational proof. Then at the end, after presenting one transmitted proof and one rational proof for the school of Imam Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him), he answered every transmitted and rational proof of the Imams of ijtihad (may Allah have mercy on them all). Thus, sometimes the six proofs of the three Imams and their six answers total twelve, and the two proofs of Abu Hanifa and one reason for preference—all together fifteen proofs appear in a few lines, and the reader is filled with wonder and astonishment.
'Al-Hidayah' was first printed by Maktaba Khairiyya, Egypt in 1326 AH. Then it was printed with the research and annotations of Shaykh Abd al-Rahim bin Mustafa al-Adawi by Mustafa al-Babi al-Halabi in 1355 AH. Then in India it was printed with the marginal notes and annotations of Allamah Abd al-Hayy al-Lakhnawi (d. 1304 AH) in 1375 AH with great care. Many eminent scholars have written commentaries, marginal notes, and hadith takhrij on al-Hidayah. Some of them are as follows:

Commentary
This passage is an introduction from the Urdu book 'Usool-e-Hidayah' that extols the virtues of Al-Hidayah, the celebrated Hanafi fiqh text by Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani (d. 593 AH). The author begins by quoting a remark of Anwar Shah Kashmiri that among all books, only four defy imitation: the Quran, Sahih Bukhari, Rumi's Mathnawi, and Al-Hidayah. This establishes the text's uniqueness. The passage then explains that despite centuries passing, Al-Hidayah remains central to the Dars-e-Nizami curriculum because no other Hanafi fiqh work matches its depth and methodology. The commentary by Muhammad Idris Kandhlawi is cited, comparing the book to a sea whose pearls require deep diving. The unique method of Al-Hidayah is described: for each issue, it presents the views of the four Imams with both transmitted and rational proofs, then gives Abu Hanifa's view with its proofs, and finally answers the proofs of the other Imams. This results in up to fifteen arguments in a few lines. The passage then notes editions of Al-Hidayah and lists famous commentaries (shuruh) and annotators (hasiyah) such as al-Sighnaqi, al-Nasafi, Ibn al-Zarkashi, and others. This is pure front matter—it sets the stage for studying Hanafi fiqh through Al-Hidayah by underscoring the book's authority and the scholarly tradition built around it.

Key Commentaries on Al-Hidayah
Listing classical Hanafi commentaries on Al-Hidayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify major Hanafi commentaries on Al-Hidayah, state their authors and distinguishing features, and explain their value in fiqh study.

Core Idea
Commentaries (shuruh) unlock the depth of Al-Hidayah; each offers unique insights.

Al-Hidayah is a cornerstone of Hanafi fiqh, but its concise text often requires elaboration. Over centuries, great Hanafi scholars wrote extensive commentaries to explain its rulings, analyze proofs, and present preferred positions. This lesson introduces you to the most significant of these works, helping you navigate the rich tradition of Hidayah scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Identify Author and Era
For each commentary, note the author's full name and death date to place the work in historical context.
2
Note the Commentary's Focus
Some emphasize hadith takhrij (e.g., Al-Binayah), others delve into usul (e.g., Fath al-Qadir). Recognize the specialty.
3
Assess the Commentary's Scope
Is it a complete sharh, a hashiya, or a talkhis? This affects its use for study or fatwa.
4
Consider the Commentary's Reputation
Which commentaries are most relied upon in later Hanafi works? This guides priority.
E.g., Al-Inayah is often cited in later fatwa collections.

Major Commentaries on Al-Hidayah
Listing major commentaries on Al-Hidayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three major commentaries on Al-Hidayah (Al-Inayah, Al-Binayah, Fath al-Qadir) and describe their distinctive features.

Core Idea
Commentaries on Al-Hidayah clarify its concise rulings and are essential for Hanafi fiqh mastery.

Al-Hidayah is a foundational Hanafi fiqh text, but its conciseness requires explanation. Scholars produced extensive commentaries to unravel its meanings, indicate preferred views, and engage with proofs. This lesson introduces the three most prominent commentaries: Al-Inayah by al-Babarti, Al-Binayah by al-Ayni, and Fath al-Qadir by Ibn al-Humam, highlighting their unique contributions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text
Recognize Al-Hidayah as the matn being commented upon. It is a concise manual of Hanafi fiqh by Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani.
Al-Hidayah is a standard reference in Hanafi curricula.
2
Survey Al-Inayah (al-Babarti)
Al-Inayah is praised for its moderate style. It explains each book's relation to others, gives lexical meanings, the importance of each topic, and indicates the preferred Hanafi view. It is printed in ten volumes.
Haji Khalifah said of it: 'He has done well and excelled.'
3
Examine Al-Binayah (al-Ayni)
Al-Binayah is described as the most distinguished commentary in terms of solving the text. It explains vocabulary, clarifies rare words, does takhrij of hadith, and responds to the other schools. Al-Ayni wrote it at age 90.
It is a comprehensive lexical and hadith analysis.
4
Analyze Fath al-Qadir (Ibn al-Humam)
Fath al-Qadir is a research-oriented, critical commentary. It includes detailed hadith criticism, rational proofs, and a balanced presentation. Ibn al-Humam died before completion; Qadi Zadah finished it.
Ibn al-Humam was a master of many disciplines.
5
Compare the commentaries
Al-Inayah is moderate and accessible; Al-Binayah is comprehensive and authoritative; Fath al-Qadir is deep and critical. Each serves a different scholarly need.
Knowing these helps in selecting appropriate references.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and bibliographical introduction to Al-Hidayah. It first describes a commentary (likely 'Al-Binayah') that includes lexical explanations, hadith extraction, criticism of narrators, and detailed mention of different madhhabs, especially the Hanafi viewpoint. The author of Al-Hidayah, Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani, died before completing his own commentary, reaching the chapter on agency, and it was completed by Qadi Zada. Then it lists major works that extract and verify the hadiths in Al-Hidayah: 'Al-Kifayah' by Turkmani, 'Nasb al-Rayah' by al-Zayla'i, 'Al-'Inayah' by al-Qurashi, and 'Al-Dirayah' by Ibn Hajar. A later authority, Zafar Ahmad 'Uthmani, praises al-Marghinani as a muhaddith (hadith scholar) because of the many hadiths he included in Al-Hidayah. He notes that even hadiths termed 'gharib' (rare) by al-Zayla'i and Ibn Hajar were found by him in the works of Abu Yusuf (e.g., Al-Kharaj, Kitab al-Athar) and Muhammad al-Shaybani (Kitab al-Athar, Kitab al-Hujaj). The passage also highlights the Arabic eloquence of Al-Hidayah, quoting a Shi'a scholar who ranked it after Sahih al-Bukhari in eloquence. However, the author of the passage argues that the Muwatta' of Muhammad and his Al-Jami' al-Saghir are even more eloquent. Finally, it gives practical advice to teachers: use 'Al-Binayah' for resolution, 'Fath al-Qadir' for details and proofs, and 'Nasb al-Rayah' for hadith extraction.

Key Terms
تخريج (takhrij): The process of tracing a hadith to its original source, identifying its chain of transmission, and often evaluating its authenticity.
محدث (muhaddith): A scholar specialized in the science of hadith—its collection, criticism, and analysis.
حافظ (hafiz): A title for a hadith scholar who has memorized thousands of hadiths with their chains, typically used for those who have mastered the field.
غريب (gharib): A hadith that is strange or rare, often because it is narrated through a single chain or has a peculiar wording. This does not automatically indicate weakness.
فصاحة (fasahah): Eloquence; clarity and beauty of expression, free from awkwardness or obscurity.

The Scholarly Ecosystem Around Al-Hidayah: Its Eloquence and Commentaries
Bibliographical introduction to Al-Hidayah's scholarly tradition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main types of scholarly works that serve Al-Hidayah and explain why its implicit principles (usul) were neglected until recent times.

Core Idea
Al-Hidayah’s eloquence and juristic principles were served by many works, yet its hidden usul remained unexplored for centuries.

This lesson introduces you to the great Hanafi text Al-Hidayah and the vast scholarly tradition that grew around it. You will learn about its unique eloquence, the various genres of works that comment on it, and a surprising gap: its underlying legal principles were never systematically compiled until modern times. Understanding this landscape will help you appreciate the depth of Hanafi scholarship and guide your own study of Al-Hidayah.

Usool-e-Hidayah Volume One, along with the four volumes of Hidayah, selected these principles and clothed them in the garb of usool. The author deserves great congratulations for this effort, as he gathered these principles with utmost effort and research. May Allah Almighty accept this effort in His court and grant him a great reward in this world and the Hereafter. Amen. The writer has made an Urdu translation, commentary, and takhreej of these principles. The takhreej of the principles is done according to the edition of Maktaba Rahmaniya Lahore. He translated the principles idiomatically, mentioned commentary and explanation concisely, and wherever a principle is used verbatim or in meaning in other Hanafi books, he referenced it in the footnote. The index of principles is arranged in the order of chapters for ease of benefit. Remember that some principles are the opinions of Hanafi imams stated in the form of principles. A few principles are those on which no derivation of subsidiary issues has been done, but the author mentioned them as principles; therefore, the writer retained them, as they are not devoid of benefit for understanding Hidayah and fiqh insight. The writer primarily benefited from 'Al-Inayah' by Allama Babarti (d. 786 AH), 'Al-Binayah' by Allama Badruddin Ayni (d. 855 AH), 'Fath al-Qadir' by Allama Ibn Humam (d. 861 AH), 'Ashraf al-Hidayah' by Maulana Jameel Ahmad Sakrodhwi, and 'Ahsan al-Hidayah' by Mufti Abdul Haleem Qasimi Bastawi. References to other books used for takhreej are given in the footnotes. My student Hafiz Muhammad Ijaz bin Taus Khan, may Allah protect him, cooperated greatly in this work. May Allah Almighty bless his knowledge, actions, and life. May Allah accept this effort and make it a provision for the Hereafter for the writer. Amen. Muhammad Numan, Teacher at Jamia Anwar al-Uloom, Mehran Town, Korangi Karachi. 27 Rabi' al-Thani 1429 AH / 15 January 2008 CE. 0332-2557675

Commentary
This is front matter (author's introduction) from Usool-e-Hidayah. The author, Muhammad Numan, explains his methodology: he selected principles (qawa'id) from the four volumes of Hidayah and compiled them into a book of usul al-fiqh. He made an Urdu translation, added concise commentary, and provided takhreej (source verification) based on the Maktaba Rahmaniya Lahore edition. He cross-referenced other Hanafi books where the same principle appears. He acknowledges that some principles are the opinions of Hanafi imams (i.e., not necessarily universal usul principles) and that a few have no derived subsidiary issues, but he kept them for their benefit in understanding Hidayah and developing fiqh insight. He lists his main sources: Al-Inayah, Al-Binayah, Fath al-Qadir, Ashraf al-Hidayah, and Ahsan al-Hidayah. He thanks his student Hafiz Muhammad Ijaz. This introduction sets the context for the book as a study aid for the Hidayah, focusing on the usul principles underlying its rulings.

Key Terms
تخریج (takhreej): The process of tracing a ruling or principle back to its original source (e.g., Quran, hadith, earlier fiqh works).
اصول (usool): The foundational principles (qawa'id) of Hanafi fiqh, extracted from the rulings of Hidayah.
