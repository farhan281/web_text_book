Property in Enemy Hands is Ghanimah
Legal maxim on spoils of war from the chapter on minerals and treasure troves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that property found in the hands of enemy combatants during lawful war is ghanimah, and distinguish it from other property categories like fay‘ and rikāz.

Core Idea
Property seized from enemy combatants in lawful war is ghanimah — booty subject to distribution per Quran 8:41.

In the course of jihad, Muslims may encounter property in the possession of enemy forces. This short but powerful maxim from the chapter on minerals and buried treasures clarifies that such property is not simply lost-and-found or ownerless property, but rather booty (ghanimah) with specific legal consequences. Understanding this rule prevents confusion between different categories of property acquired from non-Muslims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Legal Category
The act is the Muslim army finding property in the hands of enemy disbelievers. The ruling from this maxim is that such property is classified as ghanimah (war booty).
Ghanimah is distinct from fay‘ (acquired without combat) and rikāz (treasure trove).
2
Analyse the Conditions
The property must be (1) in the hands of the disbelievers, (2) found by Muslims during military operations. It does not apply to property of dhimmis or non-combatants unless they are part of the enemy forces.
This maxim is specific to enemy combatants, not civilians.
3
Understand the Consequence
Once classified as ghanimah, the property is subject to the rules of distribution: one-fifth (khums) goes to the state treasury, and the remainder is distributed among the fighters.
The details of distribution are covered in other texts; here we only establish the category.
4
Distinguish from Similar Categories
Contrast with fay‘ (property acquired without combat, e.g., from a peace treaty) and rikāz (buried treasure, which has a different ruling even if found in enemy territory). This maxim specifically addresses movable property seized from enemy forces.
Fay‘ is not divided as ghanimah; it is kept as state property.

Commentary
This maxim from the chapter on minerals and buried treasures (ma‘ādin wa al-rikāz) states that if during a military campaign, Muslims find property in the possession of enemy forces, that property is considered ghanīmah (booty) and is distributed according to Islamic law. This applies to movable property seized directly from the enemy.

Key Terms
غَنِيمَةٌ (ghanīmah): War booty seized from non-Muslims during lawful military operations.

Key Takeaway

The Rule: Force Determines Ghanimah
Legal maxim on spoils and conquest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim distinguishing ghanimah from fay' and apply it to classify property acquired from the enemy.

Core Idea
Property taken without military force is fay‘, not ghanimah.

This lesson introduces a concise maxim from Hanafi fiqh that defines the boundary between war booty (ghanimah) and peacefully acquired property (fay‘). Understanding this ruling is essential for correctly categorizing spoils in jihad, as the distribution rules differ.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim verbatim
Read the Arabic carefully: 'إِنَّ مَا لَمْ يَرِدُ عَلَيْهِ الْقَهْرُ لَا يَكُونُ الْمَأْعُوذُ مِنْهُ غَنِيمَةٌ'. Note that 'al-ma'udh' is likely a scribal variant for 'al-ma'khudh' (that which is taken). Translate: 'Indeed, that which force has not been exerted upon, that which is taken from him is not spoils.'
Memorize the Arabic to recall the criterion instantly.
2
Identify the legal criterion for ghanimah
The maxim teaches that the defining element for ghanimah is qahr (force/overpowering). If property is obtained without exerting force over the enemy (e.g., by treaty, surrender, gift), it is not ghanimah but fay‘.
Fay‘ is handled differently in distribution (e.g., Imam’s discretion vs. fixed shares).
3
Apply the criterion to a new scenario
Given a scenario: Muslim army captures enemy supplies after a battle where the enemy fled without resistance. Does this involve qahr? The enemy fled, so no actual overpowering; the supplies are obtained without force. Per the maxim, they are fay‘.
The mere presence of a military campaign does not automatically make all acquisitions ghanimah.

Indeed, what force has not been exerted upon, the sought [property] from him is not spoils.

Commentary
This maxim clarifies that property that was not taken by force (qahr) during military engagement does not qualify as ghanīmah. For something to be considered war booty, it must have been seized through overpowering the enemy. Property acquired through peaceful means is fay‘, not ghanīmah.

Key Terms
الْقَهْرُ (al-qahr): Force, overpowering, coercion in a military context.
غَنِيمَةٌ (ghanimah): War booty taken by force from the enemy.
فَيْء (fay‘): Property acquired from the enemy without force, e.g., through treaty or surrender.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Checkpoint
According to the maxim, property that is not taken by force (qahr) from the enemy is:
A) Ghanimah
B) Fay‘
C) Both
D) Not subject to Islamic law

Key Takeaway
Force (qahr) is the key determinant: property taken without force is fay‘, not ghanimah.
