Personal Performance and Timing of Voluntary Sacrifices in Hajj
Establishes principle and resolves timing dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on performing acts of worship personally, distinguish the timing rules for voluntary sacrifices versus obligatory ones, and identify the point of disagreement with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Doing worship yourself is better; voluntary sacrifices can be done before Nahr, contrary to Shafi'i.

This passage from al-Hidayah first establishes a foundational principle: in acts of worship, it is superior to perform them personally rather than delegating. It then applies this to the sacrifice in Hajj and addresses a debated issue: when may voluntary sacrifices (dam al-tatawwu') be slaughtered? Al-Quduri had restricted them to the days of Nahr, but the correct Hanafi view allows earlier slaughter, making it more virtuous to do so on Yawm al-Nahr while permissibility remains before. The Shafi'i school disagrees, equating all hady with dam al-tamattu'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
Read the opening phrase: 'إِنَّ التَّوَلِي فِي الْقُرُبَاتِ أَوْلَى'. This states that personally undertaking acts of worship is superior. Understand that this applies across all types of worship, including sacrifices.
This principle is a foundational adab in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Understand the Rationale
Personal involvement increases khushu' (humility) and sincerity. Delegation reduces the direct act of obedience. The author implies this rationale without explicit mention.
3
Locate the Dispute on Timing
The text mentions al-Quduri's view that voluntary sacrifices (dam al-tatawwu') are restricted to Yawm al-Nahr, like tamattu' and qiran. Then it presents the correct Hanafi view: permissible before Nahr, more virtuous on Nahr.
Note that the correct view is described as 'al-sahih' (the sound position).
4
Analyze the Hanafi Proof
The Hanafi argument distinguishes between dam nusuk (worship sacrifice, e.g., tamattu' and qiran) and dam jibr (compensatory blood). For dam jibr, expediting is better; for dam nusuk, it is tied to specific days.
The qurbah (act of drawing near) in voluntary hady is achieved by reaching the Haram, not specifically by the day.
5
Compare with Shafi'i View
Imam al-Shafi'i considers all hady as compensatory blood (dam jibr) and therefore analogizes voluntary ones to tamattu' and qiran, requiring all to be on Yawm al-Nahr. The Hanafi response rejects this analogy by emphasizing the difference in nature (nusuk vs. jibr).
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is that early slaughter is valid in Hanafi but invalid in Shafi'i.

Commentary

When to Slaughter: Ritual vs. Compensatory Blood
Specifies the time for slaughtering different types of sacrificial blood

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ritual sacrificial blood (dam nusuk) and compensatory sacrificial blood (dam jabr) in Hanafi fiqh, and apply the appropriate time ruling for each category.

Core Idea
Ritual blood (dam nusuk) is restricted to the Day of Nahr; compensatory blood (dam jabr) may be slaughtered earlier, and expediting it is better.

The time for slaughtering sacrificial animals in Hajj is not uniform. The Hanafi school draws a critical distinction between blood that is a ritual act of worship (dam nusuk) and blood that is compensatory (dam jabr). This lesson unpacks that distinction, explores the evidence, and contrasts it with the Shafi'i position, enabling you to correctly determine when each type must be slaughtered.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of sacrificial blood
First, recognize that the passage divides sacrificial blood into two types: dam nusuk (ritual blood, e.g., Tamattu' and Qiran) and dam jabr (compensatory blood, e.g., expiation for deficiencies). Voluntary blood (dam tatowwu') is considered a third type that resembles dam nusuk in principle.
The key is the underlying legal cause (illah): ritual worship vs. compensation.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each category
For dam nusuk (Tamattu' and Qiran): slaughter is wajib on the Day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah) and not permissible on other days. For dam jabr (expiatory blood): slaughter is allowed anytime, and it is better to do it early, to expedite the compensation. For voluntary blood (dam tatowwu'): it is specific to the days of Nahr like dam nusuk, but slaughter before is permissible, though the Day of Nahr is better.
Al-Quduri clarifies that the correct view (al-sahih) is that slaughter on the Day of Nahr is better for voluntary blood, but it is not strictly wajib.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i objection and the Hanafi response
Imam al-Shafi'i analogizes all sacrificial blood to compensatory blood (dam jabr), thus restricting all types to the days of Nahr. The Hanafi response distinguishes: dam jabr is compensatory, so early is preferable; dam nusuk is an act of worship, which has a specific time.
This is a classic example of ikhtilaf based on the characterization of the blood's nature.
4
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
Given a pilgrim performing Tamattu' (dam nusuk) who sacrifices on the 9th of Dhul Hijjah, the slaughter is invalid and must be repeated on the 10th. In contrast, a pilgrim needing to give dam kaffarah can sacrifice before the 10th, and it is better to do so promptly.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is immediate and affects the validity of the sacrifice.

Personal Performance in Acts of Worship: The Principle of al-Tawalli fi al-Qurbanat Aula
Establishes the principle that personal performance supersedes deputization in worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that personal performance is superior in worship and explain its application to the slaughter of sacrificial animals during Hajj.

Core Idea
In acts of worship, doing it yourself is better than deputizing another — greater humility, greater reward.

This passage from al-Hidayah and al-Lubab establishes a key Hanafi principle: personal involvement in devotional acts (qurbanat) is preferable to delegation. While deputization is permissible, the pilgrim who can slaughter skillfully is encouraged to do so himself, emulating the Prophet ﷺ. The increased khushu' (humility) gained through personal performance makes it more virtuous.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Recognize the Arabic phrase 'al-tawalli fi al-qurbanat aula' and its English meaning: 'Indeed, taking charge personally in acts of worship is better.'
2
Understand the rationale
Note that personal performance increases khushu' (humility), which is the reason for its superiority.
3
Apply to a specific case
The pilgrim slaughtering his hady (sacrificial animal) should do it himself if he knows how to slaughter properly.
4
Note the exception
If the pilgrim cannot slaughter well, he may appoint someone else, but it is still better to be present at the slaughter.

The Principle of Concealment for Sin-Related Sacrificial Animals
Establishes the principle of hiding penalties due to sins, applied to sacrificial animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on placing necklaces (qilada) on sacrificial animals and distinguish between worship-related and sin-related blood offerings.

Core Idea
Conceal sin, publicize worship: no necklace on sin-related sacrificial animals.

This lesson explores a subtle but important rule in Hajj rituals: how we treat sacrificial animals differs based on why they are offered. The Hanafi school applies a general principle of concealment (satr) to matters involving sin or penalty. Here, that principle determines whether a necklace (qilada) should be placed on the animal. Understanding this distinction prevents a common mistake and deepens your appreciation of how legal maxims guide practice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of concealment for sins
The passage opens with the maxim 'concealment in matters of sins is more appropriate.' This sets the tone: any act associated with sin should be kept private, not publicized.
2
Identify two categories of sacrificial blood
Sacrificial blood (dam) falls into two broad categories: blood of worship (dam nusuk) – such as the voluntary, Tamattu', and Qiran offerings – and blood that results from a sin or compulsion (dam al-jinayat, dam al-ihsar).
Dam al-ihsar is compensatory (jabir), so it is treated like sin blood.
3
Apply the principle to each category
For worship blood, placing a necklace is sunnah because it publicizes the act of worship. For sin-related blood, no necklace should be placed because concealment of the sin is better.
The Arabic quote from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explicitly excludes blood of ihsar and jinayat.
4
Note the rationale behind the classification
The necklace (qilada) is a form of display (izhar) and publicity (tashhir). Worship benefits from publicity; sin demands privacy. Even dam al-ihsar, though not a sin, is compensatory and thus joins the category of sin blood.
This shows how a legal principle (satr) shapes a specific ritual ruling.

(228) Indeed, concealment in matters of sins is more appropriate. Translation: Concealing sins is more fitting. Explanation: The meaning of this principle is that for blood that becomes obligatory due to a sin, one should not place a necklace (qilada) on the animal, because placing a necklace aims at publicity, whereas concealment is better in cases of sin. The author of Hidayah mentions this principle to explain that placing a necklace on the sacrificial animal of Tamattu' and Qiran is correct, because each is blood of worship, so publicizing it is befitting. But aside from them, animals blood for ihsar (being prevented) and for sins should not be given a necklace, because the cause of blood for sin is the sin, and concealing the sin is more appropriate. Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: It is said: 'And he should place a necklace only on the voluntary, the Tamattu', and the Qiran sacrificial animal because it is blood of worship, and in the necklace there is display and publicity, which suits it. His saying 'only' indicates that he does not place a necklace on the blood of ihsar nor on the blood of sins, because their cause is sin, and concealment is more suited to them; and the blood of ihsar is compensatory, so it joins its category.'

Commentary
This passage presents a principle about concealing sins, applied to the practice of placing necklaces (taqlid) on sacrificial animals. The Urdu commentary explains that for blood offerings that are obligatory due to a sin (e.g., expiation for hunting or other violations), the animal should not be adorned with a necklace, as that would publicize the sin. In contrast, for the sacrificial animals of Tamattu', Qiran, and voluntary offerings (which are acts of worship), placing a necklace is recommended because it publicizes worship. The Arabic quote from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies that only blood of nusuk (worship) gets a necklace; blood of ihsar (prevention from completing Hajj) and blood of sins (jinayat) do not, because their cause is sin and concealment is appropriate. The blood of ihsar is compensatory (jabir) so it is included in the category of sins.

قلادہ (qilāda): Necklace placed on sacrificial animal to mark it as a sacrifice.
دم احصار (dam al-ihsār): Blood offered when one is prevented from completing Hajj or Umrah.

Tagging Sacrificial Animals: The Hanafi Distinction Based on Concealment
Explains which sacrificial animals get tagged based on the principle of concealment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which Hanafi sacrificial animals are tagged and apply the principle of concealment (al-satr alyaqu bi al-jinayat) to distinguish between worship-based and sin-based blood offerings.

Core Idea
Tagging publicizes the animal; publicize worship, conceal sin.

This passage from al-Hidayah and al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the Hanafi rule on tagging sacrificial animals. It uses a fundamental legal maxim: concealment is more fitting for sins. By understanding which animals are tagged and why, you will grasp how the Hanafi school derives fiqh rulings from general principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Tagging (taqliq) is placing a necklace on a sacrificial animal. Determine for which animals it is sunnah and for which it is makruh according to the Hanafi school.
2
Classify the Types of Blood Offerings
Distinguish between dam al-nusuk (worship-based, e.g., tamattu', qiran, voluntary) and dam al-jinayat (sin-based) and dam al-ihsar (compensatory).
3
Apply the Maxim: Al-Satr Alyaqu bi al-Jinayat
Understand that tagging is a public act; worship deserves publicity, but sin deserves concealment. This maxim determines whether tagging is appropriate.
4
Derive the Ruling
Combine the maxim with the classification: tag only worship-based offerings; do not tag sin-based or compensatory ones.
5
Note the Practical Recommendation
The passage also recommends that the pilgrim slaughter the animal personally to increase devotion, if capable.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof, specifically an expansion of the Hanafi rule on tagging sacrificial animals. It states that only voluntary (tatattu'), tamattu', and qiran sacrificial animals are tagged, while animals for ihsar (hindrance) and jinayat (sins) are not. The proof is the principle 'concealment is more fitting for sins' (إِنَّ السَّتْرَ أَلْيَقُ بِالْجِنَايَاتِ). The reasoning: tagging publicizes the animal, which is appropriate for worship-based blood offerings (dam nusuk) like those of tamattu' and qiran, but inappropriate for blood required due to sin (dam jinayah) or deficiency (dam ihsar), where concealment is better. The passage also notes that it is better for the pilgrim to slaughter the animal himself if capable, as it increases devotion. The Arabic quote from Bahr al-Ra'iq confirms the ruling: only the sacrificial camel of tatattu', tamattu', and qiran is tagged; ihsar and jinayat are excluded because their cause is sin and concealment is more fitting. The blood of ihsar is compensatory (jabir) and thus follows the category of jinayat.
