Ruling on a Limb Cut from a Living Animal
Hanafi ruling with hadith proof against opposing qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a limb cut from a living animal and explain the hadith-based proof including the application of the absolute term (muṭlaq).

Core Idea
A limb cut from a living animal is dead (mayyit) and haram to eat, based on the hadith.

This passage addresses a key fiqh ruling: when a limb is cut from a living animal—whether during emergency slaughter or otherwise—what is its status? The Hanafi school rules that such a limb is dead (mayyit) and therefore forbidden. The proof is a hadith that uses the absolute term 'al-hayy' (the living), which the Hanafis interpret as referring to the perfect living individual. This lesson walks through the ruling, the opposing view, and the precise inferential reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? A limb cut from a living animal, especially in emergency slaughter. The Hanafi ruling: the limb is considered dead (mayyit) and haram to eat.
The act is called al-mubān min al-ḥayy.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith: 'مَا أُبِينَ مِنَ الْحَيِّ فَهُوَ مَيِّتٌ' (Whatever is cut from a living animal, it is dead). This is a sunnah ahad with zanni thubut.
Narrated by Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the ruling? The key is the absolute term 'al-hayy'. In Hanafi usul, an absolute term (muṭlaq) refers to the perfect individual—the animal alive in reality and legally. The limb cut from such an animal is therefore dead.
The perfect individual is the one who is alive in reality (bi-haqiqa) and legally (bi-hukm).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars (possibly Shafi'i) analogize cutting a limb in emergency slaughter to cutting the head in regular slaughter, where the head is eaten. Thus they may permit the limb.
Opposing dalil: qiyas.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi response: the hadith overrides the analogy because it is a specific nass that explicitly declares the cut part dead. The ruling is mufta bihi.
The hadith is a primary proof, so it takes precedence over qiyas.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on a limb cut from a living animal (al-muban min al-hayy). It contrasts two scenarios: cutting a head by legitimate slaughter (dhabh ikhtiyari) and cutting a limb in emergency slaughter (dhabh idtirari). The opponent (likely some scholars) argues that a limb cut in emergency slaughter is permissible to eat, analogizing to the head during regular slaughter. The Hanafi response cites the hadith 'What is cut from a living animal is dead' (narrated by Abu Dawud and others). The Hanafi inference (wajh al-istidlal) is that the word 'al-hayy' in the hadith is absolute, so it refers to the perfect individual: an animal that is alive both in reality (bi-haqiqa) and legally (bi-hukm). A limb cut from such an animal is considered dead (mayyit) and thus not permissible to eat. Additionally, if such an animal falls into water, it is not eaten because its death might be due to drowning. This ruling is from the chapter on hunting (kitab al-sayd).

Key Terms
المبان من الحي (al-mubān min al-ḥayy): A limb cut from a living animal.
ذكاة الاختيار (dhakāt al-ikhtiyār): Slaughter of choice (regular slaughter that properly severs the throat).
ذكاة الاضطرار (dhakāt al-iḍṭirār): Slaughter of necessity (when regular slaughter is not possible, e.g., hunting with a shot).
