Obedience According to Capacity: The Prayer of the Sick
States the legal maxim of obedience according to capacity and its application to sick prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'obedience is according to capacity' and apply it to determine the ruling for a sick person's prayer.

Core Idea
A sick person prays sitting if unable to stand, and gestures if unable to bow – because obligation does not exceed capacity.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi maxim derived from the Quran: Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity. The author of Al-Hidayah uses this principle to clarify the concession given to the sick in prayer. Understanding this maxim unlocks many leniencies in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim and Its Source
Locate the phrase 'إِنَّ الطَّاعَةَ بِحَسَب الطَّاقَةِ' (obedience is according to capacity) in the text. Recognise it as a legal maxim (qawida) derived from Quran 2:286.
The maxim is general; here it is applied to prayer.
2
Understand 'Capacity' (Taqah)
Define capacity as the ability to perform an act without excessive hardship. The Hanafi school considers normal human endurance as the baseline.
Excessive hardship (mashaqqa) justifies concession.
3
Recognise Invalidity of 'Taklif Ma La Yutaq'
Imposing an obligation beyond one's capacity is impermissible in Islamic law. This is a key principle that triggers concessions.
The text explicitly states 'تکلیف مالا يطاق جائز نہیں ہے' (imposing an unbearable task is not permissible).
4
Apply the Maxim to the Sick Person's Prayer
If a patient cannot stand, he prays sitting. If he cannot bow and prostrate, he performs them by gesture (ima'). The obligation remains but the manner is reduced.
This is the ruling (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school.
5
Note the Practical Degrees
The concession has levels: standing → sitting, then bowing/prostration → gesture. If even gesture is impossible, further concessions apply (e.g., intention).
This gradation ensures that obligation never exceeds actual capacity.

Understanding the Obligation Does Not Drop It
States the maxim that understanding an obligation prevents its dropping.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that understanding an obligation prevents its dropping due to incapacity, and apply it to the case of a severely ill patient.

Core Idea
Understanding the command keeps the obligation alive; incapacity defers it, does not cancel it.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi maxim: once a person understands that an act is obligatory (fard), that obligation does not drop even if physical performance becomes impossible. The author of al-Hidayah uses this to rule on a critically ill patient who cannot gesture. The obligation remains as a debt on the person's liability (dhimma) until recovery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is the obligatory prayer (fard ayn). The ruling is that the obligation does not drop due to physical inability.
2
Recognize the condition of understanding
The maxim applies only to those who understand the content of the command (mazmun al-khitab), i.e., they are conscious and know the act is obligatory.
Unconsciousness or ignorance may remove the understanding, potentially altering the ruling.
3
Understand the consequence
Because the obligation remains, the patient must make up (qada) the missed prayers upon recovery, even if the illness lasted longer than a day and night.
The qada is wajib (or fard) and must be performed when capacity returns.
4
Distinguish from other views
Some might mistakenly think that total incapacity absolves the obligation. The Hanafi ruling emphasizes that liability persists until performance or death.
No substitute by opinion is allowed; the text on gesture with the head is a concession, not a cancellation.

The Purpose of Standing (Qiyam) in Prayer
Explaining the wisdom behind qiyam being a pillar of salah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why qiyam is a rukn in salah according to the Hanafi school and apply the ruling for a person unable to stand or even point with the head.

Core Idea
Qiyam is a means, not an end — its pillar status serves the purpose of reaching sujud.

This short passage from Al-Hidayah reveals the wisdom behind a key pillar of prayer: standing (qiyam) is not an independent goal but a means (tawassul) to enable the actual core of sujud. Understanding this teleological relationship helps explain why certain concessions are granted to the sick and why the obligation of prayer is not simply dropped when standing becomes impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition
Locate the statement 'rukniyyat al-qiyam li-t-tawassul ila as-sajdah'. This defines the pillar-status as instrumental.
The term 'tawassul' indicates something is a means to an end.
2
Understand the purpose (illah)
The reason qiyam is a rukn is to enable sujud. This teleological reasoning (talil) is a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh.
Without the purpose, qiyam would not be a rukn.
3
Connect to the exceptional case
The passage then cites a ruling: if a sick person cannot even point with the head, they delay the prayer if conscious. This shows that when the means is impossible, the end (sujud) is still pursued by other means (delay) rather than dropping the obligation.
Contrast with the unconscious person who does not understand the address.
4
Apply the principle
Use the principle to differentiate: a conscious but severely ill person must make up the prayer later; an unconscious person is exempt because they lack comprehension.
The ruling is tied to the capacity to understand the divine address.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Qada for Extended Unconsciousness
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qada after prolonged unconsciousness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios of unconsciousness and prayer obligation, and evaluate the Hanafi dispute over qada for a conscious person exceeding a day and night.

Core Idea
Understanding the address triggers obligation; unconsciousness beyond a day and night creates an intra-Hanafi split on qada.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether a person who was unconscious for more than a day and night must make up missed prayers if they regain consciousness. The passage from al-Hidayah presents the view that qada is obligatory because the person understands the divine address when conscious, while Qadi Khan and others argue it drops. The dispute hinges on whether mere intellect suffices for address. We will unpack the arguments and the ruling relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
The passage outlines four cases based on duration and consciousness: (1) unconscious > day+night and never conscious – consensus no qada; (2) conscious  day+night and later conscious; (4) conscious < day+night and later unconscious. Scenarios 3 and 4 are disputed.
The dispute is primarily about scenario 3, which is the focus of the passage.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proof for obligation
Sahib al-Hidayah argues that a conscious person understands the content of the divine address (mazmun al-khitab), which is sufficient to impose the obligation of qada. This is based on the principle that understanding triggers address.
This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).
3
Examine the opposing proof
Qadi Khan and al-Bazdawi al-Saghir argue that mere intellect is not enough; one must be actually addressed. They cite a narration from Muhammad in al-Nawadir: a limbless person has no prayer obligation, showing that ability to understand alone does not impose duty.
The opposing view holds that qada drops if unconsciousness exceeds a day and night.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes the limbless case: such a person cannot perform any action at all, whereas a conscious person can eventually pray after recovery. Thus, the analogy is not valid.
This response supports Sahib al-Hidayah's view.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The author of al-Hidayah's position (qada is obligatory) is chosen as the operative ruling because it aligns with the general principle that understanding the address is sufficient for obligation.
Fatwa is given accordingly.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the obligation of qada (makeup prayers) for a person who was unconscious for more than a day and night but later regained consciousness. The passage begins by citing a statement from al-Hidayah: if a sick person cannot even gesture with his head, he delays the prayer, but this does not mean prayer is dropped if he is conscious (mufiq) and understands the content of the address. The author of al-Hidayah (Sahib al-Hidayah) chooses that qada is obligatory, while al-Bazdawi al-Saghir and Qadi Khan choose that it is not. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is the one from Sahib al-Hidayah, as it is more cautious and aligns with the principle that understanding the address triggers obligation.

Standing as Pillar Only as Means to Prostration
Explaining that qiyam is a pillar only because it leads to sujood

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on standing in prayer when prostration is impossible and explain the rationale (talil) behind it.

Core Idea
Standing is a pillar only because it enables prostration; when prostration is impossible, standing is no longer required.

In this lesson, we explore a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: the pillar-status (rukniyya) of standing in prayer is contingent on its purpose—leading to prostration. When that purpose cannot be achieved, the ruling changes. This illustrates the Hanafi use of talil (rationale) to derive rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Determine what the passage says about the obligation of standing when a person cannot prostrate.
2
Extract the 'illa (effective cause)
Why is standing normally a pillar? Because it is a means (tawassul) to prostration.
The 'illa is derived from the text: 'innā rukniyyata al-qiyāmi li-t-tawassuli ilā s-sajdah.'
3
Apply the 'illa to the exceptional case
When prostration is impossible, the 'illa is absent, so the ruling of pillar-status no longer applies.
This is an application of the principle: 'al-ḥukm yadūru maʿa al-ʿillah wujūdan wa ʿadaman.'
4
Determine the preferred practice
The passage states that the better option is to gesture while sitting, as the head is closer to the ground, resembling actual prostration.
5
Note the cross-school agreement
All schools agree that if one cannot prostrate, gestures suffice; this is not a matter of dispute.
Muttafaq ʿalayh.

Al-Ghalib Ka al-Muḥaqqaq: Applying Prevalent Existence to Prayer
Establishing and applying a legal maxim to prayer concessions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle of al-ghalib wujuduhu ka al-muhaqqaq, apply it to the boat prayer case, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A prevalent condition (ghalabah) is treated as actual, allowing concessions like sitting in prayer.

This lesson introduces a powerful Hanafi legal maxim: al-ghalib wujuduhu ka al-muhaqqaq (that whose existence is prevalent is like the actual). We will see how al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah applies it to two prayer situations: a person who can stand but cannot prostrate, and a person praying in a boat without illness. In the latter, Abu Hanifa rules the prayer valid due to prevalent dizziness, while his two students disagree. Understanding this principle helps you grasp how Hanafi fiqh grants ease (takhfif) based on dominant realities.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its meaning
Begin with the Arabic maxim: 'al-ghālib wujūduhu ka al-mutaḥaqqiq.' Translate it literally: 'That whose existence is prevalent is like the actual.' Understand that it treats a dominant condition as if it is definitely present.
This is a qa'ida sughra (minor maxim) within Hanafi fiqh.
2
Apply the maxim to the first scenario: unable to prostrate
A person can stand but cannot bow or prostrate. The ruling: he should sit and gesture (īmā') for rukū' and sujūd. Why? Because standing is a pillar only as a means to prostration. Since prostration is impossible, standing loses its pillar status. The gesture while sitting is more similar to actual prostration.
The maxim is not explicitly cited here but the logic of prevalent inability (ghalabah) underpins the concession.
3
Apply the maxim to the second scenario: prayer in a boat
A person prays an obligatory prayer sitting in a boat without any illness. Abu Hanifa says the prayer is valid but makruh tanzīhī (standing better). His evidence: dizziness while standing in a boat is prevalent (ghālib), and prevalent dizziness is treated as actual dizziness, thus the person is excused from standing. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the prayer is invalid unless there is illness, because standing is within his capability.
The maxim is directly invoked: 'al-ghālib fīhā dawārān al-ra's wa huwa ka al-muḥaqqaq.'
4
Trace the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Hanifa relies on the principle of ghalabah to grant dispensation. The Sahibayn hold that a rukn (standing) cannot be omitted when it is physically possible; only a genuine illness justifies sitting. The practical difference: according to Abu Hanifa the prayer is valid though the person has done wrong (by abandoning the better); according to the Sahibayn it is invalid and must be repeated.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school, not a rejection of the principle itself.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative position)
Al-Marghinani and later Hanafi commentators state that standing is better to avoid the dispute (khurūjan min al-khilāf). However, the ruling of validity according to Abu Hanifa is relied upon for dispensation (rukhsah). Thus, if someone prays sitting due to prevalent dizziness, the prayer is legally valid.
The mufta bihi is precaution: stand if possible, but the prayer sitting is accepted.

Core Idea
Multiple recitations of the same sajdah verse in one sitting require only one sajdah: tadakhul prevents hardship.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi legal principle of tadakhul (integration) as applied to sajdat al-tilawah. The passage from al-Hidayah establishes that when a person recites the same sajdah verse multiple times in a single sitting, only one sajdah is obligatory. Types of Homeland and Their Invalidation Rules
Definitions and invalidation rules for watan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the three types of watan according to the Hanafi school, distinguish between the two accepted homelands, and apply the rules for when each is invalidated.

Core Idea
An original homeland is replaced only by another original homeland when the entire family moves; residence homelands are easily invalidated.

The rulings on shortening prayer (qasr) depend on whether a traveler has established a new homeland. The Hanafi school defines two types of homeland that affect prayer length: the original homeland (waṭan aṣlī) and the residence homeland (waṭan iqāmah). A third type, waṭan suknā, is not considered valid by later authorities. This lesson unpacks the definitions and the precise rules for when one homeland invalidates another.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of watan
Read the passage carefully and list the three types mentioned by the general mashāyikh. Note their definitions: original homeland (birthplace or where one settles with family), residence homeland (intend 15+ days), and dwelling homeland (intend less than 15 days).
The dwelling homeland is later rejected by muḥaqqiqūn.
2
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Observe that the general mashāyikh mention three homelands, but the verified scholars (muḥaqqiqūn) consider only two, discarding waṭan suknā. Note the authority cited: al-Kifāyah declares the two-fold division as correct (ṣaḥīḥ).
This ikhtilaf affects which rulings apply to short stays.

The passage states that an original homeland is invalidated only by another original homeland, and only if the person moves with his entire family (bi-ahlihi). If he acquires a new family elsewhere without moving the first, both remain original homelands and he prays full in both.
Moving alone does not change the original homeland.
4
Understand the invalidation rules for residence homeland
A residence homeland is invalidated by: another residence homeland, by initiating travel (i.e., departing), and by the original homeland (i.e., returning home).
It is weaker than an original homeland.
5

The passage includes the principle that a person's residence is attached to his sleeping place. This is used when deciding which of two possible residence homelands takes effect. The place where one sleeps determines his residence.

Invalidation of Watan Asli and Watan Iqama
Ruling on invalidation of homelands.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for when watan asli and watan iqama are invalidated, and apply the principle of invalidation by equality, superiority, and opposition.

Core Idea
Watan asli is only undone by another watan asli; watan iqama is undone by any superior, equal, or opposite.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi distinction between two types of homeland (watan) and how each becomes invalid. Understanding invalidation is critical for determining when a traveler becomes a resident and vice versa, affecting salah and fasting rules.

Guided Path

Identify the two types of watan
Recognize watan asli (original homeland) and watan iqama (temporary residence with intention of 15+ days).
Watan asli is permanent; watan iqama is established by intention.
2
State the invalidation rule for watan asli
Watan asli is only invalidated by another watan asli, because it is the highest level and only its equal can override it.
No other factor (watan iqama or travel) invalidates watan asli.
3
State the invalidation rule for watan iqama
Watan iqama is invalidated by three things: watan asli (superior), another watan iqama (equal), and travel (opposite).
The maxim 'a thing is invalidated by its opposite' applies to travel.
4
Explain the rationale
The invalidation is based on superiority (watan asli above watan iqama), equality (same level), and opposition (travel is the opposite of residence).
This hierarchy ensures clear residency status.
5
Apply to a scenario
For a person with watan asli in Lahore who travels to Karachi for 20 days: watan iqama in Karachi is established, but watan asli in Lahore remains valid. If he later moves family to Karachi permanently, watan asli becomes Karachi.
Practical: determine whether to shorten prayers when traveling.

The Principle of Iqama Attached to Overnight Stay
Establishes the principle of iqama being attached to mabit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that iqama is attached to mabit to determine the residency status of a traveler intending iqama in multiple locations.

Core Idea
A traveler's residency is determined by where he sleeps at night, not by where he stays during the day.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi principle for determining when a traveler becomes a resident (muqim) and must pray complete salah. The rule is simple: your residence is where you spend the night. We will see why intending iqama in two places without a designated mabit does not make you a resident, and how the Hanafi school classifies homelands.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the core rule from Al-Hidayah: 'Indeed, a person's iqama is attached to his place of overnight stay.' This means that for the purpose of determining residency in travel, the criterion is where one sleeps at night.
2
Understand the problem of multiple iqamas
Consider a traveler intending to stay 15 days in both Makkah and Mina. If both intentions were valid, the traveler would be resident in two places, leading to an infinite regress if extended to many stops. This contradicts the nature of travel, where stopping at multiple places is normal.
The key is that intention alone in two places is not enough; a specific mabit is needed.
3
Identify the exception: designated mabit
If the traveler designates one of the two places for overnight stay (e.g., sleeping in Makkah), then the iqama attaches to that place. He becomes a muqim there and must pray complete, while the other place remains a travel destination.
The mabit is the basis for iqama, as shown by the common practice of people referring to their nighttime residence as 'home'.
4
Recognize the types of watan
The passage explains the Hanafi classification of homelands: original homeland (watan asli) where one was born or settled, homeland of residence (watan iqama) where one intends 15+ days, and homeland of dwelling (watan sukna) for less than 15 days. The muhaqqiqin (verifying scholars) accept only asli and iqama, rejecting watan sukna as a separate category.
This two-fold division is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).
5
Apply the principle to a real scenario
Ask yourself: 'Where do I sleep at night?' That is your residence, even if you work or study elsewhere during the day. The principle also governs how original and residence homelands are invalidated (e.g., by moving with family, or by the establishment of a new iqama).

The Journey Itself Is Not a Sin
Establishes that travel concessions apply regardless of the traveler's intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that travel concessions apply to all travelers regardless of intention and explain the basis in Quranic verses.

Core Idea
Travel concessions depend on the journey, not the traveler's intention.

This lesson presents a key Hanafi principle from Al-Hidayah: the journey itself is not sinful. Therefore, the concessions of travel, such as shortening prayer and breaking fast, apply equally to the obedient and disobedient traveler. We will examine the proof and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
What exactly is being ruled? The principle that travel concessions are triggered by the act of travel, regardless of the traveler's purpose.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is derived from general Quranic verses allowing travel concessions: Surah al-Baqarah 2:185 (fasting) and Surah al-Nisa' 4:101 (prayer).
These verses do not qualify travel by intention.
3
Examine the reasoning
The verses grant concessions on the condition of travel, without restricting to permissible journeys. Therefore, the concession attaches to the journey itself.
This is the wajh al-dalalah: the unqualified permission.
4
Note cross-madhhab difference
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may restrict concessions to permissible journeys. The Hanafi school holds the broader application.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Residence in Two Places: The Role of Overnight Stay
Ruling on intention of 15-day stay in two locations and the role of overnight stay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on intention of residence in two places and identify the key factor (mabit) that determines whether a traveler becomes a muqeem.

Core Idea
Residence is determined by where you sleep, not where you work — mabit makes the muqeem.

When a traveler intends to stay 15 days split between two locations, does he become a resident? The Hanafi school answers with a nuanced rule: it depends on where he intends to sleep. This lesson unpacks the proof from custom (urf) and qiyas, showing how the place of overnight stay (mabit) is the decisive factor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is intending residence (iqamah) of 15 days in two places. The ruling is that the person becomes a muqeem (resident) if he intends to spend the night in one specific place, making full prayers obligatory.
If no overnight stay is intended in any one place, he remains a musafir.
2
Understand the legal basis
The proof is based on 'urf (custom) and qiyas. In common usage, people are identified by their nighttime dwelling (mabit), not their daytime workplace. This custom is used as evidence (dalil) via istihsan.
The text cites al-Bahr al-Raiq and al-Hidayah as supporting sources.
3
Apply the condition
The condition is that the traveler must intend to stay overnight (mabit) in one of the two places. If he does, upon entering that place he becomes a muqeem.
The daytime location does not affect residency status.
4
Note the thamarah (practical effect)
As a muqeem, the traveler prays full salah (not qasr). If he then travels to the other place, he remains a muqeem unless he intends new residence there.
The ruling avoids the absurdity that a traveler could never be considered a resident if he splits his stay.

Travel Concessions Apply to All Travelers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that travel concessions apply to all travelers regardless of purpose, identify the absolute (mutlaq) texts that prove this, and evaluate the opposing view that restricts concessions to permissible travel.

Core Idea
Travel concessions are attached to the act of travel itself, not the traveler's moral state.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes a key principle: travel itself is not a sin. Therefore, the concessions of travel (such as shortening prayers and breaking fast) apply to every traveler, whether obedient or disobedient. The proof comes from the absolute wording of the Quran and Sunnah, which do not condition concessions on the purpose of travel. This ruling distinguishes the Hanafi school from others who restrict concessions to permissible journeys.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the foundational rule: 'Travel itself is not sin.' This means the act of covering distance is morally neutral; sin depends on intention and action, not on travel.
The principle is stated at the beginning of the passage.
2
Identify the Hanafi position
The author uses this principle to prove that the concessions of travel (rukhsat al-safar) apply equally to the obedient and the disobedient traveler. Both may shorten prayers and break fast during Ramadan.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
3
Locate the proofs
The author cites two texts: Quran 2:184 ("So whoever among you is ill or on a journey") and the hadith "The fard of the traveler is two rak'ahs." Both texts are unconditional (mutlaq) regarding the traveler's moral status.
The Quran verse is qat'i al-thubut; the hadith is ahad but accepted in fiqh.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The texts mention 'travel' without any qualification of obedience or permissible purpose. According to Hanafi usul, a mutlaq text applies to all its instances unless there is a proof for restriction (taqyid). Therefore, the ruling covers all travelers.
The opposing school argues that the concession implies a condition of permissible travel, but the Hanafi school rejects this as an addition without evidence.
5

Some schools (e.g., the Shafi'i) hold that travel concessions are only for permissible journeys, based on the idea that concessions are kindness from Allah and should not benefit sinners. The Hanafi response is that the texts are absolute and travel itself is not sin.
The practical difference (thamarah) is that in the Hanafi school, a person traveling to commit theft may still shorten prayers, while in the opposing school he may not.

Making Up Missed Travel and Residence Prayers: Fixed Rak'at and Illness Exception
Ruling with proof: qada follows ada with fixed rak'at count and flexible actions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the number of rak'at for making up missed travel and residence prayers, and distinguish the case of illness where physical actions are performed according to capacity.

Core Idea
The number of rak'at in qada is fixed to the original obligation; only physical actions adapt to capacity.

This passage from al-Hidayah expands on the principle that qada follows ada. It clearly states that missed travel prayers are made up as two rak'at, and missed residence prayers as four rak'at. Then it contrasts with the case of illness: if a prayer is missed while unable to bow or prostrate, when making it up in health, those actions are required; conversely, if missed in health and then one becomes ill, gestures suffice. The key distinction is that the number of rak'at is fixed, while bodily actions depend on ability at the time of making up.

The passage gives a counterexample: if a prayer is missed during illness (where one cannot bow/prostrate), it is made up in health with full actions. The counterexample is not an exception to the number of rak'at, but to the requirement of specific actions.

Taklif Depends on Ability and Its Application to Friday Prayer
Maxim with proof and application to Jumu'ah/Zuhr on Friday

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim 'al-taklif yaduru 'ala al-tamakkun' to determine the ruling of Zuhr on Friday and the make-up of missed prayers in various scenarios.

Core Idea
Obligation hinges on capacity: what you cannot do is waived, but the default obligation remains.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that obligation is based on ability. The author first proves the maxim from the Quran, then applies it to two critical cases: the relationship between Zuhr and Jumu'ah on Friday, and how missed prayers are made up by travelers and the ill. Understanding this maxim clarifies why Zuhr remains the default obligation on Friday, even though Jumu'ah supersedes it under its conditions.

1

The maxim is a general principle that limits obligation to what one can actually do.
2
Understand the application to Friday prayer
The author argues that Zuhr is the fard (obligatory prayer) of Friday, but it drops when Jumu'ah conditions are met. This is proven by the rule that if Jumu'ah is missed, one makes up Zuhr, not Jumu'ah. Reflect on why this shows Zuhr is the underlying obligation.

Study the examples: a traveler missed prayer is made up as two rak'ahs (matching the original shortened obligation); a resident missed prayer is made up as four. Contrast with the illness case where bowing and prostration are dropped due to incapacity but must be performed when health returns.
The key distinction: in travel, the number of rak'ahs is fixed; in illness, the manner (ruku/sujud) is waived but returns with ability.

Martyrdom as a Preventer, Not a Remover: The Junub Martyr
Legal maxim with proof and application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on washing a junub martyr and apply the maxim 'shahadah is a preventer, not a remover' to distinguish between obligations that are nullified by martyrdom and those that remain.

Core Idea
Martyrdom prevents the death-wash but does not remove pre-existing janabah.

This passage introduces a critical Hanafi legal maxim: martyrdom (shahadah) is a preventer (mani'), not a remover (rafi'). While martyrdom cancels the obligation to wash the deceased for death, it does not lift prior obligations such as ghusl from janabah. The author of Al-Hidayah uses this principle to justify Imam Abu Hanifah's ruling that a martyr who was in a state of janabah must still be washed to remove that impurity, citing the incident of Sayyiduna Hanzalah and an insightful analogy.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim and its components
Identify the Arabic phrase 'الشَّهَادَةَ عُرِفَتْ مَانِعَةً غَيْرَ رَافِعَةٍ'. Break it down: 'mani'ah' means preventer—it blocks an obligation from arising; 'rafi'ah' means remover—it cancels an existing obligation. Understand that martyrdom prevents the cause of death (death itself) from triggering the obligation of ghusl al-mayyit, but it does not remove obligations that already existed.
This is the core legal principle.
2
Apply the maxim to the case of a junub martyr
Consider a martyr who was in a state of janabah before death. According to the maxim, the requirement to wash for death is blocked, but the janabah remains as an existing impurity. Therefore, the martyr must be bathed specifically to remove janabah. This is not a duplicate of the death-wash but a distinct obligation.
The washing is for janabah, not for death.
3
Identify the primary dalil from the Sunnah
The text cites the incident of Sayyiduna Hanzalah, who was martyred in a state of janabah. The angels bathed him, proving that the janabah was not removed by martyrdom. Locate this proof and note its authority (hadith).
Wajh al-dalalah: the angels' act shows janabah must be removed.
4
Analyze the analogical proof (qiyas)
The text draws an analogy: if a martyr's garment has a non-blood impurity (e.g., urine), that impurity is washed off, but the martyr's blood is not washed. This shows that martyrdom only prevents the blood from being impure, not other impurities. Similarly, janabah is a prior impurity not prevented by martyrdom. Trace the reasoning: asl = garment with najasah, far = junub martyr, illa = martyrdom prevents only blood impurity.
Confirm the illa extraction (tahqiq al-manat).
5
Note intra-madhhab and cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Understand that Imam Abu Hanifah holds this view; possibly Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree (some reports say they consider the martyr not washed at all). Other schools like the Shafi'is generally do not wash martyrs under any condition. Evaluate why the Hanafi position is mufta bihi according to Al-Hidayah.
The tarjih authority is Al-Marghinani's reliance on Abu Hanifah.

Precedence and Posteriority in Congregation: The Condition of Unified Direction
Explaining that spatial precedence and posteriority matter only when the direction (side) of the imam and follower is the same.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on precedence and posteriority in congregation, and apply it to determine the validity of prayer when followers are on different sides of the imam.

Core Idea
Precedence invalidates only when direction aligns – around the Kaaba, all directions are valid.

Have you ever wondered how the Hanafis handle congregational prayer in the Sacred Mosque where worshippers circle the Kaaba? The imam stands on one side, yet followers on the opposite side end up physically closer to the Kaaba. Doesn't that make them 'ahead' of the imam? In this lesson, we unpack a key Hanafi maxim that resolves this puzzle: Taqaddum (precedence) and Ta'akhkhur (posteriority) only matter when the direction (janib) is unified. This maxim is not just a theoretical rule; it directly impacts the validity of countless prayers performed around the Kaaba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim
State the rule in Arabic and English: 'Precedence and posteriority only become apparent when the side is unified' (إِنَّ التَّقَدُّمَ وَالتَّأَخَّرَ إِنَّمَا يَظْهَرُ عِنْدَ اتِّحَادِ الْجَانِبِ).
This is the core legal maxim from Al-Hidayah.
2
Understand the Condition
Recognize that 'direction unity' is the key condition. If the imam and follower face the same direction, then spatial precedence matters; if they face different directions, precedence is irrelevant.
Direction is determined by the qiblah or the orientation towards the Kaaba.
3
Apply to Masjid al-Haram
In the Sacred Mosque, the imam leads from one side of the Kaaba. Followers on the opposite side face a different direction (towards the Kaaba from their side). Even if they are physically closer to the Kaaba than the imam, they are not considered 'ahead' because their direction differs.
The maxim resolves the apparent conflict.
4
Derive the Ruling
The prayer of those followers is valid (sahih). The condition of being behind the imam does not apply when directions differ.
This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.
5
Note the Rationale
The maxim is a rational inference (dalil aqli) based on the purpose of congregation: the follower's position is relative to the imam's direction. Without unity of direction, the concepts of ahead/behind lose meaning.
This reasoning is specific to Hanafi usul.

Hanafi Definition of the Ka'bah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of the Ka'bah and apply it to determine the validity of prayer on top of the Ka'bah.

Core Idea
The Ka'bah is the ground area and airspace up to the sky — not just the building.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi principle that redefines the qiblah. According to the Hanafi school, the term 'Ka'bah' does not refer exclusively to the physical building but encompasses the entire vertical space from the ground where the building stands up to the canopy of the sky. This definition has practical implications for the validity of prayer at heights above the Ka'bah, such as on its roof or on nearby mountains like Abu Qubays, and even in modern contexts like airplanes. The lesson will clarify the reasoning behind this definition and its application to prayer rulings.

Guided Path
1

The definition explicitly includes the vertical column from the ground up to the sky.
2
Understand the reasoning
The text explains why the building alone is not called the Ka'bah: because a building can be demolished or moved, which is incompatible with the dignity and constancy of the qiblah.
The reasoning is rational (dalil aqli): the qiblah must be permanent and not subject to change.
3
Apply to prayer
Since the Ka'bah includes the airspace, praying on the roof of the Ka'bah means one is still facing the Ka'bah, even without a sutrah (screen). The Prophet's analogy of Mount Abu Qubays is cited to show that prayer at a higher elevation is valid as long as one is within the vertical space.
The validity does not depend on the building being directly in front of the worshipper.
4
Note the disapproval
Although valid, such prayer is considered makruh tanzihi (disliked) because it involves leaving out reverence (tark al-ta'zim). The text mentions a prohibition regarding it.
The ruling balances legal validity with recommended reverence.

Explanation: The meaning of the mentioned principle is that the Ka'bah is not the name of the building, but rather the ground area where the building of the Ka'bah is situated, from there up to the sky—the entire space is called the Ka'bah. The author of al-Hidayah, by mentioning the said principle, is proving that according to the Hanafis, it is permissible to pray on the roof of the Ka'bah, even if there is no screen in front of him. The reason is that in our view, the qiblah is named after the Ka'bah, and the Ka'bah is not the name of the building, but rather the ground area where the building of the Ka'bah is, from there up to the sky space is the Ka'bah. And the building is not called the Ka'bah because the building can be demolished and moved, whereas these things are contrary to the dignity of the qiblah and the Ka'bah. Therefore, the Ka'bah is not only the name of the building, but the entire space from the Ka'bah up to the sky is called the Ka'bah. So if a person prays on the roof of the House of Allah, his prayer is valid. For this reason, if someone prays facing the qiblah at a place higher than the Ka'bah, it is valid, such as on Mount Abu Qubays or in modern times on an airplane; however, it is disliked because it involves a lack of reverence. Commentary
This passage presents a definitional principle regarding the nature of the Ka'bah as the qiblah. The text opens with principle (140): 'Indeed the Ka'bah is the ground area and the air up to the sky.' The explanation clarifies that the term 'Ka'bah' does not refer exclusively to the physical building (al-bina'), but encompasses the entire vertical space from the ground (where the building stands) up to the sky. The author of al-Hidayah uses this principle to justify the permissibility of praying on top of the Ka'bah according to the Hanafi school, even if there is no sutrah (screen) in front. The reasoning is that the qiblah is the entire area and airspace, not just the building. Consequently, praying at a height that is above the building but within the airspace of the Ka'bah is facing the qiblah. This applies to praying on Mount Abu Qubays (adjacent to the Ka'bah) or in an airplane directly above. However, the text notes that such prayer is makruh (disliked) because it involves leaving out reverence (tark al-ta'zim), and there is a prohibition (nahy) regarding it. The passage includes an Arabic quote from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq reinforcing this ruling: prayer above the Ka'bah is valid because our definition of the Ka'bah includes the airspace, not the movable building; the analogy with Mount Abu Qubays is given, but the act is disliked due to lack of reverence.

Key Terms
عرصہ ('arsah): Ground area, courtyard—the space where the building of the Ka'bah stands.
ہوا (hawa'): Air, space—the vertical column above the 'arsah up to the sky.
عنان سماء ('anan al-sama'): Canopy of the sky—the upper limit of the airspace considered as Ka'bah.

The Kaaba as Courtyard and Airspace
States the Hanafi principle defining Kaaba as including airspace, validating roof prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that defines the Kaaba as including its ground and airspace, and apply it to determine the validity of prayer on the roof of the Kaaba.

Core Idea
The Kaaba is not just the building; it is the entire airspace from the ground to the sky.

In this lesson, we explore a distinctive Hanafi principle: the Kaaba is defined as the courtyard (al-'arsah) and the airspace extending upward to the sky. This understanding has a direct practical effect: it validates prayer on the roof of the Kaaba or from a higher point, such as Mount Abu Qubays or an airplane. The passage from al-Hidayah explains the rationale and addresses a potential objection about veneration.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the ruling
Note that the author rules prayer on the roof of the Kaaba as valid (ja'iz) but disliked (makruh tanzih).
Valid means the prayer fulfills its conditions; disliked due to abandoning some reverence.
2
Identify the key principle
The passage states: 'The Kaaba is the courtyard and the airspace up to the sky.' This expands the definition beyond the building.
The Arabic term 'arsah' means the ground area; 'anān al-samā' means the sky canopy.
3
Understand the proof (dalil)
The proof is that the building can be moved or demolished, so it cannot be the essence of the qiblah. The airspace is permanent.
Analogous reasoning: prayer on Mount Abu Qubays is valid, even though no building faces the worshipper.
4
Note the practical application
Apply the principle: any location within the airspace above the Kaaba's footprint counts as facing the qiblah.
This includes rooftops, nearby mountains, and aircraft flying directly over the Kaaba.
5
Acknowledge the karahah (dislike)
Despite validity, the prayer is makruh tanzih because it involves abandoning the proper veneration of the Kaaba.
The Prophet (peace be upon him) reportedly disliked this practice.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It presents a key Hanafi principle: the Kaaba is not merely the physical building but includes the ground area (al-'arsah) and the airspace up to the sky. This principle is used to justify praying on the roof of the Kaaba or from a higher location. The author of al-Hidayah states that the building can be moved or demolished, so it cannot be the essence of the qiblah; rather the qiblah is the entire space. Thus, prayer on the roof is valid but makruh tanzihan due to abandoning some veneration. This ruling is supported by practice since the Prophet's time. A further ruling: if the imam prays in al-Masjid al-Haram, those closer to the Kaaba can follow him provided they are not on the same side (to avoid being ahead). The principle is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
