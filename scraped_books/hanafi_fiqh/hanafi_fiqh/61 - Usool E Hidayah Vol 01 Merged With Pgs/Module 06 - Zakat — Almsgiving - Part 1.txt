Complete Ownership is a Condition for Zakat
States condition for zakat obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of complete ownership for zakat and identify who is exempt due to lack of it.

Core Idea
For zakat, ownership must be complete—freedom is essential; a slave's property is not fully owned.

In the Hanafi school, the obligation of zakat is contingent upon complete ownership (milk kāmil). This principle, captured in the concise statement 'Indeed, complete ownership is through freedom,' establishes that a slave cannot have full ownership because his master ultimately owns his property. This lesson unpacks that condition and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
This passage states a condition for the obligation of zakat. Identify the condition: 'complete ownership' (kamāl al-milk). Also note the act (zakat) to which this condition applies.
2
Define 'complete ownership'
Complete ownership means full control and possession without any legal impediment, such as slavery or bankruptcy that prevents full disposal.
In Hanafi fiqh, the opposite is incomplete ownership (milk nāqiṣ), which does not trigger zakat.
3
Apply the condition to the case of a slave
A slave, even if he possesses property, does not have complete ownership because whatever he earns belongs to his master. Therefore, he is not obligated to pay zakat.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position; other schools may differ.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
The rationale is that zakat is a purification of wealth, and only the one who truly owns the wealth can be required to purify it. The slave's master is the true owner, hence zakat is on the master if conditions are met.

This principle means that for the obligation of zakat, complete ownership (milk kamel) is a condition. Similarly, a person who is not free does not have complete ownership. This is a Hanafi position.

Worship is Not Valid from a Disbeliever
States a principle about worship from non-Muslims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that worship from a disbeliever is invalid and explain why iman is a prerequisite for the validity of all acts of worship.

Core Idea
Faith (iman) is a condition for worship to be valid; without it, no act of worship is accepted.

This short but foundational principle (qa'ida) clarifies that the devotional acts of a non-Muslim have no religious validity in this world, even if they are outwardly correct. According to the Hanafi school, non-Muslims are still obligated in the branches of Shari'ah, but their performance without iman does not fulfill that obligation. Understanding this distinction between obligation and validity is key to many rulings on the acts of non-Muslims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the Arabic statement: 'Indeed, worship does not actually occur from a disbeliever.' Recognize that this is a definitive legal principle (qa'ida) in Hanafi fiqh.
The word 'tahaqqaq' implies actualization or realization — the act does not become a valid act of worship.
2
Understand the Condition of Imān
Realize that faith (iman) is a necessary condition (shart) for the validity (sihha) of any worship. Without it, the act is null (batil) in terms of religious acceptance.
Compare with other conditions like purity (tahara) for salah — iman is the most fundamental condition.
3
Distinguish Obligation from Validity
Note the Hanafi position: non-Muslims are addressed by the branches of Shari'ah (they are mukallafun in furu') but their acts are not accepted unless they believe. They will be punished for abandoning these acts in the Hereafter.
This differs from the Shafi'i view that non-Muslims are only addressed by the fundamentals of faith (usul).
4
Apply to Specific Acts
Apply the principle to various acts: a disbeliever's prayer, fasting, or zakat is not valid. They do not receive reward nor does it count toward their obligation if they later convert.
For zakat, if a non-Muslim later converts, they must pay zakat for past years if they had nisab, since their previous giving was not valid zakat.

Commentary
This principle means that the devotional acts (ibadat) of a disbeliever (kafir) are not valid in the sight of Allah. They are not accepted because faith (iman) is a condition for validity. However, the disbeliever is still obligated in the branches of faith (furu') according to Hanafis. But if he performs them without faith, they are not accepted. Basic Needs Are Non-Existent for Zakat
States principle for zakat calculation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that basic needs assets are excluded from zakat nisab calculation.

Core Idea
Basic needs are 'non-existent' for zakat: essential assets don't count toward nisab.

This short but powerful principle is a cornerstone of Hanafi zakat law. It ensures that the obligation of zakat does not impose hardship on the owner by counting assets that are essential for survival. Understanding this rule is critical for correctly calculating whether zakat is due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
State the rule in Arabic and English: 'Indeed, that which is occupied with the basic need is considered non-existent.'
Memorize this maxim as it is a key tool for zakat calculation.
2
Define 'basic need'
Determine what qualifies as a basic need (al-hajah al-asliyyah). In Hanafi fiqh, this includes the dwelling house, clothing, household utensils, means of transport, and even books for scholars. These are essential for survival and daily life.
The Hanafi definition is broader than some other schools, aiming to prevent hardship.
3
Apply to nisab calculation
When calculating whether one has reached the nisab (minimum threshold for zakat), exclude all basic needs assets. Only surplus wealth (e.g., cash, trade goods, extra property) is counted.
This ensures that zakat is only due on wealth beyond what is necessary for living.

Debt in Zakat Means Debt from People
Defines which debts affect zakat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of debt for zakat purposes and apply it to distinguish between human debts and divine obligations.

Core Idea
Only debts owed to people reduce your zakat nisab; debts to Allah do not count.

This lesson clarifies a critical distinction in zakat calculation. When assessing whether your wealth meets the nisab threshold, not all obligations are treated equally. The Hanafi school defines 'debt' in the zakat chapter narrowly: only financial claims by other people are deductible. This principle has practical consequences for how you calculate your zakat liability.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Read the Arabic principle: 'Indeed, debt in the chapter of zakat is intended to mean a debt which has a claim from the direction of the servants.' Recognize that 'servants' means people, not Allah.
2
Identify the distinction
Understand that debts are divided into two categories: debts owed to humans (with a claim) and debts owed to Allah (such as unpaid zakat or kaffarah). The Hanafi position only considers the first type for nisab deduction.
3
Apply to a scenario
Consider a person with $10,000 savings. They owe $1,000 to a friend and $2,000 in unpaid zakat from previous years. Per this rule, only the $1,000 human debt reduces the nisab, so their net zakatable wealth is $9,000.
4
Note the effect
If the net wealth after subtracting human debts is below the nisab, no zakat is due. If it is above, zakat is calculated on the net amount.

No Growth Without Ability to Dispose (Zakat Condition)
States a condition for zakat on growth assets.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for zakat on growth assets and apply it to identify whether a specific asset is subject to zakat.

Core Idea
Zakat requires growth; growth requires the owner's power to transact.

This lesson explores a foundational maxim from the Hidayah: 'لا نماء إلا بالقدرة على التصرف' (No growth except with the ability to dispose). In Hanafi fiqh, zakat is only due on assets that have the potential to increase (namā'), and that potential hinges on the owner's freedom to transact. Understanding this maxim helps explain why certain assets—like debts from insolvent debtors or frozen accounts—are not zakatable.

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the maxim
Break the Arabic maxim into two parts: 'لا نماء' (no growth) and 'إلا بالقدرة على التصرف' (except with ability to dispose). Recognize that growth (namā') is not inherent; it depends on the owner's transactional freedom.
2
Link to zakat obligation
In Hanafi fiqh, zakat is wajib on mal that is nāmi (growing). This maxim explicitly ties the existence of growth to the owner's ability to dispose. Therefore, if the owner cannot transact, the asset is not considered growing and zakat is not due.
The presence of ability to dispose is a necessary condition for namā'.
3
Apply to asset types
Consider three cases: trade goods (owner can sell → namā' → zakat due); frozen assets (owner cannot transact → no namā' → no zakat); unpayable debt (creditor cannot collect → no ability to dispose → no namā').
This step internalizes the practical application.
4
Memorize the maxim
Commit the Arabic maxim to memory as a key principle for zakat. It will serve as a quick reference in fiqh discussions and fatwa applications.
The maxim is a memorization anchor for the condition.

Source Text

Source pages p. 9

Commentary
This principle relates to zakat on trade goods and investments. Zakat is due on assets that have growth potential (nama'), but only if the owner has the ability to transact with them freely. If the asset is tied up (e.g., a debt that the debtor is unable to pay), it is not considered growing. Similarly, if the owner is prevented from disposing of it, it may not be subject to zakat. This is a Hanafi point.

Fixed Measures Cannot Be Subject to Qiyas
States a principle about qiyas and fixed measures.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that fixed measures (maqadir) are not subject to qiyas and apply it to examples such as the nisab of zakat and the number of rak'ahs.

Core Idea
Divinely fixed measures are beyond the scope of analogical reasoning.

This lesson presents a fundamental principle in Hanafi fiqh: certain measures prescribed by the Shari'ah—such as the nisab for zakat, the number of rak'ahs in prayer, and the days of fasting—are tawqifiyyah, meaning they are known only from revelation. They cannot be changed or extended through qiyas (analogical reasoning). This protects the divine boundaries from human manipulation.

Guided Path
1
Define 'al-maqadir'
Identify that al-maqadir refer to fixed quantities or measurements established by divine texts, such as the nisab of zakat (20 mithqal of gold, 200 dirhams of silver), the number of rak'ahs in each prayer, and the days of Ramadan.
The term 'maqadir' is plural of 'miqdar' (measure).
2
Understand the limitation of qiyas
Recognize that qiyas cannot be applied to these measures because they are determined by the Lawgiver and are not subject to human reasoning. The principle 'إن المقادير لا يدخلها القياس' explicitly forbids such analogy.
This is a core usul principle agreed upon by the Hanafis.
3
Memorize the Arabic maxim
Commit the maxim to memory: 'إِنَّ الْمَقَادِيرَ لَا يَدْخُلُهَا الْقِيَاسُ'. It is a concise expression of the rule.
Recite it with proper tajweed.
4
Apply to concrete examples
Test the principle by considering attempts to change a fixed measure through analogy. For instance, arguing that the nisab of gold should be higher due to inflation is invalid because the measure is fixed.
The ruling remains the original prescribed amount.

States a ruling about paying value instead of kind in zakat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paying cash value in zakat and apply it to real-life scenarios.

Core Idea
Paying zakat in cash is permissible and valid, not limited to kind.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi ruling: giving the cash equivalent (qimah) of the required zakat item is permissible. This facilitates convenience for the payer and utility for the recipient. Understanding this principle helps apply zakat obligations flexibly in modern contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is paying zakat by substituting the required item with its cash value. The ruling is 'ja'iz' (permissible).
Hanafi school explicitly permits this, while other schools may restrict to in-kind payment.
2
Understand the Scope of Permissibility
The permissibility covers all zakatable items – livestock, produce, gold, silver, trade goods, etc. The cash value must equal the market value of the required item.
It is allowed even when the specific item is available.
3
Recognize the Hanafi Position vs. Others
The Hanafi school relies on the principle that the objective of zakat is fulfilling the need of the poor, which can be achieved through value. The Shafi'i school generally requires in-kind payment.
The practical difference: Hanafis allow cash; Shafi'is require the specific item unless necessity.
4
Apply to a Scenario
If a person owes a sheep for zakat (value $200), he may give $200 cash instead. The cash payment fulfills the zakat obligation.
The same applies to zakat al-fitr: cash is permissible in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Kharāj is the land tax for conquered land owned by non-Muslims; it is more appropriate for them than 'ushr.

This lesson presents a concise maxim from the chapter on zakāt of crops and fruits. It encapsulates the Hanafi distinction between two types of agricultural land taxation: 'ushr (tithe) for Muslims and kharāj (land tax) for non-Muslims. Understanding this maxim helps clarify the legal status of land ownership and tax obligations in the Hanafi school.

This maxim appears in the discussion of zakāt on agricultural produce. It implies that kharāj is levied on lands that are not owned by Muslims, specifically conquered lands left in the hands of non-Muslims.

The two are mutually exclusive: a land is either 'ushr or kharāj.

Ownership transfer does not change classification in the Hanafi school.

Kharāj Relates to Ability to Cultivate
Legal maxim on the obligation of kharāj tax.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for kharāj obligation according to the Hanafi school and apply it to scenarios of land usability.

Core Idea
Kharāj is due when the land can be farmed, not when it is actually farmed.

This short maxim encapsulates a key Hanafi principle regarding land tax. It distinguishes between the capacity to cultivate and actual cultivation, establishing that the tax obligation arises from potential rather than performance. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying kharāj rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim's Core Claim
Read the maxim: 'Kharāj is attached to ability to cultivate.' Note that it links the tax to ability (tamakkun), not to cultivation itself.
Tamakkun means capability or possibility.
2
Define Tamakkun
Tamakkun refers to the owner's ability to cultivate the land – land that is arable, irrigable, and accessible. If the land is barren or unusable, tamakkun is absent.
It is a condition based on the land's potential, not the owner's intention.
3
Derive the Ruling
From the maxim, conclude: if the land is cultivable (tamakkun exists), kharāj is due regardless of whether the owner actually farms it. If the land is uncultivable (e.g., flooded, rocky), kharāj may be waived.
This differs from 'ushr (tithe on actually harvested crops).
4
Distinguish from Common Misunderstanding
Some think kharāj is due only when crops are harvested. But the maxim clarifies it is due upon ability to cultivate, even if the owner leaves the land idle.
The tax attaches to the land's potential, not the owner's action.
5
Apply to a Concrete Scenario
Consider: A person owns kharāj land that is fertile and watered, but chooses not to farm it. Is kharāj due? Answer: Yes, because tamakkun exists.
If the land becomes saline and unusable, tamakkun is lost and kharāj ceases.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Zakat requires complete ownership; complete ownership requires freedom; therefore a slave is not obligated to pay zakat.

In this passage, al-Hidayah establishes a key principle: complete ownership (al-milk al-tamm) is only achieved through freedom (hurriyyah). This principle directly affects the obligation of zakat: among the conditions for zakat is complete ownership of the nisab, and since a slave's ownership is deficient, zakat is not wajib upon him. The author of al-Hidayah says: 'because complete ownership is through freedom'.

Since a slave lacks complete ownership, he does not meet the condition for zakat. Thus, even if he possesses the nisab amount and a year passes, zakat is not obligatory on him.
This ruling is unanimous among the schools, though the reasoning may differ.
5

Consider other cases where ownership is incomplete, e.g., a mudharib (business partner) or a person in debt. The maxim helps determine if zakat is obligatory.
The principle extends beyond slavery to any situation where ownership is not absolute.

Debt in Zakat: Only Human Debts with Claimants Prevent
Refines the definition of 'debt' in zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between debts that prevent zakat (human debts with claimants) and those that do not (devotional debts) and apply the ruling to cases of nadhr and kaffarah.

Core Idea
Only debts owed to people with active claimants block zakat; vows and expiations do not.

We already know that debt prevents zakat because wealth needed for repayment is considered non-existent. But not all debts are equal. This passage from the Hidayah refines that principle: only debts that have an active human claimant (mutalib) prevent zakat. Debts owed purely to Allah, such as a vow (nadhr) or expiation for a broken oath (kaffarah), do not create the same pressing need and thus do not affect the obligation of zakat. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly calculating one's zakat liability.

Guided Path
1
Recall the general rule
Remember that zakat is obligatory only on wealth that is free from debt. The reason is that wealth needed to repay a debt is considered 'occupied' with an original need, like water needed for thirst.
This is based on the principle: 'al-mashghul bi-hajatihi al-asliyyah'.
2
Identify the refinement
The Hidayah specifies that the debt meant here is only debt that has a mutalib (a human claimant demanding repayment). If no human is demanding, the debt does not occupy the wealth in the same way.
The Arabic phrase 'له مطالب من جهة العباد' means 'has a demander from the side of people'.
3
Classify devotional debts
Debts like nadhr (vow to donate an animal if a wish is fulfilled) and kaffarah yamin (expiation for perjury) are owed to Allah. They have no human claimant actively demanding payment.
These are not considered 'dayn' in the sense that prevents zakat.
4
Apply the analogy to water
The author compares devotional debts to water that is needed for thirst but not immediately available. Such water is not counted as owned. Similarly, wealth needed to pay a human debt is excluded, but wealth that only has a devotional liability is still counted for zakat.
The analogy strengthens the distinction: the pressing need comes from a human creditor, not from a divine obligation.
5
Derive the ruling for a scenario
Given a person with $5000 savings and a $1000 vow (nadhr) owed if a condition is met, does the vow prevent zakat? No, because the vow has no human claimant. Zakat is wajib on the full $5000.
The same applies to kaffarah debts.

Primary Need and Its Effect on Zakat Obligation
Explaining the legal principle that debt occupied with primary need prevents zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for zakat obligation regarding debt and explain how the principle of hajat asliyyah leads to the ruling that debt preventing zakat.

Core Idea
Wealth fully encumbered by a primary need is legally non-existent, so no zakat is due.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational Hanafi principle: anything occupied with a primary need is legally considered non-existent. The author of Al-Hidayah applies this to zakat. When a person's entire wealth is matched by debt that is demanded, the wealth is treated as if it does not exist, and thus no zakat is due. We will unpack the proof and the underlying logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and condition
The ruling: zakat is not wajib when wealth is fully encumbered by debt that is demanded. The condition for zakat obligation is freedom from such debt.
This is the key shart (condition) for zakat obligation in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Understand the legal principle
The principle: 'ma shughila bi al-hajat al-asliyyah yu'addanu ma'duman' — what is occupied with a primary need is considered non-existent.
Primary need (hajat asliyyah) includes necessities like food, clothing, and repaying debts.
3
Analyze the proof from Al-Hidayah
The author states: 'wa ishturata faraghahu 'an al-dayn, la'annahu ma'ahu mashghulun bi hajatihi al-asliyyah fa'utubira ma'duman kalmā'i al-mustahaqq bi al-'atsh.' This is an istihsan-based analogy: just as water owed to a thirsty person is considered non-existent for purification, wealth owed as debt is considered non-existent for zakat.
The analogy (qiyas) connects the ruling to a known case.
4
Apply the principle to the scenario
The debtor's wealth is fully occupied with repayment, a primary need. Therefore, it is ma'dum, and no zakat is due.
This is the practical consequence: if wealth equals debt, zakat is not wajib.
5
Note the limitation
The ruling only applies when debt fully encumbers the wealth and is demanded. Partial debt does not prevent zakat on the surplus.
Common error: assuming any debt prevents zakat.

Zakat on Mal al-Dimmar: Why Inaccessible Wealth Incurs No Past Zakat
Explaining the principle that no growth without disposition ability, applied to mal al-dimmar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that no zakat is due on mal al-dimmar for past years and explain the condition of disposition ability (qudrat al-tasrruf) for zakat obligation.

Core Idea
Zakat requires wealth to be ‘growing’ (nama) – inaccessible wealth (mal al-dimmar) lacks this, so no past zakat.

The Hanafi school establishes that zakat is due only on wealth that has the potential for growth (nama). This potential, in turn, depends on the owner's ability to freely dispose of the wealth. This lesson examines how this principle applies to mal al-dimmar – wealth that is absent and not expected to be recovered – and why no zakat is owed for the years it was inaccessible, even after recovery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? Here, it is the obligation of zakat on mal al-dimmar (inaccessible wealth) for past years. The Hanafi rule: zakat is not wajib for those years.
The ruling is a negative obligation (non-obligation) for past years; for the current year of recovery, zakat is due if conditions are met.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
The author cites a saying of Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA): 'La zakata fi al-mal al-dimmar' (No zakat on mal al-dimmar). This is reported as both a mawqoof (stopping at Ali) and marfoo' (attributed to the Prophet) narration.
This hadith is the primary textual proof for the Hanafi position.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof demonstrates the ruling)
The explicit negation of zakat on mal al-dimmar directly indicates that such wealth is not subject to zakat. The linguistic root – dimmar meaning emaciated or hidden – reinforces that this wealth cannot be benefited from, thus lacks growth potential.
The Hanafi principle that 'nama requires ability to dispose' is not explicit in the hadith but derived through reasoning.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement with other schools)
Some scholars, including Imam al-Shafi'i, may hold that once mal al-dimmar is recovered, zakat is due for all past years. The Hanafis disagree because the wealth during those years lacked the growth potential necessary for zakat.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether the past years' zakat is forgiven or not.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih (preferred position)
The Hanafi position is well-established in Zahir al-Riwayah, relying on the hadith of Ali (RA) and the principle of nama. This is the operative ruling (mufta bihi) for fatwa in the Hanafi school.
The authority weight: zahir_riwayah; cross-madhhab status: mukhtalaf fih.

Learning Objective

A judge's bankruptcy ruling does not automatically remove the zakat obligation according to Abu Hanifa.

This lesson examines a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school: does a qadi's official declaration of bankruptcy (taflis al-qadi) affect the creditor's obligation to pay zakat on the debt? Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani reach opposite conclusions based on different assessments of the debtor's financial reality. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply zakat rules to modern financial situations involving insolvency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The core question: Does a qadi's declaration of bankruptcy (taflis) make the debt like lost property, so that zakat is no longer owed on it?

2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa says taflis is invalid for zakat because 'wealth comes and goes' (al-malu ghadin wa ra'ih). The debtor's financial status may improve, so the debt remains potentially recoverable and zakat is still wajib.

Muhammad says taflis makes the debtor like lost wealth (mal nawi); the debt becomes like a lost asset, so no zakat is due until actual recovery.

Under Abu Hanifa, the creditor must pay zakat yearly on the debt. Under Muhammad, he pays nothing until the debt is actually collected.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative position)
Later Hanafi works (e.g., al-Hidaya) adopt Abu Hanifa's view. Fatwa is on his position because the qadi's declaration is not definitive and human circumstances change.

Core Idea
Intention alone suffices for abstentions but must be connected to physical acts.

In Hanafi fiqh, the role of intention (niyyah) is critical but nuanced. This lesson presents a foundational principle from Al-Hidayah: intention is valid only when it is temporally connected to the action. The commentary from Fath al-Qadir clarifies that this rule applies differently to physical acts versus abstentions, with profound implications for transactions, worship, and personal status.

Al-Jibayah bi al-Himayah: Tax is Contingent on Protection
Stating a legal maxim with its application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'al-jibayah bi al-himayah' and apply it to determine when tax obligation lapses due to absence of protection.

Core Idea
Tax is due for protection — if the ruler fails to protect, the tax obligation lapses.

This lesson explores a foundational Hanafi maxim that ties the ruler's right to collect taxes to his duty of providing security. The text from Al-Hidayah states the maxim and applies it to a concrete scenario: if rebels confiscate kharaj from subjects, can a just ruler collect it again after defeating the rebels? The answer hinges on whether protection was provided at the time of collection. Understanding this maxim helps clarify the conditional nature of tax obligations in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maxim
Locate the phrase 'al-jibayah bi al-himayah' and understand its literal meaning: tax collection is justified by protection.
2
Understand the Condition
The maxim implies that the ruler's protection is a necessary condition for the obligation of tax. Without it, the obligation lapses.
Protection here means effective security provided by the ruler.
3
Analyze the Application
The text presents a case where rebels forcibly collect kharaj. Since the just imam did not protect the subjects at that time, the condition for tax is not met.
Rebels are explicitly mentioned as khawarij or baghat.
4
Derive the Ruling
Because protection was absent, the imam cannot collect kharaj again from the same subjects for that period. The tax already taken by rebels counts as payment in lieu of protection (which was not provided by the imam).
The ruling is bilateral: no second collection.
5
Generalize the Principle
This maxim is not limited to kharaj or rebels; it applies broadly to any tax (including zakat) where the ruler fails to provide security. Taxation is a reciprocal obligation.
Modern implications: tax waiver in areas where state fails to provide basic security.

Value in Non-Fungibles and Fungibles: The Hanafi Principle
Definition of property categories and ruling on ushr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for value in non-fungible versus fungible property and apply it to determine whether ushr is taken on wine or pig from a dhimmi.

Core Idea
Value in non-fungibles takes the ruling of the item itself; in fungibles, it does not.

This lesson presents a foundational Hanafi principle regarding the legal status of value when compensating or taxing non-fungible versus fungible property. The distinction is crucial for understanding rulings such as whether ushr (a tithe) is taken on items brought by a non-Muslim trader. By mastering this principle, you will be able to classify property correctly and apply the correct ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two property categories
First, understand the two categories: dhawāt al-qiyam (non-fungibles) are items with no identical substitute; dhawāt al-amthāl (fungibles) are items that can be exactly matched, like grains or oil.
This classification is specific to Hanafi fiqh.
2
State the legal principle for each
For non-fungibles, value has the same ruling as the specific item (qīma lahu hukm al-'ayn). For fungibles, value does not have that ruling.
This principle is from Zāhir al-Riwāyah.
3
Apply to the case of wine and pig
Pig is non-fungible, so taking its value is like taking the pig itself, which is not permissible property. Wine is fungible, so taking value is not taking wine; thus ushr is taken on wine but not on pig.
The text also notes a secondary reasoning regarding protection (himāya).
4
Understand the additional reasoning
The author adds that a Muslim protects his own wine for making vinegar, so he would protect it for others; but he does not protect pork, so it is not protected for others either.
This reinforces the main principle.
5
Note the authority weight
Al-Hidayah presents this as the apparent (zāhir) view, indicating it is from the core transmissions (Zāhir al-Riwāyah).
This is the muftā bihī (operative) position.

Explains the legal principle that value takes the ruling of the actual item in some categories but not in others.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'ushr for wine and swine brought by a dhimmi, and explain the distinction between dhawat al-qiyam and dhawat al-amthal that determines it.

Core Idea
Value in dhawat al-qiyam inherits the ruling of the item; in dhawat al-amthal it does not — so 'ushr is taken on wine but not on swine.

This passage from the Hidayah commentary tackles a specific question: when a dhimmi trader passes through Muslim lands with wine and swine, why is 'ushr collected on wine but not on swine? The answer reveals a deeper principle about how value relates to substance in Hanafi property law. By understanding this distinction, you will see how the school classifies goods and applies the concept of mal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of goods
Recognize that Hanafi law divides property into dhawat al-qiyam (items of value, like unique assets) and dhawat al-amthal (fungible items, like grains and liquids).
The determination is based on whether the item is measured by value or by like.
2

In dhawat al-qiyam, taking the value (qimah) is equivalent to taking the actual item. In dhawat al-amthal, taking the value is not equivalent.
This principle is the basis for the different rulings.

Categorize wine and swine
Swine (khinzir) is classified as dhawat al-qiyam because it is unique and not fungible. Wine (khamr) is dhawat al-amthal because it is a fungible liquid.
Impurity is not the deciding factor.

Since swine is dhawat al-qiyam, taking its value would be like taking swine itself, which is not mal, so no 'ushr. Wine is dhawat al-amthal, so its value can be taken as 'ushr. Additionally, the collector protects wine for vinegar production but does not protect swine.
The himayah (protection) rationale reinforces the ruling.

Mines in Conquered Lands as Ghanimah: Khums Obligation
Principle that conquered mines are ghanimah, subject to khums.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khums for minerals from conquered lands and apply the principle that such minerals are treated as ghanimah.

Core Idea
Mines in conquered lands are ghanimah: khums is wajib regardless of land type.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a key principle: anything that comes into Muslim possession through conquest from disbelievers is ghanimah. The author then applies this to mineral deposits. Understanding this principle helps you see how the Hanafi school extends the spoils-of-war framework to natural resources like gold and silver mines, making khums obligatory even on non-movable assets.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is the extraction of minerals (gold, silver, iron, lead, brass) from mines located in lands that were previously under the control of disbelievers and then conquered by Muslims. The ruling is that khums (one-fifth) is wajib on the extracted minerals.
The ruling applies to both 'ushri and kharaji lands—the key factor is the land's former status.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is not a direct text from Quran or hadith in this passage but is derived through qiyas. The general principle that 'whatever is in the hands of the disbelievers and our hands find it becomes ghanimah' is based on the established rulings for movable spoils. The author applies this principle to mines by analogy.
The hadith on rikaz (buried treasure) is often cited in other contexts, but here the focus is the broader qiyas.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The analogical link (illa) is prior possession by disbelievers followed by Muslim conquest through force. Just as movable spoils become ghanimah, so too do immovable assets like mineral deposits that are found in that conquered land. The illa is the change in control through conquest.
The illa is explicit: the land was in the hands of disbelievers, and Muslims gained possession by overcoming them.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars (e.g., Shafi'i) may restrict ghanimah to movable property only, arguing that land and its deposits are fay' (subject to kharaj/ushr) rather than ghanimah. The Hanafi school takes a broader view, treating all assets, including mines, as ghanimah if acquired by force.
This ikhtilaf is reflected in the cross-madhhab status: mukhtalaf fih.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view within the school (mufta bihi). The reasoning is that the land itself becomes ghanimah, and everything attached to it or extracted from it follows the same ruling. This is considered the stronger position due to its consistency with the principle of completeness in spoils.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah, especially from the chapter on rikaz in Al-Mabsut.

The Part Does Not Oppose the Whole: Mine in a House No Khums
Applies legal maxim to exempt house mine from khums.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-juz' la yukhalif al-jumlah' to determine the ruling on a mine found within a private residence.

Core Idea
A mine in a house shares the house's exemption from khums because the part follows the whole.

This passage introduces a powerful legal maxim in Hanafi fiqh: the part does not oppose the whole. Using the case of a mine discovered in one's home, Imam Abu Hanifa demonstrates how the ruling of the whole (the house) extends to its part (the mine), exempting it from the khums tax. Understanding this maxim helps you see how general principles can produce specific exceptions.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Identify the Arabic maxim 'al-juz' la yukhalif al-jumlah' and its meaning: the part does not oppose the whole; the ruling of the whole applies to its parts.
This is a general principle in Hanafi usul.
2
Identify the case and ruling
The case: a mine (e.g., gold, silver, iron) is found within the land of a house. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, no khums is due on this mine.
This is an exception to the general rule that khums is due on mines in 'ushri or kharaji land.
3
Apply the maxim
The mine is a part (juz') of the house's land. Since the house as a whole is not subject to khums (or pre-Islamic taxes like shams), the part shares that exemption.
The reasoning is that the part cannot oppose the whole.
4
Contrast with the general rule
The general rule (from the matn) states khums is due on mines based on the hadith 'wa fi al-rikaz al-khumus'. The house exception does not apply to mines in conquered or kharaji land.
The house is private property not conquered, so the ghanimah analogy does not hold.
5
Distinguish from buried treasure
Buried treasure (kanz/defin) is not part of the land; it was buried separately, so the maxim does not apply and khums is due.
This clarifies the boundary of the maxim.

Commentary
This passage combines a ruling and its proof. First, it introduces the legal maxim 'al-juz' la yukhalif al-jumlah' (the part does not oppose the whole). This is a general principle in Hanafi usul. The distinction between constructive (hukmi) and actual (haqiqi) possession clarifies that khums applies even though the finder has actual possession. Khums Not Obligatory on Sea-Extracted Items
Explaining the principle that ghanimah requires force, applied to sea products.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that defines ghanimah by forcible conquest and apply it to determine that sea-extracted items do not incur khums.

Core Idea
Khums is wajib on ghanimah; ghanimah requires force — sea goods are not conquered.

This lesson explains a key Hanafi principle: for an item to be considered ghanimah (booty) and thus subject to khums, it must be taken from disbelievers by forcible conquest (qahr). The author of Al-Hidayah applies this to sea products: since the sea floor was never forcibly conquered, pearls, ambergris, and sea minerals are not ghanimah, and no khums is due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The passage states that khums is not obligatory on items extracted from the sea. Trace the reasoning: it depends on the definition of ghanimah.
The ruling is negative (no obligation).
2
Understand the definition of ghanimah
Ghanimah in Hanafi fiqh is property captured from disbelievers through military force. The key element is qahr (forcible conquest).
3
Apply the definition to sea items
Pearls, ambergris, and sea minerals are not extracted from conquered land; they are taken from the sea, which was never forcibly occupied by Muslims. Hence they are not ghanimah.
The sea floor is analogized to unconquered land.
4
Derive the ruling on khums
Since khums is only wajib on ghanimah, and sea items are not ghanimah, no khums is due on them. This is the Hanafi position.
The ruling is consistent across the school (zahir al-riwayah).
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools may consider sea treasures as rikaz (buried treasure) subject to khums, but the Hanafi school distinguishes based on the absence of force.
Awareness of ikhtilaf is useful, but this lesson focuses on Hanafi view.

Kharaj is More Suitable for the Disbeliever
Stating the principle that kharaj is appropriate for a disbeliever's land.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the tax imposed on a dhimmi who purchases 'ushr land and explain the rationale based on the principle that kharaj is more suitable for a disbeliever.

Core Idea
Land tax follows the payer: a disbeliever pays kharaj, not 'ushr, because 'ushr is worship.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi principle: when a Muslim sells 'ushr land to a non-Muslim, the land ceases to be 'ushr land and becomes kharaj land. The rationale is that 'ushr is an act of worship, and a non-Muslim is not qualified to perform worship. Kharaj, being a punitive land tax, is more appropriate. This ruling illustrates how Hanafi fiqh adapts tax obligations based on the religious status of the landowner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
Determine the transaction: a Muslim sells his 'ushr land to a dhimmi (non-Muslim citizen). This triggers a change in the land's tax category.
The land was originally subject to 'ushr (tithe) because the owner was Muslim.
2
State the ruling according to Imam Abu Hanifah
The dhimmi buyer must pay kharaj on the land, not 'ushr. The land is now considered kharaj land.
This is the view attributed to Imam Abu Hanifah in al-Hidayah.
3
Identify the governing principle
The principle: 'Kharaj is more suitable to the state of the disbeliever.' This means kharaj is the appropriate tax for a non-Muslim landowner.
The suitability is based on the nature of the tax.
4
Explain the talil (justification)
'Ushr is a form of worship (ibadah), requiring intention (niyyah). A disbeliever is not capable of performing worship, so 'ushr cannot be imposed on him. Kharaj, on the other hand, is a land tax without worship elements, and is also a form of humiliation or punishment for disbelief.
The talil connects the ruling to the foundation of 'ushr as worship.
5
Note the authority and school position
This ruling is from Imam Abu Hanifah and is relied upon in the Hanafi school. It is found in al-Hidayah and is considered mufta bihi.
Other schools may differ on the effect of sale on land tax classification.

Kharaj on Potential: The Hanafi Principle of Land Taxation
Ruling statement: kharaj attached to capacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that kharaj is due on the land's potential for agriculture and apply it to determine when kharaj is obligatory.

Core Idea
Kharaj is owed on land’s capacity, not its actual yield — the potential matters.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi principle from Al-Hidayah: kharaj (land tax) is attached to the land's ability to be cultivated, not to the actual harvest. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly assessing tax liability on agricultural land. The principle prevents landowners from avoiding kharaj by simply leaving land fallow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Recognize that kharaj is a wajib (obligatory) tax on certain agricultural land. The ruling in question is that kharaj is due even if no cultivation occurs.
The hukm level is wajib, not fard, as it is a financial obligation established by ijtihad.
2
Understand the Basis: Bil-Quwwah vs Bil-Fi'l
The principle hinges on the concept of 'potential' (bil-quwwah) versus 'actual' (bil-fi'l). Kharaj is linked to the land's inherent capacity for agriculture, not the actual act of farming.
This distinction is common in Hanafi usul; it appears in other rulings like zakat on livestock.
3
Apply the Principle to a Scenario
Consider a person who owns fertile kharaj land but does not cultivate it. According to this principle, kharaj is still due because the land has the potential to produce.
This prevents manipulation by leaving land idle to avoid tax.
4
Recognize the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Some other schools tie kharaj to actual produce. In contrast, the Hanafi school makes potential the decisive factor, which is a more stringent position for the landowner.
The practical consequence: Hanafi law encourages cultivation by maintaining the tax obligation regardless.

Core Idea
Kharaj is due on land capable of cultivation, even if left barren — the potential, not the produce, triggers the tax.

This lesson examines a key principle from Al-Hidayah regarding the land tax (kharaj). The text establishes that kharaj attaches to the land's potential for cultivation, not to actual farming. This principle has practical implications, especially when the land contains mineral resources or is left fallow. We will explore the reasoning and the distinction from 'ushr (tithe).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling

2
Understand the evidence
The author provides a logical reasoning: 'Because kharaj relates to the potential for cultivation.' This is a dalil aqli (rational proof). Kharaj is a land tax compensating the state for the land's productive capacity, similar to rent.
3
Apply to mineral resource case
Consider a spring of tar or naphtha on kharaj land. The protected area (harim) around it that is suitable for cultivation is subject to kharaj, because the potential for cultivation exists there.
4
Contrast with 'ushr land
If the harim were 'ushr land (owned by a Muslim), 'ushr is only due if it is actually cultivated. If left uncultivated, no tax is due. This highlights the difference: kharaj is on potential, 'ushr is on produce.
5
Conclude the thamarah
The practical consequence: a landowner cannot avoid kharaj by leaving fertile land barren. The obligation stands as long as the land is capable of cultivation.
