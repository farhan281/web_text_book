The Principle That Means to an Obligation Become Obligatory
States the usul principle that means to an obligation are obligatory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that whatever an obligation depends on for its completion becomes obligatory to determine whether an act is fard or not, and identify the Hanafi-Sahibayn dispute over exiting prayer.

Core Idea
Whatever an obligation depends on for its completion becomes obligatory.

This lesson introduces a foundational usul principle that governs how obligations are determined. The principle states that if an obligatory act cannot be completed without another act, that other act also becomes obligatory. The author of al-Hidayah uses this principle to support Imam Abu Hanifa's view that exiting prayer by one's own action is fard, because the prayer is not considered complete without it. The Sahibayn disagree, highlighting a key methodological difference.

Guided Path
1
State the principle in Arabic and English
Memorize the exact wording: 'إن ما يتوكل إلى الفرض إلا به يكون فرضا' (or the more common 'ما لا يتم الواجب إلا به فهو واجب'). Understand its meaning: any act without which an obligation cannot be fulfilled becomes obligatory itself.
This principle is also known as 'ma la yatimmu al-wajibu illa bihi fahuwa wajib'.
2
Identify the source and context
Note that al-Hidayah cites this principle to argue that exiting the prayer by one's own action is fard according to Abu Hanifa. Without this principle, the ruling would not be derived.
The principle is not a Quranic text but a rational tool (istidlal) used by Hanafi scholars.
3
Apply the principle to the case of exiting prayer
The obligation is to complete the prayer. Exiting the prayer by one's own action (e.g., saying salam) is the act that completes it. Therefore, according to the principle, exiting becomes fard.
The Sahibayn reject this because they do not consider the principle applicable to internal acts of prayer.
4
Understand the dispute with Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa uses the principle to make exiting fard. Sahibayn argue that exiting is not fard because the prayer is completed by the intention to exit, not the physical action. This shows a difference in how the principle is applied.
The practical difference: if one forgets to say salam, Abu Hanifa requires a sajda sahwa; Sahibayn do not.
5
Recognize the principle's broader applicability
This principle applies not only to prayer but to many obligations. For example, wudu becomes fard for prayer because prayer cannot be performed without wudu. Similarly, covering the awrah for prayer, etc.
It is a key tool in deriving obligations from primary obligations.

Worship is Lifted from Children
States the principle that children are not obligated to perform worship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that children are not obligated to perform worship and explain its basis in legal capacity.

Core Idea
Children are not obligated to worship because they lack legal capacity to bear obligations.

This passage presents a foundational maxim in Hanafi fiqh: all acts of worship are lifted from children. This does not mean children cannot worship—it means worship is not legally required of them. Understanding this principle clarifies the boundary of obligation in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its scope
Read the Arabic phrase: 'إِنَّ الْعِبَادَاتِ بِأَسْرِهَا مَوْضُوعَةٌ عَنِ الصِّبْيَانِ' – all acts of worship are lifted from children. Note the word 'بِأَسْرِهَا' meaning 'all' – this covers every type of worship, not just specific ones.
2
Connect to the underlying cause
Why are they lifted? Because children lack full legal capacity (ahliyyah) to bear obligations. This is based on the hadith: 'The pen is lifted from three: the sleeping, the child until he reaches puberty, and the insane.'

Qat'i Obligations Require Qat'i Methods
States a usul principle with two fiqh applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a qat'i obligation (fard) cannot be fulfilled through a method established only by a solitary hadith (khabar wahid), using the examples of facing the Ka'bah and wiping the head in wudu.

Core Idea
A qat'i obligation requires a qat'i method; a zanni proof cannot alter how it is performed.

This lesson introduces a foundational usul al-fiqh principle from the Hanafi school: what is established as obligatory by a definitive text (the Quran or mutawatir sunnah) must be performed according to that definitive method, not according to a method proven only by a solitary hadith. The author of al-Hidayah uses two practical examples to illustrate this: the obligation to face the Ka'bah in prayer, and the obligation to wipe the head in wudu. Understanding this principle is crucial for correctly applying rulings and avoiding common mistakes in ibadah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the opening statement: 'Indeed, that whose obligation is established by the text of the Book... will not be performed as established by a solitary report.' Recognize that this sets a hierarchy: qat'i text (Quran) over zanni text (khabar wahid).
The principle applies even if the khabar wahid is authentic (sahih) — its transmission remains zanni.
2
Understand the epistemological distinction
The Quranic text (nass) is qat'i al-thubut wa al-dalalah (definitive in transmission and meaning). A khabar wahid is zanni al-thubut (probable transmission). In case of apparent conflict, the qat'i prevails.
This is a core Hanafi usul position; other schools may differ.
3
Apply to the Hatim example
Facing the Ka'bah is fard from the Quran (2:144). A solitary hadith states that the Hatim (semicircular wall) is part of the Ka'bah. However, praying facing only the Hatim is invalid because the qat'i obligation requires facing the entire Ka'bah as known from qat'i sources, not its zanni extension.
For tawaf, caution dictates including the Hatim, but not for the prayer obligation.
4
Apply to the ear-wiping example
Wiping the head (mash al-ra's) is fard from the Quran (5:6). A hadith says the ears are part of the head. Wiping only the ears without the crown of the head does not fulfill the fard, because the fard is for the head as established by qat'i text.
The ears may be wiped as sunnah after fulfilling the fard on the head.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) allow a khabar wahid to specify a general Quranic text (takhsis al-kitab bi al-khabar). The Hanafi school rejects this for definitive obligations, maintaining that the method of fulfillment must also be qat'i.
This does not mean the other school is wrong; it is a valid usul disagreement.

The Permanence of Rulings Beyond Their Causes
States a principle about the persistence of legal rulings after their cause ends

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'a ruling does not cease with the cessation of its cause' and apply it to distinguish between qat'i and zanni evidences.

Core Idea
A ruling established by definitive text persists even when its cause becomes uncertain.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim: that a ruling (hukm) does not automatically expire when its immediate cause (sabab) is no longer present. The maxim governs many areas of fiqh, particularly where primary obligations (like prayer or purification) are anchored in clear texts, even if supporting details are established through weaker evidence. We will examine the maxim's meaning through classical examples.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Begin by memorizing the Arabic formulation: 'إن الحكم لا يزول بزوال السبب'. Understand that 'hukm' is the legal ruling and 'sabab' is the cause that triggers it.
The maxim is a principle, not a specific fiqh ruling.
2
Recognize the example's structure
The text gives two parallel examples: (1) wiping the head (obligation from Quran) and ears being part of head (from ahad report); (2) facing the Ka'bah (obligation from Quran) and the Hatim being part of Ka'bah (from ahad report). In both, the obligation is qat'i, the detail is zanni.
OCR note: 'النَّيِّ' should be 'النَّصِّ' (the text); 'الْعَظِيمَ' should be 'الْحَظِيمَ' (the Hatim).
3
Analyze the application
Even if the cause (e.g., the ears being definitely part of the head) is uncertain, the ruling (obligation to wipe the head) remains binding because it is based on the Quran, not on that detail. The zanni detail cannot override the qat'i obligation.
This illustrates that the ruling persists independently of its perceived cause.
4
Apply the maxim to a new scenario
Consider the obligation of ghusl after janabah. The cause is seminal emission (or equivalent). If one is uncertain whether the cause occurred, the obligation remains until certainty is reached. The ruling does not disappear with doubt about the cause.
This extends the maxim beyond the given examples.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Custom trumps literal meaning: what people commonly understand governs, not dictionary definitions.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i over the scope of the word 'dog' (kalb) in oaths and contracts. The Hanafi school argues that customary usage, not linguistic extension, determines legal application. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly judging obligations in everyday transactions and oaths.

Commentary
This passage presents a fundamental legal principle from Hanafi usul: customary usage ('urf) takes precedence over literal linguistic meaning (haqiqah lughawiyyah). The context is a dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the word 'kalb' (dog). Imam al-Shafi'i argued that the word includes beasts of prey based on linguistic extension. The Hanafi school, through the author of al-Hidayah, counters that in common custom, 'kalb' does not refer to wild beasts; it refers specifically to the domestic dog. Since custom is what people commonly understand, it is stronger evidence. An illustrative case: if someone swears not to eat a 'head' and then eats a sparrow's head, the oath is not broken because in custom 'head' typically refers to larger animals, not birds. Thus, customary meaning governs legal obligations even if literal meaning would include the object.
