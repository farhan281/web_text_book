Core Idea
The disposition of an interdicted person is legally void — incapacity nullifies action.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi fiqh: the actions of someone under legal interdiction (maḥjūr) carry no legal consequence. This protects vulnerable individuals from exploitation. We will examine the meaning of 'maḥjūr' and the precise ruling on their transactions.

Guided Path

This ruling safeguards the interdicted person from harmful transactions.

Value in Goods of Value: The Maxim on Damān for Unique vs Fungible Goods
Legal maxim on compensation for damaged goods.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on compensating damaged goods, distinguish between qimī (unique) and mithlī (fungible) goods, and apply the appropriate compensation rule.

Core Idea
Unique goods demand value compensation; fungible goods demand like-kind compensation.

This lesson presents a foundational maxim in Hanafi fiqh of transactions and torts: the distinction between goods of value (dhawāt al-qiyam) and fungible goods (dhawāt al-amthāl). Understanding this maxim is essential for correctly determining how compensation (ḍamān) is calculated when goods are damaged or destroyed. The maxim governs whether the victim must accept the exact item back (or its value) or whether they can be compelled to accept similar items.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Learn the definitions of dhawāt al-qiyam (unique, non-fungible goods) and dhawāt al-amthāl (fungible goods with standard equivalents).
Examples: a specific painting vs. a bushel of wheat.
2
Understand the ruling for qimī goods
For unique goods, the value has the ruling of the specific item (ḥukm al-ʿayn). The owner cannot be forced to accept a substitute; compensation is in market value.
This protects owners of unique items from being forced into a replacement they may not value equally.
3
Understand the ruling for mithlī goods
For fungible goods, the value does not have the ruling of the specific item. Compensation is in kind (mithl), i.e., similar goods, not monetary value.
The obligation is to return the like, not the value, unless like goods are unavailable.
4
Apply to real scenarios
Given a damaged good, determine its category and state the correct compensation according to the maxim.
Practice with examples to solidify the distinction.

Source Text

Commentary
This maxim distinguishes between two types of property: goods of value (dhawāt al-qiyam) and fungible goods (dhawāt al-amthāl). For goods that are unique (e.g., a specific painting), if damaged, the compensation (value) is treated like the specific item itself — meaning the owner cannot be forced to accept a different item. For fungible goods (e.g., wheat), the compensation is not tied to the specific item; the owner must accept like items.

ذَوَاتِ الْأَمْثَالِ (dhawāt al-amthāl): Fungible goods that can be replaced by similar items.

4

Misclassification can invalidate a transaction.

ذوات القيم (dhawat al-qiyam): Non-fungible items whose value is unique (e.g., real estate, animals); compensation is by market value.
ذوات الأمثال (dhawat al-amthal): Fungible items that have equivalents (e.g., grains, money); compensation is by the same quantity/type.
القيمة (al-qima): Market value or monetary compensation when a non-fungible item is lost or damaged.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi classification of property into non-fungibles and fungibles determines whether compensation is by market value or by the same kind of goods.
