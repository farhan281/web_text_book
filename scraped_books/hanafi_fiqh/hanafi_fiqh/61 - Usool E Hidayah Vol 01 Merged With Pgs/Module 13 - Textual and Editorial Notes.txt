Hanafi Legal Maxims from Hidayah (212-228)
Listing of 17 Hanafi legal maxims from Hidayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recall 17 Hanafi legal maxims from the Hidayah and apply them to practical fiqh scenarios.

Core Idea
Legal maxims are concise principles that encapsulate the spirit of Hanafi law and guide rulings across chapters.

This lesson presents 17 foundational legal maxims (qawa'id fiqhiyyah) drawn from the Hidayah. These maxims serve as memory anchors for the Hanafi student, encapsulating broad principles that recur across various chapters. Understanding each maxim's core idea will enable you to derive rulings in new cases consistently.

Guided Path
1
Group maxims by thematic focus
Scan the 17 maxims and sort them into categories such as liability (212, 216, 217, 221, 222), worship (213, 214, 218, 225, 226, 227, 228), custom (219), and proxy (220, 224). This helps see the principles behind the rules.
Not all maxims fit neatly; some may overlap.
2
Memorize the Arabic phrasing
Each maxim begins with 'Inna' and follows a pattern. Repetition and recitation will solidify the wording. Focus on key terms like 'al-ma'tham', 'al-hukm', 'al-urf', 'al-muhrim'.
Use the list as a daily recitation drill.
3
Understand the fiqh implication
For each maxim, ask: 'What practical legal effect does this have?' For example, maxim 212 means that actions under duress still incur worldly liabilities, but not sin. Identify the chapter where each maxim is derived from (e.g., 220 from Hajj).
The Hidayah often places these maxims at the end of chapters as summaries.
4
Apply to a new scenario
Take a simple case and test which maxim governs. For instance, a hunter uses a trick to catch an animal: maxim 213 ('hunting is that which cannot be taken except by a trick') justifies the method.
Start with one maxim per case to isolate the rule.

The Two Types of Gha'yah: Exclusionary vs. Affirmatory Limits
Explains two types of gha'yah with legal examples and an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between gha'yah isqat and gha'yah ithbat, and apply this distinction to derive the Hanafi rulings on washing the elbows in wudu and the timing of fasting.

Core Idea
A limit can either exclude or extend: the elbows are washed but the night extends the fast without being part of it.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies a key Hanafi principle: how legal limits (gha'yah) operate in Quranic verses. The verses on wudu and fasting both use 'ila' (until), but their legal effects differ. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly deriving rulings from similar texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two Quranic verses
Locate the verses: wudu (5:6) 'wash your hands up to the elbows' and fasting (2:187) 'complete the fast until the night'. Note the common word 'ila'.
2
Understand Zufar's analogy
Zufar argued that just as night is not part of fasting, the elbows should not be part of washing. Recognize the analogical reasoning.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis distinguish between gha'yah isqat (exclusionary) and gha'yah ithbat (affirmatory). In wudu, the limit excludes the elbow from the washing obligation? Actually, it sets the boundary beyond which washing is not required, but the elbow itself is included. In fasting, the limit extends the ruling to nightfall without including night in the fast.
4
Apply the distinction
For wudu: the hand is washed up to and including the elbows. For fasting: the fast must be completed until nightfall; night is not a part of the fast.
5
Note the practical consequence
The dispute between Zufar and the majority Hanafi position affects whether the elbows are washed. The mufta bihi is that they are washed.

ہوتی تو ہاتھ مکمل کندھے تک دھویا جاتا ، اس غایہ نے مرفق" کے علاوہ ساقط کر دیا۔ اس کے برخلاف أَتِمُّوا القِيَامَ إِلَى اللَّيْلِ " میں "دلیل" چونکہ صوم" کی جنس میں سے نہیں ہے اس لئے لیل روزے کے حکم میں داخل نہیں ہوگی۔ یہ غایہ غایت اثبات ہے اور غایت اثبات مغیا کے حکم میں داخل نہیں ہوتی۔ البتہ لیل والی غایہ کا فائدہ یہ ہوگا کہ وہ حکم صوم کو اپنے تک کھینچ لائے گی ، یعنی رات صوم میں داخل نہیں ہوگی مگر صوم رات تک ہی ہوگا، رات آنے سے پہلے روزہ مکمل نہیں ہو گا۔ لغت کے اعتبار سے صوم" کا اطلاق تھوڑی دیر کھانے ، پینے اور جماع سے رکنے پر بھی ہوتا ہے، تو یہ غایہ مد حکم کے لئے ہے کہ اس نے بتلایا کہ روزہ وہ شرعاً معتبر ہے جو دلیل تک ہو۔
قَالَ زُفَرُ رَحِمَهُ اللَّهُ تَعَالَى لَا يَدْخُلَانِ؛ لِأَنَّ الْغَايَةَ لَا تَدْخُلُ تَحْتَ الْمُغَيَّا كَاللَّيْلِ فِي الصَّوْمِ قُلْنَا نَعَمْ لَكِنْ الْمَرَافِقُ وَالْكَعْبَانِ غَايَةُ إِسْقَاطِ فَلَا يَدْخُلَانِ فِي الْإِسْقَاطِ؛ لِأَنَّ قَوْلَهُ (وَأَيْدِيَكُمْ) (المائدة: ٢) يَتَنَاوَلُ كُلَّ الْأَيْدِى إِلَى الْمَنَاكِبِ فَلَمَّا قَالَ إِلَى الْمَرَافِقِ خَرَجَ مِنْ أَنْ يَكُونَ دَاخِلاَ تَحْتَ السُّقُوطِ ، لأَنَّ الْحَدَّ لَا يَدْخُلُ فِى الْمَحْدُودِ فَبَقِيَ الْغَسْلُ ثَابِتًا فِي الْيَدِ مَعَ الْمَرَافِقِ وَفِي بَابِ الصَّوْمِ لَيْسَتْ الْغَايَةُ غَايَةَ إِسْقَاطِ وَإِنَّمَا هِيَ غَايَةُ امْتِدَادِ الْحُكُم إِلَيْهَا؛ لأنَّ الصَّوْمَ يُطْلَقُ عَلَى الْإِمْسَاكِ سَاعَةً فَهِيَ غَايَةُ إِثْبَاتٍ لَا غَايَةُ إِسْقَاطِ.
If it were so, the entire hand up to the shoulder would be washed; this limit (gha'yah) excluded except the elbows. In contrast, in "Complete the fast until the night" (Quran 2:187), since the "evidence" (layl) is not of the same genus as "sawm", the night does not enter the ruling of fasting. This gha'yah is a gha'yah ithbat (limit of affirmation), and the gha'yah ithbat does not enter the ruling of the limited (mughayya). However, the benefit of the night gha'yah is that it draws the ruling of fasting to itself, meaning the night is not included in fasting but fasting lasts until the night; the fast is not complete before night comes. Linguistically, "sawm" applies to abstaining for a short while from eating, drinking, and intercourse; so this gha'yah is for extending the ruling, indicating that the fast valid in Shari'ah is the one that lasts until the evidence (night).
Zufar (may Allah have mercy on him) said: They (the elbows and ankles) are not included because the limit does not enter under the limited, like the night in fasting. We said: Yes, but the elbows and ankles are a limit of exclusion (gha'yah isqat), so they are not included in the exclusion; because His statement ("And your hands" [Quran 5:6]) encompasses all hands up to the shoulders; when He said "up to the elbows", the elbow was excluded from being included in the exclusion, because the limit does not enter into the limited. Thus washing remains established for the hand along with the elbows. In the chapter of fasting, the limit is not a limit of exclusion; rather it is a limit for the extension of the ruling to it, because fasting applies to abstaining for an hour; so it is a limit of affirmation, not a limit of exclusion.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal principle of gha'yah (limit). The author explains two types of gha'yah: gha'yah isqat (exclusionary limit) and gha'yah ithbat (affirmatory limit). In the verse of wudu (5:6): "wash your faces and your hands up to the elbows," the limit "up to the elbows" excludes the elbows from the obligation of washing, because the hand originally includes the elbow and beyond, but the limit removes that portion from the obligation. In contrast, in the verse of fasting (2:187): "and complete the fast until the night," the limit "until the night" does not exclude the night from the ruling of fasting; rather, it defines the time span of the fast. The night is not included in the fast itself but the fast must be completed by nightfall. The author then addresses Zufar's objection: Zufar argued that the limit should not include the limited, drawing an analogy with the night in fasting. The Hanafi response distinguishes between the two types: the limit in wudu is exclusionary, so the elbow is not washed but the rest of the hand is; the limit in fasting is affirmatory, extending the ruling to the night without including the night. This distinction is crucial for understanding how legal texts determine the scope of obligations.

Key Terms
غایة (ghāyah): A limit or boundary set by a legal text, indicating the extent of a ruling.
غایت اسقاط (ghāyat isqāṭ): A limit that excludes the mentioned item from the ruling, e.g., 'to the elbows' excludes elbows from washing.
غایت اثبات (ghāyat ithbāt): A limit that extends the ruling to the mentioned item without including it in the essence, e.g., 'until the night' extends fasting time.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, the legal limit (gha'yah) can be either exclusionary or affirmatory, and its interpretation determines the scope of obligation; the elbows in wudu are washed, while the night in fasting extends the duration without being part of the fast.
