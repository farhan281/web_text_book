The Denier's Statement with Oath: A Foundational Maxim
Legal maxim on evidentiary burden in disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi evidentiary rule for disputes and apply it to allocate the burden of proof between claimant and denier.

Core Idea
In a dispute, the claimant must provide proof; the denier's oath suffices.

This lesson introduces a cornerstone of Hanafi judicial procedure: the allocation of the burden of proof in disputes. The maxim 'al-qawlu qawlu al-munkari ma‘a al-yamīn' establishes that the party who denies a claim is given credence provided he takes an oath. This rule, derived from the prophetic hadith, ensures that claims cannot be proven merely by accusation, protecting the defendant's right to defend through a solemn oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
In any dispute, there is a claimant (mudda‘ī) who makes a claim, and a denier (munkar) who denies it. The maxim applies to the denier.
The claimant must be the one asserting a right or fact.
2
Understand the ruling
The denier's statement (denial) is accepted by the judge as the basis for the judgment, but only if supported by an oath.
The denier does not need to bring evidence; his denial under oath is sufficient.
3
Note the condition – the oath
The acceptance of the denier's statement is conditional upon him taking a solemn oath (yamīn) swearing by Allah that he is truthful in his denial.
The oath is central; without it, the denier's statement is not given weight.
4
Recognize the burden of proof
The claimant bears the burden of proof (al-bayyinah). He must produce evidence to support his claim; otherwise, the denier's oath prevails.
This aligns with the prophetic hadith: 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the one who denies.'
5
Apply to a simple case
If A claims B owes 100 dirhams, and B denies, B's denial under oath is accepted unless A brings witnesses or documentary evidence.
The rule protects B from being forced to pay on mere accusation.

Commentary
This maxim means that in a dispute, the party who denies a claim (al-munkar) is given credence provided he takes an oath. The burden of proof lies on the claimant (al-mudda‘ī). This is a fundamental principle of Hanafi court procedure and is derived from the prophetic hadith: 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the one who denies.'

Key Terms
الْمُنْكَرِ (al-munkar): The person who denies a claim; the defendant in a dispute.
الْيَمِينِ (al-yamīn): The oath taken by the denier to support his denial.
