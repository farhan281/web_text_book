Six Foundational Maxims of Speech and Command in Hanafi Usul
Lists six legal maxims on speech and command

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state all six maxims and explain the Hanafi principle that the imperative form indicates obligation (wujub) unless a contrary indicator is present.

Core Idea
The imperative in Shariah commands indicates obligation (wajib) unless context proves otherwise.

This passage from the Hanafi usul tradition presents six concise maxims that govern the interpretation of speech, particularly in legal contexts. These maxims are memorization anchors for students of usul al-fiqh, establishing foundational rules about how utterances, commands, and responses are understood. The most significant of these maxims, 'al-amr lil-wujub', distinguishes the Hanafi school from other madhahib such as the Shafi'is, who hold that the imperative indicates recommendation (nadb) unless there is evidence for obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six maxims
Read the passage and list each maxim numbered 97 through 102. Note that each maxim begins with 'inna' (indeed) and presents a general rule about speech or command.
Maxims 97-100 deal with general rules of speech, 101 extends a ruling, and 102 is the key principle on commands.
2
Analyze maxim 97 on custom-based understanding
The first maxim states that people's speech follows mutual understanding of custom ('urf'), relying on actual letters and comprehension of meaning. This establishes that custom plays a role in interpreting ambiguous utterances.
Hanafis prioritize 'urf' when literal meaning is unclear.
3
Examine maxims 98-100 on contextual speech
Maxims 98-100 clarify that ordinary addresses, teaching/learning contexts, and responses are all considered valid forms of speech. The key is that if a statement can serve as an answer, it is treated as one.
These maxims emphasize context over rigid literalism.
4
Understand maxim 101 on the mosque roof
The fifth maxim extends the legal ruling of the mosque to its roof. This illustrates the principle that the ruling of a place or thing applies to its integral parts.
Example: the roof of a mosque shares the sanctity and rules (e.g., no entry in state of janabah).
5
Grasp maxim 102: al-amr lil-wujub
The sixth maxim is the most famous: the imperative form indicates obligation. In Hanafi usul, when the Lawgiver issues a command, the default ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory), unless a compelling indicator (qarinah) shifts it to another category (e.g., recommendation or permission).
This is a distinctive Hanafi position; other schools require additional evidence for obligation.

The Legal Presumption of Predominant Existence
States a legal presumption based on common occurrence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim al-ghalib yunazzalu manzilat al-muhaqqaq and apply it to a simple fiqh scenario.

Core Idea
What is normally present is treated as certain in Islamic law.

This single-line maxim is a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh. It establishes that when something is overwhelmingly common in a situation, we do not need to verify its actual existence—we base rulings on the presumption of its presence. This avoids unnecessary burden in daily practice.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim
Learn the Arabic text exactly: 'إِنَّ الْغَالِبَ وُجُودُهُ كَالْمُتَحَقَّق'. The maxim is a simple statement that carries deep legal implications.
The Arabic word 'الغالب' (al-ghālib) means 'the predominant'.
2
Explain the meaning
Paraphrase in English: 'That which is predominantly present is like that which is actually realized.' The law treats a common occurrence as if it is certain.
This is not about mere possibility; it requires a strong preponderance.
3
Identify the key elements
Focus on 'al-ghālib' (predominant) and 'ka al-muḥaqqaq' (like the actual). The presumption applies only when the preponderance is overwhelming in the specific context.
Context determines what counts as predominant (e.g., water in a city vs. desert).
4
Apply to a fiqh scenario
Consider wudu: if a person is in a city where water is universally available, water is considered present even if a particular tap is dry. Tayammum is not allowed because the presumption of availability overrides the temporary absence.
This application is drawn from Hanafi rulings on tayammum and water availability.
5
Distinguish from non-application
The maxim does not apply when the occurrence is rare or uncertain. For example, in a desert with scarce water, each instance must be verified; presumption does not hold.
The threshold of 'ghalabah' (predominance) is context-dependent.

Commentary
This is a legal maxim (al-ghalib yunazzalu manzilat al-muhaqqaq). For example, in rulings of purification, if water is predominantly available in an area, it is considered as present. This principle is used in many areas of fiqh to avoid unnecessary investigation.

The Word 'Ala Implies Obligation
States linguistic principle for obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi linguistic principle that the preposition 'ala indicates obligation and apply it to derive a ruling from a Quranic verse or hadith.

Core Idea
'Ala always signals obligation in legal texts.

This lesson introduces a foundational linguistic rule in Hanafi usul: the Arabic preposition 'ala (upon) is a marker of obligation (wujub) when used in commands or duties. This principle helps us identify wajib actions from scriptural texts. For example, when the Quran says 'upon them is to...', it imposes a binding duty.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the phrase 'ala
Identify the preposition 'ala in the Arabic text of a Quranic verse or hadith. It may appear as 'ala, 'alay, 'alaykum, etc.
Pay attention to the context: the action attached to 'ala is the potential obligation.
2
Apply the rule
Remember the principle: in legal address, 'ala indicates obligation. This is a default rule in Hanafi usul.
The ruling derived is wujub (obligation) unless there is a qarina (contextual indicator) to the contrary.
3
Identify the linked action
Find the action or duty that is linked to 'ala. That action is what the text makes obligatory.
For example, in 'Wa 'ala al-mawludi lahu rizquhunna' (2:233), the action is 'rizq' (provision), so providing for the wife is wajib upon the father.
4
Derive the ruling
Conclude that the action is wajib. This ruling can be used in fiqh to establish obligations.
The principle is widely accepted; differences arise only in rare exceptions.

إِنَّ كَلِمَةَ عَلَى لِلْإِيجَابِ

Commentary
This is a linguistic rule in Arabic used in usul al-fiqh. When the Quran or Hadith uses the phrase 'ala (upon) in relation to an action, it indicates that the action is obligatory (wajib). For example, 'They have upon them' implies a duty. This is used in deriving rulings from texts.

Key Terms

Shahadah is a Preventer Not a Remover
Defines the legal nature of martyrdom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that shahadah prevents post-death rituals (ghusl, kafan) but does not remove pre-death obligations (debts, missed acts), and apply it to a martyr's estate.

Core Idea
Martyrdom prevents washing and shrouding but does not cancel debts or missed duties.

This short passage from al-Hidayah encapsulates a pivotal Hanafi principle on the legal effect of martyrdom. It distinguishes between prevention of posthumous practices and removal of prior responsibilities. Understanding this maxim is essential for correctly applying burial rules and estate obligations for a shahid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal quality being described
The passage describes shahadah (martyrdom) as having a specific legal effect: it is a preventer (mani'ah) but not a remover (rafi'ah). Recognise that this is a binary distinction: prevention blocks certain rulings from applying, while removal would erase them entirely.
The term 'mani'ah' in Hanafi usul indicates a legal impediment that stops a ruling from becoming operative, without nullifying the ruling's existence.
2
List what shahadah prevents
Based on standard Hanafi fiqh, shahadah prevents the obligatory washing (ghusl) of the body and the shrouding (kafan). Instead, the martyr is buried in his bloodstained clothes as he died. Identify these prohibited acts.
This prevention is an exception to the general obligation of ghusl for every deceased Muslim.
3
List what shahadah does NOT remove
Shahadah does not remove pre-existing obligations such as unpaid debts, missed prayers, zakat, or expiations (kaffarat). These remain due and must be fulfilled from the martyr's estate or by heirs. This distinguishes prevention from complete remission.
The principle ensures that the rights of others (adamiyyun) and rights of Allah (huquq Allah) that were obligations before death are not forfeited.
4
Apply the principle to a concrete case
Consider a martyr who died in battle while owing a debt to a fellow Muslim. According to the principle, the debt is not forgiven; it must be repaid from his estate. However, his body is not washed. Practice reasoning: 'Shahadah prevents ghusl but does not remove the debt.'
This application distinguishes the Hanafi view from folk beliefs that martyrdom erases all worldly obligations.

Indeed, martyrdom is known as a preventer, not a remover.
Source pages p. 9

Commentary
This principle explains the legal effect of being a shahid (martyr in battle). The status of shahadah (martyrdom) prevents certain rules from applying to the martyr, such as washing the body and shrouding (the martyr is buried in his clothes). However, it does not remove obligations that were due before death, such as debts or missed prayers. The rulings are 'mani'ah' (preventing) but not 'rafi'ah' (removing) past liability.

Key Terms
الشَّهَادَةَ (al-shahādah): Martyrdom in the path of Allah, specifically death in legitimate jihad.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway

Collection is Accompanied by Protection
Legal maxim linking revenue collection to protection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal maxim and explain its implication that the collection of taxes requires the state to provide protection.

Core Idea
Revenue collection and protection are inseparable duties of the state.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim of Islamic public finance. The maxim 'Inna al-jibāyata bi al-ḥimāyah' links the right to collect taxes with the obligation to provide security. It is a concise expression of the social contract between the ruler and the ruled.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic wording
Commit the maxim to memory: 'إِنَّ الْجِبَايَةَ بالحِمَايَة'. This is a concise principle that governs taxation.
2
Understand the key terms
Analyze 'al-jibāyah' (collection of revenue) and 'al-ḥimāyah' (protection). Note that both are nouns, and the preposition 'bi' indicates inseparable connection.
In Hanafi fiqh, jibāyah often refers to kharāj and jizya collection.
3
Recognize the legal implication
The maxim means that the right to collect revenue is conditional upon the duty to protect. If protection is absent, collection loses its legitimacy.
This is not a commercial transaction but a mutual responsibility.
4
Apply to the role of the customs officer (al-‘āshir)
The maxim originates from the chapter on customs officers. They have authority to collect dues only when they safeguard the merchants and travelers.
5
Connect to broader fiqh rulings
This principle underlies Hanafi rulings on kharāj, jizya, and state expenditure. The imam must use revenues for the protection of the subjects.

The right to collect revenue is linked to the duty of safeguarding the community.

The Part Does Not Contradict the Whole
Legal maxim on part-whole consistency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-juz' la yukhalif al-jumlah' to determine the consistency of rulings in composite acts.

Core Idea
The ruling of a part must be consistent with the ruling of its whole; contradiction invalidates the whole.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim that governs the relationship between a part and its whole. Understanding this maxim is crucial for ensuring internal consistency when deriving rulings for components of a compound act or contract. It prevents contradictory statements where a whole is deemed valid but a part is declared invalid without justification.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Memorize the exact wording: 'إن الجزء لا يخالف الجملة' meaning 'The part does not contradict the whole.' This is a legal axiom, not a derived ruling.
The maxim is qat'i (certain) in its application as a principle of consistency.
2
Identify the scope of the maxim
Recognize that the maxim applies wherever a 'whole' (jumlah) is composed of 'parts' (ajza'). Common fiqh contexts include contracts, acts of worship, and legal rulings themselves.
The whole can be a complete ibadah (e.g., prayer) or a muamalah (e.g., sale).
3
Apply the maxim to a concrete Hanafi example
Consider a valid sale contract: the whole (contract) is sahih. Each part (offer, acceptance, subject matter) must also be valid under its own conditions. If any part is fasid (defective), the whole becomes fasid.
This supports the Hanafi rule that a sale is invalid if any essential element is defective.

Commentary
This maxim means that the ruling of a part (juz’) cannot be inconsistent with the ruling of the whole (jumlah). For example, if a contract is valid as a whole, its components must also be valid. Or if an act is permissible in its entirety, its individual elements cannot be impermissible.

Key Terms

الْجُمْلَة (al-jumlah): The whole or composite entity consisting of parts.

Attribution Implies Exclusivity
Legal maxim on ownership presumption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-iḍāfatu tufīdu al-ikhtiṣāṣ' to determine when attribution creates a presumption of exclusive ownership or right.

Core Idea
Attribution to a person creates a rebuttable presumption of exclusive ownership.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim: 'Inna al-iḍāfata tufīdu al-ikhtiṣāṣ' — indeed, attribution implies exclusivity. This maxim is central to interpreting contracts, property rights, and any statement where something is ascribed to someone. It establishes a presumption that the attributed party has exclusive right, unless evidence proves otherwise. Mastery of this maxim sharpens your ability to analyse ownership claims in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Memorize the exact wording: إِنَّ الْإِضَافَةَ تُفِيدُ الْإِخْتِصَاصَ. Understand that 'iḍāfah' is grammatical attribution, and 'ikhtiṣāṣ' is exclusivity or special right.
This maxim is a foundational presumption in property and contract law.
2
Define the key terms
Analyze 'iḍāfah' as the ascription of something to a person, and 'ikhtiṣāṣ' as the legal consequence of exclusive right or ownership.
The term 'iḍāfah' is broader than ownership; it can also indicate relationship or authority.
3
Identify the legal consequence
The maxim creates a legal presumption (ẓāhir) that the attributed party has exclusive right. This shifts the burden of proof to anyone contesting it.
This presumption is not absolute; it can be rebutted by stronger evidence.
4
Determine when it applies
Apply the maxim any time an object or right is grammatically attributed to someone, e.g., 'Zayd's house', 'my book', 'the father's custody'.
It applies in contracts, gifts, wills, and disputes over property or authority.
5
Recognize rebuttals
The presumption can be overturned by clear evidence such as a contract, testimony, or prior claim. Understand that the maxim is a starting point, not a conclusive proof.
For example, if someone says 'my book' but another produces a bill of sale, the presumption is rebutted.

The Maxim: Rare is Like Non-existent
Legal maxim on rare occurrences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'Inna al-nādiru ka al-ma‘dūm' and apply it to determine that rare occurrences are not considered in general rulings.

Core Idea
Rare events are legally ignored — the law follows what is common, not exceptional.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi fiqh that shapes rulings in purity, contracts, and many other areas. The maxim teaches us that the shariah is built on what occurs normally, not on rare or exceptional cases. Understanding this principle helps you recognize why certain minor impurities are overlooked and why customs (urf) are given weight.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic maxim
Commit the exact wording to memory: 'إِنَّ النَّادِرَ كَالْمَعْدُوم'. This is the foundation.
The syntax is simple: 'inna' + noun + 'ka' + noun.
2
Understand its legal meaning
The rare (al-nādir) is treated as non-existent (ma‘dūm) in terms of affecting general rulings. This does not mean the rare event is denied, but that it does not alter the default rule.
Distinguish between factual existence and legal consideration.
3
Identify its scope in fiqh
This maxim applies broadly in tahārah (purity), mu‘āmalāt (transactions), and ‘urf (custom). For example, a rare impurity in water is ignored; an unusual custom does not change the norm.
It is a qā‘idah kulliyyah (universal principle).
4
Apply to a concrete example
If a drop of blood (rare impurity) falls into a large tank of water, the water remains pure because the impurity is treated as non-existent. The ruling is based on the common situation of pure water.
This is from the chapter on tahārah.
5
Recognize its limits
If a rare event actually occurs and is proven, it may have legal effect in that specific case, but it does not change the general rule. The maxim is about establishing general rulings, not about denying reality.
For example, if a specific water source is known to contain a rare impurity, that impurity must be considered.

Commentary
This maxim means that rare or exceptional cases are not given weight in general rulings. The law is based on what is common and usual (ghalib), not on rare occurrences. For example, if a rare type of impurity is found in water, it is ignored unless proven otherwise.

Key Terms
النَّادِرَ (al-nādir): Something rare or seldom occurring.

Statement Contrary to Qiyas Does Not Create Doubt
Legal maxim on statements conflicting with analogical reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim and explain why a bare statement opposing qiyas does not create legal doubt.

Core Idea
A statement opposing qiyas carries no weight to create doubt; established analogy prevails.

In Hanafi fiqh, qiyas (analogical reasoning) is a robust source of law. This maxim shields well-established analogical rulings from being undermined by mere claims that contradict them. Understanding this principle helps you distinguish between genuine legal doubt and baseless assertions, reinforcing the reliability of qiyas as a method of istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim's ruling
The text states: 'The statement of the speaker when it contradicts qiyas does not create doubt.' Recognize that this is a negative legal consequence: no shubhah arises.
Shubhah here means legal uncertainty that could affect rulings, e.g., in criminal or financial matters.
2
Define qiyas in this context
Qiyas refers to strong analogical reasoning, typically jali (clear) qiyas. Hanafis consider it a primary source for deriving rulings when no explicit text exists.
Not all qiyas is equal; this maxim likely applies to well-established qiyas with clear illah.
3
Understand the concept of doubt (shubhah)
Shubhah is a valid uncertainty that can prevent a ruling from being applied (e.g., doubt in a criminal case leads to dropping the hadd penalty). This maxim says a contrary statement without evidence does not generate such doubt.
The maxim protects the integrity of qiyas from frivolous claims.
4
Apply the maxim to an example
For instance, if it is known by qiyas that the new moon should be visible on a certain night, but someone claims to have seen it a day earlier without proof, that claim does not create doubt about the start of Ramadan.
The claim is not given legal weight because it contradicts a sound analogy.
5
Note the limits of the maxim
This does not mean qiyas is infallible; it means a mere statement opposing it is insufficient. If the claimant brings strong evidence (e.g., reliable testimony or a corroborating text), the doubt may arise.
The maxim applies to unsubstantiated statements, not to competing proofs.

The Supremacy of Text over Qiyas
Legal maxim on the supremacy of transmitted text over analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim on qiyas opposing a transmitted text and apply it to identify which analogies are invalid.

Core Idea
Qiyas opposing a clear text is invalid — transmitted revelation cannot be overridden by analogy.

This brief maxim is a cornerstone of Hanafi usul. It asserts that when a ruling is established by a definitive scriptural text (nass), no analogical reasoning (qiyas) can override it. The text is the primary source; analogy serves only in the absence of clear evidence. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for proper legal derivation.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Recite or write the maxim verbatim: 'إن القياس في مقابلة النص المنقول غير مقبول'. This is the core ruling.
Memorize the Arabic; it is a standard legal maxim.
2
Identify key terms
Break down 'القياس' (qiyas/analogy), 'في مقابلة' (in opposition to), 'النص المنقول' (transmitted text – Quran or Sunnah), and 'غير مقبول' (not accepted/invalid).
النص المنقول refers to clear, transmitted revelation.
3
Explain the consequence
If a qiyas contradicts a clear text, it is rejected (ghayr maqbul). The text takes precedence, and the analogy has no legal weight.
This applies only to definitive texts (qat'i al-thubut wa al-dalala).
4
Apply a concrete example
The prohibition of riba (interest) is established by clear texts. Any qiyas that attempts to permit it on the basis of analogy with trade is invalid because it opposes the text.
This is a standard illustration in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Clarify the scope
The maxim does not invalidate qiyas in all cases. Qiyas that does not oppose a text, or which addresses an unregulated matter, is accepted and is a valid source of law.
Qiyas remains a primary source when no text exists.

Legal maxim on linguistic interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi maxim that collective mention of days includes their corresponding nights in interpreting legal texts.

Core Idea
Mention of days in plural includes the nights — a key Hanafi principle for interpreting durations.

This short maxim is a linguistic and legal tool used by Hanafi jurists when a text refers to a number of days. It clarifies that the scope covers both day and night, unless there is evidence to the contrary. Understanding this principle will help you interpret many rulings related to i‘tikāf, fasting, and other time-bound acts.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim
Read the Arabic maxim and its literal translation. Note the key term 'al-ayyām' (days) and the phrase 'mā bi izā’ihā min al-layālī' (what is opposite them of nights). The maxim asserts that when days are mentioned collectively, they include the nights that correspond to them.
This is a rule of interpretation, not a standalone ruling.
2
Identify the legal basis
Hanafi scholars derive this principle from the fact that a 'day' in linguistic usage often encompasses the associated night. For example, the Qur'an says 'fast the whole day' but the obligation extends from dawn to sunset, implicitly acknowledging the night's role as part of the unit. Also, in legal contexts like i‘tikāf, staying in the mosque requires both day and night, so the mention of 'days' must include nights to fulfill the obligation.
3
Apply to a concrete example
Take the ruling on i‘tikāf: the Qur'an says 'wa anā aktumu fī l-masājidi 'ashrata ayyām' (and I will confine myself to the mosque for ten days). The Hanafi school rules that these ten days include the nights, so the person must stay continuously day and night. If the maxim were not applied, the obligation could be interpreted as only ten daytimes, which would not fulfill the requirement of seclusion.
This example illustrates the practical impact of the maxim.
4
Distinguish from exceptions
The maxim applies only when days are mentioned in a collective, general manner and the context does not restrict it to daytime alone. For example, if a contract says 'payment within three days,' the Hanafis would count both days and nights as part of the three-day period. However, if the text explicitly says 'three days excluding nights,' then the maxim does not apply.
The maxim is a default rule, not an absolute.

Individual rights (huquq al-'ibad) take priority over purely legal rights (huquq Allah) when they conflict.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim from the Hanafi school: the right of a human being (haqq al-'abd) is given precedence over the right of the law (haqq al-shar'). This principle governs situations where a personal claim and a legal obligation clash, ensuring that individuals are not unduly burdened by religious duties when they owe something to another person. Understanding this maxim is essential for resolving real-life conflicts between debts and religious obligations.

Guided Path
1

The maxim is authoritative in the Hanafi school.

Distinguish between 'haqq al-'abd' (right of the servant/individual) and 'haqq al-shar'' (right of the law/Allah). The first pertains to personal claims like debts or property, the second to religious obligations like zakat or penalties.
Some rights of Allah involve financial duties, but they are secondary here.

When these two rights conflict, the individual's right comes first. For example, if someone has money that must go to a creditor but also owes zakat, the debt is paid first.

The same rule applies in lifetime obligations: debts take precedence over non-financial religious duties.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that qiyas cannot oppose a transmitted text and apply it to explain why fasting is a wajib condition for i'tikaf, distinguishing the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Qiyas is invalid when it opposes a transmitted text — even a weak hadith takes precedence over qiyas.

This lesson introduces a foundational usul al-fiqh principle that governs the relationship between textual evidence and analogical reasoning. The author of al-Hidayah applies this principle to the fiqh issue of i'tikaf, where the Hanafis require fasting as a condition based on the hadith of Aisha (RA), while the Shafi'is argue by qiyas that fasting is not a condition. Understanding this principle will help you appreciate how Hanafis prioritize transmitted texts over rational inference.

Guided Path
1

Articulate the qawida: 'Qiyas in opposition to a transmitted text is not accepted.' This sets the hierarchy: nass text always prevails over analogical reasoning.

The Principle of Facility in Nafl Acts and its Application to I'tikaf
Introduces a qawida and its application with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim that nafl acts are based on facility and explain why fasting is required for nafl i'tikaf, applying the principle that weak hadith is preferred over qiyas.

Core Idea
Nafl is built on ease, but a textual condition overrides it; even weak hadith beats qiyas.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim: supererogatory acts of worship (nafl) are founded on ease and leniency. However, this does not mean that all conditions are waived. The author of al-Hidayah applies this maxim to the issue of i'tikaf, arguing that despite the general facility of nafl, a textual evidence (a hadith, even if weak) establishes fasting as a condition for validity. The passage then illustrates Imam Abu Hanifa's consistent methodology: he always gave precedence to transmitted texts—even weak hadith and traditions of the Companions—over qiyas and personal opinion.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Maxim
Read the Arabic phrase إِنَّ النَّفْلَ مَبْنَى عَلَى الْمُسَاهَلَةِ. Understand that 'nafl' refers to supererogatory acts, and 'musahalah' means ease/facility. The principle is a general rule of Hanafi fiqh.
This maxim is often invoked to relax conditions for voluntary acts, but it is not absolute.
2
Apply the Maxim to I'tikaf
The text applies the maxim to i'tikaf (spiritual retreat in a mosque). The issue: is fasting a condition for nafl i'tikaf? The narrator Hasan ibn Ziyad reports from Abu Hanifa that fasting is required even for nafl i'tikaf, citing textual evidence.
Contrast: the facility of nafl might suggest no condition, but the text overrides.
3
Analyze the Reasoning
The reasoning is that qiyas cannot oppose a text (النَّصَّ). Even if the hadith is considered weak (da'if), it is still a text and thus takes precedence over qiyas. This exemplifies the Hanafi usul that a weak hadith is preferred over qiyas.
This principle is a distinctive feature of the Hanafi school.
4
Examine the Examples
The passage provides several examples where Imam Abu Hanifa neglected qiyas in favor of weak hadith: ruling that laughing invalidates prayer, allowing ablution with date wine during travel, cutting off the hand for theft of less than ten dirhams, setting maximum menstruation at ten days, requiring a large city for Jumu'ah, and rulings on well impurities.
These show that the Hanafi school does not rely heavily on qiyas when any textual evidence exists.
5
Draw the Methodological Conclusion
The Hanafi school gives priority to textual sources, regardless of their authentication strength, over rational analogy. This refutes the common misconception that Hanafis prioritize qiyas over hadith.
This is a key point in understanding Hanafi legal theory.

Linguistic Principle: Days in Plural Include Nights
States a linguistic-legal principle and applies it to i'tikaf vow

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that 'days' in a general plural includes the corresponding nights to a vow of i'tikaf, and distinguish the ruling for obligatory vs. supererogatory i'tikaf.

Core Idea
When you vow 'days' in plural, you must include nights by custom.

Common sense and Quranic usage show that saying 'I haven't seen you for days' means nights too. The Hanafi school builds a fiqh rule on this: if someone vows i'tikaf for a number of days (plural), they must stay continuously day and night. This lesson unpacks the proof from the story of Zakariyya and shows how custom ('urf) governs interpretation.

Guided Path
1
State the principle and the ruling
Identify what the passage says: when 'days' is mentioned in a general plural, it includes nights by custom. The ruling is that a vow of i'tikaf for a specific number of days obligates both day and night.
The ruling applies only to plural forms; singular 'day' may not include night.
2
Locate the evidence (dalil)
The text gives two proofs: common usage (e.g., 'I have not seen you for days' implies days and nights) and the Quranic story of Zakariyya where 'three days' and 'three nights' refer to the same period.
The Quranic reference is from Aal 'Imran 3:41 and Maryam 19:10.
3
Analyze wajh al-dalalah
The same event is described both as 'three days' and 'three nights,' proving that 'days' can encompass nights. This shows the linguistic custom ('urf) that the passage relies on.
Other schools might require explicit mention of nights.
4
Apply to the i'tikaf vow
If someone vows 'I will perform i'tikaf for ten days' (plural), then by this principle they must also stay the nights. The author of al-Hidayah uses this to deduce the obligation.
For a single day (singular), the principle does not apply, so nights may not be required.
5
Note the exception for nafl i'tikaf
The text adds a secondary point: for supererogatory i'tikaf, a brief stay (even an hour) without fasting is valid according to Imam Muhammad, because nafl is based on ease.
This shows the madhhab's flexibility in voluntary acts.

The meaning is not that I have not seen you during the day but have seen you at night. That the mention of days in the manner of plural includes what corresponds to them of nights by custom. Commentary
This passage presents a linguistic-legal principle from the Hanafi school: when the word 'ayyam' (days) is used in a collective plural sense, it encompasses both days and their corresponding nights by custom ('urf). The author (Sahib al-Hidayah) applies this to i'tikaf: if someone vows to perform i'tikaf for a certain number of days, without specifying separately, it obligates consecutive i'tikaf covering both day and night. The proof includes common usage (e.g., 'I haven't seen you for days' implies both day and night) and the Qur'anic story of Zakariyya (AS), where 'three days' and 'three nights' refer to the same period. The passage also includes a digression on the minimum duration of nafl i'tikaf: according to a narration of Imam Muhammad, it can be as short as an hour without fasting, because supererogatory acts are based on ease. The key point is that the principle is a rule of 'urf (custom) and is used to derive legal obligations.

Key Terms
أَيَّام (ayyām): Plural of yawm (day); in this context, used in a general plural sense that includes both day and night.
عُرْف (ʿurf): Customary usage; the basis for interpreting the word 'days' in a general sense.
النَّفْل (al-nafl): Supererogatory (voluntary) acts of worship; here, nafl i'tikaf does not require fasting.
