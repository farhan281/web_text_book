The Maxim 'Harm Is Eliminated' Applied to Tayammum
Ruling with proof from a legal maxim and qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'al-darar musqit' to determine when tayammum is permissible despite the availability of water for purchase, and distinguish between acceptable overcharging (ghabn yasir) and excessive overcharging (ghabn fahish).

Core Idea
Excessive overcharging makes buying water harmful, so tayammum becomes permissible.

This passage from Usool al-Hidayah applies the Hanafi maxim 'al-darar musqit' (harm is eliminated) to the case of tayammum when water is available but only at an excessive price. It presents three scenarios of purchasing water from a companion and shows that only when the overcharging is excessive (ghabn fahish) does the obligation to buy water fall away. The reasoning uses qiyas (analogy) between harm to life and harm to wealth, both being inviolable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is tayammum (dry ablution) in place of wudu when water is available but only at a price. The ruling is that tayammum becomes permissible (ja'iz) only under specific conditions of excessive harm.
The default ruling is that tayammum is invalid if water can be obtained without excessive difficulty.
2
Recognize the three scenarios
The text outlines three scenarios of buying water: (1) market price, (2) slight overcharge (ghabn yasir), (3) excessive overcharge (ghabn fahish). Identify which permits tayammum.
Ghabn yasir and ghabn fahish are distinguished by custom (urf).
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is the maxim 'al-darar musqit' and the qiyas (analogy) made between harm to wealth and harm to life. The text quotes that a Muslim's wealth is as inviolable as his life.
The dalil is based on qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Analyze the qiyas
The analogy: As harm to life removes the obligation to use water (e.g., for a sick person who performs tayammum), harm to wealth (ghabn fahish) removes the obligation to buy water.
The common illa (effective cause) is harm (darar).
5
Note the cross-madhhab status
Some scholars may not permit tayammum when water exists at any price, interpreting 'finding water' literally. The Hanafi school relies on the concept of capability and the maxim.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different usul.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with proof from a maxim and qiyas. The author of Usool al-Hidayah explains the ruling from Al-Hidayah regarding tayammum when water is available from a companion but only at a price. The core ruling: if the companion sells water at the market price or with slight overcharging, tayammum is invalid because the traveler is considered capable of obtaining water. But if the price involves excessive overcharging (ghabn fahish), tayammum is valid. The proof is the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-darar musqit' (harm is eliminated). The reasoning (qiyas jaliy) is that a Muslim's wealth is as inviolable as his life; since harm to life removes obligations (like tayammum for a sick person), harm to wealth also removes the obligation to buy water. The passage then quotes from Al-Binayah about the meaning of 'wujud' (existence) in the Quranic verse, clarifying that 'finding water' means capability, not mere physical presence. The reference to 'mawj al-raml' (water of the animal) is to a specific case in the Hanafi school. This is a clear example of Hanafi ijtihad using qiyas on the basis of general maxims.

Key Terms
غبن فاحش (ghabn fāḥish): Excessive overcharging or loss in a transaction that is normally not tolerated by custom.
ضرر مسقط (ḍarar musqit): Harm eliminates the obligation that would otherwise exist.
غبن یسیر (ghabn yasīr): Slight overcharging that is normally tolerated in transactions.

Observing Transmitted Limits for Khilaf al-Qiyas: Wiping Over Khuffs
Applying a usul maxim to a fiqh ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim for rulings outside qiyas and apply it to determine that only wiping the exterior of the khuff is valid.

Core Idea
Rulings that are exceptions to qiyas are strictly confined to their transmitted scope.

The Hanafi school holds that when the Shari'ah establishes a ruling that departs from general analogy (qiyas), we must adhere exactly to the transmitted details and cannot extend it by analogy. This lesson applies that principle to the case of wiping over leather socks (masah ala al-khuffayn), explaining why only the exterior is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exceptional ruling
Recognize that wiping over khuffs replaces washing feet, which is the normal qiyas for wudu. The transmitted Sunnah prescribes wiping the exterior only.
2
State the Hanafi maxim
The maxim 'إِنَّ مَا هُوَ مَعْدُولٌ عَنِ الْقِيَاسِ يُرَاعَى جَمِيعُ مَا وَرَدَ بِهِ الشَّرْعُ' means that a ruling outside qiyas must be observed exactly as the Shari'ah brought it.
This principle prevents extending the concession beyond its explicit limits.
3
Apply the maxim to the ruling
Since masah is khilaf al-qiyas, we cannot analogize to wipe the interior or other parts; only the exterior, as specified in the Sunnah, is valid.
If one wipes the interior, the act is invalid because it deviates from the transmitted detail.
4
Note the Hanafi position against other schools
Some schools allow wiping the interior, but Hanafis restrict to exterior based on this maxim and the specific hadith.
This shows how the maxim is used for tarjih (preference) in inter-madhhab disputes.

Expansion of the Maxim: Tayammum and Najis Garment Cases
Expanding the maxim to further exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two additional rulings (tayammum and praying in a najis garment) that are khilaf al-qiyas and explain how the maxim restricts their application.

Core Idea
The same maxim governs all khilaf al-qiyas rulings: they are confined to their specific transmitted conditions.

The Hanafi madhhab illustrates the maxim of khilaf al-qiyas with further examples beyond masah. In this lesson, we examine the case of tayammum when water is available at an exorbitant price and the case of praying in a najis garment when no water is available. Both are exceptions to qiyas and are therefore limited by specific conditions set by the Shari'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the new exceptional rulings
Tayammum is an exception to wudu/ghusl; praying in a najis garment without water is an exception to the requirement of purity. Both deviate from the normal qiyas of purification.
2
Extract the conditions from the transmitted texts
For tayammum: a condition is that water is not available at a fair price; if excessive overcharging occurs, tayammum is allowed. For the najis garment: one prays as is, without cutting out the impurity, provided no water is available.
These conditions are part of the transmitted ruling and cannot be extended by analogy.
3
Apply the maxim to limit the rulings
Since both are khilaf al-qiyas, we cannot extend them beyond their transmitted conditions. For example, if water is available at market price, tayammum is invalid; if water is available, one must wash the garment.
The maxim prevents making the concession more general than the Shari'ah intended.
4
Contrast with the masah example
In all three cases, the Hanafi school insists on following the transmitted details exactly, highlighting the consistent application of the maxim.

This passage from Al-Hidayah examines a specific problem: what if water is available but only for a price? The Hanafi school distinguishes three price levels. Masah on Khuff: Contrary to Qiyas but Bound by Shariah
Establishes principle that masah on khuff is contrary to qiyas and must follow shariah exactly

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why masah on khuff is considered ma‘dūl ‘an al-qiyās and identify the correct practice based on the hadith of ‘Ali (radiyallahu ‘anhu).

Core Idea
Masah on khuff is an exception to analogy; it must be done exactly as the Prophet demonstrated — only on the upper part.

This passage from al-Hidayah introduces a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: when a ruling contradicts what analogy would dictate and is established solely by divine text, it must be followed precisely, without extension. The case of wiping over leather socks (khuff) during wudu is a classic example. By understanding why the Hanafi school treats this as ma‘dūl ‘an al-qiyās, you will grasp why only the upper part of the khuff is wiped, and why other parts are not included.

Identify the ruling and its exceptional status
Recognize that masah on khuff is an exception (ma‘dūl ‘an al-qiyās) because qiyas would require washing, not wiping, and wiping the inside would seem more logical.
2
Examine the counter-analogical reasoning
Understand the rational argument: the inside of the khuff touches the ground and becomes dirtier, so wiping there would be more effective. This makes the deviation from qiyas more striking.
3
Analyze the transmitted proof
Study the hadith of ‘Ali (radiyallahu ‘anhu) where he reports that the Prophet (sallallahu ‘alayhi wa sallam) wiped only the outside of the khuffs, not the inside. This text overrides qiyas.
4
Apply the principle of strict adherence
Conclude that because masah is contrary to qiyas, it must be limited exactly to what is transmitted: wiping the upper part only; no extension to other parts is allowed.

Core Idea
A ruling based on textual exception cannot be extended by analogy to other cases.

One of the fundamental uṣūl al-fiqh principles is that when the Sharīʿah introduces a ruling that departs from what normal analogy would demand, that ruling is confined to its specific textual context. In this passage, the Hanafi school uses the case of wiping over leather socks (masḥ ʿalā al-khuffayn) to illustrate this principle. Guided Path

Read the opening statement: 'What is established contrary to analogy, its like is not analogized to it.' This is the core rule. Note that it applies to any ḥukm that contradicts qiyās jaliyy (clear analogy).

Defining Heavy and Light Impurity Based on Textual Evidence
Explains how conflicting texts lead to light impurity according to Abu Hanifa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of heavy and light impurity, apply the principle of conflicting texts to classify a substance, and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement with the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Impurity classification hinges on textual conflict: an unchallenged text gives heavy impurity, conflicting texts give light impurity.

In this passage from 'Usool al-Hidayah, Imam al-Marghinani explains how the Hanafi school distinguishes between heavy (maghlazah) and light (khafifah) impurity. The classification depends not on the mere existence of a text proving impurity, but on whether any other text suggests purity. When two texts conflict—one declaring impurity, the other purity—the law adopts leniency (takhfif), reducing the impurity to the light category. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, provides examples, and explores the difference between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples on the criterion.

Guided Path
1
Recall the two impurity categories
The passage classifies impurities into heavy (maghlazah) and light (khafifah). The category determines practical rules of purification (e.g., number of washes, rubbing).
Heavy impurity requires thorough washing; light impurity may have more lenient rulings.
2
Identify the criterion for heavy impurity
Heavy impurity is proven by a definitive text (dalil qat'i) that is not contradicted by any text indicating purity. Examples include urine, blood, wine (Quran 5:90), chicken droppings, and donkey urine.
The text must be uncontested by any other primary text.
3
Identify the criterion for light impurity
When two texts conflict—one establishing impurity, the other purity—the ruling is lenient (takhfif) and the impurity is light. Example: urine of animals whose meat is eaten, due to conflicting narrations.
Conflict (ta'arud) is the trigger for lightness.
4
Apply the principle to a substance
Given a substance, first check if there is a primary text (Quran or hadith) declaring it impure. Then check if any primary text declares it pure. If only the impurity text exists, it is heavy. If both exist, it is light.
This systematic method is central to Hanafi fiqh of taharah.
5
Recognize the intra-Hanafi difference
Abu Hanifa uses the textual criterion above. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) use consensus (ijma') for heavy impurity and disagreement (ikhtilaf) for light. The practical difference appears in dung: Abu Hanifa rules it heavy (due to the uncontested hadith of Ibn Mas'ud), while the Sahibayn rule it light (because Imam Malik considered it pure).
The mufta bihi position follows Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn's view is also recorded.

Core Idea
In necessity, one-fourth pure makes a garment fully pure; abandoning a duty with a substitute is not true abandonment.

This lesson explores two foundational Hanafi maxims that resolve a practical dilemma: when a person must pray but only has an impure garment. The first maxim treats one-fourth pure as the whole under necessity; the second maxim clarifies that performing a substitute (gestures) means obligations are not truly abandoned. Together they shape the ruling and resolve an intra-Hanafi dispute.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two views: Imam Muhammad says praying in the najis garment is wajib; the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) give a choice, with preference for praying in the garment.
The Shaykhayn's view is the mufta bihi.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam Muhammad argues that praying naked abandons multiple fards (covering ‘awrah, standing, bowing, prostrating) while praying in the garment abandons only one (purity of garment).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand his reasoning: abandoning one fard is better than abandoning several, thus wajib to pray in the najis garment.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Shaykhayn use maxim 58: leaving something with a successor (gestures) is not true abandonment. Hence, only covering ‘awrah is abandoned in both options, making them equal.
Gestures (ima') replace physical postures, so those obligations are not truly abandoned.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: under Muhammad, one must pray in the garment; under Shaykhayn, one may choose but praying in garment is better.
The mufta bihi grants flexibility.

(57) Indeed, one-fourth of a thing stands in place of the whole. Translation: One-fourth of a cloth stands in place of the whole. Commentary: The author of Hidayah intends to prove with this principle that if a person has no garment other than a najis one, and no means to remove the impurity, then it is permissible for him to pray in that najis garment, and this is when one-fourth or more of the garment is pure. If he prays naked, it is not valid, because the principle is that one-fourth occupies the position of the whole. So the purity of one-fourth of the garment is like the purity of the whole, and leaving a pure garment and praying naked is not permissible. Commentary: In the previous section, this principle was mentioned: if a person has no garment other than a najis one and no means to remove impurity, he should pray in that najis garment. The second case is if less than one-fourth is pure. The author of Hidayah mentions this second case here: there is a difference of opinion. According to Imam Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on him, it is obligatory to pray in that najis garment. This is also one of the two views of Imam Shafi'i. It is evident that abandoning one obligation is preferable to abandoning several. Therefore, praying in the najis garment is obligatory. In contrast, according to the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), the person has a choice—either pray naked or in the garment—but praying in the garment is better. This is because covering the ‘awrah is not specific to prayer; rather, it is obligatory both in prayer and outside it, while purity of garment is specific to prayer. The intention of the author of Hidayah in citing the above principle is to respond to Imam Muhammad's argument. Imam Muhammad's claim that praying naked would necessitate abandoning several obligations is answered by this principle: leaving something such that its successor exists is not considered leaving. This applies here because when praying naked, although abandoning standing, bowing, etc. seems necessary, their successor—i.e., gestures (ima')—exists. Therefore, since the successor exists, it is not a true abandonment. The one praying naked, if he abandons standing, etc., instead acts upon their substitute—i.e., ima' and gestures. Hence, only one obligation is abandoned, not many.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute presented through two legal maxims from the Hidayah. It addresses the scenario where a person has only a najis (impure) garment and no means to purify it or any other cloth. The first maxim (57) establishes that if at least one-fourth of the garment is pure, it is treated as if the whole garment is pure due to necessity. Therefore, the person must pray in that garment and cannot pray naked; doing so would invalidate the prayer. The second maxim (58) is introduced to resolve the disagreement when the pure portion is less than one-fourth. Imam Muhammad holds that praying in the najis garment is obligatory to avoid abandoning multiple obligations (covering ‘awrah, standing, bowing, etc.) which would occur if one prayed naked. However, the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) argue that the person has a choice, and praying in the garment is better. Their reasoning employs the second maxim: in necessity, when a person prays naked, the obligatory physical postures are replaced by gestures (ima'), which act as a substitute. Thus, abandoning the physical postures is not a true abandonment because the substitute exists. Consequently, only one obligation—covering the ‘awrah—is truly abandoned in both options, making them equivalent. Therefore, the person is given a choice, with preference for praying in the garment to preserve modesty. The passage concludes with the ruling from Hidayah that the two options are equal, and the maxim 'leaving with a successor is not leaving' is the key response to Imam Muhammad.

Key Terms
ربع الشيء (rub' al-shay'): The one-fourth of a thing that, in this context, stands in place of the whole for determining whether a garment is considered pure enough to wear in prayer when no alternative exists.
ترك الشيء إلى خلف (tark al-shay' ila khalaf): The legal maxim that abandoning an obligatory act is not considered true abandonment if a valid substitute (the successor) is performed in its place, such as gestures replacing physical postures in prayer for the excused.
خلیفہ/خلف (khalifah / khalaf): The successor or substitute act that replaces the original obligatory action when the original cannot be performed due to necessity, e.g., gesture (ima') for ruku' and sujud.
نجس (najis): Impure; refers to a garment contaminated with ritual impurity (najasah) that normally invalidates prayer if worn without necessity.
طہارت ثوب (taharat al-thawb): The requirement that one's clothing be free of ritual impurity for the validity of prayer. In this passage, it is one of the conditions (shurut) of prayer.

Praying in a Partially Najis Garment vs Naked: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on choice vs obligation when garment pure area insufficient

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school gives a choice between praying in a partially najis garment and praying naked, and distinguish the views of Imam Muhammad and the two shaykhs using the concept of khalaf.

Core Idea
When pure part < quarter, you have a choice — gestures substitute for abandoned obligations.

In this passage, Al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about a person who only has a garment with a small pure area. The question is whether it is obligatory to pray in that garment or permissible to pray naked. We will trace the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf against Imam Muhammad, understand the role of substitution (khalaf) in Hanafi usul, and see how the principle that 'leaving something with a substitute is not abandonment' resolves the dispute.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two shaykhs) as presented in the text: the person has a choice between three options, and praying in the najis garment is afdal.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opponent and their proof
Imam Muhammad argues that it is wajib to pray in the garment. His proof: abandoning one obligation (purity of garment) is better than abandoning multiple obligations (covering, standing, bowing, prostration).
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
The text replies: 'ترك الصلاة عاريا ترك الفروض' but then rejects this by stating that the existence of a substitute (khalaf), namely gesture (ima'), means the multiple obligations are not truly abandoned. Only one obligation is actually abandoned (purity of garment).
Key principle: leaving something with a substitute is not considered abandonment.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Imam Muhammad's view, praying naked is invalid and sinful. Under the two shaykhs, it is valid but less virtuous. The practical consequence is significant for a person who cannot wash the garment.
5
Identify the tarjih and mufta bihi
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah prefers the view of the two shaykhs, and this is the relied-upon position for fatwa. Therefore, the person has a choice, with the garment being afdal.
Authority weight: Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a person whose garment is najis (impure) but less than one-quarter of it is pure. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) according to the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is that the person has three options: (1) pray in the najis garment while performing full bowing and prostration—this is most virtuous because it combines covering the private part and performing actual movements; (2) pray naked sitting with gestures for bowing and prostration—this is second best because it covers the heavy private part (the genitals) while still performing some movements; (3) pray standing naked with full bowing and prostration—this is least virtuous because it exposes the private part. Imam Muhammad held that praying in the najis garment is obligatory (wajib) because praying naked would require abandoning multiple obligations (covering the private part, standing, bowing, prostration) while praying in the najis garment only abandons the obligation of purity of garment. The Hidayah commentary refutes this by stating that when a person prays naked with gestures, they are not actually abandoning the obligations but are substituting them with gestures (ima'), and leaving something with a substitute is not considered abandonment. Therefore, the person has a choice, and praying in the najis garment is merely preferable, not obligatory.

Key Terms
الخلف (al-khalaf): Substitute or replacement, here referring to gesture (ima') replacing physical postures in prayer.
الإيماء (al-ima'): Gestures with the head or body performed in place of bowing and prostration when one cannot perform them physically.

The Effect of Turning Away in Prayer: Intent and Place
Ruling on insiraf during salah for imagined hadath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on turning away during prayer due to a false suspicion of hadath and distinguish between cases of correction and rejection.

Core Idea
Intent and location determine whether turning away invalidates prayer.

This lesson examines a key principle from Al-Hidayah: turning away (insiraf) from the qibla during prayer does not automatically break the prayer. The ruling depends on the intention (correction vs. rejection) and whether the person left the mosque. We will explore the proof based on istihsan and the distinction from the opposing qiyas argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act? Turning away from the qibla during prayer due to a false suspicion of hadath. What is the Hanafi ruling? If the intent was correction (e.g., to renew purification) and the person did not exit the mosque, the prayer remains valid and they may continue (bina). If the intent was rejection (rafd) or if they left the mosque, the prayer is invalid.
The ruling is not absolute; it hinges on two conditions.
2
Locate the dalil
The Hanafi proof is istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas. Qiyas would invalidate any turning away from the qibla, but istihsan allows building if the intention was correction and the place is unchanged.
Istihsan is a recognized source in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Why does istihsan apply? Because if hadath had actually occurred, the person would have been allowed to build upon their prayer after removing the impurity. So, if their intention was correction during a false suspicion, that intention is annexed to reality, making the turning away non-invalidating as long as they did not leave the mosque.
The key is annexing intent to actuality (ilhaq al-niyya bi al-waqi).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars (implied by the qiyas argument) hold that any turning away from the qibla invalidates the prayer absolutely. Their proof is the general rule that turning away from the qibla is a disruptive act.
The opposing view is presented as qiyas in the text.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah presents the Hanafi position as the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view. The later Hanafi tradition follows this, emphasizing detailed analysis of intent and location.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.

(87) Indeed, turning away with the intention of correction does not prevent building (upon the prayer), and turning away with the intention of rejection and abandonment prevents it. Translation: If turning away (from the qibla) is with the intention of correcting the prayer, then it does not prevent building; if with the intention of leaving and abandoning the prayer, then building upon it is not permissible. Explanation: The author of Hidayah is explaining through this principle that if a person during prayer suspects that he has become impure (hadath), so he turns away from his place of prayer, then he finds out that he did not have hadath. Now it will be seen whether his turning from the qibla was with the intention of correcting the prayer or with the intention of leaving it. If the second (leaving), then building is not permissible, whether he left the mosque or not. If the first (correction), then there are two scenarios: whether he left the mosque or not. If he left the mosque, then he should pray from the beginning; building is not permissible. If he did not leave the mosque, then he should complete his remaining prayer; there is no need to start over because this person turned with the intention of correcting the prayer, so turning does not invalidate the prayer. Indeed if what he had imagined had actually occurred (i.e., real hadath), he would have built upon his prayer. So the intention of correction is annexed to the reality of correction. Contrary to the case where he thought he started prayer without wudu, then he turned away with the intention of wudu, then found out he had wudu and his thought was wrong. In that case, his prayer becomes invalid even if he did not leave the mosque, because this turning is by way of rejection, not for correction of prayer. Indeed if his being without wudu had been actual, he would have started anew. ... The entry into his prayer with the intention of the Imam's prayer, so the Imam's prayer includes the follower's prayer, and that is meant by his (peace be upon him) saying 'the guarantor', i.e., his prayer includes the follower's prayer. So the rule and principle is: if the turning is with the intention of correction, the prayer does not become invalid, provided he did not exit the mosque; if the turning is with the intention of aversion and rejection, the prayer becomes invalid.

This passage presents a ruling with proof from Al-Hidayah on the issue of turning away during prayer due to a false suspicion of hadath. The author states the general principle: if one turns away with the intention of correcting the prayer (like renewing wudu or removing impurity), the prayer is not invalidated, and one may build upon it. Rare Occurrences in Prayer: The Principle of Nadir and Qiyas Restriction
Ruling on insanity, wet dream, fainting during prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that rare events are not analogized to textual rulings, and distinguish between cases that allow building upon prayer and those that invalidate it.

Core Idea
Rare events like fainting are not analogized to the hadith on vomiting; prayer is invalidated, not built upon.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi legal maxim: rare occurrences are not treated as falling under the meaning of what the text brought. The specific context is events during prayer that break wudu or validity. The hadith mentions vomiting and nosebleed, allowing the prayer to be preserved under certain conditions. However, rare events like insanity, wet dream, or fainting are not included in that ruling. The prayer becomes invalid and must be restarted. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rulings on prayer continuation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
State the principle: 'What rarely occurs is not in the meaning of what the text came with.' This is a restriction on qiyas (analogy).
The maxim is called 'al-nadir' - rare events are excluded from analogical extension.
2
Examine the text (hadith)
The hadith specifies vomiting and nosebleed. Under certain conditions (intention to correct, staying within mosque), the prayer can be built upon after wudu.
Hadith: 'Whoever vomits or has a nosebleed... let him turn away and perform ablution, then build upon his prayer as long as he does not speak.' (Sunan Ibn Majah)
3
Understand why rare events are excluded
Rare events like insanity, wet dream, and fainting are not in the meaning of the text because they are infrequent. The ruling for vomiting/nosebleed is a concession (rukhsah) for common occurrences; it does not extend to rarities.
The original analogy (qiyas) would invalidate any turning away, but istihsan preserved prayer for vomiting/nosebleed due to their frequency and the intention to correct.
4
Apply the ruling to rare events
If a person becomes insane, faints, or has a wet dream during prayer, the prayer is invalid (batil) and must be restarted. They cannot build upon the part already performed.
This applies whether the person is imam, follower, or praying alone.
5
Contrast with false suspicion cases
If someone turns away due to a false suspicion (e.g., thinking they saw impurity), the prayer becomes invalid even without leaving the mosque, because the turning is for rejection (rafd), not correction.
This distinguishes from the earlier principle (lesson 87) where turning for correction preserves validity.

Shahadah as a Preventer, Not a Remover: Ghusl for the Martyr
Maxim defining shahadah as preventive, not nullifying

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim that shahadah is a preventer (mani'ah) rather than a remover (rafi'ah) of the obligation of ghusl, and apply it to determine whether a martyr requires ghusl.

Core Idea
Shahadah prevents the obligation of ghusl from arising, but does not remove it once established.

This lesson introduces a central Hanafi maxim regarding the legal effect of martyrdom on the obligation of washing the deceased. The maxim, 'Indeed, martyrdom is known to be a preventer, not a remover,' clarifies that the status of shahadah only stops the initial obligation of ghusl from taking effect; it does not eliminate an obligation that was already binding, such as janabah. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rules of taharah to martyrs.

Ghusl for a Junub Martyr: Mani' vs Rafi'
States ruling and evidence for washing a junub martyr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ghusl for a junub martyr and explain how the principle of mani' (preventer) vs rafi' (remover) justifies the ruling.

Core Idea
Martyrdom prevents ghusl for the dead but does not cancel an existing obligation of ghusl for janabah.

This passage presents a specific ruling from Imam Abu Hanifa regarding a martyr who dies in a state of major impurity (janabah). While martyrs are generally not given a ritual bath, Abu Hanifa rules that a junub martyr must be washed. The key to understanding this ruling lies in the distinction between a preventer (mani') and a remover (rafi'). We will explore the evidence from the hadith of Hanzalah and the underlying legal principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Recognize that the case involves a martyr who was in a state of janabah before death. The question is whether the obligation of ghusl for janabah remains after martyrdom.
This is not about the general rule that martyrs are not washed; it's an exception for those already impure.
2
State the ruling and its basis
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the junub martyr must be given ghusl. The basis is that martyrdom prevents the obligation of ghusl for the dead (ghusl al-mayyit) but does not remove the pre-existing obligation of ghusl for janabah.
The ruling is affirmed by the quote from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira.
3
Understand the principle: mani' vs rafi'
A preventer (mani') stops an obligation from arising; a remover (rafi') cancels an existing obligation. Here, martyrdom is a preventer for ghusl al-mayyit, but it is not a remover for janabah. Thus, the junub martyr must still perform ghusl.
This is a critical distinction in Hanafi legal theory.
4
Examine the evidence: Hadith of Hanzalah
The hadith states that when Hazrat Hanzalah (RA) was martyred in a state of janabah, the angels washed him. This indicates that ghusl is required for a junub martyr; otherwise, the angels would not have done something unnecessary.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted as evidence.
5
Apply the analogy
The text provides an analogy: if there is impurity other than blood on the martyr's garment, that impurity is washed off, but the blood is not washed because martyrdom prevents blood from being impure. This illustrates that martyrdom prevents certain obligations but does not remove existing impurities or obligations.
This reinforces the principle that janabah remains and must be removed by ghusl.

A person in a state of janabah who attains martyrdom will be given ghusl. Imam Abu Hanifa's evidence is the mentioned principle: martyrdom prevents the obligation of ghusl for the dead, i.e., the ghusl that becomes obligatory due to death, martyrdom prevents it, but it does not remove it; meaning if ghusl was already obligatory before, martyrdom does not remove it. Do you not see that if there is impurity other than blood on the martyr's garment, that impurity is washed off, but the blood is not washed, due to what we mentioned. Its meaning is that martyrdom prevented his blood from being impure, but did not remove the impurity that is other than blood.'

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from Imam Abu Hanifa regarding a martyr who is in a state of major impurity (janabah). The text opens by stating the ruling: such a martyr must be given a ritual bath (ghusl). It then provides the underlying principle that supports this ruling: martyrdom acts as a preventive (man'ah) against the obligation of ghusl for the dead, but it does not lift (raf'ah) the pre-existing obligation of ghusl for janabah. In other words, because the person was already obligated to perform ghusl for janabah before death, martyrdom does not cancel that obligation; it only prevents the new obligation of ghusl for the dead. Evidence is drawn from the well-known incident of Hazrat Hanzalah (RA), who was martyred while in a state of janabah, and the angels washed him. The passage then includes an Arabic quote from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira that elaborates: the martyr's junub status is confirmed by his own statement before battle or by his wife's statement. The principle is further illustrated by analogy: if there is impurity other than blood on the martyr's garment, that impurity is washed off, but the blood itself is not washed because martyrdom prevents blood from being considered impure but does not remove other impurities. Thus, the ghusl given to the junub martyr is specifically for janabah, not for death.

Key Terms
جنابت (janabah): Major ritual impurity requiring ghusl, typically from sexual intercourse or seminal emission.
شہادت (shahadah): Martyrdom – death in battle for the cause of Allah.
مانع (mani'): A preventer – something that prevents an obligation from arising or being applied.
