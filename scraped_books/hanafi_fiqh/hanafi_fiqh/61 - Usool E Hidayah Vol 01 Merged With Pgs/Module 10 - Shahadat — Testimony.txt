The Legal Maxim of Handwriting Comparison
Legal maxim on documentary evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi legal maxim on handwriting comparison and apply it to authenticate a document.

Core Idea
A document's authenticity can be established by comparing its handwriting to a known sample.

In Islamic law, proof of authorship is crucial for contracts and letters. The Hanafi school accepts handwriting comparison as a valid method. This maxim encapsulates that principle, allowing courts to rely on script analysis, which is a recognized form of circumstantial evidence.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Maxim
Read and memorize the Arabic maxim: 'إِنَّ الْخَطَّ يُشْبِهُ الْخَطَّ' and its English translation: 'Indeed, writing resembles writing.' This is your anchor.
The term 'khatt' means handwriting or script.
2
Understand the Legal Context
Recognize that this maxim belongs to the chapter of testimony (shahadat). It addresses how written documents can be proven when the writer is absent or denies authorship.
The maxim is a principle of evidence, not a substantive rule.
3
Apply the Maxim
Consider a scenario where a disputed document's handwriting is compared with a known sample. The qadi (judge) can accept the comparison as proof of authorship, using expert witnesses if necessary.
This is a form of 'qarinah' (circumstantial evidence) recognized by the Hanafi school.

Statement of an Immoral Person is Not Accepted in Religious Matters
Legal maxim on the testimony of a wrongdoer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal maxim regarding the testimony of a fasiq and apply it to identify when testimony is rejected in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
An immoral person's testimony is rejected in religious matters due to lack of credibility.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim: the statement of a fasiq (immoral person) is not accepted in religious matters (diyānāt). This principle is derived from the Quranic command to verify news brought by a fasiq (Quran 49:6) and is consistently applied in Hanafi fiqh to matters such as witness testimony for marriage, divorce, and moon sighting. Understanding this maxim is essential for correctly evaluating the admissibility of testimony in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category
Recognize that this ruling concerns shahada (testimony) in diyanat (religious matters).
2
Recall the condition for acceptance
Testimony requires 'adalah (uprightness); a fasiq lacks it.
3
Apply the maxim
If a prospective witness is known to be fasiq, his testimony is automatically rejected.

Commentary
This maxim states that an immoral person (fāsiq) whose uprightness is not established cannot have his testimony accepted in matters pertaining to religious rulings (diyānāt), such as sighting the moon for Ramadan or testifying to a marriage. This is based on the Quranic verse: 'If a fāsiq brings you news, verify it' (49:6).

Key Terms
الْفَاسِقِ (al-fāsiq): A person who commits major sins or persists in minor sins; lacking 'adālah (moral integrity).

Key Takeaway
The testimony of an immoral person (fāsiq) is not accepted in religious matters due to the lack of upright character ('adālah).
