Obedience According to Capacity
States a principle of legal obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle 'obedience according to capacity' to determine the obligation level for a person with limited ability.

Core Idea
Allah's commands are tailored to your ability — obligation scales with capacity.

This lesson introduces a core maxim in Hanafi fiqh: 'Indeed, obedience is according to capacity.' This principle, rooted in the Quran (2:286), ensures that Islamic obligations remain manageable and just. It governs rulings such as the sick praying sitting and the traveler shortening prayer, forming the basis of many legal concessions.

Guided Path
1
State the Maxim
Recite the Arabic maxim and its literal translation. Recognize that it is a general rule for all obligatory acts.
The maxim is concise: إِنَّ الطَّاعَةَ بِحَسَبِ الطَّاقَةِ
2
Identify the Quranic Basis
Connect this maxim to Quran 2:286: 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity.' This is the primary evidence.
The Hanafi school uses this verse to justify scaling obligations.
3
Apply to Salah (Prayer)
Consider a person who cannot stand due to illness. According to the maxim, he may pray sitting, lying down, or with gestures, based on his capacity.
This is a standard Hanafi ruling found in works like Nur al-Idah.
4
Generalize to Other Acts
Extend the principle to fasting (sick traveler may break fast), zakat (only on surplus wealth), and hajj (only if physically and financially able).
The maxim applies across all fiqh chapters.
5
Clarify Common Misunderstanding
Emphasize that the maxim does not make obedience optional; it scales the obligation. A person must still perform the act to the best of their ability.
If capacity is completely absent (e.g., unconscious), the obligation falls away temporarily.

Appointment of Substitutes by Opinion is Prohibited
States a principle about appointing substitutes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that substituting a fard act with another based on personal opinion is prohibited and identify the correct basis for valid substitutes.

Core Idea
Personal opinion cannot replace Shari'ah; substitutes must be from textual evidence.

This lesson presents a fundamental principle in Hanafi fiqh: acts of worship cannot be replaced by alternatives derived from personal opinion (ra'y). While Shari'ah does provide concessions and substitutes (such as sitting for standing in prayer when unable), these are established by textual evidence, not human reasoning. Understanding this principle protects the integrity of worship and prevents innovation.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the principle
The text states that appointing substitutes (al-abdāl) by opinion is prohibited. Note the absolute prohibition.
This is a broad principle applicable across all acts of worship.
2
Distinguish valid vs invalid substitutes
Valid substitutes have textual basis (Quran, Sunnah, or valid qiyas based on text). Invalid substitutes are those invented by personal opinion without evidence.
Example: tayammum is valid because Quran permits it; making up a new form of purification is not.
3
Apply to prayer example
A sick person cannot substitute standing with a mere thought; the concession to sit is from Sunnah. If no concession exists, the act must be performed as originally obligated.

Core Idea
Understanding the command to pray makes the obligation binding, regardless of ability.

This short but foundational principle from the Hanafi school establishes a critical link between comprehension and religious duty. Once a person understands the divine command to pray, the obligation of salah never drops, even if they cannot perform it perfectly. It applies notably to the sick, who must pray according to their ability.

State the ruling explicitly
The obligation of salah (fardiyyah) is not dropped for one who understands the command. This is a general principle that does not depend on physical ability.
The ruling is unconditional regarding physical capacity.

This is based on the Quranic verse 'فَاتَّقُوا اللَّهَ مَا اسْتَطَعْتُمْ' (So fear Allah as much as you can).
4

Standing as Integral for Prostration
States the purpose of standing in prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the purpose of standing in prayer as a means to prostration and explain its practical implication in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Standing is not an end but a means to perform prostration in prayer.

This passage clarifies the purpose of qiyam (standing) in salah. It establishes that standing is an integral (rukniyyah) only because it enables the performance of prostration. This has important implications for those who cannot stand: they may pray sitting, as the objective is the prostration itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Identify the Arabic terms: rukniyyah (integral nature), qiyam (standing), tawassul (means), and sajdah (prostration). Understand their grammatical relationship in the sentence.
Rukniyyah is a nominal derived from rukn (pillar).
2
Analyze the purpose clause
The phrase li al-tawassuli ila al-sajdah indicates that standing is a means (tawassul) to reach prostration. This purpose is the raison d'etre of the rukn.
tawassul implies a mediation or pathway.
3
Derive the fiqh implication
If the purpose can be achieved without standing (e.g., sitting), then the rukn of standing is not absolute. The Hanafi school permits praying sitting for those unable to stand, as the essential objective (sajdah) remains attainable.
This aligns with the hadith: 'Pray standing; if you cannot, then sitting...'
4
Apply to a concrete scenario
Consider a sick person who cannot stand. Apply the principle: the obligation of standing drops, and the prayer is valid while sitting because the rukn's purpose (facilitating prostration) is still fulfilled.
The same logic applies to ruku and sujud; if unable, they are performed proportionately.

Cause Established for the Original Applies to the Subordinate
States a general legal maxim of causation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'cause established for the original applies to the subordinate' to cases in salah and other areas of fiqh.

Core Idea
When a legal cause applies to the primary subject (imam), it also applies to its subordinate (follower).

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim. It states that once a cause (sabab) that necessitates a ruling is established for the original entity, it is also considered established for its subordinate. This principle is especially prominent in the chapter of salah regarding sajdah al-sahw, but it applies across fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key components
Break down the maxim into its three core components: sabab (cause that obligates a ruling), asl (original/principal), and tabi (subordinate/follower). Understand that the cause must be established for the asl to then apply to the tabi.
The sabab is not just any cause; it is the 'mujib' (obligating) cause.
2
Understand the relationship
The maxim asserts that the establishment of the cause for the asl is automatically considered establishment for the tabi. No separate cause needs to exist for the tabi.
It does not say the cause arises in the tabi; rather it is a legal extension.
3
Recognize the scope of asl and tabi
In the primary example from salah, the imam is the asl and the followers are the tabi. More broadly, asl and tabi can be any such pair where one is primary and the other dependent.
This principle is applied in inheritance, contracts, and other areas.
4
Apply to the salah example
In congregational prayer, if a cause for sajdah al-sahw (e.g., forgetting a wajib) arises for the imam, the followers must also perform sajdah al-sahw because the cause is established in the imam.
Conversely, if the cause arises only in a follower, it does not affect the imam and the follower does not perform sajdah al-sahw (according to the preferred Hanafi view).
5
Extend to other areas
Consider other fiqh chapters where this maxim applies. For example, in inheritance, if a cause affecting the share of an heir is established for the ancestor, it may affect the descendant.
Always identify who is the asl and who is the tabi in the specific context.

Commentary
This is a general legal maxim used in Hanafi fiqh. For example, in prayer, the imam is the asl and the followers are tabi'. So the cause for sajdah al-sahw in the imam applies to the followers. This principle is applied in many areas of fiqh.

What is Near to Something Takes its Ruling
States a principle of legal analogy based on proximity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle of legal proximity (ma yaqrubu) and apply it to examples in salah.

Core Idea
What is near to a thing takes its ruling: proximity creates legal connection.

This passage presents a fundamental legal maxim in Hanafi fiqh: something closely connected to another entity shares its ruling. This principle is used for analogical extension, particularly in salah. Understanding it helps you see how Hanafi jurists derive rulings for connected actions.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Arabic wording
Write and repeat the maxim: إِنَّ مَا يَقُرُبُ مِنَ الشَّيْءِ يَأْخُذُ حُكْمَهُ
Focus on the verb يَقُرُبُ (yaqrubu).
2
Understand the meaning
The maxim means that something closely connected to another thing shares its legal ruling. The connection can be physical or legal.
This is a qa'ida in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Recall the example of sajdah al-sahw
In the Hanafi school, the two sajdahs in sajdah al-sahw are considered one unit due to their proximity. If one is missed, the entire unit is not repeated; rather, the sajdah al-sahw is completed.
4
Apply to the follower (muqtadi)
The follower is near to the imam, so he takes the imam's ruling. If the imam performs sajdah al-sahw, the follower must also perform it.
5
Recognize the limits
The maxim does not apply to all near things; it only applies when there is a shar'i or 'urfi connection. For example, a person standing close to the prayer but not following does not take the imam's ruling.

A deficient act cannot substitute for the complete obligation.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim: a deficient performance does not fulfill a complete obligation. The principle applies across fiqh, from prayer to zakah, and helps distinguish between valid but deficient acts and acts that fail to meet the required standard. Understanding this maxim is key to assessing when an obligation is truly satisfied.

Understand the Maxim
Grasp the Arabic words: الناقص (deficient) refers to an act lacking a required element, and الكامل (complete) is the prescribed full form. The verb يتأدى means 'is performed' or 'fulfilled'.

Identify the Complete Form
For any obligation, determine what constitutes its complete form (arkān, shurūṭ). For example, the complete prayer includes qiyām, qirāʼah, rukūʻ, sujūd, etc.
The complete form may vary with capacity (e.g., sitting prayer for the ill).
3

Take a case where someone intentionally omits a pillar. For instance, omitting rukūʻ. Apply the maxim: the deficient act does not fulfill the obligation; the prayer must be repeated.
This differs from accidental omission where sajdah al-sahw may compensate.

Sajdah al-Tilawah and the Principle of Interference
States principle about prostrations of recitation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle of tadakhul for sajdah al-tilawah and apply it to determine when one prostration suffices for multiple recitations.

Core Idea
Multiple sajdah verses can be satisfied by one prostration.

This lesson introduces a concise yet powerful principle from the Hanafi school: sajdah al-tilawah (the prostration of recitation) is built on interference (tadakhul). This means that when multiple causes for prostration occur—such as reciting several sajdah verses—the obligations overlap, and a single prostration can fulfill them all. Understanding this rule prevents unnecessary repetition and clarifies how to act when multiple sajdah verses are recited in one sitting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Recognize that the subject is sajdah al-tilawah, a prostration performed upon reciting or hearing a specific verse of sajdah. In the Hanafi school, it is wajib (obligatory) when the conditions are met.
The ruling of wajib is based on the hadith 'When the son of Adam recites a sajdah verse, the Shaytan withdraws weeping...' (Muslim).
2
Understand the Meaning of Tadakhul
The term 'al-tadākhul' means interference or overlapping. In fiqh, it refers to multiple legal causes being satisfied by a single performance. For sajdah al-tilawah, it means that if multiple sajdah verses are recited or heard, one prostration can cover all of them, rather than requiring a separate prostration for each.
Tadakhul is a general Hanafi principle applied also in ghusl, tayammum, and other acts.
3
Apply to Multiple Recitations in One Sitting
Consider a scenario where a person recites three different sajdah verses in one sitting. According to the principle, one sajdah after the last verse is sufficient to discharge the obligation for all three. The same applies if the same verse is recited multiple times.
This is the preferred Hanafi view. Some other schools require separate prostrations for each verse.
4
Consider the Limitations of Tadakhul
Tadakhul only applies when the multiple causes occur within a single session (majlis). If the recitations are separated by a significant break or a different prayer, separate prostrations may be required. The exact boundary is determined by 'urf (custom) and the mufti's judgment.
This nuance is not in the source text but is derived from later Hanafi works. The source focuses on the core principle.

Nullification of Homelands in Travel Rulings
States rules for nullification of homeland in travel rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of homeland nullification to determine when a traveler becomes a resident for the purpose of shortening prayer.

Core Idea
An original homeland is only nullified by another original; travel never nullifies it.

In Hanafi fiqh, the concept of homeland (watan) determines whether a person is considered a traveler (musafir) or a resident (muqim), which directly affects the obligation of shortening prayer (qasr). This lesson focuses on the two main types: original homeland (watan al-asli) and residence homeland (watan al-iqamah). Understanding how each is nullified is essential for correctly applying the rules of travel.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two homelands
First, recall that Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between watan al-asli (original homeland, e.g., place of birth or permanent abode) and watan al-iqamah (residence homeland, where one intends to stay for 15 days or more).
A third type, watan al-safar (travel), exists but is not discussed here.
2
State the nullification rule for original homeland
The original homeland is only nullified by establishing another original homeland. Travel—regardless of length—does not nullify it.
This means that even if you travel and stay elsewhere for years, your original homeland remains intact until you adopt a new permanent home.
3
State the nullification rule for residence homeland
The residence homeland is nullified by three things: by another residence homeland (e.g., moving to a new city for 15+ days), by travel (leaving with the intention to travel, even if short), and by returning to the original homeland.
Leaving a place of residence intentionally nullifies it immediately.
4
Apply to a concrete scenario
Take a person born in City A (original homeland) who moves to City B for a new job intending to settle permanently. His original homeland becomes City B (he acquires a new original). If he later travels to City A for a week, he is a traveler there because City A is no longer his homeland.
If instead he moved to City B for a short-term project (less than 15 days), his original homeland remains City A and he is a traveler in City B.
5
Connect to prayer rulings
The determination of homeland directly affects whether one shortens prayer. A person who is in his original homeland or residence homeland performs full prayer; a traveler shortens.
Thus, correct identification of nullification is needed to perform salah correctly.

States purposes of adhan and iqamah, ruling for traveler

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the distinct purposes of adhan and iqamah and apply the Hanafi ruling that a traveler may omit adhan when with companions.

Core Idea
Adhan summons the absent; iqamah signals the start – a traveler with company may omit adhan.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes a key principle: the adhan is meant to summon those not present, while the iqamah announces the actual commencement of prayer. From this principle, the author derives a practical ruling for a traveler whose companions are already with him. The reasoning (ta'lil) is direct: if the purpose of adhan is to gather people, then when everyone is already present, the need for adhan falls away. Iqamah, however, is still needed to signal the start, as everyone present needs that cue.

Guided Path
1
State the two purposes
First, note the distinct functions: adhan is 'lil-istiḥḍār' (for summoning the absent), iqamah is 'li-iʿlām al-iftitāḥ' (for announcing the commencement). This is the foundational distinction.
The Arabic terms are precise: istiḥḍār (استحضار) vs. iʿlām al-iftitāḥ (إعلام الافتتاح).
2
Identify the ruling for the traveler
The text says: if a traveler with companions omits adhan and only gives iqamah, it is permissible (jāʾiz). Abandoning both is makruh. The ruling is derived from the purpose.
It is better (afḍal) to say both, but permissible to drop adhan.

The proof is the statement: 'al-adhān li-istiḥḍār al-ghāʾibīn wa al-rufqatu ḥāḍirūn' (the adhan is for summoning the absent, and the companions are present). This is a direct application of the purpose: when the purpose is already fulfilled, the act is no longer needed.
This is a qiyās based on the ʿilla of 'presence of those who would be summoned'.
4

The reasoning is that the adhan's objective is informational: to bring people to the prayer. When all are already together, that objective is met, so the adhan is unnecessary. The iqamah, by contrast, signals the start, which everyone needs regardless.
This reasoning also explains why adhan before time is invalid – it would be misleading (tajhīl) rather than informing.

While the Hanafi position as given in al-Hidayah is clear, other schools may require adhan even when traveling with companions. The passage does not detail the opposition, but the ruling is mukhtalaf fīh.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilāf) is that a traveler following other schools must give adhan.

The Legal Maxim: A Quarter Stands for the Whole (Rub' Yahki Hikayat al-Kamal)
Definition of a key Hanafi maxim with fiqh application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim 'al-rub'u yahki hikayat al-kamal', apply it to the ruling on covering the shin during prayer, and distinguish the Hanafi threshold from other schools.

Core Idea
A quarter of a limb is legally treated as the whole in many rulings, including prayer validity.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that appears repeatedly across ibadat and muamalat. The maxim states that a quarter of something stands in for the whole in many legal assessments. The specific case discussed is the exposure of a free woman's shin during prayer: if a quarter or more is uncovered, the prayer becomes defective and must be repeated (wajib al-i'adah). Understanding this principle helps you grasp the Hanafi approach to minimal thresholds and their consistent application across fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the maxim and its literal meaning
The Arabic maxim 'إِنَّ الرُّبْعَ يَحْكِي حِكَايَةَ الْكَمَالِ' translates as 'Indeed, the quarter recounts the narration of the complete.' The key term 'yahki' implies that the quarter legally represents or stands in for the whole.
2
Identify the fiqh application in the passage
The immediate ruling: if a free woman prays with a quarter of her shin exposed, repeating the prayer is wajib. If less than a quarter is exposed, no repetition is required. Note that the ruling applies to the 'awrah (parts that must be covered in prayer).
The passage specifies 'free woman' because the 'awrah of a slave woman may be different in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the analogical reasoning (qiyas) behind the maxim
The author of al-Hidayah supports this ruling by citing two analogies: (1) wiping a quarter of the head in wudu suffices for the whole head; (2) shaving a quarter of the head in ihram incurs the same penalty (dam) as shaving the whole head. These parallels show that a quarter is consistently treated as equivalent to the whole.
These analogies are not the sole source; the maxim is inductively derived from many such instances.
4
Apply the maxim to the shin case and derive the ruling
Since exposing the entire shin during prayer invalidates the prayer (making repetition wajib), and a quarter is legally the whole, exposing a quarter of the shin also triggers the same wajib repetition. Less than a quarter does not reach the threshold.
This is a straightforward tatbiq (application) of the maxim.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school uses quarter as the minimum threshold. Other schools, such as the Shafi'i, hold that any exposure, no matter how small, invalidates the prayer. This difference stems from varying usul principles about the interpretation of commands to cover.
The Hanafi position is based on the quarter maxim and istihsan in some cases.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Exposure during prayer: Is a quarter 'much' or half? The answer depends on whether you define 'much' relatively or absolutely.

The Quarter Stands for the Whole: Maxim and Applications
States and proves the legal maxim that a quarter substitutes for the whole

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'al-rub' yaqumu maqam al-kull', apply it to cases of impurity in garments and hair shaving during Ihram, and distinguish the intra-Hanafi positions on its scope.

Core Idea
When a quarter is pure, it legally stands for the whole – necessity makes the fraction sufficient.

This lesson introduces a core Hanafi legal maxim: one quarter of a thing stands in place of the whole. The maxim is applied in two key areas: first, in Hajj, where cutting the nails of one foot (less than a quarter of nails) obligates a blood sacrifice, and second, in prayer, where a garment with at least a quarter clean can be worn instead of praying naked. However, the precise boundary of this rule – what happens when less than a quarter is clean – generated a well-known dispute between Imam Muhammad and the two elder imams, Abu Hanifah and Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim and its basic statement
The text opens with 'Verily, one quarter of a thing stands in the place of all of it.' This is the foundational qā'ida. Note that it is not absolute, but applies in situations of necessity (ḍarūra).
This maxim is a sub-maxim (ḍābṭ) within the larger principle of necessity.
2
Analyze the first application: Hajj nail clipping
The text analogizes clipping nails of one foot to shaving a quarter of the head. Both are a quarter of the relevant body part, so the quarter maxim triggers a blood sacrifice (dam).
The analogy (qiyās) is: as shaving a quarter of the head requires dam, so does clipping nails of one foot.
3
Analyze the second application: prayer garment impurity
If a garment is impure but a quarter or more is clean, one must pray in it – the clean quarter substitutes for the whole. Praying naked is invalid. This is the clear application of the maxim.
The 'necessity' here is the absence of clean clothes or water to remove impurity.
4

Superiority of Inquiry over Ijtihad (Taharri)
Explains the principle with qibla application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that inquiry (istikhbar) from a knowledgeable person takes precedence over personal ijtihad (taharri) when determining the qibla, and state the ruling that taharri is impermissible when inquiry is possible.

Core Idea
Inquiry from an expert is superior to personal reasoning; taharri is only allowed when no knowledgeable person is available.

In matters of religious practice, pursuing certainty is always better than relying on personal estimation. This lesson presents a key Hanafi principle: when a knowledgeable person is available to inform you of the qibla direction, you must ask; you may not resort to your own ijtihad. The rationale is that a report (khabar) binds everyone, whereas personal reasoning only binds the individual.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that you are uncertain about the qibla direction. This is a common situation when traveling or in a new location.
2
Check availability of a knowledgeable person
Determine if there is someone present—a local resident, a scholar, or a trustworthy person—who knows the qibla direction. If such a person is available, proceed to step 3. If not, skip to step 4.
The person must be knowledgeable and trustworthy.
3
Apply the inquiry rule
Since inquiry (istikhbar) is superior to personal ijtihad (taharri), you must ask that person for the qibla direction. It is not permissible (makruh tahrimi) to perform taharri in this case.
The principle: al-istikhbaru fawqa al-taharri.
4
If no knowledgeable person is available
When no one can inform you, then taharri becomes permissible. Use signs like the sun, stars, or landmarks to form a dominant opinion (ghalabah al-zann) and face that direction.
Taharri is a valid method only when stronger evidence is absent.
5
Understand the reasoning
The basis: a report (khabar) from another person is binding not only for you but for all who hear it, while taharri is only binding for the individual. Therefore, the stronger evidence takes precedence.
Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: 'the superior is not abandoned for the inferior.'

Acting on Changed Ijtihad During Prayer: Prospective Only
Ruling-with-proof: Changed ijtihad during prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that a change in ijtihad during salah obligates turning to the new direction without invalidating previously performed rak'ahs, and explain the analogy with naskh.

Core Idea
New ijtihad obligates future compliance without undoing past valid acts.

Imagine you begin your salah facing one direction based on your best reasoning (ijtihad), then during the prayer you realise you were mistaken or your opinion changes. What should you do? The Hanafi school gives a clear answer: immediately turn to the correct direction and continue, without repeating what you have already prayed. This ruling is grounded in a powerful analogy – just as abrogation (naskh) only affects future rulings, changed ijtihad only affects the remaining part of the prayer. Let us walk through the legal reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Ruling
Locate the key statement: acting upon ijtihad in the future (mustaqbal) is obligatory (wajib) without invalidating (naqd) past performance. Recognise the two-part rule: (1) wajib to follow new ijtihad, (2) past act remains valid.
The text explicitly uses 'wajib' – omitting the turn deliberately makes the prayer invalid.
2
Understand the Scenario
The passage outlines two cases: (a) someone starts salah with tahari and realises during the prayer that they were facing the wrong direction, and (b) someone's ijtihad changes to a new direction. In both, the rule is the same: turn to the new qibla without breaking the prayer.
The Urdu explanation gives these examples to clarify the abstract principle.
3
Trace the Proof: Qiyas with Naskh
The text states that the evidence (dalil) for ijtihad is like the evidence for naskh (abrogation). Just as naskh only takes effect for the future and does not undo past acts that were valid under the previous ruling, similarly, changed ijtihad only affects future actions of the prayer.
This analogy is key to understanding why the past rak'ahs are not invalidated.
4
Apply to a Concrete Example from the Commentaries
Consider the report from Al-'Inayah: if someone prays one rak'ah facing east, then his ijtihad shifts to west, so he prays the second rak'ah facing west, the salah is valid. Each rak'ah was based on ijtihad, and the change does not retroactively nullify the first.
Badai' al-Sanai' adds: 'What is executed by ijtihad is not overturned by a similar ijtihad.'

Turning During Prayer When Ijtihad Changes
Explains ruling when ijtihad direction changes mid-prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a change in ijtihad during prayer requires turning without nullifying preceding rak'ahs, and explain the basis of the analogy to naskh.

Core Idea
Ijtihad in prayer direction only affects the future; past actions remain valid.

In prayer, if one's ijtihad about the qiblah direction changes, the Hanafi school holds that one must turn immediately without nullifying the already performed portion. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind this rule, using the analogy of naskh (abrogation).

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The scenario is a person who started prayer based on ijtihad (taharr) and then during prayer either realizes an error or his ijtihad changes to another direction. The ruling is that it is wajib to turn to the new direction without breaking the prayer, and the previous portion remains valid.
2
Examine the dalil: analogy to naskh
The proof cited is qiyas (analogy) to abrogation (naskh). The text states: 'the evidence of ijtihad is like the evidence of abrogation' (لِمَا ذَكَرْنَا أَنَّ دَلِيلَ الِاجْتِهَادِ بِمَنْزِلَةِ دَلِيلِ النَّسْخِ). This is a key Hanafi principle.
The analogy is based on the shared characteristic that both ijtihad and naskh are sources of ruling that apply prospectively.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
Just as abrogation does not invalidate actions performed under the previous ruling but only affects future actions, so too ijtihad does not invalidate previous prayer portions. The effect is purely prospective: 'the effect of abrogation appears in the future, not in the past.'
This explains why the previous rak'ahs remain valid despite the change in direction.
4
Apply the principle to the expansion case
The text expands to a scenario where a person prays one rak'ah to each of the four directions as his ijtihad changes each time. This is permissible because each rak'ah was performed according to the ijtihad of that moment, and since ijtihad is prospective, each stage is valid.
This illustrates the principle: 'what is established by ijtihad is not nullified by a similar ijtihad.'
5
Recognize the consequence of delay
If a person deliberately delays turning for some time, the prayer becomes invalid (fasid). This underscores the obligatory nature of immediate compliance with the new ijtihad.
The invalidity is due to intentional violation of a wajib element, not because the previous rak'ahs are nullified.

The Principle That Conjunction (Atf) Implies Difference (Mughayarah)
Uses linguistic principle of atf to prove takbir is condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi proof that takbir tahrimah is a condition (shart) using the principle of atf, and compare it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Conjunction (atf) in the Quran proves takbir is separate from prayer — thus it is a condition, not a pillar.

In this lesson, we examine a key linguistic-legal principle used by the Hanafi school to settle a major dispute: Is the opening takbir a condition (shart) or a pillar (rukn) of prayer? The Hidayah employs the principle that grammatical conjunction implies difference (al-atf yaqtadi al-mughayarah) to derive the ruling from the Quranic verse. Understanding this principle will sharpen your ability to see how linguistic analysis shapes fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The issue is whether the opening takbir (takbir tahrimah) is a condition (shart) for the validity of prayer or a pillar (rukn) integral to it.
This classification has practical consequences for the ruling on prayer if the takbir is omitted.
2
State the Hanafi position and their proof
The Hanafi school holds it is a condition. Their proof is the Quranic verse 'wa dhakara isma rabbihi fasalla' (And he mentions the name of his Lord and prays).
The verse is found in Surah al-A'la (87:15) or similar.
3
Explain the linguistic principle
The principle is 'al-atf yaqtadi al-mughayarah' – conjunction (here the letter 'fa') implies that the two conjoined items are distinct and separate.
This is a well-known usul al-fiqh principle in the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the principle to the verse
In the verse, 'dhakara' (mention) is conjoined to 'salla' (pray) by 'fa'. Therefore, the mentioning (which includes takbir) is distinct from the prayer itself. The takbir is not part of the prayer but external to it, hence a condition.
This separates the act of takbir from the act of prayer.
5
Contrast with the Shafi'i view and note the practical consequence
Imam al-Shafi'i considers takbir tahrimah a pillar (rukn) because it is an essential component. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) appears in cases of omission: according to Hanafis, if the takbir is omitted, the prayer is invalid because the condition is missing; according to Shafi'is, the prayer is invalid because a pillar is missing. In both cases, the prayer must be repeated, but the conceptual basis differs.
The thamarah al-khilaf also affects how scholars discuss the integrity of the prayer.

Takbir Tahrima as a Condition: The Conjunction Argument
Proving takbir tahrima as condition using conjunction rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi linguistic principle of conjunction (al-‘atf) to explain why takbir tahrima is classified as a condition (shart) rather than a pillar (rukn) and identify the key evidence from the Quran.

Core Idea
Takbir tahrima is a condition, not a pillar—because joining 'prayer' to 'remembrance' in the Quran proves they are distinct acts.

In Hanafi fiqh, the opening takbir (takbir tahrima) is considered a condition for the validity of prayer, not a pillar like bowing or prostration. This lesson unpacks the unique linguistic proof used by the Hanafi school: the rule that grammatical conjunction (al-‘atf) requires difference between the conjoined items. We will see how a single Quranic verse from Surah al-A‘la (87:15) becomes the foundation for this ruling, distinguishing the Hanafi position from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling on takbir tahrima
State clearly that the Hanafi school holds takbir tahrima to be a condition (shart) of prayer, external to its structure, and not a pillar (rukn).
This is the position found in the Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Understand the linguistic rule: al-‘atf requires al-mughayara
Grasp that in Arabic grammar, when two items are joined by a conjunction (like 'fa' or 'wa'), they must be distinct in meaning or identity. This is the core tool for proving the ruling.
The rule is: 'Inna al-‘atf yaqtadi al-mughayara'.
3
Examine the Quranic dalil
Analyze the verse: 'Wa dhakara isma rabbihi fa salla' (He mentioned the name of his Lord and prayed). The verb 'dhakara' (remembrance) is conjoined to 'salla' (prayer) by 'fa'.
Reference: Surah al-A‘la (87:15).
4
Apply the rule to the verse
If takbir tahrima were a part of prayer (rukn), then 'dhakara' (which includes the takbir as remembrance) would be part of 'salla', making the conjunction join the whole (prayer) to its part (takbir). This would violate the requirement of difference, as it would be joining a thing to itself. Therefore, takbir must be external to prayer—a condition.
This reasoning excludes the possibility that takbir is a pillar.
5
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Contrast with the Shafi‘i position, which considers takbir tahrima a rukn. The Hanafi school responds that the conjunction argument prevents that classification, and the practical difference is that missing the takbir invalidates the prayer entirely (since a missing condition cannot be compensated like a missed sunnah).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Adhan in Persian is valid if customarily recognized; the purpose is notification, not language.

This passage addresses a key question: can the adhan (call to prayer) be given in a language other than Arabic? While Arabic is the original and preferred, the Hanafi school, through a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa, considers custom (ta'aruf) as a determining factor. This lesson unpacks the principle, its proof, and its practical implications for diverse communities.

Guided Path

Understand the principle of ta'aruf
Ta'aruf means customary recognition. The validity of adhan depends on whether the local community recognizes the uttered words as adhan.

The condition is that people must know that the utterance is adhan. If they do not, the purpose (notification) fails, and the adhan is invalid.

This shows that different acts have different levels of strictness based on their legal category.
5
Evaluate the practical outcome
The preferred practice is Arabic, but if custom permits non-Arabic, it is valid. This balances the Sunnah with practical necessity.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) follows the narration allowing non-Arabic adhan when customarily recognized.

Ta'awwudh Follows Recitation, Not Thana' (Abu Hanifa & Muhammad)

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ta'awwudh is tied to recitation, so only those who recite the Qur'an in prayer recite it.

In prayer, the ta'awwudh (seeking refuge) is an opening act before Qur'an recitation. But who exactly recites it? The answer depends on whether one personally recites the Qur'an during the prayer. This lesson focuses on the Hanafi position as held by Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, contrasting the masbuq (latecomer) with the muqtadi (follower who joined from the start).

Guided Path

Identify the act and its context
Read the passage and note that it discusses ta'awwudh in relation to the two types of followers: masbuq (latecomer) and muqtadi (one who joined from the beginning).

The key principle: ta'awwudh is dependent on Qur'an recitation (qira'ah), not on the opening praise (thana'). Therefore, whoever recites the Qur'an in prayer recites ta'awwudh.
The phrase 'at the two ends' refers to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (al-tarafayn), indicating this is their joint view.

The muqtadi who joined from the beginning does not recite the Qur'an behind the imam; he only recites thana' (and other remembrances). Therefore, he does not recite ta'awwudh.
The passage explicitly says: 'the muqtadi does not recite it because he says thana' and does not recite (Qur'an).'

Abu Yusuf may have held a different view (possibly allowing ta'awwudh with thana' as well). The text presents the view of the two as the relied-upon position, supported by later sources like Majma' al-Anhur and Al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya.

Ta'awwudh Is Tied to Recitation, Not Thana
Stating the Hanafi rule on ta'awwudh with an expansion on hand placement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that ta'awwudh follows recitation, identify which worshippers recite it, and apply the rule to distinguish between masbuq and muqtadi.

Core Idea
Ta'awwudh is only for those who recite Quran in prayer; masbuq recites it, muqtadi does not.

In this lesson, we examine a key principle from the Hidayah regarding the recitation of ta'awwudh (seeking refuge) in prayer. The Hanafi school ties this sunnah act to actual Quran recitation, not merely to the act of praying. This leads to a clear distinction between the masbuq (latecomer) and the muqtadi (follower). We will also explore a related rule about hand placement during different standing positions, which follows a similar logic of being tied to prescribed dhikr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
The author states that ta'awwudh is a subordinate (tab') of recitation (qira'ah), not of thana (opening praise). Understand that this means ta'awwudh is only recited when one is about to recite Quran in prayer.
The principle is attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (al-tarafayn).
2
Apply to the masbuq
A masbuq (latecomer) must recite Quran when making up missed rak'ahs. Since recitation is wajib for him, ta'awwudh is sunnah before that recitation.
The masbuq recites ta'awwudh in his qada' rak'ahs.
3
Apply to the muqtadi
A muqtadi (follower who joined from the beginning) does not recite Quran behind the imam. Therefore, ta'awwudh is not sunnah for him.
The muqtadi only recites thana and remains silent.
4
Note the broader rule on hand placement
The passage expands to a general principle: any standing (qiyam) that contains a prescribed dhikr (like thana, qunut, janazah) requires clasping the hands (i'timad), while standing without such dhikr (like qawmah after ruku, between Eid takbirs) requires letting the hands hang (irsal).
This is consistent with the idea that actions follow the presence of dhikr.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof explaining the Hanafi position on ta'awwudh (seeking refuge) in prayer. The core principle from the Hidayah is that ta'awwudh is connected to recitation (qira'ah), not to the opening praise (thana). The commentator clarifies that the two 'sides' (tarafayn) refer to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. According to them, ta'awwudh is recited only when recitation of the Quran is wajib upon the worshipper.
