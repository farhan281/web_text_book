Cause: If Not Repeated, Effect Not Repeated
Legal maxim from the book of Hajj on the non-repetition of acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-sabab idha lam yatakarrar lam yatakarrar al-musabbab' to determine when a legal obligation is repeated.

Core Idea
A legal effect occurs only as many times as its cause occurs—if the cause is single, the effect is single.

This maxim from the Hanafi school governs the repetition of obligations (wujub). Understanding it prevents the mistake of requiring multiple acts when the underlying cause has not reoccurred. It is especially relevant in Hajj, but applies across all fiqh chapters. The cause (sabab) triggers the effect (musabbab); if the cause is not repeated, the effect does not repeat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and effect
In a given scenario, determine what the legal cause (sabab) is and what the effect (musabbab) is. For example, in Hajj, the cause is the existence of ability (istita'a) and the effect is the obligation to perform Hajj.
2
Confirm the cause's repetition
Check whether the cause has occurred more than once. If the cause is a one-time condition, it is not repeated once the effect is realized.
3
Apply the maxim
If the cause occurred once, the effect occurs once; if the cause repeats, the effect may repeat. But note: after the effect is fulfilled, the cause is considered satisfied and not repeated.
4
Note exceptions and nuances
Time-based obligations (e.g., daily prayers) have a repetitive cause (entry of time), so the effect repeats. The maxim applies to obligations triggered by a single occurrence of a cause.
5
Practice with Hajj
The classic application: Hajj becomes obligatory once because the cause (ability) is a one-time condition; after performance, the cause is not considered to recur, so Hajj is not obligatory again even if ability persists.
This is the mufta bihi Hanafi position.

This maxim means that legal effects depend on their causes. If the cause (sabab) occurs only once, the effect (musabbab) occurs only once. For example, if a person performs Hajj once, the obligation is fulfilled; the cause (having the means and ability) is not repeated unless it occurs again. Thus, Hajj is not required multiple times unless the cause reoccurs.

Use your knowledge of fiqh al-hajj.

A maxim comparing the role of Talbiyah in Hajj to Takbir in Salah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the analogy between talbiyah in ihram and takbir in salah, and explain the Hanafi ruling that talbiyah is wajib upon entering ihram.

Core Idea
Talbiyah is the takbir of Hajj—both signal the start of a sacred act.

This lesson examines a powerful Hanafi maxim that draws an analogy between talbiyah and takbir. Just as takbir marks the commencement of salah, talbiyah marks the entry into ihram. By understanding this comparison, you will grasp the legal status of talbiyah in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acts compared
Recognize that the maxim compares talbiyah in ihram (Hajj/Umrah) to takbir in salah. Both are utterances that initiate a state of worship.
2
State the function of each act
Takbir (saying Allahu Akbar) initiates salah; talbiyah (saying LabbaykAllahumma Labbayk) initiates the state of ihram. Both are declarations of intention and consecration.
3
Recall the Hanafi ruling for each
In the Hanafi school, takbir is a condition (shart) for salah, meaning salah is invalid without it. Talbiyah is wajib (obligatory) upon entering ihram. However, the maxim emphasizes functional similarity, not identical legal status.
Takbir is shart; talbiyah is wajib. Both are essential but with different consequences if omitted.
4
Appreciate the pedagogical purpose
The maxim serves as a memorization anchor, highlighting the importance of talbiyah by likening it to the well-known takbir. It teaches that talbiyah is not merely a recommended utterance but a key ritual act.
5
Apply the analogy to practice
When entering ihram, a pilgrim should recite talbiyah with awareness that it is as fundamental as takbir in salah. If omitted, the Hanafi ruling is that ihram is still valid but one must offer a sacrifice (dam) as compensation.

Maxim: Fixing a Time Removes Unrestrictedness
A maxim about the effect of fixing a time on an unrestricted state.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim and apply it to distinguish between unrestricted (mutlaq) and time-bound (muwaqqat) contracts in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Fixing a time transforms an open-ended contract into a limited one.

This maxim is a core principle of Hanafi contractual law. It teaches that when a specific duration is attached to an act or contract, the inherent openness (riqqah) is removed, meaning the contract no longer has the potential for continuity. Understanding this helps you identify when a contract becomes non-extendable or when a marriage (nikah) becomes invalid due to temporary stipulation.

Guided Path
1
State the maxim in Arabic and English
Memorize the exact wording: 'إِنَّ التَّوْقِيتَ يَذْهَبُ بِالرِّقَةِ' and its translation: 'Indeed, fixing a time takes away the unrestrictedness.'
This is a memorization anchor.
2
Identify the key terms
Understand that 'tawqit' means fixing a specific time or duration, and 'riqqah' means the quality of being unrestricted or open-ended.
3
Apply to a contract: lease (ijara)
In a lease contract, if a specific term is stated (e.g., one year), the lease ends after that term. The unrestricted potential for continuation is removed.
4
Apply to marriage (nikah)
In marriage, a temporary stipulation (e.g., marriage for one month) is invalid in Hanafi fiqh because marriage is meant to be unrestricted (permanent). Fixing a time removes the unrestrictedness, and the contract becomes void (batil).
Contrast with Mut'ah marriage which is not recognized.
5
Recognize the scope and exceptions
The maxim applies to contracts and actions where continuity is inherent. It does not apply to all actions; e.g., in ibadat, timing may be a condition rather than a restriction of an open-ended nature.

The Distinction Between Quranic and Khabar Wahid Obligations
A short maxim comparing two types of evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim and explain how the certainty of the proof source affects the fulfillment of an obligation in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Quranic obligations demand qat'i fulfillment; khabar wahid obligations allow zanni flexibility.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi maxim that distinguishes obligations based on the certainty of their evidence. The Quran (nass al-kitab) is mutawatir and certain in transmission, while a solitary hadith (khabar wahid) provides only probable knowledge. Consequently, the performance of a Quranic obligation is stricter and cannot be equated with one proven by a lesser proof. Understanding this maxim is crucial for correctly applying rulings in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sources
Read the maxim and locate the two types of evidence: 'nass al-kitab' (Quran text) and 'khabar al-wahid' (solitary report). Note that Quran evidence is qat'i (certain) while khabar wahid is zanni (probable).
Khabar wahid includes ahad hadith, but not mutawatir.
2
Understand the implication
The maxim states that an obligation proven by the Quran is 'not performed in the same way' as one proven by a solitary report. This means the method and certainty of fulfillment differ; a Quranic obligation requires a stricter, more definitive performance.
The word 'yataaddaa' (fulfilled) indicates the act of discharging the obligation.
3
Apply to an example
Consider Hajj: its obligation is from the Quran (Surah Al-Imran 3:97). The maxim implies that the performance of Hajj must be done with full certainty of fulfilling the Quranic command, and cannot be relaxed based on a solitary hadith that might suggest a lesser requirement.
This maxim is a principle of usul al-fiqh applied in Hanafi school.
4
Memorize the maxim
Commit the Arabic text to memory: 'إِنَّ مَا ثَبَتَ فَرْضِيَّتُهُ بِنَصِ الْكِتَابِ لَا يَتَأَدَّى كَمَا ثَبَتَ بِخَبَرِ الْوَاحِدِ'.
This is a key maxim for understanding Hanafi evidentiary hierarchy.

A maxim about the persistence of a ruling despite the removal of its cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the maxim and apply it to explain why a state of impurity persists until purification.

Core Idea
A ruling remains after its cause ends until a specific removal condition is met.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi legal maxim: once a ruling is established by a cause, it does not automatically disappear when the cause disappears. Instead, it continues until something else removes it. This principle has wide application, especially in matters of ritual purity and contracts.

Guided Path

Identify the maxim's components

2

The cause of impurity (hadath) triggers the ruling of being impure. When the cause ends, the ruling of impurity does not automatically lift; it continues until purification (taharah) is performed.
This is the classic illustration of the maxim.
3
Contrast with transient rulings
Some rulings are coterminous with their cause, but this maxim applies when the ruling is designed to persist. Distinguish between persistent and transient rulings.
The maxim does not apply when the ruling is explicitly tied to continuous existence of the cause.
4
Apply to a contract example
If a valid sale is concluded (sabab), the ruling of ownership persists even if the parties separate, until a rescission occurs.
Shows the maxim beyond purity.
5
Memorize the Arabic
Commit the maxim to memory as a foundational principle.
Use as a memorization anchor.

Core Idea
Each circuit of tawaf is like a rak'ah of salah.

This lesson introduces a foundational maxim that links the acts of tawaf to the well-known structure of salah. By understanding this analogy, you will see how many detailed rules of tawaf—such as the necessity of intention, sequential order, and compensation for omissions—are derived directly from the rules of prayer.

Only a tawaf after which sa'y follows necessitates returning to the Black Stone.

Guided Path

Identify the two key elements

A maxim stating the ruling on voluntary sa'y.

Core Idea
Sa'y cannot be performed as a standalone nafl act; it is only valid within Hajj or Umrah.

This maxim clarifies a crucial distinction between two rituals of Hajj and Umrah: tawaf and sa'y. While tawaf may be performed voluntarily at any time, sa'y is not legislated as an independent act of worship. Understanding this rule prevents incorrect practice and ensures one's efforts are directed toward prescribed acts.

Guided Path
1

Distinguishes between rukn and wajib using tawaf and taharah as example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a rukn and a wajib in Hanafi fiqh and explain the evidentiary basis for each classification.

Core Idea
A rukn requires certain proof; a wajib only requires probable proof.

Not all obligatory elements in an act of worship carry the same weight. Hanafi jurists draw a critical distinction between a rukn (pillar) and a wajib (obligation), based on the strength of the evidence that establishes them. This lesson unpacks that distinction through the example of tawaf in Hajj and the requirement of taharah (purity) during it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the issue

2
Recognize the evidentiary basis
The author states that a rukn (like the act of tawaf itself) must be proven by a definitive text (nass) that gives certain knowledge. A wajib (like taharah in tawaf) can be proven by a solitary report (khabar wahid) which gives only probable knowledge but still demands action.

This is a usul al-fiqh principle applied to fiqh rulings.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This lesson examines a fundamental Hanafi principle: 'al-qudrah bi-qudrat al-ghayr laysa bi-mu'tabar' (ability through another is not considered). Imam Abu Hanifa uses this principle to argue that a blind person, even if provided with a guide and expenses, is not obligated to perform Hajj because his ability depends on another. The Sahibayn disagree and analogize to a lost person who, once guided, performs Hajj himself. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the conditions for wajib al-Hajj: direct personal ability (qudra mubashirah) versus indirect ability (qudra bi-ghayr).

Guided Path

This dispute reflects a deeper usul difference: Abu Hanifa requires 'qudra mubashirah' (direct ability) for obligation; Sahibayn accept 'qudra bi-ghayr' (indirect ability) if it leads to actual performance.

Precedence of the Right of the Servant over the Right of Sharia
Establishing a legal principle that the right of the servant takes precedence over the right of Sharia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that haqq al-abd precedes haqq al-shar', explain its rational basis, and apply it to determine the condition for Hajj obligation.

Core Idea
The right of a needy servant outweighs the right of the Self-Sufficient Lord.

The principle that the rights of human beings (huquq al-ibad) take precedence over the rights of Allah (huquq Allah) is a cornerstone of Hanafi fiqh. In this passage, the author of al-Hidayah invokes this principle to establish a key condition for the obligation of Hajj: one must possess wealth beyond the maintenance of one's dependents. This lesson unpacks the rational basis of the principle and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Locate the core statement: 'Inna haqq al-abdi muqaddamun 'ala haqq al-shar''. Understand that 'haqq al-abd' refers to obligations owed to humans (e.g., maintenance), and 'haqq al-shar'' refers to obligations owed to Allah (e.g., Hajj).
2
Understand the rationale
The commentator explains the logic: humans are needy (muhtaj) and Allah is free of need (ghani). Therefore, the rights of the needy must be fulfilled before the rights of the Self-Sufficient.
This is a rational (aqli) proof adopted by the Sharia.
3
Apply to Hajj
Al-Marghinani applies the principle: for Hajj to be obligatory, one must have wealth beyond the maintenance of dependents (wife and children) for the entire journey. This is a condition of obligation (shart al-wujub), not validity.
The obligation is suspended until the human right is secured.
4
Note the scope
The principle applies to all financial and physical obligations. It does not cancel the obligation entirely but postpones it until the human right is fulfilled.
5
Recognize the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i does not condition Hajj obligation on having wealth beyond dependents' maintenance; he interprets istita'ah broadly. This is a legitimate difference.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that under Hanafi, a person barely able to support his family is not obligated, while under Shafi'i he might be.

The Husband's Right Does Not Override Obligatory Duties
Ruling with proof: applying a general principle to obligatory Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a wife may perform obligatory Hajj without her husband's permission if she has a mahram, and distinguish this from supererogatory acts where permission is required.

Core Idea
Obedience to Allah through obligatory duties is never overridden by a husband's right to obedience.

This passage from Al-Hidayah introduces a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh: a husband's right to obedience does not extend to preventing his wife from fulfilling obligatory religious duties. The commentary applies this specifically to Hajj, but the principle covers all fara'id—prayer, fasting, Zakat. Understanding this boundary is crucial for correctly balancing marital rights and religious obligations, especially in matters of travel and worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Principle
Read the opening phrase: 'إِنَّ حَقَّ الزَّوْجِ لَا يَظْهَرُ فِى حَقِّ الْفَرَائِضِ' — This is the legal axiom. Understand that 'haqq al-zawj' is the husband's right of obedience, and 'haqq al-fara'id' refers to the claims of obligatory acts. The principle states that the husband's right does not manifest in the realm of obligatory duties.
This does not negate the husband's rights in other matters; it prioritizes Allah's commands over the husband's in cases of conflict.
2
Identify the Specific Ruling for Obligatory Hajj
The text then applies the principle: if a wife has a mahram (a male relative she can never marry), she may go for obligatory Hajj even without her husband's permission. Trace the condition: 'إِذَا كَانَ لَهَا مَحْرَمٌ' — the presence of a mahram is essential. Without a mahram, she cannot go even for obligatory Hajj.
The mahram's role is to ensure safety during travel, not to grant permission.
3
Analyze the Dalil (Proof)
The proof cited is the hadith: ' لَا طَاعَةَ لِمَخْلُوقِ فِي مَعْصِيَّةِ الْخَالِقِ ' (No obedience to a creature in disobedience to the Creator). Since preventing an obligatory act is disobedience to Allah, the wife must not obey a command that would cause her to miss a fard act.
The thubut (transmission) of this hadith is zanni, but its dalalah (indication) is qati (clear).
4
Differentiate Supererogatory Acts
The commentary explicitly states that in supererogatory (nafl) acts, the husband's right takes precedence. He can prevent her from voluntary Hajj, voluntary fasting, etc. This is because supererogatory acts are not obligatory, so the husband's need for companionship and obedience outweighs the lesser benefit of nafl worship.
This distinction is crucial for understanding the scope of the husband's authority.

The Principle of Not Specifying Supplications in Hajj
Explains why Imam Muhammad did not specify Hajj supplications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that specifying fixed supplications for Hajj scenes removes heartfelt tenderness, and identify its application in Imam Muhammad's non-specification.

Core Idea
Fixing specific duas for the Kaaba reduces spontaneous humility; the heart's softness is lost through limitation.

This lesson explores a subtle fiqh principle from the Hanafi school about supplication during Hajj. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani deliberately did not specify particular duas for viewing the Kaaba, because he held that fixing formulas removes the heart's tenderness (riqqah). The goal of this approach is to encourage heartfelt, spontaneous devotion rather than mechanical recitation. Understanding this deepens one's appreciation of how the Hanafi tradition values spiritual presence over rigid ritualism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Understand the phrase 'at-tawqīt yadhabu bi ar-riqqati' – specifying time or content removes tenderness. This is the core maxim being advanced.
Tawqit refers to fixing a specific supplication for a specific time or place.
2
Locate the Case
Imam Muhammad in Al-Mabsut did not prescribe any particular supplication for the 'sights of Hajj' (mushāhid al-ḥajj), including the first view of the Kaaba.
The case shows the practical application of the principle.
3
Connect Principle to Case
Why did Imam Muhammad refrain from specifying? Because specification would remove riqqah – the soft, humble state of the heart that is the very purpose of du'a.
The objective is khushu' and heartfelt presence.
4
Recognize the Nuance
The text adds: 'If one seeks blessing (tabarruk) from transmitted supplications, that is good.' This indicates that using established formulas is permissible, but not as a mandatory specification.
The principle discourages rigidity, not the use of blessed texts.
5
Apply to Other Worship Contexts
Consider other moments in worship where spontaneity might be preferable, e.g., during sajdah in prayer or when making du'a after adhan. The same principle may apply.
Not all worship contexts require fixed supplications.

Avoiding Harm to Muslims Takes Priority Over Sunan
Usul principle: wajib overrides sunnah in Hajj context

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi hierarchy of wajib over sunnah and apply it to conflicts in Hajj rituals.

Core Idea
Avoid harming others is wajib; sunnah can be abandoned if it causes harm.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a fundamental usul principle: the obligation (wajib) of avoiding harm to a Muslim overrides a recommended act (sunnah). The specific context is touching or kissing the Black Stone during tawaf. Through the hadith of ‘Umar, we learn that the Prophet (ﷺ) prohibited jostling to reach the Stone. The text then outlines graduated alternatives, demonstrating that the sunnah is not an absolute imperative when it conflicts with a higher duty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acts and their legal categories
List the act of touching/kissing the Black Stone (sunnah) and the act of avoiding harm to others (wajib). Recognize that both are recommended or required, but at different levels.
The sunnah is encouraged; the wajib is obligatory—this hierarchy governs the ruling.
2
Locate and analyze the proof
Examine the hadith cited: the Prophet (ﷺ) told ‘Umar not to jostle because he was strong and would harm the weak. Extract the principle that any act causing harm to a Muslim is prohibited, even if it is a sunnah.
The hadith is ahad (single narration), so its thubut is zanni, but it is accepted in Hanafi fiqh for deriving obligations.
3
Trace the graduated alternatives
Follow the sequence: kiss directly → touch with hand and kiss hand → touch with object and kiss object → face the Stone with takbir and tahmid. Each step is a substitute that respects the wajib of avoiding harm.
The gradation shows that the sunnah is not abandoned but performed in a way that avoids harm.
4
Apply the hierarchy principle
State clearly: sunnah cannot override wajib. The wajib of avoiding harm takes precedence, so the pilgrim must choose a less harmful method.
This is a key Hanafi usul rule: a lesser good cannot be pursued at the expense of a greater obligation.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
What is the practical difference between a pilgrim who knows this rule and one who does not? The one who knows will not harm others and will still fulfill the sunnah through substitutes; the ignorant might endanger others or sin.
This also illustrates the Hanafi emphasis on social harmony in ritual acts.
