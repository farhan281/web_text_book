The Principle of Qada for Missed Wajib Acts
A foundational principle on qada of wajib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that qada of a missed wajib requires a dalil and apply it to distinguish between fard and wajib acts.

Core Idea
A missed wajib is not made up without specific evidence — qada requires a new command.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim regarding the make-up (qaḍāʾ) of missed obligatory acts. While fard acts generally require qada when missed, the Hanafi school holds that wajib acts — those established by probable proof — are not automatically made up; a specific textual evidence (dalīl) must command it. This principle shapes rulings on prayers like witr and Eid, and distinguishes the Hanafi approach from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Category
Determine that the act in question is a missed wajib (obligatory by zanni proof). The principle applies only to wajib, not fard.
Wajib examples: witr prayer, Eid prayer (according to some), sadaqat al-fitr (obligatory but not absolutely certain).
2
Understand the Default for Fard vs Wajib
In Hanafi usul, fard is established by qati' evidence; its qada is obligatory without further proof. Wajib is established by zanni evidence; its qada requires a separate dalil.
This reflects the Hanafi hierarchy of obligations.
3
Locate the Requirement for Qada
The principle states: 'A wajib when missed is not made up except by a dalil.' Without a specific proof, qada is not required.
The proof can be from Quran, Sunnah, or consensus, but must directly address qada.
4
Apply to Concrete Examples
Example: Witr prayer is wajib. A hadith commands its qada, so it is made up. Sunnah prayers (ghayr mu'akkadah) are not wajib, so no qada. Fard prayers (e.g., Zuhr) are automatically made up.
This shows the practical implication: not every missed nafl requires makeup.
5
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Shafi'is generally treat fard and wajib as synonymous; they require qada for any missed obligatory act without distinguishing based on certainty of proof.
This ikhtilaf arises from differing definitions of wajib.

Verily, the obligatory (wajib) when it is missed from its time is not made up (qada) except by a proof (dalil).
Source pages p. 6

Commentary
This is a foundational principle (qāʿida fiqhiyya) regarding qada (making up missed acts). The ruling: if a wajib act (an obligatory act that is slightly less binding than fard, according to the Hanafis) is missed, it cannot simply be performed later as qada without a specific textual evidence (dalil) commanding its makeup. This is because the original obligation was time-specific; qada requires a new command. For example, a missed witr prayer (wajib) is made up because of a hadith; but a missed sunnah prayer (not wajib) is generally not made up. The principle distinguishes between fard and wajib: fard defaults to qada, wajib requires proof.

Key Terms
الْوَاجِب (al-wājib): An obligatory act whose obligation is established by a zanni (probable) proof, such as witr prayer.
الْقَضَاء (al-qaḍāʾ): Performing an act after its prescribed time to fulfill an obligation missed.
الدَّلِيْل (ad-dalīl): Textual evidence (Quran or hadith) or consensus that specifically commands qada.

Defining Loud and Quiet Recitation: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on definitions of jahr and mukhafatah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-Hinduwani's and al-Karkhi's definitions of jahr and mukhafatah, and explain the practical consequence (thamarah) of each definition on the validity of prayer.

Core Idea
Two Hanafi definitions of 'loud' and 'quiet' recitation exist; the operative rule follows al-Hinduwani: jahr requires others to hear.

When you pray, the recitation in certain prayers must be audible (jahr) or quiet (mukhafatah). But what exactly counts as 'audible' or 'quiet'? This short passage records a difference of opinion between two early Hanafi scholars, al-Hinduwani and al-Karkhi. Understanding their definitions is not just academic—it determines whether your prayer is valid if, for example, you recite in a noisy environment or very softly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scholars and their definitions
Locate the names al-Faqih Abu Ja‘far al-Hinduwani and al-Karkhi. For each scholar, extract their definition of jahr (loud) and mukhafatah (quiet).
Al-Hinduwani gives two definitions; al-Karkhi gives minimum thresholds.
2
Compare the definitions of jahr
Al-Hinduwani says jahr is to make others hear; al-Karkhi says the minimum jahr is to make oneself hear. Which is more inclusive?
Al-Karkhi's definition is broader — he considers self-audibility sufficient for jahr.
3
Compare the definitions of mukhafatah
Al-Hinduwani says mukhafatah is to make oneself hear; al-Karkhi says the minimum mukhafatah is correct articulation of letters (without necessarily hearing oneself). Which is more restrictive?
Al-Karkhi's definition is broader — he allows valid mukhafatah even without self-audibility.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider a scenario: someone prays alone in a noisy room and recites so quietly that he himself cannot hear his own voice, but he moves his lips and forms the letters correctly. Is his recitation valid as mukhafatah? According to al-Hinduwani, no (because he did not make himself hear). According to al-Karkhi, yes (because he articulated letters correctly).
The thamarah is the validity of the prayer in that specific case.
5
Identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position
The Hanafi school generally follows al-Hinduwani's definition for jahr (others hear) and for mukhafatah (self-hear). Al-Karkhi's view is considered weaker in practice.
This means in fatwa, jahr requires audibility to at least one other person, and mukhafatah requires self-audibility.

Commentary
This passage records a difference of opinion between two early Hanafi scholars, al-Faqih Abu Ja‘far al-Hinduwani and al-Karkhi, regarding the definitions of jahr (loud recitation) and mukhafatah (quiet recitation) in prayer. According to al-Hinduwani, jahr requires that others hear you (i.e., audibility to someone else), while mukhafatah is merely hearing oneself. Al-Karkhi, however, defines the minimum jahr as hearing oneself (so speaking aloud enough for one's own ears) and the minimum mukhafatah as merely pronouncing the letters correctly (without necessarily hearing oneself). These definitions affect the validity of prayer in certain situations, such as when one recites very quietly or in noisy surroundings. The preferred opinion in the Hanafi school for jahr is that one must make others hear (al-Hinduwani), but for mukhafatah, both agree that self-hearing suffices.

الْجَهْر (al-jahr): Loud recitation; making others hear according to al-Hinduwani; self-audible according to al-Karkhi.

Following a Nafl Imam Invalidates Fard Prayer
Ruling on a fard performer following a nafl performer as imam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a muftarid follows a mutanaqqil and explain why it invalidates the prayer.

Core Idea
A fard prayer cannot be built upon a nafl prayer; following a nafl imam invalidates the follower's fard.

This lesson addresses a critical rule in congregational prayer: the validity of following an imam depends on matching intentions. The text states unequivocally that a person performing an obligatory (fard) prayer cannot validly follow an imam performing a supererogatory (nafl) prayer. This stems from the fiqhi principle that the stronger (fard) cannot be based on the weaker (nafl). Understanding this prevents unintentional invalidation of one's prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Roles
Clearly define al-muftarid (performer of fard) and al-mutanaqqil (performer of nafl). Recognize their intentions differ in obligation level.
The roles are defined by intention, not just the type of prayer being performed.
2
State the Ruling
The ruling is: such following (iqtida) is not permissible (la yajuz). It invalidates the follower's prayer.
Not merely disliked; the act itself is invalid.
3
Explain the Principle
The underlying principle: the stronger (fard) cannot be built upon the weaker (nafl). Just as a building cannot rest on a weak foundation.
This principle is used in other fiqh areas as well.
4
Apply to an Example
Example: A person starts praying Isha (fard) behind an imam who intends the sunnah of Isha. The follower's Isha is invalid and must be repeated.
The opposite case (nafl behind fard) is permissible.
5
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Imam al-Shafi'i allows such following under certain conditions, but the Hanafi position is absolute prohibition.
This illustrates a significant ikhtilaf with practical consequences.

Source Text

Verily, the following (iqtida') of one performing an obligatory prayer (muftarid) behind one performing a supererogatory prayer (mutanaqqil) is not permissible.

Commentary
This is a specific rule from the chapter of imamah (leading prayer). The ruling: a person who is performing a fard (obligatory) prayer cannot validly pray behind an imam who is performing a nafl (supererogatory) prayer. This is because the imam's intention is weaker (nafl) compared to the follower's (fard); and the stronger (fard) cannot be built upon the weaker (nafl). For example, if someone starts praying Isha behind an imam who intends the sunnah of Isha, the follower's prayer is invalid. However, the opposite (nafl behind fard) is permissible. This is based on the principle that 'the stronger cannot be built upon the weaker' (qawī lā yabnī ‘alā ḍa‘īf).

The Strong Cannot Be Built Upon the Weak
General principle that stronger acts cannot be based on weaker ones

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-qawiyyu la yabni ala al-da'ifi' and apply it to distinguish which types of obligations or acts can or cannot be based on weaker ones.

Core Idea
A fard cannot be built upon a nafl; the strong requires strong proof.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi legal maxim that governs how obligations relate to one another. Understanding this principle will help you evaluate whether a particular ruling or act can be validly established on the basis of a weaker act or evidence. We will explore its meaning, application in salah and purity, and its basis in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the strong and weak in the maxim
In this rule, 'strong' (al-qawī) refers to a fard obligation or a strong proof, while 'weak' (al-da'īf) refers to a nafl obligation or a weak proof.
2
Explain the meaning of 'built upon'
It means establishing, validating, or making something dependent on another. The rule says you cannot use a weak element as the foundation for a strong one.
3
Apply the principle to salah (iqtida example)
If a person intends to follow an imam in a fard prayer, their intention (niyyah) must be strong enough; they cannot base their fard prayer on a nafl imamate where the imam's intention is for nafl.
4
Apply the principle to purity (taharah)
A state of major impurity (janabah) cannot be removed by a wudu that is weak; ghusl is required.
5
Generalize to other areas
In evidentiary matters, a fard cannot be proven by a weak hadith; a stronger source (mutawatir or mashhur) is needed.

Source Text

إِنَّ الْقَوِيَّ لَا يَبْنِي عَلَى الضَّعِيْفِ

Source pages p. 6

Commentary
This is a broad legal maxim (qāʿida) applicable in many areas of fiqh. It means that a ruling or obligation that is 'strong' (e.g., a fard) cannot be established or made dependent on something that is 'weak' (e.g., a nafl, or a weak hadith). For example, the validity of a fard prayer cannot be based on a defective assumption. This principle underlies the previous rule about iqtida. It also applies to issues of purity: a state of impurity (strong) cannot be removed by a weak purification. In Hanafi usul, it supports the concept that fard acts have a higher evidentiary standard than nafl.

الضَّعِيْف (aḍ-ḍaʻīf): The weaker: nafl obligation, or a weak proof.

The Principle That a Thing Does Not Imply What Is Above It
Logical principle about levels of rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that a lower-level ruling does not imply the same ruling at a higher level, and apply it to examples such as nafl vs. fard in salah.

Core Idea
A lower level of ruling does not necessitate the same for a higher level; each level requires independent proof.

In Hanafi fiqh, rulings are graded by levels of obligation: fard, wajib, sunnah, mandub, etc. A common error is to assume that if something is allowed at a lower level (like nafl), it is automatically allowed at a higher level (like fard). This lesson explains why that assumption is false: the ruling at one level does not 'imply' or guarantee the ruling at a higher level. This principle guards against incorrect analogical reasoning (qiyas) from lower to higher categories.

Guided Path
1
State the Principle
Memorise the Arabic expression: إِنَّ الشَّيْءَ لَا يَتَضَمَّنُ مَا هُوَ فَوْقَهُ. Understand its plain meaning: a thing does not imply what is above it.
Here 'above' means a higher level of obligation or legal value.
2
Identify the Core Idea
Recognise that the principle denies any necessary entailment from a lower-level ruling to a higher-level ruling. For example, permission in nafl prayer does not create permission in fard prayer.
This is a negative principle: it tells us what is NOT implied.
3
Apply to Salah Example
Consider the ruling on talking during prayer. During nafl prayer, talking unintentionally or out of necessity may be excused, but during fard prayer, talking invalidates the prayer (according to Hanafi rulings). The principle prevents us from arguing: 'since it's allowed in nafl, it must be allowed in fard'.
The illah (reason) for the lower ruling does not automatically transfer upward.
4
Extend to Other Levels
Apply the principle to other pairs: mandub vs. wajib, sunnah vs. fard, etc. Something that is recommended (mandub) does not imply it is obligatory (wajib). Similarly, a permissible (mubah) act does not imply it is recommended.
Each hukm level is distinct and requires its own evidence (dalil).
5
Guard Against Misuse
Be careful not to misapply the principle. It does not say that a lower-level ruling cannot be used as evidence for a higher-level ruling at all; rather, it says the lower-level ruling does not automatically entail the higher one. Any analogical transfer must be justified by a proper illah and must respect the hierarchy of rulings.
Qiyas from lower to higher is possible if the illah is present and strong enough, but the principle reminds us that it is not automatic.

This is a logical-legal principle that clarifies the scope of rules. For example, the permissibility of talking during nafl prayer (if needed) does not imply permissibility during fard prayer. This principle prevents qiyas from a lower to a higher rank when the higher rank requires stronger evidence. It is related to the Hanafi concept of tanaqqud (contradiction).

The Nature of Iqtida: Partnership and Agreement
Definition of iqtida as partnership and agreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that iqtida is a partnership (shirka) and agreement (muwafaqa), and identify two rulings derived from this principle.

Core Idea
Iqtida is not independent prayer; it is participation in the imam’s act and conformity to his movements.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi fiqh regarding congregational prayer: the nature of following an imam (iqtida). The source text is a concise definition that has far-reaching implications for the rules of salah behind an imam. Understanding this concept is key to deriving many detailed rulings of the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components
Read the Arabic phrase. Note that iqtida is defined as shirka (partnership) and muwafaqa (agreement). These are the two essential elements.
2
Understand the implications
Shirka means the follower shares in the imam's prayer—the imam's recitation suffices for both. Muwafaqa means the follower must conform to the imam's actions, not precede him.
This distinguishes iqtida from independent prayer.
3
Derive a ruling
From shirka: the follower does not recite in loud prayers. From muwafaqa: the follower must not precede the imam in bowing, prostrating, etc.
These are two key Hanafi rulings.

This means that the follower's prayer is intrinsically linked to the imam's; the follower is not praying independently but rather participating in the imam's prayer and must conform to his movements. For example, the follower must not precede the imam in bowing or prostrating. This principle supports many rules of congregation: the imam's recitation suffices for the follower, and the follower must not recite behind the imam in loud prayers.

Intention Determines Whether Prayer Can Be Resumed
Rule on leaving prayer temporarily to rectify a situation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that intention determines whether an interruption allows building upon the prayer, and apply it to distinguish between corrective and abandoning intentions.

Core Idea
Intention separates a lawful resumption from a nullified prayer.

This lesson examines a foundational principle in Hanafi fiqh regarding interruptions during salah. The text presents a concise ruling that hinges entirely on the worshipper's intention at the moment of leaving the prayer. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of building (bina') after an interruption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of leaving
The passage contrasts two intentions: leaving to correct (qaṣd al-iṣlāḥ) and leaving to reject/abandon (qaṣd al-rafḍ wa al-tark). First, clearly distinguish these two scenarios.
2
Connect each type to its consequence
For corrective leaving: building is allowed. For abandoning leaving: building is prevented. Note the legal effect — does it prevent building or not?
3
Apply to a concrete scenario
Consider a worshipper who steps out to remove an impurity with the clear intention to return. Apply the rule: since intention was correction, building is permitted. Contrast with someone who leaves out of frustration with no intent to resume.
This step solidifies the practical application.

For example, if during prayer a person sees an impurity on his clothes and steps aside to remove it with the intention of returning, he may continue. Rare Occurrence is Not Analogized to Text
Principle restricting qiyas on rare events

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that rare occurrences are not analogized to textual rulings to determine the validity of qiyas.

Core Idea
Rarity excludes a case from analogy to the text.

This lesson introduces a key usul al-fiqh principle that limits the scope of analogical reasoning (qiyas). The source text states that a rare occurrence (mā yanduru wujūduhu) is not considered to be within the meaning (fī maʿnā) of what the text (naṣṣ) has mentioned. This means that rulings derived by analogy from the text do not automatically extend to exceptional or unusual cases, even if they share some attributes. The principle prevents over-extension of rules and ensures that the revealed law primarily addresses common situations.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Principle's Wording
Read the Arabic principle carefully. It has three key elements: 'what is rare in occurrence' (mā yanduru wujūduhu), 'is not within the meaning' (lā yakūnu fī maʿnā), and 'what the text has mentioned' (mā warada bihi al-naṣṣ). Grasp that rarity is the condition that removes the case from analogical inclusion.
The term 'meaning' refers to the effective cause (illah) or underlying rationale of the text.
2
Recognize the Implication for Qiyas
Understand that qiyas requires a common illah between the original case (asl) and the new case (farʿ). This principle restricts qiyas when the new case is extremely rare, even if it shares the apparent illah. The text is considered to address common occurrences, so rare cases are exempt from analogy.
This does not mean rare cases have no ruling; they may have a separate ruling based on other evidence.
3
Apply to a Concrete Example
Consider the prohibition of wine (khamr) due to intoxication. If a person accidentally drinks wine while unconscious (a rare event), this principle says that the prohibition by analogy to intentional drinking does not apply. The rare occurrence is not 'within the meaning' of the text prohibiting wine.
The ruling for involuntary drinking differs (excusable) because it is not within the scope of the text.
4
Note the Exception
Some madhahib may still apply qiyas to rare cases if the illah is definitely present. However, the Hanafi position adheres to this principle, especially when the rarity is extreme. Understand that this principle is a tool to avoid strict analogy in exceptional circumstances.
The principle is not absolute; it is a weight in the process of ijtihad.

This is a usul al-fiqh principle limiting the application of qiyas (analogical reasoning). The Principle: What an Obligation Depends On Is Itself Obligatory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi maxim 'Ma la yatimmu al-wajibu illa bihi fahuwa wajib', identify its scope, and apply it to derive obligations for prerequisites such as wudu and covering the aurah.

Core Idea
What a fard depends on becomes fard itself – the chain of obligation.

This lesson introduces a foundational legal maxim that links obligations to their necessary prerequisites. Understanding this principle helps you see how Hanafi jurists derive rulings for actions without which a fard cannot be completed. The maxim is memorised as an axiom, but its application requires care: only means within one's capacity become obligatory.

1
Memorise the Arabic maxim

The Completeness of a Pillar is with the Transition

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The completeness of a prayer pillar is achieved by the transition motion, not by merely assuming the posture.

In this lesson, we examine a concise yet crucial fiqhi principle: the completion of a rukn (pillar) in salah occurs through the intiqal (transition) to the next posture. This principle is foundational for determining whether an integral act has been properly performed and whether sajdah sahw is required. It is a memorization anchor that every Hanafi student must internalize.

Guided Path
1
Understand the key terms
Define tamām (completion), rukn (pillar), and intiqāl (transition) in the context of salah. Recognize that each rukn is an essential act that must be fully performed.
Intiqāl refers to the bodily movement from one posture to the next, such as rising from ruku' to standing.
2
Connect the principle to the prayer sequence
Identify the main rukn acts in salah: qiyam (standing), ruku' (bowing), sujud (prostration), and qa'dah (sitting). Understand that the ending boundary of each rukn is defined by the beginning of the subsequent movement.
For example, ruku' ends when the person begins to rise; sujud ends when one sits up between prostrations.
3
Apply to a specific rukn: ruku'
Consider ruku': the person bows and remains still momentarily. According to this principle, the rukn of ruku' is not fully complete until the person transitions to the standing posture (qawma). The act of rising (raf' al-yadayn and straightening) is part of completing the ruku'.
If the worshipper goes directly from ruku' to sujud without rising, the ruku' is considered incomplete.
4
Test a scenario
A worshipper performs sujud, then immediately stands up without sitting between the two prostrations. Is the first sujud complete? No, because the transition (jalsa) is missing. The principle requires that the sujud be completed by the act of sitting up (jalsa) before the second sujud.
This test illustrates the practical application of the principle.
5
Note the consequence of an incomplete rukn
If a rukn is incomplete due to a missing transition, the prayer may still be valid but requires sajdah sahw (prostration of forgetfulness) if the omission was unintentional. If the transition is omitted entirely and the rukn is not completed, the prayer may be invalid and must be repeated.
This principle affects the validity and corrective measures in salah.

The Hanafi school requires that the abrogating text be later in revelation, that it cannot be repealed, and that the two rulings are genuinely contradictory. This ensures naskh is not claimed lightly.
Other schools may have slightly different criteria, but all agree on the core principle.

Core Idea
Order between prayers drops due to narrow time, forgetfulness, or many missed prayers.

You already know that tartib (performing prayers in their correct chronological order) is required between fard prayers. But what if you have many missed prayers or you are running out of time? The Hanafi school provides three clear exceptions where this requirement is lifted to prevent hardship. Let's examine each one.

1

The ruling hinges on whether the premature salam actually terminates the state of prayer or not. Imam Muhammad holds that it does not, while Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that it does, but in a suspended manner (al-tawaqquf). Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for correctly applying the rules of sajdah al-sahw.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is the premature salam (salam) performed due to forgetfulness (sahw) in prayer. The condition is that the person is still in a state of forgetfulness when uttering the salam.
This is not an intentional exit; it is a mistake.
2
State the two positions
Imam Muhammad: The salam has no legal effect; the person remains in prayer. The two Shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf): The salam does cause exit from prayer, but on the basis of suspension (al-tawaqquf).
The term 'suspension' means the exit is conditional.
3
Understand 'on the basis of suspension'
According to the Shaykhayn, the exit from prayer is not absolute. If the person performs sajdah al-sahw immediately without delay, the exit is retroactively canceled and the prayer is valid. If he delays, the exit is confirmed and the prayer is invalid.
The key is the immediacy of the sajdah al-sahw.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: According to Muhammad, the person is still in prayer and can perform sajdah al-sahw at the end. According to the Shaykhayn, he has exited but can return to the prayer state by performing sajdah al-sahw immediately; otherwise, the prayer is lost.
This affects how the worshipper proceeds after the mistake.

Intention That Changes the Legislated is Void
States principle about void intentions in prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that an intention altering the prescribed nature of an act of worship is void, and apply it to prayer scenarios.

Core Idea
An intention that changes the legislated nature of an act is void.

This passage from Usul al-Hidayah presents a fundamental principle of Hanafi fiqh regarding intention (niyyah) in prayer. It establishes that if a worshipper's intention attempts to alter the essential character of the prayer as defined by Shari'ah, that intention is legally ineffective. Understanding this principle is crucial for distinguishing valid intentions from those that contradict the act's prescribed form.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
Read the Arabic sentence and its translation. The key point: an intention that changes the legislated act is void (talghu).
Memorize the exact wording: إِنَّ النِّيَّةَ إِنْ كَانَتْ مُغَيِّرَةً لِلْمَشْرُوعِ تَلْغُو
2
Define the key terms
Break down the terms: niyyah (intention), mashru' (legislated act), mughtayyirah (altering), talghu (is void). Understand what 'legislated act' means — the specific act of worship as prescribed by Shari'ah.
The intention is a condition (shart) for prayer, not a pillar (rukn).
3
Apply to prayer scenarios
Consider examples: intending a mandatory prayer as voluntary, or vice versa. The intention that tries to change the nature is void; the act remains as what was originally intended if possible.
This does not apply to mere additional intentions (e.g., to break the prayer) — those are governed by separate rules.
4
Recognize what does NOT void
The principle only voids an intention that changes the legislated nature. Intentions that are merely inconsistent with the act (e.g., doubting) or that add valid conditions (e.g., congregation) are not covered.
Avoid the common misunderstanding that any alternative intention invalidates the act.
5
Memorize the Arabic text
Commit the exact Arabic wording to memory. This is a foundational maxim in Hanafi fiqh.
Use the transliteration: Inna al-niyyata in kānat mughayyiratan lil-mashrū‘i talghū.

Commentary
This principle means that if a person makes an intention in prayer that changes the nature of the prayer as legislated by Shari'ah, that intention is invalid. For example, if someone intends to pray an obligatory prayer but with the intention of making it a voluntary prayer, that intention is void and the prayer remains what was originally intended if possible. Or if one intends to exit prayer without proper cause, etc.

Key Terms

الْمَشْرُوعِ (al-mashrū'): That which is legislated by Shari'ah, i.e., the specific act of worship.
