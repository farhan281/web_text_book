The Prophet's Continuous Practice as Evidence of Sunnah: I'tikaf
Establishes the usul principle that the Prophet's continuous practice indicates sunnah, applied to i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul principle that the Prophet's continuous practice (muwazabah) indicates sunnah, and apply it to determine that i'tikaf is sunnah mu'akkadah.

Core Idea
The Prophet's consistent practice of an act makes it sunnah mu'akkadah, not fard — i'tikaf is the prime example.

This lesson explores a foundational usul principle from Al-Hidayah: the Prophet's consistent and uninterrupted practice of an act (muwazabah) is evidence that the act is sunnah, not fard. The author applies this to i'tikaf (spiritual retreat) in the last ten days of Ramadan, clarifying an intra-Hanafi disagreement and establishing the relied-upon ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the usul principle
The passage opens by stating that the Prophet's continuous practice is evidence of sunnah. Understand that 'muwazabah' means consistent, uninterrupted practice.
This principle is a key tool in Hanafi fiqh for distinguishing fard from sunnah.
2
Identify the application to i'tikaf
The author uses this principle to settle the ruling on i'tikaf. Notice that Imam al-Quduri considered it mustahabb, but the correct view is sunnah mu'akkadah because of the Prophet's continuous practice.
Sunnah mu'akkadah is a strongly recommended act; leaving it without excuse is makruh.
3
Locate the evidence (dalil)
The proof is the hadith of Aisha (رضي الله عنها) that the Prophet performed i'tikaf in the last ten days of Ramadan every year until his death. This demonstrates muwazabah.
The hadith is from Sahih al-Bukhari, indicating sound transmission.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The continuous practice itself is the indicator of sunnah. The Prophet's consistency over many years shows deliberate emphasis, not an occasional action.
Hanafi usul distinguishes that muwazabah indicates sunnah, not fard, because fard is established by definitive evidence (Quran, mutawatir sunnah).
5
Conclude the mufta bihi
The author of Al-Hidayah declares that the correct view is sunnah mu'akkadah. This is the operative ruling for fatwa.
The intra-Hanafi disagreement shows that later scholars relied on the evidence of muwazabah.

No Qada for Invalidating a Prohibited Fast: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qada for invalidating a prohibited fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on qada for invalidating a fast on a prohibited day, distinguish it from the Sahibayn's view, and explain the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
A prohibited fast must be nullified, not preserved — so no qada is required.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a fast begun on a prohibited day (such as the two Eids). While the fast itself is haram, the question is whether breaking it necessitates a compensatory fast (qada). Abu Hanifa holds that no qada is required, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) argue that the act of commencing a fast creates a binding commitment analogous to a vow. Understanding the reasoning behind each position sharpens one's grasp of legal causation (ta'lil) within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is beginning a fast on a prohibited day (e.g., Eid). The ruling is that the fast itself is haram.
Prohibited days include the two Eids and days of Tashriq according to the Hanafi school.
2
State the Hanafi position on qada
Abu Hanifa holds that no qada is required if the prohibited fast is broken. His reasoning: nullification of the fast is obligatory, and qada is based on preservation, which does not apply.
3
Understand the opposing view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (in Nawadir transmissions) hold that qada is required because the commencement of any fast creates a binding commitment similar to a vow.
They analogize to initiating divorce in a disliked time.
4
Examine the reasoning and analogy
The Sahibayn's analogy: just as initiating divorce in a disliked time still results in a valid divorce, commencing a fast creates an obligation to preserve it. Abu Hanifa distinguishes: the fast is prohibited from the outset, so nullifying it is mandatory, not preserving it.
The distinction lies in the nature of the act: prohibited worship vs. permissible act.
5
Determine the authoritative view
Abu Hanifa's view is Zahir al-Riwayah (the authoritative transmission), and thus it is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
The Sahibayn's view is in Nawadir, which carries less weight.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that qiyas is rejected against a transmitted text, specifically to the dispute over fasting as a condition for i'tikaf.

Core Idea
Qiyas is invalid when it opposes a transmitted text; Companion statements not based on qiyas are binding.

This passage from al-Hidayah introduces a foundational Hanafi usul principle: qiyas cannot stand against a transmitted text (nas mansul). The author then applies this principle to the issue of fasting as a condition for i'tikaf. Hanafis require fasting for i'tikaf based on statements of Aisha, Ali, and Ibn Abbas, which are considered marfu' hadiths. Imam al-Shafi'i opposes this using qiyas, arguing that fasting is an independent worship and cannot be subordinated. Understanding why Hanafis prioritize the transmitted text over qiyas is key to grasping the school's methodology.

The Hanafis rule that fasting is a condition (shart) for i'tikaf. Cite the evidence: the statements of Aisha, Ali, and Ibn Abbas.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute. It begins by stating the usul principle that qiyas (analogical reasoning) is not acceptable when it contradicts a transmitted text (nas mansul). The author then applies this to the issue of whether fasting is a condition for the validity of i'tikaf. The Hanafis hold that fasting is a condition, relying on the statements of Aisha, Ali, and Ibn Abbas that 'there is no i'tikaf except with fasting.' In Hanafi usul, such Companion statements, when not based on personal opinion (ghayr madrak bil-qiyas), take the ruling of marfu' hadith—i.e., they are attributed to the Prophet. The Shafi'is oppose this, arguing by qiyas that since fasting is an independent worship, it cannot serve as a condition for another worship; making it conditional would subordinate it. The Hanafi response cites a further usul principle from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifying that the Prophet's continuous practice (with or without occasional abandonment) indicates the level of sunnah, implying that the transmitted statements from the Companions are authoritative and must be followed over qiyas. The passage ends with multiple source references supporting the Hanafi view.

Key Terms
الْقِيَاس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning: extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common effective cause.
النَّص الْمَنْقُول (al-nas al-mansul): A divine text (Quran or Sunnah) that is transmitted from the Lawgiver, including statements of Companions that are not subject to qiyas.
مَرْفُوع (marfu'): A narration attributed to the Prophet, whether explicitly or by legal implication.

I'tikaf Requires Consecutiveness Unlike Fasting
Ruling and proof for consecutiveness in i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on consecutiveness in i'tikaf and explain why fasting is treated differently.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is inherently consecutive because all times suit it; fasting is inherently separate due to night.

This passage introduces a foundational principle distinguishing i'tikaf from fasting. Understanding why i'tikaf demands continuity while fasting does not will clarify rulings on vows and interruptions. The proof rests on the nature of each act: i'tikaf can occur at any time, so breaks are unnatural; fasting is limited to daytime, making night a natural break.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the Arabic statement: the 'mabna' (foundation) of i'tikaf is tataabu' (consecutiveness), while fasting is based on tafarruq (separation).
This is the core legal axiom.
2
Understand the proof via qiyas
The 'illa (effective cause) is that all times (day and night) are capable of i'tikaf, so no natural break exists. For fasting, night is not valid for fasting, creating an inherent separation.
This is a qiyas jali based on the nature of the acts.
3
Apply to the case of a vow
If someone vows i'tikaf for a specific number of days, consecutiveness is wajib by default, even if not stipulated. For fasting, consecutiveness is not required unless explicitly mentioned.
Practical consequence: the vow determines the obligation.

Commentary
This passage establishes a fundamental distinction between i'tikaf and fasting in the Hanafi school. I'tikaf is inherently based on consecutiveness (tataabu'), meaning that when one vows i'tikaf for a specified number of days, they must be performed continuously without breaks, even if the person did not explicitly state that condition. The rationale is that i'tikaf can be performed at any time, day or night, so there is no inherent break. In contrast, fasting (sawm) is based on separation (tafarruq) because fasting is only valid during daytime; night naturally interrupts the sequence, so consecutive fasting is not required unless explicitly stipulated. The author of al-Hidayah uses this principle to explain why i'tikaf must be done consecutively. The proof is the nature of the acts themselves: all times are fit for i'tikaf, but night is not fit for fasting.

The Hanafi Principle of Continuity in I'tikaf vs Fasting
Explains the legal principle that i'tikaf requires continuity while fasting does not.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that i'tikaf requires continuity by default and explain why fasting does not, using the Quranic proof from Zakariyya's story.

Core Idea
I'tikaf covers both day and night so continuity is built in; fasting only covers days so separation is natural.

This passage from al-Hidayah introduces a foundational legal principle that distinguishes the nature of two acts of worship: i'tikaf and fasting. Understanding this rule is crucial when dealing with vows: if someone vows an i'tikaf, it must be performed consecutively even without stipulating it, but the same is not true for fasting. The author proves this by showing that days and nights are interconnected in custom, as demonstrated in the story of Zakariyya (AS).
