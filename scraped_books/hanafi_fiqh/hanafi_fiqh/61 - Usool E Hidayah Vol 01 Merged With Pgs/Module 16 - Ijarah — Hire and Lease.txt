Borrower Cannot Hire Out the Borrowed Item
Ruling and reasoning on borrower's inability to lease.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a borrower cannot lease the borrowed item and explain the two legal reasons for this prohibition.

Core Idea
A loan grants only personal use; the borrower cannot transfer a greater right (lease) than he himself has.

Borrowing an item (ʻāriyah) gives you the right to use it, but not to pass that right on to others for a fee. This lesson explains why a borrower cannot lease the borrowed item, the reasoning behind it, and what happens if he does — including liability for loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the first sentence: the borrower is not permitted to lease the borrowed item. This is a prohibition.
2
Understand the First Reason (Comparison)
The text says a loan (ʻāriyah) is lesser than a lease (ijārah). The lesser cannot contain the greater — just as a partial right cannot grant a full right.
This is a logical argument: a person cannot give what he does not have.
3
Understand the Second Reason (Conflict with Return)
The essence of a loan is that the item must be returnable to the lender at any time. A lease creates a right in the lessee that prevents immediate return, conflicting with the loan's nature.
4
Note the Liability Consequence
If the borrower leases the item and it perishes, he is liable (ḍāmin) for its value because he acted without authority. The lender may also hold the lessee directly liable since the lessee took possession without the owner's permission.
Two possible claims: against the borrower (primary) or against the lessee (at lender's option).

وَلَيْسَ لِلْمُسْتَعِيرِ أَنْ يُؤَجِّرَ مَا اسْتَعَارَهُ فَإِنْ آجَرَهُ فَعَطِبَ ضَمِنَ، لِأَنَّ الْإِعَارَةَ دُونَ الْإِجَارَةِ وَالشَّيْء لَا يَتَضَمَّنُ مَا فَوْقَهُ وَلأَنَّ مُقْتَضَى الْعَارِيَّةِ الرُّجُوعُ وَتَعَلَّقُ الْمُسْتَأْجِرِ بِهَا يَمْنَعُ ذَلِكَ فَلِهَذَا لَمْ يَجُرُ فَإِنْ آجَرَهَا ضَمِنَ حِينَ سَلَّمَهَا، وَإِنْ شَاءَ الْمُعِيرُ ضَمِنَ الْمُسْتَأْجِرُ، لِأَنَّهُ قَبَضَهَا بِغَيْرِ إِذْنِ الْمَالِكِ.
It is not permissible for the borrower to hire out what he borrowed. If he hires it out and it perishes, he is liable, because a loan is lesser than a lease, and a thing does not encompass what is above it. Also, the requirement of a loan is return, and the lessee's attachment prevents that. Therefore, it is not permissible. If he hires it out, he is liable when he hands it over. If the lender wishes, he may hold the lessee liable because he took possession without the owner's permission.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the chapter on loans (al-ʻāriyah). The borrower (mustaʻīr) has the right to use the borrowed item but does not own it. He may not lease it out because a lease (ijārah) transfers possession (taʻalluq) to the lessee in a way that conflicts with the lender's right to reclaim the item at any time. The legal reasoning is twofold: (1) the loan is a lesser right than a lease, and a lesser right cannot include a greater one; (2) the essence of a loan is that the item remains available for return to the lender, but a lease creates a right in the lessee that prevents immediate return. If the borrower nevertheless leases the item and it perishes, he is liable for its value because he exceeded his authority. Additionally, the lender has the option to hold the lessee directly liable since the lessee took possession without the owner's consent.

Key Terms
الْإِعَارَةَ (al-ʻāriyah): A loan of an item for use without compensation; the lender retains ownership.
الْإِجَارَةَ (al-ijārah): A lease contract in which the lessor transfers the right to use the item for compensation.
ضَمِنَ (ḍamina): He becomes liable for compensation (the value of the item) if it perishes.

Key Takeaway
A borrower cannot lease a borrowed item because he lacks the authority to grant a right greater than his own, and the lease would conflict with the lender's right to reclaim the item.
