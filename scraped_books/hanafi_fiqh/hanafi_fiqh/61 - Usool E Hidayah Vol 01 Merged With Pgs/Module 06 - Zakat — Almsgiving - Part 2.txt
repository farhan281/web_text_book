Ownership Transfer (Tamlik) is a Condition for Zakat
Establishes that tamlik is a necessary condition for zakat payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition of tamlik for zakat and identify the proof from the shroud case.

Core Idea
Zakat requires making a specific poor person owner; without tamlik the payment is invalid.

This lesson examines the fundamental Hanafi principle that zakat must be given in a way that transfers ownership to a specific recipient. Simply spending money on charitable projects does not fulfill this obligation. We will explore the textual ruling, the rational proof, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
Recognize that the act is zakat (obligatory charity) and its hukm is fard 'ayn. The key condition under discussion is tamlik.
2
Understand the Condition of Tamlik
Tamlik means making the recipient an owner of the money. It is a rukn (pillar) of zakat. Without it, the payment does not count as zakat.
3
Examine the Proof (Shroud Case)
The proof is rational (dalil 'aqli). If a wolf eats a shrouded body, the shroud belongs to the person who provided it, not the heir – showing ownership never transferred to the deceased. Similarly, tamlik cannot be achieved in shrouding.
This illustrates that tamlik is absent in actions like shrouding.
4
Apply to Other Cases
The same reasoning applies to building mosques, paying off deceased debts, digging wells, etc. – none involve making a specific living poor person an owner.
5
Note the Requirement of Qabd
For tamlik to be valid, the recipient must take possession (qabd). This can be done by the poor person or their guardian/agent.
Mere specification without delivery is insufficient.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Hidayah discusses the conditions for paying zakat. The principle is that zakat requires tamlik, meaning the transfer of ownership to the recipient. Without ownership transfer, the payment is not valid. Therefore, zakat funds cannot be used to build a mosque, shroud a dead person, pay off a deceased person's debts, build public water fountains, or dig wells, because these do not involve making a specific person an owner of the money. The proof given regarding the shroud is instructive: if a wolf eats the deceased, the shroud belongs to the one who provided it (the mukaffin), not to the heir, indicating that ownership did not pass to the deceased. Hence, tamlik did not occur. Zakat must be given directly to a poor person or to a guardian/agent who takes possession on his behalf; the recipient must take possession (qabd) for the tamlik to be complete.

Key Terms
تمليك (tamlīk): the act of making someone an owner of property; a necessary condition for the validity of zakat
قبض (qabḍ): taking physical possession; required for the completion of tamlik in zakat

The Cause of Obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Maintenance and Guardianship
Principle with proof and application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the cause (sabab) of obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr in the Hanafi school and identify which dependents are included based on the condition of maintenance and guardianship.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib for every head you maintain and have guardianship over — not just yourself.

This passage from Usool al-Hidayah establishes the foundational principle for the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. The Hanafi school defines the cause (sabab) as a head (person) that one maintains and over which one has legal guardianship. This principle determines for whom a sahib al-nisab must pay, including himself, his minor children, and his slaves, and excludes those without such a relationship. The proof is the Prophetic command, "Pay for those whom you maintain."

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its two components
The passage states: sabab is 'ra's yamunuhu wa yali 'alayhi' — a head that one maintains and has guardianship over. Break this into two conditions: maintenance (nafaqa) and guardianship (wilayah). Both must be present.
Maintenance alone is insufficient; the hadith 'amman tamununan' includes both, but the Hanafi school interprets it as requiring guardianship as well.
2
Locate the hadith proof
The dalil is the hadith 'Addu 'amman tamununan' (Pay for those whom you maintain). The imperative 'addu indicates obligation. The phrase 'amman tamununan' is general, covering all dependents under one's maintenance. The Hanafi school restricts this to those also under guardianship, as explained by the principle.
This hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), so the ruling is wajib, not fard.
3
Apply the principle to the cases mentioned
Apply the two conditions to oneself: a person maintains himself and has guardianship over himself (by default), so he must pay for himself. For minor children and slaves, the father/master provides maintenance and has guardianship, so they are included. The text also includes mudabbar and umm walad as slaves.
The principle extends even to non-Muslim slaves, as maintenance and guardianship are not conditional on faith.
4
Identify the exceptions and the reason
The text excludes parents, adult children, wife, and mukatib because the person does not have guardianship (wilayah) over them. For parents, guardianship is reversed; for adult children, they are independent; for wife, the husband has maintenance but not full guardianship; for a mukatib, the slave is partially free and not under complete guardianship.
However, if the father is insane and poor, the son must pay because the son becomes the guardian (wilayah exists) and maintenance is provided.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school requires payment for non-Muslim slaves because the hadith is general. The Shafi'i school does not require payment for non-Muslim slaves, arguing that zakat is an act of purification and non-Muslims are not purified by it.
This is an example of ikhtilaf based on different interpretations of the same evidence.

Permission Established by Custom: The Hanafi Principle
Proving that implied permission through custom is legally valid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that permission can be established by custom and apply it to validate payment of Sadaqat al-Fitr on behalf of adult dependents without explicit consent.

Core Idea
Custom can establish permission as effectively as explicit text—making payment on behalf of dependents valid.

In Hanafi fiqh, permission (idhn) is normally required for one person to act on behalf of another in financial obligations. However, this lesson introduces a key principle: permission may be established by custom ('adah). The author of al-Hidayah uses this principle to validate a common family practice—paying Sadaqat al-Fitr on behalf of adult children or a wife without their explicit command. We will explore the reasoning, the contrast with zakat, and the underlying maxim: 'What is established by custom is like what is established by text.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
The lesson opens with the principle: 'Indeed permission may be established by custom.' Recognize that this is a legal maxim that allows implied consent based on customary practice.
The principle is not limited to Sadaqat al-Fitr but applies wherever custom reliably indicates permission.
2
Understand the Application
The specific case: a person pays Sadaqat al-Fitr on behalf of his adult children or his wife without seeking explicit permission. According to qiyas (analogy with zakat), this would be invalid. But istihsan (juridical preference) validates it because customarily the husband/father handles this obligation.
The text explicitly contrasts with zakat, where custom does not suffice because zakat is a 'pure act of worship' requiring explicit permission.
3
Examine the Proof
The proof is based on istihsan, not a text. The logic: custom provides reliable evidence of consent. The maxim 'al-thabitu bil-'urfi ka al-thabiti bil-nass' (what is established by custom is like what is established by text) is invoked. This overrides the strict analogical reasoning that would require explicit permission.
Istihsan is a distinctive Hanafi methodology that allows departure from qiyas for a more suitable ruling.
4
Contrast with Zakat
Why does the same reasoning not apply to zakat? Because zakat is a pure act of worship ('ibadah mahdah), which demands greater formality. Customary permission is insufficient; explicit consent is required. This distinction highlights the principle's scope.
Sadaqat al-Fitr, while also an obligation, is considered less formal in this context due to its association with the household.
5
Apply the General Rule
The broader legal maxim 'al-thabitu bil-'urfi ka al-thabiti bil-nass' means that custom can create legal effects equivalent to explicit textual provisions. In this case, customary practice establishes the required permission.
This maxim is used throughout Hanafi fiqh in areas such as contracts, family law, and obligations.

Idafa Indicates Exclusivity: Application to Sadaqat al-Fitr
Stating the maxim and its application to Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the maxim 'Idafa indicates exclusivity' to determine the time of obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr and explain the Hanafi proof based on the linguistic analysis.

Core Idea
Idafa indicates exclusivity: Sadaqat al-Fitr is tied to daytime because 'al-fitr' means the daytime break of the fast.

In this passage, al-Hidayah presents a powerful linguistic maxim: the grammatical construct (idafa) of 'Sadaqat al-Fitr' indicates exclusivity. The charity is specifically connected to 'al-fitr', and the author argues that 'al-fitr' refers exclusively to the daytime breaking of the fast, not any breaking. This determines that the obligation begins at dawn on Eid day, not the night before. We will unpack the linguistic proof and its legal consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the maxim
The passage begins by stating: 'Indeed, the idafa indicates exclusivity.' Identify that the maxim is a general rule of Arabic grammar: when a word is in idafa (genitive construct), it establishes a specific connection (ikhtisas) between the two nouns.
This is not a fiqh ruling per se but a linguistic principle used for istinbat.
2
Apply the maxim to the case
The author applies the maxim: 'adding the charity to al-fitr is for exclusivity.' This means that the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr is specifically connected to the meaning of 'al-fitr'.
The word 'al-fitr' is defined in the next step.
3
Define 'al-fitr' as daytime
The author specifies: 'al-fitr is exclusively connected to daytime, not night, because what is meant is the breaking of the fast that is the opposite of fasting.' Since fasting is only during the day, the breaking that opposes it is the daytime iftar.
Nighttime iftar occurs every night of Ramadan, but that is not the breaking that opposes fasting (since there is no fasting at night).
4
Provide the proof (dalil)
The proof is a rational argument (qiyas khafi): if 'al-fitr' included both day and night, then the obligation would attach to every time one breaks the fast, including every night of Ramadan. But it does not. Therefore, 'al-fitr' must be restricted to the daytime breaking.
This is an example of istidlal: what is the correct meaning from the text?
5
Derive the ruling
Since Sadaqat al-Fitr is specifically attached to the daytime breaking of the fast, the obligation begins at the time when that breaking occurs: at the end of Ramadan, i.e., the day of Eid. The Hanafi ruling is that the obligation becomes wajib at subh sadiq on Eid day.
This distinguishes from opinions that allow giving the charity earlier in Ramadan.
