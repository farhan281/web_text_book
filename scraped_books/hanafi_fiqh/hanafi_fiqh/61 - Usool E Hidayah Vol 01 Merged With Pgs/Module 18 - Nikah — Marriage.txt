The Husband's Right Does Not Override Obligatory Acts
Explanation of the husband's limited authority regarding his wife's worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a husband's authority over his wife's obligatory acts and apply the hadith proof to distinguish between fard and nafl.

Core Idea
A husband cannot prevent his wife from obligatory acts; obedience to Allah overrides the husband's right.

This lesson clarifies a key principle in marriage: a husband's rights do not extend to preventing his wife from fulfilling her religious obligations. While a husband may have authority over certain matters, that authority cannot supersede divine commands. The proof is a powerful hadith that sets a clear boundary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the specific act being discussed? (Husband preventing wife from an obligatory act). State the ruling: It is not allowed.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'There is no obedience to a creature in disobedience of the Creator.' Identify the source (Prophetic) and its transmission status (ahad, zanni al-thubut).
3
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the ruling? Preventing a wife from a fard act is disobedience to Allah; obeying the husband in that case would be disobedience to Allah, so the hadith negates such obedience.
4
Distinguish fard vs nafl
The principle applies only to obligatory acts (fara'id). For supererogatory acts (nafl), the husband's right is upheld – he can prevent her.
This distinction is crucial to avoid misapplication.

(193) Indeed, the right of the husband does not appear in the matter of obligatory acts. Translation: The right of the husband does not appear in the right of obligatory acts. Explanation: The meaning of the mentioned principle is that the husband's right over his wife does not appear in obligatory acts; i.e., the husband cannot prevent his wife from any obligatory act. However, he has the right to prevent her from supererogatory acts. And he cannot prevent her from obligatory acts because the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'There is no obedience to a creature in disobedience of the Creator.' (Reference: al-Hidayah book of Hajj, vol. 1 p. 251) And the right of the servant is given precedence over the right of the Shari'ah by His command, indicating that, but the intended meaning of dependents is not only the wife. And Qadi Khan (may Allah have mercy on him) said: 'In addition to the maintenance of his dependents and his young children.' And the right of the woman is given precedence over the right of the Shari'ah, i.e., over the right of Allah Almighty in worldly rulings due to the need of the servant and the self-sufficiency of Allah Almighty. (References from al-Binayah and al-Hidayah)

Commentary
This passage explains that a husband's authority over his wife does not extend to preventing her from performing obligatory acts (fara'id). For example, he cannot forbid her from performing the obligatory Hajj or prayers. However, he can prevent her from voluntary acts (nafl). The proof is the hadith: 'There is no obedience to a creature in disobedience of the Creator.' The text also connects this to the previous principle about the right of the servant being prioritized. An additional note from Qadi Khan clarifies that the right of dependents includes not only the wife but also young children.

Key Terms
فرائض (fara'id): Obligatory acts (fard) in Islam, including prayers, fasting, Hajj, etc.
