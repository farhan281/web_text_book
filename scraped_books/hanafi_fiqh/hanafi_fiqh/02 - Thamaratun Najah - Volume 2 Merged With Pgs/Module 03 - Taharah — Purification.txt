Hila for Fidyah and Ruling on Giving to One Faqir
Explains a legal stratagem for paying fidyah and the permissibility of multiple fidyahs to one person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the legal stratagem (hila) for discharging fidyah on behalf of a deceased person and state the Hanafi ruling on giving multiple fidyahs to a single faqir, distinguishing it from kaffarah.

Core Idea
Fidyah is discharged by tamlik (ownership transfer) allowing multiple fidyahs to one faqir; kaffarah requires it'am (feeding), limiting each to one.

This lesson explores a clever legal stratagem used to pay off a deceased person's missed prayers and fasts using limited inheritance. The author also clarifies a key difference between fidyah and kaffarah in terms of how they are fulfilled — a distinction rooted in the Quranic proofs each expiation is based on.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the ruling: it is permissible to give the fidyah of many prayers to one faqir collectively.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Contrast with kaffarah
Note the exception: giving multiple kaffarahs to one faqir is not permissible. Only one kaffarah is fulfilled.
Kaffarah of an oath requires feeding ten miskins individually.
3
Understand the hila (stratagem)
Work through the cycle: guardian gives wheat to faqir, faqir gifts it back to guardian, then guardian gives again, repeating until the total fidyah is discharged.
This uses the deceased's one-third property multiple times through a circle of gifts.
4
Identify the proof for fidyah
Quran 2:184 ("fidyatun ta'ami miskin") indicates giving ownership (tamlik) of half a sa' of wheat.
Tamlik suffices; actual feeding is not required.
5
Identify the proof for kaffarah
Quran 5:89 ("it'amu mishkina") indicates feeding (it'am) is the primary act, requiring each miskin to be fed individually.
One miskin can only be fed half a sa', so giving multiple portions to one fulfills only one kaffarah.

Explanation: In fidyah, giving half a sa' to the faqir is necessary; feeding is not necessary. Therefore, if the wheat of several fidyahs is given to one faqir, all fidyahs will be fulfilled. And in oath expiation, feeding is the primary act, and one miskin can be fed at most half a sa'. Therefore, if the food of several miskins is given to one, only the kaffarah of one miskin will be fulfilled, not more. Reason: (1) In fidyah of fasting, giving food is primary; its proof is in the indication of this verse: 'And upon those who are able [to fast, then] a fidyah [of] feeding a poor person.' (Al-Baqarah 2:184) This verse says: give the wheat of fidyah. (2) And in oath expiation, feeding is primary; its indication is in this verse: 'Allah will not impose blame upon you for what is meaningless in your oaths, but He will impose blame upon you for [breaking] what you intended of oaths. Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the legal stratagem (hila) for discharging fidyah on behalf of a deceased person and the permissibility of giving multiple fidyahs to a single faqir. The author first presents the ruling: It is permissible to give the fidyah of many prayers to one faqir collectively, unlike the expiation (kaffarah) of an oath, where it is not permissible to give all to one person. Then an Urdu commentary elaborates a practical stratagem using a cycle of gifts between the guardian (wali) and the faqir, effectively multiplying the limited one-third property to cover the entire fidyah owed. The ruling (704) states that giving the wheat of several fidyahs to one faqir suffices for all, because the obligation is to give (tamlik) half a sa' to the faqir, not to feed (it'am). In contrast, oath expiation requires feeding (it'am) as the primary act, and one miskin can only be fed half a sa'; thus giving the food of multiple miskins to one only fulfills one kaffarah. The proof for fidyah is Quran 2:184, which indicates giving food (fidyah) in the sense of handing over wheat. The proof for kaffarah is Quran 5:89, which commands feeding (it'am) ten miskins, thereby establishing feeding as the operative act. The Hanafi position is that in fidyah, ownership transfer (tamlik) is sufficient, whereas in kaffarah, feeding (it'am) is required, leading to the difference in whether giving to one person suffices for multiple obligations.

Key Terms
فدیہ (fidyah): Compensation for missed fasts or prayers, usually in the form of giving food to the poor.
کفارہ (kaffarah): Expiation for breaking an oath, requiring feeding or clothing ten miskins or freeing a slave.

The Method of Washing the Deceased (Ghusl al-Mayyit)
Hanafi rulings on method of washing deceased with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi method of washing the deceased, identify which steps are wajib versus sunnah, and explain the proofs from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
Wiping the deceased's nose with a wet cloth is wajib; using sidr water and odd washings are sunnah.

Washing the deceased (ghusl al-mayyit) is a communal obligation (fard kifayah). This lesson details the specific method prescribed by the Hanafi school, distinguishing between obligatory and recommended steps. The rulings are derived from the hadith of Umm ‘Atiyyah and the practice of the salaf (athar). Pay close attention to the water used, the order of washing, and the unique treatment of the nose and mouth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall obligation and its type
Recognize that ghusl al-mayyit is fard kifayah (communal obligation) – if some Muslims perform it, the rest are absolved. The specific method is detailed by the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the Companions.
2
Examine the water to be used
Note that water should be boiled with sidr (berry leaves) or ushnan for cleansing. If unavailable, pure water (qarah) suffices. The hadith mentions sidr and camphor; the athar provide alternatives like khitmi.
Khitmi (marshmallow) is recommended for washing the head and beard.
3
Understand the obligatory wiping of nose and mouth
The text states that wiping inside the nose and mouth with a wet cloth is wajib, based on the statement of Sa‘id ibn Jubayr and the practice of Ibrahim. This replaces madmadah and istinshaq to prevent harm.
This is a distinctive Hanafi ruling – other schools allow rinsing.
4
List the sunnah practices
The sunnah actions include: using sidr water, applying camphor in the last wash, an odd number of washes (3, 5, …), starting from the right side and the parts of wudu, washing the head and beard with khitmi, washing the deceased on each side, and finally seating and gently pressing the belly.
These are mustahabb – the ghusl is valid even if omitted.
5
Analyze the proofs
The primary dalil is the hadith of Umm ‘Atiyyah (Bukhari and Muslim) which commands beginning with the right side and wudu places. The athar from Sa‘id ibn Jubayr and Ibrahim al-Nakha‘i provide the ruling on not rinsing. The athar from al-Hasan and al-Aswad support the use of khitmi and the seating/pressing step.
The imperative 'ibda'' indicates recommendation (nadb) in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah covers the Hanafi method of washing the deceased (ghusl al-mayyit). It begins with the water to be used: water should be boiled with sidr (berry leaves) or ushnan (a cleansing plant) to enhance purity; if these are unavailable, pure water (qarah) suffices. The text emphasizes cleaning the inside of the nose and mouth with a wet cloth rather than rinsing (madmadah) or snuffing water (istinshaq), as the deceased may choke; this is based on the practice of early Muslims (athar). The washing is done an odd number of times (3, 5, etc.) with sidr, and camphor is applied in the last wash to preserve the body. Washing starts from the right side, beginning with the parts washed in wudu. The head and beard are washed with khitmi (marshmallow plant) for added cleanliness, though this is mustahabb (recommended), not obligatory. The body is washed by laying the deceased on the left side first to wash the right side, then on the right side to wash the left, ensuring water reaches all areas. Finally, after washing both sides, the deceased is seated upright, supported, and the stomach is gently pressed to expel any remaining fluids; these are washed away without repeating the entire ghusl. These rulings are derived from hadith (Umm ‘Atiyyah’s narration) and athar (statements of tabi‘un like Sa‘id ibn Jubayr and Ibrahim al-Nakha‘i). The overall obligation (fard kifayah) of ghusl is assumed; the specific methods are sunnah or mustahabb, except for wiping the nose which is deemed wajib (obligatory) to ensure complete cleaning.

Core Idea

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position on washing the deceased's head and beard with khitmi, a cleansing grass. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying funeral rites without undue hardship.

3

The Sunnah of Starting with the Right Side in Ghusl al-Mayyit
Ruling on the procedure of washing the deceased on both sides

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended order for washing the deceased and explain the prophetic evidence that establishes this order as sunnah.

Core Idea
Start with the right side — the Prophet's command makes it sunnah muakkadah.

In washing the deceased, the order of actions is not arbitrary. The Prophet ﷺ specifically instructed to begin with the right side and the places of wudu. This lesson unpacks that instruction, showing how the body is positioned to facilitate it and why it is considered sunnah rather than an obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the legal status of the order of washing the deceased. The act is the sequence of washing the right side before the left.
The ruling is sunnah muakkadah, not fard or wajib.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the proof cited: the hadith of Umm Atiyyah (رضي الله عنها) where the Prophet said: 'ابدأن بميامنها و مواضع الوضوء منها' (Begin with the right side and the places of wudu).
The hadith is sahih (agreed upon by Bukhari and Muslim).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Analyze how the command 'ابدأن' (begin) indicates the ruling. In Hanafi usul, a command for sequence in worship is interpreted as sunnah unless there is evidence of obligation.
The hadith does not indicate that omitting the order invalidates the ghusl.
4
Note the practical implementation
The body is laid on the left side first to expose the right side, making it easier to wash the right side first. Then the body is turned to the right side to wash the left side.
This method ensures the prophetic instruction is followed practically.
5
Conclude the mufta bihi
The accepted position is that washing the right side first is sunnah. If one reverses the order, the ghusl is still valid but the sunnah is missed.
This is the view in Zahir al-Riwayah and no intra-Hanafi disagreement exists.

Dislike of Turban in the Shroud
Ruling on the dislike of including a turban in the shroud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on including a turban in the shroud and identify the hadith evidence that supports it.

Core Idea
A turban in the shroud is disliked (makruh) because the Prophet was shrouded without one.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from the chapter of Janazah: the inclusion of a turban in the shroud. The Hanafi school considers this makruh, and the evidence comes directly from the practice of the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam). We will analyse the hadith from Aisha (radi Allahu anha) and understand why this ruling is classified as makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked) rather than haram.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is including a turban in the shroud of a deceased person. The ruling is 'makruh' (disliked), specifically makruh tanzihi, as indicated by 'fi al-asahh' (the most correct view).
The phrase 'fi al-asahh' signals that this is the strongest opinion within the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith from Aisha (radi Allahu anha) in Sahih Bukhari, Abu Dawud, and Muslim. It states that the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam) was shrouded in three white Yemeni cotton garments, without a shirt or a turban.
The hadith is ahad (single-narrator), giving it zanni status in transmission (thubut).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The absence of a turban in the Prophet's shroud indicates that including one is not part of the sunnah. This omission serves as evidence that it is disliked to add it.
The reasoning is based on the principle that the Prophet's practice (fi'l) sets the sunnah; deviations from it may be makruh.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g. Shafi'i) may consider a turban permissible, as they view the hadith as descriptive rather than prescriptive. The Hanafi school, however, deems it makruh due to the absence of precedent.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf arising from different methodologies in deriving rulings from the Prophet's actions.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that it is makruh tanzihi. The ruling is based on the hadith and is considered the strongest position within the school.
Tarjih is implicit; the author states 'fi al-asahh' indicating the chosen view.

Wrapping Order and Tying the Shroud
Ruling on the sequence of wrapping and tying the shroud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the order of wrapping the shroud and the permissibility of tying it, and cite the primary textual evidence.

Core Idea
Wrap left then right; tie only if the shroud may open.

The proper wrapping of the shroud is an act of respect for the deceased. The Hanafi school prescribes a specific sequence derived from prophetic practice and analogy. In this lesson, we will examine the ruling on the order and the evidence that supports it, as well as the concession to tie a knot when needed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
Read the matn: 'Wrap from his left side then his right, and tie if there is fear of it spreading.' The ruling is that the order is sunnah muakkadah, and tying is permissible when necessary.
2
Understand the rationale
The commentary explains that the order mirrors how living persons wear a sheet: first the left end over the right shoulder, then the right end over the left.
Analogy from customary practice.
3
Analyse the textual evidence
Two hadiths are cited: the hadith of Um Atiyyah about washing from the right (applied by analogy to shrouding) and the hadith of Layla bint Qanif showing the order of garments: izār, qamīṣ, khimār, lifāfah.
The hadith of Layla directly demonstrates the order of cloths.
4
Note the concession for tying
The matn allows tying 'if there is fear of spreading'. This indicates a conditional permissibility, not a general recommendation.
Tying is not the default; it is only when needed.
5
Apply the ruling practically
When shrouding, first lay out the lifāfah, then izār, then qamīṣ. Place the body, insert the head, fold the qamīṣ, wrap the izār, then the lifāfah – left side first, then right. If the shroud is loose, tie a knot.

Commentary
This ruling specifies the additional pieces for a woman's shroud. The matn states: In addition to the standard pieces (izār, qamīṣ, lifāfah), a woman receives a khimār (head covering) for her face and a piece (khirqah) to bind her breasts. The commentary explains that a woman's shroud consists of five garments: qamīṣ, khimār, izār, breast band, and lifāfah. The reasons given are: (1) She uses these items in life (khimār and breast band are used with other garments), so they are included in death. (2) The hadith of Layla bint Qanif reports the Prophet (PBUH) providing five cloths for his daughter Umm Kulthum: first the hiqā' (izār), then the dir' (qamīṣ), then the khimār, then the mulḥafah (lifāfah), and then she was rolled in another cloth (the fifth). (3) The statement of Umar (RA) explicitly mentions five: miṭnaq (another word for izār), dir', khimār, lifāfah, and the tied piece (breast band). (4) The breast band covers the chest, stomach, and thighs, and is wrapped over the qamīṣ, as reported by Ibn Sirīn: place the piece on her stomach and tie it around her thighs.

Key Terms
الخمار (al-khimār): A head covering that comes down to the chest, used to cover the woman's face and hair in the shroud.
الخرقة (al-khirqah): A piece of cloth used to bind the breasts, covering the chest and tying around the thighs.
الدرع (al-dir' / al-qamīṣ): A shirt-like garment covering the upper body, part of the shroud.
الإزار (al-izār): A waist wrapper or loincloth covering the lower body.
الملحفة / اللفافة (al-mulḥafah / al-lifāfah): The outer sheet that covers the entire body, used as the outermost layer of the shroud.

The Five Garments of a Woman's Shroud
Ruling on the five garments of a woman's shroud with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five garments required for a woman's shroud in the Hanafi school and cite the proofs from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
A woman's shroud requires five garments: shirt, headcover, loincloth, breast-band, and wrapper.

The shroud (kafan) is an essential part of preparing the deceased for burial. While a man's shroud typically consists of three garments, the Hanafi school teaches that a woman's shroud includes two additional pieces due to the need for extra covering. In this lesson, we will examine the ruling that a woman is shrouded in five garments, based on the practice of the Prophet ﷺ for his daughter Umm Kulthum and the statements of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is shrouding a deceased woman and that the ruling is sunnah muakkadah (emphasized sunnah) – not fard or wajib.
The hukm level is sunnah muakkadah because the proofs are strong but not at the level of obligation.
2
Locate the dalil
Examine the three proofs cited: the hadith of Layla bint Qanif about Umm Kulthum (Abu Dawud), the statement of Umar (Ibn Abi Shaybah), and the athar of Ibn Sirin (Ibn Abi Shaybah).
Each proof explicitly mentions five garments for a woman's shroud.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
For each proof, identify the specific phrase that indicates five garments. For example, in Layla's hadith, the sequence 'izar, then shirt, then khimar, then malhafah, then another cloth' shows five pieces.
Umar's statement explicitly says 'five garments (khamsatu athwab)'.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that a woman's shroud consists of three garments (izar, khimar, and wrapper) and does not require a separate breast-band or shirt.
The ikhtilaf centers on the number of garments and the inclusion of the shirt (dir') and breast-band (khirqah).
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is preferred because the hadith of Umm Kulthum is specific to women's shrouding, and the practice of the Companions (Umar and Ibn Sirin) supports the five-garment view.
The Shafi'i proof from general analogy to men's shroud is weaker when specific textual evidence exists.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah is an expansion probe on the rulings of the woman's shroud (kafan al-mar'ah). It begins by stating the matn ruling (898): in addition to the basic garments, a woman is given a headcover (khimar) and a breast-band (khirqah) in the Sunnah. The commentary then explains that the woman's shroud consists of five garments: shirt (qamis), headcover (khimar), loincloth (izar), breast-band (khirqah), and wrapper (lifafah). Proofs are provided from the hadith of Layla bint Qanif about Umm Kulthum's shroud, from the statement of Umar (رضي الله عنه), and from athar of Ibn Sirin regarding placement of the breast-band. A discussion follows about the order: whether the izar comes before or after the qamis. The preferred view is that the izar is above the qamis, as per the athar of Qatadah. Ruling (899) states that in necessity, only the head-binder suffices, and if even that is lacking, three garments (loincloth, headcover, wrapper) are permissible, based on the saying of Muhammad Ibn Sirin. Ruling (900) instructs that the woman's hair is to be made into two braids and placed on the chest over the qamis. The hadith of Umm Atiyyah mentions three plaits, but here the commentator explains it as two braids placed on the chest. Ruling (901) gives the final order of dressing: first qamis, then hair placed, then khimar over qamis under lifafah, then izar, then khirqah over izar, then lifafah over all. The proof is analogy to life practice and an athar from Umm al-Humayd, though the text cuts off. The Hanafi position is that these are sunnah practices, and the number of garments is five for a woman, though three are sufficient in necessity.

الدرع (al-dir'): A shirt (qamis) that covers from shoulders to below the knees; in the shroud, it is worn under the izar.

The Necessity Concession: Three Garments Suffice
Ruling on the necessity concession for a woman's shroud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that in necessity, a woman's shroud may consist of three garments, and cite the evidence from the Tabi'i Muhammad ibn Sirin.

Core Idea
In necessity, three garments suffice for a woman's shroud: loincloth, headcover, and wrapper.

While the sunnah requires five garments for a woman's shroud, the Hanafi school recognizes that circumstances may prevent this. The school therefore provides a concession (rukhsah) that only three garments are necessary when five are not available. In this lesson, we examine the details of this concession and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concession and its condition
The concession applies when five garments are not available (necessity). The ruling is that three garments suffice: loincloth (izar), headcover (khimar), and wrapper (lifafah).
The condition is genuine necessity (darurah) – not mere preference.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is the saying of the Tabi'i Muhammad ibn Sirin: 'The shroud of a woman who has menstruated is five garments or three.'
This is found in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
3
Understand the rationale
The justification is that in life, a woman often manages with three garments when necessary. Similarly, in death, three suffice in necessity.
This analogy to life practice reinforces the ruling.
4
Apply the ruling
If only three cloths are available, the shroud should consist of the izar, khimar, and lifafah. The shirt and breast-band are dropped.
The headcover (khimar) is still required as it is part of the three.
5
Contrast with normal sunnah
In normal circumstances, five are sunnah. The concession does not change the ideal; it only allows a reduced set when necessary.
This is a classic example of a rukhsah within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah is an expansion probe on the rulings of the woman's shroud (kafan al-mar'ah). It begins by stating the matn ruling (898): in addition to the basic garments, a woman is given a headcover (khimar) and a breast-band (khirqah) in the Sunnah. The commentary then explains that the woman's shroud consists of five garments: shirt (qamis), headcover (khimar), loincloth (izar), breast-band (khirqah), and wrapper (lifafah). Proofs are provided from the hadith of Layla bint Qanif about Umm Kulthum's shroud, from the statement of Umar (رضي الله عنه), and from athar of Ibn Sirin regarding placement of the breast-band. A discussion follows about the order: whether the izar comes before or after the qamis. The preferred view is that the izar is above the qamis, as per the athar of Qatadah. Ruling (900) instructs that the woman's hair is to be made into two braids and placed on the chest over the qamis. The hadith of Umm Atiyyah mentions three plaits, but here the commentator explains it as two braids placed on the chest. Ruling (901) gives the final order of dressing: first qamis, then hair placed, then khimar over qamis under lifafah, then izar, then khirqah over izar, then lifafah over all. The proof is analogy to life practice and an athar from Umm al-Humayd, though the text cuts off. The Hanafi position is that these are sunnah practices, and the number of garments is five for a woman, though three are sufficient in necessity.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah is an expansion probe on the rulings of the woman's shroud (kafan al-mar'ah). It begins by stating the matn ruling (898): in addition to the basic garments, a woman is given a headcover (khimar) and a breast-band (khirqah) in the Sunnah. The commentary then explains that the woman's shroud consists of five garments: shirt (qamis), headcover (khimar), loincloth (izar), breast-band (khirqah), and wrapper (lifafah). Proofs are provided from the hadith of Layla bint Qanif about Umm Kulthum's shroud, from the statement of Umar (رضي الله عنه), and from athar of Ibn Sirin regarding placement of the breast-band. A discussion follows about the order: whether the izar comes before or after the qamis. The preferred view is that the izar is above the qamis, as per the athar of Qatadah. Ruling (899) states that in necessity, only the head-binder suffices, and if even that is lacking, three garments (loincloth, headcover, wrapper) are permissible, based on the saying of Muhammad Ibn Sirin. Ruling (900) instructs that the woman's hair is to be made into two braids and placed on the chest over the qamis. The hadith of Umm Atiyyah mentions three plaits, but here the commentator explains it as two braids placed on the chest. Ruling (901) gives the final order of dressing: first qamis, then hair placed, then khimar over qamis under lifafah, then izar, then khirqah over izar, then lifafah over all. The proof is analogy to life practice and an athar from Umm al-Humayd, though the text cuts off. The Hanafi position is that these are sunnah practices, and the number of garments is five for a woman, though three are sufficient in necessity.

Core Idea
Uprooting fresh grass from graves is makruh because it glorifies Allah; dry grass may be removed without dislike.

This lesson addresses a specific ruling about the cemetery: is it permissible to remove grass or trees that grow on graves? The Hanafi school makes a clear distinction based on whether the vegetation is fresh (moist) or dry. The ruling is based on a Prophetic practice, but the lesson also corrects a common misunderstanding about placing flowers on graves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is uprooting fresh grass and trees (al-hashish wa al-shajar al-ratb) from the graveyard. The ruling is makruh (disliked). For dry vegetation (yabis), there is no harm (la ba's), meaning it is permissible.
Note that the Arabic term 'hashish' here means fresh grass, not the drug.
2

The explanation given is that fresh vegetation performs tasbih (glorification of Allah), so it should not be cut. Dry vegetation does not have this quality, so it may be removed.

The evidence is the famous hadith of Ibn Abbas: the Prophet passed two graves, took a fresh branch, split it in two, and planted each half on a grave, saying that it would lighten their punishment as long as the branch remained fresh. The hadith is from Sahih Bukhari, narrated by Ibn Abbas. It is an ahad hadith, so the ruling is based on zanni evidence.
4
Avoid the common misapplication
The text warns that this hadith does not justify the practice of placing flowers on graves. That practice is a later custom, not a sunnah, and may involve honoring the deceased or promoting trade of grave attendants.
The Prophet's action was specific and not generalized as a recommended practice for visitors.

When the Martyr Must Be Washed: Five Exceptions
Lists cases where the martyr must be washed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories of martyrs who must be washed and explain the Hanafi reasoning for each.

Core Idea
The martyr's exemption from washing does not apply to children, the insane, or those in major impurity.

The general rule in Hanafi fiqh is that a martyr (shahid) who dies in battle is not washed, because their death purifies them from sins. However, this rule has exceptions. In this passage, we learn five categories of people who must be washed even if killed in a battle or similar circumstances. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly handling the burial of those who are not purified by their martyrdom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and the exceptions
Start by recalling that the default ruling for martyrs is that they are not washed. Then list the five exceptions from the text: child, insane person, menstruating woman, postpartum woman, and a person in a state of janabah.
The text explicitly states these five categories.
2
Understand the two lines of reasoning
The text provides two distinct rationales. For the child and insane person: they are not accountable (mukallaf) and have no sins, so the killing does not purify them. For the menstrual, postpartum, and junub: they are in a state of major impurity that is not removed by death.
The child and insane lack sins; the others have an existing impurity.
3
Apply the principle that killing purifies sins only for the mukallaf
The purification by death is based on the removal of sins for those accountable. Since children and the insane are not accountable, their sins (if any) are not removed, so they remain in need of washing.
This is a qiyas (analogical reasoning) based on the illah (cause) of sin removal.
4
Derive the ruling for the three categories of impurity
For a menstruating, postpartum, or junub person, their state of impurity (hadath akbar) persists after death. The jurists reasoned that since the killing does not remove this impurity, they must be washed like any other deceased person.
This exception applies even if they are martyrs.

Core Idea
A slain person found in a city with unknown cause is washed and prayed over because the possibility of legal killing reduces the injustice.

This lesson addresses the status of a person found dead in a city when the manner of death is unknown. The Hanafi school derives the ruling from the prophetic practice of qasamah, where blood money is imposed, indicating that the killing was not a pure martyrdom. Understanding this ruling clarifies the distinction between battlefield martyrs and other deaths.

Identify the act and its ruling

The proof cited is the hadith of Sahl ibn Abi Hathmah and Rafi' ibn Khadij concerning qasamah (oath-taking) where the Prophet imposed blood money (diya) on the locality.

The Hanafi position is that uncertainty about the cause (unknown whether unjust or legal) leads to ghusl. The Shafi'i school may consider battlefield signs even in a city. The practical difference: Hanafis always wash in unknown city cases; Shafi'is may omit ghusl if wounds resemble battle.

The hukm level is sunnah ghayr muakkadah.
2
Understand the two permissible timings for ghusl
The text states that ghusl can be performed either before entering Madinah or after entering but before visiting the grave. This flexibility shows it is not a strict condition but a praiseworthy act.
3
Trace the qiyas from Makkah to Madinah
The author analogizes from the established practice of ghusl for entering Makkah (the asl) to entering Madinah or visiting the Prophet's grave (the far'). Identify the illa: both are sacred places requiring purification and respect.
The hadith of Ibn Umar is the dalil for the asl.
4
Recognize the objection and the Hanafi response
Some scholars object that ghusl is only proven for Makkah. The Hanafi response is that Madinah shares the quality of sanctity, especially due to the Prophet's presence, so the analogy holds.
This ikhtilaf is a legitimate difference in ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage outlines the recommended etiquettes for visiting Madinah and the Prophet's grave. The author states that one should perform ghusl either before entering the city or after entering but before proceeding to the grave, as is possible. Additionally, applying perfume and wearing one's best clothes are recommended as acts of respect. The evidence is provided by analogy (qiyas) with the practice of entering Makkah, for which the Prophet and the Companions performed ghusl. The author cites the hadith of Ibn Umar regarding ghusl upon entering Makkah, and a second hadith that the Prophet himself performed ghusl for entering Makkah, though the latter is incomplete here. The purpose is to demonstrate the precedent for such purification out of respect for sacred places, and by extension, for the Prophet's grave.

Key Terms
غسل (ghusl): Full ritual washing of the body; here recommended before entering Madinah or visiting the Prophet's grave.
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning; used to derive the ruling for Madinah from the established practice for Makkah.
