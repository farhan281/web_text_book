Three Conditions for a Valid Vow (Nazr)
Legal conditions for a vow to be binding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a binding nazr in Hanafi fiqh and apply them to classify whether a given vow is binding.

Core Idea
A vow is binding only if it is of a type that requires obligation, is intended for itself, and is not already wajib.

Vows (nuzur) are serious commitments in Islam. Not every promise to do a good deed becomes a binding vow. The Hanafi school specifies three precise conditions that must all be met for a nazr to be legally binding (lazim). This lesson unpacks those conditions and the evidence behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act Vowed
First, clearly identify the specific act that the person has vowed to perform. For example, vowing to pray two rak'ahs, to fast a day, or to give a specific amount in charity.
2
Apply Condition 1: Type Has a Wajib
Check whether the category of acts to which the vowed act belongs includes an obligatory (wajib) act. For example, prayer has obligatory prayers (fard), charity has obligatory zakat, fasting has obligatory Ramadan fast. Visiting the sick is not a category that includes a wajib, so such a vow is not binding.
This condition excludes acts that are purely recommended (mandub) or mubah.
3
Apply Condition 2: Maqsud bi al-Dhat
Determine whether the vowed act is intended for its own sake (maqsud bi al-dhat) or is a means (wasilah) to another act. For example, wudu is a means to prayer, not an end in itself. Vowing wudu is not valid. Vowing prayer, fasting, or charity is maqsud bi al-dhat.
Acts like tawaf, itikaf, and Hajj are also maqsud bi al-dhat.
4
Apply Condition 3: Not Already Wajib
Check whether the act is already a personal obligation (fard 'ayn) on the person. For example, vowing to pray the Zuhr prayer is invalid because Zuhr is already wajib. Vowing to pay zakat that is already due is also invalid. The vow must be for a supererogatory act that would become wajib through the vow.
If the act is already wajib, the vow adds nothing and is considered invalid (laghw).
5
Rule: Binding When All Conditions Met
If all three conditions are satisfied, the vow becomes binding (wajib) and must be fulfilled. The Quranic imperative 'liyufu nuzurahum' (let them fulfill their vows) establishes the obligation. Failure to fulfill a valid vow without a valid excuse incurs sin and requires expiation (kaffarah yamin).
The Hanafi view distinguishes between binding and non-binding vows; only valid vows require fulfillment.

Commentary
This passage establishes the legal framework for vows (nazr) in Hanafi fiqh. It states that a vow becomes binding (lazim) only when three conditions are fulfilled. First, the vowed act must belong to a category of acts that has an obligatory (wajib) counterpart. For example, visiting the sick is not of a type that is obligatory, so vowing it does not bind. Second, the vowed act must be intended for its own sake (maqsud bi al-dhat), not merely a means to something else. For instance, wudu (ablution) is not intended for itself but for prayer, so vowing wudu is not valid. Third, the vowed act must not already be obligatory (wajib) on the person. For example, vowing to pray Zuhr is invalid because it is already obligatory. The text then provides evidence: Quran 22:29 ('Let them fulfill their vows') and a hadith that if one vows something he cannot do, his expiation is the expiation of an oath, but if he can do it, he must fulfill it. The Hanafi school applies these conditions strictly, differentiating between vows that are binding and those that are not. The chapter specifically mentions fasting, prayer, and similar acts as examples of valid vows.

Key Terms
نذر (nazr): A vow to perform an act of worship or charity, making it binding under certain conditions.
مقصود بالذات (maqsud bi al-dhat): Intended for its own sake, not as a means to another act.
واجب (wajib): Obligatory act; in the context of nazr, an act that is legally required.
