Three Cases Requiring Half Sa' of Wheat in Hajj
Lists three partial violations and their penalty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three partial ihram violations that require sadaqah of half a sa' of wheat and explain the proof from the hadith of Ka'b ibn Ujrah.

Core Idea
Partial ihram violations — less than a limb or less than a day — require sadaqah of half a sa' of wheat, not a full dam.

In Hanafi fiqh, not all ihram violations require the same penalty. Major violations — like using perfume on a full limb, wearing stitched clothes for a full day, or covering the head for a full day — require a dam (sacrifice). But for lesser violations, a lighter penalty applies: sadaqah of half a sa' of wheat (or its value). This lesson identifies three such cases and traces their proof to the sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three partial violations
Read the passage and list the three acts that, when done partially (less than a limb or less than a day), require a sadaqah of half a sa'.
These are: applying perfume to less than a limb, wearing stitched clothes for less than a day, and covering the head for less than a day.
2
Determine the ruling level
The passage states that sadaqah (half a sa' of wheat) is wajib for these acts. Note that 'wajib' means obligatory, but of a lesser degree than fard.
In Hanafi usul, actions proven by zanni evidence are wajib, not fard.
3
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The author cites the hadith of Ka'b ibn Ujrah, where the Prophet gave options: fast three days, feed six poor people with half a sa' each, or sacrifice. This establishes the measure of half a sa' as the standard sadaqah for such violations.
The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim, but its transmission (ahad) gives it zanni thubut.
4
Contrast with full violations requiring dam
The commentary explains that if the same act is done to a full limb or for a full day, the penalty escalates to dam (a sheep). The half-sa' rule only applies to the partial cases.
This gradation shows the principle that penalty amount corresponds to the extent of the violation.
5
Apply to new scenarios
Consider a muhrim who wears a stitched glove (covering a hand, which is less than a full limb) for three hours. Does this require sadaqah or dam? Answer: sadaqah, because the time is less than a day, and the body part is less than a full limb.
This helps internalize the ruling.

Penalty for Shaving Head Hair in Ihram: The Quarter Threshold
Specifies the penalty for shaving less than one-fourth of the head during Ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi penalty for shaving head hair during Ihram and apply the one-fourth threshold to determine whether sadaqah or dam is required.

Core Idea
Shaving a quarter of the head or more requires a dam; less than a quarter requires sadaqah.

Many assume any hair removal during Ihram incurs the same penalty, but the Hanafi school distinguishes based on the proportion shaved. This lesson unpacks the rule and its practical application, helping you correctly identify the required expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is shaving head hair while in Ihram (without valid excuse). The ruling is that a penalty (jaza') becomes wajib. Determine the type of penalty based on the amount shaved.
The source specifies the threshold: one-fourth of the head.
2
Understand the threshold
If the shaved area is a quarter or more of the head, dam (sacrificing a sheep) is wajib. If less than a quarter, sadaqah (half sa' of wheat) is wajib.
The quarter is measured by area, not number of hairs.
3
Apply to a scenario
Given a specific case, decide whether the act triggers sadaqah or dam by estimating whether the shaved area reaches a quarter of the head.
This is a proportional measure unique to Hanafis.
4
Note cross-madhhab differences
The Shafi'i school does not use the quarter measure; any intentional head shaving requires dam. Recognize this as a valid ikhtilaf.
Hanafis rely on the principle of proportionality.

Penalty for Cutting Nails in Ihram
Ruling on the penalty for cutting one or more nails in Ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the graduated penalty for cutting nails in Ihram and apply the reduction rule when multiple nails are cut.

Core Idea
Each nail cut in Ihram requires half sa' sadaqah; multiple nails sum but are reduced to avoid reaching a full dam.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on cutting nails during Ihram. The school distinguishes between cutting one nail, an entire hand or foot, and scattered nails, each with graduated penalties. We will unpack the rule and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and base penalty
The act is cutting a nail while in Ihram. The base penalty is half a sa' of sadaqah per nail.
2
Determine penalty for a full hand or foot
Cutting all nails of one hand or foot incurs one full dam (slaughter of a sheep). This is a fixed penalty.
This is not stated explicitly in the passage but is implied by the reduction rule.
3
Summation for scattered nails
When nails are cut from different limbs, each nail incurs a separate sadaqah. These sadaqahs are summed.
The passage gives the example of five nails from separate fingers.
4
Apply the reduction rule
If the total sadaqah value equals or reaches the value of a dam, the total is reduced slightly so that it remains less than a dam. The reduction is at the person's discretion ("reduces as he wishes").
The purpose is to prevent the penalty from escalating to a full dam when the total equals a dam.

Core Idea
Tawaf with hadath requires sadaqah; tawaf al-sadr in janabah is invalid and requires dam.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on Tawaf performed without wudu (hadath) or in a state of major impurity (janabah). The text distinguishes between the two types of impurity and the different penalties, and provides a key hadith proof. We will explore the legal reasoning behind these distinctions and how they differ from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the impurity type

2

Locate the proof
The hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'Tawaf around the House is like prayer, except that you may speak in it.'
4
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah
Hanafis use this to argue that wudu is wajib for tawaf, not a condition, because speech is allowed.

Ruling on Leaving a Shawt from Tawaf al-Sadr
Ruling with proof on compensation for leaving a circuit from Tawaf al-Sadr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on omitting a circuit from Tawaf al-Sadr and identify the required compensation, citing the evidence from the hadith of Ibn Abbas.

Core Idea
For a single omitted circuit of Tawaf al-Sadr, give half a sa' of wheat; for the majority (four or more), sacrifice a sheep.

In Hajj, certain rituals are wajib. Omitting part of them requires compensation. This passage from Thamaratun Najah explains exactly what compensation is due when a pilgrim leaves out one or more circuits of Tawaf al-Sadr, and it provides the legal proof from the hadith of Ibn Abbas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Tawaf al-Sadr is wajib. If omitted entirely, a dam (sheep) is required. Determine what constitutes 'entire' omission versus partial.
Wajib means obligatory; omission requires compensation.
2
Understand the threshold: minimum vs majority
The minimum of tawaf is 3 circuits. If you omit less than the majority (1-3 shawt), each omitted shawt requires half a sa' sadaqah. If you omit the majority (4+ shawt), it is considered leaving the entire tawaf, requiring a dam.
This distinction is specific to the Hanafi school.
3
Locate the dalil
The main evidence is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'Whoever forgets or leaves something of his rituals must sacrifice blood.' This is used to justify compensation for any omission.
The imperative indicates obligation.
4
Note the Hanafi interpretation
The Hanafi school applies the hadith proportionally: for partial omission, the compensation is proportional (sadaqah); for complete or majority omission, it is a full dam.
This avoids overburdening the pilgrim.

Compensation for Omitting Pebbles from Stoning
Explains penalty for omitting pebbles from stoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for omitted pebbles and apply the principle of tadakhul to calculate the required penalty.

Core Idea
Missing a single pebble costs half a sa'; missing a full day's stoning costs a dam; and multiple days merge into one.

This passage from Thamaratun Najah explains the detailed rulings on what happens when a pilgrim misses some or all of the stoning pebbles during Hajj. Understanding this is crucial because the penalty varies based on quantity and the principle of tadakhul (merging) significantly reduces the burden.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, recognize that ramy al-jimar (stoning the jamarat) is wajib in the Hanafi school. Omission incurs a wajib compensation, either sadaqah or dam.
This is a ruling with proof; the matn states the general principle.
2
Determine the tiered compensation
For each individual pebble missed, half a sa' of wheat or equivalent sadaqah is required. If the total missed equals a full day's stoning (all pebbles of one day), a dam (slaughter of a sheep) is required instead.
A full day's stoning equals 7 pebbles on the 10th, 21 pebbles on the 11th and 12th (combined for the two days).
3
Apply tadakhul (merging)
If pebbles from multiple days are missed, the penalties do not add up; only one dam is required. This is based on the athar of 'Ata ibn Abi Rabah.
Tadakhul applies only when the omitted acts are of the same type (all ramy).
4
Review the evidence
The athar states: 'Whoever forgets one jamrah or all the jamarat until the days of tashriq pass, one dam suffices.' This supports tadakhul. Hadiths showing the Prophet performed ramy establish its wajib status.
The evidence is a mursal hadith (zanni al-thubut) but accepted by Hanafis.

Valuation of Game Killed by a Muhrim by Two Just Men
Ruling on valuation with Quran and hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on how compensation for game killed by a muhrim is determined and explain the Quranic and hadith proofs.

Core Idea
Two just men must value game at the killing site — compensation is based on expert valuation, not arbitrary payment.

This passage from a Hanafi fiqh text explains the method of determining compensation (fidyah) when a person in the state of ihram kills game. The ruling is derived from a clear Quranic command (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:95) and a hadith of the Prophet ﷺ concerning ostrich eggs. We will work through the ruling, the proof texts, and the practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is killing game (sayd) while in ihram (muhrim). The ruling is that wajib compensation must be paid, and its value is determined by two just men (adlan).
2
Locate the primary dalil
The primary proof is Quran 5:95, which commands: '...judged by two just men among you.' This establishes the requirement of expert valuation.
3
Extract the wajh al-dalalah
The operative phrase 'يَحْكُمُ بِهِ ذَوَا عَدْلٍ مِنْكُمْ' indicates that valuation must be by two just men, not the hunter alone. This is a command, so the ruling is wajib.
In Hanafi usul, the imperative form (yaf'alu) indicates obligation unless evidence shows otherwise.
4
Examine the secondary dalil
The hadith of Ka'b ibn Ujrah reports that the Prophet ﷺ assessed the value of ostrich eggs taken by a muhrim, showing that valuation in monetary terms is acceptable.
This hadith is cited to show that value (qima) is the basis for compensation when an equivalent animal is not available.
5
Apply the ruling practically
When a muhrim kills game, two just and experienced men must estimate the value at the exact location or nearby. The muhrim then pays that value as fidyah or offers an equivalent animal.
The valuation is location-specific because market prices vary.

If the muhrim's damage leaves the game able to escape and survive, only the reduction in value is due; if not, full value is due.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario during Hajj where a muhrim damages game but does not kill it. The key is not the type of damage but its consequence on the animal's ability to survive independently. The Hanafi school draws a clear line between partial and total destruction based on the animal's remaining capacity to flee from humans and protect itself. Core Idea
Hunting compensation: half a sa' per poor person or fast a day; remainder given in charity or fasted as a full day.

This lesson presents a core ruling from the chapter on hunting penalties in the Hanafi school. The text specifies the exact measure of compensation required when one hunts while in ihram. Understanding these precise amounts and the option between food and fasting ensures the penalty is fulfilled correctly.

Identify the act triggering compensation
The ruling applies to a person who hunts (or kills game) while in the state of ihram during Hajj or Umrah. The compensation is wajib.
This is a penalty, not a kaffarah; it is due once per animal.
2
State the two options for compensation
The text gives two options: either feed poor people or fast. For each poor person, you give half a sa' of food, or you fast one day instead of feeding one poor person.
The fasting option is a day per poor person you would have fed.

If after distributing half a sa' to each poor person, you have less than half a sa' left over, you still give it in charity or you fast a full day. You cannot fast a partial day.
Fasting is always a full day even if the food amount is less than half a sa'.

Core Idea
Cutting grass in the Haram is forbidden, except for idhkhir and truffles.

The sanctity of the Haram (Makkah) extends to its vegetation. In this lesson, we examine the ruling on cutting or grazing grass within the Haram boundaries, based on a clear prophetic hadith. Understanding which plants are excepted is crucial for pilgrims and residents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is cutting or grazing fresh grass (khala) in the Haram. The ruling is haram (prohibited) absolutely, unless an exception applies.
The prohibition covers both cutting and grazing, as both involve removing the grass.
2
Locate the primary evidence (dalil)
The evidence is a hadith narrated by Ibn Abbas recorded in Bukhari and Muslim. The Prophet (PBUH) said: 'Its fresh grass may not be cut' (لا يختلى خلاها).
The hadith also mentions other prohibitions: cutting trees, chasing game, and picking up lost items.
3
Identify the explicit exception
When Abbas asked 'except idhkhir?', the Prophet replied 'except idhkhir'. Therefore, idhkhir (a fragrant grass) is explicitly allowed.
Idhkhir was used by Arabs for roofing and thatching, showing that necessity or common benefit can justify an exception.
4
Derive the second exception (truffles) by qiyas
The text explains that truffles (kama't) are also excepted by analogy: they were commonly eaten as a vegetable and are not considered 'grass' in the usual sense. The underlying reason is that they provide benefit without damaging the sanctity.
Truffles are not explicitly mentioned in the hadith but are included by qiyas (analogical reasoning) because they share the same 'illa (effective cause) of being a useful plant that does not harm the Haram's character.
5
Determine the consequence of violation
Cutting prohibited grass without the exceptions necessitates compensation (dam), as mentioned in earlier lessons on violations of the Haram.
This reinforces that the prohibition is not merely advisory but carries legal liability.

Learning Objective

14 specific acts during Hajj each necessitate a dam (goat sacrifice).

During Hajj, certain violations require a blood sacrifice (dam) as expiation. This lesson presents the classic Hanafi list of 14 such acts, ranging from applying perfume to omitting a wajib act. Memorising this list is essential for every pilgrim and mufti.

1
Identify the category of offence

Work through the 14 acts one by one. Use a mnemonic: 'Perfume, henna, oil, sewn cloth, head cover, shave quarter head, cupping hair, armpit, pubic, neck, nails both, nails one, leave wajib, tawaf janabah.'

Learning Objective

Core Idea

During Hajj or Umrah, certain minor violations of Ihram require a specific expiation: half a sa' of wheat (about 1.5 kg) or its value. Knowing these thirteen cases helps pilgrims avoid or properly expiate mistakes. The Hanafi school also provides flexibility for violations committed due to a valid excuse.

Guided Path

Minimum of Hady: One Sheep
Ruling establishing the minimum animal for hady and its types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum accepted animal for hady, explain its ruling (wajib), and cite the hadith evidence that restricts the valid types.

Core Idea
Hady's minimum is one sheep; only camels, cows, and sheep are valid.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational ruling of hady: what animal is acceptable and what is the minimum. The matn clearly states that hady is from camels, cows, or sheep, and the lowest is a sheep. We will explore the evidence from the hadith of Ibn Abbas and understand why no smaller animal suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The matn establishes the minimum for hady: a sheep (shaat). Hady itself is wajib for tamattu' and qiran Hajj. Confirm that the ruling here is about the type of animal, not the obligation itself.
The hukm level of the hady sacrifice is wajib, not fard, because its evidence is ahad hadith.
2
Locate the dalil
The commentator cites the Quranic verse (2:196) and a specific hadith from Ibn Abbas (Bukhari 1988). The hadith explicitly lists camel, cow, and sheep as valid hady.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient to establish wujub.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith says 'in it is a camel, or a cow, or a sheep, or a share in a sacrifice.' The mention of only these three types indicates that no other animal (e.g., goat is included under shaat) can be used. The minimum is the sheep because it is the smallest of the listed animals.
Implicitly, animals smaller than a sheep are excluded by lack of evidence.
4
Note the grading
The commentator grades the animals: camel is best, cow is intermediate, sheep is the lowest. This does not affect the validity; any of the three fulfills the requirement, but the minimum is sheep.
For those who can afford, a camel is superior in reward.

Conditions of Hady Same as Udhiyah
Ruling equating conditions of udhiyah and hady

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid hady animal and explain why the Hanafi school applies the same requirements as udhiyah, using hadith evidence.

Core Idea
The conditions for a valid hady are identical to udhiyah: proper age and freedom from major defects.

This lesson explains a key ruling from the Hajj chapter: the animal for hady (the sacrificial offering during Hajj) must meet the same requirements as the udhiyah (Eid sacrifice). Understanding this equivalence prevents invalid offerings that would require compensation. The Hanafi school bases this on the shared purpose of drawing near to Allah through bloodshed and on explicit hadith evidence for udhiyah, extended by analogy to hady.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is offering a hady animal. The Hanafi ruling is that it must meet the same conditions as udhiyah; otherwise, the sacrifice is invalid and must be compensated.
The passage states: 'Whatever animal is permissible for udhiyah is permissible for hady.'
2
Locate the primary proof
The author cites two hadith: (1) Jabir's hadith on age requirement (musinnah) and (2) Bara's hadith listing four prohibited defects (blindness, sickness, lameness, emaciation).
These hadith directly apply to udhiyah, but the reasoning extends them to hady.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
The hadith explicitly state conditions for udhiyah. Since both udhiyah and hady share the same underlying rationale (qurbah through bloodshed), the same conditions apply via qiyas.
This is a clear example of qiyas jali (analogy) where the illa is shared purpose.
4
Identify the detailed conditions from the text
From the passage, conditions include: (a) age: the animal must be thani (camel 5 yrs, cow 2 yrs, sheep/goat 1 yr) except for sheep, a fat jadha' (6 months) is acceptable; (b) freedom from defects: no complete cut of ear, tail, hand, foot; no lost eye; not emaciated; not lame to the point of not reaching the slaughter place. These are based on the hadith.
The Hanafi position is that these conditions are identical for hady.
5
Determine the operative ruling (mufta bihi)
The mufta bihi is that the same age and defect restrictions apply. There is no intra-Hanafi disagreement on this, and it is considered muttafaq alayh among schools.
The passage emphasizes equivalence without any mention of ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Obligatory hadi must be slaughtered in the Haram; a defective voluntary hadi is slaughtered wherever it becomes defective.

In this lesson, we examine a fundamental rule of hadi: it must be slaughtered within the sacred precincts of the Haram. However, there is an important exception for voluntary (nafl) animals that become defective en route. We will explore the proofs from the Quran, sunnah, and rational argument, and understand the restrictions on who may consume the meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
State the default ruling: every hadi must be slaughtered within the Haram (the boundaries of the sacred mosque area). This applies to both obligatory and voluntary hadi under normal circumstances.
The term 'hadi' refers to an animal brought as a sacrifice during Hajj or Umrah.
2
Identify the Exception
Recognize the exception: if the hadi is voluntary (nafl) and becomes defective (e.g., becomes lame or sick) on the road, it is slaughtered at that location, not brought to the Haram.
Defective means the animal cannot proceed to the Haram without extreme hardship.
3

Analyze the five proofs cited: Quran verses (5:95 and 2:196) requiring the hadi to 'reach the Kaaba' and 'reach its place'; an athar from Imam Malik; a rational argument that sacrifice is tied to place; and a hadith about defective badanas.
Each proof reinforces the general rule or the exception.
4

Note: for a defective voluntary hadi, the wealthy (including the owner) may not eat from it; only the poor may consume. This is because it becomes like a dam ihsar (sacrifice of prevention).
This prevents the owner from benefiting from a sacrifice that did not fulfill its original purpose.
