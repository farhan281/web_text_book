Sadaqat al-Fitr for Self and Minor Children
Specifying the persons for whom Sadaqat al-Fitr must be paid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whom Sadaqat al-Fitr is obligatory for and apply the condition regarding the child's wealth.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib on the father for his poor minor children; if they have wealth, it is paid from their wealth.

This passage from Thamaratun Najah clarifies that Sadaqat al-Fitr is not only an individual duty but also extends to dependents. It explains the nuanced Hanafi position that even though the obligation is ultimately on the child, the father pays on his behalf if the child is poor, and from the child's wealth if the child is wealthy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the persons subject to obligation
The matn states one pays Sadaqat al-Fitr for oneself and for one's poor young children.
Note the phrase 'poor young children' (اولاده الصغار الفقراء) specifies the conditions.
2
Determine the ruling when the child has wealth
If the child is wealthy, the father pays from the child's wealth (وان كانوا اغنياء يُخرجها من مالهم).
The obligation shifts to the child's wealth, not the father's.
3
Examine the hadith proofs
Two hadith from Ibn Umar: first establishes Sadaqat al-Fitr on all Muslims including small and great; second ties it to those under one's support (ممن تمونون).
The second hadith directly links the obligation to maintenance.
4
Understand the basis for the father's responsibility
The commentator explains that the obligation is on the child, but the father pays if the child is poor because the child is under his maintenance.
Guardianship (wilaya) and maintenance (nafaqah) are key concepts.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
For a poor minor child, the father pays from his own wealth; for a wealthy minor child, the father pays from the child's wealth.
The source of funds depends on the child's financial status.

Grandfather's Obligation for Sadaqat al-Fitr of Grandchildren
Intra-Hanafi dispute over grandfather's Sadaqat al-Fitr obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the grandfather's obligation for Sadaqat al-Fitr and identify which position is the relied-upon (mukhtar) for fatwa.

Core Idea
In the Hanafi school, the grandfather is obligated for grandchildren's Sadaqat al-Fitr when the father is absent or poor, per the chosen opinion.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: whether the grandfather must pay Sadaqat al-Fitr for his grandchildren. The well-known transmission (zahir al-riwayah) exempts him, but the chosen opinion (mukhtar) obligates him when the father is absent or poor. Understanding the reasoning behind both views deepens your grasp of how the school handles textual evidence and analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two positions within the Hanafi school: the zahir al-riwayah and the mukhtar opinion. What does each say about the grandfather's obligation?
Note that both are Hanafi positions; the dispute is internal.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The opponent is the view from zahir al-riwayah. It holds that the grandfather is not obligated because the hadith only mentions the father.
Zahir al-riwayah is the primary transmitted corpus, but it is not always the final position.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The proof for the first view is purely textual: the hadith obligates the father, so no extension to the grandfather is made.
This is a strict textualist approach.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The chosen opinion uses analogy (qiyas): since the grandfather inherits and is responsible for maintenance in the father's absence, he is like the father for Sadaqat al-Fitr as well.
The illa (effective cause) is the maintenance obligation.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the father is poor and the grandfather is wealthy, the mukhtar view requires the grandfather to pay, while the other view does not.
This affects who must fulfill the wajib obligation.

Categories for Which Sadaqat al-Fitr is Not Obligatory
Listing those for whom one does not pay Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the categories of persons for whom Sadaqat al-Fitr is not obligatory and explain the underlying principle of kafalah.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is due only for those under your material dependency; not for those without your direct maintenance.

This lesson details the specific persons for whom a Muslim is not required to pay Sadaqat al-Fitr. Although the wife, adult children, and certain types of slaves are related, the ruling depends on whether the person bears their maintenance (kafalah). Understanding this principle removes confusion about family members and slaves and ensures correct fulfillment of the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories
List all persons mentioned in the matn: mukatab (slave with freedom contract), adult children, wife, shared slave (musharak), runaway slave (unless he returns), usurped slave (maghsud), imprisoned slave (mahbus).
The matn explicitly names these seven categories.
2
Determine the common principle
The common reason for exclusion is that these individuals are not under the full maintenance (kafalah) of the person. The hadith instructs paying for 'those you support' (tamunun).
Kafalah means bearing all expenses of living.
3
Examine each case briefly
For each category, understand why kafalah is absent: (1) mukatab works for his own freedom, (2) adult children are independent, (3) wife's nafaqah is compensation not dependency, (4) shared slave has multiple owners, (5-7) runaway, usurped, or imprisoned slaves are not in actual custody.
The wife's case is especially nuanced; her nafaqah is like wages for tamkin.
4
Apply the hadith evidence
The principle from the hadith 'support' excludes these categories. The ruling is based on the lack of full maintenance responsibility.
5
Distinguish from obligatory cases
Recognize that one must pay for oneself, minor children, and anyone else fully dependent. This lesson clarifies the boundaries.
Minor children are under full kafalah, so wajib.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib for every dependent you support — not for every relative or slave.

In this lesson, we examine whom the head of household must pay Sadaqat al-Fitr for. The Hanafi school ties this obligation to actual financial support (tamunun), not merely kinship or ownership. We will explore the rulings on grandfathers, various categories of slaves, and those who are excluded, all based on the hadith of Ibn Umar.

Guided Path
1
Understand the General Principle
The hadith states 'عمن تمونون' (from whom you support). The Hanafi school derives that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib for every person whose living expenses you are responsible for (kafalah). This includes yourself and your dependents.
The term 'tamunun' is key; it means 'those whom you support financially'.
2
Analyze the Grandfather Ruling
According to Zahir al-Riwayah, the grandfather is not obligated for grandchildren. However, the chosen position (mukhtar) is that when the father is absent or poor, the grandfather assumes the father's role and must pay.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; later authorities gave preference to the chosen view.
3
Identify Slaves for Whom Payment is Wajib
Payment is wajib for service-slaves, mudabbar, and umm walad, even if they are non-Muslim, because the master supports them. The key is actual financial support, not potential freedom status.
Contrast with the mukatab who is not supported.
4
Recognize Those for Whom Payment is NOT Wajib
No payment for: mukatab, adult child, wife, jointly-owned slave, absconding slave (unless returns), usurped slave, imprisoned slave. The reason: the master does not bear their expenses.
Wife is excluded because nafaqah is considered compensation, not true support.
5
Examine the Evidence
The primary evidence is the hadith of Ibn Umar with the imperative 'امر' indicating wujub. Additional athar from Ibn Umar and Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz support the exclusion of the mukatab.
The dalil is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but the ruling is considered wajib.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah opens the chapter on Sadaqat al-Fitr (the charity given at the end of Ramadan). The author first states the ruling that the sa' used for fitra is eight Iraqi ratls according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad. He then clarifies that everyone agrees a sa' consists of four mudds, but the disagreement is over the weight of the ratl. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is that one sa' equals eight ratls. The opposing position, held by Imam al-Shafi'i and (on this issue) Imam Abu Yusuf, is that one sa' equals five and one-third ratls. The author then presents the evidence for both sides. For the Hanafi view: (1) a hadith from Anas showing the Prophet used two ratls for wudu and a sa' of eight ratls for ghusl; (2) an athar from 'Umar stating the sa' was eight ratls, as recorded in al-Hidayah. For the opposing view: (1) a hadith from Abu Huraira implying the Madinan sa' was small; (2) a narration through Malik that he measured the Prophet's sa' and found it 5⅓ ratls; (3) the historical account of Imam Abu Yusuf visiting Madinah and verifying the sa' as 5⅓ ratls, leading him to adopt the Madinan view. The author then provides modern weight conversions: one sa' is approximately 3.538 kg (or 1.769 kg for half a sa') in the Shafi'i/Abu Yusuf system, and notes that if the Hanafi 8-ratl system is used, the ratl would be smaller (442.25 grams each). The passage thus thoroughly presents the inter-madhhab dispute on the definition of the sa' for fitra, along with practical modern equivalences.

Key Terms
صاع (ṣāʿ): A unit of volume/weight used for Sadaqat al-Fitr; equivalent to 4 mudds. The Hanafi school defines it as 8 Iraqi ratls, while others define it as 5⅓ ratls.
رطل (riṭl): A unit of weight. Iraqi ratl is used here. Its gram weight depends on the school: from the passage, if 1 sa' = 8 ratls, then 1 ratl ≈ 442.25 g; if 1 sa' = 5⅓ ratls, then 1 ratl ≈ 663.375 g.
مد (mudd): A unit of volume; one sa' equals four mudds. The mudd is the measure of what two cupped hands can hold.

Paying Cash Value (Qīmah) for Ṣadaqat al-Fiṭr
States the permissibility and superiority of paying cash value for ṣadaqat al-fiṭr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paying qīmah for ṣadaqat al-fiṭr, cite the hadith proofs, and explain when it is superior.

Core Idea
Paying cash value for ṣadaqat al-fiṭr is permissible and even superior when the poor's need is better served.

This passage from Thamaratun Najah addresses a practical question: can you give cash instead of food for ṣadaqat al-fiṭr? The author clearly states that it is permissible, and even preferable if the poor person already has food. The reasoning is supported by two hadiths from jizyah and zakāt where substitutes of equivalent value were accepted. We will examine the ruling, its evidence, and the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is paying qīmah (cash value) for ṣadaqat al-fiṭr. The ruling is jā’iz (permissible) and afḍal (superior) under certain conditions.
The text explicitly says 'اور قیمت دینا بھی جائز ہے'
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites two hadiths: one from Abū Dāwūd about Mu‘ādh taking cloth instead of dīnār for jizyah, and one from Bukhārī/Abū Dāwūd about Abū Bakr allowing dirhams/sheep instead of camels for zakāt.
Both are aḥādīth with zannī thubūt and dalāla.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalālah
The permission to accept substitutes of equivalent value in obligatory payments (jizyah, zakāt) establishes the principle that value can replace specific items in wajib duties. This is the proof for qīmah in ṣadaqat al-fiṭr.
The author explicitly states this principle at the end: 'اصول : کوئی چیز واجب ہو اس کی قیمت لگا کر کوئی دوسری چیز دینا جائز ہے۔'
4
Note the ikhtilāf
The Shāfi‘ī school generally does not allow value substitution for ṣadaqat al-fiṭr; they require the specific food items. The Hanafī position is mukhtalaf fīh.
See the hanafi_position_box for details.
5
Evaluate the Hanafī tarjīh
The author chooses permissibility based on the principle derived from the hadiths and the maslaḥa of fulfilling the poor's need more quickly. This is a muftā bihī position in later Hanafī fatwā.
Authority weight: fatawa muta'akhkhirīn.

Paying Cash Value for Sadaqat al-Fitr
Ruling with proof on paying value for Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on paying cash value for Sadaqat al-Fitr, explain the dalil from the hadith of Mu'adh and Abu Bakr, and identify the Shafi'i disagreement.

Core Idea
Paying cash value for Sadaqat al-Fitr is permissible and even preferable when it fulfills the poor's need better.

The Hanafi school uniquely permits giving the cash equivalent of the prescribed food items for Sadaqat al-Fitr. This lesson examines the textual proof from the hadith of Mu'adh and the letter of Abu Bakr, and the underlying principle that when a specific item is obliged, its value may be substituted based on istihsan. Understanding this ruling deepens appreciation of the Hanafi method of balancing textual evidence with the maqsad (purpose) of charity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its preferred application
The text explicitly states 'yajuz daf' al-qimah' (it is permissible to pay the value) and adds that it is 'afdal' (better) when the poor need other essentials. Note that this is not just a concession but a recommended practice.
The ruling applies to Sadaqat al-Fitr, which itself is wajib in the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the dalil (proofs) from the hadith
Two ahadith are cited: the first from Mu'adh ibn Jabal where the Prophet allowed taking clothes equivalent to a dinar for jizya; the second from Abu Bakr's letter on zakat allowing substitution of sheep or dirhams for a camel. Identify the key phrases that support value substitution.
These are ahad ahadith, hence zanni al-thubut, but they are strengthened by istihsan.
3
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah (point of evidence)
In the first hadith, the phrase 'aw 'adlihi' (or its equivalent) indicates that the Prophet accepted the value (thiyab) in place of the dinar. This shows that substituting value for a fixed item is allowed even in obligatory payments. The second hadith similarly shows acceptance of alternate items.
The Hanafi school extends this principle from jizya and zakat to Sadaqat al-Fitr by way of analogy (qiyas) and istihsan.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The author states: 'When a specific item is obliged, paying its value is permissible.' This is a general principle (qa'ida) derived from the proofs. It is this principle that allows cash payment for Sadaqat al-Fitr.
This principle is not universally accepted; it is specific to Hanafi usul where istihsan can override qiyas.
5
Note the cross-madhhab disagreement
The Shafi'i school does not allow paying value; they require the exact food items as specified in the hadith (e.g., sa' of wheat). The Hanafi response relies on the broader application of the principle of value substitution.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that Hanafi muftis routinely allow cash, while Shafi'i muftis require food.

When to Prefer Food over Cash for Sadaqat al-Fitr
Ruling on what to prefer in times of scarcity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding the preference for food grains over cash when paying Sadaqat al-Fitr during times of hardship.

Core Idea
In times of scarcity, giving wheat or barley is better than dirhams because it addresses immediate hunger.

This passage from Thamarat an-Najah provides a specific directive within the Hanafi school for the payment of Sadaqat al-Fitr. While the school generally allows paying the value in cash, it emphasises that when the community is suffering from scarcity, the giver should give actual staple foods. The commentary explains that this ensures the poor can prepare and consume the food without delay, fulfilling the purpose of the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is giving Sadaqat al-Fitr, and the ruling is a preference (afdal) for food over cash during hardship.
The ruling is not an obligation but a recommended superiority.
2
Note the condition
Identify the trigger 'zaman shiddah' (time of hardship/scarcity) which activates this preference.
Without this condition, cash may be equally or more preferable.
3
Understand the underlying reason
The commentary explains that the poor lack food and can cook and eat immediately, so food meets the urgent need better than cash.
This is based on the maqsad (purpose) of sadaqah to eliminate hunger.
4
Compare with normal times
In normal times, cash payment is permissible and may be better if the poor need other essentials, but scarcity adjusts the priority to food.
Flexibility is core to the Hanafi position.
5
Recognize cross-madhhab nuance
While Hanafis allow cash generally, the Shafi'i school always requires food items. This Hanafi ruling is a conditional preference within that flexibility.
Knowing this avoids confusion when comparing schools.

Exemptions from Sadaqat al-Fitr Based on Timing
Who is not obligated to give Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five exemptions from Sadaqat al-Fitr and explain the role of the sabab (cause) in triggering the obligation.

Core Idea
If the cause (dawn) does not apply to a person, Sadaqat al-Fitr is not obligatory.

We learned earlier that Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib at the true dawn of Eid day. But what if a person dies before that dawn, or becomes Muslim after it? This passage from Thamarat al-Najah clarifies five scenarios where the obligation does not arise. Understanding these exemptions hinges on the concept of sabab — the legal cause that triggers the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause of obligation
The text states that the true dawn (subh sadiq) on Eid day is the sabab. The obligation attaches when this cause is present for the person.
The cause is the time, not the person's wealth or Islam per se — those are conditions.
2
List the five exempted categories
The exemption applies if a person: (1) dies before dawn, (2) becomes poor before dawn, (3) converts to Islam after dawn, (4) becomes rich after dawn, or (5) is born after dawn.
These are the only exemptions mentioned in this lesson.
3
Understand the reason for exemption
In each case, the cause (dawn) did not occur while the person met the conditions (alive, Muslim, solvent). Therefore, the obligation never arose.
The sabab is the trigger; if it does not coincide with the person's eligibility, there is no duty.
4
Apply to a new scenario
Consider a person who is rich and Muslim at dawn, but then loses his wealth later that day. Does the obligation remain? Yes, because the cause applied at the moment of dawn.
The ruling is fixed at dawn; later changes do not affect it.

Recommended Time for Sadaqat al-Fitr Before Eid Prayer
Recommended timing for giving Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended time for giving Sadaqat al-Fitr according to the Hanafi school and identify the hadith evidence that supports it.

Core Idea
Giving Sadaqat al-Fitr before the Eid prayer is a recommended sunnah, not an obligation.

Zakat al-Fitr is an obligatory charity due at the end of Ramadan. However, its timing has a recommended aspect: giving it before the Eid prayer is a confirmed sunnah. This lesson explores the ruling and its proof from the hadith of Ibn Umar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is giving Sadaqat al-Fitr. The Hanafi ruling is that it is mustahabb (recommended) to give it before leaving for the Eid prayer.
Mustahabb is equivalent to sunnah ghayr muakkadah in the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the dalil
The dalil is a hadith from Ibn Umar (radiyallahu anhuma) that the Prophet (sallallahu alayhi wa sallam) commanded giving Zakat al-Fitr before the people go out to prayer.
The hadith is reported by Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's command (amr) regarding timing, after the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr is already established, indicates recommendation (nadb) rather than obligation, because the original obligation is determined by other evidence.
In Hanafi usul, a command after establishing an obligation often signals the preferred method, not a new obligation.
4
Note the hukm level
The ruling is sunnah muakkadah (confirmed sunnah). Giving before the prayer fulfills the sunnah; giving after still fulfills the obligation but misses the recommended timing.
There is no disagreement among the schools on this recommendation.

Timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Early or Late
Ruling with proof on early/delayed Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on giving Sadaqat al-Fitr before or after Eid prayer and identify the proofs for its validity.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is valid before or after Eid prayer, but delaying is makruh (disliked) — a flexible ruling grounded in hadith.

This lesson addresses a common question: can you give Sadaqat al-Fitr early or even after the Eid prayer? The Hanafi school provides clear flexibility, supported by the practice of Ibn Umar (RA) and a direct hadith from Ibn Abbas (RA). We will examine the ruling, the proofs, and why delaying is disliked but not invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is giving Sadaqat al-Fitr. The ruling is that it is valid whether given before or after Eid prayer, but delaying is makruh (disliked).
Makruh here is tanzihi (disliked), not tahrimi (prohibited).
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas (RA): giving after prayer is still a charity (sadaqa). This establishes validity post-prayer.
The hadith is from Abu Dawud 1609.
3
Examine the athar of Ibn Umar
Ibn Umar (RA) would give Fitr a day or two early, showing that early payment is permissible and practiced by the Companions.
Bukhari 1511, Abu Dawud 1610.
4
Understand the qiyas with zakat
The author analogizes Fitr to zakat: the cause (wealth) is present before the time of performance, so early payment is valid.
Qiyas with zakat shows the underlying principle.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: a Muslim need not worry if they give early or late; the obligation is fulfilled, though the preferred time is before Eid prayer.
This flexibility accommodates various circumstances.

Sadaqat al-Fitr: Timing and Distribution Rulings
Rulings on timing and distribution of Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the timing of Sadaqat al-Fitr (early and delayed) and identify the three distribution scenarios with the preferred method.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is valid before or after Eid; giving each fitrah to one poor is best, but pooling is permissible.

This passage from a Hanafi fiqh text addresses two practical questions about Sadaqat al-Fitr: When should it be given? And to how many recipients? The school teaches that the underlying cause (sabab) is wealth, not the day, so early payment is valid. Delaying until after Eid is disliked but still counts. On distribution, the preferred method is to give each person's fitrah to a single poor person, though there is some disagreement on splitting one fitrah among several. The text also clarifies that a group can combine their fitrahs and give to one poor person without issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on timing
The text states that delaying payment until after Eid is makruh (disliked) but valid, and that giving before Eid is permissible and even better. Understand the underlying reason: the sabab (cause) for wajib is wealth, not the day itself, just like Zakat where payment before year-end is valid.
The sabab analogy with Zakat is key to understanding early payment.
2
Examine the dalil for timing
The passage cites two pieces of evidence: the practice of Ibn Umar (giving one or two days before) and the hadith of Ibn Abbas (giving before prayer is accepted Zakat, after is general charity). Both show permissibility before and after.
Ibn Abbas hadith is from Abu Dawud.
3
Identify the three distribution scenarios
The passage presents three cases: (1) one person gives his fitrah to one poor (best), (2) one person divides his fitrah among multiple poor (disagreed upon), (3) a group gives their fitrahs to one poor (permissible, sahih).
The third scenario is stated as 'on the correct view' (على الصحيح).
4
Apply the distribution ruling and dalil
For scenario (1), the passage cites a hadith from Abu Hurairah about feeding sixty poor as support for giving to one recipient. For (3), it permits pooling. For (2), the text notes ikhtilaf without settling it, leaving the preferred view as giving to one.
The ikhtilaf on dividing one fitrah among many is not detailed here.

Just as the real cause of Zakat is ownership of nisab, and the completion of the year is the cause of performance, so if one gives before dawn, it will be valid. (2) There is an effect: Ibn Umar used to give it to those who accepted it, and they used to give one or two days before Fitr. (Bukhari, Kitab Sadaqat al-Fitr, p.246 no.1511; Abu Dawud, p.234 no.1610). (3) If one gives later, it is still valid; for this there is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'The Messenger of Allah obligated Zakat al-Fitr as a purification for the fasting from idle talk and obscenity, and as food for the poor. Translation: (1321) And each person gives his fitrah to one poor person. There is disagreement on distributing one fitrah among many poor persons. And it is permissible to give what is upon a group to one person in the correct narration. Commentary: Here are three issues: [1] One person's fitrah (half a sa' of wheat) given to one poor person is better. [2] Giving one fitrah to several poor persons is disagreed upon. [3] Several people's fitrah given to one poor person is permissible; all their fitrah will be valid. Explanation: (1) Giving one person's fitrah to one poor person: there is this hadith from Abu Hurairah: 'A man came to the Prophet... He said: Can you feed sixty poor persons?' Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the sixteen categories of people to whom zakat cannot be given and explain the rationale for each prohibition.

Core Idea
Zakat cannot be given to non-Muslims, the wealthy, family dependents, Banu Hashim, slaves, or used for shrouds/debts of the deceased.

This lesson covers the prohibitions on zakat distribution. The text lists sixteen categories of people who cannot receive zakat, including non-Muslims, wealthy individuals, one's own family (ancestors, descendants, spouse), Banu Hashim and their freed slaves, and others. Understanding these prohibitions ensures zakat is validly discharged.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah is a ruling statement that outlines the recipients of zakat (masraf al-zakat) and the categories to whom zakat cannot be given, along with the preferred order of distribution. The text first defines two categories: gharimin (those in debt due to a calamity or guaranteeing a debt) and ibn al-sabil (a traveler who is stranded despite being wealthy at home). It then lists sixteen categories of people to whom zakat is prohibited, including: non-Muslims, the wealthy who own nisab, the young child of a wealthy person, Banu Hashim and their freed slaves, the zakat giver's own ascendants and descendants, his spouse, his own slave, and the slave of a wealthy person. Additionally, zakat cannot be used for a deceased person's shroud or debt repayment, nor for purchasing a slave to free (except for expiation). However, if zakat is given to someone believed to be deserving but it later becomes apparent otherwise, the zakat is still valid. The best way to give zakat is to prioritize close relatives, then neighbors, then people of the locality, then those of the same profession, then the city, and then other cities. This order reflects the principle of beginning with those most closely connected to the giver.

Key Terms
مصرف الزكوة (masraf al-zakat): The categories of people entitled to receive zakat, as specified in the Quran and Sunnah.
غارمین (gharimin): A person in debt who cannot repay due to a calamity or having stood surety for another's debt.
ابن السبیل (ibn al-sabil): A traveler who has run out of money during a journey, even if he is wealthy at home.
بنو ہاشم (Banu Hashim): The clan of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) to whom zakat is forbidden as a form of honor.
مکاتب (mukatib): A slave who has entered into a contract of manumission (kitabah) to earn his freedom by paying installments.

Priority of Zakat Recipients
Lists the preferred order of Zakat distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred order of Zakat recipients according to the Hanafi school and identify that this order is recommended (mustahab) not obligatory.

Core Idea
Give Zakat to nearest relatives first; it's recommended, not obligatory.

The Hanafi school teaches that while any of the eight Quranic categories of Zakat recipients (masarif) are valid, there is a preferred order that maximizes reward. This lesson presents the sequence from Thamaratun Najah, beginning with the closest relatives and extending outward. Understanding this order helps a Muslim distribute Zakat in a way that fulfills both the obligation and the sunnah of prioritizing ties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first priority
The passage states that the best recipient is the nearest relative (قریب ترین رشتہ دار). This includes parents, children, siblings, and other close family members who are eligible for Zakat (i.e., poor).
Non-dependent relatives are eligible.
2
List the subsequent priorities
After relatives, the order is: neighbor, people of the locality, fellow professionals, city dwellers, and finally people of other cities. Each step moves further in relationship and proximity.
This order reflects the principle of starting with those closest to the giver.
3
Recognize the legal status
The term 'بہترین' (best) indicates that following this order is mustahab (recommended), not wajib. One may give to a lower priority recipient and the Zakat is still valid, but the reward is less.
The Hanafi position is clear: priority is sunnah, not fard.
