Core Idea
A new Muslim in non-Muslim lands is held blameless for missing obligations due to ignorance.

This lesson examines a specific concession (rukhsah) in Hanafi fiqh for a person who embraces Islam while living in a territory where Islamic law is not applied. Because the means of learning the sharīʿa are limited, the new Muslim is excused for any shortcomings caused by ignorance. This is not a general principle, but applies specifically to those who become Muslim in dār al-ḥarb.

The excuse applies until knowledge becomes accessible.

Normally, a Muslim living in dār al-Islām is not excused for ignorance because the means of learning are available. This ruling is a concession for the unique circumstances of dār al-ḥarb.

Core Idea
Ta'rif is nothing — imitating pilgrims without prophetic evidence is an innovation.

This brief lesson tackles a single, decisive statement: the practice known as ta'rif — taking sacrificial animals to an open field on the day of 'Arafah to imitate the pilgrims — has no basis in the Hanafi school. The text simply says 'it is nothing.' This ruling cuts through the common assumption that any act resembling Hajj rites is virtuous. Understanding why ta'rif is rejected helps us distinguish authentic sunnah from later innovations.

Guided Path

2
Locate the ruling
The text states explicitly: 'al-ta'rifu laysa bishay' — 'Ta'rif is nothing.' This means it carries no religious weight or recommendation.

Core Idea
Prompt the dying with the shahada gently; after burial, talqin is permissible but disputed.

This lesson addresses two distinct moments of talqin (prompting with the shahada): at the time of death and after burial. The Hanafi school considers the first a sunnah act to help the dying person end with faith, provided it is done without insistence. The second, post-burial talqin, is considered permissible (mashru') by many Hanafis, but a minority reject it. We will examine the textual evidence and the basis of the ikhtilaf.

Insistence may cause distress at a critical moment.

Examine the evidence for talqin at death
The primary dalil is the hadith 'Laqqinu mawtakum la ilaha illa Allah'. Determine its status (Sunnah ahad) and how Hanafi usul derives a sunnah ruling from it (al-amr yufidu al-nadb).
The command 'laqqinu' indicates recommendation due to the context.

The matn states 'Talaqinuhu fi al-qabr mashru'' (prescribed) and immediately mentions a contrary view: 'Wa qila la yulaqqan' (and it is said: do not prompt). Identify the two positions and the basis for each.

The preferred Hanafi view (mufta bihi) is that post-burial talqin is permissible (mashru'), provided it is done quietly as a supplication, not as loud instruction. The opposing view is considered weaker.

Core Idea
Two garments suffice for the shroud when necessity arises; the sunnah is three but necessity relaxes the requirement.

The shroud is an essential preparation for the deceased, but what if sufficient cloth is unavailable? This lesson examines the Hanafi position that in necessity, just two garments—the izaar and lifafah—are enough. We will study the proofs from the sunnah and the practice of the Companions that establish this dispensation, while recognizing that three garments remain the optimal sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling Level

The illah (effective cause) is necessity (darurah hajah).
5

Cross-madhhab comparison.

Preference for White Cotton Shroud
Ruling on the preference for white cotton cloth in shrouding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the preferred shroud material, cite the two hadith proofs, and explain why the command indicates recommendation (sunnah) not obligation.

Core Idea
White cotton is the preferred shroud – the Prophet's practice and command establish desirability, not compulsion.

When preparing a deceased Muslim for burial, the choice of shroud material and color carries legal significance. This passage from a classical Hanafi text addresses the preference for white cotton, presenting two key proofs from the sunnah. Understanding the distinction between recommended and obligatory actions is essential for correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
First, note the explicit statement: 'و فضل البياض من القطن' – white cotton is preferred. This is the core ruling: the Hanafi school holds that using white cotton for the shroud is highly recommended (sunnah muakkadah).
The ruling is about preference, not obligation.
2
Understand the first proof: Prophetic practice (fi'li)
The author cites the hadith of Aisha (ra) that the Prophet Muhammad (صلى الله عليه وسلم) was shrouded in three white Yemeni cotton garments. This actual practice (fi'l) indicates that white cotton is the preferred material, as the Prophet chose it for himself.
The hadith is recorded in Bukhari, Muslim, and Abu Dawud.
3
Understand the second proof: Explicit command (amr)
The hadith of Samurah ibn Jundub reports the Prophet's command: 'Wear white, for it is purer and better, and shroud your dead in it.' This command (amr) is a direct instruction to use white shrouds.
The command is from Tirmidhi.
4
Analyse the legal force of the command
Although the hadith contains an amr, the Hanafi school interprets it as indicating recommendation (nadb) rather than obligation (wujub). The reasoning: the Prophet himself was shrouded in white, but he did not prohibit other colors; moreover, the command is supported by the general permissibility of any clean cloth. Thus, white cotton is mustahabb, not fard.
This distinction is critical: a command can be interpreted as obligatory or recommendatory based on context and other evidences.

Core Idea
The shroud of the dead must be simple: the qamees has no sleeves, collar, or pocket; the izaar and lifafah cover from head to foot.

This lesson explains the physical description of the three recommended shroud garments in the Hanafi school. The qamees must not have sleeves (kumm), collar (dakhreez), pocket (jeeb), and its edges must not be sewn (takf). These are specifically prohibited to maintain simplicity.
The prohibition applies to the qamees; the other pieces are also simple sheets.
4
Understand the rationale behind the specifications
The shroud symbolizes humility and equality in death. Avoiding tailoring and ornamentation emphasizes that all are equal before Allah.

Walking Behind the Bier: Superior Position
Ruling with proof on the preferred position when walking with the bier

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Walking behind the bier is more virtuous than walking in front, just as fard is superior to nafl.

This lesson examines the preferred position when accompanying a funeral bier. The Hanafi school, relying on the hadith 'the bier is to be followed, not followed,' holds that walking behind is superior, but walking in front is permissible as demonstrated by the practice of the Prophet and the Caliphs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is walking with the funeral bier. The ruling states that walking behind is more virtuous (afdal) than walking in front.
The comparison is made to the superiority of fard over nafl prayer.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The first hadith (from Ibn Mas'ud) records the Prophet saying: 'The bier is to be followed, and not to be a follower; there is none with it who precedes it.' This implies the mourner should be behind.

Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'followed' (متبوعة) indicates that the natural position of the mourners is behind the bier. Walking in front would contradict this directive.
The word 'متبوعة' is a passive participle implying obligation of following.
4

A second hadith (from Salim) reports that the Prophet, Abu Bakr, and Umar walked in front of the bier. This seems to contradict the first hadith.
This hadith is found in Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school reconciles the two: the first hadith shows preference for walking behind; the second shows permissibility of walking in front. Thus, walking behind is more virtuous, but walking in front is allowed.
The mufta bihi is that walking behind is afdal, and walking in front is permissible (ja'iz).

Core Idea

Burial rites in Islam are guided by prophetic practice. Among these is the shape of the grave. The Hanafi school follows the sunnah of the Prophet (peace be upon him) in making the grave musannam (hump-shaped) rather than murabba' (square). Guided Path

2

The report from Sufyan al-Tammar states he saw the Prophet's grave as musannam (hump-shaped). This direct observation provides a tangible model to follow.
This hadith is in Sahih al-Bukhari.
3
Examine the second evidence: the hadith on leveling graves
The hadith instructs 'Ali to level any elevated grave (qabr mushrif). This prohibits excessive raising, but allows a modest hump.
This hadith is in Sahih Muslim.
4
Reconcile the two evidences
Together, the two hadiths show that a moderate hump is the sunnah (from the Prophet's grave) while extreme height is forbidden (from the leveling command).
5
Distinguish from other practices
Making the grave perfectly flat or square (murabba') is considered makruh tanzihi (disliked) in the Hanafi school.
Other schools may prefer square; the Hanafi follows the prophetic hump.

Ruling on constructing structures over graves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on building on graves, distinguish between building for adornment (haram) and for strengthening (makruh), and cite the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Building on graves for adornment is haram; for strengthening it is only makruh — intention matters.

This passage from Thamaratun Najah addresses a common concern: may one construct a tomb or marker on a grave? The Hanafi school distinguishes between building for beautification (adornment) which is forbidden, and building to preserve the grave from collapsing, which is disliked but not forbidden. It also briefly covers writing on graves and burial in houses. We will examine the evidence and the key distinction.

Guided Path
1
State the primary rulings
Identify the three acts: building on graves (with two sub-cases), writing on graves, and burying in houses. For each, note the hukm: building for adornment is haram, building for strengthening is makruh, writing is permissible, burying in houses is makruh for non-prophets.

Locate the hadith evidence
The author cites two hadith: one from Muslim and Abu Dawud prohibiting plastering (تجصيص) and building on graves; another from Muslim commanding to level elevated graves. Understand their relevance.

The prohibition in the hadith is general, but the Hanafi school interprets it as specifically targeting adornment (زينة) because the act of strengthening (إحكام) serves a different purpose and is not included in the same prohibition. The distinction is based on the intention (niyyah) behind the construction.

The ruling on building applies to graves of non-prophets. Burying in houses is disliked because it is a special practice of prophets. Writing on the grave to preserve its identity is permissible provided no disrespect.
Prophets have the distinction of being buried in homes; for others it is makruh.
5
Apply to a practical scenario
Consider a family building a dome over a grave: if for beautification, it is haram; if to prevent collapse, it is makruh. Also, adding a nameplate is permissible.
Intention is the decisive factor.

Ruling on burying deceased within homes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on burying in houses and explain the evidence from the practice of Umar (RA).

Core Idea
Burying in houses is a prophetic privilege; for others it is makruh tanzihi.

This lesson addresses a common question: can a Muslim be buried in their own home? While some may see it as an honour, the Hanafi school teaches that it is only permissible for prophets. The ruling is based on the practice of the Companions, particularly Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is burying a deceased person inside a residential house. The ruling is makruh tanzihi (disliked, not sinful).
Makruh tanzihi means it is better to avoid, but not inherently prohibited.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason given is that this practice is a special privilege (ikhtisas) exclusively for prophets. Hence, common believers should not imitate it.
Prophets are sometimes buried where they died, e.g., Prophet Muhammad (ﷺ) in Aisha's room.
3
Locate the dalil
The proof is a narration from Umar (RA) who instructed: 'If you do not permit, then return me to the public cemetery.' This shows the norm is communal burial.
Hadith from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, vol. 7 p. 436.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Umar's request to be buried in the public graveyard indicates that he considered burial in homes as outside the sunnah for ordinary people. His action is a qawl sahabi (Companion's statement/practice), a valid source in Hanafi usul.
The chain is mursal (disconnected) but accepted for makruh rulings.
5
Classify the authority and consequence
Since the dalil is zanni (speculative), the ruling is makruh tanzihi, not haram. Violation is disliked but not sinful.
If someone buries in a house, the burial is valid but contrary to the preferred practice.

This lesson examines the ruling on burying in a structure called 'fisaqi'—a solid dome-covered building used for multiple burials, especially by the wealthy in the author's time. The Hanafi school holds that such construction over graves is disliked (makruh tanzih) when done without genuine necessity, as it contradicts the Sunnah of simple graves and may involve ostentation.

Two reasons are given: (1) The structure is a solid building, which hadith prohibits (referring to a general prohibition of building over graves). (2) It is done without necessity, often for show (riya), and may involve mixing men and women, making it disliked.

The Hanafi position is that it is makruh tanzih (slightly disliked). This is based on the hadith's prohibition being general and the absence of strong harm. The mufta bihi is makruh tanzihan.

The practical difference between makruh tanzih and haram is that the former does not incur sin but is discouraged, while the latter is sinful. In Hanafi practice, it is better to avoid such structures unless necessary.

Ruling on Visiting Graves: Recommendation with Conditions
Ruling with proof on visiting graves, with conditions extracted from supporting hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on visiting graves for men and women, and identify the four conditions for women derived from the hadith.

Core Idea
Visiting graves is recommended for all Muslims, but women must observe hijab, avoid wailing, avoid innovations, and visit only occasionally.

This passage establishes the Hanafi ruling that visiting graves is mustahabb (recommended) for both men and women, citing multiple hadith proofs. The commentary clarifies that for women, four conditions must be met to avoid what the Prophet (peace be upon him) cursed. Understanding these conditions prevents common misunderstandings about women visiting graves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling from the matn
Read the first line: 'ندب زيارتها للرجال والنساء على الاصح'. This states the ruling: nadb (recommended) for men and women according to the most correct opinion.
The term 'nadb' in Hanafi fiqh is equivalent to mustahabb or mandub.
2
Locate the primary proof and its usul interpretation
The hadith of Ibn Buraydah contains the command 'fazuruha' (so visit them). In Hanafi usul, a command indicates obligation, but here the earlier prohibition ('I had forbidden you') shows the command is to remove the prohibition, thus indicating recommendation, not obligation.
This is a classic example of qarīna (context) that transforms the default wujūb to nadb.
3
Extract the four conditions for women from the supporting hadith
From the hadith of Aisha (proper hijab) and the hadith of Ibn Abbas (cursing women who visit with innovations), the commentary derives four conditions: (1) proper hijab, (2) no prostration or lighting lamps (acts of innovation), (3) no excessive wailing (jaza' faza'), (4) occasional visits only (to avoid fitna).
These conditions are derived by reconciling the apparent contradiction between the general permission and the curse.
4
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
While the Hanafi school permits women with conditions, the Maliki school discourages women's visits altogether out of caution. The text shows this is a legitimate area of ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi position is based on the authentic hadith of Aisha and the general command.

Reciting Yasin at the graveyard is recommended (mustahabb) – a proven sunna, not an innovation.

This lesson examines the ruling for reciting Surah Yasin when visiting the graveyard. The Hanafi school holds it as mustahabb based on a clear prophetic command, while some other scholars consider it an innovation. Understanding the proof and its strength is essential for correct practice.

Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'iqra'u' (recite) appears in a context that is not obligatory – visiting graves is a recommended act. According to Hanafi usul, an imperative in a non-obligatory context indicates nadb (recommendation). The phrase 'ala mawtakum' includes both the dying and the dead in graves.
The Hanafi school does not take the imperative at face value for wujub when it is overridden by contextual evidence (qarinah).

Some scholars (e.g., Ibn Taymiyyah) consider reciting Yasin at the grave an innovation because they require a specific proof for each act of worship. The Hanafi school relies on the generality of the hadith and the established practice of the Salaf. The mufta bihi is mustahabb.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both sides have evidence.
