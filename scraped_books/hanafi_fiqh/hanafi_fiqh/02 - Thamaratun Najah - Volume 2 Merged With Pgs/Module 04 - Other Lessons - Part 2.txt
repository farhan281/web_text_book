Sitting on the Grave for Recitation (966)
Ruling on sitting on the grave for Quran recitation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sitting on a grave for Quran recitation and identify the proof from the practice of a Companion.

Core Idea
Sitting on a grave for Quran recitation is not makruh; the act's purpose changes the ruling.

The text addresses a common question: Is it permissible to sit on a grave while reciting the Quran? The general ruling is that sitting on graves is disliked (makruh), but the Hanafi school makes an exception when the purpose is Quranic recitation. This lesson examines the evidence and the reasoning behind this exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is sitting on a grave specifically for Quran recitation. The ruling stated is 'la yukrahu' (not disliked) in the chosen (mukhtar) opinion.
The general ruling on sitting on graves is makruh; this is an exception.
2
Note the exception
The exception is for the purpose of recitation. Without recitation, the general dislike remains.
3
Locate the proof
The proof is the practice of the Companion Abdullah ibn Umar, who recommended reciting the opening and closing of Surah al-Baqarah at the head of the grave after burial.
The hadith is from Sunan al-Bayhaqi and is considered zanni in transmission and meaning.
4
Understand authority weight
The text says 'fi al-mukhtar' (in the chosen opinion), indicating this is the relied-upon view for fatwa among later Hanafi scholars.
The general dislike of sitting on graves is from earlier sources; this exception is a later preferred view.

Actions Disliked on Graves: The Ruling and Its Proof
Ruling on four disliked actions on graves with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four actions that are makruh on graves according to Hanafi fiqh and explain the evidence from the hadith.

Core Idea
Sitting, treading, sleeping, and relieving yourself on graves is prohibitively disliked—like sitting on fire.

This passage from Thamarat al-Najah lists four actions that are makruh (disliked) when done on graves. The text provides a hadith that powerfully illustrates the severity of such disrespect. In Hanafi fiqh, these actions are categorized as makruh tahrimi, meaning they are close to haram. Understanding this ruling helps us maintain proper adab (etiquette) when visiting graves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four disliked actions
Locate the four actions explicitly mentioned in the matn: sitting on graves (except for recitation), treading/walking over them, sleeping on them, and relieving oneself on them.
The exception for recitation is stated directly in the text.
2
Note the exception to sitting
The text says 'for other than recitation' (li-ghayr qira'ah), indicating that sitting to recite Quran is an exception and not disliked.
This exception is based on the virtue of recitation over the grave.
3
Examine the hadith proof
Read the hadith from Abu Hurairah. It compares sitting on a grave to sitting on a burning ember, showing the severity of the act.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud and is considered sound (hasan).
4
Understand the Hanafi classification
Based on the strong prohibition in the hadith, Hanafi scholars classify these actions as makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked), not haram, because the hadith is ahad (single-chain) and does not reach the level of definitive proof for haram.
Makruh tahrimi means it is sinful to do, but not as severe as haram.
5
Apply the ruling to real-life scenarios
Consider situations like visiting a grave, walking through a cemetery, or finding a secluded spot. The ruling warns against disrespect, but necessity may permit some actions (e.g., walking if there is no path).
The exception for recitation shows that the prohibition is not absolute.

Core Idea
Reciting Ya-Sin in the graveyard brings reward and lightens punishment; sitting on the grave for recitation is not disliked.

Visiting graves is a recommended act that reminds us of the afterlife. This lesson focuses on two specific rulings: the virtue of reciting Surah Ya-Sin during visitation, and whether it is permissible to sit on the grave while reciting. The Hanafi school holds that recitation is rewarded and that sitting for recitation is not disliked, based on a transmitted proof.

The hadith is considered sound enough in the Hanafi school to base virtue.

Read the second statement: 'ولا يكره الجلوس للقرائة على القبر في المختار'. The phrase 'في المختار' means the preferred opinion holds that sitting for recitation is not makruh (disliked).
This is an exception to the general dislike of sitting on graves.

Note that idle sitting (without recitation) is dislikes, as covered in the next lesson. The presence of a religious purpose (recitation) removes the dislike.

Disliked and Permissible Actions in the Cemetery
Rulings on disliked acts and uprooting vegetation

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sitting idly, treading, sleeping, and relieving oneself on graves are disliked; uprooting fresh vegetation is disliked but dry vegetation is permissible.

Respecting the deceased extends to how we behave in the cemetery. This lesson covers actions that are disliked (makruh) when done on or near graves, and the specific ruling on removing vegetation. The Hanafi school makes a clear distinction between fresh and dry vegetation based on the principle that fresh vegetation glorifies Allah.

The reason for the distinction is that fresh vegetation glorifies Allah, so removing it is disrespectful.
3

Consider a cemetery with both dry and fresh grass. You can remove dry without dislike, but avoid removing green plants unless necessary. If necessary, some scholars allow it, but the text indicates a general dislike.

Categories of Shahīd: No Ghusl for the Fully Wronged
Definition and categories of shahīd and ruling of no ghusl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for a person to be considered a shahīd (martyr) and explain when burial without ghusl is required.

Core Idea
A martyr is buried without ghusl only if the killing is fully unjust, requiring qisas not diyah.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi criteria for martyrdom (shahāda) and the practical ruling that martyrs are not given ghusl. The passage from Thamaratun Najah defines several categories of shahīd and explains the reasoning behind the omission of ghusl, relying on hadith and athar. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly applying funeral rites in accordance with the sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is the burial of a shahīd without ghusl. The ruling is sunnah muakkadah (established practice) for those who meet the specified conditions.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites multiple proofs: the hadith of 'Ammar ibn Yasir instructing burial in his clothes without ghusl, the athar from 'Amir regarding a man killed by thieves, and the athar from Abu Ishaq.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
These proofs indicate that the body is not washed: 'Ammar's explicit command 'Bury me in my clothes' and the practice of burying those killed unjustly without ghusl.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some Shafi'i scholars require ghusl for martyrs killed by Muslims, but the Hanafis distinguish based on whether the killing necessitates qisas (retaliation) or diyah (blood money).
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is that when qisas is required, the victim is fully wronged (mazlum) and qualifies as a shahīd without ghusl; if diyah applies, the wrong is less complete and ghusl is given.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah defines the categories of a shahīd (martyr) in Hanafi fiqh. The passage is a ruling with proof, as it provides both the legal definitions and supporting evidence from hadith and athar. The author begins by listing the types of individuals who are considered martyrs: those killed by rebels, highway robbers, or thieves in their home at night, those found dead in the battlefield with a wound, and those killed unjustly by a Muslim with a sharp weapon. The key condition is that the killing must be wrongful (zulman), and the martyr must be Muslim, adult, and free from major ritual impurity (janabah), menstruation (hayd), and postnatal bleeding (nifas). The commentary explains that if a person is found in the battlefield with a wound, it is assumed they were killed unjustly; if no wound, they may have died of fear and are not a martyr. For the case of killing by a Muslim with a sharp weapon, the critical distinction is whether the killing necessitates qisas (retaliation) or diyah (blood money). If qisas is required, the victim is considered fully wronged and is a martyr; if diyah is required, the wrong is considered less severe, and ghusl is given. The proofs include the hadith of 'Ammar ibn Yasir, who instructed his companions to bury him in his clothes when killed by rebels, and the athar from 'Amir about a man killed by thieves. These establish the practice of burying martyrs without ghusl. The passage thus clarifies the Hanafi position that ghusl is not given to martyrs who meet these conditions.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahīd): A person killed unjustly in certain specified ways, who is to be buried without ghusl and in his clothes.
محدد (muhaddad): A sharp object that cuts, such as a sword or knife.
معركة (ma’rakah): Battlefield.
اثر (athar): A wound or sign that indicates the person was killed unjustly in the battlefield.

Irtithath: When a Martyr Must Be Washed
Ruling on irtithath with proof from action of 'Umar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a martyr who benefits after being wounded and apply the proof from the incident of 'Umar.

Core Idea
A martyr who benefits after the wound loses the exception from ghusl.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on irtithath — a condition that determines whether a martyr is buried without ghusl or requires the full funeral wash. The passage clarifies that a martyr who eats, drinks, or receives any worldly benefit after being wounded and after the battle ends is no longer considered a 'complete oppressed one' and must be given ghusl. The proof comes from the incident of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, who was stabbed but then ate and drank, and consequently was washed, enshrouded, and prayed over.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what action is being ruled upon and what the hukm (legal classification) is. Here, the act is a wounded person who benefits from worldly things after the battle ends. The ruling: ghusl is wajib for such a person.
The default for a martyr who dies on the battlefield is no ghusl; irtithath changes that.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The text cites the incident of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab (from Sunan al-Bayhaqi) as evidence. In that incident, after being stabbed, 'Umar ate and drank, and then was given ghusl. This is a qawl sahabi (statement of a Companion), which is a source in Hanafi usul.
The athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i is also cited to confirm the distinction between one who dies immediately and one with a sign of life.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal link)
The operative element in the dalil is the fact that 'Umar ate and drank after being wounded. This constitutes irtithath (benefiting from worldly life). Since the Prophet's Companions treated him with ghusl, it establishes that irtithath removes the special status of a martyr. The link is: benefit after wound → loss of complete oppression → ghusl becomes wajib.
The Hanafi principle: the general rule of ghusl for every deceased Muslim applies unless a specific exception is proven. Irtithath prevents the exception from applying.
4
Note the scope and mufta bihi position
The ruling applies to any soldier who, after being wounded in a legitimate jihad and after the war has ended, benefits from worldly things (eating, drinking, treatment, etc.). The mufta bihi position is that such a person must be given ghusl. There is no intra-Hanafi disagreement.
The other schools may have different criteria, but within Hanafism this is the relied-upon view from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage discusses the condition of irtithath, which refers to benefiting from worldly life after being wounded in battle. The ruling: if a martyr does not eat, drink, or benefit after being wounded and before death, he is considered fully wronged (kamil mazlum) and is buried without ghusl. However, if he does benefit (e.g., eats, drinks, receives medical treatment), then he is not at the level of the martyrs of Uhud and must be given ghusl. The key takeaway: the martyr's immunity from ghusl is contingent on his being a complete victim; any worldly benefit after the injury diminishes that status and requires normal funeral rites.

No Obligation to Make a Will for Missed Fasts Before Excuse Ends
Ruling on the obligation of a will for missed fasts when death occurs before excuse ends.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on making a will for missed fasts when death occurs before the excuse ends, and explain the evidence from Quran and athar.

Core Idea
Obligation of qada arises only after capacity; death before capacity leaves no duty.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a person misses Ramadan fasts due to a valid excuse (illness or travel) but dies before the excuse ends. Is there an obligation to make a will for qada or fidyah? The Hanafi school, relying on the Quranic deferral and a Tabi'i report, rules that no such obligation exists. We will unpack the legal reasoning and evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question and Hanafi ruling
The passage asks whether making a will (wasiyyah) to compensate for missed Ramadan fasts is obligatory when death occurs while the excuse (illness or travel) still persists. The ruling: it is not obligatory (la yajib).
This applies to both qada (making up fasts) and fidyah (compensatory feeding).
2
Locate the primary dalil (Quran)
The verse Surah al-Baqarah 2:185 is cited. It allows the ill and traveler to defer fasting to other days. The operative phrase is 'فعدة من أيام أخر' – 'an equal number of other days'.
This indicates the obligation is deferred, not immediately due.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The deferral implies that the fast is not obligatory until the person has the ability to perform it. If death intervenes before that capacity exists, the obligation never becomes binding. Hence, a will for qada or fidyah is not required.
Hanafi principle: obligation (wujub) requires capacity (qudrah).
4
Examine the secondary dalil (Tabi'i athar)
The passage quotes Ata ibn Abi Rabah who said: if a man falls ill in Ramadan and dies without recovering, nothing is due from him. This supports the same conclusion.
This athar is from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, a reliable source.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi and note cross-madhhab difference
The relied-upon position is that no will is obligatory. However, some other schools (e.g., Maliki) may require fidyah if there was any capacity. The Hanafi view is based on the literal deferral and the principle of ease (yusr).
The practical difference: Hanafi heirs have no obligation; other schools might impose compensation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A vow is binding only if its kind is obligatory, it is intended for itself, and it is not already obligatory.

Vows (nadhr) are pledges to perform an act of worship. But not every vow is binding. The Hanafi school lays down three precise conditions that must be met for a vow to be obligatory to fulfill. This matn from Thamarat al-Najah condenses these conditions, and we will examine each one, supported by the Quranic imperative to fulfill vows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions

Memorize these conditions in order.
2
Understand the rationale for each condition
Condition 1 ensures the vow is placed on something whose category is legally recognized; condition 2 distinguishes acts that are ends from means (e.g., wudu is a means); condition 3 avoids vowing what is already required.
These conditions prevent frivolous or redundant vows.
3
Apply to the given examples
Work through the four counterexamples: wudu (condition 2: not maqsud), sajda tilawa (condition 3: already wajib upon recitation), visiting sick (condition 1: no obligatory counterpart), and any already obligatory act (condition 3).
Each example illustrates a distinct condition failure.

The imperative 'وَلْيُوفُوا نُذُورَهُمْ' (Surah al-Hajj 22:29) establishes the obligation of fulfilling vows, but the three conditions define which vows are binding under Hanafi law.
The dalil is general; conditions are derived from other principles.
5
Distinguish from other schools
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) allow vowing any good deed. Hanafi restriction is based on the concept that vows must emulate obligatory acts in kind.
This is a key point of ikhtilaf between madhhabs.

The Absolute Vow (Nadhr Mutlaq)
Ruling on absolute vows and their fulfillment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define nadhr mutlaq, state that it creates an immediate wajib obligation, and identify that it has no specified time for fulfillment.

Core Idea
An absolute vow is immediately binding but not time-bound.

In Hanafi fiqh, a vow (nadhr) is a commitment to perform an act of worship. When made absolutely, without any condition, it becomes obligatory immediately. This lesson examines the ruling and practical implications of the absolute vow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the absolute vow
Recognize that the vow is stated without any condition, e.g., 'I have a two-day fast' or 'I must give ten dirhams in charity'.
2
State the ruling (hukm)
The vow becomes wajib (obligatory) immediately upon utterance.
The obligation is a direct result of the vow itself.
3
Note the time flexibility
No specific time is attached; fulfillment can be at any time.
If time is not specified, the person may choose when to perform it.
4
Confirm conditions of validity
The vowed act must be an act of worship and not already obligatory (e.g., not a daily prayer).
If the act is not worship or is already fard, the vow is invalid.

Disregarding Specifications in Vows (Ilghaa Ta'yin)
Ruling on substituting time, place, coin, or recipient in a vow.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that specifications in a vow are not binding and apply it to substitute time, place, coin, or recipient without invalidating the vow.

Core Idea
Specifications in a vow are disregarded; the act itself fulfills the vow.

This passage from Thamaratun Najah clarifies a common concern: must a vow be fulfilled exactly as stated? The Hanafi school replies that the specific details of time, place, the exact coin, or the intended recipient are not binding. The vow is about the act of worship itself, not its incidental particulars. These examples illustrate that substitution is valid and the vow is discharged.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The passage states that we disregard (ilghaa) the specification of time, place, dirham, and poor person in a vow. This means the vow is fulfilled by performing the essential act even if the specifics differ.
The ruling applies to nafl (supererogatory) vows; obligatory acts are not in view.
2
Work through each example
Examine each example: vowing a fast in Shaban can be fulfilled by fasting in Rajab; vowing two rak'ahs in Mecca can be done in Egypt; specifying a particular dirham can be replaced by any dirham; specifying a recipient (e.g., Zayd) can be changed to another (e.g., Umar).
All examples show that the act itself, not the detail, is what matters.
3
Extract the underlying principle
The principle is that a vow is an undertaking to perform an act of obedience; the incidental details (time, place, etc.) are not part of the essence of the vow. Therefore, they are not binding.
This principle is rooted in the idea that the vow's purpose is the act itself, not the particulars.
4
Apply to new scenarios
Using this principle, you can now assess any similar case: if the act of obedience remains the same, substitution of details is valid. If the act itself is changed (e.g., vowing prayer but fasting instead), that would be invalid.
The illa (effective cause) is that the specification is incidental.

Commentary
This passage presents a mixed ruling on vows (nazar) with proofs. The first part (ruling 1129) states two distinct rulings: (1) If a vow is made conditional upon a future event, fulfilling it before that event occurs is insufficient; it must be fulfilled after the condition. (2) If a vow specifies a particular poor person, it is valid to spend on another poor person instead. The commentary then expands with four examples covering time, place, object, and recipient specification, showing that changing the specified element does not invalidate the vow. The proof for the first part (time/place flexibility) is drawn from a mursal hadith where the Prophet directed a companion who vowed to pray in Jerusalem to pray in Mecca instead, and from a saying of Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab allowing i'tikaf in a different mosque than vowed. This establishes that the specific location or time is not binding. The second part (conditional vows) is proven by analogy with oaths: just as an oath-breaker must first break the oath then expiate, so must the condition occur before the vow becomes binding. The hadith of Abu Huraira is cited, where the Prophet instructs one who swears an oath and finds a better course to take that better course and then expiate. The key legal principle is that the condition (shart) is a prerequisite for obligation; the vow only becomes binding after the condition is fulfilled.

Key Terms
نذر (nazar): A vow (nadhr) is a promise to perform an act of worship or charity, making it obligatory upon oneself.
تعلیق (ta'liq): Suspending a vow upon a condition; the vow becomes binding only when the condition occurs.
جزء (juz'): Sufficiency; an act that fulfills the obligation of the vow.

Invalidation of I'tikaf by Leaving the Mosque Without Necessity
Explaining ruling and proofs for leaving mosque during i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that leaving the mosque without necessity invalidates i'tikaf, distinguish the consequences for obligatory versus supererogatory i'tikaf, and identify the five proofs (qiyas and four naqli proofs) that support this ruling.

Core Idea
Leaving the mosque without necessity invalidates i'tikaf because it contradicts the very definition of i'tikaf as staying in the mosque.

This passage from the chapter of i'tikaf addresses a critical practical question: what happens if a person in i'tikaf leaves the mosque? The Hanafi school rules that any exit without a genuine human need invalidates the i'tikaf, but the consequences differ depending on whether the i'tikaf was obligatory (by vow) or supererogatory (sunnah). The author then presents five proofs to justify this ruling—one based on qiyas (analogy) and four based on transmitted evidence (hadith and athar). Understanding these proofs and their implications is essential for correctly applying the rules of i'tikaf in daily practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act under discussion is leaving the mosque during i'tikaf without a genuine human need. The ruling is that this act invalidates (fasid) the i'tikaf. Note the threshold mentioned: even half a day's exit triggers invalidation.
The passage uses 'half a day' as an example; the key is 'without necessity'.
2
Distinguish Between Wajib and Sunnah I'tikaf
The author clarifies that if the i'tikaf was obligatory (e.g., by a vow), the entire period is invalidated and must be repeated in full. If it was supererogatory (sunnah), only the completed portion counts; the remaining days cannot be appended and are lost.
This distinction is critical for fatwa: the consequence affects whether the mu'takif must make up the entire period or just the missed part.
3
Analyze the Qiyas Proof
The first proof is based on qiyas: i'tikaf linguistically means staying in the mosque. Leaving without necessity contradicts this definition, so analogically the act of leaving invalidates the i'tikaf, just as any action contrary to the essence of an act invalidates it.
This is a straightforward qiyas jali (clear analogy).
4
Examine the Naqli Proofs
Four transmitted proofs are cited: (1) a hadith that the Prophet would not stop to visit the sick while in i'tikaf; (2) a hadith explicitly stating the mu'takif should not go out except for what is unavoidable; (3) a hadith where the Prophet accompanied Safiyyah only to the mosque door; (4) an athar that 'A'ishah passed by sick persons without stopping. Each proof indicates that any unnecessary exit is prohibited and thus invalidates i'tikaf.
The author uses these proofs to establish the ruling, though the hadith have varying levels of authenticity (all ahad, zanni al-thubut).
5
Synthesize the Ruling's Application
Using the ruling, you can now apply it to real scenarios: if a person in i'tikaf leaves to buy food (non-essential if food is available in the mosque), the i'tikaf is invalid. If they leave for a medical emergency (essential), it remains valid.
The definition of 'necessity' (ma la budda minhu) is key and subject to scholarly interpretation.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on i'tikaf presents the ruling that a mu'takif who leaves the mosque without a genuine human need, even for half a day, invalidates his i'tikaf. It distinguishes between obligatory (wajib) i'tikaf (e.g., by vow) and supererogatory (sunnah) i'tikaf: for the obligatory, the entire period is invalidated and must be made up completely; for the sunnah, only the completed portion counts, and the remainder cannot be appended. The author then provides five proofs (wujuh): (1) qiyas: the definition of i'tikaf as staying in the mosque is violated by leaving without necessity. (2) Hadith from Abu Dawud showing the Prophet did not stop for sick visits, used by Imam Abu Hanifah to argue prolonged stops invalidate i'tikaf. (3) Another hadith specifying that a mu'takif should not go out for anything except unavoidable needs. (4) Hadith from Bukhari where the Prophet accompanied Safiyyah only to the mosque door, implying going beyond invalidates. (5) Athar from 'A'ishah that she passed by sick persons without stopping, indicating stopping would invalidate. All proofs support the ruling that unnecessary exit nullifies i'tikaf.

The Life of the Prophet in His Grave: A Hanafi Creedal Affirmation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the Prophet's life in the grave, cite the Quranic proof from Surah Al-Imran, and explain the two special rulings that follow from this belief.

Core Idea
The Prophet is alive in his grave with sustenance and enjoyment, though hidden from our sight.

This passage addresses a central tenet of Hanafi creed: the life of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) in his grave. It is not merely a metaphysical claim but has tangible legal consequences, such as the non-division of his inheritance and the prohibition of marrying his wives after him. The proof is drawn from the Quranic verse about martyrs, extended by a superior analogy—since prophets are higher in rank, their life is even more certain.

Guided Path
1

State the core claim: the Prophet is alive in his grave, receiving sustenance, and experiencing pleasures. Note that this is the view of the verifying scholars (muhaqqiqun).
The claim is about barzakh life, not physical life in this world.
2
Understand the Nature of Barzakh Life
Differentiate between worldly death and barzakh life. Prophets are alive in the barzakh, and their pure bodies are not consumed by the earth.
This is a unique state between this life and the Hereafter.
3
Examine the Two Special Rulings
Identify the two legal implications: (1) their inheritance is not distributed, (2) their wives cannot be remarried. These rulings apply to all prophets.
These rulings are based on hadith and are signs of their barzakh life.
4

Read the verse (Surah Al-Imran 3:169) about martyrs being alive. Then apply qiyas al-awla (superior analogy): since prophets are greater than martyrs, they are certainly alive.
The verse's thubut is qati, but its dalala is zanni, allowing for ijtihad.
5

This belief is part of the Hanafi creed (mufta bihi). It is not a legal ruling but a matter of i'tiqad affirmed by the majority of Ahl al-Sunnah.
Some other schools interpret the Prophet's life differently, but the Hanafis follow the zahir al-riwayah.

The Virtue and Method of Sending Salawat upon the Prophet
Explains virtue of salawat with Quran and Hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Quranic and Hadith proofs for sending salawat upon the Prophet and explain the recommended formula and its reward.

Core Idea
One salawat earns ten from Allah, and the Prophet hears it whether near or far.

This lesson explores the immense virtue of sending salawat (blessings) upon the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). Allah Himself sends salawat upon the Prophet and commands believers to do so. The lesson provides the primary proof from the Qur'an, the specific formula taught by the Prophet, and the remarkable reward: one salawat earns ten from Allah. Understanding these proofs motivates abundant salawat, especially when visiting Madinah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary scriptural proof
Locate the Quranic verse (Surah al-Ahzab 33:56) that commands believers to send salawat upon the Prophet. Note that Allah and His angels also do this.
The verse is the foundational dalil for the command.
2
Understand the nature of the command
In Hanafi usul, the imperative 'صلوا' indicates a command. However, the ruling outside salah is mustahabb (recommended) because the act is not obligatory in daily life, though it is wajib in the last sitting of salah.
The Hanafi school distinguishes between obligation in salah and recommendation outside.
3
Learn the specific formula taught by the Prophet
When the Companions asked how to send salawat, the Prophet taught them to say: 'Allahumma salli 'ala Muhammad wa 'ala ali Muhammad…' (as in the hadith from Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas).
This formula is the sunnah method.
4
Recognize the immense reward
The hadith from Abu Huraira states: 'Whoever sends one salawat upon me, Allah sends ten upon him.' This multiplier encourages frequent recitation.
The reward is 10-fold, showing the generosity of Allah.
5
Apply the knowledge to practice
When visiting the Prophet's grave in Madinah, send abundant salawat knowing he hears it from near and it is conveyed from afar. Also, incorporate the formula into daily supplications.
This is an act of love and respect.

Chapter on visiting the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace. You proceed towards the noble grave and stand at a distance of four cubits from the noble enclosure with utmost respect, facing the grave with your back to the qibla, aligned with the head of the Prophet, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, and his most generous face, observing that his blessed gaze is upon you and he hears your words and returns your greeting and says 'Amin' to your supplication (1477). Then you say: Peace be upon you, O my master, O Messenger of Allah; peace be upon you, O Prophet of Allah; peace be upon you, O Beloved of Allah; peace be upon you, O Prophet of Mercy; peace be upon you, O Intercessor of the Ummah; peace be upon you, O Master of the Messengers; peace be upon you, O Seal of the Prophets; peace be upon you, O Enwrapped One; peace be upon you, O Enshrouded One; peace be upon you and upon your pure ancestors and your purified family, from whom Allah has removed impurity and purified them thoroughly (1478). May Allah reward you on our behalf with the best of what He has rewarded a prophet on behalf of his people and a messenger on behalf of his community. I bear witness that you are the Messenger of Allah: you conveyed the message, fulfilled the trust, advised the community, clarified the proof, struggled in the way of Allah with due striving, and established the religion until certainty came to you (1479). May Allah bless you and grant peace, and also upon the most honored place that was honored by the settlement of your noble body therein, with perpetual blessings and peace from the Lord of the worlds, as numerous as what has been and what will be by the knowledge of Allah, a blessing with no end to its term. The grave said: Peace be upon you, O Messenger of Allah; peace be upon you, O Abu Bakr; peace be upon you, O Umar (Sunan al-Bayhaqi, section on visiting the grave of the Prophet, volume 5, p. 402, no. 11027). This action of the Companion mentions greeting the Prophet and Abu Bakr and Umar. Vocabulary: Muzammil: derived from zammala, to wrap oneself in cloth, the one enveloped in a blanket. In the Quran: 'O you who are wrapped in garments' (Surah al-Muzzammil, verse 1) calling the Prophet. Mudaththir: derived from dathara, to cover with a cloak, the one wrapped in a shawl. In the Quran: 'O you who are covered in a cloak' (Surah al-Muddaththir, verse 1) calling the Prophet. Al-Rijs: sin, impurity. In this verse it is said for the Ahl al-Bayt that Allah Almighty purified the Prophet's family from the filth of shirk: 'Allah only intends to remove impurity from you, O people of the household, and purify you with a thorough purification' (Surah al-Ahzab, verse 33). Explanation: After prayer, proceed to visit the noble grave. Under the dome built over the chamber, the noble grave is located. Therefore stand four cubits away from this dome [nowadays the wall has been extended far, so standing next to the wall is sufficient]. Stand such that your face is toward the grave and your back is toward the qibla, standing with extreme respect. At that time, think that the Prophet's gaze is upon you, and he hears your words, and responds to your greeting, and says 'Amin' to your supplication. So stand with utmost respect. Reason: (1) Because the hadith states that when someone greets from near, I hear him. Therefore stand with the thought that the Prophet hears my greeting. From Abu Hurairah from the Prophet, peace be upon him: 'Whoever sends blessings upon me near my grave, I hear it, and whoever sends blessings upon me from afar, it is conveyed to me' (Bayhaqi in Shu'ab al-Iman, chapter on glorifying the Prophet and venerating him, vol. 2, p. 218, no. 1583). This hadith states that if one sends blessings near my grave, I hear it, and if one sends blessings from afar, it is conveyed to me.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the etiquettes of visiting the Prophet's grave. The author begins by describing the physical posture: one should stand about four cubits from the enclosure, with the back to the qibla and facing the grave, aligned with the Prophet's head. The key spiritual mindset is that the Prophet (peace be upon him) sees the visitor, hears the greeting, returns the salutation, and says Amin to the supplication. This is based on hadith, as cited later. Then the specific wording of the salutation is given. This includes multiple titles of the Prophet, such as 'O Master of the Messengers', 'O Seal of the Prophets', 'O Enwrapped One' (Muzammil), and 'O Enshrouded One' (Mudaththir), which are Qur'anic titles. After the salutation, one acknowledges the Prophet's complete fulfillment of his mission, and prays for blessings. The author provides the legislative basis: the hadith of Abu Hurairah that the Prophet hears the greetings of those near his grave, and the report of Ibn Umar's practice of greeting the Prophet and the two caliphs. The Hanafi position is that this ziyarah (visit) is a recommended act (sunnah), and the prescribed greetings are derived from the practice of the Companions and the teachings of the scholars. The visitor must have firm belief that the Prophet is alive in his grave (hayat al-anbiya) and is aware of those who visit him, without believing that he is a deity. This visit is a means of spiritual connection and seeking intercession.

Key Terms
مزمل (muzammil): Title of the Prophet meaning 'the one wrapped in garments', derived from Qur'an 73:1.
مدثر (mudaththir): Title of the Prophet meaning 'the one covered with a cloak', derived from Qur'an 74:1.
الرجس (al-rijis): Impurity, sin, or spiritual filth; reference to the Ahl al-Bayt being purified from shirk.
زيارة القبر (ziyarat al-qabr): Visiting the grave of the Prophet as an act of devotion, following specific etiquettes.

Visiting the Prophet's Grave: Ruling and Supplication
Supplication for visiting the Prophet's grave with commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on visiting the Prophet's grave and explain the content and purpose of the recommended supplication.

Core Idea
Visiting the Prophet's grave is mustahabb; the ziyarah prayer seeks intercession while affirming tawhid.

This passage provides the standard Hanafi supplication for visiting the Prophet's grave, along with its commentary. The prayer combines greetings to the Prophet using his Quranic titles, acknowledgment of his prophetic duties, and a request for his intercession. The Hanafi school considers visiting the Prophet's grave a recommended act (mustahabb) and this specific du'a part of the established adab (etiquette).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being described and its hukm level in Hanafi fiqh. The act is ziyarah (visiting the Prophet's grave). It is mustahabb (recommended) based on hadith and consensus.
Hanafis classify it as sunnah or mustahabb, not wajib.
2
Outline the structure of the supplication
Break down the du'a into its components: greetings, acknowledgment of the Prophet's mission, request for reward from Allah, and plea for intercession. Note the Quranic references (al-Muzzammil, al-Mudaththir, al-Maqam al-Mahmud).
The prayer is formulaic and can be recited in Arabic with understanding.
3
Recognize the proof for ziyarah
The author cites a hadith from Sunan al-Bayhaqi where a Companion greets the Prophet, Abu Bakr, and Umar at the grave. This serves as a precedent (amal al-sahabi) for visiting and greeting.
The proof is a weak hadith but is supported by the school's reliance on general sunnah.
4
Understand the Hanafi position on tawassul
Notice that the du'a asks Allah through the Prophet's rank (tawassul). Hanafis permit tawassul as long as the ultimate request is directed to Allah alone. This refutes the misconception that such prayers are shirk.
The text clarifies that intercession is sought from Allah using the Prophet's status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct order of supplication when visiting the Prophet's grave and explain the significance of each category.

Core Idea
The visitor prays for self, parents, requesters, then all Muslims.

This brief instruction from a classical Hanafi text teaches the proper sequence of dua after the formal greeting at the Prophet's grave. The order reflects a beautiful expansion of concern: from oneself, to one's parents, to those who specifically asked to be remembered, and finally to the entire Muslim Ummah. Understanding this sequence enriches our practice and deepens the social spirit of ziyarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is supplication (dua) that the visitor makes after the initial greetings (salam) at the grave.
This is a personal dua, not a ritual prayer.

Why this order? It begins with the most immediate (self), then expands to family (parents), then to those with a personal request, finally to the entire community. This mirrors the Hanafi emphasis on starting with obligations nearest to one.
This order is adab (etiquette) and not a condition of validity.

The phrase 'those who asked him to supplicate' highlights that it is commendable to fulfill the request of a fellow Muslim who has asked for dua, especially when visiting a sacred place.
This is a sunnah of responding to a request.
5
Appreciate the communal dimension
Including all Muslims ensures that one's supplication benefits the entire Ummah, living and deceased. This reflects the Hanafi view on the permissibility of sending reward (isal al-thawab) and general supplication for the dead.
The text uses 'jamī' al-muslimīn' meaning all Muslims.

Burial Practices: Placing in Grave and Reciting Quran
Sunnah actions when placing deceased in the grave

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The sunnah of burial includes lahd, specific words, and reciting first and last verses of Baqarah.

This passage from Sunan al-Bayhaqi teaches us the recommended practices when burying a deceased. It includes physical placement, verbal remembrance, and Quran recitation. Understanding these actions helps us follow the Prophet's example and recognize a legitimate scholarly difference regarding recitation at graves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Sunnah Actions
Read the hadith and list the three specific actions mentioned: placing in lahd, saying 'Bismillah wa 'ala sunnati Rasulillah', and reciting the beginning and end of Surah Al-Baqarah at the head.
These are not obligatory but recommended (sunnah muakkadah) in the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the Dalil
The evidence is an ahad hadith from Sunan al-Bayhaqi. The Prophet (peace be upon him) instructs his companions about his own burial.
The chain is not mutawatir, so the ruling is sunnah, not wajib.
3
Analyze the Dalala (Indication)
The imperative verbs (ضعوني, قولوا, اقرأوا) indicate recommendation because they describe a specific practice, not a general obligation. Hanafi usul: al-amr after prohibition or in context of practice indicates nadb (recommendation).
The same verbs could be interpreted as obligation by other schools, but Hanafi tarjih is sunnah.
4
Recognize the Ikhtilaf
Reciting Quran at the grave is a contested issue. Some scholars (e.g., some Malikis) consider it disliked or bid'ah. The Hanafi school accepts this hadith and considers it mustahabb.
The ikhtilaf is based on different evaluations of the hadith's authenticity and interpretation.
