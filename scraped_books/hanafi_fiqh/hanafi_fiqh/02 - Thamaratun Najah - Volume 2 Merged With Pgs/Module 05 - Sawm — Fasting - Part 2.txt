Leniencies in Testimony for Ramadan Crescent
Expands the conditions for Ramadan crescent testimony with six leniencies.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six leniencies that the Hanafi school grants for testifying to the Ramadan crescent and identify which conditions are waived.

Core Idea
For Ramadan crescent, even a single woman, slave, or repentant slanderer can testify—religious matters have wider acceptance.

The Hanafi school recognizes that the Ramadan crescent sighting is a religious duty affecting the entire community. As such, it adopts a more flexible approach to testimony compared to transactions or hudud. This passage details six specific leniencies, including accepting shahada 'ala al-shahada, female and slave witnesses, and those who have repented from qadhf. Formal wording and lawsuit are not required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The passage begins by stating that even if someone testifies based on another's testimony (shahada 'ala al-shahada), it is accepted. This is the first leniency.
This principle is unique to religious matters.
2
List the witness qualifications
The witnesses can be a woman, a slave, or a repentant qadhif (one who received hadd for false accusation of zina). Note that these are not normally accepted in hudud or transactions.
Repentance is key for the qadhif.
3
Note procedural relaxations
Formal wording of 'shahada' is not required; any expression indicating sighting suffices. Also no lawsuit (da'wa) needs to be filed, unlike in transactions.
This reduces barriers for ordinary Muslims.
4
Contrast with other matters
The table in the text shows stricter requirements for zina, murder, transactions, marriage, and Eid crescent. Ramadan requires only one person, male or female, 'adil or mastur.
Eid crescent requires two men or one man + two women.
5
Conclude with Hanafi position
The Hanafi position is that these leniencies are valid and the testimony is accepted (sahih). Other schools may require two witnesses even for Ramadan.
This is mukhtalaf fih between madhahib.

For Eid crescent, two witnesses count; one man and two women also suffice when sky is cloudy.

Eid Crescent Testimony: Obstruction and Clear Sky
Rulings on Eid crescent testimony with and without sky obstruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for accepting crescent testimony for Eid in obstructed and clear skies, and apply the proof from hadith.

Core Idea
With sky obstruction, two witnesses suffice; without it, a large crowd ensures certainty.

When the sky is cloudy or dusty, the crescent might not be widely visible, so Islamic law relaxes the witness requirement to two individuals. But when the sky is clear, the crescent should be easily seen by many; hence the Hanafi school demands a large crowd to confirm the sighting, leaving the exact number to the judge's discretion. This distinction balances practical reality with the need for certainty in determining the start of Eid.

Core Idea
A clear sky demands many witnesses; an obstructed sky needs only two.

When the new moon appears, testimony must meet conditions that vary with atmospheric visibility. 5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The correct opinion (al-ashah) leaves the number to the imam's judgment to achieve certainty. This balances caution and practicality.

If the month ends on one testimony but no moon is seen on a clear night, you must fast a 31st day.

This lesson addresses a delicate scenario: the month of Ramadan was started on the testimony of a single reliable witness, but when the 30th day arrives and the sky is clear, no one sees the crescent of Shawwal. According to a narration from Imam Abu Hanifah (via Hasan ibn Ziyad), it is not permissible to break the fast; one must fast another day and then celebrate Eid. We will examine the proof from hadith and the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
First, note that the month was started based on one witness (shahādat fard). After 30 days, the sky is clear but no moon is seen. The ruling: it is haram to break fast; instead, complete 31 days.
This is from a Nawadir transmission; the Zahir al-Riwayah may differ.
2
Understand the principle: one witness for start but not for end
In Hanafi fiqh, starting Ramadan requires only one just witness (due to ease and to avoid missing a single day). However, ending the month requires stronger evidence: public sighting (ru'yat 'āmmah) on a clear night, or at least two witnesses on a cloudy night.
The asymmetry is based on the hadith 'The fast is the day you all fast'.
3
Analyze the hadith proof and its indication (dalāla)
The hadith 'الصوم يوم تصومون، والفطر يوم تفطرون' implies that the community's practice is authoritative. On a clear night, if the moon were visible, many would see it; the lack of sighting indicates the month is not over.
The hadith is from Tirmidhi and Abu Dawud; its transmission is zanni but its meaning is clear.
4
Note the practical observation about moon visibility
The author adds an experiential argument: the moon is only visible about 18 hours after new moon. If the sky is clear and no one sees it, the crescent has not formed yet; a single claim is likely false.
This reinforces that public sighting is the reliable standard.
5
Recognize the khilāf and the Nawādir nature of the narration
This ruling is transmitted from Imam Abu Hanifah through Hasan ibn Ziyad (Nawādir). Some later Hanafi scholars may have followed a different view. However, the text presents it as the relied-upon position for this specific case.
Other schools (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) generally accept one witness for the end as well, but this passage insists on public sighting.

Commentary
This passage deals with a specific scenario: the month of Ramadan is started based on the testimony of a single person (which is allowed in the Hanafi school for beginning the fast). The month runs its full term (30 days), but on the 30th day, despite a clear sky, no one sees the crescent of Eid. The question is: should the people break the fast and celebrate Eid, or continue fasting? The ruling here, attributed to Imam Abu Hanifah via Hasan ibn Ziyad (a Nawadir transmission), is that it is not permissible to break the fast. Instead, they must complete a 31st day of fasting and then celebrate Eid. The reasoning is that while one witness was sufficient to start the fast (due to the sacredness of Ramadan), the same does not apply to ending it; ending requires stronger evidence (public sighting or two witnesses). The hadith 'The fast is the day you all fast' implies that the community's practice is the guide. The author also includes a practical observation: on a clear night, if the moon is truly there, it is visible to many; if not, it is impossible. Therefore, relying on single testimony on a clear night often leads to false claims. A note criticizes some Arab scholars who rely on one or two witnesses regardless of sky condition, leading to a premature calendar and causing the loss of a day of obligatory fast. The correct Hanafi position is to insist on public sighting (ru'yat 'ammah) when the sky is clear.

Key Terms
رویت عامہ (ru'yat 'āmmah): Public sighting of the moon by a large number of people such that it becomes widely known.
شہادت فرد (shahādat fard): Testimony of a single individual, which is sufficient to start Ramadan but not sufficient to end it according to this narration.

Ruling on One Witness and Clear Sky: When to Fast an Extra Day
Determines when one witness suffices and Eid is not allowed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the sky is clear and the Eid crescent is not sighted after 30 days established by one witness, and explain the proofs including the hadith and the principle of ru'yat 'ammah.

Core Idea
When the sky is clear, general sighting (ru'yat 'ammah) is required; one witness is not enough for Eid.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: Ramadan is confirmed by a single witness, but after 30 fasts, the Eid crescent is not seen despite a clear sky. What should the individual do? The Hanafi school, based on a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad, rules that one must fast an additional day. The passage provides textual proof, empirical reasoning, and warns against relying on individual testimony when general sighting is expected.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the core ruling
Identify the situation: one witness for Ramadan, 30 days passed, sky clear, no crescent. The ruling from the narration is: do not break fast, fast an additional (31st) day.
2
Identify the primary proof (hadith)
The hadith 'الصوم يوم تصومون، والفطر يوم تفطرون' is cited. Analyze its indication (wajh al-dalalah): the community's consensus defines the day, not an individual. Thus if the community does not see the moon, it is not Eid.
3
Understand the principle of ru'yat 'ammah
The passage argues that when the sky is clear, many people should see the moon. If only one or two claim sighting, it is likely false or mistaken. This is supported by astronomical experience (moon visibility 18 hours after new moon).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi and cross-madhhab difference
This ruling comes from the Nawadir (narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad), not the default Zahir al-Riwayah. Some scholars (e.g., in Arabia) accept one or two testimonies even when the sky is clear, but the author criticizes this practice as leading to loss of obligatory fasts.
5
Apply the ruling to practice
The mufta bihi is to fast 31 days and then celebrate Eid. The passage warns against following lunar calendars that accept early testimonies, as it may result in missing an obligatory fast.

Daytime Crescent Sighting Has No Effect for the Past Night
Ruling on crescent sighted during daytime

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a crescent sighting during the day and apply it to determine the start of the new month.

Core Idea
A daytime sighting, before or after noon, belongs to the coming night, not the past one.

When someone claims to have seen the crescent moon during daylight hours, the first instinct might be to check which lunar day it belongs to. This lesson clarifies that such a sighting is always assigned to the upcoming night, regardless of the time of day. Understanding this rule is crucial for correctly determining the start of Ramadan, Eid, and other lunar month observances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act in question is the sighting of the crescent moon during the daytime, whether before or after zawal (solar noon). Determine which night the sighting relates to.
The lunar day begins at sunset, so a daytime sighting occurs after the night has already ended.
2
State the ruling
The Hanafi ruling is that such a sighting is not considered for the night that just passed; rather, it is assigned to the coming night. This is the mukhtar (preferred) position.
3
Understand the rationale
The new moon becomes visible shortly after sunset. A daytime sighting indicates the moon was present at the previous sunset but was not seen. Hence, it logically belongs to the following night.
This is based on the principle that the lunar day starts at sunset.
4
Apply the ruling
If someone sights the crescent on the 30th day of Ramadan at 10 AM, it does not make that day the 1st of Shawwal. Instead, Eid begins after the coming night's sunset.

Intention alone, involuntary smoke, and external application of kohl do not break the fast as they lack consumption.

The Hanafi school rules that none of these nullify the fast, based on the principle that only deliberate ingestion breaks it. Medical and fiqh reasoning support these rulings.

In Hanafi fiqh, anything that reaches the stomach naturally, even if not typically food or medicine, can break the fast if it provides nourishment.
3

The fast breaks when the substance enters the stomach (via mouth or nose reaching the throat) or the brain (via ears, eyes, nose if a passage exists). Understand that entry into these cavities is the criterion.
The brain is mentioned because some medicines or substances can reach the brain via the nose or ear if they penetrate through fissures.
4

Apply the negative implication: substances that do not enter the stomach or brain do not break the fast. For example, tasting food without swallowing, applying oil on skin, or using a toothstick (miswak).
There are further conditions, such as knowledge and intent, but this principle focuses on the physical event.
5

Practice applying the principle to real situations: e.g., swallowing saliva does not break fast (it is not food/medicine entering externally), but swallowing a pill breaks the fast.
In doubt, the default is that the fast remains valid unless entry of a substance is certain.

Core Idea
Kaffarah requires six conditions — it is not automatic for every broken fast.

This passage lists the conditions that must be met for both qada and kaffarah to be obligatory when a fast is broken. The author first gives the general ruling for 22 actions (to be detailed later) and then elaborates six specific conditions that limit the application of kaffarah. Understanding these conditions is crucial because they reflect the Hanafi precision in determining when the expiation is due, based on the hadith of the man who had intercourse in Ramadan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
The author states that doing any of 22 actions voluntarily, intentionally, and without coercion entails both qada and kaffarah. This sets the default obligation.
2
List the six conditions for kaffarah
From the explanation extract each condition: (1) willingly, (2) knowingly, (3) it is a Ramadan fast, (4) broken in Ramadan, (5) the fast was intended from the previous night, (6) no later excuse (menstruation, travel) occurs before sunset.

3

Acts done under coercion or forgetfulness do not trigger kaffarah – only qada (if the fast is invalidated). This excludes genuine mistakes and duress.
Forgetfulness: the fast does not break at all, so no qada or kaffarah.
4
Analyze the timing conditions
Kaffarah only applies to a valid Ramadan fast that is intentionally broken during Ramadan daylight hours. If it is a qada fast or a nafl fast, only qada is due. Also, the fast must have been intended from the night before (otherwise the fast was invalid from the start).
Condition 5 (intention from night) distinguishes a valid fast from an invalid one.
5

If after breaking the fast an event occurs that would have excused the fasting (e.g., menstruation, travel before sunset), the obligation of kaffarah drops, though qada remains.
This shows that kaffarah is conditional on the entire day being subject to the fast.
6

The hadith of the man who had intercourse provides the primary evidence. The Prophet prescribed freeing a slave, fasting two months, or feeding sixty poor people. From this, the Hanafis derive the six conditions by noting that the man’s act was voluntary, intentional, in Ramadan, etc. The imperative indicates obligation (wujub).

Intentional action breaks the fast; involuntary does not — the key is deliberation.

Rainwater entering the mouth while fasting is a common scenario. Does it break the fast? The answer depends on whether the action was deliberate. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi ruling based on the critical distinction between active swallowing and passive entry.

Guided Path

The act is swallowing rainwater that has entered the mouth. The key condition is whether the swallowing was intentional or involuntary.

If the water goes down the throat without any deliberate action (e.g., it drips by itself), the fast is not broken.

The underlying principle: any deliberate intake of an external substance through the mouth invalidates the fast. Involuntary intake does not.
Applies to other liquids too.

Exception from Kaffarah: Ignorance Due to Fatwa or Hadith
Exception to kaffarah based on fatwa or hadith ignorance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two exceptions that exempt a fasting person from kaffarah and explain the role of ta'weel (Hanafi interpretation) in determining whether ignorance is excused.

Core Idea
A fasting person is excused from kaffarah if he relied on a jurist's fatwa or was unaware of the Hanafi interpretation of a hadith.

The general rule is that deliberately breaking one's fast without valid excuse requires kaffarah (expiation). However, the Hanafi school recognizes two exceptional situations where the person's ignorance of the correct ruling excuses them from this heavy penalty. This lesson examines those exceptions and the crucial role of ta'weel — the Hanafi interpretation — in determining whether the person is genuinely excused or liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two exceptions
The text gives two scenarios: (1) the person acted on a fatwa from a qualified faqih that the act breaks the fast, and (2) the person heard a hadith about the act but did not know its Hanafi interpretation (ta'weel). In both cases, no kaffarah is due.
The fatwa must be from a qualified jurist; the hadith must be from an authentic source.
2
Understand the role of ta'weel
The Hanafi school interprets the hadith about cupping ('The cupper and the cupped break their fast') as meaning 'close to breaking' due to weakness, not actual nullification. This ta'weel is based on Hanafi usul and other evidences.
Ignorance of this interpretation excuses the person from kaffarah because they cannot be expected to know the school's technical interpretation.
3
Determine the ruling when the person knows the ta'weel
If the person knows the correct Hanafi interpretation (i.e., that the act does not break the fast), then performing the act deliberately and breaking the fast will require kaffarah. Knowledge removes the excuse.
The final part of the text states: 'but if he knew its interpretation, kaffarah becomes obligatory upon him.'

Commentary
This passage is an exception to the previous ruling. It states that if a fasting person performs an action (like cupping) based on a fatwa from a faqih (jurist) that it breaks the fast, and then eats, no kaffarah is due. Similarly, if he hears a hadith (like 'the cupper and the cupped break their fast') without knowing its proper interpretation according to the Hanafi school (i.e., that it means 'close to breaking' due to weakness, not actual invalidation), and then eats, no kaffarah is due. However, if he was aware of the correct interpretation of the hadith, then kaffarah is obligatory because he is not excused. The Urdu commentary elaborates that the relevant hadith is from Usamah ibn Zayd: 'The cupper and the cupped have broken their fast' (Sunan al-Bayhaqi). The Hanafi school interprets this to mean 'nearly broke' due to weakness, so the fast remains valid. Thus, ignorance of this interpretation excuses from kaffarah, but knowledge does not.
