Learning Objective

Core Idea
Consent to intercourse during fast with a coerced partner: the willing party owes kaffarah; the coerced does not.

Islamic law distinguishes between voluntary and coerced acts, especially in matters of expiation (kaffarah). This lesson tackles a specific scenario: a man is forced to break his fast by having intercourse, but the woman is willing. Who bears the burden of kaffarah? The Hanafi ruling is clear, based on the principle that coercion removes culpability for the coerced while the consenting party remains fully liable.

Guided Path
1

Note that the man is coerced (mukrah) and the woman willingly consents (tawa'at). Distinguish each one's legal status.
Coercion (ikrah) is a legally recognized excuse that can mitigate liability.
2
Determine the ruling for the consenting woman
The text states: 'And kaffarah is obligatory upon the one (female) who obeyed the coerced one.' Deduce that she must both make up the fast (qada) and pay kaffarah.
3
Determine the ruling for the coerced man
By implication (since not mentioned in the text), the coerced man does not owe kaffarah. However, his fast is still invalidated, so he owes only qada.
The ruling is based on the principle that coercion removes the obligation of kaffarah.
4
Apply the principle to a similar scenario
Consider if the genders were reversed: a coerced woman and a willing man. The same principle applies: the willing party (the man) owes kaffarah; the coerced woman owes only qada.
This extension confirms that the key factor is willingness, not gender.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions that nullify an already-obligated kaffarah and explain the Hanafi Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Kaffarah drops when a legitimate excuse occurs on the same day.

Kaffarah (expiation) is normally incurred when a fasting person deliberately breaks the fast. However, the Hanafi school teaches that if a legitimate excuse arises before sunset on that same day, the obligation is lifted. This lesson examines three specific excuses and their Quranic basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act? Deliberately breaking the fast incurs kaffarah. The ruling here is that kaffarah is nullified under certain conditions.
2
List the nullifying conditions
The text mentions three: menstruation (hayd), postpartum bleeding (nifas), and a sickness that permits breaking the fast.
All must occur on the same day before sunset.
3
Locate the dalil
The Hanafi school uses Quran 2:185 as proof, which allows the sick to break the fast and make up later.
The verse indicates that the day is effectively a non-fasting day.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The occurrence of such excuses makes the day like a non-fasting day, so the earlier violation is overridden.
The verse's concession retroactively applies.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Some schools hold that kaffarah remains because the violation already occurred. The Hanafi school relies on a broader interpretation of the concession.
This is a legitimate scholarly difference.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for breaking Ramadan: free a slave first; if unable, fast two consecutive months excluding Eid and Tashreeq.

This lesson covers the expiation (kaffarah) required when a person deliberately invalidates a Ramadan fast by eating, drinking, or intercourse. The Hanafi school prescribes a progressive penalty: first freeing a slave, and if that is impossible, fasting two consecutive months. The passage also clarifies that the slave need not be Muslim, and specific days are excluded from the fast.

Recognize that the passage addresses the kaffarah for deliberate fast-breaking. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) as a penalty.
The kaffarah is not required for accidental breaking.

The primary form is freeing a slave (tahrir raqabah). The secondary form is fasting two consecutive months, only if freeing a slave is impossible.
This is a progressive obligation: one must attempt the first before moving to the second.

The text explicitly allows freeing a non-Muslim slave (ولو كانت غير مؤمنة). This is a distinctive Hanafi ruling.

The fast must be two consecutive lunar months (شهرين متتابعين), and it must exclude the days of Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the days of Tashreeq (11th-13th of Dhul-Hijjah). These days are inherently prohibited for fasting.
If any of these days occur during the two months, they are simply omitted; the consecutiveness is not considered broken.

Core Idea
The kaffarah for breaking a Ramadan fast is sequential: free a slave, or fast two months, or feed sixty poor — in strict order of ability.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on the kaffarah (expiation) required when a person deliberately breaks a Ramadan fast. The ruling is based on a sahih hadith and supported by analogy to the verse on zihar. The school insists on a strict sequence: one must first free a slave; if unable, then fast two consecutive months; if unable, then feed sixty poor persons. Understanding this sequence is crucial for correctly fulfilling the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger for kaffarah
Determine which act necessitates kaffarah: deliberate invalidation of a Ramadan fast, e.g., by intercourse, eating, or drinking, without a valid excuse.
This ruling does not apply to accidental breaking or breaking due to forgetfulness.
2
State the first option and its condition
The primary option is freeing a slave. The Hanafi school allows freeing a non-Muslim slave; it fulfills the requirement.
If one cannot find or afford a slave, proceed to the next option.
3
State the second option and its conditions
If unable to free a slave, one must fast two consecutive lunar months. The fasts must be consecutive, without interruption, except for days when fasting is prohibited (Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the days of al-Tashriq). If such a day occurs, the sequence does not break; instead, one skips that day and continues. If the consecutiveness is broken due to a genuine excuse, one must restart the two months.
The two months are lunar; if the invalidating act occurs mid-month, one counts from that day.
4
State the third option and its method
If one is genuinely unable to fast (e.g., due to chronic illness or old age), then feed sixty poor persons. Each poor person receives half a sa' (approximately 1.5 kg) of staple food, or two complete meals that fill them. The food can be given raw or cooked; giving dates suffices.
One may also give the equivalent value as charity, but the preferred method is the food itself.
5
Confirm the sequential order
The options must be taken in order: slave first, then fasting, then feeding. One cannot skip to feeding unless unable to free a slave and unable to fast. This order is wajib (obligatory).
The Shafi'i school similarly holds this order, while the Maliki school in some cases prioritizes fasting. The Hanafi mufta bihi is the sequential order.

Commentary
This passage details the three-tiered expiation for deliberately breaking a Ramadan fast. The first and most preferred option is freeing a slave (even a non-Muslim slave). If one cannot afford or find a slave, the second option is fasting two consecutive lunar months, with the stipulation that no days of Eid (al-Fitr, al-Adha) or days of al-Tashriq (11-13 Dhul-Hijjah) occur within those two months, as those days have a prohibition on fasting. If one is unable to fast due to a valid excuse (e.g., old age, chronic illness), then the third option is feeding sixty poor persons, giving each person half a sa' (approximately 1.5 kg) of staple food, or providing them with two complete meals (breakfast and dinner) that fill them. The ruling is supported by the famous hadith of the man who had intercourse with his wife during a Ramadan fast and the Prophet (peace be upon him) prescribed these three options sequentially. Additionally, the verse on zihar (Surah al-Mujadilah 58:4) is cited, and the Hanafi school uses qiyas (analogy) to apply the same three-tiered expiation to breaking the fast, since both involve invalidating a fast or oath with a need for expiation.

How to Feed the Poor for Kaffarah: Two Meals or Equivalent
Ruling on how to feed the sixty poor in kaffarah: either two full meals or equivalent amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the valid methods of feeding the poor for kaffarah and explain the Hanafi position on the required amount.

Core Idea
Feeding the poor for kaffarah is fulfilled by providing two filling meals per person or giving half a sa' of staple food.

When a person cannot free a slave or fast two consecutive months for kaffarah, feeding sixty poor individuals becomes obligatory. The Hanafi school provides flexible methods to fulfill this, ensuring the poor are properly nourished. This lesson clarifies the various valid ways to feed each poor person.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
The primary method is to feed each poor person two filling meals: one daytime meal (ghadā') and one evening meal (ʿashā').
The meals must be substantial enough to satisfy hunger.
2
Explore alternative timings
You may also feed each person two night meals, or two daytime meals, or one dinner and one pre-dawn meal (suhur).
The key is that the person receives two meals, regardless of the specific day/night distribution.
3
Understand the principle of equivalence
The goal is to provide nourishment equal to two meals. This can also be fulfilled by giving each poor person half a sa' (approx. 1.5 kg) of staple food (e.g., wheat, dates).
Half a sa' is the standard measure in Zahir al-Riwayah for kaffarah feeding.
4
Note the flexibility
The school allows various methods as long as the poor are adequately fed. This flexibility is based on the hadith where the Prophet (ﷺ) gave a basket of dates to a man for his family.

One Kaffarah for Multiple Violations: The Principle of Tadakhul
Ruling on tadakhul of kaffarah with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of tadakhul (intermixing) to determine when multiple violations of the fast require a single kaffarah and when they require separate kaffarahs, distinguishing between the sahih view and the zahir al-riwayah position.

Core Idea
Multiple fast violations merge into one kaffarah if no expiation intervenes, even across two Ramadans.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: a person nullifies the fast repeatedly over several days. Did they incur multiple kaffarahs or just one? The answer depends on whether any expiation was performed in between the violations. The Hanafi school has a nuanced ruling with an internal dispute. This lesson will walk through the conditions for tadakhul and the two positions within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the obligation
The acts are multiple violations of the fast (intercourse or eating) on different days. Each violation incurs a wajib kaffarah.
2
Determine if tadakhul applies
Check whether any expiation (kaffarah) was performed between the violations. If no expiation intervened, tadakhul merges all obligations into one. If expiation intervened, each subsequent violation requires its own kaffarah.
The condition for tadakhul is the absence of intervening expiation.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
The zahir al-riwayah position does not allow tadakhul across two Ramadans if expiation intervened. The sahih view allows tadakhul even across two Ramadans, as long as no expiation intervened.
The text prefers the sahih view (على الصحيح).
4
Apply to a concrete example
Example from the text: four days of intercourse without intervening expiation → one kaffarah suffices. If expiation had been done after the second day, then two kaffarahs would be required.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether multiple acts of nullifying the fast require multiple kaffarahs. The matn states the ruling: one kaffarah suffices for multiple acts (intercourse or eating) occurring on different days, as long as no expiation has been performed in between, even if the acts are from two different Ramadans (according to the correct view). Explains tadaakhul of kaffarah for multiple violations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on tadaakhul of kaffarah and apply it to scenarios with and without intervening expiation.

Core Idea
Multiple violations merge into one expiation if no expiation has been performed in between; otherwise each requires separate expiation.

When a person commits multiple violations (like intercourse or eating) on different days during Ramadan, how many expiations are due? The answer depends on whether any expiation has been performed between those days. This lesson explains the Hanafi principle of tadaakhul (overlap) and the intra-madhhab difference between the correct view and the apparent narration.

Identify the act and its ruling category

Understand the condition for overlap
The key condition is that no expiation has been performed between any two violations. If expiation is performed, overlap is prevented and each subsequent violation requires its own expiation.

5
Identify the mufta bihi
The text indicates the correct view (one expiation suffices if no intervening expiation) is the relied-upon position, even though the apparent narration is also known.

Commentary
This passage states a Hanafi ruling on the principle of tadaakhul (overlap) in expiations for violations occurring in separate days. The ruling is that if a person commits multiple violations (like intercourse or eating during Ramadan) on different days without having performed any expiation in between, then all these violations are covered by a single expiation. This applies even if the violations span two different months of Ramadan. The author distinguishes between the 'correct view' (al-sahih) and the 'apparent narration' (zahir al-riwaya). Commentary: He did not intend to fast at night or during the day, and he sat without eating, so qada is necessary, not kaffarah, because he did not break the fast. (1081) Or he ate suhur or had intercourse in a state of doubt about dawn, or he broke fast assuming sunset while the day remained. Commentary: He doubted whether true dawn had occurred, and in that state he ate or had intercourse, and later found that true dawn had occurred, so kaffarah is not necessary because he did not break the fast knowingly. The author, following the Hanafi school, enumerates several scenarios and provides evidence. The passage begins by stating the topic: 'What invalidates fasting without kaffarah.' The author explains the underlying principle: kaffarah is required only if breaking the fast was not permissible from the start due to illness, travel, menstruation, etc. This principle is derived from Quran 2:185. For the case of doubt (1081), the author cites a hadith from Asma bint Abi Bakr and athar from Umar ibn al-Khattab to show that qada is sufficient, not kaffarah. For seminal emission (1082), the author uses a hadith about bestiality and sayings of tabi’in to argue that since there is no complete intercourse (i.e., no full penetration), kaffarah is not required. The commentary follows the author's order: presentation of the ruling, explanatory note (tashreeh), and evidence (wajh). All examples and reasoning are firmly within the Hanafi tradition.

Key Terms
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation required for intentionally invalidating a Ramadan fast through intercourse or eating/drinking without valid excuse; not required in the cases listed.

This passage addresses a scenario where a person who did not intend the fast from the night wishes to intend during the day. Ruling with proof on qada for insane days.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qada for days of insanity during Ramadan and apply the distinction between continuous and partial insanity.

Core Idea
Continuous insanity exempts from fasting and qada; partial insanity requires qada for all missed days.

This passage clarifies the critical difference between insanity (junūn), which removes legal responsibility, and unconsciousness (ighmā'), which does not. It then presents two scenarios: full-month insanity (no qada) and partial insanity (qada for both sane and insane days), along with a subtle sub-case where recovery occurs after the time for intention has passed, exempting the entire month. Understanding this ruling prevents errors in determining when qada is obligatory.

Distinguish unconsciousness from insanity
Identify the key difference: unconsciousness preserves intellect, so obligations continue; insanity destroys intellect, lifting all obligations.

If a person is insane for the entire month of Ramadan, no qada is required because no day of the month had the obligation of fasting apply.

If a person has any period of sanity during Ramadan, the obligation of fasting applies for the entire month; they must fast the sane days and make up the insane days later.

If a person is insane before Ramadan and recovers after the time for intention (e.g., after Dhuhr) on the first day, the entire month's fast is not required because there was no valid opportunity to intend the fast.
This is a lenient view, considered mufta bihi.

Unconsciousness and Insanity During Fasting: Hanafi Rulings
Ruling with proof on ighma and junun during fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on unconsciousness and insanity during fasting, distinguish between their legal effects, and apply the relevant proofs (dalil).

Core Idea
Unconsciousness does not break the fast; insanity lasting the whole month removes obligation entirely.

This passage addresses a common concern: what happens to one's fast if they become unconscious or insane during Ramadan? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced answer based on the nature of each condition. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rules of qada and kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between ighma (unconsciousness) and junun (insanity)
In unconsciousness, reason remains; in insanity, reason is lost. This difference determines the legal status.
Unconsciousness is like sleep; both preserve reason.
2
State the ruling for unconsciousness
If a person becomes unconscious after making intention at night, that day's fast is valid. However, subsequent days without intention require qada (no kaffarah).
Qada is wajib, not the fast itself.
3

If insanity covers the entire month, no obligation exists. If there are periods of sanity, qada is required for the sane days.
The hadith 'رفع القلم عن ثلاث' removes obligation from the insane.

Examine the proofs
For unconsciousness: the athar of Ibn Umar (he would fast voluntarily, become unconscious, and not break). For insanity: the hadith of lifting the pen from the insane.

Core Idea
Individuals who become eligible for fasting during the day must abstain from food and drink out of respect for Ramadan.

In this lesson, we examine a specific Hanafi ruling concerning those whose fast becomes void or who become eligible to fast during the day. The school holds that they must refrain from eating and drinking for the remainder of the day, not as a full fast but as an obligatory act of respect. We will explore the eight categories and the proof from the hadith of Ashura, which is analogically extended to Ramadan.

Obligation to Refrain from Eating for the Rest of the Day (Imsak)
States the ruling and evidence for abstaining mid-day.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the eight categories required to observe imsak, explain the evidence from the hadith of Ashura, and distinguish imsak from a full fast.

Core Idea
Those who become eligible mid-day must abstain out of respect, even without a full intention.

In this passage, the Hanafi school lays out a unique ruling: eight types of individuals who only become capable of fasting after dawn must still refrain from eating and drinking for the rest of the day. This obligation, called imsak, is not a full fast but a duty to honor Ramadan. The ruling is derived from the Prophet's command on Ashura and analogized to similar situations. Let's explore the details.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Scope
The passage begins by stating an obligation (wujub) to refrain from the rest of the day for a list of people whose fasts became invalid or who newly became eligible. Note that this is not a full fast but an act of respect.
The ruling is wajib, not fard, due to the evidence being ahad.
2
List the Eight Categories
Read through the eight categories enumerated in the text: traveler returning, menstruating woman purified, woman in postnatal bleeding purified, new Muslim, child reaching puberty, sick person recovered, one who ate intentionally, and one who ate forgetfully. Understand why each is included.
The last two are fasting persons whose fast became invalid mid-day.
3
Analyze the Primary Evidence
Examine the hadith of Ashura where the Prophet commanded those who had eaten to complete the rest of the day fasting. Identify the key command and how it implies an obligation of abstention.
The hadith is ahad, giving zanni thubut, hence wajib level.

Understand the Qiyas (Analogy)
The Hanafi method extends the Ashura ruling to other categories through qiyas. The common factor is becoming eligible to fast mid-day. Reflect on how the illa (effective cause) is the eligibility to fast out of respect for Ramadan.
The athar from Ibrahim supports the same rule for traveler and menstruating woman.

Distinguish Imsak from a Full Fast
Note that imsak only requires refraining from mufattirat; it does not require intention (niyyah) and the day must still be made up later (except child and new Muslim). This distinction is a key feature of the Hanafi position.

According to this passage, which of the following is NOT required to make up (qada) the fast for the day on which they became eligible?
A) A traveler who returned home at midday
B) A child who reached puberty at midday
C) A menstruating woman who became pure at midday
D) A fasting person who forgot and ate at midday

Key Takeaway
A new Muslim and a child reaching puberty do not owe qada for past fasts; others must make up missed days.
