Vow to Perform Hajj Walking: Ruling and Proofs
Ruling on a vow to walk for Hajj with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a vow to walk for Hajj, identify the obligation to walk until Tawaf al-Ziyarah, and apply the rule that riding before that requires a dam sacrifice.

Core Idea
A vow to walk for Hajj is binding; riding before Tawaf al-Ziyarah breaks the condition, requiring a dam sacrifice.

This lesson addresses a specific issue in the law of vows (nadhr) related to Hajj. When someone vows to perform Hajj on foot, that condition becomes wajib. The matn gives the rule, but the hadith proofs provide nuance for when walking is impossible. We will unpack both the basic ruling and its evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is vowing to perform Hajj walking. The ruling is that it becomes wajib (obligatory) to fulfil the vow exactly as made.
The matn says 'لزمه' (it is incumbent upon him).
2
Determine the condition of the vow
The condition of the vow is walking until Tawaf al-Rukn (Tawaf al-Ziyarah). The vow is not fulfilled by walking the entire journey; it is fulfilled by walking before that critical tawaf.
'ولا يركب حتى يطوف للركن' – he may not ride until he performs the Tawaf of the pillar.
3
Identify the penalty for violation
If he rides before that tawaf, he must offer a dam (a sacrificial animal). This is a compensation for breaking the condition.
'فان ركب اراق دما' – if he rides, he pours blood.
4
Examine the hadith evidence
Two hadith from Abu Dawud: one where the Prophet allowed a woman unable to walk to ride and offer a badanah; another where he allowed a woman who vowed hardship to ride and fast three days instead.
These show that if walking is impossible, riding is permitted with expiation.
5
Apply the exception
If one is genuinely unable to walk (illness, exhaustion), the ruling softens: he may ride and offer a dam, or break the vow with kaffarah yamin (feeding ten poor or fasting three days).
This is from the commentary and the second hadith.

Commentary
This is a ruling on a vow to perform Hajj by foot. The matn states that if one makes such a vow, it becomes binding. He must not ride until he completes the Tawaf al-Rukn (the obligatory Tawaf al-Ziyarah). If he rides before that, he must offer a dam (sacrifice). The commentary clarifies: if one is unable due to illness or exhaustion, he may ride and give a dam, or break the vow by kaffarah yamin (feeding ten poor or fasting three days). The proofs come from hadith where the Prophet (PBUH) instructed those who vowed hardship to ride and offer a hady, or to make expiation.

Key Terms
نذر (nadhr): A vow to perform an act of worship if a condition is met or as an unconditional promise.
دم (dam): A sacrificial animal (usually a sheep) offered as expiation for violating a condition of Ihram or a vow.
طواف الركن (tawaf al-rukn): The obligatory Tawaf al-Ziyarah (Ifadah), a pillar of Hajj without which Hajj is invalid.
بدنة (badanah): A large sacrificial animal, typically a camel or cow, offered as a hady (sacrificial offering).

Supplication Upon Seeing the Walls of Madinah
Ruling on supplication upon first sight of Madinah's walls

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah du'a upon first sight of Madinah's walls and identify the condition that triggers it.

Core Idea
The sunnah du'a upon first sight of Madinah's walls seeks the Prophet's intercession and divine protection.

When a traveler first sees the walls of Madinah, a special sunnah is recommended. This lesson presents the prescribed du'a and explains when and why it is recited. Understanding this practice enriches one's spiritual journey and aligns with Hanafi etiquette of visiting the Prophet's city.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The act is reciting a specific du'a and sending blessings upon the Prophet upon first sight of Madinah's walls. This falls under adab (etiquette) of visiting Madinah.
It is not a pillar of Hajj or Umrah but a recommended sunnah.
2
Determine the hukm level
Hanafi scholars consider this act sunnah (mustahabb). It is not wajib or fard.
The evidence is from practice of the Salaf and the sanctity of Madinah.
3
Identify the trigger condition
The condition is the first visual sight of Madinah's walls. The text says: 'When he sees the walls of Madinah al-Munawwarah.'
The sunnah begins upon sight, not upon entering the city.
4
Analyze the du'a content
The du'a acknowledges Madinah as the Prophet's sanctuary and place of revelation, and asks for entry, protection from Hell, and the Prophet's intercession.
It reflects the high spiritual status of Madinah.
5
Connect to the benefit
The benefit is seeking Allah's favor and the Prophet's shafa'ah through this prescribed supplication.

Ghusl Before Entering Madinah: A Recommended Veneration
Ruling on performing ghusl before entering Madinah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on ghusl before entering Madinah and identify the qiyas basis from entering Makkah.

Core Idea
Ghusl before entering Madinah is sunnah, established by qiyas on the sunnah of ghusl before entering Makkah.

This passage presents the ruling on personal preparation before visiting the Prophet's mosque in Madinah. While the act of ghusl is not obligatory, it is a highly recommended sunnah that reflects reverence for the Prophet ﷺ. The Hanafi school derives this ruling through analogy (qiyas) with the sunnah of ghusl before entering Makkah for pilgrimage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage commands ghusl, applying perfume, and wearing best clothes before entering Madinah or before visiting the Prophet's grave. Determine the legal status (hukm) of these acts.
The text uses imperative forms, but the context and later explanation clarify it is sunnah, not wajib.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari and Sunan al-Tirmidhi that reports Ibn Umar and the Prophet performing ghusl before entering Makkah. This hadith is sahih but its application to Madinah is by analogy.
The hadith is cited in the commentary as the basis for qiyas.
3
Trace the qiyas reasoning
Understand the qiyas panel: the asl is entering Makkah for pilgrimage, where ghusl is sunnah; the illa (effective cause) is that both Makkah and Madinah are sacred places (haram) and entering for worship requires veneration (ta'dhim); the far' is entering Madinah for visitation; thus the ruling (hukm) of sunnah applies.
The extraction method is tahrir al-manat (identification of the illa).
4
Note the hukm level and thamarah
The ruling is sunnah (recommended), not wajib. The practical consequence (thamarah) is that performing ghusl brings reward, while omitting it does not incur sin but misses an opportunity for greater respect.
The common misunderstanding is that it is wajib; the passage corrects this.

Entering Madinah and the Prophet's Mosque
Rulings on entering Madinah, reciting du'a, and praying tahiyyat al-masjid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended actions when entering Madinah and the Prophet's mosque, including the du'a and the ruling on tahiyyat al-masjid near the minbar.

Core Idea
Enter Madinah with humility, recite the prescribed du'a, and pray tahiyyat al-masjid near the minbar.

This passage from Thamarat al-Najah guides the pilgrim upon arrival in Madinah. It covers three sequential acts: entering the city with proper demeanor, reciting a specific du'a, and greeting the Prophet's mosque with prayer. Understanding these actions helps the pilgrim perform the visit with reverence and follow the sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sequential actions
The passage prescribes actions: entering Madinah, reciting du'a, and praying tahiyyat al-masjid. Recognize each as a distinct recommended act.
These are consecutive steps upon arrival.
2
Analyze the conditions for entering Madinah
Walking is preferred if possible, after securing luggage, with humility (sakinah) and dignity (waqar). Consider the sacredness of the place.
Walking is mustahabb (recommended), not obligatory.
3
Examine the du'a components
The du'a includes basmalah, affirmation of the Prophet's religion, Quranic verse 17:80, salawat, and supplication for forgiveness and mercy.
The passage allows additional supplications.
4
Understand the ruling on tahiyyat al-masjid
Upon entering the Prophet's mosque, pray two rak'ahs near the minbar. This is sunnah mu'akkadah. The hadith about the minbar area being a garden of Paradise provides the virtue.
The hadith is from Sahih Muslim.

Etiquette of Approaching the Prophet's Grave
Ruling on standing position and distance from the grave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended posture and distance when standing before the Prophet's grave and explain the Hanafi proof from the hadith.

Core Idea
Stand four cubits away with your back to the qibla, facing the grave — the Prophet hears your salaam.

After completing prayers at the minbar, the visitor turns to the Prophet's grave. The Hanafi school specifies a precise posture: four cubits from the enclosure, back to the qibla, opposite the head. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its scriptural basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prescribed posture
State the recommended distance (four cubits), orientation (back to qibla), and alignment (opposite the Prophet's head).
This is the outer form of adab.
2
Understand the basis in practice
The Hanafi position is sunnah muakkadah, derived from the practice of the companions, especially Ibn Umar.
Not directly from the Qur'an or mutawatir hadith.
3
Examine the dalil
The hadith 'Whoever sends salaam upon me at my grave, I hear him' (Bayhaqi) establishes the Prophet's awareness of the visitor.
This is an ahad hadith with zanni thubut.
4
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'I hear him' (سمعته) indicates direct hearing, justifying the belief that the Prophet sees and hears the supplicant.
Thus the visitor must hold this belief to fulfill proper adab.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Hanafis face the grave with back to qibla; some other schools permit facing the qibla. The Hanafi view is based on companion practice.
The thamarah al-khilaf is the physical orientation at the grave.

The Complete Greeting at the Prophet's Grave
Text of the complete salutation (salam) for the Prophet's grave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recite the full Hanafi greeting at the Prophet's grave and explain its status as sunnah muakkadah.

Core Idea
Greet the Prophet with his noble titles and his purified family—this is the complete sunnah.

When visiting the Prophet's grave in Madinah, we are not just visitors but guests seeking intercession. The Hanafi school has preserved a comprehensive salutation transmitted from the Companions. This lesson presents the exact wording you should recite with presence of heart.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the Sequence
Learn the series of greetings by heart: begin with 'al-salamu 'alayka' followed by each title. The order is fixed.
Start with the most general titles, then move to specific Quranic epithets (Muzzammil, Muddaththir).
2
Understand the Titles
Each title has a meaning: 'Rasul Allah' (Messenger), 'Nabi Allah' (Prophet), 'Habib Allah' (Beloved), 'Nabi al-Rahma' (Prophet of Mercy), 'Shafi' al-Umma' (Intercessor), 'Sayyid al-Mursalin' (Master of Messengers), 'Khatam al-Nabiyyin' (Seal of Prophets), 'Muzzammil' (Enwrapped), 'Muddaththir' (Covered).
These reflect the Prophet's status and attributes.
3
Include the Family
After greeting the Prophet, send salutation upon his pure family (Ahl al-Bayt), referencing the purification verse (33:33).
The Hanafi school includes the wives in Ahl al-Bayt.
4
Recite with Presence
Recite with full awareness that the Prophet hears your greeting and responds. This is sunnah muakkadah.
The ruling is sunnah muakkadah—not wajib, but highly recommended.
