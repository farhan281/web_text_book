The Qawmah: Fard or Wajib? An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on whether the standing after bowing (qawmah) is fard in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the qawmah, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifah and Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other, and apply the evidence from Quran and qiyas to determine the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The qawmah is not fard according to the Hanafi majority; omission requires sajdah al-sahw, not repetition.

This lesson examines a classical dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the status of the qawmah (the standing position after bowing) in prayer. While Abu Yusuf considered it fard, the majority position (Abu Hanifah and Muhammad) holds that it is not fard but wajib, and its omission does not invalidate the prayer. We will explore the evidence from the Quran, sunnah, and qiyas, and understand the practical implications of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi majority position
State the view of Abu Hanifah and Muhammad: the qawmah is not fard but wajib.
The passage explicitly says 'ليست بفرض في ظاهر الرواية وهو قولهما'.
2
Identify the opposing view and its evidence
Note that Abu Yusuf (and al-Shafi'i) consider it fard, using qiyas on the sitting between prostrations and hadith commands.
The qiyas argument: just as jalsah is fard according to Abu Yusuf, so is qawmah.
3
Analyze the Hanafi majority's proof
Examine the Quranic evidence (22:77) which mentions only bow and prostrate, and interpret the hadith as indicating perfection, not obligation.
The verse ’ar-ka‘ū wa-s-judū is taken as delimiting the obligations.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position and thamarah al-khilaf
The relied-upon view is the majority view; the practical difference: omission requires sajdah al-sahw, not invalidity.
The passage ends with 'في ظاهر الرواية' indicating authoritative transmission.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on the qawmah (the standing position after rising from bowing) in prayer. According to al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah and al-Siraj al-Wahhaj, the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah) is that this standing is not fard (obligatory) but rather wajib or sunnah. This is the view of the majority of the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifah and Muhammad). Abu Yusuf, however, considered it fard, and Imam al-Shafi'i agreed with him. Abu Yusuf's evidence is analogical: just as the sitting between the two prostrations (jalsah) is fard according to him, so too the standing after bowing. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifah and Muhammad) relies on the Quranic verse that only mentions bowing (ruku') and prostration (sujud) as obligations, and the standing between them is not explicitly commanded. The hadith of the Bedouin and the command to stand upright and straighten the spine are interpreted as indicating something less than fard (i.e., wajib or mustahabb). The passage ends with the Quranic evidence, indicating that the apparent meaning of 'bow and prostrate' suffices for obligation, and the qawmah is not included.

The Qawma: Is It an Obligatory Pillar or a Required Act?
Dispute on whether qawma is fard, with proofs and responses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the qawma, identify the evidence for each school, and explain the practical consequence of the dispute.

Core Idea
The qawma is not fard but wajib—omitting it does not invalidate prayer but is severely disliked.

The qawma—the brief standing after bowing—is a point of contention between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. While both agree it is part of the Prophet's prayer, they differ on its legal weight. This lesson unpacks the proofs and reasoning that lead the Hanafi school to classify it as wajib rather than fard.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested act
State clearly what the qawma is and what ruling is disputed: is it fard (obligatory, void if omitted) or not?
The act is the standing up after bowing, before prostration.
2
State the Hanafi position and its Quranic evidence
The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) says it is not fard, relying on the verse 'Bow and prostrate' which implies permissibility without an intervening stand. Also note the principle that a pillar is not repeated in one rak'ah.
The Quranic evidence is considered qat'i in transmission but zanni in meaning.
3
State the opposing position and its evidence
Abu Yusuf (intra-Hanafi) and the Shafi'i school hold it is fard. Their evidence includes the hadith of the Bedouin and the analogy to the sitting between prostrations.
The hadith 'The prayer of one who does not straighten his back is not accepted' is their key textual proof.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response interprets the hadith as referring to perfection, not validity, and argues that ahad hadith cannot add to the Quran. They also distinguish the qawma from the jalsa (sitting) because the latter is necessary for transition, while from bowing one can go directly to sujud.
The principle that khabar al-ahad cannot add to the Quran is central to Hanafi usul.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical difference: if omitted intentionally, Hanafi holds prayer valid but severely disliked (makruh tahriman); Shafi'i says prayer invalid. The relied-upon Hanafi position is that it is not fard but wajib.
The mufta bihi is based on zahir al-riwayah, the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Standing After Ruku: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Fard or Wajib
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qawma's ruling with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers the qawma wajib not fard, and identify the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
The standing after bowing is wajib, not fard; omission does not invalidate prayer but is severely disliked.

After bowing, the worshipper stands briefly before prostrating. Is this standing a mandatory pillar (fard) or a lesser obligation (wajib)? The Hanafi school holds it as wajib, but this is contested even within the school itself. In this passage, the commentator defends the majority Hanafi view and explains why the evidence does not elevate qawma to fard status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the core issue
Name the act under discussion (qawma: standing after bowing) and state the central question: is it fard or not?
2
State the Zahir al-Riwayah position and the intra-Hanafi variant
According to al-Khujandi, the relied-upon (zahir al-riwayah) view is that qawma is not fard. Note that Abu Yusuf held it is fard.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Follow the Hanafi dalil
Examine the Hanafi proof: the Qur'an only commands bowing and prostrating, and a solitary hadith cannot add a new fard. The hadith cited by opponents is interpreted as nafy al-fadila (negation of excellence).
Key usul principle: khabar wahid cannot abrogate or add to the Qur'an.
4
Analyze the distinction between qawma and jalsah (sitting between prostrations)
Opponents analogize qawma to the sitting between prostrations, which is considered necessary. The Hanafi response: the sitting is necessary because transition to the next prostration is impossible without it, whereas after bowing one can go directly to prostration without standing.
This refutes the analogy.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and the thamarah al-khilaf
The school adopts Abu Hanifa's view: qawma is not fard. The practical consequence: if omitted, the prayer is valid but severely disliked (makruh tahrimi). Forgetful omission requires sajdah al-sahw; intentional omission is sinful.
In contrast, Abu Yusuf and the Shafi'i school would require repetition of the prayer.
