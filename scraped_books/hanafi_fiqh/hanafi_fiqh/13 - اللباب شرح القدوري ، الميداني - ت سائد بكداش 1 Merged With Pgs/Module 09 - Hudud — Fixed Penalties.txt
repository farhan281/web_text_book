The Ruling on Asking About Time in Confession of Zinā
Hanafi ruling on time question in iqrār of zinā

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on asking about time in iqrār of zinā and explain the distinction from shahāda based on taqādam al-‘ahd.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, time does not invalidate a confession of zinā, but it does invalidate testimony.

When a person confesses to zinā, the judge must ask specific details to confirm the confession. However, unlike testimony where the passage of time bars acceptance, confession remains valid regardless of when the act occurred. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling and its basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the confession (iqrār) of zinā. The ruling is that the judge must ask about the act, how, where, and with whom, but not about the time.
The ruling is derived from al-Hidayah.
2
Understand the reason
The omission of time is because taqādam al-‘ahd (lapse of time) prevents testimony but does not prevent confession. Confession is a self-implicating admission that remains valid even after a long period.
Testimony relies on memory, which fades; confession is an act of self-disclosure.
3
Recognize the alternative opinion
There is a view (qīla) that it is permissible to ask about time, as the confessor might have been a minor at the time, which would exempt from hadd.
This view is not the relied-upon position.
4
Apply the distinction
Distinguish between iqrār and shahāda: in shahāda, witnesses must be asked about time to ensure memory reliability; in iqrār, the confessor's own admission is sufficient proof.

This passage is a textual critical remark from a modern study of Mukhtasar al-Quduri. The Question of Time in Zina Confession
Textual variant and ruling analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a judge must ask about the time of zina during confession, and distinguish between confession and testimony regarding the effect of time lapse.

Core Idea
Time lapse prevents testimony but not confession — asking about time is optional, not obligatory.

This lesson examines a textual variant in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar concerning the judge's questions during a confession of zina. Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah noted that al-Quduri omitted the question about time, but later manuscript findings include it. We will explore the substantive ruling: why asking about time is not required in confession, and the practical difference it makes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling being discussed
The passage discusses whether the judge must ask the confessing adulterer about the time (zaman) of the act. The ruling is that it is not obligatory to ask, but permissible.
The textual variant does not change the ruling itself; it only affects the wording of the matn.
2
Trace the dalil for the ruling
The proof is that the passage of time (taqadum al-'ahd) prevents acceptance of testimony but does not invalidate confession. This is stated explicitly in the text.
Dalil: 'because the passage of time prevents testimony but not confession'.
3
Understand the textual variant
Al-Marghinani claimed that al-Quduri did not mention the question about time, but the author of this passage found old manuscripts that include 'Mata zana?'. The variant does not affect the ruling.
Manuscripts: 611 AH, 649 AH, etc.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi difference
There is an opinion (qil) that asking is permissible to check if the act occurred in childhood, but the standard view is that it is not required. The thamarah al-khilaf is that if asked and the confessor says he was a minor, the hadd is dropped.
The Hanafi school is united that the question is not obligatory; the difference is only about permissibility.
5
Apply the ruling in practice
The judge may choose to ask about time as a precaution, but if he does not, the hadd is applied even for acts long past.
This parallels the duty of the judge to verify legal competence.

Commentary
This passage is a textual-critical discussion within a larger study of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The author presents an example of a textual variant concerning the confession of zina (adultery) in the Book of Hudud. According to al-Marghinani's al-Hidayah, when the confessor confesses four times, the judge asks him specific questions about the act (what, how, where, with whom) but does not ask about the time (zaman). The reasoning given is that the passage of time (taqadum al-'ahd) prevents the acceptance of testimony but does not prevent the validity of a confession; thus, asking about time is unnecessary in confession but required in testimony. However, another opinion (qil) states that if the judge does ask, it is permissible, because the confessed act might have occurred during childhood. Al-'Ayni in al-Binayah confirms that al-Quduri did not mention the question about time. The author then reveals that he possesses old manuscripts of the Mukhtasar that do include the question 'Mata zana?' (When did he commit zina?). Consequently, the author argues that al-Marghinani's statement was based on limited access to manuscripts, and the correct text of al-Quduri includes this question. This does not change the ruling that the question is not obligatory, but the textual variant informs the student of the importance of manuscript verification in Hanafi fiqh studies.

Condition of Minted Dirhams for Theft Amputation
Textual dispute over condition of minted dirhams for theft amputation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition that stolen dirhams must be minted for the hadd of amputation and distinguish between the zahir al-riwaya position and the variant transmitted by some later scholars.

Core Idea
For the hand to be cut, the stolen dirhams must be minted — this is the most authoritative Hanafi position.

This passage explores a subtle textual dispute within the Hanafi school concerning a condition for the hadd of theft: whether the stolen dirhams must be minted (مضروبة) or whether unminted coins also suffice. The source contrasts the ruling in al-Hidayah with variant readings of al-Quduri's matn, illustrating how later scholars resolve apparent contradictions in the transmitted texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed condition
Recognize that the passage focuses on the requirement for stolen dirhams to be minted (مضروبة) as a condition for amputation. Note that this is a shart (condition) for the hadd, not the hadd itself.
2
Locate the two conflicting transmissions
Al-Hidayah attributes to al-Quduri the statement that the dirhams must be minted. However, al-Allama Qasim notes that in some manuscripts of al-Quduri, the text reads 'minted or unminted' (مضروبة أو غير مضروبة). This creates an intra-madhhab conflict.
3
Determine the authoritative position
Al-Hidayah declares the minted condition to be 'zahir al-riwaya' (the apparent narration from the six core books) and 'al-ashah' (the most correct). This is the relied-upon view.
Zahir al-riwaya carries greater authority than single narrations or later commentaries.
4
Understand the tarjih mechanism
The tarjih (preference) is based on the weight of transmission. Al-Hidayah's author, al-Marghinani, prefers the version that restricts the condition to minted coins, considering the variant as a textual corruption or a minority view.
The principle followed: when two transmissions conflict, the one consistent with the majority of the school's usul is preferred.
5
Note the practical consequence
Under the mufta bihi (operative position), if a thief steals unminted silver or gold (ingots, bars, or raw metal), the hand is not cut. Only minted coins (or equivalent stamped currency) satisfy the condition.
This highlights the Hanafi insistence on property being of a type subject to riba (gold/silver) and also minted to complete the analogy (qiyas).

Manuscript Variation on Condition of Minting in Theft
Manuscript variation analysis on theft condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct Hanafi position on the condition of minting in theft and explain the manuscript evidence that resolves the apparent contradiction within the school.

Core Idea
Manuscript variation does not change the ruling: theft of non-minted valuables triggers amputation.

This lesson examines a textual-critical problem within the Hanafi school: does the stolen item have to be minted coins for the hadd penalty to apply? The Mukhtasar of Al-Quduri has two different manuscript readings, leading to an apparent contradiction. By tracing the transmission through Al-Quduri's student, we find the correct position of Abu Hanifah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
State the apparent disagreement: some Hanafi texts (Hidayah, Fath al-Qadir) imply that stolen dirhams must be minted (مضروبة), while others (authentic copies of Al-Quduri) say they may be minted or non-minted.
The issue is a condition (shart) for the hadd of theft.
2
Locate the conflicting manuscript readings
Find the source of the conflict: the text of Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar exists in two versions—one reading 'minted' alone, another reading 'minted or not minted.' The author of Al-Hidayah relied on the first version; Allamah Qasim in Tashih al-Quduri points out the second, more accurate version.
The version with 'minted or not minted' is transmitted by Abu Nasr al-Aqta', a direct student of Al-Quduri.
3
Trace the authentic transmission
Establish the reliable riwayah: Al-Aqta' commentary explicitly confirms the phrasing 'minted or not minted' as the saying of Abu Hanifah. This is the authoritative transmission because it comes from a student of Al-Quduri who had direct access to the master's text.
The weak version likely arises from a scribal error or confusion with another book (Al-Asl by Muhammad al-Shaybani).
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that minting is not a condition. This is supported by the majority of manuscripts and the ruling of Abu Hanifah himself, as preserved by his students.
The Hidayah's reading is considered a mistake or a reference to a different source.
5
State the practical consequence
If minting were required, theft of gold/silver in raw form (bullion, lumps, broken jewelry) would not trigger amputation. Since it is not required, the hadd applies whenever the value reaches the nisab, regardless of form.
This has significant practical implications for theft cases involving non‑coined precious metals.

Commentary
This passage is a textual-critical discussion from the commentary Al-Lubab on Al-Quduri. It examines a dispute arising from variant manuscript readings of Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar regarding the condition for amputating a thief's hand. The Hidayah states that the stolen dirhams must be minted (struck as coins) for hadd to apply, and attributes this view to Al-Quduri's text. However, Allamah Qasim notes that actual copies of Al-Quduri read 'minted or not minted'. The phrase 'minted or not minted' is the authentic transmitted wording from Al-Quduri, as confirmed by Abu Nasr al-Aqta', a direct student of Al-Quduri, and this is the position of Abu Hanifah. The author of Al-Lubab highlights that this textual difference has a significant effect on the ruling: if the condition is minted coins, then theft of non-minted items like gold bullion may not trigger amputation; if the condition is not required, then amputation applies regardless. The correct position in the school is the one transmitted by al-Aqta'—i.e., no condition of minting—which is the ruling of Abu Hanifah.

Key Terms
مضروبة (madruba): Minted coins; coins that have been struck as official currency.
غير مضروبة (ghayr madruba): Non-minted; uncoined gold or silver, e.g., bullion or lumps.
