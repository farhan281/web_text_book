Faraid Calculation Using the Habba Unit
Explains faraid calculation using the habba unit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the habba unit for an inheritance case and convert fractional shares into whole habbas using the correct custom-based denominator.

Core Idea
The habba unit eliminates fractions in inheritance distribution by using a custom-defined denominator.

In Hanafi faraid, distributing shares often involves fractions. To make distribution practical, scholars developed the habba (grain) as a common unit. This passage from al-Lubab introduces three customary definitions of the habba and then demonstrates how to convert the shares of heirs into habbas. The core legal operation is dividing each heir's share (from the tashih-corrected base) by the extracted habba denominator. This lesson will guide you through that method, which is essential for precise estate division in contexts where fractional currency units are not available.

The habba is a unit of weight. The passage lists three customs: 1) 72 habbas per unit (common in Iraq), 2) 60 habbas per unit, 3) 48 habbas per unit (the author's choice). The denominator of the habba is the number of habbas per unit. Here, 48 is used, so one unit = 48 habbas.

Explains conversion of shares to grain units.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi method of converting inheritance shares into grain units using a chosen base denominator, and apply it to a given set of heirs.

Core Idea
Inheritance shares are converted to grains by dividing the adjusted base by the grain unit's denominator.

In Hanafi inheritance law, after determining each heir's fractional share, the estate may be distributed in physical units like grains. The definition of a 'grain' varies by custom; the author follows the Iraqi custom of 48 grains per unit. This lesson walks through the conversion method using a sample case, emphasizing the process over specific numbers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Heirs and Their Quranic Shares
List all heirs and their prescribed fractions from the Quran. For example, husband gets 1/4 if there is a child, mother 1/6, father 1/6, son gets the residue as asabah.
2
Adjust the Base for Fractional Shares (Tasheeh)
If the denominators do not produce whole numbers for each heir, adjust the base (asl al-masalah) to the smallest number that gives whole shares. The author uses a base of 48 for his example.
The numbers in the source may be corrupt; focus on the method.
3
Determine the Grain Unit Denominator
Choose the custom of the region. The author uses the custom where one unit equals 48 grains. This is the 'makhraj al-habbah'.
Other customs use 60 or 72 grains.
4
Divide the Base by the Grain Denominator
Divide the adjusted base (48) by the grain denominator (48) to get the quotient, which represents one grain. In this custom, the quotient is 1.
5
Convert Each Heir's Share to Grains
For each heir, take their share in the adjusted base and divide by the quotient to get their grain amount. For example, if husband has 23 shares, dividing by 1 gives 23 grains. (The source's fractions are due to corruption.)

Explaining the method for munasakhah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method for munasakhah by determining whether shares are compatible (muwafaqah) and performing the appropriate multiplication to merge two inheritance cases.

Core Idea
In munasakhah, merge the two cases: multiply directly if shares are incompatible, or use the common factor if compatible.

This passage from al-Lubab explains the Hanafi method for handling multiple deaths before distribution. When an heir dies before the first estate is divided, the shares must be recalculated by merging the two inheritance cases into one. The key distinction is whether the shares of the second deceased from the first case have a common factor (muwafaqah) with the first case's base number.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two inheritance cases
Determine the base number (asl al-mas'alah) for the first deceased and the shares each heir receives. Then identify the second deceased (an heir from the first case) and compute the base number for their own estate.
Each case is solved independently first, as in standard faraid.
2
Check compatibility of shares
Compare the share of the second deceased (from the first case) with the base number of the second case. If the two numbers are coprime (no common factor), there is no muwafaqah. If they share a common factor, muwafaqah exists.
Compatibility includes direct divisibility (tadakhul) as a special case.

Apply the merging method
If no muwafaqah: multiply the two base numbers directly to get the combined base. If muwafaqah: multiply the base of the second case by the common factor (wafq) of the second deceased's share and that base.
The final base is the new asl for the whole estate.
4
Recalculate each heir's share
Each heir from the first case gets their original share multiplied by the factor used in step 3. Heirs of the second deceased get their shares from the second case multiplied by the same factor.
The total of all shares must equal the combined base.

Method of Munasakha (Recalculation of Inheritance)
Explains the procedure for munasakha recalculation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of munasakha to recalculate inheritance shares when an heir dies before distribution, using tawafuq to find a common base number.

Core Idea
Munasakha recalculates shares using tawafuq to align multiple deaths.

When an heir dies before the estate is distributed, the shares must be recalculated to account for both deaths. This process, called munasakha, ensures each heir receives their combined entitlement from both estates. The Hanafi method uses concordance (tawafuq) to find a new base number that integrates the shares of the first and second deceased.

Guided Path
1
Identify heirs of the first deceased
List all surviving heirs at the time of the first death. In the passage, the heirs are women and two brothers.
2
Determine the base number for the first estate
Calculate the asl al-mas'alah (least common denominator) based on the Quranic shares of the first deceased's heirs.
For example, if a wife and two brothers, base = 4.
3
Determine shares from the first estate
Assign each heir their fractional share from the first estate using the base number.
4
Handle the second death
When an heir dies before distribution, identify the second deceased's heirs and calculate their base number. Find tawafuq (common factor) between the share of the deceased heir from the first estate and the base of the second estate.
If no common factor (tabayun), multiply the two bases directly.
5
Compute the new base and final shares
Multiply the first base number by the second base number (or by the appropriate factor from tawafuq) to get the new base. Then scale all shares proportionally and distribute the first estate using the new base.
The passage gives an example where the new base is 48.

Munasakhat: Combining Inheritance Shares When Heirs Die Before Distribution
Explains method for distributing shares when two deceased have mutual heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of munasakhat to calculate final shares when two deceased persons have mutual heirs.

Core Idea
When an heir dies before distribution, estate shares are recalculated by multiplying each heir's share by the base of the other estate.

This lesson explains a key faraid technique for a common scenario: a person dies, and before the estate is distributed, one or more of the original heirs also die. The shares must be recalculated to account for the new heirs from the second deceased. The Hanafi method uses multiplication of bases to combine the two estates into a single common denominator. The source passage from Al-Lubab illustrates this with a practical example.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the munasakhat scenario
Identify that two or more deaths occurred before distribution of the first estate. The heirs of the first deceased may include some who later die, leaving their own heirs.
This triggers the need for combined recalculation.
2
Determine the base (asl) of each estate independently
For each deceased, list their heirs and their Quranic shares, then find the smallest common denominator (base) that allows integer shares. This base is the LCM of share denominators.
If tashih (correction) is needed, apply it to get the base.
3
Multiply shares by the other estate's base
For an heir appearing in the first estate only, multiply their share in the first estate (as an integer from the base) by the base of the second estate. For an heir appearing in the second estate only, multiply their share in the second estate by the base of the first estate.
This step converts each heir's share into the combined base (base1 × base2).
4
Handle heirs appearing in both estates
For an heir who appears in both estates (e.g., the first deceased's heir who also inherits from the second), first calculate their share from the first estate as above, then separately calculate their share from the second estate as an heir of the second, and add the two products.
This heir receives a portion from each estate.
5
Express all shares in a common denominator
The new base is base1 × base2. Sum all the calculated shares; the total should equal the new base. Express each heir's share as a fraction of this new base.
The example in the passage uses a final division by 8, indicating a simple scenario with small bases.

Inheritance of Parents and Mawla

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when parents and a mawla are present, the mother is preferred over the father for the surplus after the mawla's share.

Core Idea
Mother takes surplus after mawla; father excluded when mawla inherits as asabah.

This lesson deals with a specific scenario in Hanafi faraid where the deceased leaves behind both parents and a mawla (a freed slave or master who inherits as asabah). The source passage, though corrupted, indicates that the mawla receives his fixed share first, and the mother is entitled to the remaining surplus, excluding the father. This ruling reflects the Hanafi principle that the mother is closer in relation than the father when a mawla is present.

Allocate the surplus
After the mawla's share, the mother is entitled to the surplus. The father does not inherit in this scenario because the mother is preferred over him for the residue.

Apply the ruling
If the mawla's share leaves a surplus, it goes to the mother, not the father. This is a specific rule when parents and a mawla coexist.
This ruling is agreed upon by Abu Hanifa and Malik.
