Giving Zakat to One's Spouse: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat to spouse with proof analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on giving obligatory zakat to a spouse, identify the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Zakat to one's spouse is prohibited because spouse-sharing prevents true ownership transfer.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether obligatory zakat may be given to one’s spouse. The matn states a clear prohibition, but Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allowed a wife to give to her husband. We will trace the proofs and understand why the school follows Abu Hanifa’s position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue and the Matn Ruling
State the act in question (giving obligatory zakat to one's spouse) and the ruling in the matn: it is prohibited for both husband and wife according to Abu Hanifa.
The matn is the base text being commented on.
2
Understand the Sahibayn's View and Their Proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say a woman may give her zakat to her husband. Their proof is the hadith of Zainab bint Mas'ud, where the Prophet said she gets two rewards.
They take the hadith as applying to obligatory zakat.
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's Response and Basis
Abu Hanifa argues that the hadith refers to voluntary charity, not obligatory zakat. His basis is the Quranic verse (93:8) indicating that spouses share benefits and wealth, preventing true ownership transfer (tamlik).
The concept is ishtirak fi al-manafi' (sharing in benefits).
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi and Practical Consequence
The relied-upon position is the prohibition (Abu Hanifa's view). Therefore, a person cannot fulfill zakat by giving to their spouse. The practical difference is that in Shafi'i fiqh, it is valid.
The tarjih favors caution and the principle of complete tamlik.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of giving obligatory zakat to one's spouse. The matn (base text) states that a zakah-payer should not give zakat to his wife, nor should a woman give to her husband according to Abu Hanifa. The reasoning is that there is a sharing of benefits and intermingling of wealth between spouses, making it akin to giving to oneself. As evidence, the verse 'He found you poor and made you rich' is cited, interpreted as enrichment through Khadija's wealth, implying that spousal support is already mutual. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that a woman may give her zakat to her husband, relying on the hadith of Zainab bint Mas'ud, who asked the Prophet about giving charity to her husband and was told she would receive two rewards: one for charity and one for maintaining family ties. Abu Hanifa reconciles this by interpreting the hadith as referring to voluntary charity (sadaqat al-tatawwu'), not obligatory zakat, because Zainab was a skilled artisan and wealthy, so her giving was beyond what was obligatory. This distinction is crucial: for obligatory zakat, the ruling follows Abu Hanifa's view (as stated in the matn), while for voluntary charity, the Sahibayn's permissive view is accepted. The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that zakat cannot be paid to one's spouse, as it does not constitute true ownership transfer (tamlik) due to the mutual benefit.

Key Terms
اشتراك في المنافع (ishtirak fi al-manafi'): Sharing in benefits – the mutual enjoyment of wealth and support between spouses that prevents true ownership transfer.
صدقة التطوع (sadaqat al-tatawwu'): Voluntary charity, as opposed to obligatory zakat.
أجران (ajran): Two rewards – one for charity and one for maintaining family ties (silat al-rahim).

Giving Zakat to One's Spouse: Intra-Hanafi and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Comparing two Hanafi commentaries on the ruling of zakat to spouse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on giving zakat to one's spouse and evaluate the differing proofs from the commentaries.

Core Idea
Spouses cannot give zakat to each other due to shared benefits, but the wife may give according to the Sahibayn.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi dispute: Can a husband give his obligatory zakat to his wife? Can a wife give hers to her husband? Two commentaries on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar present the reasoning, revealing how textual interpretation and rational arguments lead to different conclusions within the school. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace how a hadith can be understood differently based on context and legal maxims.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling from Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding each spouse giving zakat to the other. Note that the husband is prohibited from giving to his wife according to all Hanafis.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn differ from Abu Hanifa on the wife giving to her husband. Imam al-Shafi'i also agrees with the Sahibayn. State the opposing position clearly.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Sahibayn and Shafi'i rely on the hadith of Zaynab, where the Prophet said she receives two rewards for giving charity to her husband. Understand the literal implication.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa interprets the hadith as referring to voluntary charity (sadaqah tatawwu') because the woman in the report was wealthy (mūsirah) and not obligated to pay zakat. Explain why this interpretation is key.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: If a wife gives zakat to her husband, according to Abu Hanifa the zakat is not discharged (batil); according to the Sahibayn it is valid. Identify which view is often adopted in later fatwa practice.

Commentary
This passage is a comparative discussion from two Hanafi commentaries on the ruling of giving zakat to one's spouse. It presents the issue: may a husband give his obligatory zakat to his wife, and may a wife give her zakat to her husband? The first commentary, al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, states the general rule that a zakat payer does not give his zakat to his wife, based on two reasons: shared benefits and intermingling of wealth. It cites the Quranic verse about Allah enriching the Prophet through Khadija's wealth as an indication of spousal partnership. Then it addresses the wife giving to her husband: according to Abu Hanifa, she may not give, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) allow it, citing the hadith of Zaynab bint Jahsh (or Zaynab the wife of Ibn Mas'ud) where the Prophet said she would get two rewards for giving charity to her husband. Abu Hanifa interprets this hadith as referring to voluntary charity (sadaqah tatawwu') rather than obligatory zakat, because the woman in the hadith was wealthy enough to be a craftswoman and thus zakat was not obligatory on her. The second commentary, al-Siraj al-Wahhaj, elaborates further. It adds another reason for the husband not giving to his wife: the existence of a cause of inheritance (like wala') that makes them akin to each other. It also brings the explanation from al-Idah, which distinguishes two 'illahs (reasons): first, that the wife's maintenance is on the husband, making her like a dependent child; second, that their wealth is intermixed. According to the second 'illah, the wife is also prevented from giving to her husband (Abu Hanifa's view), but according to the first 'illah, she may give (the Sahibayn's view). The passage then repeats the hadith and notes that Imam al-Shafi'i also allows the wife to give to her husband. Abu Hanifa's ta'wil (interpretation) that the hadith applies to voluntary charity is reinforced by the report that the woman was a craftswoman earning her own income, so she was not poor and zakat was not obligatory on her. This lesson illustrates how Hanafi jurists use both textual and rational arguments to derive rulings, and how differences in understanding a hadith lead to different positions within the school.

صدقة تطوع (ṣadaqat tatawwu'): Voluntary charity, not obligatory but recommended.
موسرة (mūsirah): A woman who is wealthy, possessing the nisab (minimum threshold) making zakat obligatory on her.

Giving Zakah to One's Spouse: Rulings and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Rulings on zakah to spouse with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on giving zakah to one's spouse and explain the basis of the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a wife giving zakah to her husband.

Core Idea
A man cannot give zakah to his wife; a wife giving to her husband is disputed due to competing legal causes.

This passage from al-Lubab presents two rulings on giving zakah to a spouse. The first is agreed: a husband cannot give his obligatory zakah to his wife. The second is disputed within the Hanafi school: can a wife give her zakah to her husband? The disagreement stems from two different legal causes (illat) identified in the text: maintenance obligation and wealth intermixing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage states two rulings: (1) a husband cannot give zakah to his wife (agreed), and (2) a wife giving to her husband is disputed between Abu Hanifah and the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
2
Understand the reasons for the first ruling
The text gives two reasons: mutual inheritance without obstruction (like wala') and sharing of benefits. These establish a strong connection that prevents zakah transfer.
These reasons are cited from al-Nihayah.
3
Recognize the two 'illat in al-Idah
Al-Idah provides two legal causes for the prohibition: (a) maintenance obligation (al-muwasah) similar to a child, and (b) intermixing of wealth (tamazuj) because spouses customarily do not withhold from each other.
4
See how each 'illa leads to a different outcome for the wife-to-husband case
If the cause is maintenance (first 'illa), then the wife is already supporting the husband (since he is not obliged to support her), so she can give zakah. If the cause is intermixing (second 'illa), then the transfer is blocked. Abu Hanifah applies the second 'illa; the disciples apply the first.
This is the core of the dispute.
5
Examine the hadith of Zaynab
The disciples and al-Shafi'i cite the hadith where the Prophet told Zaynab bint Mas'ud that she gets double reward for giving charity to her husband. Abu Hanifah interprets this as voluntary charity because Zaynab was not wealthy enough to owe zakah.
This shows how text is interpreted differently based on context.

Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on ushr/kharaj classification of revived dead land based on water source

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad regarding the tax (ushr/kharaj) of revived dead land based on the irrigation water source, and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.

Core Idea
The tax on revived dead land depends on the water source: natural rivers make it ushr; man-made kharaj rivers keep it kharaj.

Reviving dead land is permissible and grants ownership, but the tax classification is not automatic. This passage from al-Lubab presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: does the water source determine whether the land pays ushr (10% of produce) or kharaj (fixed land tax)? Abu Hanifa looks to the surrounding land, while Imam Muhammad distinguishes between natural and man-made rivers. The relied-upon position follows Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling on reviving dead land
The passage states that reviving dead land makes it the property of the reviver, and the Imam should leave it to him. If the reviver acted negligently, the Imam may reclaim it. This sets the context for the tax discussion.
2
Identify the point of dispute
The dispute is about the tax (ushr or kharaj) imposed on revived dead land when the Imam leaves it to the reviver. The key factor is the water source used for irrigation.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that the land's tax follows the ruling of the surrounding land where the river is located. If the river runs through kharaj land, the revived land is kharaj; if through ushr land, it is ushr. His basis is that initiating kharaj on a Muslim is impermissible, but if the water itself is from kharaj land, it may be a continuation.
This is a qiyas-based reasoning linking the new land to the existing land's status.
4
State Imam Muhammad's position and its basis
Muhammad distinguishes between natural major rivers (like the Nile) and artificial rivers dug by the Imam from kharaj funds. Land irrigated by natural rivers is ushr, while land irrigated by man-made kharaj-funded rivers is kharaj. His basis: natural rivers are not 'created' by the state, so they do not transmit kharaj status.
Muhammad's view incorporates an istihsan (juristic preference) considering the origin of the water.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and the reason for tarjih
Al-Tahawi adopted Muhammad's view, and it is the relied-upon position. The reason is that Muhammad's distinction is more specific and addresses the practical difference between natural and artificially created water sources.
The tarjih authority is al-Tahawi, a major Hanafi scholar.

Chapter Three: Commentaries on Mukhtasar al-Quduri. Reviving (dead land): it becomes his property. It is preferable for the Imam to assign it to him and not reclaim it from him. This is if he left out seeking permission out of ignorance; but if he left it out due to negligence towards the Imam, he (the Imam) may reclaim it from him as a deterrent. If the Imam leaves it for him, he leaves it subject to ushr or kharaj, unless the man is among those entitled to stipends, in which case the Imam places it upon him as a stipend. Thus in Qadi Khan. And in al-Hidayah: it is obligatory to pay ushr on it, because initiating the imposition of kharaj on a Muslim is not permissible, unless he irrigates it with water that is subject to kharaj, because then it is a continuation of kharaj taking into account the water. Al-Khujandi said: If one owns dead land with the Imam's permission or without it according to the disagreement, and cultivates it: if he irrigates it with rainwater, it is ushr land. If he irrigates it with water from a river from the rivers of the Muslims: according to Abu Hanifa, its ruling is the ruling of the land in which that river is located: if it is in kharaj land, it is kharaj land; if it is from ushr land, it is ushr land. According to Muhammad: if the water he brings to it is from the water of major rivers like the Nile, Euphrates, and the like, it is ushr land; if it is from a river dug by the Imam from kharaj funds, it is kharaj land, and al-Tahawi adopted this. * His saying: 'Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said he owns it by reviving': this is if he left out seeking permission out of ignorance; but if he left it out due to negligence, the Imam reclaims it from him. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the tax classification of land revived from dead land (ihya al-mawat). The discussion centers on the effect of the water source used for irrigation. The author begins by stating that upon reviving dead land, it becomes the property of the reviver. It is recommended for the Imam to grant it and not reclaim it, but if the reviver acted negligently in not seeking permission, the Imam may reclaim it as a deterrent. If the Imam leaves it, it remains subject to ushr or kharaj, except for stipend recipients. Al-Hidayah states that ushr is obligatory because initiating kharaj on a Muslim is impermissible, but if irrigated with kharaj water, kharaj continues. Al-Khujandi then distinguishes: if with rainwater, it is ushr land; if with river water, Abu Hanifa says it follows the surrounding land's tax status (kharaj or ushr), while Muhammad distinguishes between major rivers (like Nile) making it ushr and artificially dug rivers from kharaj funds making it kharaj. The passage also notes that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that revival alone grants ownership without permission, but if negligent, the Imam can reclaim. The author concludes by highlighting new additions by al-Haddad.

Key Terms
إحياء الموات (ihyā' al-mawāt): Reviving dead (unowned, arid) land by bringing it under cultivation.
أرض العشر (arḍ al-'ushr): Land on which one-tenth (ushr) of produce is paid as tax.
أرض الخراج (arḍ al-kharāj): Land on which a fixed land tax (kharaj) is levied, often originally conquered land.
