Why Manuscript Details Matter for Hanafi Fiqh
Bibliographic description of three manuscript copies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the reliability of a Hanafi manuscript based on its physical features and explain how codicology supports textual authenticity.

Core Idea
Manuscript details verify the chain of transmission and textual integrity of Hanafi fiqh works.

When studying classical Hanafi texts, scholars rely on authentic manuscripts. This lesson examines three copies of Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri, focusing on folio count, line density, vowelling, and completeness. These seemingly mundane details are critical for establishing which copy represents the author's original work and how the text was transmitted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the manuscript's age and provenance
Locate the date (AH) and place of copying. This tells you the historical context and potential chain of transmission.
Older copies closer to the author's time are generally more reliable, but later copies can be valuable if copied from a good exemplar.
2
Examine physical features: folios, lines per page, words per line
These numbers indicate the scribe's style and the density of text. Compare with other manuscripts of the same work to detect missing or added sections.
3
Check for vowelling (shakl) and marginal comments
Vowelling shows the scribe's care and helps in reading the text correctly. Marginal comments often contain additional explanations or corrections, indicating scholarly use.
4
Note completeness and missing parts
Determine if the manuscript covers the entire book or only parts. Missing sections, as in the 892 AH copy lacking the Book of Inheritance, affect its utility for certain fiqh topics.
A complete manuscript is always preferred, but even incomplete copies can be useful for the chapters they preserve.
5
Assess overall reliability
Combine all factors: age, condition, clarity, vowelling, completeness, and provenance. This yields a judgment on how much trust to place in the manuscript for textual reconstruction.
The 847 AH copy is described as 'good' and 'very clear', making it a strong candidate for a base text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the deviant sects condemned in this passage and explain why they are considered astray according to Hanafi orthodoxy.

Core Idea
The saved sect is al-jama'ah; those who deviate are astray.

In this concluding passage from al-Lubab, the author reinforces the necessity of adhering to the orthodox Sunni community (al-jama'ah) and warns against sectarian divisions that lead to misguidance. Despite the corruptions in the text, we can discern the condemnation of groups such as the Jahmiyya, Jabriyya, and Qadariyya, whose innovations violate the consensus of the early Muslims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the keyword
Find the term 'al-jama'ah' and understand its significance as the orthodox Sunni community.
The text uses 'الجماعة' which in Hanafi theology refers to the followers of the Sunnah.
2
List the condemned sects

Each sect represents a deviation from orthodox beliefs.
3
Explain their deviation
Jahmiyya negates divine attributes, Jabriyya denies free will, Qadariyya claims complete free will – all opposed to the balanced Hanafi position.
These sects contradicted the consensus of the Salaf.
4
Note the author's judgment
The author declares them 'ضالون' (astray), not kuffar, indicating misguidance but not outright excommunication.
This distinction is crucial in Hanafi theological methodology.

The Importance of Manuscript Dating in Hanafi Fiqh
Describing manuscripts of al-Lubab with dates and provenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the dating of Hanafi manuscripts like al-Lubab is crucial for establishing the reliable transmission of the school's legal rulings.

Core Idea
Manuscript dates trace the chain of transmission: older copies bring us closer to the author’s original text.

This lesson examines two dated manuscripts of al-Lubab, a core Hanafi fiqh commentary. While the passage is brief, it introduces a fundamental principle of Islamic textual criticism: the earlier the manuscript, the fewer intermediaries in the transmission chain. Understanding manuscript dating is essential for any serious student of Hanafi fiqh who wants to verify the authenticity of the texts they rely on.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its author
Al-Lubab is a commentary by al-Maydani on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. Recognize al-Lubab as a standard Hanafi textbook and al-Quduri as the author of a foundational matn.
2
Locate the manuscript dates
The passage mentions two copies: one dated 745 AH (last page) and another dated 768 AH (first page). Note these dates precisely.
745 AH corresponds to 1344 CE; 768 AH to 1367 CE.
3
Recognize the significance of early manuscripts
Manuscripts within two centuries of the author's death (al-Maydani died ca. 675 AH/1276 CE) are considered relatively early. Early copies minimize scribal errors and later interpolations, increasing textual reliability.
Compare: later copies (e.g., 1000 AH) may have accumulated more errors and changes.
4
Connect to fiqh study
When studying a fiqh text, the edition used can affect the accuracy of rulings derived. Knowing manuscript dates helps select the best available version for fatwa and teaching.
Modern editors often rely on the oldest surviving manuscripts.

Bibliographic list of books referenced in al-Lubab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify Hanafi sources from a classical bibliography and classify them by type, author, and significance.

Core Idea
Knowing the sources behind a fiqh text unlocks the chain of transmission and authority.

This passage from al-Lubab provides a valuable reference list of books that the author relies upon. While it may seem like a dry catalog, each entry represents a link in the Hanafi scholarly tradition. This lesson will help you navigate such bibliographies and understand the hierarchy of sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work's genre
Determine if the entry is a commentary (sharh), a fatwa collection, a language dictionary, or a treatise on a specific topic. This helps understand its purpose.
For example, 'al-‘Inaya Sharh al-Hidaya' is a commentary, while 'al-Fatawa al-Sughra' is a fatwa collection.
2
Record the author and his death date
Note the author's name and death date (e.g., al-Babarti, d. 786 AH). This situates the work in time and helps assess its authority within the school.
Later authors may have incorporated earlier works, but earlier works hold foundational status.
3
Check the publication status
Look for symbols: 'خ' means manuscript (unpublished), 'ط' means printed, 'طبع قسم منه' means partially printed. This affects accessibility for research.
Manuscript does not imply weakness; many important works remain in manuscript.
4
Determine the relationship to the main text
Many entries are commentaries on al-Hidaya, the central Hanafi text. Others are independent fatwa collections or linguistic works. This shows the interconnectedness of Hanafi scholarship.
For instance, 'al-‘Inaya', 'al-Ghaya', and 'Ghayat al-Bayan' all comment on al-Hidaya.
5
Evaluate the significance for Hanafi fiqh
Consider the work's role: fatwa collections provide applied rulings; commentaries explain methodology; linguistic works aid interpretation. This helps prioritize sources for study.
Later supercommentaries (hashiyas) like al-‘Azmiyya refine earlier works.

Textual Precision in Pre-emption Settlement: The Difference Between ‘alā and ‘an
Rectifies a textual error in al-Quduri

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct wording for a settlement in pre-emption and explain the legal consequences of the difference between 'صالح عليها' and 'صالح عنها'.

Core Idea
The preposition changes the object of settlement: property or right — and thus whether pre-emption is established.

A single letter in al-Quduri's Mukhtasar changes the entire legal meaning of a pre-emption settlement. Al-Marghinani's correction is not pedantry: it determines whether the settlement waives the pre-emption right or merely confirms it. This lesson unpacks the variant and its juristic impact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two variant phrases
Locate the two prepositional phrases: 'عليها' (upon it) and 'عنها' (about it). The first appears in most copies of the Mukhtasar; the second is the correct version according to al-Marghinani.
The phrase 'صالح عليها' refers to settling on the property; 'صالح عنها' refers to settling on the pre-emption right.
2
Understand the grammatical difference
In Arabic, 'على' indicates a direct object or 'upon', while 'عن' indicates 'about' or 'concerning'. Here, the preposition determines the legal object of the settlement.
Grammar directly impacts fiqh: 'على' makes the property the object; 'عن' makes the right the object.
3
Connect preposition to legal object
If the settlement is 'on the property', it means the parties are settling the sale or ownership dispute. If it is 'about the right', it means they are settling the pre-emption claim itself.
4
Derive the ruling consequence
When settlement is about the right (عن), pre-emption is extinguished if the buyer denies the right, but established if he acknowledges it. The wrong wording (على) would imply pre-emption is always triggered.
The correct wording avoids confusion and aligns with Hanafi principles.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi position
Al-Marghinani states that the correct version is 'يصالح عنها بإنكار' (he settles about it with denial). This is the authoritative reading used in later Hanafi texts.
The erroneous 'عليها' is rejected; all reliable copies follow 'عنها'.
