Navigating the Hanafi Fiqh Sources in al-Lubab
Bibliography listing sources with author and date.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the major Hanafi fiqh works listed in al-Lubab by author, century, and genre.

Core Idea
Knowing the sources behind a commentary is essential for weighing authority and understanding chains of transmission.

This lesson introduces you to the bibliography of al-Lubab – a list of over 30 works that the author, al-Maydani, consulted. These are not random books; they represent the backbone of Hanafi fiqh literature: matn, sharh, fatwa compilations, and usul texts. By learning to recognize titles and authors, you gain a map of the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the entry number and title
Each entry begins with a number and the title of the work. Read the Arabic title carefully – many share common patterns like 'al-Muhīt', 'al-Mukhtār', or 'al-Multaqat'.
Titles often indicate the genre: al-Muhīt (encyclopedic), al-Mukhtasar (abridgment), al-Sharh (commentary).
2
Note the author and death date
After the title, the author's full name and death year (AH) are given. The death date helps place the work chronologically within the Hanafi school.
Entries like 'al-Sarakhsī d. 571' belong to the 6th century AH, a period of major Hanafi codification.
3
Check the source status
Look for the abbreviations (خ) for manuscript and (ط) for print. This tells you the availability of the work in the author's time and now.
Most entries are marked (خ) manuscript, indicating they were not yet printed when al-Lubab was written.
4
Classify by genre
Decide whether the work is a primary fiqh matn (e.g., Mukhtasar al-Qudūrī), a commentary (sharh), a fatwa collection (e.g., al-Multaqat fi al-Fatāwā), or an usul text (e.g., al-Mustasfā).
This classification helps you understand the work's purpose and authority level.

Bibliographical listing of commentaries on Mukhtasar al-Quduri

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the methodology of Multaqa al-Abhar and identify how it integrates multiple Hanafi texts to provide the preponderant view for fatwa.

Core Idea
Multaqa al-Abhar merges four major Hanafi texts and clearly ranks opinions for fatwa using systematic tarjih.

Bibliographical sections in Hanafi commentaries are not mere lists; they reveal the architecture of the school's transmission. This passage from Al-Lubab introduces three key commentaries on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, with special attention to Multaqa al-Abhar. Understanding this work's method of tarjih is essential for navigating later Hanafi fatwa literature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and purpose
Find who wrote Multaqa al-Abhar and what goal he states in his introduction.
The author is Ibrahim al-Halabi (d. 956 AH).
2

These four are foundational Hanafi works from different periods.
3
Analyze tarjih indicators
Examine how al-Halabi marks preponderance: using phrases like 'qīla' with 'al-aṣaḥ' and the dual form for the Sahibayn.
This system helps the mufti quickly identify the authoritative position.
4

This is a standard Hanafi convention used in many works.
5
Evaluate the work's role in fatwa
Consider why Multaqa al-Abhar became a standard fatwa reference, as al-Halabi claims it presents 'the most correct and strongest' positions.
The work is still consulted by Hanafi muftis today.

Key Sources in the Hanafi Fiqh Tradition
Bibliography of Hanafi fiqh sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify Hanafi fiqh sources as matn (primary text), sharh (commentary), or hashiya (gloss) based on their titles and author lineages.

Core Idea
Know your sources: matn, sharh, hashiya – each layer shapes the authority of a ruling.

This passage lists the sources used in a critical edition of Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri. While it appears as a simple bibliography, it offers a window into the Hanafi textual tradition. Understanding the hierarchy of texts – from foundational matn works like Mukhtasar al-Quduri to detailed commentaries like al-Bahr al-Ra'iq – is essential for navigating fiqh rulings and assessing their weight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of source
For each entry, determine if it is a matn (primary text), sharh (commentary), hashiya (gloss), or a modern edition. For example, Multaqa al-Abhar is a compilation of matn texts, while al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a sharh.
Titles often indicate the genre: 'Sharh' in the title usually means commentary.
2
Note the author and century
Observe the author's name and death date. This helps place the work in the madhhab's historical development. Earlier texts like Mukhtasar al-Quduri (5th century AH) are more foundational; later commentaries like Minhat al-Khaliq (13th century AH) represent later preferred views.
Look for the death date (هـ) after the author's name.
3
Understand relationships between works
Recognize connections: al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a sharh on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, and Minhat al-Khaliq is an abridgment of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. This chain shows how rulings evolved.
Some works are explicitly described as 'combined' or 'abridged'.
4
Apply to Hanafi study
When researching a ruling, start from the matn, then consult the sharh for proofs, and finally refer to later hashiya for mufta bihi positions. This method ensures you use the most authoritative layer.
For example, to understand a ruling on prayer, you might begin with Mukhtasar al-Quduri, then refer to al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Key Terms
مختصر القدوري (Mukhtasar al-Quduri): The concise legal text by Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Quduri (d. 428 AH), a foundational work of the Hanafi school.
شرح (Sharh): A commentary or explanation of a primary legal text.
ترجمة (Tarjamah): A translation or, in this context, a translation and sometimes commentary.
