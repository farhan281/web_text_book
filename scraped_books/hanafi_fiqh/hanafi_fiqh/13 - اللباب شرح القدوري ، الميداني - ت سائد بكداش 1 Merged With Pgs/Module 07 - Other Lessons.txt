Sunnah Shroud Garments for Men and Women
States the sunnah number of shroud garments and permissibility of fewer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah number of shroud cloths for a man and a woman in the Hanafi school and identify when fewer cloths are permissible.

Core Idea
Sunnah shroud: three cloths for a man, five for a woman; fewer permissible if needed.

In this passage, al-Quduri's commentator specifies the recommended (sunnah) set of garments for shrouding the deceased. Understanding these details ensures that funeral rites are performed in the best manner while recognizing flexibility for hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is shrouding the deceased. The ruling is sunnah (recommended) to use the specified number of cloths.
2
List the sunnah garments for a man
Three garments: izār (lower wrap), qamees (shirt), and lifāfah (outer sheet).
3
List the sunnah garments for a woman
Five garments: izār, qamees, khirqah (breast binder), khimār (head cover, implied), and lifāfah.
The text explicitly mentions only four names; khimār is commonly included by commentators.
4
Note the permissibility of fewer cloths
For a man, one cloth suffices; for a woman, three cloths suffice. Fewer are valid when necessary.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from a commentary on al-Quduri's matn, specifying the sunnah (recommended) number of shrouds for the deceased. For a man, the sunnah is three cloths: the izār (a lower wrap), the qamees (a shirt), and the lifāfah (a large outer sheet that covers the entire body). It is permissible to use only one cloth if necessary, though the complete set is more virtuous. For a woman, the sunnah is five cloths: izār, qamees, a khirqah (a cloth to bind the breasts), a khimār (head covering, presumed), and lifāfah. Again, using only three cloths is permissible. The text also mentions placing hanut (a mixture of camphor and other aromatic substances) on the head and private parts (though the wording is unclear), and placing the woman's hair on her chest. Additionally, it forbids combing the hair, dyeing it, clipping nails, or washing the deceased after the ritual bath (ghusl) as these actions are not part of the sunnah. The rulings are in accordance with the Hanafi school; other schools may have different customs.

Imam's Permission in Reviving Dead Land: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute with proofs and practical consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the necessity of Imam's permission for ownership through revival of dead land, and apply the thamarah al-khilaf to a concrete scenario.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires Imam's permission for land ownership; the Sahibayn do not.

This lesson unpacks a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: does reviving a dead land automatically grant ownership, or must the ruler's permission be obtained? The answer determines the reviver's legal rights and the state's authority over land. By studying the proofs from hadith and qiyas, you will see how the same sources yield different rulings within one madhhab.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify what Abu Hanifa holds: ownership of revived land is conditional on the Imam's permission. Without it, the land remains unowned by the individual.
This is the mufta bihi position in later fatwa, as per the text.
2
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that revival alone, without permission, grants ownership immediately.
This is the minority view within the Hanafi school.
3
Follow the proofs for each side
Sahibayn cite the hadith 'Whoever revives a dead land, it is his.' Abu Hanifa cites 'A person has nothing except what his Imam's soul is pleased with' and analogizes to the public treasury.
The qiyas to Bayt al-Mal is central to Abu Hanifa's argument.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the opposing proof
Abu Hanifa's response: the hadith about Imam's pleasure is general and restricts the general implication of the revival hadith. The analogy to mubah (firewood) is weaker because land is a communal resource, not like firewood.
The text does not explicitly quote a response from Sahibayn to this.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, a person who revives land without permission does not become owner until the Imam grants it; the Imam may reclaim it if the person acted out of disdain. Under Sahibayn, ownership is automatic and the Imam cannot reclaim it.
This affects taxation and land rights.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the issue of reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat) without the Imam's permission. The passage is an expansion from two commentaries on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah and al-Siraj al-Wahhaj. The ruling in the matn is that revival with permission grants ownership, but without permission, Abu Hanifa holds that ownership is not acquired, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that it is. Abu Hanifa's proof is the hadith 'A person has nothing except what his Imam's soul is pleased with' and a qiyas to the Bayt al-Mal (public treasury), which is a collective right of Muslims and cannot be taken without the Imam's authorization. The Sahibayn rely on the famous hadith 'Whoever revives a dead land, it is his' and a qiyas to mubahat (permissible things) like firewood, where prior possession (sabq al-yad) establishes ownership. The text then details Abu Hanifa's position: if the person is ignorant and the Imam later grants the land, the Imam should not take it back; but if the person deliberately disrespected the Imam, the Imam may reclaim it as a deterrent. If the Imam lets him keep it, the land is subject to 'ushr (tithe) or kharaj (land tax), as discussed in al-Hidayah, which explains that initially imposing kharaj on a Muslim is impermissible unless the water used for irrigation is from a kharaj source. The passage is a typical example of how Hanafi scholars reconcile textual proofs and rational analogy within the madhhab.

Key Terms
إحياء الموات (ihya' al-mawat): Reviving dead land by cultivation or construction, bringing it into productive use.
إذن الإمام (idhn al-imam): Explicit permission from the Muslim ruler (Imam) to revive and own dead land.
مال بيت المال (mal bayt al-mal): The public treasury of the Muslim state, considered a collective right of all Muslims.

Reviving Dead Land: Ownership Dispute and Tax Rules
Commentary on ihya al-mawat with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership of revived dead land without imam's permission and apply the tax determination rules based on water source.

Core Idea
Reviving dead land without permission: Abu Hanifa denies ownership, Sahibayn grant it — tax follows the water source.

This passage from Lubab al-Quduri presents a foundational intra-Hanafi dispute on whether reviving unowned land requires the imam's permission for ownership. Understanding this dispute is crucial because it directly affects land rights and tax liability (ʿushr vs kharaj). We will first examine the two positions and their proofs, then see how the tax is determined based on the water source—a secondary dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue and the Two Positions
The passage discusses what happens when someone revives dead land (ihya al-mawat) without the imam's permission. Abu Hanifa says the reviver does not own it; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say he does. Note the practical consequence: ownership or removal.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Proof
Abu Hanifa relies on the hadith 'A person has nothing except what his Imam is pleased with' and an analogy to public treasury: no one may take communal wealth without the imam's permission. Land is a communal right, so permission is required.
His proof is both naqli (hadith) and aqli (qiyas).
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's Proof
They rely on the hadith 'Whoever revives a dead land, it is his' and an analogy to permissible wealth like firewood: if you take it first, you own it. They argue that revival, like gathering firewood, is a cause of ownership without need for permission.
The Sahibayn's hadith is more direct on the issue.
4
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, if someone revives land without permission, the imam may take it back and give it to someone else; under the Sahibayn, the reviver owns it and cannot be removed. The text recommends the imam grant it to the reviver in any case.
The mufta bihi position often follows the Sahibayn for ease.
5
Apply the Tax Rules Based on Water Source
The tax on the revived land depends on the water used. If rainwater, it is ʿushr land. If from a river, Abu Hanifa looks at the classification of the land where the river originates; Muhammad distinguishes between large natural rivers (ʿushr) and canals dug with kharaj funds (kharaj). Al-Tahawi adopts Muhammad's view.
This shows that within the Hanafi school, there are further subsidiary ikhtilafat.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the Hanafi rulings regarding reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat), containing an intra-Hanafi dispute and subsequent rulings on taxation. The author first states that if someone revives land without seeking the Imam's permission out of negligence, the Imam may take it back as a deterrent. If the Imam permits him to keep it, the land is subject to 'ushr or kharaj. Al-Hidayah clarifies that 'ushr is obligatory because a Muslim cannot initially be charged kharaj unless he uses water from a kharaj stream; then kharaj is retained in consideration of the water. The author then quotes from Al-Siraj al-Wahhaj, which presents the dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that reviving dead land without the Imam's permission does not grant ownership, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that it does. The proof for Abu Hanifa is the hadith 'A person has nothing except what his Imam is pleased with,' and analogizing to public treasury wealth. The Sahibayn rely on the hadith 'Whoever revives a dead land, it is his,' and analogize to collecting firewood. Despite the dispute, it is recommended for the Imam to grant the land to the reviver. If the reviver acted ignorantly, the Imam should not take it back; if negligently, he may. The tax on the land is determined by the water source: if rainwater, it is 'ushr; if from a Muslim river, Abu Hanifa follows the classification of the land where the river originates, while Muhammad distinguishes between large rivers (like the Nile) which make it 'ushr, and rivers dug with kharaj funds which make it kharaj. Al-Tahawi adopts Muhammad's view. The passage follows the order: initial ruling, dispute with proofs, practical conclusion, and detailed tax rules.

إحياء الموات (ihya al-mawat): Reviving dead (unowned, uncultivated) land, making it arable.
الخراج (al-kharaj): A land tax imposed on non-Muslims or on land owned by the state; in this context, a tax on land irrigated with water from kharaj sources.
العشر (al-'ushr): One-tenth tax on agricultural produce from 'ushr land (usually owned by Muslims).
