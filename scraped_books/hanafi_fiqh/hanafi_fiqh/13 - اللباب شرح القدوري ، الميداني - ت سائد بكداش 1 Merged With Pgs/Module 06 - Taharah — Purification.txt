The Two Types of Najasah: Haqiqi and Hukmi
Defining najasah and its two categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between najasah haqiqiyyah and najasah hukmiyyah, and state the Hanafi definition of each.

Core Idea
Najasah is either tangible filth (najs) or a legal state (hadath) – both must be removed for valid salah.

This opening passage of Kitab al-Tahara defines the foundational concept of najasah (impurity) in Hanafi fiqh. The author divides impurity into two distinct categories: real, tangible impurity (najasah haqiqiyyah) and legal, ritual impurity (najasah hukmiyyah). Understanding this division is essential because the rulings for purification depend on which type is present.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of najasah
The text states that najasah is either haqiqi (real) or hukmi (legal). First, recognize these two broad categories.
2
Define najasah haqiqiyyah
The text equates haqiqi with 'najs' – tangible impurities like blood, urine, etc. This is a physical substance that must be removed from the body, clothes, or place of prayer.
Najasah haqiqiyyah is actual filth.
3
Define najasah hukmiyyah
The text equates hukmi with 'hadath' – a legal or ritual state of impurity, not a physical substance. This includes janabah (major) and wudu-breaking (minor) hadath.
Hadath is a legal status, not a physical thing.
4
Note the sub-division of hadath
The text mentions that hadath is divided into major (al-kubra) and minor (al-sughra). Major requires ghusl; minor requires wudu.
The exact wording at the end is garbled, but the division is standard.
5
Understand the practical consequence
Both types of najasah invalidate salah unless removed. The method of removal differs: physical washing for haqiqi, and specific acts of purification (wudu/ghusl) for hukmi.
Purification from both is obligatory (fard) before salah.

The Obligations of Wudu: The Hanafi Position on Wiping the Head
Opening of the Book of Taharah with Qur'anic verse and statement of the fara'id of wudu.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four fara'id of wudu according to the Hanafi school and explain why the obligatory amount for wiping the head is one quarter.

Core Idea
The four fara'id of wudu are derived from Surah al-Ma'idah; the fard for wiping the head is only one quarter, not the whole scalp.

This lesson opens the Book of Taharah with Al-Quduri's matn. The author cites the foundational Qur'anic verse for wudu and immediately clarifies the Hanafi position on the extent of wiping the head. Understanding that the obligation is limited to one quarter—based on the prophetic practice—is essential for correct practice and distinguishes the Hanafi school from others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four limbs mentioned in the verse
Read the verse carefully: 'wash your faces and your forearms to the elbows and wipe over your heads and wash your feet to the ankles.' List the four acts: washing the face, washing the hands/arms to the elbows, wiping the head, and washing the feet to the ankles.
The verse uses different verbs: 'fa-ghsilu' (wash) for the first two and last, and 'imsaḥū' (wipe) for the head.
2
Understand the Hanafi interpretation of 'imsaḥū'
The imperative 'imsaḥū' indicates obligation, but its extent is not specified in the verse. The Hanafi school relies on the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) wiping his head to determine that the obligatory amount is the forelock, i.e., one quarter of the head.
The hadith is cited as evidence, though the text in this copy is slightly unclear.
3
Recognize the inter-madhhab dispute
The Shafi'i school holds that wiping the entire head is obligatory. The Hanafi position is that one quarter suffices. This difference stems from how each school interprets the hadith evidence and the scope of the Qur'anic command.
Later Hanafi works (e.g., Al-Hidayah) uphold the quarter as the relied-upon position.
4
Memorize the four fara'id for practical application
Commit to memory: washing the face, washing the hands (including elbows), wiping one quarter of the head, and washing the feet (including ankles). These are the obligatory acts of wudu according to the Hanafi school.
The elbows and ankles are included in the washing; the head wiping is limited to a quarter.

The Nullifiers of Wudu in the Hanafi School
Lists the six nullifiers of wudu.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six nullifiers of wudu according to the Hanafi school and apply them to determine if wudu is broken in given scenarios.

Core Idea
Six things break wudu: from the two passages, blood/pus, deep sleep, insanity/fainting, and loud laughter in prayer.

Wudu is a prerequisite for prayer, but certain events nullify it. This passage from the classical Hanafi text Al-Lubab enumerates the six nawāqid (nullifiers). Memorizing these is essential for every Muslim, but understanding their conditions and exceptions is equally important.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source
This passage is from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, a standard Hanafi matn. Recognize it as a concise list of nullifiers (nawāqid).
2
List each nullifier
Read the Arabic carefully and extract each item: 1) anything from the two passages, 2) blood/pus/fluid that exits and reaches a place requiring washing, 3) deep sleep (lying/leaning), 4) loss of reason (fainting/insanity), 5) loud laughter in prayer.
The fifth item (qahqaha) is specific to prayer.
3
Understand the conditions
For blood/pus, it only nullifies if it reaches a place that needs washing (e.g., fills the mouth). For sleep, only deep sleep that relaxes the muscles nullifies; light sleep (e.g., sitting without support) does not.
Smiling does not nullify; only loud laughter (qahqaha) during prayer.
4
Distinguish between similar actions
Compare smiling vs. laughing, deep sleep vs. light drowsiness, and a few drops of blood vs. a flowing quantity. The distinctions affect whether wudu remains valid.
Hanafi school uses the principle of 'flowing' for blood: if it flows beyond the wound, it nullifies.
5
Apply to scenarios
Given a fictional scenario (e.g., someone sleeps upright, then bleeds a little, then laughs quietly), decide whether wudu is broken based on the conditions.
Practice with real-life examples to internalize the rules.

Key Terms

The Four Fard Acts of Wudu and Their Sunnah Complements
States obligatory and recommended acts of wudu with Quranic proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four fard acts of wudu as derived from Quran 5:6, list the sunnah acts, and explain why the imperative form in the verse indicates obligation.

Core Idea
Wudu has four fard acts: washing face, hands, wiping head, washing feet. Sunnah acts perfect the worship.

This lesson examines the foundational ruling of wudu: the obligatory (fard) acts that must be performed for wudu to be valid, and the recommended (sunnah) acts that enhance its perfection. The passage from al-Quduri's matn, explained by al-Maydani, anchors the fard acts in the clear command of Allah in Surah al-Ma'idah verse 6. Understanding this distinction is critical for every Muslim's daily worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage discusses wudu, specifically its fard (obligatory) and sunnah (recommended) components. The act is wudu, and the ruling is that the four acts are fard (obligatory) and the listed practices are sunnah (recommended).
Fard omission invalidates wudu; sunnah omission does not.
2
Locate the dalil
The author quotes Quran 5:6: 'فاغسِلوا وُجوهَكُم وأيدِيَكُم إلى المَرافِق وامسَحوا بِرُؤوسِكُم وأرجُلَكُم إلى الكعبَين' as the primary evidence for the fard acts. This is a clear Quranic command.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative verbs 'faghsilu' (wash) and 'imsahu' (wipe) indicate obligation (wujub) per the Hanafi usul principle that al-amr yufidu al-wujub. Thus, these four acts are fard.
Hanafi school holds that a command without a qualifying context indicates obligation.
4
List the fard acts
Based on the verse: 1) Washing the entire face, 2) Washing both hands including the elbows, 3) Wiping the head (at least a quarter per Hanafi, but the passage says 'wipe the head'), 4) Washing both feet including the ankles.
These are the four fard acts in the Hanafi school.
5
Distinguish the sunnah acts
The passage then lists sunnah acts: washing hands before rinsing the mouth, starting wudu with basmalah, madmadah (rinsing mouth), istinshaq (rinsing nose), wiping ears, tahlil al-lihyah (running fingers through beard), and repeating each wash up to three times. These are recommended but not obligatory.
Sunnah acts enhance wudu; omitting them does not invalidate but loses reward.
