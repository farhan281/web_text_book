Division Among Wives (Qasm) – Obligation of Equal Rotation
Ruling statement on qasm (division of nights)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasm (division of nights) among free wives, explain the exceptions for slave wives and travel, and apply the rule to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Equal division of nights among free wives is wajib, regardless of age, virginity, or marriage order.

The equitable treatment of co-wives is a cornerstone of Islamic family law. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position on qasm (division of nights) as summarized in Al-Hidayah. The obligation to maintain absolute equality among free wives is clear, with specific rules for mixed free-slave households and the concession of travel. Understanding these rulings is essential for applying justice in polygamous marriages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the text discusses qasm (division of nights) among wives. The act is wajib (obligatory) when the husband has two or more free wives.
Some Hanafi scholars consider it sunnah mu'akkadah, but the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is wajib.
2
List the conditions for equal division
Equality applies to all free wives regardless of whether they are virgins (bikr) or previously married (thayyib), and regardless of which wife married first (qadimah or jadidah).
The text explicitly mentions both scenarios to remove any doubt.
3
Note the exception for slave wives
If one wife is free and the other is a slave, the free wife receives two nights for every one night of the slave wife because the slave has lesser entitlement.
This is a specific Hanafi ruling based on the differential status.
4
Understand the travel exemption
During travel, the husband may take any wife he chooses without maintaining rotation. The wives left behind are not entitled to compensatory nights.
This concession is due to the practical difficulty of equal division while traveling.

The General Principle of Fosterage Prohibition
General principle and exceptions of rida'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that foster relationships mirror blood relationships for marriage prohibitions and identify the key exceptions.

Core Idea
Foster relationships through breastfeeding create the same marriage prohibitions as blood relationships.

This lesson focuses on the foundational principle of rida' (fosterage) in Hanafi fiqh: that breastfeeding creates a bond similar to lineage, prohibiting marriages that would be prohibited by blood. You will learn the general rule, the specific exceptions, and the concept of laban al-fahl, which attributes the milk to the father.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general rule
The passage states that relationships forbidden by blood are also forbidden by fosterage. This is the core principle of rida'.
Remember: this is an established principle based on Quran and Sunnah.
2
Identify the exceptions
The text lists specific exceptions: marrying the sister of a foster son is allowed, but marrying the wife of a foster father or foster son is not allowed. Also, a foster brother's sister is permissible.
Exceptions arise when the relationship is not directly through the foster bond.
3
Grasp laban al-fahl
The prohibition attaches through laban al-fahl (milk of the male). This means the milk is attributed to the man whose sperm caused the lactation, so his lineage also creates prohibitions.
This is a distinctive Hanafi view; other schools differ.
4
Apply the rule
Consider a scenario where a boy and girl are nursed by the same woman. They become foster siblings and cannot marry. Similarly, a foster mother cannot marry her foster son's sons.
These applications follow from the general principle.

Conditions for Prohibition: Quantity, Period, and Milk Dominance
Conditions for prohibition: quantity, period, and mixing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi conditions for fosterage prohibition: required quantity, nursing period, and the rule of dominancy when milk is mixed with other substances.

Core Idea
Fosterage prohibition only establishes when the milk is human, ingested in a certain quantity during the nursing period, and is the dominant substance in a mixture.

In Hanafi fiqh, not every instance of breastfeeding creates marriage prohibitions. There are specific conditions regarding the amount of milk, the time period, and the purity of the milk. This lesson covers those conditions, including how to rule when milk is mixed with water, food, or medicine, and the principle of taking the predominant substance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the required quantity
The passage starts by asking 'what quantity of milk establishes the prohibition?' Although the exact quantity is not given here, it refers to the Hanafi view that a certain minimum amount (like a few sips) suffices.
The exact quantity is detailed in other texts; here it is noted as a condition.
2
Understand the nursing period
The text states that after the nursing period (generally two years according to Hanafis), breastfeeding does not create prohibition.
This is based on the Quranic verse about weaning in two years.
3
Apply the rule of dominance in mixtures
If milk is mixed with water, food, or medicine, the ruling follows the predominant substance. If the dominant substance is human milk, prohibition attaches; otherwise not.
This demonstrates the Hanafi use of 'ghalaba' (predominance) to resolve conflicts.
4
Consider the origin of milk
If two women's milk is mixed, the dominant one's milk establishes prohibition. Also, if a virgin's milk flows and is fed, prohibition attaches.
The milk must be human; animal milk does not create prohibition.

Special Situations: Virgin's Milk, Enema, Man's Milk, and Testimony
Special cases and evidence rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on several special breastfeeding scenarios: virgin's milk, deceased woman's milk, milk enema, man's milk, animal milk, the co-wife case, and the testimony requirement for establishing fosterage.

Core Idea
Not all milk from a human creates prohibition; man's milk, enema, and animal milk do not, while milk from a virgin or deceased woman does.

The Hanafi school addresses many edge cases in the law of fosterage. This lesson covers unusual situations such as a virgin's sudden lactation, milk extracted from a deceased woman, milk given as an enema, a man's milk, two children drinking goat milk, and a unique case where a co-wife nurses the other. It also covers the rule that the testimony of women alone is not accepted to prove breastfeeding.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the special cases of human milk
The passage lists: virgin's milk (if a virgin's milk flows and she feeds a child, prohibition attaches), deceased woman's milk (if her milk is extracted and fed, prohibition attaches), man's milk (if a man's milk flows, prohibition does not attach).
This shows that prohibition is tied to the natural function of nursing, not just any milk production.
2
Understand the enema and animal milk rulings
If milk is given as an enema (حقنة), prohibition does not attach because it is not ingested normally. If children drink goat milk, no prohibition attaches.
This reinforces that direct ingestion through the mouth is required.
3
Examine the co-wife case
If a man marries a young girl and an adult woman, and the adult woman breastfeeds the young girl, both become forbidden to the man.
This illustrates the principle of rada' creating a mother-daughter relationship.
4
Remember the testimony rule
In matters of fosterage, the testimony of women alone is not acceptable; generally required is either two men or one man and two women.
This is a procedural rule to prevent false claims.

Minimum Fosterage Duration: Six Months and Response to Sahibayn
Presents Hanafi rational proof and rebuts Sahibayn's evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Imam Abu Hanifa's rational proof for the six-month minimum fosterage period and explain how the Hanafi school reinterprets the two-year evidence from Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Fosterage prohibition requires exclusive milk diet for six months—the change of nourishment analogy with pregnancy proves it.

You know that breastfeeding can create a permanent marriage prohibition, but how long must it last? The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rely on the Quranic verse about two years and a hadith 'no breastfeeding after two years.' Imam Abu Hanifa disagrees—he uses a rational proof based on how a child's nourishment changes. Let's follow his logical argument and then see how he reinterprets the Sahibayn's evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule in dispute
The disputed issue: what is the minimum period of breastfeeding for it to create the prohibition of marriage (radā')? Abu Hanifa says six months; the Sahibayn say two years (or at least that the prohibition does not extend beyond two years).
2
Follow Abu Hanifa's rational proof
The proof: For fosterage to cause prohibition, the child's growth (nushu') must depend on the milk. This requires a change of nourishment (taghayyur al-ghidhā') from exclusive milk to solid food. Sudden weaning is harmful; the child needs to get accustomed gradually. The minimum period for this change is six months, analogized to the minimum pregnancy period.
The analogy: just as the minimum pregnancy (six months) is enough to change the fetus's nourishment from umbilical to milk, so six months of breastfeeding is enough to change the infant's nourishment from milk to solid food.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's opposing proof
The Sahibayn cite the hadith 'lā raḍā'a ba'da ḥawlayn' (no breastfeeding after two years) and the Quran 2:233 mentioning two years of breastfeeding.
4
Apply the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa argues these texts refer to the mother's right to wages, not to the prohibition. The same verse makes weaning conditional on mutual consent—if breastfeeding after two years were inherently wrong, it would not be left to mutual agreement.
Thus the two-year limit is about financial support (ujrah), not about creating mahram status.
5
Determine the practical effect
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence): If a child is breastfed for, say, 8 months, Abu Hanifa says the prohibition is established; the Sahibayn say it is not (if they require two years). The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's, so the minimum is six months.

Fosterage Prohibition After Weaning: The Hanafi Position
Ruling with proof: fosterage prohibition after weaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that breastfeeding after the weaning period does not create fosterage prohibition, identify the proofs (hadith and rational) and the intra-Hanafi exception, and apply the ruling to determine prohibition in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Prohibition of fosterage is tied to growth, which only occurs within the breastfeeding period; after weaning, no prohibition attaches.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a fundamental rule regarding fosterage prohibition: it only applies within the designated breastfeeding period (approximately two years). The author presents the primary proof from the Prophet's hadith and a rational argument based on the 'illa of growth. Additionally, the passage addresses a key exception: weaning before the period is not considered in the default position (Zahir al-Riwayah), but a riwaya from Abu Hanifa allows it if the child becomes completely independent. The passage also clarifies that the hadith of Aisha, which seems to allow fosterage after the period, is considered abrogated.

Guided Path

State the general rule and its rationale
Identify the overarching ruling: once the breastfeeding period has passed, breastfeeding does not create fosterage prohibition. Understand the reason: prohibition is based on growth (nushur), which occurs only within the period.

Examine the hadith 'La rada'a ba'da al-fisal' (No breastfeeding after weaning) and the rational proof that an older child is not nurtured by milk. Understand how these establish the rule.

Note the riwaya from Abu Hanifa via Hasan ibn Ziyad: if the child becomes completely independent of milk (mustaghni), weaning is considered and subsequent breastfeeding does not create prohibition. Understand the basis: growth through milk has ceased due to change of nourishment.
This is a weaker riwaya; Zahir al-Riwayah is the default.

Resolve the cross-madhhab objection

Other schools may differ; Hanafis rely on the abrogation argument.

The permissibility ruling is derived from the principle that what is allowed by necessity is limited to the extent of necessity.

The General Rule of Fosterage Prohibitions and Its Exception
States the general rule with one exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule that fosterage creates the same marriage prohibitions as lineage, and identify the single exception regarding the mother of a foster-sister.

Core Idea
Fosterage mirrors lineage in marriage prohibitions, except the foster-sister's mother is permissible.

The law of fosterage (rida') establishes mahram relationships similar to those of blood lineage, based on a prophetic hadith. However, there is one notable exception: the mother of one's foster-sister is not considered a mahram, and thus marriage to her is permissible. This lesson examines both the general rule and the specific exception, helping you apply this principle correctly in marriage matters.

Guided Path
1
Understand the General Rule
The text states that fosterage prohibits what lineage prohibits. This is based on the hadith 'Yahrumu min al-rada'i ma yahrumu min al-nasab'. Recognize that all direct blood relations that are mahram (mother, sister, daughter, etc.) become mahram through fosterage when the foster relationship is established (i.e., the wet nurse and her milk children).
The general rule is a memorization anchor: fosterage = lineage for prohibitions.
2
Identify the Exception
The one exception explicitly stated is 'except the mother of his foster-sister'. This means a man may marry the mother of his foster-sister. Understand why: in lineage, the mother of one's sister is one's own mother or stepmother (both prohibited), but in fosterage, the foster-sister's mother is not the foster mother—she is a stranger.
The exception is specific: only the mother of the foster-sister, not other relatives.
3
Contrast with Lineage
Compare the lineage case: if a man's sister from his own mother or father, her mother is his mother (or stepmother) and thus prohibited. In fosterage, the foster-sister is the wet nurse's daughter, but the wet nurse's mother is not his mother. The text explicitly contrasts: 'because she is his mother or his father's wife' for lineage, but for fosterage that reasoning does not apply.
The illa (reason) for prohibition in lineage does not exist in fosterage for this case.
4
Apply the Ruling
Given a scenario, determine whether marriage is permissible or prohibited based on this principle. If the relationship is a direct foster relative (foster mother, foster sister, foster daughter), it is prohibited. If it is the mother of a foster sister, it is permissible. For other indirect relatives (e.g., foster aunt), the general rule does not automatically extend; one must check if they are covered by lineage analogy.
The exception is limited; do not extend it to other indirect relatives without evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on fosterage prohibition when milk is mixed with food, and explain the basis for Abu Hanifa's preferred view.

Core Idea
When milk mixes with food, prohibition depends on whether milk is dominant or subordinate; Abu Hanifa says food remains the origin regardless.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, we encounter a nuanced intra-madhhab disagreement about the effect of mixing milk with food on fosterage prohibition. Imam Abu Hanifa's view—that no prohibition attaches even if milk predominates—is compared with the position of his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the broader principles of 'consideration of the dominant' (al-‘ibrah li’l-ghālib) versus 'the origin and follower' (al-aṣl wa’t-tābi‘) in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the exact legal question: does the prohibition of marriage due to fosterage (riḍā‘) attach when milk is mixed with food?
The default ruling for consuming pure foster-milk is prohibition; the dispute is about mixture.
2
Identify the ikhtilaf
Note Abu Hanifa’s position: no prohibition even if milk is predominant. Contrast with Sahibayn: prohibition attaches if milk is dominant and food uncooked.
The common ground: cooked food – all agree prohibition does not attach.
3
Note the qualification (taqyīd)
Observe that the Sahibayn’s view is restricted to uncooked food. For cooked food, all three imams agree prohibition does not attach.
Cooking changes the mixture; the food becomes the base? The text does not explain why, but it is a key qualification.
4
Examine the proofs (adillah)
Analyze the Sahibayn’s proof: the dominant element determines the ruling (analogy to milk mixed with water). Then analyze Abu Hanifa’s proof: food is the origin (aṣl) and milk is a follower (tābi‘); nourishment comes from food, so milk is subordinate.
Abu Hanifa’s reasoning is based on the purpose (maqṣūd) of eating: food, not milk, is primary.
5
Determine the muftā bihi
Note that the author of al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) declares Abu Hanifa’s view as correct (al-ṣaḥīḥ), making it the relied-upon position for fatwa.
The tarjīḥ is based on the principle that what is dominant in purpose prevails over mere quantity.

Subjugated milk does not establish fosterage prohibition because it cannot cause growth.

When milk is mixed with water, does a child's drinking it create a fosterage relationship that prohibits marriage? The Hanafis and Shafi'is differ on the answer. This lesson unpacks the dispute, focusing on the Hanafi principle that the subjugated component in a mixture is legally disregarded, and the Shafi'i reliance on the sensible presence of milk. The practical consequence: a child who drinks a water-dominant milk-water mixture is not a foster-child for marriage prohibitions according to Hanafis, but is according to Shafi'is (if 4 sips of milk present).
This difference directly affects who one may marry.

Identify the legal issue

Commentary
This passage addresses whether milk expressed from a deceased woman and fed to a child establishes fosterage prohibition. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that since the woman herself is the basis of prohibition, and she no longer exists as a legal subject after death, the prohibition cannot be transmitted. He analogizes it to the case of sexual intercourse with a deceased woman, which does not establish prohibition of affinity (musahara). The Hanafi school responds that the cause of prohibition in rada' is the 'semblance of partiality' (shubhat al-juz'iyya) which is present in the milk itself due to its property of causing growth (insha' and inbat). This cause remains in the milk even after the woman's death. Therefore, prohibition attaches. Moreover, the effect of this prohibition is practically manifested: the deceased woman becomes the rada'i mother of the child, so the child's husband becomes her son-in-law (daamad), making him a mahram for her. Thus, he can perform her burial and tayammum if no other mahram is available. This practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) shows that the prohibition is indeed established. The distinction between rada' and musahara is crucial: rada' prohibition is based on the growth caused by the milk, which exists in the milk itself, regardless of the woman being alive or dead. Musahara prohibition, however, is based on lineage through childbirth, which requires a living woman with a womb for 'cultivation' (mahall al-harth). Since death removes the womb, musahara does not result. Analogy (qiyas) between the two is therefore invalid.

Enema with milk does not establish fosterage
Ruling with intra-Hanafi dispute and distinction from fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fosterage via enema and man's milk, explain the illa of growth, and distinguish Imam Muhammad's minority view from the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Fosterage requires growth from oral feeding; enema feeds but does not cause growth, so no prohibition.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses two scenarios: giving an infant an enema with a woman's milk, and a man nursing an infant with his own milk. In both cases, the manifest Hanafi position is that fosterage prohibition does not arise. However, Imam Muhammad argued by qiyas to fasting that an enema should establish prohibition. The text explains the distinction in the underlying cause (illa).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on enema and fosterage
According to the majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad in the manifest view), an enema with milk does not establish the prohibition of fosterage. The ruling is grounded in the principle that fosterage requires growth (nashw) caused by nutrition entering from above (the mouth).
The text says 'ala al-zahir' (on the manifest position) indicating the relied-upon view.
2
Identify Imam Muhammad's counter-argument
Imam Muhammad reportedly held that an enema with milk does establish fosterage, drawing an analogy (qiyas) to fasting: just as an enema invalidates the fast, it should also create fosterage prohibition because milk enters the body.
Imam Muhammad's view is recorded in the Nawadir (rare transmissions) and is not part of Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Analyze the distinction between the illa of fasting and fosterage
The invalidator in fasting is 'islah al-badan' (improving the body), which can occur from below through medicine. In contrast, the cause of fosterage prohibition is 'nashw' (growth), which only occurs when nutrition enters from above (the mouth). An enema does not produce growth, so the analogy fails.
This is a classic example of distinguishing between two legal causes (tafrid al-illa).
4
State the ruling on man's milk
If a man produces milk and breastfeeds an infant, fosterage is not established. The text states that man's milk is 'not truly milk' because milk is only produced by those who can give birth (women). Thus it does not cause growth.
Both Hanafis and Shafi'is generally agree that man's milk does not create fosterage.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi (operative ruling for fatwa) is that an enema with milk does not establish fosterage, and man's milk does not establish fosterage. The minority view of Imam Muhammad is not relied upon.
The text's phrase 'ala al-zahir' indicates this is the manifest and authoritative position.

Core Idea
Fosterage prohibition depends on growth (nashw) via oral ingestion; enema bypasses this 'illa.

This passage addresses a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute: if an infant receives milk through an enema, does it create the same fosterage prohibition as breastfeeding? The relied-upon position (Zahir al-Riwayah) says no, while Imam Muhammad argues by analogy to fasting. The key lies in the effective cause ('illa) of the prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
The passage states the Zahir al-Riwayah position (no prohibition) and Imam Muhammad's dissenting view (prohibition by analogy to fasting). Label each clearly.
Zahir al-Riwayah is the main transmitted corpus of the school.
2
Extract the 'illa for fosterage
The text says the prohibitor in fosterage is 'the meaning of growth' (nashw). Identify that as the effective cause. Understand that growth requires nourishment entering via the upper digestive tract.
The term 'nashw' is explicitly used.
3
Analyze the analogy to fasting
Imam Muhammad reasons: just as an enema invalidates fasting, it should create fosterage prohibition. Break down his qiyas: fasting's invalidator is entry into the body (for nourishment/medication); similarly, milk enters the body via enema.
He equates the two 'illas.
4
Perform wajh al-farq (point of distinction)
The Hanafi response distinguishes the 'illas: fasting's 'illa is general bodily maintenance (islah al-badan), which occurs via enema; fosterage's 'illa is specific growth (nashw), which only occurs via oral ingestion. Since enema does not produce growth, the analogy fails.
This is a methodological example of talil and qiyas with rebuttal.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The passage indicates the relied-upon position is 'the apparent' (Zahir al-Riwayah). Conclude that the enema does not establish fosterage prohibition.
Imam Muhammad's view is from the Nawadir (rare transmissions).

And if a boy is given an enema with milk, prohibition does not attach to it. And from Muhammad that prohibition is established by it, just as fasting is broken by it. And the face of difference according to the apparent (narration) is that the spoiler in fasting is the improvement of the body, and that is found in medicine. As for the prohibitor in fosterage, it is the meaning of growth, and that is not found in enema because the nourishing thing arrives from above.

Commentary
This passage deals with the ruling of administering milk via enema (huqnah) to an infant. The relied-upon position (Zahir al-Riwayah) is that fosterage prohibition is not established because the milk does not reach the stomach via the natural route of ingestion. Growth and nourishment occur through the upper tract. Imam Muhammad analogizes to fasting: just as an enema breaks the fast, milk via enema should create prohibition. However, the difference (wajh al-farq) is that in fasting, the invalidating factor is the entry of anything into the body for nourishment or medication, whereas in fosterage, the effective cause is growth and development (nashw), which requires ingestion through the mouth. Since enema bypasses the mouth, the prohibition does not attach. This is a clear application of Hanafi usul: identifying the 'illa (effective cause) and restricting the ruling to cases where the 'illa is fully present.

Key Terms
احتقان (iḥtiqān): Administration of liquid medicine or milk via enema into the lower body.
نشو (nashw): Growth and development; the effective cause for fosterage prohibition.
اصلاح البدن (iṣlāḥ al-badan): Improvement or maintenance of the body; the 'illa for invalidation of fasting via enema.
