Navigating the Table of Contents (Fihrist) of Ashraf al-Hidaya
Table of contents listing topics and page numbers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate any given topic in the nikah section by using the table of contents (fihrist) of Ashraf al-Hidaya, volume 4.

Core Idea
The fihrist is your map; knowing how to use it saves time and deepens your study.

This lesson introduces the fihrist (table of contents) of volume 4 of Ashraf al-Hidaya, an Urdu commentary on al-Hidaya. The fihrist lists all major topics in the nikah (marriage) chapter with their page numbers. Mastering this tool will help you efficiently navigate the book and locate specific rulings when studying Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the topic you need
Determine the specific fiqh issue you are studying (e.g., prohibition of combining a woman and her paternal aunt in marriage).
The fihrist groups related topics under broad headings.
2
Scan the fihrist for the heading
Look through the list of headings in the fihrist. The headings are in Urdu and use key fiqh terms.
Page numbers are given for each heading.
3
Note the page number
Once you find the heading, note the page number next to it.
Page numbers may appear as digits or Urdu numerals; check the footer of the book pages.
4
Turn to that page
Navigate to the indicated page in the book. The discussion of the ruling will be there.
If the page number is garbled (as in OCR), use adjacent headings to estimate.

Navigating the Nikah and Mahr Fihrist of Ashraf al-Hidayah
Table of contents for the Nikah and Mahr chapter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the fihrist (table of contents) of Ashraf al-Hidayah Vol. 4 to identify the main topics on marriage and dower rulings and locate specific rulings by page number.

Core Idea
The fihrist is your roadmap to the Hanafi rulings on marriage and dower – use it to navigate.

This passage is the table of contents (fihrist) for the Nikah and Mahr chapter of Ashraf al-Hidayah, the Urdu commentary on al-Hidayah. While it does not contain detailed legal proofs, it provides a bird's‑eye view of the rulings covered. Familiarising yourself with the fihrist helps you efficiently locate the discussion on any specific issue.

Guided Path
1
Recognise the structure
The fihrist is a list of topic headings, each summarizing a legal scenario and its ruling. Some entries include page numbers (e.g., '111', '112', '127') to direct you to the full discussion.
The headings are not complete rulings – they are pointers.
2
Scan for major themes
Look for recurring ideas: rulings on mahr mithl, disputes over dower, marriage of slaves, fasid marriage, and conditions in marriage. This gives you the scope of the chapter.
Themes include 'Mahr Mithl', 'Nikah al-Raqiq', etc.
3
Identify a specific ruling
Pick an entry that interests you (e.g., 'یک سال کی خدمت یا تعلیم قرآن کو مہر مقرر کرنے کا حکم'). Note its page number if given; otherwise, it will appear in the main text sequentially.
Use the page numbers to jump directly to the discussion.
4
Connect to the main text
Understand that each fihrist entry corresponds to a detailed analysis in the main commentary. The fihrist is not a substitute for the full text but a navigation aid.
When studying, first consult the fihrist, then read the main passage.

The Placement of Kitab al-Talaq in al-Hidayah
Explains placement of divorce after marriage and fosterage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author of al-Hidayah placed the chapter on divorce after marriage and fosterage, using the principles of temporal priority and strength of ruling.

Core Idea
Divorce follows marriage in existence and fosterage in strength of prohibition.

In Islamic legal texts, the order of chapters is not arbitrary. The author of al-Hidayah deliberately placed the rulings of divorce after those of marriage and fosterage. This lesson unpacks the rationale: marriage is prior in existence, and fosterage creates a stronger prohibition. Understanding this ordering helps you appreciate the systematic thinking behind classical fiqh works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two relationships explained in the passage
The passage gives reasons for placing divorce after marriage and after fosterage. Identify each relationship separately.
One is temporal, the other is based on legal effect.
2
Analyze the temporal priority argument
Why does marriage come before divorce? Because marriage exists first and divorce terminates it. Teaching should follow the natural sequence.
This reflects the principle: al-ashya' bi-asliha.
3
Analyze the strength of prohibition argument
Both fosterage and divorce cause prohibition of marriage, but fosterage's prohibition is permanent (mu'abbad) while divorce's is temporary (ghayr mu'abbad). The stronger ruling takes precedence.
This follows the fiqh maxim: al-asabba yuqaddam 'ala al-asab.
4

This exercise reinforces the method of ordering by priority.

Definition of Yameen and Order of Immediate and Suspended Divorce
Introduces chapter, defines yameen, and justifies presentation order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term yameen as used in the Hanafi school and state the two reasons why immediate divorce precedes suspended divorce in al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
Yameen means strength; in divorce it covers oaths and conditional statements. The root (asl) always precedes the branch (far').

This opening of the chapter on oaths in divorce (bab al-aiman fi al-talaq) sets the stage by defining the key term 'yameen' and explaining the logical order of topics. Understanding the root-branch relationship helps you see the structure of Hanafi divorce law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The passage defines yameen. Note its linguistic meaning (strength) and its technical usages: the right hand, an oath by Allah, and a conditional statement.
The poetic quote illustrates the linguistic sense of power.
2
Classify the usages of yameen
Distinguish between yameen as an oath by Allah (hiff billah) and as a conditional statement involving divorce (talaq mu'allaq).
The text states yameen is applied to both.
3
Determine the intended usage in this chapter
In this context, yameen refers to suspended divorce (talaq mu'allaq) where divorce is conditioned on a future event.
The author says 'here that is what is meant'.
4
Analyze the author's rationale for presentation order
Two reasons: (1) immediate divorce (talaq munajjaz) is the root (asl) and suspended divorce is the branch (far'), and (2) immediate divorce is simple (mufrad) while suspended divorce is compound (murakkab).
The rule is that the root precedes the branch, and the simple precedes the compound.
5
Apply the principle generally
Recognize that the same asl-far' logic appears throughout Hanafi fiqh: simpler cases are taught before complex ones.
This is a teaching methodology, not just a rule for divorce.

Therefore, immediate divorce was mentioned first and suspended divorce later. The second reason is that suspended divorce is composed of divorce and a conditional particle, while immediate divorce is simple. And the rule is that the simple is presented before the compound. Therefore, immediate divorce was mentioned first and suspended divorce later.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the chapter on oaths in divorce (bab al-aiman fi al-talaq). It first defines the term 'yameen' linguistically (strength) and technically (oath by Allah, and also conditional statements). The author then explains why the chapter proceeds with immediate divorce first and suspended divorce later, giving two reasons: (1) immediate divorce is the root (asl) and suspended divorce is the branch (far'), so the root precedes; (2) immediate divorce is simple (mufrad) while suspended divorce is compound (murakkab) of divorce and a conditional particle, and the simple precedes the compound. This sets the stage for the detailed rulings that follow.

Key Terms
یمین (yameen): In this passage, yameen is defined as an oath or conditional statement that expresses strength and is used in oaths by Allah or conditional divorce.
طلاق منجز (talaq munajjaz): Immediate divorce that takes effect at the moment of utterance without any condition.
طلاق معلق (talaq mu'allaq): Suspended divorce, where the divorce is conditioned upon a future event.
