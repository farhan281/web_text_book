The Obligation of 'Iddah After Divorce in All Khalwah Scenarios
Explains 'iddah obligation even in irregular khalwah due to caution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that 'iddah is wajib after divorce in all khalwah cases and explain the proof based on istihsan and the hadith.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, 'iddah is obligatory in every khalwah case due to caution and the possibility of pregnancy, even without actual intercourse.

This lesson expands on the ruling of 'iddah after divorce when a couple has had some form of seclusion (khalwah). The Hanafi school takes a cautious approach, requiring 'iddah even in cases where actual intercourse did not occur, because of the reasonable possibility (wahm) of pregnancy. This is a departure from strict analogy (qiyas) and is based on istihsan (juristic preference) to protect the rights of the Shari'ah and the child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is the observance of 'iddah by a woman after divorce when there has been khalwah (valid, invalid, or with a man lacking the organ). The ruling is that 'iddah is wajib (obligatory).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites the hadith: 'Whoever believes in Allah and the Last Day should not water another's field with his water.' This hadith is used to establish the child's right to clear lineage, which 'iddah preserves.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but still binding in fiqh.
3
Understand the basis of istihsan
The ruling is contrary to qiyas because qiyas would not require 'iddah without confirmed intercourse. However, istihsan (juristic preference) overrides qiyas due to caution (ihtiyat) and the possibility (wahm) of pregnancy. The wahm arises from potential semen transfer during friction or insertion.
4
Analyze the non-acceptability of denial
Even if both spouses agree that no intercourse occurred, their statement is not accepted to drop 'iddah. Why? Because 'iddah is the right of the Shari'ah (a divine obligation) and the right of the child (to a clear lineage). The rule is that one's statement is not accepted to nullify the right of another.
Contrast with mahr: mahr is mere wealth, so the couple's denial is accepted for mahr.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school does not consider wahm alone sufficient for 'iddah; they require actual intercourse or its clear possibility. Thus, in invalid or mafqud al-dhikr khalwah, they do not impose 'iddah.

Key Terms
عدت ('iddah): A waiting period incumbent upon a woman after divorce or death of husband to ensure the womb is clear of pregnancy.
وہم (wahm): A reasonable possibility based on external factors that may lead to pregnancy, even if actual intercourse did not occur.
خلوت (khalwah): Seclusion between spouses in a place where no third party can see them, which legally can substitute for intercourse in certain rulings.

Barriers to Capability and Their Effect on 'Iddah Obligation
Distinguishes Shar'i and real barriers for 'iddah obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Shar'i and real barriers to capability and determine whether 'iddah is obligatory in each case.

Core Idea
A Shar'i barrier does not negate actual capability for intercourse, so 'iddah remains wajib; a real barrier negates capability, so no 'iddah is required.

This lesson focuses on a key detail from Quduri's commentary: the type of barrier that prevents intercourse during khalwah. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a barrier imposed by the Shari'ah (like menstruation or post-childbirth bleeding) and a real physical barrier (like illness or childhood). The ruling on 'iddah depends on whether actual capability for intercourse exists at the time of khalwah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of barrier
Determine whether the barrier preventing intercourse is Shar'i (legal, like hayd or nifas) or real (physical, like severe illness or the husband being a minor incapable of intercourse).
The barrier is evaluated at the time of khalwah, not afterwards.
2
Evaluate capability for intercourse
For Shar'i barriers: despite the legal impediment, actual capability for intercourse exists (the couple could have intercourse; they are just prohibited by Shari'ah). For real barriers: actual capability is absent (the husband cannot physically perform intercourse).
The Arabic term used is 'qudrah' (capability).
3
Determine the existence of wahm
If actual capability exists (even with a Shar'i barrier), then there is a reasonable possibility (wahm) that semen could reach the womb, so 'iddah is wajib. If actual capability is absent (real barrier), then no wahm exists, so 'iddah is not wajib.
Wahm is the basis for caution.
4
Apply the ruling
If a Shar'i barrier was present during khalwah, 'iddah is wajib post-divorce. If a real barrier was present, 'iddah is not wajib.
Example: a woman in menses who has a valid khalwah and then divorces must observe 'iddah. A severely ill husband who divorces after khalwah does not impose 'iddah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on fosterage prohibition when milk is mixed with water and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, fosterage prohibition depends on milk being the dominant component; mere presence is insufficient.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: a child drinks a mixture of milk and water. Does fosterage prohibition apply? The Hanafis and Shafi'is differ based on how they view the legal existence of the subordinate component in a mixture. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the proof structure behind the Hanafi position.

Guided Path

State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling as stated in the matn: prohibition attaches if milk is dominant; if water is dominant, prohibition does not attach.

This lesson examines a key passage from al-'Inayah that clarifies the meaning of 'haydah' in the hadith of istibra'.
