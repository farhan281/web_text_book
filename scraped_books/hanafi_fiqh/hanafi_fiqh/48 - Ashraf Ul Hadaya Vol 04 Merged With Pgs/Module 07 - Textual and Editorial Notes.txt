Testimony for Fosterage vs Meat: Why Two Witnesses?
Ruling with proof via qiyas khafi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school distinguishes testimony requirements for fosterage (rada') and meat prohibition using the criterion of ownership removal.

Core Idea
Fosterage nullifies marriage, needing two witnesses; meat prohibition leaves ownership intact, so one suffices.

This passage from the Ashraf al-Hidayah commentary clarifies a key evidentiary distinction: why proving fosterage (rada') requires two witnesses while proving meat is forbidden requires only one. The reasoning hinges on the legal effect of each prohibition. Fosterage creates a permanent prohibition that invalidates the marriage contract itself, a family law matter demanding stronger proof. Meat prohibition, by contrast, does not remove ownership; it is a purely religious matter, for which one male witness is sufficient.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Rulings
Note the two scenarios: (1) proving fosterage that creates permanent prohibition (hurmah muabbadah), and (2) proving that meat is forbidden (e.g., from unslaughtered animal).
The question: why different witness requirements?
2
Analyze the Legal Effect of Fosterage
Fosterage nullifies marriage (nikah). Since marriage is a contract, its nullity must be proven with strong evidence: two male witnesses or one male plus two females.
This is because the prohibition dissolves the contract itself.
3
Compare with Meat Prohibition
Meat prohibition does not remove ownership. Ownership can coexist with prohibition (e.g., a Muslim owning wine, or owning dirt). The testimony only concerns the religious (dini) prohibition, not a contractual change.
Thus, the evidence needed is lighter.
4
Apply the Earlier Principle
Recall that for religious matters (umur diniyya), a single male witness suffices. This principle is applied here.
This principle was established earlier in the text.
5
Formulate the Hanafi Position
Thus, rada' requires two witnesses (or equivalent); meat prohibition requires one witness. The distinction is based on whether the prohibition affects ownership or not.
This is mufta bihi according to later authorities.

Book of Fosterage. Ashraf al-Hidayah, Urdu commentary of Hidayah, volume four. The marriage is established, and the ownership of marriage remains. Because with permanent prohibition, continuance of marriage is not possible. So when prohibition is established due to fosterage, the marriage also becomes null. And to prove the nullity of marriage, testimony is necessary, i.e., two witnesses. Therefore we said that two men or one man and two women should testify. In contrast to meat, because the prohibition of eating something can be separate from removal of ownership. That is, it is possible that eating something becomes prohibited but its ownership does not cease; rather it remains. The conclusion is that prohibition of consumption and ownership can coexist. For example, a disbeliever owns wine. Then this disbeliever becomes Muslim. For this Muslim, drinking that wine is forbidden while his ownership remains. Similarly, a person owns dirt, so eating dirt is forbidden for him while his ownership remains. So similarly, this meat is forbidden for the buyer, but his ownership is not removed. In that case, the testimony is only regarding the prohibition of meat, not regarding removal of ownership. And prohibition is a religious matter. It has been known earlier that for a religious matter, the testimony of one person is sufficient. Therefore, by the report of one person, this meat becomes forbidden. And Allah knows best. Jamil Ahmad Sukkardawi. O Allah, forgive its writer, his parents, and whoever endeavored in it.

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi legal analysis distinguishing the testimony requirements for proving fosterage (rada') versus proving that meat is forbidden. The passage is a ruling with proof, using rational comparison (qiyas) to explain the difference. The author, writing in Urdu, explains that when fosterage creates a permanent prohibition (hurmah muabbadah) affecting the marriage itself, the nullity of marriage requires proof through two male witnesses or one male and two females. This is because the prohibition dissolves the marriage contract (nikah). In contrast, the prohibition of eating meat (e.g., from an animal not properly slaughtered) does not remove ownership. Ownership can remain even if consumption is forbidden. Examples: a Muslim who owns wine cannot drink it but still owns it; a person who owns dirt cannot eat it but still owns it. Since the testimony in the meat case is only about a religious prohibition (hurmah), and not about ownership, a single witness (male) is sufficient. This follows the earlier principle that religious matters require a single witness. The passage is from a later commentary (Ashraf al-Hidayah), so the authority weight is from the later works (fatawa mutakhkhirin).

Key Terms
رضاع (rada'): Fosterage; the act of breastfeeding that creates a permanent prohibition of marriage between the child and the wet nurse's relatives.
حرمت موبدہ (hurmah muabbadah): Permanent prohibition; a prohibition that lasts forever, such as between foster relatives.
شہادت (shahadah): Testimony; legal evidence given by witnesses in court.
زوال ملک (zawal al-milk): Removal of ownership; the legal effect when ownership is terminated.
