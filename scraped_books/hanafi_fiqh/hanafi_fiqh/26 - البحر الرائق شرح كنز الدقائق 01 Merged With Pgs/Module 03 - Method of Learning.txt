The Definition of Fiqh and Its Three Conditions
Commentary on the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of fiqh and identify which types of knowledge are excluded by each condition (ahkam, shar'iyyah, amaliyyah).

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of practical Shar'iah rulings—not rational, sensory, or doctrinal matters.

The classic Hanafi definition of fiqh is precise: 'Knowledge of practical Shar'iah rulings derived from detailed evidence.' Each word carries weight. In this lesson, we dissect how the three restrictions—rulings, Shar'iah, and practical—draw sharp boundaries, ensuring fiqh is distinguished from theology, rational sciences, and mere belief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition components
Write down the full definition: 'Knowledge of practical Shar'iah rulings derived from detailed evidence.' Note the three qualifiers: 'ahkam' (rulings), 'shar'iyyah' (Shar'iah), and 'amaliyyah' (practical).
The definition appears early in the passage; the rest is commentary.
2
Apply the first qualifier: 'ahkam'
Identify what is excluded by the word 'ahkam'. It restricts fiqh to rulings, excluding mere knowledge of essences, attributes, or actions of objects (e.g., knowing that fire burns or that the sky is blue).
Rulings involve a relation (e.g., obligation, prohibition) between an action and a subject.
3
Apply the second qualifier: 'shar'iyyah'
This excludes rational, sensory, or conventional rulings. For example, the rational judgment that the world is originated, or the linguistic rule that the subject is in the nominative case, are not fiqh.
Shar'iyyah means derived from Islamic revelation, not from reason, sense, or custom.
4
Apply the third qualifier: 'amaliyyah'
This excludes purely doctrinal (i'tiqadi) matters such as the existence of Paradise or the beatific vision. Fiqh concerns the modality of action—what to do, not what to merely believe.
'Amaliyyah' includes heart actions like intention (niyyah) because they relate to modality of practice, but not abstract belief.
5
Handle the edge case: well-known rulings
The passage addresses an objection: if a ruling is well-known (e.g., prayer is obligatory), is it still fiqh? The answer is yes, because it is still derived from detailed evidence (Quran and Hadith), even if now obvious.
This ensures fiqh includes both obscure and obvious rulings as long as they are practical and Shar'iah.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the definition of fiqh and the term 'hukm' (ruling) used in that definition. The author, Zayn al-Din al-Razi (?) in Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, explains the nuances of the classic definition: fiqh is knowledge of practical Shar'iah rulings derived from detailed evidence. The text begins by discussing the meaning of 'knowledge' and 'readiness' for rulings, using references to various works like Al-Tawdih, Al-Talwih, and Al-Tahrir. It then addresses a dispute: some, like al-Sayyid, hold that 'hukm' in the definition refers to 'tasdiq' (affirmation/judgment), but others argue that tasdiq is knowledge of a relation, which would make fiqh knowledge of religious sciences—but that is not the intention. The correct view, according to the verifiers (al-muhaqqiqun), is that 'hukm' here does NOT mean Allah's address (khitab) concerning the actions of the obligated, because that would make the qualifiers 'shar'iyyah' and 'amaliyyah' redundant. Instead, hukm refers to the complete relation between two things, knowledge of which is tasdiq. The passage then explains the three restrictive conditions in the definition: 'ahkam' excludes knowledge of essences, attributes, and actions; 'shar'iyyah' excludes rational, sensory, and conventional matters (e.g., fire burns, subject in nominative case); 'amaliyyah' excludes purely doctrinal matters like the existence of Paradise or seeing Allah in the afterlife. However, matters like intention (niyyah) and prohibition of hypocrisy are included because they relate to the modality of heart action. The text clarifies that knowledge of obligatory acts like prayer is indeed fiqh, even if well-known, because it comes from detailed evidence and pertains to practical action. The commentary by Ibn Qasim and others is cited to distinguish between belief (i'tiqad) as a non-action vs. heart actions. The overall lesson is a rigorous dissection of the definition to ensure it is precise and exhaustive.

Key Terms
الفقه (al-fiqh): Knowledge of practical shar'iah rulings derived from detailed evidence, as per the definition being discussed.
الأحكام (al-ahkam): Rulings; the first qualifier that restricts fiqh to judgments about actions, excluding knowledge of essences, attributes, and actions that are not rulings.
الشرعية (al-shar'iyyah): Relating to the shar'iah; rulings derived from religious texts, excluding rational, sensory, or conventional matters.
العملية (al-amaliyyah): Practical; rulings pertaining to the modality (kayfiyyah) of action, whether of the limbs or the heart. Excludes purely doctrinal (i'tiqadi) matters.

The Meaning of Hukm: Divine Address or Complete Relation?
Commentary on the meaning of hukm in the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi verifiers interpret 'hukm' in the definition of fiqh as the complete relation (nisbah tammah) rather than Allah's address (khitab), and evaluate the implications of both views.

Core Idea
Hukm in the definition of fiqh is the complete relation between two things, not the divine address itself.

A central debate in Hanafi usul is what 'hukm' means within the definition of fiqh. Some, like al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani, say it is tasdiq (affirmation). Others say it is the divine address (khitab). The verifiers (muhaqqiqun) argue it is the complete relation (nisbah tammah). This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each position and shows why the complete relation view is preferred.

Guided Path
1
State the two main views on 'hukm'
First, identify that the passage presents three relevant positions: (1) al-Sayyid: hukm is tasdiq; (2) some earlier scholars: hukm is khitab Allah; (3) the verifiers: hukm is the complete relation. Focus on the contrast between khitab and complete relation.
The passage refutes both tasdiq as awareness and khitab, settling on complete relation.
2
Understand the refutation of the 'khitab' view
The passage argues that if hukm were khitab (divine address), then the qualifiers 'shar'iyyah' and 'amaliyyah' would be redundant, because khitab itself is already from Allah and about actions. This logical redundancy shows the definition would be poorly constructed.
Redundancy in a definition is a serious flaw in usul methodology.
3
Comprehend the 'complete relation' alternative
The verifiers say hukm is the complete relation (nisbah tammah) between two things. For example, the relation of obligation between 'prayer' and 'a Muslim' is a hukm. Knowledge of that relation is tasdiq (affirmation). This avoids redundancy because the relation itself is not the address but the result of derivation.
The complete relation is the ruling itself, not the speech act that indicates it.
4
Trace the dispute on 'tasdiq'
Al-Sayyid says hukm is tasdiq, but the passage refutes this: tasdiq is knowledge of a relation, so that would make fiqh 'knowledge of religious sciences', which is too broad. However, a response accepts 'tasdiq' if it means the proposition (qadiyyah), not the mental act.
This subtlety shows the precision required in usul terminology.
5
Identify the thamarah (practical consequence)
The difference matters because if hukm is khitab, then fiqh would be directly knowledge of Allah's speech, which is impossible since not all rulings are explicitly in the texts. If it is the complete relation, then fiqh encompasses derived rulings, which is the actual work of the faqih.
The thamarah determines whether fiqh includes ijtihad-derived rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the definition of fiqh and the term 'hukm' (ruling) used in that definition. The author, Zayn al-Din al-Razi (?) in Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, explains the nuances of the classic definition: fiqh is knowledge of practical Shar'iah rulings derived from detailed evidence. The text begins by discussing the meaning of 'knowledge' and 'readiness' for rulings, using references to various works like Al-Tawdih, Al-Talwih, and Al-Tahrir. It then addresses a dispute: some, like al-Sayyid, hold that 'hukm' in the definition refers to 'tasdiq' (affirmation/judgment), but others argue that tasdiq is knowledge of a relation, which would make fiqh knowledge of religious sciences—but that is not the intention. The correct view, according to the verifiers (al-muhaqqiqun), is that 'hukm' here does NOT mean Allah's address (khitab) concerning the actions of the obligated, because that would make the qualifiers 'shar'iyyah' and 'amaliyyah' redundant. Instead, hukm refers to the complete relation between two things, knowledge of which is tasdiq. The passage then explains the three restrictive conditions in the definition: 'ahkam' excludes knowledge of essences, attributes, and actions; 'shar'iyyah' excludes rational, sensory, and conventional matters (e.g., fire burns, subject in nominative case); 'amaliyyah' excludes purely doctrinal matters like the existence of Paradise or seeing Allah in the afterlife. However, matters like intention (niyyah) and prohibition of hypocrisy are included because they relate to the modality of heart action. The text clarifies that knowledge of obligatory acts like prayer is indeed fiqh, even if well-known, because it comes from detailed evidence and pertains to practical action. The commentary by Ibn Qasim and others is cited to distinguish between belief (i'tiqad) as a non-action vs. heart actions. The overall lesson is a rigorous dissection of the definition to ensure it is precise and exhaustive.

Key Terms
الحكم (في تعريف الفقه) (al-hukm): The complete relation (nisbah tammah) between two things, knowledge of which is tasdiq. Not the divine address.
التصديق (al-tasdiq): Affirmation; in this context, it is either the mental act of judging or the proposition. The Hanafis restrict it to definitive awareness.
النسبة التامة (al-nisbah al-tammah): The complete relation between two things, such as the relation of obligation between an act and a subject.
خطاب الله (khitab Allah): The divine address; Quran and Sunnah speech.

Refining the Definition of Fiqh: Including Heart Actions
Defining fiqh and refining 'عمليات' to include heart actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi refinement of the term 'عمليات' in the definition of fiqh and distinguish between heart actions and mere beliefs.

Core Idea
Fiqh concerns the modality of action, not just beliefs—heart actions are included, theoretical beliefs are not.

The definition of fiqh is carefully crafted to include all practical rulings while excluding purely theoretical matters. But what about actions of the heart, like intention? Are they 'practical'? This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq shows how Hanafi scholars refined the term 'عمليات' (practical) to ensure that rulings about heart actions are included, while beliefs like the existence of Paradise remain outside fiqh. Understanding this refinement is key to grasping the precise scope of fiqh in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
State the standard definition
Recall the definition: 'Knowledge of the practical legal rulings derived from their detailed evidences.' Focus on the word 'practical' (العملية).
The definition comes from Kanz al-Daqa'iq.
2
Identify the two possible interpretations of 'practical'
Interpretation 1: limb actions only. Interpretation 2: both limb and heart actions. Each leads to a problem: either the definition is not comprehensive (heart actions excluded) or not exclusive (beliefs included).
This is the core objection the text addresses.
3
Analyze the Hanafi solution
The solution is to interpret 'practical' as 'pertaining to the modality of action' (kayfiyyah 'amal). Thus, rulings about heart actions (e.g., obligation of intention) concern the modality of a heart action, while rulings about beliefs (e.g., Paradise exists) concern existence, not action modality.
This refinement is attributed to Jalal al-Mahalli and supported by Ibn al-Humam.
4
Distinguish heart actions from assent (taṣdīq)
Heart actions like intention are acts of will (irādah), whereas assent (taṣdīq) is a type of knowledge ('ilm) and not an action in the true sense. Assent may be called an action metaphorically in the context of faith.
This distinction preserves the exclusion of beliefs from fiqh.
5
Note the alternative substitution
Some scholars replaced 'practical' with 'subsidiary' (الفرعية) to avoid the objection entirely, but the refinement of 'practical' remains the relied-upon position.
This shows the flexibility within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of the definition of fiqh as found in Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq begins by analyzing the restriction 'practical' (al-'amaliyyah) in the definition: 'knowledge of the practical legal rulings derived from their detailed evidences.' He explains that this restriction excludes theoretical beliefs (like that consensus is a proof or faith is obligatory), but then addresses an important objection: if 'action' means only limb actions, then knowledge of heart actions like intention and prohibition of envy would be excluded, making the definition incomplete. If 'action' includes both heart and limb, then belief itself might enter, making the definition overbroad. The solution adopted is that 'practical' means 'pertaining to the modality of action' (kayfiyyah 'amal). Thus, rulings about heart actions (like obligation of intention) are included because they concern the modality of a heart action, while rulings about mere beliefs (like that Paradise exists) are excluded because they concern the existence of something, not a modality of action. The passage further distinguishes between a heart action (like intention, which is a type of will/irādah) and assent (taṣdīq, which is a kind of knowledge/'ilm). The latter is not an action in the true sense, though it may be called an action metaphorically. The discussion also touches on the knowledge of the muqallid (blind follower) and why it is excluded from the definition: fiqh requires knowledge derived from examining evidence, not merely accepting authority. The passage concludes by noting that the definition must not exclude obvious matters like the obligation of prayer and fasting, which the Companions knew necessarily, implying that such knowledge is indeed fiqh. This supports the position of Ibn al-Humam who refined the definition.

Key Terms
فق (fiqh): Knowledge of the practical legal rulings derived from their detailed evidences.
عملية (ʿamaliyyah): Practical, meaning pertaining to the modality of action (kayfiyyah ʿamal), whether limb or heart action.
تصدیق (taṣdīq): Assent; a type of knowledge or unveiling that occurs after proof, not an action of the soul.
كيفية عمل (kayfiyyat ʿamal): Modality of action; the manner or quality of an action, whether physical or of the heart.

Types of Fard: Qat'i and Zanni
Defining fard qat'i and fard zanni

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between fard qat'i and fard zanni and state the consequences of denying each.

Core Idea
Fard qat'i is from definitive evidence; its denial is kufr. Fard zanni is from probable evidence; its denial is fisq, not kufr.

In Hanafi jurisprudence, not all obligations are equal in certainty. This lesson examines the division of fard into two categories: definitive (qat'i) and speculative (zanni). Understanding this distinction is crucial for appreciating the Hanafi approach to usul al-fiqh and the legal consequences for those who deny or neglect obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of fard
The source text divides fard into fard qat'i (definitive) and fard zanni (speculative). Fard qat'i is established by evidence that leaves no room for doubt (muhkam Quran, mutawatir Sunnah). Fard zanni is established by evidence that is probable (zahir, nass, mashhur).
2
Understand the evidentiary basis
The passage states that fard qat'i cuts off all possibility of doubt ('yaqta' al-ihtimal aslan'), while fard zanni cuts off possibility arising from evidence ('yaqta' al-ihtimal al-nashi' an dalil'). This means the evidence itself allows for some interpretation.
The difference lies in the strength of the proof (dalil).
3
Recognize the consequences of denial
Denying a fard qat'i renders one a kafir (apostate) because it rejects a matter known from the religion by necessity. Denying a fard zanni does not lead to kufr; the denier is considered a fasiq (sinner) due to the presence of doubt (shubhah) in the proof.
The passage emphasizes that even people of innovation are not declared kafir if they have a strong interpretative doubt.
4
Examine examples from the text
The text gives examples: washing the elbows and ankles is fard by consensus (qat'i). Wiping the head (masah) is fard zanni; even the precise measure is subject to ijtihad. The last sitting (qa'dah akhirah) is based on a solitary hadith, making it zanni.
These examples illustrate how different acts have different levels of certainty.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
This classification is unique to the Hanafi school. Other schools (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) do not divide fard into qat'i and zanni; for them, fard is always definitive, and what is established by probable evidence is simply called wajib, considered equally binding.

The Hanafi Distinction between Fard and Wajib
Distinguishing fard from wajib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the epistemological distinction between fard and wajib in Hanafi usul and identify the practical consequences (thamarah) of this distinction.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, fard is established by definitive evidence (qat'i); wajib is established by speculative evidence (zanni). Denial of fard is kufr; denial of wajib is fisq.

In the Hanafi school, the terms 'fard' and 'wajib' are not synonymous. While other schools treat them as equivalent, Hanafis maintain a technical distinction based on the strength of the evidence. This lesson explores the definition of wajib, its relationship to fard, and the practical implications of this classification.

Guided Path
1
Define fard and wajib in Hanafi usul
Fard is what is established by definitive evidence (qat'i). Wajib is what is established by speculative evidence (zanni). The passage quotes Fakhr al-Islam: fard is binding in knowledge and action; wajib is binding only in action, because its proof contains doubt.
This is a core Hanafi principle.
2
Understand the epistemological basis
The certainty of the proof determines the category. If the evidence is qat'i (Quran, mutawatir), the obligation is fard. If it is zanni (zahir, nass, mashhur), the obligation is wajib. This does not affect the requirement to act, but affects the ruling on the denier.
The passage emphasizes that wajib is 'in the position of fard in action, not in knowledge'.
3
Examine the three-tier hierarchy
The text outlines a hierarchy: (1) fard qat'i (definitive), (2) fard zanni which is equivalent in strength to wajib (like masah), and (3) wajib which is below fard but above sunnah (like reciting al-Fatihah). The term 'wajib' is used for the third category, but also sometimes used broadly.
Note that fard zanni is sometimes called wajib, but its denial still does not incur kufr.
4
Analyze examples from the text
Examples: Masah (wiping) is fard zanni/wajib. Reciting al-Fatihah is wajib. Witr is wajib according to Abu Hanifah. The text also shows that the term 'wajib' can be used loosely to refer to what is actually fard in common usage.
The passage from Al-Tasrih clarifies that usage may vary.
5
Contrast with other schools
The Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali schools do not differentiate between fard and wajib; both are equally binding and denial of either is kufr if the evidence is clear. The Hanafi position is unique and is based on recognizing degrees of certainty in the sources.
This difference can lead to different rulings on excommunication (takfir) in matters of obligation.

The Hanafi Definition of Shart: Distinguishing Condition from Cause
Critical refinement of the definition of shart in fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shart (condition) and distinguish it from sabab (cause), and explain why rational and artificial conditions are not considered conditions of prayer.

Core Idea
A condition (shart) is a prerequisite without effect; a cause (sabab) has effective influence.

In Hanafi fiqh, precise terminology is crucial. The term 'shart' (condition) is often confused with 'sabab' (cause). This passage from Fath al-Qadir refines the definition of shart, especially as it applies to the conditions of prayer (shurut al-salah). It critiques earlier commentators who attempted to exclude rational and artificial conditions, showing that their definition fails, and clarifies that the shurut al-salah are purely practical prerequisites, not rational or artificial ones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definition of Shart under Critique
The earlier commentators defined shart as 'something that precedes the conditioned thing.' They intended to exclude rational conditions (e.g., life for pain) and artificial conditions (e.g., entering a house for divorce). Understand this initial definition and its intended exclusions.
The exclusion of rational and artificial conditions is an attempt to narrow the scope.
2
Analyze the Critique of the Exclusion
Fath al-Qadir argues that both rational and artificial conditions do precede their effects (life precedes pain; entering the house precedes divorce), so they are not excluded by the 'precedence' criterion. Recognize that the definition fails to exclude what it intended.
The critique hinges on the fact that precedence is satisfied for all types.
3
Distinguish Shart from Sabab
The text distinguishes shart from sabab: a sabab (like pronouncing divorce) has its effect delayed until the condition is met, but the sabab itself is effective in initiating the process. A shart is merely something upon which the existence of the effect depends, without contributing to it directly. Grasp this key difference.
In Hanafi usul, sabab has ta'athir (effect), shart does not.
4
Apply the Definition to Shurut al-Salah
The passage notes that the phrase 'conditions of prayer' is a restricted usage. There is no artificial condition for prayer (like entering a house for divorce). Rational conditions like life are not discussed because fiqh deals only with practical matters. Also, conditions for exiting prayer (like sitting) are conditions for a different act, not for prayer itself. So calling them 'conditions of prayer' is metaphorical.
The proper scope: shurut al-salah are only those prerequisites directly related to the validity of prayer, such as wudu, facing qiblah, etc.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi Position
Thus, the correct Hanafi definition: shart is that upon which something else depends, without having any effect on it. In fiqh, it applies only to practical prerequisites within the chapter being discussed, not to rational or artificial conditions.
This refined definition avoids the pitfalls of the earlier attempt.

Cause. His saying: (And what the commentators mentioned etc.) He said in Fath al-Qadir: This explanation is given, and it is said to exclude the rational condition like life for pain, and the artificial condition like entering the house for divorce, and it is said to exclude what does not precede it like sitting is a condition for exiting and arrangement of what is not legislated repeatedly is a condition for remaining in validity. According to the second, the condition is either rational or other, and it is preceded, so the restriction of precedence does not exclude rational and artificial conditions because life and entering the house are certainly prior to pain and the occurrence of divorce. It cannot be said that the artificial condition is for the occurrence of the suspended matter, for the condition only affects the opposite. The condition is that upon which something else depends, with no effect of its own, except that it is called condition linguistically. But we refute that; rather the cause (sabab) is his saying 'You are divorced' and its action is delayed until the existence of the artificial condition, so it is true that it depends on it but it is not effective; thus the first is determined. And because his saying 'which precede it' is a restriction on the conditions of prayer, not on absolute condition, and there is no artificial condition for prayer, and it is far-fetched to exclude its rational condition like life, etc., because the book is for explaining practical matters, so others are not considered. And the condition for exiting and remaining in validity are not conditions for prayer but for something else, namely exiting and remaining. Rather, it is permissible to say 'condition of prayer' through a kind of metaphorical usage, applying the name of the whole to the part and to the adjacent description.

Commentary
This passage from Fath al-Qadir critiques the definition of 'shart' (condition) given by earlier commentators. They had defined it as something that precedes the conditioned thing, intending to exclude rational conditions (like life preceding pain) and artificial conditions (like entering a house preceding divorce). However, the author argues that both rational and artificial conditions do precede their effects, so they are not excluded. He then clarifies that the term 'shart' in the context of shurut al-salah (conditions of prayer) is restricted to practical matters of fiqh, not rational or artificial ones. The conditions for exiting prayer (e.g., sitting) are not conditions for prayer itself but for a different act. Therefore, calling something a 'condition of prayer' is a metaphorical extension of the term from its proper meaning.

Key Terms
شرط (shart): Something upon which something else depends, but it does not have an effect on it; distinguished from sabab (cause) which does have an effect.
سبب (sabab): A cause that has an effective influence (ta'athir) in bringing about a legal effect, though its action may be delayed until a condition is met.
شرط عقلي (shart 'aqli): A rational condition, like life for pain; not within the scope of fiqh as it deals with practical matters.
شرط جعلي (shart ja'li): An artificial condition, like entering a house for divorce; it is a condition created by human stipulation.
