Fasting Makeup for a Mustahada Who Forgot Her Pattern
Details the number of fasting makeup days for a mustahada who forgot her pattern.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the number of qada days for a mustahada who has forgotten her menstrual pattern, applying the Hanafi principle of ihtiyat (precaution).

Core Idea
A mustahada must make up double the maximum possible missed days to ensure certainty.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the detailed calculations for a woman with chronic uterine bleeding (mustahada) who has forgotten her habitual menses pattern. Since she cannot be sure which days she was pure, the Hanafi school requires her to make up extra days beyond the actual missed ones, as a precaution (ihtiyat). The ruling depends on whether she remembers the time of onset (night or day) and whether she knows her cycle length.

Guided Path
1
Identify knowledge state
Determine if she knows when her menses used to begin (night or day) and if she knows the length of her cycle. The answer depends on these variables.
Three basic cases: knows onset at night, knows onset at day, or does not know.
2
Compute maximum invalid days
Calculate the worst-case number of days her fast could have been invalidated due to menses. For night onset: 10 days; day onset: 11 days; unknown: consider both possibilities.
Hanafi rule: menses does not exceed 10 days.
3
Apply ihtiyat double
Multiply the maximum possible invalid days by two as a precaution against the possibility that makeup fasts themselves coincide with menses.
Thus 10×2=20, 11×2=22.
4
Adjust for unknown cycle length
If she does not know the duration of her menses (e.g., 9 or 10 days), additional sub-cases from the commentary apply, such as 8, 15, 20, or 25 days.
The text provides examples for 10-day and 9-day menses with various onset knowledge.
5
Recognize intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
When onset is unknown, the majority say 20 days, but al-Hindwani says 22 days. The author declares al-Hindwani's view as 'al-ashah' (sounder) due to greater ihtiyat.
Mufta bihi is 22 days.

If menses began at night, she makes up twenty days; if daytime, twenty-two days; if unknown, the majority say twenty, but Abu Ja'far al-Hindwani says twenty-two as precaution. The principle is to double the maximum number of days that could have been spoiled due to possible menses during fasting. The passage emphasizes ihtiyat (precaution) to ensure all missed fasts are made up with certainty.

The Precautionary Method in Makeup Fasts: Abu Ja'far's Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on makeup fasting days

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of ihtiyat to determine the required number of makeup fasting days for a woman with an irregular menstrual cycle and distinguish between the positions of the general scholars and Abu Ja'far al-Hindawani.

Core Idea
When certainty is impossible, the precautionary estimate doubles the possible missed days to ensure the obligation is fulfilled.

This lesson examines a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a woman who must make up missed Ramadan fasts due to menstruation but is uncertain about the exact days. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq systematically presents scenarios based on her knowledge of her cycle and the onset time of menstruation. The general scholars (عامة مشايخنا) offer a baseline number, while al-Faqih Abu Ja'far al-Hindawani insists on a higher count to eliminate all doubt. Understanding their reasoning sharpens our grasp of the usul principle of ihtiyat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenarios
Distinguish between knowing the cycle (monthly pattern) versus not, and knowing the onset time (night or day). These factors change the calculation.
2
Apply the General Scholars' Principle
Menstruation max is 10 days per month. If she does not know onset, she makes up 20 days (double 10) because after 10 days of makeup, some might fall in menses.
This assumes the worst case of 10 missed days.
3
Apply Abu Ja'far's Precaution
He considers that onset by day could add one more missed day (11 max), so he doubles that to 22 days for the same scenario.
His reasoning is more cautious.
4
Extend to Unknown Cycle
When the cycle is unknown, more missed days are possible (e.g., up to 16), leading to 25 or more days. The same logic of doubling the maximum possible applies.
The numbers vary based on connected vs. separated makeup.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference between the two opinions: the number of required makeup days differs by 2 days in the basic scenario, and by larger margins in complex cases.
The text prefers Abu Ja'far's view as الأصح.

Qada Days for Uncertain Hayd Start: The 33/37 vs 32/38 Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute over qada numbers for unknown hayd start

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the initial calculation of 33 (connected) and 37 (separated) days, identify why a correction to 32 and 38 is proposed, and evaluate the reasoning behind the correction.

Core Idea
The first day of Shawwal cannot be a fasting day, so the qada count must exclude it — reducing 33 to 32 and increasing 37 to 38.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the author discusses the qada of Ramadan fasts for a woman who does not know whether her menstruation began at night or during the day. He first presents a calculation from an earlier source, which yields 33 days if she fasts consecutively (wasl) and 37 if she fasts non-consecutively (fasl). However, a later scholar ('some virtuous person') corrects this to 32 and 38, noting that the first day of Shawwal is the day of Eid and cannot be a fasting day. The author suspects an omission in the original text and consults al-Tatarkhaniyya. This lesson examines this initial dispute and the reasoning behind the correction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial calculation
The text states: if she fasts connected (wasl), she makes up 33 days; if separated (fasl), 37 days. Note the components: 14 valid, 11 invalid, 1 valid day at the end for wasl; for fasl, a different pattern leads to 37.
These numbers assume a specific pattern of menstruation within the 30-day Ramadan.
2
Recognize the issue with the first day of Shawwal
The first day of Shawwal is the Eid al-Fitr, a day when fasting is prohibited. Therefore, a woman cannot begin her qada on that day, which affects the count.
This is a fixed rule: fasting on Eid is haram.
3
Analyze the correction
The virtuous person corrects to 32 (wasl) and 38 (fasl). For wasl, the count drops by one because the first day cannot be used; for fasl, the count rises by one because the total days of fasting increase to cover the missed first day in the separated scheme.
The correction is attributed to al-Sadr al-Shahid as quoted in Maqsad al-Talib.
4
Understand the author's response
The author notes that the original expression likely had an omission or distortion; the correct context is when she knows her menses began during the day. He then consults al-Tatarkhaniyya to confirm.
This shows the Hanafi method of verifying transmitted texts.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The qada count depends on whether the woman knows her hayd began at night or day, with the precautionary principle taking the maximum possible days.

In the previous lesson, we saw the initial dispute over the qada count. Here, we delve into the author's resolution by consulting al-Tatarkhaniyya and the summary of Muhammad al-Birkawi. The key is distinguishing whether the woman knows her hayd began at night or during the day. The precautionary principle (ihtiyat) dictates taking the largest possible count to ensure all missed fasts are covered. This lesson will equip you to apply the correct ruling based on the woman's knowledge and the length of Ramadan.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish night-start vs day-start
The ruling differs fundamentally. If she knows her hayd began at night, the qada is fewer days (20/24); if during the day, more days (32/38). This changes the base calculation.
The original text's omission caused confusion; al-Tatarkhaniyya clarifies.
2
Apply the length of Ramadan
Ramadan can be 29 or 30 days. The qada count adjusts slightly. For example, with day-start and Ramadan 30: wasl 32, fasl 38; with Ramadan 29: wasl 32, fasl 37.
This shows meticulous accounting for calendar variation.
3
Understand the precautionary justification
The numbers are derived by considering all possible menstrual patterns (e.g., 5+9, 10+4, mid-cycle) and taking the maximum qada required. This is ihtiyat.
Ihtiyat ensures the woman's obligation is certainly fulfilled.
4
Use al-Birkawi's summary as a reference

Later Hanafi works often compile such summaries for easy reference.
