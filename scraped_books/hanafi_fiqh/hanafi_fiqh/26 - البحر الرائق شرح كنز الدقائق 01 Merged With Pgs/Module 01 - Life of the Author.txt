Biographical introduction to the author and his scholarly credentials

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the scholarly profile of Ibn Nujaym, including his teachers, students, spiritual path, and major works.

Core Idea
Ibn Nujaym mastered both sacred law and the Sufi path, leaving works like al-Bahr and al-Ashbah that still guide Hanafi practice.

This lesson introduces you to the life and legacy of Zayn al-Din ibn Nujaym, the prolific Hanafi jurist and author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. The passage highlights his comprehensive Islamic training, his role as both a teacher of fiqh and a seeker of spiritual purification, and the enduring influence of his scholarly output. Understanding his biography helps contextualize his authoritative works within the Hanafi tradition.

Among them: The biography of the author of al-Bahr. He is the Imam, the great scholar, Shaykh Zayn ibn Ibrahim ibn Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Bakr, famously known as Ibn Nujaym – a name of some of his ancestors – the distinguished scholar whose like the eyes of the later and earlier generations have not seen. He studied, persevered, became unique, excelled, issued fatwas, taught, and fortune assisted him in his life and after his death. He was blessed with fortune in all his writings and compositions; every paper he wrote caused people to struggle to obtain it. He was born in Cairo in the year 926 AH and studied under its scholars, receiving fiqh from Shaykh Amin al-Din ibn Abd al-Al al-Hanafi, Shaykh Abu al-Fayd al-Sulami, Shaykh Sharaf al-Din al-Bulqini, and Shaykh al-Islam Ahmad ibn Yunus known as Ibn al-Shalabi. He studied Arabic and rational sciences from many others, including: the great scholar Nur al-Din al-Daylami al-Maliki and the great scholar Shuqayr al-Maghribi. Many benefited from him: his brother the scholar Umar (author of al-Nahr), the scholar Muhammad al-Ghazzi al-Tamartashi (author of al-Minhah), Shaykh Muhammad al-Ilmi (grandson of Ibn Abi Sharif, originally from Jerusalem but residing in Sham), and Abd al-Ghaffar (Mufti of Jerusalem). The gnostic Abd al-Wahhab al-Sha'rani mentioned him in his Tabaqat, stating that he was a pious scholar, and all the Sufi poor concurred on his character and majesty; none refused to acknowledge him except one who had envy or ignorance of his station. He had a taste for solving the difficulties of the folk [Sufis] and had great belief in their circle. He took the path [tariqa] from the gnostic Shaykh Sulayman al-Khudayri. Shaykh Abd al-Wahhab said: I accompanied him for ten years and never saw anything that would disgrace him in his religion. I performed Hajj with him in the year 953 AH and found him of great character with his neighbours and servants, both going and returning, even though travel reveals the character of men. He consulted me about leaving teaching and focusing on the path of the poor Sufis, so I said to him: Do not enter the path until you are fully grounded in the sciences of Sharia, and he agreed to that. I ask Allah the Exalted to increase him in knowledge and righteous deeds and to gather us in his company with the acting scholars and the mujtahid imams under the banner of the Master of the Messengers. The translated author's works include: al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, Mukhtasar al-Tahrir, Sharh al-Manar, al-Fawa'id al-Zayniyya, and al-Rasa'il al-Zayniyya which were arranged by his daughter's son. As for his glosses on the margins of books and his writings on queries of mustaftis and the papers he blackened with excellent investigations, it is something that cannot be enumerated. Were it not for the hastening of the term before the attainment of the goal... [From] Muhammad.

And although he is of great rank and famous mention, his merits cannot be fully explored in volumes, yet we desired not to miss the blessing of mentioning something of his biography, because when the righteous are mentioned, mercies descend. So we say: He is the precise scholar and the versatile imam, Sayyid Muhammad Amin Abidin, son of the noble sayyid Umar Abidin. His noble lineage ends with Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq ibn Muhammad ibn Ali ibn al-Hasan ibn Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah honor his face). His deceased son, the scholar Sayyid Muhammad Ala al-Din, has fully mentioned his noble ancestors along with a pious portion of his pleasing biography and noble character and his states in the beginning of his book 'Qurrat Uyun al-Akhyar: Takmila of Radd al-Mukhtar ala al-Durr al-Mukhtar.' The summary of the statement concerning the aforementioned biographical subject is that he (may Allah have mercy on him) was one by whom the conduct of the righteous predecessors is remembered, due to abundance of knowledge, great versatility, and strength of religion. His fame and deep delving into the sciences is attested to by his famous works and what they contain of his penetrating understanding and his ability to solve difficulties and unveil many obscured matters. So he has, may Allah have mercy on him, among his compositions: Radd al-Muhtar ala al-Durr al-Mukhtar, al-Uqud al-Durriyya fi Tanqih al-Fatawa al-Hamidiyya, a hashya on al-Baydawi, a hashya on al-Mutawwal, a hashya on Sharh al-Multaqa, and a hashya on al-Nahr, except that the latter two were not prepared for publication. And this is the hashya on al-Bahr. He also has a collection in literature and about thirty treatises and other works. He was of good character and qualities, dividing his noble time among types of acts of obedience; perhaps he spent his entire night reciting the Quran and weeping, and he would not leave any time without ritual purity. He gave much charity, avoided doubtful matters, and would not eat except from the wealth of his trade. He was respected and his word was obeyed. In summary, his noble qualities are unlimited. He was born, may Allah have mercy on him, in the year 1198 and died, may Allah have mercy on him, in the forenoon of Wednesday, the twenty-first of Rabi' al-Thani, year 1252, at approximately fifty-four years of age in Damascus of Syria and was buried in its cemetery at Bab al-Saghir – may clouds of mercy continue to rain upon him, and may eternity be his abode in the most famous station. Then this hashya increased in adornment thanks to the embellishment of the erudite imam and sagacious leader, the unique of his time and solitary of his age, the deceased Sayyid Ahmad Abidin, cousin of the author, with his noble handwriting and his correction through reading, deep thought, and constant upright examination. And upon commencement of printing, the goodwill of his heirs – may Allah cause the existence to benefit from their continuance and perpetuate upon Muslims the blessing of their breaths and the benefits of their knowledge – permitted the giving of that hashya along with the Sharh of al-Bahr whose gleaming parts were adorned with the author's handwriting with this hashya, so that printing and correction could be done upon those brilliant scripts. May Allah reward that good deed with goodness and grant them pleasure and protect them from harm. Amen.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction to Sayyid Muhammad Amin Ibn Abidin (d. 1252 AH), the author of the famous hashiya (marginal commentary) on Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. The text begins by stating its purpose: to provide a blessed mention of a great scholar. It then details his lineage, tracing back to Imam Ja'far al-Sadiq and ultimately to Ali ibn Abi Talib. His son, Muhammad Ala al-Din, wrote a biography in his work 'Qurrat Uyun al-Akhyar' as a supplement to Ibn Abidin's own Radd al-Mukhtar. The passage highlights Ibn Abidin's scholarly stature: he was a precise and versatile scholar whose works demonstrate deep understanding and ability to resolve difficult issues. It lists his major works, including the famous 'Radd al-Muhtar ala al-Durr al-Mukhtar' (a super-commentary on al-Durr al-Mukhtar), 'al-Uqud al-Durriyya', and several hashiyas, noting that two were not finalized. The present work is the hashya on Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. Beyond his writings, Ibn Abidin is described as a devout and ascetic person who spent nights in Quran recitation and weeping, maintained constant purity, avoided doubtful wealth, and was respected in his community. His birth (1198 AH) and death (1252 AH at age 54) are recorded, along with his burial in Damascus. The passage concludes by acknowledging that the hashya was further enhanced by his cousin, Sayyid Ahmad Abidin, who wrote it out and corrected it. The heirs granted permission for printing using the author's original manuscript for accuracy. This biography serves as an introduction to the hashya, establishing the authority and piety of its author.

Key Terms
حاشية (hāshiyah): A marginal commentary or super-commentary on a primary text; here specifically the hashya on Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.
مترجم (mutarjam): The person being translated or whose biography is given; here referring to Ibn Abidin.
رد المحتار على الدر المختار (Radd al-Muhtar ala al-Durr al-Mukhtar): Ibn Abidin's famous super-commentary on al-Durr al-Mukhtar, a key Hanafi fiqh text. It is the basis for the later 'Qurrat Uyun al-Akhyar' by his son.
التفنن (al-tafannun): Versatility or mastery in multiple disciplines of Islamic knowledge.
