Selling Dogs: Permissibility and Conditions
Hanafi ruling on sale of dogs, with purity and training conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling dogs and identify the conditions under which a dog may be sold.

Core Idea
A trained dog (mu'allam) can be sold; an untrained dog may not, because permissibility depends on purity and value.

This lesson focuses on the sale of dogs, a matter of practical importance. The Hanafi school distinguishes between trained and untrained dogs, and the ruling hinges on whether the dog is considered ritually pure (tahir al-ayn). You will learn the preferred view (mufta bihi) and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
The act is the sale (bay') of a dog, which is a contract of exchange. Determine its default ruling.
Sale is generally mubah (permissible) unless prohibited by explicit text or analogy.
2
Understand the basis: purity of the dog
The permissibility depends on whether the dog's essence (ayn) is pure or impure. If pure, sale is permissible; if impure, it is like a pig and sale is invalid.
The preferred view in the school is that a dog is tahir al-ayn.
3
Apply the distinction between trained and untrained
According to Qadikhan, only a trained dog (mu'allam) may be sold. An untrained dog is not permissible to sell by implication.
Training makes the dog valuable and serves a legitimate purpose (hunting, guarding).
4
Consider the sale of dog meat
If a dog is slaughtered, its meat is pure and may be sold, unlike pig meat which is impure.
This reinforces the purity view.

Commentary
This passage sets out the Hanafi ruling on transactions involving dogs. The permissibility of selling and owning dogs depends on the view of the dog's essential purity. According to the preferred view (taharat al-ayn), it is permissible to sell, buy, and own dogs, and compensation is due if someone destroys a dog. However, according to the minority view (najis al-ayn), the dog is like a pig, and its sale is invalid for Muslims. The author notes a nuance from Qadikhan: even on the permissive view, selling a trained dog (mu'allam) is allowed, implying that untrained dogs may not be sold. Additionally, selling the meat of a dog that has been slaughtered is permissible because the meat is pure. Regarding leasing, it is permissible to lease a trained dog (e.g., for hunting), but no wage is due for hiring a trained dog or falcon for hunting because there is no established custom or need for such a contract. The passage concludes by noting that this is a special feature of this book.

Leasing Dogs and Cats: Permissibility and Custom
Hanafi ruling on leasing dogs and cats, with a note on custom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on leasing dogs and cats and explain why no wage is due for hiring a trained dog for hunting.

Core Idea
Leasing a trained dog is permissible, but no wage is due for hunting because custom lacks; a cat cannot be leased as it cannot be trained.

This lesson addresses the leasing (ijarah) of animals, specifically dogs and cats. The Hanafi school permits leasing a trained dog but notes that hiring a dog for hunting yields no wage due to the absence of custom and need. Cats are not eligible for leasing because they cannot be trained.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act: leasing (ijarah)
The act is the contract of ijarah for an animal's services. Determine the permissibility.
Ijarah generally requires that the benefit be known and permissible.
2
Check the status of the dog
A trained dog can be leased, as per 'Umdat al-Mufti. An untrained dog's lease is not addressed, but by implication, may not be permissible.
Training makes the dog valuable and its benefit defined.
3
Examine the exception: hunting with no wage
Hiring a trained dog (or falcon) for hunting is considered a gratuitous arrangement; no hire is due because there is no established custom or need for such a contract.
The author suggests 'urf (custom) is a key factor.
4
Contrast with cats
Cats cannot be leased because they are not trained (la yu'allam). Their benefit is not reliably controllable.
This reinforces the principle that the animal must be trainable.

Hanafi ruling on liability for destroying a dog.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that if a dog is destroyed, compensation is due, and explain the basis for this ruling.

Core Idea
A dog has value and its destruction requires compensation, confirming its status as property.

This lesson covers the liability (daman) for destroying a dog. According to the Hanafi school, if a person destroys someone's dog, they must pay compensation. This ruling reinforces the permissibility of owning and valuing dogs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act: destruction (itlaf) of a dog
The act is causing the death or loss of a dog. Determine whether compensation (daman) is due.
Daman is generally due for destruction of property (mal).
2
Establish that the dog is property (mal)
The ruling presupposes that a dog is considered mal that has value. Since the dog is pure and can be sold, it is property.
According to the purity view, a dog is a valuable asset.
3
Apply the ruling of liability
Al-Siraj al-Hindi quotes al-Tajrid: 'If a person destroys a dog, he is liable for its value.' This shows compensation is due.
The compensation likely equals the market value of the dog.
4
Understand the implication
This ruling confirms that dogs are not worthless; they are treated as property in Hanafi fiqh.
Contrast with schools that consider dogs najis and thus valueless.

Purity of Dog's Teeth in Hanafi Fiqh
Explains the Hanafi ruling on the purity of dog's teeth based on the applicability of dhakah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the purity of dog's teeth and explain the proof based on the applicability of dhakah.

Core Idea
Any animal subject to ritual slaughter has pure bones—thus dog's teeth are pure.

This passage from Bahr al-Raiq establishes a key principle: the bones of animals eligible for slaughter (dhakah) are pure. This is applied to dogs, lions, and foxes, leading to the ruling that their teeth are pure. The passage also distinguishes these animals from humans and pigs, which are not subject to dhakah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the ruling on dog's teeth: they are pure and do not invalidate prayer.
The passage begins by stating that the tooth of a dog is washed and prayer is complete.
2
Locate the proof
Find the dalil: 'because slaughter applies to it, and everything that slaughter applies to, its bone is not impure.'
This is an explicit qiyas argument.
3
Understand the principle
Grasp the general principle: any animal subject to dhakah has pure bones.
This principle is used to derive the ruling for dogs, lions, and foxes.
4
Note the exceptions
Identify that humans and pigs are excluded because dhakah does not apply to them.
The passage explicitly contrasts with human and pig.

Commentary

This passage from al-Walwaliji addresses a practical question: if someone draws water from another's well without permission, is he liable? The answer hinges on whether water in a well is considered owned property. In the Hanafi view, well water is not owned, so no liability arises.
