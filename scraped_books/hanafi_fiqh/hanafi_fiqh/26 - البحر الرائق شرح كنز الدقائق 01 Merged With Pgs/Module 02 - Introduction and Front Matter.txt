Introduction to Kanz al-Daqa'iq and Its Commentary
Author's introduction and praise of Kanz al-Daqa'iq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the author's motivation for writing al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and identify the distinguishing features of Kanz al-Daqa'iq.

Core Idea
Fiqh is the noblest science; Kanz al-Daqa'iq is its finest summary, and al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies its precise meanings.

This lesson introduces the grand commentary al-Bahr al-Ra'iq by Zayn al-Din ibn Nujaym and the matn it explains, Kanz al-Daqa'iq by al-Nasafi. We explore why fiqh is considered the highest knowledge, what makes Kanz al-Daqa'iq special among Hanafi mukhtasarat, and why the commentator felt a new explanation was needed despite existing works like al-Tabyin.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the status of fiqh
The author begins by asserting that fiqh is the noblest science, quoting hadith and statements like 'learning fiqh is better than learning the rest of the Quran' to establish its superiority.
This sets the importance of the work being introduced.
2
Understand the value of Kanz al-Daqa'iq
The author praises Kanz al-Daqa'iq as the best Hanafi mukhtasar, focusing on frequently occurring issues and avoiding complexities.
Al-Nasafi's work is concise yet comprehensive for daily practice.
3
Compare with existing commentary
The author acknowledges al-Tabyin as the best commentary but notes it is lengthy on khilafiyat (disputed issues) and does not clarify mantiq and mafhum (explicit and inferred meanings).
This critique motivates the new commentary.
4
Identify the commentator's aim
The commentator wrote al-Bahr al-Ra'iq to clarify the explicit and inferred meanings of the matn, addressing the deficiency he saw in al-Tabyin.
The title 'al-Bahr al-Ra'iq' means 'the Clear Sea', reflecting its purpose.
5
Note the matn author's method
Al-Nasafi explains that he abridged his larger work al-Wafi, selecting commonly occurring issues, and used symbols (like 'ta' for absolute cases) to denote sources like fatawa and waqi'at.
This methodology ensures the text is practical and easy to reference.

بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم
الحمد لله الذي دبر الأنام بتدبيره القوي، وقدّر الأحكام بتقديره الحنفي، وهدى عباده إلى الرشاد، وأنطقهم بألسنة حداد، وجعل مصالح معاشهم بالعقول محوطة، ومناجح معادهم بالعلم منوطة، فضَّل نبيه بالعلم تفضيلاً، وأنزل عليه القرآن تنزيلاً، صلى الله عليه وعلى آله كنوز الهدى وعلى أصحابه بدور الدجي.
أما بعد، فإن أشرف العلوم وأعلاها، وأوفقها وأوفاها علم الفقه والفتوى، وبه صلاح الدنيا والعقبي، فمن شمر لتحصيله ذيله وأدرع نهاره وليله، فاز بالسعادة الآجلة والسيادة العاجلة، والأحاديث في أفضليته على سائر العلوم كثيرة، والدلائل عليها شهيرة، لا سيما وهو المراد بالحكمة في القرآن على قول المحققين للفرقان وقد قال في الخلاصة: إن النظر في كتب أصحابنا من غير سماع أفضل من قيام الليل وقال : إن تعلم الفقه أفضل من تعلم باقي القرآن وجميع الفقه لا بد منه ا هـ . وإن كنز الدقائق للإمام حافظ الدين النسفي أحسن مختصر صنف في فقه الأئمة الحنفية، وقد وضعوا له شروحاً وأحسنها «التبيين» للإمام الزيلعي، لكنه قد أطال من ذكر الخلافيات ولم يفصح عن منطوقه ومفهومه، وقد كنت مشتغلاً به من ابتداء حالي معتنياً بمفهوماته، فأحببت أن أضع عليه شرحاً يفصح عن منطوقه
متن الكنز
الحمد لله الذي أعز العلم في الأعصار وأعلى حربه في الأمصار، والصلاة على رسوله المختص بهذا الفضل العظيم وعلى آله الذين فازوا منه بحظ جسيم : قال مولانا الحبر النحرير صاحب البيان والبنان في التقرير والتحرير كاشف المشكلات والمعضلات مبين الكنايات والإشارات، منبع العلى، علم الهدى، أفضل الورى، حافظ الحق والملة والدين شمس الإسلام والمسلمين، وارث لعلوم الأنبياء والمرسلين، أبو البركات عبد الله بن أحمد بن محمود النسفي : لما رأيت الهمم مائلة إلى المختصرات والطباع راغبة عن المطوّلات أردت أن ألخص الوفي بذكر ما عم وقوعه وكثر وجوده لتكثر فائدته وتتوفر عائدته، فشرعت فيه بعد التماس طائفة من أعيان الأفاضل وأفاضل الأعيان الذين هم بمنزلة الإنسان للعين والعين للإنسان مع ما بي من العوائق وسميته بكنز الدقائق وهو وإن خلا عن العويصات والمعضلات فقد تحلى بمسائل الفتاوى والواقعات معلماً بتلك العلامات وزيادة الطاء للإطلاقات والله الموفق للإتمام والميسر للاختتام والأقطع بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم الحمد لله
Khutbah of the Book: In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Praise be to Allah who manages creation with His strong management, and decrees rulings with His Hanafi decree, and guides His servants to right guidance, and makes them speak with sharp tongues, and encloses the interests of their livelihood in intellects, and attaches the successes of their afterlife to knowledge. He preferred His Prophet with knowledge as a preference, and sent down upon him the Quran as a revelation. May Allah bless him and his family, treasures of guidance, and his companions, full moons of darkness. To proceed: The noblest of sciences and the highest, the most appropriate and the most fulfilling, is the science of Fiqh and fatwa. Through it comes the well-being of this world and the Hereafter. Whoever rolls up his sleeve to acquire it and wears his day and night as armor, attains future happiness and present mastery. The ahadith on its superiority over other sciences are many, and the evidences for it are well-known, especially as it is what is meant by 'wisdom' in the Quran according to the saying of the verifiers of the Furqan. It has been said in al-Khulasa: 'Looking into the books of our companions without hearing is better than praying at night.' And he said: 'Learning Fiqh is better than learning the rest of the Quran, and all of Fiqh is indispensable.' End quote. And indeed Kanz al-Daqa'iq by Imam Hafiz al-Din al-Nasafi is the best mukhtasar composed in the fiqh of the Hanafi imams. They have written commentaries on it, and the best of them is al-Tabyin by Imam al-Zayla'i, but he has lengthened by mentioning differences (khilafiyat) and did not clarify its apparent and inferred meanings. I have been engaged with it from the beginning of my state, paying attention to its inferred meanings. So I wished to place upon it a commentary that clarifies its apparent meaning. Matn al-Kanz: Praise be to Allah who strengthened knowledge in ages and elevated its war in lands. Blessings upon His Messenger favored with this great virtue and upon his family who obtained a great share from it. Said our master, the erudite scholar, possessor of eloquence and facility in exposition and revision, revealer of problems and difficulties, explainer of metonymies and indications, source of highness, guidance of the rightly guided, best of mankind, guardian of truth, religion, and faith, sun of Islam and Muslims, inheritor of the sciences of the prophets and messengers, Abu al-Barakat Abdullah ibn Ahmad ibn Mahmud al-Nasafi: When I saw that aspirations incline towards abridgments and natures turn away from lengthy works, I intended to abridge al-Wafi by mentioning what frequently occurs and is abundant in existence, so that its benefit increases and its return is abundant. I proceeded with it after request from a group of eminent nobles and noble eminent ones who are like the human being to the eye and the eye to the human being, despite the obstacles I have. I named it Kanz al-Daqa'iq. Although it is free from complexities and difficulties, it is adorned with the issues of fatawa and real cases, marked with those signs, with an additional 'ta' for absolute forms. Allah is the Grantor of success for completion and the Facilitator for conclusion. And I begin: In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Praise be to Allah.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the book, consisting of two introductions: the author's own khutbah and the opening of the matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) by al-Nasafi. The author (Zayn al-Din ibn Nujaym, the commentator) begins by praising Allah, noting that Allah manages creation and decrees rulings, and guides people. He emphasizes the superiority of fiqh over other sciences, quoting from al-Khulasa that studying fiqh from books without a teacher is better than night prayer, and that learning fiqh is better than learning the rest of the Quran (meaning after the essential parts). He then praises Kanz al-Daqa'iq as the best mukhtasar in the Hanafi school, and notes that while al-Zayla'i's Tabyin is the best commentary, it is lengthy on khilafiyat and does not clarify the mantiq and mafhum (explicit and inferred meanings). Therefore, he writes this commentary (al-Bahr al-Ra'iq) to clarify the explicit meanings. Then he quotes al-Nasafi's own introduction to Kanz al-Daqa'iq, where al-Nasafi explains that he wrote this abridgment because people prefer short works. He abridged his own larger work al-Wafi, focusing on commonly occurring issues, and named it Kanz al-Daqa'iq. He mentions that he uses certain marks to denote fatawa and waqi'at, and adds a 'ta' (ط) for absolute cases. The passage ends with the basmala and hamdalah.

Key Terms
كنز الدقائق (Kanz al-Daqā'iq): A Hanafi fiqh mukhtasar (abridgment) by al-Nasafi, considered one of the most important texts in the school.
التبيين (al-Tabyīn): A commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq by Imam al-Zayla'i, noted for lengthy discussions of khilafiyat.
النسفي (al-Nasafī): Abu al-Barakat Abdullah ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi, the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq and other Hanafi works.

Understanding the Author's Preface: Purpose and Sources of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq
Author's preface outlining methodology and source list

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author's purpose for writing al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and list the major commentary and fatwa sources he relied upon.

Core Idea
Preface reveals methodology and sources, essential for assessing the authority of rulings.

This passage is the opening of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq Sharh Kanz al-Daqa'iq by Zayn al-Din ibn Nujaym. It is not a legal ruling but a methodological preface. It extols the virtue of fiqh, introduces the core text (Kanz al-Daqa'iq), and then lists the many commentaries and fatwa collections that form the basis of this work. Understanding this preface is crucial for a student of Hanafi fiqh because it tells you which sources carry authority and how the commentary relates to the primary texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's purpose
Read the author's statement about why he wrote this commentary. He says that previous commentaries, especially al-Tabyin by al-Zayla'i, were lengthy and did not clarify the literal meaning (mantuq) and implied meaning (mafhum) of the matn. His aim is to write a commentary that clarifies both and incorporates fatwa rulings with many subdivisions.
The terms mantuq and mafhum are usul al-fiqh concepts: mantuq is the explicit meaning, mafhum is the implied meaning.
2
Extract the list of commentary sources
The author lists many commentaries he used. Some key ones: Sharh al-Jami' al-Saghir by Qadi Khan, al-Mabsut, Sharh al-Kafi, al-Hidaya and its commentaries (Ghayat al-Bayan, al-Nihaya, al-Inaya, etc.), al-Tabyin, and others. Note which ones are frequently cited in Hanafi fiqh.
al-Hidaya is a central text; its commentaries are essential for advanced study.
3
Extract the list of fatwa sources
The author names fatwa collections such as al-Muhit, al-Dhakhira, al-Bada'i', al-Ziyadat by Qadi Khan, al-Khulasa, al-Bazzaziya, and others. These represent the practical application of fiqh.
Fatwa collections are used to determine the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) when there is ikhtilaf.
4
Understand the hierarchy of sources
The author states he 'returns the branches of fatwas and commentaries to them' (the matn). This means he derives rulings from the core text and supports them with fatwa sources, showing a method of grounding rulings in the primary texts.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on returning to authoritative sources.
5
Recognize the title's significance
The title 'al-Bahr al-Ra'iq' means 'the Clear Sea'. The author likely chose this to indicate that the commentary is vast (like a sea) and clear in its explanations. Reflect on how the title relates to the content.
Many Hanafi works have metaphorical titles; understanding them can give insight into the author's intent.

Definition of Fiqh: Linguistic and Technical
Definition of fiqh with linguistic and technical explanations and scholarly discussions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and technical definitions of fiqh according to the Hanafi school and explain why the term 'ilm is used despite the speculative nature of most fiqh evidence.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of practical Sharia rulings derived from detailed evidence, even if the evidence is only speculative.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents the foundational definition of fiqh as understood in the Hanafi tradition. It distinguishes the linguistic meaning (understanding) from the technical meaning (knowledge of practical Sharia rulings from detailed evidence). The author also addresses a key epistemological issue: that most fiqh rulings are based on speculative (zanni) evidence, yet the term 'ilm (knowledge) is used due to the strength of the mujtahid's opinion.

The term is 'fiqh' (الفقه). Locate it in the text and note that the passage discusses both its linguistic and technical definitions.
2
Extract the linguistic meaning
The text states: 'فالفقه لغة الفهم' (so fiqh linguistically is understanding). Note the different verb forms: fagiha (with kasra) for understanding, fahuqa (with damma) for becoming a jurist.
The vowel variations indicate nuances in usage.
3
Extract the technical definition
The technical definition is: 'العلم بالأحكام الشرعية العملية المكتسبة من أدلتها التفصيلية بالاستدلال' – knowledge of practical Sharia rulings derived from detailed evidence by deduction.
This definition is standard in Hanafi usul.
4
Analyze the use of 'ilm
The passage explains that 'ilm is used despite the evidence being zanni because the mujtahid's strong zann is close to knowledge and must be acted upon. An objection about ijma' being qat'i is addressed by noting that qat'i rulings are excluded from fiqh or the expression of zann is on a basis of predominance.

Commentary
This passage is a front matter defining the science of fiqh from multiple perspectives. It begins with the definition of fiqh as knowledge of subsidiary Shari'ah rulings derived from their evidences, clarifying that rulings explicitly stated in texts are not considered derived, but the phrase 'from their evidences' includes qiyas and other proofs. The author then presents Imam Abu Hanifa's broader definition: 'knowledge of the self what is for it and what is against it', which encompasses beliefs (faith), inner states (ethics and Sufism), and actions (practical fiqh). The technical fiqh is limited to actions. The passage notes that Abu Hanifa did not add 'actional' because he intended a comprehensive science, and he even called theology 'greater fiqh'. The commentary by Allamah Khusraw explains that psychological faculties are not fiqh in themselves but their effects on outward actions are. Next, the passage discusses the meaning of fiqh for the people of reality (sufis) as detachment from the world, and for the fuqaha (jurists) as including both scholars and students of fiqh. It then defines fiqh in al-Hawi al-Qudsi as comprehension of textual meanings. The passage distinguishes between the mujtahid, who is a true faqih, and the muqallid who memorizes issues, the latter being called faqih metaphorically but in common usage it is applied to those who memorize branches. Finally, the subject matter of fiqh is identified as the actions of the mukallaf from the aspect of being legally responsible, meaning the adult sane person. Actions of non-mukallaf (children, insane) are not directly subject, but their guardians are addressed in certain matters like damages and maintenance. The validity of a child's worship is rational, not a command, to train them. The aspect of responsibility includes both positive (obligation, prohibition) and negative (permissibility, recommendation) rulings. The passage ends with a metaphysical note from al-Hawi al-Qudsi about describing actions with legal terms and how God's single act is named differently based on the attribute of the object. The commentator then warns that the latter part pertains to usul, not fiqh terminology.

Key Terms
الفقه (al-fiqh): Knowledge of subsidiary Shari'ah rulings derived from their evidences; also defined by Abu Hanifa as knowledge of what is for and against the self, encompassing beliefs, inner states, and actions, but technically restricted to actions.
الفقيه (al-faqih): One who comprehends the meanings of texts and their implications; in technical usul, only a mujtahid; in common usage among fuqaha, one who memorizes fiqh issues even without evidence.
الاستنباط (al-istinbat): Derivation of rulings from evidences through ijtihad.
المقلد (al-muqallid): One who follows a mujtahid without knowledge of evidences.

Subject Matter of Fiqh: Actions of the Mukallaf
Defining the subject matter of fiqh as actions of the mukallaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject matter of fiqh and explain why the actions of non-mukallaf are not directly addressed.

Core Idea
Fiqh studies the actions of a responsible person (mukallaf) from the angle of legal prescriptions.

Every science has a subject matter. For fiqh, it is the actions of the mukallaf (the legally responsible adult sane person). In this lesson, we examine why children and the insane are not directly included, and how their actions are handled through guardianship. We also explore the distinction between rulings as descriptions of actions versus as descriptions of God's act of ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject matter
The text states: 'موضوعه ففعل المكلف من حيث إنه مكلف'. Recognize that the subject is the action of the mukallaf considered with respect to legal responsibility.
Not just any action, but actions as they relate to rulings (halal, haram, wajib, etc.).
2
Define the mukallaf
The mukallaf is the adult sane person. Non-mukallaf (children and insane) are not directly subject to fiqh rulings.
However, their guardians are addressed for damages and maintenance.
3
Understand the treatment of children's acts
Children's worship is valid and rewarded, but they are not commanded. The purpose is training, not obligation.
This is a rational linking of rulings to causes, not a command.
4
Clarify the aspect of responsibility
The action is considered from the perspective of taklif (responsibility), which includes both positive commands (obligation/prohibition) and negative (permissibility/recommendation).
Even permissible actions have a ruling (mubah) that removes burden.
5
Distinguish between describing actions and describing God's ruling
It is figurative to say 'halal is God's ruling' because God's act of making halal is one, but it is called differently based on the attribute of the object.
This is a metaphysical point from al-Hawi al-Qudsi, more usuli than fiqhi.

Commentary
This passage is a front matter defining the science of fiqh from multiple perspectives. It begins with the definition of fiqh as knowledge of subsidiary Shari'ah rulings derived from their evidences, clarifying that rulings explicitly stated in texts are not considered derived, but the phrase 'from their evidences' includes qiyas and other proofs. The author then presents Imam Abu Hanifa's broader definition: 'knowledge of the self what is for it and what is against it', which encompasses beliefs (faith), inner states (ethics and Sufism), and actions (practical fiqh). The technical fiqh is limited to actions. The passage notes that Abu Hanifa did not add 'actional' because he intended a comprehensive science, and he even called theology 'greater fiqh'. The commentary by Allamah Khusraw explains that psychological faculties are not fiqh in themselves but their effects on outward actions are. Next, the passage discusses the meaning of fiqh for the people of reality (sufis) as detachment from the world, and for the fuqaha (jurists) as including both scholars and students of fiqh. It then defines fiqh in al-Hawi al-Qudsi as comprehension of textual meanings. The passage distinguishes between the mujtahid, who is a true faqih, and the muqallid who memorizes issues, the latter being called faqih metaphorically but in common usage it is applied to those who memorize branches. Finally, the subject matter of fiqh is identified as the actions of the mukallaf from the aspect of being legally responsible, meaning the adult sane person. Actions of non-mukallaf (children, insane) are not directly subject, but their guardians are addressed in certain matters like damages and maintenance. The validity of a child's worship is rational, not a command, to train them. The aspect of responsibility includes both positive (obligation, prohibition) and negative (permissibility, recommendation) rulings. The passage ends with a metaphysical note from al-Hawi al-Qudsi about describing actions with legal terms and how God's single act is named differently based on the attribute of the object. The commentator then warns that the latter part pertains to usul, not fiqh terminology.

Definition of Fiqh and the Faqih: Literal and Metaphorical Usage
Definition of fiqh and faqih with terminological nuances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the literal and metaphorical uses of the term 'faqih' in Hanafi terminology and explain the difference between a mujtahid and a muqallid.

Core Idea
A true faqih is a mujtahid; calling a muqallid a faqih is metaphorical in Usul, but literal in the custom of jurists.

This passage from the opening of Bahr al-Raiq explores the precise meanings of 'fiqh' and 'faqih'. It clarifies that in its original sense, fiqh is knowledge of rulings derived directly from evidences, making a faqih a mujtahid. However, common juristic usage extends the term to those who merely memorize rulings, creating an important terminological distinction that affects endowments, bequests, and scholarly authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts of the term 'faqih'
Notice that the author discusses 'faqih' both in the context of Usul al-Fiqh (the science of evidences) and in the custom of the fuqaha (juristic practice).
This dual usage is key to understanding the passage.
2
Analyze the distinction between literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz)
In Usul, applying 'faqih' to a muqallid is metaphorical because the muqallid lacks direct knowledge from evidences. In juristic custom, it becomes literal as the term is commonly used for memorizers of rulings.
The evidence for the custom is that endowments and bequests to 'fuqaha' are interpreted as referring to those who memorize branches.
3
Consider the implications for the minimum qualification of a faqih
Al-Muntaqa states that a person is called a faqih if they know at least three rulings. Al-Tahrir adds that the common usage applies to anyone who memorizes branches, whether with or without evidences.
This shows the practical leniency in the term's application.
4
Connect to the broader Hanafi epistemological framework
The passage also describes the psychological process of acquiring knowledge and quotes Abu Yusuf's statement that fiqh became like a natural disposition to him, emphasizing the deep internalization of legal knowledge.
This highlights the ideal that fiqh should be deeply ingrained, not just memorized.
