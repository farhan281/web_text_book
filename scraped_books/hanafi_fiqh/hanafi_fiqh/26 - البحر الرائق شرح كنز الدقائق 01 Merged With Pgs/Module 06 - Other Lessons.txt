Tanning of Hides: Hanafi Position and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Inter-madhhab dispute on tanning hides with detailed proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on tanning hides, identify the exact scope of purification, and compare it with the views of Shafi‘i, Hanbali, and Maliki schools.

Core Idea
Tanning purifies the hide of edible dead animals for use, but not for eating—the meat prohibition remains.

Have you ever wondered whether the hide of an animal that died on its own can ever become pure? The Hanafi school, drawing on specific hadiths, holds that tanning (dabgh) transforms such hides into usable material. But not all scholars agree. In this passage from al-Bahr al-Ra’iq, the commentator lays out the Hanafi ruling and then compares it with the positions of Shafi‘i, Ahmad ibn Hanbal, and Malik. You will not only learn the ruling but also the proofs that shape each school’s view and the practical consequences that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon—here, tanning the hide of a dead animal. Note the Hanafi ruling: the hide becomes pure (tahir) for use in non-food items, but eating it remains haram.
The hide is pure, not the meat.
2
Locate the primary dalil
Find the key hadith cited: 'Any hide that is tanned is purified' (إيما إهاب دبغ فقد طهر) and the incident of the dead sheep of Maymunah. Understand that these specify the general prohibition of dead animals.
The hadith qualifies the general verse (5:3).
3
Map the schools’ positions
List the four schools: Hanafi (edible animals only), Shafi‘i (all except dog/pig), Hanbali (no purification), Maliki (all, but only outer layer). Note the thamarah al-khilaf: what each school permits regarding use.
Focus on the scope of animals included.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to opponents
See how the Hanafi author rebuts the opposition: the general prohibition of dead animals is specified by the tanning hadiths, and the exclusion of pig is justified by the Qur'anic verse calling pig 'impure' (rijis).
The grammatical discussion on pronoun reference (عودة الضمير) is a subtle but important proof.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Confirm that the Hanafi position—tanning purifies hides of edible animals—is the relied-upon view, with the additional rule that eating such hide remains forbidden by consensus among later Hanafis.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It begins by discussing the ruling that tanning (dabgh) purifies the hide of a dead animal, but only if the animal's meat is edible. The Hanafi position is that tanning purifies the hide of edible animals, making it permissible to use and (according to some) even to eat, although the correct Hanafi view is that eating is not permissible because the meat itself is dead. The passage then delves into a detailed comparison of the positions of Imam al-Shafi'i, Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal, and Imam Malik. Al-Shafi'i holds that tanning purifies the hide of all animals except the dog and pig, and humans are excluded because they are not impure by death. Ahmad holds that tanning does not purify any hide, citing the general prohibition of dead animals and a hadith prohibiting use of hide and sinew. Malik holds that tanning purifies everything, including dog and pig, but only externally, so tanned hides can be used for dry items but not wet. The Hanafi author presents the evidence for each view: the Shafi'i proofs include the hadith 'Any hide that is tanned is purified' and the story of the dead sheep; the Hanafi school aligns with the Shafi'i view except for the exclusion of the pig, which they argue is supported by the Qur'anic verse calling pig flesh 'impure' (rijis). The author then engages in a technical discussion about pronoun reference in Arabic grammar to justify that the verse refers to the pig itself being impure, not just its flesh. The passage concludes by reiterating the Hanafi ruling that tanned hides of edible animals are permissible to use but not to eat, and hides of non-edible animals are not permissible by consensus.

Key Terms
الدبغ (al-dabgh): The process of tanning animal hides to make them usable.
الميتة (al-maytah): An animal that dies without being properly slaughtered.
الإهاب (al-ihab): The hide or skin of an animal.

The Nature of Death: Sunni vs Mu'tazili Dispute and Ruling on Musk
Explaining the theological dispute on death and concluding with a fiqh ruling on musk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the nature of death (wujudi vs adami), distinguish it from the Mu'tazili view, and identify the ruling that musk is pure (tahir).

Core Idea
Death is an existent created entity, not mere absence — thus non-existence cannot be created.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq delves into a theological dispute between Ahl al-Sunnah and the Mu'tazila/Qadariyya regarding the nature of death. The debate has practical implications for fiqh rulings on impurity from dead animals. The author concludes with a specific ruling on musk, affirming its purity unequivocally.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central dispute
Determine what the two schools disagree about: is death an existent (wujudi) attribute or mere non-existence (adami)?
2
Examine the Sunni proof
Analyze the Quranic verse 'He who created death and life' (67:2) and the rational argument that creation applies only to existents.
3
Understand the Mu'tazili counterargument
Recognize the claim that death is merely the absence of life, and that the word 'created' is figurative or refers to the qualifier that enables life.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response
See how the Hanafi school refutes this: if death were non-existent, it could not be created; non-existence is eternal and uncreated.
5
Note the practical ruling on musk
Identify the appended ruling that musk from the musk deer is absolutely pure (tahir mutlaqan) according to the most correct view.
This ruling is independent of the theological dispute but shows the author's commitment to the Sunni position.

Some usul scholars in Sharh al-Mughni said this difference is only according to the terminology of theologians, but according to the terminology of usul scholars, the opposite is what opposes a thing and between them is extreme difference, whether both are existent or one is existent and the other non-existent. The author of al-Kashshaf chose that death is non-existent, saying: Life is that with whose existence sensation is possible; it is said that it is what makes a thing alive, that is, capable of knowledge and power. Death is the absence of that in it. The meaning of creating death and life is bringing that qualifier into existence and annihilating it. Al-Tibi in his gloss said: His statement 'and death is the absence of that in it' is a compromise for the Qadariyya belief that death is non-existent, while the Sunni belief is that it is an existent opposite to life. How can it be non-existent when it is described as being created? The non-existence of events is eternal; if non-existent could be created, it would entail the occurrence of events eternally, which is clearly false. The author of al-Fawa'id said: If death were the absence of life, it would be impossible for it to be created. Then he said: The meaning of creating death and life is bringing that qualifier into existence and annihilating it; this is also questionable. Imam said: It is the attribute by which the described becomes such that it can know and have power. They differed about death: some say it is the absence of this attribute, others say it is an existent attribute opposing life because of His statement 'He who created death and life' (Quran 67:2); non-existence cannot be created. This is the investigation; up to here is the statement of al-Tibi. Imam al-Qurtubi in his Tafsir said: The scholars said death is not pure non-existence nor mere annihilation; rather, it is the attachment of the soul to the body and its separation, a barrier between them, a change of state, and a transfer from one abode to another; life is the opposite. He transmitted other statements about them which we will not detail. The conclusion is that the school of Ahl al-Sunnah is that death is an existent like life, and the school of the Mu'tazila (as in al-Kashf) or the Qadariyya (as in the gloss) is that it is non-existent. According to both, there is no dispute that death occurs after life, since what has not previously had life is not described as death truly in language and custom. Hence al-Sayyid al-Sharif in Sharh al-Mawaqif said after interpreting death as absence of life from what is supposed to be alive, the more apparent is to say absence of life from that which had life. But then it may be said that an answer is needed to His statement 'And you were dead, and He brought you to life' (Quran 2:28). In al-Kashshaf: If it is asked, how were they called dead when they were inanimate? Death is only said of something that can have life. Answer: Rather, it is said of them as inanimate for they lack life, just as He says 'a dead town' (Quran 25:49) and 'a sign to them is the dead earth' (Quran 36:33), and 'they are dead, not alive' (Quran 16:21). It is possible that it is a metaphor for their similarity in having no life and no sensation. Al-Qutb in his gloss explained the metaphor: the inanimate is compared to a dead thing in lacking spirit, then the word is borrowed. And Allah knows best. Defining Decisive Evidence in Impurity Strictness
Defining a key term via commentary expansion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'al-dalil al-qat'i' in the Hanafi context of impurity strictness and distinguish it from other meanings like certain transmission.

Core Idea
Decisive evidence means evidence that obligates action, not necessarily certain transmission.

In Hanafi fiqh, the term 'al-dalil al-qat'i' (decisive evidence) appears when discussing the strictness of certain impurities. But what does 'decisive' really mean? This lesson unpacks the commentaries on Al-Hidayah to clarify that it refers to evidence that decisively requires action, free from factors that would necessitate leniency, such as conflicting texts or necessities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim from Al-Hidayah

Interpret with Fath al-Qadir
Fath al-Qadir explains that 'decisive' here means the evidence decisively requires action (wujub al-'amal), not that its transmission is certain (qat'i al-thubut).
This is a key distinction: actionability vs. transmission certainty.
3
Recognize the preferred interpretation
The commentator suggests that the 'decisive evidence' likely refers to ijma' (consensus), which is qat'i in both transmission and meaning.

Add Al-Inayah's condition
Al-Inayah adds that 'decisive evidence' must be free from factors that necessitate leniency: conflicting texts (ta'arud al-nass), conflicting ijtihad, and alleviating necessities.
If any such factor exists, the evidence is not considered decisive for strictness.
5
Synthesize the legal definition
Combine all: al-dalil al-qat'i in this context is evidence that obligates action (even if zanni al-thubut) and is immune from countervailing factors that would reduce its binding force.
This definition determines which impurities receive strict treatment.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Impurity is based on filth (mustaqdhar), but birds that excrete in flight and are halal to eat have pure droppings.

Not all animal droppings are treated equally in Hanafi fiqh. The ruling depends on the nature of the animal: whether it excretes while flying or on the ground, whether its meat is halal or haram, and whether it lives among people or in the wild. This lesson unpacks the criteria the Hanafi scholars used to classify bird and animal droppings as pure, impure, or light impurity.

Assess diet and lifestyle
For birds that excrete in the air, check if the bird's meat is halal (e.g., pigeon, sparrow) or haram (e.g., falcon, hawk). For birds that do not excrete in the air, consider if they live among people or not.
The lifestyle affects the ruling for ducks: domestic (najis) vs wild (like pigeon).

Apply the general rule
If the bird does not excrete in the air, its droppings are impure (najis) because they are mustaqdhar (filthy). If it excreates in the air and is halal to eat, its droppings are pure (tahir). If it excreates in the air and is haram to eat, its droppings are light impurity (mukhaffaf).

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: The Severity of Animal Dung

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn regarding the impurity classification of animal dung and evaluate the proofs for each position.

Core Idea
Text vs. Balwa: Abu Hanifa prioritizes the hadith calling dung 'riks'; Sahibayn prioritize widespread affliction.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq reveals a fascinating internal dispute within the Hanafi school. While Abu Hanifa classifies the dung of horses, donkeys, cows, and camels as severe impurity based on a direct prophetic statement, his two principal students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argue for a lighter classification due to the widespread affliction (balwa) of such dung filling streets. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to see how different usul principles—textual authority versus practical necessity—compete within the same school.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Identify that Abu Hanifa holds dung from non-bird animals as severe impurity (najasah ghalizah). The evidence is the Prophet's statement that a dung (rawth) is 'riks' (impure). Note that he considers the text decisive and not contradicted.
The hadith is not explicitly graded in the passage, but it is accepted as evidence.
2
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider it light impurity. Their arguments: (1) The Maliki school considers it pure, indicating difference; (2) Widespread affliction (balwa) makes strictness impractical; (3) The ground absorbs the impurity, unlike donkey urine.
Imam Muhammad later returned from Rayy and adopted a more lenient view due to direct observation of balwa.

Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, if a person's clothing or shoe is soiled with dung (exceeding a dirham), prayer is invalid unless washed. Under Sahibayn's light view, rubbing (dalk) suffices for sandals, and even a large amount does not invalidate prayer as long as it is not excessive.
This affects everyday fatwa about purification.
4

Abu Hanifa argues that the text (hadith) must take precedence over balwa. The effect of balwa is already accommodated in the ruling for sandals (purified by rubbing); adding further leniency is baseless. The claim that balwa is never considered in the presence of a text is rejected; it is considered only when a separate text negates hardship.
This reflects a strict usul position prioritizing nass.
5
Evaluate the later adoption
Despite Abu Hanifa's strong textual argument, later Hanafi fatwa often adopts Sahibayn's lighter view due to the prevalence of balwa, as seen in Imam Muhammad's own change of opinion. This shows how practical necessity can influence mufta bihi positions.

Miscellaneous Impurities: Snake Skin, Worms, Donkey, and Environmental Factors
Collection of fatwa rulings on various impurities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rulings on snake skin, worms, donkey drinking, wind carrying odor, vapors, and toilet flow, and apply the principles of severity and necessity.

Core Idea
Snake skin is impure (cannot be tanned), but its slough is pure; worms from private parts are impure, from meat pure; vapors do not transmit impurity.

This lesson gathers several practical rulings on impurities that are not directly related to animal dung. Topics include the impurity of snake skin, worms, the effect of a donkey drinking juice, the transfer of impurity via wind and vapors, and the recommendation to wash off toilet flow. These rulings illustrate the Hanafi method of balancing strictness with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of impurity
Each item is examined separately: snake skin, worm, donkey, wind, vapor, toilet flow.
Sources vary from al-Zahiriyyah to al-Bazzaziyyah.
2
Determine the ruling on impurity
For snake skin: impure because it cannot be tanned. For worm from private parts: impure; from meat: pure. For donkey drink: juice becomes impure.
Reasoning given: inability to tan, origin of worm.
3
Assess the mode of transmission
Wind carrying fecal odor: if smell detected on wet clothes, clothes become impure. Vapors: correct view is that clothes do not become impure.
Touch vs. odor as evidence of impurity.
4
Apply the principle of doubt
For toilet flow on clothes: washing is recommended but not obligatory unless one is certain of impurity. This is based on the default of purity and avoiding hardship.
Dominant opinion (aghlab al-ra'y) triggers obligation.

Human Body Parts: Skin, Bone, Teeth, and Intra-Madhhab Differences
Rulings on human body parts and intra-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on human skin, bone, teeth, and ears, explain the intra-madhhab differences between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, and identify the adopted view.

Core Idea
Human body parts are impure when separated from a living person, but Abu Yusuf exempts the ear and tooth for the owner; Muhammad's view (impure) is adopted.

This lesson focuses on the impurity of human body parts: skin, bone, ear, tooth. The Hanafi school has an intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the purity of detached ears and teeth for their owner. The passage also includes a commentary that restricts the ruling on human skin to moist pieces.

Guided Path
1
Identify the body part and its condition
The passage covers: human skin (size of fingernail), bone, ear, tooth. Note whether the part is moist or dry, and its size.
Size threshold: as big as a fingernail for skin.
2
Determine the default Hanafi ruling
Separated human body parts are generally impure (najis), because they come from human flesh and are no longer attached.
Exception: small pieces (less than fingernail) may be excused.
3
Note the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf holds that a detached ear or tooth is pure for its owner, even if large. Muhammad holds they are impure, and this is the adopted view.
The dispute is based on whether the attachment to the owner affects purity.
4
Apply the commentary's restrictions
The commentary by Ibn Amir Haj restricts the ruling on human skin: it must be moist to spoil water; dry skin does not spoil water. Also, the water must be a small quantity (qalil).
This is based on a principle from Muhammad regarding dry skin of dead animals.

Commentary
Passage type: collection of subsidiary rulings. This lengthy passage brings together various practical rulings on different types of impurities. The first ruling: barley found in camel or sheep dung is washed and eaten, but not in cow dung because cow dung is not solid. Second: if mouse droppings are found in bread and are hard, the dropping is removed and the bread is eaten. Earlier it was mentioned that mouse droppings spoil things, but here it states that in oil, water, or wheat, it does not spoil—there is a contradiction noted. Third: camel dung that falls into the milking vessel during milking and is removed before disintegrating does not make the milk impure. Fourth: if one walks in muddy impure ground, washing is not required unless visible; prayer is valid. The author emphasizes that precaution in prayer is important but also practical. He mentions that carrying a prayer mat is preferred to avoid impurity. If a foot enters a stable and touches dung, prayer is allowed if not excessive; this is a preference for the Sahibayn's view. Then there is a note that later fatwa books discuss many branches but do not always specify severity, but the default is that these are severe impurities. Then various items: snake skin is impure (cannot be tanned), but its slough is pure. Worms from private parts are impure; from flesh (meat) they are pure. If a donkey drinks juice, the juice is impure. Wind carrying fecal smell makes wet clothes impure if smell detected, but vapors of impurities (steam) do not make clothes impure (correct view). Liquid from a toilet: washing is recommended but not obligatory unless one is sure. Human skin: if it falls into little water, it spoils the water if the skin is the size of a fingernail; a fingernail alone does not spoil. A dead disbeliever is impure even after washing; likewise a dead Muslim (after washing, difference?). Human bone is impure according to Abu Hanifa; Abu Yusuf says pure. A cut ear or extracted tooth is pure for its owner even if large (Abu Yusuf); Muhammad says teeth are impure, and by analogy a cut ear is impure, and this is the adopted view. If a tooth falls into little water, it spoils it; if ground into wheat, the wheat is not eaten unless taste changes (doubtful). The commentary of Ibn Amir Haj restricts the human skin ruling: only moist skin of fingernail size spoils little water; dry skin does not. Similarly, dry skin of a dead animal does not spoil water (from Muhammad). Thus, the ruling on human skin is conditioned on it being moist.

The Final Sitting: Original Pillar or Legislated Fard?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the final sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether the final sitting is a rukn asli and explain the evidence from the oath case.

Core Idea
The final sitting is fard but not a rukn asli; the essence of prayer is complete upon rising from prostration.

In Hanafi fiqh, there is an internal dispute about the status of the final sitting (qa'dah) in prayer. While all agree it is obligatory (fard), some early Hanafi scholars considered it an original pillar (rukn asli). This lesson examines the debate and the stronger view that it is not a rukn asli, based on a unique proof from oath-breaking.

Guided Path
1
State the agreed-upon obligation
Both parties agree that the final sitting is fard (obligatory). The dispute is about its classification: is it a rukn asli or not?
2
Identify the dissenting view
Some Hanafi scholars, such as Isam ibn Yusuf, hold that the final sitting is a rukn asli, meaning it is an integral part of the prayer's essence.
The text does not provide their explicit proof.
3
Analyze the stronger proof
The author argues from an oath case: if a man swears not to pray, his oath is broken upon rising from prostration, without requiring the sitting. This shows the essence of prayer (mahiyyah) does not depend on the sitting.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on legal inference.
4
Determine the practical consequence
The author notes that the thamarah al-khilaf (practical result) of this difference is not discussed in earlier works. However, understanding this classification affects how we conceptualize the mandatory elements of prayer.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the nature of the final sitting (qa'dah) in prayer. The author begins by stating that the correct opinion is that the obligatory part of the sitting is the measure of tashahhud. He then discusses an objection about the purpose of the sitting. He cites a fatwa from al-Walwaliji about a case where a person had two brief sittings - the validity depends on each being the measure of tashahhud, without requiring continuity. After agreement that the sitting is fard, scholars differed on whether it is a rukn asli (an original pillar). Some, like Isam ibn Yusuf, considered it an original rukn. The author notes that he has not seen anyone who explored the practical consequence of this difference (thamarah).

The Obligation to Believe in the Execution of Divine Threat on Some Sinners
Discussing the consensus that Allah's threat of punishment must be executed on some sinners and the extent of required belief.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the consensus of Ahl al-Sunnah regarding the execution of divine threat on sinners and identify the two positions on the scope of required belief (per sin category vs. overall).

Core Idea
Allah's threat is true and must be executed on some sinners; it is obligatory to believe this, but the extent is debated.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a theological refinement of a line in Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The commentator clarifies that while it is permissible to pray for forgiveness for all believers, one must not hold the belief that every believer will be unconditionally forgiven. The consensus (ijma) of Ahl al-Sunnah is that the divine threat (wa'id) must be carried out on a group of monotheistic sinners. However, a question remains: does this obligation require belief that each category of major sin will have its own punished members, or is it sufficient to believe that some sinners overall will be punished? The passage transmits the views of al-Ubbi, al-Laqqani, and al-Nawawi, leaning towards the former.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Claim Being Refined
The commentator is responding to a matn statement that might be misinterpreted as permitting the belief that all believers will be forgiven. Your task is to pinpoint the claim: 'It is not definitive (laysa bi-hatm) in our view.' Understand that the commentator is narrowing this to intellectual permissibility, not legal or creedal permissibility.
The distinction between 'jā'iz shar'an' and 'jā'iz 'aqlan' is crucial here.
2
Understand the Consensus (Ijma) on Wa'id
The author cites multiple scholars (al-Ubbi, al-Laqqani, al-Nawawi) confirming ijma that the divine threat must be executed on some sinners (al-muwahhidin). This is a binding creedal point. Recognize that this consensus is transmitted from Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah.
The foundation: 'Kalāmuhu ta'āla ṣidq falā budda min wuqū'ih' – His speech is truth, so it must occur.
3
Distinguish Between Intellectual and Legal Permissibility
The commentator explains that the matn's claim 'it is not definitive' is only acceptable if meant as intellectual possibility (jā'iz 'aqlan) – i.e., it is not rationally impossible for Allah to forgive all. But it is not legally permissible (jā'iz shar'an) because the Shari'ah has conveyed the threat and confirmed its execution for some.
This refines the reading: what is not 'hatm' (definitive) is the particular application to all sinners, not the general principle.
4
Examine the Sub-Issue: Scope of Required Belief
After establishing the general obligation, the passage raises a nuanced question: is it necessary to believe that every type of major sin (kabīra) will have a group of its practitioners punished, or is it enough to believe that some sinners overall will be punished? The author presents hesitation (taraddud) from al-Ubbi and then the apparent view that each category is separate.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within Ahl al-Sunnah; the mufta bihi position is the first (per category).
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi Position
Based on al-Laqqani's commentary and the judge's (al-Qāḍī) apparent speech, the relied-upon view is that every category of major sin must have some punished. This is because Allah threatened each category individually. As a student, you should adopt this as the more cautious (aḥwaṭ) and apparent position.
This position does not require believing that each individual sinner will be punished, only that for each sin type, some will be.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage is a multi-faceted commentary from al-Bahr al-Raiq on a line from Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding the Hafazah (guardian angels). It begins with a consensus statement on the hierarchy of creation. The author first establishes that prophets are the best, followed by specific archangels. Then he notes unanimous agreement that Companions, Followers, martyrs, and righteous are better than ordinary angels. The key intra-Hanafi dispute arises: whether other ordinary Muslims are better than ordinary angels. Imam Abu Hanifa says yes, citing Quran 13:23 where angels visit believers in Paradise, implying the visited (believer) is superior. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (Sahibayn) hold ordinary angels are superior. The author then explains the term 'Hafazah': it refers to angels who preserve human deeds or protect from harm, and can include all accompanying angels or specifically the two recording angels. He recounts a hadith describing five angels per believer with specific roles, and other narrations stating sixty or one hundred sixty angels. However, al-Hidayah advises not to intend a fixed number due to varying reports, analogous to belief in prophets. The passage then discusses whether the two recording angels change with day and night: some say yes based on the hadith of succession, others say no, they remain. Finally, various opinions on their physical location are given: mouth, palate, or right and left, and that the scribe of evil deeds leaves during defecation, intercourse, and prayer (as added by al-Qurtubi). Throughout, the author presents rival views and occasionally notes the preferred position, such as Imam Abu Hanifa's view being the relied-upon one.

الحفظة (al-ḥafaaẓah): Guardian angels; the term is used for the angels assigned to every person to record deeds and protect from harm. The passage notes two interpretations: specifically the two scribe angels, or all accompanying angels including protectors.
الملكين الكاتبين (al-malakayn al-kātibayn): The two scribe angels, one on the right (recording good deeds) and one on the left (recording evil deeds). The passage discusses whether they change with night and day or remain, and their physical location.

The Hierarchy of Imamate: The Dispute Between Knowledge and Recitation
Explaining the ranking of who is most deserving to lead prayer with proofs and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi hierarchy for imamate and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf concerning the precedence of knowledge over recitation.

Core Idea
In imamate, knowledge of prayer rulings takes precedence over recitation, as it benefits all pillars.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq expounds on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding who is most deserving to lead the prayer. It establishes a clear hierarchy—most knowledgeable, best reciter, most pious, eldest—and delves into an intra-Hanafi disagreement: does the most knowledgeable (a'lam) take precedence over the best reciter (aqra'), or vice versa? By the end of this lesson, you will understand the proofs on both sides and the Hanafi tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hierarchy and the point of dispute
The author states the order: a'lam, aqra', awra', asan. Then the commentary focuses on the contest between a'lam and aqra'. Note that Abu Yusuf differed with Abu Hanifa on this point.
The hierarchy is the base ruling; the dispute is about the relative priority of the first two.
2
Analyze the dalil for the opposing view
Abu Yusuf relied on the hadith 'Let the one most learned in Allah's Book lead.' He interpreted 'aqra'ahum' as the best reciter. The hadith is sahih. Consider its apparent meaning.
This hadith is also used by Imam al-Shafi'i.
3
Evaluate the Hanafi response
Al-Hidayah responds that in early Islam, aqra' meant a'lam because the Companions learned rulings from the Quran. Today, recitation skill does not imply fiqh. Also, knowledge benefits all pillars, recitation only one. The hadith of Abu Bakr is preferred evidence.
The response uses historical context and a comparative analysis of the needs of prayer.
4
Define the remaining terms and conditions
The passage defines awra' as most avoidant of doubts, asan as earliest in Islam, and adds conditions for the imam: must avoid open sins, must be able to recite sufficient Quran, and the permanent imam is immune from being replaced.
These conditions refine the application of the hierarchy.
5
Examine the critical analysis of the author's reasoning
The passage concludes with a critique: the hadith only covers those who combine recitation and knowledge, not one who excels in only one area. Therefore, the author's deduction that knowledge alone is preferred is not directly from the hadith.
This is a nuanced point showing scholarly rigour in istidlal.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq is an expansion on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq concerning the hierarchy of who is most deserving to lead the prayer (imamate). The author begins by stating the ruling: the most knowledgeable (al-a'lam) is most entitled, then the best reciter (al-aqra'), then the most pious (al-awra'), then the eldest (al-asan). Additionally, it is disliked (makruh) to appoint a slave or a Bedouin as imam. The commentary then focuses on the definition of 'knowledge' (al-ilm) intended here: it is knowledge of the rulings of prayer—what validates and invalidates it—not general fiqh. This is because the source of prayer rulings is the Sunnah, not the Quran directly. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa (as per the text) prefers the most knowledgeable, while Abu Yusuf prefers the best reciter based on a hadith from the Sahihayn. The author of al-Hidayah responds that in early Islam, the best reciter was also the most knowledgeable because they learned rulings from the Quran; today, recitation skill does not imply knowledge of fiqh, so precedence is given to knowledge. Additionally, knowledge benefits all parts of prayer, while recitation only benefits one pillar. The hadith of Abu Bakr is cited as preferred evidence: the Prophet ordered Abu Bakr to lead, even though there were better reciters present, indicating that knowledge (of rulings) takes precedence. The passage also discusses conditions for being 'a'lam': the imam must avoid open sins, and if he is not the permanent imam (imam ratib), the permanent imam is more entitled regardless of knowledge. Also, the imam must be able to recite the Quran to the extent required for valid prayer (the obligatory and Sunnah amounts). The commentary on 'al-aqra' clarifies that it means the most memorizer of Quran, not the best reciter in terms of tajwid, though the latter is also considered. The distinction between wara' (avoiding doubtful matters) and taqwa (avoiding prohibitions) is explained. The eldest (al-asan) is defined as the earliest in Islam, not merely in age, based on the hadith of Malik ibn Huwayrith and the principle that longer life in obedience indicates greater virtue. A minor contrary view from al-Muhit is mentioned but not adopted. The passage concludes with a critical analysis of the author's reasoning regarding the hadith's application to those who are solely knowledgeable or solely reciters, suggesting that the text only applies when both qualities are combined. Finally, the requirement for the imam to memorize at least the wajib amount of Quran is reaffirmed.

Key Terms
الأعلم (al-a'lam): The most knowledgeable in the rulings of prayer (what validates and invalidates it) and the Sunnah related to it.
الأقرأ (al-aqra'): The best memorizer of the Quran (or best reciter with tajwid) – the commentator prefers the first meaning.
الأورع (al-awra'): The most avoidant of doubtful matters (shubuhat).
الأسن (al-asan): The eldest in Islam (earliest in conversion or longest in submission), not necessarily in age.

Explains ruling when an illiterate person leads a literate person or substitutes an illiterate in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the validity of prayer when an illiterate imam leads a literate follower, explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah) of the dispute.

Core Idea
When an illiterate imam leads a literate follower, Abu Hanifa deems the prayer invalid; the Sahibayn consider it valid.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about congregational prayer where the imam cannot recite the Quran (ummi) and the followers include a literate person (qari). The ruling hinges on whether the imam is considered 'capable' of recitation through the presence of a literate follower. Abu Hanifa and his two distinguished students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on this point. We will follow the reasoning in Al-Hidayah and Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq to understand the arguments and the practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
Determine the exact scenario: an illiterate imam leads a congregation that includes a literate person, or an illiterate imam substitutes another illiterate in the last two rak'ahs. The matn states the prayer is invalid.
The term 'illiterate' (ummi) means one who cannot recite al-Fatiha from memory.
2
Extract the Two Hanafi Positions
Abu Hanifa holds the prayer is invalid (fasid). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold it is valid (sahih). Note that the folower's prayer is also affected.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf; both sides are within the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the imam abandoned the obligatory recitation despite being able to perform it. By leading, he had the capacity to recite because the follower's recitation could have been his if he had followed. Therefore, omitting recitation invalidates his prayer.
Key premise: ability (qudrah) to recite through following a literate person is present.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's Proof
The Sahibayn analogize to a naked imam leading both naked and clothed followers. Just as the naked imam is excused (ma'dhur) from covering, the illiterate imam is excused from recitation. They argue the imam is excused, so the prayer is valid.
The comparison (qiyas) is based on the imam's incapacity to perform the act himself.
5
Determine the Thamarah (Practical Consequence)
The difference affects whether the prayer must be repeated. According to Abu Hanifa, both imam and follower must repeat. According to the Sahibayn, no repetition is needed. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is generally the Sahibayn's view for ease, though some later scholars preferred Abu Hanifa's caution.
The text in Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq notes a conflict; the author does not give a definitive tarjih here.

An illiterate person and a literate person [praying] behind an illiterate person, or if he substitutes an illiterate person in the last two [rak'ahs], their prayer is invalid. His statement: (If an illiterate and a literate follow an illiterate, or he substitutes an illiterate in the last two, their prayer is invalid) As for the first issue, it is according to Abu Hanifa. They (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: The prayer of the imam and the one who did not recite is complete because he is excused; he led an excused and non-excused people, so it is like when a naked person leads naked and clothed people. And his (Abu Hanifa's) evidence is that the imam abandoned the obligatory recitation while being able to perform it, so his prayer becomes invalid. This is because if he followed a literate person, that person's recitation would be his recitation, unlike that issue and its likes, because what exists in the right of the imam does not exist in the right of the follower. He restricted with 'following' because if the illiterate prays alone and the literate prays alone, it is permissible; that is the correct view because they did not show desire for congregation. Thus in al-Hidayah. And in al-Nihayah: If the illiterate starts [prayer] then a literate arrives, there are two opinions. And if the illiterate arrives after the literate has started, and he does not follow him and prays alone, the correct view is that his prayer is invalid. And he indicated by 'invalidity of prayer' the validity of the literate's commencement, because they are equal in the obligation of the opening takbir. They differed only in recitation. And it cannot be said: why is the follower not required to make up [the prayer] when he invalidated it, while his commencement was valid? We say: When he started the prayer of an illiterate, he obligated upon himself a prayer without recitation, so he is not required to make it up, like a vow to pray without recitation is not binding except in a narration from Abu Yusuf. And he gave it unrestrictedly, so it includes whether the illiterate knows that behind him is a literate or not, and that is the apparent narration because obligations do not differ by situation. The follower repeats. Impurity less than a dirham, and the follower believes it is a preventer while the imam does not [so believe], he repeats. And in the opposite case, and the imam does not know that, he does not repeat. And if one of them follows the other and there is a drop of blood, and each claims it is from the other, his prayer is invalid in any case. Thus in al-Bazzaziyyah.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the validity of prayer when an illiterate person (أمي, who cannot read the Quran) leads a literate person (قارئ) in congregational prayer, or when an illiterate imam substitutes another illiterate in the last two rak'ahs. The ruling is presented first as a matn: the prayer is invalid. Then the commentary explains the ikhtilaf. Abu Hanifa holds the prayer invalid because the imam abandoned the obligatory recitation (فَرْض القِراءة) despite being able to fulfill it through following a literate person in the congregation. Since the illiterate imam showed intention for congregation (by leading), he is considered capable of recitation via the follower, so omitting it invalidates his prayer. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold the prayer valid because the imam is excused (مَعْذور) when leading others who are also excused or not. They analogize to a naked person leading both naked and clothed people. Abu Hanifa's reasoning is that if the illiterate imam had followed a literate (as a follower), the literate's recitation would count for him, but here the illiterate is leading, so the follower's recitation does not benefit him. The commentary elaborates on conditions: when they pray separately it's valid, when the illiterate starts and a literate arrives (two opinions), and when the literate starts and an illiterate arrives (invalid if the illiterate prays separately). The later part discusses the follower's obligation to repeat prayer if there is disagreement about impurity amount. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the Sahibayn's view? Actually, the text says 'authenticated in al-Dhakhirah' the invalidity, but the Hidayah's reasoning favors validity. The author notes a conflict. The practical consequence (thamarah) is that if the illiterate leads a literate, the prayer is invalid according to Abu Hanifa, but valid according to the Sahibayn. In fatwa, the view of the Sahibayn may be easier for people.

Key Terms
أمي (ummī): A person who cannot read or recite the Quran from memory; in this context, one who cannot recite al-Fatiha in prayer.
قارئ (qāri’): A literate person who can recite the Quran properly in prayer.
استخلف (istakhlafa): The act of the imam appointing a substitute to lead the rest of the prayer, e.g., in the last two rak'ahs.

The Legal Definition of Ihtilam: Sign, Not Essence of Puberty
Clarifies whether ihtilam means puberty or dream, with linguistic and juristic discussion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the linguistic and technical meanings of ihtilam and explain why it is considered a sign of puberty rather than puberty itself.

Core Idea
Ihtilam is a sign of puberty, not its definition — confusing the two leads to legal error.

This lesson clarifies a common terminological confusion in Hanafi fiqh: whether the term ihtilam (wet dream) means puberty (bulugh) itself or is merely one of its signs. Al-Nahr's commentary points out that equating ihtilam with puberty contradicts its linguistic origin and leads to redundancy in standard legal formulations. We will trace the correct understanding through lexicographical and juristic analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested claim
Locate the statement 'Thus ihtilam is puberty' from the text. This is the claim that al-Nahr critiques.
2
Analyze the linguistic objection
Examine al-Nahr's use of lexicographers: ihtilam originally means 'what the sleeper sees' (a dream), not the state of being mature. Understand that language usage can shift (taghlub) but the core meaning remains.
Linguistic shift: from specific dream to general term for nocturnal emission.
3
Examine the legal implication
If ihtilam meant puberty, the phrase 'puberty by ihtilam' in al-Quduri would be redundant, akin to saying 'puberty by puberty'. This reveals the logical problem.
4
Evaluate the forced interpretation
The motive for equating ihtilam with puberty was to explain why sleep is mentioned alongside it. Critique this as a 'takalluf' (forced effort) that misrepresents the term.
Sheikh Isma'il notes that al-Inayah and al-Mughrib correctly treat ihtilam as a metonym for reaching puberty.
5
Conclude the correct legal meaning
Synthesize: ihtilam is a sign (amarah) of puberty, not the thing itself. This preserves linguistic coherence and legal precision.

His statement: (Thus ihtilam is puberty.) Al-Nahr said: There is something to consider in this, because the lexicographers say that ihtilam is a name for what the sleeper sees, then it became predominant for what he sees of a particular kind. Commentary
This lesson clarifies the legal definition of ihtilam (احتلام), a term that appears in the context of invalidators of prayer (wet dream) and the onset of puberty. The passage begins by quoting a statement that 'ihtilam is puberty,' but al-Nahr (a commentary) objects lexically, noting that ihtilam literally means a dream, especially a nocturnal emission, not puberty itself. If it meant puberty, then the standard phrasing in works like al-Quduri (that puberty of a boy is by ihtilam, impregnation, or ejaculation, otherwise at age eighteen) would be redundant. The commentator suggests the motivation for equating ihtilam with puberty is the mention of 'sleep' alongside it in the matn, but this is a forced interpretation. A clearer approach, as in al-Inayah and al-Mughrib, is that ihtilam originally means 'one who dreams' but later became a metonym for one who reaches puberty. Thus, in legal contexts, ihtilam is used to indicate one of the signs of puberty, not the definition of puberty itself.

Key Terms
الاحتلام (al-ihtilam): The state of having a nocturnal emission (wet dream); used as a sign of puberty.
البلوغ (al-bulugh): Puberty; the age of legal responsibility (taklif).
