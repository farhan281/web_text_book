Estimating the Tuhr for a Mustahada: The Hanafi Resolution of an Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Examination of different scholarly estimates for the clear period between bleeding episodes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five scholarly estimates for the tuhr of a mustahada, explain the reasoning behind the two-month estimate, and apply it to calculate the iddah in a typical scenario.

Core Idea
When no clear habit exists, Hanafi jurists estimate the tuhr as two months—the easiest fatwa for women and muftis alike.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq tackles a practical problem: a woman with continuous bleeding (mustahada) who cannot distinguish her menstrual from pure days. Without a fixed habit, how long is her waiting period after divorce? Scholars differed, but the Hanafi school settled on an estimate of two months for the pure interval, based on the recurring custom of menstruation. This lesson unpacks the dispute, the reasoning, and the final ruling (mufta bihi).

Guided Path
1
List the estimates
Identify the five different estimates mentioned: no estimation (endless iddah), one year, six months minus an hour, six months plus ten days (Zayla'i's addition), and two months (Muhammad ibn al-Hasan).
2
Trace the reasoning for two months
Understand the evidence: transmission from Muhammad via Ibn Sam'ah, customary recurrence (ada), two months establishes a habit.
Custom is derived from the majority of women menstruating once a month.
3
Identify the tarjih mechanism
Why did al-Hakim al-Shahid choose two months? Because it is easier for the mufti and women.
The principle of ease (al-yusr) governs the fatwa.
4
Calculate the iddah
Apply the two-month tuhr to compute the waiting period: three tuhrs = 6 months, plus three menses = 1 month, total 7 months.
This is the straightforward calculation from al-Shurnublaliyyah.
5
Examine the refinement
Note the additional nuance from al-Siraj if divorce occurred at the start of menstruation, adding ten days minus an hour.
This shows the precision of Hanafi jurists in accounting for realistic scenarios.

Commentary
This passage addresses a key problem in Hanafi fiqh: how to determine the tuhr (clean interval) for a woman who experiences continuous bleeding (mustahada) and has no known habit or cannot differentiate. Without a definitive text, scholars differed on the estimation. The author lists several views: some refused any estimation, leading to an endless iddah; the majority estimated one year; Al-Maydani estimated six months minus an hour; Al-Zayla'i considered adding ten days; but the relied-upon view is from Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan, transmitted by Ibn Sam'ah, that the tuhr is estimated as two months. This is based on the principle that custom (ada) transfers after repetition, and since most women menstruate once a month, two consecutive months of purity establish a new habit. The fatwa is on this view because it is easiest for the mufti and the woman. The Shurnublaliyyah explains that with a two-month tuhr, the iddah (waiting period for divorce) ends after seven months: three tuhrs (6 months) plus three menstrual periods (1 month). However, Al-Siraj adds a qualification: if the divorce occurred at the beginning of menstruation, that menstrual period is not counted, so the iddah becomes seven months and ten days minus an hour. This shows the precision of Hanafi jurists in applying the rules to realistic scenarios.

Key Terms
طهر (ṭuhr): The clear, blood-free period between two episodes of menstruation or postpartum bleeding.
عدة (ʿiddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of husband before she can remarry.
مستحاضة (mustahāḍah): A woman experiencing prolonged or continuous bleeding outside the normal menstrual period.
