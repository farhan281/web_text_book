Hajj Rulings for a Menstruating Woman: Tawaf and Marital Relations
Rulings on tawaf and marital relations during hayd in Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a menstruating woman regarding tawaf al-ziyarah, tawaf al-sadr, and the use of tahari to determine purity for marital relations.

Core Idea
A menstruating woman must delay tawaf al-ziyarah until after purity, but tawaf al-sadr is not required; tahari in matters of private parts is disputed.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq, the author addresses three connected issues for a woman experiencing menstruation during Hajj: the obligation of tawaf al-ziyarah, the exemption from tawaf al-sadr, and the marital relations during the uncertainty of purity. The central question is how to navigate the tension between ritual obligations and the natural state of menstruation, with a nuanced intra-Hanafi disagreement on using guesswork (tahari) to determine permissibility of intercourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rulings
Read the passage and list the three distinct legal rulings it contains: (1) tawaf al-ziyarah must be performed after purity, (2) tawaf al-sadr is not required for a menstruating woman, (3) the husband should not approach his wife by guesswork (tahari) to avoid intercourse during menses.
These rulings are interlinked by the common factor of hayd.
2
Analyze the basis for tawaf al-ziyarah
The passage calls tawaf al-ziyarah a rukn (pillar) and states she repeats it after ten days (i.e., after purity). Identify the ruling: it is wajib/rukn and requires purification. Note that if she were pure, she would perform it immediately; otherwise she delays.
The 'ten days' implies the maximum duration of hayd, after which she must do ghusl.
3
Analyze tawaf al-sadr
Tawaf al-sadr (farewell tawaf) is not repeated if she was pure at that time; if she is menstruating, it is not obligatory upon her. Recognize the exception: hayd lifts the obligation.
This differs from tawaf al-ziyarah, which remains obligatory.
4
Examine the tahari dispute
The passage presents two views: (1) tahari is not permissible in matters of private parts (furuj), (2) some scholars allow it because the time of purity is more prevalent. Identify the reasoning for each view and note the analogy with animal hides.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the first view is more cautious.
5
Apply the rulings to a scenario
Consider a woman who begins her menses on the day of tawaf al-ziyarah. Determine: she must wait until her menses ends (up to 10 days), perform ghusl, then do tawaf al-ziyarah. If she also misses tawaf al-sadr due to hayd, it is not required. For marital relations, the husband should avoid intercourse until certainty of purity, and he cannot rely on tahari.
This integrates all three rulings.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hajj rituals for a woman experiencing menstruation. It states that tawaf al-ziyarah (the obligatory tawaf) is a rukn (pillar) and she must perform it after purification, even if it means repeating it after ten days (i.e., after her menses ends). Tawaf al-sadr (the farewell tawaf) is not repeated if she is pure at the time, but if she is menstruating, it is not obligatory upon her. The passage also addresses the issue of a husband having intercourse with his wife during the period when she is possibly menstruating: he should not approach her to avoid the risk of intercourse during hayd, and he must not rely on guesswork (tahari) to decide if she is pure, because guesswork is not valid in matters of private parts (furuj) according to one view. However, another view from some mashayikh allows guesswork because the time of purity is more prevailing, and permissibility is given precedence. The passage cites al-Muhit for this.

Key Terms
طواف الزيارة (tawaf al-ziyarah): The obligatory circumambulation of the Kaaba performed after standing at Arafah on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah; a rukn of Hajj.
تحري (tahari): Guesswork or ijtihad to determine the ruling when the exact situation is unclear
طواف الصدر (tawaf al-sadr): The farewell tawaf performed when leaving Mecca; wajib for those not residing in Mecca.
ركن (rukn): A pillar or essential component of an act of worship; omission invalidates the act.

Two Rak'ats of Tawaf: Like Nazir Prayers, Following Prohibited
Ruling on following in the two rak'ats of tawaf and refutation of a partial-following exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on following in the two rak'ats of tawaf and explain why it is analogous to nazir prayers, and evaluate the dispute regarding partial following.

Core Idea
Two rak'ats of tawaf are like nazir prayers: each is a separate obligation, so following is invalid.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a specific ruling: one who prays the two rak'ats of tawaf cannot follow another person who is also praying the two rak'ats of a different tawaf. The author compares this to the case of nazir (vowed) prayers, where each vow is unique. The text then engages with a counterargument about partial following and refutes it, affirming the absolute prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the opening sentence: 'And the two rak'ats of tawaf are like the nazirs.' What is the ruling being established? The ruling is that one who is praying the two rak'ats of tawaf cannot act as a follower behind another who is also praying the two rak'ats of a different tawaf.
The analogy to nazir highlights the individualized obligation.
2
Understand the analogy
Why are the two rak'ats of tawaf likened to nazir prayers? Because each tawaf is a separate act of worship with its own specific obligation, just as each vow is unique. Thus, there is no unity of intention between the imam and the follower.
Unity (ittihad) is a condition for the validity of following in prayer.
3
Trace the dispute
The text mentions a counterargument: some claimed the prohibition of fard behind nafl only applies to the entire prayer, not to partial following. This is attributed to Muhammad al-Shaybani in one narration. The evidence cited is the case of a substitute (khalifah) who performs two prostrations that are considered nafl.
The counterargument relies on a specific case to limit the prohibition.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response denies the premise. The two prostrations of the substitute are not nafl; they are actually fard for him because he assumes the imam's obligation. Therefore, the counterargument fails. The general prohibition against a fard follower behind a nafl imam remains absolute.
This refutation is supported by al-Fath and al-Nahr.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is absolute prohibition: it is invalid for one praying the two rak'ats of tawaf to follow another, whether for the entire prayer or any part of it. This is the view taken by the majority of Hanafi scholars.
Mufta bihi: the operative ruling for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling for the two rak'ats of tawaf (circumambulation) around the Kaaba. They are considered like the prayers of those who made a vow (nazir) because each tawaf is a separate act; thus one praying the two rak'ats of his own tawaf cannot follow another person praying the rak'ats of a different tawaf, as unity is lacking. The commentary then addresses a counterargument: some claimed that the prohibition of following the fard behind nafl only applies to the entire prayer, not a part. The refutation states that the prohibition is absolute, and the example of two prostrations performed by a substitute (khalifah) are not nafl but fard for him. The final part mentions that the objection is baseless.

Key Terms
ركعتي الطواف (rak'atay al-tawaf): Two rak'ats prayed after completing tawaf; considered wajib in the Hanafi school.
الناذر (al-nadhir): One who has vowed to pray a specific prayer; his prayer becomes obligatory but specific to him.
