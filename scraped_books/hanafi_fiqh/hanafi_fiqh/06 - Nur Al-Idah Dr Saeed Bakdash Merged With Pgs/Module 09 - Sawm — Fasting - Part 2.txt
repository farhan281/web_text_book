Lists excuses for breaking fast and defines valid fear.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six circumstances that permit breaking the Ramadan fast and explain the condition for valid fear (al-khawf al-mu‘tabar).

Core Idea
Breaking the fast is permitted only under specific valid excuses: illness, fear of harm, severe hunger/thirst, or travel.

This passage lists the lawful excuses that allow a fasting Muslim to break their fast during Ramadan. These exemptions are based on necessity and fear of harm to oneself or others. The ruling is a permission, not an obligation, and is subject to conditions that ensure the fear is genuinely grounded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general categories
Note that the passage lists three main categories: sickness, pregnancy/nursing fears, and extreme hunger/thirst. Travel is separate.
The sick and traveler are mentioned first; pregnancy/nursing is a subcategory under fear.
2
Examine the subcases for pregnant/nursing women
Fear may be for loss of intellect, death, or illness affecting the woman herself or her child (biological or through breastfeeding).
The child can be either nasaban (lineage) or rada‘an (by suckling).
3
Understand the condition of valid fear
Fear must be based on strong probability (ghalabat al-zann), derived either from personal experience or from the report of a skilled, just Muslim doctor.
The doctor must be Muslim, skilled (hadhiq), and just (‘adl).
4
Apply the ruling for the traveler
The traveler is permitted to break the fast, but it is more beloved (ahabbu) to fast if it does not cause hardship.
This reflects the Hanafi preference for fasting when able, though breaking is allowed.

A traveler may break the fast, but fasting is preferred unless harm, group consensus, or shared expenses dictate otherwise.

This passage from Nur al-Idah clarifies the Hanafi position on fasting while traveling. While breaking the fast is permissible, the school encourages fasting when it does not cause harm and when the traveler's company is generally fasting and not sharing expenses. The ruling balances individual discretion with social harmony within the travel group.

Fasting is more beloved (mustahabb) only when three conditions are met: (a) it does not harm the traveler, (b) the majority of the traveling companions are not breaking the fast, and (c) the companions are not sharing expenses.

If any condition fails—especially if the majority of companions are breaking the fast or if expenses are shared—then breaking the fast is preferred (afdal) to maintain unity with the group.

Rulings on Bequest and Making Up Missed Fasts
Axiomatic rulings on bequest and qada for fasts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five rulings concerning bequest and making up missed fasts, and explain when bequest is not obligatory.

Core Idea
No bequest if death before excuse ends; qada can be spaced and yields to the next Ramadan.

This passage from Nur al-Idah clarifies the obligations for those who missed fasts due to a valid excuse and then died, and the rules for making up those fasts. It addresses whether a bequest for kaffarah is needed, the requirement to make up according to ability, the permissibility of non-consecutive qada, the priority of the next Ramadan, and the absence of fidya for delay.

Guided Path
1
Understand the bequest ruling
The text states that if a person dies while still having a valid excuse (e.g., illness, travel), they are not required to make a bequest for kaffarah because the obligation to make up the fasts was never established.
The footnote clarifies that 'bequest' here means instructing heirs to pay kaffarah for missed fasts.
2
Identify the qada obligation
Those who become able to make up missed fasts must do so for the number of days they broke, but only to the extent of their health and residence (i.e., when they are well and at home).
Qada is wajib upon those who are able.
3
Note that consecutiveness is not required
The text explicitly says 'tawaatu' is not required in making up.' This means the missed fasts can be made up on non-consecutive days.
This is a Hanafi position; other schools may differ.
4
Learn the priority rule when another Ramadan arrives
If the next Ramadan comes before all qada fasts are completed, the current Ramadan takes precedence. Qada should be delayed and completed after the current Ramadan.
This ensures the obligation of the current year is fulfilled first.
5
Understand no fidya for delay
Delaying qada until after the next Ramadan does not require a fidya (expiation payment), unlike some other rulings (e.g., delaying a missed prayer).
This applies as long as there was a valid excuse for delay (e.g., continuous illness).

Elderly Person's Permission to Break Fast with Fidya
Ruling on elderly who cannot fast: break and pay fidya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a decrepit elderly person who cannot fast and calculate the required fidya.

Core Idea
A permanently unable elderly person breaks fast without qada but must pay fidya: half a sa' of wheat per day.

Fasting is obligatory on every able Muslim, but Allah grants concessions for those with chronic incapacity. This lesson clarifies the ruling for the very old and weak (shaykh fani) who cannot fast at all. Unlike a temporary illness requiring makeup later, such a person is excused from fasting and instead compensates with fidya every day.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Who is the 'shaykh fani'? A man or woman so old that they can no longer fast without extreme hardship or harm. This is a chronic condition, not a temporary one.
The term 'fani' implies approaching death, not just old age.
2
Determine the permissibility
The text states 'yajuz al-fitr' – it is permissible to break the fast. It is not a sin or makruh; it is a legitimate concession.
This permissibility is conditional on genuine inability.
3
Establish the obligation
Fidya is wajib (obligatory) for each missed day. It is not merely recommended. The amount is half a sa' of wheat (or its value).
Half a sa' is approximately 1.6 kg wheat. Other grains may be used, but wheat is the default.
4
Distinguish from temporary excuses
Unlike a sick person who must qada when healthy, the shaykh fani has no hope of recovery. Hence fidya substitutes the missed fast permanently.
If the person later becomes able (unlikely), some opinions require qada, but the relied-upon view is fidya suffices.
