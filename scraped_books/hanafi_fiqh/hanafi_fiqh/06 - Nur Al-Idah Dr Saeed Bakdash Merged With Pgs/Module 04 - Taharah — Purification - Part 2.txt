Sunan of Wudu and Ruling for the Excused Person
Lists sunan and a footnote on ma'dhur wudu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list five sunan of wudu from the matn and explain the Hanafi ruling on wudu validity for a person with a chronic excuse (ma'dhur) when a prayer time enters.

Core Idea
Five sunan enhance wudu; a ma'dhur's wudu invalidates at prayer time entry — renew for each prayer.

This passage from Nur al-Idah presents two distinct but related fiqh points. First, it lists five recommended acts (sunan) that complete the wudu but are not obligatory. Second, a footnote clarifies an important ruling for those with chronic excuses: the Hanafi school ties the validity of wudu to the prayer times, requiring renewal for each. We will examine both aspects to solidify your understanding of the sunan and the special case of the excused.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five sunan
Read items 10-14 carefully. They list: (10) rinsing mouth (madmadah) and sniffing water (istinshaq) with the right hand, blowing nose (imitkhat) with left; (11) performing wudu before the time for the non-excused; (12) reciting the shahada after wudu; (13) drinking leftover wudu water standing; (14) saying the supplication. Note each act.
These are sunnah, not fard or wajib.
2
Understand the hand specifications
Item 10 specifies using the right hand for madmadah and istinshaq, and the left for imitkhat. This follows the general principle of using the right for clean actions and the left for removing impurities.
Using the opposite hand would be makruh tanzih.
3
Recognize post-wudu practices
Items 12-14 are after completing wudu: recite the shahada, drink the leftover water while standing, and say a specific supplication. These increase reward.
Drinking leftover water is recommended, not obligatory.
4
Distinguish sunan from fard/wajib
Omission of any sunnah does not invalidate wudu, but omission of fard or wajib would. The sunan are meritorious enhancements.
A common misunderstanding is treating sunan as obligatory.
5
Analyze the footnote on the excused (ma'dhur)
Read the footnote (1). It defines the ma'dhur's wudu rule: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, wudu is invalidated (intuqada) upon entry of the prayer time, unless there is a gap between times (e.g., between Fajr and Zuhr). If there is a gap, wudu remains valid until the next time enters. The Maliki school provides a concession: wudu is not invalidated, and renewal is recommended but not obligatory.
The Hanafi view is the relied-upon position in the text.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Six acts are disliked in wudu; extravagance and stinginess are opposites, both disliked.

This passage from Nur al-Idah lists six acts that are makruh (disliked) during wudu. Understanding these helps you avoid actions that reduce the reward or are near to prohibition. The footnotes clarify some nuances from later Hanafi authorities like Ibn Abidin and al-Tahtawi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six items
Read the list in order: 1) israf, 2) taqtir, 3) darb al-wajh, 4) speaking worldly talk, 5) seeking help without excuse, 6) tathlith al-mash with new water.
Memorise the sequence as given.
2
Note the nature of karahah
For each, note if the text specifies the type of dislike. Israf has a dispute (tahrimi or tanzihi). Darb al-wajh is tanzih. Seeking help is not disliked with an excuse.

Check exceptions for seeking help
The footnote from Ibn Abidin says there is no harm in seeking help; the dislike applies only when there is no excuse.
Understand the condition attached to item 5.

Understand tathlith al-mash
This means wiping each limb three times, each time with new water. The disliked aspect is the waste of water; reusing the water from the hand for successive wipes is preferred.
Contrast with the sunnah of triple washing of limbs, which does not require new water each time.

Core Idea
Six acts in wudu are disliked – not prohibited – and avoiding them perfects your worship.

The Hanafi school identifies six actions that are makruh (disliked) during wudu. These are not invalidators but acts that diminish the perfection of the ritual. Understanding them helps you perform wudu with greater care and reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The six acts fall into three pairs: water usage (israf, taqtir), physical actions (darb al-wajh, kalam al-nas), and social/ritual behaviors (isti'ana, tathlith al-mash).

Explain israf and taqtir
Israf (extravagance) is using too much water; taqtir (stinginess) is using too little. Both are disliked; moderation is recommended.

3
Explain darb al-wajh
Striking the face with water (darb al-wajh) is disliked because it wastes water and is against the proper gentle washing.
4
Explain kalam al-nas
Engaging in worldly speech (kalam al-nas) during wudu distracts from the act of worship and is therefore disliked.
This includes talking about business, entertainment, etc.
5
Explain isti'ana and tathlith al-mash
Seeking help (isti'ana) from others without a valid excuse (like illness) is disliked because wudu is a personal act. Repeating the wipe three times with fresh water (tathlith al-mash bi ma' jadid) is disliked; the sunnah is to use the same water for all three wipes.
If there is an excuse, isti'ana becomes permissible.

The Three Categories of Wudu: Fard, Wajib, Mandub
Classifying wudu into obligatory, necessary, and recommended based on purpose

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the three categories of wudu, IDENTIFY which acts require fard, wajib, or mandub wudu, and EXPLAIN the Hanafi distinction between fard and wajib.

Core Idea
Wudu is fard for prayer and Quran, wajib for tawaf, and mandub for sleep, sin, and renewal.

This lesson presents the classical Hanafi classification of wudu from Nur al-Idah. Understanding whether wudu is fard, wajib, or mandub determines the validity of worship and the reward. The passage lists specific acts that trigger each category, highlighting the Hanafi distinction between fard (definitive evidence) and wajib (probable evidence).

Guided Path
1
Memorize the three categories
Identify fard, wajib, and mandub as the three levels. Note that fard and wajib are both obligatory but differ in evidence strength; mandub is recommended.
Fard is proven by Quran and mutawatir hadith; wajib by ahad hadith or qiyas.
2
List the acts for fard wudu
From the passage: prayer (even supererogatory), funeral prayer, sajdat al-tilawa, and touching the Quran (even one verse).
These are acts for which wudu is a condition of validity.
3
List the acts for wajib wudu
Only tawaf of the Kaaba is mentioned as wajib.
Omitting wudu for tawaf invalidates the tawaf and requires a dam (sacrifice).
4
List the acts for mandub wudu
Sleeping with purity, waking up, maintaining wudu, renewing wudu, after backbiting, lying, slander, after any sin, and reciting vile poetry.
These are recommended; omitting wudu does not invalidate the act but loses reward.
5
Apply the distinction to new scenarios
Given a scenario (e.g., someone wants to touch the Quran), determine whether wudu is fard, wajib, or mandub based on the passage.
Consider the Hanafi ruling: touching the Quran without wudu is haram because wudu is fard.

Delaying Tayammum: When It Is Recommended and When It Is Obligatory
Ruling statement on delaying tayammum with two sub-cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two rulings on delaying tayammum (recommended vs obligatory) and apply them to scenarios involving hope or promise of water.

Core Idea
Delay tayammum if you hope for water, but you must delay if you have a promise of equipment — as long as you do not fear missing the prayer time.

The passage on 'Ta'khir al-Tayammum' provides nuanced guidance: it is recommended to wait when you hope water will arrive, but obligatory to wait when you have a concrete promise of a water-skin or clothing to fetch water. In both cases, the overriding condition is that the prayer time must not be at risk.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first case: recommended delay
If you hope to find water before the prayer time ends, it is recommended (mandub) to delay tayammum. This is a matter of preference to use water (the original purification).
2
Understand the second case: obligatory delay
If someone promises to provide a water-skin (to fetch water) or clothing (to protect against cold), it becomes obligatory (wajib) to delay tayammum, as long as there is no fear of missing the prayer time.
The difference is the certainty of obtaining water.
3
Identify the limiting condition for both
In both cases, if delaying would cause you to miss the prayer time, you must not delay — you must perform tayammum immediately. Fear of missing the time overrides both the recommendation and the obligation.

Core Idea
Tayammum has two pillars: wiping the face and hands; its cause is the intention to perform an act requiring purification.

This passage from Nur al-Idah introduces the chapter on tayammum (dry ablution). It provides a compact definition, clarifies that touching earth with intention suffices for the two strikes, lists two additional conditions specific to tayammum, and states its two pillars. It affirms the validity of wiping for minor hadath for both men and women, and lists seven conditions for permissibility. The text elaborates on the first three conditions: wearing after washing feet, covering the ankles, and being durable enough for walking. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly applying this dispensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling
The passage states that wiping over khuffs is valid (ṣaḥḥa al-masḥ) for minor hadath, for both men and women, even if the khuffs are made of thick material other than leather.
This establishes the permissibility of the practice.
2
Understand condition 1: Wearing after washing feet
The khuffs must be put on after washing the feet. It is sufficient if the feet are washed even if wudu is not yet complete, as long as wudu is completed before a nullifier occurs.
This ensures the feet are pure before covering.
3
Understand condition 2: Covering the ankles
The khuffs must cover the ankles, i.e., the area that is washed in wudu. This is necessary because wiping substitutes washing only the covered area.
If the khuffs are short, the uncovered part must be washed.
4
Understand condition 3: Durability for walking
The khuffs must be sturdy enough to allow continued walking for approximately 4.5 km (one farsakh) without tearing. This excludes fragile materials like glass, wood, or iron.
This condition ensures the khuff can be worn for travel, the primary context of the concession.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school accepts wiping over khuffs based on hadith, while Shafi’i and Maliki schools require washing the feet and do not consider wiping sufficient.
This highlights a legitimate ikhtilaf.

States the ruling, seven conditions, and duration for wiping over khuffs

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Wiping over khuffs is valid only when seven precise conditions and time limits are met.

This passage from Nur al-Idah lays out the Hanafi rules for wiping over leather socks (khuffs), a concession (rukhsah) that eases wudu. It specifies seven conditions to ensure the khuff is a proper substitute for washing the feet, along with the duration of the license for residents and travelers. Mastering these details is essential for correctly performing this common practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The passage states that wiping over khuffs is valid (sahih) for minor ritual impurity, for both men and women, and even if the khuffs are made of thick material other than leather. Understand that this is a substitute for washing the feet.
The ruling applies to any durable foot covering that meets the conditions, not just traditional leather.

3
Understand each condition's rationale
Each condition ensures the khuff effectively covers the foot and is durable enough to serve as a substitute for washing. For example, the tear limit ensures the water cannot reach the skin through a large hole.
The condition about walking one farsakh (~4.5 km) ensures the khuff is suitable for normal use without tearing.

The duration begins from the moment of hadath (minor impurity) after wearing the khuffs, not from the time of wiping. Residents may wipe for 1 day/night, travelers for 3 days/nights. If a resident travels during his duration, he completes the traveler's duration.
The duration is counted from the first time you break wudu after putting on the khuffs.

The editor adds that the khuff must be made of pure (tahir) material and not from impure substances like pigskin. Though not in the original matn, it is a necessary condition.
This condition is not explicitly listed by the Hanafis but is agreed upon.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
For wiping over khuffs to be valid, the khuffs must be intact, stay on unaided, block water, and cover the front foot.

The previous lesson covered the basic conditions for wiping over khuffs—like wearing them after full wudu and within the time limit. Here, four more conditions are added that concern the khuff itself: its material integrity, fit, waterproofness, and coverage of the necessary part of the foot. These ensure that the wiping, which substitutes for washing, is a genuine concession and not a loophole.

Guided Path
1
State the fourth condition
Identify the condition: each khuff must be free of a hole larger than three small fingers. Understand that a smaller hole is excused because water can still be blocked effectively.
The measure uses the small fingers of the hand, not the toes.
2
State the fifth condition
Identify the condition: the khuffs must stay on the feet without tying. This means they are tight enough to remain in place during wudu without additional straps.
If they slip off or require tying to stay, the condition is not met.
3
State the sixth condition
Identify the condition: the khuffs must prevent water from reaching the skin. They act as a barrier; if water seeps through, washing the feet becomes necessary.
Leather naturally blocks water; modern materials must be tested.
4
State the seventh condition
Identify the condition: the front part of the foot (muqaddam al-qadam) must be covered by at least three fingers' width of the khuff. If the front part is amputated or uncovered, wiping is invalid even if the heel is covered.
The front part includes the toes and the area up to the instep.

Wiping over khuffs is a concession that replaces washing the feet in wudu. But how long can you keep wiping? And when does the clock start? This lesson from Nur al-Idah gives the clear Hanafi answer – and also what to do if your travel status changes mid-period.

Recall: turban, cap, face veil, gloves. Memorize the Arabic terms.
Use the key terms to reinforce vocabulary.
2
Understand the ruling
Wiping over these items does not fulfill the obligation of washing the feet; the wudu becomes invalid.
3
Contrast with khuffs
Khuffs are foot coverings, and the dispensation is specific to them. Head and hand coverings are not analogous.
4
Apply the ruling
If someone wipes over any of these items, he must repeat wudu and wash his feet properly.

What is not permissible to wipe over: It is not permissible to wipe: 1. Over a turban. 2. Over a cap. 3. Over a face veil. 4. Over gloves. ***** (1) The author (may Allah have mercy on him) did not mention the issue of wiping over socks (jurabayn). Its summary: It is permissible according to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) to wipe over them if they are thick, not letting water through, and one can walk in them for one farsakh or more, and they stay up on their own. As for Imam Abu Hanifa, he used to say: It is not permissible to wipe over them unless they are leather-lined or soled. Then he retracted to their view in his final illness, and this is the basis for fatwa. See al-Hidayah 1/30, Ibn Abidin 2/196, al-Lubab by al-Maydani, and Ghunyat Dhuwi al-Ahkam fi Bughyat Durar al-Hukkam (hashiya of al-Shurunbulali on al-Durar wa al-Ghurar) 1/36, which mentions that the retraction by Imam Abu Hanifa is probable, not explicit from him, and this has been transmitted from al-Sarakhsi, al-Sadr al-Shahid, and Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement from the matn of Nur al-Idah listing four items that cannot be wiped over when performing wudu instead of washing the feet: the turban, the cap, the face veil, and gloves. These items are not considered like khuffs (leather socks) for the dispensation of wiping. The author's footnote explains a related ruling: wiping over ordinary socks (not leather) is a disputed issue. According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it is permissible if the socks are thick enough to prevent water penetration and can be walked in independently. Imam Abu Hanifa initially required them to be leather-lined or soled, but he reportedly retracted to the companions' view on his deathbed, and the fatwa is now on that position, though the retraction is not definitively established.

Key Terms
عمامة ('imamah): A turban wrapped around the head.
قلنسوة (qalansuwah): A cap or hood worn on the head.
برقع (burqu'): A face veil covering the face, often with eye openings.
قفازان (quffazan): Gloves covering the hands.

Key Takeaway
Wiping over head and hand coverings is not permissible in wudu; only foot coverings (khuffs) qualify for the dispensation.
