The Ruling on Wiping over Thick Socks (Jurabayn): An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains the intra-Hanafi dispute on wiping over thick socks.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mufta bihi Hanafi position on wiping over thick socks and explain the reasoning of the Sahibayn versus Imam Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Wiping over thick socks is permissible according to the relied-upon opinion, despite Imam Abu Hanifa's initial objection.

The allowance to wipe over khuffs in wudu raises the question of whether thick socks (jurab) qualify. This lesson examines the intra-madhhab dispute among Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples, clarifying the operative position for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the dispute
The issue is wiping over thick socks (jurabayn) instead of washing feet. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) permit it under conditions; Imam Abu Hanifa initially required leather lining/sole.
2
State the conditions for the Sahibayn's view
Socks must be thick, waterproof, stay up on their own, and allow walking for one farsakh or more.
These conditions ensure the socks function like khuffs.
3
Understand Imam Abu Hanifa's original position
He required socks to be leather-lined or soled (like mukhaffafat). This was a stricter view.
4
Note the retraction and its reliability
Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly retracted to the Sahibayn's view on his deathbed, but the retraction is not explicitly confirmed. Later scholars accept it for fatwa.
5
Apply the mufta bihi
The operative ruling is the Sahibayn's view: wiping over thick socks meeting the conditions is valid and suffices for wudu.
This is the position relied upon in fatwa.

Effect of Splint Falling Off or Being Replaced

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings when a splint falls off before healing and when it is replaced, and explain why rewiping is recommended but not obligatory.

Core Idea
The original wiping on a splint remains valid if the splint falls off or is replaced before healing.

This lesson addresses two common situations that may arise when a person has a splint and performs wudu by wiping over it. The Hanafi school provides clear guidance: the original wiping is not nullified by the splint falling off prematurely, and a new splint does not require a fresh wipe, though it is recommended to do so. Understanding these rulings prevents unnecessary repetition of wudu.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage presents two distinct scenarios: (1) the splint falls off before healing, and (2) the splint is replaced with another. Identify each scenario in the text.
2
State the ruling for the first case
For falling off: the wiping is not invalidated. This means the original wudu remains valid, and no rewiping is needed. State this ruling clearly.
The key phrase is 'لا يبطل المسح بسقوطها قبل البرء'.
3
State the ruling for the second case
For replacement: it is permissible to replace the splint, and rewiping is not obligatory. State this ruling, noting the recommended (afḍal) action to rewip.

4
Identify the recommendation
In the replacement case, although rewiping is not obligatory, it is better (afḍal) to do so. Recognize this as a recommended (sunnah) action, not a requirement.

Intention Not Required for Wiping Over Khuff and Splint
States that intention is not obligatory for wiping.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that intention is not required for wiping over the khuff, splint, and head in the Hanafi school, and explain the proof based on qiyas.

Core Idea
Wiping is purification with water and part of wudu', so no intention needed.

Many assume that every act of worship requires a specific intention. But the Hanafi school distinguishes between acts that are conditional on intention and those that are not. In this lesson, we examine the ruling on wiping over the leather socks (khuff), the splint (jabirah), and the head during wudu'. The text states that intention is not required for these acts, and provides a clear proof from qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts mentioned
The passage mentions wiping the khuff, splint, and head. Note all three are included in the ruling.
2
Recognize the ruling
The ruling is that intention (niyyah) is not required (yuf'qar ila) for these wipes. They are valid without a specific intention.
3
Analyze the first proof
First proof: Wiping is purification with water (taharah bi al-ma'), analogous to washing limbs in wudu'. In Hanafi, washing limbs does not require intention, so wiping follows suit.
This is qiyas on the general principle that purification with water does not require intention.
4
Analyze the second proof
Second proof: Wiping is part of wudu' (ba'd al-wudu'). Since wudu' as a whole does not require intention in Hanafi, its parts also do not require intention.
This is an argument from inclusion: the part takes the ruling of the whole.
5
Contrast with other schools
The passage implicitly contrasts with schools that require intention for wudu' and its parts, such as the Shafi'i school. Understanding this difference is important for fiqh comparison.

The Ma'dhur: Wudu Validity and Conditions of Excuse
Rulings on the ma'dhur's wudu and conditions for the excuse status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the ma'dhur's wudu and apply the three conditions for being, remaining, and ceasing to be a ma'dhur.

Core Idea
A ma'dhur's wudu lasts only until the prayer time ends; three conditions govern becoming, continuing, and ceasing to be excused.

The concept of a ma'dhur (excused person) is essential for those suffering from chronic conditions that break wudu. The Hanafi school provides clear rules to maintain ritual purity while accommodating the excuse. This lesson explains when a person qualifies as a ma'dhur, how their wudu is governed, and when the status ends.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on the ma'dhur's wudu
The passage states: 'The wudu of the excused person is invalidated only by the exit of the time.' This means the ma'dhur can pray multiple prayers with one wudu within a single prayer time, and the wudu is not broken by the occurrence of the excuse itself.
Contrast: a non-ma'dhur's wudu breaks immediately upon any hadath.
2
Understand the condition for becoming a ma'dhur (thubut)
To become a ma'dhur, the excuse must fill an entire prayer time without a pause long enough to perform wudu and the obligatory prayer. If the excuse stops for that duration, the person does not achieve ma'dhur status.
This is a condition of establishment (shart al-thubut).
3
Understand the condition for continuing as a ma'dhur (dawam)
Once established, the ma'dhur status continues as long as the excuse occurs at least once in every subsequent prayer time.
Even a single occurrence within each time suffices.
4
Understand the condition for ceasing to be a ma'dhur (inqita')
The status ends when a full prayer time passes without any occurrence of the excuse. After such a clean time, the person is no longer a ma'dhur and their wudu breaks upon any hadath.
This is the condition for cessation (shart al-inqita').
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Maliki school holds that the ma'dhur's wudu is not invalidated by time exit; their wudu continues until a hadath occurs, regardless of time passing.
This difference impacts how long a ma'dhur can use one wudu.

Prayer of the Sick: Adapting to Ability
Listing rulings on sick prayer and make-up limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for how a sick person performs prayer and determine when missed prayers due to insanity or unconsciousness must be made up.

Core Idea
Prayer never falls away due to illness; only the method adapts to ability, and extended insanity limits make-up obligations.

This lesson covers the essential rulings on the prayer of the sick from Nur al-Idah. The school emphasizes that the obligation of prayer remains even when one cannot perform the physical movements; the method is adjusted according to what one can do. You will learn when to pray sitting, when to use head gestures (ima'), and the critical distinction between building upon a partial prayer versus starting anew. Additionally, the lesson addresses the unique rule for making up prayers missed due to insanity or unconsciousness, which is limited to a maximum of five prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the ruling concerns the obligatory prayer (salat) for a person who is ill or incapacitated. The prayer is still wajib, but the method of performance is tailored to the individual's ability.
2
Understand the hierarchy of ability
Determine if the person can stand, bow (ruku), and prostrate (sujud). The order: if unable to stand, pray sitting; if unable to bow/prostrate, use head gestures (ima'); if unable to gesture with head, do not use eyes or eyebrows.
Gesturing with eyes is not valid in the Hanafi school.
3
Apply the rule for building or restarting
If a person prays sitting but is able to bow and prostrate properly, the prayer is valid and they can build upon it (continue from where they left off). If they were using head gestures (ima') and later recover ability, they must start the prayer anew.
This distinction is based on whether the person performed the rukn actions correctly or by substitute.
4
Apply the rule for missed prayers due to insanity/unconsciousness
If a person becomes insane or unconscious and misses five or fewer prayers (i.e., up to five), they must make them up after recovery. If they miss more than five, no make-up is required—the obligation is dropped beyond that limit.
This is a special dispensation; normal missed prayers due to forgetfulness or sleep must be made up regardless of number.

Doubt (Shakk) in Prayer: Invalidation and Frequent Doubt
Rulings on when doubt invalidates prayer and how to handle frequent doubt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which doubt invalidates prayer and apply the rule for frequent doubt according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Doubt invalidates prayer only when it is first-time or non-habitual; frequent doubt is managed by acting on predominant probability.

In prayer, occasional uncertainty about the number of rak'ahs can leave a worshipper confused. The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines: not every doubt breaks the prayer; only specific types do. This lesson unpacks those conditions and also covers the special case of frequent doubt, where the ruling is more lenient to avoid constant repetition of prayer.

Guided Path
1
Define shakk
Recall that shakk means equal probability between two possibilities (e.g., unsure if you prayed 3 or 4 rak'ahs). If one side is stronger (zann), it is not considered shakk.
Hanafi definition: equality of two sides.
2
Identify invalidation conditions
The prayer becomes invalid (batil) if the doubt occurs before completing the prayer AND it is either the first time such doubt has occurred (in that prayer) OR the doubt is not habitual for the person.
Both conditions must be met: doubt before salam + first occurrence OR non-habitual.
3
Rule for doubt after salam
If a person doubts after having already said the salam, the doubt is ignored; the prayer is considered valid. The exception is if he becomes certain of having omitted something.
Certainty overcomes doubt.
4
Apply rule for frequent doubt
When a person frequently experiences doubt in prayer (kathrat al-shakk), the ruling changes. He should act on his predominant probability (ghalabat al-zann). If no side is more probable, he takes the lesser number and sits after every rak'ah he thinks might be the last, reciting tashahhud.
Frequent doubt is defined by habit, not a set number.
5
Summarize the difference
Occasional, first-time doubt invalidates prayer; frequent doubt does not, provided the person uses the prescribed method to resolve uncertainty.
This preserves both the sanctity of prayer and ease for the worshipper.

In the Hanafi school, it is wajib (obligatory) on both the reciter and listener, with certain exceptions. Expedite the preparation, cover the 'awrah, and give wudu without mouth-rinsing unless the deceased was junub.

The preparation and washing of the deceased is a communal obligation with specific sunnah rulings. This lesson covers the key steps from the moment of death to the washing, highlighting the Hanafi positions on wudu, water, and special cases for children and those in a state of janabah.

Initial Rulings upon Death
Initial funeral rulings and prohibitions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the initial sunnah actions upon a person's death and identify the rulings on hand placement, Qur'an recitation, and informing others.

Core Idea
The deceased's hands are placed at his sides; placing them on the chest is impermissible as it resembles the People of the Book.

When a Muslim dies, certain actions are recommended immediately. This lesson covers the sunnah supplication, the placement of iron on the stomach, the position of the hands, the ruling on reciting Qur'an, and informing the community. These initial steps set the stage for the washing and burial.

Guided Path
1
Recite the Sunnah Supplication
Upon seeing the deceased, recite the prescribed dua: 'Bismillah wa 'ala millati rasulillah...' to ask for ease and a good meeting with Allah.
This dua is a sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ.
2
Place Iron on the Stomach
Place a piece of iron on the stomach of the deceased to prevent bloating. This is a practical measure.
Iron is used due to its weight and property.
3
Position the Hands at the Sides
Place the deceased's hands at his sides, not on the chest. The text explicitly prohibits placing them on the chest because it resembles the practice of the People of the Book.
Contrary to some common practices, the Hanafi school holds this as the sunnah.
4
Refrain from Qur'an Recitation Before Washing
It is makruh (disliked) to recite the Qur'an near the deceased until after he is washed. This ruling is specific to the time before washing.
After washing, recitation is recommended.
5
Inform People of the Death
There is no harm in informing the community of the death; in fact, it is desirable to allow them to attend the funeral prayer.
Announcement should be done without excessive lamentation.

The Washing Procedure (Ghusl al-Mayyit)
Detailed washing steps for the deceased.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the complete washing procedure for a deceased Muslim according to the Hanafi school and identify the recommended steps and substances.

Core Idea
Washing the deceased is a fard kifayah, and the detailed steps ensure the body is purified with care and respect.

After the initial actions, the body is prepared for washing. The Hanafi scholars have outlined a step-by-step procedure that includes using water boiled with lotus leaves, washing the head with marshmallow, and washing the body in a specific order. This lesson covers the entire process.

Guided Path
1
Hasten the Preparation
It is recommended to expedite the washing and burial of the deceased. The body is placed on a bench that has been fumigated an odd number of times.
An odd number is preferred (e.g., 3 times).
2
Cover the Private Parts and Remove Clothes
The private parts (awrah) must be covered, and the clothes are removed. This is for modesty.
Covering with a cloth is sufficient.
3
Perform Wudu with Exceptions
The deceased is given wudu (ablution) but without rinsing the mouth and nose unless he was in a state of janabah (major ritual impurity). If a child is too young to understand prayer, wudu is not given.
This wudu is similar to that for prayer but incomplete.
4
Wash with Sidr Water
Pour water that has been boiled with sidr (lotus leaves) or hurdh (a type of clay) over the body. If these are unavailable, use pure water (qarah).
Sidr water is preferred for its cleansing properties.
5
Wash the Head and Beard with Marshmallow
Use marshmallow (khatmi) to wash the head and beard. This is a specific cleaning agent.
Marshmallow is used for its gentle cleaning.
6
Wash the Body on Left and Right Sides
The body is laid on its left side and washed until water reaches the lower back. Then it is turned onto its right side and washed similarly.
This ensures complete coverage.
7
Seat the Body and Wipe the Stomach
Seat the body leaning back, gently wipe the stomach, and wash any discharge that comes out. Do not repeat the full wash.
This step empties any residual fluids.
8
Dry and Apply Perfumes
Dry the body with a cloth, then apply hanut (compound perfume) to the head and beard, and camphor (kafur) to the prostration points (forehead, nose, knees, feet).
These are sunnah practices.

Prohibited Actions and the Ruling on Spouses Washing Each Other
Prohibited acts and permissibility of spouse washing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the actions that are not performed on the deceased and state the ruling on a wife washing her husband according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Cotton is not used in washing the deceased, nails and hair are not cut, and a woman may wash her husband if she is in revocable iddah.

Not everything is permitted in the washing of the deceased. This lesson covers what should be avoided: the use of cotton (according to the apparent narrations), cutting nails or hair, and combing. Additionally, it addresses the specific ruling that a woman may wash her husband, provided she is still in the revocable divorce waiting period (iddah).

Guided Path
1
Understand the Ruling on Cotton
According to the apparent narrations (zahir al-riwayah), cotton is not used in the wash. Some scholars permit it, but the relied-upon position is not to use it.
Cotton here refers to using cotton balls to apply water, not the shrouding.
2
Know the Prohibition on Cutting Nails and Hair
The nails and hair of the deceased are not to be cut. This includes the mustache, but the text specifically mentions nails and hair.
Cutting would be considered mutilation.
3
Recognize That Combing is Not Permitted
The hair and beard are not combed. This respects the natural state of the deceased.
Combing may cause hair to fall out.
4
Understand the Ruling on a Wife Washing Her Husband
A woman may wash her deceased husband if she is in a revocable divorce (talāq rajʿī) waiting period. If the iddah has ended or the divorce is irrevocable (talāq bāʾin), the ruling may differ.
This is based on the hadith that Ali washed Fatima (as a special case) and analogy.
