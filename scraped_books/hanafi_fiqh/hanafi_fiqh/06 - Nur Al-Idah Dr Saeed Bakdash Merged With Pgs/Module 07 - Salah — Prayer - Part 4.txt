Prayer in and on the Kaaba
Rulings on prayer in/on Kaaba and following imam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the validity of prayer inside and on top of the Kaaba, and explain the conditions for valid following (iqtida) when the imam is inside the Kaaba.

Core Idea
Prayer inside and atop the Kaaba is valid in the Hanafi school, but following the imam requires correct orientation—turning one's back to the imam's face invalidates the prayer.

This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines special rulings regarding prayer in the holiest site in Islam. While the general rules of prayer apply, the uniqueness of the Kaaba introduces specific considerations for both the person praying and the person following an imam. Understanding these rulings clarifies how the Hanafi school balances validity with respect for the sacred house.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that both obligatory (fard) and voluntary (nafl) prayers are valid when performed inside the Kaaba. Similarly, prayer on top of the Kaaba is valid, but with an additional consideration regarding the sutra.
2
Understand the dislike for praying on top without a sutra
If one prays on top of the Kaaba without a sutra (a barrier in front), the prayer is valid but makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked) due to disrespect (isti'la) of being above the Kaaba. The sutra is not obligatory here, but its absence is discouraged.
The dislike is for the specific act of being above the Kaaba without a barrier, not for the prayer itself.
3
Analyze the orientation of the follower inside or on top
When both imam and follower are inside or on top of the Kaaba, the follower's prayer is valid unless he turns his back directly towards the imam's face. If he faces any other direction, it is valid.
This rule emphasizes proper etiquette of following (iqtida) – the follower must not be positioned with his back to the imam.
4
Determine the condition for following an imam from outside
It is valid for a person outside the Kaaba to follow an imam inside the Kaaba only if the door is open, allowing visual or auditory connection. If the imam is outside and the followers sit around the Kaaba, it is valid for most, except for the one closest to the Kaaba in the direction of the imam.
The excepted follower would have his back to the imam, which invalidates the following, similar to the earlier rule.
5
Summarize the key principles
The Hanafi school upholds the validity of prayer in relation to the Kaaba while maintaining respect for its sanctity. The rulings on orientation and door condition ensure proper connection between imam and follower.

Commentary
This passage contains rules regarding prayer in relation to the Kaaba (baytullah al-haram). The first ruling (1) establishes that both obligatory (fard) and voluntary (nafl) prayers are valid when performed inside the Kaaba. Similarly, praying on top of the Kaaba is valid, but if one does not use a sutra (a barrier in front of the prayer space), it is makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked) because standing above the Kaaba shows disrespect (isā'at al-adab) towards the house of Allah. The second ruling (2) addresses the orientation of the follower relative to the imam when both are inside or on top of the Kaaba. If the follower faces any direction other than directly facing the imam's face, the prayer is valid; but if he positions himself so that his back is towards the imam's face, the prayer is invalid because it violates the proper etiquette of following (iqitidā). The fourth ruling (4) deals with following an imam who is inside the Kaaba while the follower is outside. This is valid provided the door is open, allowing visual or auditory connection. If the imam is outside and the followers sit around the Kaaba, it is also valid for most, except for those who are directly between the imam and the Kaaba (closest to the Kaaba in the direction of the imam), because they would have their backs to the imam.

سُتْرة (sutrah): An object placed in front of a person praying to prevent passersby from crossing in front of him; here, on top of the Kaaba, it is not required but its absence makes the prayer disliked.
استعلاء (istiʿlāʾ): Being above or on top of the Kaaba, which implies a degree of superiority over the sacred house, considered disrespectful.
الاقتداء (al-iqtidāʾ): Following the imam in congregational prayer.

Definition of Legal Travel Distance in the Hanafi School
Defines minimum travel distance for legal concessions and medium pace

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of legal travel distance, explain its components (duration, pace, terrain), and identify which rulings change with travel.

Core Idea
Travel concessions require a minimum of three days' journey at a medium pace.

In the Hanafi school, many rulings—such as shortening prayer or breaking fast—depend on whether one is a traveler (musafir). But not every departure from home qualifies. This lesson defines the precise threshold: a journey of three days at a medium pace, excluding night travel. Understanding this definition is essential for correctly applying the concessions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Components of the Definition
The passage defines legal travel as a journey of three days, from the shortest days of the year, at a medium pace, including rest stops. Note that night is not counted for travel—only daytime.
The shortest day ensures the threshold is not missed in winter.
2
Understand the Medium Pace
The medium pace (al-wasat) is defined as the pace of a camel or walking on foot on land. In mountains, the pace is adjusted because travel is slower. This accounts for terrain differences.
The pace is a standardized measure, not a precise speed.
3
Note the Exclusion of Farsakhs
The madhhab does not rely on farsakhs (distance units) because they vary with terrain. However, many scholars estimated the three days as 18 farsakhs (approx. 97.2 km), which is the fatwa position.
This resolves the intra-madhhab difference between those using days and those using farsakhs.
4
List the Rulings That Change with Travel
The passage lists: obligation to shorten prayer (qasr), permissibility to break fast in Ramadan, change in wiping duration over khuffs (3 days), dropping of Friday/Eid/sacrifice obligations, and obligation of mahram for women.
These concessions apply only when the travel meets the defined threshold.
5
Relate to Other Schools
Other madhahib (e.g., Shafi'i) define travel by distance (48 miles). The Hanafi definition by time is a key point of ikhtilaf.
Understanding the basis of the Hanafi position strengthens legal reasoning.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Legal travel is 3 days moderate walking; the relied-upon distance is 18 farsakhs (~97 km).

This lesson explains the Hanafi definition of legal travel as a journey of three days with moderate walking. While the school disregards fixed distances, later scholars determined the fatwa to be 18 farsakhs, which we convert to kilometers for practical understanding.

Guided Path

The established madhhab position disregards farsakhs, but many scholars used them. The most relied-upon fatwa is 18 farsakhs.

Rulings for performing salah when unable to stand, bow, or prostrate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a sick person's prayer when unable to stand, bow, or prostrate, and apply them to determine the correct method of prayer and the obligation of qada.

Core Idea
If standing, bowing, or prostration is genuinely impossible, the sick person prays by sitting or gesturing, and must still face the qibla.

This lesson covers the detailed Hanafi rules for performing salah in illness. The text from Nur al-Idah provides a step-by-step approach: from sitting when standing is impossible, to gestures when bowing and prostration are impossible, to lying down when sitting is difficult, and finally the dispute on whether missed prayers must be made up if gesture is impossible for an extended period. Understanding these concessions helps balance the obligation of prayer with genuine incapacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the degree of inability
Check whether standing is completely impossible (ta'azzur) or severely difficult (ta'assur). If it is, the sick person may sit. If partial standing is possible, he must stand for that portion.
The text defines three causes: severe pain, fear of worsening illness, or slow recovery.
2
Determine if bowing and prostration are possible
If ruku and sujud are impossible, the sick person sits and gestures with the head, making the gesture for sujud lower than for ruku. Failure to lower it invalidates the prayer.
Gesturing (ima') is a substitute for physical movement.

If sitting is also difficult (ta'assur), the next option is to gesture while lying on the back or side, with the back preferred. Place a pillow under the head to face the qibla, and bend knees if possible.

If even gesture is impossible, the prayer is delayed as long as the person understands speech. The sound view (sahih) holds that qada remains obligatory upon recovery. A secondary view drops qada after five consecutive missed prayers.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; both views are transmitted.

Making Up Missed Prayers for the Insane and Unconscious
Ruling on qada for insane/unconscious and gesture case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qada for missed prayers due to insanity or unconsciousness, distinguish it from the case of one who prays by gesture, and apply the five-prayer limit.

Core Idea
Insanity or unconsciousness for up to five prayers requires qada; beyond that, the obligation is waived.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from Nur al-Idah about the obligation to make up prayers when a person loses intellect or consciousness. The passage also addresses a separate case: a person who prays by gesture (imā') due to illness. Understanding the precise limits of qada and the principle of 'building' (binā') versus starting anew is essential for correct fiqh application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage presents two distinct scenarios: the first concerns a person who gestures in prayer (mu'mī), and the second concerns one who becomes insane (junna) or unconscious (ughmiya 'alayh). Note the different rulings for each.
The mu'mī case is introduced with 'la' (no), meaning he does not build upon his gesture prayer.
2
Parse the ruling for the insane/unconscious
The text states: 'five prayers: makes up; if more: no.' Extract the exact condition: if the insanity/unconsciousness lasts for exactly five prayers (or less), qada is wajib. If it lasts for six or more, only the first five are made up, and the rest are waived.
The number is liturgical: five prayers correspond to one day and night.
3
Understand the principle of building
The parenthetical note clarifies that the person does not 'build' (yabnī) upon his prior prayer state. Instead, he must start afresh when he recovers. 'Building' would mean continuing the prayer sequence, but here the break is too severe.
Contrast with cases where building is allowed, e.g., after minor impurity (hadath) in wudu.
4
Note the epistemological weight
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah, the core transmitted texts. It is the relied-upon (muftā bihī) position in the Hanafi school. However, other schools like the Shāfi'ī hold that all missed prayers must be made up regardless of number.
The difference stems from varying views on the obligation of qada when the cause (loss of intellect) removes accountability.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Imagine a person falls unconscious for seven consecutive prayer times. According to this text, he makes up only the first five and does not make up the sixth and seventh. If he were gesturing in prayer before fainting, that previous prayer is invalid for building purposes.
This highlights the practical application of the five-prayer limit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on praying a current fard while remembering a missed prayer, distinguish between the two outcomes (valid vs. nafl), and apply the concept of suspended invalidity (al-fasad al-mawquf).

Core Idea
A current fard prayed while remembering a missed prayer is suspended—it may become valid or nafl depending on when the missed prayer is made up.

This passage from Nur al-Idah addresses a nuanced situation: you are praying a current fard prayer, but you remember that you have a missed prayer (qada) that you haven't made up yet. In the Hanafi school, this does not automatically invalidate the current prayer. Instead, it triggers a suspended invalidity (al-fasad al-mawquf) which can be resolved by later actions. Understanding this ruling clarifies the delicate balance between the obligation to maintain order (tartib) between qada and current prayers and the practical need to avoid unnecessary repetition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Default Ruling
The act is praying a current fard while intentionally remembering a missed prayer. The text states that this causes fasad (invalidity), but that invalidity is mawquf (suspended)—meaning it is contingent on future events.
Suspended invalidity is a key Hanafi concept, unique in Islamic jurisprudence.
2
Understand the Two Scenarios
The outcome depends on whether the missed prayer is made up before the time of the fifth prayer (relative to the current prayer) passes. Scenario (a): If the time of the fifth prayer passes without making up the missed prayer, all five prayers become valid. Scenario (b): If the missed prayer is made up before that time, the five prayers lose their fard status and become nafl (supererogatory).
The 'fifth prayer' refers to the fifth prayer after the current one in the sequence, including the current prayer as part of the count.
3

The condition that determines the outcome is the timing of qada (making up the missed prayer) relative to the expiry of the time of the fifth prayer after the current prayer. If qada occurs before that time, the suspended invalidity becomes actual invalidity, but the fard is converted to nafl. If qada occurs after that time, the suspended invalidity is lifted and the prayers become valid.
This shows the Hanafi preference for flexibility and avoiding total invalidation.
4

Imam al-Halabi's example: A person missed Fajr, then prays Zuhr, Asr, Maghrib, Isha, and Fajr of the next day while remembering the missed Fajr. If he makes up the missed Fajr before the time of Zuhr of the next day (the fifth prayer after the sequence) expires, those five prayers become nafl. If he delays qada until after that time, the five prayers become valid.
Note that the 'fifth prayer' in this example is Zuhr of the next day, counting from the first prayed (Zuhr) to the fifth (next day's Zuhr).

Cutting Individual Prayer to Join Congregation
Detailed ruling set for cutting prayer to join congregation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for cutting a fard prayer to join the congregation, differentiating based on how many rak'ahs have been completed and the type of prayer.

Core Idea
Cut your fard prayer to join congregation, but the method depends on how many rak'ahs you have completed.

One of the great mercies of Islamic law is the encouragement to pray in congregation. Sometimes you begin an obligatory prayer alone, and then the congregation for that same prayer begins. Should you continue alone or join? The Hanafi school provides detailed guidance based on how far you have progressed in your prayer. This lesson will walk you through those rulings so you can act correctly in the moment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of prayer you began
Determine if it is a four-rakah prayer (Zhuhr, Asr, Isha), a three-rakah prayer (Maghrib), a two-rakah fard (Fajr), or a sunnah prayer (e.g., sunnah of Jumu'ah or Zhuhr).
The ruling differs based on whether it is a fard or nafl, and the number of rak'ahs.
2
Determine your current stage in the prayer
How many rak'ahs have you completed? Have you prostrated in the current rak'ah? Are you standing or sitting? The stage determines the exact action.
Key distinction: before vs. after prostration in the first rak'ah, and after first prostration in non-four-rakah vs four-rakah.
3

Match your situation to one of the scenarios listed. For example, if you have not yet prostrated in the first rak'ah, cut immediately with a taslim. If you have prostrated in a non-four-rakah prayer, cut after that prostration. For four-rakah prayers, add a second rak'ah if you prostrated once, etc.
Pay attention to exceptions: Asr prayer when you have prayed three rak'ahs, you complete it and do not join as mutanaffil.
4
Understand the intention after cutting
After cutting, you join the congregation with the intention of fard (if you had not completed a valid rak'ah count) or as a supererogatory worshipper (mutanaffil) if you completed the fard.
The terms muftariḍ (obligated) and mutanaffil (supererogatory) are used in the text.
5

If you are praying the sunnah of Jumu'ah or Zhuhr and the congregation starts, cut after two rak'ahs and make up the sunnah later. If you arrive while the imam is in the fard, join immediately and skip sunnah unless in Fajr and sure to catch the congregation.
This prevents missing the congregation reward.

Making Up Missed Sunnah Prayers
Rulings on making up missed sunnah prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which the sunnah of Fajr and the sunnah before Zhuhr can be made up according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Fajr sunnah is only made up if missed with fard; Zhuhr sunnah is made up in its time before the fard.

This passage from Nur al-Idah clarifies the Hanafi position on qada of sunnah prayers. While most sunnah prayers are not made up if missed, two specific sunnah prayers have a dispensation: the two rak'ahs of Fajr and the four rak'ahs before Zuhr. Understanding these exceptions is important for daily prayer practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sunnah prayers discussed
Note that the passage addresses only two sunnah prayers: the sunnah of Fajr (two rak'ahs before Fajr fard) and the sunnah before Zhuhr (four rak'ahs before Zhuhr fard).
Other sunnah prayers are not mentioned; the general rule is they are not made up.
2
Analyze the condition for Fajr sunnah qada
The text states: 'The sunnah of Fajr is not made up except by missing it together with the fard.' This means if you missed both the sunnah and the fard, you can make up the sunnah after the fard before sunrise.
If you only missed the sunnah but prayed the fard on time, the sunnah is not made up.
3
Analyze the condition for Zhuhr sunnah qada
The text says: 'He makes up the sunnah that is before Zhuhr, in its time, before its shaf'.' This means if you missed the sunnah before Zhuhr, you can make it up within the time of Zhuhr (i.e., before Asr time begins) and before the fard prayer (shaf' refers to the four rak'ahs of fard).
It can be prayed after the fard as well, as long as it is within Zhuhr time.
4
Contrast with the general rule
For most sunnah prayers, qada is not prescribed. The Hanafi school makes an exception for these two due to their strong emphasis (sunnah mu'akkadah) and the hadith evidence.

Catching Part of Congregation and Its Effect on Oaths
Ruling on catching the virtue of congregation and an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on catching part of a congregational prayer for the purpose of an oath, and distinguish the position on catching one rakah versus three rakahs.

Core Idea
Catching part of the congregation does not fulfil an oath to pray in congregation; you need the entire fard with the imam.

This passage from Nur al-Idah addresses a specific intra-Hanafi dispute regarding oaths. It clarifies that catching the virtue of congregation is not the same as catching the congregation itself. The ruling has practical implications for when a person swears to pray in congregation, as only performing the entire fard with the imam fulfills the oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath scenario
Consider a person who swears to pray Zhuhr in congregation. The ruling depends on how many rakahs he catches with the imam.
The oath is about the entire prayer, not just any part.
2
Analyze the first case – catching one rakah
If he catches only one rakah, the ruling is clear: he does not break his oath because he only prayed part of it in congregation. He obtains the virtue of congregation but not the full congregational prayer.
Based on the footnote: 'he does not break his oath because he did not catch the entire congregation'.
3
Analyze the disputed case – catching three rakahs (or two in Maghrib)
There is disagreement among Hanafi scholars. The apparent answer (zāhir al-jawāb) is that he still does not break his oath, as he prayed some rakahs alone. The opposing view holds that catching the majority suffices.
The text indicates that the apparent answer is the stronger view.
4
Understand the distinction between virtue and congregation
Catching any part of the prayer with the imam earns reward, but the legal definition of congregational prayer for oaths requires the entire fard to be performed with the imam.
This distinction is crucial for oath-related cases.
5
Note the tarjih (preferred view)
The later scholars (like Tahṭāwī) uphold the apparent answer: even catching three rakahs does not fulfil the oath. This is the muftā bihī position.
The tarjih is based on the principle that what remains (even one rakah) is prayed alone.

Ruling on performing voluntary prayer before fard with time constraint

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for performing voluntary prayers before a fard prayer and apply it to a time-constrained scenario.

Core Idea
Fard prayer takes priority over voluntary ones when time is limited.

This short passage from Nur al-Idah establishes a critical principle in Salah: the obligation of the fard prayer overrides the desirability of sunnah or nafl when there is a risk of missing the prescribed time. Understanding this priority ensures that obligations are fulfilled before optional acts, preventing sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its structure
The text gives a ruling: voluntary prayer before fard is allowed, but only under one condition. Identify the act (voluntary prayer) and its default recommendation, then note the condition that limits it.
This is a conditional ruling: if X, then do Y; otherwise, do not do Y.
2
Isolate the condition
The condition is 'if he is secure from the time passing' (amn fawt al-waqt). This means if the remaining time for the fard is sufficient to complete both the voluntary and the fard prayers, he may pray the voluntary first. Otherwise, he must skip the voluntary and pray the fard immediately.
Security here means certainty that performing the voluntary will not cause the fard to be delayed beyond its time.
3
Apply the condition to a scenario
Imagine the time for Zuhr is ending in 10 minutes. Praying the sunnah (e.g., 4 rak'ahs) would take about 8 minutes, leaving only 2 minutes for Zuhr (4 rak'ahs). Since Zuhr takes at least 5 minutes, the time is insufficient; the condition is not met, so he should not perform the sunnah.
The exact time needed varies, but the principle is clear: if performing the voluntary would cause missing the fard, it is prohibited.
4
Recognize the general principle
This ruling reflects the broader Hanafi rule that obligations (fard) take precedence over supererogatory acts (nafl). The same logic applies to all voluntary prayers before any fard, not just in congregation.
A corollary: if one starts a voluntary prayer and then realizes the time is ending, he should break it and pray the fard.
