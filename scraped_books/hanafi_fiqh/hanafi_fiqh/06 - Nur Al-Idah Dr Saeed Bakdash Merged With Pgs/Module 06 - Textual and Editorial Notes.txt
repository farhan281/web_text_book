The Value of Manuscript Colophons in Hanafi Textual Transmission
Scribal notes indicating completion dates and copyists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a classical Hanafi manuscript colophon and explain its role in establishing textual authenticity.

Core Idea
A colophon is a chain of custody for sacred knowledge.

This lesson examines two colophons from Hanafi fiqh manuscripts. Though brief, these scribal notes carry immense historical weight—they date the text, name the copyist, and link us to the transmission chain. Understanding them is essential for any serious student of Hanafi fiqh who works with manuscripts.

Guided Path
1
Read the Colophon Aloud
Read the Arabic lines carefully. Notice the structure: praise of Allah, name of copyist, date, and prayers.
Focus on the dating formula: يوم الاثنين ثامن عشر صفر سنة 1264.
2
Identify the Copyist and Date
Extract the name of the copyist (Muhammad Fa'izah) and the exact date (Monday, 18 Safar 1264 AH).
1264 AH corresponds to approximately 1848 CE.
3
Identify the Second Colophon
The second colophon is from an Azhari copy of Nur al-Idah dated 1278 AH. Note the phrase 'نسخة أزهرية' indicating its provenance.
Azhari copies often carry additional prayers and blessings.
4
Analyze the Prayers and Phrases
Observe the standard Islamic phrases: الحمد لله, سبحان ربك رب العزة, etc. These reflect the scribe's piety and the tradition of closing a manuscript with supplication.
Such prayers are part of Islamic manuscript culture.
5
Connect to Hanafi Manuscript Transmission
Consider how colophons help scholars verify the textual chain. For fiqh texts, the copyist's reliability and date are crucial for determining the version used by later commentators.
This is especially important for works like Nur al-Idah, a foundational Hanafi text.

Manuscript Verification in Hanafi Fiqh: The Manuscripts of Nur al-Idah
Describes manuscript copies used in the edition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two manuscript copies used in verifying Nur al-Idah and explain the importance of tahqeeq (textual verification) for preserving reliable Hanafi legal texts.

Core Idea
The legal reliability of a fiqh text depends on the authenticity of its manuscript chain.

Before studying the legal rulings in Nur al-Idah, it is essential to understand the textual foundation. This lesson introduces the manuscript copies relied upon by the editor to produce a reliable edition. Recognizing the role of manuscript verification (tahqeeq) helps you appreciate how classical Hanafi texts have been preserved and transmitted accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Manuscripts
Locate the names and dates of the two manuscript copies mentioned: an Azhari copy dated 1278 AH and a Saqizli copy without date.
The term 'Azhari' refers to the library of al-Azhar; 'Saqizli' likely designates a private collection.
2
Understand the Role of Tahqeeq
Explain that tahqeeq is the scholarly process of comparing multiple manuscripts to reconstruct the author's original text, ensuring accuracy for deriving rulings.
A corrupted manuscript can lead to incorrect legal conclusions.
3
Recognize the Impact on Fiqh
Reflect that the editorial choices affect every ruling in the book; a poorly verified text weakens the authority of the madhhab's transmitted positions.
This is why later Hanafi scholars stress relying on verified editions.
4
Sample Content Awareness
Note that the passage includes a sample excerpt on i'tikaf from the manuscript, not a new ruling. Distinguish between editorial description and substantive fiqh.
This avoids confusion when reading similar front matter.

Refutation of Two Innovations at the Kaaba
Condemns two popular Kaaba innovations as baseless bid'ah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the two innovations (al-urwah al-wuthqa and surrat al-dunya) and explain why they are considered false innovations.

Core Idea
Any practice introduced into religion without evidence is a false innovation (bid'ah).

This lesson addresses two popular but baseless practices involving the Kaaba: the belief that a high spot on the wall is the 'al-urwah al-wuthqa', and that a nail in the center marks the 'navel of the world'. The author of Nur al-Idah condemns them as bid'ah and foolishness. Understanding this helps you avoid innovations in worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the innovations
Read the passage and list the two beliefs/practices being condemned: the identification of 'al-urwah al-wuthqa' with a physical spot on the Kaaba wall, and the nail called 'surrat al-dunya' on which people place their navels.
2
Determine the legal classification
Note the author's ruling: 'bid'ah batilah la asla laha' (a false innovation with no basis). For the second practice, he calls it an act of one without intellect.
Bid'ah in religion is generally haram.
3
Understand the basis for rejection
Recognize that the rejection is because these have no basis in Quran or Sunnah. The term 'al-urwah al-wuthqa' in Quran refers to faith, not a physical spot. There is no evidence for the navel nail practice.
Any act of worship must be based on revealed evidence (tawqif).
4
Apply the ruling to similar cases
Use the principle that any innovation in religious rituals lacking evidence is prohibited. Be cautious of similar practices that might arise.
This is a key application of the bid'ah concept.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling against two widespread innovations (bid'ah) associated with the Kaaba. First, people claim that a high spot on the Kaaba's wall is the 'al-urwah al-wuthqa' (the firmest handhold) mentioned in the Quran, and that touching it brings spiritual benefit. The author declares this a false innovation with no basis in Islam. Second, there is a nail in the center of the floor of the Kaaba called 'surrat al-dunya'. People uncover their navel and place it on the nail, believing this connects them to the center of the world. The author strongly rebukes this as an act of ignorance and folly. The reference to 'al-Kamal' is to Kamal al-Din Ibn al-Humam, a major Hanafi jurist, who also condemned these practices. The lesson is that any practice introduced into religion without evidence from the Quran or Sunnah must be rejected, no matter how widespread. Muslims should avoid such innovations and adhere strictly to the prescribed rituals.

Key Terms
العروة الوثقى (al-urwah al-wuthqa): In this passage, a false attribution to a physical spot on the Kaaba wall; originally a Quranic term meaning 'the firmest handhold' (Islam)
سرة الدنيا (surrat al-dunya): A false belief that a nail in the center of the Kaaba marks the navel of the world; considered a baseless innovation
بدعة (bid'ah): An innovation in religion that has no basis in the Quran or Sunnah; here declared 'bāṭilah' (false)

Reading a Hanafi Fiqh Bibliography
List of scholarly references

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a Hanafi fiqh source by its title and author, and assess its authority level in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
A bibliography maps scholarly authority—knowing which texts are listed reveals the backbone of Hanafi fiqh.

The lesson presents a sample bibliography from Dr. Saeed Bakdash's study of Nur al-Idah. It includes core Hanafi works like al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Maraqi al-Falah, as well as hadith collections and biographical dictionaries. Understanding such lists helps you locate sources and judge their reliability.

Guided Path
1
Identify Core Hanafi Texts
Scan the list for well-known Hanafi fiqh works (e.g., entries 37, 35, 34, 40). Note the titles and authors.
Look for terms like 'Sharh', 'Hashiyah', 'Kanz', 'Durr'.
2
Classify by Genre
Determine whether each entry is a matn (core text), sharh (commentary), hashiya (gloss), or auxiliary (hadith, biography).
For example, al-Durr al-Mukhtar is a sharh; Maraqi al-Falah is a matn.
3
Assess Authority Level
Note the death dates (Hijri) and cross-references. Later works (e.g., al-Haskafi d. 1088) often build on earlier ones. The presence of a work by Ibn Abidin indicates reliance on the mutakhkhirin tradition.
Entries marked '=' show dependency (e.g., entry 37 = Hashiyat Ibn Abidin).
4
Note Relationships
Observe how entries reference each other. For instance, al-Durr al-Mukhtar is often studied with Ibn Abidin's gloss. This shows the layered nature of Hanafi scholarship.
Such linkages help you trace a ruling back to its original matn.

This lesson introduces you to the structure of Nur al-Idah, a classic Hanafi fiqh text focusing on salah. The table of contents (fihris al-mawdu'at) outlines the major chapters and sections.
