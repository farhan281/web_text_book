The Legislation and Virtue of I'tikāf
Doxology praising i'tikāf and its legislative basis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legislative basis of i'tikāf and explain its virtue according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
I'tikāf is legislated by the Quran and is a noble act of worship.

This lesson introduces the virtue of i'tikāf, explaining that it is established by the Qur'an and is one of the most excellent acts when done with sincerity. The doxology highlights the spiritual benefit of emptying the heart from worldly concerns and devoting oneself to Allah. Even without detailed rulings, the passage sets the foundation for understanding i'tikāf as a confirmed sunnah in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legislative source
Read the first phrase: 'والاعتكاف مشروع بالكتاب' (i'tikāf is legislated by the Book). This directly states the Qur'an as the basis.

Recognize the virtue
The text calls i'tikāf 'من اشرف الأعمال' (among the noblest deeds). This indicates its high rank when performed with sincerity (إخلاص).
Virtue is linked to ikhlas; sincerity transforms the act into a superior form of devotion.
3
Understand the spiritual benefit
The doxology explains that i'tikāf empties the heart from worldly occupations and surrenders the soul to the Master (Allah).
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on spiritual purification through worship.
4
Analyze the simile
The mu'takif is compared to a person standing at a great door for a need, saying 'I will not leave until it is granted.' This illustrates persistence in supplication and devotion.
The simile underscores the patient, earnest nature of i'tikāf.
5
Connect to Hanafi fiqh
Based on this foundation, the Hanafi school classifies i'tikāf as sunnah mu'akkadah (confirmed sunnah), especially in the last ten days of Ramadan.
The ruling derives from the Qur'anic legislation and the Prophet's practice, as detailed in later texts.

Permissible Waters for Purification: Categories and Rulings
Lists seven pure waters and defines three of the five water categories with their rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the seven types of permissible waters and classify any given water sample into one of the five Hanafi categories based on its purity and purifying ability.

Core Idea
Seven natural waters are pure and purifying; used water is pure but cannot purify.

This lesson opens the Book of Purification with a fundamental classification: which waters are fit for ritual purification? The text lists seven natural sources and then categorizes waters into five types based on their legal purity and purifying power. Understanding these categories is essential for every act of wudu and ghusl.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the seven waters
Commit to memory the seven water sources: rainwater, seawater, river water, well water, melted snow, hail, and spring water. These are all absolute water (māʾ muṭlaq) as long as they remain unchanged.
They are ṭāhir muṭahhir.
2
Understand the three given categories
The text explicitly gives three categories: (1) pure and purifying, not disliked (absolute water); (2) pure and purifying, disliked (cat water, small amount); (3) pure but not purifying (water used to lift ḥadath or for qurba). The remaining two categories (mixed with pure substance, impure) appear in subsequent lessons.
Dislike is tanzīhī.
3
Apply the ruling to scenarios
For any water sample, first classify it: Is it from the seven sources and unchanged? Then it is absolute water. If a cat drank from it and it is less than two kulal, it is disliked but still pure and purifying. If it was used for ḥadath or qurba, it is pure but not purifying.
Use the rules for qillat (small quantity): less than two kulal.

The Five Categories of Water in Hanafi Fiqh
Classification of water categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories of water in Hanafi fiqh and identify which category applies to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Water's purity and purifying ability depend on its category; only absolute water is both pure and purifying.

In Hanafi jurisprudence, water is classified into five categories that determine whether it can be used for purification (wudu or ghusl). This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines three explicit categories, with the remaining two (najis and doubtful) inferred from the broader tradition. Understanding these categories is essential for ensuring valid purification in daily practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five categories
From the text and commentary, recognize that water is divided into five types: (1) pure & purifying (absolute), (2) pure & purifying but disliked (water drunk by certain animals in small amounts), (3) pure but not purifying (used water), (4) impure (najis), and (5) doubtful (mashkuk). Only the first three are explicitly detailed here.
The last two categories are covered elsewhere in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Understand the three explicit categories
Absolute water (ma' mutlaq) is pure and purifying without dislike. Water drunk by a cat or similar animals (e.g., free-range chicken, snake, mouse) is also pure and purifying but its use is disliked (makruh tanzihan) if: (a) the amount is small (qalil), and (b) other absolute water is available. Used water (musta'mal) that has been applied to remove hadath or for an act of worship is pure but cannot be used again for purification.
'Makruh tanzihi' means the action is not sinful but better to avoid.
3
Note the conditions affecting the ruling
The dislike for cat-drunk water depends on quantity (small becomes disliked, large remains permissible) and availability of alternative absolute water. If no other absolute water exists, the water becomes permissible without dislike. For used water, the key condition is that it has been used for purification and has separated from the limb.
The term 'qalil' in Hanafi usul refers to water less than 10×10 cubits in area (approximately 200 liters).
4
Apply the categories to practical scenarios
Practice classifying water samples: a bucket of rain water (absolute), a small cup from which a cat drank (disliked if other water available), water that drips from limbs during wudu (used water).
Always check the availability of other absolute water before ruling on disliked water.

Waters Invalid for Wudu and the Rule of Ghalabah
Listing invalid waters and defining ghalabah criteria

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which waters are invalid for wudu in the Hanafi school and apply the rule of ghalabah to determine when a mixture invalidates water's purifying property.

Core Idea
Water loses its purifying property when its natural fluidity is removed or when a dominant mixture alters its attributes.

This passage from Nur al-Idah presents two key rulings: first, the types of water that cannot be used for wudu (tree/fruit water and water whose natural properties are overcome); second, the detailed criteria for determining when a mixture dominates (ghalabah) and nullifies the water's purifying quality. The Hanafi school draws careful distinctions between solid and liquid mixtures, and within liquids, the number of attributes the additive possesses. Mastering these rules will protect you from invalid purification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of invalid water
Read the first two prohibitions: water from trees/fruits (even without pressing) and water whose natural properties (tab') are removed by cooking or by dominance of another substance.
Tree/fruit water is not water in the absolute sense; it is considered juice or plant fluid.
2
Understand tab' (natural properties) of water
The footnote defines tab' as thinness (riqqah), fluidity (sayalan), ability to quench (irwa'), and causing growth (inbat). If these are lost, water is no longer mutahhir.
These properties distinguish water from other clear liquids.
3
Distinguish ghalabah in solids vs. liquids
For solids, ghalabah occurs when the water loses its thinness and fluidity (becomes thick). Changing color/taste/smell alone does not harm. For liquids, the rule depends on how many attributes (color, taste, smell) the additive has.
Solids like saffron or fruit change attributes but do not cause ghalabah unless they remove fluidity.
4
Apply the attribute-based rule for liquids
If the additive has two attributes (e.g., milk: color and taste), appearance of one attribute in the water constitutes ghalabah. If it has three attributes (e.g., vinegar: color, taste, smell), appearance of two constitutes ghalabah. If the additive has no attributes (e.g., used water), any change? The text implies it is easily dominant.
This is a precise legal measure to determine when water's purity is compromised.

Dead Animal in a Well: Presumption and Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on retroactivity of impurity from a dead animal in a well

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi presumption for the time of impurity based on swelling, and distinguish between the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding retroactivity of impurity.

Core Idea
A dead animal contaminates a well from one day (or three if swollen) ago, but should past worship be repeated?

Finding a dead animal in a well raises practical questions: when did it fall? How far back does impurity affect our prayers and clothes? The Hanafi school presumes a time based on the animal's condition, but the Imam and his two companions disagreed on whether that presumption requires repeating past acts. This lesson unpacks both the presumption rule and the intra-madhhab dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual presumption
Note that the text presumes the time of fall based on the animal's state: if not swollen, from one day and night; if swollen, from three days and nights. This is a legal presumption (taqdīr) when the actual time is unknown.
This presumption is agreed upon by Imam and Sahibayn; the dispute is about its legal consequence.
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position
Imam holds that the well is impure from the presumed time of fall. Therefore, any wudu, ghusl, or washing of clothes done with that water in the presumed period is invalid. Prayers performed after such wudu/ghusl must be repeated. However, if only clothes were washed (without using the water for wudu/ghusl), only the clothes need rewashing—prayers are not repeated by agreement.
Imam applies the presumption retroactively, treating the past as having been in a state of impurity.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the well is impure only from the time of discovery. Therefore, no past acts (prayers, washings) need to be repeated or redone unless the exact time of fall is verified.
The Sahibayn base their view on the principle that rulings depend on knowledge and certainty; uncertainty about the past prevents obligation.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The difference matters when water was used before the discovery. Under Imam's view, many prayers may need repeating. Under Sahibayn, no repetition is required.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of the Sahibayn, as it is easier and avoids unnecessary hardship.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a well with a non-swollen dead animal discovered today, wudu was performed yesterday. According to Imam, yesterday's prayers are invalid due to impurity from one day ago. According to Sahibayn, yesterday's prayers remain valid because impurity starts only from today.
The student should be able to trace the practical outcome of each view.

The Obligatory Acts and Conditions of Wudu in the Hanafi School

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four fard acts of wudu, identify the conditions of obligation and validity, and apply them to a practical scenario.

Core Idea
Wudu has four fard acts; its conditions of obligation differ from conditions of validity.

This foundational matn from Nur al-Idah outlines the essential elements of wudu in the Hanafi school. Understanding the difference between what is fard, what is a condition of obligation, and what is a condition of validity is crucial for correct practice. This lesson will guide you through the precise rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four fard acts
Locate the statement 'أركان الوضوء أربعة' and list them: washing the face, hands with elbows, feet with ankles, and wiping a quarter of the head.
In Hanafi fiqh, the pillars (arkan) are identical to the obligatory acts (fara'id).
2
Understand the spatial limits
For the face, note the defined boundaries: length from forehead to chin, width between earlobes. For hands and feet, the elbows and ankles are included.
Abu Hanifa includes the area between earlobes; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad exclude the ears from the face.
3
Distinguish conditions of obligation from conditions of validity
The source lists seven conditions for obligation (e.g., sanity, puberty, Islam, ability, hadath, absence of menses, time constriction) and three conditions for validity (covering skin with pure water, cessation of invalidating states, removal of barriers).
Time constriction means wudu becomes wajib only when prayer time is about to expire, not at its entry.
4
Recognize the unique Hanafi position on wiping the head
The text specifies 'مسح ربع رأسه' (wiping a quarter of the head). This is a distinct Hanafi position; other schools require wiping the entire head.
This is a point of ikhtilaf (legitimate difference) among the madhhabs.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a person who is awake, Muslim, adult, sane, and has hadath. It is not yet constricted prayer time. Is wudu obligatory? No, because time constriction is a condition for obligation. If the time constricts, then it becomes obligatory.
Conditions of obligation must all be met for wudu to be wajib; conditions of validity must be met when performing wudu.

Conditions of Wudu (Shurut al-Wudu)
Lists the three conditions for wudu validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the three conditions for the validity of wudu and explain why each is necessary.

Core Idea
Wudu is invalid if any condition is missing: water must reach all skin, no ongoing nullifiers, and no barriers.

Wudu, the minor purification, is a prerequisite for prayer. But simply performing the actions is not enough—three conditions must be met for wudu to be valid. This lesson from Nur al-Idah lays out those conditions concisely. Understanding these shurut is essential for every Muslim to ensure their acts of worship are legally sound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine that the act being discussed is wudu (minor ablution) and that the passage lists conditions for its validity (shurut al-sihha).
Conditions are prerequisites that must exist before or during the act; missing any invalidates wudu.
2
Understand the concept of shurut
Shurut are external conditions, distinct from arkan (pillars). If a condition is absent, the act is void from the start. In contrast, a missing pillar is a defect requiring repetition.
Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between shurut, arkan, and wajibat.
3
Memorize the three conditions
The three conditions are: (1) covering the entire skin with tahur (pure and purifying) water, (2) cessation of everything that nullifies wudu (menstruation, postpartum bleeding, hadath), and (3) removal of any physical barrier that prevents water reaching the skin (e.g., wax, fat).
These are explicitly listed in the matn.
4
Note additional prerequisites
The passage also mentions the narrowness of time (diq al-waqt) and the absence of hayd and nifas as preceding matters. These are implied in the condition of cessation of hadath.
For women, wudu is not valid during hayd or nifas, even if they perform the actions.
5
Apply the conditions in practice
Before starting wudu, ensure: (a) you are free from hadath (minor or major), (b) there is no substance on your skin that blocks water, and (c) you use water that is tahur. If any condition is missing, wudu is invalid.
Common barriers: nail polish, paint, grease, or thick dough.

No Repeat Wiping After Hair Removal
States three rulings about not repeating wiping/washing after hair/nail removal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that wiping/washing is not repeated after shaving hair or cutting nails, and explain the underlying principle.

Core Idea
Once a limb is washed, shaving hair or cutting nails afterward does not obligate a repeat.

A common practical question: after performing wudu or ghusl, if you shave your head or trim your nails, must you wash those areas again? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer, based on the principle that the obligation was already fulfilled on the original surface. This lesson explains the ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The text explicitly states that wiping (mas-h) on the head or washing (ghusl) of a limb is not repeated after shaving hair (halq) from that spot. Similarly, washing is not repeated after cutting nails or trimming the mustache.
The ruling applies to both wudu and full-body ghusl.
2
Understand the Legal Principle
The obligation (fard or wajib) was fulfilled at the time of washing the original surface. Removing hair or nails does not create a new obligation because the action was already validly performed on the area that then existed.
This is based on the broader usul principle that obligations are not repeated due to changes after completion.
3
Contrast with a Common Misconception
Some may think that because new skin is exposed after shaving, the area must be washed again. The Hanafi school rejects this: the original washing suffices.
This highlights the distinction between the act and the later change in the limb's surface.
4

During wudu, a person washes his forearms, then shaves the hair on his arms. The washing is still valid; no need to repeat. During ghusl, a person washes his head, then shaves it. The ghusl is complete.
The same applies to cutting fingernails after washing hands.
