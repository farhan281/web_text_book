When Sujud al-Sahw Drops
Three causes that drop sujud al-sahw

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three causes that drop the obligation of sujud al-sahw and apply them to a given prayer scenario.

Core Idea
Sujud al-sahw can drop if sunrise, redness, or a breaking act occurs after salam.

Sujud al-sahw is normally wajib when you forget something in prayer. But what if you realize you missed it after saying salam, and then something happens that makes performing it impossible or inappropriate? In Hanafi fiqh, there are specific events that cause the obligation to drop entirely, so you don't have to worry about it. This lesson explains those events so you know when your prayer is complete without the extra sujud.

Guided Path
1
State the original ruling of sujud al-sahw
Sujud al-sahw is wajib when a mistake occurs. But it can drop if specific events happen after the salam.
This is the baseline: sujud is required unless a cause drops it.
2
Memorize the three causes in the text
1) Sunrise after salam in Fajr. 2) Redness of sun after salam in Asr. 3) Anything that prevents resumption (yamna' al-bina) after salam, like talking, eating, or leaving with intention to abandon.
These are the only ways sujud al-sahw drops in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Distinguish between the conditions
The first two are time-based; the third is act-based. Understand what 'prevents resumption' means: acts that break the continuity of the prayer state.
Not just any action; specific acts nullify continuity.
4
Apply to a scenario
Given a scenario of a person who forgets sujud al-sahw, check if any of these three causes occurred after salam. If yes, sujud drops; if not, sujud is still required.
This is the practical test.

Commentary
This passage lists the causes that cause the obligation of sujud al-sahw to drop after it was required. If a person forgets sujud al-sahw and gives salam, then before performing the sujud, one of these events occurs: (1) sunrise after the Fajr prayer salam (if sujud was due), (2) redness of the sun (i.e., during ‘Asr time, after the salam before sujud is performed), or (3) anything that prevents resumption of prayer (like talking, eating, or leaving the mosque with intention to abandon). In these cases, sujud al-sahw is no longer required, and the prayer remains valid without it. This is based on the principle that sujud al-sahw is a compensation that can be abandoned if the time for it has passed or if continuing is no longer possible.

Key Terms
سقوط (suqut): The dropping or lapsing of an obligation due to an impediment, not requiring performance.
يمنع البناء (yamna' al-bina'): Something that prevents building upon the prayer, such as intentional nullification of wudu, eating, or walking away.

Sujud al-Sahw for the Follower and Latecomer
Rulings on sujud al-sahw for followers and latecomers in congregation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on sujud al-sahw for the ma'mum and masbuq, and apply them to scenarios in congregational prayer.

Core Idea
The follower follows the imam's forgetfulness; the latecomer joins and then prostrates again if he forgets in his own qada.

In congregational prayer, the imam carries the burden of forgetfulness for the followers. But what happens when you join late? This passage from Nur al-Idah clarifies the obligations of the ma'mum and the masbuq regarding sujud al-sahw. Understanding these rules ensures your prayer remains valid even when forgetfulness occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four rulings
Scan the Arabic text and list the four distinct rulings it contains. This helps you see the full scope of the passage.
The rulings are: (1) ma'mum must follow imam's forgetfulness, (2) masbuq prostrates with imam then completes his prayer, (3) masbuq prostrates for his own forgetfulness in qada, except the lahiq, (4) imam does not perform sujud al-sahw in Jumu'ah and Eid.
2
Analyze the ma'mum's obligation
The first sentence states 'ويلزم المأموم بسهو إمامه لا بسهوه'. Understand why the ma'mum is bound by the imam's forgetfulness and not his own. Consider the hadith that the imam is a guarantor (al-Imam damin).
This is based on the principle that the follower's prayer is linked to the imam's; the imam's sujud covers both.
3
Trace the masbuq's sequence
The masbuq joins the imam's sujud al-sahw, then after the imam's salam, stands to complete his missed rak'ahs. Work through this sequence step by step, noting when he prostrates and when he stands.
The masbuq must perform sujud al-sahw with the imam even if he joined late; otherwise, his prayer may be deficient.
4
Distinguish masbuq from lahiq regarding qada
The text says if the masbuq forgets during his qada, he prostrates for it, but not the lahiq. Understand the difference between masbuq (joined late) and lahiq (joined on time but missed a unit due to sleep etc.). The lahiq does not perform sujud for his own forgetfulness because his situation is different.
The lahiq's prayer is considered continuous with the imam in a different way; some scholars say he does not do sujud in qada.
5
Note the exception for Jumu'ah and Eid
The last ruling says the imam does not perform sujud al-sahw in Jumu'ah and the two 'Eids. Consider the wisdom: these are large gatherings where sujud might cause confusion or the khutbah has already ended.
Hanafis generally hold that sujud al-sahw is not performed in these prayers, following the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him).

Forgetting the First Sitting (Qa'dah Ula): When to Return and Sujud
Rulings for one who forgets the first sitting in obligatory prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a forgetful person must return to the first sitting, identify when sujud al-sahw is required, and explain the Hanafi ikhtilaf on prayer invalidity after returning from full standing.

Core Idea
In fard prayer, return before full standing; muqtadi returns even after; sujud al-sahw depends on proximity to standing.

When you forget the crucial first sitting (qa'dah ula) in prayer, the rulings depend on whether you are praying fard alone, leading, or following, and on how far you have stood up. This lesson clarifies exactly when you must return, when you need sujud al-sahw, and whether returning after full standing invalidates the prayer. Understanding these distinctions helps you maintain the validity of your prayer while fulfilling the wajib of the first sitting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and context
Determine whether the prayer is fard (obligatory) or nafl. The first sitting is wajib in all prayers of three or more rak'ahs.
2
Apply the ruling for fard prayer
For a person praying fard alone or as imam: if they forget the first sitting, they must return to sitting as long as they have not fully straightened (stood upright). Full straightening is when the hands leave the knees and the back is erect.
If fully standing, they do not return and continue; no sujud al-sahw required.
3
Apply the ruling for muqtadi (follower)
For a follower in congregation: the ruling is like that of someone praying nafl—they must return even if fully straightened.
This ensures synchronization with the imam.
4
Determine sujud al-sahw requirement
Upon returning, if the person is closer to standing (i.e., had risen significantly but not fully), sujud al-sahw is wajib. If closer to sitting (had barely started rising), no sujud is required.
Distance is measured by the position of the body at the moment of remembering.
5
Evaluate the ikhtilaf on invalidity
If one returns after fully standing, there is ikhtilaf whether the prayer becomes invalid. The stronger Hanafi view (mufta bihi) is that it does not invalidate.
Some early authorities held it invalid due to the addition of a prolonged standing.

Forgetting the Final Sitting (Qa'dah Akhirah)
Ruling for forgetting the final sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for forgetting the final sitting, including the condition for returning and the obligation of sujud al-sahw.

Core Idea
Forgetting the final sitting requires return before the next sajdah, then sujud al-sahw.

In the Hanafi school, the final sitting (qa'dah akhirah) is fard (obligatory). If a person forgets it and stands up, the prayer is not immediately invalid. This passage gives the specific condition under which he must return and the subsequent action required. Understanding this ruling prevents unnecessary repetition of the prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act discussed is the final sitting (al-qu'ud al-akhir) in prayer. In the Hanafi school, it is fard (obligatory).
Distinguish between fard and rukn: final sitting is fard, not a rukn.
2
State the condition for return
If one forgets the final sitting and stands up, he must return to the sitting position only if he has NOT yet performed the sajdah (prostration) of the next rak'ah. The text says 'ma lam yasjud' (as long as he has not prostrated).
The 'next sajdah' refers to the prostration of the rak'ah he is about to perform after forgetting.
3
State the obligation after return
After returning to the sitting position, he must perform sujud al-sahw (prostration of forgetfulness) to compensate for the delay.
Sujud al-sahw is wajib in this case because a fard was delayed.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
In the Shafi'i school, the final sitting is considered a rukn (pillar). Forgetting it may require repeating the prayer if the prayer ends without it, whereas Hanafi allows return and compensation.
This difference affects the severity of the omission.

Adding an Extra Rak'ah to a Fard Prayer
Ruling on adding a rak'ah to a fard prayer turning it into nafl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when an extra rak'ah is added to a fard prayer and explain why no sujud al-sahw is required.

Core Idea
An extra rak'ah transforms fard into nafl; no sujud al-sahw needed.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: unintentionally adding a rak'ah during an obligatory prayer. The Hanafi school provides a unique ruling that the prayer does not become invalid but instead converts to nafl, allowing further adjustment. Understanding this ruling prevents unnecessary repetition and clarifies the role of sujud al-sahw.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is mistakenly adding an extra rak'ah to a fard prayer and completing the sajdah. The text begins with 'If he prostrates' meaning the extra sajdah is performed.
Focus on the moment after the prostration of the extra rak'ah.
2
Note the immediate effect
The fard prayer becomes nafl (supererogatory). This is a key transformation: the obligation is lifted and the prayer continues as voluntary.
This is the core ruling of the passage.
3
Permissibility of further addition
One may add another rak'ah to make the total even: for 'Asr (normally 4) add a sixth; for Fajr (normally 2) add a fourth. There is no dislike (karahah) in this addition.
The text specifies only 'Asr and Fajr, but qiyas covers other prayers.
4
Sujud al-sahw not required
The most correct view (al-ashah) is that no sujud al-sahw is performed. This is because the prayer is no longer fard, and the forgetfulness is compensated by the transformation.
An internal Hanafi ikhtilaf exists, but the mufta bihi is no sujud al-sahw.

Ruling on Mistakenly Standing After Final Tashahhud
Ruling on mistakenly standing after final tashahhud and possible compensations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for standing after final tashahhud in two scenarios and apply the compensation (sujud al-sahw) when required.

Core Idea
Standing after tashahhud without prostration requires return; with prostration, add a rak'ah and do sujud al-sahw.

In salah, after completing the final tashahhud, the prayer is nearly complete. However, a person may forgetfully stand up, thinking there is another rak'ah. This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines the Hanafi ruling for two distinct scenarios: one where no prostration has occurred, and one where a prostration has been performed. Understanding these rulings helps preserve the validity of the prayer while compensating for the mistake.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage describes two cases: (1) the worshipper stands after final tashahhud but has not yet performed a prostration (sajdah) for the extra rak'ah; (2) the worshipper has already performed a prostration (i.e., began the next rak'ah).
The key is whether a sajdah has been performed after standing.
2
Determine the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1: the worshipper returns to sitting and gives salam without repeating the tashahhud. For scenario 2: the prayer remains valid; the worshipper adds another rak'ah (so that the two extra rak'ahs form a pair) and then performs sujud al-sahw.
Adding another rak'ah is only required if a prostration was made; otherwise, no extra rak'ah is needed.
3
Understand why the prayer is not invalidated
In both cases, the fard prayer remains sahih (valid) because the mistake was forgetful (sahw) and not intentional. The extra movement or rak'ah can be compensated through sujud al-sahw, which covers the forgetfulness.
The Hanafi school is lenient with sahw; the prayer is not nullified by an extra action if it is followed by the prescribed compensation.
4
Apply the compensation mechanism
In scenario 2, after adding the extra rak'ah, the worshipper performs sujud al-sahw at the end. This prostration rectifies the forgetful addition and delay.
The sujud al-sahw is wajib in this case.

تكبير التشريق (takbir al-tashriq): The takbir recited after fard prayers from Fajr of Arafa (9th of Dhul Hijjah) until Asr of the 13th of Dhul Hijjah.
مصر (misr): A major city with a congregational mosque and authority figures.

Key Takeaway
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is that Takbir al-Tashriq is wajib upon every person who performs any fard prayer during the prescribed days, regardless of being alone or in congregation.
