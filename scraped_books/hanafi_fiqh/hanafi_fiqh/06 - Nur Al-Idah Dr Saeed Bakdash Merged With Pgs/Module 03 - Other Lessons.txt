Renewal of Faith and Marriage in Hanafi Fiqh
Manuscript colophon with supplication for renewal of faith and marriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tajdid al-iman (renewal of faith) and explain when tajdid al-nikah (renewal of marriage) is necessary.

Core Idea
Apostasy dissolves marriage; renewal of faith requires renewal of nikah.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rulings on renewing faith (tajdid al-iman) and marriage (tajdid al-nikah). Though the source passage is corrupted, it highlights a practice where these renewals are sought. In Hanafi law, renouncing Islam (apostasy) invalidates one's faith and also dissolves the marriage contract. Upon repentance and return to Islam, the individual must renew the marriage contract with their spouse. We will explore the conditions and implications of these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Define tajdid al-iman
Renewal of faith: a declaration of belief and repentance from disbelief. In Hanafi fiqh, it is wajib (obligatory) upon an apostate and recommended as a precaution for others.
Distinguish between wajib (obligatory) and sunnah (recommended) levels.
2
Identify the effect of apostasy on marriage
Apostasy (riddah) from Islam invalidates the marriage contract immediately in the Hanafi school. The couple becomes unlawful to each other.
This is different from the Shafi'i view where marriage is not dissolved.
3
State the condition for tajdid al-nikah
If the apostate returns to Islam and the spouse wishes to remain married, a new marriage contract (tajdid al-nikah) must be performed. The spouse must be Muslim or convert.
The 'idda period may also apply.
4
Differentiate between fard, wajib, and recommended
In Hanafi usul, tajdid al-iman is wajib for an apostate (based on zanni evidence). Tajdid al-nikah is also wajib if the marriage is to be revived. For others, repeating the shahada is recommended.
Wajib means omission is sinful but does not invalidate faith in itself.
5
Apply to a scenario
A Muslim utters a clear word of kufr. He must repent, renew his faith by reciting the shahada with intention, and if married, renew the marriage contract with his wife.
The renewal of nikah requires a new mahr and witnesses.

Visiting the Cemeteries of Madinah
recommended visitation of Madinah cemeteries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on visiting the graves of Madinah and identify the recommended etiquettes upon visiting.

Core Idea
Visiting the graves of Madinah is mustahab, not obligatory, and includes greeting and supplication.

This passage from Nur al-Idah details the recommended practice for pilgrims and visitors to Madinah after completing their visit to the Prophet's mosque. It specifies key individuals to visit, such as Hamza, the martyrs of Uhud, and the family of the Prophet, and provides an example of a supplication to recite.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage begins with 'wa yustahabbu' indicating the act is mustahab (recommended). The act is going out to Baqi' and visiting graves.
Mustahab is a level of recommendation, not obligation.
2
Note the specific individuals mentioned
The passage lists: Hamza (master of martyrs), al-Abbas, al-Hasan, Uthman, Ibrahim (son of the Prophet), the Prophet's wives, Safiyyah, and other Companions and martyrs of Uhud.
These are major figures of Islam buried in Madinah.
3
Recognize the etiquettes of visitation
The visitor should give the Islamic greeting (salam) and then supplicate. The passage provides a sample dua, but one may say what they wish.
Greeting the deceased is a sunnah based on the Prophet's example.
4
Understand the sample supplication
The dua asks for removal of distress and sorrow, modeled on the supplication of Prophet Dawud. It includes sending blessings on the Prophet and praises of Allah.
This is an example; one is not bound to recite it exactly.
5
Distinguish from Hajj rituals
Visiting graves in Madinah is a recommended practice but it is not a part of the Hajj or 'Umrah rites.
Many pilgrims mistakenly think these visits are obligatory.

Recommended Visits in Medina (Mustahab Acts)
Colophon listing mustahab acts of visiting holy sites in Medina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended (mustahab) acts of visiting holy sites in Medina according to the Hanafi school and distinguish them from obligatory acts.

Core Idea
Visiting holy sites in Medina is mustahab, not wajib — a recommended devotion, not a required ritual.

This passage from Nur al-Idah concludes the work with guidance for visitors to Medina. It lists recommended acts (mustahab) such as visiting the Prophet's pulpit, the Baqi' cemetery, and the martyrs of Uhud. Understanding these acts as recommended (not obligatory) is crucial to avoid distorting their legal status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Classification
The text uses 'مستحب' (mustahab). In Hanafi fiqh, mustahab is equivalent to sunnah ghayr muakkadah: an act whose performance earns reward but whose omission is not sinful.
2
List the Specific Recommended Acts
The passage mentions: placing the hand on the Prophet's pulpit, visiting the Baqi' cemetery (including the grave of Hamza, al-Abbas, al-Hasan, Uthman, Ibrahim, wives of the Prophet, aunt Safiyyah, Companions and Followers), and visiting the martyrs of Uhud with recitation of greetings and supplications.
3
Note the Order of Visitation
The text suggests first visiting the Prophet's relics (pulpit, pillar), then Baqi', then the martyrs of Uhud. This order is based on transmitted practice.
4
Recognize the Basis of These Acts
These acts are derived from the practice of the Salaf (early Muslims) and are not obligatory (wajib) nor part of the Hajj rituals. They are additional voluntary devotions.
5
Differentiate from Obligatory Acts
Unlike the obligatory acts of Hajj (e.g., Tawaf, Sa'i), these visits are not required. A person who does not perform them does not incur sin or deficiency in their Hajj.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Sajdat al-Shukr
States Abu Hanifa's and the two companions' positions on prostration of gratitude

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on sajdat al-shukr and identify the mufta bihi ruling with its basis.

Core Idea
Sajdat al-shukr evolved from makrūh (Abu Hanifa) to mustahabb (later fatwa) — an intra-madhhab tarjīh.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute over the prostration of gratitude (sajdat al-shukr). While Imam Abu Hanifa considered it disliked, his two chief disciples viewed it as a meritorious act. Later Hanafi authorities reconciled these views and settled on a recommended ruling. Understanding this dispute illustrates how the school's positions are refined over time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the rulings of Abu Hanifa (makrūh, no reward) and the Sahibayn (qurbah, rewarded).
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's reasoning
He said it is not obligatory because blessings are continuous, making perpetual obligation an impossible burden.
This reasoning is preserved in al-Muhīt.
3
Examine the interpretations of Abu Hanifa's statement
Four possible meanings of 'I do not consider it anything' were given, ranging from 'not a sunnah' to 'makrūh'.
The fourth interpretation (makrūh) was attributed to the majority.
4
Identify the later tarjīh
Authorities like Ibn Abidin and al-Durr al-Mukhtār ruled that the dispute is over sunan status, not permissibility, and the mufta bihi is mustahabb.
Al-Ṭaḥṭāwī also preferred this.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Abu Hanifa's view gives no reward; the mufta bihi gives reward. Both agree it is not obligatory.

Core Idea
A man's sunnah shroud is three pieces: shirt, izār, wrapper; the turban is disliked.

Preparing the deceased for burial is a communal obligation (fard kifāya). The Hanafi school prescribes a specific shroud for a man, distinguishing between the complete sunnah and the sufficient minimum. This lesson details each piece, preferred materials, and practices to avoid.

Guided Path

Identify the two categories of shroud
The passage distinguishes between sunnah (recommended) and kifāya (sufficient). The sunnah is the complete set, while kifāya is the minimum that fulfills the obligation.

The sunnah shroud for a man consists of three pieces: a shirt (qamīṣ), a waist-wrapper (izār), and a large wrapper (lifāfa). These should be from the type of cloth he wore in life.

List the sufficient pieces
The kifāya shroud requires only two pieces: an izār and a lifāfa. The shirt is not obligatory.

White cotton is preferred. Both the izār and lifāfa must extend from the head to the foot (min al-qarn ilā al-qadam).

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Idah is a concise listing of recommended acts (mustahabbat) for a pilgrim in Medina after performing the Hajj or Umrah. It is a ruling_statement text, providing instructions without explicit proofs, as these are established from the Sunnah. The author first describes the visit to the Prophet's Mosque (al-Masjid al-Nabawi). The visitor should go to specific pillars: the pillar of Abu Lubaba, where the Companion tied himself in repentance; the Rawdah (the area between the Prophet's grave and his pulpit, considered a garden of Paradise); the pulpit itself, placing a hand on its pomegranate-shaped top in remembrance of the Prophet's hand; and the weeping pillar, which marks the tree trunk that wept when the Prophet left it for the pulpit. These actions are for tabarruk (seeking blessing). Then the passage urges reviving nights in devotion and making frequent visits to the Prophet's presence.
