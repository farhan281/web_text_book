Ruling and Virtues of I'tikaf
Passage stating the legality and virtues of i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling of i'tikaf and explain its spiritual benefits as presented in the text.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is a sunnah act of seclusion for worship, legislated by Quran and Sunnah, offering spiritual purification.

In this lesson, we examine a concise passage from Nur al-Idah that establishes the legal basis and spiritual merits of i'tikaf. The author declares i'tikaf as legislated (mashru') by Quran and Sunnah, then lists its benefits and concludes with an analogy from 'Ata'. Understanding this passage equips you to identify the ruling of i'tikaf within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the term 'mashru'
The passage opens by stating i'tikaf is 'mashru' (legislated). In Hanafi fiqh, 'mashru' indicates a recommended act (sunnah) rather than an obligatory one (fard). This sets the legal status.
The term 'mashru' is broader than 'wajib' and is used for acts established by the sources.
2
Identify the sources cited
The author cites 'the Book and the Sunnah' (Quran and Hadith) as the basis for legislation. This anchors the ruling in primary evidence.
No specific verses or hadith are quoted, but the general reference suffices to establish legitimacy.
3
Extract the spiritual benefits
The passage lists four benefits: emptying the heart from worldly worries, submitting the soul to Allah, adhering to worship, and being confined in a spiritual fortress. These encourage devotion and detachment.
These benefits are motivational rather than legal conditions.
4
Analyze 'Ata's analogy
The comparison to a persistent supplicant at a great door illustrates the mu'takif's attitude: unwavering hope for forgiveness and refusal to leave until the goal is achieved.
This analogy reinforces the virtue of persistence in worship.
5
Synthesize the ruling and virtues
Combine the legal status (sunnah) with the spiritual merits to form a complete understanding of i'tikaf as both a recommended practice and a means of spiritual growth.
The passage does not specify conditions like fasting or location, which are covered in other texts.

Conditions for Obligation of Prayer and Its Times
Lists conditions and defines prayer times

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for the obligation of prayer and explain the Hanafi concept of wujub musa'.

Core Idea
Prayer becomes obligatory only with Islam, puberty, and sanity; its cause is the entry of time, which creates a broad obligation.

The first step in mastering prayer is knowing when it becomes required. This passage from Nur al-Idah lists the three conditions that trigger the obligation of prayer in the Hanafi school. It also clarifies that children are to be trained before puberty, and introduces the critical concept that the obligation is broad (wajib musa'), meaning you have the entire prayer window to perform it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
List Islam, puberty, and sanity. Note that sanity (العقل) is also a condition, mentioned after the five times in the text but logically part of the initial list.
The text says 'يشترط لفرضيتها ثلاثة أشياء: الإسلام، والبلوغ، والعقل' though the order is a bit jumbled.
2
Separate condition from cause
Conditions are personal attributes (Islam, puberty, sanity). The cause (sabab) is the entry of each prayer time. Understand the difference.
Conditions must exist for obligation; cause triggers the obligation.
3
Distinguish broad obligation vs restricted
Wajib musa' means the obligation is immediate upon time entry but not restricted to the first moment. The entire time window is equally valid.
Contrast with fasting (wajib mudayyaq).
4
Note the training rule for children
Children are commanded (recommended) to pray at seven and beaten lightly at ten for neglect. This is training, not obligation.
The text says 'تؤمر بها الأولاد لسبع سنين وتضرب عليها لعشر'.

The Cause and Extensive Obligation of Salah
Defines the cause and nature of salah's obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the cause (sabab) of salah in the Hanafi school and explain the meaning and implications of 'wujub musawwa'.'

Core Idea
Salah becomes obligatory at the time's entry, but you may delay it within the time without sin.

This passage from Nur al-Idah defines the very foundation of salah's obligation: its cause (sabab) is the entry of the prescribed time. It further clarifies that the obligation is 'extensive' (wajib musawwa'), meaning it binds you from the first moment but permits performance at any point before the time ends. Understanding this distinction is essential for knowing when you are accountable and how much flexibility you have.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause (sabab) of salah
Find in the text what triggers the obligation of salah. The text states: 'وأسبابها : أوقاتها' – its causes are its times. So the entry of the time is the sabab.
Sabab is a legal cause; without it, the obligation does not arise.
2
Determine when the obligation begins
The text says: 'وتجب بأول الوقت' – it becomes obligatory at the very beginning of the time. This is the moment the time starts, not later.
Contrast with obligations that become binding only at the end of the time.
3
Understand the nature of the obligation: wujub musawwa'
The phrase 'وجوباً موسعاً' qualifies the obligation as extensive. This means the binding is immediate but the performance can be spread over the entire time window. Delaying until the last moment is permissible, as long as it is done before the time expires.
This is the default for all five daily prayers in the Hanafi school.

Maghrib should be hastened immediately, but on cloudy days it is delayed slightly; delaying it too much (until stars appear) becomes detested (makruh tahrimi or tanzihi). Isha should be delayed until one third of the night; delaying to half the night is permissible, but beyond half is makruh tahrimi. On cloudy days, Isha should be hastened. Witr is recommended to be delayed to the last part of the night for one who is confident of waking. Footnotes clarify the rulings for women and the degrees of dislike for delaying Maghrib and Isha.

Core Idea
Salah is valid only when all conditions (external) and pillars (internal) are fulfilled; omission of a condition prevents validity, whereas a missing pillar invalidates the act.

This passage from Nur al-Idah presents a comprehensive list of what is indispensable for the validity of salah. Understanding which are conditions (shurut) and which are pillars (arkan) is critical for correct fiqh reasoning.

The Four Pillars of Prayer and Their Conditions
Listing pillars and conditions of prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four pillars of prayer according to the Hanafi school and distinguish them from the two categories of conditions (internal and external).

Core Idea
Only four actions are pillars: standing, recitation, bowing, and prostration.

Many students assume that all actions performed during prayer are pillars (arkan). This passage from Nur al-Idah clarifies that the pillars are only four, while other requirements are conditions (shurut) for validity. Understanding this distinction is crucial for knowing what invalidates prayer and what requires compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four pillars
From the list of seven items (20-27), the author explicitly states that only four are pillars: standing, recitation, bowing, and prostration. Memorize these as the essential integrals.
The text says: 'And the pillars from what has been mentioned are four: Standing, Recitation, Bowing, and Prostration.'
2
Classify the remaining items as conditions
All other actions mentioned (e.g., placing hands, rising from prostration, final sitting) are conditions, not pillars. Some conditions are prerequisites for starting prayer (external, like wudu), others must be maintained (internal, like being awake).
The text: 'And the rest are conditions, some of which are conditions for the validity of starting the prayer, namely what is outside it, and others are conditions for the continuity of its validity.'
3
Note the intra-madhhab difference on the final sitting
The text reports a minority view that the final sitting is a pillar. However, the relied-upon Hanafi position is that it is wajib (obligatory), not a rukn. Its omission requires sajdah al-sahw but does not invalidate the prayer.
This distinction affects whether a missed final sitting invalidates the prayer (Shafi'i view) or merely requires compensation (Hanafi view).

The Final Sitting: Pillar or Condition?
Ruling classification of prayer components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the final sitting is a rukn and distinguish between external conditions (shurut al-shur') and internal conditions (shurut al-dawam) for prayer validity.

Core Idea
Omission of the final sitting invalidates the prayer — it is a pillar, not just a wajib.

This passage from Nur al-Idah gives a clear taxonomy of the components of prayer: some are pillars (arkan) and others are conditions (shurut). Understanding this classification is essential for knowing what invalidates the prayer and what does not. We will focus on the final sitting as a pillar and the two categories of conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on the final sitting
The passage explicitly classifies the final sitting (qa'dah akhirah) for the tashahhud as a rukn (pillar). This means if it is omitted, the entire prayer is invalid (batil) and must be repeated.
Other schools view it as only a sunnah or wajib; the Hanafi relied-upon position is rukn.
2
Classify the remaining elements
All other elements besides the arkan are conditions (shurut). They do not invalidate the prayer if missing entirely (some conditions, if missing, prevent starting), but they affect validity in different ways.
3
Distinguish external and internal conditions
External conditions (shurut al-shur') must be present before beginning the prayer (e.g., wudu, covering awrah, facing qibla). If they are missing, the prayer cannot be started validly. Internal conditions (shurut al-dawam) must persist throughout the prayer (e.g., intention, facing qibla continuously). If they are broken mid-prayer, the prayer becomes invalid.
The passage says: 'ba'duha shart li sihhat al-shuru' fi al-salat, wa huwa ma kana kharijaha, wa ghairuhu: shart li dawam sihhatiha.'
4
Apply the distinction
For example, if a person begins prayer without wudu, the condition is missing externally, so the prayer never becomes valid. If during prayer the intention is lost (e.g., doubt), the condition is missing internally, and the prayer becomes invalid at that moment.

This distinction is important in Hanafi fiqh for understanding the effect of missing different components.

Rulings for One Who Lacks Covering in Prayer
Rulings for one who lacks proper covering for prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for a person who lacks proper covering for prayer, including when to use impure cloth, how to cover partial awrah, and the recommended posture.

Core Idea
When proper covering is unavailable, even impure cloth is used, and partial covering is obligatory; prayer is still valid.

Covering the awrah is a condition of prayer, but what if you have no cloth? The Hanafi school provides detailed rulings for such emergencies. This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines the options: from using silk or grass, to choosing between covering front or back, to the recommended sitting posture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main scenario
The passage deals with a person who lacks any covering for the awrah (private parts).
2
Determine the available material
If any material is found, even if impure (like silk, grass, mud), it must be used. The ruling depends on how much of it is pure.
If at least a quarter is pure, praying naked is invalid; if less than a quarter, he has a choice.
3
Apply the rule for partial covering
If the material covers only part of the awrah, the front and back (qabl and dubur) must be covered if possible.
4
Recognize the ikhtilaf
If only one of the two can be covered, there is a difference of opinion whether to cover the front or the back.
Both opinions are valid; the prayer is correct in either case.
5
Note the recommended posture
For the naked person, it is recommended to pray sitting with head gestures (ima'), extending legs toward the qibla. Standing with gestures or with full bowing and prostration is also valid.

Definition of Awrah and Rulings on Uncovering in Prayer
Definition of awrah and invalidation by uncovering

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of awrah for men, free women, and female slaves, and apply the rule that uncovering a quarter of a limb invalidates prayer.

Core Idea
Covering the awrah is a condition for prayer; uncovering a quarter of a limb or more invalidates it.

The awrah (parts to be covered) differs by gender and status in the Hanafi school. This lesson presents the precise limits for men, free women, and female slaves, and explains the threshold of exposure that breaks the prayer. Understanding these definitions is essential for validating salah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the awrah definitions
For men: from the navel to the knee inclusive. For female slaves: same as men plus belly and back. For free women: all except face, palms, and feet. Note that the navel is not included but the knee is.
The Hanafi school considers the knee part of the awrah.
2
Understand the threshold for invalidation
Uncovering a quarter of any single awrah limb invalidates the prayer. If the exposure is scattered over multiple limbs, sum the areas; if the total reaches a quarter of the smallest exposed limb, it invalidates.
Scattered exposure is assessed by the smallest limb among those exposed.
3
Recognize the implications for prayer
Covering the awrah is a condition (shart) of prayer. Deliberate or accidental exposure beyond the threshold breaks the prayer, requiring repetition if not corrected immediately.
Intentional exposure without covering nullifies the prayer entirely.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that for women the hands are awrah (except palms) and the feet are also awrah. The Hanafi exception for feet is based on the difficulty of covering them while walking.
This difference affects the validity of prayer for women in mixed settings.

Rulings on Facing the Qibla: Exemptions and Taharrī
Rulings for facing qibla when unable or uncertain

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions that exempt one from facing the qibla and apply the Hanafi ruling on the validity of prayer when taharrī is omitted.

Core Idea
When qibla is ambiguous, taharrī is wajib; omitting it can invalidate prayer if certainty arises during the act.

Facing the qibla is a fundamental condition for prayer, but what happens when you cannot determine it or are unable to face it? This passage from Nur al-Idah lays out clear exemptions and the critical role of taharrī (reasoned estimation). We will examine the scenarios where facing qibla is waived and the nuanced rulings on prayer validity when taharrī is neglected.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios where facing qibla is not required
List the three exemptions: illness, inability to dismount from a mount, and fear of an enemy. Note that the qibla becomes whatever direction one can safely manage.
2
Understand the obligation of taharrī when qibla is ambiguous
If no informer or mihrab is available, taharrī (estimation based on signs) becomes wajib. The prayer remains valid even if later found to be wrong.
3
Apply the ruling for discovering an error during prayer
If one realizes during prayer that they are facing the wrong direction, they should turn to the correct qibla and continue (build upon the prayer).
This does not require repetition of the completed portion.
4
Analyze the two cases of omitting taharrī
Case 1: After completing prayer, one learns they were correct → prayer is valid (footnote 1). Case 2: During prayer, one becomes certain they are correct → prayer is invalid (footnote 2). Understand the reasoning: in case 2, strong certainty cannot be built on weak foundation (lack of taharrī).
This is the point of dispute with Abu Yusuf.
5
Consider the ruling for a congregation with differing estimations
If each member estimates a different direction and they are ignorant of the imam's direction, their prayers are valid individually.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When qibla is ambiguous, taharri is wajib; its omission invalidates salah if discovered during prayer, but not if discovered after.

This lesson from Nur al-Idah presents the Hanafi rules for prayer when the qibla is not easily determined or when circumstances prevent facing it. The central concept is taharri—a reasoned effort to find the qibla—and its legal consequences, including a notable dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf. Understanding these rulings is essential for a mufti advising on prayer in unusual circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
The passage covers two main situations: inability to face qibla (due to not dismounting or fear) and ambiguity of qibla direction. Note that in the first case, one prays toward the direction of ability; in the second, taharri is required.
Inability removes the obligation of facing the exact qibla; ambiguity triggers the obligation of taharri.
2
Understand taharri and its effect
Taharri is a wajib effort to determine the correct direction using available signs. Once performed, the prayer is valid even if the direction is mistaken, and no repetition is required.

If one realizes an error during prayer, he must turn toward the correct direction and continue. If he realizes after prayer, the prayer stands and no qada is needed.

If one prays without taharri, two sub-cases: (a) if correctness is known after completion, prayer is valid; (b) if correctness is known during prayer, prayer is invalid according to Abu Hanifa (mufta bihi). Abu Yusuf deems it valid.

If a congregation each perform taharri facing different directions and are ignorant of the imam's qibla, their prayers are acceptable once they have made their own taharri.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between arkan (pillars) and wajibat (obligations). While both are required, missing a rukn invalidates the prayer, while missing a wajib requires compensation through sajdah sahw. This passage enumerates the wajibat, beginning with eighteen items. Rulings on Forgetting Surah or Fatiha in Prayer
Two contrasting rulings on forgetfulness in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a surah or al-Fatiha is forgotten in the first two rak'ahs of a fard prayer and identify the required remedy (recitation in last two rak'ahs versus sujood as-sahw).

Core Idea
Forgetting a surah requires recovery recitation; forgetting fatiha requires only sujood as-sahw.

You already know that reciting al-Fatiha and an additional surah are central to each rak'ah. But what happens if you forget one of these in the first two rak'ahs? Does the prayer break, or can you fix it later? This lesson clarifies two distinct Hanafi rulings: the missed surah is made up in the later rak'ahs, while a missed fatiha is not repeated—sujood as-sahw compensates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two separate cases: forgetting to recite a surah after fatiha in the first two rak'ahs, and forgetting to recite al-Fatiha itself in those rak'ahs.
2
Analyze the remedy for forgotten surah
For a forgotten surah, the ruling states: the surah must be recited in the last two rak'ahs, along with al-Fatiha, and this recitation is done loudly (if the prayer is audible). The remedy is wajib (obligatory).
This is based on the author's view that adding a surah is not wajib in the first two rak'ahs; only when missed does it become wajib to recover.
3
Analyze the remedy for forgotten fatiha
For a forgotten fatiha, the ruling states: it is not repeated in the later rak'ahs. Instead, the worshipper performs sujood as-sahw (prostration of forgetfulness) at the end of the prayer.
This is because fatiha is a wajib, not a rukn; its omission does not invalidate the prayer but requires compensation via prostration.
4
Recognize the underlying principle
The difference illustrates a key Hanafi principle: a wajib that is omitted can be compensated by a specific action (sujud as-sahw or later recitation), whereas a rukn would require repeating the rak'ah. Here, both surah and fatiha are wajib, but their remedies differ based on the nature of the omission.
The surah recovery takes place within the prayer itself; the fatiha omission is compensated externally.

Item 18 lists where silent recitation (isrār) is required: Zuhr, ‘Asr, the later rak‘ahs of Maghrib and ‘Isha, and voluntary prayers during the day. One praying alone has the choice to recite audibly or silently, like a person performing optional night prayer. The passage then addresses a sub-issue: if the imam omits the additional surah after al-Fatiha in the first two rak‘ahs, he must recite it in the later rak‘ahs along with al-Fatiha audibly, because the author holds that reciting a surah in the first two units is not wajib. However, if he omits al-Fatiha entirely, he does not repeat it in the later units; instead, he should perform the prostration of forgetfulness (sujūd al-sahw), as indicated in footnote (4). Footnote (3) notes a difference of opinion: the soundest view is that he recites both al-Fatiha and the surah audibly in the later units, but some say only the surah audibly. All rulings adhere to the Hanafi school, with the muftā bihī being that reciting the surah in the first two rak‘ahs is not wajib, hence the compensation for omission.
