Definition and Conditions of Zakat
Definition of zakat and conditions of obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of zakat and identify the seven conditions that make zakat obligatory.

Core Idea
Zakat is an obligation on productive wealth held for a lunar year, free of debt and basic needs, by a free adult Muslim.

Zakat is one of the five pillars of Islam, but its obligation is not unconditional. In this passage from Nur al-Idah, we learn the precise definition of zakat as a transfer of ownership, and the seven specific conditions that must be met for zakat to become obligatory. Understanding these conditions is essential for every Muslim to fulfil this duty correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
State the Hanafi definition of zakat as tamlik (transfer of ownership) of specific property to a specific person.
This definition distinguishes zakat from other charitable obligations.
2
List the conditions of obligation
The passage lists seven conditions: Islam, freedom, mukallaf (mature and sane), ownership of nisab (gold/silver or trade goods of equal value), freedom from debt and basic needs, productive wealth (actual or constructive growth), and the passage of one lunar year.
Each condition must be met for zakat to be fard.
3
Distinguish between real and constructive growth
Productive (nama') includes actual growth (breeding, trade) and constructive growth (potential to grow, e.g., cash that can be invested). Non-productive wealth (e.g., household furniture, work animals) is exempt.
This distinction affects modern assets like savings accounts.
4
Apply the conditions to a scenario
Given a hypothetical person, assess each condition and determine if zakat is obligatory.
Practice with multiple scenarios to internalise the checklist.
5
Note the historical context
Zakat was ordained in the second year of Hijra, like fasting.
This contextualises the legal ruling.

Commentary
This passage introduces the chapter on Zakat. It defines zakat as the transfer of ownership of specific property (i.e., a portion of one's wealth) to a specific person (the eligible recipient). Then it lists the conditions for zakat to be obligatory (fard). The conditions are: 1) The person must be a free Muslim (so non-Muslims are not obligated; slaves are not obligated because they do not own property fully). 2) The person must be mukallaf (legally responsible, i.e., mature and sane). 3) The person must own a nisab of gold or silver, whether in the form of bullion, jewelry, or utensils, or trade goods that equal the value of gold/silver nisab. 4) The wealth must be free from debt and from the person's basic needs (such as housing, clothing, and daily necessities). 5) The wealth must be productive (growing), either actually (through breeding, trade) or constructively (potential to grow, like money that can be invested). Finally, a key condition for the actual payment (ada') is the passage of one lunar year on the original nisab. The footnote adds historical context: zakat was mandated in the second year of Hijra.

الحول (al-hawl): The passage of one complete lunar year on the nisab. This is a condition for the obligation to pay zakat.
النماء (al-nama'): Growth or productivity of wealth. Real growth is through breeding, offspring, and trade. Constructive growth is the ability to obtain growth (i.e., potential for growth).

Conditions for Valid Performance of Zakat
States conditions for valid zakat payment: intention and recipient unawareness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid zakat payment under the Hanafi school and apply the ruling when the recipient is unaware of the payment's nature.

Core Idea
Intention is the key condition; the recipient need not know it is zakat.

This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines the essential conditions for a valid zakat payment. The primary condition is intention (niyyah) that coincides with the act of giving, with a legal flexibility allowing intention even after the payment if the wealth remains with the recipient. Additionally, the recipient's awareness that the payment is zakat is not required, meaning you may give a gift or loan while intending it as zakat, and the obligation is fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is paying zakat (zakat) after it becomes obligatory. The ruling is that zakat is fard, and for the payment to be valid (sahih), certain conditions must be met.
Zakat obligation arises from ownership of nisab for one lunar year.
2
Extract the first condition: intention (niyyah)
The text states that intention must coincide (muqāranah) with the payment to the poor, the deputy, or with setting aside the amount. Intention can be legal (hukmiyyah) if it occurs while the wealth is still in the recipient's hand.
This allows for a slight delay in forming the intention.
3
Understand legal coincidence (muqāranah ḥukmiyyah)
If you pay without intention, then later intend while the poor person still has the money, it is considered valid. The intention is legally present even though not simultaneous.
4
Extract the second condition: recipient's knowledge not required
The text explicitly states that the poor person does not need to know the payment is zakat. This is the strongest position (ala al-sahh) in the Hanafi school.
Thus, you may call it a gift or loan and intend it as zakat.
5
Apply the rulings to scenarios
Test the conditions: if you give all your wealth as charity without intending zakat, the obligation drops because you no longer own wealth. But for partial payment, intention is critical.

Commentary
This passage explains the conditions for the validity (sihha) of zakat payment. The key condition is intention (niyya) that coincides with the act of giving. The intention must be present when handing the zakat to the poor person or his deputy, or when setting aside the amount due (azl). If one pays without intention but then intends while the wealth is still in the recipient's possession, it is valid (hukman). Furthermore, it is not necessary that the poor person knows it is zakat; the intention of the giver suffices. Thus, if the giver calls it a gift or loan but intends zakat, the zakat is valid. Finally, if one gives away all his wealth as charity without intending zakat specifically, the obligation is dropped because the payment fulfills the duty of charity, but for zakat the intention is needed; however, here the text says 'sakat anhu farduha' meaning the obligation of zakat is lifted because the wealth is no longer owned. This is a nuanced point: if one gives all his wealth away without zakat intention, he has no more nisab, so no zakat obligation remains.

مقارنة حكمية (muqāranah ḥukmiyyah): Legal coincidence: intention is considered present even if not articulated at the exact moment of payment, as long as it occurs while the wealth is still in the recipient's hand.

Zakat on Strong Debts: Ruling Upon Recovery
Classifies debts for zakat and details the strong category.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a strong debt for zakat purposes and state the Hanafi ruling on paying zakat for past years upon its recovery.

Core Idea
Upon recovering a strong debt, one must pay zakat for all past years the debt was owed.

Not all debts are treated equally when it comes to zakat. The Hanafi school divides debts into three categories based on the likelihood of repayment. This lesson focuses on the strongest category—debts that are virtually certain to be repaid. Understanding this classification is essential for correctly fulfilling the zakat obligation on wealth that was out of reach for years.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three-way classification
The text introduces three types of debt for zakat: strong (qawi), medium (wasat), and weak (da'if). Only the strong type is described here. This classification is unique to Hanafi fiqh and determines when zakat becomes payable.
Medium and weak debts are covered in later sections; only strong debts are in this passage.
2
Identify the two cases of strong debt
The strong debt includes (a) the substitute of a loan (qard) where the debtor acknowledges the debt, even if insolvent, and (b) the price of trade goods (mal al-tijara) recovered from a debtor who acknowledges or against whom there is evidence. Both cases share the common feature that repayment is certain.
Acknowledgment or evidence removes doubt about the debt's existence.
3
Apply the ruling: pay zakat for past years
When the creditor eventually recovers the debt, they must calculate and pay zakat for each full lunar year that the debt was outstanding, assuming the amount reached the nisab threshold annually.
Zakat is not due annually during the period of non-recovery; it is paid in arrears upon recovery.

Ruling on Newly Acquired Wealth (Mustafad) During the Zakat Year
States the ruling for adding newly acquired wealth to existing zakatable wealth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mustafad and apply it to determine when zakat is due on newly acquired wealth.

Core Idea
Mustafad is merged with existing wealth of the same type; zakat is due at the end of the original year.

This lesson addresses a common question: if you already possess the nisab and acquire additional wealth during the zakat year, does that new wealth trigger a separate zakat year? The Hanafi school provides a clear and practical ruling: it is added to its type and the zakat falls due at the end of the original year. This rule applies to all acquisitions – trade, inheritance, gifts – and includes an allowance for advancing zakat payments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of acquisition
Determine whether the newly acquired wealth is of the same type as your existing nisab (e.g., cash, gold, livestock). The ruling only applies when there is a similarity in category (mujānis).
If it is a different type, a separate nisab and year may be required.
2
Check the existence of a valid original nisab
Confirm that at the beginning of the zakat year you already possessed a full nisab. The mustafad is piggybacked onto that original nisab.
If you had no nisab initially, the newly acquired wealth starts its own year.
3
Add the mustafad to the existing wealth
Mentally add the new wealth to the existing wealth of the same type. The combined total is subject to zakat at the end of the original year, not a new year.
If the original nisab was already of a zakatable amount, the mustafad does not need its own year.
4
Apply the ruling to the zakat due date
Zakat on the combined total becomes due at the completion of the original year, regardless of when the mustafad was acquired.
This holds whether the acquisition was early or late in the year.
5
Consider early payment (ta'jīl)
Note that the text also allows advancing zakat for several years if one possesses the nisab, which is a valid concession.
This is an exception to the usual rule of waiting for the year to end.

Instead, it is added to the pre-existing wealth, and zakat on it becomes due at the end of the original zakat year, not after a new year from the acquisition date. This applies whether the acquisition is through trade, inheritance, gift, or any other means. The passage also states that if a person who possesses the nisab pays zakat in advance for several years, it is valid (sahih). This is a concession (rukhsah) allowing early payment before the year completes.

Zakat on Debts: Three Categories and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Classifies debts and gives zakat rulings with an intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify debts into strong, medium, and weak, and state the Hanafi ruling on zakat for each category, including the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
Debts are not all equal for zakat: strong debts trigger zakat for past years, medium debts only after receipt, and weak debts require a new year after receipt.

Understanding zakat on debts (zakat al-dayn) requires knowing that debts differ in strength. The Hanafi school classifies debts into three categories: qawi (strong), wasat (medium), and da'if (weak). Each category has different rules for when zakat becomes due and how past years are calculated. Additionally, there is an intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether zakat is owed proportionally upon receipt from any debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the debt's origin
Determine if the debt arose from a loan/trade (strong), sale of personal property (medium), or non-wealth items like mahr (weak).
The origin determines the category.
2
Apply the receipt condition
For strong debts, zakat is due for past years upon receipt; for medium, zakat is not due until receipt of nisab; for weak, a full year after receipt is required.
3
Calculate the nisab threshold
Zakat is paid only when the received amount reaches nisab (40 dirhams silver equivalent). Less than one-fifth of nisab is excused ('afw).
The nisab is 40 dirhams of silver.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Imam Abu Hanifah holds that weak debts require a new year after receipt; the Sahibayn hold that zakat is due proportionally on all received debts. There is ikhtilaf al-tashih among later scholars.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi ruling on zakat on debts (zakat al-dayn). The debts are categorized into three types based on strength. Strong debt (qawi) includes loans (qard) and trade credit that the debtor acknowledges; zakat becomes due for past years upon receipt, but payment is deferred until one receives at least forty dirhams (since one-fifth of the nisab of silver is forty dirhams). Medium debt (wasat) is the price of non-trade items like used clothes or dwelling house; zakat is not due until one receives a full nisab, but then it is calculated for the past year (from when the debt was incurred). Weak debt (da'if) includes items not originally wealth, like mahr, bequest, khul', etc.; zakat is only due after receiving a nisab and a full year after receipt, according to Imam Abu Hanifah. The passage then mentions an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that zakat is due on what is received from all three types proportionally, without restriction. Additionally, there is a specific report from Muhammad that there is no zakat on it at all. The later scholars have different corrections (tashih) on which view is preferred.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Zakat is a right attached to specific wealth; if it perishes without your fault, the obligation perishes with it.

Zakat becomes obligatory when a full lunar year passes on wealth that meets the nisab and conditions. But what if that wealth is destroyed before you have a chance to pay? Does the obligation remain as a debt, or does it drop? This passage from Nur al-Idah, explained by Ibn Abidin, gives the Hanafi answer: the zakat obligation is linked to the actual existence of the wealth. If the wealth is destroyed without your negligence or direct action, the obligation is cancelled. However, if you deliberately destroy it yourself, you remain liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes two cases: (1) the owner is negligent (mufarrat) but does not personally destroy the wealth – no guarantee; (2) the owner destroys it (mutlif) – he guarantees the zakat.
Negligence alone (e.g., poor storage) is not enough to impose liability; actual destruction by the owner triggers liability.
2
Understand the legal basis
The ruling rests on the principle that zakat is a right attached to the specific wealth (ayn). If that wealth ceases to exist without the owner's fault, the right ceases too. This contrasts with a debt, which remains even if the debtor's property is destroyed.
This principle distinguishes zakat from ordinary debts.
3
Apply to partial destruction
If only part of the wealth is destroyed, the zakat obligation is reduced proportionally. For example, if half the wealth perishes, only half the zakat is due.
The obligation falls only on the remaining wealth.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position differs from some other schools (e.g., Maliki) which treat zakat as a debt that remains even if the wealth is destroyed. Know that this is a legitimate area of ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi view is based on the nature of zakat as a right in the property itself.

Making the Poor Independent (Ighna')
Rulings on making the poor independent through zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on ighna' and distinguish between the disliked and recommended amounts.

Core Idea
Giving a poor person enough to exceed a nisab after debts is disliked; lifting him from begging is recommended.

This lesson clarifies the delicate balance between generosity and practicality in zakat distribution. The Hanafi school discourages over-giving that makes the recipient wealthy but encourages sufficient giving to eliminate need.

Guided Path
1
Define ighna'
Ighna' means giving the poor person enough that after paying his debts and giving each dependent less than a nisab, he still possesses a nisab of his own.
The key is the surplus after debts and dependents.
2
State the ruling: makruh
Giving this amount is makruh tanzihi (disliked) because it may lead to the recipient becoming rich enough to owe zakat himself.
3
Identify when it is not disliked
If after giving each dependent less than a nisab, the poor person does not have a nisab left, then it is not disliked.
4
State the recommended amount
It is recommended (mandub) to give enough to make the poor person independent from begging, i.e., sufficient for his basic needs without excess.

Conditions of Zakat al-Fitr in the Hanafi School
Begins conditions for Zakat al-Fitr obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the obligation of Zakat al-Fitr and distinguish its nisab requirements from zakat on property.

Core Idea
Zakat al-Fitr is wajib on every Muslim who owns a nisab at Eid Fajr — no growth or annual cycle required.

Zakat al-Fitr is a unique charity that purifies the fast. Unlike zakat on wealth, its obligation does not depend on trade growth or the passage of a year. This passage from Nur al-Idah lays out the precise conditions that trigger its obligation, and clarifies what is— and isn't— required of the nisab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligated person
Read the phrase 'على مسلم مالك لنصاب'. Zakat al-Fitr is obligatory on every Muslim who owns a nisab (minimum wealth). Note that the text does not mention sanity or adulthood — the obligation applies to all Muslims, including minors and the insane, as long as they possess the nisab.
This is the Hanafi view; some other schools restrict to mukallaf.
2
Pinpoint the time of obligation
The phrase 'عند طلوع' refers to the rising of Fajr on Eid day. Obligation is triggered at that moment. If a person acquires the nisab after Fajr, no obligation arises.
The full phrase continues 'الفجر من يوم العيد' as per the footnote.
3
Distinguish the nisab from zakat on wealth
The text explicitly says growth through trade (النمو بالتجارة) and passage of a year (حولان الحول) are NOT required. This nisab is the same as for Udhiyah, family maintenance, and it makes the owner ineligible to receive zakat.
For zakat on property, the nisab must be growing (or capable of growth) and a year must pass.
4
Recognize the shared nisab
The same nisab description applies to Udhiyah (sacrificial animal) and support of relatives. Understanding this linkage helps in categorizing different obligations.
5
Apply the consequence
Owning this nisab also makes the person prohibited from receiving charity (تحرم به عليه الصدقة). This reinforces that Zakat al-Fitr is for those who are not themselves eligible for zakat.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Zakat al-fitr is due on surplus wealth beyond essential needs, defined by sufficiency (kifayah), not a fixed threshold.

This lesson clarifies what counts as essential needs (hajat asliyyah) in the Hanafi school for zakat al-fitr. Understanding this concept is crucial because it determines who is exempt from paying zakat al-fitr. The passage explains that the measure is sufficiency, not a fixed amount, and lists six categories of essential needs.

Guided Path

Identify the criterion

List the essential needs
The passage enumerates six items: dwelling, furniture, clothing, horse, weapon, and service slaves.
These are considered the basic necessities that exempt a person from zakat al-fitr.

Note: 'nama' (growth) is not considered for such items, but surplus value is still counted.

Who Must Pay Zakat al-Fitr
Specifies those obligated to pay zakat al-fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of people for whom zakat al-fitr is wajib according to the Hanafi school and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the rich minor and the grandfather.

Core Idea
Zakat al-fitr is wajib for oneself and dependents; rich minors pay from their own wealth, and the grandfather is like the father when the father is absent.

This passage from Nur al-Idah specifies the individuals for whom zakat al-fitr must be paid. While the primary obligation is on oneself, the school extends it to certain dependents and slaves. The text also records an important intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the rich minor child and the status of the grandfather.

Guided Path
1
Identify the self-obligation
The first obligation is for oneself. Every free Muslim with surplus wealth must pay zakat al-fitr for himself.
2
Identify dependents: poor minor children
The father must pay for his poor minor children out of his own wealth.
This includes children who have no wealth of their own.
3
Note the ruling for rich minor children
If minor children are wealthy, zakat al-fitr is paid from their own wealth. This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
Imam Muhammad disagrees; he says it is not obligatory on the rich minor.
4
Examine the grandfather's obligation
In the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah), the grandfather is not obligated. However, a chosen view treats him like the father when the father is absent or poor.
This chosen view is based on a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad and preferred by later scholars.
5
List the slaves covered
The obligation extends to one's slaves for service, mudabbar (a slave promised freedom after death), and umm walad (a slave mother of the master's child), even if they are non-Muslim.
These are considered dependents for whom the master must pay.

The grandfather's obligation is an intra-Hanafi dispute; the mufta bihi treats him like the father when the father is absent or poor.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Nur al-Idah regarding Sadaqat al-Fitr. The text presents the general obligation for dependents then enters an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf about the grandfather.
