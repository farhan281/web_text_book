Responsibility for Funeral Expenses: Hierarchy of Payers
Details who is financially liable for the deceased's burial

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchy of financial responsibility for funeral expenses in Hanafi fiqh and apply it to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The husband's duty to shroud his wife holds even in poverty—a debt of marriage beyond death.

When a person dies, who pays for the shroud and burial? The answer depends on the deceased's wealth and relationships. This passage from Nur al-Idah lays out a clear hierarchy: starting with the spouse, then family members who owed maintenance, then the public treasury, and finally the community. Understanding this cascade ensures that no person is left unburied due to poverty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary responsible party
Start with the spouse. The husband is responsible for his wife's funeral even if he is poor. This is the soundest Hanafi opinion.
This applies only to the wife; the wife is not responsible for her husband's funeral.
2
If the deceased has no wealth
Then the responsibility falls on those whose maintenance was obligatory upon him during life (e.g., father for child, son for parent).
This includes both lineal ascendants and descendants.
3
If no such person exists or they are unable
Then the expense is taken from the public treasury (bayt al-māl) of the Islamic state.
This is the public treasury, not a charity fund.
4
If the treasury fails or is unjust
Then the obligation falls upon the wealthy Muslims in the community as a collective duty.
The community must ensure the burial.
5
Understand the auxiliary rule
One should ask for help in preparing the deceased from those who cannot do it themselves—meaning if the deceased has no one capable, the community should facilitate.
This ensures no neglect.

Expenses of preparing the deceased: 1- The husband is responsible for preparing his wife, even if he is poor, in the soundest opinion. 2- Whoever has no wealth: his shroud is on the one whose maintenance is obligatory upon him. Commentary
This section details the financial responsibility for the funeral expenses (kafan, etc.). The main points are: (1) The husband must pay for his wife's funeral even if he is poor (this is the soundest view—al-asahh), because the marriage contract entails maintenance even after death in this respect. (2) If the deceased has no wealth, the shroud is the responsibility of those who were obligated to support them during life (e.g., father for child). (3) If no such person exists, the expense falls on the public treasury (bayt al-māl). (4) If the public treasury fails to provide due to inability or injustice, then the obligation falls upon the wealthy Muslims (the community). Additionally, one should ask for the preparation of the deceased from someone who cannot do it himself (i.e., if the deceased had no one to prepare him, people should ask others to assist). These rulings reflect the Hanafi principle that funeral expenses are a debt on the estate if wealthy, but if destitute, the community is responsible.

Key Terms
التجهيز (al-tajhīz): Preparing the deceased for burial, including washing, shrouding, and funeral.
بيت المال (bayt al-māl): The public treasury of the Islamic state.
