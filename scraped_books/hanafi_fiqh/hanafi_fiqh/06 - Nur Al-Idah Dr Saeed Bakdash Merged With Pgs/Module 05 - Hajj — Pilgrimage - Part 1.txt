Recommended Visits in Medina (Mustahabbat al-Madinah)
Ruling statement detailing recommended visits and supplications in Medina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the recommended acts (mustahabbat) of visiting Medina according to the Hanafi school and distinguish them from obligatory Hajj rituals.

Core Idea
Visiting the Prophet's mosque and graves in Medina is mustahabb (recommended), not wajib, but carries immense reward.

After completing the rituals of Hajj or Umrah, many pilgrims travel to Medina to visit the Prophet's Mosque, his grave, and other sacred sites. This passage from Nur al-Idah lists the recommended acts and supplications for such a visit. Understanding these as mustahabb (non-emphasized sunnah) helps avoid the misconception that they are obligatory for Hajj's validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key ruling word
Find the word 'يستحب' (yustaḥabbu) in the Arabic text. This indicates the entire set of actions that follow are recommended (mandub/mustahabb), not obligatory.
The term 'يستحب' is the hallmark of a recommended act in Hanafi fiqh.
2
List the recommended visits
Scan the text for place names and persons. It mentions: (1) going out to al-Baqi', (2) visiting the grave of Hamza (master of martyrs), (3) visiting the other al-Baqi' (graves of al-Abbas, al-Hasan, and family), (4) visiting Uthman ibn Affan, (5) visiting Ibrahim (son of the Prophet), (6) visiting the martyrs of Uhud.
Although the OCR is corrupted, these key names are discernible.
3
Identify the recommended supplications
The passage then describes saying salam, reciting Surah al-Ikhlas (likely), dedicating reward to all believers, and making a specific du'a (starting with 'Ya sariha…').
The exact wording is uncertain but the structure is clear.
4
Classify the ruling level
Because these acts are introduced with 'يستحب', in the Hanafi school they fall under sunnah ghayr muakkadah (non-emphasized sunnah) or mustahabb. They are praiseworthy but not required; no sin is incurred by omitting them.
Contrast with acts of Hajj which are fard or wajib.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical scenario
Consider a pilgrim who finishes Hajj and goes to Medina. According to this passage, they should strive to visit these graves and recite the supplications. If they leave Medina without doing so, their Hajj remains valid.
The mufta bihi is that it is mustahabb for men; for women, some later Hanafi scholars restrict ziyarah due to fitnah.

Combining Prayers in Hajj: The Exceptional Rulings for Arafa and Muzdalifa
Ruling statement on prohibition of combining prayers except in Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on combining obligatory prayers in Hajj and identify the conditions required at Arafa and Muzdalifa.

Core Idea
Combining prayers is only allowed at Arafa and Muzdalifa for the Hajj pilgrim with the Imam and Ihram.

In the Hanafi school, combining two obligatory prayers into one time is generally prohibited regardless of excuse. However, an exception is made for the pilgrim during Hajj at two specific locations: Arafa and Muzdalifa. This lesson explains the ruling and its conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The default ruling in the Hanafi school: it is not permissible to combine two obligatory prayers in one time for any excuse (e.g., travel, rain). This passage begins by stating that prohibition.
2
Spot the exception
The exception is only for the Hajj pilgrim at Arafa. Look for the phrase 'إلا في عرفة للحاج' (except at Arafa for the pilgrim). Note that Muzdalifa is mentioned separately.
3
List the conditions for Arafa
Two conditions are required for combining at Arafa: (1) the presence of the supreme imam (al-imam al-a'zam) leading the Hajj, and (2) the state of ihram. Without these, combining is not allowed.
The supreme imam is the caliph or his deputy; in practice, the official Hajj imam.
4
Specify the types of combination
At Arafa, Zuhr and Asr are combined with jam' taqdim (early combination, i.e., both prayed in Zuhr time). At Muzdalifa, Maghrib and Isha are combined with jam' ta'khir (delayed combination, i.e., both prayed in Isha time).
Jam' taqdim means the second prayer (Asr) is brought forward to the time of the first (Zuhr). Jam' ta'khir means the first prayer (Maghrib) is delayed to the time of the second (Isha).
5
Recognize the edge case for Maghrib
The text states that Maghrib is not valid if prayed on the way to Muzdalifa (before arriving). However, a footnote adds that if one prays it on the way and does not repeat it until dawn is about to rise (or fears its rising), it becomes valid.
This shows the importance of timing and location for the combination.

However, if someone mistakenly prays Maghrib on the way and does not repeat it until just before dawn or fears the rising of dawn, then it is considered valid (according to the footnote).

Definition of Hajj and Its Conditions of Obligation
Defines Hajj and lists its eight conditions of obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of Hajj, identify its ruling (fard once immediately), and list the eight conditions for its obligation.

Core Idea
Hajj is fard immediately upon ability; without the eight conditions, no obligation.

This lesson opens the Book of Hajj by defining the pilgrimage and establishing its obligatory nature. We first learn that Hajj is the visitation of specific sacred sites with specific actions during specific months. The text then confirms that Hajj is a once-in-a-lifetime obligation that must be performed at the earliest opportunity (fawr). Finally, it enumerates the eight preconditions that must be present for Hajj to become binding upon a person.

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the definition
Identify the three components: specific places (Kaaba, Arafat, etc.), specific actions (tawaf, sa'y, wuquf), and specific months (Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah).
Each component is essential to the legal definition.
2
Identify the ruling and its timing
Note that Hajj is fard once in a lifetime and must be performed immediately (fawr) according to the most authentic view. This means no delay without excuse.
There is a weaker view allowing delay (tarakhkhi), but the relied-upon position is fawr.
3
List the eight conditions
Enumerate them: Islam, sanity, puberty, freedom, time (Hajj season), ability for provisions, ability for a mount, and ability for a safe path.
The eighth condition (path safety) is implied but not fully listed in the text due to a page break.
4
Understand the legal implication
If any condition is missing, Hajj is not obligatory. For example, a child or insane person is not obligated even if they have means.
Ability includes both financial and physical capacity.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi scholars allowed delay (tarakhkhi) rather than immediate performance, but the text states the asahh is fawr.
This ikhtilaf affects whether one may postpone Hajj without sin.

This passage opens the Book of Hajj by defining Hajj and stating the preconditions for its obligation. The definition: 'visitation of specific places (the Kaaba, Arafat, etc.) with specific actions (tawaf, sa'y, standing at Arafat) in its specific months (Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah).' Definition and Conditions of Hajj Obligation
Defining Hajj, its obligation, and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of Hajj, list its eight conditions of obligation, and explain why it is fard 'ala al-fawr.

Core Idea
Hajj is fard once in a lifetime immediately upon meeting eight conditions: Islam, sanity, puberty, freedom, time, provisions, mount, and surplus capacity.

Hajj is one of the five pillars of Islam, but not everyone is obligated to perform it. This passage from Nur al-Idah defines what Hajj is and details the precise conditions that make it an immediate personal duty. Understanding these conditions is essential for determining when Hajj becomes obligatory and when it can be delayed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Legal Ruling
The passage states that Hajj is fard (obligatory) once in a lifetime, and that it must be performed immediately ('ala al-fawr) once conditions are met. Note the intra-Hanafi difference: some scholars allowed delay, but the matn affirms immediacy as the more correct view.
This is a ruling definition—it does not discuss the validity conditions of the act itself, only the conditions for its obligation.
2
Memorize the Definition of Hajj
The passage defines Hajj as 'visitation of specific places, with specific actions, in its months'—referring to the Kaaba, Arafat, etc., during Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah.
This concise definition captures the essence of Hajj as a spatiotemporal ritual.
3
Enumerate the Eight Conditions of Obligation (Shurut Faradiyyah)
List the eight conditions: Islam, intellect (sanity), puberty, freedom, arrival of the Hajj season, ability for provisions (zad), ability for a mount (rahila), and that the ability is surplus over essential needs (family expenses, debts, etc.).
The last condition is composite: surplus over own expenses, dependents, indispensable property, and debts.
4
Understand the Details of Ability (Istita'ah)
Ability includes having provisions and a mount (or a share in a litter). For non-Makkans, walking is acceptable if feasible without hardship; otherwise, a mount is necessary. The ability must be surplus over all essential needs until return.
Note the exception: for Makkans, provisions are still needed even though they are already at the sacred site.
5
Note the Additional Condition for New Muslims in Dar al-Harb
Hajj does not become obligatory for a person who accepts Islam in dar al-harb (non-Muslim territory) until they have knowledge of its obligation or move to dar al-Islam.
This condition accounts for lack of da'wa exposure.

Commentary

The definition is concise: Hajj involves visiting specific sacred sites (the Kaaba, Arafat, etc.) with prescribed actions during the months of Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah. The Four Foundations of a Valid Hajj in Hanafi Fiqh
Enumeration of four essentials for valid Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four requirements for a valid Hajj, distinguishing between conditions (shurut) and pillars (arkan) according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Hajj is valid through two conditions (ihram and Islam) and two pillars (standing at Arafat and tawaf) — missing a pillar invalidates Hajj.

This passage from Nur al-Idah provides a concise summary of the absolute essentials for a valid Hajj. It is a memorization anchor for the basic structure: conditions that must be present and pillars that must be performed. Understanding this classification is critical because conditions and pillars have different legal consequences — a missing condition means the Hajj never started validly, while a missing pillar means the Hajj is invalid and must be repeated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four elements and their categories

In Hanafi fiqh, a shart must be present for the act to be valid; a rukn is an integral part without which the act is void. The text explicitly labels ihram and Islam as shartan (two conditions), and the standing and tawaf as ruknan (two pillars).
Conditions are prerequisites; pillars are constitutive parts.

These are the most meritorious times.
3
Understand the attached acts
Items 7-10 are supplementary: sending blessings on the Prophet, asking for Paradise, seeking refuge from Hell, and asking for righteous company.
These are often recited after the talbiyah.

Times of Rami (Stoning) and Their Rulings
Defines permissible, disliked, and preferred times for rami.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah, permissible, and disliked times for rami in Hajj according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Stoning at night is valid but disliked—the night is attached to the following day, except the night after Arafah.

The timing of rami (stoning the jamarat) is regulated in detail. This lesson defines the sunnah, permissible, and disliked times for rami across the days of Hajj, including the unique Hanafi ruling that stoning at night is valid (sahih) but disliked (makruh tanzih) because the night is legally attached to the following day, except for the night of Eid and the nights of stoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the days of rami
Rami occurs on three or four days: the 10th (Yawm al-Nahr), 11th, 12th, and optionally 13th of Dhul Hijjah. Know which days are required and which are optional.
The first day is Yawm al-Nahr.
2
Distinguish sunnah time from permissible time
On the first day, the sunnah time is from sunrise to noon; the permissible time extends from noon to sunset. On remaining days, the sunnah time is from noon to sunset (no earlier).
The permissible time is the fallback if sunnah is missed.
3
Identify the disliked times
Stoning is disliked on the first and fourth days between dawn and sunrise (before the sunnah window), and during the three nights following the stoning days.
Night stoning is disliked but still valid.
4
Understand the legal reasoning for night stoning
The Hanafi school attaches the night to the following day, except the night after Arafah (night of Eid) and the nights of stoning, which are attached to the preceding day. This makes night stoning valid but not preferred.
This attachment is a basis for the ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a concise summary of the actions and prohibitions for entering the state of ihram for Hajj, from the miqat until after assuming ihram. It is presented as a numbered list for easy memorization. The rulings are based on the Hanafi school: ghusl before ihram is recommended (mustahabb), not obligatory; but if one performs wudu instead, it is sufficient. The prohibition of sewn garments means the man must wear two unstitched cloths (izār and ridā'). Buttoning or tying them is disliked (makruh) but does not invalidate ihram. The talbiyah is the verbalization of intention; without it, ihram is not valid according to the majority of Hanafis. The prohibitions listed (raftah, fisq, jidal, hunting) are essential to maintain the sanctity of ihram. Killing game is haram, and even pointing to it is prohibited because that leads to hunting.

Key Terms
الرفث (ar-rafath): In the context of ihram, refraining from intercourse and its precursor speech.
الفسوق (al-fusūq): Sinfulness or disobedience to Allah, including all prohibited acts.
الجدال (al-jidāl): Argumentation or disputation, especially with fellow pilgrims and servants.
صيد البر (ṣayd al-barr): Hunting land game, which is completely prohibited during ihram.

The Talbiyah, Entering Ihram, and Prohibitions
Rulings from supplication to times of talbiyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct talbiyah wording, explain the ruling on adding extra phrases, list the prohibitions of ihram, and identify permissible acts during ihram.

Core Idea
Ihram is entered by talbiyah with intention; its violations include sexual activity, sins, and sewn garments.

This lesson covers the practical steps of entering ihram: the recommended supplication, the exact talbiyah formula, and the additional phrases that are sunnah. You will also learn the comprehensive list of prohibitions (muharramat) that must be avoided once in ihram, as well as what remains permissible. Mastering these rulings is essential for performing Hajj or Umrah correctly.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the supplication before ihram
Read the Arabic supplication 'اللهم إني أريد الحج...' and understand its meaning. This is a recommended du'a before entering ihram.
2
Learn the talbiyah wording and its additions
Recite the core talbiyah 'لبيك اللهم لبيك...' exactly. Then note the additional phrases ('البيك وسعديك...') which are sunnah. Understand the dispute about mandubah vs sunnah.
The footnote reconciles: if 'sunnah' is taken in its absolute sense, there is no contradiction.
3
Identify the moment of entering ihram
Ihram is entered when you recite the talbiyah with intention. No other action is required.
Hanafi view differs from some schools that require niyyah alone.
4
List the prohibitions of ihram
Go through each item in the list: sexual activity, obscenity, disobedience, argumentation, killing land game, wearing sewn garments, covering head/face, perfume, shaving. Understand each term.
Rafath includes both intercourse and mentioning it in presence of women.
5
Distinguish permissible acts
Note that bathing, seeking shade under a tent, and wearing a money belt are permissible. These are exceptions to the general restrictions.
The hadith about supplication upon seeing the Kaaba belongs to the next lesson.

Etiquette of Entering Mecca and the Sacred Mosque
Etiquette from reaching Mecca to entering the mosque

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the recommended actions when entering Mecca and the Sacred Mosque, and explain the hadeeth about supplication upon seeing the Kaaba.

Core Idea
Entering Mecca and the Kaaba is a sacred moment: purify yourself, enter humbly, and supplicate.

This lesson focuses on the adab (etiquette) of entering the holy city of Mecca and the Masjid al-Haram. The Prophet (peace be upon him) taught specific practices to maximize the spiritual benefit. You will also learn a famous hadith about times when supplication is answered, including upon seeing the Kaaba, which is supported by the Prophet's own practice.

Guided Path

Learn the recommended actions before entering Mecca

Bab al-Salam (Gate of Peace) leads directly to the Kaaba viewing area.
3
Identify the recommended supplications and actions inside
The text lists: saying takbir (Allahu Akbar), tahlil (La ilaha illa Allah), sending blessings on the Prophet (sallahu alayhi wasallam), being gentle with others, and making du'a for whatever you wish.
Supplication is especially answered upon seeing the Kaaba.
4
Analyze the hadith about answered supplication
The hadith (though with a weak chain) mentions four places: when ranks meet in battle, when rain falls, at iqamah, and upon seeing the House (Kaaba). The weakness is noted but the meaning is supported by the Prophet's action.
In matters of virtues of deeds (fada'il al-a'mal), even weak hadith may be acted upon according to many scholars.
