Makruh Actions in Prayer: A Comprehensive List
Enumerates disliked actions in prayer with brief rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling (makruh tanzihi) for each listed action and identify which actions are disliked but do not invalidate the prayer.

Core Idea
Disliked actions in prayer reduce reward but never invalidate it.

This lesson presents a memorization anchor from Nur al-Idah enumerating actions that are disliked (makruh) in salah according to the Hanafi school. These actions do not break the prayer but diminish its perfection. Understanding them helps you avoid common mistakes and improve your prayer quality.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the category
Identify that this is a list of makruh actions. In Hanafi fiqh, makruh is usually tanzihi (slightly disliked) unless specified. The ruling: performing them is not sinful but avoiding them is rewarded.
A few items (e.g., leaving shoulders uncovered) are explicitly makruh tanzihi.
2
Group the actions by type
The list covers clothing (e.g., rolling sleeves, i'tijar), posture (cross-legged, stretching), recitation (repeating surah, prolonging), and miscellaneous (yawning, killing lice). Group them mentally for easier memorization.
Items 10-19 focus on attire, 20-25 on recitation, 26-31 on movements, 32-43 on other actions.
3
Note the conditions and exceptions
Some actions are only makruh under certain conditions. For example, rolling sleeves is disliked if done during prayer, but there is ikhtilaf if rolled before (item 10). Covering shoulders is mustahabb, so leaving them uncovered is makruh tanzihi (item 19).
Check footnotes for specifics like the Tahawi and Maraqi references.
4
Distinguish makruh from invalidating actions
None of these actions invalidate the prayer. They only reduce reward. For instance, closing eyes (item 34) is makruh but the prayer is still valid. Memorize this distinction to avoid over-correcting.
This is a common misconception – that any disliked action breaks the prayer.

Disliked Actions in Prayer: Body and Attire (Items 26-45, 56-57)
Listing of disliked bodily actions and attire in prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and categorize the disliked actions related to body movements and clothing during prayer according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Makruhat do not invalidate prayer but detract from its perfection—avoid them for khushu.

This lesson introduces a list of actions that are considered makruh (disliked) during prayer according to the Hanafi madhhab. The focus is on actions involving the body, such as fidgeting, yawning, closing eyes, and improper prostration, as well as attire like work clothes or uncovered head. Understanding these helps the worshipper refine their prayer.

Guided Path
1
Read the list of actions
Review items 26-45 and 56-57. Note each action and its specific description.
Pay attention to footnotes (1)-(3) which clarify exceptions.
2
Identify the common theme
Group the actions: those that involve unnecessary movement, those that affect focus, and those related to clothing.
Most detract from khushu (humility) or violate adab.
3
Determine the ruling level
Understand that each action is makruh (disliked) and does not invalidate prayer.
Hanafi school divides makruh into tahrimi and tanzihi; here most are makruh tahrimi.
4
Apply the ruling to scenarios
Consider why prostrating on a turban fold is makruh (prevents forehead contact) and why killing a louse is makruh without excuse.
The exception for harm shows the school's practicality.

This passage is a compilation of actions that are makruh (disliked) during the performance of salah according to the Hanafi school. The list begins with item 26 and continues through item 57, covering various physical actions, positions, and external conditions. Most of these actions detract from the humility and focus required in prayer, but they do not invalidate it. Disliked Places and Conditions for Prayer (Items 46-55)
Listing of disliked places and the impurity ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the disliked places for prayer and the ruling on praying with a small amount of impurity.

Core Idea
The physical environment of prayer affects its perfection; avoid places that compromise dignity or purity.

This lesson covers the disliked places to pray, such as roads, bathhouses, graveyards, and the ruling on praying with a small amount of impurity. It also explains the exception when one fears missing the time or congregation. Understanding these helps the worshipper choose the best environment for prayer.

Guided Path
1
Read the list of places
Items 46-55 list places and conditions makruh for prayer. Note each one and the accompanying footnotes.
Focus on why each place is disliked (distraction, impurity, disrespect).
2
Understand the impurity ruling
Item 55 (combined with 56?) Actually the last part addresses najasah ghayr mani'ah. Understand the threshold and the exception.
Non-preventing impurity is excused if quantity is small; otherwise it is makruh.
3
Apply the exception
When fearing loss of time or congregation, one may continue praying even with impurity; otherwise it is recommended to cut the prayer and clean.
This shows the school's balance between perfection and practicality.

Makruhat al-Salah: Disliked Actions in Prayer
List of disliked actions with explanatory notes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the makruh actions in salah according to the Hanafi school, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute regarding the imam's position in the mihrab.

Core Idea
Disliked actions reduce reward but do not invalidate prayer; exceptions exist for images and imam's position.

This passage from Nur al-Idah enumerates actions that diminish the perfection of salah but do not nullify it. Understanding these rulings helps a worshipper avoid what is blameworthy while recognizing that valid prayer retains its essential reward. The list includes distractions, physical arrangements, and visual elements, with a notable disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions on the imam's standing in the mihrab.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish makruh tanzihi from tahrimi
All items in this list are makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked) unless noted. They do not make the prayer invalid or sinful in a way requiring punishment, but they reduce the reward and are blameworthy.
Makruh tahrimi is closer to haram and is not mentioned in this passage.
2
Categorize the disliked actions
Group the items: distractions (food, busy mind, counting), imam's position (mihrab, platform, alone), rows and sutrah, images and fire, recitation habits, and wiping dust. Each has a specific rationale.
Notice the pattern: anything that disrupts khushu' or visual focus is disliked.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking a sutrah (sunnah muakkadah) and list the recommended practices regarding its placement and dimensions.

Core Idea
A sutrah is sunnah muakkadah to block passersby, not a fard.

The sutrah is an important sunnah for maintaining focus in prayer by preventing people from crossing in front. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi rules on its use: when it is recommended, its size, and the proper way to position it. Understanding these details helps a worshipper implement the sunnah correctly without overburdening it with obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
The act is 'taking a sutrah' (placing a physical screen in front of the worshipper). In Hanafi fiqh, this is a sunnah (recommended) act, not obligatory (fard or wajib).
Sutrah is not a condition of prayer validity.
2
State the hukm level
The hukm for sutrah is sunnah muakkadah (confirmed sunnah) because the Prophet ﷺ regularly practiced it and commanded it. It is mustahabb when one suspects passersby.

3
Describe the recommended dimensions
The sutrah should be at least the length of a forearm (about 50 cm) and the thickness of a finger (about 2 cm). It should be firmly planted in the ground.
The thickness is to ensure it is visible to deter passersby.

Explain the sunnah placement
One should stand close to the sutrah, aligning it with one of the eyebrows (i.e., slightly to one side) and not stare directly at it. The point is to have a visual barrier, not to focus on the object.
Placing it directly in front and staring is disliked.
5
Note the alternative if no object is available
If no physical object can be set up, one may draw a line on the ground. The preferred orientation is widthwise (like a crescent) according to some scholars, though lengthwise is also acceptable.
A line serves as a symbolic barrier.

Ruling on Fighting the Passer-By During Prayer
Clarifying the ruling on fighting a passer-by in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fighting a passer-by during prayer and explain the basis of abrogation (naskh) for the conflicting hadith.

Core Idea
The hadith commanding to fight the passer-by is abrogated; the praying person must not fight.

In prayer, a subtle tension exists between maintaining focus and preventing disturbances. The Hanafi school rules that one must not physically fight a passer-by who crosses in front, despite a sahih hadith that appears to command fighting. This lesson unpacks the naskh (abrogation) mechanism that resolves the apparent contradiction, grounding the ruling in sound usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of fighting (مقاتلة) the passer-by during prayer. The ruling in the matn is clear: 'لا يُقاتِل المار' – he does not fight the passer-by.
This is a negative ruling: not permissible (makruh tahrimi).
2
Locate the conflicting hadith
The matn itself acknowledges a famous hadith from Sahih Muslim: 'When one of you prays, do not let anyone pass in front of him; repel him as much as possible; if he refuses, then fight him, for he is a devil.' This hadith seems to command fighting.
The apparent command conflicts with the Hanafi ruling.
3
Understand the abrogation mechanism
The matn states the hadith is 'mawwal' (interpreted) and 'nusikha' (abrogated). Al-Sarakhsi cites another hadith, 'Indeed prayer preoccupies' (إن الصلاة لشغلاً), which prohibits such physical action during prayer. The later hadith abrogates the earlier command to fight.
Naskh is a valid mechanism in Hanafi usul; the later revelation overrides the earlier.
4
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
While the Hanafi school holds the command abrogated, some other schools may still consider the hadith operative. The practical difference: Hanafi prohibits fighting; others may allow or even require it.
The ikhtilaf arises from different methodologies regarding naskh and the strength of the abrogating evidence.
5
Synthesize the proof chain
The Hanafi ruling relies on: 1) The hadith 'الصلاة لشغلاً' which indicates prayer is a preoccupation and thus physical fighting contradicts its essence. 2) The principle that commands in prayer are limited to light actions (like gestures). 3) The acceptance that the earlier hadith is abrogated.
Thus the proof is dalil naqli supported by usul al-naskh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on Witr, describe its performance, and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad on hand placement and follower recitation.

Core Idea
Witr is wajib in Hanafi: three rak'ahs, one taslim, qunut before ruku', but within the school details on hand placement and follower recitation differ.

Witr prayer holds a special status in the Hanafi school as an obligatory (wajib) prayer, falling just below the level of fard. This passage from Nur al-Idah gives a concise description of its form and the essential supplication (qunut), along with two internal disagreements among the Hanafi imams: how to place the hands during qunut and whether the follower recites the post-qunut du'a or says 'amin'. Understanding these details is key to performing Witr correctly and appreciating the depth of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Core Description
Start by noting that Witr is declared 'wajib'—a distinct Hanafi category. Then list the five points of description: three rak'ahs with one taslim, recitation of Fatiha and a surah in each, sitting after two rak'ahs for tashahhud only, no opening supplication when standing for the third, and qunut after recitation before ruku'.
The ruling is explicit: 'al-witr: wajib'.
2
Note the Qunut Exclusivity and Volume
Qunut is only performed in Witr (not other prayers), and it is recited softly ('mukhafatan') even by the imam—though an exception is mentioned for teaching purposes in non-Arab lands.
The footnote clarifies the correct position for recitation volume.
3
Examine the Hand Placement Disagreement
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the right hand is placed over the left during qunut; Muhammad holds that the hands are left hanging. Recognize this as an intra-Hanafi difference on a detail of posture.
The text also reports a variant from Abu Yusuf about raising the hands.
4
Understand the Difference on Follower Recitation
After the qunut, there is an additional du'a. Abu Yusuf says the follower recites it along with the imam; Muhammad says the follower should say 'amin' instead. This is another accepted difference within the school.
The footnote states that for the follower, qunut itself is sunnah, not wajib.
5
Apply the Knowledge to Practice
When performing Witr in congregation, the imam will recite the qunut and du'a softly. The follower must decide whether to recite or say 'amin'—the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is to say 'amin' to avoid errors. Also, the hands should be placed right over left for the dominant view.
The tarjih authority lies with later Hanafi scholars like al-Haskafi and Ibn Abidin.

Rulings on Performing Nafil Prayers: Sitting, Istiftah, and Preferred Method
Rulings on sitting, istiftah, istihsan, disliked amounts, and preference dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on the first sitting and istiftah in four-rak'ah nafil, and explain the preference regarding night prayer rak'ahs.

Core Idea
In mu'akkadah four-rak'ah nafil, the first sitting excludes salawat and the third rak'ah excludes istiftah; at night, praying two-by-two is the relied-upon position.

The matn from Nur al-Idah provides specific guidelines for how to perform voluntary prayers of more than two rak'ahs. It also addresses a well-known disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his students about night prayers. This lesson unpacks those rulings and the reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Understand the first sitting ruling
In the first sitting of a mu'akkadah four-rak'ah nafil, you recite only tashahhud, not the salawat. This is because the prayer is considered a single unit; the first sitting is a wajib that is concluded before the final sitting.
For mandatory nafil, you recite istiftah in the third rak'ah and the first sitting is not restricted.
2
Understand the istiftah omission
In the third rak'ah of the same prayer, you do not recite the opening supplication (istiftah) because it was already recited at the beginning of the prayer (the prayer is treated as one continuous unit).
For mandatory, you recite istiftah at the start of each pair.
3
Analyze the istihsan ruling
If someone prays more than two rak'ahs of nafil and only sits at the end (i.e., no first sitting), the prayer is valid by istihsan. Normally, missing a wajib (first sitting) would require sajdah sahw; but here, since the prayer is considered a single unit, the final sitting suffices. However, it is still makruh to omit the first sitting intentionally.
Istihsan is a Hanafi principle that allows leniency in certain cases.
4
Understand disliked increases
It is makruh tanzihi to pray more than four rak'ahs with one taslim during the day, and more than eight at night.
These are limits; exceeding them without splitting is disliked but not haram.
5
Examine the preference dispute
Abu Hanifa says four-by-four is best for both day and night; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say two-by-two at night is best, and this is the fatwa. The hadith 'صلاة الليل مثنى مثنى' supports the latter.
The mufta bihi is the two-by-two at night.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ship prayer is valid only when stable ground is present; otherwise invalid unless necessity excuses.

Prayer on a ship presents unique challenges: maintaining stability and facing the qibla. The Hanafi school provides clear rulings on when such prayer is valid and what to do if the ship deviates from the qibla. This lesson examines the conditions and exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is performing obligatory prayer (standing) on a ship. The ruling: if part of the ship is on stable ground, the prayer is valid; otherwise it is invalid on the preferred view (al-mukhtar).
Stable ground means the ship is touching the earth or anchored.
2
Note the exception
If the worshipper cannot get off the ship (e.g., at sea), the prayer becomes valid even without stable ground, due to necessity.
This exception shows that necessity can override the condition of stability.
3
Understand the qibla obligation
The worshipper must face the qibla at the opening takbir. If the ship turns during prayer, he must turn to face it again, and continue doing so until completion.
Facing qibla is a condition (shart) for validity; turning is required as long as possible.
4
Note the limitation on turning
If the worshipper is unable to turn (e.g., due to overcrowding or inability to move), he should stop the prayer (according to Maraqi al-Falah footnote).
This indicates that inability to fulfill a condition may necessitate abandoning the prayer.
5
Summarize the practical takeaway
The validity depends on ability: as long as you can fulfill conditions (stable ground and facing qibla), you must; if unable, necessity may excuse.
Always strive to meet conditions; if truly impossible, the prayer may still be valid or you must stop.
