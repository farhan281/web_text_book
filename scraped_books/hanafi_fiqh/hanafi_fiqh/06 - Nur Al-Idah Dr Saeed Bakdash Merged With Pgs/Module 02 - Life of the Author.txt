Author's full name, lineage, nisbah, and early life

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the full name, nisbahs, and key biographical details of Imam Hasan al-Shurunbulali.

Core Idea
Hasan al-Shurunbulali, author of Nur al-Idah, was a Hanafi jurist from Egypt whose early move to Cairo shaped his scholarly formation.

This lesson introduces the life of Imam Hasan al-Shurunbulali, the author of the foundational Hanafi matn Nur al-Idah. Understanding his biography helps contextualize his fiqh work and appreciate the chain of transmission. The passage traces his name, lineage, birthplace, and early education.

Guided Path
1
Identify the full name and kunyah
From the first line, extract the author's full name: Hasan ibn 'Ammar ibn 'Ali ibn Yusuf, and his kunyah: Abu al-Ikhlas.
The kunyah 'Abu al-Ikhlas' reflects a common practice among scholars.
2
Explain the two nisbahs
Examine 'al-Shurunbulali' (from the village Shubra Bulula) and 'al-Wafa'i' (affiliation with the Wafa'i Sufi order). Understand why 'al-Shurunbulali' is irregular.
The original village name is Shubra Bulula, but the nisbah takes an irregular form.
3
Summarize his early life journey
Describe his move from Shubra Bulula to Cairo at age six, his memorization of the Qur'an, and his pursuit of knowledge there.
This early exposure to Cairo's scholarly environment was crucial for his development.
4
Note his birth year and death
State the birth year: 994 AH. The death year is not given here but is known to be 1069 AH.
Birth and death years place him in the late 10th and 11th centuries AH.
5
Identify key biographical sources
List the sources referenced in the text for further study: Khulasat al-Athar, Hadiyyat al-'Arifin, al-Ta'liqat al-Saniyyah, al-A'lam, Mu'jam al-Mu'allifin, and the edition by Shaykh Muhammad Talhah Bilal Ahmad Minyar al-Makki.
These sources allow deeper exploration of the author's life and works.

The Authority of Imam al-Shurunbulali: His Chain and Praise
Biographical sketch establishing author's credentials.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the significance of al-Shurunbulali's scholarly chain and praise in establishing the authority of his fiqh works.

Core Idea
A Hanafi scholar's biographical chain (sanad) is essential to verify the authenticity and authority of his fiqh transmission.

This lesson introduces the author of Nur al-Idah, Imam al-Shurunbulali. While a biography may seem like a historical note, in the Hanafi tradition it serves a crucial role: it certifies the text's reliability by tracing the author's knowledge back to Imam Abu Hanifa. Understanding this context helps you trust the rulings in his works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Death and Lifespan
Note the exact date of death (11 Ramadan 1069 AH, age 75). This is a marker of when the author lived and taught.
Knowing the era helps place the author within later Hanafi scholarship (mutakhkhirin).
2
List the Key Teachers
Examine the three named teachers. Each is described as an Imam, jurist, and scholar. Their own chains connect al-Shurunbulali to earlier authorities.
Teachers like Ibn al-Salbi wrote glosses on major Hanafi works, showing depth of learning.
3
Trace the Students and the Chain
Identify three prominent students. Then follow the sanad: al-Tahtawi read Nur al-Idah with al-Jabarti, who received it from the author's son, who received it from the author, who had a chain to Abu Hanifa.
This chain (sanad) is the direct line of transmission, essential for authenticity.
4
Understand the Scholarly Praise
Read the praise from al-Muhibbi: al-Shurunbulali was the foremost jurist, relied upon for fatwas, and a master of fiqh texts and rules.
This establishes that his works, including Nur al-Idah, are authoritative within the school.
5
Connect to Textual Authority
Summarize: the biography shows that Nur al-Idah comes from a reliable chain and a scholar of high standing. This is why the text is used as a foundational manual in Hanafi fiqh.
Without this background, one might undervalue the text's authority.

He died on Friday after the Asr prayer, the eleventh of Ramadan, year 1069 AH, at the age of seventy-five.
Among his most important teachers from whom he studied:
1. Sheikh Ahmad ibn Muhammad, famously known as Ibn al-Salbī, the Imam, jurist, hadith scholar, author of the gloss on Tabīn al-Ḥaqā’iq Sharḥ Kanz al-Daqā’iq, died around twenty and one thousand AH.
2. Sheikh Muhammad ibn Abd al-Rahman al-Ḥamawī, the Imam and scholar, died 1017 AH.
3. Sheikh Muhammad ibn al-Muḥibb al-Muḥibbī al-Miṣrī, the Imam and scholar, died 1041 AH, and others.
* Imam al-Shurunbulālī taught at al-Azhar Mosque, and many people studied with him and benefited from him, among them:
1. Sheikh Ismā‘īl ibn ‘Abd al-Ghanī ibn Ismā‘īl al-Nābulusī al-Dimashqī, the Imam and scholar, died 1062 AH, and he is the father of the famous scholar Sheikh ‘Abd al-Ghanī al-Nābulusī, died 1143 AH.
2. Sheikh Aḥmad ibn Muḥammad al-‘Ajamī al-Shāfi‘ī, the last of the hadith scholars in Egypt, died 1086 AH.
3. The scholar Sheikh Shihāb al-Dīn Aḥmad ibn Muḥammad al-Ḥusaynī al-Ḥamawī then al-Miṣrī, author of the gloss on al-Ashbāh wa al-Naẓā’ir titled “Ghamz ‘Uyūn al-Baṣā’ir,” died 1098 AH.
Al-Shurunbulālī’s Chain in Fiqh:
Al-Shurunbulālī has a famous and widespread chain in fiqh, connecting to Imam Abū Ḥanīfa al-Nu‘mān, may Allah have mercy on him. Al-Ṭaḥṭāwī (d. 1231 AH) mentioned it and paid attention to it in his thabat, stating that he read Nūr al-Īḍāḥ with the scholar and jurist Ḥasan ibn Ibrāhīm ibn Ḥasan al-Jabartī (d. 1188 AH), who received it from the son of the author, Ḥasan ibn Ḥasan ibn ‘Ammār al-Shurunbulālī (d. 1123 AH), from his father, the author.
Scholars’ Praise of Him and His Status in Fiqh:
Muḥammad Amīn al-Muḥibbī, author of Khulāṣat al-Athar, said:
“He was among the foremost jurists and virtuosos of his time, whose fame spread and whose matter became widespread. He was the best of the later scholars, the most prolific in editing and composition, and the one relied upon for fatwas in his era.” End quote.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to decode the abbreviations ق (folio count) and دق (very short treatise) from a classical Hanafi manuscript list, and identify the fiqh topic of each treatise based on its title or parenthetical note.

Core Idea
A single letter ق encodes the length of a treatise; understanding these abbreviations unlocks the bibliographic scope of a Hanafi scholar.

Al-Shurunbulali, author of Nur al-Idah, was a prolific Hanafi jurist who wrote many short treatises on specific fiqh topics. This lesson guides you through the list of his works (numbers 42-60 and three additional ones), teaching you to interpret the manuscript abbreviations and recognize the wide range of fiqh subjects he addressed. This skill is essential for any student of Hanafi fiqh researching primary sources.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the List Structure
Each entry is numbered with a title, followed by a parenthetical note. The note typically states the fiqh topic (e.g., 'On pledge') and the length in folios (e.g., '4ق'). Some entries also mention publication or commentary details.
The list is not exhaustive but represents the major known works of al-Shurunbulali.
2
Decode the Abbreviation ق
The symbol ق (qaf) stands for 'waraqah' (folio), a unit of manuscript length. A number before it (e.g., 11ق) means 11 folios. This is NOT a century or a page number in a modern book.
Very short treatises may use دق (daqaiq), meaning 'a few pages' or 'minutes' of reading.
3
Identify the Fiqh Topic
The fiqh chapter is often stated explicitly (e.g., 'On pledge', 'On lease'). If not, the title itself indicates the topic (e.g., 'The Clear Path for Avoiding the Flaw of the Proof' is about judiciary).
Al-Shurunbulali wrote on ʿibādāt (prayer, fasting), muʿāmalāt (sales, pledge, lease), and other areas like taqlid and diyat.
4
Appreciate the Author's Breadth
Scan the list and note the variety: from theoretical topics like taqlid to practical issues like breastfeeding and church rulings. This shows the comprehensive scope of a Hanafi jurist's output.
Many treatises are very short (2-5 folios), indicating focused, problem-specific writing.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hafs al-Kabir represents the diversity and independent reasoning within the Hanafi school.

The Hanafi school is not monolithic; later mujtahids regularly exercised ijtihad within its framework. One such figure is Abu Hafs al-Kabir, a student of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani and the leading Hanafi authority of Transoxiana. His independent opinions (ikhtiyarat) sometimes differed from the mainstream view, reminding us that the school's jurisprudence is a living tradition of reasoned elaboration.
