Core Idea
Washing the deceased is a sunnah muakkadah with a precise order; each step preserves the dignity of the Muslim.

This passage from Nur al-Idah outlines the recommended method (sunnah) for washing a deceased Muslim. The steps ensure thorough purification while maintaining respect. The Hanafi school also specifies what is not done – such as cutting nails or combing hair – to preserve the natural state.

Identify the sequence of washing

The Right to Wash a Deceased Spouse: Conditions and Exceptions
Rulings on spouses washing each other after death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on spouse washing and identify the conditions that preserve or nullify this right.

Core Idea
Washing the deceased spouse is permitted only if the marriage bond exists at death.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling of janazah (funeral) rites: who may wash the deceased spouse. The Hanafi school permits a wife to wash her husband and vice versa, but this right depends on the continuation of the marriage contract at the moment of death. We will explore the key exceptions—revocable divorce, irrevocable divorce, and the case of the umm walad—and understand the legal reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
The text states: 'And the wife washes her husband.' The husband may similarly wash his wife. This is the default rule in the Hanafi school, considered a sunnah mu'akkadah (stressed recommendation).
2
Recognize the essential condition
The permissibility hinges on the existence of a valid marriage bond at the time of death. If the marriage is severed, the right is lost.
3
Apply the condition to revocable divorce (talaq raj'i)
During the 'iddah of a revocable divorce, the marriage is still considered intact for certain rulings, including the right to wash. So the wife may wash her husband.
4
Identify when the right is lost
If the 'iddah has expired or the divorce is irrevocable (ba'in), the marriage is severed (inqita' al-nikah). Similarly, an umm walad becomes free upon the master's death, ending any spousal-like relationship. In both cases, washing is not permitted.
The rationale: the marriage bond is the basis for the permission.
5
Examine the evidence (dalil)
The text provides two short proofs: 'Due to the severance of marriage' for the irrevocably divorced woman, and 'Because she becomes free upon his death' for the umm walad. This shows that the ruling is rationally derived from the existence or absence of the marital relationship.

Tayammum substitutes for ghusl when washing would expose 'awrah before a non-mahram or when gender is ambiguous.

The Hanafi school obligates ghusl for the deceased Muslim, but in exceptional circumstances—such as when only non-mahram individuals are present or the gender is ambiguous—tayammum is performed to preserve the deceased's dignity. These rulings relax the strict gender segregation rules for children.

The goal is to avoid looking at or touching the 'awrah of the deceased by a non-mahram. When washing would require that, tayammum is substituted.
This is derived from the general obligation to cover 'awrah.

Responsibility for Funeral Expenses
Ruling on funeral expense hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchy of responsibility for funeral expenses according to the Hanafi school and apply it to specific cases.

Core Idea
Funeral costs fall first on the husband for his wife, then on the maintenance obligor, then the treasury, then the community.

This passage from Nur al-Idah establishes the sequence of who must cover the expenses of preparing a deceased person for burial. Understanding this hierarchy ensures that the deceased is honoured appropriately and that the financial burden is placed on the correct party according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary obligation: husband for wife
The first ruling states that the husband is responsible for his wife's funeral expenses, even if he is poor. This is the most sound opinion (al-aṣaḥḥ) in the Hanafi school.
The obligation is a debt on the husband, to be paid even if he is currently unable.
2
Note the condition: the deceased has no wealth
The cascade applies to 'man lā māla lah' (the one who has no wealth). If the deceased has assets, those are used first.
3
Identify the secondary obligation: maintenance obligor
For a deceased with no wealth, the person who was obliged to provide maintenance during life (e.g., father for child, guardian for ward) must cover funeral costs.
This applies if that person is financially able, otherwise it falls to the next level.
4
Recognize the tertiary obligation: bayt al-mal
If no such maintenance obligor exists (or none is able), the expense is taken from the public treasury (bayt al-māl) of the Islamic state.
This assumes a functioning treasury; if absent, see next step.
5
Understand the final fallback: the Muslim community
If the treasury cannot provide due to inability (lack of funds) or injustice (unjust ruler withholding), then the cost becomes a collective obligation (fard kifāyah) upon the Muslim community.
Any Muslim may volunteer, or the community may be taxed equitably.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on joining the funeral prayer late and identify the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding waiting for the takbir.

Core Idea
When joining the funeral prayer late, the Hanafi school prefers allowing immediate entry over waiting, overriding the zahir al-riwaya.

This passage from Nur al-Idah addresses a common scenario in funeral prayer: arriving after the imam has started. The ruling distinguishes between those who join during the opening takbir (tahrima) and those who arrive between takbirs. An important intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf exists between the three founders of the madhhab on the second scenario, with the later fatwa adopting the view of Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage deals with two scenarios: (1) arriving during the opening takbir, and (2) arriving between two takbirs. Focus on the second scenario where the dispute arises.
The ruling for the first scenario is straightforward: join immediately.
2
State the majority position (zahir al-riwaya)
Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad hold that the latecomer must wait for the imam's next takbir before joining. This is the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwaya).
This position is based on maintaining correct sequence of takbirs.
3
State Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Abu Yusuf holds that the latecomer should not wait but should begin immediately with his own takbir, which counts as part of the prayer.
This view is preferred by later authorities (al-Kafi, al-Durr) and is the basis for fatwa.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the majority view, the latecomer misses some takbirs by waiting; under Abu Yusuf's view, he starts immediately and his takbir counts, but he must later make up the missed takbirs.
The number of missed takbirs to be made up is also disputed.
5
Note the tarjih and mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., author of al-Kafi and al-Durr) preponderated Abu Yusuf's view. Therefore, the operative ruling for fatwa is that the latecomer should not wait but begin immediately.
This is a case where the mufta bihi differs from the zahir al-riwaya.

The Parable of the Mu'takif: The Spiritual Fortress of I'tikaf
Commentary expansion on the matn line with a parable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the spiritual significance of i'tikaf as expressed in 'Ata's parable and identify its role as sunnah mu'akkadah in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is spiritual persistence at Allah's door, seeking forgiveness without leaving.

This lesson explores a beautiful parable from the tabi'i scholar 'Ata ibn Abi Rabah, which illustrates the essence of i'tikaf. While the matn of Nur al-Idah describes i'tikaf as fortifying oneself in Allah's fortress, 'Ata's parable deepens our understanding by comparing the mu'takif to a person who comes to a great door for a need and refuses to leave until it is granted. This captures the attitude of humble persistence and hope that defines i'tikaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn line being commented on
The matn states 'wa al-tahassun bi-hisnihi' (and fortifying oneself in His fortress). This sets the context: i'tikaf is a form of spiritual fortification.
2
Analyze 'Ata's parable
The mu'takif is like a person who repeatedly comes to a great door for a need and says, 'I will not leave until He forgives me.' This teaches that i'tikaf involves persistent devotion and hope for divine mercy.
The parable emphasizes the mu'takif's state of humble persistence (tahassun) rather than mere physical presence.
3
Connect the parable to the concept of tahassun
Tahassun means fortification. The mu'takif fortifies himself by staying in the mosque, the 'fortress of Allah,' and by persisting in worship and seeking forgiveness, as illustrated in the parable.
The parable shows that the fortification is both physical (remaining in the mosque) and spiritual (persistent supplication).
4
Note the biographical context
'Ata ibn Abi Rabah was a prominent tabi'i, teacher of Imam Abu Hanifa, and a leading scholar of his time. His inclusion adds weight to the commentary.
The author mentions two similar reports, indicating that the parable is a recognized spiritual teaching.
5
Reflect on the significance for the Hanafi school
In the Hanafi school, i'tikaf is sunnah mu'akkadah during Ramadan, especially the last ten days. This parable underscores the importance of intention and sincerity in the act.
The parable aligns with the Hanafi emphasis on the spiritual dimension of acts of worship.

Definition and Conditions of Zakat Obligation
Defines zakat and lists conditions for its obligation and payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of zakat, list the conditions for its obligation (shurut al-wujub), and identify the condition for its payment (shart al-ada).

Core Idea
Zakat is an obligation on productive wealth after a lunar year, not on idle assets.

Zakat is one of the five pillars of Islam, but its obligation is conditional. In this passage from Nur al-Idah, we learn the precise Hanafi definition and the conditions that must be met for zakat to be obligatory. Understanding these conditions prevents common errors in calculating and paying zakat.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the definition
The text defines zakat as 'transfer of ownership of specified wealth to a specified person'. Note that this is a tamlik (transfer) definition, not just a discharge. This impacts how zakat is given.
The word 'specified' (makhsus) refers to the nisab and recipients.
2
List the conditions for obligation
The text gives six conditions: Muslim, mukallaf, owner of nisab, free from debt and basic needs, wealth is productive (actual or potential), and passage of a year.
The last condition (hawl) is actually a condition for performance, but listed here.
3
Distinguish between shurut al-wujub and shart al-ada
The condition for performance (ada) is the passing of the lunar year. The other conditions are for the obligation itself. Note that if the year passes but the other conditions are not met, no zakat is due.
This distinction is critical for knowing when the obligation actually crystallizes.
4
Apply the rule for acquired wealth
Wealth acquired during the year (mustafad) is added to its type and zakat is paid at the end of the original year. This is a facilitation.
This rule simplifies tracking multiple acquisitions.
5
Note the ruling on advance payment
Paying zakat early (ta'jil) for several years is valid if the payer already owns a nisab.
Advance payment is permissible but not mandatory.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on Zakat from Nur al-Idah. It begins with the legal definition of zakat as the transfer of ownership of specified wealth to a specified person. Then it lists the conditions for zakat to become obligatory (shurut al-wujub): the person must be Muslim, of sound mind and adult (mukallaf), own a nisab (minimum threshold) of currency (gold, silver, or their equivalents like jewelry, vessels, or trade goods), be free from debt covering the nisab, have basic needs met, and the wealth must be productive (nama'), either actually or potentially. Potential productivity (nama' taqdiri) means the wealth is capable of growth, such as money that can be traded. Then it states the condition for paying zakat (shart al-ada'): the passing of a full lunar year (hawl) on the original nisab. If additional wealth is acquired during the year (mustafad), it is added to the same type and zakat is paid at the end of the original year. Finally, it permits advance payment (ta'jil) of zakat for several years, which is valid.

Key Terms
زكاة (zakāh): Transfer of ownership of specified wealth (mal makhsus) to a specified person (shakhs makhsus).
نصاب (niṣāb): A minimum threshold of wealth (for currency it is 20 mithqal of gold or 200 dirhams of silver) that triggers zakat obligation.
حول (ḥawl): A full lunar year that must pass on the nisab for zakat to be obligatory.
نام (nāmin): Productive wealth, either actually (through trade or reproduction) or potentially (capable of growth).

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib on every free Muslim with surplus nisab at dawn, without haul, beyond basic needs.

This lesson covers the core conditions that trigger the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. Unlike annual zakat, the nisab here does not require a year's passage or growth. The text also defines 'basic needs' (hajah asliyyah) that are exempt from calculation. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly determining who is liable to pay Fitr.

Define basic needs (hajah asliyyah)
Enumerate the six items: dwelling, furniture, clothes, horse, weapon, slaves for service. Criterion is sufficiency (kifayah), not fixed estimation.

Rulings of Tawaf, Ramal, and Sa'i
Sequence of actions for tawaf and sa'i with conditions and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the correct sequence of actions for tawaf and sa'i, and state the ruling (sunnah/wajib) for each action including ramal and istilam.

Core Idea
Tawaf must be performed behind the Hatim; ramal is sunnah mu'akkadah, and istilam has a substitute if crowding occurs.

This lesson unpacks the sunan and conditions of tawaf and sa'i as prescribed in the Hanafi school. You will learn the proper etiquette of greeting the Black Stone, the method of tawaf with idtiba', the ruling of ramal, and the steps of sa'i between Safa and Marwah. A critical footnote clarifies a common misconception about ramal being obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Greet the Black Stone (Istilam)
Identify the correct procedure: face the Stone, raise hands, place them on it, and kiss without sound. If unable without annoyance, touch it with an object and kiss that, or gesture from afar.
Istilam is sunnah, not fard.
2
Perform Tawaf
Tawaf consists of seven circuits starting from the side of the door, taking to the right, in the state of idtiba'. Must be performed behind the Hatim. Conclude with two rak'ahs at Maqam Ibrahim.
Idtiba' is sunnah for men; tawaf al-qudum is sunnah for non-Meccans.
3
Understand Ramal
If sa'i follows immediately, perform ramal (quick walking with shoulder movements) in the first three circuits. If crowded, stop and resume when possible, as ramal has no substitute.
The footnote corrects the text: ramal is not obligatory but sunnah mu'akkadah.
4
Perform Sa'i
Ascend Safa, face Ka'ba, make du'a, descend, walk quickly in the valley between green markers, then ascend Marwah and repeat. Each crossing is one circuit; total seven circuits.
Sa'i is wajib in the Hanafi school, but the passage focuses on the sunan of its performance.
5
Distinguish Substitute and Ruling
Compare istilam (has substitute: gesture) with ramal (no substitute: must wait for opening). Learn the consequence: ramal's sunnah status means it does not invalidate tawaf if missed.
This distinction is key to avoid unnecessary repetition.

The text is a straightforward instructional sequence, typical of a memorization text. It begins with the etiquette of greeting the Black Stone (istilam): one should face it, raise hands, place them on the Stone, and kiss it without sound. Commentary
This passage is a practical manual (matn) from Nur al-Idah, detailing the recommended supplications and etiquette for visiting the grave of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the grave of Abu Bakr as-Siddiq ﷺ. The text begins by affirming that the Prophet hears the visitor's words, returns the greeting, and says 'Amin' to their supplications—a point based on hadith evidence about the Prophet's awareness of those who send salutations upon him. Then it provides the full sequence of salutations (salam) addressing the Prophet by various honorific titles such as 'O Intercessor of the Nation' and 'O Seal of the Prophets'. After that, the visitor bears witness to the Prophet's fulfilment of his mission and sends abundant blessings. The passage then includes a confession of sins and a plea for intercession, citing verse 4:64 and encouraging the visitor to ask for intercession three times. A second part instructs the visitor to convey greetings from others who have requested it. Then the visitor moves to the grave of Abu Bakr (ﷺ), positioned about a cubit away, and addresses him with praises for his companionship, his caliphate, his role in fighting apostasy, and his steadfastness. This du'a encapsulates the Hanafi reverence for the Prophet and his Companions, without delving into legal rulings on the permissibility or conditions of ziyarah, which are discussed elsewhere in the books of fiqh.

Key Terms
الشفاعة (ash-shafa'ah): Intercession; specifically, the Prophet's intercession on the Day of Judgment and during visitation, when the visitor asks the Prophet to intercede with Allah for forgiveness.
السلام عليك (as-salamu 'alayka): A greeting of peace directed to the Prophet or the Companion, used during visitation as a form of salutation and respect.
زيارة النبي (ziyarat an-nabi): Visiting the Prophet's grave in Medina, an act of devotion and seeking blessings, with prescribed prayers and etiquette.
