Definition and Divisions of Taharah
Definition and classification of taharah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of taharah, distinguish between actual (khabath) and legal (hadath) impurity, and identify the corresponding purification methods.

Core Idea
Taharah is purification from both physical and legal impurity, achieved through wudu, ghusl, or tayammum.

This opening passage of Kitab al-Taharah establishes the foundational definitions. You will learn that taharah is not merely physical cleanliness but a legal state required for worship. The author divides impurity into two types and presents the corresponding purification methods, highlighting the importance of wudu due to its frequent repetition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of taharah
Find the linguistic and legal definitions. Linguistically, it means cleanliness. Legally, it means cleanliness from impurity: either actual (khabath) or legal (hadath).
2
Distinguish types of impurity
Khabath is physical impurity (e.g., blood, urine). Hadath is legal impurity (minor or major). Note that hadath prevents valid prayer.
Minor hadath requires wudu; major hadath requires ghusl.
3
Classify purification methods
Major purification (ghusl) for major hadath; minor purification (wudu) for minor hadath. Tayammum substitutes for both when water is unavailable.
4
Understand the ordering rationale
Acts of worship are given precedence; prayer is the mainstay; purification is its key; wudu is most frequently needed.
This explains why Kitab al-Taharah comes first in fiqh books.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Purification (Kitab al-Taharah). It begins with the basmalah, then provides a definition of taharah. Linguistically, it means cleanliness. Legally, it means cleanliness from impurity (najasah). Impurity is of two types: actual (khabath) which is physical filth, and legal (hadath) which is a state of ritual impurity. حدث (hadath): Legal impurity that prevents valid prayer; either major (janabah) or minor (lack of wudu).
تيمم (tayammum): Dry purification using clean earth as a substitute for wudu or ghusl when water is unavailable or harmful.

Definition and Types of Taharah (Purity) in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines taharah and divides it into types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definition of taharah and identify the three types of taharah and their causes.

Core Idea
Taharah is both physical and ritual purity; it includes ghusl, wudu, and tayammum.

This passage opens the chapter on taharah by defining it both linguistically and legally. It then divides taharah into three types: major (ghusl), minor (wudu), and substitute (tayammum). Understanding this classification is essential, as the entire book of ibadat builds on the concept of purity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
The author begins by stating that taharah linguistically means cleanliness (النظافة). This is the basic lexical sense.
2
Identify the legal meaning
Legally, taharah means purity from two kinds of impurity: physical (najasah) and legal (hadath). Physical impurity is actual filth; legal impurity is a state that prevents worship.
3
List the three types of taharah
Taharah is divided by the second consideration (cause) into major (ghusl), minor (wudu), and a substitute (tayammum). Note that ghusl is required for major hadath, wudu for minor hadath, and tayammum for either when water is unavailable.
4
Recognize the rationale for ordering
The author explains why the book begins with taharah: worship is the most important, prayer is the pillar of worship, taharah is the key to prayer, and wudu is the most frequently performed type of taharah.

The Fard (Obligatory Acts) of Wudu in the Quran
Cites the wudu verse and states the obligatory acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four fard acts of wudu (washing face, hands, feet; wiping head) and explain why they are considered fard qat'i based on the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Washing the three limbs and wiping the head are fard because the Quran commands them with imperative verbs, which in Hanafi usul indicate obligation.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri opens with the famous wudu verse from Surah al-Ma'idah (5:6) and draws from it the obligatory acts of wudu. The author also clarifies the term 'fard' to emphasize that these acts are proven by definitive evidence (qat'i), distinguishing them from acts of lesser certainty. Understanding this derivation is foundational to Hanafi fiqh of taharah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text
The author begins by citing the Quranic verse (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:6). Note that he does this as a proof and for blessing, even though his usual custom is to avoid citing proofs prominently in this text.
The verse is the primary dalil for the obligations of wudu.
2
Extract the commanded actions
From the verse, identify the imperative verbs: 'faghsilu' (wash) for face, hands, and feet; 'imsahu' (wipe) for the head. These are direct commands.
The word 'arjulakum' (feet) is connected to 'faghsilu' in the Hanafi qira'a, indicating washing, not wiping.
3
Determine the ruling level
Apply the Hanafi usul principle: al-amr yufidu al-wujub (the imperative indicates obligation). Since the proof is the Quran, which is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalalah, the obligation is at the level of fard, not merely wajib.
Fard is defined as an obligation established by definitive proof with no ambiguity.
4
Count the limbs
The text mentions three limbs but counts the hands and feet as two groups, making five actual organs. They are legally treated as three categories: face, hands (including arms), feet (including legs to ankles), and head (wiping).
This explains why the author says 'three limbs' when there are five.
5
Understand the term 'fard'
The passage gives a legal definition: 'what is established as binding by a definitive proof with no ambiguity'. This is the Hanafi distinction between fard (qat'i) and wajib (zanni).
The washing and wiping are fard 'ilman wa 'amalan (in belief and practice).

The Obligatory Acts of Wudu: Fard Qat'i and Fard Ijtihadi
Opens Kitab al-Taharah with Qur'anic evidence and defines fard

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four obligatory acts of wudu (fard) according to the Hanafi school and distinguish between fard qat'i and fard ijtihadi with examples.

Core Idea
Wudu's obligatory acts are founded on a definitive Qur'anic proof, but the exact measure of wiping the head is open to ijtihad.

This passage opens the Book of Purification in Al-Lubab, a commentary on Al-Quduri's matn. The author begins by citing the well-known verse of wudu (Surah Al-Ma'idah 5:6) as the primary proof. He then defines the obligatory acts: washing the face, hands to elbows, feet to ankles, and wiping the head. Importantly, the text introduces a key Hanafi distinction between two categories of fard: that which is established by definitive proof (fard qat'i) and that which is determined through ijtihad (fard ijtihadi). Understanding this distinction is crucial for recognizing the relative strength of each obligation and the consequences of denying them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Qur'anic proof
The author begins with the Qur'anic verse (5:6) that establishes the obligatory acts. Note that the verse uses the imperative 'فَاغْسِلُوا' and 'وَامْسَحُوا', which indicates obligation (wujub) by the principle that al-amr yufidu al-wujub.
The verse is cited as a proof (dalil naqli). The imperative form is key.
2
List the obligatory acts (fard)
From the verse, the text extracts four acts: washing the face, washing the hands to elbows, wiping the head, and washing the feet to ankles. They are collectively called 'washing the three limbs and wiping the head' because hands and feet are each treated as one limb in the ruling.
These are the fard acts in wudu according to the Hanafi school.
3
Distinguish between fard qat'i and fard ijtihadi
The text defines fard linguistically and legally. Fard qat'i is established by a decisive proof (like the general obligation to wash and wipe). Fard ijtihadi is determined by scholarly reasoning and its omission invalidates the act, but denial does not cause disbelief (e.g., the minimum amount of head to wipe).
This distinction affects the ruling on a person who denies a fard: denial of qat'i is kufr, denial of ijtihadi is not.
4
Define the anatomical boundaries
The text provides definitions for the face (from forehead hairline to chin, between earlobes), the elbows (joint connecting forearm to upper arm), and the ankles (protruding bones at the foot-leg joint). These define the limits that must be washed.
Knowing these boundaries ensures the validity of wudu. Washing must cover these areas.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school requires wiping at least one-quarter of the head (fard ijtihadi), while the Shafi'i school requires wiping the entire head (fard qat'i for them). This difference stems from the interpretation of the preposition 'ب' in 'وامسحوا برءوسكم'.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf (mukhtalaf fih). Both schools base their view on the same verse.

Washing in wudu requires water to drip; the face, elbows, and ankles must be washed within defined limits.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the obligatory amount of wiping the head and identify the hadith proof from al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah.

Core Idea
The obligatory part of wiping the head is only the forelock (a quarter), based on the Prophet's action.

The Hanafi school specifies that the fard (obligatory) amount for wiping the head during wudu is the forelock, which constitutes approximately one quarter of the head. This lesson examines the textual proof from the hadith of al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah, where the Prophet (SAW) wiped only his forelock. Understanding this ruling clarifies the minimum requirement for valid wudu according to Abu Hanifa's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The passage explicitly states that the obligatory (al-mufrud) amount in wiping the head (masah al-ra's) is the forelock, i.e., a quarter of the head. Recognize that this is a ruling on the minimum part that must be wiped for wudu to be valid.
This is a ruling on the fard amount, not on the desirability of wiping the entire head.
2
Locate the Dalil
The proof cited is a hadith from al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah: the Prophet (SAW) went to a rubbish dump, urinated, performed wudu, and wiped over his forelock (nasiyah). This hadith is ahad (single narration) and thus zanni al-thubut, but it serves as the primary evidence for the Hanafi position.
The hadith is narrated in Sunan Abi Dawud and other collections.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The Prophet's action of wiping only his forelock indicates that wiping the entire head is not obligatory. The Hanafi principle is that the Prophet's act (fi'l) can specify the general command in the Quran. Here, the command 'amsahu bi ru'usikum' (wipe your heads) is rendered specific by the sunnah.
The forelock (nasiyah) is the front part, amounting to about a quarter.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
While Abu Hanifa holds the quarter view, his two principal students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, held that wiping the entire head is obligatory. Their proof is the apparent generality of the Quranic command.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position for fatwa is Abu Hanifa's quarter view.
5
Evaluate the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference is that under Abu Hanifa's view, wiping only the forelock suffices for wudu, while according to the Sahibayn, one must wipe the whole head. This affects ease of practice, especially in cold weather or when wearing a cap.
Know that both views are within the Hanafi school, but the quarter view is the operative one.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Intention, wiping the whole head, order, and starting with the right are sunnah or mustahabb — not fard — in Hanafi wudu.

In this passage from al-Lubab, we examine four actions that al-Quduri initially listed as mustahabb (recommended but not consistently performed) but later Hanafi authorities elevated to sunnah (consistent practice of the Prophet). The disagreement among early and later scholars reflects a deeper methodological issue: how do we classify an act that the Prophet performed occasionally versus consistently? This lesson will help you navigate such classification disputes and apply the relied-upon ruling in your wudu.

Guided Path

2
Understand the classification terms
The text distinguishes 'mustahabb' and 'sunnah'. Al-Quduri called these actions 'mustahabb', but the author of al-Hidayah and later scholars called them 'sunnah'. Know that 'sunnah' implies the Prophet consistently did it, while 'mustahabb' implies it is good but not consistently performed.
The definition of mustahabb in the text: 'what is done once or twice'.
3

Al-Quduri's position (mustahabb) vs. the majority of later mashayikh (sunnah). The text quotes al-Fath: 'There is no basis for al-Quduri… in making intention, wiping the whole head, and order mustahabb rather than sunnah.'

When performing wudu, include these four actions. Since they are sunnah/mustahabb, omitting them does not invalidate wudu, but doing them earns reward and is the preferred practice.

Defining Nawaqid al-Wudu and the Term Ma'ani
Defines two terms: ma'na (mental concept) and nawaqid (nullifiers) for wudu.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the terms 'ma'na' and 'naqid' as used in Hanafi fiqh and explain the analogy between physical and conceptual nullification.

Core Idea
To nullify wudu (naqd) means to remove it from its intended purpose, just as breaking a physical object destroys its structure.

This passage opens a key discussion in Hanafi taharah by defining two critical terms: 'al-ma'ani' (the meanings) and 'al-nawaqid' (the nullifiers). Understanding these definitions is essential before studying the specific actions that break wudu. The author uses a conceptual analogy: naqd in physical objects is breaking, and in legal meanings it is removing the act from its intended function.

Guided Path

Identify the definition of 'ma'na'
Read the first part of the passage: 'al-ma'ani' is the plural of 'ma'na', which is the mental form intended by a word. Note that it is the concept in the mind, not the physical action.
This distinction is important because 'nawaqid' are actions that affect this conceptual 'ma'na' of wudu.
2
Identify the definition of 'al-nawaqid li-l-wudu'
The passage defines 'nawaqid' as that which takes wudu out from serving its intended purpose. The intended purpose of wudu is to enable acts like prayer.
The term is general; specific nullifiers are listed later in the text.
3
Analyze the physical vs. conceptual analogy
The author explains that 'naqd' in physical objects means destroying their composition. In meanings (legal concepts), it means removing them from their intended function. This analogy clarifies that nullifiers break the legal effect of wudu.
This reasoning (qa'ida mantiqiyya) helps extend the concept to other acts of worship.

Nullification of Wudu: The General Rule and Conditions
Explains the general rule and conditions for nullification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that every exit from the two passages (sabilayn) nullifies wudu unconditionally, and explain that impurities exiting elsewhere require flow (sailan) and reaching a place subject to purification (tajawuz) to nullify.

Core Idea
Wudu is nullified by any exit from the two passages, but from elsewhere only if impurity flows and reaches a place that must be purified.

This passage from Sharh al-Quduri establishes the foundational distinction for nullification of wudu. Understanding when an impurity nullifies wudu is critical for daily practice. The Hanafi school uniquely emphasizes the conditions of flow and tajawuz for exits other than the two passages, setting it apart from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the exit of a substance from the body. The ruling is that wudu is nullified (batil) when conditions are met. First, note the two categories: exit from the two passages (unconditional) and exit from elsewhere (conditional).
2
Locate the dalil
The text does not cite a specific Quranic verse or hadith but relies on the principle that the two passages are loci of impurity (mahall al-najasa), so mere appearance indicates transfer. For other exits, the impurity must reach a place that requires purification in wudu (tajawuz) and have flowing ability (sailan). This is based on qiyas and istihsan.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The text states: 'کل ما خرج من السبيلين' unconditionally nullifies because the place itself is impure. For other exits, it says 'إذا خرج من البدن فتجاوز إلى موضع يلحقه حكم التطهير' – the impurity must go beyond its original site to a place that is washed in purification. This is the key proof-text for the condition of tajawuz.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that blood exiting anywhere nullifies wudu without requiring flow, based on a hadith. The Hanafi school restricts nullification to conditions of flow and tajawuz, arguing that the hadith is weak or interpreted differently.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative position) in the Hanafi school is that nullification from other exits requires both flow (sailan) and reaching a place subject to purification (tajawuz). This is the position of the majority of Hanafi scholars and is relied upon for fatwa.

Sleep and Unconsciousness as Nullifiers of Wudu
Explaining when sleep and unconsciousness break wudu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish sleep postures that nullify wudu from those that do not, and explain the Hanafi criterion of complete relaxation (istirkha').

Core Idea
Sleep nullifies wudu only when relaxation is complete – lying, leaning; not in prayer postures.

Sleep is a common experience, yet it does not always break wudu in the Hanafi school. This lesson unpacks the precise conditions: sleep nullifies only when the body is in a state of complete relaxation, such that if any support were removed, the person would fall. Understanding this criterion saves one from unnecessary repetition of wudu or praying with an invalid state.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Sleep is a presumptive nullifier (naqid hukmi) – it nullifies wudu only under certain conditions. Identify the ruling: sleep nullifies when complete relaxation occurs.
2
Distinguish nullifying from non-nullifying postures
List the postures that nullify: lying down, leaning on one hip, leaning against something such that if removed he would fall. Non-nullifying: standing, sitting, bowing, prostrating (in prayer or otherwise).
The key is that in non-nullifying postures, some muscular retention remains.
3
Understand the underlying principle: istirkha'
Complete relaxation (istirkha') is the criterion. If the muscles are fully relaxed, control is lost, and wudu is nullified. Partial retention preserves wudu.
The text from al-Fath adds that even with complete relaxation, emission of wind is possible unless wakefulness prevents it.
4
Extend to unconsciousness and madness
Unconsciousness (ighma') and madness (junun) overpower the mind and result in complete loss of control, similar to deep sleep. Therefore, they also nullify wudu.
These conditions are analogically derived from the ruling on sleep.
5
Apply the ruling to new scenarios
Use the same criterion to evaluate modern postures: e.g., sleeping in a car seat with a backrest involves leaning; if the support were removed, the person would fall, so it nullifies. Sleeping upright on a plane seat without head support may not nullify if the muscles retain some tension.
The degree of relaxation is assessed individually.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for ghusl obligation due to maniy, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on interrupted desire, and apply the mufta bihi (relied-upon) ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
For ghusl to be wajib, maniy must exit with gush and desire; interruption of desire before exit is disputed.

The obligation of ghusl due to the emission of maniy is a foundational rule of taharah. The Hanafi school defines maniy precisely and conditions its emission on two elements: gushing (dafaq) and sexual desire (shahwah). However, what happens if the desire is broken before the maniy fully exits? This passage from al-Lubab presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi usul in nuanced cases of purity.

Guided Path
1
Define maniy and its obligatory conditions
The text defines maniy as a thick white fluid with specific characteristics, and conditions ghusl on two elements: dafaq (gush) and shahwah (desire). Note that Abu Yusuf further conditions shahwah at the exact moment of exit.
2
Analyze the wet dream scenario of interrupted desire
The text gives a case: a person experiences a wet dream, maniy begins to detach with desire, but he tightens his penis to break desire, then releases and maniy flows without desire. Identify the question: is ghusl wajib?
3
Compare Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa says no, because the final exit lacks desire. Sahibayn say yes, because the initial detachment occurred with desire and the obligation is already triggered.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
In practice, a person who consciously stops emission mid-way must determine whether to repeat ghusl if fluid later emerges without sensation. The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view: ghusl is required.
5
Apply the second scenario: residual maniy after ghusl without urination/sleep
Confirm that the same dispute applies: Sahibayn require a second ghusl if residual maniy comes out before urination/sleep; Abu Hanifa does not. After urination/sleep, consensus (ijma) holds that no ghusl is needed.

(The meanings that necessitate ghusl: 1. Emission) i.e., detachment (of maniy), and it is a thick white fluid, from which the penis weakens at its exit, its smell resembles the smell of palm blossom when fresh, and the smell of egg when dry, (upon the mode of gushing) i.e., ejection, (and desire): i.e., pleasure at the time of its separation from its place even if it did not exit the private part in that manner, and Abu Yusuf conditioned it. So if someone had a wet dream and it separated from him with desire, when it neared appearance, he tightened his penis until his desire broke, then he left it and it flowed without desire: ghusl is obligatory according to both of them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), contrary to him (Abu Hanifa). Similarly, if a person who had intercourse performed ghusl before urinating or sleeping, then the remainder of his maniy came out after ghusl: he must repeat ghusl according to both of them, contrary to him. And if it came out after urination or sleep: he does not repeat, by consensus.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri outlines the first cause of obligatory ghusl: emission of maniy. It defines maniy physically (thick, white fluid) and its characteristics (penis weakens, smell like palm blossom or egg). The condition for obligation is that the emission occurs 'with gushing and desire' (على وجه الدفق والشهوة). This condition is stressed: even if the maniy does not exit the private part in the typical manner, as long as it separates with pleasure, ghusl is required. Abu Yusuf further stipulates that desire must be present at exit. The text then presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: if a person experiences a wet dream and maniy begins to come out with desire, but then he tightens his penis and the desire breaks, and then the maniy flows without desire, ghusl is obligatory according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), but not according to Abu Hanifa. Sahibayn argue that since the initial detachment occurred with desire, the obligation stands; Abu Hanifa considers the final exit without desire as a separate event. A similar dispute occurs in the case of a person who had intercourse, performed ghusl without urinating or sleeping, and then residual maniy exits. Sahibayn require a second ghusl, while Abu Hanifa does not. However, if the person urinated or slept before the residual exit, the ghusl is not repeated by consensus, as these actions are believed to cut off the original desire-based emission.

Key Terms
المني (al-maniy): Thick white fluid that exits from the private part with gush and desire; it is the seminal fluid that necessitates ghusl.
الدفق (al-dafaq): Gushing or ejection; the forceful exit of fluid.
الشهوة (al-shahwah): Sexual desire or pleasure at the time of emission.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ghusl for Mani with Interrupted Shahwa and Residual Mani after Ghusl
Defines mani and presents intra-Hanafi dispute on interrupted shahwa and residual mani.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the obligation of ghusl for two specific scenarios: interrupted shahwa and residual mani after ghusl, and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
The obligation of ghusl hinges on emission with shahwa; the Sahibayn extend it even when shahwa is interrupted initially.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri delves into an intra-Hanafi disagreement over the conditions for ghusl due to mani (semen). While all agree that ghusl is wajib for mani emitted with dafq (force) and shahwa (lust), a dispute arises in edge cases: when shahwa is broken before the actual flow, or when residual mani emerges after a ghusl performed before urination or sleep. Understanding these positions is crucial for applying the correct ruling in ambiguous situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core condition for ghusl
Recognize that Hanafis require mani to be emitted with dafq (force) and shahwa (lust) for ghusl to be wajib. This is the baseline rule.
Abu Yusuf adds that shahwa is required even if the exit is not from the private part.
2
Analyze the first disputed scenario
Consider the case where mani separates with shahwa in a wet dream, but the person tightens his organ, breaks the shahwa, and then the flow occurs without shahwa. Determine the ruling for each imam.
Abu Hanifa rules no ghusl; Sahibayn rule ghusl wajib.
3
Analyze the second disputed scenario
Consider the case where a person performs ghusl for janabah, then residual mani comes out before urination or sleep. Compare the ruling with the consensus after urination/sleep.
Sahibayn require a second ghusl; Abu Hanifa does not.
4
Understand the consensus exception
Note that if residual mani appears after urination or sleep, there is agreement (ijma') that no repeat ghusl is needed. Al-Zayla'i explains that these actions sever the connection to the original emission.
This consensus supports the Sahibayn's logic that before such actions, the residual is still linked to the original emission.
5
Evaluate the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is whether a second ghusl is required. In later Hanafi practice, caution often favors the Sahibayn's view.
The passage itself does not give tarjih.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the obligation of ghusl due to the emission of mani (semen). It first defines mani and its emission: it must be emitted with dafq (force) and shahwa (lust). The author notes that Abu Yusuf conditioned the emission with lust even if it does not exit from the private part. The core dispute is presented in two scenarios: (1) A person experiences a wet dream and the mani separates with lust, but before it exits he tightens his organ, breaking his lust, and then the mani flows without lust. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), ghusl is still wajib because the substance is the same mani that began with lust. Abu Hanifa disagrees because the actual flow occurred without shahwa, so ghusl is not wajib. (2) A person performs ghusl for janabah before urinating or sleeping, and then residual mani comes out after the ghusl. Again, the Sahibayn require a second ghusl, while Abu Hanifa does not. However, if the residual emission occurs after urination or sleep, there is consensus that no repeat is necessary because those actions indicate the original fluid has been expelled. The passage includes lexical notes comparing the smell of mani to that of pollen and eggs, and a citation from al-Zayla'i explaining the rationale for the consensus. The key legal point is that the obligation of ghusl hinges on the emission of mani with shahwa; emission without shahwa does not necessitate ghusl except according to the stricter view of the Sahibayn in the specific cases where the emission began with shahwa.

Key Terms
الدفق (al-dafq): The forceful emission of semen, a characteristic of mani.
الشهوة (al-shahwa): The sexual pleasure experienced at the moment of emission of mani.
الغسل (al-ghusl): The full-body ritual washing that becomes obligatory due to janabah, hayd, nifas, or recommended for certain occasions.
