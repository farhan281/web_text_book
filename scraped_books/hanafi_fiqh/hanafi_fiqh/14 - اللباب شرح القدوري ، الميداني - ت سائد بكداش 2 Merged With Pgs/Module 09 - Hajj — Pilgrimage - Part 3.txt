Months of Hajj and Ruling on Ihram Before Them
Defines Hajj months and rules on premature ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on premature ihram for Hajj and explain why it is valid but disliked using the condition/pillar distinction.

Core Idea
Ihram before Hajj months is valid as a condition, but disliked because only the majority of Hajj actions occur in the months.

This passage from Al-Lubāb defines the specific months of Hajj and addresses a key question: what if someone enters ihram for Hajj before these months? The Hanafi school rules that the ihram is valid and the Hajj is concluded, yet it is disliked. Understanding this ruling hinges on the distinction between a condition (sharṭ) and a pillar (rukn), and the principle that the majority of a ritual has the ruling of the whole.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hajj months
List the months as per the text: Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah. Note that only the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah are included, not the entire month.
This definition is specific to the Hanafi school.
2
Determine the ruling on premature ihram
State that if one enters ihram before these months, the ihram is valid and the Hajj is concluded (inʿaqada ḥajjan). However, the text adds that it is disliked (kuriha).
3
Analyze the reasoning
The text explains that ihram is a condition (sharṭ), and conditions can be brought forward. The dislike arises because it resembles performing a pillar before its time. Also, the principle 'al-akthar lahu ḥukm al-kull' (the majority has the ruling of the whole) is invoked: if most of the Hajj actions occur before the months, it would be treated as if all occurred, but here the acts themselves (like standing at ʿArafat) cannot be performed before the months.
4
Distinguish condition vs. pillar
In Hanafi fiqh, ihram is a condition (sharṭ) for Hajj, not a pillar (rukn). A condition can be fulfilled before the time, whereas a pillar must be performed within its time. This explains why ihram is valid even if done early.
5
Note the practical implication
Even though ihram is valid, the pilgrim cannot perform any of the specific Hajj actions (wuqūf, ṭawāf, etc.) until the months begin. The early ihram simply establishes the state of consecration.

An excused muhrim may choose among fasting, charity, or sacrifice, but the sacrifice is valid only in the Haram.

The Prophet (SAW) explained the verse in the hadith of Ka'b ibn Ujrah, detailing the three options and the amounts.

One of the gravest violations during Hajj is sexual intercourse before the standing at Arafat. This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling: the Hajj becomes invalid (fasid), yet the pilgrim must continue the rites, offer a dam (sheep or seventh of a camel), and perform a makeup Hajj immediately, even if the original was voluntary. Compensation for Leaving Tawaf al-Sadr
Explanation of compensation for leaving parts of the farewell tawaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the compensation required for omitting circuits of Tawaf al-Sadr and explain the distinction between leaving three or fewer circuits versus four or more.

Core Idea
Leaving three or fewer circuits: sadaqah per circuit; four or more circuits: a sheep (dam).

This passage from Al-Lubab explains the graduated compensation for omitting parts of the farewell tawaf. Understanding this scale is crucial for performing Hajj correctly and knowing the consequences of unintentional omissions. The Hanafi school treats Tawaf al-Sadr as wajib, so its omission requires a specific sacrifice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that Tawaf al-Sadr is a wajib act in the Hanafi school. Its omission does not invalidate Hajj but requires compensation.
Wajib in Hanafi: less than fard but obligatory; omission expiated by dam.
2
Determine the number of circuits omitted
Count how many circuits of the farewell tawaf were left out. The compensation depends on whether the omission is partial (1–3 circuits) or substantial (4+ circuits or the entire tawaf).
Whole circuits only; fractions are not considered.
3
Apply the corresponding compensation
For 1–3 circuits: sadaqah (half a sa' of grain) per circuit, unless the total reaches the value of a dam. For 4+ circuits or the entire tawaf: one sheep (shah).
Sadaqah is a type of charity, not a general term here.
4
Understand the legal reason (talil)
The compensation is due because leaving a wajib or its majority incurs a dam. While in Makkah, the pilgrim should repeat the omitted circuits to fulfill the wajib in its time.
This principle is stated in Al-Hidayah.

Ruling on Leaving Sa'y: Compensation without Invalidation
Statement of compensation for omission or error in Sa'y

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios that require a dam for Sa'y and explain why Hajj remains valid according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Leaving Sa'y requires a sheep but does not break Hajj — it is wajib, not rukn.

In the Hanafi madhhab, the ritual walking between Safa and Marwah (Sa'y) is an obligatory act (wajib), not a pillar (rukn). This distinction carries practical consequences: if a pilgrim omits Sa'y entirely, most of it, rides without excuse, or reverses the order, he must offer a blood sacrifice (dam) as compensation, but his Hajj remains valid and complete. This lesson clarifies the four specific cases that incur dam and the underlying principle that distinguishes wajib from rukn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios requiring dam
Read the passage carefully and list the four acts that trigger a blood sacrifice: leaving Sa'y entirely, leaving most of it, riding without a valid excuse, and starting from Marwah instead of Safa.
Each scenario represents a failure to fulfill the wajib in its proper manner.
2
Understand the consequence: dam vs. invalidation
Note that in all cases, the Hajj is 'tamm' (complete). The compensation is a sheep (shah), not repetition of the act or nullification of the pilgrimage. This is because Sa'y is wajib, not a rukn.
The talil (legal reason) is given: 'because they are obligations'.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Compare this ruling with the Shafi'i school, where Sa'y is a rukn (pillar). In that view, omitting Sa'y invalidates Hajj and requires making it up.
The difference stems from the classification of Sa'y as wajib vs. rukn.
4
Apply the general Hanafi principle
Generalize: in Hanafi fiqh, any omission of a wajib act in Hajj (e.g., staying at Muzdalifah, throwing pebbles) incurs a dam, but the Hajj remains valid. This lesson reinforces that principle.
The ruling on Sa'y is a specific application of this broader rule.

Leaving Arafat Before the Imam: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Dropping the Dam
Intra-Hanafi dispute on dropping the dam after returning to Arafat post-sunset.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the zahir al-riwayah and the nawadir position regarding dropping the dam for returning to Arafat after sunset, and state the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Returning after sunset does not cancel the dam — the missed wajib cannot be restored after its time.

This passage from al-Maydani's commentary on al-Quduri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about the penalty (dam) for leaving Arafat before the Imam and before sunset. The central question is whether returning to Arafat after sunset can cancel the dam. We will examine the two positions and see which one is relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is leaving Arafat before the Imam departs after sunset. The default ruling is that such a pilgrim owes a dam.
The dam is a wajib penalty for leaving a wajib (the proper timing of departure).
2
Note the condition for dropping the dam according to zahir al-riwayah
The dam is dropped only if the pilgrim returns to Arafat before sunset. If the return occurs after sunset, the dam remains.
This is the apparent (zahir) position transmitted in the six core books.
3
Recognize the opposing intra-Hanafi narration
Ibn Shuja' narrated from Abu Hanifa that the dam is dropped even if the return occurs after sunset. Al-Quduri considered this narration authentic.
This is a nawadir (rare) transmission, not part of zahir al-riwayah.
4
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
The majority view: the omitted act is the timely standing after sunset; returning after sunset cannot restore it because the time has passed. The minority view: the omitted act is the departure after sunset; returning and staying after sunset makes up for it.
The difference hinges on what is considered the missed wajib.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position, supported by al-Bada'i and later scholars, is that the dam is not dropped after sunset. This is the operative fatwa.
Al-Maydani clarifies the confusion and upholds the majority view.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on waiver of sacrifice by returning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two narrations regarding the waiver of the blood sacrifice for leaving Muzdalifah, explain the cause of the difference, and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The blood sacrifice for leaving Muzdalifah is waived by returning to Arafah only if the cause is departure before sunset, not before the Imam.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with a specific obligation in Hajj: the standing at Muzdalifah. Leaving it without excuse incurs a blood sacrifice. However, there is an internal Hanafi dispute about whether returning to Arafah after sunset cancels that obligation. The passage clarifies the cause and resolves the ikhtilaf by preferring one narration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its compensation
First, note that the act under discussion is leaving the standing (wuquf) at Muzdalifah without excuse. The compensation is a blood sacrifice (dam). This is undisputed.
2
Understand the consensus case
The text states that if the pilgrim leaves Muzdalifah and does not return before sunset, the sacrifice is confirmed without dispute. This is because the sun set before his return, so the obligation is fixed.
3
Identify the two narrations and their causes
The passage presents two narrations within the Hanafi school. According to al-Asl, the cause for the sacrifice is leaving before the Imam (the leader of the pilgrims). According to Ibn Shuja', the cause is leaving before sunset. The operative cause determines whether returning can waive the sacrifice.
The difference arises from the definition of the cause (sabab).

Analyze the rationale for waiver
If the cause is leaving before the Imam, then returning after sunset does not undo that cause because the Imam's departure has already occurred. But if the cause is leaving before sunset, returning after sunset compensates for the lost standing at the right time.

Recognize the tarjih and mufta bihi
Al-Quduri considered the narration of Ibn Shuja' to be correct and that of al-Asl to be confused (mudtarib). The relied-upon position is that the sacrifice is waived if the pilgrim returns to Arafah after sunset.
This is an example of tarjih among conflicting riwayat.

4
Identify the tarjih and mufta bihi
Later authorities (al-Isbijabi, al-Nasafi, al-Mahbubi) have settled the dispute: the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (value always) is the correct mufta bihi. This is now the operative ruling for fatwa.
The tarjih is documented in works like al-Tashih and is cited in this passage.
5
Apply the ruling to a modern scenario
Consider a muhrim today who hunts a wild gazelle. Under the mufta bihi view, he must pay the market value of the gazelle in his locality. Under Muhammad's view, he would need to sacrifice a sheep. The difference is practical and affects the ease of compensation.
The mufta bihi view is considered more flexible and universally applicable.

Commentary
This passage covers the rulings for fidyah (compensation) for a muhrim who hunts game. It begins by stating the specific animal equivalents for common game animals as per the established texts: for a gazelle (zabi) a sheep, for a hyena (dhab') a sheep, for a hare (arnab) a young she-goat (anaq), for an ostrich (na'amah) a she-camel (badanah), and for a jerboa (yarbu') a jafrah (a goat kid over four months). These are the fixed equivalents for game that has a 'like' (nazir) – i.e., an animal similar in size and nature that can be sacrificed. For game that does not have a like, such as small birds (asfour, hamam), the penalty is the market value (qimah) of the animal, and this is agreed upon. The commentary then clarifies the definitions of the young animals. A significant intra-Hanafi dispute is mentioned: Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf hold that the value (qimah) is due for all game, regardless of whether it has a like, because the compensation is monetary. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani distinguishes: if the game has a like (muhl), like a gazelle, then the like animal must be sacrificed; only if it has no like (like a sparrow) does value apply. The passage then presents the tarjih (preponderant view) from later authorities: according to al-Isbijabi, al-Nasafi, and al-Mahbubi, the correct and mufta bihi position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, i.e., value is due in all cases. This is the relied-upon ruling in the Hanafi school. The note about the addition in some copies of al-Quduri for the wild ass is a textual variant but does not affect the core ruling.

Key Terms
نظير (nazīr): An animal similar to the hunted game in type and size, used for fidyah (e.g., a sheep for a gazelle).
مثل (mithl): The equivalent animal (like) that is due as fidyah for game with a like.
قيمة (qīmah): Market value; the amount of money equivalent to the game animal.
عناق (ʿanāq): A female kid (young goat) that has not yet completed one year.
بَدَنَة (badanah): A she-camel, specified as the fidyah for an ostrich.
جَفْرَة (jafrah): A young goat that is older than four months, specified for the jerboa.
