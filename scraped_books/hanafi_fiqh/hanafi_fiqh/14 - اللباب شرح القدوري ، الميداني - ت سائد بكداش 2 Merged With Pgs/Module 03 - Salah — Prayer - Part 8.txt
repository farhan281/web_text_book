Recommended Acts for the Prayer for Rain
Lists the mustahabbat for istisqa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended acts (mustahabbat) for the prayer for rain and explain the wisdom behind each.

Core Idea
Istisqa is performed with humility, charity, and even animals to evoke divine mercy.

This lesson explores the sunnah practices surrounding the prayer for rain. These recommended acts transform the supplication into a communal display of helplessness and need before Allah. Understanding them ensures the sunnah is fulfilled completely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
Recognize that the prayer for rain is a recommended act (sunnah muakkadah) and the preferred location is the open desert except in Makkah and Bayt al-Maqdis where the mosque is used.
The text begins with an exception for Makkah and Bayt al-Maqdis.
2
List personal recommended acts
Extract the acts of personal conduct: walking, wearing old/unwashed clothes, displaying humility, lowering heads, giving charity before going out, and renewing repentance.
These acts emphasize tawadu' (humility) and tawbah (repentance).
3
Note inclusion of vulnerable individuals
Identify that the weak, elderly, women, and children are specifically mentioned to be part of the gathering to increase the plea for mercy.
The presence of the weak and innocent is a means of seeking Allah's compassion.
4
Explain the purpose of bringing animals
Understand that domestic animals and their young are brought and separated so that their cries create an atmosphere of need and helplessness (tahattun), which is more likely to evoke divine mercy.
The term 'tahattun' refers to the humidity and noise created by the animals.

Ruling on Turning Cloaks During Istisqa Prayer
Defines the term and states it is not practiced

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on turning the cloak during istisqa prayer, define the term qalb al-rida', and explain the evidential basis for the Hanafi position.

Core Idea
Turning the cloak in istisqa is not a sunnah because the Prophet did not command it.

In this brief passage from al-Lubab, we encounter a seemingly simple ruling: the community does not turn their cloaks during the rain prayer. Yet this ruling encapsulates a key Hanafi methodological principle — rulings are derived from what the Prophet commanded, not from what he merely did or from later practices. The passage defines the act of 'turning the cloak' and then provides the evidence: absence of a command. Let's unpack this ruling and see why the Hanafi school takes this position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
Recognize the term qalb al-rida' (turning the cloak) and its physical description: turning it inside out or swapping right and left sides.
The definition is provided in the sharh from Jawhara.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The matn and sharh clearly state: 'The people do not turn their cloaks.' This is the operative ruling.
The hukm level is mubah (permissible) but the act is not practiced as a sunnah.
3
Identify the proof
The sharh from al-Hidayah gives the evidence: 'because it was not transmitted that he commanded them to do that.' The proof is the absence of a command.
Hanafi usul requires a command to establish an act as sunnah.
4
Compare with the opposing view
The Shafi'i school considers turning the cloak a sunnah based on a hadith of the Prophet's action. Understand that the Hanafi school prioritizes command over action.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in different usul principles.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Salat al-Istisqa is performed with humility and inclusion of the vulnerable to evoke Allah's mercy for rain.

This passage from al-Lubab outlines the mustahabb (recommended) practices for the rain prayer (Salat al-Istisqa). It goes beyond the essential rulings to describe the ideal manner that reflects humility and seeks Divine mercy. Understanding these acts enriches our practice and connects us to the prophetic tradition of turning to Allah in times of need.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary venue for istisqa
The general rule is to go out to the desert (al-sahra'). The exception is Jerusalem (al-Muqaddas), where the prayer is performed in the mosque instead, and they continue for three days.

2
Recognize the recommended personal practices
The worshippers walk to the prayer, wear old worn clothes, show humility, lower their heads, give charity before leaving, and renew repentance. These acts cultivate tawadu‘ (humility).
These are all mustahabb, not obligatory.
3
Understand the inclusion of vulnerable groups
The weak, elderly, old women, and children are brought along. Their presence and heartfelt supplications are believed to evoke mercy and increase the likelihood of acceptance.
There is a wisdom in invoking the blessings of the vulnerable.
4
Analyze the livestock practice
Livestock and their young are brought out, and the young are separated from their mothers. This creates a scene of tenderness (tahanun) and loud cries (dujaj) for needs, symbolizing helplessness and desperation for rain.
The text uses 'tahanun' (tenderness) – the original has 'tahanun' but OCR read 'tahattun'.

Dhimmis Excluded from Istisqa Prayer
Ruling that non-Muslim dhimmis should not attend the rain prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dhimmi attendance at istisqa and explain the Quranic and rational evidence for it.

Core Idea
Dhimmis do not attend istisqa because their supplication is astray and mercy descends only on believers.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Hidayah regarding the participation of non-Muslim citizens (ahl al-dhimma) in the rain prayer (istisqa). The Hanafi school prohibits their attendance based on both a Quranic text and a rational argument about the nature of mercy. You will learn the textual proof and the legal reasoning behind this exclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Recognize that the ruling concerns the attendance of ahl al-dhimma at istisqa prayer, and that the hukm is haram (prohibited).
2
Locate the first dalil
Find the Quranic verse cited: 'And the supplication of the disbelievers is only in error' (Surah Ar-Ra'd 13:14 or Ghafir 40:50). Understand that this directly applies to the supplicatory nature of istisqa.
The verse is not quoted in full, but the meaning is clear.
3
Analyse the second argument
The purpose of istisqa is to bring down mercy (istinzal al-rahmat), but mercy does not descend upon disbelievers; rather, curse (la'nat) descends on them. Hence, their presence contradicts the aim.
This is a rational (ma'qul) proof complementing the textual one.
4
Note the school-specific application
This is the Hanafi position from Zahir al-Riwayah. Other schools may have different views; the Hanafi school holds that dhimmis are absolutely excluded from attending.
Cross-madhhab: mukhtalaf fih.

The Structure and Ruling of Tarawih Prayer
Rulings on the recommended tarawih prayer structure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the structure (five tarwihahs of four rak'ahs each) and the hukm (sunnah mu'akkadah) of tarawih prayer according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Tarawih is a confirmed sunnah of 20 rak'ahs, performed in congregation after Isha in Ramadan.

In this passage, the author of al-Lubab details the recommended night prayer of Ramadan, known as tarawih. He specifies its timing, number of rak'ahs, and the recommended breaks, and clarifies that it is a confirmed sunnah distinct from general nawafil. This ruling is based on the practice of the Caliph Umar and the consensus of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage discusses Qiyam Ramadan (tarawih). The ruling is 'mustahabb' (recommended), which is clarified in the footnote as sunnah mu'akkadah (confirmed sunnah).
Sunnah mu'akkadah is a level above ordinary sunnah; omitting it is blameworthy but not sinful.
2
Understand the temporal and communal context
Tarawih is performed every night in Ramadan after the Isha prayer. It is recommended to delay Isha to one-third or half of the night. The prayer is performed in congregation, led by an imam.
Congregation is sunnah for men; individuals may pray alone, especially at home.
3
Parse the structure: tarwihahs and rak'ahs
The imam leads five tarwihahs. Each tarwihah consists of four rak'ahs performed as two units of two rak'ahs each (i.e., with a taslim after every two rak'ahs). Thus total rak'ahs = 5 × 4 = 20.
The term 'tarwihah' derives from 'raha' (rest), because a sitting break occurs after each tarwihah.
4
Examine the recommended sitting breaks
Between every two tarwihahs, and also between the fifth tarwihah and witr, there is a recommended sitting of approximately the duration of a tarwihah. During this sitting, worshippers may engage in tasbih, Quran recitation, silence, or pray individually.
This break is sunnah, not obligatory. It distinguishes tarawih from regular sunnah prayers.
5
Recognize the authority of the ruling
The footnote cites al-Jawharah and al-Hidayah to confirm that the relied-upon view is sunnah mu'akkadah. This is the position of the Zahir al-Riwayah and the mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.
Some early Hanafis considered it sunnah ghayr mu'akkadah, but the later consensus settled on mu'akkadah.

The Hanafi Ruling on Tarawih and Witr Congregation in Ramadan
Ruling with proof from consensus on tarawih structure and witr congregation restriction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for tarawih (number, timing, and practice) and explain why witr congregation outside Ramadan is disliked, citing the proof of consensus.

Core Idea
Tarawih is 20 rak'ahs in congregation every Ramadan; witr congregation is exclusive to that month by consensus.

Ramadan night prayer has a special structure in the Hanafi school. This lesson unpacks the classical text on tarawih and witr, clarifying the number of rak'ahs, the recommended sitting, and the critical distinction that witr in congregation is only for Ramadan—a ruling established by consensus. Understanding this helps preserve the unique sunnah of the month.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Tarawih is a stressed sunnah (sunnah muakkadah) in congregation every night of Ramadan. Witr congregation outside Ramadan is disliked (makruh).
The base matn uses 'yustahabb' but the relied-upon view is sunnah muakkadah (see al-Jawharah).
2
Determine the number and structure of tarawih
Five tarawihs, each tarawih = 4 rak'ahs (2 taslimahs), total 20 rak'ahs. Between each two tarawihs, sit for the duration of a tarawih. Sit between the fifth tarawih and witr as well.
Each tarawih consists of two separate two-rak'ah prayers.
3
Locate the proof for witr congregation ruling
The proof is consensus (ijma) of the Muslims as transmitted in al-Hidayah: witr is not prayed in congregation outside Ramadan. Doing so by mutual invitation (tada'i) is makruh.
Consensus is zanni al-thubut but qati' al-dalalah in this context.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on witr congregation
Some later Hanafi scholars (al-Nawazil, al-Yanabi') allowed witr congregation outside Ramadan but without recommendation. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) remains the consensus view: it is makruh.
The alternative view is nawadir, not acted upon.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hidayah and consensus on the practice of tarawih and witr congregation in Ramadan. The author opens a separate chapter for the night prayers of Ramadan because they have specific rulings distinct from general nawafil. The core ruling: It is recommended (mustahabb) for Muslims to gather in the mosque after Isha prayer every night of Ramadan and pray tarawih in congregation led by the imam. The number is five tarawihs, each tarawih being four rak'ahs with two salams (i.e., two two-rak'ah units), totaling 20 rak'ahs. The sitting between each two tarawihs is for rest and is recommended. The congregation sits for a duration equal to a tarawih, and they may engage in tasbih, recitation, silence, or individual prayer. After the fifth tarawih, the imam leads witr prayer with loud recitation. The phrase 'then' indicates that the usual practice is to pray tarawih before witr, though the correct view is that tarawih time extends from Isha to the end of night, both before and after witr. Outside Ramadan, witr and other nafl prayers are not prayed in congregation; to do so by mutual invitation (tada'i) is disliked (makruh) according to the relied-upon position, and this is the consensus of the Muslims. However, there is a minority view from some later scholars that it is permissible but not recommended. The Hanafi position emphasizes the special virtue of congregational tarawih in Ramadan and restricts witr congregation to that month to preserve the distinctive sunnah.

Core Idea
Salat al-Khawf is valid upon certain fear of an enemy or predator; intensity is not required.

Salat al-Khawf is a special congregational prayer for situations of fear. The Hanafi school provides a detailed method that allows Muslims to pray while maintaining defense. In this lesson, we examine the essential condition—certain presence of an enemy or predator—and the precise method of performing the prayer with two groups.

Presenting an intra-Hanafi dispute on the continuity of Salat al-Khawf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the continuity of Salat al-Khawf after the Prophet's death and state the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf argued Salat al-Khawf ended with the Prophet; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold it remains permissible.

Within the Hanafi school, there is a difference of opinion about whether Salat al-Khawf can still be performed after the Prophet's death. This lesson explores the arguments of Abu Yusuf (who held it was specific to the Prophet's time) and the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (that it continues).

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The core question: Is Salat al-Khawf permissible after the death of the Prophet?
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Yusuf and the other two.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and his dalil
Abu Yusuf holds it was only for the Prophet's time. He cites verse 4:102, which says 'When you are among them', implying the Prophet's presence. After his death, each group can pray separately, so the special method is not needed.
He also argues that the virtue of praying behind the Prophet was the purpose.
3
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They say it remains permissible. The verse is instructional, not limited to the Prophet. The fear condition still exists, so the concession remains general.
This is the majority view and the mufta bihi.
4

The Hanafi response is that the verse provides a general instruction for any imam. The condition of fear is the basis, and it remains after the Prophet.
The thamarah al-khilaf: if Abu Yusuf's view is followed, the prayer would not be performed as a special congregation.
5

The view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is preferred and is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Al-Marghinani and later authorities have affirmed this tarjih.

Salat al-Khauf: Method and Conditions in the Hanafi School
Explains the method of fear prayer for a traveler imam with two groups.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi method of Salat al-Khauf for a traveler imam and identify the conditions for its validity, including the requirement to walk between positions.

Core Idea
Salat al-Khauf is not a separate prayer; it is the obligatory prayer performed in a modified manner under enemy threat.

This lesson explains the Hanafi ruling on Salat al-Khauf (Fear Prayer). You will learn how the imam divides the congregation and leads the prayer in two parts to maintain security. The passage from al-Quduri's matn with al-Maydani's commentary provides the legal details, including crucial conditions such as walking on foot. Understanding this ruling ensures the validity of the prayer in times of danger while fulfilling the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the imam's role
The imam is a traveler praying a two-rak'ah prayer (e.g., Fajr, shortened Zuhr/Asr/Isha, Jumu'ah, or Eid) while facing an enemy. Certainty of fear (yaqin) is required.
If the imam doubts the enemy's presence, Salat al-Khauf is not valid.
2
Trace the two-group division and the sequence
The imam divides the congregation into two groups: one group stands guard facing the enemy, the other prays behind him. The imam prays one rak'ah (two prostrations) with the first group, then they go to guard, and the second group comes.
Movement between positions must be on foot, not riding.
3
Analyze how each group completes their prayer individually
After the imam says salam alone, each group completes their prayer individually. The first group (lahiqun) prays one rak'ah without recitation; the second group (masbuqun) prays one rak'ah with recitation.
They are considered lahiq and masbuq respectively because they missed part of the prayer with the imam.
4
Note the condition of walking and what invalidates the prayer
The movement must be on foot. Riding or any excessive action beyond necessary walking invalidates the prayer. This is to maintain the validity while facing the enemy.
This is derived from the commentary's explicit qualification: 'qayyadna bi-madi al-musallin mushatan'.
5
Distinguish between a traveler imam and a resident imam
If the imam is a resident (muqim), he prays two rak'ahs with the first group (in a four-rak'ah prayer, this would be the first two). The groups then complete their prayers similarly, with the first group as lahiq (no recitation) and the second as masbuq (with recitation).
This applies only to prayers that can be shortened; the Hanafi school only permits Salat al-Khauf for two-rak'ah prayers.

Intense Fear Prayer: Individual Gesture Prayer Without Qibla
Ruling on intense fear prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for fear prayer when fear becomes intense and explain why facing qibla and full physical movements are dropped.

Core Idea
In extreme fear, prayer with gestures and any direction is valid due to necessity.

Fear prayer (salat al-khawf) has various forms depending on the level of danger. When fear is so intense that the enemy attacks, the normal requirements of prayer are relaxed. This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains that in such situations, Muslims may pray individually, gesturing for bowing and prostrating, and may face any direction if unable to face the qibla. Understanding this ruling highlights the principle that necessity can ease even the most fundamental obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the ruling applies when fear is intense (al-khawf al-shadid) – so severe that the enemy is attacking and Muslims cannot pray in congregation or face the qibla.
The text says 'if fear becomes intense, such that the enemy does not leave them to pray'.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that they may pray individually (each alone) while riding or on foot, making gestures (ima) for ruku and sujud, and they may face any direction.
Congregational prayer is not valid because people are scattered.
3
Identify the dropped elements
Facing the qibla and full physical postures (standing, bowing, prostrating) are dropped. They are replaced by gestures.
The reasoning: 'just as the pillars are dropped due to necessity, facing the qibla is dropped'.
4
Note the exception for invalidating actions
If they perform excessive unnecessary movements (like much walking without necessity), the prayer becomes invalid. However, walking needed for arranging rows is permissible.
This is a separate ruling from the first part of the passage.

Rulings on Positioning and Prompting the Dying
Ruling on positioning and prompting the dying person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on positioning and talqin for a dying person, and distinguish between the sunnah and the preferred (mukhtar) position.

Core Idea
The dying are turned to the qibla gently; talqin is prompting, not commanding.

When death approaches, certain sunan are recommended to ease the transition and remind the dying of faith. This passage from Al-Lubab explains two key acts: turning the person towards the qibla and prompting the shahada. While the sunnah is on the right side, the preferred view in the Hanafi school is on the back for comfort.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sunnah acts
Read the passage and note the two actions: turning the dying person to face the qibla, and prompting them with the shahada (talqin).
2
State the sunnah and preferred position
The sunnah is to turn the person on their right side. However, the chosen view (mukhtar) is on the back, as it is easier for the soul's exit. Identify this difference.
The text cites 'Jawharah' for the back position.
3
Explain the ruling for talqin
Talqin is done by reciting the shahada in the person's presence, not by commanding them to repeat. Once is sufficient unless they speak other words.
The goal is that the shahada be their last words.
4
Note the exception for difficulty
If turning the person causes difficulty, they are left as they are. Ease is prioritized.
This reflects the principle of removing hardship.

Funeral Prayer Over the Grave: Time Limits
States the ruling and correct criterion for prayer over the grave after burial

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct Hanafi criterion for praying over a grave after burial and explain why the fixed-day rule is overridden by the likelihood of decomposition.

Core Idea
The prayer over the grave is valid as long as decomposition is not strongly suspected — the 'illa overrides fixed days.

Al-Quduri's matn provides a simple rule: three days in winter, seven in summer. But Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah refines this based on the effective cause (ta'lil). The actual criterion is whether the body is likely to have decomposed. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and shows how the school prioritizes the 'illa over literal numbers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling from the matn
Read the first part: prayer over the grave is allowed up to three days in winter, seven in summer. This is a fixed-time ruling.
2
Identify the refinement from Al-Hidayah
The commentary states 'the correct view is: as long as it is not strongly thought that he has decomposed'. This overrides the fixed days.
3
Determine the effective cause ('illa)
The 'illa is tafassukh (decomposition). The prohibition after a certain time is due to the body beginning to decay, which makes prayer inappropriate.
4
Understand how the 'illa overrides the literal rule
Because conditions vary (climate, soil, etc.), the fixed days are not reliable indicators. The school uses istihsan (juristic preference) to base the ruling on the actual state of the body rather than a rigid time frame.
5
Apply the criterion
In practice, ghalabat al-zann (overwhelming presumption) of decomposition is the test. If it is likely that the body has decomposed, prayer is not performed; otherwise it is valid.

The Fourth Takbir: Du'a or Not? (Hanafi Ruling and Istihsan)
Ruling on du'a after fourth takbir with istihsan and proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on du'a after the fourth takbir of funeral prayer and explain the role of istihsan in permitting it.

Core Idea
The Hanafi default is no du'a after the fourth takbir, but istihsan allows it as a recommended act.

The funeral prayer consists of four takbirs. After the fourth, one normally makes salam. But what about supplication? The Hanafi school's established position (zahir al-riwayah) is that no du'a is prescribed. However, some later scholars favored a du'a based on istihsan. This lesson examines the ruling, the proof, and the intra-madhhab difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default Hanafi ruling
State that after the fourth takbir, one makes salam without any du'a. This is the position of Zahir al-Riwayah.
The fourth takbir itself is wajib; the du'a is not part of the prayer.
2
Examine the istihsan of some mashayikh
Some later Hanafi scholars recommended reciting 'Rabbana atina...' after the fourth takbir based on istihsan (juristic preference).
Istihsan allows a ruling that differs from the apparent evidence due to suitability.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i position and their hadith evidence
The Shafi'i school recommends du'a after the fourth takbir, citing the hadith of 'Abdullah ibn Abi Awfa where the Prophet (peace be upon him) made du'a after the fourth takbir.
The hadith is ahad and authenticated by some scholars.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school considers the hadith as ahad (zanni al-thubut) and not sufficient to change the established practice. The default remains no du'a, but istihsan permits it as a recommended addition.
This reflects the Hanafi priority of the established practice (zahir al-riwayah) over a single narration.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The mufta bihi is not to recite du'a after the fourth takbir; however, doing so with the intention of istihsan is permissible and considered hasan (good).
Thus, the prayer remains valid whether or not du'a is said.

Funeral Prayer in a Mosque: Ruling and Following the Imam in Extra Takbirs
Rulings on funeral prayer in mosque and following imam in extra takbirs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on performing funeral prayer in a mosque (karahah tahrimiyyah) and explain how to act when the imam says more than four takbirs.

Core Idea
Funeral prayer in a mosque is prohibitively disliked (makruh tahriman); if the imam exceeds four takbirs, the follower must not follow but wait to make salam with him.

The chapter on funeral prayer includes ancillary rulings about where the prayer may be performed and what to do if the imam adds extra takbirs. These rulings reflect the school's concern for maintaining the sunna and preventing confusion. This lesson examines the karahah of praying in the mosque and the proper procedure when the imam departs from the standard four takbirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling on mosque prayer
The text states: 'Funeral prayer should not be performed over a deceased in a mosque with congregation.' This is a prohibition.
The ruling applies to mosques that are used for Friday prayer (jami') and other mosques.
2
Differentiate between the two scholarly opinions
Some Hanafi scholars say it is makruh tahriman (prohibitively disliked), while others say it is makruh tanzihan (lightly disliked). The preponderant view (rajih) is makruh tahriman.
The commentary indicates that Ibn al-Humam and others gave preponderance to makruh tahriman.
3
Understand the scope of the ruling
The ruling specifically mentions 'masjid jam'ah' meaning the mosque where Friday prayer is held (congregational mosque).
Some extend it to all mosques, but the text specifies.
4
Consider the ruling about following the imam in extra takbirs
If the imam says more than four takbirs, the follower should not follow him by doing the extra takbirs. Instead, he should wait until the imam makes salam, then make salam with him.
This is the chosen view (al-mukhtar), as stated in Al-Hidayah.
5
Note the underlying principle
The follower does not follow the imam in actions that are not part of the sunna, but he maintains unity by making salam together.
This prevents corrupting the sunna while avoiding disunity.

Funeral Rulings: Carrying the Bier and Burial Procedures
Rulings on funeral procession and burial procedures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rulings on carrying the bier, walking pace, grave digging, and placing the deceased, and identify the disliked actions.

Core Idea
Honour the deceased: carry from all four sides, walk briskly, dig respectfully, and place facing qibla.

The Hanafi school provides detailed guidance for the funeral procession and burial to ensure respect and practical care. This lesson covers how to carry the bier, the recommended walking speed, how to dig the grave, and the proper placement of the deceased. It also highlights actions considered disliked (makruh) and the reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the method of carrying the bier
The bier should be carried from its four legs, with the carrier placing the front on his right shoulder and walking ten steps, then the back similarly, then front on left, then back. This shows respect.
The number ten is not fixed; it illustrates the principle of rotating sides.
2
Determine the walking pace
Walk briskly (isra') but avoid running (khabab). Running is disliked (makruh) because it may cause distress or lack of dignity.
Al-khabab is a quick trotting pace, distinct from a steady brisk walk.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The martyr is honored by not being washed; his clothes become his shroud, but the funeral prayer is still obligated.

Identify the definition of martyr

The Time of Standing at Arafah
Defining the valid time for Wuquf at Arafah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exact time window for Wuquf at Arafah and explain why a momentary standing suffices for the validity of Hajj.

Core Idea
A single moment at Arafah within the defined time window validates the entire Hajj.

This passage from Al-Lubab defines the temporal boundaries of the essential pillar (rukn) of standing at Arafah. Understanding this precise window is crucial: missing it entirely invalidates the Hajj, but capturing even an instant within it secures the Hajj's validity. The author emphasizes the minimum requirement, clarifying a common misconception about duration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is al-wuquf bi Arafah, which is a rukn (pillar) of Hajj. Its ruling is fard (obligatory).
All schools agree it is a pillar.
2
Identify the beginning time
The time begins at zawal al-shams (noon) on the Day of Arafah (9th Dhu al-Hijjah).
Zawal is when the sun passes its zenith.
3
Identify the end time
The time ends at dawn (fajr) on the Day of Sacrifice (10th Dhu al-Hijjah).
Dawn here refers to true dawn (fajr sadiq).
4
Determine the minimum requirement
Even a momentary standing (law lahza) within this window is sufficient to fulfill the pillar. Missing it entirely means the Hajj is invalid; the only remaining pillar is Tawaf al-Ziyarah.
The phrase 'adraka al-hajj' indicates safety from invalidity.

Source Text

This ruling defines the time for the essential pillar (rukn) of standing at Arafah. The sharh clarifies that 'adraka al-hajj' means he is safe from the invalidity of the Hajj (i.e., the Hajj is sound).

The Ruling on Wuquf Without Awareness: Intention Not Required
Ruling on validity of standing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that wuquf at Arafah is valid even without awareness and explain why intention is not a condition.

Core Idea
Physical presence at Arafah fulfills the rukn of wuquf; intention is not required.

In the Hanafi school, the essence of the standing at Arafah (wuquf) is simply being present in the designated area during the prescribed time. This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a pilgrim who passes through Arafah while asleep, unconscious, or unaware of the location. Does this count as valid wuquf? The source text gives a clear answer that highlights a distinctive Hanafi principle regarding the role of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The text describes three cases: a pilgrim who is asleep, unconscious, or does not know that the place is Arafah.
2
Identify the ruling
The text states that this suffices (اجزأه) for the standing of Arafah.
3
Identify the reasoning
Because the pillar (rukn) of standing has occurred, and ignorance affects intention, but intention is not a condition for wuquf.

Source Text

ومن اجتاز بعرفة وهو نائم أو مغمى عليه، أو لم يعلم أنها عرفة: أجزأه ذلك عن الوقوف؛ لأن الركن وهو الوقوف قد وجد، والجهل يخل بالنية، وهي ليست بشرط فيه.
And whoever passes through Arafah while he is asleep or unconscious, or he did not know that it was Arafah, that suffices him for the standing, because the pillar, which is the standing, has occurred, and ignorance affects the intention, but intention is not a condition in it.
Source pages p. 442

Commentary
This ruling states that the physical act of being present at Arafah is the essential element (rukn) of standing (wuquf). Intention (niyyah) is not a condition for the validity of the standing. Therefore, if a person passes through Arafah even while asleep, unconscious, or unaware of the location, the standing is valid and suffices for Hajj. The sharh explains that the pillar of standing has been physically achieved, and since intention is not a required condition (shart) for this pillar, ignorance or lack of awareness does not invalidate it. This highlights that the rukn is the actual being there, not the conscious act.

Key Terms
النية (al-niyyah): Intention; here it is stated that intention is not a condition for the validity of the standing.
