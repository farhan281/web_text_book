Umrah: No Time Limit and Five Disliked Days
Memorization anchor: two rulings on Umrah's time flexibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the permissibility of Umrah throughout the year and identify the five days when it is disliked.

Core Idea
Umrah has no time limit — it can be performed any day except the five days of Arafah and Tashriq.

This short matn from Al-Quduri gives two foundational rulings about Umrah: it is not bound by a specific time, and it is permissible all year except for five days. Unlike Hajj, which has a fixed time and can be missed (fawāt), Umrah can be performed at any time. The five exceptions are days when it is best to avoid Umrah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage states two main points: (a) Umrah does not lapse (fawāt) because it is not restricted to a specific time; (b) it is permissible throughout the year except for five days during which it is disliked.
2
Understand the reason
The reason Umrah is not subject to fawāt is that it is not time-bound (ghayr muwaqqatah bi-waqt). Hajj has specific dates; missing them means missing Hajj. For Umrah, there is no such deadline.
3
Memorize the five days
The five days are: the Day of Arafah (9th Dhul Hijjah), the Day of Nahr (10th), and the three days of Tashriq (11th, 12th, 13th). Performing Umrah on these days is makruh tanzih (slightly disliked).
This clarification comes from the Hanafi commentaries, not the matn itself.
4
Apply the ruling
If someone performs Umrah on one of those five days, the act is valid (sahih) but considered makruh tanzih. It is better to avoid it, but no penalty is incurred.

An obligatory hady lost on the way must be replaced; a voluntary hady does not.

Ruling on a Disabled Sacrificial Animal: Voluntary vs Obligatory
Ruling on disabled sacrificial animal with proof and istihsan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a voluntary and an obligatory sacrificial animal that becomes disabled before reaching its destination, and explain the legal reasoning (istihsan) behind the prohibition on the owner and rich from consuming the voluntary animal.

Core Idea
A disabled voluntary hadiy must be sacrificed and marked for the poor; an obligatory one must be replaced.

Sacrificial animals (hadiy) may become disabled before reaching the Haram. What should the pilgrim do? The Hanafi school distinguishes between voluntary and obligatory sacrifices. This lesson presents the ruling from al-Hidayah, showing how the obligation level changes the legal consequence. We will explore the reasoning based on the condition of reaching the destination and the preference for charity over waste.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of hadiy
Distinguish between voluntary (tatawwu') and obligatory (wajibah) hadiy. Voluntary is offered for extra reward; obligatory is due to a vow or as a required part of Hajj (tamattu'/qiran).
2
State the ruling for disabled voluntary hadiy
The animal must be sacrificed, its garland dyed with its blood, and the side struck with the dyed garland. The owner and rich may not eat from it; only the poor may consume it.
The marking alerts people that it is a hadiy, so the poor know to eat.
3
State the ruling for disabled obligatory hadiy
A substitute animal must be offered in its place. The disabled animal becomes the owner's property to dispose of freely, since it is no longer fit for its purpose.
The obligation is to deliver an animal; the designated one failed, so a replacement is needed.
4

The permission to consume the animal is conditional upon reaching its destination. Before that, it is not permissible. However, giving it in charity to the poor is better than letting it become carrion. This is an istihsan (juristic preference) that overrides the general rule.
The reasoning balances the owner's loss with the benefit to the poor.
5
Note the practical difference (thamar al-khilaf)
The Hanafi prohibition on the owner and rich eating from a disabled voluntary hadiy distinguishes this from some other schools that might allow it. The obligation to replace an obligatory hadiy is universally recognized, but the disposal of the disabled one differs.
This ikhtilaf affects what a pilgrim does with an injured animal.

In the chapter of hadiy (sacrificial animals), the Hanafi school distinguishes between animals that are garlanded and those that are not. Commentary
This passage is a table of contents from the Hanafi fiqh book 'Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri' by al-Maydani. It lists chapter headings and page numbers for the section on Hajj (pilgrimage). The index covers topics ranging from the rituals of Hajj (e.g., going to Mina, standing at Arafat, stoning the jamarat) to special chapters on types of Hajj (Qiran and Tamattu') and the penalties (jinaayat) incurred by a muhrim (person in the state of ihram) for various violations such as wearing perfume, shaving, or intercourse. The page numbers allow the reader to locate detailed rulings within the book. This is purely organizational material and contains no legal rulings itself.

Key Terms
التمتع (al-tamattu‘): A type of Hajj where one performs Umrah first during the Hajj months, then disengages from ihram and later enters ihram for Hajj from Mecca, with a sacrifice required.
القرآن (al-qiran): A type of Hajj where one enters ihram for both Umrah and Hajj together, without disengaging between them, and must offer a sacrifice.
الجنايات (al-jinaayat): Violations of ihram prohibitions committed by a muhrim, each incurring a specific penalty (dam, sadaqah, etc.).
