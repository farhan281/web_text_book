Definition and Linguistic Derivation of the Martyr (Shahid)
Defines the martyr and explains his linguistic status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a martyr and explain its two key conditions and the linguistic derivation of the term.

Core Idea
A martyr is one killed by enemies or found dead on the battlefield with wounds; the term reflects both being witnessed for Paradise and being a witness of divine life.

This passage opens the chapter on the martyr (shahid) and provides its essential definition. Understanding this definition is crucial because it determines the funerary rulings and special status for martyrs in Islamic law. The text also explores the linguistic depth of the word shahid, showing how its form conveys both active and passive meanings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
The text states two ways one becomes a martyr: being killed directly by polytheists, or being found dead on the battlefield. These are the primary cases.
The first category is explicit; the second is implied by 'or found in the battlefield'.
2
Note the common condition: evidence of wounding
In the second category, the person must have a visible wound or fracture (athar al-jarahah). This ensures that death was due to combat.
This condition does not apply to the first category because the cause is known.
3
Understand the scope: includes bandits
The battlefield includes both war against disbelievers and fighting against bandits (qutta' al-tariq) within Muslim territory. Thus the definition is broader.
Hanafis include internal security operations under martyrdom.
4
Analyze the linguistic derivation
The word 'shahid' follows the pattern fa'eel, which can be interpreted as maf'ool (passive: 'one who is witnessed') or fa'il (active: 'one who witnesses'). Both interpretations are valid.
The passive meaning points to the martyr being witnessed for Paradise or by angels; the active meaning points to the martyr being alive and witnessing.
5
Connect the two meanings to the martyr's status
The dual derivation reflects the martyr's unique status: he is honored by being witnessed and also witnesses the divine reality as a living soul.
This linguistic insight deepens the theological understanding of martyrdom.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on the martyr (shahid) and provides its definition. In Hanafi fiqh, a martyr is primarily one killed directly by polytheists in battle. However, the definition also includes one found dead on the battlefield with visible wounds, even if the cause is not directly confirmed. This applies both in legitimate jihad against disbelievers and in fighting against bandits within the Muslim territory. The word 'shahid' is then analyzed linguistically: it can be interpreted as the passive participle (maf'ool) meaning 'one for whom Paradise is witnessed' or 'one whose death the angels witness', or as the active participle (fa'il) because the martyr is alive with his Lord, thus witnessing. This dual derivation reflects the martyr's special status.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahid): A martyr: one killed by disbelievers or found dead on the battlefield with wounds.
المعترك (al-mu'tarak): Battlefield or place of combat.
أثر الجراحة (athar al-jarahah): Evidence of wounding, such as a wound or fracture.

Definition and Rulings of the Martyr (Shahid) in Hanafi Fiqh
Defines the martyr and states the ruling on washing, shrouding, and praying.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a martyr and apply the three conditions to determine whether a deceased qualifies as a martyr, and recall the funeral rulings.

Core Idea
Martyr: killed by polytheists in battle or found dead with battle wounds; no washing, but shrouded in his clothes and prayed over.

This lesson defines who is considered a martyr (shahid) in Hanafi fiqh and outlines the unique funeral rulings. The passage from al-Lubab explains the three categories of martyrdom and the conditions for the special treatment. Understanding this is crucial for correctly applying burial rites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category
Recognize that the passage is defining the term 'shahid' and its associated funeral rulings. Note that the definition has three distinct causes.
2
List the three causes of martyrdom
From the text: (1) killed by polytheists, (2) found dead in battle with a trace of injury, (3) killed unjustly by a Muslim without blood-money due from the very act.
Injury trace: wound, fracture, burn, or blood from ear/eye (not mouth/nose/anus).
3
Identify the conditions
The deceased must be a legally responsible (mukallaf) Muslim in a state of purity (tahir) at the time of death. These are prerequisites for the martyr's rulings.
These conditions are mentioned at the end of the passage as 'by agreement'.
4
State the funeral rulings
The martyr is not washed (ghusl), is shrouded in his own clothes (including blood-stained ones), and the funeral prayer (janazah) is performed over him.
The shroud in his own clothes is a sunnah; the prayer is fard kifayah.
5
Apply exceptions and nuances
If blood-money (diyah) is due from the killing itself (e.g., accidental), the deceased is not a martyr. However, if diyah is waived due to settlement or paternity, martyr status is not negated.
This is explained in the commentary regarding intentional killing by a father.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal category of the martyr (shahid) in battle and the related funeral rulings. The author begins by giving the linguistic meaning: 'shahid' is a hyperbolic form meaning 'one witnessed for Paradise' or 'witnessed by angels', or 'one who is alive with his Lord'. The legal definition has three parts: first, one killed by the polytheists, whether directly or by any means attributed to them (e.g., pushing into fire, causing an animal to trample, releasing water). Second, one found dead in the battlefield with visible signs of injury (wound, fracture, burn, or blood from ear or eye), because blood from mouth, nose, or private parts could have other causes. Third, one killed unjustly by Muslims without blood-money being due, meaning the killing required qisas and no diyah was obligated by the act itself; if diyah was due from the outset (e.g., accidental killing), the deceased is not a martyr. An exception is when diyah is waived due to settlement or the father's immunity—the martyr's status is not negated. The ruling: such a martyr is not washed but is shrouded in his own clothes, and the funeral prayer is performed, provided he was a legally responsible Muslim in a state of purity. This is agreed upon (ittifaqan).

Key Terms
الشهيد (al-shahid): The martyr: one killed by disbelievers in battle, or found dead with battle wounds, or killed unjustly without blood-money due, who is not washed but shrouded and prayed over.
المشركون (al-mushrikun): Polytheists, pagans, or hostile disbelievers at war with Muslims.
الجراحة (al-jarahah): A wound or injury, specifically a trace of battle injury such as a cut, fracture, burn, or blood flow from ear or eye.
الدية (al-diyah): Blood-money or compensation paid to the victim's family in cases of unlawful killing that do not require retaliation (qisas).
