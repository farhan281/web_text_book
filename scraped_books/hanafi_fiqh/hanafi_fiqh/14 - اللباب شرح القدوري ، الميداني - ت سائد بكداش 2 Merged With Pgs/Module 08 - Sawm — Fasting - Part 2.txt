Exemptions for Pregnant, Nursing, and Elderly Persons from Fasting
Explains two exemptions with proof from Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for pregnant/nursing women and the elderly decrepit person regarding breaking the fast and the required compensation (qada or fidya), and cite the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Pregnant/nursing women owe only qada; the elderly owe fidya feeding — distinct categories with different compensations.

This lesson covers two specific exemptions from the obligation to fast during Ramadan: women who are pregnant or nursing and fear harm to themselves or their child, and the elderly person who has reached decrepitude and cannot fast. The Hanafi school distinguishes these cases: the former are treated like the sick (qada only, no fidya), while the latter are treated like those with permanent incapacity (fidya feeding, no qada). We will examine the rulings, their basis in the Quran, and the practical details.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories and rulings
The passage addresses two distinct groups: (1) pregnant/nursing women who fear for their child or themselves, and (2) the decrepit elderly who cannot fast. For the first group, the ruling is: break the fast and make up (qada) later, no fidya. For the second: break the fast and feed a poor person for each missed day.
Note that the elderly are not required to make up the fast; feeding substitutes entirely.
2
Understand the basis for pregnant/nursing
The text states that breaking the fast for pregnant/nursing women is due to 'inability' (al-ajz), analogized to the sick and traveler. Therefore only qada is required, not fidya. The principle of removing hardship (raf' al-haraj) applies.
This ruling is from Al-Hidayah and is Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Understand the basis for the elderly
For the elderly, the proof is the Quranic verse (Al-Baqarah 2:184): 'And upon those who are able to fast, a ransom of feeding a poor person.' The commentator interprets 'able' (yutiqunahu) as meaning those who cannot fast due to chronic incapacity, i.e., the elderly. Thus feeding (fidya) replaces the fast.
The verse is ambiguous (zanni al-dalala), and the interpretation is based on the context.
4
Note the amount and condition for feeding
The amount of feeding is the same as in expiations: half a sa' of wheat, or one sa' of dates or barley. This is only required if the elderly person is wealthy; otherwise, he seeks forgiveness. This condition is from Ibn Abidin.
The measurement: sa' is a volume measure; half sa' is approximately 2 kg of wheat.
5
Recognize cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position restricts pregnant/nursing women to qada only. Some other schools require fidya as well. The Hanafi view is based on qiyas to the sick, while others consider the fear for the child as a separate ground requiring expiation.
The mukhtalaf fih status is noted in the text implicitly through the commentary.

The classical Hanafi texts rule that if they genuinely fear harm to themselves or their child, they may break the fast and later make up the days, but no compensation (fidyah) is required. This ruling is justified by qiyas (analogy) to the sick and traveler, who also break due to incapacity and only owe qada.

The Decrepit Old Man: Fidyah for Permanent Inability to Fast
Ruling on permanently infirm elderly who cannot fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for the permanently infirm elderly regarding Ramadan fasting and explain the condition under which fidyah becomes void and qada becomes obligatory.

Core Idea
A permanent inability to fast triggers fidyah; a return to ability voids the fidyah and reinstates qada.

In this lesson, we examine the ruling for the elderly person whose strength has permanently diminished to the point of being unable to fast. The Hanafi school, relying on a specific interpretation of Quran 2:184, prescribes fidyah (feeding a poor person) for each missed day. However, a key condition—the continuance of inability—determines whether fidyah remains valid or must be replaced by qada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
Who is the 'decrepit old man'? He is one who cannot fast due to advanced age or loss of strength. The ruling: he breaks the fast (does not fast) and feeds a poor person for each day.
This applies equally to elderly women (al-ajuz al-faniyah).
2
Locate the Quranic proof
The proof is Surah Al-Baqarah 2:184: 'And upon those who are able [to fast], a fidyah of feeding a poor person.'
The apparent meaning is for those who can fast with difficulty, but Hanafi interpretation redirects it.
3
Understand the Hanafi interpretation of the verse
The Hanafis interpret 'yutiqoonahu' (they can bear it) as 'la yuteeqoonahu' (they cannot bear it), based on a known qira'ah and context. Thus the verse applies to those permanently unable to fast, not those who find it hard but can still fast.
This interpretive step is crucial for the ruling.
4
Apply the condition of khilfiyyah (substitution)
Fidyah is a substitution for the actual fast. A key condition for substitution (khilf) is the continuance of the original inability. If the person later becomes able to fast, the substitution becomes void.
The text states: 'wa law qadira ba'du 'ala al-sawmi yubtalu hukmu al-fida' because the condition of substitution is the continuance of inability.'
5
Note the consequence of recovery
If health returns, the fidyah already given is not counted; the missed fasts must be made up (qada). The fidyah was valid only as long as the inability persisted.
This highlights the difference between permanent and temporary inability.

Feeding for a Deceased Who Missed Fasts: Conditions and Obligation
Ruling on feeding poor for missed fasts of a deceased who bequeathed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on feeding for missed fasts of a deceased and identify when it is wajib (obligatory) and when voluntary.

Core Idea
Feeding for a deceased's missed fasts is only wajib if bequeathed and from one-third of the estate.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling from the Hidayah: when a person dies with outstanding make-up fasts from Ramadan and has made a bequest for feeding the poor on their behalf. The Hanafi school distinguishes clearly between obligatory feeding (when bequeathed and within the one-third limit) and voluntary feeding (when no bequest exists). We will unpack the conditions, the analogy to the decrepit old man, and the cross-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Is it obligatory, recommended, or voluntary? The text states that the guardian feeds for the deceased 'وجوباً' (necessarily) under certain conditions.
The act is feeding a poor person for each missed fast day.
2
Locate the dalil (legal proof)
What proof does the author cite for this ruling? The text uses analogy (qiyas) to the decrepit old man (shaykh fani) who cannot fast and must feed instead.
No direct Quranic or hadith quote is given; the proof is qiyas jaliy.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof establishes the ruling)
How does the analogy work? The illa (effective cause) is permanent inability to fast: the old man due to old age, the deceased due to death. Therefore, the substitution of feeding applies.
This is a clear analogy (jaliyyah) because the cause is evident.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
Which school disagrees and on what basis? The Shafi'i school holds that feeding is required even without a bequest if the deceased had the ability to fast but did not.
The disagreement centres on whether the missed fast is a debt that must be settled from the estate regardless of a bequest.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih (preferred position)
Why does the Hanafi school condition the obligation on a bequest? Because the missed fast is an act of worship that cannot be performed by another; the substitute (feeding) requires the deceased's directive through a bequest.
The mufta bihi is that without bequest, feeding is not obligatory, though voluntary feeding is permissible.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: a child reaches puberty or a person embraces Islam in the middle of a Ramadan day. What is their obligation for that day? Identify the two cases

They must fast all remaining days of Ramadan, just like any other obligated Muslim.

This distinguishes Hanafi fiqh from schools that might require qada' for the full day.

Core Idea
Intention made at night validates the fast even if unconsciousness occurs; absent intention requires make-up.

The fast of Ramadan requires intention (niyyah) before dawn. What happens if a person becomes unconscious during the day or the night? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on when the unconsciousness began relative to the intention. Intention is a condition for the validity of the fast in Hanafi fiqh.

For unconsciousness on a day (or night) – if it began at night, intention existed, so no make-up. If during the day, intention was absent, so make-up is required. For the entire month, all days must be made up because each day lacked intention.

The text states that unconsciousness does not remove intellect like insanity, so it is an excuse for delay but not for dropping the obligation. The make-up is wajib.

Ruling statement with exception and comparison.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a madman who regains sanity during Ramadan and identify the exception when the insanity covers the entire month.

Core Idea
A madman must make up missed fasts unless hardship requires otherwise.

The Hanafi school balances the cause of obligation (the month of Ramadan) with the principle of hardship (haraj). This lesson clarifies when a madman must make up missed days and when the obligation is lifted due to extreme burden.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the missed fasts during the period of insanity. The ruling is that the person must make them up (qada) as a wajib obligation.
The source states: 'qada ma madā minhu' — he makes up what passed.
2
Understand the cause and capacity
The cause (sabab) is the month of Ramadan. The capacity for obligation (ahliyyat al-wujub) is established by the existence of the person's liability (dhimma), which is not removed by insanity as long as the person is Muslim.
Thus, the obligation to fast arises even if the person is insane, but performance is not possible due to lack of intellect.
3
Recognize the exception for covering the entire period
If the insanity lasted for the entire time during which fasting is possible (i.e., the whole month), the person does not have to make up any days because that would cause undue hardship (haraj).
This exception is based on the principle of removing hardship: al-mashaqqa tajlib al-taysīr.
4
Contrast with unconsciousness
Unconsciousness (ighmā') does not normally cover the entire month, so its ruling does not include the hardship exception. The obligation to make up remains even if the unconsciousness lasts the whole month, but such duration is rare.
Hardship is only considered when it is typical, not in rare cases.

Al-Kamal ibn al-Humam supported this. However, the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that i'tikaf is valid in any mosque, regardless of whether the five prayers are held. Al-Saruji endorsed their view, and al-Tahawi chose it. Al-Khayr al-Ramli considered it easier, especially in modern times, and the author suggests reliance on this view as the mufta bihi. Finally, for women, i'tikaf is valid in the designated prayer area of their home (masjid baytiha), as it fulfills the purpose of anticipation.

Prohibited Acts and the Dispute on Leaving in I'tikaf
List of prohibitions and ruling on leaving with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibited acts during i'tikaf and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding leaving without excuse during voluntary i'tikaf.

Core Idea
Touching with desire invalidates i'tikaf if it leads to ejaculation; leaving without excuse splits the Hanafi school.

This lesson covers the core prohibitions during i'tikaf: intercourse, touching, and kissing. It then addresses a practical dispute within the Hanafi school: does leaving the mosque without a valid excuse break a voluntary i'tikaf? The majority holds it does not, but later scholars like Ibn al-Humam and Ibn Abidin argued otherwise for the emphasized sunnah of the last ten days. We will examine both positions and the evidence.

2
State the General Ruling on Leaving
Leaving is only allowed for natural needs (urination, defecation, impurity removal). For obligatory i'tikaf, any other exit invalidates it. For voluntary, the majority (Zahir al-Riwaya) holds it does not invalidate.
Obligatory i'tikaf includes vowed i'tikaf.
3
Identify the Opposing View
Ibn al-Humam and Ibn Abidin argue that the sunnah mu'akkadah i'tikaf (e.g., last ten days of Ramadan) is invalidated by an unnecessary exit, while other voluntary is not.

Under the majority: a mu'takif in the last ten days can briefly leave without invalidation. Under Ibn Abidin: leaving without excuse invalidates that specific i'tikaf.

Commentary
This passage covers the prohibitions during i'tikaf and the rules for leaving the mosque. The author begins by forbidding three acts: sexual intercourse (wat'), touching (lams) with desire, and kissing (qabla). If any of these lead to ejaculation (inzal), the i'tikaf becomes invalid (fasid) and must be repeated (qada). The primary proof is the Quranic verse (2:187): 'Do not have sexual relations with them while you are secluded in the mosques.' Touching and kissing are included because they are precursors to intercourse. The author then addresses leaving the mosque: the mu'takif may only leave for unavoidable natural needs (hajat al-insan) like urination, defecation, or removing impurity from oneself. Any other exit invalidates the i'tikaf if it is obligatory (wajib, e.g., by vow). For voluntary (nafl) i'tikaf, the Zahir al-Riwaya states that leaving without excuse does not invalidate it. This is the dominant view in Hanafi books. However, a later dispute is noted: Ibn al-Humam and Ibn Abidin argue that the emphasized sunnah i'tikaf (like the last ten days of Ramadan) is invalidated by an unnecessary exit, though ordinary nafl i'tikaf is not. But this view is criticized by al-Sindi and others, who stick to the traditional position. The text cuts off mid-discussion.
