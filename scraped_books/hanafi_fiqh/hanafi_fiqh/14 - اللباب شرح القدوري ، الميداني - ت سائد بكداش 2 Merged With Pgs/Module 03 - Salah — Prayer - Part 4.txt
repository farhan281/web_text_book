Two Cases of Invalid Iqtida: Condition vs. Prayer Type
Distinguishing two scenarios of invalid iqtida

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two cases of invalid iqtida and state the consequence for each regarding the validity of the prayer and the effect on wudu from loud laughter.

Core Idea
If iqtida fails due to missing condition, no prayer; if due to different prayer type, it becomes nafl ghayr mamdūn.

When following an imam becomes invalid, the outcome depends on the reason. Missing a condition like purity kills the prayer entirely, but a difference in obligatory prayer types transforms it into a voluntary prayer that doesn't require repetition. This distinction, reported from al-Zayla'i, also affects whether loud laughter breaks wudu.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two causes of invalid iqtida
The passage distinguishes between (1) loss of a condition (shart) of prayer, e.g., a pure person following a ma'dhūr (chronically impure imam), and (2) difference in the types of prayers (ikhtilāf al-ṣalātayn), e.g., Dhuhr behind Asr.
This is the foundational categorization.
2
Determine the legal effect on the follower's prayer
In case (1), the prayer does not commence (lam tan'aqid) at all. In case (2), it commences as a voluntary prayer (nafl) that is 'ghayr mamdūn' – meaning if it becomes invalid, there is no obligation to repeat it.
The key term 'ghayr mamdūn' distinguishes it from a supererogatory prayer that is binding (like sunnah mu'akkadah).
3
Apply the practical consequence: loud laughter and wudu
According to the Hanafi school, loud laughter (qahqaha) in a valid prayer invalidates both prayer and wudu. Since in case (1) no valid prayer was established, laughter does not break wudu. In case (2), a valid nafl prayer existed, so laughter invalidates wudu.
This thamara (practical outcome) is the reason the distinction matters.

Rules of Iqtida': Following a Different Fard and Nafl Behind Fard
Rulings on iqtida' with different prayers and effects of invalidity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid iqtida' (unity of prayer type) and explain the rulings when a muftarid follows a different muftarid versus a mutanaffil following a muftarid, including the consequence of qahqaha.

Core Idea
Iqtida' requires unity of prayer type; nafl behind fard is valid as building weak on strong.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn and its commentary clarifies two key scenarios of iqtida' (following the imam). First, a person performing a fard prayer cannot follow an imam performing a different fard prayer because partnership requires agreement. Second, a person performing a nafl prayer may follow a fard imam, as the weak is built upon the strong. The text also examines the validity of the follower's prayer when iqtida' fails and the practical effect of loud laughter (qahqaha).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage addresses two main cases: (A) a muftarid following a different muftarid, and (B) a mutanaffil following a muftarid. Identify each act and who is following whom.
Scenario A is prohibited; scenario B is permitted.
2
Apply the principle of iqtida'
The ruling is based on the definition of iqtida' as partnership and agreement (shirkah wa muwafaqah). Unity of prayer type is essential for a valid fard following. Understand why difference of fard breaks the partnership.
That is why the text says 'la budda min al-ittihad'.
3
Analyze the consequence of invalid iqtida'
When iqtida' fails due to missing condition (e.g., pure behind excused), the prayer does not start at all. When it fails due to difference in prayers (e.g., Zuhr behind Asr), the follower's prayer becomes a nafl prayer that is not required to be repeated (naflan ghayra madmun).
The thamarah (practical outcome) is that qahqaha invalidates wudu only if the prayer was valid as nafl.
4
Confirm the permissibility of nafl behind fard
The proof given is 'building the weak upon the strong' (bina' al-da'if 'ala al-qawi). This analogy (qiyas) allows a lesser act (nafl) behind a greater one (fard).
This is the relied-upon Hanafi position (zahir al-riwayah).

Follower Discovering Imam's Lack of Purity
Ruling when follower discovers imam's impurity after prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three scenarios of a follower discovering the imam's lack of purity and apply the Hanafi ruling in each case.

Core Idea
The follower's prayer depends on his own belief about the imam's validity.

In congregational prayer, the follower (muqtadi) relies on the imam's validity. But what happens if the follower later discovers that the imam was impure? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling based on whose belief (za'm) is considered. This lesson walks through three distinct scenarios from the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed-upon scenario
When both the imam and follower later agree that the imam had no wudu, the follower must repeat the prayer by consensus. This is because the invalidity is apparent to both.
2
Analyze the scenario where the imam's prayer is valid in his view but invalid in the follower's view
If the imam follows a school that deems his wudu valid, but the follower's school considers the imam's wudu invalid, the follower must also repeat. The reasoning: the follower believes he is following an invalid imam, so his prayer is built on what he considers invalid.
There is some disagreement (khilaf) on this case, but the text notes that each considered it correct.
3
Examine the third scenario: imam's prayer invalid in his own view, but follower unaware
If the imam himself knows his prayer was invalid (e.g., he forgot his wudu), but the follower does not know and believes the imam is pure, the follower's prayer is valid according to the majority. The criterion is the follower's own belief (al-mu'tabar fi haqqihi ra'yu nafsihi).
This is the relied-upon position (al-asahh) in the Hanafi school.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The key principle is that for the validity of the follower's prayer, what matters is the follower's own view (i'tiqad) about the imam's state. The objective reality of the imam's purity is not decisive when the follower is unaware.
This is taken from the gloss of Shaykh ar-Rahmati.

Validity of a Follower's Prayer When the Imam's Purity is Compromised: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on follower's prayer validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a follower's prayer when the imam's wudu is invalid, distinguishing three scenarios and applying the principle that the follower's own knowledge governs.

Core Idea
The follower's prayer is valid if he is unaware of the imam's impurity — his own opinion, not the imam's, determines validity.

In congregational prayer, the purity (taharah) of the imam is a condition for the validity of the prayer. But what happens when the follower later discovers that his imam was not in a state of purity? The Hanafi school addresses this in three distinct scenarios, and there is an important intra-madhhab disagreement. This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains the rulings and the ikhtilaf, culminating in the preferred view (mufta bihi).

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
There are three possible situations: (1) both imam and follower know of the impurity, (2) the follower knows but the imam does not, and (3) the imam knows but the follower does not. Distinguish each clearly.
The passage explicitly lists the first and third scenarios. The second is implied by 'same as above' reasoning.
2
Determine the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1, both agree the prayer must be repeated. For scenario 2, the follower's prayer is invalid because he consciously follows an impure imam. For scenario 3, there is disagreement: most Hanafis hold the follower's prayer is valid, while a minority require repetition.
The key is what the follower knows, not the imam's actual state.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
In scenario 3, the dispute turns on whether the follower's prayer is built on the imam's objective situation or on the follower's perception. The majority (al-akthar) and the most correct (al-ashah) opinion is that it is valid.
The principle is: what matters in ruling on the follower's prayer is his own belief and intention.
4
Apply the principle of 'i'tibār ra'y al-nafs'
The follower's own opinion governs his act of worship. Since he sees his imam as valid, his prayer is sound. This reflects a broader Hanafi usul: the obligation of prayer is tied to one's knowledge and intention.
This principle also appears in other fiqh chapters, e.g., following a silent imam.

Ruling on Sitting Cross-Legged in Prayer
Ruling on sitting posture in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on sitting cross-legged (tarbbu') in prayer and identify the only condition under which it is permitted.

Core Idea
Sitting cross-legged in prayer abandons the sunnah; it is only allowed with a valid excuse.

In the Hanafi school, the manner of sitting during prayer follows a specific sunnah. The final tashahhud requires sitting in the tawarruk posture, while crossing the legs (tarbbu') is contrary to this sunnah. However, the jurists recognized that circumstances may prevent a person from sitting properly, so they made an exception: tarbbu' is permitted when there is a legitimate excuse. This lesson unpacks that ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act is sitting cross-legged (tarbbu') during the prayer.
Tarbbu' means to sit with legs folded or crossed, as opposed to the sunnah postures of iftirash and tawarruk.
2
Determine the default ruling
The matn states 'لا يتربع' meaning it is not allowed; it is makruh because it abandons the sunnah of sitting.
The ruling is makruh tanzihi, not haram, because leaving a sunnah is disliked but not sinful.
3
Find the exception
The phrase 'إلا من عذر' except with an excuse provides a concession.
An excuse can be illness, injury, or any valid reason preventing proper sitting.
4
Understand the reason
Because tarbbu' involves leaving the sunnah posture (sunnat al-qu'ud).
The sunnah for the final tashahhud is tawarruk; for the first it is iftirash.
5
Apply the exception
When an excuse exists, tarbbu' becomes permissible and the prayer is valid without dislike.
Example: a person with a leg cast may sit cross-legged.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from Quduri's matn with commentary by al-Maydani. It covers two prohibitions during prayer. First, sitting cross-legged (tarbbu') is against the sunnah; the preferred sitting for the tashahhud is to sit on the left foot with the right foot upright (iftirash) for the first tashahhud and tawarruk (sitting on the left hip) for the final tashahhud. Second, eating and drinking during prayer invalidates it completely, whether the person did so intentionally or forgetfully. This is because eating and drinking are external actions inconsistent with the nature of prayer (salat). The hanafi position is that even a small amount of food (like a morsel) invalidates the prayer if swallowed. Immediate Procedure When Overtaken by Hadath
Core ruling on immediate departure and building after hadath.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the immediate steps required when overtaken by unintentional hadath during prayer and identify actions that invalidate the prayer.

Core Idea
Hadath does not nullify prayer if you leave immediately and return without speaking.

When a person is overtaken by an unavoidable hadath like nosebleed during prayer, the Hanafi school provides a clear procedure that preserves the prayer's continuity. This lesson walks through the obligation to depart instantly, the permitted actions while performing wudu, and the conditions for resuming the prayer from where you left off.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scenario
Identify that the hadath occurs involuntarily (e.g., nosebleed, vomiting). This is not about intentionally breaking wudu.
'Samawi' hadath (heavenly) is the key term.
2
Immediate departure
You must leave the prayer at once, without delay. Standing for the duration of one rukn invalidates the prayer.
3
Permitted actions during wudu
While going to make wudu, you may walk, scoop water, turn from qiblah, wash impurity, and do istinja' without exposing awrah. But do not pass a nearby water source to go farther.
Excessive walking without need invalidates.
4
Building on prayer
After wudu, resume from where you stopped, provided you did not speak. If you spoke, the prayer is invalid and must be restarted.
No speaking is a condition.
5
Consequences of violation
Any of: delaying departure, speaking, or excessive walking invalidates the prayer entirely.

This passage, from al-Quduri's matn with al-Maydani's commentary, addresses the situation when a person experiences hadath (event that nullifies wudu) during prayer without choice, such as nosebleed or vomiting. Rulings for Imam, Ma'mum, and Choices After Building
Special rules for imam, ma'mum, and choices after building.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate the rules for an imam and a ma'mum when overtaken by hadath, and apply the choices given to a munfarid and ma'mum after building.

Core Idea
An imam must appoint a substitute; a follower returns to his place unless the imam finishes.

The previous lesson covered the basic procedure for anyone overtaken by hadath. This lesson examines the special rules when the affected person is the imam or a follower, including the mechanism of istikhlaf (deputizing) and the options after completing wudu. The passage distinguishes between three roles: munfarid, ma'mum, and imam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the role
Determine whether the person overtaken is an imam, a ma'mum (follower), or praying alone (munfarid). Each has distinct rules.
2
Imam: appoint a substitute
If the imam is overtaken, he must pull a follower forward (istikhlaf) by his garment to lead the remainder of the prayer. The imam then becomes a follower if he returns.
The substitute leads the rest; the original imam completes individually.
3
Ma'mum: return to place or finish individually
A follower who leaves for wudu must return to his original spot in the row to rejoin the imam. However, if the imam has already finished the prayer before he returns, the follower has the same choice as a munfarid (see next step).
4
Choices for munfarid and ma'mum after imam finishes
A person praying alone has two valid options: return to his original prayer spot (better) or complete the prayer in the place where he performed wudu (to minimize walking). A ma'mum, if the imam has finished, also has these two choices.
Returning to the original spot is afdal (better) because it unifies the prayer location.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
After hadath, you may build upon prayer if you haven't spoken or delayed excessively, but restarting is better to avoid ikhtilaf.

When a person's wudu breaks during prayer, the default reaction might be to restart. Categories of Followers: Mudrik, Lahiq, Masbuq
Defines the three main types of followers in congregational prayer based on timing of joining

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of followers in congregational prayer and apply the correct ruling for making up missed rak'ahs based on the follower's category.

Core Idea
The follower's category determines whether he recites when making up missed rak'ahs.

In congregational prayer, not all followers who miss rak'ahs are treated the same. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a follower who joined late (masbuq) and one who started on time but missed rak'ahs due to a valid excuse (lahiq). Understanding these categories is crucial for correctly completing your prayer after a disruption. This lesson defines the three categories and their key differences.

Guided Path
1
Define the three categories
Read the passage carefully to understand the definitions of mudrik, lahiq, and masbuq. Note that the passage also mentions a fourth category: lahiq masbuq (a combination).
Mudrik catches the entire prayer with the imam from at least the ruku' of the first rak'ah.
2
Distinguish lahiq from masbuq
The key difference is the presence of a valid excuse. A lahiq missed rak'ahs due to an excuse (e.g., sleep, crowding, hadath) after starting the prayer with the imam. A masbuq joined the prayer after the imam had already begun, without an excuse.
Lahiq can be 'lahiq only' or 'lahiq masbuq' if he also joined late.
3
Identify the ruling for making up missed rak'ahs
For the masbuq: when making up missed rak'ahs, he recites the opening supplication (thana'), ta'awwudh, and Quran as if praying alone. For the lahiq: he does not recite when making up; he makes up without recitation and follows the imam when possible.
The lahiq's makeup is opposite to the masbuq: he starts with the last of his prayer.
4
Apply to scenarios
Practice determining the category of a follower based on the description and then state how he should make up missed rak'ahs.
Focus on the presence or absence of an excuse.

Invalidators of Prayer: Sleep, Insanity, Fainting, Laughter, and Speech
Rulings on what nullifies prayer and requires renewal of wudu and prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on prayer invalidators (sleep with wet dream, insanity, fainting, loud laughter, and speech) and explain the evidence and practical consequences including renewal of wudu and prayer.

Core Idea
Loud laughter and any speech invalidate prayer and require renewal; sleep with wet dream, insanity, fainting also invalidate and require wudu (or ghusl for wet dream).

This passage from al-Hidayah presents two categories of prayer invalidators. The first set — sleep with wet dream, insanity, fainting, and loud laughter — requires renewal of both wudu and prayer (with ghusl for wet dream). The second — any speech, whether intentional or forgetful — invalidates prayer outright. The Hanafi proof is a hadith that shows speech breaks the prayer's continuity, and the school distinguishes these rare events from those covered by the hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sets of invalidators
First set: sleep with wet dream, insanity, fainting, loud laughter (qahqaha). Second set: any speech, intentional or forgetful.
Loud laughter (qahqaha) is distinguished from light laughter (ibtisam) which does not invalidate.
2
Determine the consequences for each invalidator
For the first set: renew wudu (or ghusl for wet dream) and prayer entirely. For the second set: prayer becomes invalid (batil) without renewing wudu.
For wet dream, ghusl is obligatory because it involves janabah.
3
Understand the reasoning behind the first set
The commentator from Hidayah notes that these occurrences are rare (nādir), so they are not covered by the specific hadith about vomiting, nosebleed, and madhi (which allow building upon prayer).
Rarity makes them analogous to other major invalidators.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proof for speech
The text cites the hadith: 'Whoever vomits, or has a nosebleed, or emits madhi in his prayer: let him leave and perform wudu, and build upon his prayer as long as he does not speak.' The phrase 'as long as he does not speak' implies that speech invalidates.
The hadith is mursal, but Hanafis accept mursal as proof.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school holds that both intentional and forgetful speech invalidate prayer. Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) differentiate: intentional speech invalidates, while forgetful speech does not (for the general public).
The Hanafi view is based on the general command to avoid speech in prayer.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
After the final sitting, the prayer's pillars are complete; only salam remains, so subsequent events do not invalidate the prayer.

The Twelve Issues (al-Masa'il al-Ithna 'Ashariyyah) are a famous set of intra-Hanafi disputes between Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The first four cases all involve an event that would normally break the prayer – such as seeing water after tayammum – but occurring after the final sitting. Abu Hanifa holds the prayer is already complete, while the Sahibayn say it must be repeated. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common hinge
In all four cases, the event (seeing water, expiration of mash, removing khuffain gently, illiterate learning a surah) occurs after the person has sat for the measure of tashahhud. This is the key temporal point.
The final sitting (qa'dah akhirah) is a rukn according to many, but here it is after the tashahhud is done.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
Abu Hanifa rules that the prayer is completely fulfilled and valid. Only the wajib of salam remains, which can be performed after renewing wudu if needed.
This is based on the idea that the prayer's essence (arkan) is already accomplished.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
The Sahibayn hold that the prayer becomes invalid because the event introduces a new condition (e.g., need for wudu) that cannot be fulfilled without breaking the prayer.
They consider the prayer as still ongoing until salam is said.
4

For example, a mutayammim who sees water after final sitting: Abu Hanifa says complete prayer with salam; Sahibayn say repeat prayer after wudu. The same applies for the other cases.
This has direct consequences for daily practice.
5
Note the exception of much action
If removing khuffain involves much action (e.g., needed effort or movement), all agree the prayer is complete because the exit from prayer is by one's own action, not an external event.
This shows that the dispute is limited to minimal or involuntary events.

Core Idea
Tarteeb is wajib for up to six missed prayers; beyond that, the obligation drops due to hardship.

This passage from the chapter on making up missed prayers clarifies a crucial ruling: the chronological order (tarteeb) is obligatory only when the number of missed prayers is six or fewer. When they exceed six, the obligation drops because of the hardship it would cause. This is a clear example of the Hanafi principle of avoiding extreme difficulty in acts of worship. Let's break down the ruling step by step.

What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of making up missed prayers (qada') with order. The ruling is that tarteeb is wajib when fawa'it are six or less, and not wajib when more than six.

What proof does the author cite? The proof is rational: the hardship (haraj) that would result from requiring order for many missed prayers. This is a dalil aqli (istihsan based on hardship).

Prohibition of Prayer at Sunrise: Duration and Severity
States that prayer is not permissible at sunrise and explains the exact duration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prayer at sunrise, identify the exact duration of prohibition, and distinguish the effect on obligatory vs. supererogatory prayers.

Core Idea
Prayer at sunrise is prohibited until the sun rises a spear's length; obligatory prayers are invalid while supererogatory are valid but severely disliked.

Prayer is one of the most important acts of worship, and its validity depends on proper timing. The Hanafi school specifies certain times when prayer is disliked or prohibited. In this lesson, we examine the ruling on prayer at sunrise: when does the prohibition start and end, and what happens if one prays during that time?

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act under discussion? Prayer (salat) at sunrise. What is the hukm? The passage states 'لا تجوز الصلاة' meaning it is not permissible. In Hanafi terminology, for fard and wajib prayers, it is batil (invalid); for nafl, it is makruh tahrimi (severely disliked) but valid.
Makruh tahrimi is close to haram; omitting it is rewarded but performing it incurs sin.
2
Identify the prohibited duration
From when does the prohibition start? From the moment the sun's disc appears at the horizon. Until when? Until the sun has risen a spear's length (as per al-Asl) or until one cannot look at the disc (as per al-Fadli). The relied-upon view is the spear's length.
A spear's length is roughly 12-15 degrees above the horizon, about 20-30 minutes after sunrise.
3
Distinguish between fard/wajib and nafl
What is the effect if one begins a fard prayer at sunrise? The prayer is invalid (batil) from the start; it does not count and must be repeated. For nafl, the prayer is valid but severely disliked (makruh tahrimi).
This distinction affects other rulings: if a fard prayer is invalid, laughter during it does not break wudu because the prayer never commenced.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
Two views on the end of the prohibited time: al-Asl (spear's length) and al-Fadli (inability to look at the disc). Both are valid transmissions, but the mufta bihi is the spear's length.
This is a valid ikhtilaf within the school; both imams had strong evidence.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is the spear's length measurement from al-Asl, as indicated by the commentary and later authorities like Ibn Abidin.
Always follow the mufta bihi in practice; the other view is known but not acted upon.

Times When Prayer is Disliked – Ruling and Exceptions
States times of karahah for nafl and allowed prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which prayers are disliked (makruh tanzih) after Fajr and Asr, and identify the exceptions (qadha, sajdah tilawah, janazah) based on the Hanafi legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Nafl after Fajr and Asr is makruh tanzih, but missed obligations and sajdah/janazah are allowed because the prohibition is tied to the time being 'occupied' by the fard.

This lesson unpacks the classic Hanafi ruling on praying in the times after Fajr and Asr. While supererogatory prayers are disliked, the madhhab makes clear exceptions for making up missed prayers, performing the prostration of recitation, and offering the funeral prayer—all of which remain valid and free of dislike. We will explore the legal reasoning (ta'lil) that explains this nuanced position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is performing any supererogatory (nafl) prayer after the Fajr prayer until sunrise, and after the Asr prayer until sunset. The Hanafi ruling is that it is makruh tanzih (slightly disliked).
Makruh tanzih means the act is not sinful but is better avoided; omission is rewarded.
2
Recognize the exceptions
The text explicitly states that there is no harm in praying missed obligatory prayers (fawa'it), performing sajdah tilawah, and offering janazah prayer in these times. These are not subject to the karahah.
The exception applies only to these three categories; no other prayers are exempted.
3
Understand the ta'lil (reasoning)
The prohibition on nafl is because the time is legally considered as if occupied by the obligatory prayer of the moment, which is superior to nafl. Thus, the prohibition only affects acts that are less virtuous (nafl), not acts of equal or greater virtue (qadha, etc.).
This contrasts with the inherently prohibited times (sunrise, zenith, sunset) where the prohibition is due to a meaning in the time itself, affecting all prayers.
4
Distinguish from the three absolute prohibited times
The text notes that prayer at sunrise, zenith, and sunset is prohibited due to an inherent meaning in the time, which nullifies even non-nafl prayers. The times after Fajr and Asr are only disliked for nafl, not for obligations.
This distinction is crucial: the karahah after Fajr/Asr is not due to the time itself but due to a legal construct.
5
Note the intra-madhhab variation
Some later Hanafi scholars (e.g., Ali al-Qari) hold that there is no karahah even for sajdah tilawah, while the relied-upon position (Ibn Abidin) is that sajdah tilawah is makruh tanzih. Janazah prayer has no karahah according to all.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on voluntary prayers during the disliked times after Fajr and Asr, and identify which prayers are excepted from this dislike.

Core Idea
Voluntary prayers are makruh tanzih after Fajr and Asr, but missed prayers, sajdah tilawah, and funeral prayers are exempt.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on performing voluntary prayers during the two disliked periods: after Fajr until sunrise, and after Asr until sunset. While the default is dislike (makruh tanzih), there are important exceptions that reflect the legal reasoning behind the prohibition. Understanding these exceptions requires appreciating the difference between a prohibition due to an inherent meaning in the time and one due to a consideration external to the time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two disliked times and the general ruling
Note that the text states: 'It is disliked to perform voluntary prayer after Fajr until sunrise, and after Asr until sunset.' This is the basic ruling.

2
List the exceptions explicitly permitted

3

The prohibition after Fajr and Asr is due to the time being 'occupied' by the obligatory prayer of that time, not due to any inherent defect in the time. This rationale is key to understanding the exceptions.

Compare with missed prayers which are obligatory without being self-imposed.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab, a commentary on al-Quduri, opens the chapter on supererogatory prayers (nawafil). The commentator first defines nafilah (pl. nawafil) linguistically as 'increase' and legally as an act prescribed in the Shari'ah that is not fard (obligatory), wajib (necessary), or even sunnah (the Prophet's regular practice). This definition highlights that nawafil are voluntary acts beyond the confirmed sunnah, as noted in the Jawahirah. The passage then mentions that although the chapter is titled 'nawafil', it discusses the sunnan (plural of sunnah) because nawafil is a broader category encompassing sunnan and more. The author then explains the term 'sunnah', defining it as a path in religion followed without the level of obligation of fard or wajib. He then lists the sunnah (or nafilah) prayers associated with the five daily prayers, beginning with the two rak'ahs after Fajr (the most emphasized sunnah, almost approaching wajib). Next is four rak'ahs before Zuhr with one taslim, noting that in the first sitting one only recites the tashahhud without the opening supplication or additional blessings—this is the rule for every emphasized four-rak'ah prayer (sunnah muakkadah). For recommended (mustahabbah) prayers, one does recite the opening supplication, seek refuge, and send blessings on the Prophet. The commentator notes a difference of opinion: some later scholars uphold the opening supplication in the first sitting of muakkadah prayers, but it is not transmitted from the early Hanafi masters. After Zuhr, two rak'ahs are recommended (mustahabbah). Before Asr, four rak'ahs are sunnah (or four, or if one wishes two), with four being better. After Maghrib, two rak'ahs are emphasized (muakkadah). Before Isha, four rak'ahs are sunnah with one taslim. This passage sets out the normative Hanafi practice for the regular supererogatory prayers, emphasizing the gradations between muakkadah (emphasized) and ghayr muakkadah (non-emphasized).

Key Terms
نافلة (nafilah): Legally: a prescribed act that is not fard, wajib, or even sunnah—a voluntary supererogatory act.
سنة (sunnah): The path followed in religion without the level of obligation of fard or wajib.
مؤكدة (muakkadah): Emphasized sunnah, like the two rak'ahs after Fajr, four before Zuhr, etc.
