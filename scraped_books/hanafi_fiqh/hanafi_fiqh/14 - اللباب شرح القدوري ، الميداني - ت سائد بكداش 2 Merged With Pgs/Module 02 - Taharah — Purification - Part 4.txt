Wiping Over Thick Socks: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
States conditions for wiping over socks and reports intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for wiping over socks in the Hanafi school, identify the disputed scenario (thick but unsoled socks), and explain the mufta bihi position based on Imam Abu Hanifa's retraction.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa ruled against wiping over thick-only socks; he retracted, so the fatwa follows Sahibayn's permissibility.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses the conditions for wiping over socks (jorabayn). The basic ruling is that thick, non-porous socks are required. However, there is a key intra-Hanafi dispute: are the socks also required to be soled (mun'al) like khuff? The text reports that Imam Abu Hanifa initially did not permit wiping over thick socks without soles, but he later retracted to the view of his two companions, and the fatwa today is upon that permissibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions from the matn
Read the matn: 'al-jorabayn idha kana thakhinayn la yushaffan al-maa.' The two conditions are: (1) thickness (thakhāna) and (2) non-porosity (lā yushaffān). The sharh adds that thickness means the socks stay on the foot without tying.
Non-porosity means water does not seep through to the foot.
2
Understand the definition of 'thick'
The sharh explains 'thakhinayn' as: 'yastamsekan 'ala al-rijl min ghayr shadd' (stay on the foot without tying) and 'la yushaffan al-maa' (do not let water through). These two are effectively the same condition of thickness.
3
Distinguish the three scenarios
Qazi Khan's summary in the passage presents three cases: (1) thick and soled (mun'alayn) – permissible by consensus; (2) not thick and not soled – not permissible by consensus; (3) thick but not soled – disputed.
The dispute only concerns scenario 3.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that wiping over thick but unsoled socks is not permissible. Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that it is permissible. The text reports that Imam Abu Hanifa retracted to their view on his deathbed.
The basis for the dispute: Abu Hanifa likely required an additional condition of being soled (mun'al) to analogize to khuff; Sahibayn considered thickness sufficient.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is the permissibility of wiping over thick socks even if unsoled, due to Imam Abu Hanifa's retraction and the consensus of later Hanafi authorities.
This is stated explicitly: 'wa 'alayhi al-fatwa'.

Non-Permissibility of Wiping Over Turban, Cap, Veil, Gloves
Ruling statement with rationale based on non-analogy of concession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wiping over headgear and hand coverings and explain the reason based on the principle that concessions (rukhas) are not extended by analogy.

Core Idea
Wiping over a turban is invalid because the concession for khuff is an exception that cannot be analogized.

In wudu, the obligation is to wipe part of the head and wash the hands. A concession allows wiping over khuff (leather socks) instead of washing the feet. This lesson explains why this concession cannot be extended to other coverings like turbans, caps, veils, or gloves. The key legal principle is that a ruling that deviates from the general rule (khilaf al-qiyas) remains confined to its textual basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the items mentioned
List the four items: turban ('imamah), cap (qalansuwah), face-veil (burqu'), and gloves (quffazayn). Understand that these cover the head or hands.
These are common head and hand coverings in classical and modern contexts.
2
State the ruling for each
The text says 'لا يجوز المسح' — it is not permissible to wipe over these items for the purpose of wudu. That means wiping over them does not fulfill the obligation.
The ruling is uniform for all four; no exception is made.
3
Analyze the reason given
The reason is: 'because wiping over the khuff is established contrary to analogy, so nothing else is attached to it.' This means the concession for khuff is a special exception; it cannot be used as a basis for analogical extension (qiyas).
This is a key principle in Hanafi usul: rukhas (concessions) are not subject to qiyas.
4
Apply the principle to new cases
Any other covering not explicitly mentioned in the texts (e.g., medical bandage, raincoat) must be judged by the general rule: direct wiping on skin or washing is required unless a specific concession exists.
Bandages have their own rules under 'mash al-jabirah' which are different.

Permissibility of Wiping Over Splints (Jaba'ir)
Ruling that wiping over splints is allowed even if applied without wudu, with reasoning based on hardship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wiping over splints and explain the proof based on hardship (haraj), and distinguish it from the ruling on khuff.

Core Idea
Wiping over splints is valid even without prior purity because hardship is removed by Shariah.

In previous lessons, you learned about the concession of wiping over khuff (leather socks) under specific conditions. Now we encounter a different kind of facilitation: wiping over splints (jaba'ir). Unlike khuff, this concession does not require the splint to be applied in a state of purity. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling and its proof, grounded in the principle of removing hardship (رفع الحرج).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
State the act: wiping over a splint (jabirah) during wudu or ghusl. The ruling: it is permissible (ja'iz) even if the splint was applied without wudu or in a state of janabah. The text says: 'ويجوز المسح على الجبائر وإن شدها على غير وضوء'.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is istihsan based on the removal of hardship (haraj). The text says: 'لأن في اشتراط الطهارة في تلك الحال حرجاً، وهو مدفوع'. Requiring purity at the time of application would cause hardship, which is repelled by Shariah.
This is a general principle: hardship necessitates facilitation (المشقة تجلب التيسير).
3
Distinguish from khuff
Contrast with the ruling on khuff, where wiping is a separate concession that requires the khuff to be put on in a state of purity. For splints, the obligation to wash the injured part is transferred to the splint itself because washing underneath is not feasible. The text says: 'بخلاف الخف'.
4
Apply the fall-off rule
If the splint falls off before the fracture heals, the previous wiping is not invalidated. The text: 'فإن سقطت عن غير برء : لم يبطل المسح' because the excuse (the injury) remains. New splints can be wiped similarly.
The ruling lasts as long as the excuse (al-'udhr) exists, regardless of the state of the splint.

Invalidation of Wiping over Khuffs When the Excuse Ceases
Ruling on invalidation of wiping when khuff falls off after cure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why wiping over khuffs becomes invalid when the khuff falls off after the excuse has healed, and state the ruling when this occurs during prayer.

Core Idea
When the original obligation becomes possible, the substitute ceases to be valid.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, the great Hanafi jurist al-Marghinani clarifies a crucial detail about wiping over khuffs (mash 'ala al-khuffayn): the substitute wipes are valid only as long as the excuse (such as a wound) persists. If the khuff falls off after the excuse has healed, the permission to wipe ends immediately. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its logical basis, including the practical consequence for someone praying at that moment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wiping over khuffs (mash) while having a valid excuse (e.g., a foot wound). The ruling: if the khuff falls off after the excuse has healed (bur'), the wiping becomes invalid (batil).
The invalidation is immediate; no time limit applies.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): the substitute (badal) is permitted only until the original obligation (washing feet) becomes feasible again. Here, with healing and the khuff off, one can now wash the feet directly.
The Qur'an and hadith establish the original obligation of washing feet; the wiping is a rukhsa (concession) that ends when the necessity ends.
3
Understand the consequence during prayer
If the khuff falls off after cure while in prayer, the prayer is invalid because the condition of valid wudu (taharah) is lost. The person must wash his feet, redo wudu, and restart the prayer.
This is because the prayer was built on an invalid taharah from the moment the khuff fell after cure.
4
Grasp the underlying principle
The key principle: al-badal yatba' al-mubdal (the substitute follows the original). When the original becomes attainable, the substitute no longer holds.
This principle applies across many fiqh rulings, not just in taharah.

The first narration does not allow beginning or ending menstruation with purity. The passage references the Hidayah as source and provides Quranic basis regarding the recitation of "yathurna".

Wudu for a Ma'dhur and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Time
Ruling on wudu validity for a ma'dhur, limited to each prayer time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wudu for a ma'dhur and explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding wudu performed before the prayer time.

Core Idea
A ma'dhur's wudu lasts for the entire prayer time and can serve multiple prayers, even if performed before that time's start.

This lesson covers the rule for a person with a continuous excuse (ma'dhur) such as chronic bleeding or incontinence. The core ruling is that they must perform wudu at the beginning of each obligatory prayer time, and that wudu remains valid for all prayers within that time, until the time exits. An important intra-Hanafi dispute arises: if a ma'dhur makes wudu for a supererogatory prayer like Eid before Zuhr time, can he use that same wudu for Zuhr? We will explore the positions of the early Hanafi masters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ma'dhur and their excuses
List the persons considered ma'dhur from the text: mustahadhah, one with urinary incontinence, chronic nosebleed, non-clotting wound, chronic diarrhea, constant wind, constant tearing, or any continuous discharge from ear, breast, or navel due to illness.
The common factor: a continuous hadath that cannot be controlled.
2
State the basic ruling
The ma'dhur must perform wudu at the start (after time begins) of each obligatory prayer time. With that wudu, they can pray any number of fard, wajib, or nafl prayers within that time.
The wudu is not invalidated by the continuous excuse itself, only by the exit of the time or a new, different hadath.
3
Understand the time-bound validity
When the prayer time ends, the wudu becomes invalid (the underlying hadath is considered to have occurred). A new wudu is required for the next time.
This is a special concession – normally, any discharge invalidates wudu, but the ma'dhur is excused.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
If a ma'dhur performs wudu for Eid prayer (before Zuhr time), can he pray Zuhr with that same wudu? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say yes; Abu Yusuf says no, arguing the wudu must be tied to the specific prayer time. The text declares Abu Hanifa/Muhammad's view as correct (al-sahih).
The dispute hinges on whether the wudu is 'for the time' or 'for the prayer'.

This passage of the commentary expands on the ruling for a mustahadhah (woman with chronic uterine bleeding) and all persons with permanent excuses (asmaab al-a'dhar) such as urinary incontinence, constant nosebleed, wounds that do not clot, chronic diarrhea, constant passing of wind, constant tearing due to sickness, and any similar discharge from ear, breast, or navel due to a chronic condition. Core Ruling on Wudu for Chronically Excused Persons
Basic ruling on wudu for chronic excuses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the wudu ruling for chronically excused persons and identify when their wudu becomes invalid.

Core Idea
A chronically excused person’s wudu lasts one prayer time and permits multiple prayers; it expires at the end of that time.

Imagine you have a medical condition that causes continuous impurity. How can you pray? The Hanafi school provides a specific concession. This lesson explains the basic ruling: perform wudu once per prayer time, and that wudu suffices for any number of obligatory and voluntary prayers within that time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the persons covered
The text lists mustahadah, those with urinary incontinence, continuous nosebleed, and non-clotting wounds. These are examples of chronic excuses.
The list is expanded in the commentary to include anal incontinence, wind, etc.
2
State the wudu rule
Such persons perform wudu for the time of each obligatory prayer. This wudu is valid for the entire time period.
They do not need to repeat wudu for each prayer.
3
Determine what can be prayed with that wudu
They may pray any number of fard, wajib, and nafl prayers, both on time and qada, within that time.
No limit on quantity.
4
Identify when wudu becomes invalid
The wudu becomes invalid when the prayer time exits. Upon time exit, the underlying impurity takes effect.
It does not become invalid before that, unless a different impurity occurs.
5
Note the exception for a different impurity
If a new impurity that is not part of the chronic excuse occurs (e.g., someone with urinary incontinence also vomits), the wudu is invalidated immediately.
This is not a nullifier from the excuse itself.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling and commentary on the legal status of individuals with chronic or persistent impurities (أصحاب الأعذار) in the Hanafi school. It begins by stating the core rule: those such as the mustahadah (woman with chronic vaginal bleeding), the person with urinary incontinence, continuous nosebleed, or a wound that does not clot, must perform wudu for the time of each obligatory prayer. This wudu remains valid for that entire time period, during which they may pray any number of obligatory (fard), mandatory (wajib), and voluntary (nafl) prayers. However, once the time exits, their wudu becomes invalid and they must renew it for the next prayer. The commentary then expands the list of excuses to include anal incontinence, passing wind, tearing eyes from a cause, and anything exiting from an ear, breast, or navel due to a chronic condition. It then introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, if an excused person performs wudu before the time of a prayer (e.g., for Eid), that wudu is valid for the subsequent prayer (e.g., Dhuhr) as well, and is not invalidated merely by the entry of the new time. This is declared the relied-upon (sahih) position, with Abu Yusuf's differing view noted. The commentary clarifies that the invalidation upon time exit is not because time itself is a nullifier of wudu, but rather the time was a barrier preventing the underlying impurity from taking effect (to avoid hardship). When the time ends, the barrier falls and the pre-existing impurity becomes effective. Therefore, if an excused person performs wudu during a gap in their condition and it continues until time exits, the wudu remains valid. Next, conditions for the excuse are given: for initial establishment, the excuse must cover the entire time of a prayer, such that the person cannot find any moment within that time to perform a pure wudu and pray. For continuation, it suffices that the excuse occurs at least once in each time period. For the excuse to end, the interruption (no impurity) must cover an entire time. A note follows: the excused person is not obligated to wash their garment if it will become impure again before finishing the prayer. The conclusion imposes a duty to repel the impurity if possible, or at least minimize it. Citing al-Bahr, if the person can stop the flow with a bandage or by sitting instead of standing, they must do so and are no longer excused. If the impurity flows when moving, they must pray sitting with gestures, as abandoning prostration is easier than praying with impurity.

Conditions for Establishing, Continuing, and Ending the Chronic Excuse
Conditions for the excuse status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions for a chronic excuse to be established, continued, and ended.

Core Idea
An excuse is established only if impurity covers an entire prayer time, continues with one occurrence per time, and ends only with a full clean time.

Not every occasional impurity qualifies for the concession. The Hanafi school sets precise conditions for who is considered 'ma'dhur' and how the status changes. This lesson covers the initial requirement, the continuation, and the cessation of the excuse.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the three phases
The excuse has three phases: initial establishment, continuation, and cessation. Each has different conditions.
2
Initial establishment: full time coverage
To become a ma'dhur, the impurity must occur throughout the entire time of an obligatory prayer, without any break long enough to perform wudu and pray the fard.
Even a single moment of purity within that time prevents the establishment.
3
Continuation: one occurrence per time suffices
Once established, the excuse continues as long as it occurs at least once in each subsequent prayer time.
The excuse does not need to be continuous for the next times.
4
Cessation: complete interruption for one full time
If the impurity completely stops for an entire prayer time (no occurrence at all), the excuse ends.
Partial interruption (e.g., stops for half the time) does not end the excuse.
5
Note the garment washing exemption
The excused person is not required to wash their garment if it will become impure again before finishing the prayer.
This is to avoid hardship.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Validity of Wudu Performed Before the Prayer Time
Internal Hanafi dispute on time-based wudu validity

Learning Objective

Core Idea
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, wudu performed before the time remains valid for the next prayer; Abu Yusuf disagrees.

What if a chronically excused person performs wudu before the actual prayer time, like for Eid prayer? Is that wudu valid for the subsequent obligatory prayer? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement. This lesson presents the two views and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1

The scenario: an excused person performs wudu for an optional prayer (Eid) before the time of the next obligatory prayer (Dhuhr). The question: can they use that same wudu for Dhuhr?
2

They hold that the wudu is valid for Dhuhr and is not invalidated merely by the entry of the new prayer time.
They allowed the wudu to be performed before the time.

Abu Yusuf holds that the wudu is invalidated upon entering the new prayer time. He does not allow the wudu to carry over.
This view is also transmitted from him.
4

The text declares the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad as 'sahih' (correct) and references al-Hidayah.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
Understand the rationale behind the majority view
The commentary explains that the prayer time acts as a barrier to the appearance of the underlying impurity. While the time is ongoing, the impurity is not considered effective; wudu remains valid. If wudu is performed during a pure gap before the time, it remains valid because the time (which would later act as barrier) has not yet started? Rubbing Dry Impurities from Khuffs and Dried Mani
Specifies rulings for removing dry impurity from leather socks and dried mani from garments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which rubbing (fark) suffices for purifying a khuff from dry impurity and for purifying dried mani from a garment, and identify when washing is required.

Core Idea
Rubbing suffices for dry impurities on hard surfaces and for dried semen – a Hanafi concession.

In the previous lessons, we learned about different types of impurities and general methods of purification. This passage addresses two specific scenarios where rubbing (fark) is accepted as a valid purification method, providing ease for those dealing with dry impurities on items like leather socks (khuff) and dried semen on clothing. The rulings are based on the nature of the material and the state of the impurity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage covers two distinct rulings: (1) removing a dry, solid impurity from a khuff or similar hard surface by rubbing on the ground; (2) removing dried mani (semen) from a garment or body by rubbing.
Each case has its own conditions and evidence.
2
Analyze the condition for khuff
For the khuff, the condition is that the impurity must have a visible mass (jirm) after drying, and it must be dry. If wet, washing is required. The rationale is that leather is hard and does not absorb deeply; the dry mass can be scraped away.
This applies to all similar hard surfaces like sandals (na'l).
3
Analyze the condition for mani
For mani, the condition is that it must be dry. If wet, washing is obligatory. When dry on a garment (even if new or lined) or on the body, rubbing suffices. The proof is the Prophet's instruction to Aisha (hadith).
Mani is classified as a heavy impurity (najasah mughallazah) in the Hanafi school.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that mani is pure (not najis) and thus no purification is needed. The Hanafi position is that it is najis but rubbing suffices when dry. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The practical impact is on validity of prayer: a Shafi'i may pray with semen on clothes, a Hanafi must remove it by washing or rubbing.
5
Apply the ruling to practice
If you have dry mud with visible solids on your leather boots, you can rub it off on the ground. If you have dried semen on your trousers, you can rub it off without needing to wash. But if the impurity is wet, you must wash.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Purification of Earth by Drying
Ruling on earth becoming pure when it dries and traces vanish

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on purification of earth by drying, distinguish between taharah and tahuriyyah, and apply the ruling to similar cases (e.g., plants and trees).

Core Idea
Earth that dries clean is pure for prayer, but not purifying enough for tayammum.

Have you ever wondered if you can pray on a spot of earth that became impure and then dried? The Hanafi school provides a detailed answer that distinguishes between two key concepts: taharah (purity) and tahuriyyah (purifying quality). This lesson unpacks the ruling from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar and Al-Maydani's commentary, showing you how the school derives a practical rule for prayer and tayammum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is the purification of earth after it has been contaminated by najasah (impurity). What happens when it dries and the traces vanish? The text gives two rulings: prayer on that spot is permissible, but tayammum (dry ablution) using that earth is not.
The ruling is split: one for prayer, one for tayammum.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The author, quoting Al-Jawhara, states that the restriction to the sun is not a condition; drying in the shade with disappearance of traces suffices. The rational proof is the distinction between taharah and tahuriyyah: for prayer, only taharah is needed; for tayammum, tahuriyyah is required.
This is a rational (aqli) proof, not a direct Quranic or hadith text.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal link)
The link is that once the traces (color, taste, smell) vanish, the substance of najasah is gone, making the earth tahir (pure). However, tayammum requires earth that is inherently purifying (tahir fi nafsihi wa mutahhir li ghayrihi). Once the earth has been contaminated, even if it becomes pure, it loses its original tahuriyyah.
Tahuriyyah is a quality that earth possesses as a creation; najasah removes it permanently according to this view.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly disagreement)
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i, Maliki) generally do not accept purification by drying alone; they require washing with water or removal of the contaminated soil. The Hanafi school holds that drying with trace elimination suffices for taharah.
This ikhtilaf stems from different definitions of 'removal of najasah'.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih and application
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position in the Hanafi school is that prayer is valid on such earth, but tayammum is not. This ruling extends to other items fixed to the ground like plants and trees.
The authority is from Zahir al-Riwayah via Al-Jawhara.

(And its trace disappeared – trace meaning color, taste, and smell: prayer is permissible on that spot, but tayammum from it is not permissible) because for prayer, purity (taharah) is required, and for tayammum, purifying quality (tahuriyyah) is required. And the same ruling applies to other things laid down, such as plants, grass, and trees standing in the earth, they become pure by drying.
Source pages p. 102

Commentary
This passage explains the Hanafi ruling on purifying earth that has been contaminated by najasah. Al-Maydani quotes from Al-Jawhara that the sun is not a necessary condition; drying in the shade with the disappearance of traces (color, taste, smell) suffices. The ruling: prayer is valid on that spot, but tayammum (dry ablution) cannot be performed from that earth because tayammum requires earth that is essentially purifying (tahur), not merely pure (tahir). The reasoning: for prayer, the ground need only be free from filth (taharah), but for tayammum, the earth must be clean enough to be used as a purifying agent (tahuriyyah). This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh. The same rule applies to other items attached to the earth, like plants and trees; they become pure by drying.

Key Terms
طهورية (tahuriyyah): The quality of being purifying (mutahhir), necessary for an object to be used for tayammum.

Defining Heavy Impurities Excused in Small Amounts
Listing heavy impurities and their excused status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of heavy impurities (najasa mughallaza) that are excused in small quantities according to the Hanafi school and state the ruling for each.

Core Idea
Small amounts of blood, urine, feces, and wine are excused in prayer, but only if from non-eaten meat sources.

This lesson clarifies which impurities the Hanafi school considers 'heavy' and thus subject to stricter rules. Understanding this distinction is essential for knowing when purification is required and when small amounts are overlooked. The passage lists the categories and sets the foundation for the excused amount ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category
Recognize that the passage is about 'heavy impurity' (najasa mughallaza) and that the Hanafi school distinguishes it from light impurity.
Heavy impurities require special attention; small amounts are excused, but larger amounts invalidate prayer.
2
List the specific items
From the text, extract the four items: blood, urine (from animals whose meat is not eaten, including human infants not yet eating solid food), feces, and wine.
Note the condition 'from non-eaten meat' applies to urine; for blood, it is also implied.
3
Determine the ruling
Understand that these items are inherently najis (impure), but small amounts (less than a dirham) are excused. The prayer remains valid, though it is makruh tahrimi to pray with such impurity intentionally.
The exact excused amount is not in this passage but is established elsewhere in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Analyze the condition
Focus on the phrase 'من غير مأكول اللحم' which restricts urine (and by analogy blood) to sources whose meat is not eaten. This excludes urine from halal animals (e.g., sheep, cow) which is light impurity.
The exception for a small child who has not eaten solid food is a special case.
5
Contrast with other impurities
Compare with light impurities (najasa khafifa) like urine of halal animals, which have different thresholds. This helps contextualize the heavy category.
Other schools may classify differently; this is the Hanafi view.
