Obligations of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Dependents and Disputes
Explains categories for fitra payment with conditions and intra-Hanafi disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the persons for whom fitrah is obligatory, apply the Hanafi rule based on the cause (sabab) of authority and maintenance, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi disputes regarding affluent children and jointly owned slaves.

Core Idea
Fitra is wajib for every head you support and have authority over.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains the scope of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It details whom you must pay for, whom you are not required to pay for, and the disputes within the Hanafi school regarding children who possess wealth and slaves jointly owned by two partners. The reasoning is grounded in the cause (sabab) of 'a head that one supports and has authority over.' Understanding these rules ensures the obligation is fulfilled correctly according to the Hanafi madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and cause
State that fitra is wajib for oneself, minor children (if poor), and slaves for service. The cause is that they are under one's authority and maintenance.
2
List those for whom fitra is NOT obligatory
Identify categories: wife, adult children, mukatab, trade slaves, and jointly owned slaves. Note exceptions like mudabbar and umm walad.
Check the reasoning: lack of guardianship or combination with zakat.
3
Analyze the first dispute: children with wealth
Compare Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (pay from child's wealth) vs. Muhammad (father pays). Understand the cause argument.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
4
Analyze the second dispute: jointly owned slaves
Contrast Abu Hanifa (no fitra due to deficient cause) with Sahibayn (pay for whole heads). Note the relied-upon position.
Later authorities prefer Sahibayn's view.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains the obligations of Sadaqat al-Fitr (often called Zakat al-Fitr). The author, al-Maydani, begins by stating the general rule: one must pay fitra for oneself, for one's minor children (who are poor), and for one's slaves who are kept for service. The reason (sabab) is that these are dependents under one's authority and maintenance. The passage then clarifies that if minor children or insane adults have their own wealth, the Hanafi school (according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) requires payment from their own wealth, while Imam Muhammad says the father pays from his own wealth. The author notes that al-Hidayah and later scholars like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi prefer the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, and this is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position. Next, the text lists those for whom fitra is not obligatory: the wife (the husband is not required to pay for her), adult children (even if they live with him), the mukatab (a slave under contract of manumission, as he is not a dependent), and slaves kept for trade (because zakat is already due on them as trade goods, and fitra does not combine with zakat). An exception is made for the mudabbar (a slave to be freed after the master's death) and umm walad (a slave who bears her master's child) because the master retains guardianship over them. Finally, the passage addresses a dispute about a slave jointly owned by two partners: Abu Hanifa holds that no fitra is due from either partner because the authority and maintenance are incomplete in each; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that each must pay fitra for the number of whole slaves they own, ignoring fractional shares. The commentary emphasizes that the relied-upon position is that of the Sahibayn, as supported by al-Hidayah and later authorities. Throughout, the reasoning is based on the cause (sabab) of 'a head that one supports and has authority over,' which defines who must be covered by fitra.

Key Terms
فطرة (fitrah): Zakat al-Fitr, a compulsory charity given at the end of Ramadan, also called Sadaqat al-Fitr.
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has entered into a contract of manumission (kitabah) with his master, agreeing to pay a sum for freedom.
مدبَّر (mudabbar): A slave whom the master has declared will be freed upon the master's death (tadbir).
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has given birth to a child by her master, and thus she becomes free upon the master's death.

Exceptions to Fitrah Obligation: Children & Slaves
Explains exceptions to fitrah obligation using talil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on whom Sadaqat al-Fitr is obligatory and explain the reasoning based on guardianship and maintenance.

Core Idea
Fitrah is only obligatory for those under complete guardianship and maintenance — incomplete ownership or care lifts the obligation.

In this lesson, we explore the exceptions to the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. The Hanafi school carefully defines whom the payer must include, based on whether he has full guardianship (wilaya) and bears the entire maintenance (munna). Adult children, mukatab slaves, trade slaves, and jointly owned slaves are excluded because these conditions are missing. We will also examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on joint slaves and the rule for non-Muslim slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the excluded categories
List the persons for whom the master/payer is NOT required to pay fitrah: major children, mukatab, slaves for trade, and jointly owned slaves.
The matn states these exceptions upfront.
2
Understand the legal rationale (talil)
For each exclusion, the commentary gives a reason: lack of guardianship (wilaya) or deficiency of maintenance (munna). For major children, guardianship is absent; for mukatab, both guardianship and the slave's poverty; for trade slaves, zakat on their value replaces fitrah; for joint slaves, each partner lacks full guardianship and full maintenance.
The reasoning is consistent: fitrah requires complete responsibility.
3
Recognize the exceptional cases where fitrah IS due
The mudabbar and umm walad are still under full guardianship, so fitrah is due. Also, a Muslim master must pay fitrah for his non-Muslim slave.
The cause (sabab) for obligation is the existence of the slave, and the master is legally capable.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on joint slaves
Abu Hanifa holds that no fitrah is due from any partner. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) hold that each partner pays for the number of whole slaves he owns, excluding fractional shares. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) view is Abu Hanifa's, with Abu Yusuf agreeing.
The dispute revolves around the completeness of ownership and maintenance.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri expounds the rulings on whom Sadaqat al-Fitr is obligatory. The matn states that fitrah is not due for major children, even if they live with the father, because the father lacks guardianship over them. However, the commentary adds that if he pays without their permission, it suffices by istihsan, as custom grants implied permission. Similarly, fitrah is not due for a mukatab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom) because the master lacks full ownership, nor does the mukatab pay for himself due to his poverty. For a mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death) and umm al-walad (slave mother of the master's child), the master's guardianship is complete, so he pays. Slaves owned for trade are exempt because zakat is due on their value, and zakat and fitrah do not combine. For a slave jointly owned by two partners, neither pays because each lacks complete guardianship and the full burden of maintenance. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa says no fitrah on either partner, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say each partner pays fitrah for the number of whole slaves he owns, ignoring fractional shares. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) is the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, as stated in the footnote. Finally, a Muslim master must pay fitrah for his non-Muslim slave because the cause (the slave's existence) is present and the master is legally obliged.

أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master.
شقاص (ashqāṣ): Plural of shiqs, meaning shares or portions; used here to refer to fractional ownership shares.

Measurement of Raisins for Sadaqat al-Fitr: Half or Full Sa'?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the measurement of raisins for Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two narrations regarding the required amount of raisins for Sadaqat al-Fitr, identify the mufta bihi, and explain the practical recommendation.

Core Idea
Raisins for fitrah: half a sa' or one sa'? The relied-upon fatwa is one sa'.

This passage from al-Lubab commentary on al-Hidayah addresses the measurement of Sadaqat al-Fitr for raisins. While the base text states that fitrah is half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates, raisins, or barley, a note reveals an internal dispute. We will examine the two narrations from Abu Hanifa and the views of the Sahibayn, and see how later Hanafi authorities settled the issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic rule
The matn specifies fitrah as half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates, raisins, or barley. This is the default rule for each item.
Wheat is an exception; all other listed items require a full sa'.
2
Locate the dispute on raisins
The commentary and footnote show two narrations from Abu Hanifa: half sa' (from al-Jami' al-Saghir) and one sa' (also attributed to Abu Hanifa and the view of Sahibayn).
Opposing view: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say raisins are like barley, requiring a full sa'.
3
Understand the later tarjih
Although al-Hidayah preferred the half sa' narration, later authorities (al-Bahansi, al-Burhan, Ibn Abidin) adopted the one sa' as the fatwa. A compromise is suggested: give half sa' only if its value equals half a sa' of wheat.
Mufta bihi: one sa'. But value consideration is a cautious option.
4
Apply the ruling
When giving fitrah, normally give one sa' of raisins. If giving half sa', ensure it is high-quality such that its market value equals that of half a sa' of wheat.
This balances the narrations and ensures the poor receive adequate value.

Commentary
This passage from al-Hidayah, commented upon in al-Lubab, specifies the quantity of Sadaqat al-Fitr. The matn states that fitrah is half a sa' of wheat, or one sa' of dates, raisins, or barley. The commentary clarifies that wheat includes its flour and sawiq. For raisins, however, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute. Zakat al-Fitr is a wajib charity due at the end of Ramadan. But its obligation is tied to a specific time: the dawn of the day of Eid. In this lesson, we examine a ruling from the Hanafi school that clarifies who is and is not obligated to pay fitrah, based on the presence or absence of the sabab (cause) at that critical moment.

The key is the presence of the person as a Muslim with nisab at the moment of dawn on Eid day.

Key Terms
صدقة الفطر (sadaqat al-fitr): An obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan before the Eid prayer.
صوم (sawm): Fasting, which is abstaining from eating, drinking, and marital relations from dawn to sunset with intention.
اعتكاف (i'tikaf): Seclusion in a mosque for worship, especially during the last ten days of Ramadan.
حج (hajj): Pilgrimage to Mecca at a specified time with specific rituals.
