Obligatory Ghusl: Causes and Conditions
Lists the obligatory causes of ghusl with detailed condition for ikhtitan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three causes of obligatory ghusl (ikhtitan, hayd, nifas) and state the condition that triggers each, with explanation of the rationale for ikhtitan without ejaculation.

Core Idea
Concealment of the glans during intercourse, even without ejaculation, necessitates ghusl; ghusl after menses and postpartum is only valid once bleeding ceases.

This lesson continues from the previous on the causes of ghusl. Here we cover the remaining obligatory causes: ikhtitan (the meeting of circumcised parts) with its precise condition of ghaybubat al-hashafah, and the two blood-related causes: hayd and nifas. Understanding these causes ensures that you perform ghusl at the correct times.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three causes
List the three causes: ikhtitan (intercourse), hayd (menstruation), and nifas (postpartum bleeding).
Ikhtitan is detailed with a condition; hayd and nifas are straightforward.
2
Analyze the condition for ikhtitan
Recognize that the condition is ghaybubat al-hashafah (concealment of the glans) in the front or rear, even without ejaculation. The commentary clarifies that insertion in the rear also necessitates ghusl.
The phrase 'meeting of circumcised parts' is imprecise for anal intercourse.
3
Understand the rationale without ejaculation
Intercourse is a cause for ejaculation, and since it is hidden, the law treats the act itself as the cause of janabah.
This is based on 'kamaal al-sababiyya' (completeness of causation).
4
Apply the rule for severed glans
If the glans is severed, ghusl is obligatory by inserting the equivalent length of the organ.
This is an exception to the condition of glans concealment.
5
Identify conditions for hayd and nifas
Ghusl is obligatory after the blood stops completely; as long as bleeding continues, ghusl is not valid.
This is a condition for the validity of ghusl, not just its obligation.

Ghusl on these occasions is for cleanliness and honor of the specific worship or day.

Definition of Iltiqa' al-Khitanayn and Obligation of Ghusl
Defines iltiqa' al-khitanayn and its ruling on ghusl without ejaculation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of iltiqa' al-khitanayn and explain why it obligates ghusl even without ejaculation.

Core Idea
The meeting of circumcised parts alone, without emission, obligates ghusl because it is the manifest cause of ejaculation.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri defines one of the key causes of obligatory ghusl: the meeting of the two circumcised parts (iltiqa' al-khitanayn). The commentator clarifies that the defining criterion is the disappearance of the glans into an orifice — whether front or back. Understanding this definition is essential because it determines when ghusl becomes obligatory in various scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the meeting of circumcised parts. The ruling is that ghusl becomes fard (obligatory) upon its occurrence.
The ruling applies even without ejaculation.
2
Locate the definition
The author defines iltiqa' al-khitanayn as alignment (muḥādhātu humā) with disappearance of the glans (ghaybūbat al-ḥashafah).
‘Khitan’ refers to the place of cutting on the male and female organs.
3
Analyze the expansion
The commentator from al-Jawhara suggests a more comprehensive definition: disappearance of the glans in the front or back passage, because anal intercourse also obligates ghusl without meeting of two circumcised parts.
This expansion includes both orifices and avoids the literal ‘two circumcised parts’ for anal cases.
4
Understand the causality (sababiyyah)
The meeting is a cause for ghusl because it normally leads to ejaculation, which is hidden; thus the manifest cause stands in for the hidden effect.
This is a principle of usul: the sabab (cause) triggers the hukm (obligation).
5
Note the exceptional case
If the glans is cut off, ghusl is obligatory by inserting the equivalent length from the remaining organ.
The measure is the typical length of the glans, not the exact original.

When Ghusl is Valid After Hayd and Nifas
States the condition for valid ghusl after hayd/nifas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for the validity of ghusl after hayd/nifas and explain why ghusl is not valid while bleeding continues.

Core Idea
Ghusl is only valid after bleeding stops completely; performing it during hayd/nifas does not lift impurity.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies a crucial temporal condition: the obligation and validity of ghusl for menstruation and postpartum bleeding depend on the complete cessation of the blood flow. Understanding this prevents the common mistake of performing ghusl prematurely, which would leave one in a state of impurity and render subsequent acts of worship invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage discusses ghusl after hayd (menstruation) and nifas (postpartum bleeding). The act is ghusl, and the ruling is that it is obligatory (fard 'ayn) after the bleeding stops.
2
Identify the condition for validity
The text explicitly states that as long as the blood remains (i.e., continues to flow), ghusl is not valid. Therefore, the condition for a valid ghusl is the complete cessation of bleeding.
3
Understand the consequence of premature ghusl
If you perform ghusl while bleeding continues, the ghusl does not remove the state of impurity (hadath). The person remains in a state of ritual impurity and must repeat the ghusl once bleeding stops.
4
Note the ruling's epistemological weight
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah (core Hanafi texts) and is agreed upon by the school. It is a fundamental rule in taharah.

Sunnah Ghusl for Friday, Eid, Ihram, and Arafah: The Purpose Dispute
Lists sunnah ghusl occasions and discusses the purpose of Friday ghusl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the purpose of Friday ghusl and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
The Friday ghusl is sunnah but its purpose—prayer or day—affects whether a break in wudu cancels its reward.

The Hanafi school prescribes sunnah ghusl for Friday and other occasions. However, a nuanced intra-madhhab dispute exists: is this ghusl for the prayer or for the day? The answer determines whether performing wudu after ghusl and before prayer fulfills the sunnah. This lesson examines the dispute between Abu Yusuf and Hasan ibn Ziyad, and the later preferred view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common ruling
The text states that the Prophet prescribed ghusl for Friday, the two Eids, Ihram, and Arafah. The Hanafi school considers these sunnah muakkadah, not wajib.
Some earlier texts called them mustahabb, but the relied-upon view is sunnah muakkadah.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and his proof
Abu Yusuf holds that the Friday ghusl is for the prayer (al-salât). His reasoning: the prayer has greater virtue than the time, and purification is specifically linked to the prayer.
This view is attributed to Abu Yusuf and considered sound in earlier sources.
3
State Hasan ibn Ziyad's position and proof
Hasan ibn Ziyad (and also Imam Muhammad and another narration from Abu Yusuf) holds that the ghusl is for the day (al-yawm) itself, to honor Friday. The wisdom is cleanliness and removing odor, not ritual purity.
This view is supported by later authorities like Ibn Abidin.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If one performs ghusl, then has an event (e.g., passes wind) and does wudu only for prayer: according to Abu Yusuf, the sunnah of Friday ghusl is not fulfilled; according to Hasan, it is fulfilled because the ghusl was for the day.
The difference hinges on whether the ghusl is tied to prayer time or to the entire day.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position
Ibn Abidin and al-Nabulsi favored Hasan ibn Ziyad's view, that it is for cleanliness and the day, so the sunnah is fulfilled even if wudu is needed later.
This is the operative position for fatwa in the later Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Five water sources—rain, valley, spring, well, sea—are all valid if unchanged.

Purification (taharah) is essential for many acts of worship, and its validity depends on using the correct water. The Hanafi school, through the matn of al-Quduri, specifies five water sources that are permissible. This lesson helps you memorize these sources and understand the underlying condition of muṭlaq water.

All five are valid only if they remain muṭlaq (absolute) water. Muṭlaq water is water that has not been changed by an impurity or by a pure substance such that its color, taste, or smell is altered.

This passage from al-Lubab establishes a clear rule: purification with liquids pressed from trees and fruits is not valid. Core Idea
Water is NOT purifying if a dominant substance changes its nature or gives it a new name.

Water mixed with pure substances remains valid for wudu as long as its fluidity dominates and it is still called water.

Guided Path

This shows the importance of distinguishing between a change in properties and a loss of identity.
4

The text cites al-Mustasfā and al-Jawharah to affirm that even if all three descriptions change, wudu is valid provided the water remains fluid and retains the name 'water'.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.
5
Apply the rule to examples
Examples include rainwater (mā' al-madd) mixed with mud and leaves – valid as long as wateriness dominates; water mixed with potash, soap, or saffron – valid if fluid; but if it becomes thick or is called 'soap water', it becomes invalid.
The key is to assess fluidity and naming, not just the degree of change.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Flowing water remains pure unless a trace (taste, color, smell) of impurity appears.

In daily life, we often use tap water, streams, or bathhouse basins. Impurities might fall into them. How should we determine if the water is still pure? This passage from al-Lubab explains the Hanafi position: flowing water is treated leniently because its movement prevents impurity from settling. The ruling is based on observable traces and the force of the flow.

Guided Path

The 'force' of water refers to its current strength.

A bathhouse (hammam) basin with water flowing from above and people drawing water is treated like flowing water. Even if a dirty bowl or hand is inserted, the water does not become impure unless a trace is visible.
This is an expansion by analogy (ilhaq).
5

In all cases, the decisive factor is the appearance of taste, color, or smell of impurity. If none are present, the water is pure.
This is agreed upon across schools.

Identify the act and its ruling

This highlights the importance of the medium of contact.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, by al-Maidani, is a commentary expanding the rulings on well purification. The author begins by establishing that the size of the animal that falls into a well does not affect the ruling—whether small or large, the same number of buckets must be drawn. This is because the impurity spreads throughout the water particles. The evidence for the method of determining the number of buckets is that the relevant hadith reports came in general terms (mutlaq), so they are interpreted according to the most common and predominant practice, which is the medium bucket used in the majority of lands. However, al-Hidayah mentions that the consideration is the bucket of that particular well, and another view says a bucket that holds one sa'. The author notes that many have chosen the common bucket view. If a huge bucket is used, it counts as its equivalent in medium buckets, e.g., if it holds twenty medium buckets, it counts as twenty. Then the passage turns to the case of a spring well (birk ma'in) whose water flows from below and cannot be emptied. For such a well, since the water is continuously replenished, the obligation is to remove the amount of water that was in the well at the start of the purification process. Abu Yusuf's method is to dig a pit of the same volume as the well's water cavity and pour the drawn water into it; when the pit is filled, all the original water has been removed. Muhammad al-Shaybani, in a riwaya (transmission) from al-Nawadir, gives a fatwa of 200 to 300 buckets for the wells of Baghdad, due to their abundant water from the Tigris, with the third hundred being commendable (mandub). The passage thus presents two Hanafi positions on spring wells, with Muhammad's being a fatwa for a specific context.
