Exemptions from Friday Prayer Obligation
List of who is not required to attend Friday prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories exempted from Friday prayer and state the reason for each exemption.

Core Idea
Friday prayer is not obligatory on the traveler, woman, sick, and caregiver — each exempted due to hardship or prohibition.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies that while Friday prayer is fard 'ayn on every free, adult, male resident, certain individuals are exempted. Understanding these exemptions is crucial for correctly applying the obligation in various circumstances. The text lists four categories with brief reasons.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the four exempted categories
Learn the list: traveler, woman, sick person, and caregiver (when the patient would be left unattended).
The caregiver is exempted only if the patient needs care.
2
Understand the rationale for each
Traveler: hardship; Woman: prohibited from going out; Sick: inability; Caregiver: necessity of care.
These reasons are derived from the talil in the text.
3
Distinguish exemption from prohibition
These individuals may still attend Friday prayer; it is simply not obligatory. Attending is valid and rewarded.
Do not assume they cannot attend.
4
Apply the exemption correctly
Recognize that a healthy resident man is not exempted. If a person falls into one of the four categories, they may pray Zuhr instead.
The exemption applies when the condition (e.g., travel) exists at the time of Friday prayer.

Commentary
This passage enumerates four categories of people for whom Friday prayer is not obligatory, although they may attend if they wish. First, a traveler (musāfir) is exempted due to the hardship of travel. Second, a woman is exempted because she is prohibited from leaving her home for such gatherings, though if she attends, it is valid. Third, a sick person (marīḍ) who cannot go to the mosque or fears aggravated illness is excused. Fourth, the caregiver (mumarriḍ) of a patient is also exempted if the patient would be left without care. The bases for these exemptions are either hardship or prohibition. It is important to note that these exemptions do not apply if the person in question still attends; the reward remains. The Hanafi school holds that Friday prayer is fard 'ayn upon every free, adult, male Muslim who is a resident and has no excuse.

مريض (marīḍ): A sick person who cannot go to the mosque without fear of worsening his condition.

Recitation in Friday Prayer: Jahr and No Specific Surah
Ruling on recitation aloud and permissibility of any surah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the Imam recites aloud (jahr) in the two rak'ahs of Friday prayer and identify that no specific surah is obligatory, while distinguishing between wajib (jahr) and sunnah (recommended surahs).

Core Idea
Imam must recite aloud in Jumu'ah; choice of surah is free, though some are sunnah.

Friday prayer has specific regulations for the Imam's recitation. This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri establishes two key rulings: the Imam recites audibly in both rak'ahs, a practice based on continuous transmission; and there is no requirement to recite any particular surah. While commentators recommend specific surahs like al-Jumu'ah and al-Munafiqun, or al-A'la and al-Ghashiyah, these are merely recommended and not obligatory. The passage also warns against consistently reciting the same surahs to avoid giving the impression that they are mandatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage states: (1) The Imam recites aloud in both rak'ahs, (2) No specific surah is required. Understand that these are separate rulings: one about audibility, one about choice of surah.
Jahr is a separate issue from surah selection.
2
Understand the basis for jahr
The author says 'because it is transmitted' (لأنه المتوارث). This refers to the continuous practice of the Prophet (ﷺ) and the early Muslims. This transmission makes jahr a wajib act for the Imam.
Wajib here means obligatory, based on mutawatir practice.
3
Analyze the expansion on surahs
The commentary quotes Sharh al-Tahawi recommending Surah al-Jumu'ah and al-Munafiqun, and al-Zahidi recommending Surah al-A'la and al-Ghashiyah. Note that these are recommendations, not requirements.
Multiple authorities mention different surahs, showing flexibility.
4
Understand the caution against constancy
Al-Bahr adds that the Imam should not always recite the same surahs to prevent the common people from thinking they are obligatory. This reflects a principle of avoiding practices that could be misconstrued as fard.
This is a fiqhi consideration to protect the clarity of the religion.

Who is Exempt from Friday Prayer
List of exempted persons for Friday prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the nine categories exempt from Friday prayer and explain the rationale for each exemption.

Core Idea
Jumu'ah is wajib on every free, adult, resident male Muslim—but the Shari'ah exempts those facing genuine hardship or preoccupation.

Friday prayer is a weekly obligation with great merit. Yet, not everyone is required to attend. The Hanafi school provides a clear list of those excused, based on the principle of removing hardship. Understanding these exemptions ensures we neither burden the excused nor neglect the obligation when able.

Guided Path
1
State the general obligation
Identify who is obligated for Friday prayer: free, adult, resident, healthy male. Then note that the passage lists those who are excused.
The base ruling is: Jumu'ah is fard 'ayn on those meeting conditions.
2
List the exempt categories
Read the passage and note each category: traveler, woman, sick, caretaker, slave, chronically ill (zamin), blind, one in fear, one excused due to weather.
These are nine categories; some have sub-discussions.
3
Understand the rationale
For each category, the author gives a reason: traveler faces hardship, woman is prohibited from going out, sick is unable, caretaker prevents harm, slave is occupied, etc.
The common theme is raf' al-haraj (removal of hardship).
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on the blind
Note the ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa says the blind is always exempt; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say if a guide is available, Jumu'ah becomes wajib. The mufta bihi is the view of the Sahibayn.
This shows that exemption depends on whether hardship exists.
5
Apply to modern scenarios
Consider a person with a chronic illness, a woman with no mahram, or a person fearing political unrest. Determine if the exemption applies.
Always check if the hardship or excuse is valid in Shari'ah.

Exemptions from Friday Prayer and Rulings on Attendance and Leading
Lists exemptions, then rulings on attendance and leading.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six categories exempt from Friday prayer in the Hanafi school, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the blind person, and apply the ruling that attendance and leading by exempt individuals are valid and fulfil the obligation.

Core Idea
Exemption from Friday prayer is a concession; attendance and leading are valid and become obligatory upon participating.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri lists who is excused from Friday prayer and then explains the rulings if such individuals attend or lead. Understanding these exemptions clarifies the boundaries of the obligation and the wisdom of concessions in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six exempt categories
List them: slave, blind, chronically ill (zamin), one in fear (kha'if), one excused by severe weather, and rural dweller (qarawi).
Each category has a specific rationale.
2
Understand the rationale for each exemption
Read the author's talil: slave occupied with master, blind cannot see, chronically ill cannot stand, fear of harm, hardship of weather, rural dweller not of the town.
The reasons are based on hardship or occupation.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on the blind
Abu Hanifa exempts absolutely; the Sahibayn obligate if a guide (volunteer or paid) is available. Later fatwa follows the Sahibayn.
This affects whether the blind person must attend if he can find a guide.
4
Analyze the ruling if exempt individuals attend
If they attend and pray with the people, it suffices for the obligatory prayer of the time. They are like a traveler who chooses to fast.
Attendance removes the concession and makes the prayer obligatory for them.
5
Analyze the ruling on leading
It is permissible for a traveler, slave, sick person, and similar exempt males (except women) to lead Friday prayer. Their leading is valid and the prayer is fard for them.
Leading is allowed; the imam's prayer counts as his obligation.

Attendance by a non-obligee suffices; praying Dhuhr alone without excuse is sinful but valid.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of al-Quduri regarding Friday prayer. This is because by attending they assume the hardship, analogous to a traveler who fasts in Ramadan. The commentary then states it is permissible for such individuals to lead the Friday prayer, except a woman. The reason is that the exemption from obligation on them is a concession to remove hardship; if they choose to attend, the prayer becomes obligatory for them. Such an act is disliked in the sense of prohibition (makruh tahrimi), because it constitutes abandoning a definitive obligatory duty (al-fard al-qat'i) by consensus of the scholars. Nevertheless, his Dhuhr prayer is valid (ja'iz) but with a suspended validity (mawquf) as will be explained later. Obligation to Attend Friday Prayer upon the First Adhan
Ruling statement on obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when the obligation to leave trade and attend Friday prayer becomes binding, and identify which adhan is relied upon (mufta bihi).

Core Idea
The first adhan after zuhr makes it wajib to stop trade and proceed to the mosque with dignity.

In the Hanafi school, Friday prayer is preceded by two adhans. This lesson clarifies which adhan triggers the obligation to close business and go to the mosque. We also examine the intention behind the Qur'anic command to "proceed" and the proper posture of movement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger
The text states: 'When the muezzins give the first adhan on Friday, people leave selling and buying and proceed to Friday prayer.' Identify that this first adhan is the one after zuhr (zawal).
2
Distinguish between the two adhans
Historically, only one adhan existed when the imam ascended the minbar. A first adhan was later added for notification. Understand the difference and which one is operative for the obligation.
The more correct view (al-ashahh) is that the first adhan after zuhr is the one considered.
3
Apply the mufta bihi
According to Al-Hidaya, the first adhan after zuhr is the one that triggers the prohibition of selling and the obligation to attend. This is the relied-upon position.
Reason: it attains notification (ihsan al-i'lam).
4
Interpret the command 'sa'yu'
The text uses 'tawajjahu' (proceed) to indicate that the Qur'anic command 'sa'yu' means going with tranquility (sakinah) and dignity (waqar), not running.
This understanding is unique to the Hanafi school in emphasizing dignified movement.
5
Note the exception for qada'
During the khutbah, making up a missed prayer (qada') with order is allowed because it is necessary for the validity of Friday prayer itself.
This exception is for a person who has a missed prayer with a due order (tartib).

Time of Eid Prayer
Defines the valid time for Eid prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the valid time window for Eid prayer according to the Hanafi school and determine the consequence of praying outside that window.

Core Idea
Eid prayer time starts after sunrise by a spear's length and ends at zawal.

The time of Eid prayer is precisely defined in the Hanafi school: it begins when the sun has risen approximately one spear's length above the horizon and ends when the sun reaches its zenith (zawal). This lesson will help you understand why timing is essential for the validity of Eid prayer and what happens if the prayer is offered too early or too late.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal ruling for Eid prayer time
Read the passage carefully. It states that when prayer becomes permissible (i.e., after sunrise by a spear's length), the time for Eid prayer enters.
This is the start condition.
2
Determine the start time precisely
The passage uses the phrase 'irtifā‘ al-shams qadr ramḥ' — the sun's elevation equal to a spear's length. This is the Hanafi marker for the beginning of Eid prayer time.
Other schools may use a different measure (e.g., sunrise itself), but the Hanafi school requires this waiting period.
3
Determine the end time
The end time is defined as 'zawāl', the sun's decline from the zenith. Once the sun passes the meridian, the time for Eid prayer exits.
This is the same across all schools.
4
Understand the consequence of praying before time
If one prays before the proper time, the prayer does not count as Eid prayer. Instead, it is considered a forbidden (muharram) voluntary prayer because it is performed at a time when prayer is disliked.
The term 'muharram' here means that the act is sinful, not that the prayer is invalid in itself.
5
Understand the consequence of time exiting during prayer
If the sun reaches zawal while one is in the middle of the Eid prayer, the prayer becomes invalid because the time condition is no longer met.
This ruling emphasizes the strict time requirement for Eid prayer.

Timing of Takbir al-Tashriq: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Expansion on end time with intra-Hanafi disagreement and tarjih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the end time of Takbir al-Tashriq, identify the mufta bihi position, and explain the practical consequence in the number of prayers.

Core Idea
Takbir al-Tashriq: Abu Hanifa counts 8 prayers; the Sahibayn count 23; the fatwa follows the Sahibayn.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement on when the Takbir al-Tashriq ends. While all agree it begins after Fajr on the day of 'Arafah, Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differ on the final prayer. The passage also resolves a linguistic issue about the term 'tashriq' and cites later authorities who settled the fatwa on the Sahibayn's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that Takbir al-Tashriq is a wajib recitation after fard prayers. Note the agreed starting point: after Fajr of 9th Dhul Hijjah.
This is wajib for those who prayed fard in congregation.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the takbir ends after 'Asr of Yawm al-Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah). Count the prayers: 5 on 9th + 3 on 10th = 8 prayers.
The days of Tashriq (11-13) have no takbir in his view.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the takbir continues until 'Asr of the last day of Tashriq (13th). Count: 5 on 9th + 5 on 10th + 5 on 11th + 5 on 12th + 3 on 13th = 23 prayers.
The phrase 'with inclusion of the end' means the last 'Asr is included.
4
Identify the tarjih mechanism
Later Hanafi authorities (Burhan al-Shari'ah, Sadr al-Shari'ah, author of al-Ikhtiyar, al-Ispijabi) explicitly state that the fatwa is on the Sahibayn's view. Thus the mufta bihi is 23 prayers.
This shows that not every view of the Imam is automatically the operative ruling.
5
Address the linguistic point
The term 'tashriq' is ambiguous; it can mean 'drying meat' or 'takbir'. The idafah (construct) is explanatory: 'the takbir which is tashriq'.
This rebuts the objection that Abu Hanifa's view would make the name 'tashriq' irrelevant.

Eclipse Prayer: Structure and Recitation Dispute
Ruling for eclipse prayer with intra-Hanafi recitation dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for salat al-kusuf and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on silent vs. audible recitation.

Core Idea
Eclipse prayer is like nafl in structure, but the recitation mode splits the Hanafi school.

The eclipse of the sun is a moment of natural wonder and awe. The Prophet ﷺ taught us to turn to Allah in prayer at such times. The Hanafi school describes this prayer as two rak'ahs similar to the supererogatory (nafl) prayer in form, but with prolonged recitation. However, there is a notable difference within the school: should the imam recite aloud (jahr) or silently (ikhfa)? Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, held opposing views. This lesson examines both positions and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Salat al-kusuf is the prayer performed when a solar eclipse occurs. According to the text, the imam leads two rak'ahs with a single ruku' per rak'ah, resembling the nafl prayer. The ruling is sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah) according to the majority of Hanafis.
A minority view within the school held it to be wajib, but the relied-upon position is sunnah.
2
Describe the structure
The prayer has no adhan, iqamah, or khutbah. It has two rak'ahs, each with one ruku'. The recitation is prolonged, as are the bowing and prostration.
This distinguishes it from the Shafi'i view where each rak'ah has two ruku'.
3
State the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa held that the imam recites silently (ikhfa), just like a daytime nafl prayer. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) held that the imam recites aloud (jahr), similar to a congregational prayer with a large gathering.
The text does not resolve which view is preferred (mufta bihi), but later Hanafi works often choose jahr for night eclipses and ikhfa for solar eclipses (daytime).
4
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
Abu Hanifa's qiyas (analogy) is to daytime nafl prayers, where recitation is always silent because the worshipper is alone with his Lord. The Sahibayn's qiyas is to other congregational prayers (like Friday or Eid) where audible recitation suits the occasion and benefits the attendees.
This difference in analogy is the root of the ikhtilaf.
5
Identify the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that in a solar eclipse prayer, if the imam recites aloud, some followers may consider it valid only according to the Sahibayn; if he recites silently, it follows Abu Hanifa. A mufti would likely advise the imam to follow the prevalent custom or the congregation's expectation.
No single position is definitively chosen in this passage.

The Prayer for Rain: Rulings and Intra-Hanafi Disputes
States the Istisqa prayer rulings and disagreements among Hanafi imams.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rulings of Salat al-Istisqa according to the Hanafi school and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the Imam turning his cloak.

Core Idea
Imam's turning of cloak is sunnah according to Sahibayn, but Abu Hanifa disagreed; both views are valid within the school.

This lesson explores the rulings of the prayer for rain (Salat al-Istisqa) as presented in al-Quduri's matn and its commentary. It covers the format of the prayer, the sermon, and a key intra-Hanafi dispute: whether the Imam should turn his cloak. The hadith of the Prophet is cited as evidence, and the passage also clarifies that the congregation does not perform this act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its rules
State the structure of Istisqa prayer: two rak'ahs with audible recitation, sermon(s), qiblah supplication, and cloak turning by the Imam.
Eid prayer analogy is used for the format.
2
Locate the hadith proof
Find the dalil from Bukhari and Muslim for the Imam turning his cloak. The hadith describes the Prophet's action.
The hadith is the primary evidence for the Sahibayn.

Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Notice that Abu Hanifa holds that the Imam should NOT turn his cloak, while Muhammad and the Sahibayn say he should. This is a legitimate difference within the school.
The dispute is about whether turning is sunnah or not.
4
Trace the tarjih
Examine the two 'corrections' (tas'hihan): one favors Muhammad's view (turning) as mufta bihi, another favors Abu Hanifa's view (no turning). Different later scholars preferred each.

Observe that the congregation does not turn their cloaks, and understand the method of turning (square: top to bottom; round: right side to left).
