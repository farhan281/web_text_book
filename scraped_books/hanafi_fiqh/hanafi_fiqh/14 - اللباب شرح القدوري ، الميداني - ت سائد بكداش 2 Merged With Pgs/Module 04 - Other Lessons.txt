Immediate Recommended Acts Upon Death: Binding the Jaw and Closing the Eyes
Describes recommended actions immediately upon death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended actions upon death, identify who should perform them, and recite the prescribed supplication.

Core Idea
Upon death, respectfully bind the jaw, close the eyes, and remove the impure—all mustahabb, not fard.

This lesson covers the immediate sunan (recommended acts) after a person passes away. These actions show respect for the deceased and ease their transition. The Hanafi school emphasises that these are not obligatory but virtuous practices rooted in the Prophetic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commanded acts in the matn
The base text states: 'When someone dies: they should tie his jaw and close his eyes.' Recognize these as the primary recommendations.
The Arabic uses a passive command form (شَدُّوا, غَمَّضوا) indicating desirability.
2
Examine the commentary's procedural details
The sharh specifies binding the jaw with a bandage from below, tying it above the head, and closing the eyes. This clarifies the method.
The act is done 'تحسيناً له' (for beautification), signaling the hukm level.
3
Determine the hukm (legal status)
The phrase 'تحسيناً له' shows it is mustahabb (recommended), not wajib. The Hanafi school considers these acts sunnah.
Essential distinction: mustahabb omissions are not sinful.
4
Identify the performer and supplication
The gentlest family member should perform the acts and recite the prescribed du'a asking Allah to ease the deceased's affair.
The du'a includes 'بسم الله، وعلى ملة رسول الله' and other pleas.
5
Note the exclusion of impure persons
Menstruating women, postpartum women, and those in janabah should leave the room out of respect.
This is also sunnah, not a condition for validity.

The Hanafi Ruling on Talqin (Prompting the Deceased) in the Grave
Discusses the legitimacy of talqin and the differing opinions within the school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on talqin and identify the evidence from the hadith of al-Bara' ibn 'Azib that supports it.

Core Idea
Talqin is recommended because the deceased is alive in barzakh, as proven by authentic hadith.

This lesson examines the controversial practice of talqin – prompting the deceased in the grave with the shahadah. The Hanafi school holds it to be sanctioned, based on the belief that the deceased experiences a form of life in the barzakh, supported by the hadith of al-Bara' and other narrations. Understanding the proofs helps us appreciate how rules are derived from primary sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what 'talqin' is and state the Hanafi ruling from the text: it is 'mashru' (sanctioned/recommended).
The term 'mashru' in Hanafi fiqh corresponds to mustahabb (recommended).
2
Locate the dalil
Find the evidence cited: the hadith of al-Bara' ibn 'Azib regarding life in the grave, and the report of talqin from the Prophet (peace be upon him).
The hadith is in Sunan Abi Dawud and other collections.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the hadith proves the ruling: the life in the barzakh allows the deceased to benefit from talqin.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Recognize that some scholars disagree: some say talqin should not be done, others say it is neither commanded nor prohibited.
This ikhtilaf is within the Hanafi school as well as between schools.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The author of Al-Lubab favors the view of mashru'iyyah, supported by the evidence, making it the mufta bihi.
The relied-upon position is that talqin is recommended.

Hastening to Pay the Deceased's Debts
Ruling with proof on hastening debt payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hastening to pay the deceased's debts and explain the evidence from hadith, including the condition of intention.

Core Idea
A believer's soul is suspended by debt; hastening to pay it releases the soul and is strongly recommended.

In the Hanafi tradition, settling the debts of a deceased person is a matter of great importance. This lesson examines the ruling that it is recommended (yustahabb) to hasten in paying off the deceased's debts or obtaining a discharge. The proof is drawn from the hadith: 'The believer's soul is suspended due to his debt until it is paid off for him.' We will also explore how the Prophet's own practice and the role of intention shape this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The text states that it is 'yustahabb' (recommended) to hasten in paying the deceased's debts. Identify the linguistic nuance of 'yustahabb' in Hanafi fiqh, which places it between sunnah and mubah, but here treated as sunnah muakkadah due to emphasis. Note the evidence: the soul is suspended, implying a state of need.
The key term is 'mu'allaqa'—suspended or withheld.
2
Analyze the primary hadith proof
The hadith from Tirmidhi and Ibn Majah: 'The believer's soul is suspended due to his debt until it is paid off for him.' Understand the meaning of 'suspended' as withheld from its noble station. This creates a strong recommendation for prompt payment.
This hadith is hasan (good) in chain, but not mutawatir; hence zanni al-thubut, affecting the ruling level.
3
Consider the balancing evidence from Sahih al-Bukhari
The Prophet said: 'Whoever takes people's money intending to repay it, Allah will repay it for him.' This shows that sincere intention to repay, even if death intervenes, absolves the person from liability. The author cites Ibn Hajar and al-Munawi to clarify that such a person is not punished.
This softens the apparent severity of the first hadith.
4
Examine the Prophet's practice regarding debtors
Initially, the Prophet refused to pray over debtors who left no means, but later after conquests he took responsibility for their debts. This indicates that the ruling for the Muslim ruler is different (incumbent) while for ordinary heirs it is recommended, not obligatory.
The ruler's duty is to pay for the insolvent deceased; the heirs are merely encouraged.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi position
Bring together the proofs: the recommendation is established by the suspension hadith, but qualified by the intention and the ruler's role. The Hanafi view is that it is sunnah muakkadah for heirs to hasten, not fard or wajib. The practical outcome: reward for promptness, no sin for delay if there is a valid reason.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Commentary

The author states that it is mustahabb (recommended) to promptly settle the debts of the deceased or obtain a discharge from the creditors, because the soul of the deceased is suspended due to unpaid debt. Overall, the lesson emphasizes the importance of addressing debts promptly and that sincere intention combined with inability due to circumstances does not cause punishment.

The Sunnah Shroud for a Man
Describes the preferred shroud for a man and permissibilities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah number of garments for shrouding a man and explain the permissibility levels for fewer garments.

Core Idea
Three garments are sunnah; two suffice; one is disliked except in necessity.

Shrouding the deceased is a communal obligation, but the number and type of garments have specific sunnah preferences. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position on the ideal shroud and the rulings when fewer garments are used. We will also note the recommended qualities and wrapping procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its hukm level
The act is shrouding a deceased man. Shrouding is fard kifayah, but the number of garments is sunnah. The passage states that three garments is Sunnah muakkadah.
2
List the three sunnah garments
The three garments are: izar (waist-wrapper from head to foot), qamis (seamless shirt from neck to feet), and lifafah (wrapper longer than the body to allow tying).
The izar for the deceased is longer than the living izar.
3
Understand the permissibility levels
Three garments: sunnah. Two garments (izar and lifafah): permissible and sufficient. One garment: disliked (makruh tanzihan) unless necessary.
Two garments is called 'kafan al-kifayah' (shroud of sufficiency).
4
Learn the wrapping procedure
When wrapping the lifafah, begin with the left side, place it on the body, then the right side, like in life. If there is fear of it coming undone, tie it at the top and bottom.
5
Note the recommended qualities
The shroud should be made from the best clothes worn on Fridays and Eids, preferably white cotton. Extravagance is avoided.

Definition, Ruling, and Conditions of I'tikaf
Definition, ruling, and conditions of i'tikaf with an intra-Hanafi dispute on the valid mosque

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of i'tikaf, identify its ruling as sunnah mu'akkada (with the proof), list its conditions and pillars, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the valid place for i'tikaf.

Core Idea
I'tikaf is sunnah mu'akkada, proven by the Prophet's continuous practice; its rukn is staying, and conditions include intention and, if obligatory, fasting.

I'tikaf is a spiritual retreat in the mosque that draws the believer closer to Allah, especially during the last ten nights of Ramadan. The Hanafi school defines it precisely, establishes its ruling based on the Prophet's consistent practice, and sets conditions that ensure its validity. This lesson unpacks the definition, the proof behind the ruling, and the key conditions — including an important debate about where i'tikaf can be performed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the matn statement: 'al-i'tikaf mustahabb'. Then see how al-Hidayah refines this to sunnah mu'akkada by citing the Prophet's continuous practice (muwazaba). Understand that al-Zayla'i further divides i'tikaf into wajib (vowed), sunnah (last ten days), and mustahabb (other times).
The matn alone is insufficient; the sharh corrects the ruling.
2
Analyze the proof for sunnah mu'akkada
The proof is the Prophet's continuous performance (muwazaba) of i'tikaf in the last ten days of Ramadan. In Hanafi usul, muwazaba indicates sunnah, not mustahabb. Recognize this as a dalil naqli (Sunnah fi'liyyah).
This is a key example of how a ruling is elevated from mustahabb to sunnah via proof.
3
Break down the definition into rukn and shurut
The definition has three elements: staying (lubth) — the rukn (pillar); fasting (sawm) — a condition for obligatory i'tikaf; and intention (niyyah) — a condition for all. Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether fasting is required for nafl i'tikaf: one narration requires it, but the zahir al-riwaya does not.
Staying is the essence; without it, i'tikaf is invalid.
4
Examine the dispute on the valid place
Abu Hanifa restricts i'tikaf to mosques where the five daily prayers are held, based on i'tikaf being an act of 'waiting for prayer'. The two Imams (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allow any mosque. The later tarjih, for ease, adopts the view of the two Imams, especially in our time.
The practice of selecting a 'better' mosque is not required.
5
Apply to women's i'tikaf
Women perform i'tikaf in their designated prayer space at home (the 'mosque of her house'), because the concept of waiting for prayer is realized there.
This is a concession to avoid difficulty.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A mu'takif may buy/sell necessities inside the mosque but must not bring goods in.

The mu'takif is confined to the mosque, but what if he needs to purchase food or other essentials? Leaving would break his i'tikaf, yet bringing trade goods into the mosque seems improper. This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies the balance: necessary transactions are allowed, but bringing merchandise into the mosque is disliked—for the mu'takif and anyone else.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is buying and selling inside the mosque by a mu'takif. The text says 'there is no harm' (la ba'sa), indicating permissibility (mubah) under certain conditions.
The ruling applies to both obligatory and supererogatory i'tikaf.
2
Identify the condition of necessity
The permissibility is limited to what is necessary (ma la budda minh), such as food and similar items. The reason is that leaving the mosque for such needs would nullify the i'tikaf.
Thus, the necessity is tied to preserving the validity of i'tikaf.
3
Identify the disliked aspect
Even though the transaction is permissible, the mu'takif must not bring the merchandise (sil'ah) into the mosque. Doing so would occupy the mosque with worldly goods, which is disliked (makruh).
This dislike is general—it applies to anyone, not just the mu'takif.
4
Understand the reasoning
The mosque is a place protected (muhraz) from the rights of people—meaning it is kept free from worldly engagements. Bringing goods in distracts from worship and disrespects the sanctity of the mosque.
This reasoning is part of the Hanafi legal principle of preserving the mosque's dignity.
5
Compare with the non-mu'takif
The text notes that bringing goods into the mosque is disliked for non-mu'takif as well. However, the non-mu'takif is not under the same constraint of necessity; he can easily leave to conduct transactions outside.
The mu'takif's necessity creates an exception for the transaction itself, but not for the physical presence of goods.

This distinction prevents over-extension of the qiyas.

Rulings on Vowed I'tikaf: Days Include Nights and Consecutiveness
Clarifying that a vow of multiple days includes nights and requires consecutiveness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on vowed i'tikaf for multiple days, explain why nights are included and consecutiveness is required, and identify the contrasting default for fasting.

Core Idea
When you vow i'tikaf for days, the nights are automatically included, and the days must be consecutive—unlike fasting, where discontinuity is the default.

When someone makes a vow to perform i'tikaf for a specified number of days, a set of binding details come into play: the nights are automatically included and the days must be observed consecutively. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind these rules, showing how the Hanafi school derives them from linguistic principles and the essential nature of i'tikaf. Understanding this distinction with fasting is key to avoiding common mistakes in vow fulfillment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the person vowing to do? He vows i'tikaf for multiple days (at least two). The ruling is that it becomes wajib upon him, and specific conditions attach automatically.
The vow makes i'tikaf obligatory; the details are part of the obligation.
2
Locate the linguistic dalil
The phrase 'أيام' (days) in a collective plural sense includes the corresponding nights by default. This is a linguistic rule: mentioning days in a general plural encompasses nights.
This is based on Arabic usage, not on explicit stipulation.
3
Identify the qiyasi reasoning (talil)
Why must i'tikaf be consecutive even without stipulation? Because the 'foundation' (مبنى) of i'tikaf is upon consecutiveness—all times are suitable for it. This is contrasted with fasting, whose foundation is discontinuity because nights are not suitable for fasting.
The illa is the suitability of all periods for i'tikaf vs. unsuitability of nights for fasting.
4
Note the exception: intending only days
If the person explicitly intends only the days (without nights), it is valid because he intended the literal meaning of 'days' (the daytime periods). But the default rule when saying 'days' without qualification includes nights.
This shows that the inclusion of nights is the default, not an absolute requirement.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Given this rule, if someone vows 'I will observe i'tikaf for three days starting tomorrow', he must observe it for three full days and nights consecutively, beginning at dawn of the first day and ending at sunset of the third day (i.e., 72 hours).
Consecutiveness means no break: the i'tikaf continues through the nights.

This contrasts with fasting, where nights cannot be fasted (since fasting is only during daylight), so the default is discontinuity (tafarruq) unless one explicitly stipulates consecutive days. Finally, if a person vows only the days (i.e., without nights), his intention is valid because he intended the literal meaning of 'days', but the ruling above (including nights) applies when one says 'days' without qualification. The footnote clarifies that the discussion is about i'tikaf, as mentioned in al-Jawharah.

Ruling on Vowed I'tikaf: Days Include Nights and Must Be Consecutive
States rulings for a vowed i'tikaf of multiple days

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a vowed i'tikaf of multiple days must include the nights and be consecutive, and explain the linguistic and analogical proofs for these requirements.

Core Idea
A vow of i'tikaf for 'days' obligates both days and nights, consecutively, due to linguistic inclusion and the nature of i'tikaf.

When a person makes a vow to perform i'tikaf for a certain number of days, what exactly is required? Does the vow bind only the daylight hours, or do the nights also become part of the obligation? And must the i'tikaf be performed continuously, or can it be done in separate segments? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on these questions, drawn from the chapter on i'tikaf in Al-Hidayah. The key lies in understanding how the Arabic language treats collective nouns and how the legal nature of i'tikaf differs from that of fasting.

Guided Path

What is the act being ruled upon? A vowed i'tikaf for multiple days. The ruling: the i'tikaf must include the nights and must be consecutive, even if not stipulated.

2
Analyze the proof for including nights
The source uses the phrase 'ayyam' (days) in a collective sense. In Arabic, when days are mentioned collectively without qualification, they include the corresponding nights. This is a linguistic principle (al-jam' yush'iru bi al-damm).
This is a proof from lughah (language), not explicit text.
3
Analyze the proof for consecutiveness
The text argues that i'tikaf is built upon consecutiveness (tatabu') because all times (day and night) are suitable for it. Compare with fasting: fasting is only by day, so its nature is discontinuity unless specified otherwise.
This is a qiyas (analogy) based on the nature of the acts.
4

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the chapter of I'tikaf. It addresses the situation where a person makes a vow (obligates upon himself) to perform i'tikaf for a number of days. The ruling states that the vow obligates both the days and the corresponding nights, and the days must be consecutive even if the person did not explicitly stipulate consecutiveness. The reasoning is that when days are mentioned collectively in Arabic, it logically includes the nights that accompany them. Furthermore, i'tikaf by its nature requires continuity because all times (day and night) are equally suitable for it, unlike fasting which only applies to daylight hours. Therefore, consecutiveness is inherent to i'tikaf unless the person explicitly specifies discontinuity. The text also notes a separate ruling: if someone touches their spouse during i'tikaf, it is similar to intercourse and invalidates the fast (and i'tikaf), but if there is no emission, it does not invalidate although it is prohibited. The commentary from Al-Hidayah reinforces the main ruling.
