Standing (Qiyam) in Prayer: Its Obligation and Levels of Recitation
Explanation of the second pillar: standing and its graded recitation levels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the fard, wajib, sunnah, and mandub amounts of standing in prayer based on recitation.

Core Idea
Standing is a pillar in fard prayers; the minimum is one verse, but the complete wajib is a sura after Fatiha.

The standing posture (qiyam) is the second pillar of prayer. While merely standing upright fulfills the pillar, the prayer's completeness depends on the recitation performed while standing. This lesson unpacks the graded obligations: from the minimal fard of one verse to the recommended extensions in supererogatory prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that standing (qiyam) is a rukn (pillar) in obligatory (fard) prayers and annexed prayers. For the one able to stand and prostrate, it is required.
2
Determine the minimum fard duration
The fard amount of standing is the time it takes to recite one verse of the Quran. This is the minimum required to fulfill the pillar.
One verse can be as short as a few words, e.g., 'Thumma nazara' (one complete verse).
3
Recognize the wajib duration
The wajib duration is the time to recite Surah al-Fatiha and a short surah (or three verses). This is a necessary completion beyond the pillar; omission requires sajda al-sahw.
In the first two rak‘ahs of fard, this wajib is stressed.
4
Understand the sunnah duration
Sunnah is to recite longer surahs appropriate to the prayer time (e.g., long surahs in Fajr, medium in Zuhr, short in ‘Asr and Maghrib). This enhances perfection but is not required.
5
Note the mandub extension
In supererogatory prayers like Tahajjud, reciting beyond the sunnah length (e.g., entire Juz) is recommended (mandub) for extra reward.
Mandub means recommended but not sunnah, with more flexibility.

Commentary
This passage discusses the second pillar of prayer: standing (qiyam) and its description. The author states that standing is required in obligatory prayers and what is annexed to them (like wajib prayers, e.g., a vowed prayer or the sunnah of Fajr which is annexed to fajr). The standing should be such that if one extends his hands, they do not reach his knees, indicating an upright posture. The passage then details the recommended stance: it is sunnah to separate the feet about four finger-widths apart, as this aids humility (khushu). This is reported from Imam Abu Hanifa via Muhammad al-Shaybani's Kitab al-Athar. Later Hanafi scholars like al-Shurunbulali, al-Lakhnawi, and al-Ayni confirm this. For a fat person, the exact separation may be custom-based. The passage also categorizes the obligatory (fard), wajib, sunnah, and mandub amounts of standing based on recitation: one verse is fard (minimum), reciting al-Fatiha and a sura or three verses is wajib (required, but omission requires sajda al-sahw), reciting longer surahs according to the occasion is sunnah, and extra recitation in supererogatory prayers like Tahajjud is mandub. This shows the layered obligations in prayer beyond the minimum pillar.

Key Terms
القيام (al-qiyam): The standing posture in prayer, such that one remains upright with the torso erect and feet on the ground.
فرض (fard): The obligatory (pillar) minimum of standing: duration of one verse.
واجب (wajib): The required (necessary) duration of standing: reciting al-Fatiha and a sura; omission requires sajda al-sahw.

The Pillars of Prayer: Standing, Recitation, Bowing, and Prostration
Defining pillars of prayer with conditions and disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the pillars of prayer (qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud) according to the Hanafi school, identify the fard/wajib/sunnah amounts of standing, and apply the ruling on standing when unable to prostrate.

Core Idea
Standing is fard to the extent of one verse; if unable to prostrate, one sits and gestures.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri defines the second through fifth pillars of salah. It explains the obligatory amounts of standing, the recommended posture of the feet, and the conditions for valid prostration. A key dispute addresses whether a person who can stand but cannot prostrate must stand or may sit and gesture. The Hanafi school adopts the latter, based on the principle that a means to an act drops when the act itself is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pillars and their rulings
Locate each pillar (qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud) in the text. For each, determine its hukm level: fard, wajib, sunnah, or mandub, as specified.
Qiyam is subdivided into fard (one verse), wajib (Fatiha + sura), sunnah (long/medium surahs), and mandub (extra in tahajjud).
2
Analyze the conditions of standing
Identify the conditions for qiyam: the worshipper must be able to stand and prostrate. The text also mentions a recommended feet position (four fingers apart) and notes that for the obese, it is left to custom.
The condition 'able to stand and prostrate' excludes those unable to prostrate, leading to the subsequent dispute.
3
Examine the conditions of prostration
Prostration requires placing the forehead, one hand, one knee, and part of the toes on a solid surface that does not give way completely. The text specifies that prostrating on cotton or soft cushions is invalid.
The condition 'solidity' ensures the prostration is physically meaningful.
4
Trace the dispute on standing without prostration
Identify the issue: a person can stand but cannot prostrate. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa) says to sit and gesture (ima′). Zufar and the other three schools say to stand if possible.
The Hanafi proof: standing is a means (wasila) to prostration; if the end is impossible, the means falls away.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its application
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that such a person should gesture while sitting. This is cited from al-Durr and endorsed by later authorities like Ibn Abidin.
The practical difference: In the Hanafi school, a patient who can stand but cannot prostrate may pray sitting; in other schools, he must stand.

Pillars and Obligations of Prayer: Hanafi Distinctions
Ruling with proof on pillars and wajibat of prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six pillars of prayer, distinguish between fard, wajib, and sunnah acts, and apply the Hanafi ruling on standing when unable to prostrate.

Core Idea
In Hanafi prayer, six pillars are fard; additional acts are wajib (compensable) or sunnah; means drop when the goal is impossible.

This passage from al-Lubab lays out the building blocks of prayer (arkan) and then clarifies the uniquely Hanafi hierarchy: some acts are obligatory (wajib) but not pillars, and their omission requires sajdah al-sahw. The text also resolves a practical dispute: what if you can stand but cannot prostrate? The Hanafi position, based on istihsan, is to gesture while sitting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six pillars of prayer
From the text, list qiyam, qira'ah, ruku', sujud, and the final sitting. Note conditions: prostration requires placing forehead, one hand, one knee, and some toes on a firm surface.
The text mentions 'final sitting to the extent of tashahhud' as the sixth pillar.
2
Distinguish fard, wajib, and sunnah
The author states that what exceeds the pillars is sunnah, but then corrects: some acts are actually wajib, proven by sunnah. Understand that in Hanafi fiqh, wujub is a lower grade than fard, with different consequences for omission.
Wajib is proven by zanni dalil; fard by qati.
3
Examine the dispute on standing without prostration
The text notes that Zufar and other schools require standing even if unable to prostrate. Analyze the Hanafi reasoning: standing is a means to prostration, so if prostration is impossible, the means drops.
This is an example of istihsan overriding qiyas.
4
Apply the rule to real scenarios
Consider a person who can stand but cannot bow or prostrate due to illness. According to the Hanafi school, he sits and gestures with his head. Compare with other schools.
This is the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab, a commentary on al-Quduri, begins with the chapter on the description of prayer (bab sifah al-salah). The author lists the pillars (arkan) of prayer: standing (qiyam), recitation (qira'ah), bowing (ruku'), prostration (sujud), and the final sitting (qa'dah akhiriyah). Wajibat in Prayer: Last Sitting and Beyond

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sixth wajib act in prayer (last sitting), list the other wajib acts mentioned, and explain why omission of a wajib requires sajdah sahw rather than invalidating the prayer.

Core Idea
Acts proven by Sunnah are wajib in Hanafi prayer; forgetting one requires sajdah sahw.

This passage from al-Maydani's commentary covers two crucial points: first, the last sitting (qa'dah akhirah) is the sixth wajib of prayer, and second, many acts commonly called 'sunnah' are actually wajib in the Hanafi school. Knowing the difference is essential for correct prayer and proper compensation when mistakes occur.

Learn the consequence of omission
If any one of these wajibat is left out forgetfully, sajdah sahw (two extra prostrations) is required. The prayer is still valid.
Intentionally omitting a wajib is sinful and the prayer should be repeated, but it is not invalid.

First, recognize the specific practice in dispute: joining the heels (ilṣāq al-ka‘bayn) during ruku‘. Some later Hanafis (e.g., al-Zāhidī) considered it sunnah.
The term 'ilṣāq' implies pressing together, not merely placing close.
2
Identify the majority position and its source
The jumhūr of Hanafi fuqahā’ did not mention this practice, and it is absent from authoritative books like al-Hidāyah. The well-known sunnah is to place the feet with a gap of four fingers.
Absence from the core Zahir al-Riwayah texts is a strong indicator of authenticity.
3
Examine the evidence for each side
The later scholars relied on a hadith of tatbīq (clasping hands) and argued that its abrogation left the feet-jointing intact. The responders (al-Laknawi, al-Sindī) demonstrated that tatbīq was fully abrogated and does not support heel joining.
Al-Laknawi quotes al-Sindī's detailed analysis rejecting the analogy.
4
Apply the principle of authority hierarchy
Within the Hanafi school, the Zahir al-Riwayah (six core works of Imam Muhammad) holds the highest authority. Later fatwa collections cannot override this unless supported by strong evidence. Here, the later opinion lacks such support.
The later scholars' approval without explanation (taqrīr) does not create a binding sunnah.
5
Conclude the mufta bihi
The resolved position (mufta bihi) is that joining the heels is not sunnah. The established sunnah is to keep a gap of four fingers between the feet during ruku‘.
This is the position taken by al-Laknawi and al-Sindī, and confirmed by later authoritative references.

Tasbih in Ruku' and the Imam's Dhikr After Rising
Intra-Hanafi dispute on imam's dhikr after ruku'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah of tasbih in ruku' and distinguish the two Hanafi positions on whether the imam adds tahmid after rising.

Core Idea
The imam's dhikr after ruku' is tasmi‘ only, but the Sahibayn say to add tahmid silently.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies the sunnah actions in ruku' and after rising. While the tasbih is straightforward, there is a notable intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Hanifa holds the imam says only 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah', while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad add 'Rabbana lakal hamd' silently. We will examine both views and the basis of each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah of tasbih in ruku'
Note the matn says to say 'Subhana Rabbiyal 'Azim' three times, and the commentary adds that three is the minimum perfection, five is moderate, seven is most complete.
The tasbih is sunnah muakkadah, with three as the minimum.
2
Identify the dhikr after rising for imam and follower
The matn states the imam says 'Sami'a Allahu liman hamidah' while rising, and the follower says 'Rabbana lakal hamd'. This is the basic ruling.
The follower does not say tasmi'; the imam may or may not add tahmid.
3
Analyse Abu Hanifa's position
According to Abu Hanifa, the imam suffices with tasmi' only. This is the position found in the mutun (core relied-upon texts).
His dalil is that the Prophet (peace be upon him) is reported to have said only tasmi'.
4
Analyse the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the imam adds 'Rabbana lakal hamd' silently after the tasmi'. This is also a narration from Abu Hanifa, and later scholars like al-Tahawi preferred it.
Their dalil is that the complete sunnah includes both tasmi' and tahmid.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The mutun (and the fatwa of many early scholars) rely on Abu Hanifa's view (tasmi' only). However, the Sahibayn's view is strong and adopted by many later Hanafis.
Practical rule: The imam may follow either, but the standard fatwa is tasmi' only.

Recitations in Ruku' and Rising: The Hanafi Sunnah and Internal Dispute
Describes the sunnah recitations in ruku' and the differing views on imam's and follower's statements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah recitations during ruku' and rising, identify the minimum, middle, and complete levels of tasbih, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the imam's addition of tahmid.

Core Idea
The minimum sunnah in ruku' is three tasbihs; the imam may silently add tahmid according to the Sahibayn.

This passage from the matn of al-Quduri outlines the recommended recitations during ruku' and when rising from it. It presents a clear hierarchy of sunnah perfection and highlights an important internal Hanafi dispute: whether the imam should confine himself to tasmi' or silently add tahmid. Understanding this disagreement and its resolution sharpens your ability to see how later Hanafi scholars (mutakhkhirun) can prefer a view different from the original matn.

Guided Path
1
Classify the recitations in ruku'
Identify that the tasbih (Subhana Rabbiyal-‘Adheem) is sunnah muakkadah. Note the three levels: minimum (3), middle (5), complete (7). Understand that these are degrees of perfection, not obligations.
The term 'adna kamal al-sunnah' indicates the lowest perfect sunnah; omitting it leaves the prayer valid but deficient.
2
Analyze the imam's rising recitation
The imam says 'Sami‘a Allahu liman hamidah'. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, he suffices with this tasmi' alone. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), he should silently add the tahmid 'Rabbana lakal-hamd' (or similar).
A narration from Abu Hanifa himself supports the Sahibayn's view, creating a multi-layer ikhtilaf.
3
Apply the tarjih (preference)
Later scholars like al-Fadli, al-Tahawi, and the author of Nur al-Idah preferred the Sahibayn's view. The mufta bihi for the imam is to combine tasmi' and tahmid silently, even though the classical matn texts follow Abu Hanifa's original position.
This shows that later fatwa can adopt a different even if weaker transmission when it is more comprehensive.
4
Derive the follower's and lone worshipper's rules
The follower says 'Rabbana lakal-hamd' (best: 'Allahumma Rabbana wa lakal-hamd', then dropping the waw, then dropping 'Allahumma'). The lone worshipper (munfarid) combines both tasmi' and tahmid according to the most authentic view (as in al-Hidayah and al-Multaqa).
The lone worshipper acts as both imam and follower, hence the combination.

The Ruling of Prostrating on the Nose and Forehead
Ruling on prostration with one part

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on prostrating on the nose and forehead and identify when it becomes disliked or permissible.

Core Idea
Prostration requires both nose and forehead; using only one is valid but disliked if it's the nose.

In this passage, the classical Hanafi text defines the proper method of prostration and addresses a common question: what if only the nose or only the forehead touches the ground? Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling provides a practical flexibility, but later scholars refined the position to discourage omission without excuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wajib elements of sajdah
The text states that one must prostrate on both the nose and the forehead. This is the wajib (obligatory) part of sajdah.
Wajib in Hanafi means required; if omitted without excuse, the prayer is still valid but compensation (sajdah al-sahw) may be needed.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's ruling on limiting to one part
Abu Hanifa says it is permissible (ja'iz) to confine prostration to one part if the other is omitted. This means the prayer is valid.
The ruling is based on ease and necessity.
3
Distinguish between nose-only and forehead-only
If only the nose touches, it is disliked (makruh). If only the forehead touches, it is not disliked (la yukrah).
The reason: the forehead is considered the primary part; the nose is supplementary.
4
Note the later Hanafi position
Later scholars (e.g., Ibn Abidin) consider omitting either part without excuse as makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked), requiring sujud al-sahw.
The mufta bihi today is the stricter view, but the text preserves Abu Hanifa's original ruling.
5
Apply to practice
In daily prayer, ensure both nose and forehead touch the ground. If one is omitted due to excuse (e.g., bandage), the prayer is valid but caution is advised.
If no excuse, the later view should be followed to avoid makruh tahrimi.

Prostration: Required Organs and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sufficing with nose or forehead in prostration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the required organs for prostration and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on sufficing with only the nose or forehead.

Core Idea
Prostration requires both nose and forehead; sufficing with only one is invalid in the mufta bihi position.

Prostration (sujud) is a fundamental act of prayer. While all agree the forehead is essential, the Hanafi school has an internal dispute about whether the nose is also required or whether sufficing with one suffices. This lesson examines the positions of Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, and identifies the relied-upon fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
The act is sujud in salah; the dispute is whether sufficing with only the nose or only the forehead is valid.
The passage describes the recommended sequence first, then the dispute.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa: sufficing with one organ is permissible; if only the nose, it is disliked; if only the forehead, not disliked.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: sufficing with only the nose is not permissible without an excuse; they do not make an exception for the forehead.
4
Examine the tarjih
An alternate narration from Abu Hanifa agrees with the Sahibayn. Later scholars (al-Mahbubi, Sadr al-Shari'ah) relied on this view, and it became the basis for fatwa.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its consequence
The mufta bihi: both nose and forehead are required. Omitting either without excuse invalidates the sujud and thus the prayer.

Prostration on a garment is permissible but disliked; gender-specific postures in sujud reflect modesty.

This lesson covers two important aspects of prostration in salah: the ruling on covering the forehead with a turban or garment, and the recommended physical postures for men and women. Permissible and Invalidating Supplications in Prayer
Explains permissible supplication in prayer and conditions for invalidation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on supplication in prayer to determine whether a given phrase invalidates the prayer.

Core Idea
Phrases from Quran or Hadith never invalidate; other phrases invalidate only if they are requests possible from humans and recited before tashahhud.

When praying, a person may supplicate with words that resemble the Quran and transmitted supplications. However, using everyday human speech can invalidate the prayer under specific conditions. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling, the evidence, and the preferred view on this matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is supplication (du'a) performed during prayer. The ruling is mubah (permissible) with certain conditions; violation can lead to invalidation.
The ruling applies whether the supplication is in qira'ah or tashahhud.
2
Classify the phrase
Determine if the phrase is from the Quran, a transmitted hadith, or something else. Quranic and hadith phrases are always permissible.
Even if the phrase is a Quranic verse but intended as supplication, it is permissible.
3
Apply the condition for invalidation
If the phrase is not from Quran or Hadith, check if it is something that could be asked of a human (e.g., 'Give me a cup of water'). If not (e.g., 'Forgive my sins'), it does not invalidate.
Impossibility of asking from humans is key.

If the phrase is a possible human request, check when it was said. If before the sitting of tashahhud, it invalidates; if after, it does not invalidate but is makruh tahrimi.
The sitting is defined as the amount of tashahhud.

Combine conditions: invalidates if (a) not from Quran/Hadith, (b) possible from humans, (c) said before tashahhud. Otherwise valid.

The Salam in Prayer: Wording and Timing for the Follower
Intra-Hanafi dispute on timing of follower's salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct wording of the salam according to the Hanafi school and apply the mufta bihi ruling on the follower's timing of the salam.

Core Idea
The salam is wajib; the follower must say it after the imam, not simultaneously.

The concluding salam (taslim) ends the prayer. Its wording and the timing for a follower (muqtadi) praying behind an imam are governed by specific rulings. The Hanafi school considers the salam wajib, and the transmitter wording is fixed. A key intra-Hanafi dispute exists on whether the follower should salam immediately after the imam or at the same time. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) clarifies the correct practice, ensuring the follower does not precede or coincide with the imam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The salam is the concluding act of the prayer. According to the Hanafi school, it is wajib (obligatory) – not merely sunnah. Omission or incorrect timing can affect the prayer's validity.
Distinguish wajib from fard: wajib is proven by zanni evidence; fard by qat'i.
2
Examine the prescribed wording
The source specifies the wording: 'Al-salamu alaykum wa rahmatullah' for both right and left. Adding 'wa barakatuhu' is not transmitted (ghayr ma'thoor) and is makruh according to al-Hadadi.
The wording is part of the sunnah; deviation may affect the reward but not the validity if the meaning remains.
3
State the intra-Hanafi dispute on follower's timing
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (Sahibayn) hold that the follower must say salam after the imam completes his salam. From Abu Hanifa, two narrations exist: one agrees with Sahibayn, another allows the follower to say salam simultaneously with the imam.
The dispute is about the precise moment of the follower's salam relative to the imam's.
4
Apply the tarjih (preference)
The later Hanafi masters (e.g., al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah) preferred the view of Sahibayn, making it the mufta bihi (operative ruling). Thus, the follower should wait until the imam finishes his first salam, then say salam after him.
The other narrations from Abu Hanifa are considered weak or less reliable for fatwa.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If a follower says salam before or simultaneously with the imam, the prayer may be invalid (if before) or makruh tahrimi (if simultaneous). The mufta bihi ensures clarity and avoids uncertainty.
The difference affects the validity of the follower's prayer and the imam's responsibility.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Jahr is wajib in specific prayers; ikhfa in others – proven by unbroken practice, not isolated hadith.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies a core element of Hanafi prayer practice: when the imam must recite aloud (jahr) and when quietly (ikhfa). The ruling is based on the continuous practice (mutawarith) of the Prophet transmitted through the generations, a weighty proof in Hanafi usul. Understanding this distinction is essential for both the imam and the congregation.

Follower's Salam Following Imam's Salam
Ruling on timing of follower's salam relative to imam's salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the timing of the follower's salam relative to the imam's salam.

Core Idea
The follower’s salam follows the imam’s salam sequentially—right after right, left after left.

In congregational prayer, the follower (muqtadi) must not precede the imam. This lesson clarifies the precise timing of the taslim for the follower, ensuring that the prayer is completed correctly according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the follower's taslim (salam). The Hanafi ruling is that it is wajib and must be performed after the imam's salam, not before or simultaneously.
Taslim is a wajib act in Hanafi prayer.
2
Understand the sequence
The imam gives two salam: first to the right, then to the left. The follower must wait for each individual salam and then respond with the corresponding salam.
The follower gives right salam only after imam's right, and left after imam's left.
3
Apply the ruling
In practice, the follower should not rush to complete both salam after the imam's first. Instead, he should follow the imam step by step.
This ensures the follower does not precede the imam in any part of the taslim.

Ruling on loud recitation for imam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the specific prayers in which the imam must recite loudly (jahr) and explain why this ruling is classified as wajib rather than fard.

Core Idea
Jahr is wajib for the imam in Fajr, first two rak'ahs of Maghrib and Isha, and other congregational prayers.

In this portion of Al-Lubab, the commentator clarifies the obligation of loud recitation (jahr) for the imam. The ruling is based on the inherited practice (mutawarith) from the Prophet ﷺ and the Companions. The imam must recite loudly in Fajr, the first two rak'ahs of Maghrib and Isha, and also in Jumu'ah, Eid, Tarawih, and Witr in Ramadan. After the first two rak'ahs, he recites silently. If he recites loudly beyond the specified places, he has done wrong (asa'a). This obligation is classified as wajib, not fard, because its evidence is from transmitted practice which is zanni al-thubut.

After the first two rak'ahs in Maghrib and Isha, the imam recites silently (ikhfa').

Jahr is wajib, not fard, because the evidence (inherited practice) is zanni al-thubut.

If the imam recites loudly where he should not, he commits a sin (asa'a).
However, the prayer remains valid.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri addresses the rulings on loud (jahr) and silent (ikhfa') recitation during salah. The commentator expands on the matn, stating that the imam must recite loudly in Fajr, the first two rak'ahs of Maghrib and Isha, and also in congregational prayers like Jumu'ah, Eid, Tarawih, and Witr in Ramadan. This is an inherited practice (mutawarith) from the Prophet through the companions. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) for the imam; if he adds extra loud recitation, it is considered wrong (asa'a). After the first two rak'ahs, the imam recites silently. For the one praying alone (munfarid), he has the choice between loud and silent recitation, but the best is to recite loudly to resemble the congregation. The passage also delves into an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the minimum definition of ikhfa': al-Karkhi and al-Balkhi hold it as correct pronunciation of letters without requiring audible sound, while al-Hindwani and others require that the person hear himself. The commentator notes that al-Hindwani's view is considered more sound (asahh) and preferable (arjah), so the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that the minimum of silent recitation is to make oneself hear, or at least those nearby, with the loudest silent recitation being mere letter pronunciation (al-Karkhi's view) but not considered sufficient. This ikhtilaf affects the validity of recitation: if one does not hear himself, according to al-Hindwani, the recitation is not valid, whereas according to al-Karkhi it is valid as long as the letters are pronounced correctly. The text also references sources like Hidayah and Bada'i' al-Sana'i'.

جهر (jahr): Loud recitation that makes oneself and others hear; obligatory for imam in specific prayers.
مخافتة (mukhafatah): Silent recitation; minimum defined as either correct letter pronunciation or making oneself hear.
منفرد (munfarid): One praying alone; given choice between jahr and ikhfa'.

According to the Hanafi school, which of the following is NOT one of the prayers where the imam must recite loudly?
A) Fajr
B) Jumu'ah
C) Dhuhr
D) Tarawih

Key Takeaway
The imam must recite loudly (jahr) in specified prayers; this is wajib, not fard, and omitting it is sinful but does not invalidate the prayer.
