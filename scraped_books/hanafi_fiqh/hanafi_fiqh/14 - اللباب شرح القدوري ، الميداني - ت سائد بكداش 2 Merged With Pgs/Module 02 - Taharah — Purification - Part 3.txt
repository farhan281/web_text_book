Purifying a Well That Never Runs Dry
Intra-Hanafi dispute on well purification method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the methods of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani for purifying a well that does not run dry and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
For a replenishing well, remove the contaminated water; Muhammad's fixed measure of 200-300 buckets is the relied-upon method.

This lesson addresses a practical taharah scenario: a well whose water continuously replenishes. The Hanafi school agrees on the obligation to remove the contaminated water, but there is an internal disagreement on how to determine the amount. Understanding this dispute helps you apply the easier and more precise ruling, as favored by later scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation
Recognize that removing the impure water from a well that does not run dry is wajib (obligatory). The passage states 'وقد وجب نزح ما فيها' (it is obligatory to remove what is in it).
This obligation applies regardless of the method used.
2
Understand the challenge
Since the well replenishes indefinitely, you cannot simply empty it entirely. The question is: how much water must be removed to achieve purification?
3
Learn Abu Yusuf's method
Abu Yusuf prescribes digging a pit equal to the water volume of the well, then pouring removed water into it until the pit is full, thereby measuring the exact amount that was originally in the well.
This method aims for exactness but is labor-intensive.
4
Learn Muhammad al-Shaybani's method
Muhammad gives a fixed estimate of 200 to 300 buckets, with the third hundred being recommended (mandub). This was his fatwa for Baghdad's wells due to their abundant water from the Tigris.
The phrase 'مائتا دلو إلى ثلاثمائة' indicates the third hundred is not obligatory.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Ikhtiyar, al-Nahr) chose Muhammad's view for its precision and ease, making it the mufta bihi (operative ruling).
The choice is based on practical applicability, especially for large wells.

Finding a Dead Animal in a Well without Known Time
Ruling on finding a dead mouse in a well with unknown time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on finding a dead animal in a well with unknown time, and identify the conditions that affect the obligation to repeat prayers and wash items.

Core Idea
When a dead animal is found in a well with unknown time, only one day's prayers are repeated—not all past prayers.

Imagine you draw water from your family well for wudu and drinking. One day, a dead mouse is found floating. No one knows when it fell, and it hasn't started to bloat or decompose. Are all your previous prayers invalid? Do you need to wash everything the water touched? This passage from the Hanafi tradition gives a clear and practical rule: only one day's prayers need repeating, and only items that actually got wet must be washed. The ruling balances the probability of impurity with the burden of repeating worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is finding a dead animal (e.g., mouse) in a well used for wudu. The ruling is a composite obligation: repeat prayers of one day and night, and wash everything the well water touched.
The ruling applies only if the animal is of a type that spoils water (small animals like mice, birds, etc.).
2
List the Conditions
Three conditions must be met: (1) the time it fell is unknown, (2) the animal has not bloated (intafakhat), (3) the animal has not decomposed (tafassukhat).
Bloating and decomposition are signs that the animal has been in the water for some time; their absence indicates a recent fall.
3
Determine the Consequences
If the conditions are met: repeat the prayers of one day and night (those performed after using the water), and wash every garment or item that came into contact with the water.
The 'one day and night' refers to the last 24-hour period, not all prayers since using the well.
4
Note the Exceptions
If the wudu was renewing (without hadath) or the garment was washed for non-impurity reasons, then only the garments need washing; prayers are not repeated.
This exception shows the ruling is tied to actual impurity affecting worship.

Well Water Purity After Discovering a Dead Mouse
Ruling for well water with undiscovered dead animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for well water when a dead mouse is found, distinguish cases based on bloating, and explain the intra-madhhab difference on washing clothes.

Core Idea
Water becomes doubtful after a dead mouse; rulings differ by bloating, and certainty dictates prayer repetition.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a common scenario: a dead creature found in a well. The ruling hinges on whether the animal shows signs of decay, which determines if the water is doubtful or impure. Moreover, within the Hanafi school, there is a nuanced disagreement about washing clothes when the water was not used for removing impurity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and animal condition
State the scenario: a dead mouse (or similar) is found in a well, and the time of its fall is unknown. Note whether the animal is bloated or decomposed.
Bloating or decomposition indicates longer presence and greater impurity.
2
Determine water status
If not bloated/decomposed, the water is doubtful (mashkuk). If bloated/decomposed, the water is considered impure (najis).
This distinction is key to the ruling.
3
Apply prayer repetition ruling
For non-bloated case: repeat prayers of one day and night if wudu was for hadath. For bloated case: repeat prayers of three days.
The number of prayers differs based on water status.
4
Analyze washing clothes obligation
If the water touched clothes, wash them. But there is a dispute when the water was used without hadath or impurity: some scholars exempt washing, others require it based on absolute texts.
Understand the two positions and their proofs.
5
Grasp the underlying principle
The principle al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk (certainty is not removed by doubt) explains why those already in wudu do not repeat prayer.
This principle reconciles the disputes.

Repeating Prayers Due to Uncertain Impurity – Abu Hanifa vs the Sahibayn
Discusses the ruling on repeating prayers when a dead body is found in water, focusing on the Hanafi intra-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding repeating prayers when a dead body is found in water, and explain the proofs and tarjih.

Core Idea
Certainty is not removed by doubt, but an apparent cause allows estimation for practical obligation.

This classic Hanafi dispute arises when a dead body is found in water used for wudu, but the time of its fall is unknown. Must past prayers be repeated? Abu Hanifa uses physical signs like swelling to estimate the period; the Sahibayn insist on certainty. The later school preferred Abu Hanifa's view as istihsan and more precautionary in worship.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify the ruling: if the body is swollen or decomposed, repeat prayers for three days and nights; if not, repeat for one day and night. This is the mufta bihi position.
Also note the consensus that prayer repetition is required in principle; the disagreement is on the duration.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's opposing view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that no prayers need repeating until the exact time of the impurity is known with certainty.
They rely on the principle that certainty is not removed by doubt.
3
Follow Abu Hanifa's proof
Death has an apparent cause (falling into water), so the impurity is attributed to that. Swelling indicates remoteness, so estimate three days; lack of swelling indicates recentness, so estimate one day.
This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas.
4
Follow the Sahibayn's proof
They analogize to a person who finds impurity on his garment and does not know when it occurred: no prayers are repeated. The time cannot be assigned without certainty.
This is qiyas (analogy).

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the ruling when a dead body is found in water and the time of its fall is uncertain. The text first establishes that if a dead body is found swollen or decomposed, indicating it has been there for some time, then those who used that water for ablution or washing clothes must repeat their prayers for three days and nights according to Abu Hanifa. The reason is that the water is now doubtful in purity: certainty (of prior purity) is not removed by doubt, but here there is an apparent cause (the fall), so the default is to attribute the impurity to the earliest possible time based on signs. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they say no repetition is required until the exact time is known with certainty, applying the principle that certainty is not nullified by doubt. They analogize to a person who finds impurity on his garment and does not know when it occurred—he does not have to repeat prayers for the past. The text then discusses a further internal disagreement about whether clothes need to be rewashed: some later Hanafi authorities say yes, some say no. Finally, the issue of tarjih is presented: although al-`Atabi considered the Sahibayn's view as chosen, the majority of later scholars (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shari`ah) preferred Abu Hanifa's view, which is based on istihsan and is more precautionary in worship.

Core Idea
Su'r is pure from edible meat animals, impure from beasts of prey, and disliked only if alternative water exists.

In the Hanafi school, leftover water (su'r) after drinking carries different rulings based on the drinker. This lesson presents a clear three-tier categorization: pure (tahir), impure (najis), and disliked (makruh tanzih). An important nuance is that disliked su'r becomes permissible when no other water is available.

Ruling on the Su'r of Donkey and Mule
Ruling on residual water of donkey and mule being doubtful

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using the su'r (residual water) of a donkey and mule for purification, and explain the condition that requires combining wudu/ghusl with tayammum.

Core Idea
The su'r of donkey and mule is pure but not purifying; when no other water is available, combine wudu/ghusl with tayammum.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri addresses a common scenario: you have water left by a donkey or mule, but you are unsure if it can be used for purification. Guided Path

Identify the subject and initial classification
The passage speaks about the su'r (residual water) of a donkey and a mule. It states that both are 'doubtful' (mashkuk fihima). Identify what is being classified: the su'r itself, not the animal.

2
Clarify the nature of the doubt
The commentary specifies that the doubt pertains to the tahuriyya (purifying quality) of the water, not its tahara (inherent purity). The water is physically pure (tahir) but its ability to purify others (mutahir) is uncertain.

If a person finds no other water that is certainly purifying, he must perform wudu or ghusl with this su'r and also perform tayammum. Both actions are wajib (obligatory) in this situation.
The combination ensures certainty of purification: wudu/ghusl uses the physically pure water, and tayammum compensates for the doubt in its purifying ability.

Note the permissibility of any order
The text states that whichever act is performed first (wudu/ghusl or tayammum) is permissible. The order does not affect validity.
This flexibility is based on the most sound opinion (al-ashahh).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tayammum is a substitute for water; its permission depends on the real absence of water, not travel alone.

This lesson begins the chapter on tayammum from the Hanafi text Al-Lubab. After covering primary purification with water, the author turns to its substitute: tayammum. Guided Path

Tayammum is the substitute for wudu and ghusl. The author follows the pattern of explaining the original purification first, then its substitute. Recognize that substitutes always follow the original in legal texts.
3
Identify the condition of permissibility
The condition is 'not finding water' (lam yajid al-ma'). This is not tied to travel alone; it can occur even in a city if water is far away. The text specifies a distance of about a mile as the measure.
The mile is defined linguistically as the limit of eyesight (approximately 2-3 km).
4
Analyze the rationale for the mile measure
The author states that the mile is the preferred measure (al-mukhtar) according to Al-Hidayah and Al-Ikhtiyar. The commentary explains that cities are not devoid of water, so the condition applies specifically to those outside the city; however, if water is equally distant inside the city, the ruling is the same.
The mile is the distance at which one is not obligated to fetch water, making tayammum permissible.

Core Idea
Water purifies by itself; earth purifies only with intention—hence intention is fard in tayammum, mustahabb in wudu.

This lesson examines a key distinction between wudu and tayammum regarding the role of intention. While a common assumption is that intention is required in both, the Hanafi school teaches that water's inherent purifying property makes intention merely recommended in wudu, whereas earth requires intention to become a valid purifier. The proof is drawn from the famous hadith 'Actions are by intentions' and an analogy to washing garments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Note that the passage states two distinct hukm: intention is fard in tayammum, and intention is mustahabb in wudu. This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.

The illa for the difference is the nature of the purifying agent: water is inherently purifying (mutahhir bi nafsihi), so it does not need intention; earth is not inherently pure (it is mulawwath), so it requires intention to effect purification.

The proof cited is the hadith 'Innama al-a'malu bi al-niyyat' (Actions are by intentions). The qiyas is to ghasl al-thawb (washing a garment) – water cleans without intention.

The Shafi'i school holds that intention is fard in wudu as well. Their dalil is also the hadith, but they apply it generally to all acts of worship without exception. The Hanafi response distinguishes based on the illa of inherent purity.
This ikhtilaf has practical consequences: a Shafi'i must renew wudu if performed without intention; a Hanafi does not need to.
5

The Hanafi position is zahir al-riwayah and mufta bihi. Al-Laknawi strengthens the proof by explicitly structuring the istinbat: hadith applied to tayammum, excepted for wudu via qiyas. This provides a clear framework for the ruling.

Wiping Over Khuffs: Method and Obligatory Amount
Definition ruling with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the place, sunnah method, and fard amount for wiping over khuffs, and apply the mufta bihi position in a case of intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
The fard is three fingers of the hand; the sunnah is to use fingers in lines on top only.

Wiping over khuffs is a concession in wudu that resembles the actual washing of the feet. Because it is a departure from qiyas, its method is strictly defined by transmitted texts. In this lesson, you will learn exactly where to wipe, how to wipe in the sunnah way, and what the obligatory minimum area is — a point of some internal Hanafi difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal nature
Recognize that wiping over khuffs (mash 'ala al-khuffayn) is a substitute for washing the feet in wudu. Its rules are based on transmitted texts, not mere analogy.
The text says it is a departure from analogy (خروج من القياس).
2
Specify the obligatory place
The place of wiping is the upper surface (dhaahir) of the khuff only, not the sole, heel, or leg.
Wiping on any other part is invalid.
3
Differentiate sunnah method from permissible alternative
The sunnah is to wipe with the fingers making lines (خطوطاً بالأصابع), starting from the toes towards the shin. However, wiping with the whole palm or reversing direction is permissible.
The fard amount is separate from the method.
4
Determine the fard amount
The obligatory minimum is the area covered by three fingers of the smallest hand, both in length and width.
This is the minimum required for validity.
5
Resolve the intra-Hanafi difference
Al-Karkhi said three fingers of the foot, but the relied-upon opinion (mufta bihi) is three fingers of the hand because the instrument of wiping is the hand.
Al-Hidayah and most later scholars prefer the hand.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Wiping is valid unless the hole exposes three small toes below the heel; smaller holes are excused by custom.

This lesson examines the detailed conditions for wiping over khuffs (leather socks) when they have holes. You already know that wiping over khuffs is a concession in wudu. But what if the khuff has a tear or hole? The Hanafi school provides precise criteria: not every hole invalidates wiping. The threshold is three of the smallest toes, and location matters—holes below the heel are problematic, but above the heel are not. Additionally, the material's stiffness and the presence of an inner lining can preserve the ruling. Let's unpack these conditions step by step.

Note that 'large hole' is defined by the exposure of three toes. If the hole is on the toes themselves, each toe is counted individually. If on the heel, most of the heel must be exposed to invalidate.
The location changes the criterion: three-toe measurement applies to non-toe/heal areas.

Examine exceptions: if the khuff is so stiff that the hole does not open during walking, even a large hole does not prevent wiping. Similarly, if only the outer layer (ziharah) is torn but the inner lining (batinah) remains intact, the khuff is considered whole.
These exceptions preserve the khuff's functional integrity.

Wiping Over Khuffs Invalid for Ghusl
States ruling and commentary on wiping being impermissible for one in need of ghusl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that wiping over khuffs does not satisfy ghusl and apply the principle that ghusl requires washing all body parts, including feet.

Core Idea
Wiping over khuffs is invalid for ghusl because the khuff prevents water from reaching the feet — ghusl requires full body washing.

This passage establishes the boundary of the rukhṣah (concession) of wiping over khuffs. While it is permitted for wudū', it does not extend to ghusl (janābah). The commentator explains the rational basis: ghusl necessitates complete coverage of the body, and the khuff acts as a barrier. The uṣūl principle 'al-manfiyyu lā yalzimu taṣwīruhu' is invoked to avoid unnecessary speculation, though later scholars did provide a hypothetical scenario to clarify the application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
State the ruling: it is not permissible (lā yajūz) to wipe over khuffs for a person who is obligated to perform ghusl (man wajaba 'alayhi al-ghusl). The central issue is the invalidity of mash for major impurity.
The term 'ghusl' here refers to the full-body ritual purification required after janābah, menstruation, etc.
2
Trace the rational proof (dalīl ‘aqlī)
The commentator gives the reason: ghusl requires washing all parts of the body (yastawjibu ghusla jamī‘i al-badan). The khuff is worn and prevents water from reaching the feet, making it impossible to fulfill the obligation. Thus wiping cannot substitute for washing.
This is a rational argument based on the nature of the obligation, not a textual proof.
3

The phrase 'al-manfiyyu lā yalzimu taṣwīruhu' means that when a ruling is negated, one is not required to conceive an actual scenario where the negated action could occur. It is enough to state the rule; speculation is unnecessary.
This principle prevents futile mental exercises but does not prohibit giving examples for clarification.

The Three Nullifiers of Wiping Over Khuffs
Lists and explains the three nullifiers of wiping over khuffs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three nullifiers of wiping over khuffs according to the Hanafi school and explain the legal reasoning (talil) for each.

Core Idea
Three things nullify the wipe: wudu nullifiers, removing khuff(s), and time expiry — because the wipe is part of wudu.

In this passage, al-Quduri enumerates the nawaqid (nullifiers) of mash 'ala al-khuffayn. The Hanafi school recognizes three distinct events that invalidate the legal effect of wiping. Understanding them is essential for maintaining valid purification during the wiping period. The commentator, al-Maydani, expands on the reasoning (ta'lil), showing how each nullifier logically follows from the function of the khuff as a barrier and the wipe as a substitute for washing.

Guided Path
1
State the three nullifiers
List the three nullifiers as given in the text: (1) anything that nullifies wudu, (2) removal of the khuff (one or both), (3) expiration of the prescribed time.
2
Examine the ta'lil for the first nullifier
The text says 'because it (the wipe) is part of it (wudu)' (li-annahu ba‘duhu). Since the wipe is a substitute for washing the feet, any event that nullifies wudu necessarily nullifies the wipe.
This is a direct relation, not qiyas.
3
Examine the ta'lil for the second nullifier
Removing the khuff removes the barrier (mani‘) that prevented the hadath from reaching the foot. The hadath that was present on the foot before the wipe now becomes effective, so the foot is impure again.
This applies even if only one khuff is removed.
4
Examine the ta'lil for why removing one khuff also nullifies
The text states 'due to the impossibility of combining washing and wiping in one function' (li-ta‘adhdhuri al-jam‘i bayna al-ghusli wa al-mashi fi wazifatin wahidatin). You cannot have one foot washed and the other wiped simultaneously in a single wudu act.
5

Expiration of the mudda (time period) nullifies the wipe automatically. The footnote clarifies that this applies only after the start of the wiping period; if no wipe has been performed yet, there is nothing to nullify.
For a resident, the period is 24 hours; for a traveler, 72 hours.

Commentary
This brief passage states that wiping over the turban (ʿimāmah), the cap (qalansuwah), and the face veil (burqaʿ) is not permissible in wudu. These items are headgear or face coverings that do not have the same status as khuffs or socks. The general rule is that wiping over any covering is not allowed except for the khuffs (and socks under the fatwa) due to specific textual evidence. Therefore, one must directly wipe the head and face, not over these coverings.

Key Terms
العمامة (al-ʿimāmah): A turban, wrapped around the head.
القلنسوة (al-qalansuwah): A cap worn under or without a turban.
البرقع (al-burqaʿ): A face veil for women.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school strictly forbids wiping over any head covering in wudu; the head must be wiped directly.
