Definition and Conditions of Zakat
Opening of the Zakat book: definition and obligatory conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of zakat and identify the seven conditions that make it obligatory.

Core Idea
Zakat is fard upon a free Muslim adult who owns a productive nisab for a lunar year with complete ownership.

This passage opens the Book of Zakat with a crisp definition and a list of conditions for obligation. The author stresses that 'wajib' here means fard because the evidence is definitive. We will parse both the definition and each condition, building a clear understanding of when zakat becomes binding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on zakat. The hukm level is fard (obligatory), clarified as 'wajib' meaning fard due to definitive proof.
2
Parse the definition
Distinguish the linguistic definition (purity and increase) from the legal definition (transferring ownership of a specific portion of specific wealth to a specific person by Allah's command).
The legal definition emphasizes tamleek (transfer of ownership), a key Hanafi point.
3
List the conditions
Identify all seven conditions: free, Muslim, adult, sane, owning a nisab (free from debt and basic needs, productive, complete ownership), and a lunar year passing.
These are shurut al-wujub (conditions of obligation), not shurut al-sihha (conditions of validity of payment).
4
Understand the epistemological weight
Note that zakat is fard (not just wajib) because it is proven by Quran, mutawatir sunnah, and ijma. This affects the level of sin for neglecting it.

The Obligatory Conditions for Zakat
Ruling with proof: statements of conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the five personal conditions and two wealth conditions for zakat obligation and explain why the Hanafi school equates 'wajib' with 'fard' in this context.

Core Idea
Zakat is fard upon every free, adult, sane Muslim who owns a nisab for a full lunar year.

This lesson unpacks the foundational conditions for zakat obligation as stated in the Hanafi matn. The passage begins by declaring zakat wajib (obligatory) and immediately clarifies that 'wajib' here means 'fard' (compulsory) because the evidence is free from doubt. We will then examine each condition: the person must be free, Muslim, adult, sane, and must own a nisab that is complete, free from debt and basic needs, growing (even potentially), and held for one year.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is paying zakat. The ruling is 'wajib', which the text equates with 'fard'. Note that 'fard' in Hanafi usul means an obligation proven by definitive evidence.
This is a crucial distinction: some schools differentiate between fard and wajib; here the commentator removes any doubt.
2
List the personal conditions of the obligated person
The text states: 'on the free, Muslim, adult, sane person'. These are the four personal conditions. A fifth condition is complete ownership of the wealth.
Missing any one of these conditions removes the obligation.
3
List the wealth conditions
The wealth must: (1) be at the nisab threshold, (2) be owned completely, (3) be free from debt that is demanded, (4) be free from basic needs (haja asliyya), (5) be growing (nami) even by estimation, (6) have a full lunar year pass over it.
Condition (5) is unique: growth potential, not actual growth, suffices (e.g., trade goods).
4
Note the commentary's explanation of 'wajib' as 'fard'
The commentator quotes al-Hidayah to affirm that there is no ambiguity (shubha) in the obligation. This strengthens the Hanafi position that zakat is fard, not merely wajib in the weaker sense.
This is a tarjih (preference) that sets the Hanafi view apart.

Exemptions from Zakat: Minors, Debt, and Essential Assets
Ruling with proof: exemptions from obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify four categories of exemption from zakat (minors/insane/mukatab, debt, basic needs assets) and apply the rule of surplus over debt.

Core Idea
Zakat is not due on those lacking complete ownership or on wealth tied to basic needs.

After establishing the conditions for zakat, the passage turns to exemptions. It explicitly excludes minors, the insane, and mukatab slaves because they lack legal capacity or complete ownership. Then it addresses the common situation of debt: if debt equals or exceeds wealth, no zakat; if wealth exceeds debt, zakat is due on the surplus. Finally, it lists essential assets (houses, clothes, furniture, riding animals, servant slaves, weapons) that are exempt because they are needed for daily life and are not intended for growth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of persons exempt
The text directly says: no zakat on a minor (sabi), an insane person (majnun), or a mukatab slave. The reason is they are not legally addressed (ghayr mukhatabin) or lack complete ownership.
Mukatab is a slave who has contracted to earn his freedom; his wealth is not fully his.
2
Understand the debt exemption
If a person has debts that cover all his wealth or leave less than nisab, no zakat. The wealth is considered effectively non-existent because it is needed to repay debt. The text compares it to water that is needed due to thirst.
This is based on the condition that wealth must be free from debt. Debt is a 'haja asliyya' (basic need).
3
Apply the surplus rule
If wealth exceeds debt, zakat is payable on the surplus after deducting debt, provided the surplus reaches nisab and the year condition is met.
The year is calculated on the original wealth, but if debt arises mid-year, some scholars recalculate.
4
List exempt assets as basic needs
The text enumerates: residential houses (dur al-sukna), body garments (thiyab al-badan), household furniture (athath al-manzil), riding animals (dawab al-rukub), servant slaves (abid al-khidma), and use weapons (silah al-isti'mal). These are all exempt because they are tied to basic needs and are not growing assets.
The double rationale: occupied by need and not growing (laysat bi-namiya).

Exemptions from Zakat Obligation in Hanafi Fiqh
Expansion of zakat exemptions with reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exemptions from zakat obligation according to the Hanafi school and identify the reasons for each exemption.

Core Idea
Zakat is not due on wealth needed for basic survival or owned by those without legal capacity.

In this passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri, we learn about specific persons and assets that are exempt from zakat in the Hanafi school. Understanding these exemptions is crucial to correctly applying zakat rules. We will examine the reasoning behind each exemption, including the concepts of legal capacity, debt, and basic needs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exempted persons
List the three categories of people on whom zakat is not obligatory: the minor (sabi), the insane (majnun), and the mukatab (slave with contract of manumission). Note the reason given: lack of legal address (khiṭāb) or incomplete ownership.
The mukatab is exempt because he does not have full ownership over his wealth.
2
Analyze the effect of debt
Determine how debt affects zakat obligation. If debt covers all wealth or leaves less than nisab, no zakat. If wealth exceeds debt, zakat is due on the surplus above nisab.
The passage uses the analogy of water needed for thirst, which permits tayammum.
3
List the exempt assets
Identify the six categories of assets on which zakat is not due: residential homes, clothes, household furniture, riding animals, servant slaves, and weapons for use. Recognize the rationale: they are basic needs (al-ḥājah al-aṣliyyah) and not growth assets (nāmin).
These items are exempt even if they are valuable.
4
Synthesize the underlying principle
Generalize that zakat exemption applies when wealth is either lacking in ownership capacity, consumed by essential needs, or not growth-oriented.

Giving All Wealth Without Zakat Intention: Hanafi Istihsan
Ruling that giving all wealth without zakat intent discharges the obligation by istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that giving all wealth without zakat intention discharges the obligation and apply the principle of istihsan to similar cases.

Core Idea
Giving all wealth as charity without zakat intention discharges zakat by istihsan because the wajib portion is automatically included.

This lesson examines a unique Hanafi ruling: a person who gives away all his wealth as general charity is freed from his zakat obligation even if he did not specifically intend zakat. The proof is based on istihsan (juristic preference), which overrides strict qiyas that would require separate intention. We will analyse the reasoning and its application to specific assets like books and tools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is giving all one's wealth as charity without making a specific intention for zakat. The ruling is that the zakat obligation falls (saqt) from him.
This is an exception to the usual requirement of niyyah for zakat.
2
Understand the proof: istihsan
The proof is istihsan (juristic preference). The text gives a rational argument: the wajib portion is a part of the total wealth, so when all wealth is given, that portion is automatically included; thus no separate intention is needed.
Strict analogy (qiyas) would require intention, but istihsan departs from it for ease.
3
Follow the reasoning: wajib is part of wealth
The key illa: the zakat due is a fraction (e.g., 2.5%) of the wealth. Giving the whole means that fraction is necessarily given. Therefore, the obligation is fulfilled without additional specification.
This applies regardless of the amount given, as long as it is all.
4
Apply the extension to books and tools
The same ruling extends to items that are not normally zakatable unless intended for trade, such as scholars' books and craftsmen's tools. If the owner gives them all as charity, any zakat that would have been due on them (if they were trade goods) is discharged.
The extension is based on the same rationale: the wajib portion is part of the given property.
5
Note the distinction for recipients
The commentator adds: if the property given reaches nisab, scholars and craftsmen (ahl) may accept zakat from it, but others may not, as per al-Durr.
This is a separate fiqh point about eligibility to receive zakat.

Zakat on Camels: Nisab and Rates Schedule
States nisab and zakat rates for camels in tabular form.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the nisab thresholds for zakat on camels and identify the correct zakat due for any herd size up to 200.

Core Idea
Zakat on camels follows a fixed schedule: for 5-24 camels, one sheep per 5; from 25 onward, specific aged female camels are due, with cycles resetting after 150.

Zakat on camels is more complex than on gold or silver because the rates change with the herd size. This lesson presents the complete Hanafi schedule as a memorization anchor, enabling you to calculate the zakat for any camel herd up to 200. The schedule is derived from the Prophetic sunnah and is a fard obligation when the nisab is reached.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nisab threshold
The minimum nisab for camels is 5. Below 5, no zakat is due.
2
Learn the rates for 5-24 camels
For every 5 camels from 5 to 24, one sheep is due. This means 5-9: 1 sheep, 10-14: 2 sheep, 15-19: 3 sheep, 20-24: 4 sheep.
This is a simple linear phase.
3
Learn the rates for 25-90 camels
At 25 camels, the obligation switches to a female camel of a specific age: 25-35: bint makhad (2nd year), 36-45: bint labun (3rd year), 46-60: hiqqa (4th year), 61-75: jadh'a (5th year), 76-90: two bint labun.
Each range has a fixed type; memorize the intervals.
4
Learn the rates for 91-150 camels
From 91-120: two hiqqa; 121-129: two hiqqa + one sheep; 130-134: two hiqqa + two sheep; 135-139: two hiqqa + three sheep; 140-144: two hiqqa + four sheep; 145-149: two hiqqa + one bint makhad; 150-154: three hiqqa.
Note the sheep are added for every 5 camels beyond the base of two hiqqa.
5
Understand the reset after 150
After 150, the calculation resets: for 155-159: three hiqqa + one sheep; and so on, adding a sheep for every 5 camels. At 196-200: four hiqqa. The pattern repeats indefinitely: for every 50 camels, the hiqqa count increases and sheep are added for the excess.
Also note the rule: male camels are not accepted for zakat except by their market value for females.

Ruling and dispute on zakat for cows

Learning Objective

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mules and donkeys are not zakatable as livestock; only trade triggers zakat.

In this short passage from al-Lubab, the author clarifies a key point in zakat law: mules and donkeys are not subject to the same zakat as camels, cattle, or sheep. They are not considered livestock for zakat purposes. However, if they are bought and sold as merchandise, they fall under the rules of trade goods ('urud al-tijarah), and zakat becomes due on their market value.

Guided Path

When mules/donkeys are trade goods, the zakat is calculated on their market value at the end of the lunar year, provided the total value reaches the nisab (e.g., 85g gold or 595g silver). The rate is 2.5%.
This is the standard rule for 'urud al-tijarah.

Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: zakat on adults covers young calves; Abu Yusuf: a calf must be given separately.

This passage from al-Lubab presents a disagreement among the Hanafi imams regarding zakat on young calves (عجاجيل) when adult cattle are present. Identify the parties and their positions

Core Idea
When the required age is missing, the collector takes a better one with refund or a lesser one with payment.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a practical issue in zakat collection. Often, the obligated person may not have the exact animal of the prescribed age. The ruling provides clear guidance for the collector (musaddiq) on how to proceed without delay or conflict, ensuring the obligation is fulfilled while maintaining fairness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Recognize that this rule applies when the person obligated to pay zakat on livestock does not possess the animal of the exact required age (sinn).
2
List the two options
State the two permissible actions: (1) take a higher-age animal and refund the surplus value to the owner, or (2) take a lower-age animal and collect the deficiency from the owner.
3
Distinguish legal nature
Understand that the first option involves an implicit sale (the collector buys the extra value), so the owner can refuse. The second option is a forced compensation, like collecting the value difference directly.
4
Apply the rule
In practice, the collector may choose either, but if the owner objects to the sale in the first option, the collector must resort to the second.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Substituting Age Categories in Zakat: When the Owed Age Is Missing
Ruling on substituting age categories in zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two options available to the zakat collector when the exact owed age is not present, and distinguish between the legal character of each option.

Core Idea
If the exact aged animal is missing, the collector may take higher/lower with value adjustment, but only the lower option is compulsory.

Zakat often requires a specific age of animal, but what if the owner does not possess that exact age? The Hanafi school provides a practical solution: the collector may take an animal of a higher or lower age, with a value adjustment. But these two options are not equal in legal effect. Understanding this rule ensures fair zakat collection without forcing hardship on the owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The ruling applies when a person owes a specific age category (سن) of cattle for zakat but does not possess that exact age.
Example: owes a 2-year-old cow but only has 1-year-old or 3-year-old.
2
State the two options for the collector
The collector may: (1) take a higher age and return the value difference to the owner, or (2) take a lower age and collect the value difference from the owner.
These are the two possibilities mentioned in the text.
3
Analyze the first option (taking higher)
The collector is not compelled to take a higher animal; he may refuse and demand the exact owed or its price. The reason: taking a higher animal involves a purchase element (شراء) where the collector buys the higher age with the extra value.
The text says: 'له أن لا يأخذ، ويطالب بعين الواجب، أو بقيمته؛ لأنه شراء'.
4
Analyze the second option (taking lower)
The collector is compelled to accept a lower age and collect the deficit from the owner. This is not a purchase but a sale of the deficiency (إعطاء بالقيمة). No purchase contract is involved; it is simply taking the value for the missing portion.
The text says: 'يجبر؛ لأنه لا بيع فيه، بل هو إعطاء بالقيمة'.
5
Contrast the two options
The key difference: taking higher involves a purchase (need for mutual consent), so the collector cannot force it. Taking lower is not a purchase, so the collector can force it for the owner's benefit (to discharge the obligation).
The practical import: the owner cannot be forced to give a higher animal but can be forced to give a lower animal with compensation.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri clarifies a critical distinction in Hanafi zakat law: the effect of the wealth perishing (hulak) vs. the owner consuming it (istihlak). Zakat is attached to the specific asset (al-'ayn), not to the owner's liability (al-dhimmah). Perishing eliminates the asset, so the obligation ceases. Consumption does not eliminate the obligation because the owner is a trustee.

After zakat becomes due, the property is like a trust (amanah). If the owner consumes it, he breaches the trust and must compensate, just as if he consumed a deposit (wadi'ah).

Advancing Zakat Payment Before the Year Ends
Ruling with proof: paying zakat before the year is valid if one owns nisab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on advancing zakat payment before the completion of the year and identify the distinction between the cause (sabab) and condition (shart) in zakat obligation.

Core Idea
Ownership of nisab is the cause of zakat; the year is only a condition, so early payment is valid.

Zakat is generally due after a full lunar year of owning the nisab. But what if you pay early? The Hanafi school permits advancing zakat before the year ends, so long as you already own the nisab. This ruling is based on a careful distinction between the cause (sabab) that triggers the obligation and the condition (shart) for its fulfillment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the passage: the ruling is that advancing zakat before the year is permissible (ja'iz). The text explicitly says 'جاز' (it is permissible).
The ruling is unconditional as long as one owns the nisab at the time of payment.
2
Identify the proof
The proof is 'لوجود السبب' (because the cause exists). The cause (sabab) for zakat is ownership of nisab. The year is not the cause but a condition (shart).
The proof is qiyas-based: since the cause exists, payment is valid even before the condition is fully met.
3
Distinguish sabab from shart
The Hanafi school distinguishes between sabab (cause) and shart (condition). The cause makes the act due in principle; the condition defines when it becomes obligatory to perform. Here, nisab is the cause, the year is the condition.
This distinction is key: once the cause exists, early payment is considered fulfilling the obligation, even if the condition is not yet fully complete.
4
Understand the application for more than a year
The text also permits advancing zakat for more than one year in advance. This is because the cause (nisab) is continuous, so one can pay multiple years ahead.
This shows that the permissibility is not limited to just a few months before the year ends.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is specific to their sabab-shart analysis. Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) hold that the year is an integral part of the cause, so early payment does not discharge the obligation.
Understanding this difference helps in fiqh comparisons and iftaa considerations.

Core Idea

If wealth is destroyed after the year, no zakat is due; if the owner consumes it, he must still pay. Advance payment is valid and can be made for more than one year.

Zakat on silver is wajib at 1/40 when you own 200 legal dirhams (70 barley grains each) for a full lunar year.

Core Idea

This lesson clarifies how zakat applies to gold coins (dinars) that are mixed with base metals. The key principle is 'ghulbah' (predominance): if the gold content is more than half, the coin is treated as gold; if the alloy is more than half, it is treated as a trade good. We will explore the conditions for zakat in each case and note an important dispute among Hanafi scholars regarding equal proportions.

Guided Path

If gold is predominant (ghalab al-dhahab), the coin is in the ruling of gold. Zakat is due on the total weight when it reaches the nisab of gold (20 dinars, ~85g), regardless of trade intention.

This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is to give zakat.

Zakat on Trade Goods: Obligation, Valuation, and Nisab Continuity
Ruling on zakat obligation, valuation, and nisab continuity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on zakat of trade goods, explain the valuation rule using the more beneficial nisab, and apply the condition of nisab at beginning and end of the year.

Core Idea
Zakat on trade goods is wajib if value at year's end reaches either nisab, using the lower one to benefit the poor.

Trade goods (urud al-tijarah) are a significant category of wealth. The Hanafi school obligates zakat on them, distinct from the Shafi'i position. This lesson unpacks the three core rules of this chapter: when zakat is due, how to value goods, and the continuity of nisab throughout the lunar year.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Obligation
The text begins by stating that zakat is wajib on trade goods of any type when their value reaches a nisab of either gold or silver. Note that this includes goods that themselves are not zakatable (like clothing) as well as those that are (like grazing animals).
This generality highlights the Hanafi expansion of zakat beyond the two currencies.
2
Understand the Valuation Rule
The owner must value the goods using whichever nisab (gold or silver) is more beneficial to the poor — i.e., the lower nisab (usually silver, 200 dirhams). This ensures that zakat becomes due if the goods reach that lower threshold, protecting the rights of the needy.
This is an application of istihsan (juristic preference) for public interest.
3
Apply the Nisab Continuity Condition
Zakat is only obligatory if the nisab is complete at both the beginning and end of the lunar year. However, a mid‑year decrease (e.g., market fluctuation) does not cancel the obligation because the year has been anchored with the nisab at both ends.
This rule contrasts with some other wealth categories where nisab must continue throughout the year.

Conversely, if one has 10 dinars (worth 50 dirhams) and 100 dirhams, the nisab is complete by weight according to the Sahibayn, and Abu Hanifa also agrees as a precaution for the poor.

Finally, later authorities (al-Isbijabi, al-Zuzani, al-Nasafi, Burhan al-Shari'ah, Sadr al-Shari'ah) preferred Abu Hanifa's view, as it is more beneficial for the poor and more precautionary in worship.

Abu Hanifa's Ruling on the Universality of 'Ushr
Defining 'ushr as zakah and stating Abu Hanifa's ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on the obligation of 'ushr on all produce of the earth and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
All produce of the earth is subject to 'ushr except firewood, reeds, and grass.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position on agricultural zakah. According to Abu Hanifa, 'ushr is due on all produce regardless of quantity or irrigation method, with only a few specific exceptions. Understanding this rule is essential for proper calculation of zakah on crops.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Abu Hanifa states that 'ushr is due on all produce of the earth, whether little or much, and whether irrigated by flowing water or rain.
This includes both staple crops and minor produce.
2
Note the exceptions
The exceptions are firewood, Persian reeds, and grass, as they are not usually cultivated for land exploitation.
These are items that grow spontaneously on the fringes.
3
Understand the condition for exceptions
If the land is intentionally turned into a plantation for these items and irrigated, then 'ushr becomes obligatory as per the Jawharah footnote.
Intention and active cultivation change the ruling.
4
Recognize the scope
There is no minimum quantity (nisab) required; 'ushr applies regardless of the amount harvested.
This differs from other schools that require a nisab.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The Imam and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differed on some conditions for 'ushr, but this does not remove 'ushr from being zakah.
The disagreement is about subsidiary conditions, not the core obligation.
