Recitation in the First Sitting of Four-Rak'ah Sunnah Prayers
Explanation of recitation in first sitting for stressed vs recommended sunnah prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the recitation required in the first sitting of stressed (mu'akkadah) and recommended (mustahabbah) four-rak'ah sunnah prayers.

Core Idea
In stressed sunnah prayers, only tashahhud is recited; in recommended ones, salawat and istiftah are added.

Understanding what to recite in the first sitting of a four-rak'ah sunnah prayer is essential for correct salah. The Hanafi school differentiates between stressed sunnahs (mu'akkadah) and recommended ones (mustahabbah), and this affects the recitation in the julus al-awwal. Let's examine the ruling from the source text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prayer type
Determine whether the four-rak'ah sunnah is stressed (mu'akkadah) or recommended (mustahabbah). This distinction is key to applying the correct ruling.
Mu'akkadah sunnahs are those the Prophet consistently performed, e.g., the four before Zuhr.
2
Define the first sitting
The first sitting occurs after the second rak'ah. For all four-rak'ah prayers, tashahhud is recited.
The tashahhud is the minimum required.
3
Apply the ruling for mu'akkadah
For a stressed sunnah, only the tashahhud is recited. No istiftah or ta'awwudh is said at the start of the third rak'ah.
This is the established view in the school.
4
Apply the ruling for mustahabbah
For a recommended sunnah, after the tashahhud, one recites the salawat upon the Prophet. Then, upon standing for the third rak'ah, one recites the istiftah and ta'awwudh.
This adds extra worship beyond the minimum.
5
Note the later opinion
The commentator notes that the ruling for mustahabbah is not from the early imams but a choice of later scholars. Recognize this as a non-zahir al-riwayah position.
This indicates flexibility in practice.

The Regular Sunnah Prayers: Numbers and Rulings
List of sunnah rak'ats with legal status and hadith basis for the twelve rak'at

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the twelve rak'at of sunan rawatib, distinguish between mu'akkadah and mustahabb, and state the hadith basis.

Core Idea
The Prophet's constant practice on twelve rak'at defines the stressed sunnahs; others are recommended.

This lesson covers the regular sunnah prayers (sunna rawatib) that accompany the five daily prayers. The Hanafi school classifies some as stressed (mu'akkadah) based on the Prophet's constant practice, and others as merely recommended (mustahabb) due to lesser emphasis. The primary evidence is the hadith promising a house in Paradise for the one who is constant on twelve rak'at.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the regular sunnah prayers (sunna rawatib). The ruling is sunnah mu'akkadah for twelve rak'at, and mustahabb for additional ones like four before Asr and four before Isha.
The passage lists specific numbers and uses terms 'mu'akkadah' and 'mustahabb'.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is the hadith: 'Whoever is constant upon twelve rak'at in the day and night, Allah will build for him a house in Paradise.'
This hadith is cited in al-Hidayah and is the basis for identifying the twelve stressed rak'at.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith encourages constancy on twelve rak'at. The Hanafi school interprets these twelve as the specific sunan rawatib derived from the Prophet's practice.
The passage notes that the four before Asr and four before Isha are not part of these twelve due to lack of muwazaba.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The status of the four before Asr and four before Isha is not agreed upon; they are classified as mustahabb because they are not in the hadith of twelve.
Al-Hidayah explains that al-Asl called the four before Asr 'hasan' (good) and did not mention the four before Isha, indicating they are not stressed.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that the four before Asr and before Isha are mustahabb, and four is better than two where there is a choice.
This is supported by the authority of al-Hidayah and the early sources like al-Asl.

The Night Prayer Dispute: Is It Two or Eight Rak'ahs?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on night voluntary prayer units

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the number of rak'ahs for night voluntary prayers with one taslim, identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf), and explain the later difference in tarjih regarding karahah.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa permits up to eight rak'ahs per taslim at night; the Sahibayn restrict it to two — the difference affects tahajjud practice.

Voluntary prayers (nawafil) have flexibility, but the night prayer (tahajjud) has a specific intra-Hanafi dispute. The matn you are about to read states the general rule for day prayers (choice of two or four per taslim, with dislike for more), then presents the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two major disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on the night prayer. Understanding this ikhtilaf helps you navigate actual practice and fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the contested issue
The act is night voluntary prayers (nawafil al-layl). The contested issue is the maximum number of rak'ahs permitted with one taslim (i.e., without renewing the sitting and salam).
The day prayer rule is not disputed: two or four per taslim, and it is makruh to exceed four.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that up to eight rak'ahs with one taslim is permissible (ja'iz) without karahah; exceeding eight is makruh. The best (afdal) in his view is to pray four by four (i.e., four rak'ahs, then taslim, then another four).
Some later scholars say even exceeding eight is not makruh (al-Sarakhsi).
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that at night, one should not exceed two rak'ahs per taslim. They agree with Abu Hanifa on the day rule (two or four), but restrict the night to two only.
Their proof is likely the hadith 'Night prayer is two, two' (صلاة الليل مثنى مثنى).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: a person following Abu Hanifa may pray four, six, or eight rak'ahs with one taslim; following the Sahibayn, one must make taslim after every two rak'ahs.
Most later mashayikh follow Abu Hanifa's view, with a dispute over karahah beyond eight.
5
Check the later tarjih
The footnote reports that most shuyukh (mashayikh) consider exceeding eight rak'ahs at night as makruh (according to Abu Hanifa's view), but al-Sarakhsi and others say it is not makruh. The verification differs among later authorities.
In fatwa, one should be aware of the difference and follow the mufta bihi of their locality/teacher.

Core Idea
Each pair of nafl is an independent prayer – recitation is required in every rak'ah, as in the first two of a fard prayer.

The reasoning is that each two-rak'ah unit of nafl is a separate prayer, so the third rak'ah begins a new prayer unit. The Obligation to Make Up an Invalidated Nafl Prayer
Ruling on qada' of nafl when invalidated, with conditions and exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on making up an invalidated nafl prayer and identify the condition of intentional entry that triggers the obligation.

Core Idea
A nafl prayer entered intentionally becomes a binding debt; if invalidated, it must be repaid with two rak'ahs.

This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies a common point of confusion: what happens if you start a supererogatory prayer and then break it? The Hanafi school holds that you must make it up, but only under certain conditions. Let's unpack the ruling step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is entering a nafl prayer with intention (qasd) and then invalidating it (ifsad). The ruling is that one must make it up (qada') as an obligation (wajib).
The obligation is not fard but wajib, based on the strength of the evidence.
2
Note the condition of intentional entry
The text explicitly conditions 'qasdan' (intentionally). If the entry was forgetful (sahw), no makeup is required.
Example: standing for a fifth rak'ah forgetfully and then invalidating does not require qada'.
3
Understand the exception for forgetful entry
Forgetful entry means the prayer was not validly commenced, so there is no 'debt' to repay.
This exception stems from the rule that intention (niyyah) is a condition for the validity of prayer entry.
4
Recognize the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Abu Yusuf held that one must make up the number of rak'ahs intended, not just two. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa: two rak'ahs regardless of intention.
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani reportedly agreed with Abu Hanifa.
5
Apply the mufta bihi ruling
In practice, if you intentionally enter any nafl prayer and invalidate it, you must pray two rak'ahs to make it up.
This applies whether you invalidated by your own action (e.g., talking) or by an external factor (e.g., seeing water for a mutayammim).

Core Idea
Qada of invalidated nafl is wajib only after the first pair is completed; standing for third rak'ah is a new tahrimah.

This passage from Lubab al-Quduri addresses a common scenario: a person starts a supererogatory (nafl) prayer, then invalidates it. The Hanafi school holds that nafl, once entered, becomes binding (lazim). But how many rak'ahs must be made up? The text clarifies that the answer depends on structural completion and intention, and it highlights a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his student Abu Yusuf. Understanding this ruling helps you apply the principle that a completed pair with sitting and tashahhud constitutes a separate unit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling and its basis
First, note that if one intentionally enters a nafl prayer and then invalidates it, he must make it up (qada) as an obligation (wajib). The basis is that the opening takbir (tahrimah) makes the prayer binding.
The ruling applies whether invalidation is by one's own action or by an external factor like seeing water during tayammum.
2
Analyze the structure of a four-rak'ah nafl
The passage explains that if the first two rak'ahs are completed with a sitting and tashahhud, they form a complete unit (shaf' first). If the worshipper then stands for the third rak'ah (commencing the second pair) and invalidates, only the second pair needs to be made up—two rak'ahs.
This is because the standing for the third is treated like a new tahrimah, so only the second pair is affected.

Three conditions are given: (1) entry must be intentional (forgetful entry like standing for a fifth rak'ah voids the obligation to qada), (2) the first pair must have been completed with sitting, and (3) the invalidation must occur after commencing the second pair (i.e., after standing for the third). Abu Yusuf disagrees on two points: (a) if one intended more than two rak'ahs in the second pair, he must make up the entire intended amount, and (b) invalidation even before standing for the third rak'ah (i.e., during the first sitting) still requires qada.
The text rejects both, relying on Abu Hanifa's view that the second pair only begins with the new tahrimah.

The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's: if the first pair is complete, only two rak'ahs are made up; if invalidation occurs before starting the second pair, no qada is required. This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Core Idea
Four causes trigger Sujud al-Sahw: increase, omission of a wajib, leaving Fatiha, or leaving Qunut.

Sujud al-Sahw compensates for minor errors in prayer. This lesson presents the four specific causes that make it obligatory according to the Hanafi school. Understanding these causes helps the worshiper know when to perform sujud and why the school treats it as wajib (obligatory) rather than sunnah.

Guided Path

Identify the ruling and the list
The passage begins by affirming that Sujud al-Sahw is wajib. Then it lists four causes: (1) increase of an action of the same type, (2) omission of a masnun (wajib-by-Sunnah) act, (3) leaving recitation of Fatiha or most of it, (4) leaving Qunut or its takbir.

The text cites al-Hidayah: an increase in prayer necessarily involves either delaying a rukn or omitting a wajib. Both require compensation via Sujud al-Sahw. This is a talil (causal reasoning).

A follower's forgetfulness never triggers sajdah; only the imam or a masbuq in his make-up prays.

Stating the ruling for mistakenly adding a fifth rak'ah by a single prostration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a person mistakenly adds a fifth rak'ah by a single prostration and explain the required corrective actions (adding a rak'ah and performing sajda sahw).

Core Idea
Adding a fifth rak'ah by one prostration requires completing two nafl rak'ahs and sajda sahw; the fard prayer remains valid.

In the Hanafi school, forgetfulness (sahw) during prayer has specific corrective measures. This lesson examines a precise scenario: a worshipper inadvertently adds a fifth rak'ah by performing only one prostration. The ruling balances the validity of the obligatory prayer with the need to compensate for the omission of the wajib act of proper exit and delayed salutation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mistake
The worshipper intended to complete the fard prayer but, due to forgetfulness, performed an extra prostration without standing for a full rak'ah. This constitutes adding a partial fifth rak'ah with only one sajdah.
A full rak'ah requires two prostrations; here only one is performed.
2
Determine the required actions
The text commands: 'ضم إليها ركعة أخرى' (add another rak'ah to it). This means the worshipper must complete the partial prostration into a full rak'ah by standing, reciting, bowing, and prostrating twice. Then two additional rak'ahs are performed, which become nafl.
Adding one rak'ah would result in an odd number (nafl witr), which is disliked (makruh); thus another is added to make it two (even).
3
Assess the validity of the fard prayer
The fard prayer is valid ('قد تمت صلاته') because the final sitting (julus akhir) occurred at its proper place. The extra rak'ahs do not invalidate the fard; they are supererogatory.
The final sitting is a rukn; its occurrence in the correct position secures the fard prayer.
4
Understand the rationale for sajda sahw
Sajda sahw is wajib due to two reasons: delaying the salutation (tasleem) beyond its wajib time and exiting the prayer in an improper manner (not in the wajib way). Both are compensable omissions.
The deficiency (nuqsan) is in the wajib, not in the rukn, so sajda sahw suffices.

Building (Bina') in Prayer When Illness Recovers: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explaining that one who prays sitting due to illness then recovers may continue standing.

Core Idea
A standing person can follow a sitting imam, so you can continue your prayer standing after recovery — but Muhammad requires restart.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a practical scenario: you start praying sitting due to illness, then you recover mid-prayer. Can you stand up and continue, or must you start over? The Hanafi school has a well-known position based on an analogy to following a sitting imam, but within the school there is a disagreement. Let's walk through the reasoning.

The matn states: 'he builds upon his prayer standing.' Understand that 'building' (bina') means continuing the same prayer without restarting, even though the posture changes from sitting to standing.

The commentary gives the 'illah: 'building is like following (iqtida'); a standing person follows a sitting person.' In Hanafi prayer, a standing person can validly follow a sitting imam. The analogy is based on the permissibility of iqtida' when the imam is sitting and the follower is standing.

Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani disagrees: he says the person must restart (yastaqbil) the prayer. His principle: 'a standing person does not follow a sitting person.' For him, the follower's posture cannot be higher than the imam's. Thus, the analogy fails and building is invalid.
