Conditions for Obligation of Sujud al-Tilawah
Rulings on conditions for sujud al-tilawah with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions that make sujud al-tilawah obligatory and state the ruling for scenarios involving non-human sounds and disputed cases.

Core Idea
Sujud al-tilawah is wajib only when recited by a human, sane, awake reciter; echoes and recordings do not obligate.

Sujud al-tilawah is an act of worship triggered by reciting or hearing specific Quranic verses. However, not every hearing obligates prostration—the source and state of the reciter matter. This lesson examines the Hanafi conditions for obligation, including an intra-school dispute over hearing from a sleeping person and a modern application to recorded recitations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Sujud al-tilawah is wajib (obligatory) upon hearing a verse of prostration from a qualified reciter. Determine that the ruling is not fard but wajib due to the evidence being from ahad hadith.
The act is performed by prostrating once.
2
Determine the reciter's conditions
The reciter must be a human, sane, and awake. Even if they are in a state of ritual impurity (junub, menstruating, postpartum) or are a disbeliever, child, or intoxicated, the obligation remains. The key is that the sound is a genuine recitation by a rational human.
Non-human sounds like a bird or echo do not constitute recitation.
3
Identify exceptions and disputed cases
Hearing from a sleeping, unconscious, or insane person is disputed: one narration says not obligatory, but the later relied-upon view (Al-Khulasa, Al-Khaniyah) says it is obligatory. Hearing from a bird or echo does not obligate.
4
Apply the knowledge condition
Obligation also depends on the listener knowing that the verse is a sajdah verse, even if by being informed. If one does not hear due to sleep or preoccupation, it is not obligatory.
5
Consider modern applications
Recorded recitations (tapes, radio, TV) do not obligate sujud because they are not live human recitations; they are analogous to echoes. However, caution (ihtiyat) recommends performing the prostration.

Rulings for Imam and Followers in Sujud al-Tilawah
Rulings for imam and follower in sujud al-tilawah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for sujud al-tilawah in congregational prayer, distinguishing when prostration is required, omitted, or deferred.

Core Idea
The follower's prostration depends on the imam; the follower's own recitation has no effect.

This passage continues the rules for sujud al-tilawah in congregation. It clarifies three scenarios: when the imam recites, when the follower recites, and when an outsider recites nearby. Understanding these distinctions is vital for correct prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and context
Recognize that sujud al-tilawah in congregation involves three parties: imam, follower, and outsider.
2
State the ruling when the imam recites
The imam prostrates, and the follower must prostrate with him due to the obligation of following (mutaba'ah).
3
State the ruling when the follower recites
If the follower recites a sajdah verse, neither the imam nor the follower prostrates, because the follower's recitation is legally invalid (mahjur) in congregation.
4
State the ruling when an outsider recites
If someone not in the prayer recites a sajdah verse, the congregation does not prostrate during prayer but must prostrate after completing it.

The follower's recitation has no legal effect because he is under the imam's authority.

The Hanafi school holds that the follower is bound by the imam's recitation and his own recitation has no legal consequence—even if it includes a prostration verse. Hearing a Prostraction Verse from External Reciter During Prayer
Ruling on hearing a prostration verse from outside prayer while in prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hearing a prostration verse from a non-participant during prayer and explain why the sujud is performed after prayer.

Core Idea
Prostration for an external recital cannot be done inside prayer—it belongs outside.

Imagine you are in the middle of salah and hear someone reciting a prostration verse from outside the prayer. Should you prostrate immediately? The Hanafi school has a clear ruling based on the principle of salatiyyah—what belongs to the prayer. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Determine the situation: a person in prayer hears a prostration verse recited by someone not in the same prayer.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The person does not prostrate during the prayer; instead, they perform the sujud after completing the prayer.
3
Analyze the reason (ta'lil)
The ruling is because the act of hearing is not an action of the prayer (not salatiyyah), so the sujud cannot be integrated into the prayer.
Anything not part of the prayer is prohibited during it.
4
Understand the consequence
The cause (sabab) of the sujud—hearing the verse—is realized immediately, but its performance is deferred to a permissible time: after the prayer.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An invalid prostration does not break the prayer because mere prostration does not violate ihram.

This lesson examines a precise ruling: what happens if someone during prayer prostrates for a verse of prostration (sajdah tilawah) that is external to the prayer? The Hanafi school distinguishes between the validity of the prostration itself and its effect on the overall prayer. A single sujud suffices for multiple recitations of the same verse in one sitting due to tadakhul (overlap).

Sujud al-tilawah is an obligatory act upon reciting or hearing a prostration verse. When the same verse is recited multiple times, questions arise whether multiple prostrations are needed. The Hanafi school, emphasizing ease, rules that one sujud covers all recitations in the same gathering, provided conditions are met. Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
The passage presents four distinct cases: (1) performing sujud al-tilawah during prayer, (2) reciting outside prayer then entering prayer and reciting again, (3) prostrating outside prayer before entering prayer and reciting again, and (4) repeating the same verse multiple times in one sitting. List each scenario clearly.
These cases revolve around the timing and context of recitation and prostration.
2
Determine sufficiency of one sujud
For each scenario, decide whether one sujud suffices for multiple recitations. The rulings vary: in scenario 1, the in-prayer sujud does not suffice (obligation remains); scenario 2, one sujud suffices; scenario 3, a new sujud is required; scenario 4, one sujud suffices when the same verse and sitting are repeated.
Pay attention to the conditions of sama' al-majlis and the relative strength of prayer context.

The text emphasizes 'ittihad al-majlis' (unity of sitting) as a condition for tadakhul. The same verse recited in one sitting allows overlap. Also, the prayer context is considered stronger, which affects the subsidiary relationship.
The term 'ittiḥād al-majlis' is central to Hanafi methodology here.

Tadakhul means that a single act can fulfill multiple causes when the causes are similar and occur in one sitting. This principle prevents hardship. Apply it to scenario 4: multiple recitations of the same verse in one sitting are covered by one sujud.
The preferred practice is to perform the sujud after the first recitation, though delaying is also valid as a precaution.

The Traveler's Obligatory Prayer: Qasr is Wajib
Stating the traveler's fard is two rak'ahs and ruling on praying four with tashahhud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the traveler's obligatory prayer in four-rak'ah prayers and explain the consequences of adding two rak'ahs intentionally or forgetfully.

Core Idea
The traveler's fard is two rak'ahs; adding more intentionally is sinful but not invalid if tashahhud is performed.

This passage lays down a core Hanafi principle: the traveler must shorten four-rak'ah prayers to two rak'ahs. Adding extra rak'ahs intentionally is not permissible, yet if one does so and sits for tashahhud after the second rak'ah, the prayer is valid but sinful. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its conditions, and the remedy for forgetfulness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage is about the traveler's obligatory prayer (fard) in four-rak'ah prayers (Zuhr, Asr, Isha). The ruling is that it becomes two rak'ahs.
This is known as qasr, and in the Hanafi school it is wajib (obligatory), not merely permissible.
2
Determine why qasr is wajib
Understand that the obligation is based on the Prophet's ﷺ practice and the command to shorten when traveling. The text says 'لا تجوز له الزيادة' (it is not permissible to increase), indicating obligation.
The Hanafi school considers qasr as part of the fard for the traveler; it is not a concession but a requirement.
3
Analyze the case of adding two extra rak'ahs
If the traveler prays four rak'ahs, the ruling depends on whether he sat for tashahhud after the second rak'ah. The text explicitly says 'فإن صلى أربعاً، وقد قعد في الثانية مقدار التشهد' (if he prays four and sat in the second to the extent of tashahhud).
Sitting for tashahhud indicates completion of the two-rak'ah fard; the extra two become an addition that does not invalidate the prayer but violates the wajib of qasr.
4
Distinguish intentional vs. forgetful addition
If done intentionally, the prayer is valid but sinful (makruh tahrimi). If done forgetfully, the prayer is valid and sajda al-sahw becomes wajib to rectify the omission of the wajib.
The text states 'ويجب سجود السهو إن كان سهواً' (sajda al-sahw becomes obligatory if it was by forgetfulness).
5
Note the scope: only fard prayers
Qasr applies only to four-rak'ah obligatory prayers. It does not apply to witr or sunnah prayers. The text explicitly says 'قيد بالفرض؛ لأنه لا قصر في الوتر والنفل' (restricted to fard because there is no qasr in witr and nawafil).
Also, the distance required for travel is three days' journey, which is a separate condition.

The Obligation of Qasr: Traveler’s Prayer is Two Rak‘ahs
Defines traveler’s obligation and conditions for shortening

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that qasr is obligatory (fard) for the traveler in four-rak‘ah prayers and identify which prayers are exempt from shortening.

Core Idea
The traveler’s fard in four-rak‘ah prayers is exactly two rak‘ahs; deliberate increase without tashahhud invalidates the prayer.

This passage from al-Lubab establishes a foundational rule of Hanafi fiqh: for the traveler, the obligatory prayer in Zuhr, Asr, and ‘Isha is reduced to two rak‘ahs. This is not a mere permission (rukhsah) but an obligation. The text also clarifies that sunan prayers during travel are recommended only when conditions allow. Understanding this ruling is crucial for every Muslim who travels, as it directly affects the validity of their daily prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on the traveler’s prayer in four-rak‘ah obligatory prayers. The hukm is that the fard is two rak‘ahs – not a choice, but a binding obligation.
The text begins: 'Wa fard al-musafir ‘indana fi kulli salatin ruba‘iyyatin: rak‘atan'.
2
Locate the exception clause
The phrase 'لا تجوز له الزيادة عليهما عمداً' prohibits deliberate addition. This indicates that adding rak‘ahs without a valid excuse renders the prayer invalid if the condition of tashahhud is not met.
The text explicitly says it is not permissible to increase deliberately.
3
Examine the conditions that qualify the ruling
The author restricts the ruling to 'four-rak‘ah' prayers, excluding two-rak‘ah fard (Fajr) and three-rak‘ah witr, as well as nafl. Also, shortening does not apply to these categories.
‘Qayyada bil-fard; li’annahu la qasra fil-watr wa al-nafl’.
4
Understand the ikhtilaf about sunan
There is a dispute whether the traveler should perform sunan prayers. The chosen view (al-mukhtar) is to perform them if the traveler is in a secure and settled situation, but to omit them if in haste or flight.
The text references al-Nahr and states the preferred position.
5
Contrast with other madhhabs
Unlike the Hanafi school, the Shafi‘i school allows the traveler to choose between shortening or completing the prayer. This difference stems from differing interpretations of the evidence.
The Hanafi position is that qasr is obligatory; the Shafi‘i treats it as optional.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling when a traveler prays four rak‘ahs intentionally and determine validity based on whether tashahhud was performed after the second rak‘ah.

Core Idea
If the traveler sits for tashahhud after the second rak‘ah, the prayer is valid but he is sinful; if not, the prayer is invalid.

This lesson dives into a specific scenario that often causes confusion: what happens when a traveler deliberately adds two extra rak‘ahs to a prayer that should be shortened? The text clarifies that the key factor is whether he sat for tashahhud after the second rak‘ah. Understanding this nuance ensures that the prayer is either salvaged or repeated correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The text presents two conditions: praying four rak‘ahs and sitting for tashahhud after the second, or not sitting. Recognize which case is being analyzed.
The phrase 'fa in salla arba‘an' introduces the conditional.
2
Analyze the case with tashahhud
If he sits for tashahhud after the second rak‘ah, the first two count as fard, the last two as nafl. He is sinful (musi') for the deliberate increase.
The text says 'ajza’athu al-rak‘atan ‘an fardihi, wa kānat al-ukhrāyāni lahu nāfilatan, wa yakūnu musī’an'.
3
Analyze the case without tashahhud
If he does not sit for tashahhud after the second, the entire prayer is invalidated. The reason is mixing the nafl with the fard before completing the fard portion.
The text says 'batalat salātuhu; li-ikhtilāṭ al-nāfilah bihā qabla ikmālihā'.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The tashahhud acts as a separator (qāṭi‘) between the fard and nafl. Without it, the two become mixed in a way that invalidates the prayer.
This is based on the requirement to complete the fard with proper structure.
5
Apply the ruling to new scenarios
Now that you know the condition, test with variants: what if the traveler prays four but intends the whole as nafl? Or what if he prays six? The same principle applies to any four-rak‘ah prayer.
The text does not cover all variants, but qiyas can be applied.

When Travel Begins and Ends: Conditions for Qasr
Defines start and end of travel status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the conditions that start and end the travel ruling: leaving the city limits starts travel, and intending to reside for 15 days or more ends it.

Core Idea
Travel status begins when the traveler leaves the built-up area of his city and ends when he intends to reside for 15 days or more.

This lesson clarifies the boundaries of the travel status. The text specifies that a person becomes a traveler when he departs from the houses of his city (biyut al-misr) from the side he leaves. This is a concrete, practical definition. The travel status continues until the traveler has a genuine or effective intention to stay in a place for fifteen days or more, even if that intention is implied by circumstances, such as a pilgrim entering Syria with plans to stay half a month. Understanding these conditions prevents common errors in determining when to shorten prayers.

Guided Path
1
Define the start of travel
Travel begins when the person physically leaves the built-up area of his city from the side he departs. It does not matter if he has not passed the city from the other side; the act of exiting triggers the ruling.
The text says: 'idha fāraqa buyūt al-miṣr min al-jānib alladhi kharaja minhu'.
2
Understand the rationale
Residence (iqamah) is tied to entering the city, so travel is tied to leaving it. This is a logical pairing: you are either a resident (when inside) or a traveler (when outside).
'li’anna al-iqāmah tata‘allaqu bi-dukhūlihā fayata‘allaqu al-safar bi-al-khurūj ‘anhā'.
3
Define the end of travel: literal intention
Travel ends when the traveler intends to reside (niyyat al-iqāmah) in a single place for fifteen days or more. This is a literal intention to stay.
The text says: 'hattā yunwī al-iqāmah haqīqatan'.
4
Define the end of travel: effective (hukmi) intention
Even without a literal statement, an effective intention occurs when circumstances imply the traveler will stay fifteen days or more. Example: a pilgrim enters Syria before Shawwal and plans to leave with a caravan mid-Shawwal – he is effectively intending at least fifteen days.
The example: 'ka mā law dakhala al-ḥājj al-Shām qabla dukhūl Shawwāl ... a-tamma'.
5
Apply the condition of suitable residence
The place of intended residence must be suitable for dwelling: a city, a village, or even a desert for tent dwellers (ahl al-akhbiyah). The key is that the traveler must settle in a location conducive to normal life.
The text mentions 'fī balad wāḥid, aw mā fī ḥukmihā mimmā yaṣluḥ li-al-iqāmah'.

Traveler's Prayer: Two Rak'ahs Suffice with Sitting Condition
Ruling on traveler's prayer and condition of sitting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the traveler's four-rak'ah prayer and explain why sitting after the second rak'ah is essential for validity.

Core Idea
A traveler's four-rak'ah prayer is invalid without sitting after two rak'ahs because the extra nafl corrupts the fard.

This passage from Al-Lubab presents a unique Hanafi ruling: when a traveler prays a four-rak'ah obligatory prayer, only the first two count for the obligation, and the remaining two become supererogatory (nafl). However, this transformation depends on sitting for tashahhud after the second rak'ah. Without that sitting, the prayer is invalid due to mixing the nafl with the fard before completion. Additionally, the passage clarifies when the travel prayer begins—upon leaving the built-up area of the town.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a traveler performing a four-rak'ah fard prayer (e.g., Zuhr). The ruling: the first two rak'ahs suffice for the obligation, the last two are nafl. If he does not sit after the second rak'ah for the amount of tashahhud, the prayer is invalid.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof is rational (istihsan): 'because the supererogatory becomes mixed with it before its completion.' The Hanafi school uses istihsan to give precedence to order and separation.
The proof is not a text but a legal reasoning (dalil aqli).
3
Trace the reasoning
Sitting after two rak'ahs distinguishes the fard portion from the nafl. Without it, the sequence is corrupted—the nafl interferes before the fard is completed, invalidating the entire prayer.
This is specific to the traveler; otherwise, sitting for tashahhud is wajib, not a rukn, but here omission invalidates due to mixing.
4
Note the condition for travel
The travel prayer (qasr) begins when the traveler leaves the houses of the town (misr) from the side he is departing. It is not required to leave from all sides.
Residence is established by entering the town; travel begins by exiting.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence
If a traveler prays four rak'ahs intending the extra two as nafl, he must sit after the second. If he omits the sitting, his prayer is invalid, and he must repeat it as two rak'ahs.
Some may think the prayer is valid as a full four-rak'ah fard, but that is not the Hanafi position.

When a Traveler Becomes a Resident: The 15-Day Intention Rule
Rulings on when a traveler becomes a resident by intention to stay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a Hanafi traveler becomes obliged to perform full prayers, and apply them to scenarios including constructive intention and Bedouin residence.

Core Idea
A traveler becomes a resident by intending fifteen days in one place — intention less than that leaves travel rulings intact.

The Hanafi school draws a clear line between traveler and resident: it is not the number of days spent that matters, but the intention. This passage from al-Lubab explains that as long as a traveler does not intend to stay fifteen days or more in one location, the concessions of travel remain. However, once that intention is formed — even constructively — the traveler must complete his prayers. Understanding this rule is essential for calculating prayer obligations in journey scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage begins by stating the overarching principle: a traveler remains under the rulings of travel until he intends residence, either actually or constructively.
Note that the rulings of travel include the permission to shorten prayers (qasr) and break fast during Ramadan.
2
Determine the conditions for residency
The intention must be for fifteen days or more in one specific location suitable for settlement (city, village, or desert for Bedouins with sufficient provisions).
Intending residence in two separate places, like Makkah and Mina, is invalid according to the text.
3
Apply the constructive intention example
A pilgrim who enters Syria before Shawwal and plans to leave with the caravan in mid-Shawwal is considered to have intended residence constructively because he knows his stay will exceed fifteen days.
Constructive intention (hukman) applies when the circumstances clearly indicate a prolonged stay, even without an explicit verbal intention.
4
Examine the Bedouin exception
For Bedouins (people of tents), the desert within dar al-Islam is a valid place of residence if they have sufficient water and pasture for the intended period.
This exception recognizes that for Bedouins, the desert is their home; non-Bedouins cannot intend residence in the desert.
5
Analyze the waiting scenario
If a traveler enters a city without a fifteen-day intention but is simply waiting for departure (e.g., saying 'I will go tomorrow or the day after'), he must complete his prayers if he has already traveled the required distance.
If he has not traveled the distance, he is not a traveler at all and must complete prayers anyway.

A traveler who follows a resident imam must complete the full prayer because following overrides his license to shorten.

Traveller Following a Resident: Within Time vs Make-Up Prayer
Ruling with talil (reasoning) on traveller following resident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for a traveller following a resident imam in both current and make-up prayers, explain the reasoning based on the role of time, and apply it to determine validity.

Core Idea
A traveller's obligation changes only while the current prayer time lasts — make-up prayers retain the original obligation, preventing valid following.

This passage presents two contrasting rulings for a traveller who follows a resident imam. In a current prayer, the traveller's fard changes to four, making his prayer valid. But in a make-up prayer, the change does not occur, leading to a mismatch that invalidates the prayer. Understanding the cause—the prayer time—is key to applying these rules correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the two distinct cases: (1) traveller joins resident within the current prayer time, (2) traveller joins resident in making up a missed prayer after its time.
The passage also notes an exception for non-quartered prayers (Fajr, Maghrib) where there is no change due to travel.
2
Examine the cause (ʿilla) and its effect
The ruling hinges on the prayer time being present. Within time, the cause (time) is active and can change the traveller's obligation from two to four, analogous to intending to stay (niyyat al-iqama). After the time, the cause has ended, so the obligation remains unchanged.
This is a clear example of taʿlīl (reasoning) based on an effective cause.
3
Apply the rule to the within-time scenario
If the traveller joins the resident's current prayer (even at the end, provided he catches the takbir), his prayer is valid and he must complete four rakʿahs because his fard changes by following the imam.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the rule to the make-up scenario
If the traveller joins the resident in making up a four-rakʿah prayer after its time, his prayer is invalid. His fard remains two, leading to a mismatch: the resident's first sitting (wajib) becomes a fard sitting for the traveller, or the resident's recitation in the last two is nafl while for the traveller it is fard.
The mismatch means the traveller is following a supererogatory act with a fard, which is invalid.
5
Generalise to non-quartered prayers
If the missed prayer is Fajr (2) or Maghrib (3), travel does not change the rakʿah count, so following is valid even in make-up. This exception reinforces the rule.

Ruling on a Traveler Following a Resident in a Missed Four-Rak'ah Prayer

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A traveler's fard remains two rak'ahs even in make-up prayers; following a resident's four creates a structural mismatch that invalidates the prayer.

Consider this: you are a traveler who missed Zuhr. You find a resident imam leading the make-up prayer. Can you join him? In the Hanafi school, the answer is no, and the reason lies in the nature of a traveler's obligation. This passage from Al-Lubab explains why the prayer becomes invalid despite the common time of performance. We will walk through the legal reasoning and the critical distinction between fard and nafal rak'ahs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a traveler following a resident imam in a missed four-rak'ah prayer. The ruling is that the prayer is batil (invalid).
Batil means the prayer has no legal effect and must be repeated.
2
Locate the underlying principle
The traveler's obligation does not change after the time expires, just as it does not change by merely intending residence. The cause for qasr (travel) is still present.
This principle is key: once a traveler, always qasr for missed prayers.
3
Analyze the mismatch scenario
Consider two possibilities: if the traveler joins in the first two rak'ahs, his obligatory sitting (tashahhud) occurs while the imam stands for the third, breaking the connection. If he joins in the last two, his obligatory recitation is built on the imam's nafal recitation (since the imam's fard recitation was in the first two).
In both cases, the follower's fard is built on the imam's nafal or misplaced.
4

In the first two rak'ahs, the follower's fard is sitting (which is wajib in the two-rak'ah prayer) but the imam stands, so the follower cannot properly follow. In the last two, the follower's fard is recitation (which is fard for him) but the imam's recitation is nafal, leading to building fard on nafal.
Hanafis require the follower's fard to align with the imam's fard in kind, though not in degree.
5
Recognize the scholarly difference
Other schools like Shafi'i may permit this, but Hanafis hold it invalid due to the structural mismatch.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is mufta bihi.

The commentator, al-Maydani, reports that the preferred view in the commentaries is that of Abu Hanifa, and it was adopted by al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi. The text further clarifies that the congregation must remain until the imam performs the first prostration (sajdah) of the first rak'ah. If they disperse after that, the imam may complete the prayer alone as Jumu'ah; if they disperse before, the Jumu'ah is invalid because the condition of congregation is not fulfilled.

Key Terms
جماعة (jamā'ah): A congregation consisting of at least a certain number of men besides the imam for the validity of Friday prayer.

Recitation in Friday Prayer: Jahr and Choice of Surah
Ruling on jahr and surah choice in Friday prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on recitation in Friday prayer and distinguish between the sunnah of jahr and the mubah nature of surah choice.

Core Idea
The imam recites aloud in Friday prayer, but no surah is mandatory—variety prevents misconception of obligation.

This passage clarifies two key points about recitation in Friday prayer according to the Hanafi school. First, the imam recites aloud in both rak'ahs, based on the inherited practice of the Prophet (s). Second, there is no required surah; while certain surahs are recommended, the imam may choose any surah. The text also warns against always reciting the same surahs to avoid the public thinking they are obligatory.

Commentary
This passage covers two rulings regarding the recitation in Friday prayer. First, the imam must recite aloud in both rak'ahs. This is based on the continuous practice (mutawarith) from the Prophet. Second, there is no prescribed surah that must be recited; it is permissible to recite any surah. However, the commentator quotes Sharh al-Tahawi recommending Surah al-Jumu'ah and al-Munafiqun, and al-Zahidi recommending Surah al-A'la and al-Ghashiyah. But al-Bahr cautions against consistently reciting the same surahs lest laypeople believe they are obligatory. Thus, while there are recommended surahs, they are not required, and it is better to vary the recitation to avoid creating a false obligation.
