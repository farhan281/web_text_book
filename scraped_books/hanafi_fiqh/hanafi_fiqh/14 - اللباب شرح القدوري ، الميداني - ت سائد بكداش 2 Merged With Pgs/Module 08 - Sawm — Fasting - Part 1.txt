Definition and Divisions of Fasting in the Hanafi School
Definition of sawm and its two categories with a terminological preview.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of sawm according to the Hanafi school, identify its key elements, and distinguish between the broad and narrow uses of 'wajib'.

Core Idea
Fasting is abstention from nullifiers with intention — and 'wajib' means both 'obligatory' in general and a specific level below fard.

This opening passage of the Book of Fasting establishes the foundational definition and classification of sawm. The author, Imam al-Quduri, begins with linguistic and legal meanings, then divides fasting into obligatory and voluntary. Pay close attention to the nuanced use of the term 'wajib' — it will be used differently in later sections, where it becomes a technical intermediate between fard and nafl.

Guided Path
1
Parse the linguistic vs. legal definition
Identify the two definitions: linguistically, 'imsak' is absolute abstention; legally, it is restricted to abstention from nullifiers with additional conditions.
The legal definition adds specificity and legal consequences.
2
Break down the legal definition's components
Extract the four elements: (1) abstention from nullifiers, (2) either literally (haqiqah) or by legal ruling (hukman), (3) at a specific time (waqt makhsus), and (4) with intention from a qualified person (ahl al-niyyah).
The hukman aspect covers cases like forgetfulness.
3
Distinguish the two categories
Note that sawm is divided into wajib (obligatory) and nafl (voluntary). Here 'wajib' is used generically against nafl.
Do not yet confuse with the later technical distinction from fard.
4
Understand the Hanafi wajib/fard distinction
The text signals that later 'wajib' will be restricted to a level between fard and nafl, established by probable evidence (zanni), while fard is from definitive evidence (qat'i).
This is a key Hanafi principle; Shafi'i uses fard for both.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Fasting. It defines sawm both linguistically and legally. Linguistically, it is absolute abstention from anything. Legally, it is a specific abstention: from nullifiers (e.g., food, drink, marital relations) either literally (actual abstention) or legally (e.g., one who eats forgetfully is still considered keeping the fast by legal ruling). Conditions include a specific time (from dawn to sunset) and intention from a qualified person (Muslim, sane). The passage then divides fasting into two categories: wajib (obligatory) and nafl (voluntary). It clarifies a Hanafi terminological nuance: here 'wajib' is used broadly to include any obligatory fast in contrast to nafl. However, the author notes that later in the book, 'wajib' will be used in a narrower sense, distinct from 'fard', as in the distinction between the fast of Ramadan (fard) and a vowed fast (wajib). This is a distinctive Hanafi principle where fard is established by definitive evidence (Qur'an, mutawatir sunnah) and wajib by probable evidence.

Key Terms
الصوم (al-sawm): Legally: abstention from nullifiers with intention at a specific time.
الإمساك (al-imsak): Abstention; linguistically absolute, legally restricted to nullifiers.
المفطّرات (al-mufattirat): Things that break the fast, such as eating, drinking, sexual intercourse.
حقيقة (haqiqah): Literally; actual abstention from nullifiers.
حكماً (hukman): According to legal ruling; e.g., one who eats forgetfully is legally considered fasting.
نية (niyyah): Intention; a required condition for the validity of the fast.
ضربان (darban): Two types: wajib and nafl.
واجب (wajib): Obligatory; here used broadly to include all obligatory fasts, but later distinguished from fard.
نفل (nafl): Voluntary, supererogatory.
فرض (fard): Strictly obligatory, established by definitive evidence.

Definition of Fasting and Its Divisions
Definition and primary division of fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of fasting, list its two primary divisions, and explain the dual usage of the term 'wajib'.

Core Idea
Fasting in Sharia is intentional abstinence with conditions; it is either obligatory or voluntary.

This lesson introduces the concept of fasting (sawm) as defined by the Hanafi school. Imam al-Quduri and his commentator al-Maydani establish the foundational definition and the primary categorization into obligatory (wajib) and voluntary (nafl). Understanding this definition is essential before studying the detailed rulings of fasting.

Guided Path
1
1. Understand the linguistic meaning
Start with the linguistic definition: 'al-sawm' means absolute abstention. This is the basis for the legal definition.
2
2. Study the legal definition
The legal definition adds conditions: abstention from nullifiers (in reality or by legal ruling) at a specified time, with intention from a person qualified to fast.
3
3. Identify the two divisions
The text states that fasting is of two types: wajib (obligatory) and nafl (voluntary). Note that here 'wajib' includes fard and other obligatory fasts.
4
4. Recognize the dual usage of 'wajib'
The commentator clarifies that 'wajib' can mean the opposite of nafl (as in this passage), or it can be an intermediate category between fard and nafl (as in later passages distinguishing Ramadan as fard and vowed fast as wajib).
This distinction is a unique feature of Hanafi usul.

For time-specific obligatory fasts, intention can be made until before midday, defined from dawn to the greater forenoon.

Timing of Intention for Ramadan and Specified Nadhr
Ruling on timing of niyyah for Ramadan and specified nadhr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the timing of niyyah for time-specific wajib fasts and distinguish the relied position from Zufar's opinion.

Core Idea
For Ramadan and specified nadhr, intention can be after dawn before half the day, not strictly at night.

This passage addresses a practical question: if you forget to intend for a wajib fast at night, can you still intend in the morning? The Hanafi school, based on the authority of al-Jami' al-Saghir, allows it up to half the day. But what exactly is 'half the day'? The commentary refines the limit to al-dahwa al-kubra, roughly 50 minutes before zawal, to ensure the intention covers most of the day. We will also examine an intra-school disagreement with Zufar, who required night intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is fasting a time-specific wajib fast (Ramadan or specified nadhr). The ruling is that intention can be made at night (best) or after dawn until half the day.
The passage uses 'wajib' for the fast category, but Ramadan is fard; the category here is about time-specific obligation.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is from al-Jami' al-Saghir: intention before half the day suffices. The reasoning is that intention must cover most of the day.
The text states 'al-ashahh' (most correct) based on this source.
3
Understand the talil (rationale)
Why half a day? Because the intention must be in the majority of the day. Half the day is defined from dawn to al-dahwa al-kubra, not to zawal.
This refinement ensures the intention is present in the larger part of the day.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Zufar holds that intention after dawn is not valid even before half the day. The text rejects this.
The text says 'contrary to Zufar' without detailing his proof.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied position is the majority view: intention before half the day is valid. This is reinforced by al-Hidayah.
The mufta bihi applies to both resident and traveler.

Commentary

The author first states the ruling from the matn: it is permissible to make intention at night, which is the best. Individual Sighting and Testimony with Obstruction
Rulings on individual sighting and testimony with obstruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on individual crescent sighting and on accepting one witness when the sky is obstructed, and explain the reasoning based on analogy to riwayah.

Core Idea
Personal certainty of the crescent obligates fasting; one upright witness suffices when sky is cloudy, like narrating hadith.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents two distinct but related rulings on crescent sighting for Ramadan. First, it addresses the individual who sees the crescent alone: does he fast even if the imam rejects his testimony? Second, it addresses the imam: how many witnesses does he accept when the sky is obstructed? The answers reveal the Hanafi school's nuanced approach, balancing personal obligation with communal authority and drawing an insightful analogy between crescent sighting and the transmission of religious reports (riwayah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two separate rulings
The matn gives two distinct cases: (1) a person who sees the crescent alone, and (2) the imam's acceptance of testimony when the sky is obstructed. Notice that the first ruling is about the individual's personal obligation, while the second is about the imam's procedure.
The passage uses 'wa' (and) to connect the two rulings, but they are logically independent.
2
Analyze the individual sighting ruling
For the individual: he must fast (صام) even if the imam rejects his testimony. The reason given is that he is personally obligated by his knowledge. If he breaks the fast, he owes qada' but no kaffarah due to the doubt (shubhat al-radd) created by the rejection.
Shubhat al-radd is the doubt that prevents the full expiation; it is not a valid excuse to break the fast intentionally.
3
Analyze the testimony with obstruction ruling
When the sky is obstructed (clouds, dust, etc.), the imam may accept the testimony of a single upright witness ('adl). This includes both men and women, free and slave. The commentary adds that a mastur (one whose justice is not apparent but not known to be evil) is also acceptable according to the correct view.
The condition of 'adalah is required; a known sinner's testimony is rejected in religious matters.
4
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The ruling is justified by analogy to narrating hadith (riwayah). Crescent sighting is a religious matter (amr dini), not a legal claim (huquq al-'ibad), so it does not require the strict conditions of legal testimony (shahadah) such as two male witnesses.
This is a key Hanafi principle: religious reports need credibility, not the two-witness standard.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position differs from the Shafi'i school, which requires two male witnesses for crescent sighting even with obstruction. The Hanafi response is that the hadith cited by the Shafi'is is not strong enough to override the analogy to riwayah.
This difference has practical implications for moon sighting announcements in Muslim communities.

Hilal Testimony in Clear Skies: The Requirement of a Multitude
Ruling with proof: testimony of hilal in clear skies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hilal testimony in clear skies, explain the rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī), and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shāfiʿī position.

Core Idea
In clear skies, a solitary sighting is presumed mistaken; a multitude is required for certainty or overwhelming probability.

When the sky is clear and everyone is looking for the crescent, a single person claiming to have seen it is highly suspect. The Hanafi school therefore rejects solitary testimony in such conditions and requires a large group whose report yields certainty or strong probability. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its rational proof, and the internal and external disagreements surrounding it.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling for clear skies
Identify the core ruling: testimony of hilal sighting is not accepted unless a great multitude (jamaʿ kathīr) reports it, producing certainty or overwhelming probability.
The ruling applies only when the sky is clear (lā ʿillah fī al-samāʾ).
2
Understand the rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī)
Analyse the reasoning: the observation point (maṭlaʿ) is unified, obstacles are absent, eyesight is sound, and everyone is eager to see the crescent. Therefore, a solitary sighting among a huge crowd is likely an error. Hence, multiple witnesses are needed to overcome this presumption.
This is a qiyās based on common experience, not a direct textual proof.
3
Examine the ikhtilāf on the definition of 'great multitude'
Note that Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah does not specify a number. Some scholars delegate it to the imam’s discretion; others (e.g., al-Usbījābī) require witnesses from different districts to ensure reliability.
The muftā bihi is that it is left to the imam’s judgment.
4
Evaluate the dispute with the Shāfiʿī school
Contrast the Hanafi position with the Shāfiʿī view, which accepts one just witness in all conditions. Understand the practical consequence: under clear skies, a single witness is insufficient in the Hanafi school but sufficient in the Shāfiʿī school.
The Hanafi tarjīḥ is based on the strength of the rational proof.
5
Address the secondary issue: testimony of a repentant qadhf-offender
Recognise that according to Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah, the testimony of one punished for qadhf (false accusation) is accepted after repentance because it is a religious report (riwāyah), not a legal testimony (shahādah). A narration from Abū Ḥanīfa rejects it.
The relied-upon position (muftā bihi) is acceptance.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The qada of Ramadan fasts is obligatory but flexible in timing — delay until the next Ramadan incurs no penalty.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri explains the key rulings on qada (make-up) of missed Ramadan fasts according to the Hanafi school. It covers when qada becomes obligatory, the choice in spacing the make-up days, and the important ruling that delaying make-up until the next Ramadan does not require a fidya. The proof is the Quranic command "فَعِدَّةٌ مِّنْ أَيَّامٍ أُخَرَ" (a number of other days), which establishes the obligation with flexibility in timing.

The passage addresses three situations: (1) illness/travel – no qada required during the excuse, (2) recovery/residency then death – qada proportional to healthy/resident days, (3) delaying qada until the next Ramadan – fast the current Ramadan first, then make up missed days, no fidya.
Focus on the last scenario as it is a distinctive Hanafi position.

Qada is fard (obligatory) based on the Quranic verse. The term 'فَعِدَّةٌ' indicates a quantified make-up, but the indefinite 'أَيَّامٍ أُخَرَ' allows flexibility in when these days are performed.

The text states 'إن شاء فرقه وإن شاء تابعه' – the person may make up the missed days separately or consecutively. The recommended (mustahabb) is consecutively to hasten discharge of the obligation.
Choice is given; the rule is permissibility, not obligation.
4
Understand the delay ruling
If the next Ramadan arrives before qada is completed, one must fast the current Ramadan as ada (performance), then make up the missed days after. No fidya is required because the obligation of qada is ala al-tarakhi (with latitude).
Contrast with schools that mandate immediate qada.

The dalil is Quran 2:184. In Hanafi usul, the imperative for qada is not restricted to immediate performance (mujarrad al-amr la yufid al-fawr). The flexibility is derived from the absence of a time constraint in the text.
The Hidayah quote confirms this position.

( وإن صح المريض ، أو أقام المسافر، ثم ماتا: لزمهمـا القضاء بقدر الصحة والإقامة ؛ لوجود الإدراك بهذا المقدار، وفائدته: وجوب الوصية بالإطعام.
( وقضاء رمضان) مخيَّرٌ فيه : ( إن شاء فرَّقه، وإن شاء تابعه) ؛ لإطلاق النص ( وهو قول الله تعالى: ﴿فَعِدَّةٌ مِّنْ أَيَّامٍ أُخَرَ. البقرة / ١٨٤ )، لكنَّ المستحب المتابعة؛ مسارعة إلى إسقاط الواجب.
( وإن أخره حتى دخل رمضان آخر: صام رمضان الثاني)؛ لأنه وقته، حتى لو نواه عن القضاء : لا يقع إلا عن الأداء، كما تقدم، (وقضى الأول بعده) ؛ لأنـه وقـت القضاء ، (ولا فدية عليه)؛ لأن وجوب القضاء على التراخي، حتى كان له أن يتطوع. «هداية».
The Book of Fasting. It does not obligate them to make up. If the sick person recovers, or the traveler becomes resident, then they die: they are obligated to make up to the extent of health and residency. Making up Ramadan: if he wishes he may separate them, and if he wishes he may make them consecutive. If he delays it until another Ramadan enters: he fasts the second Ramadan, and makes up the first after it, and no expiation is upon him. Sickness and travel: (it does not obligate them to make up) because they did not attain the countable number of other days. (If the sick person recovers, or the traveler becomes resident, then they die: they are obligated to make up to the extent of health and residency) because of the existence of attainment to this extent, and its benefit is the obligation of a will to feed. (Making up Ramadan) has a choice in it: (if he wishes he may separate, and if he wishes he may make consecutive) due to the absolute nature of the text (which is the saying of Allah the Exalted: then a number of other days. Al-Baqarah 2:184), but the recommended is consecutive; hastening to discharge the obligation. (If he delays it until another Ramadan enters: he fasts the second Ramadan) because it is its time, so that if he intends it for the make-up, it does not occur except as performance, as has preceded. (And he makes up the first after it) because it is the time of make-up. (And no expiation is upon him) because the obligation of make-up is with latitude, so that he may perform supererogatory acts. Al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sawm in Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri addresses several rulings regarding the make-up (qada) of missed Ramadan fasts. The passage begins with a general statement: sickness and travel do not obligate make-up during the period of the excuse, because the person did not have the ability to fast those days. However, if a sick person recovers or a traveler becomes resident before death, then they are obligated to make up the fasts proportional to the days of health/residency, because they now had the ability. The benefit mentioned is the obligation of a will to feed if they die before making up.

Then the ruling on the timing of qada: the person has the choice to fast the make-up days separately or consecutively, based on the absolute wording of the Quranic verse (Surah al-Baqarah 2:184). However, it is recommended (mustahabb) to make them consecutively to discharge the obligation quickly.

Finally, if one delays qada until the next Ramadan begins, the correct practice is to fast the current Ramadan first (as its time is now), then make up the missed days afterward. There is no expiation (fidya) for this delay because the obligation of qada is open-ended (ala al-tarakhi), meaning it does not have to be done immediately, and one may even perform voluntary fasts before completing the qada. This is supported by a quote from Al-Hidayah.

The proof for the ruling on separation/consecutive is the Quranic verse itself, which does not specify a particular order. The recommended consecutive fast is based on the principle of hastening to fulfill obligations.

This passage reflects the Hanafi position that qada is obligatory but flexible in timing, contrary to some schools that require immediate action.

Key Terms
قضاء (qada'): Making up a missed obligatory act of worship outside its prescribed time.
صوم (sawm): Fasting, specifically abstaining from food, drink, and marital relations from dawn to sunset with intention.
فدية (fidya): A compensatory payment or expiation, usually in the form of feeding a poor person.
