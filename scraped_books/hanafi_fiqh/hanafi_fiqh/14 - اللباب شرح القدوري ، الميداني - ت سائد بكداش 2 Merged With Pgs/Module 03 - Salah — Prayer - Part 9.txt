Women's Dress in Ihram: Covering Head and Uncovering Face
Ruling on women's dress during Hajj and Ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a woman's head and face covering during ihram and identify when she must cover her face.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, a woman in ihram covers her head (fard) and uncovers her face (sunnah), but covers face if non-mahram men are present (wajib).

Women perform most Hajj rituals similarly to men, but there are specific differences regarding dress. This lesson focuses on the unique rule: while a woman's head must always be covered because it is awrah, her face is normally uncovered in ihram. However, the presence of non-mahram men changes the ruling, illustrating the Hanafi principle of preventing fitnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline rule
The author states that women are like men in all hajj rulings except where specified. This establishes the general framework.
The exception is the focus of this lesson.
2
Determine the ruling for the head
The text says 'she does not uncover her head because it is awrah.' In Hanafi fiqh, the head of a free woman is awrah at all times, so covering it is wajib (obligatory) even in ihram.
Awrah here means parts that must be concealed.
3
Determine the default ruling for the face
The text says 'she uncovers her face.' This is the default: the face is not awrah, so uncovering is sunnah (recommended) in ihram.
This is unique to ihram; outside, the face is generally covered.
4
Identify the exception
The text adds: 'but if there is someone from non-mahram men looking at her, then she is prohibited from revealing the face.' This means that in the presence of non-mahram men, covering the face becomes wajib.
The condition triggers a change in ruling due to fitnah (temptation).
5
Understand the underlying principle
The ruling balances the sunnah of uncovering in ihram with the obligation to prevent harm (fitnah). When harm is likely, prevention takes priority.
This is a key Hanafi usul principle: 'al-ḍarar yuzāl' (harm must be removed).

Sadaqa or Dam?: Cutting Scattered Nails in Ihram
Intra-Hanafi dispute on expiation for scattered nails

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on cutting scattered nails in ihram and determine the mufta bihi expiation.

Core Idea
Scattered nails mean multiple violations; each nail requires its own sadaqa.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the expiation for a muhrim who cuts five fingernails scattered across both hands and feet. The disagreement between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hinges on whether scattered nails constitute multiple violations or a single violation, with each view supported by qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two positions
The act: a muhrim cuts five nails from different hands and feet. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say: a sadaqa for each nail (total 5 sadaqas). Muhammad says: one dam.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi; all agree expiation is wajib, but differ on the type.
2
Examine the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf)
They consider each nail a separate violation because the nails come from different limbs. Therefore, each requires its own sadaqa (half sa' of food).
This view is relied upon by al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi.
3
Examine Muhammad's view
Muhammad analogizes (qiyas) to cutting five nails from one palm (which requires a dam) and to shaving a quarter of the head from scattered places (also dam). He argues that the number of nails (five) triggers the dam, regardless of location.
His qiyas uses the asl of 'cutting five nails from one palm' and extends the ruling to scattered nails.
4
Understand the reasoning behind the ikhtilaf
The key is whether 'five nails' is considered a single violation (Muhammad) or whether each location creates a separate violation (Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf). The word 'scattered' (متفرقة) is contested.
The majority view emphasises multiplicity of locations; Muhammad emphasises the number of nails.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: a sadaqa per nail. This is the view adopted by the later Hanafi authorities (al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi).
Thus, in fatwa, each scattered nail requires a separate sadaqa.

Returning to Arafat After Departing Before the Imam
Explaining the penalty for leaving Arafat before Imam and effect of return with intra-Hanafi narration conflict.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the dam for leaving Arafat before the Imam, identify the two narrations (al-Asl vs Ibn Shuja'), and apply the mufta bihi position to determine whether returning before sunset drops the penalty.

Core Idea
Returning to Arafat before sunset drops the dam; after sunset, the penalty is fixed.

Al-Quduri’s matn states that departing Arafat before the Imam incurs a dam (penalty). However, the extended commentary from al-Badai’ reveals a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school: does returning to Arafat cancel the obligation? The answer hinges on timing and the concept of istidrak (catching up a missed wajib). This lesson unpacks the three scenarios of return, the conflicting narrations, and the preferred position adopted by al-Quduri.

Guided Path
1
State the basic ruling from Al-Quduri
Identify the act (departing Arafat before the Imam) and its consequence (wajib dam).
The 'Imam' here implies after sunset, so departing before sunset triggers the dam.
2
Examine the three return scenarios
The text distinguishes: (1) return before sunset and before Imam leaves, (2) return before sunset after Imam leaves, (3) return after sunset. For each, note whether the dam drops.
Scenario (3) is unanimously no drop; (1) and (2) are disputed.
3
Understand istidrak
Istidrak means catching up the omitted wajib. Here, the wajib is the departure after sunset. If the pilgrim returns before sunset and then departs after sunset, he has performed the wajib and the penalty drops.
This principle is the basis for the narration of Ibn Shuja'.
4
Compare the two narrations
Narration of al-Asl: dam does not drop under any circumstances. Narration of Ibn Shuja': dam drops if return before sunset. The dispute arises from disagreement about the cause (sabab) of the dam.
According to Ibn Shuja', the cause is departing before sunset; catching the sunset removes the cause.
5
Apply al-Quduri's tarjih
Al-Quduri preferred the narration of Ibn Shuja', declaring it correct and the al-Asl narration contradictory. The mufta bihi position is that returning before sunset drops the dam.
Authority weight: nawadir (rarer narration) but preferred because it aligns with the objective of the rite.

Delay of Halq and Tawaf al-Ziyarah: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Dam
Intra-Hanafi dispute on delay penalty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on delaying halq and tawaf al-ziyarah, explain the Sahibayn's opposing view, and identify the tarjih authority that establishes the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Delaying halq or tawaf al-ziyarah beyond the Days of Nahr triggers a dam according to Abu Hanifa, not the Sahibayn.

In Hajj, each ritual has a prescribed time and order. This passage from al-Lubab examines what happens when a pilgrim delays shaving the head (halq) or the essential circumambulation (tawaf al-ziyarah) beyond the Days of Nahr. The Hanafi school itself has a disagreement: Abu Hanifa requires a compensatory sacrifice (dam), while his two chief disciples hold that no penalty is due. The commentator then resolves the dispute by affirming Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon position.

1
Identify the disputed act
Note the two rituals in question: halq (shaving the head) and tawaf al-ziyarah (the essential circumambulation). Both have a specific time: the Days of Nahr (10th–13th of Dhul Hijjah).
Halq is wajib; tawaf al-ziyarah is a rukn.
2
State the positions
Abu Hanifa holds that delaying either ritual beyond its time incurs a dam. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that nothing is required.
The text does not give their proofs, only the conclusion.
3
Recognize the broader ikhtilaf
The same disagreement applies to delaying the stoning (rami) and to performing rituals out of order (e.g., shaving before stoning).
This shows the principle extends to other inversions.

Identify the tarjih
Later authoritative scholars (al-Isbijabi, Burhan al-Shari'ah, Sadr al-Shari'ah, al-Nasafi) confirm that the correct position is Abu Hanifa's.
Thus the mufta bihi is that a dam is required.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, a Hanafi mufti would rule that a pilgrim who delays halq or tawaf al-ziyarah without valid excuse must offer a dam.
The dam is a sheep or a share in a camel/cow.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri is a commentary on the chapter of offenses of the muhrim (the person in the state of ihram). It is not permissible to give less than half a sa' per person, as the food is to be given according to the customary measure in the Shari'ah. Ruling with proof via qiyas

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two non-muhrims jointly killing game in the Haram owe one penalty because the guarantee is for the place, not the person.

This lesson explains the ruling when two individuals who are not in ihram (non-muhrims) jointly kill game within the sacred precinct of Mecca (al-Haram). The Hanafi school holds that they are liable for a single penalty divided between them, based on the principle that the prohibition here attaches to the sanctity of the place, similar to financial damages. An analogy from accidental killing (diyah) illustrates the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act is two non-muhrims (halalan) jointly killing game within the boundaries of al-Haram (the sacred precinct of Mecca). Note that the actors are not in a state of ihram.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that they owe one collective penalty (jaza' wahid) divided between them. The penalty is not multiplied by the number of participants.
3
Understand the rationale
The text explains that the guarantee (daman) is for the sanctity of the Haram (hurmat al-Haram), which is one unified prohibition. Therefore, it is treated like financial damages to property: if two people jointly cause damage to a single item, they share one compensation.

The Hidayah provides an analogy: two men who accidentally kill a man owe one diyah (blood money) between them, but each must also offer a separate kaffarah (expiation). The shared diyah corresponds to the shared penalty for game, while the personal kaffarah for killing game is not detailed here but may be personal.
The analogy shows that liability for a single object (the victim's life) is shared, while personal sins require individual expiation.
5
Distinguish from muhrims
If the killers were muhrims (in ihram), each would owe a full penalty because each violates their own personal ihram. The key distinction is whether the prohibition arises from a personal state (ihram) or from the sanctity of the place (Haram).
This distinction is critical for understanding the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).
