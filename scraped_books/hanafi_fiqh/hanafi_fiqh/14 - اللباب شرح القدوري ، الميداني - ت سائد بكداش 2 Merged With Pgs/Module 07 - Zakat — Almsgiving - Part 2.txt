Dispute on Nisab for 'Ushr: Imam vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nisab for 'ushr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the nisab for 'ushr, identify their respective proofs, and apply the dispute to a given agricultural scenario.

Core Idea
Imam says 'ushr on all produce; Sahibayn require nisab and storability.

This lesson explores a classic intra-Hanafi disagreement on the conditions for the tithe ('ushr) on agricultural produce. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that 'ushr is due on everything the earth yields, regardless of quantity or storability, while his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, insist on a minimum of five awsuq (aqiq) and that the crop must be storable. Understanding this dispute clarifies the school's internal legal reasoning and the practical differences in zakat obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify each imam's ruling
Read the passage carefully. Locate the statements of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. State in your own words what triggers the obligation of 'ushr according to each.
Pay attention to the exceptions in Abu Hanifa's position (firewood, qasab, hashish).
2
Analyze the proofs (adilla)
Identify the evidence each side uses. Abu Hanifa relies on the nature of 'ushr as a land due (kharaj-like), while the Sahibayn cite the hadith about a nisab of five awsuq. Note how the passage explains the reasoning behind each position.
Imam's proof: 'ushr is like maintenance (mu'na); Sahibayn's proof: the hadith 'la sadaqa fi ma duna khamsati awsuq'.
3
Understand the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider a farmer who harvests 3 awsuq of wheat. Apply each position: what is the 'ushr obligation? Then consider a farmer who harvests perishable fruits like mangoes. How does each view apply?
The dispute has real financial implications for farmers.
4
Evaluate the later Hanafi fatwa
The passage does not specify which opinion is preferred. Research or reason: which view is usually adopted in later Hanafi fiqh, and why might that be?
Many later authorities (e.g., al-Marghinani) favor the Sahibayn because of the clear hadith.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri begins the chapter on the zakat of crops and fruits (Zakat al-Zuru' wa al-Thimar). The author first presents the position of Imam Abu Hanifa: the tithe ('ushr) is due on everything the earth produces, whether little or much, regardless of the irrigation method (flowing water or rainwater). The only exceptions are firewood, qasab (a type of reed), and hashish (wild grass) — these are not normally cultivated and are incidental growth on the edges of land. However, the commentary from al-Jawhara clarifies that if one intentionally plants these and irrigates them, the tithe becomes due. Then the passage presents the differing view of the two companions (al-Sahibayn), Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani: the tithe is only obligatory on produce that can be stored (thamra baqiya) for a year, such as wheat, barley, dates, and raisins, and only if the crop reaches a minimum quantity of five awsuq. The wasq is defined as 60 sa', with the sa' being the Prophet's sa' (about 3.64 kg). The reason for Imam Abu Hanifa's position is that 'ushr is like kharaj (land tax) — it is a due on the land itself and does not require the passage of a year. Therefore, it is obligatory on all output, even from the land of minors, the insane, etc., and it is taken even if the owner has debts, from the estate of the deceased, and from waqf land. The dispute is not about the obligation itself but about the conditions (nisab and storability). The intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is clearly demonstrated: Imam without nisab, Sahibayn with nisab. Later Hanafi practice often adopts the Sahibayn's view due to the hadith, but the text does not explicitly prefer one over the other.

Key Terms
العشر (al-'ushr): The tithe (one-tenth) due on agricultural produce; a type of zakat.
الوسق (al-wasq): A volume measure equal to sixty sa' by the Prophet's sa', used as the nisab for 'ushr by the Sahibayn.
السيح (al-sayh): Flowing surface water used for irrigation, such as from a river or spring.

No 'Ushr on Vegetables? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling that 'ushr is not due on vegetables according to the Sahibayn, with commentary on the dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding 'ushr on vegetables, explain the rationale for each, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The Sahibayn require lasting fruit for 'ushr; the Imam does not — the mufta bihi follows the Imam.

This passage presents an internal Hanafi disagreement: do vegetables (including fruits that do not store) attract 'ushr? The Sahibayn say no, because they lack durable fruit; Imam Abu Hanifa says yes. The commentator then notes that later authorities prefer the Imam's view. This lesson will unpack the arguments and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling from the Sahibayn
The Sahibayn rule that there is no 'ushr on khudrawat. Note that khudrawat includes both fruits like apples and greens like leeks — anything that does not keep for a year.
The term 'khudrawat' is defined broadly.
2
Understand the Rationale
The reason given is 'li-'adam al-thamarah al-baqiyah' (due to the lack of lasting fruit). The Sahibayn require the produce to be storable for a year, which vegetables are not.
This is a condition (shart) they impose.
3
Identify the Two Disputed Points
The commentator explicitly states the dispute has two points: (1) requirement of nisab, and (2) requirement of lasting fruit. The Sahibayn require both; the Imam requires neither for the obligation of 'ushr.
Thus the disagreement is over preconditions, not the obligation itself.
4
Note the Tarjih and Mufta Bihi
The commentator reports that al-Tuhfa, al-Nasafi, and Sadr al-Shari'ah all considered Imam Abu Hanifa's view correct. Therefore the mufta bihi (operative fatwa) is the Imam's position: 'ushr is due on all produce, including vegetables.
This is an example of later Hanafi masters settling ikhtilaf.
5
Recognize the Exception for Trade
A footnote from al-Jawhara clarifies that if vegetables are grown for trade, then zakat as trade goods (zakat al-tijarah) becomes obligatory by consensus when the value reaches 200 dirhams, regardless of the dispute.
This shows the dispute is specific to agricultural zakat, not commercial.

Zakat on Vegetables and Irrigation: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on vegetables and agreed irrigation rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on zakat of vegetables, explain the role of lasting fruit and nisab, and apply the ruling on irrigation rates.

Core Idea
Zakat on vegetables is due according to Abu Hanifa but not his students – the key condition is lasting fruit.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on whether vegetables (including fruits and greens) are subject to ushr. The Sahibayn require the crop to have a lasting fruit (thamara baqiyah) that can be stored, while Imam Abu Hanifa does not. The passage also resolves the rate for artificially irrigated crops – a point of agreement. Let's trace the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and the act
What is the ruling being debated? The text states: 'And there is no tithe on vegetables according to them both (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).' So the Sahibayn exempt vegetables from ushr. Note that 'vegetables' includes fruits like apples and greens like leeks.
The term khudrawāt is defined in the commentary as fruits and greens without lasting fruit.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's condition: lasting fruit
The Sahibayn require the crop to have a lasting fruit (thamara baqiyah) that can be stored. Vegetables perish quickly, so they do not meet this condition. Their evidence: zakat is on wealth that remains, and vegetables do not remain.
This is a qiyas: asl is crops with lasting fruit, hukm is ushr, illa is thamara baqiyah.
3

Imam does not require lasting fruit or nisab. His illa is simply growth from the earth. All crops share this cause, so ushr is due on vegetables. He cites the general command of Quran 6:141.

Under Imam, a farmer owes ushr on any vegetable harvest (rate depends on irrigation). Under Sahibayn, no zakat at all on vegetables unless they are trade goods (then by consensus).
The dispute is about the existence of obligation, not the rate.
5

Regardless of the dispute on vegetables, all agree that artificially irrigated crops (by bucket, waterwheel, or camel) incur half ushr (1/20), while naturally irrigated (rain, river) incur full ushr (1/10). For mixed irrigation, the majority method determines the rate.

Nisab for Non-Measurable Crops: Abu Yusuf vs Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi disagreement on nisab estimation for non-measurable crops.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the nisab for crops not measured by wasq (like saffron and cotton), and apply their valuation methods.

Core Idea
For crops without a standard volume measure, Abu Yusuf values them by the cheapest wasq, while Muhammad requires five amthal of the best crop.

While the Sahibayn agree that nisab is required for zakat on crops, they differ on how to determine the nisab for crops that cannot be measured by wasq, such as saffron and cotton. This passage from al-Lubab presents their two methods: Abu Yusuf uses the value of five wasq of the cheapest measurable crop, while Muhammad requires the crop itself to reach five amthal of the best crop. Let's see the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
What is the nisab for crops like saffron and cotton that have no standard volume measure (wasq)? The Sahibayn agree on nisab (5 wasq) for measurable crops but differ on these.
The text calls them 'mā lā yūsaqu' (non-measurable).
2
State Abu Yusuf's method
Abu Yusuf says: the tithe is obligatory when the value of the crop reaches the value of five wasq of the cheapest crop that falls under wasq (e.g., corn in his time). Rationale: since direct measurement is impossible, value is used, analogous to trade goods (ʿurud al-tijarah).
The text quotes al-Hidayah: 'because legal determination is not possible in it; so value is considered, as in trade goods.'
3
State Muhammad's method
Muhammad says: the tithe is obligatory when the external yield (al-khārij) reaches five amthal (multiples) of the best crop that is measured by wasq. This is a quantity-based approach: the actual produce must equal five times the volume of the best crop's standard wasq.
The text says 'when the yield reaches five multiples of the best.' This is a stricter condition because it requires a larger physical amount.
4
Compare the two methods and their rationale
Abu Yusuf's method uses market value, making it easier to reach nisab if the crop is high-value. Muhammad's method uses physical quantity, which may be harder if the crop is lightweight. Both use qiyas: Abu Yusuf analogizes to trade goods, Muhammad to measurable crops.
The practical difference: for saffron, a small amount can have high value; Muhammad's method might require a much larger harvest.
5
Determine the practical consequence
A farmer with a small amount of high-value saffron may owe zakat under Abu Yusuf but not under Muhammad. The mufti must know which method is followed in his school.
The text does not give a tarjih; both are valid within the Sahibayn.

Zakat Recipients: 'Fi Sabilillah', 'Ibn al-Sabil', and Prohibition for Dhimmis
explaining two zakat categories and a prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the preferred Hanafi interpretation of 'fi sabilillah' for zakat, define 'ibn al-sabil', and explain the prohibition of giving zakat to a dhimmi.

Core Idea
The preferred Hanafi view limits 'fi sabilillah' to stranded fighters; the difference only affects wills, not zakat rulings.

Zakat has eight categories of recipients specified in the Quran. Among them, 'fi sabilillah' and 'ibn al-sabil' have been subject to detailed interpretation by Hanafi scholars. This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri explains the preferred view, the intra-Hanafi differences, and the rule that zakat cannot be given to a dhimmi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories under discussion
The passage explains 'fi sabilillah' and 'ibn al-sabil'. Recognize that both are among the eight zakat recipients listed in Surah al-Tawbah 9:60.
2
Examine the intra-Hanafi differences on 'fi sabilillah'
List the positions: Abu Yusuf says stranded fighters; Muhammad says stranded pilgrims; some say students of knowledge; al-Bada'i' says all acts of obedience. Note that al-Isbijabi declares Abu Yusuf's view as sahih (correct).
This is a valid ikhtilaf; the preferred view is Abu Yusuf's.
3
Understand the concept of 'thamar al-khilaf'
The disagreement in interpretation only affects contexts outside zakat, such as wills and endowments. In zakat distribution itself, all agree that the recipient must be poor (except the worker).
In a will, if someone says 'give in fi sabilillah', the executor follows the preferred interpretation (fighters) or the broader one depending on the school.
4
Define 'ibn al-sabil' precisely
Ibn al-sabil is a traveler who has wealth in his homeland but is in another place with nothing; he may take only enough zakat to return home. If he has provisions and a mount, he is not eligible.
This definition is agreed upon by all Hanafi imams.
5
Apply the rule on paying to one category
The owner may pay zakat to each category or limit himself to one category, even to a single person. The definite article 'al-' in 'al-fuqara' indicates the genus, not plurality, so it does not require distribution to all.
6
State the prohibition for dhimmis
Zakat cannot be given to a dhimmi (non-Muslim subject of the Islamic state) due to the command of the Lawgiver. The disagreement among scholars is only in interpretation of the verse, not in the ruling.
The difference is verbal (lafzī); all agree that zakat is exclusively for Muslims.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter 'On whom Zakat may be given' lists categories of people who are prohibited from receiving Zakat. First, a wealthy slave: since the slave and his property belong to the master, giving Zakat to the slave effectively benefits the master, who may not be deserving. Second, a wealthy child (a minor with a wealthy father): the child is considered wealthy by virtue of the father's wealth, even though the child does not own it. However, if the child is an adult but poor, he may receive Zakat despite his father's wealth because he is no longer considered wealthy by association. The main ruling prohibits giving Zakat to Banu Hashim, the clan of the Prophet (peace be upon him), including the families of Ali, Abbas, Ja'far, Aqil, and Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib. The reason is that Allah has honored them by making the 'impurities of people' (i.e., Zakat) forbidden for them, and instead compensated them with a share from the khums (the fifth of spoils). The text excludes Abu Lahab, the Prophet's uncle who was a bitter enemy, so his descendants may receive Zakat. Additionally, the clients (freed slaves) of Banu Hashim are also prohibited from receiving Zakat because they are considered part of the clan. The commentary includes a hadith from Sahih Muslim that directly supports the prohibition. The footnotes discuss an ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school: the apparent madhhab is absolute prohibition, but a narration from Abu Hanifa permits it if the khums share is not available. Also, Abu Yusuf alone allows Banu Hashim to give Zakat to one another. Voluntary charity (non-Zakat) is permissible for them. This passage is crucial for understanding the social honor of the Prophet's family in Islamic law.

The Prohibition of Zakat to Banu Hashim and Their Clients
Defines prohibited recipients: Banu Hashim and their clients, with proof and internal dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibition of Zakat to Banu Hashim and their clients, explain the reasoning based on hadith and khums compensation, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding exceptional circumstances.

Core Idea
Zakat is forbidden to Banu Hashim because they are honored and compensated by khums; this prohibition extends to their clients.

One of the distinctive rulings in Hanafi Zakat law is the exclusion of the Prophet's family (Banu Hashim) from receiving Zakat. This lesson examines the proof, the definition of Banu Hashim, and the internal scholarly disagreement about whether Zakat may be given to them if their khums share is unavailable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling: Zakat to Banu Hashim is haram
The text explicitly states that Zakat is not given to Banu Hashim because Allah has forbidden them the 'impurities of people' (أوساخ الناس) and compensated them with a share of khums. This indicates a clear prohibition (haram).
The prohibition applies to both the giver and the recipient; giving Zakat to a Hashimi is sinful and the Zakat is not discharged.
2
Understand the dalil: the hadith and the wisdom of khums compensation
The text cites the hadith from Sahih Muslim: 'These charities are only the impurities of people, and they are not permissible for Muhammad or the family of Muhammad.' This is the primary proof. The wisdom is that Banu Hashim are honored by being given a share of the khums as compensation.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear (qati al-dalala).
3
Define who is included in Banu Hashim
The text lists five families: the families of Ali, Abbas, Ja'far, Aqil, and al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib. It explicitly excludes Abu Lahab and his descendants, because he was an enemy of the Prophet. Thus, the prohibition is a honor for those who supported the Prophet.
Note that the definition includes the descendants of these individuals even if they are poor; the honor is permanent.
4
Note the extension to clients (mawali)
The prohibition also applies to the freed slaves (clients) of Banu Hashim. They are considered part of the clan for the purposes of this ruling.
This extension ensures that the ruling cannot be circumvented by giving Zakat to someone closely associated with the clan.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on exceptional circumstances
The apparent madhhab (zahir al-riwayah) is absolute prohibition in all times and circumstances. However, there is a narration from Abu Hanifa (rejected by Ibn Nujaym) that permits giving Zakat to Banu Hashim if they do not receive their khums share. Also, Abu Yusuf alone allowed Banu Hashim to give Zakat to one another. The mufta bihi is the absolute prohibition.
The dispute hinges on whether the khums compensation is a permanent substitute or conditional. The majority holds it is permanent.

Prohibited Recipients of Zakah: Banu Hashim and Their Freed Slaves
Ruling statement specifying prohibited recipients of zakah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the specific descendants of Banu Hashim who are prohibited from receiving zakah and explain the reasoning behind the prohibition, including the exclusion of Abu Lahab's line.

Core Idea
Zakah is prohibited to specific families of Banu Hashim and their freed slaves as an honor, except descendants of Abu Lahab.

This lesson delineates the precise categories of individuals to whom zakah may not be given according to the Hanafi school. While zakah is generally given to the poor and needy, there is an exception for certain descendants of Banu Hashim as a mark of honor. The passage from al-Quduri specifies five families and their freed slaves, explaining why Abu Lahab's line is excluded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited groups
List the five families (Al 'Ali, Al 'Abbas, Al Ja'far, Al 'Aqil, Al al-Harith ibn 'Abd al-Muttalib) and their freed slaves (mawali). Note that these are the only ones prohibited from receiving zakah.
The term 'Al' refers to the family or descendants, not the individual alone.
2
Understand the reason for prohibition
The prohibition is an honor from Allah for their support of the Prophet (peace be upon him) in both Jahiliyyah and Islam. It is not a punishment but a distinction.
This explains why the prohibition is limited to those who supported the Prophet.
3
Recognize the exception of Abu Lahab
Abu Lahab was hostile to the Prophet, so his descendants are not included in the honor. Thus, they may receive zakah if they are poor and Muslim.
The text explicitly excludes Abu Lahab's line from the prohibition.
4
Apply the hadith on freed slaves
The hadith 'Mawla al-qawm minhum' (The freed slave of a people is one of them) extends the ruling to freed slaves of these families, and by even stronger reason, to their current slaves.
This is based on a hadith transmitted by the Companions.
5
Determine the ruling when zakah is given to a prohibited recipient
If zakah is given to any prohibited recipient, the obligation is not fulfilled; the payment must be made again to an eligible recipient.
The zakah is ghayr sahih (invalid) for that portion.

Commentary
This passage defines the obligation of sadaqat al-fitr. It begins by explaining the title: it is called 'zakat al-fitr' because it is attributed to its cause (al-fitr, i.e., breaking the fast). The ruling: it is wajib upon every free Muslim, including minors and the insane, provided they own a nisab amount (any wealth) beyond their basic needs. Basic needs include dwelling, clothing, furniture, horse, weapon, and servants for personal service. The reasoning: these items are considered non-existent because they are needed for one's essential living. Unlike zakat, the nisab for sadaqat al-fitr does not need to be growing (productive) wealth; even worn clothes that are used and have no growth potential trigger the obligation if their value equals nisab. The footnote (1) clarifies that 'wajib' in this context means a category between fard and sunnah (i.e., not as strong as a definitive obligation but still binding). Footnote (2) elaborates that growth (nama) refers to the increase through trade or potential for increase, as required in zakat, but it is not a condition for sadaqat al-fitr. Thus, any surplus wealth beyond basic needs, even if non-productive, renders the obligation.

نصاب (niṣāb): The minimum amount of wealth that triggers the obligation of sadaqat al-fitr, equivalent to 200 dirhams (approx. 612.5 grams of silver) or its value.
الحاجة الأصلية (al-ḥājah al-aṣliyyah): Basic or original needs that are exempt from the nisab calculation, such as home, clothing, furniture, horse, weapon, and servants for service.

Sadaqat al-Fitr: Ruling, Conditions, and Whom to Pay For
Stating the obligation, conditions, and scope of Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on Sadaqat al-Fitr, identify the conditions for its obligation, and determine for whom it must be paid, including the intra-Hanafi dispute about payment from a child's wealth.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib on every free Muslim with nisab above basic needs, for self, minor children, and service slaves, but not for wife.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri lays down the fundamental rules of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It establishes the obligation (wujub), the threshold of wealth (nisab) that triggers it, and who must pay for whom. Understanding these rules is essential because the Hanafi school treats Sadaqat al-Fitr as a wajib duty, distinct from both fard and sunnah, with specific conditions that differ from zakat. This lesson will walk you through each condition and the scope of obligation, including a key disagreement between the founding imams of the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its legal level
The passage states Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib, not fard. Understand why the Hanafi school uses 'wajib' here (due to zanni proof).

2

The payer must give for himself, his young children (and insane poor), and his service slaves.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri opens the chapter on Sadaqat al-Fitr (the obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan). The author states the ruling: Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib (obligatory) upon every free Muslim who possesses a nisab (minimum threshold of wealth) that is surplus to his basic needs—namely, his dwelling, clothes, household furnishings, horse (used for transport), weapon, and slaves designated for his service. The reasoning given is that those basic necessities are considered legally nonexistent for the purpose of obligation, and unlike zakat, growth of the wealth is not required. The obligation holds even for minors and insane persons under guardianship, as their guardian pays on their behalf. The payment is due for oneself, one’s young children (and insane children) who are poor, and one’s slaves solely for service (not trade slaves). The text clarifies that the wife is not included; the husband does not have to pay fitr for her. The passage also notes that the same nisab threshold applies to the prohibition of receiving zakat, the obligation of sacrifice, and fitr. Then attention turns to an intra-Hanafi dispute: If a child has wealth, should the fitr be paid from the child’s wealth or the father’s? According to Abu Hanifah and Abu Yusuf, the father pays from the child’s wealth, while Muhammad al-Shaybani says the father pays from his own. The author of al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) preferred the view of the two imams, and later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi followed that. The passage concludes by distinguishing between slaves for service (for whom fitr is paid) and slaves for trade (for whom zakat is paid, not fitr).

Key Terms
صدقة الفطر (ṣadaqat al-fiṭr): A wajib charity given at the end of Ramadan, calculated per head, from surplus wealth beyond basic needs.
واجبة (wājibah): In this context, a legal obligation that is between fard and sunnah; established by a zanni (probability) proof.
النصاب (al-niṣāb): The minimum threshold of wealth that triggers the obligation; here defined as any surplus above basic needs (masakin, etc.) equal to 200 dirhams or equivalent.
الفاضل عن الحاجة الأصلية (al-fāḍil ʿan al-ḥājah al-aṣliyyah): Surplus wealth after deducting necessary dwelling, clothes, household goods, horse, weapon, and slaves for service.
