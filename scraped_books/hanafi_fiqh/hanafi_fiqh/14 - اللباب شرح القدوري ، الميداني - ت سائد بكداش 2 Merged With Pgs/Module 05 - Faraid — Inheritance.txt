The Hanafi Ruling on the Stillborn: Full Rites vs. Washing Only
Defines when a miscarried child receives full funeral rites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a stillborn child and distinguish between the case with and without istihlal.

Core Idea
A child's sign of life determines full funeral rites; the stillborn is washed and named but not prayed over.

When a child is born but dies shortly after, the legal rites depend on whether any sign of life (istihlal) was observed. The Hanafi school holds that a child who cries out receives full janazah rights. However, there is an internal difference regarding the child who shows no sign: the earlier view of Imam Abu Hanifa excluded washing, while the later relied-upon view includes washing and naming.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The passage distinguishes between a child who cries out (istahalla) and one who does not. Istihlal includes any indication of life such as crying, sneezing, yawning, or emergence of most of the body.
The text specifies criteria: if born head-first, the chest is the marker; if feet-first, the navel.
2
State the Ruling for Each Scenario
A child who shows signs of life is named, washed, shrouded, prayed over, inherits, and is inherited from. A child who does not show signs is only wrapped in a cloth and not prayed over.
This is the basic rule from the matn.
3
Highlight the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Hanifa held that if no sign of life, no ghusl and no prayer. Abu Yusuf and later scholars like al-Tahawi said the stillborn is washed and named, but still no prayer.
The commentary cites al-Hidayah, al-Fath, al-Dirayah, and al-Tahawi from Abu Yusuf as preferring washing.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon Hanafi position is to wash and name the stillborn, but not to perform the janazah prayer. This applies even if the child is not fully formed.
This is the chosen view (al-mukhtar) in later sources.
5
Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: in the earlier view, the stillborn is buried without washing; in the later view, it is washed and named before burial, which changes the burial procedure (e.g., need for a washer and naming ceremony).
The prayer is never performed for a stillborn without life signs in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the rulings for a miscarried or stillborn child. The core ruling from the matn distinguishes between two cases: a child who shows signs of life (istihlal) after birth receives a name, ghusl, kafan, janazah prayer, and inheritance; one who does not show signs is only wrapped in a cloth and not prayed over. The commentary clarifies that istihlal includes any indication of life like crying, sneezing, etc. It also details that if the child emerges head-first, the criterion is the chest emerging; if feet-first, the navel. The intra-Hanafi dispute arises regarding the child that did not cry: the matn says no ghusl and no prayer, but the commentary states that in the chosen view (al-mukhtar), the child is washed even if not fully formed, and is also named. This is attributed to Abu Yusuf and is found in later works like al-Hidayah, al-Fath, and al-Dirayah. Thus, the operative fatwa (mufta bihi) is for washing and naming the stillborn, though the prayer is not performed.

Key Terms
استهل (istahalla): To cry out or give a sign of life after birth.
سقط (siqt): A child born prematurely or dead.
إدراج في خرقة (idraj fi khirqa): Wrapping the stillborn in a cloth without washing or prayer.

Ruling on Missing Hajj and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ihram
Ruling on fawat and dispute over nature of ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fawat, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the nature of ihram after fawat, and evaluate the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
In fawat, the original Hajj ihram remains; one exits by Umrah acts — a dispute with real practical consequences.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a pilgrim who misses the Hajj after entering ihram. While the basic ruling (make up next year, no dam) is agreed upon, a key intra-madhhab dispute arises between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on one side and Abu Yusuf on the other regarding the nature of the ihram after fawat. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying subsequent rulings, such as the validity of a second Hajj ihram or the fulfilment of the lifetime Umrah obligation.

Guided Path
1
State the basic ruling on fawat
Identify the two components: the obligation to make up the missed Hajj the following year, and the absence of any dam (blood sacrifice).
2
Analyse the proof (talil)
Trace the reasoning: the tahlul (exit from ihram) is achieved by the acts of Umrah, which is analogous to the role of the dam for a muhsar (one prevented by an enemy). Hence, no additional penalty is due.
This is a qiyas: fawat is analogous to ihsar.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
Recognise that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the original Hajj ihram remains, while Abu Yusuf holds that it transforms into Umrah ihram.
The relied-upon view (al-sahih) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
4
Follow the thamarah al-khilaf
Work through the two practical consequences: (1) if the pilgrim enters a second Hajj ihram, and (2) whether this Umrah counts as the obligatory Umrah of a lifetime.
According to Abu Yusuf, the second Hajj is a valid qiran; according to the other two, it is an invalid combination of two Hajjs.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Conclude that the operative position for fatwa is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the original ihram remains Hajj, and the Umrah acts merely serve to exit it.
The footnote also notes that the 'obligatory Umrah' mentioned assumes the view that Umrah is obligatory, which is not the relied-upon position (it is sunnah mu'akkadah).

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents an intra-madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the status of a person who misses the Hajj (fawat) after having entered ihram. The passage opens with the basic ruling: he must make up the Hajj the following year and is not required to offer a dam (blood sacrifice). The reason given is that his tahlul (release from ihram) was achieved by performing the acts of Umrah (tawaf, sa'y, halq), analogous to the dam that releases a muhsar (one prevented by an enemy). Since he already discharged his obligation through umrah, no additional penalty is needed. The text then reports a key difference between the founding imams: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the original ihram for Hajj remains intact; he merely exits by doing umrah acts. Abu Yusuf, however, holds that the ihram itself transforms into an ihram for Umrah. The relied-upon position (al-sahih) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. The footnote from al-Jawharah elaborates on the practical consequences (thamar al-khilaf): if this person later enters another Hajj, according to Abu Yusuf it becomes a valid qiran (combining Hajj and Umrah), while according to the other two, it would be an invalid combination of two Hajjs and must be abandoned. Also, this Umrah counts as the obligatory Umrah of one's lifetime according to Abu Yusuf, but not according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. The commentator adds that the reference to 'obligatory Umrah' assumes the view that Umrah is obligatory, which is not the relied-upon position in the madhhab (it is an emphasized sunnah). Thus, this lesson illustrates both the textual ruling and the hermeneutical principles that generate legal differences within the school.

Key Terms
فوات (fawāt): Missing the Hajj after having entered ihram due to staying at Arafah after the appointed time.
تحلل (taḥallul): Coming out of the state of ihram, here achieved by performing the acts of umrah.
محصر (muḥṣar): One who is prevented from completing Hajj or Umrah by an enemy or illness.
