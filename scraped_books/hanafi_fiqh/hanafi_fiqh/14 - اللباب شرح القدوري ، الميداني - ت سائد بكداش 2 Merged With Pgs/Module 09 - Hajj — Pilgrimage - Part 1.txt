Definition and Conditions of Obligation of Hajj
Defines Hajj linguistically and legally, then states obligation conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of Hajj and identify the five conditions that make Hajj obligatory in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Hajj is obligatory once in a lifetime for free, adult, sane, healthy Muslims who can afford the journey.

This lesson unpacks the opening of the Book of Hajj in al-Lubab. It clarifies the linguistic and legal definitions of Hajj and then lists the conditions that trigger its obligation. Understanding these conditions is crucial before studying the rituals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic definition
Read the linguistic definition of 'hajj' as 'absolute intention' and note the nuance that some scholars restrict it to 'intending something great'.
The term 'hajj' with fatha or kasra on the ha means the same thing.
2
Extract the legal definition
Locate the shar'i definition: visiting a specific place (Kaaba) at a specific time (months of Hajj) with specific actions (ihram, tawaf, sa'i, wuquf).
This definition sets apart Hajj from other acts of worship.
3
List the conditions of obligation
From the text: free, Muslim, adult (bulugh), sane ('aql), healthy (sihha), and able to afford provision (zad).
These are shurut al-wujub, not shurut al-ada.
4
Understand the ruling
The text states 'wajib' and immediately clarifies 'ay fard' – obligatory once in a lifetime. Note that in this context, wajib and fard are used interchangeably.
In Hanafi usul, there is a distinction, but the matn treats them as synonymous.

Conditions of Istita'a for Hajj and the Safety Dispute
Author defines istita'a for Hajj and introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute on road safety.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions of istita'a for Hajj and explain the two positions regarding road safety as a condition of obligation or performance.

Core Idea
Without a safe road, is Hajj still obligatory? The Hanafi school splits: Abu Hanifa says no, later authorities say yes but performance is deferred.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri defines the precise conditions that trigger the obligation of Hajj. Beyond having a mount and financial surplus, the safety of the road is critical. However, the Hanafi school has two competing understandings of whether safety is a precondition for the obligation itself or merely for its performance—a distinction with practical consequences for making a will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Hajj is wajib once in a lifetime upon fulfillment of istita'a. The passage lists the components of istita'a.
Istita'a is a condition of obligation (shart al-wujub).
2
List the Conditions of Istita'a
Three elements: 1) a mount (راحلة), 2) surplus beyond dwelling, necessities, and dependents' maintenance, 3) a safe road (أمن الطريق).
Surplus includes funds for self and family until return.
3
Locate the Dispute on Safety
The passage presents two views: safety as a condition of obligation (shart al-wujub) or condition of performance (shart al-ada').
The first view is narrated from Abu Hanifa; the second is preferred in the Hidayah.
4
Understand the Consequence of Each View
If safety is a condition of obligation, Hajj is not obligatory when the road is unsafe—no will required. If it is a condition of performance, Hajj remains obligatory but must be performed when safe; one should make a will if unable.
The mufta bihi position is the latter: safety conditions performance.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Book of Hajj, specifying the conditions for istita'a (ability) that make Hajj obligatory. The author lists three elements: having a mount (راحلة) for the journey and back, having surplus wealth beyond essential needs like dwelling and household items, and having sufficient funds to support dependents until return. The third element is the safety of the road, which is necessary for establishing ability. The other view, attributed to later scholars, is that it is a condition of performance, so the obligation remains but cannot be fulfilled until safety is established. The author cites the Hidayah for this discussion.

شرط الوجوب (shart al-wujub): A condition that must be present for the obligation to arise; if absent, the act is not obligatory.
شرط الأداء (shart al-ada'): A condition for the valid performance of an obligation; if absent, the obligation remains but cannot be performed until the condition is met.

Women's Hajj: The Mahram or Husband Condition
Author states the condition of mahram or husband for women's Hajj obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for a woman's Hajj regarding a mahram or husband and explain the intra-madhhab dispute on whether it is a condition of obligation or performance.

Core Idea
A woman's Hajj is valid without a mahram if the road is safe, but prohibitively disliked if travel exceeds three days.

This lesson builds on the general conditions of Hajj obligation by examining the specific requirement for women: a mahram or husband. The passage explains when this condition applies and introduces an important intra-Hanafi disagreement over its legal nature. Understanding this dispute is key to applying the correct ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its conditional ruling
The text rules on a woman performing Hajj. It states she must have a mahram or husband to accompany her. Identify the act and the condition attached to it.
2
Locate the distance threshold
The condition applies only when the travel distance is three days and nights or more. Note the specific threshold mentioned in the text.
For shorter distances, the condition does not apply.
3
Distinguish between validity and permissibility
The text says 'her Hajj is valid' (يجوز حجها) but performing it without a mahram is 'prohibitively disliked' (يكره تحريماً). Separate the ruling on the act's validity from its moral permissibility.
This distinction is typical in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
The text notes a difference of opinion: some say the mahram is a condition for obligation (shart al-wujub), others for performance (shart al-ada'). Link this to the earlier dispute on road safety.
This affects whether a woman is sinning if she goes without mahram when the road is safe.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position is that it is a condition of performance, so her Hajj is valid even without a mahram, but it is still disliked if the distance is three days or more.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Woman's Requirement of a Mahram for Hajj
Ruling on necessity of mahram for woman's hajj journey

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a woman's requirement of a mahram for Hajj and explain the difference between mahram being a condition for obligation vs. performance.

Core Idea
A woman's Hajj without mahram is valid but sinful if the distance is three days or more.

This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies the Hanafi position on a woman's need for a mahram or husband when traveling for Hajj. It establishes that the Hajj is valid even without one, but the act is prohibitively disliked. Additionally, it highlights a scholarly difference: is the mahram a condition of obligation (shart al-wujub) or performance (shart al-ada)? Understanding this distinction is crucial for both the obligated person and the mufti.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on woman traveling without mahram
The text says 'لا يجوز' (it is not permissible), but the commentary clarifies it is makruh tahriman (prohibitively disliked). The Hajj is valid, but the woman commits a sin.
The ruling applies only when the distance is three days or more.
2
Define the conditions of a valid mahram
The mahram must be adult (بالغ), sane (عاقل), not a fasiq (غير فاسق), and related by blood or marriage. An adolescent (مراهق) is treated as adult per Al-Durr.
The mahram must be someone whom the woman can never marry.
3
Determine the distance threshold
The prohibition applies when the journey to Makkah is three days and nights or more. Below that, the ruling does not apply.
This threshold is equivalent to approximately 88 km.
4
Analyze the ikhtilaf on the status of mahram
Scholars differed: is mahram a condition of obligation (shart al-wujub) so that a woman without a mahram is not obligated, or a condition of performance (shart al-ada) so she must find one? This relates to road safety. The relied-upon view is that it is a condition of performance.
If she cannot find a mahram, she is still obligated but performs Hajj with sin.

Effect of Change of Status During Hajj: Minor and Slave
Ruling on the validity of Hajj performed by a minor or slave who later becomes legally responsible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the Hajj of a minor who reaches puberty or a slave who is freed after entering ihram, and distinguish the binding nature of their ihram.

Core Idea
Minor's ihram is not binding; slave's is binding, so neither automatically becomes obligatory Hajj.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common scenario: a minor or slave enters ihram for Hajj but gains full legal capacity before completing the rites. Does their Hajj then count as the obligatory Hajj? The answer depends critically on the binding nature of their ihram at the time of contracting it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage treats two distinct cases: a minor who reaches puberty after ihram, and a slave who is freed after ihram.
2
State the common ruling
For both, the Hajj does not suffice for the obligatory Hajj (Hajjat al-Islam).
The reason given: their ihram was contracted for nafl (voluntary) and cannot transform into fard.
3
Analyze the distinction in renewal
The minor may renew his ihram before standing at Arafah with intention of fard, making it valid. The slave cannot do so.
Why? The minor's ihram is not binding (due to lack of legal capacity), so he can annul it and re-enter. The slave's ihram is binding, so he cannot exit it by commencing something else.
4

The Obligatory Path of Tawaf: Behind al-Hatim and the Sunnah of Raml
Rulings on Tawaf path and raml

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on circumambulating behind al-Hatim (wajib) and on performing raml (sunnah mu'akkadah), and apply Ibn 'Abidin's distinction for when to stop due to crowding.

Core Idea
Circumambulating inside al-Hatim is invalid; raml is a confirmed sunnah, but continuity takes priority during Tawaf.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri deals with two key rulings of Tawaf: the obligatory path behind al-Hatim (al-Hijr) and the sunnah of raml (brisk walking) in the first three circuits. The source also presents an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether a pilgrim should stop when crowded to perform raml, resolved by Ibn 'Abidin's distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text states that the pilgrim must make Tawaf from behind al-Hatim. This is wajib because six cubits of al-Hatim are originally part of the Kaaba; circumambulating through the gap between al-Hatim and the Kaaba would be invalid.
Wajib level is based on precaution (ihtiyat) and the certainty that part of al-Hatim belongs to the House.
2
State the sunnah of raml
Raml (brisk walking with short steps and slight shoulder-shaking) is a sunnah mu'akkadah for men in the first three circuits from the Black Stone to the Black Stone.
The passage notes that if a pilgrim is crowded, he should stop (stand, not sit) and wait for an opportunity to perform raml, because there is no substitute for it.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on crowding
There are two views: (1) Stop and wait for raml (Hidayah) because it is a confirmed sunnah; (2) Continue walking to preserve continuity (muwalat) of Tawaf, as held by 'Ali al-Qari, who argues that continuity is an agreed-upon sunnah or even obligatory.
The dispute is over which sunnah takes precedence when they conflict.
4
Apply Ibn 'Abidin's tarjih
Ibn 'Abidin reconciles: if the crowd occurs before starting Tawaf, the pilgrim should stop and wait to perform raml; but if the crowd occurs during Tawaf, he should continue without raml to avoid breaking continuity.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position in the late Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri details several sunan and rulings related to the performance of Tawaf (circumambulation) around the Kaaba. The commentary opens by affirming that the circumambulation must be performed from behind al-Hatim (also known as al-Hijr). This ruling is based on precaution and the understanding that that space belongs to the House itself.

The passage then discusses raml, which is a brisk walk with short steps and a slight shaking of the shoulders. Raml is a sunnah performed by men in the first three circuits of Tawaf, starting and ending at the Black Stone. The source text notes that if a person is overwhelmed by the crowd, he should stand still (without sitting) and wait for an opportunity to perform raml, as there is no substitute for it. For the remaining circuits (the last four), the pilgrim walks at a normal, dignified pace. The text then addresses the touching (istilam) of the Black Stone: it should be touched at each passing, if possible, analogous to opening each rak'ah of prayer with the takbir. The Tawaf is concluded with istilam. Additionally, touching the Yemeni Corner (al-Rukn al-Yamani) is also recommended; some scholars, such as Muhammad al-Shaybani, consider kissing it to be a sunnah, supported by a hadith in Ibn Khuzaymah.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri details several sunan and rulings related to the performance of Tawaf (circumambulation) around the Kaaba. The commentary opens by affirming that the circumambulation must be performed from behind al-Hatim (also known as al-Hijr). This is because a portion of the original Kaaba was incorporated into al-Hatim, and circumambulating through the narrow gap between al-Hatim and the Kaaba is not valid. This ruling is based on precaution and the understanding that that space belongs to the House itself.

The passage then discusses raml, which is a brisk walk with short steps and a slight shaking of the shoulders. Raml is a sunnah performed by men in the first three circuits of Tawaf, starting and ending at the Black Stone. The source text notes that if a person is overwhelmed by the crowd, he should stand still (without sitting) and wait for an opportunity to perform raml, as there is no substitute for it. The commentary cites 'Ali al-Qari and Ibn 'Abidin, the latter offering a preferred view: if the crowd occurs before starting Tawaf, one should stop and wait to perform raml (which is a confirmed sunnah), but if the crowd occurs during Tawaf, one should not stop to avoid breaking the continuity (muwalat) of the circuits, which is either a sunnah or an obligation.

For the remaining circuits (the last four), the pilgrim walks at a normal, dignified pace. Combining Zuhr and Asr on Arafah: The Individual's Ruling

Core Idea
The munfarid does not combine — the permission is tied to the imam's congregation.

We have seen that the imam combines Zuhr and Asr at Arafah. But what if a pilgrim prays alone? Does the ruling extend to him? This passage from Al-Lubab presents a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on this precise question, exposing the tension between preserving prayer times and facilitating the hajj standing. By the end, you will understand why Abu Hanifah held firm to individual prayer times and how the Sahibayn reasoned differently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default practice at Arafah
The imam leads the congregation in Zuhr and Asr combined at Zuhr time with one adhan and two iqamahs. This is the established practice from which the dispute arises.

State the issue for the munfarid
What if a person prays Zuhr alone in his camp, not with the imam? Does he combine Asr immediately or pray it at its own time? This is the point of contention between Abu Hanifah and the Sahibayn.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifah's position and proof
Abu Hanifah says each prayer must be in its own time. His proof: preserving the fixed prayer times is a fard obligation based on textual evidence (Qur'an and Sunnah), and deviation is only allowed where explicitly permitted by the Lawgiver, which is the congregational combination with the imam.
The key dalil is the general obligation to pray at appointed times, combined with the specific permission limited to the imam's congregation.
4
Examine the Sahibayn's position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the munfarid may also combine. Their proof: the rationale (illah) for combination is the need to maximize the standing at Arafah, and this need applies equally to the individual.
They extend the permission based on a common reasoning (qiyas on the imam's case).
5
Determine the mufta bihi view
Later authorities such as al-Isbijabi, Burhan al-Shari'ah, and al-Nasafi affirmed Abu Hanifah's view as the relied-upon position. Although al-Durr al-Mukhtar considered the Sahibayn's view more evidentially sound, the classical texts and fatwa practice follow Abu Hanifah.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab sharh al-Quduri discusses the recommended acts (mustahabbat) during the hajj journey from Arafat to Muzdalifah (known as al-ifadah). The author first mentions that it is recommended to perform ghusl before the standing at Arafat, comparing it to the day of Jumu'ah and the two Eids because it is a day of gathering. Then he emphasizes striving in supplication (du'a) since Arafat is one of the most hopeful places for dua to be answered. The key ruling is that when the sun sets on the 9th of Dhul Hijjah, the Imam and the pilgrims depart calmly in their formation (on their usual riding animals or walking) via the path of al-Ma'zamayn until they reach Muzdalifah, where they stay overnight. The boundary of Muzdalifah is defined as from the two Ma'zam of Arafat to the two Ma'zam of Muhassir. It is recommended to stay near the mountain called Quzah, where a fire was traditionally lit to guide people, hence its name (al-mayqadah). The Imam then leads the Maghrib and Isha prayers combined at the time of Isha with a single adhan and a single iqamah. This is contrary to the combination at Arafah (the jam' taqdim) where two iqamahs are used. The author notes a difference: Zufar and al-Tahawi hold that one adhan and two iqamahs should be used, analogizing to the combination at Arafah. However, the relied-upon Hanafi position is with one adhan and one iqamah, as this is the established practice (sunnah) of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The passage also includes a footnote explaining the term 'Muhassir' and the recommendation to speed up (al-harwalah) when passing through that valley, based on the Prophet's action. The origin of the name is given from the story of the elephant (the year of the Elephant).

The Sunnah of Combined Prayer at Muzdalifah
Ruling statement with reasoning about Muzdalifah sunnahs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah actions at Muzdalifah regarding the place of camping and the adhan for the combined Maghrib and Isha prayer.

Core Idea
At Muzdalifah, combine Maghrib and Isha with one adhan and one iqamah — a sunnah.

This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies two important sunnahs of Hajj at Muzdalifah: camping near the mountain of Quzah (the Mash'ar al-Haram) and the specific form of the combined Maghrib and Isha prayer. Understanding these practices helps you perform the rites correctly and appreciate the wisdom behind the actions of the Prophet ﷺ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the location and its significance
The passage describes the mountain of Quzah, which is the Mash'ar al-Haram. The caliphs used to light a fire there for guidance. Camping near it is recommended (mustahab).
2
Examine the ruling on the combined prayer
The Imam prays Maghrib and Isha together at the time of Isha with one adhan and one iqamah. This distinguishes it from the combined prayer at Arafah, where both prayers are offered out of their proper times and require two adhans.
3
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The text says: 'because Isha is in its time, so there is no need for notice.' At Muzdalifah, Isha is prayed in its actual time, so one adhan suffices. At Arafah, both prayers are delayed, so two adhans are needed to alert the congregation.
4
Apply the ruling to your pilgrimage
When performing Hajj, you should camp near the Mash'ar al-Haram (Quzah) and during the combined prayer, listen for a single adhan and iqamah. This is the established sunnah.
