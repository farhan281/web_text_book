Ruling on Impurity Drying and Losing Its Trace
Ruling on impurity drying and losing trace

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on impurity that dries and loses its trace, distinguish between taharah and tahuriyyah, and apply the ruling to fixed objects.

Core Idea
Drying removes impurity for prayer but not for tayammum.

In this lesson, we examine a concise but important ruling from al-Lubab: when a heavy impurity dries and leaves no trace—no color, taste, or smell—the affected place becomes pure for prayer. However, tayammum from that place remains invalid because it lacks the purifying quality (tahuriyyah). This distinction is rooted in the different conditions required for salat versus tayammum. We will also see how the rule extends to objects fixed in the ground, such as mats, trees, and grass.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The passage rules on prayer (salat) and tayammum when an impurity has dried and left no trace. For prayer, it says 'jaza' (valid); for tayammum, 'la yajuz' (invalid).
The act of prayer is the primary focus; tayammum is mentioned to highlight the limitation.
2
Define the condition for prayer: taharah
The text states: 'the condition for prayer is taharah (purity).' When the impurity's trace (color, taste, smell) vanishes, the place becomes tahir (pure). Thus prayer is valid.
Taharah here means absence of physical impurity on the place of prayer.
3
Define the condition for tayammum: tahuriyyah
Tayammum requires a substance that is tahur (purifying). Even if the dried impurity is no longer najis (impure), the earth that was contaminated loses its purifying quality. Therefore tayammum is invalid from that spot.
Tahuriyyah is a higher status than taharah; a pure substance may not be purifying (e.g., water mixed with something pure).
4
Apply to fixed objects
The final sentence extends the ruling: mats, trees, and grass fixed in the ground become pure by drying when impurity dries and vanishes. They are treated like the ground itself.
This is based on the inability to wash such objects easily; drying substitutes for washing.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position relies on istihsan (juristic preference) for ease. Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require washing; drying alone does not purify.
The difference stems from how each school defines the removal of impurity (izalat al-najasa).

The Excused Amount of Heavy Impurity in Prayer
Specifies that a dirham's worth of heavy impurity is excused, and more invalidates prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the excused amount of heavy impurity during prayer, identify its rationale, and apply the dirham measurement to determine when prayer is valid.

Core Idea
Up to a dirham of heavy impurity is excused in prayer because small amounts are unavoidable.

This passage from al-Lubab explains a key concession in Hanafi fiqh: a small amount of heavy impurity on one's body or clothes does not invalidate prayer. The ruling is based on the principle of relieving hardship (raf' al-haraj), as it is practically impossible to avoid all traces of impurity. The measurement is one dirham, derived from the area of the place of istinja'. Understanding this limit helps you assess the validity of your prayer when impurity is present.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is praying while having heavy impurity on the body or clothes. The ruling is that prayer is valid if the impurity is a dirham or less, and invalid if it exceeds that.
This is a concession making prayer sahih despite impurity.
2
List the types of impurity included
Heavy impurities include blood, urine, feces, wine, and droppings of birds that do not projectile defecate (e.g., chicken, duck, goose).
Impurity must be from animals whose meat is not eaten.
3
Understand the rationale
The proof is that 'a small amount cannot be guarded against' (al-qalil la yumkin al-taharrur 'anhu). This is istihsan to avoid hardship.
The ruling is based on necessity, not on a direct textual command.
4
Apply the measurement
The dirham is measured by area (masahah), roughly the size of the istinja' area (approx. 2.5 cm diameter). So it is the spread of impurity, not weight.
The area-based measurement is from a narration (ruwiya).
5
Contrast with other schools
Shafi'is and Malikis have different standards; for example, Shafi'is do not excuse any avoidable impurity. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in the madhhab.

Three Narrations on Non-Khamr Intoxicants
Presents three narrations on impurity of non-khamr intoxicants and the fatwa of later scholars.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi narrations on the impurity of non-khamr intoxicants and explain why the later fatwa adopts purity while maintaining the prohibition of consumption.

Core Idea
Non-khamr intoxicants have three narrations; later fatwa adopts purity for ease, but drinking remains haram.

Within the Hanafi school, the ritual impurity of intoxicating drinks other than wine (khamr) is not settled by a single definitive text. Instead, three narrations (riwayat) have been transmitted, each bearing different practical consequences. This passage from a modern verification of Ibn Abidin's commentary presents these narrations and shows how later scholars like al-Zarqa and al-Kawthari chose the view of purity, balancing legal theory with the needs of their time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three narrations
List the three narrated positions on non-khamr intoxicants: taghleez (heavy impurity), takhfeef (light impurity), and taharah (purity). Understand what each means for purity and prayer.
These categories are unique to Hanafi fiqh regarding impurity levels.
2
Locate earlier preferences
Note that in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, heavy impurity was preferred, while in al-Nahr al-Fa'iq, light impurity was preferred. This shows the pre-modern division.
3
Understand the later fatwa
Sheikh Ahmad al-Zarqa and Sheikh Muhammad Zahid al-Kawthari adopted the narration of purity (taharah). Their reasoning includes ease for the community and the principle of original permissibility.
This fatwa is for external use; drinking remains haram.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider practical consequences: if impure, contact invalidates prayer; if light, less than quarter excused; if pure, no issue. The purity fatwa facilitates medical and everyday use.
Modern example: alcohol-based hand sanitizers are considered pure, so prayer is valid.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The later fatwa is mufta bihi today. It rests on istihsan (juristic preference) and the absence of a clear text declaring non-khamr intoxicants impure, distinguishing them from khamr.
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) hold all intoxicants impure; this is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

The Excused Amount of Impurity: Dirham Measure and Classification in Hanafi Fiqh
Explaining the ruling of excused impurity amount and intra-Hanafi differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the excused amount of impurity in Hanafi prayer and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the classification of certain impurities as heavy or light.

Core Idea
Impurity less than a dirham is excused in prayer; the measure is by area for light impurities and by weight for thick impurities.

This lesson delves into the Hanafi ruling on the amount of impurity that is overlooked (ma'fuww) during prayer. You will learn the standard measure of a dirham, the rationale behind excusing small amounts, and an important disagreement within the Hanafi school regarding the classification of certain impurities as heavy (ghaliza) or light (khafifa). Understanding these details is essential for correctly applying the rules of purity in daily worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling and excused amount
The default ruling is that any impurity (najāsa) on the body or clothes invalidates prayer unless it is excused. The excused amount is up to the size of a dirham. Less than that, prayer is permissible; more than that, prayer is not valid.
The dirham measure is derived from the area of the istinjā' site.
2
Understand the measurement of a dirham
There are two transmissions: one by area (width of the palm) and one by weight (a mithqāl). The preferred reconciliation is to use area for light (thin) impurities and weight for thick (heavy) impurities.
This reconciliation gives effect to both narrations and is chosen by later authorities like al-Yanābi‘ and al-Zāhidī.
3
Apply the ruling to specific cases
For bird droppings that do not defecate in the air (like chicken, duck, goose), the same dirham rule applies. For light impurities (e.g., urine of edible-meat animals), the area measure is used.
The text gives the example of horse urine as a light impurity.
4
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilāf on heavy vs. light impurities
Abū Ḥanīfa considers impurities from sea creatures, horse dung (rawth), and bovine dung (khuthī) as heavy (ghalīza). Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad consider them light (khafīfa). This affects the ruling on excused amount: for heavy impurities, the stricter weight measure is applied? The text does not elaborate, but the classification difference is significant.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilāf) is that the dirham measure may be interpreted differently based on the classification.
5
Review the rational proof (dalīl aqlī)
The reasoning is that small amounts of impurity are unavoidable in daily life; to prevent hardship, they are excused. This is based on the principle of removing hardship (raf‘ al-ḥaraj).
This is a rational proof, not a direct textual proof, but it is well established in uṣūl al-fiqh.

Light Impurity (Najasah Mukhaffafah): Classification and Excused Amount
Intra-Hanafi dispute on light impurity classification and excused amount, with qiyas khafi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of light impurity, distinguish between the narrations on its measure, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on its excused amount.

Core Idea
Light impurity is excused up to a quarter of the affected spot, but its definition and precise measure are debated within the Hanafi school.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a practical issue in ritual purity: how much light impurity can be present on a garment or body without invalidating prayer. The author traces the intra-Hanafi disagreement on what counts as light impurity and the proper measure of excused amount, culminating in a relied-upon fatwa. Understanding this ruling requires navigating both textual evidence and the method of qiyas khafi.

Guided Path
1
Define light impurity
Identify which impurities are light and heavy according to Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn. Light: urine of edible animals. Heavy: dung and dry dung of non-edible animals (Abu Hanifa); Sahibayn consider all animal excreta as light.
The dispute affects the excused amount: light has a larger allowance (quarter of spot), heavy has a smaller allowance (size of dirham).

The Measure of Impurity That Prevents Prayer
Intra-Hanafi dispute on impurity quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two Hanafi positions on the measure of impurity and apply the muftā bihī view (quarter of the smallest garment) to practical scenarios.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school moved from a subjective 'excessive' standard to an objective quarter measure for ease.

This lesson addresses an internal Hanafi dispute on how much impurity on clothing or body invalidates prayer. The original position of Imam Abu Hanifah relied on a qualitative criterion—whether the impurity is 'much-excessive' (kathīr fāhish). Later jurists, seeking convenience for the common people, set a fixed measure: one quarter of the smallest garment used in prayer. Understanding both views and the rationale behind the shift is essential for correct application in daily practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
First, note that Imam Abu Hanifah did not fix a specific quantity; he considered whether the impurity is 'much-excessive' (fāhish). Later, some scholars, including al-Aqta‘, adopted a fixed measure: one quarter of the smallest garment (adnā thawb).
The original view is from the zahir al-riwayah; the quarter view is a later facilitation.
2
Understand the rationale for the shift
The later jurists changed the expression from 'much-excessive' to the quarter measure taysīran (to make things easier) for the people, especially those without legal insight. The quarter measure serves as a concrete, objective yardstick.
The shift is not a contradiction but a practical extension of the original principle.
3
Analyze the application for congealed light impurities
If a light impurity (mukhaffafah) is solid and accumulates, does the quarter measure apply? The passage clarifies: if when melted it would reach a quarter, it is treated as reaching the quarter; otherwise not. The original (fāhish) criterion also applies.
This nuance shows that the quarter measure is not absolute; it is a convention linked to the volume of the impurity.
4
Determine the muftā bihī position
The passage states that the fatwa is upon the second view (the quarter measure), and it is more cautious (aḥwaṭ). Thus, in practice, Hanafi muftis rely on the quarter measure, while being aware of the original.
The quarter view is not the only valid one, but it is the operative ruling for fatwa.
5
Apply the rule to a real scenario
Consider a stain of blood (heavy impurity) on a sleeve. Is the prayer valid? Since blood is a heavy impurity (najāsah mughallaz̧ah), the measure is the same: if it equals or exceeds a quarter of the smallest garment, the prayer is invalid. If less, it is valid (subject to the original 'excessive' criterion if it is clearly excessive).
The same measure applies to heavy and light impurities; the difference is in the method of purification.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A visible impurity is purified by removing its substance, even with one wash; residual traces that are difficult to remove are excused.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi classification of impurity purification. The text from Lubab al-Ashraf distinguishes between visible and non-visible impurities and explains the ruling for each. Identify the two categories
Impurity either has a visible substance ('ayn mar'iyyah) like blood, or it does not (non-visible, like urine that has dried). The purification method differs.

Define 'visible substance'
The visible substance is the physical matter of the impurity that can be seen, such as blood, stool, or wine. It has mass and color.

This is a practical criterion for the mufti and the layperson.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissible positions for a sick person's prayer and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on qada' when unable to gesture.

Core Idea
If you cannot gesture, you delay the prayer — but later rulings differ on making it up.

Two Hanafi positions are presented, with the later fatwa taking a lenient view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hierarchy of positions
Beginning from inability to sit, trace the sequence: lying on back (mustalqi) → lying on side (mudtaji') → head gesture (i'ma') → delay. Note that each level is only permitted when the previous is impossible.
Lying on back is preferred over side; right side over left.

Observe that i'ma' is valid only with the head. Gesture with eyes, heart, or eyebrows is invalid. Understand why: these are not considered intentional substitutes for ruku' and sujud.

One position (early, al-Hidayah) holds qada' wajib for all missed prayers even if numerous. The other (later, al-Zuhayriyya, mufta bihi) holds that after many consecutive prayers without ability, qada' is not obligatory.

Based on the textual evidence from al-Zuhayriyya and other fatwa works, the relied-upon position is that qada' is not required when the inability to gesture continues for many prayers. The student must recognize the tarjih and its basis in later Hanafi fatwa.

Commentary
This passage details the Hanafi method for washing the deceased. It begins with arranging the body on a raised bed for drainage, covering the private parts (the thick 'awrah is sufficient), and removing clothes to allow proper cleaning. Wudu is performed similarly to living prayer, but without mouth or nose rinsing due to hardship; however, some scholars permit doing so with a cloth, and that is the practiced view. For a junub, menstruating, or postpartum woman, wudu is done without exception. Water is poured over the entire body as in life, and the bed is fumigated with incense to mask odor and show respect. The water is heated with sidr (lotus leaves) or hurth (soapwort); if unavailable, pure water suffices. The head and beard are washed with khatmi (marshmallow) or soap. The body is then washed twice: first on the left side, then on the right, ensuring water reaches the underside. Finally, the body is gently sat up and the stomach is wiped to expel any remaining material. These steps are sunnah and mustahabb within the communal obligation of ghusl al-mayyit.

Method of Washing the Deceased (Ghusl al-Mayyit)
Hanafi procedure for washing the deceased with details on materials and steps.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi procedure for washing a deceased Muslim, identify the recommended materials, and apply the ruling that pure water is sufficient.

Core Idea
Pure water suffices; sidr and khatmi are sunna, not obligatory.

Washing the deceased is a communal obligation (fard kifayah). This lesson details the recommended Hanafi method, distinguishing between obligatory elements (pure water, full coverage) and recommended practices (khatmi, sidr, tripling). Understanding this procedure ensures proper respect for the deceased and valid performance of a collective duty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching ruling
Recognize that washing the deceased is fard kifayah: if some Muslims perform it, the obligation is lifted from the rest.
The act itself is obligatory; the method described is the sunna procedure.
2
Outline the sequential steps
List the steps: wash head/beard with khatmi → side washes (left then right) with sidr → sit up and wipe stomach → dry → shroud → apply hanut and camphor.
The tripling of the full wash is sunna, performed after the two side washes.
3
Distinguish obligatory from recommended
Obligatory: pure water (al-ma' al-qarah) covering the entire body at least once. Recommended: warming water, using khatmi (or soap), sidr, tripling, gentle stomach wipe, and specific perfumes.
If sidr or khatmi are unavailable, plain water alone suffices.
4
Handle impurities during washing
If waste exits during the stomach wipe, wash it off but do not repeat the entire ghusl; the obligation is fulfilled.
This is because the deceased is not required to have wudu, and the impurity is removed locally.
5
Apply the rulings on perfumes
Hanut (compound perfume) is recommended on the head and beard, but saffron and wars are prohibited for men. Camphor is placed on the prostration points.
These are sunna; omitting them does not invalidate the wash.

Three garments (izar, qamis, lifafah) are sunnah; two suffice; one is disliked except necessity.

This passage from al-Lubab explains the recommended shroud for a deceased man. It details the three specific garments and their precise measurements, and clarifies the permissibility of using fewer garments in cases of necessity or need. Understanding this ruling ensures that the burial is conducted in accordance with the sunnah while accommodating practical constraints.

The Murtath: Definition and Ruling
Definition of murtath and ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the actions that constitute irtithāth and apply the Hanafi ruling that such a person is washed and prayed upon.

Core Idea
Lingering after battle cuts off worldly martyrdom — the murtath is washed and prayed upon.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi definition of the murtath—a wounded soldier who lingers after battle—and the legal consequences. The source passage clarifies that certain actions, such as eating, drinking, receiving treatment, or surviving past a prayer time while sane, break the continuity of battlefield death and restore the normal funeral obligations. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of ghusl and salat al-janazah to martyrs.

Guided Path
1
Define the term murtath
Read the passage and identify the definition of al-irtithāth. Note the specific actions listed: eating, drinking, sleeping, receiving treatment, living past a prayer time while sane, and being moved from the battlefield (except for fear of horses).
The definition is given in the sharh as the actions that 'cut off' the ruling of worldly martyrdom.
2
Identify the ruling for the murtath
The passage states that the murtath is washed (ghusl) and the funeral prayer is performed over him. This contrasts with a shahīd of this world, who is neither washed nor prayed upon.
The ruling is fard kifayah upon the Muslim community.
3
Understand the 'illa (effective cause)
The reason (ʿilla) is that the ruling of worldly martyrdom depends on immediate death in battle. If the person lingers and performs any of the listed actions, that cause is negated, and the normal obligations apply. The person still receives the reward of a martyr in the Hereafter.
This is a talīl (causal reasoning) argument, not a direct textual proof.
4
Recognize the exception
The passage notes that being moved from the battlefield due to fear of being trampled by horses does not constitute irtithāth. This is an exception that preserves the martyrdom ruling.
This shows that the 'illa of immediate death is not violated by necessary relocation.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Consider a wounded soldier who is moved from the battlefield to save him from enemy capture, then dies after one prayer time. Determine if he is a murtath or a shahīd of this world.
If moved due to necessity (not personal action), the ruling may differ.

A rebel or robber killed justly in combat is not washed nor prayed over.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents a specific ruling concerning those killed while fighting against legitimate authority. The Hanafi school distinguishes between those killed in active rebellion (who are not given full funeral rites) and those captured and executed later (who receive normal rites). Ruling on One Who Commits Suicide
Ruling on washing and funeral prayer for a suicide victim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on washing and praying over a suicide victim and explain why it remains a communal obligation (fard kifayah).

Core Idea
Suicide is a major sin, but the victim remains a Muslim entitled to full funeral rites.

This lesson addresses a clear ruling from the matn of al-Quduri: that a person who takes their own life is still to be given a ritual wash (ghusl) and the funeral prayer (salat al-janazah). This may surprise some, but it underscores the Hanafi principle that a Muslim does not lose their Islamic identity through sin, however grave. The ruling is a straightforward statement that requires memorization and understanding of its legal basis.

1

This is the core of the lesson.
2
Identify the legal category of these acts
Washing and praying over a deceased Muslim are fard kifayah (communal obligation). This ruling confirms they apply even in suicide.
The obligation does not change because of the manner of death.
3
Recognize the implications
Suicide does not strip a person of their Muslim identity; hence, the rights of a Muslim in death remain intact.
This distinguishes from other categories like rebels (bughat) who forfeit these rights.
4
Contrast with other categories
Compare with those killed in rebellion (baghy): they are not washed nor prayed over. The key difference is the legitimacy of the community's relationship with the deceased.
This contrast aids in memorizing the ruling.
