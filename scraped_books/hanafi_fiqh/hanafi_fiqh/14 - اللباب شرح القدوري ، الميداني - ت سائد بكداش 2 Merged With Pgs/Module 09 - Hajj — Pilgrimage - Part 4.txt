Compensation for Partial Injuries to Game and Breaking Eggs
Ruling on compensation for partial damage to game and egg breaking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios of compensation for damage to game by a muhrim and identify the key criterion (hīz al-imtinā') that changes the ruling.

Core Idea
Partial damage to game: pay decrease; loss of defense: pay full value; egg: pay value due to potential.

In this passage, we learn how a muhrim must compensate for harming game during ihram. The ruling depends on whether the injury leaves the animal able to defend itself (hīz al-imtinā'). If the animal can still flee, only the decrease in value is due; if it becomes defenseless, full value is required. Even breaking an egg triggers liability, because it had the potential to become game. This lesson breaks down each scenario and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three actions
The passage describes three types of harm: (1) wounding, plucking hair, or cutting a limb; (2) plucking feathers or cutting legs; (3) breaking an egg. Note how each is treated.
The first includes actions that do not necessarily remove defense; the second specifically does.
2
Apply the criterion: hīz al-imtinā'
For the first kind, determine whether the animal loses its ability to defend itself. The text says: if it does NOT leave hīz al-imtinā', then only decrease in value. If it DOES (second kind), then full value.
Hīz al-imtinā' means the capacity to escape or fight back.
3
Understand the egg ruling
For breaking an egg, there is no hīz al-imtinā' concern; instead, liability is based on the egg's potential (aradiyyah) to become game. The muhrim pays its market value as a precaution.
This treats the egg as game in origin.
4
Trace the reasoning
The commentary provides rationale: partial injury without loss of defense is like general tort (considering part as whole). Loss of defense means the muhrim has effectively taken possession, so full value. Egg has potential, so treated as game.
This links to broader fiqh principles of liability.

States ruling on compensation for partial injury to game

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for partial injury to game and identify the condition that determines whether partial or full compensation is due.

Core Idea
Partial harm to game requires proportionate compensation if the animal remains able to escape.

This lesson addresses a specific fiqh matter: when a muhrim (pilgrim in ihram) injures game without killing it. The classical Hanafi text of Al-Quduri, as explained in Al-Lubab, establishes that compensation is due for the decrease in value, provided the game remains in its 'domain of prevention' (hayz al-imtina'). Understanding this condition is key to distinguishing partial liability from full liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states that a muhrim who wounds game, plucks its hair, or cuts a limb must pay compensation. Identify the specific acts mentioned and the general ruling: liability for the decrease in value.
The act is a violation of ihram restrictions on hunting.
2
Locate the critical condition
The sharh adds a condition: 'and does not thereby cause it to leave the domain of prevention (hayz al-imtina)'. Determine what this condition means: the game retains its ability to escape or resist capture.
This condition determines whether partial or full value is owed.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The author provides a talil: 'considering the part as the whole, as in the rights of people.' Analyse this analogy: just as killing the whole game requires full compensation, injuring a part requires compensation proportional to the harm. This mirrors civil liability for partial damage to property.
The analogy is drawn from general principles of property law.

Core Idea
Incapacitating game so it cannot escape is equivalent to killing it for compensation purposes.

In ihram, harming game triggers penalty. But what if you don't kill it, just render it unable to escape? This passage clarifies that if you pluck feathers or cut legs such that the game leaves its 'domain of prevention' (حيز الامتناع), you owe full value. The reasoning is that you have destroyed its means of security.

Identify the specific act

2
Determine the outcome

3
State the ruling

Some other schools may require full compensation only upon actual killing, not incapacitation. This is a point of ikhtilaf.

Breaking Game Eggs: Compensation for Eggs and Dead Chicks
States rulings on breaking eggs and dead chick

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the compensation due when a muhrim breaks a game egg and determine the ruling when a dead chick emerges, applying the Hanafi principle of precaution (ihtiyat).

Core Idea
Breaking a game egg requires compensation; if a dead chick emerges, value of a live chick is due by precaution.

This lesson from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses a specific scenario during Ihram: breaking a game egg. The Hanafi school treats the egg as potential game, and even if a dead chick emerges, the muhrim is liable for the full value of a live chick. We will examine the reasoning and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine the action (breaking a game egg) and the ruling: wajib compensation equal to the value of the egg.
The egg must not be rotten (غير مذر).
2
Understand the legal reasoning
The egg is the origin of game (أصل الصيد) and has the potential (عرضية) to become game, so it is treated as game by precaution (احتياطاً).
This is a qiyas-based reasoning with potential (aradiyyah) as the effective cause.
3
State the second ruling
If a dead chick emerges from the broken egg, the muhrim owes the value of a live chick (قيمته حياً).
This applies even if the death is unknown to be before or after the break.
4
Analyze the extension by precaution
The egg is made to produce a live chick; breaking it prematurely is a cause of death, so liability is attributed to the breaker due to precaution.
Precaution here means assigning responsibility to the apparent cause.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi tarjih
The correct and relied-upon opinion is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, as stated by al-Isbijabi and endorsed by al-Nasafi and al-Mahbubi.
Muhammad al-Shaybani may have differed, but the mufta bihi is the majority view.

Core Idea
A muhrim may kill certain harmful animals without penalty; the exception is for animals that do not initiate harm.

The state of ihram prohibits hunting and the killing of game, but this prohibition has clear exceptions. The Hanafi school specifies a set of animals considered inherently harmful (mu'dhiya), and killing them incurs no penalty (jaza'). This principle is consistent with the broader fiqh rule that necessity lifts prohibitions.

Liability for Breaking an Egg in Ihram
Ruling with commentary and qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muhrim who breaks an egg and apply the qiyas to similar cases of unintended harm to animal offspring.

Core Idea
Breaking an egg in ihram incurs wajib payment of the chick's live value, even if death is uncertain.

The state of ihram imposes strict liability for harm to animals. This lesson examines a specific ruling: if a Muhrim breaks an egg and a dead chick emerges, he must pay its value as if alive. The reasoning is precautionary: the egg is intended for life, and the break is the likely cause of death. We will also see how this ruling extends by analogy to a pregnant doe.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act is breaking an egg by a person in ihram (Muhrim). The context is a hunting/jinayat offense during Hajj/Umrah. Note the ruling applies when the chick emerges dead or death is unknown.
The ruling is from the chapter 'Jinaayat al-Muhrim' in Al-Quduri.
2
State the ruling and its hukm level
The ruling: 'alayhi qeematuhu hayyan' (he must pay its value alive). The hukm level is wajib (obligatory) because it is compensation for destruction of property.
The value is assessed as if the chick were a live bird of its kind.
3
Understand the legal basis
The commentary gives the reason: the egg is prepared (mu'add) to produce a live chick; breaking it prematurely is a cause of death. When death is uncertain, liability is attributed to the breaker as a precaution (ihtiyatan).
This uses the principle of causality (sababiyyah) and precaution in cases of doubt.
4
Analyze the qiyas analogical reasoning
The text extends the ruling to a man who strikes a pregnant doe, causing abortion and death of the mother. He must pay the value of both the fetus (janin) and the doe. This analogy reinforces that liability applies even when the harm is indirect.
The analogy is based on the common illa (cause): causing death of an unborn or dependent life by a prohibited act.

Hunting or killing game within the sacred boundaries of the Haram incurs a penalty (jaza') that is wajib. This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri addresses situations where two or more people share in such a killing. Sale of Game by a Muhrim: Two Cases
States two rulings on sale of game involving muhrim buyer/seller

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the sale of game when the seller or hunter is a muhrim, and distinguish between fasid and sahih contracts in this context.

Core Idea
A muhrim can sell lawfully owned game after leaving ihram, but not during it.

This lesson examines two contrasting rulings from Al-Quduri's matn, as expanded by Al-Lubab. The key factor is the state of the seller at the time of contract: ihram restricts legal capacity, rendering the sale defective (fasid) but not void. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying Hanafi fiqh to transactions during Hajj.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their states
In the first case, a non-muhrim hunts game, and a muhrim sells it. In the second case, a muhrim hunts (sinfully) and later sells it after leaving ihram. Note the seller's state at contract time.
The state of the hunter is relevant only for ownership, not the ruling.
2
Determine the ruling for the first case
The sale is fasid (defective). The object (game) is lawful, but the seller lacks full capacity due to ihram. The contract is valid in essence but flawed in description.
If both parties are muhrim, the sale becomes batil (void), as noted in the commentary.
3
Determine the ruling for the second case
The sale is sahih (valid). The seller hunted while muhrim, but only owned the game after taking possession post-ihram. At the time of sale, he is non-muhrim and lawfully owns the object.
The sin of hunting during ihram does not affect the validity of the later sale.
4
Distinguish fasid from batil
Fasid: curable defect (here, seller's capacity is temporary). Batil: essential flaw (both parties muhrim). Hanafi fiqh treats fasid as potentially rectified.
Other schools may not recognize this distinction; they often deem any defect as batil.

(ومن أحرم بالحج فرضاً أو نفلاً صحيحاً أو فاسداً، ففاته الوقوف بعرفة حتى طلع الفجر من يوم النحر : فقد فاته الحج؛ لما تقدم أن وقت الوقوف يمتد إليه، وأن الحج عرفة. ويجب عليه إذا أراد التحلل: أن يتحلل بأفعال العمرة، بأن يطوف، ويسعى من غير إحرام جديد لها، ويتحلل بالحلق أو التقصير. ويقضي الحج من قابل، ولا دم عليه؛ لأن التحلل وقع بأفعال العمرة؛ فكانت في حق فائت الحج: بمنزلة الدم في حق المحصر، فلا يُجمع بينهما. قال الإسبيجابي : ثم عند أبي حنيفة ومحمد : أصل إحرامه بالحج باق، ويتحلل بعمل عمرة، وعند أبي يوسف: يصير إحرامه إحرام عمرة، والصحيح قولهما.
وفائدة هذا الخلاف وثمرته أنه لو أحرم بحجة أخرى : تلزمه ويؤديها عند أبي يوسف، لأنه ضم حجةً إلى عمرة، وعندهما ضم حجة إلى حجة : فيلزمه رفضها، ثم يقضيها. وفائدة أخرى أن هذه العمرة تُسقط عنه العمرة التي تلزمه في جميع عمره عنـد أبي يوسف، وعندهما لا تسقط . اهـ من الجوهرة ٢٢٠/١. قلت : وقوله : «العمرة التي تلزمه» : هذا على القول بوجوبها، كما يرى صاحب الجوهرة، وسيأتي بعد قليل أن المذهب المعتمد القول بسنيتها المؤكدة، لا وجوبها.)
Chapter of Fawāt. Whoever enters ihram for Hajj and misses the standing at Arafah until the dawn of the Day of Nahr has missed Hajj, and he must perform tawaf, sa'y, and exit ihram, and he makes up the Hajj the following year and there is no blood sacrifice upon him. (And whoever enters ihram for Hajj, whether obligatory or voluntary, valid or invalid, and misses the standing at Arafah until dawn of the Day of Nahr has missed Hajj; because it was previously stated that the time for standing extends to that, and that Hajj is Arafah. And it is obligatory upon him, if he wants to exit ihram, to do so by performing the actions of Umrah: tawaf, sa'y without a new ihram, and exit by shaving or trimming. And he makes up Hajj the following year and there is no blood sacrifice upon him; because the exit occurred by the actions of Umrah, so it is like the blood sacrifice for the one prevented (muhsar), so the two are not combined. Al-Isbijabi said: According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, his original ihram for Hajj remains, and he exits by performing the actions of Umrah, while according to Abu Yusuf, his ihram becomes an ihram for Umrah, and the correct view is theirs. The benefit and fruit of this difference is that if he enters ihram for another Hajj: it becomes binding and he performs it according to Abu Yusuf, because he added a Hajj to an Umrah, while according to them, adding Hajj to Hajj requires him to abandon it, then make it up. Another benefit is that this Umrah fulfills the Umrah that is obligatory upon him in his lifetime according to Abu Yusuf, while according to them, it does not. I say: His statement 'the Umrah that is obligatory upon him' is based on the opinion that Umrah is obligatory, as the author of al-Jawharah holds, and it will come shortly that the relied-upon madhhab is that it is a confirmed sunnah, not obligatory.)

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the ruling of fawāt, i.e., missing the essential pillar of Hajj—the standing (wuquf) at Arafah. The text first states the matn: whoever misses wuquf until dawn of the Day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah) has missed Hajj entirely. He must then exit his ihram by performing the actions of Umrah (tawaf, sa'y, and halq or taqsir) without a new ihram, and he must make up the Hajj the following year with no dam (sacrificial animal) due. The commentary explains that this applies whether the original Hajj was obligatory or voluntary, valid or invalid, and provides the proof: the time for wuquf extends to dawn of 10th, and the hadith 'Hajj is Arafah.' It also clarifies that no dem is required because the exit actions take the place of the dam in the case of ihsar (prevention). The passage then records an important intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the original ihram for Hajj remains intact, and the person simply performs Umrah actions to exit; according to Abu Yusuf, the ihram transforms into an ihram for Umrah. The text declares the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad as correct (wa al-sahih qawluhuma). The practical consequences (thamarah) of this difference are then given: if the person later enters ihram for another Hajj, Abu Yusuf sees it as valid (adding Hajj to Umrah), while the other two require him to abandon the second Hajj; also, the Umrah performed satisfies the obligation of a lifetime Umrah according to Abu Yusuf, but not according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. Finally, a comment notes that the relied-upon madhhab is that Umrah is a confirmed sunnah (sunnah mu'akkadah), not wajib, as some earlier scholars held.

Key Terms
الفوات (al-fawāt): Missing the standing at Arafah until dawn of the Day of Nahr, resulting in the invalidity of the Hajj.
الإحصار (al-iḥṣār): Being prevented from completing Hajj due to an enemy, illness, or other external cause.
التحلل (al-taḥallul): Exiting the state of ihram by performing the actions of Umrah (tawaf, sa'y, and shaving/trimming).

Key Takeaway
When wuquf is completely missed until dawn of 10th, the Hajj is invalid; exit by Umrah actions and makeup Hajj are wajib, no dam, and the original ihram remains for Hajj (mufta bihi).
