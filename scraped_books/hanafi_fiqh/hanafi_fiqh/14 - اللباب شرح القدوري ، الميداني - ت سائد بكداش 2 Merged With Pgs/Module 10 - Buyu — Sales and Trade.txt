Muhrim's Sale or Purchase of Game: Void Contract
Ruling on a muhrim's sale or purchase of game

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a muhrim's transaction involving land game and explain why it is void using the Quranic evidence and qiyas.

Core Idea
A muhrim cannot own game, so any sale or purchase of game is absolutely void.

When a person enters ihram for Hajj or Umrah, certain prohibitions apply, including hunting or owning land game. This lesson examines the ruling when a muhrim engages in a sale or purchase of game. The Hanafi school holds that such a transaction is void from the start, unlike some other schools that may deem it valid but sinful. We explore the reasoning based on the Quranic prohibition and analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage states that if a muhrim sells or buys game (sayd al-barr), the sale is batil (void). Determine that the act is a sale or purchase, and the ruling is voidness.
2
Locate the primary proof
The commentator references the Quranic verse: 'And forbidden to you is game of the land as long as you are in ihram' (5:96). This is the primary textual evidence.
The verse directly prohibits game for the muhrim.
3
Understand the rationale
The muhrim cannot own game by hunting; therefore, he cannot own it by purchase either. The game is a prohibited substance in his regard, like wine, so it has no legal value (taqwim).
The analogy is drawn from the inability to own through hunting.
4
Examine the alternative scenarios
If the muhrim sells live game, he is interfering with its security (which is prohibited). If he sells dead game, it is carrion, also prohibited. Both lead to the sale being void.
The text covers both selling and buying scenarios.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is that the contract is void; other schools may consider it valid but sinful. Recognize where ikhtilaf occurs.
This is a legitimate difference of opinion.

Commentary
This passage rules that any transaction involving game (land game) by a person in ihram (muhrim) is null and void—whether he is selling or buying. The reason is that the muhrim is prohibited from hunting and cannot own game by hunting. Consequently, he cannot own it by purchase either, because ownership of such an item is legally impossible for him. The footnotes elaborate: if the muhrim sells game while it is alive, he is interfering with the security of the game, which is forbidden; if he sells it after killing it, it is a dead animal (carrion), which is also not permissible to sell. Similarly, if he buys game alive, it is a prohibited substance for him (like wine) because he cannot make use of it during his ihram; thus it has no legal value (taqwim) for him. The foundation is the Quranic verse: 'And forbidden to you is game of the land as long as you are in ihram' (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:96). Therefore, the contract is void (batil) from the start.

Key Terms
محرم (muhrim): A person in the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah.
صيد (sayd): Land game (wild animals) that is permissible to hunt when not in ihram.
باطل (batil): Void ab initio; the contract has no legal effect.

Rulings on Sale of Game Involving a Muhrim
Rulings on sale of game involving a muhrim, with three subcases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on sale of game involving a muhrim, distinguish between batil and fasid, and apply the Quranic dalil to derive these rulings.

Core Idea
A muhrim cannot own or sell game; sales are batil if the muhrim is seller, fasid if buyer from a halal, and ja'iz if sold after exiting ihram.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri addresses a key intersection of Hajj and sales: the legal capacity of a muhrim to transact in game. It presents three distinct scenarios—sale by a muhrim, purchase by a muhrim from a non-muhrim, and sale by a former muhrim after becoming halal—each with a different ruling. Together, they illustrate how the prohibition of hunting (Quran 5:96) affects the validity of contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage discusses: (1) muhrim sells game, (2) muhrim buys game from a non-muhrim, (3) muhrim hunts then sells after becoming halal. Each has a distinct ruling: batil, fasid, ja'iz respectively.
The ruling depends on who owns the game at the time of sale.
2
Apply the Quranic prohibition
Quran 5:96 forbids hunting for the muhrim. This prohibition extends to ownership and trade; thus a muhrim cannot be a valid seller (he cannot own the hunted game) nor a valid buyer (the game is muharram al-'ayn for him, like wine).
The verse is decisive: 'Forbidden to you is game of the land as long as you are in ihram.'
3
Differentiate batil and fasid
In Hanafi, batil means the contract is null ab initio, with no legal effect. Fasid means the contract is valid in its essence but defective in a description (here, the buyer's state). In sale to a muhrim, the seller is halal and can own the game, so the contract is fasid, not batil.
Batil cannot be rectified; fasid can be rectified by removing the defect (e.g., the muhrim exiting ihram before taking possession).
4
Analyze the exception
If a muhrim hunts sinfully while in ihram, he does not own the game. However, after becoming halal, he can take possession (istila') and then sell it validly. This sale is ja'iz because ownership was acquired while halal.
The exception shows that the prohibition is tied to the state of ihram, not the animal itself.
5
Determine practical consequences
For batil, the game must be returned (if alive) or disposed of (if dead); contract has no effect. For fasid, the contract is rescinded; if possession occurred, the buyer must return the game and cannot consume it. For ja'iz, the sale is fully valid.
Know the difference to advise pilgrims correctly.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri discusses the rulings on sale of game (sayd) involving a muhrim. The main ruling: if a muhrim sells game (alive or dead) or buys it, the sale is invalid (batil). The reasoning: a muhrim cannot own game through hunting because it is forbidden to hunt while in ihram. Therefore, he cannot own it, and since he cannot own it, he cannot transfer ownership through sale. The commentary explains that selling a live game violates the security granted to it, and selling a dead one is selling carrion (maytah), both invalid. Buying is invalid because the game is considered 'forbidden substance' (muharram al-'ayn) for him, like wine, thus valueless. The Quranic verse (5:96) is cited: 'Forbidden to you is game of the land as long as you are in ihram.' Two subcases are then presented from Al-Jawharah: (1) If a non-muhrim (halal) hunts the game and sells it to a muhrim, the sale is fasid (defective), not batil. Reason: the sale is valid in its origin because the seller is non-muhrim and can own the game, but the element of dealing with a muhrim makes it defective. If the buyer is also a muhrim, then it becomes batil because no valid ownership can arise. (2) Conversely, if a muhrim hunts the game (while in ihram, sinfully) and then sells it after becoming halal, the sale is permissible (ja'iz). The commentary provides the necessary scenario: the muhrim hunted but did not acquire ownership; he kept the game until he exited ihram, then obtained ownership by istila' (taking possession) while halal, then sold it. This path makes the sale valid.

Key Terms
باطل (batil): Invalid sale with no legal effect; cannot transfer ownership
فاسد (fasid): Defective sale; valid in origin but invalid due to a description defect
صيد (sayd): Game animal of the land that is permissible to hunt for non-muhrims
محرم (muhrim): A person in the state of ihram (for Hajj or Umrah)
حلال (halal): A person not in the state of ihram; permitted to hunt and own game
