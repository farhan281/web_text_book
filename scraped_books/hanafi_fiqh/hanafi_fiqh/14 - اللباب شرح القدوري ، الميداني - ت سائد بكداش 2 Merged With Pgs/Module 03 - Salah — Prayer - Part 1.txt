The Obligatory Amount of Wiping the Head in Wudu
Defining the fard amount of wiping the head with hadith proof and intra-madhhab ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the obligatory amount of wiping the head, identify the hadith proof, and explain why the quarter is the mufta bihi despite an alternative view.

Core Idea
The obligatory wiping of the head is only one quarter — the forelock — as proven by the Prophet's practice, not the entire head.

Many students assume that wiping the entire head is required in wudu. However, the Hanafi school, based on a clear hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him), holds that only one quarter of the head is obligatory. This lesson unpacks the evidence, the exact measure, and an internal Hanafi difference that has been resolved in favor of the quarter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Wiping the head is an obligatory act (fard) of wudu. The question here is the minimum amount required for validity. The Hanafi matn states it is the measure of the forelock (al-nasiyah).
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith of Mughirah ibn Shu'bah: the Prophet (peace be upon him) wiped his forelock (nasiyah) during wudu. This hadith is a proof (dalil) for the obligatory amount.
Hadith is from Sunan Ibn Majah and Sahih Muslim.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The forelock is defined as the front part of the head, approximately one quarter. By wiping the forelock, the Prophet established that this measure fulfills the command of wiping the head in the Quran. Thus, the fard amount becomes a quarter.
The Quranic command is mujmal and the hadith provides bayan.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some later narrations attribute to Muhammad al-Shaybani a view that three fingers suffice. The reasoning: the hand is the instrument of wiping, and three fingers are the majority of the hand.
This view is from the Nawadir, not Zahir al-Riwayah.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The quarter view is zahir al-riwayah (from Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad in the core texts). Al-Karkhi, al-Tahawi, al-Marghinani, and Ibn al-Humam all affirm the quarter as mufta bihi. The three-finger view is weak and not relied upon.
Tarjih authority: al-Hidayah and Fath al-Qadir.

Drowning and the Obligation to Repeat Missed Prayers
Intra-Hanafi dispute on missed prayers for a drowned person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on missed prayers for a drowned person and explain why Abu Hanifa's view is preferred as the more cautious opinion in acts of worship.

Core Idea
Certainty of death requires prayer repetition; only signs guide the exact duration.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the obligation of a drowned person's family to make up missed prayers. Imam Abu Hanifa requires repetition for a limited period based on physical signs, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, argue no repetition is needed unless the exact time of death is known. We will explore each position's proof and understand why later authorities preferred Abu Hanifa's view as the more cautious approach in worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Read the passage to identify the two opposing views within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa's position versus that of the Sahibayn. Note the practical difference: whether the family must repeat prayers, and for how long.
The disagreement is about the extent of obligation, not its basis.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Analyze the reasoning attributed to Abu Hanifa: death has an apparent cause (drowning), and signs (bloating or its absence) indicate the time frame. Less than a day cannot be pinned down, so estimates are based on observable signs.
This reasoning is based on istihsan (juristic preference) and circumstantial evidence.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
Understand the Sahibayn's argument: certainty (prayers were obligatory before death) cannot be removed by doubt (unknown time). They analogize to impurity on a garment when the time of occurrence is unknown.
They rely on qiyas and the maxim 'al-yaqin la yuzalu bi al-shakk'.
4
Understand the tarjih process
The passage cites later authorities (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shari'ah) who prefer Abu Hanifa's view. Note the criterion: in acts of worship, the more cautious (ahwat) option is chosen to ensure fulfillment of obligations.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that in cases of doubt in ibadat, the stricter view is adopted.

The Scope of Tayammum: Multiple Prayers and Concessions in the City
Ruling with proof on scope of tayammum and special concessions in cities.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that one tayammum suffices for multiple prayers and identify the conditions under which a healthy person in a city may perform tayammum for funeral, Eid, and Friday prayers.

Core Idea
One tayammum suffices for multiple prayers; healthy city dwellers may use it for specific prayers they would otherwise miss.

This lesson examines two key rulings from the Hanafi school regarding tayammum. First, that a single tayammum is valid for as many obligatory and supererogatory prayers as one wishes, as long as the conditions of tayammum persist. Second, that a healthy person residing in a city may perform tayammum for a funeral prayer when the guardian is someone else, and similarly for Eid and Friday prayers, if he fears missing the prayer due to the time required for wudu. The commentary in al-Lubab explains the reasoning behind these concessions and addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding Friday prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main rulings
The passage states two distinct rulings. Find them: (a) the general rule that one tayammum works for multiple prayers, and (b) the specific exceptions for healthy persons in cities.
The first ruling is unconditional; the second is restricted by conditions (city, healthy, fear of missing specific prayers).
2
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil) for the first ruling
Why does one tayammum suffice for many prayers? The sharh says it is a tahur (purification) like wudu, so it continues as long as its conditions remain.
The key is that tayammum is a valid substitute for water purification, not a temporary dispensation.
3
Analyze the restrictions for the second ruling
The permission for healthy persons in a city is restricted to when a funeral is present and the guardian (wali) is someone else. Why these restrictions? Because in the desert water is typically absent, and for the sick the need is always present. The wali restriction prevents the guardian from using tayammum because he can repeat the prayer.
The wali's ability to lead and repeat the funeral prayer removes the 'fear of loss' condition.
4
Extend to Eid and Friday prayers
The same reasoning applies to Eid and Friday prayers. Note the intra-Hanafi dispute for Friday: some (e.g., the initial view in al-Hidayah) restricted it, but the correct view (al-sahih) allows it because the person can repeat the prayer individually.
The repetition option makes Friday different from funeral and Eid, which cannot be repeated.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents rulings on the validity of tayammum for multiple prayers and special concessions for healthy persons in urban settings. The matn states two key rules: (1) A person who performs tayammum may pray as many obligatory and supererogatory prayers as he wishes with that single tayammum, as long as the conditions of tayammum remain intact. However, there is an intra-Hanafi difference: some hold that the guardian cannot perform tayammum because he has the right to repeat, while others, following the zahir al-riwayah, allow it for all. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the allowance for all, as stated by Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani and supported by the majority.

This lesson addresses the beginning time of the Fajr prayer. The author of al-Quduri begins his discussion of prayer times with Fajr because its start and end are agreed upon by the scholars, unlike other prayers. Dhuhr starts at zenith and ends when an object's shadow (minus fay' al-zawal) becomes twice its height.

Prayer times are determined by the position of the sun. For Dhuhr, the starting point is agreed upon: when the sun passes its zenith. However, the end time involves a well-known ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school. This passage presents the view of Abu Hanifa and contrasts it with that of his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn).

1
Identify the beginning of Dhuhr
Look at the first sentence: 'when the sun passes its zenith.' This is the universally agreed start of Dhuhr time.

The end of Maghrib time depends on shafaq—red or white—and the Hanafi school has a well-known internal dispute on this.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the times of Maghrib prayer, specifically the end time defined by the disappearance of shafaq (evening twilight). Definition of Shafaq: Intra-Hanafi Dispute and the Relied-upon Fatwa
Intra-Hanafi dispute on shafaq definition and its resolution in fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the definition of shafaq, identify the proofs, and apply the mufta bihi ruling to determine the beginning of Isha prayer.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school has two definitions of shafaq; the relied-upon fatwa defines it as the redness, making Isha begin earlier.

This lesson examines an important intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the definition of al-shafaq (twilight) and its impact on the timing of Isha prayer. While Imam Abu Hanifa originally defined shafaq as the whiteness after sunset, his two principal students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, defined it as the redness. The passage from al-Maydani's Lubab presents the dispute, the proofs, and the eventual tarjih that settled the fatwa on the view of the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State which imam holds which definition: Abu Hanifa says shafaq is the whiteness (bayad); Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say it is the redness (humrah). Note how this affects the start of Isha: according to Abu Hanifa, Isha begins when the whiteness disappears; according to the Sahibayn, it begins when the redness disappears.
The whiteness remains longer than the redness, so Abu Hanifa's view results in a later Isha time.
2
Examine the proofs for each view
Abu Hanifa's proof is based on the linguistic meaning of shafaq (the term can encompass both redness and whiteness) and the fact that the whiteness lasts longer, making it a more reliable sign. The Sahibayn rely on the practice of the majority of the Companions (qawl sahabi) who interpreted shafaq as redness, and a report that Abu Hanifa himself retracted his view.
The Hanafi school considers qawl sahabi as a valid proof when there is no opposing evidence.
3
Trace the tarjih process
Later scholars include both a defense of Abu Hanifa's original view (by al-Kamal and 'Allama Qasim) and widespread adoption of the Sahibayn's view. The text lists many works that explicitly state 'upon it is the fatwa' (عليه الفتوى), and notes that the practice of people in most lands is upon the Sahibayn's view.
The tarjih is driven by the strength of the Companion practice and the practical ease of the earlier time.
4
Apply the mufta bihi
Given that the relied-upon fatwa defines shafaq as redness, determine the beginning of Isha in a practical scenario: Isha time begins when the redness has disappeared from the western horizon. This is typically about 12–15 minutes after sunset, but can vary by location and season.
This is the time used in most Hanafi prayer timetables today.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Witr's time: Abu Hanifa says it starts with Isha time; the Sahibayn say it starts after Isha is prayed.

In this lesson, we examine a subtle yet consequential disagreement within the Hanafi school regarding when the Witr prayer begins. While the time of Isha is clearly defined from twilight to dawn, the timing of Witr is linked to Isha, leading to a dispute that affects the validity of Witr when Isha is invalid.

Guided Path
1
State the agreed-upon time of Isha
Isha time begins after the disappearance of the twilight (shafaq) and ends just before the true dawn (fajr thani). This is agreed upon in the school.
2
Identify the point of disagreement
The dispute is about when Witr time begins: with the entry of Isha time (Abu Hanifa) or after the Isha prayer is actually performed (Sahibayn).
This disagreement stems from the status of Witr as wajib vs sunnah mu'akkadah.
3
Present Abu Hanifa's view and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that Witr's time is the same as Isha time because Witr is wajib and its time is the night. However, its performance should follow Isha (i.e., not before Isha if one remembers).
The authority is Abu Hanifa (the Imam).
4
Present the Sahibayn's view and its basis
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that Witr time begins only after Isha is performed, because Witr is sunnah mu'akkadah and follows Isha in the Prophet's practice.
The authority is the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).

If one prays Isha without wudu forgetfully, then prays Witr with wudu, then remembers, according to Abu Hanifa only Isha is repeated (Witr valid); according to the Sahibayn both are repeated (Witr invalid because its time had not started).
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical fruit of the difference).

The Fourth Condition: Intention (Niyyah) for Prayer in the Hanafi School
Definition and ruling on intention as a condition for prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of niyyah as a condition for prayer, identify when it must be present, and apply the rules of specification for fard, nafl, sunnah, and wajib prayers.

Core Idea
Intention is a condition of the heart that must coincide with the opening takbir; specification is required for fard and wajib, but not for nafl.

This lesson examines the fourth condition of prayer in Hanafi fiqh: intention (niyyah). We will explore its definition, timing, what separates it from the takbir, and the degree of specification required for different categories of prayer.

Guided Path
1
Define niyyah and its legally required attributes
Identify the definition: an intention of the heart to perform a specific prayer. Hanafi school treats it as a condition (shart) that must be present at the time of the opening takbir (tahrimah).
Niyyah is an act of the heart, not verbal declaration.
2
Understand the timing requirement and sequence
The intention must not be delayed after the takbir; simultaneous (muqārana) is recommended, but the correct view in the school is that it cannot lag. A foreign action between them invalidates the intention.
If intention lags behind takbir, prayer is invalid according to the apparent transmission (zāhir al-riwāyah).
3

Commentary

If one asks but does not get an answer, he may proceed with taharri (informed judgment). This entire ruling is based on the principle that Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity.

Exceptions and Ijtihad: When Facing the Qibla is Excused or Unknown
Commentary on exceptions and ijtihad for qibla.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exceptions to facing the qibla, explain the procedure of ijtihad when the qibla is unknown, and apply the rule that no repetition is required after ijtihad.

Core Idea
Fear and incapacity excuse facing the qibla; ijtihad suffices when direction is unknown.

This lesson covers the situations where facing the qibla is not required due to fear or hardship, and what to do when the qibla is unclear. The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines: if you are fearful, pray as you can; if you do not know the direction, make ijtihad and pray; and once prayed, no repetition is required even if later proven wrong.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exceptions
List the cases where facing qibla is excused: fear of enemy or wild animal, risk of drowning on a ship, sickness with no help, etc.
These are genuine excuses (a'dhar).
2
Determine the ruling when qibla is unknown
If no one knowledgeable is present, one must make ijtihad (effort) to find the direction and pray accordingly.
Ijtihad is defined as badd al-majhud li-nayl al-maqsud.
3
Obligation to ask if someone is present
If there is a trustworthy person who knows the qibla, one must ask and follow their guidance, even if it contradicts one's own opinion.
The condition is that the person is from the locality and acceptable in testimony.
4
Consequence of error after ijtihad
If after praying one learns that the direction was wrong, no repetition is required because the person did what was within their capacity.
This is based on the principle of 'la yukallifu nafsan illa wus'aha'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for prayer when qibla is uncertain, identify the conditions for valid ijtihad, and apply the rule that no repetition is required after post-prayer discovery of error.

Core Idea
If you performed ijtihad for the qibla and prayed, you are not required to repeat even if you later discover you were wrong.

This passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri explains the Hanafi position on prayer when the qibla direction is ambiguous or when fear prevents facing it. It introduces key concepts like ijtihad and taharri, and clarifies the legal effect of discovering an error after prayer. Understanding these rulings reveals the Hanafi emphasis on human capacity and valid excuses.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri expands on the rulings related to the direction of prayer (qibla) under exceptional circumstances. The author first states the general rule: if a person is fearful (e.g., of an enemy or beast, or in a life-threatening situation like on a ship at risk of drowning, or a sick person who cannot be turned without harm), they may pray in any direction they are able, because the excuse is confirmed (li tahqīq al-‘udhr). This is an exception to the normal obligation of facing the qibla, which is a condition for the validity of prayer. Then, the text addresses the case where the qibla is ambiguous (ishtabahat) and no knowledgeable person is present to ask. In such a case, the person must perform ijtihad (independent reasoning to determine the qibla) and pray accordingly. A key point is that if, after completing the prayer, they learn they were mistaken (either by being informed or by their own ijtihad changing), no repetition (i‘ādah) is required because they performed what was within their capacity (mā fī was‘ihi). However, if the mistake is realized during the prayer, they must turn to the correct direction and continue the prayer, building upon what has already been performed. Similarly, if their ijtihad changes during the prayer, they adopt the new direction. The author also discusses a scenario involving an imam leading prayer on a dark night: if the imam prays facing east and the followers each make their own ijtihad, their prayers are valid as long as they are unaware of the imam's error. But if a follower knows the imam is wrong, the follower's prayer becomes invalid because they are knowingly following an erroneous ijtihad. This passage demonstrates the Hanafi school's emphasis on necessity and the limits of human effort in fulfilling religious obligations.

This lesson addresses two related scenarios: (1) a person in prayer realizes the correct qibla or his ijtihad changes, and (2) a congregation prays in darkness where each person independently determines the qibla. The key Hanafi principle is that ijtihad is binding for future acts, and past valid acts remain valid. The text uses an analogy to praying inside the Kaaba to illustrate that differing directions do not invalidate the prayer, provided each person acts on valid ijtihad. The proof for the permissibility of differing directions is an analogy (qiyas) to praying inside the Kaaba, where worshipers may face different internal sides yet the prayer is valid. The common illa (effective cause) is that each person faces a direction based on valid ijtihad.

State the two positions
According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the takbir is a condition (shart) of prayer, external to its essence. According to Imam Muhammad alone, it is a pillar (fard/rukn) integral to the prayer.
The text says 'shart cindahuma, wa fard cinda Muhammad'.
3
Examine the door analogy
Abu Hanifa counted the takbir among the pillars by analogy to a door: the door is distinct from the house but considered part of it. This supports the pillar view, but the preferred position is the condition view.
The analogy is used to justify counting it as a pillar, not to prove conditionhood.
4
Trace the thamarah (practical consequences)
Two scenarios illustrate the difference: (1) If a fard prayer becomes invalid, according to the two companions it becomes nafl, but not according to Muhammad. (2) If one begins Zuhr before noon and time enters during the takbir, the two companions deem it valid, but Muhammad does not.
The condition view allows more flexibility.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that takbirat al-ihram is a condition (shart), as held by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and supported by later authorities like al-Durr al-Mukhtar.
This is the operative ruling in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal nature of the opening takbir (takbirat al-ihram). The author, al-Maydani, states that according to Abu Hanifa's two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the opening takbir is a condition (shart) for prayer, external to its essence, while according to Imam Muhammad alone, it is a pillar (fard/rukn) integral to the prayer. The practical difference (thamarah) is illustrated with two scenarios: (1) If an obligatory prayer becomes invalid (e.g., one prayed Fajr before its time and later learns it was before dawn), according to the companions, it can be considered a nafl prayer because the condition can be attached to another act; according to Muhammad, it remains invalid. (2) If one begins Zuhr prayer before noon (before the time enters) and completes the takbir, then the sun passes noon, according to the companions the prayer is valid because the condition was fulfilled when the time entered; according to Muhammad, it is invalid as part of the prayer occurred outside its time. Abu Hanifa himself counted the takbir among the pillars analogizing it to the door of a house, which, though distinct from the house, is considered part of it. The passage also explains the name 'tahrima': it prohibits things that were permissible before it, such as eating, drinking, and speaking, while the prayer itself is distinct from those acts.

Key Terms
تحريمة (tahrima): The opening takbir of prayer; called so because it prohibits (tuharrim) previously permissible actions like eating, drinking, and speaking.
شرط (shart): A condition external to the essence of an act that must be present for the act to be valid.
ركن (rukn): A pillar or integral part of an act; its omission invalidates the act entirely and requires full repetition.
