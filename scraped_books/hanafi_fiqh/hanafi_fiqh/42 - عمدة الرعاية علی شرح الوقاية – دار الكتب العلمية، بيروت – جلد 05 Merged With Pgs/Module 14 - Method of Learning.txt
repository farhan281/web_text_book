Introduction to Hawalah and Its Conditions
Defines hawalah, provides evidence, lists conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hawalah, identify its evidence from Sunnah and Ijma, and list the conditions for its validity according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Hawalah: transfer of a binding debt requires consent of all parties and a competent transferor.

This passage from the commentary on al-Wiqayah introduces the contract of hawalah (transfer of debt). It explains why it follows kafalah, provides linguistic and technical definitions, cites the hadith and consensus as evidence, and details the conditions for validity. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly applying hawalah in financial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal concept
Define hawalah in technical terms: transfer of a debt from one liability to another. Recognize the parties: muhil, muhtal, muhtal 'alayh.
Hawalah is specific to debts, not chattels.
2
Analyze the evidence
Quote the hadith: 'Whoever is transferred to a wealthy person should follow him.' Explain how the imperative implies permissibility per Hanafi usul. Note the consensus.
The dalil is ahad, giving zanni certainty.
3
List the conditions of validity
For the transferor: sanity, consent (adulthood for enforceability). For the transferee: sanity, consent (adulthood for enforceability), presence or proxy acceptance. For the subject matter: must be a binding debt.
Absence of the party charged does not invalidate; he can accept later.
4
Understand the rationale for specificity to debts
Debt is a legal construct; hawalah is a legal transfer. Physical property requires physical delivery.
This distinction impacts when hawalah can be used.

In their custom it is said: the debtor is muhil, the creditor is muhtal (or muhtal lahu or muhal lahu), and the one who accepts the transfer is muhtal 'alayh (or muhal 'alayh), and the property is muhal bihi. Hawalah is legislated by consensus, and the Prophet said: 'Whoever is transferred (to a wealthy person) should follow (him).' Reported by al-Tabarani from al-A'raj from Abu Hurayrah. The command to follow indicates permissibility; and because it is an undertaking of what one is able to deliver, so it must be judged valid to fulfill a need. Its conditions of validity: Concerning the transferor (muhil): sanity; so the transfer of an insane person or a child who does not understand is not valid; consent, so the transfer of one coerced is not valid. Adulthood is a condition for enforceability, so the validity of the transfer of a discerning child is suspended upon the permission of his guardian. Freedom is not a condition, so the transfer of a slave is valid absolutely, but the authorized slave is pursued immediately, and the restricted slave after emancipation. Soundness of health is not a condition, so it is valid from a sick person. Concerning the transferee (muhtal): sanity and consent; adulthood is also a condition for enforceability, so the contract of a discerning child is suspended upon the permission of his guardian if the second is a hopeful creditor from the first, like the guardian's transfer for the orphan's property. And one of its conditions of validity is the session (majlis). (2) It is stated in al-Khaniyyah: The condition is the presence of the transferee only, so it is not valid in his absence unless someone else accepts on his behalf. The absence of the party charged (muhtal 'alayh) does not prevent, so if he transfers to him and the news reaches him and he accepts, it is valid. Similarly in al-Bazzaziyyah. Consent in acceptance is necessary, so if he is coerced to accept it is not valid. Concerning the subject matter (muhal bihi): it must be a binding debt, so it is not valid for kitabah payment, like kafalah. As stated in al-Nahr and others, and further detail is in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq and al-Fatawa al-Alamgiriyyah; if you wish, refer to them. [1] His statement: 'by a debt'; and hawalah is specific to debt because it indicates transfer and shifting, which only applies to debt, because debt is a legal attribute, and this transferred thing is a legal ruling whose effect appears in claim, so it is possible for the legal to affect what is legally established. As for specific property, it is physical, so it does not transfer by legal transfer but by physical transfer. Therefore it is necessary that the transferee have a debt upon the transferor. Thus it is stated in al-Qunyah: If he transferred one hundred measures of wheat and the transferor had nothing upon the party charged nor did the transferee have a debt upon the transferor, and the party charged accepted that, nothing is upon him. Commentary
This passage begins the Book of Hawalah (Transfer of Debt) in the commentary on al-Wiqayah. The author explains why the topic of Hawalah follows Kafalah: both are contracts of guarantee, but Hawalah includes a restricted discharge of the original debtor, making it like a compound concept, while Kafalah is simpler and more general (applying to both debts and chattels), so Kafalah is placed first. A linguistic analysis is given: the root h-w-l denotes movement and transfer, and the technical definition is 'transfer of a debt from one liability to another.' The parties are named: muhil (transferor/debtor), muhtal (transferee/creditor), muhtal 'alayh (the one charged/party accepting the debt), and the debt itself is muhal bihi. The legal basis is consensus (ijma') and the hadith: 'Whoever is transferred to a wealthy person should follow him', reported by al-Tabarani. The command indicates permissibility, and because it is an undertaking of something deliverable, it is valid to meet a need. The conditions of validity are detailed: the transferor must be sane and consenting (adulthood is only for enforceability, not validity); thus a discerning child's transfer is suspended on guardian's permission. Freedom is not required—a slave can transfer, but an authorized slave is pursued immediately while a restricted one after emancipation. Illness does not invalidate. The transferee must also be sane and consenting; his adulthood is for enforceability. The session (majlis) is a condition—the presence of the transferee is needed (unless another accepts on his behalf), but absence of the party charged does not prevent validity if he accepts later. Acceptance must be voluntary. The subject matter must be a binding debt, not a contingent one like kitabah payments. The passage explains why hawalah is specific to debts: because it is a legal transfer, and debts are legal constructs, whereas chattels are physical and require physical transfer. Therefore, the transferee must have a debt on the transferor; otherwise, the transfer is ineffective. All these rulings are based on Hanafi sources like al-Khaniyyah, al-Bazzaziyyah, al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, and al-Fatawa al-Alamgiriyyah.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-ḥawālah): Transfer of a debt from one liability to another; a contract wherein a debtor transfers his debt to another person who becomes liable.
المحيل (al-muḥīl): The original debtor who transfers his debt to another person.
المحتال (al-muḥtāl): The creditor who accepts the transfer and now claims from the new debtor.
المحتال عليه (al-muḥtāl ʿalayh): The person who assumes the debt and becomes liable to the creditor.

Definition and Conditions of Ijma (Consensus)
Defines Ijma and discusses disagreements on its binding conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of Ijma, identify its conditions, and explain the dispute over majority vs. unanimity.

Core Idea
Ijma is the agreement of qualified mujtahids; the Hanafi school holds that majority agreement suffices.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya defines Ijma and explores its conditions. Understanding Ijma is crucial because it is the third primary source of Islamic law after Quran and Sunnah. The Hanafi school has a distinctive position on what constitutes a valid consensus, allowing for majority agreement, which differs from the stricter requirement of unanimity held by some other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of Ijma
Extract the linguistic and technical definition of Ijma from the passage. Note the key elements: agreement of qualified mujtahids, one era, on a matter not decisively established by Quran and Sunnah.
The definition excludes matters already clear from definitive texts.
2
List the conditions for Ijma
From the passage, identify what is NOT a condition: Ijma does not require it to be from Companions, from Ahl al-Bayt, from Madinah, or the extinction of the era.
These are negative conditions; the positive conditions are implicit in the definition.
3
Understand the core dispute
The central dispute is whether Ijma requires unanimity of all mujtahids or is established by majority agreement. The passage lists several opinions on the minimum number and the positions of various scholars.
The Hanafi school (al-Hidayah) chooses the majority view.
4
Analyze the Hanafi position and its evidence
The Hanafi position is that agreement of the majority suffices, and opposition of the minority is not considered. The passage cites al-Hidayah and also notes that many usul scholars consider minority opposition, but the Hanafi relied position is the former.
The Mu'tazila also argue for majority, but the Hanafi response reinterprets the hadith they use.
5
Apply the rule to a judge's ruling
The passage states that if a judge rules contrary to established Ijma, his ruling must be overturned. However, if the matter is one of legitimate ikhtilaf (like Ibn Abbas's view), the ruling is not against Ijma.
This application shows the practical force of Ijma.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the definition and conditions of Ijma (consensus) from the Hanafi perspective. The author begins by stating the core issue: whether Ijma is established by the agreement of the majority of mujtahids or requires unanimity. He then provides a ruling: if a judge rules contrary to established Ijma, his ruling must be overturned. However, if the matter is one of legitimate difference (such as Ibn Abbas's view on the exclusion of the mother), a judge's ruling based on that difference is valid and not opposed to Ijma. The author then defines Ijma linguistically and technically: it is the agreement of qualified mujtahids of the Muslim community in one era on a matter not explicitly decided by definitive texts. Notably, the Hanafi school does not require Ijma to be from the Companions only, nor does it require the extinction of the era. The passage then lists various scholarly opinions on the minimum number required for Ijma: three (preferred by al-Sarakhsi), two, or even one (based on the verse calling Ibrahim a 'nation'). It opens by presenting the position from Al-Hidayah that the agreement of the majority is sufficient, and that the opposition of the minority is not considered. Al-Birjandi notes that some later scholars hold that the majority opinion is a proof but not Ijma. The Hanafi response is that this hadith refers to after Ijma is already established; deviation after that leads to hell. The passage then notes an apparent contradiction between Al-Kifayah (which says Ijma is formed by majority) and the Usul al-Fiqh books (which require unanimity). The resolution provided by Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi is that the former applies when the dissenter's ijtihad is allowed and he is not rejected; in that case, his opposition prevents Ijma. The latter applies when the dissenter's ijtihad is not allowed and his view is rejected; then the majority opinion constitutes Ijma. Since the Companions allowed Ibn Abbas his ijtihad and did not reject him, his opposition prevented Ijma. Thus, the operative rule is that Ijma requires either unanimity or, if there is dissent, that the dissenters are considered mistaken and their view rejected.

Validity of Ijma' with Dissent: The Hanafi Reconciliation
Expansion on ijma' conditions with dissenter's status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on when a dissenting scholar prevents ijma' and apply the reconciliation between the two usul texts.

Core Idea
Consensus is valid despite dissent when the dissenter's view is censured; only tolerated differences prevent consensus.

This passage tackles a critical methodological question: Can ijma' (consensus) be established when even one scholar dissents? The apparent contradiction between two Hanafi usul texts is resolved by distinguishing whether the dissenter's ijtihad was permitted and uncensured. Through the example of Ibn Abbas, we see how the early community treated differences. This lesson will clarify the Hanafi framework for evaluating dissent and its effect on consensus.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the initial claim from Al-Kifayah that majority agreement suffices for ijma', even if a minority opposes. Recognize that this appears to contradict other usul texts stating that even one dissenter prevents consensus.
The contradiction is intentional to prompt resolution.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i and some Hanafis (e.g., al-Karkhi) hold that any dissenter, even one, blocks consensus. Their proof is the hadith: 'My companions are like stars; by whichever of them you follow, you are guided.'
This hadith is used to argue that all Companion opinions are valid, so dissent must be accommodated.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the opponent's dalil: the hadith implies that each Companion's view is a source of guidance, so to ignore a dissenter's view would be to disregard a valid source. Therefore, consensus cannot exclude any dissenting Companion.
This reasoning would make ijma' nearly impossible if any difference existed among the Companions.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi reconciliation distinguishes between two scenarios: when the majority permits the dissenter's ijtihad and does not censure him, his view prevents consensus; when they censure his view and reject it, consensus stands without him. This is exemplified by Ibn Abbas: his view on inheritance was tolerated, so no consensus; his view on riba was censured, so consensus was established.
The key is the community's reaction to the dissent, not the mere existence of difference.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: later scholars who oppose the early majority but whose views are not rooted in tolerated early differences cannot block consensus. Their opinions may be disregarded if they lack early authority or are censured.
This prevents novelty from undermining established consensus.

This appears to contradict the principle in some usul works that even a single dissenter prevents consensus. The reconciliation provided here is crucial: when the dissenter is allowed to exercise ijtihad and is not censured, his view prevents consensus; but when he is censured and his view is rejected, consensus stands despite his dissent. The hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) comparing companions to stars is used by al-Shafi'i to argue that each valid opinion must be considered. The Hanafis respond by distinguishing between permitted and censured ijtihad. The example of Ibn Abbas opposing the majority on the mother's share in inheritance and on riba illustrates this: in the first case, his view was tolerated, so consensus was not complete; in the second, it was rejected, and he retracted, so consensus was established. This passage also clarifies that only differences among the early generations (Companions and Followers) are considered legitimate for later ijtihad; later differences do not create genuine room for ijtihad.

Key Terms
إجماع (ijma'): Consensus of scholars on a legal ruling; a primary source of law.
مخالف (mukhalif): A scholar who dissents from the majority opinion on a legal issue.
صدر أول (sadr awwal): The early generation of scholars, including the Companions and the Followers.
