Scope of 'My Property is Sadaqah': Zakatable Wealth Only
Explaining the legal effect of a sadaqah declaration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the declaration 'my property is sadaqah' and identify which assets are included, applying the principle that the type (jins) of zakatable wealth determines inclusion.

Core Idea
A charity declaration applies only to zakatable wealth — the type (jins) matters, not the value or conditions.

This passage distinguishes between a will (wasiyyah), which applies to all property, and a sadaqah declaration, which is restricted to zakatable wealth. The key is the type (jins) of asset, not its amount or whether it meets zakat conditions. We'll explore which assets are included and an intra-Hanafi disagreement about 'ushr land.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish will vs sadaqah declaration
Note that a will of one-third applies to all property, like inheritance. In contrast, 'my property is sadaqah' is limited to zakatable wealth only.
The contrast helps understand the specific scope of sadaqah.
2
Identify the general rule on sadaqah declaration
The text states it falls on mal al-zakat: gold, silver, grazing animals, and trade goods, regardless of nisab or debt. The criterion is the type (jins) of wealth that is zakatable.
The illah is the type, not amount or conditions.
3
Explore the intra-Hanafi dispute on 'ushr land
Abu Yusuf includes 'ushr land because its proceeds go to zakat recipients. Muhammad excludes it because it is a tax (mu'nah) and is obligatory even on minors and awqaf, showing the tax aspect is stronger.
Both views are recorded in the commentaries.
4
Note the exception for 'what I own' (milk)
Some scholars argue that 'milk' is broader than 'mal', so saying 'what I own' would obligate charity on all possessions. However, the relied-upon position restricts it to zakatable wealth as per the matn.
The matn itself uses 'mal' (property) not 'milk' (ownership).

Commentary
This passage addresses the legal effect of two statements: (1) a will (wasiyyah) of one-third of one's property, and (2) a declaration that 'my property is sadaqah' or 'what I own is sadaqah'. For the will, the commentator explains that the one-third applies to all of the testator's property, because a will is similar to inheritance (it applies to all assets). For the declaration of sadaqah, it applies only to zakatable wealth (mal al-zakat), such as gold, silver, grazing livestock, and trade goods, regardless of whether they reach the nisab or whether there is debt. The reasoning is that the word 'sadaqah' is associated with zakat, and the effective cause (illah) is the type of wealth that is subject to zakat, not the specific conditions. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding 'ushr land (land subject to the tithe): Abu Yusuf includes it because it is a cause for sadaqah (its proceeds go to the same recipients as zakat), while Muhammad excludes it because it is a tax (mu'nah) and the aspect of tax is preponderant (it is obligatory even on minors and awqaf). The commentary also notes that if one says 'what I own is sadaqah', some scholars consider the word 'milk' (ownership) to be more general than 'mal' (wealth), so one must give away all possessions, not just zakatable assets. However, the relied-upon position appears to be that it applies only to zakatable wealth, as indicated by the matn.

Validity of Will Without Executor's Knowledge
Hanafi ruling distinguishing will from agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a will without the executor's knowledge and explain the rational distinction from agency.

Core Idea
A will is a succession (khilafah), not a deputyship; knowledge is not required.

This lesson examines a unique Hanafi ruling: a will (wasiyyah) is valid even if the appointed executor (wasi) is unaware of his appointment. This contrasts sharply with agency (wakalah), where the agent's knowledge is a condition for validity. The underlying rationale is that a will is a form of succession that takes effect after death, when deputyship ends, analogous to the heir who may act without knowing of the death. Let us dissect the passage to understand the proof and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Pinpoint the exact legal rule stated: the will (wasiyyah) is valid (sahih) even without the executor's knowledge.
The text says: 'And a will is valid without the knowledge of the executor.'
2
Locate the analogy (qiyas)
The passage uses an analogy to the heir: if the heir sells the deceased's property without knowing of the death, the sale is valid. This is the asl (original case).
The ruling for the heir is established; the far' (new case) is the executor's sale.
3
Extract the illah (effective cause)
The illah is that both the will and inheritance are forms of succession (khilafah) attached to after death, a time when deputyship (inayabah) ends.
This is why knowledge is not required: the appointment itself creates the capacity.
4
Contrast with agency
Agency (wakalah) is a deputyship during life, which requires the agent's knowledge and acceptance.
Without knowledge, the agency contract is not complete; the agent's actions are invalid unless ratified.
5
Apply the distinction
Thus, in practice, an executor who sells property before learning of his appointment acts validly, whereas an agent in a similar situation does not.
The thamarah (practical consequence) is clear: executors enjoy broader authority from the moment of appointment.

Validity of Will without Executor's Knowledge vs Agency
States ruling and proof for validity of وصية without knowledge of وصي

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a will without the executor's knowledge, apply the qiyas to an heir's sale, and distinguish it from agency.

Core Idea
A will is a succession after death; knowledge of the executor is not required, unlike agency which requires knowledge during life.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: a will (wasiyyah) remains valid even if the appointed executor is unaware of his appointment, whereas an agency (tawkil) is invalid without the agent's knowledge. The underlying principle is that a will is a transfer added to after death—a time when deputyship ends—so the executor's knowledge is irrelevant. We will analyze the qiyas (analogy) to an heir's disposal and explore an intra-Hanafi difference with Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Note the matn statement: a will is valid (sahih) without the executor's knowledge; agency is not. Identify that the ruling applies to the validity of the executor's sale of estate property after the testator's death.
The 'act' here is the sale by the executor, which is deemed valid.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The commentary provides a qiyas: the executor is analogous to an heir. Just as an heir's sale of inherited property is valid even if he did not know of the death, so is the executor's sale.
This is a qiyas jaliy as the common factor (illa) is the timing of the transfer after death.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The illa is that both will and inheritance involve transfer of ownership at death, a time when deputyship (إنابة) is impossible. Therefore, knowledge of the appointee is not a condition for validity.
This distinguishes from agency, which operates during life and requires the agent's consent.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf holds that even the executor's sale is invalid without knowledge, reasoning that both will and agency are forms of deputyship. The difference in timing (life vs. death) does not change the need for consent.
The matn implies the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The phrase 'وعند أبي يوسف' indicates that the contrary view is attributed to Abu Yusuf, while the default position is that of Abu Hanifa. Later Hanafi works uphold Abu Hanifa's view as the operative one.
Authority weight: Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary

The matn states that a will is valid without the executor's knowledge, but an agency is not.
