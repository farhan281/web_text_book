Intentional Omission of Tasmiya: A Ruling Contrary to the Quran
Ruling with proof: deliberate tasmiya omission and Quranic contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on meat slaughtered without tasmiya intentionally, identify the Quranic proof, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the enforceability of a contrary judge's ruling.

Core Idea
Intentional omission of tasmiya makes meat haram, explicitly prohibited by Quran 6:121; a judge's ruling to the contrary is invalid.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explores a critical principle: a judge's ruling that directly contradicts the Quran is void. The example given is the deliberate omission of tasmiya (saying Bismillah) during slaughter. We will see the Quranic proof, the distinction between intentional and forgetful omission, and a grammatical refutation of an alternative interpretation. We also touch on an intra-Hanafi difference regarding whether such a ruling can be enforced.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its proof
The passage states that intentionally omitting tasmiya makes the animal's meat haram, citing Quran 6:121 as the proof.
The verse is quoted: 'And do not eat of that upon which the name of Allah has not been mentioned'.
2
Distinguish intentional vs. forgetful omission
The restriction to 'aamid' (intentional) excludes forgetfulness. If one omits tasmiya without knowing it is required or forgetfully, the meat is permissible.
The gloss explicitly says the forgetful person is like the one who forgets.
3
Understand the grammatical argument
The phrase 'wa innahu lafisqun' is analyzed. The waw is conjunctive, not circumstantial. The emphasis (inna, lam) shows the act is inherently sinful, not a conditional prohibition.
The text refutes the possibility that the verse means 'do not eat if it is a transgression' by arguing that the emphasis negates that interpretation.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on enforceability
Abu Hanifa holds that even a contrary judge's ruling (declaring such meat permissible) is enforceable (nāfidh), while Abu Yusuf disagrees. The text implies the stronger position is that the ruling is invalid.
The matn's example classifies this as a ruling contrary to the Quran, therefore invalid, siding with Abu Yusuf's view.

[٢] قوله : فإنّه مخالف لقوله تعالى ... الخ، بناءً على أن الواو في قوله : وَإِنَّهُ لَفِسْقٌ (۳) للعطف، والضمير راجع إلى مصدر الفعل الذي دخل عليه حرفُ النهي أو إلى الموصول، واحتمال كونها حالية، فتكون قيدا للنهي. رد بأن التأكيد بأن واللام ينفيه ؛ لأنَّ الحال في النهي مبناه على التقدير؛ كأنه قيل : لا تأكلوا إن كان فاسقاً فلا يصلح، وإنه لفسق، بل هو فسق، ولو سلم فلا تسلم، أنه قيد للنهي ، بل هو إشارة في المعنى الموجب له فلا تُهن زيداً وهو أخوك ، ولا تشرب الخمر وهو حرام عليك. كذا في «المحيط» نقلا عن «النوازل». إذا قضى بحل متروك التسمية عامداً ينفذ عند أبي حنيفة له خلافاً لأبي يوسف ذكره البرْجَندِي ؛ ولذا قال القُهُسْتانِيُّ (٤) : والأحسن أن يُمثل بالقضاء بتقديم الوارث على المديون، فإن الأوَّلَ نافذ عن الطرفين. كذا في «المغني»، وغيره. انتهى.
1. Contrary to the Book: like intentionally omitting the tasmiya, for it is contrary to His saying, 'And do not eat of that upon which the name of Allah has not been mentioned' (6:121). Or to the well-known sunnah: like ruling that a thrice-divorced woman is permissible by marrying a second husband without intercourse, according to the madhhab of Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab. (1) His saying: 'intentionally omitting the tasmiya'; its scenario is that he knows that the tasmiya is a condition, and he abandons it while being aware of it. But if he abandons it without knowing it is a condition, he is in the same ruling as the forgetful, as they have explicitly stated. The restriction to 'intentional' excludes the case of forgetfulness, for whoever omits it forgetfully, his slaughtered animal is eaten. Al-Shafi'i said: The slaughtered animal of the one who omits the tasmiya is eaten in both cases. The detailed proofs will come to you, Allah willing, later in 'Kitab al-Dhaba'ih'. [2] His saying: 'for it is contrary to His saying...' etc., based on the fact that the waw in His saying 'And indeed it is a transgression' (6:121) is for conjunction, and the pronoun refers to the infinitive of the verb that entered by the particle of negation or to the relative clause, and the possibility of it being a circumstantial clause, thus it becomes a restriction for the prohibition. It is refuted that the emphasis with 'inna' and 'lam' negates that; because the circumstantial clause in a prohibition is based on estimation; as if it was said: 'Do not eat if it is transgression' is not valid, and it is indeed transgression, rather it is transgression. And if it is granted, it is not granted that it is a restriction for the prohibition, rather it is an indication in meaning that necessitates it, so do not dishonor Zaid while he is your brother, and do not drink wine while it is forbidden to you. Thus in 'al-Muhit' quoting from 'al-Nawazil'. If a judge rules that intentionally omitted tasmiya is permissible, it is enforceable according to Abu Hanifa, contrary to Abu Yusuf – as mentioned by al-Barjandi. Hence al-Quhustani said: It is better to exemplify with the ruling preferring the heir over the debtor, for the former is binding on both sides. Thus in 'al-Mughni' and others. End.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It addresses the fourth type of judge's ruling that is invalid: one contrary to the Quran. The example given is a judge who permits the meat of an animal slaughtered without tasmiya intentionally. The text argues that intentionally omitting tasmiya is a direct violation of Quran 6:121, which forbids eating from animals not slaughtered in Allah's name. The gloss clarifies that the condition applies only when the omission is deliberate and knowing; if the person is unaware of the requirement or forgetful, the animal is permissible. The author then provides a detailed grammatical refutation of a potential objection by al-Shafi'i, who held that the verse does not imply prohibition. The text uses the phrase 'wa innahu lafisqun' to argue that the act is inherently sinful, not a conditional prohibition. Finally, it notes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that even if the judge's ruling contradicts the Quran, it is enforceable, whereas Abu Yusuf disagrees. The preferred example for a clearly invalid ruling is something else (e.g., giving precedence to an heir over a creditor).

Key Terms
تسمية (tasmiya): Saying 'Bismillah' during slaughter; a condition for the meat's permissibility.
عامد (aamid): One who acts intentionally and knowingly, aware of the ruling.
فسق (fisq): Transgression or sin; used in the verse to describe the act of eating without tasmiya.
