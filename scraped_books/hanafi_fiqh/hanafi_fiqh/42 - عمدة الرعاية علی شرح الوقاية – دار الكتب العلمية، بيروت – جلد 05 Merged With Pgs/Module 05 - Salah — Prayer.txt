Sale During Friday Adhan: The Role of 'Illa
Commentary on the ruling of sale at the first Friday adhan after zawal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on sale during the Friday adhan and identify when the prohibition is lifted due to absence of the effective cause.

Core Idea
The prohibition of sale during Friday adhan hinges on distraction; if walking, the cause vanishes.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary clarifies the ruling of sale at the first Friday adhan. While the Quranic command to hasten to the prayer and leave off sale appears absolute, the Hanafi school analyzes it through the lens of 'illa (effective cause). The key question is: does the prohibition apply even when the parties are walking to the mosque? The author explores a dispute rooted in different usul positions on whether Quranic texts are mu'allala (causally motivated). We will unpack this ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? Sale during the first Friday adhan after zawal. The ruling is makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked).
Not haram, but close to it.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is Quran 62:9: 'So hasten to the remembrance of Allah and leave off sale.' The imperative 'leave off' indicates prohibition.
The dalil is Qur'anic, qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Hanafi school interprets the command as makruh tahrimi, not haram, because the prohibition is not absolute; it is tied to a specific cause (illa).
The wording 'adhuru al-bay'a' is general, but the 'illa restricts its application.
4
Explore the ta'lil
The 'illa of the prohibition is distraction from the wajib act of hastening to prayer. If the sale occurs while walking, there is no distraction, so the 'illa is absent, and the ruling vanishes.
This is based on the Hanafi principle that texts are mu'allala.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars hold that the prohibition applies even when walking, because they view the text as not mu'allala. The author favors the first view, citing sources like al-'Inayah.
Practical difference: the ruling changes based on one's usul stance.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn's phrase 'and the sale at the Friday call to prayer.' The author explains that the prohibition is due to 'qabih mujawir' (a contiguous evil) — specifically, missing the obligatory hastening to Friday prayer commanded in Quran 62:9. However, if the sale occurs while both parties are walking to the mosque, there is no objection according to the relied-upon sources (al-'Inayah, al-Nihayah, al-Kifayah). A difference of opinion is noted: some scholars consider it still makruh even when walking. This disagreement depends on the usul issue of whether the textual prohibition is 'mu'allala' (has an identified effective cause, 'illa) or not. According to the Hanafi position that texts are mu'allala, the 'illa of the prohibition is being distracted from the sa'y. If the parties are walking, the 'illa is absent, so the ruling (prohibition) is also absent. The author leaves further discussions due to their subtlety. He clarifies that the relevant call to prayer is the first one after zawal (noon).

Key Terms
قبيح مجاور (qabih mujawir): An evil that is contiguous to the act (distraction from wajib), not inherent in the sale itself.
علة ('illa): The effective cause of the ruling: here, being occupied from sa'y.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi ruling on sale during Friday adhan is makruh tahrimi, but if the parties are walking to the mosque, there is no karahah due to the absence of the effective cause (distraction).

Prohibition of Partial Substitution in Salam Iqalah
Explains prohibition of partial substitution in salam cancellation with hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that in salam iqalah one must take either the full capital or the full goods, and apply the hadith proof to determine permissible actions.

Core Idea
In salam iqalah, you must take either the full capital or the full goods — never a mixture of both.

When a salam contract is cancelled by mutual agreement (iqalah), the parties may either proceed with the contract or cancel it. The Hanafi school, relying on authentic hadith and companion practice, strictly prohibits taking part of the capital and part of the goods. This lesson examines the evidence and the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the options in salam iqalah
What are the two permissible outcomes when parties agree to cancel a salam contract? The text states: 'do not take the salam goods... or your capital...' meaning either proceed (take the goods) or cancel (take back the capital).
The two options are: take the full salam goods (as if continuing the contract) or take back the full capital.
2
State the prohibition
What exactly is prohibited? The hadith and athar explicitly forbid taking some of the capital and some of the goods. The text says: 'Do not take some of our capital, or some of our food, but take all of our capital, or full food.'
Partial substitution is prohibited.
3
Locate the dalil
Identify the evidence: the hadith of Abu Sa'id al-Khudri (Sunan Abi Dawud, Ibn Majah) and the athar of Ibn Umar and Abdullah ibn Amr as compiled in Nasb al-Rayah.
The hadith states: 'من أسلف في شيء فلا يصرفه إلى غيره' (Whoever advances payment for something should not divert it to something else).
4
Explain the wajh al-dalalah
How does the dalil prove the ruling? The imperative 'لا يصرفه' indicates prohibition of substitution. The athar shows the practical application: rejection of partial taking.
The prohibition is understood to include partial taking, as that is a form of substitution.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a salam for 100 kg of wheat with capital of 100 dirhams, if both agree to cancel, can the buyer take 50 kg wheat and 50 dirhams? Apply the ruling: no, he must take either 100 dirhams or 100 kg wheat.
The ruling protects against uncertainty and complies with the hadith.

Requirement of Qabd in the Sitting After Iqalah: Salam vs Sarf
Explains the difference between salam and sarf regarding the need for qabd in iqalah, with intra-Hanafi reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why qabd (receipt) in the sitting is not required after iqalah in salam but is required in sarf, and apply the distinction to determine the validity of iqalah.

Core Idea
In salam iqalah, receipt in the sitting is not required because substitution is prohibited; in sarf it is required because substitution is allowed.

After establishing that partial substitution is prohibited in salam iqalah, the text addresses a further question: is immediate receipt (qabd) in the sitting necessary for the iqalah to be valid? The Hanafi school distinguishes salam from sarf (currency exchange) based on whether substitution is permitted. This lesson unpacks that distinction and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracts compared
The passage compares salam (advance payment sale) with sarf (currency exchange). Both have specific rules for qabd in the original contract. What is the rule for qabd in the original salam? It is required at the sitting for validity. For iqalah, the question is whether that requirement persists.
In the original salam, qabd of capital is a condition (shart) for validity.
2
State the Hanafi position on qabd after iqalah in salam
According to al-Bada'i and al-Bahr, qabd is not required in the sitting after iqalah in salam. Why? Because the purpose of qabd is specification (ta'yin) to avoid 'debt for debt'. In salam, substitution is prohibited, so the capital after iqalah is already specified (it returns to the capital that was paid).
Specification is inherent; no need for separate qabd.
3
Contrast with the ruling in sarf
In sarf, even in iqalah, qabd in the sitting is required. Why? Because in sarf, substitution of the countervalue is allowed. Therefore, specification is not inherent; it is only achieved by actual receipt at the sitting.
In sarf, the debt can be substituted, so receipt is needed to make it specific.
4
Follow the Hanafi response to the opposing analogy
The opposing view (attributed to Shafi'i) analogizes salam to sarf, requiring qabd in iqalah. The Hanafi response: salam differs because substitution is prohibited, fulfilling the purpose of specification differently.
The thamarah al-khilaf: in sarf, iqalah requires sitting qabd; in salam, it does not.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If parties iqalah a salam contract but do not exchange the capital on the spot, is the iqalah valid? Yes, because qabd is not a condition in the sitting. However, in a sarf contract, failing to exchange on the spot would invalidate the iqalah.
The ruling is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.

Possession by Command: When the Buyer Orders the Seller to Measure into His Container
Ruling on possession by command in salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a buyer becomes a possessor when ordering the seller to measure goods into the buyer's container, and identify when possession is not achieved.

Core Idea
Possession by command is valid when the container belongs to the buyer and the order is over owned property.

In salam contracts, delivery of the purchased item is essential. The buyer may instruct the seller to measure the grain into a container. This lesson examines when such an instruction results in valid possession (qabd). The ruling hinges on ownership of the container and the nature of the property being measured.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The buyer orders the seller to measure the purchased grain into a container.
The buyer is absent.
2
Check container ownership
If the container belongs to the buyer, his command is over his own property.
If the container belongs to the seller, the buyer has not taken possession of the container.
3
Determine if the command is over owned property
The command must be over a specific owned item (ayn), not a debt.
A debt is not a specific item; ownership is by liability.
4
Conclude possession
If both conditions are met, the buyer becomes a possessor because the seller acts as his agent by necessity.
Possession can be established implicitly.
5
Contrast with seller's container
If the container belongs to the seller, the buyer does not become a possessor because the container is not in his hand.
Analogous to ordering measurement in a corner of the seller's house.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Hidayah deals with a specific scenario in the salam sale: the buyer orders the seller to measure the purchased grain into the buyer's own container. The ruling is that if the buyer owns the container and the order is given while the buyer is absent, the buyer becomes a possessor (qabid) of the grain because the order is validly given over his own property. This is based on the principle that the buyer owns the grain by purchase, so his command is over his own property, and the seller acts as his agent by necessity. The commentary then discusses the details: if the container belonged to the seller, the buyer does not become possessor because he borrowed it without taking actual possession. The main legal issue arises when the buyer has both a debt (from the salam) and a specific item ('ayn) to be measured in the same container. If the seller begins by measuring the specific item, the buyer becomes possessor of both because the debt attaches to his ownership through connection. If he begins with the debt, a difference of opinion arises among the Hanafi imams. Abu Hanifa holds that the buyer does not become possessor of either: the command over the debt is invalid (since the debt is not a specific owned item), and the specific item becomes mixed with the seller's property, which constitutes consumption (istihlak) and invalidates the sale. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), mixing is not consumption, so the buyer has an option: either cancel the sale or share in the mixture proportionally. However, there is a further intra-madhhab difference: Abu Yusuf says if the seller begins with the debt, the buyer becomes possessor of both because the mixture is still connected to his ownership; Muhammad says he becomes possessor only of the specific item, not the debt, so they share the mixture for the specific item, but the debt remains. The passage also compares to a loan scenario for analogy. The footnotes further discuss the nature of agency and possession by connection.

Key Terms
السلم (al-salam): A sale with advance payment for deferred delivery of specified goods.
القبض (al-qabd): Possession or taking delivery; here it can be by actual hand or by connection to ownership (ittisal bi-milk).
العين (al-'ayn): Specific tangible item, as opposed to debt (dayn).
الدين (al-dayn): Debt or liability, here the quantity of wheat due from the salam contract.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute over Starting with Debt vs. Specific Item in Salam
Intra-Hanafi dispute on possession when starting with debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi positions on possession when the seller begins by measuring the debt into the buyer's container, and identify the practical consequence of each.

Core Idea
When measuring debt and specific item together, starting with the debt invalidates possession according to Abu Hanifa, but Sahibayn grant an option.

This lesson delves into the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the scenario where the buyer has both a debt from a salam contract and a purchased specific item, and the seller begins measuring the debt into the buyer's container. The imams differ on whether possession is achieved and on the legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scenario
The buyer owns a debt (dayn) from a salam contract and also buys a specific item ('ayn). He orders the seller to put both in his container.
2
Identify the starting point
If the seller begins with the specific item, the buyer becomes possessor of both. If he begins with the debt, a dispute arises.
This is the key variable.
3
State Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa: mixing the buyer's specific item with the seller's property (the debt is in seller's possession) constitutes consumption (istihlak). Thus, the sale is rescinded and no possession is achieved.
Command over debt is invalid because debt is not a specific owned item.
4
State Sahibayn's view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: mixing is not consumption. The buyer has an option: either cancel the sale or share in the mixture proportionally.
Within Sahibayn, there is further difference.
5
Distinguish between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Abu Yusuf: buyer becomes possessor of both because mixture connects to his ownership. Muhammad: buyer becomes possessor only of the specific item, not the debt; they share the mixture for the specific item, and the debt remains.
This affects the buyer's liability.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Hidayah deals with a specific scenario in the salam sale: the buyer orders the seller to measure the purchased grain into the buyer's own container. The ruling is that if the buyer owns the container and the order is given while the buyer is absent, the buyer becomes a possessor (qabid) of the grain because the order is validly given over his own property. This is based on the principle that the buyer owns the grain by purchase, so his command is over his own property, and the seller acts as his agent by necessity. The commentary then discusses the details: if the container belonged to the seller, the buyer does not become possessor because he borrowed it without taking actual possession. The main legal issue arises when the buyer has both a debt (from the salam) and a specific item ('ayn) to be measured in the same container. If the seller begins by measuring the specific item, the buyer becomes possessor of both because the debt attaches to his ownership through connection. If he begins with the debt, a difference of opinion arises among the Hanafi imams. Abu Hanifa holds that the buyer does not become possessor of either: the command over the debt is invalid (since the debt is not a specific owned item), and the specific item becomes mixed with the seller's property, which constitutes consumption (istihlak) and invalidates the sale. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), mixing is not consumption, so the buyer has an option: either cancel the sale or share in the mixture proportionally. However, there is a further intra-madhhab difference: Abu Yusuf says if the seller begins with the debt, the buyer becomes possessor of both because the mixture is still connected to his ownership; Muhammad says he becomes possessor only of the specific item, not the debt, so they share the mixture for the specific item, but the debt remains. The passage also compares to a loan scenario for analogy. The footnotes further discuss the nature of agency and possession by connection.

Istihsan in Sarf: Selling a Dinar for a Debt of Ten Dirhams
Analyses a sarf transaction with istihsan and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on selling a dinar for an unspecified debt of ten dirhams, state the role of istihsan in overriding qiyas, and distinguish this case from salam.

Core Idea
Istihsan validates a set-off that creates a new exchange, avoiding riba and debt-for-debt prohibition.

This passage from a Hanafi fiqh commentary addresses a nuanced transaction in currency exchange (sarf). The key question: can a creditor sell a dinar to his debtor for an unspecified ten dirhams that the debtor already owes? The Hanafi school says yes, based on istihsan (juristic preference), even though strict analogy (qiyas) would prohibit it. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, the intra-Hanafi disagreement with Zufar, and the critical distinction from salam (forward sale).

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the transaction
Zayd owes Amr ten dirhams. Amr sells a dinar to Zayd for an unspecified ten dirhams (not tied to the existing debt). Amr hands over the dinar. Now both owe each other ten dirhams, and they set off the mutual debts.
The key is that the debt is 'unspecified' (مطلقة) – not explicitly linked to the owed dirhams.
2
Understand the qiyas objection (Zufar's view)
By qiyas, this transaction is invalid because it appears to be a substitution for a currency exchange (بدل الصرف) – paying a debt (the owed dirhams) with another debt (the price of the dinar). This is prohibited to avoid riba. Zufar, a Hanafi scholar, holds this view.
Qiyas analogizes to the rule that in sarf, both countervalues must be specified and exchanged hand-to-hand.
3
Grasp the istihsan reasoning (Hanafi position)
Set-off (المقاصة) does not merely substitute one debt for another; it dissolves the first sale (dinar for unspecified dirhams) and creates a new exchange: the dinar for the specific debt of ten dirhams. This implied new contract avoids the prohibition of debt-for-debt exchange.
The analogy given: if two parties contract for 1000 and then renew for 1500, the first contract is necessarily dissolved. Here, the set-off implies a similar dissolution and new creation.
4
Distinguish from salam
Salam (forward sale) is an exception to qiyas, allowed for necessity. In salam, the capital must be handed over immediately. Set-off of the capital with a pre-existing debt is invalid because it would involve debt-for-debt exchange, which is prohibited. Unlike sarf, salam cannot be initiated with a debt as capital.
This distinction shows why the same logic does not apply to salam.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
According to the Hanafi position (istihsan), the sale is valid and the set-off is effective. According to Zufar's qiyas, the transaction is invalid and would involve riba. The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position of permissibility.
The reliance on istihsan reflects the Hanafi methodology of giving weight to custom and necessity when qiyas leads to hardship.

Dominant Metal in Coins and Set-Off (Taqas) in Sarf

A coin is legally what dominates its substance; set-off in sarf is valid even if the debt arises later.

The passage begins by stating that if silver dominates the dirhams and gold dominates the dinar, they are legally treated as silver and gold. The ruling applies to the scenario where one sells a gold dinar for ten unspecified dirhams. If the dirhams are predominantly silver, they are legally silver, and the sale is valid. Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah discusses two related rulings in the chapter of Sarf (currency exchange). First, the legal status of coins when they contain alloy: if the predominant substance is silver in dirhams and gold in dinars, they are legally treated as silver and gold. This determines the applicability of ribawi rules. Second, the issue of taqas (set-off) in exchange contracts. The author presents an istihsan reasoning: when two parties set off debts, it implies the rescission of the original exchange and the formation of a new one. Therefore, the new debt need not pre-exist the exchange; it suffices that it exists at the time of set-off. A contrary opinion (qila) is rejected as weaker. The passage distinguishes this from salam (advance payment), where set-off with a later debt is absolutely invalid because salam is a concession given for necessity, and set-off would negate that necessity. The ruling is from the Hanafi school, with the relied-upon position being that set-off in sarf is valid with a debt arising after the contract.

غلب (ghalaba): To dominate or predominate; here, if silver constitutes the majority of the coin's substance, it is legally treated as silver.
تقاص (taqāṣ): Mutual set-off of debts between two parties, here in the context of a sarf contract.
استحسان (istiḥsān): A method of juristic preference that overrides strict analogy due to necessity or custom.

Ruling on Coins with Dominant Adulterant and Sale for Pure Silver
Ruling on coins when adulterant dominates, treating them as commodities, and sale for pure silver

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for coins with dominant adulterant and apply the conditions for a valid sale with pure silver.

Core Idea
When adulterant dominates, coins become commodities; sale for pure silver is valid only if pure silver is more and immediate delivery occurs.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah addresses a key nuance in the fiqh of currency exchange (sarf). While pure gold and silver must be exchanged in equal weight, coins with dominant adulterant are treated as ordinary goods. Understanding this distinction helps you navigate modern transactions involving alloyed metals.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule of sarf
Recall that pure silver coins must be exchanged weight-for-weight with immediate delivery. This is the baseline from which the exception is made.
Sarf rules apply to pure gold and silver.
2
Identify the exception for dominant adulterant
When the adulterant (ghish) in the coins is dominant, they are no longer treated as currency but as commodities (ard). The reasoning is that the silver is considered consumed (mustahlik).
Dominant ghish is determined by customary assessment.
3
Apply the three scenarios for hilyat al-sayf
Analyze the sale of such coins for pure silver: if pure silver is equal, less, or unknown, invalid; if more, valid provided immediate delivery occurs. The excess pure silver compensates for the adulterant.
This is the classical example of sword ornament (hilyat al-sayf).
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi difference on possession
Note al-Tahtawi's comment that if coins are commodities, possession (qabd) might not be required, but the preferred view retains it. Authorities like al-Kanz and al-Fath support the stated conditions.
The mufta bihi is to require immediate delivery for caution.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah addresses the ruling on coins when the adulterant (ghish) dominates. The base matn states that it is not permissible to sell pure gold/silver coins for each other except in equal weight. However, when adulterant dominates, the coins are treated as commodities (arad), not as monetary units. Consequently, selling such coins for pure silver is subject to the rules of barter, not ribawi exchange. The text outlines three scenarios for selling adulterant-dominated dirhams for pure silver in the context of hilyat al-sayf (sword ornament): if the pure silver is equal to or less than the silver content of the dirhams, or unknown, the sale is invalid; if the pure silver is more, it is valid provided immediate delivery (qabd). The reasoning is that currency normally contains some adulterant for minting, and a small amount is overlooked. But when adulterant dominates, the silver is considered consumed (mustahlik) and the coin is legally a commodity. The commentary by al-Aqta' clarifies that if the silver can be extracted without damage, it is not consumed. Al-Tahtawi explains that when classified as commodities, the conditions of sarf (like immediate possession) do not apply; rather, it is a simple barter. The final note from al-Fath states that if the pure silver is more than the silver in the dirhams, the sale is valid with the excess compensating for the adulterant.

Key Terms
غش (ghish): Adulterant or alloy mixed with precious metal in coins.
عرضين (ʿaraḍayn): Commodities (pl. of ʿarḍ); coins when adulterant dominates are treated as ordinary goods, not as currency.
حلية السيف (ḥilyat al-sayf): Sword ornament; a specific case where pure silver is exchanged for adulterated silver dirhams for the purpose of making ornaments.
