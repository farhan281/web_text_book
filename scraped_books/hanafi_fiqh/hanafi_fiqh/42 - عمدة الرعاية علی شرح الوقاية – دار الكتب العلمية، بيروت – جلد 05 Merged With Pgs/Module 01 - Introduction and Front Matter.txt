Understanding the Author's Introduction in Hanafi Works
Opening of the book with basmala and author's preamble

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conventional components of a Hanafi fiqh work's introduction (basmala, hamd, salawat, amma ba'd) and explain the function of the author's self-description as 'al-faqir al-haqir'.

Core Idea
A scholar's introduction is a portal to his intellectual identity and methodology.

This lesson examines the opening lines of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a super-commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah. The author, Abu Hamid Muhammad Abdul Hamid al-Ansari al-Hanafi, follows the classical structure of Hanafi fiqh texts, beginning with praise of Allah and salawat upon the Prophet. His humble self-description 'al-faqir al-haqir' is not mere rhetoric but reflects the scholarly tradition of acknowledging one's dependence on divine guidance. Understanding this preamble equips you to place any Hanafi text in its proper scholarly context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structural elements
Look for basmala, hamd (praise of Allah), salawat (blessings on the Prophet), and amma ba'd (to proceed). The first words after basmala are the title of the book.
2
Analyze the author's praise of Allah
Note the phrases describing Allah's attributes: 'exalted beyond perception', 'sanctified beyond likeness'. This establishes the theological framework.
3
Decode the author's self-description
The author calls himself 'al-faqir al-haqir'. In the Hanafi tradition, this humility signifies that the author is not claiming infallibility; his work is a humble effort.
Such expressions are conventional.
4
Note the author's full name and lineage
The name includes 'al-Hanafi' indicating his madhhab affiliation. This is crucial for assessing the authority and perspective of the commentary.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter of the book 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah 'ala Sharh al-Wiqayah'. The author begins with the basmala, then praises Allah and sends salawat upon the Prophet Muhammad. He explains the purpose of the work: to elucidate the principles of fiqh and guide students to certainty. This opening follows the traditional structure of Islamic scholarly works: praise of Allah, salawat, and a statement of intent. The text sets the tone for a comprehensive commentary on the Wiqayah, a foundational Hanafi legal text.

The Preeminence of Fiqh and the Lineage of Hanafi Texts: An Introduction to 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah
Author's introduction praising fiqh and introducing the texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the virtue of fiqh according to the cited hadith and identify the chain of Hanafi texts (matn, sharh, hashihya) described in this introduction.

Core Idea
Fiqh is the key to distinguishing halal from haram; its study is a sign of divine goodness.

This passage opens 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a super-commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah. The author, Muhammad 'Abd al-Hamid al-Ansari, begins with the traditional basmalah and praise of Allah, then extols the virtue of fiqh, citing two prophetic hadith. He proceeds to introduce the foundational texts that his work builds upon: Wiqayat al-Riwayah and its commentary. This introduction establishes the authority and importance of the Hanafi tradition for the reader.

Guided Path
1
Identify the introduction's structure
Locate the standard elements: basmalah, hamdalah, shahadah, salawat, amma ba'd, self-introduction. This structure is common in classical Hanafi works.
The self-introduction includes the author's full name and a supplication.
2
Extract the core claim about fiqh
The author states that fiqh is the means to know the details of rulings and to distinguish halal from haram. This underlines its central role in Islamic practice.
The phrase 'تفصيل الأحكام' (tafsil al-ahkam) is key.
3
Note the prophetic hadith cited
Two hadith: 'Whomsoever Allah desires good for, He gives him deep understanding in religion' and 'One jurist is more severe against Satan than a thousand worshippers.' These establish the virtue of fiqh.
The first hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim; the second is from Tirmidhi and Ibn Majah.
4
Identify the textual lineage
The author names the matn: Wiqayat al-Riwayah by Burhan al-Shari'ah Mahmud, and its sharh by Sadr al-Shari'ah 'Ubayd Allah. 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is a hashihya on that sharh.
This chain shows the layered nature of Hanafi scholarly production.
5
Recognize the purpose of the introduction
The introduction is not a legal ruling but a framing device that honors the discipline of fiqh, the Prophet, the previous scholars, and the texts being commented upon.
It serves to motivate the reader and establish the authority of the work.

Commentary
This is the opening passage of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a super-commentary (hāshiyah) on Sharh al-Wiqayah by Sadr al-Shari'ah. The author, Abu Hamid Muhammad 'Abd al-Hamid al-Ansari al-Hanafi al-Lakhnawi (d. 1353 AH), begins with the basmalah and a title for his work. He then praises Allah for the blessing of fiqh, which is the means to understand detailed rulings and distinguish halal from haram. He offers the shahadah and salawat upon the Prophet, emphasizing his role as the cause of all existence and the bearer of the brilliant Shari'ah. After the customary 'amma ba'd, he introduces himself humbly and includes a long supplication. The passage is essentially a traditional introduction (khutbah) that also serves as an eulogy for his teachers: Shaykh Abu al-Haya' Muhammad 'Abd al-Halim and Shaykh Abu al-Baqa Muhammad 'Abd al-Hakim. He then extols the virtue of fiqh, citing hadiths: 'Whomsoever Allah desires good for, He gives him deep understanding in religion' (Bukhari, Muslim) and 'One jurist is more severe against Satan than a thousand worshippers' (Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah). He notes that many concise texts and commentaries were written, but the most excellent is Wiqayat al-Riwayah by Burhan al-Shari'ah Mahmud, and its commentary by Sadr al-Shari'ah 'Ubayd Allah. The author praises these works for containing subsidiary issues and legal proofs. This is a typical opening of a Hanafi commentary, setting the stage for the detailed fiqh discussions to follow.

صدر الشريعة (Ṣadr al-Sharīʿah): A title referring to the commentator on Wiqayat al-Riwayah; here, it is 'Ubayd Allah ibn Taj al-Shari'ah Mahmud, a Hanafi jurist.

The Virtue of Fiqh and the Chain of Hanafi Scholarship
Author's preface praising fiqh and introducing his work

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of a Hanafi author's preface, including the praise of fiqh, the chain of transmission, and the purpose of the commentary.

Core Idea
A preface anchors the work in the living chain of Hanafi learning and authority.

This lesson examines the opening passage of 'Zubdat al-Nihayah', a commentary on 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah'. The author begins by extolling the science of fiqh, citing Prophetic hadith on its virtue, and then establishes the scholarly lineage of the texts he builds upon. Understanding this preface is crucial for appreciating the authority and methodology of the work.

Guided Path
1
Identify Structural Components
Read the passage to find the main parts: praise of Allah and prayer for scholars, virtue of fiqh with hadith, mention of previous works (Wiqayat al-Riwayah and its commentary), description of the ta'liq by his teacher, and his own motivation to complete the remaining volumes.
Preface often serves as an 'isnad' (chain) for the text.
2
Extract the Core Hadiths
Find the two hadiths cited: 'Whoever Allah wishes good for, He gives them understanding of the religion' and 'One faqih is more severe against Satan than a thousand worshippers.' Note their significance in establishing fiqh's high rank.
These hadiths are commonly used in Hanafi texts to encourage study of fiqh.
3
Trace the Scholarly Chain
List the works in order: Wiqayat al-Riwayah (by Burhan al-Shari'ah Mahmud) → Sharh al-Wiqayah (by Sadr al-Shari'ah 'Ubayd Allah) → 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah (ta'liq by Muhammad 'Abd al-Hayy al-Lucknawi on first two volumes) → Zubdat al-Nihayah (the author's completion).
This chain shows continuity of scholarship within the Hanafi school.
4
Understand the Author's Role
The author is not the original commentator but a continuator: he completes the ta'liq on the last two volumes left unfinished by his teacher. He also wrote 'al-Hall al-Daruri' on Mukhtasar al-Quduri.
Recognizing whether a text is original or a completion shapes how we read it.

Understanding the Hanafi Preface: Authority and Purpose
Author's preface explaining motivation and methodology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the purpose and key elements of a Hanafi fiqh preface and explain its role in establishing scholarly lineage.

Core Idea
A preface establishes authority, lineage, and purpose for the work.

This passage is the author's preface to his commentary Zubdat al-Nihayah. Prefaces are not legal rulings but essential for understanding the author's motivation, methodology, and scholarly chain. This preface positions the work within the Hanafi tradition, highlighting its reliance on earlier commentaries and the author's teacher.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and his teacher
Read the preface to find the author's name (Muhammad Abdul Hayy al-Laknawi's student) and his teacher (Muhammad Abdul Hayy al-Laknawi). Note their scholarly titles.
Teacher is described as a hadith scholar, exegete, etc.

Trace the chain of texts
Identify the three works mentioned: Sharh al-Wiqayah (by al-Marghinani), Umdat al-Ri'ayah (by al-Laknawi), and Zubdat al-Nihayah (by the current author). Understand the relationship: the latter are commentaries on the former.

This reveals the author's commitment to both naqli and aqli proofs.

Note the supplication

Supplication is a standard feature of classical prefaces.
