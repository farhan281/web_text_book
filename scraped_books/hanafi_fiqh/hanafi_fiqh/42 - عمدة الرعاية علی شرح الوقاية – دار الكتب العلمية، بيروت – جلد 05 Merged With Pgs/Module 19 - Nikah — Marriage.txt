The Requirement of Consummation for Muhallil Marriage
Explaining the hadith on muhallil and the requirement of actual intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a woman divorced thrice cannot return to her first husband until her second husband consummates the marriage, and explain the proof from the explicit hadith.

Core Idea
A woman divorced thrice cannot return to her first husband until her second husband consummates the marriage — the hadith explicitly requires tasting the honey.

This lesson examines the well-known hadith on muhallil marriage. The Hanafi school holds that mere contract without intercourse does not make the woman permissible. We will explore the textual proof and address the rare opinion of Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab, which contradicts the established Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its prohibition
The central ruling is that a woman who has been divorced three times is prohibited (haram) for her first husband until she marries another man and that second husband consummates the marriage. A valid marriage contract alone does not remove the prohibition.
The prohibition is based on Qur'an 2:230, which requires the woman to marry another husband. The hadith specifies that consummation is necessary.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith narrated by Aisha: the Prophet (peace be upon him) said, 'No, until the other tastes from her sweetness what the first tasted.' This is a clear textual evidence for the condition.
The hadith is narrated by the six major hadith collections, establishing its authenticity and widespread acceptance.
3
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'لا' (no) indicates a prohibition. The phrase 'حتى يذوق' (until he tastes) establishes a condition. The metaphorical 'tasting of honey' refers to sexual intercourse. Thus, the hadith explicitly makes permissibility conditional upon actual intercourse.
In Hanafi usul, a conditional statement with 'حتى' indicates that the ruling is suspended until the condition is fulfilled.
4
Note the ikhtilaf and its resolution
Sa'id ibn al-Musayyab held that a valid marriage contract alone suffices without intercourse. However, this view is considered strange (mustaghrab) and is rejected because it contradicts the explicit hadith. The majority of scholars, including all four imams, require consummation.
The passage notes that the hadith apparently did not reach Sa'id, which is why his opinion differs. The Hanafi position is based on the well-known and acted-upon Sunnah.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi
The operative ruling (mufta bihi) is that consummation is required. The woman does not become permissible to her first husband until the second husband actually has intercourse with her. This is the position of the Hanafi school and the majority.
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon for fatwa.

The Invalidity of Mut'ah Marriage and Consensus on Its Prohibition
Stating the ruling that mut'ah marriage is invalid and that permitting it is disbelief

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mut'ah marriage, explain why it is invalid, and identify the proof of consensus that prohibits it.

Core Idea
Mut'ah marriage is invalid by binding consensus; permitting it contradicts a necessarily known ruling of Islam.

Mut'ah (temporary marriage) was briefly permitted in early Islam but was later abrogated. This lesson examines the definitive prohibition established by the consensus of the Companions and addresses a common misattribution to Imam Malik. You will understand why any ruling permitting mut'ah is void and why it amounts to disbelief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Mut'ah marriage is defined as a contract for a fixed period in exchange for wealth. The Hanafi ruling is that it is invalid (bāṭil) and absolutely prohibited.
The invalidity is immediate; the contract is void from its inception.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the consensus (ijmāʿ) of the Companions that mut'ah was abrogated. The hadith of ʿAlī at Khaybar also explicitly prohibits it.
Consensus is a qaṭʿī (definitive) source of law.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalālah
The phrase 'abrogated by the consensus of the Companions' indicates that the prohibition is binding and cannot be overturned. The hadith confirms the abrogation.
The custom of the Companions is to act on such prohibitions.
4
Note the ikhtilāf
A statement attributed to Imam Mālik suggesting permissibility is examined. The author shows this attribution is a mistake; Mālik himself narrated the prohibition hadith.
No valid difference exists among the four Sunni schools; all prohibit mut'ah.
5
Evaluate the tarjīḥ
The correct position is that mut'ah is prohibited by consensus. Any contrary view is rejected as either mistaken or from outside Sunni Islam.
The muftā bihī (operative ruling) is absolute prohibition.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the ruling on mut'ah (temporary marriage). It defines mut'ah as a marriage for a fixed period in exchange for wealth. The text states that mut'ah was initially permitted between the events of Khaybar and the conquest of Mecca, but was later abrogated by the consensus of the Companions. Consequently, any judicial ruling allowing mut'ah is invalid, and one who permits it becomes a disbeliever (since they are contradicting a known consensus). Furthermore, Malik himself narrated the hadith of Ali prohibiting mut'ah in his Muwatta, and his practice was to act on what he narrated. Therefore, mut'ah is definitively prohibited.

The Abrogation of Mut'ah Marriage by Consensus
Ruling on mut'ah marriage: originally permitted, then abrogated by consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mut'ah marriage and explain why it is haram based on ijma' al-sahabah and the hadith of 'tasting sweetness'.

Core Idea
Mut'ah marriage is haram by the consensus of the Companions; ruling otherwise leads to kufr.

This passage addresses a critical issue in Hanafi fiqh: the definitive prohibition of mut'ah (temporary marriage). While it was briefly permitted during the early period, the Companions reached a consensus on its invalidity, making it a binding ruling. The commentator not only affirms the prohibition but also warns that a judge who rules it permissible commits apostasy. We will examine the proofs and the refutation of those who claim otherwise.

Internal Validity of a Marriage Judgment Based on False Testimony
Commentary on false testimony's effect on marital relations and judgment validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the internal and external validity of a marriage judgment based on false testimony and identify which position is the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A judge's ruling based on false testimony creates a valid marriage externally but not internally according to the relied-upon fatwa.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explores a critical intra-Hanafi dispute: what is the effect of a judge's ruling when it is based on false testimony (shahadat al-zur) in marriage? Abu Hanifa held the ruling is valid both externally and internally, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf later and Muhammad) held it is valid only externally. The fatwa follows the Sahibayn, meaning the marriage is enforced by the court but is not lawful in the sight of Allah. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling

Commentary
This passage discusses a nuanced issue in Hanafi fiqh: the effect of a judge's ruling based on false testimony (shahadat al-zur) in marriage. The commentator expands on the matn's statement that if a man brings false witnesses claiming he married a woman, and the judge rules accordingly, then it becomes permissible for her to allow him to have intercourse (yahillu laha tamkinuhu). The discussion centers on whether this validity is only external (zahiran) or also internal (batinan) between the parties and Allah.

The author presents the positions:
- Abu Hanifa (and initially Abu Yusuf) hold that the judgment is valid both externally and internally. This means the woman is lawful for him in reality, and any subsequent intercourse is not sinful.
- The later positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) hold that the judgment is valid only externally: the judge hands over the wife and orders her to comply, but in reality (between them and Allah) the marriage is not valid, and sexual relations are sinful.

The commentator then raises an objection: How can a ruling based on pure falsehood (haram) create a lawful relationship? Abu Hanifa's response is that the judge's ruling is not treated as a confirmation of the false testimony; rather, it functions as a new contract (ka-insha' 'aqd jadid). This is supported by the hadith of 'Ali (RA) who told a woman, 'Your witnesses have married you,' and did not require a new marriage contract.

The passage also compares this to other cases: (a) in talaq (divorce) with false witnesses, the same principle applies; (b) in sales for less than value, the judgment is not valid internally because the judge cannot give away another's property; (c) in li'an (mutual cursing), the separation is valid internally even though one spouse is lying.

Finally, the commentator states that the fatwa (operative ruling) is according to the Sahibayn (external validity only), and that the false witnesses commit a grave sin. This means that in practice, a marriage based on false testimony is considered invalid in the sight of Allah, and the couple must separate, but the judgment is enforced externally to settle the dispute.

Key Terms
شهادة الزور (shahadat al-zur): False testimony or perjury; giving evidence that is untrue.
نفاذ القضاء (nafadh al-qada'): Enforcement or execution of the judge's ruling; whether it takes effect externally (zahiran) and/or internally (batinan).
باطناً (batinan): Inwardly, internally, in reality; i.e., in the sight of Allah and according to the actual truth.

Effect of Witness Disagreement on Dower in Marriage: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of marriage with differing dower testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on witness disagreement regarding dower, distinguish Abu Hanifa's istihsan from the Sahibayn's qiyas, and apply the ruling to a marriage dispute scenario.

Core Idea
When witnesses disagree on the dower, marriage stands with the lesser amount by istihsan.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The issue: if two witnesses testify to different amounts of dower (mahr) in a marriage contract, is the marriage valid? Abu Hanifa rules it valid with the lesser dower via istihsan (juristic preference), while the Sahibayn rule the testimony rejected, making the marriage invalid via qiyas (analogy). Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi method of prioritizing the primary purpose (maqsud) over subsidiary matters.

Guided Path
1
State the core dispute
Identify the two opposing positions: Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling (marriage valid with lesser dower by istihsan) and the Sahibayn's ruling (testimony rejected, marriage invalid by qiyas).
The Sahibayn are Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's dalil: qiyas to sale
Understand why the Sahibayn analogize marriage to sale: in sale, disagreement on price invalidates the testimony; similarly, dower is the consideration in marriage, so disagreement should invalidate testimony and thus the contract.
Qiyas: 'asl' is sale, 'far'' is marriage, 'illa' is the presence of a consideration.
3
Identify Abu Hanifa's dalil: istihsan
Understand why Abu Hanifa departs from qiyas: in marriage, dower is subsidiary (taba') to the contract; the primary purpose is the contract itself (permissibility). Disagreement on a subsidiary does not affect the primary. The witnesses agree on the lesser amount, so that amount is taken and the marriage remains valid.
Istihsan: a stronger evidence (here, the nature of marriage as a primary contract) overrides the qiyas.
4
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the marriage stands and the husband pays the lesser dower; under the Sahibayn, the marriage is invalid and must be remade.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon opinion) is Abu Hanifa's view, as stated in the text.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is a commentary on a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the effect of a difference in witness testimony on the amount of dower (mahr) in a marriage contract. The passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The author opens by stating the core ruling: marriage is valid with the lesser amount of dower by istihsan (juristic preference) according to Imam Abu Hanifa, whereas the Sahibayn hold that the testimony is rejected entirely, which would invalidate the marriage. The author then explains the reasoning behind the Sahibayn's position as qiyas (analogy): since the purpose is the contract from both sides, it is like a sale where disagreement on the price invalidates the testimony. He then presents Abu Hanifa's istihsan: in marriage, the dower is subsidiary (taba') to the contract, which is the primary aim. Marriage can even be valid without specifying a dower. Disagreement on the subsidiary does not affect the original contract. When disagreement occurs on the subsidiary, judgment is given for the lesser amount because both witnesses agree on that amount. The author further refines the discussion by considering who is the claimant (husband or wife) and concludes that the sound opinion (al-sahih) is that the ruling applies in both cases. The passage also references previous discussions in al-Hidayah and al-Inayah. This is a critical refinement because it clarifies the precise legal reasoning and the proper application of istihsan versus qiyas in this specific scenario.

Key Terms
إستحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference: departing from an established qiyas to a more suitable ruling based on a stronger evidence or need.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning: extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common effective cause (illa).
شهادة (shahada): Testimony given in court by witnesses; its validity depends on agreement among witnesses on the fact testified.
مهر (mahr): Dower: mandatory payment from husband to wife upon marriage. It is a financial obligation but subsidiary to the marriage contract.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The marriage contract stands even if witnesses disagree on mahr because the contract's validity is independent of the mahr's exact amount.

When witnesses give conflicting testimony about the mahr (dower) in a marriage contract, does that void the contract? The Hanafi school is divided.
