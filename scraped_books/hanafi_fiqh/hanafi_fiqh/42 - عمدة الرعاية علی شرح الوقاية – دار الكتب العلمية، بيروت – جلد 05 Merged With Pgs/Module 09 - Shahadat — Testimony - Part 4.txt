False Witness: Punishment and Proof – An Intra-Hanafi Refinement
Intra-madhhab dispute and critique of a prior textual claim in testimony law.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi disagreement on ta'zir for a false witness and apply the principle that false testimony can be proven without confession.

Core Idea
False witness incurs discretionary ta'zir (not fixed), and falsehood can be established by contradictory facts without needing a confession.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya refines two key points in Hanafi testimony law. First, the punishment for a false witness: Abu Hanifa differs from his two disciples, arguing that ta'zir is discretionary and not a fixed beating and imprisonment, and that Umar's practice was a political measure. Second, the commentator refutes the claim that false testimony can only be proven by the witness's confession, providing examples where the falsehood is evident from external facts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two issues
Locate the parts of the passage dealing with punishment (from 'وقالا' to the end of the siyasa discussion) and with proof of falsehood (from 'قد قيل' to the end).
The passage transitions between these topics.
2
For punishment: state the views and proofs
State the view of the Sahibayn (beat and imprison) and Abu Hanifa (discretionary ta'zir, using Umar's action as siyasa). Identify the proof: Umar's practice for the Sahibayn, and the prohibition of muthla and the hadd limit for Abu Hanifa.
Consider the thamarah: difference in minimum punishment and role of imprisonment.
3
For proof: identify the original claim and refutation
The base text claimed false testimony is only known through confession. The commentator refutes with examples: testifying to death, crescent sighting, etc.
This refutes the exclusivity (hasr) using clear factual contradictions.
4
Connect the two issues
Both show the commentator's method of critical refinement: citing alternative interpretations and counterexamples to earlier texts.
The commentator supports Abu Hanifa on punishment and broadens the means of proof.
5
Determine the mufta bihi for each
For punishment: the relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's (discretionary ta'zir, max 39). For proof: the commentator's view (falsehood can be proven without confession) is the operative position.
The commentator does not explicitly state the mufta bihi but his refutation carries weight.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya, a super commentary on Sharh al-Wiqaya, addresses two distinct but related issues within the Hanafi school: the punishment for false witnesses (shahid zur) and the means of establishing that a testimony is false. The commentary opens by stating the position of the two companions of Abu Hanifa — Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani — that the judge should physically punish (يوجعه ضرباً) and imprison the false witness. This is also the view of Imam al-Shafi'i. The author then clarifies that according to some sources, the false witness is also paraded around (يشهر) as a form of disgrace. The punishment (ta'zir) and imprisonment are at the judge's discretion, with the maximum ta'zir being thirty-nine lashes and the minimum three. Notably, Abu Yusuf allows up to seventy-five lashes, while the judge may also combine beating with imprisonment. The textual evidence cited is the practice of Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab, who ordered that false witnesses be beaten forty lashes, their faces blackened with soot, their heads shaved, and imprisoned for a long period. This tradition is recorded by Muhammad ibn al-Hasan in his book 'al-Athar' and by Ibn Abi Shayba. In response, the commentator presents the rebuttal from Imam Abu Hanifa's side. The Imam interprets Umar's action not as a fixed ta'zir penalty but as a matter of public policy (siyasa). This is argued on two grounds: first, if it were ta'zir, it would not reach forty lashes because that number would approach the fixed hadd penalties for other crimes (i.e., it would blur the distinction between ta'zir and hadd). Second, the blackening of the face is a form of mutilation (muthla), which is generally prohibited in Islamic law. Ibn al-Humam counters that muthla only applies to limbs, while some scholars hold that Umar's action was purely siyasa, which the judge may adopt if he sees public benefit. The commentator concludes that siyasa is any measure taken by the ruler for public welfare that has no specific textual basis. After these lexicographical notes on the word 'sakhkhama' (to blacken), the passage moves to the second issue: the principle that false testimony is only known through confession (iqrar). The author of the original text (possibly al-Wiqaya) had placed this discussion under the chapter on confession because, in his view, falsehood cannot be proven by evidence (bayyina). The commentator (the author of 'Umdat al-Ri'aya) refutes this with multiple counterexamples: if someone testifies to Zayd's death and then Zayd appears alive, the falsehood is evident without any confession. Similarly, if a person testifies to sighting the crescent moon and then thirty days pass without any atmospheric obstruction and the moon is not seen, the testimony is proven false. Other examples include a woman who testifies to having given birth but is later found to be a virgin. Thus, the commentator demonstrates that false testimony can be established through clear contradiction of facts, without any need for the witness's confession. The passage ends with a critique of the original author's claim of exclusivity (hasr), affirming that falsehood can be proven by circumstantial evidence and qiyas (analogy). Overall, the commentary serves to refine and critique the earlier text, asserting the Hanafi position on both the discretionary nature of ta'zir for false witnesses and the broader scope for establishing falsehood.

Key Terms
شاهد الزور (shāhid al-zūr): A false witness; one who gives false testimony in a legal proceeding.
تعزير (taʿzīr): Discretionary punishment for crimes that do not have fixed hadd penalties; administered at the judge's discretion.
سياسة (siyāsa): Public policy; measures taken by the ruler or judge for the public welfare, not necessarily based on a specific textual ruling.
إقرار (iqrār): Confession; admission of a fact or claim, used in legal proceedings as proof.
مثلة (muthla): Mutilation; disfiguring the body as punishment, which is generally prohibited in Islamic law.

Definition and Conditions of Retracting Testimony
Defines retraction, its pillar, condition, and ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define retraction of testimony (al-rujūʿ), identify its pillar, condition, and ruling, and apply these to determine the validity of a witness's retraction.

Core Idea
Retraction of testimony is only valid before a judge; the retracting witness faces ta'zir and damān if property was lost.

This lesson introduces the legal concept of retracting testimony (al-rujūʿ ʿan al-shahādah). The passage defines retraction, distinguishes its essential components, and establishes the strict requirement that retraction must occur in the presence of a judge. Understanding this framework is crucial for evaluating the legal consequences when a witness withdraws his testimony.

Guided Path
1
Define retraction
Identify the linguistic and technical definition of al-rujūʿ. Note that it is 'negating what one has affirmed.'
Technically, it is nafy mā athbatah.
2
Identify the three components
The passage states retraction has a rukn (pillar), shart (condition), and hukm (ruling). List each with its specific detail.
Rukn = verbal statement; Shart = before a judge; Hukm = ta'zir and damān after judgment.
3
Explain why it must be before a judge
Two reasons: (a) retraction nullifies testimony, which is a court act, so it must occur in the same forum; (b) retraction is repentance, which must match the offense (public offense requires public repentance).
The text says 'al-rajʿu yafsakhu al-shahādah' and 'al-tawbatu ʿalā ḥasabi al-jināyah'.

Determine who qualifies as a judge
The term qāḍī is absolute, including any judge, not just the original one. Retraction is invalid before a non-judge, even a policeman.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of a new chapter on retraction in testimony (رجوع عن الشهادة). The commentator first explains why this chapter is placed after the discussion of false testimony: retraction indicates that the prior testimony was false, and although it negates the testimony, it is treated as a form of confession. He defines retraction linguistically as the opposite of going, and technically as negating what one has affirmed. The retraction has three components: a pillar (ركن), a ruling (حكم), and a condition (شرط). The pillar (rukn) is the witness's explicit utterance that he retracts his testimony or admits it was false. The condition (shart) for the retraction to be valid is that it must be done in the presence of a judge (قاضي). The ruling (hukm) is that if the retraction occurs after a judgment has been passed and the property has already been transferred without compensation, the witness is subject to both discretionary punishment (ta'zir) and financial liability (damān). The retraction is accepted regarding the witness's own liability but does not overturn the judgment in favor of the plaintiff. The commentator then elaborates on why the retraction must be before a judge: because it nullifies the testimony, which is itself a court act, and because retraction is a form of repentance that should match the nature of the offense. The judge can be any judge, not necessarily the one before whom the original testimony was given. The retraction is not valid before a non-judge, even a police officer (shurtī), as stated in al-Bahr al-Raiq quoting al-Muhit.

Key Terms
الرجوع (al-rujūʿ): In legal terminology, negating what one has affirmed (نفي ما أثبته), specifically the witness's withdrawal of his testimony.
الشهادة (al-shahādah): Testimony given by a witness in a legal case, which this chapter discusses retracting.
القاضي (al-qāḍī): The judge before whom retraction must occur; includes the judge who heard the original testimony or any other judge.
التعزير (al-taʿzīr): Discretionary punishment imposed by the judge for offenses without fixed hadd penalties, applied here due to false testimony.
الضمان (al-ḍamān): Financial liability incurred by the retracting witness if property was lost due to his testimony.

Retraction of Testimony: Definition, Pillar, Condition, and Ruling
Defines retraction, its pillar, condition, and ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of al-ruju' (retraction), identify its pillar and condition, and apply the ruling of ta'zir and daman when retraction occurs after judgment.

Core Idea
Retraction of testimony is valid only before the judge; if after judgment, it triggers ta'zir and compensation.

This lesson unpacks the legal concept of retracting testimony. You will learn what constitutes a valid retraction, where it must be made (the court), and what consequences follow if the witness recants after the judge has already issued a ruling. The key is that retraction annuls testimony itself, so it must be done in the same setting where testimony was given.

Guided Path
1
Define retraction linguistically and technically
Start with the literal meaning of al-ruju' (to return) and then the technical definition: the witness negating what he previously affirmed. This sets the foundation for understanding the legal structure.
The text says: 'It is the negation of what he affirmed.'
2
Identify the pillar (rukn)
The pillar is the witness's explicit statement that he retracts or states he testified falsely. Without this statement, no retraction occurs.
The text says: 'Its pillar: the witness's statement after testifying: I retract...'
3
Identify the condition (shart)
The condition for validity is that the retraction must be made before the judge who heard the case. Retraction before any other authority is invalid.
The text says: 'The condition for its validity is that the retraction be before the judge.'
4
State the ruling (hukm) when retraction occurs after judgment
If retraction happens after the judge has already ruled and the property has been transferred, the witness faces discretionary punishment (ta'zir) and must compensate (daman) for the property he caused to be wrongfully taken.
The text says: 'Its ruling: the obligation of ta'zir and compensation...'
5
Analyze the rationale for the judge requirement
The text gives two reasons: (1) retraction annuls testimony, so it must be done where testimony is given—the judge's court; (2) retraction is repentance, and a public sin requires public repentance. This explains why a mere officer or any other person is insufficient.
The text says: 'because retraction annuls testimony, so it is specific to what testimony is specific to' and 'repentance is according to the sin'.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Hidayah introduces the chapter on retraction of testimony. It defines retraction, its linguistic and technical meanings, and then outlines its pillar, condition, and ruling. The pillar is the witness's explicit statement of retraction. The condition is that it must be done before the judge who heard the case, not before any other authority. The ruling is that if retraction occurs after judgment and the property has been transferred, the retracting witness must be given discretionary punishment (ta'zir) and must guarantee the property. The text explains why retraction is only before a judge: because retraction annuls the testimony, and testimony is specific to the court, so its annulment must also be in court. Additionally, retraction is a form of repentance, and public sin requires public repentance. The witness should not refrain due to embarrassment because God's pleasure is more important.

Key Terms
الرجوع (al-ruju‘): In this text, الرجوع means the witness's act of negating his earlier testimony, either by stating 'I retract' or 'I testified falsely'.
التعزير (al-ta‘zir): Discretionary punishment imposed by the judge for offenses that do not have fixed hadd penalties; in this context, it is required upon retraction after judgment.
الضمان (al-daman): Legal liability to compensate for loss; here, the witness must guarantee the property he caused to be wrongfully transferred through his false testimony.

Effect of Witness Retraction Before and After Judgment
Ruling with rational proof on retraction timing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on retraction of testimony before and after judgment and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) based on contradiction and infinite regress.

Core Idea
Retraction before judgment invalidates testimony; retraction after judgment does not overturn the ruling.

This lesson examines a key principle in testimony law: the timing of witness retraction. The Hanafi school distinguishes sharply between retraction before and after the judge's ruling. The source text explains the legal consequences and the rational proof that prevents the ruling from being undone, emphasizing that contradictory statements cannot be accepted and that overturning rulings would lead to infinite regress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note the difference between retraction before judgment and retraction after judgment. The ruling changes: testimony is dropped before, not invalidated after.
2
Understand the reason for the distinction
Before judgment, no right has been established; after judgment, the right is fixed. Contradictory statements cannot be the basis of a ruling.
The principle: 'al-hukm yathbutu bi al-qada'' (the right is established by judgment).
3
Examine the proof against overturning after judgment
Accepting retraction after judgment would lead to infinite regress (tasalsul) because each retraction could be retracted. Both testimony and retraction are equally indicative of truth, so no preference exists.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on logical necessity.
4
Note the liability rule
Witnesses are not liable in either case because they did not cause any loss; the claimant's failure is due to his own inability to prove his case.
Even if the claimant loses the chance to win, that is not attributable to the witnesses.
5
Consider the practical exception
If witnesses retract after judgment, the defendant cannot produce new evidence of retraction except if it occurred before a judge and payment was guaranteed; otherwise the proof is rejected.
This prevents misuse of retraction to undermine final judgments.

Commentary
This passage addresses the effect of retraction of testimony at different stages. If witnesses retract before the judge issues a ruling, their testimony is voided and they bear no liability because no right has been established yet. Their contradictory statements prevent the judge from ruling. If they retract after the ruling, the ruling stands and is not overturned because considering the retraction would lead to an infinite regress of retractions and counter-retractions. Additionally, the witnesses are not liable for compensation if they retract before judgment because they haven't caused any loss; the claimant's failure to prove his case is due to his own inability to present consistent testimony. The commentary emphasizes that a retraction made before someone other than the judge is invalid, even if proven later with evidence.

التسلسل (al-tasalsul): Infinite regress; the argument that if retraction after judgment were accepted, it would lead to endless cycles of retracting retractions.
التناقض (al-tanaqud): Contradiction between two statements; here, the witness's testimony and retraction are contradictory, so the judge cannot base a ruling on them.

Retraction of Testimony After Judgment: Ruling and Dispute
Explains the ruling on witness retraction before and after judgment, including disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness retraction after judgment and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) that supports it.

Core Idea
Retraction after judgment does not annul the verdict; contradictory statements cannot undo a valid ruling.

In this lesson, we examine a key procedural ruling in Hanafi fiqh: the effect of witnesses retracting their testimony after the judge has issued a judgment. The Hanafi school holds that the judgment remains valid and is not annulled, supported by a rational proof against infinite regress. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the underlying reasoning, and the intra-madhhab dispute over Abu Hanifa's early view.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its timing
Identify what the matn says about retraction before and after judgment: before judgment, testimony falls; after judgment, it is not annulled.
The key contrast is between pre- and post-judgment retraction.
2
Locate the dalil aqli
The rational proof: contradictory statements cannot nullify a judgment because it would lead to infinite regress. Both statements are equal in truth-indication, but the judgment's connection to the first statement gives it preference.
This is a qiyas argument based on the impossibility of infinite regress.
3
Understand the non-liability of witnesses
Witnesses are not liable for loss because they did not cause it. The plaintiff's non-establishment of his right is due to his own inability, not the retraction. Also, the possibility of witness disqualification after judgment is irrelevant.
Liability (daman) requires direct causation, which is absent here.
4
Analyze the intra-madhhab dispute
Abu Hanifa initially held that retraction after judgment annuls it if the witness is more upright at retraction. He later retracted this view. The detail from Khizanat al-Muftin is rejected by the majority.
The tarjih authority (Fath al-Qadir) confirms the retraction.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position: judgment is not annulled, and witnesses are not liable. This is the majority view in texts, commentaries, and fatwas.
This ruling applies absolutely, regardless of the witness's state at retraction.

Witness Retraction After Judgment: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on retraction after judgment

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A witness's retraction after judgment never annuls the judgment; liability depends on receipt of property.

This passage delves into an important intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Imam Abu Hanifa initially held that if a witness becomes more pious at retraction, the judgment is annulled and property returned. However, he later retracted this view, adopting the absolute rule that a judgment already passed remains binding, regardless of the witness's post-testimony state. Understanding this evolution is crucial for correctly applying the school's position on witness liability.

Guided Path
1

2

This is the mufta bihi position.
3

The commentator in 'al-Bahr' rejects the detailed view (that annulment depends on the witness's state) as inconsistent with the school's transmitted position requiring liability after retraction.
He also verified Sarakhsi's 'al-Muhit' which agrees with the majority.
4

'Fath al-Qadir' clarifies that the detailed view was Abu Hanifa's first opinion, which he later retracted in favor of the absolute rule.
This resolves the inconsistency and establishes the authoritative final view.
5

Core Idea
Witness liability upon retraction requires plaintiff's receipt — unless the subject is property, where some Hanafis impose liability immediately.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the condition that makes witnesses liable after retraction. The central point is that liability is not automatic but hinges on the plaintiff's receipt, except in certain property cases. You will learn the reasoning behind this and the intra-Hanafi difference.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is a commentary on al-Hidayah discussing the liability of witnesses who retract their testimony. The ruling is that witnesses are liable for the loss caused by their testimony only after the plaintiff has actually taken possession of the claimed property, whether it is a debt or a specific item. This is because the loss is not realized until possession occurs. The Shafi'i school holds that witnesses are never liable because the direct cause of the loss is the judge's ruling, and causation is not considered when there is a direct actor. The Hanafi response is that the judge cannot be held liable because he is compelled to judge based on the testimony; imposing liability on him would deter people from becoming judges. Thus, the secondary cause (tashabbub) is considered. Within the Hanafi school, there is a difference of opinion: Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi and the later relied-upon view (mufta bihi) require possession for both debt and specific items. The passage also discusses real estate, noting that it is guaranteed by false testimony even without possession, analogizing it to a deposit where destruction is established by speech. The author concludes by directing the reader to more detailed works.

Liability of Witnesses Who Retract After Judgment
Ruling on two witnesses' liability when they retract after execution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness retraction and distinguish the liability for specific property versus debt.

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract after judgment are liable for compensation, even if the plaintiff never took the money.

This lesson examines a critical rule in Hanafi judicial procedure: the liability of witnesses who retract their testimony after a judgment has been executed. The ruling balances the need for justice with the reality that false testimony can cause real harm. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing between two types of subject matter—specific property and debt—and recognizing that the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position holds witnesses liable regardless of whether the plaintiff has actually taken the money.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the retraction of testimony after judgment. The Hanafi ruling is that the witnesses must compensate the party harmed by their false testimony.
The ruling is wajib (obligatory compensation).
2
Understand the basis: causation (tasbib)
Direct liability is on the judge, but he is compelled (malju') in judgment. Therefore, liability shifts to the witnesses as the indirect cause (tasbib) of the loss.
The principle: 'If direct action is impossible, causation is considered.'
3
Distinguish between 'ayn and dayn
For specific property ('ayn), witnesses guarantee the value regardless of whether the plaintiff took possession. For debt (dayn), they are only liable after the plaintiff has actually collected the debt, and they must then pay a similar debt.
The rationale: retraction for property is a form of destruction (itlaf), while for debt it creates a new debt.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some sources (like Al-Hidayah) restricted liability to when the plaintiff took the money. However, the mufta bihi (from Al-Minhaj, Al-Khulasa, etc.) is that liability applies whether or not the plaintiff took the money.
The later opinion of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn supports the broader liability.

Partial Retraction of Witnesses: The Hanafi Rule on Liability
Rulings on liability when only some witnesses retract among two or three

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on partial retraction of witnesses to determine liability when one or more witnesses retract among two or three.

Core Idea
When witnesses retract, liability depends on whether enough proof remains—the remaining witness counts, not the retractor.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling in Hanafi fiqh: what happens when only some witnesses retract their testimony? The key principle is that consideration is for the remaining witnesses, not the retractors. Through detailed examples and qiyas with the zakat year, the Hanafi school establishes a consistent rule for liability. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly applying the law of evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Read the matn: if one of two witnesses retracts, he guarantees half; if one of three retracts, no liability; if two of three retract, each guarantees half.
The number of witnesses and how many retract determines the outcome.
2
Understand the underlying principle
Each witness provides half the proof (hujja). The ruling stays with the remaining proof. Consideration is for the remaining witness, not the retractor.
This is based on the idea that a ruling can remain by part of its effective cause.
3
Apply the principle to two-witness and three-witness cases
For two witnesses, if one retracts, only half the proof remains, so the retractor must guarantee the lost half. For three, if one retracts, full proof remains (two witnesses), so no liability. If two retract, only one remains, so half proof remains; each retractor guarantees half.
The nisab (two witnesses) is the threshold for full proof.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The three Imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) are reported to consider the retractor, not the remaining witness. However, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that consideration is for the remaining witness.
This difference does not affect the final ruling but shows the method of tarjih.

This passage provides detailed rulings on partial retraction of witnesses. The main matn states: If one of two witnesses retracts, he guarantees half; consideration is on the remaining witness, not the retractor. Core Idea
Liability is proportional to proof lost, not number of retractors.

In Hanafi law, witnesses who retract after judgment must compensate for the loss caused. When multiple women and one man testify and only the women retract, the compensation distribution among them is disputed. Abu Hanifa holds that every two women count as one man (analogy to inheritance), so the compensation is divided into sixths. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that all women collectively count as one man, so the split is half for the man, half for the women.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf): under Abu Hanifa, women pay more (five-sixths of the half) than under the Sahibayn (half of the half).
4
Apply the analogy to inheritance used by Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa uses qiyas: in inheritance, two daughters take the place of one son, and their share does not increase when mixed with a son. Similarly, each pair of women witnesses replaces one male witness, so their number affects the compensation division.
The commentary cites al-Tabyīn for this reasoning.
5
Determine the sale scenario and its conditions
In a sale, if the buyer claims a price lower than value, retracting witnesses pay the difference. If the seller claims a price higher than value, they pay the excess. If the price equals value, no compensation. The key is who suffered harm: the buyer from overpayment, the seller from underpayment.
The witnesses only guarantee the loss they caused; if the claimant was satisfied with the price (e.g., seller claiming a low price), no compensation.

Commentary
This passage is from the chapter of testimony and retraction (ruju'). It provides detailed rulings on the compensation (damān) owed by witnesses who retract their testimony. The text first addresses a scenario with multiple witnesses: two men and several women testify, then only the women retract. By consensus, the women pay half the compensation because the man's testimony established half the claim. Then an intra-Hanafi dispute is presented: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that every two women count as one man for compensation, so they pay five-sixths of the total, while the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that all women collectively count as one man, so the man pays half and the women pay half. The commentary defends Abu Hanifa using analogy to inheritance where pairs of women replace a son. Next, the passage rules that if two men and one woman testify and all but the woman retract, the woman owes nothing because a single woman's testimony is insufficient to establish any right; only the men's testimony counts. Then come rules for marriage and sale testimony. In a marriage with a specified dower, the retracting witnesses only owe the amount exceeding the fair dower (mahr al-mithl) because the usufruct of the vulva (manāfi' al-buḍ') is not compensable (ghayr mutaqawwam) upon destruction—it only becomes compensable once owned by the husband, but when testifying, they are destroying the usufruct before ownership, which is not financially compensable. In a sale, the witnesses owe only the amount by which the price is less than the item's value (if the buyer claims a low price) or the excess (if the seller claims a high price), based on who initiated the claim and the satisfaction of the parties. The commentary explains the rationale: compensation requires equivalence (mumāthala), which does not exist for the vulva's usufruct. The relied-upon (muftā bihi) position in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view on the women's compensation, as supported by the commentary.

Liability of Retracting Witnesses in Marriage
Ruling on witness liability in marriage with rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness liability upon retraction in marriage and apply it to different dower scenarios.

Core Idea
Witnesses in marriage are liable only for the excess over mahr al-mithl, because the usufruct of the vulva is non-compensable.

This lesson examines the liability of witnesses who retract their testimony in a marriage case. The key factor is whether the specified dower equals, exceeds, or falls short of the proper dower (mahr al-mithl). The Hanafi position rests on the nature of the usufruct of the vulva: it is not compensable at the time of destruction, but becomes compensable after possession. Understanding this distinction clarifies when witnesses must pay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is witness retraction in a marriage testimony. The general ruling: witnesses are not liable unless the specified dower exceeds mahr al-mithl.
This is a ruling with a rational proof.
2
Distinguish the three scenarios based on dower amount
Scenario 1: specified dower equals mahr al-mithl → no liability. Scenario 2: specified dower is less → no liability. Scenario 3: specified dower is more → liability for the excess.
The first two scenarios involve no destruction (itlaf).
3
Understand the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The usufruct of the vulva (manafi‘ al-bud‘) is not compensable (ghayr mutaqawwam) at the time of destruction. Therefore, no equivalent exists for compensation. But after the husband takes possession, it becomes compensable to signify its importance.
This is based on Hanafi qiyas and the concept of taqwim.
4
Apply the rule to a concrete example
If witnesses testify to a marriage with a dower of 500, but mahr al-mithl is 300, and they retract after judgment, they must pay 200 (the excess). If the dower was 200 or 300, they pay nothing.
The liability is only for the excess over mahr al-mithl.

Liability of Retracting Witnesses in Sale
Ruling on witness liability in sale with emphasis on condition of claim origin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on witness liability upon retraction in a sale and differentiate based on who made the claim (buyer vs seller).

Core Idea
In sale, witnesses are liable only for the deficiency when the price is less than value and the claim is from the buyer.

This lesson expands on the liability of retracting witnesses in a sale. The ruling is nuanced: witnesses are liable only when the price is below market value and the claim comes from the buyer. If the seller claims, the seller consented to the price, so no liability. Additional details cover testimony on payment of price. Understanding this helps in evaluating witness-induced losses in commercial transactions.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling for witness liability in sale
The matn says: 'and in a sale except for what falls short of the value of the item sold.' This means witnesses are liable only for the deficiency (shortfall) between the price and the actual value.
This is an exception to the principle that witnesses are not liable for truthful testimony.
2
Distinguish based on who makes the claim
If the buyer claims the sale price was less than value, witnesses are liable for the deficiency. If the seller claims, there is no liability because the seller consented to the price.
Consent removes the element of loss.
3
Analyze the proof for liability
Witnesses are liable because they caused a loss (the deficiency) without compensation to the buyer. If the price equals or exceeds the value, there is no loss.
The proof is rational: destruction without consideration leads to liability.
4
Consider additional cases with testimony on payment of price
If witnesses testify to both sale and payment, they may be liable for the full price if they retract from both. If they testify to sale only, they owe only the deficiency. If they testify to purchase (buyer's side), they owe the excess over value if the buyer claimed a higher price.
The details clarify the extent of liability depending on what the testimony covered.

Liability of Retracting Witnesses in Sale Testimony
Clarifying witness liability after retraction in sale testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which witnesses who retract after testifying to a sale must guarantee the deficiency and distinguish it from cases where no guarantee is due.

Core Idea
Witnesses guarantee only the uncompensated loss – a sale below market value triggers liability.

When witnesses retract their testimony after a judge has ruled, the original judgment stands, but the witnesses may become liable for damages. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling on a specific scenario: testimony to a sale. The key is whether the loss caused was compensated or not – a sale at or above market value leaves no uncompensated loss, so no guarantee, while a below-market sale triggers liability for the deficiency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario of testimony
The passage focuses on the case where the buyer claims the seller sold, and witnesses testify against the seller that a sale occurred. Determine whether the witnesses testified only to the sale, or also to receipt of the price.
If the claim is from the seller, a different set of rules applies (discussed later).
2
Apply the principle of loss with compensation
The Hanafi principle: causing loss with an equivalent or greater compensation is not true loss. So if the sale was at or above market value, the seller received a fair price – no uncompensated loss exists. If below, the deficiency is uncompensated loss.
This principle is stated as 'al-itlaf bi-iwad kala itlaf'.
3
Analyze the subcases based on testimony details
If witnesses testify only to the sale: no guarantee if sale ≥ market price; guarantee deficiency if < market price. If they testify both to sale and receipt of price in separate acts after ruling, they guarantee the full price (even if less than market value) plus any increase. If they testify to both in one statement, they guarantee only the market value.
The difference hinges on whether the loss from both testimonies is separable or combined.
4
Distinguish the counterpart case: testimony to purchase
If witnesses testify against the buyer (claim from seller) that the buyer purchased, and retract: if the purchase price ≤ market value, no guarantee; if price > market value, they guarantee the excess.
This mirrors the sale case but from the buyer's perspective.

Correct Phrasing in Testimony on Sale: Buyer vs. Seller Claims
Clarifying correct wording for testimony on sale when buyer or seller claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct phrasing for testimony when the buyer claims versus when the seller claims, and explain why precision matters.

Core Idea
Testimony wording must mirror the claim: 'sale' for buyer's claim, 'purchase' for seller's claim.

In Hanafi testimony law, precise wording is crucial because the witness's statement must exactly match the litigant's claim. This passage from `Umda al-Ri`aya addresses a subtle but important distinction: when the seller claims, witnesses must testify 'on the purchase' (shahida `ala al-shira'), not 'on the sale'. Understanding this distinction prevents invalidation of testimony due to ambiguity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize when the claim is from the buyer (claiming seller sold) versus when it is from the seller (claiming buyer purchased).
2
Match the claim to the correct testimony phrasing
For buyer's claim, testimony is 'against the seller regarding the sale' (shahida `ala al-bay'). For seller's claim, testimony is 'regarding the purchase' (shahida `ala al-shira').
The object of testimony should be the action being claimed.
3
Understand the linguistic subtlety
The word 'sale' (bay`) is an antonym that can also mean 'purchase', but precise legal phrasing avoids ambiguity.
Al-Hidayah used 'sale' in the buyer's claim scenario; some may misinterpret it as acceptable for seller's claim.
4
Evaluate the commentator's critique
The author of the hashiya argues that the commentator's assertion in `Umda al-Ri`aya that al-Hidayah's wording 'cannot be interpreted' is too strong, because bay` can mean shira`.
This shows the importance of nuanced language in legal commentary.

Guarantee of Half Mahr in Retracted Divorce Testimony
Ruling with proof and illustrative examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling and reasoning for witnesses retracting testimony of divorce before consummation, and apply it to cases with specified mahr or mut'ah.

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract testimony of pre-consummation divorce must compensate for the mahr or mut'ah they helped confirm.

When witnesses testify to a divorce before consummation and later retract, they may be liable for half the mahr or mut'ah. This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling, its rationale (istihsan), and how the amount they guarantee depends on whether a mahr was specified.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Identify the act: witnesses testifying to divorce before consummation, then retracting. The ruling: they guarantee half the mahr (or mut'ah if no mahr).
The guarantee is wajib, not merely recommended.
2
Understand the rationale (istihsan)
The reasoning: by their testimony, they confirmed an obligation that was on the verge of dropping due to possible annulment (e.g., apostasy, mahram breastfeeding). Thus, they caused the mahr to become fixed and must compensate.
The dalil is istihsan based on the principle of preventing loss from false testimony.
3
Differentiate between specified mahr and mut'ah
If a mahr was specified, they guarantee half of it. If no mahr was specified, they guarantee the mut'ah (consolatory gift) because that is the obligatory payment at divorce before consummation.
Even if they testified to a different settlement (e.g., a slave for mut'ah), they guarantee the mut'ah unless the judge adjudicated differently.
4
Note the variation when judge adjudicates
If the judge adjudicated a specific item (e.g., a slave) based on their testimony, then they guarantee that item's value upon retraction, not the mut'ah.
This shows that the guarantee follows the actual loss caused by the testimony.

Guarantee for Retracted Testimony in Manumission and Qisas
Ruling that retracted testimony on manumission guarantees value, on qisas guarantees diyah only

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the guarantee for retracted testimony in manumission and qisas, and explain the wealth condition for the manumission guarantee.

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract after causing manumission guarantee full value; after qisas, they pay diyah only.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in the law of testimony: when witnesses retract after their testimony has been acted upon. You will learn the Hanafi rulings on what compensation the witnesses owe in two separate cases—manumission of a slave and retaliatory execution—and why the Lawgiver distinguishes between them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two distinct scenarios
The passage covers retracted testimony in manumission (i'tāq) and in qisas. What are the general rulings for each?
Manumission: witnesses guarantee the slave's value. Qisas: witnesses guarantee diyah.
2
Understand the guarantee in manumission
The witnesses guarantee the full value of the slave to the master. The wala' does not transfer. The guarantee is for property loss.
This is considered 'property destruction' (itlaf al-milk).
3
Understand the guarantee in qisas
The witnesses guarantee diyah (blood money) only. The loss is of life, not property, so the compensation is fixed as diyah.
Diyah is obligatory upon all witnesses regardless of wealth.
4
Note the wealth condition for manumission guarantee
The manumission guarantee is a form of solace (silah) and is only obligatory upon the witnesses if they are wealthy (musir). The qisas guarantee applies regardless of wealth.
This distinction is derived from the nature of the compensation: solace vs. property destruction.

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract after qisas are liable for diyah, not qisas, because their testimony is not a direct cause of death; forgiveness is recommended.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah details the consequences when witnesses retract their testimony after the judgment has been carried out. You will learn the Hanafi school's nuanced approach to liability: whereas the Shafi'i school applies qisas, the Hanafis impose only diyah for killings, and for financial losses they require compensation based on the value lost. The reasoning hinges on the concept of indirect causation (tasbib) and the recommended nature of forgiveness.

The act is retraction (ruju') of testimony after the judgment has been executed. The ruling is liability (daman) for the loss caused. Note that the loss differs by scenario: bodily harm vs. financial loss.

False Testimony: Qisas or Diyah? The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Hanafi rebuttal of Shafi'i view on qisas for false witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the liability of false witnesses and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
False witnesses who cause an execution owe diyah, not qisas, because voluntary choices sever causation.

This passage presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the liability of false witnesses whose testimony leads to an execution. The Shafi'i school analogizes the witnesses to a coercer (mukrih) and argues for qisas, while the Hanafi school refutes this by emphasizing that the heir's option to forgive and the judge's ruling break the chain of causation. Understanding this dispute reveals the Hanafi school's nuanced approach to causation (tasbib) and the role of shubha in penal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contested Issue
State the precise legal question: are false witnesses who cause the execution of an innocent person liable for qisas (retaliation) or only for diyah (blood money)?
The ruling depends on whether their testimony is considered a direct cause of death.
2
State the Shafi'i Position and Its Proof
The Shafi'i school argues that witnesses are indirect causes (tasbib) analogous to a coercer (mukrih). Since a coercer is subject to qisas, the witnesses are even more deserving because the heir is assisted in exacting qisas.
Shafi'i examples include cases of partial ownership, kitabah, and istilad.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Rebuttal
The Hanafi response distinguishes causation from mere opportunity: testimony does not usually lead to death because forgiveness is recommended and the heir suffers no harm by forgiving. Also, the judge's ruling and the heir's voluntary choice are intervening voluntary acts that sever attribution to the witnesses.
Key concepts: tasbib (causation) vs. direct action; shubha (dubiety) drops qisas but not diyah.
4
Apply the Principle of Shubha
Even if causation were established, the presence of shubha (dubiety) due to the intervening choices would prevent qisas. Qisas is the ultimate penalty and requires direct intentional killing without any ambiguity.
Diyah is obligatory even with shubha, unlike qisas.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence: under Shafi'i law, false witnesses could be executed; under Hanafi law, they only pay diyah.
This difference affects actual legal outcomes in cases of false testimony leading to death.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on whether false witnesses who cause the execution of an innocent person are liable for qisas (retaliation) or only diyah (blood money). The Shafi'i position, shown first with examples, holds that the witnesses are indirect causes of death, analogous to a person who coerces someone to kill; since the coercer is liable for qisas, the witnesses are even more deserving. The Hanafi response argues that the witnesses are not causes because testimony does not usually lead to execution—the heir is encouraged to forgive, and the judge's ruling and the heir's voluntary choice intervene. Thus the killing is not attributable to the witnesses. Moreover, qisas is the ultimate punishment and requires direct intentional killing; the witnesses' act is neither direct nor causative. The Hanafi school therefore holds that only diyah is due, not qisas.

ملجأ (mulja’): One who is compelled by fear of worldly harm, losing effective choice.

Liability upon Retraction: The Branch vs. the Original Witness
Rulings on liability when branch or original witnesses retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the liability of the branch witness from that of the original witness upon retraction, and state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the original witness who admits a mistake.

Core Idea
The branch witness is always liable upon retraction; the original is never liable unless he admits a mistake, and even then, the majority view absolves him.

This passage from 'Umda al-Ri'aya clarifies the liability of witnesses who retract their testimony after a judgment has been executed. The key distinction is between the branch witness, who testifies based on the original, and the original witness himself. Understanding this distinction prevents confusion in cases where multiple witnesses withdraw and helps identify who bears financial responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of witnesses
Recognize that an 'aṣl' (original) is the one with personal knowledge who appoints a 'far‘' (branch) to testify. The branch testifies based on the original’s information.
In Hanafi testimony law, the branch is a surrogate; his testimony is considered direct in court.
2
Determine the ruling for each retraction scenario
When the branch retracts alone, he is liable. When the original makes a claim (e.g., he never appointed the branch, or he was mistaken), that claim is not heeded, and the original is not liable. When both retract, only the branch is liable.
The text explicitly states: 'wa ḍamina al-far‘u bil-rujū‘' – the branch is liable upon retraction.
3
Analyze the original's statements
If the original says 'I did not make you a witness on my testimony,' his statement is ignored. If he says 'I made you a witness and I was mistaken,' there is a dispute: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say no liability; Muhammad says he is liable.
The phrase 'wa ghalatṭu' (and I was mistaken) triggers the ikhtilāf.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the original bears no liability even if he admits error; under Muhammad, he would be jointly liable with the branch if the branch also retracts (depending on circumstance).
The text does not explicitly state the mufta bihi, but the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is likely the relied-upon position.

Liability of Secondary Witnesses Upon Retraction: The Direct Causation Principle
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability when witnesses retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on liability when original witnesses claim error and both retract, and apply the direct causation principle to determine who is liable.

Core Idea
Liability follows direct causation: the secondary witness who testifies in court is the direct agent, not the original outside court.

When secondary witnesses retract their testimony in a property case, the question arises: who compensates the damaged party? The Hanafi school splits on this when the original witnesses claim they made an error in appointing the secondaries. This lesson unpacks the dispute, rooted in the distinction between direct causation (mubashir) and indirect causation (musabbib). Understanding this distinction is key to predicting liability in witness retraction cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and scenarios
Distinguish between the original witness (asl) who first witnessed the event and the secondary witness (far') who conveys that testimony to court. The passage discusses two scenarios: the original denies making the secondary a witness, and the original admits it but claims error.
Only the second scenario is disputed; the first is settled: the original is not liable.
2
Understand the causal reasoning
The key legal concept is that the direct cause of the loss (the testimony given in court) is attributed to the secondary witnesses. They are the mubashir (direct agent). The original witnesses are merely a musabbib (indirect cause). In Hanafi law, when both a direct agent and a cause are present, liability falls on the direct agent.
Examples from the text: the indicator of a thief, the opener of a cage, the untier of a fetter — all direct agents bear liability.
3
Compare the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that only the secondary witnesses are liable because the judgment was based solely on their testimony in court. Muhammad holds that the claimant may choose to hold either the original or the secondary liable because the secondary acted as an agent for the original.
The difference hinges on whether the secondary's testimony is seen as independent or as a mere conveyance.
4
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
For the two shaykhs: the judge only hears the secondary's testimony; the original's statement outside court is not evidence. For Muhammad: the secondary stands in the place of the original, so the original's testimony (via the secondary) is what the judgment is based on; thus both share responsibility.
Muhammad's view treats the original as having effectively testified directly through the secondary.
5
Note the practical consequence and later tarjih
Under the two shaykhs, the claimant must sue the secondary witnesses only. Under Muhammad, the claimant can choose to sue either the original or the secondary. Later authorities differ on which view is mufta bihi: al-Multaqa prefers Muhammad; al-Tanwir and al-Haskafi prefer the two shaykhs.
This tarjih difference must be considered when issuing fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the liability (daman) when witnesses retract their testimony in property cases. The core ruling states that if the secondary witnesses (far') retract their testimony, they are liable for the subject matter, and the original witnesses (asl) are not liable when they say 'I did not make you a witness' or 'I made you a witness and I was mistaken.' However, if both original and secondary witnesses retract, the secondary is charged. The commentary explains the reasoning: The direct cause of the loss is the testimony given in court by the secondary witnesses, so they are the direct agents (mubashirun) and are liable. The original witnesses are mere causes (musabbibun), and when a direct agent and a cause combine, liability falls on the direct agent. The text then addresses two specific scenarios: first, when the original denies having made the secondary a witness—this is not accepted because it conflicts with the established testimony; second, when the original admits making the secondary a witness but claims error (ghalat). On this, the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) hold the secondary liable, while Imam Muhammad holds that both are liable and the claimant may choose whom to hold liable. Muhammad's reasoning is that the secondary acted as agent of the original, so the original's liability is like direct testimony. The two shaykhs counter that the judgment is based solely on the secondary's testimony in court, so the secondary is the direct cause. The passage ends with a note that later authorities differ in tarjih: al-Multaqa prefers Muhammad's view, while al-Tanwir and al-Haskafi prefer the view of the two shaykhs. This illustrates the nuanced Hanafi method of assigning liability based on causation in legal procedure.

Core Idea
A branch's accusation against the principal is disregarded; a certifier's retraction triggers liability according to Abu Hanifa.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies two distinct rules within testimony law. First, a branch witness cannot undermine a judgment by accusing his principal of lying or error. Second, the retraction of a certification (tazkiyah) creates liability according to Imam Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn disagree. Understanding the underlying causal reasoning is key to grasping the school's position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage begins with the ruling on the branch's statement, then moves to the muzakki's liability upon retraction. Note that they are separate issues.
The first ruling is undisputed; the second involves ikhtilaf.
2
Analyze the reason for disregarding the branch's statement
The branch's statement that his principal lied or erred is treated as nothing because the branch has not retracted his own testimony, and the principal's alleged fault cannot be proven through the branch's later claim.
The branch's testimony stands as given; he cannot undo it by blaming another.
3
Understand the core dispute on muzakki's liability
Abu Hanifa holds the muzakki liable because the judge acts based solely on the certification, making it like a cause of the cause (illat al-illah). The Sahibayn argue that certification is mere praise, and retracting praise does not create liability.
Focus on the causal logic: certification → judge's acceptance → loss.
4
Examine the exception for error
If the muzakki intentionally certified falsely (e.g., knowing witnesses are slaves), he is liable. But if he merely erred, all agree he is not liable. This scope is important.
Intent makes a difference: 'amdan vs. khata'.
5
Reflect on the broader causation model
The text explains that both principals (asl) and branches (far‘) are direct causes of loss. The principals by appointing branches, the branches by transmitting. This underpins the liability structure.
Each party's role is contributory; no recourse between them.

Liability of Ihsan Witnesses and the Analogy to the Muzakki
Ruling that ihsan witnesses are not liable upon retraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on whether the muzakki is liable upon retraction, and identify the legal basis (shart vs. illat al-illah) that distinguishes the two cases.

Core Idea
Ihsan witnesses are not liable because ihsan is a mere condition; the disagreement is whether tazkiyah is analogous or distinct.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya examines a specific case in retraction of testimony: witnesses who testify to the accused's ihsan (the condition for stoning) in a zina case. The ruling is clear — they are not liable — but the reasoning opens a key internal Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the parallel situation of a muzakki (certifier of witnesses). Understanding this ikhtilaf requires precise classification of legal categories: shart mahd, alamah, and illat al-illah. The commentator resolves the dispute by upholding Abu Hanifa's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Agreed Ruling
Note that all Hanafi imams agree: if witnesses testify to the accused's ihsan, then retract after stoning, they are not liable for damages. This is the agreed premise of the disagreement.
The liability question only arises if the retraction leads to a reversal of the penalty.
2
Understand the Reason Given in the Text
The text states that ihsan is a 'shart mahd' (pure condition). The ruling of stoning is attributed to the zina itself, not to the condition of ihsan. Therefore, the ihsan witnesses' testimony is not a direct cause of the judge's action.
The commentator elaborates with definitions from al-Zaylai and al-Minh al-Ghaffar, distinguishing condition, cause, and sign.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's Analogy
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani argue that the muzakki (the one who certifies the witnesses' trustworthiness) is similar to the ihsan witnesses: both provide information that is a prerequisite, and just as ihsan witnesses are not liable, the muzakki should not be liable upon retraction.
Their qiyas is based on the apparent similarity: both are 'testifying to a condition'.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Response
Abu Hanifa rejects the analogy. He argues that tazkiyah is not a mere condition; it is an 'illat al-illah' (cause of the cause). The judge acts directly upon the tazkiyah — without it, he would not accept the witnesses. In contrast, the judge does not act upon ihsan; ihsan is only a condition for the penalty to apply. Thus the muzakki is more causally connected to the loss and is liable.
The thamarah al-khilaf: only in Abu Hanifa's view is the muzakki liable; the Sahibayn hold him not liable.

Explaining the ruling on witnesses retracting ihsan testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi fiqh holds that witnesses to ihsan are not liable upon retraction, distinguish between witnesses to a cause ('illah) and witnesses to a condition (shart), and apply the rule to analogous cases.

Core Idea
Ihsan is a pure condition (shart mahd); retraction incurs no liability because the ruling is attributed to the cause, not the condition.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah delves into a nuanced ruling on witness liability. When witnesses testify to a person's ihsan (being muhsan) and later retract after a stoning, are they liable for damages? The Hanafi school says no, because ihsan is a mere condition, not the effective cause. But what about witnesses to tazkiyah (character certification) or to an oath? The text clarifies these distinctions, drawing on Hanafi legal theory and an intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Ruling
The passage opens with the ruling: witnesses to ihsan who retract after stoning are not liable. Read the first line and the reason given: 'because ihsan is a pure condition (shart mahd) to which the ruling is not attributed.'
Focus on the phrase 'shart mahd' – this is the key legal concept.
2
Contrast with Tazkiyah and Oath Witnesses
The text contrasts ihsan witnesses with witnesses to tazkiyah (character certification) and witnesses to an oath (shahid al-yamin). Trace why tazkiyah is considered 'illat al-'illah and why oath witnesses are liable while condition witnesses are not.
Note the analogy the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) draw, and how the text refines it.
3
Analyze the Usul Distinctions
The passage includes definitions from al-Zayla'i and al-Mannah: shart (condition), 'illah (cause), sabab (conduit), and alamah (sign). Understand how ihsan fits as a condition – necessary but not effective.
Ponder the statement: 'the ruling is attributed to the cause in reality, to the condition metaphorically.'
4
Examine the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
The text discusses Zufar's view: when condition witnesses retract together with cause witnesses, both groups are liable. Then it examines the case where condition witnesses retract alone – some mashayikh hold liability, but the correct view (from al-Ziyadat) is no liability.
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in who pays damages.
5
Apply the Principle to New Cases
Now apply the rule: given a scenario where a witness testifies to a condition (e.g., that a slave was owned before manumission), determine liability upon retraction. Use the hierarchy of cause vs. condition.
Remember: the mufta bihi position is that condition witnesses are never liable.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute and legal analysis regarding the liability of witnesses who retract their testimony on ihsan (the condition of being muhsan) after a stoning sentence has been carried out. The passage type is a ruling with proof through rational argument and analogy. The author begins by stating the ruling: witnesses who testify to ihsan and later retract are not liable for damages. The reason given is that ihsan is a pure condition (shart mahd), not a cause ('illah) to which the ruling (stoning) can be attributed. The ruling is then contrasted with tazkiyah (character certification), where retraction does incur liability because tazkiyah is considered a cause of the cause ('illat al-'illah). The two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, analogize the case of tazkiyah to ihsan, arguing that just as a character witness who retracts is not liable, the ihsan witness should also not be liable. However, the text clarifies that the distinction lies in the nature of ihsan: it is a mere sign (alamah) or condition, not effective in the ruling. The discussion then extends to the case of a witness to an oath (shahid al-yamin) who is liable upon retraction because he testifies to the cause itself (e.g., manumission or divorce). In contrast, a witness to a condition (shahid al-shart) is generally not liable, because the condition is not the effective cause. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether witnesses to a condition are liable when they retract alone: some scholars (like Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi) hold liability, while the correct view (as per al-Ziyadat) is no liability. The text thus presents the hierarchy of cause, condition, and sign in Hanafi legal theory, emphasizing that rulings are attributed to effective causes, not to conditions or mere signs.

Key Terms
شرط محض (shart mahd): A pure condition that is not effective in causing the ruling but upon which the existence of the cause depends.
تزكية (tazkiyah): Character certification of witnesses by a witness attesting to their uprightness; without it the judge does not accept testimony.
شاهد اليمين (shahid al-yamin): A witness who testifies that someone has taken an oath (e.g., to divorce or manumit), thereby establishing the cause of the effect.

Liability of Witnesses of the Cause and Condition Upon Retraction
Explaining the difference in liability between witnesses of the cause and witnesses of the condition when retracting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the liability of witnesses of the cause (shāhid al-yamīn) and witnesses of the condition (shāhid al-shart) when retracting testimony, and state the correct Hanafi position (muftā bihi) with its evidential basis.

Core Idea
Only the witness of the cause is liable; the witness of the condition is never liable.

In cases of conditional manumission or divorce, witnesses may testify to the conditional utterance (the cause) or to the occurrence of the condition. When they retract, the question arises: who must compensate the loss? This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āya explains the key distinction between the witness of the cause (shāhid al-yamīn) and the witness of the condition (shāhid al-shart), and resolves an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the latter's liability. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly assigning damages in such cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of witnesses
Read the passage and identify who is the witness of the cause (shāhid al-yamīn) and who is the witness of the condition (shāhid al-shart). The cause is the utterance that triggers manumission or divorce; the condition is the event upon which the utterance is contingent.
The witness of the cause testifies to the phrase 'You are free' or 'You are divorced' being spoken conditionally; the witness of the condition testifies that the condition actually occurred.
2
State the basic ruling for each witness
According to the text, what is the liability (damān) of each witness upon retraction? The witness of the cause is liable; the witness of the condition is not. Note the special restriction for divorce before consummation.
For divorce after consummation, the witness retracting is not liable because the dower is already due.
3
Understand the cause vs. condition distinction (talīl)
The passage explains that the ruling is attributed to the cause truly (ḥaqīqatan) and to the condition only metaphorically (majāzan). Metaphor cannot oppose truth. The condition is merely a preventer (māniʿ) whose removal allows the effect, but the effect is caused by the utterance.
This is a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: the cause ('illa) is the direct effective factor; the condition (sharṭ) is a prerequisite but not causative.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi disagreement
The text presents two alternative views: (1) Zufar holds that witnesses of the condition are jointly liable if they retract with the witnesses of the cause. (2) Some mashāyikh hold that witnesses of the condition are liable if they retract alone. Identify the evidence for each and the Hanafi response.
Zufar's argument: loss occurred due to both testimonies. Mashāyikh's argument: when no cause opposes, the condition can substitute as a cause.
5
Determine the muftā bihi position
The text states 'al-ṣaḥīḥ' (the correct position) that witnesses of the condition are never liable, as explicitly stated in al-Ziyādāt and supported by Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsī. This is the relied-upon view for fatwa.
Fakhr al-Islām al-Bazdawī inclined to liability when alone, but the majority and the text of the Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah support non-liability.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the liability (damān) of witnesses when they retract their testimony in cases of conditional manumission or divorce. The passage begins by stating the rule: the witness of the oath (shāhid al-yamīn), i.e., the one who testifies to the utterance of the conditional phrase (e.g., 'You are free' upon a condition), is liable for the loss when he retracts, because his testimony is the cause ('illa) of the loss. The witness of the condition (shāhid al-shart), who testifies to the occurrence of the condition, is not liable, because the condition is merely a metaphorically attributed factor. The text then explains the reasoning: the cause (the utterance) is the true effective factor, while the condition is only a preventer that, when removed, allows the effect. The author restricts the liability in divorce to cases before consummation because after consummation the dower is already due. Then the text explores scenarios: if witnesses of the condition retract together with witnesses of the cause, they are not liable according to the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn), but Zufar holds them jointly liable. The Hanafi rebuttal is that the cause is truth while the condition is metaphorical, and metaphor cannot oppose truth. When witnesses of the condition retract alone, some mashāyikh hold them liable because the condition can substitute for the cause, but the correct position (ṣaḥīḥ) as explicitly stated in al-Ziyādāt (one of the six core books of the Zahir al-Riwayah) is that they are never liable. Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsī supports this, while Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawī leans toward liability when alone. The passage concludes with a summary: when all witnesses retract, only the witnesses of the oath are liable.

Key Terms
شاهد اليمين (shāhid al-yamīn): The witness who testifies to the utterance of the conditional phrase (the cause) that triggers manumission or divorce.
شاهد الشرط (shāhid al-shart): The witness who testifies to the occurrence of the condition upon which the utterance (cause) is contingent.
العلة (al-'illa): The cause (effective factor) that directly produces the legal effect; here, the utterance of manumission or divorce is the 'illa.
الشرط (al-shart): The condition upon which the effect (manumission/divorce) depends; its existence is necessary but not causative.
ضمان (damān): Liability to compensate for a loss caused by the witness's retraction; here, payment of the slave's value or half the dower.

Liability of Witnesses Upon Retraction in Conditional Emancipation
Explaining ruling when witnesses retract conflicting testimonies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between direct and indirect causation in witness testimony and state which witnesses are liable upon retraction in conditional emancipation cases.

Core Idea
Liability follows the cause: only the witnesses of the conditional oath are liable, not the condition's witnesses.

This passage from Kitab al-Shahadah presents a nuanced ruling on witness retraction. When multiple witnesses testify to different parts of a conditional emancipation, their roles and liability differ. The key principle is that liability attaches to the witness whose testimony directly caused the damage (sahib al-illa). In this lesson, we will analyse the ruling and its causal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Here, it's the retraction of witnesses after judgment. The ruling is that only the witnesses of the oath are liable.
2

The text states 'because they are the ones who caused the damage.' Identify which witnesses are the 'sahib al-illa'.
3
Apply the causation principle
Explain why the oath witnesses are the direct cause and the condition witnesses are not. The oath witnesses established the conditional link; the condition witnesses only confirmed an event.
4
Understand the scope of retraction
The pronoun 'they' includes all three types of witnesses (oath, condition, ihsan). But liability is not shared equally.
5
Evaluate the practical outcome
The thamarah: only the oath witnesses pay compensation. The condition witnesses face no liability despite retracting.
