Imprisonment for Maintenance and the Prohibition of Imprisoning a Father for His Child's Debt
Ruling with proof on imprisonment for maintenance and child's debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two rulings regarding imprisonment for non-payment of maintenance and for a child's debt, and distinguish the conditions that trigger each.

Core Idea
A father is imprisoned for refusing to pay maintenance after judicial assessment, but never for his child's debt — respect for paternity prevents it.

In this passage, the author clarifies two distinct scenarios involving imprisonment in family financial obligations. First, when a husband or father refuses to pay court-assessed maintenance, he is imprisoned because his refusal constitutes oppression. Second, a father is never imprisoned for the debt of his child, due to the principle of respect for ascendants. The text also addresses the mechanism for enforcing payment from a wealthy father without imprisonment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two distinct rulings
Read the passage to find the two main rulings: (1) imprisonment for non-payment of maintenance of wife and child after judicial assessment, and (2) no imprisonment for a child's debt. Note the different conditions.
The passage uses 'يحبس' (he is imprisoned) and 'لا يحبس' (he is not imprisoned).
2
Understand the condition for maintenance becoming a debt
The text states that maintenance becomes a debt only after a judicial decree or mutual agreement. Without that, past maintenance lapses. Identify this condition for both wife and child.
This is crucial for knowing when imprisonment is lawful.
3
Trace the reasoning for not imprisoning the father
The author provides an analogy: just as there is no retaliation for killing one's child, no hadd for slandering one's child, etc., so too a father is not imprisoned for the child's debt. Recognize this qiyas (analogical reasoning).
The reasoning is based on the principle that a father does not deserve punishment due to his child.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on enforcement
The passage mentions that according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), if a father is wealthy and refuses to pay his child's debt, the judge can sell his real estate (like movable property). This differs from a possible stricter view. Observe the tarjih (preference) indicated.
The text says 'al-sahih 'indahuma' (the sound position according to them) implying this is the relied-upon view.

[3] His statement: 'And on the maintenance of his wedding' – meaning if the judge assessed maintenance for a man's wife, and agreed on an amount, and he did not spend on her, and she complained to the ruler, he imprisons him; because in refraining from spending on her he becomes an oppressor. 'And his child' – meaning he is imprisoned for the maintenance of his child, because it is for the child's survival. We only say 'if the judge assessed' because the maintenance of a wife does not become a debt on the husband except by judicial order or mutual agreement. So if a period passes before judgment or agreement, it lapses. Similarly, the maintenance of a poor minor child. As for maintenance of other relatives, it lapses after time, even after judgment or agreement, unless it was incurred by order of a judge, then it does not lapse. This is in Radd al-Muhtar and others. [4] His statement: 'Not on his debt' – meaning the judge does not imprison the father for the debt of his child. Every ascendant, even if high, (is not imprisoned) for the debt of his descendant; because he does not deserve punishment due to his child. Hence, there is no retaliation against him for killing him, nor does he kill his heir, nor is he subjected to hadd for slandering him, nor does he slander his deceased mother at his request. Their statement here that there is no retaliation for killing him implies that the ascendant means father and mother, paternal and maternal grandfathers, because they have stated in the chapter of injuries that the maternal grandfather is not subject to retaliation for killing his daughter's child. So similarly, he is not imprisoned for his debt. In al-Muhit: The two parents, grandparents, and grandmothers are not imprisoned except for the maintenance of their child. End. And their apparent absolute statement indicates no distinction between the wealthy and the poor. But one should be aware of something: if he is wealthy and refrains from paying his child's debt, the judge pays his debt from his wealth, if it is of the same type, otherwise he sells it to pay, like selling the property of an imprisoned person who refuses to pay his debt. And according to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), he sells his real estate like his movables. This is in al-Bahr.

Commentary
This passage covers two main rulings: imprisonment for non-payment of maintenance, and the prohibition of imprisoning a father for his child's debt. For maintenance, the judge must first assess the amount; then if the husband refuses, he is imprisoned because he becomes an oppressor. Maintenance of a wife only becomes a debt after a judicial order or agreement; prior periods lapse. For a child's maintenance, the same applies. As for the father's debt for his child, he is not imprisoned because punishment is not due on an ascendant for a descendant's obligation. The commentators analogize to other areas: no retaliation for killing one's child, no hadd for slandering one's own child, etc. However, if the father is wealthy and refuses to pay, the judge can take the money from him directly, and according to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), even sell his real estate like movable property.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance; financial support required for wife and children, and in some cases other relatives.
دين الولد (dayn al-walad): Debt of the child; a father is not imprisoned for it but may be compelled to pay from his wealth if able.
أصل (asl): Ascendant; parent or grandparent.
