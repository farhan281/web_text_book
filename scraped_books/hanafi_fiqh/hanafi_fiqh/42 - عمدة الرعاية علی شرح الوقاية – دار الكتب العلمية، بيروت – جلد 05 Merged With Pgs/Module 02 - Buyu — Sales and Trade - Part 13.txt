Buyer's Recourse Against Executor for Reclaimed Slave
Ruling with rationale and internal dispute on executor's recourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two-step recourse chain when a purchased slave is reclaimed or dies before possession, and explain the rationale behind the executor's secondary recourse.

Core Idea
The buyer reclaims from the executor; the executor reclaims from the creditors because he acted as their deputy.

When a slave purchased from an executor's sale is reclaimed by a rightful owner or dies before the buyer takes possession, and the price has already been paid, the buyer may suffer a loss. The law provides a two-step recourse: first against the executor who contracted, then the executor can in turn reclaim from those for whose benefit he acted. This lesson unpacks the ruling and the reasoning behind it, including a scholarly difference on the executor's recourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the two recourse claims
The scenario: a slave purchased from an executor (wasi) managing the deceased's estate is reclaimed (by a third-party owner) or dies before the buyer takes possession, and the price is already paid. Two claims arise: (1) the buyer has recourse (ruju') against the executor, (2) the executor has recourse against the creditors (ghurama') or heirs.
Focus on the chain: buyer → executor → creditors/heirs.
2
Trace the buyer's recourse to the executor
The buyer's recourse is directly against the executor because the executor is the contracting party (al-'aqid). The executor stands in place of the deceased, whether appointed by the deceased or by the judge. Therefore, the buyer's claim for return of the price is against the executor.
This is the first and undisputed part of the ruling.
3
Analyze the executor's recourse: the rationale of acting as deputy
The executor can reclaim from the creditors (or heirs) because he acted on their behalf and for their benefit. If he incurs liability due to that work, he can seek reimbursement from those for whom the work was done. The executor is a deputy (na'ib) of the deceased, and the rights and liabilities ultimately return to the estate beneficiaries.
The hashya clarifies that this applies regardless of how the executor was appointed.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on executor's recourse
Some Hanafi scholars argue the executor does not have recourse because the liability arose from his own action (his contract). The preferred opinion (al-awlah) in the commentary is that he does have recourse. The difference affects whether the executor personally bears the loss or can shift it to the estate.
The commentator declares the first opinion 'asah' (more correct).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi (operative position for fatwa) is that the executor does have recourse against the creditors/heirs. This is the ruling adopted in the zahir al-riwayah and preferred by later authorities.
Knowing the mufta bihi is essential for practical application.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn of al-Wiqayah, commented upon in 'Umda al-Ri'aya. The ruling: if a slave purchased from an executor's sale is reclaimed by a rightful owner, or dies before the buyer takes possession, and the price is lost (i.e., the buyer has already paid), the buyer has recourse against the executor (wasi). The executor then has recourse against those for whose benefit he acted (the creditors or heirs of the deceased). The rationale: the executor is the contracting party, so the buyer's claim is against him. But since the executor acted as a deputy for the deceased and for the benefit of the creditors (ghurama'), he can reclaim from them any loss he suffers. The first hashya clarifies that this applies whether the executor was appointed by the deceased or by the judge. The second hashya discusses the basis for the executor's recourse: he acted for the benefit of the creditors, and if liability befalls him due to his work, he can reclaim it. There is an alternative view that he cannot because the liability is due to his own action, but the preferred view is that he can.

Key Terms
وصي (wasi): The executor of a will or guardian appointed to manage the deceased's estate.
غرماء (ghurama'): Creditors of the deceased, for whose benefit the executor acts when settling debts.
رجوع (ruju'): Recourse: the legal right to claim compensation or reimbursement from another party.

Conditions for Rejecting Testimony: Major Sins and Indecency
Explaining that committing hadd-able offenses, entering bathhouse without covering, and usury disqualify witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three actions that disqualify a witness in Hanafi fiqh and explain why each is considered a major sin.

Core Idea
Testimony is invalid from a witness who commits major sins like hadd-able offenses, public indecency, or usury.

In Hanafi law, the integrity of a witness ('adalah) is paramount. This lesson examines three specific acts that destroy 'adalah: committing a crime punishable by hadd, uncovering the 'awrah in public (e.g., entering a bathhouse without an izar), and dealing with riba. These acts are considered major sins, and the school holds that no reliable testimony can come from one who commits them. The passage also extends the principle to acts like walking in trousers alone, which show lack of muru'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three disqualifying actions
Read the passage and list the three acts mentioned: committing a hadd offense, entering a bathhouse without an izar, and eating riba.
The text uses 'eating' riba but it means 'taking' the excess amount.
2
Determine the underlying reason for each
For each act, identify why it disqualifies. The passage states that each is a major sin (kabira) or involves haram (uncovering 'awrah). Lack of muru'ah is an additional factor.
Muru'ah is social integrity; even minor indecency like wearing only trousers in public suffices to disqualify.
3
Locate the textual proofs
The passage refers to Quranic prohibition of riba and the general impermissibility of uncovering 'awrah. The commentary uses qiyas to extend the ruling from bathhouse to public walking in trousers.
No explicit hadith is quoted; the proof is based on established principles.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi application
The ruling is drawn from al-Manah and the incident involving Abu Hanifa. It is part of the zahir al-riwayah and mufta bihi.
No disagreement mentioned among the three imams on these points.
5
Apply to practical scenarios
Consider modern equivalents: a witness known to charge interest, or one who swims in public in swimwear only. Would their testimony be accepted? Apply the same principles.
The key is whether the act indicates a major sin or lack of muru'ah.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof, explaining three specific conditions that disqualify a witness: committing hadd-able offenses, entering a bathhouse without covering, and eating riba. The commentary opens by affirming that anyone who commits a major sin (kabira) is rejected as a witness, because major sins lead to lying. The text then addresses each condition: (1) Committing what incurs hadd: this is a major sin, so testimony is rejected. (2) Entering a bathhouse without a waist-wrapper (izar): uncovering the 'awrah is haram, and this act shows lack of modesty and muru'ah (integrity). The commentary cites an incident where Abu Hanifa saw a man in a bathhouse without covering and rebuked him. It further notes al-Karkhi's view that walking in the road wearing only trousers (i.e., revealing too much) also disqualifies due to lack of muru'ah. (3) Eating riba (usury): this is also a major sin. 'Eating' here means 'taking' the extra amount, not literal eating. The mention of riba is derived from the Quranic verse where 'akl (eating) is used figuratively for taking property wrongfully. The commentary clarifies that the prohibition applies to taking any excess (al-zai'id) on loans. All three acts are major sins that destroy the moral character required for testimony.

Key Terms
المروءة (al-muru'ah): Social and moral integrity that a witness must maintain; it includes proper public conduct and avoiding even minor acts that hint at immorality.
الربا (al-riba): Usury; the excess amount taken over the principal in a loan or exchange of goods.
