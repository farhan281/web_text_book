Conflicting Testimonies on Murder Location: Both Rejected
Ruling on contradictory testimonies about murder location

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on contradictory testimonies about the place of murder and explain the rational proof.

Core Idea
Contradictory testimonies about a single murder occurring in two places are both rejected because reason proves they cannot both be true.

Imagine two sets of witnesses who both saw a murder but disagree on the city—Mecca vs Kufa. How should a Hanafi judge decide? This passage gives the clear ruling: both are rejected outright. The reason is not about credibility but about logical impossibility. Let's work through the legal reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Two witnesses claim Zaid was killed in Mecca on a given day; two others claim he was killed in Kufa on the same day. The Hanafi ruling is that both testimonies are rejected (mardudatan).
2
Understand the rational proof
The ruling is based on the logical impossibility of a single murder occurring in two different places simultaneously. This is a dalil aqli (rational proof), not a textual source.
3
Note the effect of a prior judgment
If the judge has already issued a verdict based on one set of testimony, the later conflicting testimony is automatically rejected. A prior judgment creates legal certainty that cannot be overturned by subsequent contradictory evidence.
4
Recognize the analogy to taharri
The commentary compares this to taharri (determining qibla): once a decision is made based on ijtihad, a later contrary ijtihad does not overturn it.
5
Apply the ruling to a similar scenario
Consider a case where the location differs but the time is the same. The same ruling applies: both testimonies rejected because the inconsistency is irreconcilable.

Commentary
This passage provides a ruling on contradictory testimonies regarding a murder. The commentator explains that if two witnesses claim the murder occurred in Mecca on a specific day, and another two claim it occurred in Kufa on the same day, both testimonies are rejected. The reason is that one of them must be false, as a single murder cannot happen in two different places simultaneously. Since neither set of testimony has priority, both are disregarded. However, if the judge issues a verdict based on one set (e.g., he rules that Zaid was killed in Mecca), and then later the other set of witnesses appear, the second testimony is rejected because a prior judgment has already determined the location, and contradicting it would undermine the legal certainty. The commentator analogizes this to taharri, the process of determining the direction of qibla after individual ijtihad; once a judgment is established, it is not overturned by another conflicting ijtihad.

Key Terms
بينة (bayyinah): Testimony, proof; here it refers to each set of two witnesses.
تحري (taḥarrī): The process of exerting effort to determine the correct direction (e.g., of qibla) after conflicting ijtihad, where a prior decision is not revoked by later differences.

Key Takeaway
Contradictory testimonies about the place of a single murder are both rejected because a single act cannot occur in two places, and a prior judgment based on one set precludes the other.
