The Transferor's Consent in Hawala: Intra-Hanafi Dispute and Its Resolution
Intra-Hanafi dispute over transferor's consent in hawala, with reconciliation and practical application.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi narrations on the transferor's consent in hawala, explain the reconciliation between them, and apply the correct ruling based on who initiates the hawala.

Core Idea
The transferor's consent is required only when he has a claim against the debtor; otherwise, the debtor's initiation suffices.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqaya addresses a critical dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the necessity of the transferor's (muhil) consent for a valid hawala (transfer of debt). Two narrations exist: one from Al-Quduri requiring consent, and another from Al-Ziyadat deeming it unnecessary. The commentators reconcile these by distinguishing the scenario where the transferor has a claim against the debtor from the scenario where the debtor initiates the transfer. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the mufta bihi ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the basic requirement
List the three parties: transferor (muhil), transferee (muhtal), debtor (muhtal 'alayh). Note that consent of transferee and debtor is always required. The dispute is only about transferor.
The passage explicitly requires consent of all three initially, then delves into the dispute.
2
State the two Hanafi narrations
Al-Quduri: consent required because people of honor rarely accept others bearing their debt. Al-Ziyadat: valid without consent because transferee acts in his own right and transferor benefits.
The author of the commentary prefers Al-Quduri's narration.
3
Analyze the reconciliation
The transferor's consent is required only when he has a claim against the debtor (so hawala acts as discharge of his claim). Otherwise, if the debtor initiates, the hawala is valid without transferor's consent.
Al-Zaylai and Al-Inayah offer this reconciliation.
4
Contrast with other schools
Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali generally do not require transferor's consent. The Hanafi position is nuanced.
The passage cites the Shafi'i 'asahh' view.
5
Apply the mufta bihi ruling
In practice, if debtor says to creditor 'transfer your debt to me,' the creditor may do so without original debtor's consent.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqaya discusses the conditions for a valid Hawala (transfer of debt). It identifies three parties: the transferor (muhil), the transferee (muhtal), and the debtor (muhtal alayh). The passage begins by stating that Hawala requires the consent (rida) of all three parties, and defines consent as acceptance in the session of offer. It then provides rational justifications for requiring the consent of the transferee (because the debt is his right and obligations differ in trustworthiness) and the debtor (because the claim varies in intensity and he must assume the obligation). The passage then addresses the requirement of the transferor's consent, noting a dispute within the Hanafi school. According to the narration of Al-Quduri (in Mukhtasar al-Quduri), the transferor's consent is required because people of honor rarely agree to others bearing their debt. The author of the commentary (likely the author of 'Umdat al-Ri'aya) chooses this view. However, in Al-Ziyadat, it is narrated that Hawala is valid without the transferor's consent because the transferee is acting in his own right and the transferor is not harmed; rather, he benefits from the immediate cessation of claims. The passage reconciles these views by suggesting that Al-Quduri's narration applies when the transferor himself has a debt claim against the debtor (so the hawala effectively discharges that claim, requiring his consent), while Al-Ziyadat's narration applies when the debtor initiates the transfer (the transferee is assuming a new obligation of his own accord). The passage then presents two scenarios: one where the debtor asks the creditor to transfer the debt to him (the creditor's consent suffices, and the original debtor is discharged), and another analogous to guaranty where the debtor guarantees the debt without the original debtor's order. The first is a true hawala requiring the transferor's consent, while the second is an ihtiyal (causing a transfer) which does not require it. The passage critiques those who absolutely require consent based on Al-Ziyadat, noting that the correct view is that consent is required for recourse or discharge, not for validity. Finally, it clarifies the definition of hawala as a transfer of debt from one obligation to another and provides a ruling on the liability of the debtor if he pays the transferor after the hawala.

Definition of Hawala and the Condition of the Original Debtor's Consent
Defining hawala and presenting two narrations on consent of the original debtor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of hawala and explain the two narrations regarding the requirement of the original debtor's consent.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers liability; consent of the original debtor is not essential for validity but affects his recourse.

This lesson introduces the foundational concept of hawala (transfer of debt) in Hanafi fiqh. It begins with the definition, then delves into a critical intra-madhhab dispute: whether the consent of the original debtor (muheel) is a condition for the validity of hawala. You will explore two narrations, their practical forms, and the school's relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Define hawala
Identify the core definition: hawala is the transfer of a debt from one liability (dhimma) to another. Note the three parties: muheel (transferor/original debtor), muhtal (creditor/transferee), and muhtal alayh (new debtor). The passage emphasizes that the debt is transferred, not extinguished.
The term 'muhtal' in the source refers to the creditor who initiates or accepts the transfer.
2
Identify the two narrations
Al-Quduri's narration requires the consent of the original debtor for the hawala to be valid and free him. Al-Ziyadat's narration holds it valid without his consent. These represent different transmissions within the Hanafi school.
The mufta bihi is the narration of al-Ziyadat according to later scholars.
3
Understand the two practical forms
The passage describes two scenarios: (1) the original debtor initiates the transfer (iḥālah) – requires his consent; (2) a third party assumes the debt without the original debtor's order (iḥtiyāl) – does not require his consent, analogous to kafala.
The second form is likened to a guarantee (kafala) where the guarantor acts voluntarily.
4
Analyze the resolution on consent
According to al-Zayla'i and al-Inayah, consent is not a condition for the validity of hawala but is required for the original debtor to have recourse against the transferee or for his own debt to be discharged.
This aligns with the analogy to kafala: it is valid without the guaranteed person's consent.

This passage is a definition and ruling on the nature of hawala (transfer of debt) and the condition of the original debtor's consent. It begins by stating the definition: hawala is the transfer of a debt from one liability to another. The author then clarifies that the phrase 'by the debt' refers to the debt of the transferee against the transferor. Al-Zayla'i is quoted to support the validity without consent, noting that consent is only needed for recourse or discharge, analogous to kafala. The Original Debtor's Freedom in Hawala: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the extent of the muheel's freedom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the muheel's liability after hawala, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah) of their difference.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers the debt, but does it free the original debtor completely or only from being sued? The two disciples differ.

This lesson examines the effect of completion of hawala on the original debtor. While the Hanafi school holds that the original debtor is freed, there is an internal disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the extent of that freedom. Understanding this difference is crucial for determining the creditor's rights and the debtor's liability when problems arise.

Guided Path
1
State the basic ruling and its linguistic proof
The passage states that when hawala is completed with acceptance, the transferor (muheel) is freed from the debt. The Hanafi proof is based on the linguistic meaning of hawala: transfer, which necessitates removal of the debt from the original debtor's liability.
The proof uses the principle that shar'i rulings follow linguistic meanings.
2
Identify the opposing views from other scholars
Zufr and al-Qasim ibn Ma'in argue that hawala is only for securing the debt (like kafala), so it does not extinguish liability. Ibn Abi Layla goes to the other extreme, saying the debtor is freed even in kafala.
The Hanafi response distinguishes hawala (transfer) from kafala (joining).
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to an objection
The objection: hawala without the debtor's order seems to involve no transfer. The answer: transfer is realized upon payment; before payment, the debt remains but the transferor is not gratuitous because he faces recourse if the transferee defaults.
This preserves the meaning of transfer while explaining the non-gratuitous nature.
4

Commentary
This passage from the hanafi fiqh work 'عمدة الرعاية' explains the rulings of restricted hawala (transfer of debt tied to a specific asset). The author first addresses a preliminary issue: the validity of a judge's declaration of insolvency (taflis). According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (and Imam al-Shafi'i), such a declaration is legally effective because the creditor has clearly become unable to collect his debt, similar to the death of the debtor. Abu Hanifa, however, holds that a declaration of insolvency is not effective because it requires proof of a negative (that the debtor has no property), which is impossible to establish with certainty. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) according to commentators like Ibn Abidin is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa, though some affirm the view of the two companions in certain contexts like interdiction on a fool. The main subject is the types of restricted hawala (muqayyada), where the transfer is tied to a specific asset. The author lists three types: (1) with a deposit (wadi'a), (2) with usurped property (maghsubah), and (3) with a specific debt (dayn). For each, the ruling on discharge of the transferee upon destruction of the asset is given. For a restricted hawala on a deposit, if the deposited dirhams are destroyed in the hands of the transferee (who is the depositee), he is discharged from the hawala because he had undertaken to pay from those specific dirhams, and they were destroyed as a trust (amanah). The debt then reverts to the transferor (muhil). For usurped property, if the usurped dirhams are destroyed, the transferee (the ghāsib) is NOT discharged because the usurper is liable for the value of the property, which serves as a substitute. The hawala remains valid, and the creditor can claim the value. For a specific debt, the hawala is valid, and the transferor cannot demand the debt from the transferee after the hawala, because the creditor's right is attached to it. The commentator clarifies that in all three cases of restricted hawala, the transferor loses the right to demand the asset or debt from the transferee, since the creditor's right is tied to it. If the transferee nonetheless gives it to the transferor, he is liable to the creditor. This is unlike absolute hawala (mutlaqah), where the transferee's liability is not tied to a specific asset.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the transferee's status after the transferor's death and evaluate Zufar's qiyas argument explaining why it is rejected.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers the right to collect, not ownership; without possession, the transferee is an ordinary creditor, not a preferred one.

The Distinction Between Mutlaqa and Muqayyada Hawala: The Muḥīl's Right
Commentary on the distinction between unrestricted and restricted hawala regarding the muḥīl's right.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutlaqa and muqayyada hawala regarding the muḥīl's right to take the property, state the legal consequence for each, and explain the underlying reasoning based on consent.

Core Idea
In mutlaqa, the muḥīl may take the property without voiding the hawala; in muqayyada, doing so voids it and the debt returns.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary explains a crucial distinction in the contract of hawala (transfer of debt). The key question is whether the original creditor (muḥīl) can demand payment or take the specific property from the new debtor (muḥtāl ʿalayhi) without invalidating the transfer. The answer depends on whether the hawala was unrestricted (mutlaqa) or restricted (muqayyada). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rulings of hawala in practice.

Guided Path
1
Define mutlaqa and muqayyada hawala
Mutlaqa is an unrestricted transfer not tied to a specific property held by the transferee. Muqayyada is restricted to a specific property (e.g., deposit, debt) held by the transferee.
The key is whether the hawala is linked to a particular asset.
2
State the ruling for the muḥīl's right in mutlaqa
In mutlaqa, the muḥīl retains the right to demand the property from the muḥtāl ʿalayhi, and the hawala remains valid even if he takes it.
The muḥtāl ʿalayhi's consent is not violated because the transfer was not conditioned on being free from the muḥīl's claim.
3
State the ruling for muqayyada
In muqayyada, the muḥīl has no right to take the specified property. If he does, the hawala becomes void and the debt returns to the muḥīl.
The muḥtāl ʿalayhi only accepted the transfer to use that property, so taking it breaks the consent.
4
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The ruling is based on the principle that the muḥtāl ʿalayhi's consent is the foundation. In muqayyada, the consent is tied to the specific property; in mutlaqa, it is not.
This is a case of istihsan (juristic preference) as the analogy with a pledge would suggest invalidity, but custom and need justify the exception.
5
Apply the distinction to practical examples
Consider scenarios with deposits, debts, or usurped property. Identify whether the hawala is mutlaqa or muqayyada and determine the consequences of the muḥīl's action.
The passage gives examples: if the property perishes or is reclaimed, the hawala may be affected differently.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probe from the commentary on a text (likely al-Hidayah) regarding hawala (transfer of debt). It explains the ruling that in an unrestricted (mutlaqa) hawala, the original creditor (muḥīl) retains the right to demand payment from the transferee (muḥtāl ʿalayhi), and the hawala is not invalidated if the muḥīl takes the property (debt, deposit, or usurped item) from the muḥtāl ʿalayhi. The author then clarifies the reasoning: the muḥtāl ʿalayhi only accepted the transfer to either own the debt in his liability or to pay from that property, so if the muḥīl takes it, the consent for the transfer is lost, and the hawala would be void. However, unlike a pledge (rahn), where the pledgee has a right of execution, in hawala the muḥīl's taking is not allowed, but the text says it is not void in mutlaqa. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) considering the muḥtāl ʿalayhi's consent. The passage then distinguishes between mutlaqa and muqayyada (restricted) hawala in terms of the transferee's (muḥtāl) right to compete with other creditors. In mutlaqa, the muḥīl is discharged and the muḥtāl becomes a creditor of the muḥtāl ʿalayhi, so he does not compete with the muḥīl's creditors. In muqayyada, if the muḥīl takes the specified property, the hawala becomes void and the debt returns to the muḥīl, giving the muḥtāl a claim against the muḥīl. The commentary also provides examples: a deposit perishing or a debt being discharged. The final glosses clarify pronoun references and show the reasoning for the muḥtāl ʿalayhi's liability if he pays the muḥīl in a restricted hawala.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-ḥawālah): A contract transferring a debt from the debtor's liability to another person's liability, with the consent of all parties.
المطلقة (al-muṭlaqa): An unrestricted ḥawāla where the transfer is not tied to a specific property or debt held by the transferee.
المقيدة (al-muqayyada): A restricted ḥawāla where the transfer is conditioned upon a specific property (e.g., deposit, debt) held by the transferee.
المحيل (al-muḥīl): The original creditor who transfers the debt to another party.
المحتال عليه (al-muḥtāl ʿalayhi): The person upon whom the debt is transferred (the new debtor).

In mutlaqa hawala, the muḥīl retains the right to demand payment from the muḥtāl ʿalayhi without voiding the contract; in muqayyada, doing so voids the hawala and returns the debt to the muḥīl.
