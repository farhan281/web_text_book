Validity of Pledge and Surety for Land Tax (Kharaj)
Ruling with proof for pledge and surety on kharaj, with expansion on the distinction from zakat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that pledge and surety are valid for kharaj and explain why kharaj is classified as a debt despite its resemblance to zakat.

Core Idea
Kharaj is a debt, not a religious transfer; thus pledge and surety are valid.

This passage from the Hanafi hashiyah literature addresses the ruling on pledge (rahn) and surety (kafalah) for land tax (kharaj). The base text states these are valid, but the commentator anticipates a doubt: since kharaj is a religious obligation like zakat, why can it be secured? The response clarifies the legal nature of kharaj as a debt that can be demanded, distinct from zakat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the ruling: pledge and surety are valid for kharaj.
2
Understand the legal nature of kharaj
Kharaj is a debt (dayn) that can be demanded.
3
Recognize the potential objection
Some may analogize kharaj to zakat, where pledge and surety are not valid.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The commentator distinguishes kharaj as a financial obligation (not a transfer of ownership) and cites the Prophet's hadith to support the concept of repaying debts.
5
Apply the distinction
Understand why kharaj differs from zakat in this context.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof, presented in the form of a hashiyah commentary on a base text. The ruling states that pledge (rahn) and surety (kafalah) are valid for land tax (kharaj). The primary reason given is that kharaj is a debt (dayn) that can be demanded (mutalab). The commentator then addresses a potential objection: since kharaj is similar to zakat in being a religious obligation, one might think that pledge and surety are not valid for it, just as they are not valid for zakat. The response is that kharaj is unlike zakat because zakat is not truly a debt but a transfer of ownership (tamlik al-mal mubtada), whereas kharaj is a financial obligation that can be demanded from the estate after death. A proof is cited involving the Prophet and one of the Ghifari companions, though the connection is not clarified in the text. The hadith likely demonstrates the permissibility of demanding repayment or guaranteeing debts. The commentator also explains the linguistic derivation of 'tuhmah' (accusation) and notes that the ruling applies specifically to the levied tax (al-muwazzaf) because it is a valid debt possible to collect. The passage also contrasts with claims of wealth where imprisonment is not imposed without complete proof, indicating a difference in evidentiary standards.

Key Terms
الخراج (al-kharāj): Land tax levied on agricultural land, considered a debt in this context.
الرهن (al-rahn): A pledge given as security for a debt.
الكفالة (al-kafālah): A contract of surety where one person guarantees the obligation of another.
التهمة (al-tuhmah): Accusation; here referring to the reason for imprisonment pending proof.
