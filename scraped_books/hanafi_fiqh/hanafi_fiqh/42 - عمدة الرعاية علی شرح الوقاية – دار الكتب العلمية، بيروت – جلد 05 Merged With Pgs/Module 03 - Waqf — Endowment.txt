Selling Private Property Mixed with Waqf
Validity of selling private property mixed with waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on selling private property mixed with waqf, explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, and apply the analogy to a mixed-sale scenario.

Core Idea
Private property mixed with waqf can be sold separately — the waqf remains intact.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: you own a house built on waqf land, or a village that includes a mosque (waqf). Can you sell the private portion without selling the waqf? The Hanafi school has two narrations, but the relied-upon position makes the private sale valid, provided the waqf is properly excluded. Understanding this ruling prevents invalid transactions and preserves the waqf's perpetuity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Issue
The issue is whether mixing private property with waqf automatically voids the sale of the private part. Determine the legal problem: waqf is inalienable, but the private share still belongs to its owner.
2
State the Two Intra-Hanafi Narrations
One narration (from Nawazil) says the private sale is fasid because the waqf cannot be sold, like selling a free man and a slave together. The other (sahih) says it is valid.
Know the basis of each view.
3
Understand the Qiyas Used
The opposing view uses qiyas with a free man+slave combination. The majority uses qiyas with the mudabbar case (where the private part of a mudabbar can be sold). Identify which analogy is stronger according to the later authorities.
4
Apply the Mosque Exception
If the waqf is a mosque, the sale of the entire village is invalid unless the mosque is excluded, either explicitly or by mental exception (istithna' ma'nawi). This is because a mosque has a higher sanctity.
The exception comes from al-Fara'id and al-Muhit.
5
Recognize the Mufta Bihi
The sahih (valid) view is the operative position for fatwa. The sale of the private portion is valid when the waqf is excluded.
Al-Ayni and the author of al-Muhit support this.

Like property added to waqf as correct. [Commentary:] As the sale of property added to waqf is correct according to its origin, which is the confinement of the asset in the ownership of the waqf founder. Then it is permissible to sell the added property for its share. And it is said: it is not correct. It is transmitted in Majma' al-Anhar from al-Fara'id: 'This is for other than a mosque; as for a mosque, it is not correct in the added property, so a village is not sold unless the mosques and graveyards are excluded, and there is discussion.'

Commentary
This passage deals with a mixed sale where private property is combined with a waqf (endowment) property. The ruling states that the sale of the private (milk) portion is valid for its share of the price, analogously to the earlier case of a slave and mudabbar. There is a difference of narration: some say the private part's sale is fasid because the waqf part cannot be sold, but the established view (al-sahih) is that it is valid because waqf property is still considered wealth (mal) in a sense. The passage also notes a special rule for mosques: if a village includes a mosque, the sale of the whole village is not valid unless the mosque is excluded, either explicitly or by mental exception, as in al-Muhit. Al-Ayni mentions two narrations; the preferred one upholds the validity of the private part's sale.

Key Terms
وقف (waqf): An endowment; property that is inalienable and its usufruct is for charitable purposes.
ملك (milk): Private ownership, full property rights.
استثناء معنوي (istithna' ma'nawi): Mental exception; excluding something from a sale by intention without verbal specification.

Key Takeaway
Private property mixed with waqf can be sold for its share, provided the waqf portion is excluded; otherwise the sale is invalid.
