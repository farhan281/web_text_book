Imprisonment and Surety in Qadhf and Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on imprisonment vs surety for suspicion in hudud and qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on imprisonment for suspicion in hudud and qisas, and differentiate Abu Hanifa's position from the Sahibayn's alternative narrations.

Core Idea
Suspicion alone justifies imprisonment without surety in qadhf and qisas, even without full proof.

In cases of qadhf (false accusation of adultery) and qisas (retaliation), the Hanafi school allows the judge to take preventive measures based on suspicion. This lesson examines the ruling when only two masked witnesses (masturan) or a single just witness (adl) testify—sufficient for suspicion but not for a final judgment. The passage also reveals an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedural context
Understand that the discussion begins after a claim is made: the claimant can keep the defendant present until the judge adjourns. If the claimant brings two masked witnesses or one just witness, the judge must decide whether to imprison or demand surety.
The key distinction is between evidence for suspicion (tuhma) and evidence for judgment (hujja tamma).
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa rules that the defendant is imprisoned without being compelled to provide a surety. Imprisonment here is a disciplinary punishment (ta'zir) based on suspicion.
The testimony of masked witnesses, though insufficient for final judgment, establishes suspicion.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's two narrations
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad have two reported views: (a) imprison without surety (agreeing with Abu Hanifa), and (b) take surety without imprisonment, because the crime is not established by complete proof.
The divergence stems from weighing the lack of full proof against the need for preventive action.
4
Analyze the legal basis for imprisonment
The passage cites a hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) imprisoned one suspect on suspicion of theft, establishing that imprisonment for suspicion (tuhma al-fasad) is legislated. The commentator notes that testimony of a single just witness or two masked witnesses suffices for suspicion, just like a single reporter's report in religious matters.
Imprisonment is not for precaution (ihtiyat) but for suspicion; hence no surety is needed.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's: imprisonment without surety. The text implies this through the commentator's phrasing and the hadith support.
Al-Tumurtashi extended this ruling to theft, treating it as a right of the servant because a claim is required.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute explaining the ruling on imprisonment and surety in cases of qadhf (false accusation of adultery) and qisas (retaliation for bodily harm). The core ruling is that the defendant is not imprisoned until two masked witnesses (مستوران, whose character is not fully known but outwardly acceptable) or one just witness (عدل) testify. The commentator first clarifies the procedure at Abu Hanifa: the claimant can require the defendant to stay until the judge adjourns; if the claimant brings clear evidence, the judge proceeds. If he brings two masked witnesses or one just witness, Abu Hanifa holds that the defendant is not compelled to provide a surety (كفالة) but is imprisoned. This differs from other hudud punishments where such testimony would not suffice. Al-Tumurtashi analogized theft to qadhf and qisas by treating it as a right of the servant (حق العبد) since a claim is required. The commentator then explains that a 'just witness' is one known to the judge as such, so he does not need explicit ta'dil. If the witness is unknown, the defendant is not imprisoned due to lack of number and justice. Imprisonment here is for suspicion (تهمة), not precaution (احتياط). The testimony of masked witnesses, though insufficient for a final judgment, is sufficient to establish suspicion, just like a single reporter's report in religious matters (ديانات). The passage then notes an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani have two narrations. In one, they agree with Abu Hanifa: imprison without surety. In the other, they say take surety without imprisonment, because the crime (qadhf or qisas) is not established with complete proof. The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's. Finally, the commentator provides a hadith from Abd al-Razzaq's Musannaf: the Prophet (peace be upon him) imprisoned one of two suspects from Banu Ghifar on suspicion of camel theft, supporting the legality of imprisonment for suspicion. The passage concludes that the imprisonment is for suspicion until the truth becomes clear; if nothing is brought, the defendant is released.

Key Terms
مستور (mastur): A witness whose external justice is apparent but whose inner state is not fully known; acceptable for establishing suspicion but not for final judgment.
عدل (adl): A just witness known to the judge; his testimony can establish suspicion alone.
التهمة (al-tuhma): Suspicion of crime or corruption; basis for imprisonment as disciplinary punishment.

Exceptions to Rejection of Testimony: Flogged Apostate and Worldly Enemy
Explains exceptions to rejection of testimony with Qur'anic evidence and case comparisons.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the cases where testimony is accepted despite prior rejection and apply the ruling to the dhimmi convert and the worldly enemy.

Core Idea
Testimony rejected due to sin can be acquired anew after Islam, but worldly enmity permanently prevents acceptance.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains two exceptions to the general rule that certain individuals' testimony is rejected. The first is a dhimmi who was flogged for qadhf and then accepts Islam—his testimony becomes acceptable because it is a new testimony gained after conversion. The second is a person who is an enemy due to worldly reasons—his testimony is rejected because worldly enmity is forbidden and casts doubt on his integrity. We will examine the reasoning behind these rulings and how they differ from similar cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two exceptions
Locate the two categories mentioned: (1) a person flogged while in kufr who later embraces Islam, and (2) an enemy due to worldly reasons. Note the contrasting outcomes.
The first exception applies only to a dhimmi convert, not to a Muslim who was flogged for qadhf.
2
Understand the Qur'anic dalil
The passage cites Surah al-Ma'idah 5:34 (verse of muharibun) to show that repentance before capture drops the hadd, illustrating the principle that repentance can remove the effect of a prior sin on testimony.
The dalil is used to justify the exception for the dhimmi convert, drawing an analogy to the muharibun.
3
Compare with the freed slave case
Contrast the dhimmi convert with a slave who is flogged and then freed. The freed slave's testimony is not accepted because at the time of flogging he had no capacity to testify, so rejection was established after emancipation.
This comparison shows that the timing of acquiring testimony capacity matters.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on worldly enemy
Note that there is disagreement: some narrations from Abu Hanifa accept the testimony of a worldly enemy if he is 'adil, while later scholars reject it. The mufta bihi is rejection.
Understand that the dominant later position is based on the principle that worldly enmity leads to untrustworthiness.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of al-Wiqayah, explaining exceptions to the rule that testimony is rejected for certain individuals. The text first discusses the exception of a person who was flogged (hadd) while in a state of disbelief (kufr) and later embraced Islam. The commentary from 'Tahrir al-Kamal' and 'al-Talwih' clarifies that the exception in the Qur'anic verse regarding those who accuse (qadhf) refers to them being sinful except those who repent. Then it addresses the exception in the verse of muharibun (those who wage war), explaining that the exception returns to the entire category because the verse says 'before you have power over them' – if the exception only applied to the 'great punishment', it would have no benefit since repentance removes that punishment absolutely; thus the benefit is the dropping of the hadd (corporal punishment). The text then quotes 'al-Bahr' from 'al-Bada'i' that every sinner's testimony is accepted after repentance except two: one flogged for qadhf and one known for lying. The reason is that a known liar's truthfulness cannot be assured even after repentance. Then the commentary details the case: if a dhimmi is flogged for qadhf, his testimony against fellow dhimmis is not accepted. But if he later becomes Muslim, his testimony becomes acceptable against both dhimmis and Muslims, because this is a new testimony acquired after Islam, not the same one that was rejected. This is contrasted with a slave who is flogged and then freed: his testimony is not accepted because at the time of flogging he had no testimony (slaves cannot testify), so the rejection was delayed until after emancipation, and then acceptance is not possible. Finally, the text discusses 'an enemy due to worldly reasons' – such a person's testimony is not accepted because worldly enmity is forbidden and leads to untrustworthiness, whereas enmity for religious reasons indicates piety and justice.

Key Terms
محدود في قذف (maḥdūd fī qadhf): A person who has been flogged (hadd) for the crime of false accusation of adultery (qadhf).
عدو بسبب الدنيا (adū bi-sabab al-dunyā): An enemy due to worldly reasons, such as disputes over property or personal grudges, as opposed to enmity for religious reasons.
