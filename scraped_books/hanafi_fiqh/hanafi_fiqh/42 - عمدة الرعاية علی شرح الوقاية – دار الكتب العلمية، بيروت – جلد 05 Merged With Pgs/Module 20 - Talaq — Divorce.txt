False Testimony in Triple Divorce: Hanafi Imams' Positions
Intra-Hanafi dispute on false testimony in triple divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the effect of false testimony in triple divorce claims and identify the thamarah al-khilaf for each.

Core Idea
A judgment based on false testimony is externally effective but internal permissibility depends on actual reality.

In Hanafi law, a judge's ruling is binding on the public, but what happens when the ruling is based on false testimony? This passage presents a nuanced dispute among the Hanafi imams regarding a woman who falsely testifies to triple divorce. The ruling affects the permissibility of intercourse and remarriage, depending on whose fatwa is followed.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi positions
Identify the views of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf regarding the permissibility of intercourse for the first husband after a false testimony judgment of triple divorce.
Abu Hanifa prohibits; Muhammad permits until second consummation; Abu Yusuf permits secretly.
2
Identify the disagreement
What exactly do they differ on? The fundamental question is whether the judgment creates a real separation (Abu Hanifa) or merely an apparent one (Muhammad, Abu Yusuf).
3
Analyze the underlying principle
Why does Abu Hanifa say the judgment is effective? Because a claim with a specific cause (sabab mu'ayyan) like marriage or divorce can be imagined to have actually occurred; the judgment merely confirms it.
For claims without a specific cause (e.g., gift), it is different; there are two narrations.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
What are the practical consequences for the woman and the husbands? Under Abu Hanifa, the woman can remarry immediately and the first husband is prohibited. Under Muhammad, she must wait for consummation by a second husband. Under Abu Yusuf, the first husband can still have intercourse secretly.
This affects the legality of subsequent marriages and intercourse.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Which view is mufta bihi? The text does not specify a preferred view; later practice often follows Abu Hanifa, but all opinions are considered valid.
Shaykh al-Islam and others mention these positions without tarjih.

Commentary
This passage deals with specific cases where a claim is made by a specific cause (سبب معين) such as marriage, divorce, sale, etc. The key point is that for contracts like marriage and sale, a judgment based on false testimony can be effective externally and internally according to Abu Hanifa because the cause (the contract) can be imagined to have occurred prior to the judgment. For example, in marriage, it is possible that the marriage actually took place earlier, and the false testimony merely proves it. However, for contracts like gift or charity, there are two narrations. The text then discusses a specific scenario: a woman claims her husband divorced her three times and brings false witnesses. The judge rules for separation. According to Abu Hanifa, the husband is prohibited from intercourse with her both externally and internally, so she can marry another man, and that marriage is valid. According to Muhammad, the first husband can still have intercourse until the second husband consummates; after that, the first is prohibited, and the second is also prohibited if he knows the truth. Abu Yusuf later opined that the first husband can have intercourse secretly. This highlights the nuanced differences within the Hanafi school regarding the effect of false testimony on marital status.

الطلاق الثلاث (al-ṭalāq al-thalāth): Three divorces in a single utterance or three separate divorces that make the woman irrevocably divorced

Retraction of Testimony on Divorce Before Consummation
Ruling on witnesses who retract after testifying to a divorce before consummation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the liability of retracting witnesses in a divorce before consummation case and apply the reasoning to determine what they guarantee.

Core Idea
Witnesses guarantee half the dower if specified, or mut'ah if not, because they confirmed a debt about to fall.

This passage examines a nuanced ruling: when witnesses testify to a divorce before consummation and then retract, they become liable for a portion of the dower. The reasoning hinges on the fact that the dower was not yet definitively owed due to possible annulments. Understanding this rule illustrates how Hanafi fiqh assigns responsibility for the consequences of testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What legal act is being ruled upon? Here, it is the retraction of witnesses after testifying to a divorce before consummation. The ruling is that they guarantee half the dower or mut'ah.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a talil: 'because they confirmed against him something that was on the verge of falling.' Identify this phrase in the Arabic text.
The reasoning is not a direct text from Quran or Hadith but a legal causation (talil).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Explain why the dower was 'on the verge of falling' – because before consummation, events like apostasy or a stepson's kiss can annul the marriage and drop the dower. The witnesses' testimony removed that possibility, fixing the dower.
This establishes causation (tasabbub) for liability.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Although the text does not detail other schools' positions, it acknowledges the ruling is mukhtalaf fih. Recognize that other madhahib may have different views on witness liability in this scenario.
The Hanafi position is the one presented here as mufta bihi.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that witnesses guarantee half the specified dower or mut'ah if no dower was specified. This is supported by Al-Munah and Al-Bahr citing Al-Muhit.
The tarjih authority is clear from the sources cited.

Commentary
This passage explains a separate ruling: when witnesses testify that a husband divorced his wife before consummation, and then retract, they are liable for half the specified dower. The reasoning is that at the time of their testimony, the dower was not yet definitively owed; divorce before consummation normally entitles the wife to half the dower, but there are causes (like apostasy of the wife, or the husband's stepson kissing her) that could annul the marriage and thus nullify the dower. By testifying, the witnesses effectively confirmed the divorce and thus obligated the half dower when it might have otherwise fallen. If no dower was specified, they guarantee the mut'ah (a consolatory gift) because that becomes obligatory. The passage also discusses a case where witnesses testify to a compromise on mut'ah but retract; they don't guarantee the specific object of the compromise but the actual value of mut'ah.

Key Terms
المهر (al-mahr): The dower paid by the husband to the wife upon marriage; here, half is owed upon divorce before consummation.
المتعة (al-mut‘ah): A consolatory gift given to the wife upon divorce before consummation if no dower was specified or the dower was not stipulated.

Retraction Testimony After Consummation: No Guarantee
Distinction that retraction after consummation does not incur guarantee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that retraction of testimony after consummation does not incur guarantee, and explain the rational basis for the distinction between pre- and post-consummation cases.

Core Idea
After consummation, the mahr is fixed by the act itself, not by testimony; retraction creates no liability.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: witnesses who testified to a divorce after consummation and later retract. The Hanafi school holds they are not liable for the mahr. Why? Because consummation itself perfects the mahr obligation. The testimony merely confirmed what already existed. This distinction highlights a key principle: liability arises only when the testimony creates a new obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Witnesses testify that a husband divorced his wife after consummation. The judge rules accordingly. Then the witnesses retract. The Hanafi ruling: no guarantee of the mahr.
Focus on the timing: after consummation.
2
Locate the ratio legis
The mahr is 'perfected' (ta'akkada) by consummation itself, not by the testimony. This is the key principle.
Consummation is the cause of the mahr being fully due.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds the witnesses are liable because their testimony led to the judgment of divorce and the resulting payment of mahr.
The disagreement is about causation: testimony vs. consummation.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The testimony only confirmed an existing obligation; it did not create a new one. Therefore, retraction does not cause a loss that needs compensation.
This is a rational (aqli) argument based on the nature of obligations.
5
Derive the general principle
An act that merely confirms an already fixed obligation does not create liability for guarantee upon retraction.
This principle applies beyond divorce testimony, e.g., in cases of manumission and qisas as mentioned in the text.

Commentary

Therefore, the testimony did not cause any new obligation; it merely confirmed what already existed. Thus, retraction does not cause a loss. The text notes that Imam al-Shafi'i disagreed, but the Hanafi position is as stated. This contrasts with the case before consummation where the mahr could have been dropped entirely.
