Inheritance Dispute Over Conversion Timing
Ruling on credibility in inheritance conversion disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on burden of proof when a spouse claims conversion at a different time and explain the role of istishab al-hal.

Core Idea
Heirs are believed because istishab presumes continuity; wife's claim is a new fact she must prove.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains a key procedural rule in inheritance law: when the timing of a wife's conversion is disputed, the heirs' claim is given credence. The ruling relies on the Hanafi principle that istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is a defensive proof, not sufficient to establish a new right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize the symmetrical cases: Christian man dying with Muslim wife claiming conversion after death, and Muslim man dying with Christian wife claiming conversion before death.
2
Determine the ruling in each
In both cases, the heirs are believed over the wife. The wife's claim is not accepted without clear evidence.
3
Understand the proof: istishab al-hal
Istishab presumes that a state existing at one time continues. Here, the deceased's religion at death is presumed to be his known religion. The wife claims a change, so she must prove it.
4
Distinguish the two types of istishab
One type infers present from past (e.g., missing person presumed alive); the other infers past from present (e.g., mill water). But Hanafis treat istishab as a defensive (dafi') proof, not an establishing (muthbit) one.
5
Apply the principle to the case
The wife is seeking to establish a right (inheritance based on conversion); she cannot rely on istishab. The heirs are defending the status quo; they can use istishab. Hence they are believed.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof (istishab al-hal) concerning inheritance disputes about the timing of conversion. Two symmetrical cases are presented: (1) A Christian man dies; his Muslim wife claims she converted after his death—if so, she inherits? Actually, a Muslim can inherit from a Christian if conversion is after death? No, the text does not specify inheritance; it's about the credibility of her claim. The ruling: the heirs (the deceased's relatives) are believed over the wife. (2) A Muslim man dies; his Christian wife claims she converted before his death—again, the heirs are believed. The reasoning: The wife is claiming a right (e.g., inheritance) based on a new fact. The principle of istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is used: the deceased is presumed to have died in his religion; any change requires proof. The wife's current state (Muslim or Christian) is not sufficient to prove her state at the time of death. The commentator distinguishes two types of istishab: one that derives past from present (e.g., missing person presumed alive) and one that derives present from past (e.g., mill water assumed to have been flowing). However, in Hanafi usul, istishab is a defensive proof (hujjah dafi'ah) not an establishing proof (muthbitah); it can protect an existing right but not create a new one. Here, the wife is seeking to establish a new right (inheritance based on conversion), so she cannot rely on istishab. The heirs, on the other hand, are defending the status quo (the deceased died as a non-Muslim or Muslim as the case may be), so they can use istishab to support their position. The 'zahir al-huduth' (appearance of newness) also works against the wife: her current religion might be newly adopted, not proven continuous.

Key Terms
استصحاب الحال (istishāb al-ḥāl): Presumption of continuity: the legal principle that a state (e.g., life, religion) existing at one time is presumed to continue until evidence of change.
ظاهر (ẓāhir): Apparent evidence or outward circumstance; here, the wife's current religion is a ẓāhir that suggests conversion, but it is not sufficient to establish her claim.

Inheritance Claim with an Absent Brother: Abu Hanifa's Ruling
Specific case: judge awards half to claimant, leaves other half with possessor without kafil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on the distribution of inherited property when one heir is absent and identify the reasoning for not requiring a surety from the possessor.

Core Idea
The judge awards half to the present heir and leaves the other half with the possessor without a surety.

In this lesson, we examine a specific case from the Hanafi tradition: Zayd claims a property as inheritance for himself and his absent brother, and proves his claim. According to Abu Hanifa, the judge grants Zayd half the property and leaves the remaining half with the current possessor, without taking any surety (kafil). This ruling is based on the principle that the judge does not interfere with possession when the opposing party is absent and the possessor's hand is respected.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the claim
Understand who is involved: Zayd (present heir), his absent brother, and the possessor (dhu al-yad). The claim: the property is inheritance from their father.
2
Determine the judge's ruling
The judge awards half the property to Zayd and leaves the other half with the possessor. No surety is taken from the possessor.
This is Abu Hanifa's position; note the intra-Hanafi difference.
3
Analyze the reasoning for no surety
The possessor's possession is respected because the deceased may have chosen him. The present heir is not a representative of the absent. The judge does not interfere without the opponent present.
This reasoning is analogous to a case where the judge knows property belongs to someone but sees it in another's hand; he does not take it.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
While Abu Hanifa's ruling is presented, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) may require a surety. This distinction is important for fatwa.
The text suggests that the ruling is specific to Abu Hanifa.

[1] His saying: 'And a property...etc.'; meaning: if a man claims a property against another as inheritance for himself and his absent brother, and presents evidence that his father died and left that property as inheritance between him and his brother so-and-so the absent, the judge awards him half of that property and leaves the rest, i.e., the other half, in the hand of the one who had it, without taking a surety from the possessor, whether the possessor denies this claim or not.

The Requirement of Jar in Inheritance Testimony
Intra-Hanafi dispute on jar requirement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi requirement of jar in inheritance testimony and distinguish the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa/Muhammad from Abu Yusuf, applying the practical consequence.

Core Idea
Inheritance testimony requires explicit linking to death—jar—because the heir's ownership is renewed, not a mere substitution.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains a key requirement in Hanafi testimony law: when a claimant seeks to prove inheritance, witnesses must explicitly connect the property to the deceased's death (jar). An intra-madhhab dispute arises with Abu Yusuf, who considers inheritance as substitution (khilafa) thus eliminating the need for jar. We will examine the proofs and practical difference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Imam/Muhammad)
Identify the requirement of jar: witnesses must state that the deceased died and left the property as inheritance for the claimant, or establish the deceased's possession at death.
2
Identify the opponent and his view
Abu Yusuf holds that testimony of ownership for the deceased suffices for the heir because inheritance is substitution (khilafa).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Abu Yusuf uses the principle of khilafa: the heir stands in the deceased's place, so rights and obligations transfer automatically.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Imam and Muhammad argue that the heir's ownership is renewed after death, citing istibra' for an inherited slave-girl and the change in charity eligibility as evidence.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Imam/Muhammad, if witnesses only say 'this belonged to the deceased,' the testimony is rejected. Under Abu Yusuf, it is accepted. The mufta bihi is the former.

The witness must connect (jar) the inheritance by saying: 'He died and left it as inheritance for him,' or 'He died and it was in his possession,' or 'in his hand.' That is, if the witnesses say, 'This deceased (property) belonged to this claimant,' the heir will not be judged until they connect the inheritance to the claimant by saying, 'He died and left it as inheritance for him,' etc., contrary to Abu Yusuf, may Allah have mercy on him, for he does not require connection (jar). But testimony on the existence of the deceased's ownership at the time of death is sufficient because the transfer is then established by necessity. Similarly, testimony on the existence of his possession; because hands at the time of death turn into ownership hands by guarantee, as the apparent state of a Muslim at that time is to settle his affairs and clarify what was deposits and usurpations; if he does not clarify, the apparent state is that what is in his hand is his property, so the hand is made...

Commentary
This passage discusses the requirement of 'jar' (connecting or linking) in testimony regarding inheritance. When a claimant (heir) claims property in someone's possession as inheritance, witnesses must explicitly connect the property to the deceased's death. They can do this either by stating directly: 'He died and left it as inheritance for the claimant,' or by stating a legal equivalent: 'He died and it was his property,' or 'in his hand/possession.' If witnesses merely say 'It belonged to his father,' the testimony is insufficient because there is no explicit linking of the death to the transfer. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that since inheritance is substitution, the heir stands in the deceased's place, so testimony of ownership for the deceased suffices for the heir. The response of the two (Imam and Muhammad) is that the heir's ownership is renewed after death; they give examples such as the requirement of istibra' for a inherited slave-girl (to ensure she is not pregnant from the deceased) and the ruling that a wealthy heir may benefit from charity that was given to a poor deceased. These show that the heir's ownership is distinct and new, thus requiring explicit transfer. However, they do accept testimony that the deceased owned the property at the time of death, because transfer at death is a necessary consequence. Similarly, testimony that the deceased had possession (yad) at death is sufficient because at death, a Muslim's possession is presumed to be ownership unless evidence to the contrary. The text ends abruptly, but the method is clear.

Key Terms
الجر (al-jarr): The requirement that witnesses explicitly link the property to the inheritance by stating that the deceased died and left it as inheritance for the claimant.
الاستبراء (al-istibra'): The waiting period to ensure a slave-girl is not pregnant before the new owner can have intercourse with her. Required from the heir for an inherited slave-girl.
الخلافة (al-khilafa): Substitution or succession; Abu Yusuf's argument that the heir takes the place of the deceased in ownership.
