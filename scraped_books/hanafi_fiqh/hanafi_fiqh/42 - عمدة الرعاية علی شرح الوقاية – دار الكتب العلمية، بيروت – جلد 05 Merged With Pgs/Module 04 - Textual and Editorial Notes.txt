The Six Contested Narrations: Abu Yusuf's Objections
List of six contested narrations with Abu Yusuf's objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six disputed narrations and explain the impact of each dispute on the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf's denials show that not all transmissions from Abu Hanifa are equally reliable; the school often preferred Muhammad's version.

This passage from ‘Umdat al-Ri‘āya presents six specific fiqh issues where Abu Yūsuf explicitly denied having narrated a ruling from Abū Ḥanīfa as reported by Muhammad al-Shaybānī. Understanding these cases is crucial for grasping how the Hanafī school verifies its transmissions and why later authorities sometimes opted for one narration over another. Each issue illustrates the internal process of riwāyah verification within the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context of the list
Recognize that these six issues are canonical examples of contested riwāyah within the Hanafī school. They are often cited in works like al-Bahr al-Rā’iq and al-‘Ināyah.
The list is attributed to Ibn Nujaym quoting al-Sirāj al-Hindī.
2
Review each contested issue
Go through each of the six cases: (1) supererogatory prayer with omitted recitation, (2) timing for a mustahāḍah’s wuḍū’, (3) emancipation by a buyer from a usurper, (4) marriage of a migrant woman, (5) pardon by a co-owner of a slave who killed their master, (6) a slave claiming emancipation with a debt. Note what Abū Yūsuf denied and what the later preferred position is.
The passage indicates that for most issues, the school (mashāyikh) relied on Muḥammad’s narration.
3
Analyze the tarjīḥ (preference) mechanism
Understand that the later Hanafī jurists (mashāyikh) did not automatically accept Abū Yūsuf’s denial; they weighed the transmissions. In the first issue, they adopted Muḥammad’s narration (four rak‘ahs). In others, the preferred ruling may differ.
For example, in the mustahāḍah case, the later school generally follows Abū Yūsuf’s version (until entry of ẓuhr), but the passage states 'our scholars adopted Muḥammad's narration' for the first issue.
4
Connect to broader uṣūl al-fiqh principles
Relate these disputes to the Hanafī principle of tarjīḥ based on riwāyah strength and the role of the ṣāḥibān (Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad) as primary transmitters. These cases also highlight that ẓāhir al-riwāyah is not monolithic.
The existence of such disputes underscores the importance of isnād verification.

Commentary
This passage lists six specific fiqh issues where Abu Yusuf disputed the narration attributed to Abu Hanifa by Imam Muhammad. These are well-known cases in Hanafi literature where Abu Yusuf claimed that he had not narrated that particular position from Abu Hanifa, and that the correct transmission differed. The issues cover: (1) repetition of supererogatory prayer due to missed recitation; (2) the time limit for a mustahadah's prayer after wudu; (3) emancipation by a buyer from a usurper after owner's validation; (4) marriage of a migrating woman and waiting period; (5) pardon by one co-owner of a slave when the slave kills their master; (6) a slave claiming emancipation and a creditor's claim. The passage notes that the later preferred position in the school (mufta bihi) may differ depending on the issue. It also mentions that some later scholars, like Ibn Nujaym, recorded these six issues but did not include the main building issue discussed in the surrounding text.

Principle of Ijtihad: Every Mujtahid is Correct
Explains the principle and addresses error in ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the ruling of a mujtahid who errs and distinguish between blameworthy and blameless error.

Core Idea
A mujtahid who errs is rewarded unless negligent in the face of clear evidence.

The statement 'every mujtahid is correct' is a foundational principle in usul al-fiqh. This lesson unpacks its meaning, addresses the case of Ibn Abi Layla, and clarifies when an erring mujtahid is excused versus punished. Understanding this principle prevents dismissing valid scholarly differences and highlights the importance of sincere effort.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Statement and Its Definition
Read the opening line 'كل مجتهد مصيب' and its gloss: every mujtahid hits the rule of Allah through ijtihad. Note that this is about the effort, not infallibility.
The term 'musib' here means aiming correctly in terms of reward, not necessarily hitting the actual divine ruling.
2
Analyze the Response to Ibn Abi Layla
The passage addresses an objection: how could Imam Abu Hanifa criticize Ibn Abi Layla if every mujtahid is correct? The answer quotes al-Taftazani: the erring mujtahid is excused and rewarded unless he was negligent.
This shows that even great Imams can be subject to criticism if their ijtihad is flawed by negligence.
3
Distinguish Excusable vs. Punishable Error
If the evidence was hidden (khafiy) and the mujtahid exerted full effort, he is rewarded. If the evidence was clear (bayyin) and he erred due to negligence, he is punished.
The key factor is clarity of evidence and effort exerted.
4
Understand Context of Early Scholarly Criticism
Criticisms among early scholars (like Ibn Abi Layla) were based on the critic's assumption that the correct path was obvious. This does not negate the principle.
Such criticisms are about the specific case, not the general rule.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary clarifying the principle 'every mujtahid is correct.' It first defines the statement, indicating that a mujtahid aims to reach Allah's ruling through ijtihad. Then it addresses the case of Ibn Abi Layla, who was a mujtahid but criticized. The commentator explains, quoting al-Taftazani, that a mujtahid who errs is not punished nor considered misguided, provided he exerted full effort; he is excused and rewarded. However, if the correct evidence was clear and he erred due to negligence or lack of thoroughness, he is punishable. Criticisms among early scholars were based on the critic's belief that the right path was obvious. This is a usul al-fiqh principle about the validity of ijtihad.

Key Terms
مجتهد (mujtahid): A scholar who performs ijtihad to derive legal rulings from evidence.
مصيب (musib): One who hits the target, i.e., arrives at the correct ruling in the eyes of Allah.
اجتهاد (ijtihad): The utmost effort in deriving legal rulings from their detailed evidence.
