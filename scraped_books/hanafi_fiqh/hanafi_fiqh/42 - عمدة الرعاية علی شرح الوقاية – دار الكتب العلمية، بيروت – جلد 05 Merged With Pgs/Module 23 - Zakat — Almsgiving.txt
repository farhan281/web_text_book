Scope of 'My Wealth is Charity' vs. Bequest
A ruling-with-proof passage explaining the scope of charity vows versus bequests in Hanafi fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the phrase 'my wealth is charity', explain its limitation to zakatable assets, and distinguish it from a bequest.

Core Idea
A vow of charity only applies to zakatable wealth, not all property, because it is tied to Allah's obligation.

In Hanafi fiqh, a bequest (waṣiyyah) of one-third of one's wealth applies to all types of property. But when someone vows 'my wealth is charity', the scope is far narrower. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind that limitation, the proofs from the Quran, and the disagreement with Zufar. By understanding the distinction, you will avoid applying the wrong rule to charity vows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two legal acts
Read the first sentence: bequest vs. charity vow. Notice that bequest covers 'everything', while charity vow is restricted. Ask: why the difference?
The bequest is like inheritance; charity vow is like zakat.
2
Note the Hanafi position on charity vow
The text says 'my wealth is charity' applies only to zakatable wealth (gold, silver, trade goods, grazing livestock). This is the mufta bihi view.
The proof: Quranic verses (70:24, 9:103) use 'amwal' for a specific portion (zakatable surplus).
3
Analyse the proof and the response to Zufar
Hanafi proof: The servant's obligation is tied to Allah's obligation – charity vows mirror zakat obligations. Zufar argued for generality like bequest. The Hanafi response: bequest is different because it is tied to inheritance, which covers all property. Also, istihsan overrides qiyas to prevent hardship.
Istihsan: customary understanding that the vower intends surplus, not necessities.
4
Understand the detailed inclusions and exceptions
The text then lists specific items: 'ushri land is included per Abu Yusuf but not according to Muhammad. Kharaji land, slaves, real estate, furniture, ordinary clothes, weapons are excluded. The vower may keep his sustenance if he has only included assets.
The ruling on 'ushri land is a sub-disagreement between the two Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof discussion on the scope of bequests and charity vows in Hanafi fiqh. The author distinguishes between a bequest (وصية) and a vow of charity (صدقة). For a bequest of one-third of one's wealth, it applies to all types of property, whether zakatable or not, because a bequest is akin to inheritance which encompasses all property. However, when someone says 'my wealth is charity' or 'what I own is charity', it is restricted to zakatable assets (like gold, silver, trade goods, grazing livestock) according to the Hanafi school. The proof is that the servant's obligation is tied to Allah's obligation; the Quranic verses about charity (e.g., 'In their wealth is a known right') show that the term 'wealth' in charity contexts is understood to mean surplus wealth beyond basic needs, which is exactly zakatable wealth. Zufar opposed this, arguing that the general wording should apply to all property, just like in bequests. The author responds that the analogy to bequest is invalid because bequest is connected to inheritance, which has a different legal basis. The passage also discusses the difference between 'مال' (wealth) and 'ملك' (ownership). Some scholars said 'ملك' is more general, but the correct view is that both terms are limited to zakatable wealth in the context of charity vows, because the vow is understood to be from surplus property. The passage then addresses specific types of property: 'ushri land (subject to 'ushr) is included according to Abu Yusuf because it is a cause of charity, but Muhammad excludes it because it is a cause of tax (kharaj). Kharaji land, slaves for service, real estate, household items, ordinary clothes, and weapons for use are not included. Finally, if the person has no wealth except that which is included, he may keep enough for his sustenance and give the rest later. No quantification is specified because people's needs vary.

Key Terms
مال (māl): In this context, 'wealth' that is subject to zakat and charity vows; it is limited to assets like gold, silver, trade goods, and grazing livestock.
ملك (milk): Ownership; can include non-material things like rights. In this text, some Hanafi scholars say it is more general than 'mal', but the correct view is that both are limited to zakatable wealth in charity vows.
وصية (waṣiyyah): A bequest made by a person for after their death, up to one-third of the estate. It applies to all types of property, unlike charity vows.
أرض عشرية (arḍ 'ushriyyah): Land that is subject to 'ushr (one-tenth tax) because it is owned by a Muslim in a conquered land that was given as fay'. It is considered a cause of charity according to Abu Yusuf and thus included in the charity vow.

Amount a Needy Person May Keep Before Giving Charity
States the amount a person may retain for sustenance before giving sadaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of earners and the recommended amount each may retain for sustenance before giving sadaqah.

Core Idea
Charity begins at home: the needy may keep enough for immediate needs before giving.

The Hanafi school recognizes that a person's obligation to give sadaqah is conditioned upon having wealth beyond one's own and dependents' basic needs. This passage outlines how much a person may retain based on their earning cycle, emphasizing flexibility and the avoidance of begging.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
Read the passage and list the four types of earners: the craftsman, the owner of rental income, the landowner, and the merchant. Note the specific terms used in Arabic.
These categories cover different income cycles: daily, monthly, seasonal, and trade cycles.
2
Extract the retention period for each category
For each category, determine the maximum amount they may keep: craftsman = one day, rental owner = up to one month, landowner = up to one year, merchant = until trade capital returns. Understand that these are guidelines, not fixed obligations.
The text says 'most of that is a month/year' indicating upper limits.
3
Understand the principle of flexibility
The commentary from Al-Mabsut emphasizes that Imam Muhammad did not specify exact amounts because need varies by dependents and earning patterns. This teaches us that the ruling is based on need, not a rigid formula.
The key principle: one's own sustenance and family take precedence over charity.
4
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Given a person with a specific earning cycle (e.g., a freelancer paid weekly), determine how much he may keep. Use the analogy from the four categories to infer a proportional rule.
The principle: keep enough until the next expected income, but not beyond a reasonable period (a year at most).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement on how much a person in need may keep from his wealth before giving charity (sadaqah). It discusses four categories: the craftsman (محترف) may keep one day's sustenance for himself and his family; the owner of rental income (صاحب المستغل) may keep what he needs until his rent arrives, typically up to a month; the landowner (صاحب الضياع) may keep until his harvest arrives, typically up to a year; and the merchant (صاحب التجارة) may keep until his trade capital returns. The commentary from Al-Mabsut notes that Imam Muhammad did not specify exact amounts in Al-Mabsut because the need varies by dependents and earning cycles. The commentator then defines 'مستغل' linguistically and practically: it refers to someone who owns properties rented monthly, so he can retain up to a month's expenses. This ruling is based on the principle that one's immediate need for basic sustenance takes precedence over charity, and it is not wise to give away all wealth and then beg.

Key Terms
المحترف (al-muḥtarif): A craftsman or skilled laborer who earns daily or periodically.
المستغل (al-mustaghil): An owner of rental income, such as houses or shops leased monthly.
صاحب الضياع (ṣāḥib al-ḍiyā‘): A landowner who owns agricultural land, waiting for harvest.
