Trustee's Conflicting Acknowledgments: First Heir Prevails
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on trustee's conflicting heir acknowledgments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a trustee's conflicting heir acknowledgments and explain the application of istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) to defend the first acknowledged heir.

Core Idea
The present state justifies past validity — istishab al-hal defends the first heir, not the second.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar on the validity of a trustee's late acknowledgment of a second heir after already acknowledging and paying the first. The ruling affirms that the first heir retains the property. The key legal tool is tahkim al-hal (considering the present state) as a form of istishab, which serves as a proof for defense (daf') but not for entitlement (istihqaq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting acknowledgments
The trustee first says 'this is son X, sole heir' and pays him, then later acknowledges son Y. Determine which acknowledgment is contested (the second) and what the trustee claims.
The first acknowledgment was acted upon; the second contradicts it.
2
State the Hanafi position
According to Abu Hanifa, the first son gets the property; the second acknowledgment is invalid because it contradicts the first and the present state (first son already in possession) supports the first.
The text says 'فهي له' (it is for him) referring to the first son.
3
Understand the Hanafi proof (istishab al-hal)
The cause of deprivation (the second son's claim) is established at present (the first son already has the property), so by istishab al-hal (tahkim al-hal) it is considered to have existed in the past. This supports the first son's possession as a defense (daf').
The water mill analogy illustrates judging the past by the present state.
4
Contrast with Zufar's view
Zufar attributes the event of Islam (or the second son's conversion) to the nearest time (after death), potentially favoring the later acknowledgment. The passage states 'قول قولها' which suggests his basis.
Zufar's view is mentioned but not fully expanded; the Hanafi rebuttal centers on the distinction between daf' and istihqaq.
5
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The present state (haal) is valid for defense but not for entitlement. Therefore, it protects the first son's receipt but cannot create a new claim for the second son.
This distinction ensures finality of distributions once acted upon.

However, the Hanafi proof: The cause of deprivation (i.e., the second son being a distracter) is established in the present (since the first son already took the property), so it applies retroactively by considering the present (istishab al-hal). This is analogous to the example of the water mill: if there is a dispute about whether the water was flowing during the lease period, the judge looks at the current state. If the mill is currently flowing, the landlord's claim that it was flowing throughout is accepted; if not, the tenant's claim. Similarly, the present state (the first son having received the property) indicates that he was the rightful heir. The commentary then explains the principle: if a depositary acknowledges someone as the heir of a deceased depositor, he must pay him because his acknowledgment effectively admits that the property belongs to the heir as a successor. This is similar to acknowledging that it was the property of the deceased while alive. However, if he acknowledges someone as the agent of the depositor to receive the property and that the depositor sold it, he is not required to pay because that would invalidate the depositor's ownership. But after the depositor's death, it's different. Also, a debtor who acknowledges an agent of the creditor to receive the debt must pay, because it's an acknowledgment of the creditor's pure right (since debts are fungible).

The Ruling on Taking Surety from Heirs/Creditors After Estate Division
Ruling that no surety is taken from heirs/creditors; caution is oppression

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on requiring a surety from heirs or creditors after estate division and explain why it is considered oppression.

Core Idea
No surety is required from heirs or creditors after estate division; requiring one is judicial oppression.

This passage from ‘Umdat al-Ri‘ayah clarifies a specific judicial practice in estate division. It addresses a situation where witnesses testify without explicitly stating that they know of no other claimants. Some judges, like Ibn Abi Layla, used to demand a surety as a precaution. But Abu Hanifa rejected this, calling it oppression. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its broader implications for judicial conduct.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the judge requiring a surety (kafil) from heirs or creditors after dividing the estate based on witness testimony. The ruling is that this should NOT be done.
2
Understand the context
The division is made on the testimony of witnesses who did not include the phrase 'we know of no other heirs/creditors'. The judge might be tempted to take a surety as a precaution against future claims.
3
Recognize the counter-practice
Some judges, such as Ibn Abi Layla in Kufa, used to take a surety as ihtiyat (caution). Abu Hanifa condemned this as zulm (oppression), meaning deviation from the straight path (sawa' al-sabil).
4
Grasp the definition of zulm
Here zulm does not simply mean injustice; it means going beyond the legal bounds. The established ruling is that no surety is required, so requiring one is an excess that harms the heirs/creditors.
5
Reflect on the theological aside
The commentator defends Abu Hanifa against the claim that he said 'every mujtahid is correct' in a relativistic sense, clarifying that it means correct in effort, not in absolute truth—only one view is correct before Allah.
This shows that the Hanafi school upholds the objectivity of truth in ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling that when an estate is divided among heirs or creditors based on the testimony of witnesses who merely say they are heirs or creditors without stating that they know of no others, the judge should not require a surety (kafil) from them. Some judges, like Ibn Abi Layla in Kufa, used to take a kafil as a precaution, but Abu Hanifa condemned this as oppression (zulm). The commentary explains that 'zulm' here means deviation from the right path. Then it addresses a theological issue: Abu Hanifa is attributed with the saying 'every mujtahid is correct' which might imply relativism. The commentator defends Abu Hanifa by interpreting that every mujtahid is correct in his ijtihad (effort), even if he is mistaken in Allah's actual judgment. The proof is that Abu Hanifa also said 'the truth with Allah is one', which would contradict the literal meaning of 'every mujtahid is correct' if taken as everyone hitting the truth. Therefore, the correct interpretation is that each mujtahid is rewarded for his ijtihad, but only one is correct in Allah's knowledge.

Surety in Estate Distribution: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on judicial precaution in estate distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on requiring surety in estate distribution, explain Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's proofs, and apply their reasoning to a similar judicial scenario.

Core Idea
The judge must act on apparent evidence; requiring a surety for hypothetical absent heirs can be an unjust delay.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah presents a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on whether a judge should take a surety (kafeel) from heirs or creditors when distributing an estate. The matn declares such precautionary surety to be 'oppression' — a strong rebuke from Abu Hanifa against the practice of Ibn Abi Layla. The commentary unpacks the legal reasoning behind both positions, using analogies to property and debt cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the dispute
The case: witnesses testify for heirs/creditors but do not say 'we know of no other heir/creditor'. The question: should the judge take a surety from the recipients?
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
Extract Abu Hanifa's position and principle
Abu Hanifa: no surety. Principle: the judge is only obligated by what appears to him (الظاهر عنده). The rights of present parties are established; delaying for hypotheticals is not justified.
Abu Hanifa analogizes to purchase from a possessor and debt against a slave.
3
Extract the Sahibayn's position and principle
The Sahibayn: the judge takes a surety. Principle: the judge is a guardian for absent parties; the possibility of absent claimants is apparent because death can come suddenly.
They analogize to returning lost property and giving maintenance from an absent husband's wealth.
4
Analyze the proofs (qiyas)
Both sides use qiyas. Abu Hanifa: the judge acts on established rights like in purchase. Sahibayn: precaution is needed like in lost property. Identify the 'illa' (effective cause) in each analogy.
Abu Hanifa's illa: absence of known counterclaim. Sahibayn's illa: possibility of absent claimant.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf and note tarjih
Practical difference: immediate distribution vs. delay for surety. The matn's phrase 'this precaution is oppression' suggests preference for Abu Hanifa, but the commentary does not give explicit tarjih.
Both positions are considered legitimate ijtihad within the school.

Leaving Property with Possessor in Inheritance Claims: Abu Hanifa vs. the Two Disciples
Intra-Hanafi dispute on judicial procedure for inherited property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding leaving property with the possessor in inheritance claims, explain the legal reasoning behind each position, and identify the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
The possessor's hand is not weakened without the absent heir's claim; but a denier may be treacherous.

This passage addresses a scenario where one heir proves inheritance of real estate, but the other heir is absent. The judge gives the present heir his half, but what about the other half held by a third-party possessor? Should it remain with him, or be moved to a trustee? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples disagree on whether the possessor's denial changes the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal question
Read the passage: Zayd establishes that a property belongs to him and his brother as inheritance from their father. The judge gives Zayd half. What should be done with the other half, which is in the possession of a third party (dhī al-yad)? The question is whether the judge should leave it with the possessor or take it from him.
The possessor may either deny the claim (jāḥid) or not.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the judge should leave the property with the possessor regardless of denial, without requiring a surety. His reasoning: the possessor was chosen by the deceased, so his possession should not be disturbed for a claim not made by the absent heir. The present claimant is not an agent for the absent one.
The possessor is considered trustworthy until the absent heir himself makes a claim.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad distinguish: if the possessor denies the claim, he is considered treacherous (khāʾin) and the property must be taken from him and placed with a trustee. If he does not deny (i.e., he acknowledges or remains silent), the property is left with him without surety.
Their reasoning: the denier cannot be trusted; the judge is the guardian of the absent heir.
4
Analyze the key difference: treatment of the denier
The crux is whether denial renders the possessor untrustworthy. Abu Hanifa does not automatically treat denial as treachery; the denier may be ignorant, and leaving the property with him maintains his liability (ḍamān) if it perishes. The Sahibayn see denial as bad faith requiring removal to a trust (amānah).
The commentator argues that leaving with the possessor is better because it keeps him liable, whereas a trustee would not be liable.
5
Consider extension to movables and the final tarjih
The text states that the same rule applies to movable property (manqūl). For movables, Abu Hanifa's view is aẓhar (more apparent) because the possessor is already liable (ḍāmin), so removal would weaken the absent heir's claim. In later fatwa, the Sahibayn's view is often used for real estate, but the commentator defends Abu Hanifa's position.
The mufta bihi may vary by context.

Core Idea
For certain notifications, one 'adl or two mustoor suffice; the corrrect view accepts two fasiqeen.

This passage addresses a key procedural point in Hanafi fiqh: what type of report (khabar) is acceptable for certain notifications that have legal consequences? Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Sahibayn) differ on whether the reporter needs to be 'adl (just) or merely discerning. The later commentary refines the ruling, ultimately accepting even two fasiq (sinners) due to the strength of numbers. Let's examine the dispute and the final relied-upon position.

Guided Path

Understand the reasoning: Abu Hanifa sees an element of obligation (ilzam) resembling testimony; Sahibayn view them purely as transactions.

This tarjih is supported by Al-Durr al-Mukhtar and is the mufta bihi.
4

The commentary expands the list to ten matters, including reporting a defect to a buyer, interdiction of a minor, dissolution of partnership, dismissal of a judge, and trustee of a waqf. In all these, one part of testimony (either 'adala or number) is required, not the full wording of shahada.

Qadi's Liability for Selling Debtor's Slave
Ruling that qadi and his ameen are not liable for loss of price or slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a qadi and his ameen are not liable for losses in judicial sales, identify the istihsan reasoning (sadd al-dhara'i), and distinguish between an ameen appointed by explicit wording and a mere agent.

Core Idea
A qadi is not liable; creditors bear the risk to prevent abandonment of the judiciary.

When a qadi sells a debtor's property to pay creditors, normal agency liability would make the qadi responsible for losses. Yet Hanafi law exempts the qadi and his ameen. This lesson unpacks that exemption: its ruling, its policy-based proof, and the precise definition of 'ameen' that triggers the exemption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a judicial sale of a debtor's slave by the qadi or his ameen. The ruling is that neither is liable if the price is lost or the slave is reclaimed. Instead, the buyer recourses against the creditors.
This is a hukm wad'i (declaratory ruling) transferring liability.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is istihsan – juristic preference based on public interest (maslaha). No explicit text; it is a rational argument to prevent harm.
Istihsan overrides the qiyas that would normally impose liability on the seller-agent.
3
Understand the reasoning (sadd al-dhara'i)
If qadis were personally liable, qualified people would refuse the position, causing loss of judicial functions and rights. Therefore, liability is removed to protect the system.
This is a form of sadd al-dhara'i (blocking excuses).
4
Note the practical consequence (thamarah)
The buyer cannot sue the qadi; he must claim from the creditors, who requested the sale and thus bear the 'uhda (warranty).
The buyer is not left without remedy; his property is protected.
5
Distinguish the ameen definition
Only a person explicitly appointed as 'ameen' by the qadi benefits from the non-liability. If the qadi merely says 'Sell this slave,' there is disagreement; the correct view according to Khwaharzadah is that no liability attaches either, but the ruling is clearer with explicit appointment.
This nuance affects who qualifies for the immunity.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn concerning the liability of a qadi when selling property of a debtor. Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hanafi school on the acceptance of a judge's statement regarding judgments he has passed. The author classifies judges into four categories based on knowledge (knowledgeable/ignorant) and justice (just/sinful) and gives different rulings for each. For a knowledgeable just judge, his statement about his own judgment (e.g., that he stoned, cut a hand, or beat someone) is accepted without need for evidence, because the presence of only one judge in a locality necessitates reliance on his word; otherwise two judges would be needed. For a just ignorant judge, his statement is accepted only if he gives a good explanation of the reason for his ruling, since ignorance may lead to mistaken reasoning. For a sinful judge (whether knowledgeable or ignorant), his statement is not accepted at all unless the person sees the evidence himself, due to the possibility of error or corruption. The passage also discusses the case of a judge who has been removed from office: if he makes a statement about a past judgment (e.g., that he took money and gave it to a third party, or that he ordered a hand to be cut) and the affected party admits that the event occurred during the judge's tenure, the judge's statement is believed because it is presumed that a judge does not oppress. However, if the affected party claims the event occurred before or after the judgeship, then the judge is believed only if the party lacks evidence; if the party has evidence, the judge's statement is rejected. The passage includes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Muhammad initially agreed with Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf that the knowledgeable just judge's statement is accepted without evidence, then differed and required seeing evidence, but later returned to the original position. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the acceptance without evidence for the knowledgeable just judge, though some later scholars preferred the stricter view due to prevalent corruption.

Key Terms
عالم عدل ('alim 'adl): A judge who is knowledgeable in Islamic law and just in his conduct. His statement about his own judgment is accepted absolutely.
جاهل عدل (jahil 'adl): A judge who is just but lacks knowledge. His statement is accepted only after he gives a good explanation of the reason for his ruling.
فاسق (fasiq): A judge who is sinful, whether knowledgeable or ignorant. His statement is not accepted unless the person sees the actual evidence of the judgment.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Judge's Statement and the Ruling for Removed Judges
Ruling on when a judge's statement about his judgment is accepted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the sequence of Imam Muhammad's opinions on accepting a judge's statement and apply the rule for accepting a removed judge's statement.

Core Idea
Imam Muhammad changed his view twice; the final position agrees with Abu Hanifa, but later scholars preferred the stricter view due to corruption.

The Hanafi school's position on accepting a judge's statement was not monolithic: Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani initially agreed with Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, then retracted requiring evidence, and finally returned to the original view. Moreover, the passage extends the ruling to judges who have been removed from office, specifying when their statements are believed.

Guided Path
1
Trace Imam Muhammad's three positions
First, he agreed with Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf that a knowledgeable just judge's statement is accepted without evidence. Then he retracted, saying evidence is required. Finally, he returned to the original position.
The passage confirms the final return via al-Bahr and Hisham's narration.
2
Understand the reasoning behind Muhammad's intermediate view
He feared that the judge's statement might contain error or mistake, and correction after implementation (e.g., execution) is impossible.
This shows sensitivity to irreversible punishments.
3
Note the later scholars' preference
Many later Hanafi scholars (kathir min mashayikhina) adopted Muhammad's intermediate view in their time due to widespread corruption among judges.
They made an exception for the letter of a judge to a judge (kitab al-qadi) out of necessity.
4
Apply the rule to a removed judge
If a removed judge makes a statement about a past judgment and the affected party admits it occurred during the judgeship, the judge is believed. Otherwise, the judge is believed only if the party lacks contrary evidence.
The key is the affected party's admission of the timing.

The author classifies judges into four categories based on knowledge (knowledgeable/ignorant) and justice (just/sinful) and gives different rulings for each. For a knowledgeable just judge, his statement about his own judgment (e.g., that he stoned, cut a hand, or beat someone) is accepted without need for evidence, because the presence of only one judge in a locality necessitates reliance on his word; otherwise two judges would be needed. For a just ignorant judge, his statement is accepted only if he gives a good explanation of the reason for his ruling, since ignorance may lead to mistaken reasoning. For a sinful judge (whether knowledgeable or ignorant), his statement is not accepted at all unless the person sees the evidence himself, due to the possibility of error or corruption. The passage also discusses the case of a judge who has been removed from office: if he makes a statement about a past judgment (e.g., that he took money and gave it to a third party, or that he ordered a hand to be cut) and the affected party admits that the event occurred during the judge's tenure, the judge's statement is believed because it is presumed that a judge does not oppress. However, if the affected party claims the event occurred before or after the judgeship, then the judge is believed only if the party lacks evidence; if the party has evidence, the judge's statement is rejected. The passage includes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Muhammad initially agreed with Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf that the knowledgeable just judge's statement is accepted without evidence, then differed and required seeing evidence, but later returned to the original position. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the acceptance without evidence for the knowledgeable just judge, though some later scholars preferred the stricter view due to prevalent corruption.

A dispute arises: The author of Al-Hidayah holds that the judge's statement is always accepted regardless, drawing an analogy to a person who says 'I divorced while insane'— if insanity was known of him, his claim is credited. Al-Zayla'i and others affirm this as the correct and well-known position, supported by Fakhr al-Islam, al-Bazdawi, and al-Sadr al-Shahid, and it is also upheld in Al-Multaqa.

Dispute between Judge and Plaintiff over Timing of Action
Ruling on credibility of judge's claim about timing of his action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whose statement is accepted when a judge and plaintiff differ over the timing of the judge's action, and explain the proof using the analogy to a claim of insanity.

Core Idea
The judge's statement is accepted when he claims a state negating liability, like insanity.

In judicial procedure, disputes can arise about whether a judge's action occurred before or after his tenure. The Hanafi school offers two views: one relying on the general principle of attributing events to the nearest time, and another that gives weight to the judge's claim of a familiar state inconsistent with liability. This lesson explores the reasoning behind each view and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and the parties
Recognize that the conflict is between the judge (al-qadi) and the plaintiff (al-mudda'i) over the timing of a judicial action relative to the judge's tenure.
The judge claims he was not in office at the time; the plaintiff insists he was.
2
State the first position (Sarakhsi) and its reasoning
Shams al-Aimma al-Sarakhsi holds that the plaintiff's statement is accepted because the action is a new event, so it is attributed to the nearest possible time (i.e., during the judge's tenure).
This follows the legal maxim: 'al-asl idafat al-hadith ila aqrab awqatihi'.
3
State the second position (Hidayah) and its analogy
The author of Al-Hidayah holds that the judge's statement is accepted. The judge attributes his action to a state he is known to have experienced, which negates liability. This is analogous to a person claiming divorce during insanity.
If insanity was familiar from the person, the claim is accepted. Similarly, if being non-judge was a familiar state for the judge, his claim is accepted.
4
Compare the two proofs and identify the practical difference
Sarakhsi relies on attribution to the nearest time; Hidayah relies on ascription to a familiar state. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is whose statement is believed in court.
If the judge's state was not familiar, the ruling may differ.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi and authority hierarchy
The later Hanafi scholars, including al-Zayla'i and the author of Multaqa al-Abhar, have adopted the view of Al-Hidayah as the correct one. Thus, the judge's statement is accepted.
This is the operative position for fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah discusses a dispute in judicial procedure: when a judge and a plaintiff differ over whether the judge's action occurred before his appointment or after his removal, whose statement is accepted? The passage presents two Hanafi positions. First, Shams al-Aimma al-Sarakhsi held that the plaintiff's statement is believed because the action is a new event and should be attributed to the nearest possible time (i.e., after the judge's tenure if that is claimed). He supports this by analogy to a dispute over the flow of mill water, where the current state is taken as reference. Second, the author of Al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) holds that the judge's statement is also accepted, and this is the correct view. The reasoning is that the judge is attributing his action to a familiar state that negates liability (i.e., he was not a judge at the time). This is analogous to a person claiming to have divorced or freed a slave while insane, where the claim is accepted if insanity was a known condition. Al-Zayla'i in Tabyin al-Haqa'iq affirms this as the famous view and notes that it was chosen by Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi and al-Sadr al-Shahid, and it is also upheld in Al-Multaqa. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that the judge's statement is credible when he claims a state inconsistent with liability.
