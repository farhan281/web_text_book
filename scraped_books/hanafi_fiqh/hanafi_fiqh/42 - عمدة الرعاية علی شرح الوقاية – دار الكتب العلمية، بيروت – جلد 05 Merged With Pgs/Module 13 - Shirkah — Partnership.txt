Debt Recovery After Dissolution of Mufawada
Ruling on creditor's rights after dissolution of mufawada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on creditor's rights after dissolution of a mufawada partnership and explain the limitation on recourse between partners, distinguishing the scenario where the debt originated from a pre-dissolution purchase by one partner.

Core Idea
In mufawada, the creditor may demand the full debt from either partner, but the paying partner recovers only the surplus over half.

This passage addresses a practical question: when a mufawada partnership ends, how does a creditor recover debts? The ruling hinges on the unique structure of mufawada, which embeds suretyship into partnership. Understanding the distinction between principal and surety roles is key to applying the correct recovery rules. The commentary also clarifies a nuance when a debt arises from a partner's purchase before dissolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two-part ruling
The passage gives two rulings: (1) The creditor may claim the entire debt from whichever partner he chooses, and (2) the paying partner has recourse against the other only for the amount paid in excess of half the debt.
This follows from the nature of mufawada.
2
Understand the underlying reason (talil)
Mufawada includes mutual suretyship and agency. Each partner is principal in half and surety in half. Since the principal aspect outweighs the surety aspect, payments first satisfy the principal part, and only the excess is recoverable.
The text states: 'جهة الأصالة راجحة على جهة الكفالة'.
3
Distinguish the special case of a personal purchase
If one partner purchased something before dissolution and the seller claims from the buyer directly, the buyer is principal in half and agent in half. He recovers half from the partner, not just the excess.
This nuance is raised in the commentary (أقول: في هذه المسألة إشكال).
4
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Consider a mufawada partnership dissolved with a debt of $1000 to a supplier. The supplier demands $1000 from partner A. A pays. A can only recover $500 from partner B? No, A recovers only the excess over his half: since half is $500, A's own principal obligation is $500, so he can recover $500 from B (the excess). But if the debt was from a purchase by A personally before dissolution, A pays the full $1000 to the seller and recovers $500 from B.
The difference hinges on whether the debt is a partnership debt or an individual purchase debt.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the dissolution of a mufawada partnership. The mufawada partnership is a contract of equality in capital, freedom, intelligence, and religion. It inherently includes both agency (wakalah) and suretyship (kafalah). Because each partner is both an agent and a surety for the other, the creditor, upon dissolution of the partnership, may demand the entire debt from whichever partner he chooses. This is because the suretyship that was established by the partnership contract continues even after dissolution. The partner who pays, however, does not have automatic recourse against the other for half; rather, he has recourse only for the amount he paid in excess of half. The reason is that the paying partner acts as a principal in half of the debt (since it is his own obligation) and as a surety in the other half. Since the aspect of being principal outweighs the aspect of being surety, the first half of what he pays is considered to be his own principal obligation, and only the portion exceeding half is considered a payment on behalf of the other. The commentary then raises a potential difficulty: if one partner made a purchase before dissolution, and the seller demands the price from that buyer directly, this scenario does not involve suretyship. In that case, the buyer is principal in half and agent in half, so whatever he pays, he should be able to recover half from his partner. The text advises understanding this nuance. A subsequent note by Ya'qub Pasha indicates further discussion on this point.

Key Terms
مفاوضة (mufawada): A Hanafi partnership of equals in capital, freedom, intelligence, and religion, including mutual agency and suretyship.
كفالة (kafala): Suretyship; a contract where one person (the surety) guarantees the debt or obligation of another.
أصالة (asalah): The status of being a principal in a transaction; a party with primary liability.

Key Takeaway
In mufawada, the creditor can claim full debt from either partner, but the paying partner recovers only the surplus over half, except when the debt originates from a personal purchase by one partner.
