Automatic Inclusion of Rights of Way in Ijarah vs Bay'
Comparing ijarah and bay' regarding automatic inclusion of rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that rights of way are automatically included in ijarah but not in bay', and apply this rule to new scenarios.

Core Idea
In ijarah, rights of way are automatically included because without them the benefit cannot be realized; in sale, they are not because sale focuses on the corpus.

This lesson examines a key difference between lease (ijarah) and sale (bay') in Hanafi fiqh: the automatic inclusion of ancillary rights like water, road, and drainage. Understanding this distinction is essential for drafting valid contracts and resolving disputes. We will explore the legal reasoning behind the rule and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for each contract
Read the passage and note the explicit statement: rights of way enter into ijarah without mention, but in sale they do not unless specified.
This is the core legal position.
2
Understand the legal basis
Ijarah is a contract for usufruct (manfa'ah); usufruct cannot be realized without access rights. Thus, they are automatically part of the lease.
The reasoning is talil (cause-based).
3
Compare with sale
Sale transfers ownership of the corpus (raqabah). The buyer can benefit by reselling, so benefit is secondary. Hence, rights are not automatically included.
The contrast highlights the different objectives.
4
Analyze the dual characterization
The passage describes these rights as subsidiary (tab') in one sense (they depend on the property) and principal (asl) in another (they can exist independently). The Hanafi school acts on both analogies, requiring explicit mention in sale.
This shows the nuanced application of qiyas.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Test your understanding: if a person leases a shop that requires a specific access path, is that path automatically included? Use the rule derived above.
Apply the same reasoning.

Indeed, the right to drinking water, the road, and the drainage channel enter into a lease without mentioning the rights or appurtenances, because a lease is contracted upon usufruct, and usufruct cannot be realized without these things. As for sale, it pertains to the corpus. Also, the buyer can potentially benefit by trading (the property), whereas this is not the case in a lease. These three things (i.e., water, road, drainage) are subsidiary in one respect and principal in another. As for being subsidiary, it is because they are outside the boundaries of the property and are intended for use within it. As for being principal, it is because they can be conceived without the sold item, so they do not enter except by mentioning the rights or appurtenances, acting on both analogies. Commentary
This passage is a ruling comparing lease (ijarah) and sale (bay') regarding the automatic inclusion of rights of way (such as water, road, drainage). The author states that in a lease, these rights are automatically included without explicit mention because the benefit (manfa'ah) which is the object of the lease cannot be realized without them. In contrast, in a sale, the contract pertains to the corpus (raqabah), and the buyer can benefit by reselling the property; thus the rights are not automatically included unless explicitly mentioned. The commentary clarifies that ijarah is solely for usufruct, so without these rights the usufruct is impossible, whereas sale is for ownership, and benefit is a secondary consequence. The buyer can still profit by trading the property, but a lessee cannot do so. Therefore, an item with no benefit (like an unusable item as mahr) can be sold but not leased. The Hanafi school bases this on the nature of the contract and the purpose of the transaction.

Key Terms
الإجارة (al-ijarah): A contract for usufruct (manfa'ah) in exchange for rent.
البيع (al-bay'): A contract for the transfer of ownership of the corpus (raqabah).
الشرب (al-shurb): Right to drinking water, typically from a well or stream.
المسيل (al-masil): Right to drainage channel or watercourse.
الطريق (al-tariq): Right of way or pathway.
