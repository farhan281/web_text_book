Liability of Guarantor for Slave's Value
Ruling with proof by qiyas on guarantor liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds a guarantor liable for a guaranteed slave's value when the claimant proves ownership, and apply the qiyas reasoning to distinguish ownership claims from debt claims.

Core Idea
The guarantor stands in the place of the possessor—if the slave dies, the guarantor pays the value, but only for ownership, not debt.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi fiqh of kafalah (guarantee): when a slave guaranteed by his person dies, and the claimant proves ownership with evidence (bayyina), the guarantor must pay the slave's value. The ruling is derived through qiyas: the guarantor takes the place of the current possessor, who would be liable to return the slave or pay its value if it dies. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the distinction between guaranteeing for ownership versus guaranteeing for debt, which determines whether the guarantor is liable or freed upon the slave's death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is: a slave is guaranteed 'by his person' (kafala bi al-raqaba), the slave dies, and the claimant proves ownership by evidence. The ruling is that the guarantor must pay the slave's value.
The ruling applies only when the guarantee is for the slave's person, not for a debt.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The primary proof is qiyas (analogy). The asl (original case) is the liability of the current possessor (dhul yad) who is obligated to return the slave; if the slave perishes, he must pay its value. The far' (new case) is the guarantor.
The source also mentions a contrast with debt claims where no analogy applies.
3
Examine the illa (effective cause)
The illa connecting asl and far' is that the guarantor stands in the place of the possessor (qā'im maqām al-asīl). This is a jaliyyah (clearly perceptible) cause extracted by tanqih al-manat.
The illa is 'standing in the place of'—the guarantor inherits the possessor's liability regarding the guaranteed object.
4
Note the limit of the analogy
The analogy does not extend to cases where the claim is a debt owed by the slave. In debt claims, the guarantee is for the slave's person so the slave can be sold to pay the debt; the slave's death frees the slave from the obligation, and thus frees the guarantor. The text explicitly distinguishes this.
The key difference: in ownership claims, the slave is inherently guaranteed (maḍmūn bi nafsihi); in debt claims, the slave is only a means to satisfy the debt.
5
Determine the thamarah (practical consequence)
The ruling creates a clear test: if a guarantor guarantees a slave's person and the slave dies, the guarantor is liable only if the claimant proves ownership. If the claim is a debt, the guarantor is free. Additionally, the guarantor's right to recourse depends on whether the guarantee was by the slave's or master's command.
The recourse details are handled in later discussions; the main ruling here is the liability condition.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with proof on the liability of a guarantor when a guaranteed slave dies. The ruling: If a slave who is guaranteed 'by his person' (i.e., the guarantee is for the slave's physical presence, not a debt) dies, and the claimant establishes by evidence that the slave is his property, then the guarantor must pay the slave's value. The proof is based on analogy (qiyas): The guarantor stands in place of the current possessor, who is obligated to return the slave; if the slave perishes, the possessor must pay the value. Therefore, the guarantor similarly must pay. The commentary distinguishes this from a case where the claim is a debt owed by the slave. In that case, the guarantor guarantees the slave's person for the purpose of the slave being sold to pay the debt; if the slave dies, the slave is freed from the debt obligation, and the guarantor is also freed, because the principal's freedom entails the guarantor's freedom. The passage also discusses details: the guarantor's right of recourse against the slave after emancipation depends on whether the guarantee was by the slave's command or the master's command. If by the slave's command, recourse is only after emancipation; if by the master's command, recourse is immediate. The proof for the ruling uses qiyas and legal reasoning, not direct nass.

Key Terms
كفالة بالرقبة (kafala bi al-raqaba): Guarantee of the slave's person (physical presence) for the purpose of returning ownership to the claimant.
المكفول به (al-makful bihi): The thing or person guaranteed; here, the slave's person.
بينة (bayyina): Legal evidence, typically two male witnesses or equivalent, establishing ownership.
إقرار (iqrar): Confession; here, the current possessor's admission that the slave belongs to the claimant.

No Recourse Between Master and Slave After Emancipation
Ruling on cross guarantees after emancipation with talil reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that after emancipation, neither master nor slave can reclaim guarantee payments, and explain the reasoning based on iqrar being a deficient proof.

Core Idea
After the slave is freed, guarantee payments are not recoverable; the guarantee is gratuitous and iqrar binds only the confessor.

In this passage, the Hanafi school addresses cross-guarantees between master and slave made before emancipation. The key question: can the paying party seek reimbursement after the slave is freed? The ruling is clear—no recourse. The underlying principle is that iqrar (confession) is a deficient proof (hujja qasira) that only binds the confessor, not the other party. We'll explore the reasoning and distinguish it from related cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) the master guarantees for his slave, and (2) the slave guarantees for his master. In both, the slave is later freed.
The guarantee may be for property or for the person.
2
State the ruling
After emancipation, whatever each party paid under the guarantee cannot be reclaimed from the other.
This applies whether the slave had debts or not, and whether the guarantee was for person or property.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The reason given is that 'al-iqrar hujjatun qasira' — a confession is a deficient proof that only takes effect against the one who made it.
Therefore the master's confession (by guaranteeing) doesn't bind the slave, and vice versa.
4
Understand the gratuitous nature
The guarantee is considered a tabarru' (gratuitous act) by the guarantor, not a loan or transaction requiring compensation.
Hence after emancipation, there is no legal basis for recourse.
5
Contrast with the earlier case
The commentary distinguishes this from a case where a guarantor for a slave's person is liable for the slave's value if the slave dies and ownership is proven, because the guarantor stands in place of the possessor.
That ruling is based on different reasoning (obligation to return the object).

Master-Slave Suretyship: No Recourse After Emancipation
Ruling with proof on master-slave suretyship and recourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on recourse in master-slave suretyship after emancipation and explain the reasoning, including the minority opinion of Imam Zufar.

Core Idea
Suretyship between master and slave creates no right of recourse, even after emancipation, because the initial contract lacked that effect.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in kafala: when a master and slave stand surety for each other, and the slave is later freed, can either claim reimbursement? The Hanafi school holds that no recourse exists, while Imam Zufar differs if the suretyship was by command. Understanding this ruling reveals how the Hanafi school treats the effect of status changes on contractual rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
Note the two cases: master as surety for slave, and slave as surety for master (the slave must not be indebted to the master for validity).
The suretyship occurs before emancipation.
2
State the ruling
After emancipation, neither party can claim reimbursement from the other for what they paid under the suretyship.
The ruling applies regardless of whether the suretyship was by command.
3
Analyze the dalil
The suretyship 'occurred not necessitating recourse' because the master and slave do not owe each other debts; the contract's initial nature is decisive.
The daleel is the legal nature of the contract, not a specific text.
4
Consider Zufar's objection
Imam Zufar argued that if the suretyship was by the other's command, recourse becomes possible after emancipation because the impediment (slavery) is removed.
Command creates a cause for recourse, and the removal of slavery allows it to take effect.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school rejects Zufar's view: the right of recourse depends on the initial contract; command does not retroactively create a right that was absent from the inception.
Analogous to suretyship without command followed by approval – still no recourse.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the rulings of kafala (suretyship) involving slaves. It is from a Hanafi legal commentary, explaining the positions of the Hanafi school and a minority opinion from Imam Zufar. The text covers two main scenarios: suretyship between a master and his slave, and suretyship for a slave whose ownership is claimed by a third party. For the first scenario, the Hanafi position is that when a master stands surety for his slave (or vice versa), and the slave is later emancipated, neither party can seek reimbursement from the other for what they paid under the suretyship. This is because the suretyship was contracted in a manner that did not create a right of recourse initially—the master is not obligated to the slave, and the slave is not obligated to the master—and emancipation does not retroactively change that. The text notes that according to Imam Zufar (a Hanafi scholar), if the suretyship was done by the command of the other party, then recourse is possible after emancipation because the impediment of slavery has been removed. However, the relied-upon Hanafi position is that no recourse exists, even with command, because the initial lack of right of recourse cannot be reversed. For the second scenario, the commentary explains that if a person claims ownership of a slave, and another person stands surety for the slave's person, then the slave dies, and the claimant proves ownership, the surety must pay the value of the slave. This is because the surety stands in the place of the person in possession (the original defendant) who is obligated to return the slave. In contrast, if the claim is a debt against the slave, and another person stands surety for the slave's person, then if the slave dies, the surety is discharged because the object of suretyship (the slave's person) has perished, and the principal (the slave) is discharged from the debt. The commentary uses qiyas (analogy) and istihsan to derive these rulings, and it resolves apparent contradictions between the scenarios.

الأصيل (al-asil): The principal; the person on whose behalf the surety stands.

Suretyship for a Claimed Slave: Liability for Person vs. Debt
Ruling with proof explaining two outcomes in suretyship for a slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between suretyship for a claimed slave's ownership and suretyship for a debt against a slave, and apply the Hanafi rule that the surety pays value in the first case but is discharged in the second.

Core Idea
When a surety guarantees a slave's person in an ownership claim, he must pay the value if the slave dies; if the claim is a debt, the surety is discharged by the slave's death.

This lesson unpacks two contrasting rulings in kafala involving a claimed slave. If someone claims ownership of a slave and another stands surety for the slave's person, the surety is liable for the value if the slave dies. But if the claim is a debt, the surety is discharged. Understanding the distinction hinges on whether the surety stands in place of the person in possession or the slave himself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First scenario: A person claims ownership of a slave in another's possession, and a third party stands surety for the slave's person. Second scenario: A person claims a debt against the slave, and a third party stands surety for the slave's person.
In both cases, the slave dies before delivery to the claimant.
2
State the rulings
In the ownership claim, the surety must pay the slave's value. In the debt claim, the surety owes nothing.
The rulings are opposite despite similar facts.
3
Analyze the difference in obligation
In the ownership claim, the surety stands in place of the person in possession (the defendant), who is obligated to return the slave. Since the slave is a guaranteed property (ayn madmunah), the surety must pay its value if it perishes. In the debt claim, the surety stands for the slave himself; the slave's death discharges him from the debt, and the surety is discharged as well.
The nature of the claim determines whom the surety represents.
4
Apply the analogy with free persons
The text draws an analogy: if a free person is the object of suretyship for property, the surety is discharged when the principal is discharged. Similarly, suretyship for a slave's person in a debt claim ends with the slave's death.
This confirms the consistency of Hanafi reasoning.
5
Confirm the key distinction
The decisive factor is whether the claim is for the slave's ownership (ayn) or for a debt (dayn) owed by the slave. Ownership claim makes the surety a substitute for the possessor; debt claim makes him a substitute for the slave.
This distinction is crucial for correct application.

For the first scenario, the Hanafi position is that when a master stands surety for his slave (or vice versa), and the slave is later emancipated, neither party can seek reimbursement from the other for what they paid under the suretyship. For the second scenario, the commentary explains that if a person claims ownership of a slave, and another person stands surety for the slave's person, then the slave dies, and the claimant proves ownership, the surety must pay the value of the slave. This is because the surety stands in the place of the person in possession (the original defendant) who is obligated to return the slave. In contrast, if the claim is a debt against the slave, and another person stands surety for the slave's person, then if the slave dies, the surety is discharged because the object of suretyship (the slave's person) has perished, and the principal (the slave) is discharged from the debt. The commentary uses qiyas (analogy) and istihsan to derive these rulings, and it resolves apparent contradictions between the scenarios.

This lesson unpacks the dialectical structure of the proof, including the objection and response, and clarifies the final mufta bihi with the tawa exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing views
Recognize that Zufar and al-Qasim ibn Mu'ayn argue the muhil is not freed, while Ibn Abi Layla argues the muhil is freed even in kafalah.
Both are intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
2
Understand Zufar's proof
Zufar's position: hawalah is for security (tawthiqa) like kafalah, so it does not remove the claim against the muhil.
This is based on the purpose of the contract, not its linguistic meaning.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school argues that legal rulings follow linguistic meanings. Hawalah means naql (transfer) and tahwil (shifting), which requires vacating the original debtor's liability. Kafalah means damm (inclusion), which implies adding a liability, not removing.
This is a usul al-fiqh principle: al-ahkam al-shar'iyya tathbutu 'ala wafq al-ma'ani al-lughawiyya.
4
Objection and response
An objector points out that hawalah without order (bi ghayr amr) is valid yet seems to involve no transfer. The Hanafi response is twofold: (a) transfer becomes apparent after payment; (b) the muhil is not a volunteer because he anticipates possible recourse (tawa).
This refutes the claim that no transfer occurs.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon view is that the muhil is freed from the claim, but the debt remains in liability until payment by the new debtor, allowing recourse in case of tawa.
This balances the linguistic argument with the practical security need.

The meaning of kafalah is inclusion, so its implication is that its requirement is the joining of liability to liability, and that is not realized with the acquittal of the original debtor. Thus it is known that the debt remains in his liability as it was, so the meaning of transfer and shifting did not exist. Commentary
This passage is intra-Hanafi dispute and proof regarding the effect of hawalah. The matn states that when hawalah is completed by acceptance, the original debtor (muhil) is freed from the debt. The commentary first explains the difference between kafalah and hawalah: if kafalah is conditioned on the acquittal of the original debtor, it becomes hawalah; if hawalah is conditioned on non-acquittal, it becomes kafalah. It then addresses Zufar and al-Qasim ibn Mu'ayn, who argue that the debtor is not freed because hawalah is for security (tawthiqa), like kafalah, so it does not remove the claim. Ibn Abi Layla goes further, saying even in kafalah the debtor is freed, by analogy with hawalah. The Hanafi response ("wa lana") argues that legal rulings follow linguistic meanings. Linguistically, hawalah implies transfer (naql) and shifting (tahwil), which requires that the original debtor's liability become free. Kafalah means inclusion (damm), which implies adding one liability to another, not freeing the first. The objector raises the case of hawalah without the debtor's order (hawalah bi ghayr amr), which is valid yet seemingly involves no transfer. The Hanafi replies that transfer becomes apparent after payment; moreover, if the debt had truly transferred, the muhil would be a volunteer, but in hawalah the creditor is forced to accept, proving the muhil is not a volunteer. A further answer: the muhil is not a stranger because he expects possible recourse if the new debtor becomes insolvent (tawa), thus he acts to protect himself.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-hawalah): A transfer of debt from one debtor to another, resulting in the original debtor being freed upon acceptance.
الكفالة (al-kafalah): A contract of suretyship where the surety joins his liability to the original debtor without freeing him.
البراءة (al-bara'ah): Freedom from debt or claim; in hawalah, the original debtor is freed from the debt or from the claim depending on the ikhtilaf.
المحيل (al-muhil): The original debtor who transfers his debt to another.
المحتال (al-muhtal): The creditor who accepts the transfer and looks to the new debtor for payment.
المحتال عليه (al-muhtal 'alayh): The new debtor who assumes the debt through hawalah.

Imprisonment for Suretyship and Exception for Ascendant
Exception to imprisonment for surety of ascendant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the exception to imprisonment for a surety of an ascendant and explain the rationale of preserving respect.

Core Idea
A surety for his own ascendant is never imprisoned to avoid imprisoning the ascendant.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqaya addresses the types of imprisonment for financial obligations. One important exception is the surety for one's own parent or grandparent. Let's understand the rule and its basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the imprisonable obligations
The matn lists suretyship, replacement for property, and maintenance for wedding and child as obligations that can lead to imprisonment for a debtor who is able but refuses.
The mention of 'not on his debt' is clarified as not being the primary subject here.
2
Locate the exception
In the commentary, an exception is stated from al-Shurnubaliyya: a surety for his ascendant (e.g., father or mother) is not imprisoned at all.
Ascendant includes parents and grandparents upward.
3
Understand the rationale
Imprisoning the surety would lead to imprisoning the ascendant with him because the ascendant would be indirectly confined, which is disrespectful.
The rationale is to avoid disrespect to the parent, not because the debt is invalid.
4
Appreciate the methodological principle
The commentator notes that when matn texts and fatwas conflict, the matn is preferred, and commentaries are preferred over later fatwas.
This justifies relying on the matn's exception over contradictory fatwa of Qadi Khan.

Imprisonment for Immediate Dower: Custom as Evidence
Distinction between immediate and deferred dower for imprisonment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why only immediate dower triggers imprisonment, using custom as evidence, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
Only immediate dower leads to imprisonment because entering marriage implies ability to pay it, unlike deferred dower.

The passage distinguishes between immediate and deferred dower regarding imprisonment. The commentator provides a custom-based argument and also notes a conflicting view from al-Minh and al-Asl. Let's analyze the reasoning and the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
The matn says imprisonment applies for 'mahr', and the commentator clarifies it means the immediate dower (mu'ajjal).
The term mu'ajjal in classical texts can mean immediate (due now) or deferred; context determines meaning.
2
Understand the custom-based proof
The commentator argues that by custom ('urf), immediate dower is delivered at the time of marriage, so a man's agreement to such a condition indicates his ability to pay. Hence, if he refuses after marriage, imprisonment is justified.
This is a dalil 'aqli (rational proof) based on customary practice.
3
Contrast with deferred dower
For deferred dower, there is no immediate demand, so inability or refusal cannot be assumed. The man's act of marrying does not imply he has the money now for a future obligation.
The key difference is the timing of demand and the implication of ability.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
The texts al-Minh and al-Asl state that the husband is not believed regarding the dower irrespective of it being immediate or deferred, implying imprisonment for both. However, the commentator prefers the matn's distinction based on custom and cites al-Bazzaziyya.
The dispute is about whether the presumption of ability applies only to immediate dower.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that only immediate dower triggers imprisonment. This is based on custom and supported by the matn and later authorities like al-Bazzaziyya.
The later fatwa practice follows the matn over the earlier texts due to custom.

[1] His statement: 'And suretyship' – an exception is made in al-Shurnubalaliyya: a surety for his ascendant, such as if he is surety for his father and mother, then he is not imprisoned at all because it would necessitate imprisoning the father with him. This is in Radd al-Muhtar. [2] His statement: 'What is meant is the immediate dower' – because custom has proceeded on delivering the immediate, so his entering into marriage is evidence of his ability to deliver the immediate, so he is not imprisoned for the deferred. If it is said: The deferred dower also he has undertaken by contract, so what is the difference? I say: Since it is known that he is not demanded for it immediately, it does not indicate his ability to it, unlike the immediate by condition or custom. And in al-Minh and al-Asl: He is not believed regarding the dower without distinction between deferred and immediate. This is in al-Bazzaziyya. End.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on the chapter of imprisonment in the Book of Judging. It begins by presenting the matn (core text) listing cases where a person can be imprisoned for financial obligations: suretyship (kafalah), replacement for property (like price of sold goods), maintenance of wife and child, but not for his own debt or suretyship? The commentator then clarifies a methodological principle: when there is conflict between the matn texts and later fatwa collections, the matn is given precedence. Then he explains each item. For suretyship, an exception is made when a person acts as surety for his own ascendant (father, mother, etc.) – he is not imprisoned because imprisoning him would lead to imprisoning the ascendant with him, which is avoided out of respect. For dower (mahr), the text means the immediate (mu'ajjal) dower, not the deferred (mu'ajjal). The reasoning is that by custom, immediate dower is delivered at the time of marriage, so entering into marriage implies ability to pay it. Deferred dower, being demanded later, does not imply current ability. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: in al-Minh and al-Asl, the debtor is not believed regarding the dower regardless of whether it is immediate or deferred.

Key Terms
مهر معجل (mahr mu'ajjal): Immediate dower; the portion of dower payable at the time of marriage.
مهر مؤجل (mahr mu'ajjal): Deferred dower; the portion of dower payable at a later date (e.g., upon divorce or death).
