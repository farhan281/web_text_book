The Timing and Naming of Takbir al-Tashriq
Establishes the timing of takbir and explains its naming

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on the ending time of Takbir al-Tashriq and explain how the naming is justified by proximity.

Core Idea
Takbir al-Tashriq is named by proximity to the Days of Tashriq, even though it begins before them according to Abu Hanifa.

This passage addresses a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute: when exactly does the Takbir al-Tashriq end? While Abu Hanifa held that it ends before the Days of Tashriq begin, his two students extended it until the last Asr of those days. The fatwa follows the students' view, but a question arises: how can it be called 'Takbir of Tashriq' if it ends before Tashriq? The answer lies in the linguistic principle of naming by proximity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Abu Hanifa holds that the takbir ends at Asr of the first day of Tashriq. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that it ends at Asr of the last day (13th).
The passage states 'they both said' referring to the Sahibayn, but earlier it implicitly gives Abu Hanifa's view.
2
Determine the fatwa
The mufta bihi (operative view) is that of the Sahibayn, as stated in al-Musaffa.
This means the default practice is to continue takbir until Asr of the 13th.
3
Analyze the naming objection
If Abu Hanifa's takbir ends before the Days of Tashriq, how can it be called 'Takbir of Tashriq'? This is a linguistic challenge.
The objection is presented as a question: 'How can it be called Takbir of Tashriq?'
4
Understand the Hanafi response
The naming is by proximity (qurb). The takbir occurs close to the Days of Tashriq, and in Arabic, things are named after what they are near.
This is a rational (aqli) argument based on language usage.
5
Visualize the calendar
Days of Nahr: 10th (Nahr only), 11th (both), 12th (both), 13th (Tashriq only). The takbir starts on 9th (Arafa) so it is adjacent to Tashriq.
This clarifies the proximity.

The Obligation of Takbir After Fard Prayers: Conditions and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditions for wajib takbir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for wajib takbir according to Abu Hanifa and explain the difference with the Sahibayn's view.

Core Idea
Takbir after fard is wajib, but Abu Hanifa restricts it to free male residents in congregation; Sahibayn extend it to all.

This passage clarifies that the ruling that takbir is wajib after fard prayers is not absolute—it depends on one's position within the Hanafi school. While the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) apply it to everyone who performs a fard prayer, Imam Abu Hanifa imposes a set of conditions that limit the obligation to specific individuals. Understanding these conditions and their exceptions is essential for correctly applying this sunnah practice, especially given that the fatwa follows the more inclusive view of the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and the ikhtilaf
Start by reading the opening sentence: 'And the takbir is wajib after the obligatory prayers.' Note that the commentary immediately qualifies this as the view of the Sahibayn. Recognize that there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa restricts the obligation, while the Sahibayn generalize it.
This is a classic example of a ruling_with_proof where the proof is not directly quoted but implied by the reasoning.
2
Extract Abu Hanifa's conditions
From the text, list the five conditions mentioned in al-Khujandi: (1) city dwellers, not villagers; (2) residents, not travelers (unless following a resident); (3) praying in congregation, not alone; (4) men, not women (unless following a male imam); (5) only the five daily fard prayers, not supererogatory, witr, or Eid.
These conditions are specific to Abu Hanifa. The Sahibayn do not require them.
3
Examine exceptions and subsidiary rulings
Look at the additional details: praying behind a slave imam (the correct view is that takbir is still obligatory), and the two narrations from Abu Hanifa regarding travelers praying in congregation in a city. These show that even within Abu Hanifa's framework, there is some flexibility.
These sub-rulings illustrate how the conditions are applied in borderline cases.
4
Understand the Sahibayn's counterposition
The Sahibayn hold that takbir follows the fard prayer unconditionally. According to them, every person who performs a fard prayer—regardless of gender, residency, congregation, etc.—must perform the takbir after it.
This view is simpler and more inclusive, which is why it is preferred for fatwa.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its implications
The text indicates that the fatwa is on the view of the Sahibayn. Therefore, in practice, takbir is wajib after every fard prayer for all who pray it, without the restrictions of Abu Hanifa. However, knowing Abu Hanifa's conditions is important for understanding the school's internal reasoning and for cases where one follows the older view.
This is common in Hanafi fiqh: the fatwa may follow one opinion while the school's theoretical framework includes another.

Who Must Recite Takbir al-Tashriq? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains conditions for wajib takbir and the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the obligation of takbir al-tashriq, explain the conditions according to Abu Hanifa, and identify the mufta bihi as the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Takbir al-tashriq is wajib; the fatwa obliges it for anyone who prays a fard prayer.

The takbir of tashriq is a wajib recitation after fard prayers during the days of tashriq. However, within the Hanafi school, there is a significant difference of opinion regarding who must recite it. Imam Abu Hanifa laid down strict conditions, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, held a more inclusive view. This lesson unpacks that dispute and explains why the fatwa follows the latter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act in question is the recitation of takbir after fard prayers during the three days of tashriq. In the Hanafi school, this is wajib (obligatory), not fard, because the evidence is from ahad hadith.
The ruling level is wajib, which means omission requires compensation but does not invalidate the fard prayer itself.
2
List Abu Hanifa's five conditions
According to Abu Hanifa, the takbir is only wajib on: (1) residents of major cities (not villages), (2) residents (not travelers), unless the traveler follows a resident imam, (3) those praying in congregation (not alone), (4) men (not women, unless they follow a man who intends to lead them), (5) after the five daily prayers (not after nawafil, sunan, witr, or Eid).
These conditions severely limit who must recite the takbir.
3
Understand the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rejected these conditions. Their principle is: 'Al-takbir yatba' al-faridah' – the takbir follows the obligatory prayer. Therefore, anyone who performs any fard prayer must recite the takbir, regardless of residence, travel, congregation, or gender.
This position greatly expands the obligation.
4
Note the tarjih (weighting)
The text states 'wal-fatwa 'ala qawlihima' – the fatwa is upon the statement of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. This means the operative ruling used for issuing legal opinions follows their inclusive view.
This tarjih is based on later Hanafi authorities deeming their view more comprehensive and practical.
5
Apply to examples
Under the fatwa, a traveler, a villager, a person praying alone, and a woman praying alone are all obligated to recite the takbir after each fard prayer during the days of tashriq.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that the obligation is universal for all who perform fard prayers.

Commentary

How to Recite Takbir al-Tashriq and When to Say It When Making Up Missed Prayers
Explains the wording of takbir and the rules for qada during tashriq.

Learning Objective

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the prescribed method for salat al-istisqa (prayer for rain). It presents the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa and the dissenting views of his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The passage also includes practical etiquettes for the congregation.

The author begins by citing Abu Hanifa's position: there is no sunnah congregational prayer for rain; rather, istisqa is simply supplication (du'a) and seeking forgiveness (istighfar). The proof is the Quranic verse from Surah Nuh where Allah links rain to istighfar. If individuals pray, it is permissible and not disliked.

Then the author presents the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): the imam should lead the people in two rak'ahs with audible recitation, followed by a sermon, and then face the qiblah for supplication. This is considered sunnah according to them, analogized to the Eid prayer, though without the additional takbirs.

There is a subtle difference between the two companions: in al-Mabsut, Abu Yusuf is reported as agreeing with Abu Hanifa, while in al-Khujandi, he is with Muhammad. Regarding the khutba, Abu Yusuf holds one sermon, Muhammad holds two, and Abu Hanifa holds none because there is no congregation.

The passage also mentions the recommended manner of going out: walking humbly in old clothes, giving charity beforehand, etc., as stated by al-Halwani.

Thus, the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa (no congregational prayer), though later fatwa may consider the practice of some regions. The authority_weight is Zahir al-Riwayah from the Quduri matn.

Key Terms
استسقاء (istisqa): Seeking rain from Allah through supplication and prescribed acts; in this context, the chapter on the prayer for rain.
مسنونة (masnunah): Prescribed as sunnah; an act established by the Prophet's practice that is recommended but not obligatory.
وحدانا (wihdanan): Individually, not in congregation. Used here to indicate that prayer for rain is permissible alone.

Chapter on the Prayer for Rain (Salat al-Istisqa')
Intra-Hanafi dispute on Salat al-Istisqa'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the differences between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding Salat al-Istisqa' and identify the practical implications (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Istisqa' has no khutbah according to Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn require it after the prayer. The difference stems from the presence or absence of congregation.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on the prayer for rain. While the Sahibayn see it as a congregational act with a sermon and flipping of the cloak, Abu Hanifa views it as an individual prayer without these elements. Understanding these differences is essential for knowing the operative ruling in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that Salat al-Istisqa' is a sunnah prayer performed during drought. Both sides agree it is sunnah, but differ on its form.
The hukm level is sunnah mu'akkadah according to the Sahibayn; Abu Hanifa does not emphasize congregation but still considers it sunnah.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
According to Abu Hanifa, the imam prays individually (no congregation), then supplicates. There is no khutbah and no flipping of the cloak. Dhimmis do not attend.
His reasoning: the khutbah depends on congregation, and there is no congregational istisqa' in his view.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the imam leads two rak'ahs aloud (like Eid), then delivers one or two sermons after the prayer, flips his cloak during the sermon, and faces qibla for supplication. Dhimmis are excluded.
They analogize to Eid prayer and emphasize communal humility.
4
Identify the proofs used
The text cites Quran 13:14 and a hadith from Sunan al-Tirmidhi to justify excluding dhimmis. For the khutbah and flipping, analogy (qiyas) to Eid prayer is implied.
The dalil is naqli for dhimmi exclusion; qiyas for the form of prayer.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the prayer is performed individually without formal sermon; under the Sahibayn, it is congregational with full rites. The mufta bihi in later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn.
This affects the imam's actions and the community's participation.

Tarawih Prayer: Ruling, Proof, and Congregation
Explains the ruling, proof, structure, and congregation of tarawih with intra-Hanafi preferences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tarawih as sunnah mu'akkadah, identify its prophetic proof, and explain the intra-Hanafi differences on praying at home versus in congregation.

Core Idea
Tarawih is sunnah mu'akkadah, not merely recommended; its 20 rak'ahs and congregation are established by prophetic sunnah.

Tarawih prayer holds a special place in Ramadan. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies its legal status, distinguishing between the prayer itself and the recommendation of congregation. It also presents a nuanced disagreement among the three major Hanafi imams on whether one should pray at home or in the mosque, a question many face today.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the prayer in question is the night prayer of Ramadan (tarawih). The correct ruling in the Hanafi school is sunnah mu'akkadah (confirmed sunnah), not merely mustahabb (recommended).
The word 'mustahabb' in the matn is specifically for congregation, not the prayer itself.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the Prophet's hadith: 'Indeed Allah has made obligatory upon you fasting Ramadan, and I have prescribed for you its standing.' Note the verb 'sanantu' (I have prescribed) which indicates a sunnah mu'akkadah in Hanafi usul.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), which supports a non-obligatory but strongly recommended status.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The operative phrase is 'sanantu lakum qiyamahu'—the Prophet's act of prescribing establishes a sunnah. In Hanafi usul, the Prophet's sunnah (outside the Qur'an) is not fard but sunnah mu'akkadah. The imperative here is understood as recommendation.
This is why the school differentiates between the wajib of fasting and the sunnah of tarawih.
4
Understand the structure and congregation
Tarawih consists of 20 rak'ahs in 5 tarwīḥāt (each tarwīḥa = 4 rak'ahs with 2 salams). Congregation is recommended (mustahabb), not a condition. Between tarwīḥāt, a sitting of similar duration is recommended, filled with tasbīh, tahlīl, or silence.
The term 'tarwīḥa' derives from rest after the four rak'ahs.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on location
Abū Ḥanīfa said praying at home is better for one skilled in recitation. Muḥammad said the mosque is better. Abū Yūsuf made it conditional: home is better if one can pray equally well there, but if one is a leader whose presence increases the congregation, he should not abandon the mosque.
The muftā bihī for the general public is congregation in the mosque, while for the skilled individual, home is better unless he is a leader.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sitting in Tarawih without excuse is permissible but not recommended—the analogy with Fajr sunnah fails because Fajr sunnah is sometimes deemed wajib.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ruling on performing Tarawih prayer while sitting without a valid excuse. The passage shows how scholars use qiyas (analogical reasoning) and how the classification of a companion sunnah (Fajr) affects the ruling. You will learn the agreed-upon point (sitting is not mustahabb) and the disputed point (whether it is permissible at all), along with the proofs and the preferred position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed and disputed points
The text first states that all scholars agree sitting is not recommended (mustahabb). Then it notes disagreement on permissibility. This is the basic structure: consensus on lack of recommendation, dispute on permissibility.
Consensus is among Hanafi scholars as this is an intra-madhhab discussion.
2
State the opposing view and its proof
One group says sitting is not permissible (la yajuz). Their proof is qiyas (analogy) to the sunnah of Fajr, because both Tarawih and Fajr sunnah are classified as sunnah mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah). They argue that since sitting in Fajr sunnah without excuse is not permissible, the same should apply to Tarawih.
The 'illa (effective cause) is the shared attribute: both are sunnah mu'akkadah.

The text says 'Wa huwa al-sahih' indicating the preferred view.

Identify the logical structure: Opposing uses qiyas jali (clear analogy) on the basis of shared 'illa (sunnah mu'akkadah). The Hanafi response challenges the 'illa: the sunnah of Fajr has a unique feature (being considered wajib by some) that Tarawih lacks, so the analogy is not sound.
This shows that for qiyas to be valid, the 'illa must be present in both cases without relevant difference (tafarrud).
5

The opposing view renders sitting impermissible (makruh tahrimi), meaning the person would be sinful for sitting without excuse. The preferred view makes it permissible (mubah) but not recommended, so the person does not sin but loses the reward of standing.
The practical consequence: a person may choose to sit without incurring sin, but should prefer standing for greater reward.
