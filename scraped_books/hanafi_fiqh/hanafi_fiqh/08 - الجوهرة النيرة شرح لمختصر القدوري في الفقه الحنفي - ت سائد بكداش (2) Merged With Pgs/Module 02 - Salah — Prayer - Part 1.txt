The Hanafi Definition of Travel That Changes Rulings
Defining the travel distance that alters legal rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of travel that changes legal rulings and identify the specific rulings that are altered.

Core Idea
Three days' journey (including nights) is the threshold for travel to alter rulings in Hanafi law.

This lesson examines the foundational definition of 'travel' that the Hanafi school uses to determine when certain legal concessions apply. The source passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar sets a clear minimum distance: a three-day journey including nights. Understanding this threshold is essential because it gates provisions like shortening prayer, breaking the fast, and others. We will also explore why this chapter appears after the one on prostration of recitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definition
Locate in the Arabic text the precise phrase that defines travel. Note that it specifies 'three days and their nights' (thalāthat ayyām wa layālīhā).
2
Understand the Legal Effects
The passage lists several rulings that change once travel reaches this distance. Make a mental list: qasr al-ṣalāh, ibāḥat al-fiṭr, extension of mash, dropping of Friday/Eid/aḍḥiyah, etc.
3
Analyze the Chapter Placement
The commentator explains why this chapter follows the one on prostration of recitation. The analogy is that both have a cause (sabab) leading to an act. Recognize that worship (the recitation) is given precedence over permissible acts (travel).
4
Map the Inter-Madhhab Difference
Contrast the Hanafi three-day definition with the Shāfi‘ī two-day (or 48-mile) definition. Note that this is a legitimate ikhtilāf based on different hadith interpretations.
5
Apply the Definition
Practice using the definition: if a traveler intends a destination 2.5 days away (by the normal means of the time), does this trigger the legal changes? According to the Hanafi school, it does not because the minimum is three days.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on the prayer of the traveler from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The author first defines the travel that triggers changes in legal rulings. According to the Hanafi school, travel is defined as intending a destination at a distance of three days' journey (including their nights). This is a definition with legal consequences: certain obligations are modified or dropped. The commentator then explains the placement of this chapter after the chapter on the prostration of recitation, drawing an analogy between the two: both involve a cause (sabab) that leads to a specific act. However, prostration of recitation is worship-based, while travel is a permissible act, so worship takes precedence in ordering. The commentator then lists the rulings that change due to travel: shortening of four-rak'ah prayers, permission to break the fast in Ramadan, extension of the wipe over socks to three days, dropping of Friday prayer, Eid prayers, and sacrifice (for the one traveling), and the prohibition of a woman traveling without a mahram. The passage then returns to the definition, clarifying that the distance is measured from the traveler's place of residence (misr) to the destination.

Key Terms
سفر (safar): Travel that changes rulings is defined as a journey of three days.
مسيرة ثلاثة أيام (masīrat thalāthat ayyām): A distance that takes three days of travel by the normal means of the time.
قصر الصلاة (qasr al-ṣalāh): Shortening of four-rak'ah prayers to two rak'ahs during travel.

Definition of Travel That Alters Rulings
Defining the legal concept of travel (safar) and its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of travel (safar) that triggers changes in legal rulings, including its three conditions (intention, movement, distance of three days by shortest days) and explain why both intention and movement are required.

Core Idea
Travel that changes rulings requires both intention (qasd) and movement (sayr) for a distance of three days by the shortest days.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi definition of legal travel (safar) as presented in the chapter on the traveler's prayer. Understanding this definition is crucial because it determines when a Muslim may shorten prayers, break fast, or omit Friday prayer. The passage clarifies that travel is not mere movement but a combination of intention, movement, and a specific distance measured by the shortest days of the year. We will walk through each condition and its legal implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal cause being defined
Recognize that the passage defines 'safar' as a sabab (legal cause) that triggers multiple rulings. It is not a ruling itself but a trigger.
2
Extract the three conditions of safar
The definition includes three elements: intention (qasd), movement (sayr), and distance (masīratu thalāthat ayyām). Note that intention alone or movement alone is insufficient.
The text explicitly says 'الاعتبار باجتماعهما' (the consideration is their combination).
3
Analyze the distance measurement
The distance is 'three days' by the shortest days of the year, counting daytime only (nights excluded for rest). Continuous travel from dawn to dusk is not required; one may rest at midday and resume the next day.
Some riwayat include nights; the preferred view excludes them.
4
List the legal effects of valid travel
Identify the rulings that change due to travel: shortening prayer, breaking fast, extending masah to three days, omission of Friday/Eid/sacrifice, and requirement of a mahram for women.
These effects are mentioned but not detailed in this passage.
5
Note the flexibility in travel continuity
Travel is not required to be continuous from dawn to dusk; resting at midday is allowed. This accommodates human and animal needs.
The text cites al-Fatawa for this ruling.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on the traveler's prayer (Salat al-Musafir). The author begins by defining the type of travel that triggers changes in legal rulings (safar alladhi tata'ayyar bihi al-ahkam). He states that travel is constituted when a person intends (yaqsidu) a place that is a three-day journey away from his home city. The distance includes both days and nights according to some versions, but the preferred position is that only the days are counted, as the night is for rest. The commentator explains that the ruling of travel is not simply movement; it requires both intention (qasd) and actual travel (sayr). Mere movement without intention does not make one a traveler, and intention without movement is also insufficient. Both must be present. The days are measured by the shortest days of the year, and travel each day does not have to be continuous until night; one may rest at midday and continue the next day. This flexibility accommodates the need for rest for both the traveler and his mount. The passage also lists the rulings that change due to travel: shortening prayer, permitting fasting break, extending masah to three days, omission of Friday prayer, Eid prayer, sacrifice, and requirement of a mahram for women. Thus, this is a foundational definition of travel in Hanafi fiqh.

السفر (al-safar): Legally: the act of moving from one's residence with intention to a destination at least three days' journey away.
القصد (al-qasd): Intention or resolve to reach a specific destination; a necessary component of travel.
مسيرة ثلاثة أيام (masīratu thalāthat ayyām): A distance equivalent to three days' travel by the shortest days of the year, without counting nights.

The Two Essential Components of Legal Travel: Intention and Movement
Definition of the two components of travel: intention and movement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two essential components of legal travel (safar) in the Hanafi school and apply them to determine whether a given journey qualifies for prayer concessions.

Core Idea
Neither mere intention nor mere movement alone constitutes legal travel—both must combine.

In the Hanafi school, the concession of shortening prayer (qasr) is only permitted for a journey that meets specific legal criteria. The commentator begins by defining travel as requiring two inseparable elements: intention (qasd) to go to a specific destination and actual movement (sair). This lesson unpacks why both are necessary and how they relate.

Guided Path
1
Define the term 'qasd' as used in the text
The text says 'al-qasd huwa: al-iradatu lima azuma alayh' – qasd is the will for what one has resolved upon. Identify that it means a firm intention to go to a specific place.
Qasd is not just a vague wish but a deliberate resolve.
2
Understand why qasd alone is insufficient
The commentator argues: if a person tours the entire world without intending a specific destination three days away, he does not become a traveler. Recognize that aimless wandering does not create legal travel.
This highlights the need for a destination.
3
Understand why sair (movement) alone is insufficient
The text states that mere intention without travel is not considered. This means that if one intends to go somewhere but never actually moves, he is not a traveler.
Movement is essential to actualize the intention.
4
Apply the combination rule
The commentator concludes: 'la mu’tabara bi al-qasdi al-mujarradi ani al-sair, wa la bi al-sairi al-mujarradi ani al-qasd, bal al-mu’tabaru ijtima’uhuma.' Both must coexist.
This is the key takeaway: ijtima’ of qasd and sair.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira on the chapter of the traveler's prayer from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It defines the legal concept of 'travel' (safar) that triggers concessions in prayer (qasr). The commentator explains that travel requires both intention (qasd) to go to a specific destination and actual movement (sair). Neither alone suffices. The minimum distance is set at three days' journey by moderate camel caravan pace, counting only daytime hours, using the shortest days of the year (winter solstice). The journey need not be continuous from dawn to dusk; partial travel each day with rest is sufficient, as established in al-Fatawa. The wisdom behind the three-day period is that complete hardship—leaving one's family and staying among strangers—is realized on the second day. Travel by sea is judged by its own standard: moderate wind for three days. Comparisons between land and sea routes are treated distinctly. Finally, a textual note mentions that some old manuscripts of Al-Quduri include the phrase 'and walking on foot.'

Key Terms
قصد (qasd): Intention or resolve to go to a specific destination; a necessary component for legal travel.
سير (sair): Actual physical movement or travel; required in conjunction with qasd.

Measuring the Travel Distance: The Three-Day Rule
Explanation of the three-day measurement and related rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school measures the minimum travel distance (three days) for qasr, including the treatment of nights, shortest days, rest periods, and travel by sea.

Core Idea
Three days means daytime travel at moderate pace using shortest days, with rest considered part of travel.

Once we know travel requires both intention and movement, the next question is: how far must one go? The Hanafi school sets the minimum at a journey of three days by moderate camel caravan pace. But this measurement has nuances: only daytime counts, the shortest days of the year are used, rest stops are included, and sea travel has its own standard. This lesson unpacks these details from the commentary.

Guided Path
1
Understand that the three days count daytime only
The text says 'masiratu thalathati ayyam: ya'ni naharan, duna layaliha' – three days means daytime without nights. Identify the reasoning: 'because the night is for rest.'
This affects calculation: a journey that takes 3 full days and 2 nights is still within the limit.
2
Note the use of the shortest days of the year
The text states that 'three days' refers to the shortest days, i.e., winter solstice. This ensures that the concession is not restricted during longer days.
This is a conservative measure.
3
Recognize that continuous dawn-to-dusk travel is not required
The commentary cites al-Fatawa: if one travels each day only until noon and rests, over three days he still becomes a traveler. The rest is attached to the travel out of necessity.
This accommodates practical travel with rest.
4
Understand the wisdom behind the three-day measure
Complete hardship (kumal al-mashaqqa) is realized on the second day: leaving family on day 1, staying among strangers on day 2, and returning on day 3. This rationalizes the three-day period.
This serves as a legal ratio legis (illah).
5
Apply the rule to travel by sea
Sea travel is judged by its own standard: three days with moderate wind. Land and sea are not equated. If a place has two routes, the one taken determines the ruler.
This prevents mechanical conversion.

Travel concessions require three days' normal pace by camel—distance measured by time, not miles.

The Hanafi school uniquely defines the travel distance that triggers concessions like shortening prayer (qasr) by the time it takes to travel: three days of normal travel by camel or on foot. This lesson unpacks that definition and the wisdom behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
The minimum travel distance is three days of normal travel by camel or on foot. This is the standard for all concessions like qasr.
Also includes walking on foot as equivalent.
2
Understand the measure
'Sair al-ibil' means the pace of a normal caravan, not express mail (barid). It is the default standard.
This excludes post-horses or fast riding.
3
Analyse the rationale
The license addresses hardship of being away from family: first day departing, second day completely away, third day returning. The peak hardship is on the second day.
This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) for ease.
4
Distinguish from other schools
The Shafi'i school uses a fixed distance of 48 miles, while Hanafi uses a time-based measure. This affects whether a traveler qualifies in specific scenarios.
The difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Applications and Nuances: Routes, Sea Travel, and Fast Travel
Defining the travel distance that permits concessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi definition of travel distance to different scenarios including sea travel, mountain routes, varying travel speeds, and multiple routes.

Core Idea
Special cases: sea travel measured by steady wind; faster transport still uses normal camel pace; route choice affects ruling.

The Hanafi definition of travel distance as three days by camel/on foot is not applied rigidly. This lesson explores how it is adapted to sea travel, mountain terrain, and variable speeds, and how a traveler's choice of route can determine the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Sea travel
Sea travel is measured by three days with steady wind. If the wind is calm or stormy, the measure still assumes normal conditions.
Sea is not analogized to land; each has its own measure.
2
Mixed terrain
If a mountain route takes three days while the plain route takes less, the mountain route's measure is used if that is the route taken.
The measure is per the actual terrain.
3
Fast transport
If someone travels quickly on horseback, the measure is still the normal three-day camel pace, not the actual time. So he still qualifies for qasr if the normal distance is three days.
The measure is objective, not subjective.
4
Multiple routes
If a destination has two routes of different lengths, the ruling depends on which route the traveler chooses. Abu Hanifa ruled: if he chooses the longer (three-day) route, he shortens; if the shorter, he does not.
Intention matters, not just the actual path.

Traveler's Prayer: Shortening Four-Rakaat Fard to Two Rakaat
Stating the ruling on shortening fard prayers for the traveler

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the traveler's prayer (qasr), identify the conditions for its application, and explain the consequence of intentionally praying four rakaat.

Core Idea
Shortening four-rakaat fard to two rakaat is obligatory (fard) for the traveler; intentionally praying four is sinful, but the prayer remains valid with the first two counting as fard.

In this passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, the author lays down the fundamental rule for the traveler's prayer: the fard of every four-rakaat prayer is reduced to two rakaat. He then clarifies the scope and the consequence of violating this rule. Understanding this ruling is essential for every Muslim traveler, as it directly affects the validity of their daily prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Ruling
The passage states: 'The fard of the traveler according to us in every four-rakaat prayer is two rakaat.' This is the central rule: qasr (shortening) reduces the obligatory rakaat from four to two.
This applies to Zuhr, Asr, and Isha prayers.
2
Determine the Scope
The author restricts the ruling with two qualifications: 'four-rakaat' excludes Fajr (2 rakaat) and Maghrib (3 rakaat); 'fard' excludes sunan prayers, which are not shortened.
Thus, qasr applies only to the fard of four-rakaat prayers.

The Obligation of Qasr and Its Conditions
Ruling statement with clarification and conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the traveler's prayer, identify the conditions that trigger qasr, and explain the consequence of praying four rak'ahs with a tashahhud after two.

Core Idea
Qasr is obligatory for the traveler; intentional increase is sinful, but a proper sitting can salvage the obligatory portion.

This lesson unpacks the foundational ruling of the traveler's prayer from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The passage establishes that every four-rak'ah obligatory prayer is shortened to two rak'ahs for the traveler, and explores the conditions under which this ruling applies. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly applying qasr in daily travel.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the traveler's prayer for four-rak'ah obligations; the ruling is that the fard is two rak'ahs. Recognize that this applies only to four-rak'ah prayers (Zuhr, Asr, Isha), not to Fajr or Maghrib.
2
Understand the consequence of increase
If a traveler prays four with tashahhud after two, the first two count as fard, the last two as nafl. He is blameworthy (musi') but the fard is fulfilled. If no sitting, the prayer is invalid.
The sitting for tashahhud is the key factor that separates the obligatory from the supererogatory.
3
Determine the travel distance
Travel distance is measured by three days' normal travel (or its equivalent). Faster travel still qualifies for qasr. Abu Hanifa adds that if a town has two routes, the distance depends on which route the traveler chooses.
4
Identify the spatial condition
Qasr begins when the traveler leaves the built-up area of the town from the side of departure. He does not need to leave all sides of the city.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira is a sharh (expansion) on the matn of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, specifically the chapter on the traveler's prayer. It opens by stating the core ruling: for a traveler, every four-rak'ah obligatory prayer (like Zuhr, Asr, Isha) is shortened to two rak'ahs. This is the Hanafi position. The matn clarifies that the traveler cannot intentionally increase beyond two. The commentator explains that the restriction to 'four-rak'ah' excludes Fajr and Maghrib, which are not shortened. The restriction to 'obligatory' excludes sunan prayers, which are also not shortened. If a traveler prays four rak'ahs with the proper sitting for tashahhud after two, the first two count as his obligatory prayer and the remaining two as nafl (supererogatory), though he is considered blameworthy for delaying the salam. If he does not sit for tashahhud, the entire prayer is invalid because the supererogatory becomes mixed with the obligatory before its pillars are complete. The passage then discusses the travel distance: it is measured by three days' journey in the mountains or two days in the plain at normal pace; but if one travels faster, reaching in less time, he still shortens. Abu Hanifa adds that if a town has two routes, one longer and one shorter, the traveler's shortening depends on which he chooses. Finally, the condition for commencing qasr is to have left the built-up area of the town from the side of departure, not necessarily the whole city. The passage ends with an intra-Hanafi dispute: if one intends four while traveling, or combines two intentions (obligatory and nafl) in one prayer, Abu Yusuf says the obligatory suffices and the nafl is invalid, while Muhammad says the entire prayer is invalid because initiating two prayers simultaneously is impossible. Some say it all becomes nafl. This shows the nuance in Hanafi law regarding intention and validity in the traveler's prayer.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Intention in the Traveler's Prayer
Intra-madhhab dispute on the validity of combined intentions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the validity of combining fard and nafl intentions in the traveler's prayer, identify the mufta bihi position, and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
When a traveler intends four rak'ahs or combines fard and nafl, Sahibayn differ: Abu Yusuf validates the fard, Muhammad invalidates the entire prayer.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute on the traveler's intention. The passage presents the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani when a traveler combines intentions or prays more than two rak'ahs intentionally. Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for correctly navigating the school's rulings on the traveler's prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
The scenario: a traveler begins prayer with the intention of four rak'ahs for Zuhr, or combines the intention of two fard and two nafl. The dispute considers whether such a prayer is valid and what portion counts.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that the prayer suffices for the obligatory portion; the first two rak'ahs count as fard, and the remaining two are invalid as nafl. The fard is fulfilled.
He prioritizes the dominant intention of fard.
3
State Muhammad's position
Muhammad holds that the entire prayer is invalid because opening two prayers simultaneously is impossible. The takbir cannot initiate both fard and nafl; thus the prayer is void.
He emphasizes the impossibility of dual intentions in a single prayer act.
4
Note the third view
Some scholars hold that the entire prayer becomes nafl. This is a minority view not relied upon.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi aligns with the text's earlier ruling: if the traveler sits for tashahhud after two, the fard is valid. Therefore, in practice, the prayer is accepted if the sitting occurs, and the intention of four is not fatal. The traveler should intend only two rak'ahs to avoid dispute.
The safer course is to intend qasr from the start.

This lesson examines a specific scenario where a person's travel status changes between the performance of two prayers, and later both prayers are found invalid. Traveler Following Resident for a Missed Prayer: The Hanafi Ruling and Exceptions
Ruling on traveler following resident for a missed prayer after time with exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a traveler following a resident imam for a missed prayer (fā’ita) and identify the exceptions regarding timing and raka’t count.

Core Idea
Following a missed prayer after its time is invalid unless you entered on time or it's not four rak'ahs.

The text discusses a specific scenario: a traveler (musafir) joining a resident imam for a prayer that is overdue for both. The default ruling is that such a prayer is invalid. However, the commentator introduces critical exceptions. This lesson unpacks those nuances, including the intra-Hanafi debate on when Zuhr time ends, which determines whether the follower’s prayer is considered timely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The text states that a traveler's prayer behind a resident for a missed four-rak'ah prayer is not valid. Note that 'missed' means the prayer time has exited for both imam and follower.
2
Recognize the exceptions
The prayer may be valid if it is three or two rak'ahs, or if only the imam missed it but the follower did not. An example is given with Zuhr time difference between Abu Hanifa and the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
Follower can join if he follows Abu Hanifa's view that Zuhr ends at mithl, even if imam follows the two's view that it ends at mithlayn.
3
Understand the time distinction
The prohibition applies only if the follower enters after the prayer time has exited for him. If he enters within his own time and time exits later, the prayer remains valid.
The key is the time of entry, not the time of completion.
4
Apply the analogous example
The text gives an example: a traveler following a resident in Asr, and the sun sets right after his takbir. He must complete four rak'ahs because he entered within time. Contrast this with a resident praying one rak'ah of Asr and a traveler joining after sunset – that joining is invalid.

Traveler Leading Residents in Prayer
Ruling on traveler leading residents and how they complete

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a traveler leads residents in prayer and explain why residents complete individually without recitation.

Core Idea
A resident's fard does not change when following a traveler; they complete missed rak'ahs individually without recitation.

This lesson examines a specific scenario: a traveler acting as imam for residents. The ruling might seem counterintuitive, but it rests on a key principle about the nature of obligation (fard) and how a follower (muqtadi) whose fard differs from the imam is treated. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi approach to congregational prayer when the imam and followers have different obligatory rak'ah counts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The passage describes a traveler (musafir) leading residents (muqimun) in prayer. The traveler's fard is two rak'ahs, while the residents' fard is four.

State the Ruling
The traveler prays two rak'ahs with the congregation, then says salam. The residents then complete their prayer individually (وحداناً) without reciting Qur'an in the extra rak'ahs.

The proof is a general principle (asl): a resident following a traveler is valid both within the prayer time and after it has expired, because the resident's fard (four rak'ahs) does not change. This principle is based on qiyas: the imam's act does not alter the follower's obligation.
The dalil is 'aqli (rational) rather than textual, hence 'dalil_aqli'.
4

The text contrasts this with a traveler following a resident: that is only valid if the prayer time remains. This shows the rule is not symmetrical.
The reason: the traveler's fard is reduced to two only if travel conditions are met; following a resident might imply completion, which is incompatible after travel ends.

If a traveler leads a group of residents in 'Asr prayer, the same ruling applies: the traveler prays two, the residents complete four individually without reciting. This applies even if the prayer is made up later (qada) for the traveler.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition that Friday prayer is valid only in a misr jami' and explain the hadith proof.

Core Idea
Friday prayer is valid only in a large city – the hadith proves it.

This lesson extracts a key condition for the validity of Friday prayer from the Hanafi text. The author links this to the previous chapter on travel, noting both use an intermediary. The core ruling is simple: Friday prayer is not valid outside a major congregational city (misr jami'). The proof is a hadith that explicitly restricts these prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its conditions
Read the matn: 'لا تصح الجمعة إلا في مصر جامع'. Determine the ruling: Friday prayer is sahih (valid) only with this condition. Note that the condition is 'misr jami', not merely any gathering.
The negation 'لا' plus 'إلا' indicates a restrictive condition.
2
Locate the evidence (dalil)
Find the hadith: 'لا جمعة ولا تشريق، ولا فطر إلا في مصر جامع'. Identify the source mentioned: Muhammad ibn al-Hasan in al-Asl, Athar of Abu Yusuf, and others.
The discussion about chain (marfu', gharib, mauquf) is secondary; the ruling is established.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Understand why the hadith proves the ruling: The construction 'لا... إلا' is used for restriction (hasr). It limits the permissibility of Friday, Eid al-Adha, and Eid al-Fitr to a misr jami'.
This is a standard usul principle: 'al-hasr yufid al-shartiyyah'.
4
Consider the epistemological status
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear (qat'i al-dalalah). The Hanafi school relies on it because it is supported by practice and other evidences.
Despite some weakness in chain, the ruling is firmly held.

Core Idea
Jumu'ah is only valid in a major congregational city or its legal extensions.

Jumu'ah is a fard 'ayn with strict location conditions. This lesson unpacks the primary ruling that it is only valid in a 'miṣr jāmi'' (congregational city), supported by a hadith, and extended to the prayer ground and open areas within voice range. Understanding this condition is essential to avoid performing an invalid Friday prayer.

Guided Path

Identify the act and its ruling

The Hanafi school treats this as a strong restriction.

The ruling extends to the prayer ground (muṣallā) of the city because it has the same legal status, and to all open spaces (afniyah) of the city within the range of voice and adhan from the city center.
