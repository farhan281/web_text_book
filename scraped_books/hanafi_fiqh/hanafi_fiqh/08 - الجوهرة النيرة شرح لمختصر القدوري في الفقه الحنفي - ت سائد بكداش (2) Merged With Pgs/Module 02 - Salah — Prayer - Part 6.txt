Time Limits for Funeral Prayer at the Grave
Intra-Hanafi dispute on time limit for grave prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the time limit for funeral prayer at the grave and explain why the preferred view relies on decomposition rather than fixed days.

Core Idea
The time to pray at the grave ends when the body decomposes, not when a fixed number of days has passed.

When a person is buried without the funeral prayer, the prayer can still be offered at the grave, but only within a limited period. The Hanafi school debated whether this period is fixed—three days in winter, seven in summer—or depends on the actual state of decomposition. This lesson explores the two views and the reasoning behind the preferred position, which is based on the variability of decomposition rates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is performing the funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) at the grave after burial. It is allowed only within a certain window. Identify the primary ruling: it is permitted, but time-limited.
This is a fard kifayah.
2
Locate the two Hanafi views
The matn (Quduri) states fixed days: three in winter, seven in summer. Al-Hidayah states the criterion is tafassukh (decomposition) without fixed days. Identify each view and its source.
The fixed days are based on typical decomposition rates per al-Ayni.
3
Examine the proof for each view
For fixed days: al-Ayni explains that winter ground warmth speeds decomposition, summer coolness slows it. For tafassukh: al-Hidayah argues that decomposition varies with the deceased's body type, climate, and soil, so a fixed number is unreliable.
The proof for the preferred view is aqli (rational) based on observable variation.
4
Understand the tarjih (preference)
Al-Hidayah's view is declared 'as-sahih' (correct). The later Hanafi school relies on tafassukh as the operative criterion. Note that the fixed days can serve as a guideline but not a binding limit.
The tarjih is based on the strength of the rational proof.
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the body decomposes before three days, prayer is invalid even within the fixed window. If it remains intact after seven days, prayer may still be valid. This affects when the obligation drops.
The mufta bihi is the tafassukh criterion.

Exhuming the Body for Washing and Repeating Prayer
Ruling on forgotten washing after burial

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on exhuming a buried body when washing was forgotten, and explain the role of istihsan in this case.

Core Idea
If washing was forgotten before burial and soil has not been poured, the body is exhumed for washing; after pouring soil, prayer is repeated at the grave by istihsan.

Sometimes after burial, it is discovered that the deceased was not washed before the funeral prayer. This lesson examines the Hanafi remedy. The ruling distinguishes between two situations: whether soil has been poured over the grave or not. In the first, exhumation is required for proper washing and repetition of prayer. In the second, exhumation is avoided, and only the prayer is repeated at the grave by way of istihsan (juristic preference), because the original prayer was invalid due to missing the purification (washing), and now washing is no longer possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The scenario: The deceased was buried after the funeral prayer, but it is later discovered that the body was not washed. The ruling depends on whether soil has been poured (ihalah al-turab) into the grave.
Two subcases.
2
Analyze the first subcase: soil not poured
If the soil has not yet been poured, the body should be exhumed, washed, and the funeral prayer repeated. This is because washing is a necessary condition (taharah) for the prayer, and it is still possible.
Exhumation is permissible for a valid reason.
3
Analyze the second subcase: soil poured
If the soil has been poured, the body is not exhumed. Instead, the prayer is repeated at the grave. This is by istihsan (juristic preference), because the original prayer was invalid due to missing purification while it was possible, but now that possibility has ended.
The obligation of washing falls (sakatat fardiyyah al-ghusl).
4
Understand the reasoning behind istihsan
Istihsan is applied to avoid the hardship of exhuming a body after the grave is sealed. The fard of washing is considered dropped because it is no longer feasible without excessive disturbance. Therefore, only the prayer is repeated to fulfill the remaining obligation.
This is a practical concession.
5
Note the legal implication
The original prayer is invalid because a condition (taharah) was missing. The repetition at the grave is a new prayer, not a completion of the first. The obligation of washing is waived due to impossibility.
This shows the role of istihsan in adjusting rulings based on changed circumstances.

Opening the Funeral Prayer: Takbir and Thanā'
States the first part of the funeral prayer: say takbir and then praise Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the first two actions of the Hanafi funeral prayer and identify the thanā' recited after the first takbir.

Core Idea
The funeral prayer begins with takbir; immediately after, praise Allah with the thanā'.

This lesson focuses on the very beginning of the funeral prayer (ṣalāt al-janāzah). According to the Hanafi school, the prayer is initiated by saying 'Allāhu Akbar' (takbīrat al-tahrīm), and then, without delay, the worshipper praises Allah with the thanā'—the same opening praise used in the ordinary prayer. Understanding this opening sequence is essential because it sets the foundation for the remaining takbīrāt and supplications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the passage describes the first part of the funeral prayer: saying takbir and then praising Allah. The funeral prayer as a whole is fard kifāyah (communal obligation).
The passage only covers the opening; the full prayer includes four takbīrāt and specific supplications.
2
Learn the sequence
The text explicitly says: '...say a takbir, praises Allah immediately after it.' The order is: first takbir → thanā' (praise). The commentator clarifies that the thanā' is 'Subḥānaka Allāhumma wa biḥamdika...'.
Use the same thanā' as in regular prayer.
3
Understand the legal status
In the Hanafi school, the takbīrat al-tahrīm and the subsequent three takbīrāt are pillars (arkān) of the funeral prayer. The thanā' after the first takbir is sunnah (recommended), not obligatory. However, the matn emphasizes it as part of the description, highlighting its importance.
If one omits the thanā' unintentionally, no compensation is required; the prayer is valid.
4
Memorize the thanā' text
Commit to memory: 'Subḥānaka Allāhumma wa biḥamdika, wa tabārakasmuka, wa ta'ālā jadduka, wa lā ilāha ghayruk.' This is recited after the first takbir in the funeral prayer.
This is the standard thanā' used in all Hanafi prayers.

Procedure of the Funeral Prayer: First Two Takbirs
Describes the first two takbirs with praise and salawat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prescribed recitations after the first two takbirs of the funeral prayer and explain the order based on Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The funeral prayer consists of four takbirs; the first is followed by praise, the second by salawat.

In the funeral prayer, we stand and recite specific supplications after each takbir. This lesson covers the first two takbirs: the praise of Allah (thana) and the blessings on the Prophet (salawat). Understanding this sequence ensures the prayer is performed correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its parts
Recognize that the funeral prayer is a sequence of four takbirs, each followed by a specific recitation while standing. There is no ruku' or sujud.
2
Locate the first takbir and its attached recitation
The first takbir is followed by praising Allah (thana). The standard wording is 'Subhanaka Allahumma wa bihamdika...' (the same thana used in the start of daily prayers).
The thana is a form of glorification.
3
Locate the second takbir and its attached recitation
The second takbir is followed by sending salawat upon the Prophet ﷺ. The prescribed wording is the same as in the tashahhud: 'Allahumma salli 'ala Muhammad...'
4
Note the reasoning behind the order
The commentator explains that praising Allah is followed by salawat, as seen in the khutbah and tashahhud. This order reflects a general principle of beginning with divine praise before invoking blessings.
This is an analogy (qiyas) based on other acts of worship.
5
Recognize the overall structure
Understand that after the third takbir, a supplication for the deceased is recited, and after the fourth, a general supplication. The prayer concludes with taslim. This lesson focuses only on the first two takbirs.
Further details are covered in later lessons.

Decomposition and Burial Without Washing: A Flexible Hanafi Approach
Ruling on decomposition and burial without washing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when to omit the funeral prayer due to decomposition and apply the procedure if burial occurred without washing.

Core Idea
Decomposition time is not fixed; it depends on body and environment. After soil is poured, exhumation is forbidden, but prayer on the grave suffices.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses two practical issues: when to skip the funeral prayer due to decomposition, and what to do if a body was buried without being washed. The Hanafi school stresses real-world variability over arbitrary fixed periods, and uses istihsan (juristic preference) to overcome impossibility.

Guided Path
1

The Fourth Takbir and Taslim in Janazah: Ruling and Dispute
Ruling with proof on fourth takbir and taslim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the fourth takbir and taslim in funeral prayer and explain why additional supplications after the fourth takbir are not part of the relied-upon practice.

Core Idea
After the fourth takbir, only the salam; no extra dua is permitted per the apparent madhhab.

The funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) consists of four takbirs. Between the takbirs, specific supplications are recited. The fourth takbir is followed immediately by the salutation (taslim). This lesson clarifies the exact ruling on what is said after the fourth takbir, the evidence for the dua before the fourth takbir, and the intra-Hanafi difference about adding Quranic verses after the fourth takbir. Understanding this precision protects the prayer from innovation and ensures conformity with the established sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the fourth takbir in the funeral prayer. The ruling is that immediately after it, one gives the taslim (salutation) without any intervening supplication. This is a sunnah practice, and the relied-upon Hanafi position (mufta bihi) is that no extra words are added.
Some later scholars allowed reciting Quranic verses, but the apparent madhhab (zahir al-madhhab) rejects this.
2
Locate the dalil for the dua before the fourth takbir
The text provides the wording of the dua recited after the third takbir (the standard dua for the deceased). The hadith from Tirmidhi and Nasa'i is cited: 'O Allah, grant life to those of us whom You keep alive upon Islam, and take the souls of those of us whom You take upon faith.' This proves the sunnah dua for an adult sane person.
The dalil is a hadith ahad (zanni al-thubut) but still authoritative for practice.
3
Examine the conditions for the dua
If the deceased is a mature sane adult, the above dua is recited. If the deceased is a child or insane, a different dua is recommended: asking Allah to make the child a 'preceding provision' and an accepted intercessor. If one does not know any dua, a general phrase 'O Allah, forgive us, our parents, him, and all believers' suffices.
All supplications are to be said quietly (mukhafata), as the sunnah of dua is lowering the voice.
4
Analyze the ruling on saying anything after the fourth takbir
The matn (Quduri) states: 'Then he makes the fourth takbir and gives salutation.' The sharh (commentary) explicitly says: 'And he does not make any supplication after it.' Some mashayikh (e.g., from the later period) deemed it good to recite certain Quranic verses (e.g., 'Rabbana atina fid-dunya hasanah'). However, the commentator overrides this by stating 'the apparent madhhab is that he should say nothing after it except the salutation.'
This is a classic case of intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved by Zahir al-Riwayah.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: If one follows the view of the mashayikh, they would recite a verse after the fourth takbir before the salam. If one follows the apparent madhhab (mufta bihi), they would immediately say the salam. The mufta bihi is to follow the apparent madhhab and avoid any added supplication.
This ensures unity in practice and avoids potential bid'ah.

Disliked Materials for Grave Construction: The Hanafi Position on Baked Bricks and Wood
Expansion on the ruling of disliked materials for grave construction from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why baked bricks and wood are disliked, evaluate the refined illah offered by al-Sarakhsi, and identify when necessity overrides the dislike.

Core Idea
Baked bricks strengthen the grave; a grave should decay, not endure.

The matn states that baked bricks (ājurr) and wood are disliked. The commentator then unpacks the reasoning, refutes an alternative cause, and presents exceptions where necessity permits them. This lesson focuses on the ta'lil and the intra-Hanafi discourse.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its basis
Baked bricks and wood are makruh tanzih in grave construction. The original reasoning: they strengthen construction, which is not befitting for the grave as a place of decay.
2
Examine the alternative illah and its refutation
Some said dislike is because fire touched the bricks (ill omen). Al-Nihayah refutes this: washing the deceased with hot water also involves fire, so it cannot be the cause.
3
Apply al-Sarakhsi's refinement
The actual cause is the combination of ajurr and wood for solidity, not fire alone. Wood alone does not have the same effect.
4
Recognize exceptions and necessity
Scholars of Bukhara permit ajurr when soil is weak. Muhammad ibn al-Fadl permits iron coffins. Tamartashi distinguishes: ajurr next to the body is disliked, but above the brick layer is permissible as protection.

Rulings on Condolences (Ta'ziyah)
Explains timing, wording, and preferable time for condolences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the timing and wording of ta'ziyah and explain why it is sunnah muakkadah.

Core Idea
Condolences are sunnah within three days, better after burial, and must not renew grief.

This passage from Mukhtasar al-Quduri explains the proper etiquette of offering condolences in the Hanafi school. It covers when to offer them, what to say, and the evidence from the Prophet's practice. The ruling is sunnah muakkadah, not obligatory, but carries great reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the time frame and exception
Note that ta'ziyah is prescribed from death until three days, and it is disliked after that because it renews grief. However, if the mourner or comforter was absent, it is permitted after three days.
The exception is based on the need to comfort when grief is still fresh.
2
Compare before vs after burial
Understand that offering condolences after burial is better than before, because the family is busy with preparation before burial and their loneliness is greater after burial.
This is the default ruling unless extreme grief is observed.
3
Note the condition about extreme grief
If the family shows extreme grief (jaza' shadid), condolences should be offered earlier to calm them, even before burial.
This prioritizes comfort over the general preference for after burial.
4
Study the recommended wording
Learn the recommended Arabic phrasing and the superior wording of the Prophet ﷺ as recorded in Sahih Muslim.
The prophetic wording emphasizes tawheed and patience.
5
Examine the hadith evidence
The hadith 'Inna lillahi ma akhadh...' is the proof for the recommended manner. Understand its meaning: all belongs to Allah, and the deceased had a fixed term.
The hadith is used to encourage patience and seeking reward.

Istihlal and Its Legal Effects
Defines signs of life after birth and their legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the signs of life (istihlal) for a newborn and apply the Hanafi rules for funeral rites based on whether the infant cries or not.

Core Idea
Only voluntary, stable movements after complete separation qualify as istihlal — involuntary twitches are disregarded.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi definition of istihlal (crying out) for a newborn. The distinction between voluntary and involuntary movements determines whether full funeral rites are performed or simply wrapping in cloth. We explore the conditions for life at birth and the intra-Hanafi difference on washing a stillborn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the text and note the two categories: (1) the infant who cries out (mustahill) and (2) the infant who does not cry out (stillborn). State the legal consequence for each: full funeral rites vs. wrapping in cloth only.
These rulings are based on the presence or absence of istihlal.
2
Define istihlal precisely
From the commentary, list the specific actions that constitute istihlal: crying, screaming, sneezing, yawning, or any voluntary movement indicating stable life. Also note what does not count: convulsions, hand movements of the slaughtered animal (harakat al-madhbuh).
The key is that the movement must be voluntary and indicate life is stable.
3
Identify the condition of complete separation
The text stresses that life must be present at the time of complete separation from the mother. If the head emerges and the infant cries but the rest is stillborn, the infant is not considered alive.
This condition affects inheritance and funeral rites.
4
Apply the ruling to the stillborn scenario
For an infant who does not show istihlal: wrap in a cloth, do not pray over him, and (according to the correct view) do not wash him. Note the two narrations on washing.
The commentator declares the correct position (sahih) as not washing.
5
Resolve the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
State the two narrations on washing the stillborn: one says wash, the other says do not wash. Identify the mufta bihi (operative ruling) as not washing, per the commentator’s tarjih.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school; the preferred view is based on the absence of signs of life.

Washing the Stillborn: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on washing the stillborn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three Hanafi positions on washing a stillborn and state the mufta bihi according to al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school has multiple transmissions on washing the stillborn; the chosen position is to wash, though the apparent transmission says not.

This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the ghusl of a stillborn. The text presents three views: the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwaya), the view of al-Tahawi, and the preferred view from al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling for stillborn
The matn says the stillborn is wrapped in a cloth and not prayed upon. It does not mention washing. The commentary then discusses washing separately.
The omission of washing in the matn is the basis for the dispute.
2
Identify the three positions on washing
The text says: 'Regarding washing: there are two transmissions; the correct (apparent) opinion is that he is not washed. Al-Tahawi said: he is washed. In al-Hidayah: he is washed, in other than the apparent transmission, and it is the chosen one.' So there are three: 1) No washing (zahir al-riwaya), 2) Washing (Tahawi), 3) Washing as chosen (al-Hidayah).
Note that al-Hidayah's view is called 'al-mukhtar' (the chosen), which is often the mufta bihi.
3
Understand the concept of zahir al-riwaya vs nawadir
Zahir al-riwaya refers to the most widely transmitted positions from the founding imams. The other transmission (Tahawi's view) is a nawadir position. Al-Hidayah prefers the nawadir view.
This shows that the mufta bihi can deviate from the zahir al-riwaya when later scholars choose a different view.
4
Determine the operative ruling (mufta bihi)
According to this text, the mufta bihi is to wash the stillborn, as stated in al-Hidayah, because it is called 'al-mukhtar'.
However, some may follow the apparent transmission; the mufti should be aware of the weight of each opinion.

Testimony on the Newborn's Life: Prayer vs Inheritance
Testimony rules for prayer and inheritance regarding newborn's life

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when the testimony of the mother and midwife is accepted for funeral prayer versus inheritance, and explain the ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The midwife's testimony is accepted for prayer but not for inheritance according to Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn accept it for inheritance if she is just.

This lesson examines the rules of witness testimony regarding the newborn's life. The text distinguishes between two legal contexts: the right to perform funeral prayer (haqq al-salat) and the right to inherit (mirath). The mother's testimony is rejected for inheritance by consensus due to bias, while the midwife's testimony is disputed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts for testimony
The text mentions two contexts: 'in the right of prayer upon him' and 'in the right of inheritance'. These have different rules.
Prayer here refers to the janazah prayer; inheritance refers to receiving a share of the estate.
2
State the rule for prayer
Both the mother and the midwife's testimony about the child's cry (istihlal) is accepted for the purpose of offering the funeral prayer.
The reason is that prayer is a worship act and lesser evidence suffices.
3
State the rule for inheritance regarding the mother
The mother's testimony is rejected by consensus because she is accused (mutahama) of bias—she stands to inherit or not inherit depending on the child's life.
Consensus here refers to the Hanafi school or broader.
4
State the rule for inheritance regarding the midwife
Abu Hanifa rejects the midwife's testimony for inheritance, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad accept it if she is just (adilah).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, if only the midwife testifies, no inheritance; under the Sahibayn, if she is just, inheritance is granted.
This shows the practical consequence of the difference.

Defining the Martyr (Shahid) in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition of martyr with conditions and exclusions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a martyr, identify the categories included (highway robbers, rebels), and apply the conditions to distinguish genuine martyrdom from accidental or self-inflicted death.

Core Idea
A martyr is one killed by polytheists in battle, including their equivalents, but not from accidents or flight.

In this passage, the classical Hanafi commentator defines the martyr (shahid) and clarifies the boundaries of this legal status. While the basic idea is well-known, the precise conditions matter greatly in practice: not every Muslim death in war counts as martyrdom. The author expands the definition to include highway robbers and rebels, and then sets clear limits excluding accidental deaths from animal shying or self-inflicted flight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
Read the opening: 'الشهيدُ: مَن قَتَلَه المشركون' – the martyr is one killed by the polytheists. This is the foundational element.
The definition is explicit: it must be the polytheists (mushrikun) who cause the death.
2
Understand the expansion via qiyas
The text adds 'وفي معنى المشركين: قطاع الطريق، والبغاة' – highway robbers and rebels are legally analogous. Recognize this as an analogical extension (qiyas).
The common factor: they are unlawful combatants who fight against Islam.
3
Examine causation scenarios
The author specifies that killing can be direct (e.g., sword) or indirect (تسبباً بحديد أو غيره). Then he gives two specific cases: enemy's animals trampling, and a Muslim's horse shying. The key is whether the enemy's action was the direct cause.
Indirect causation is included as long as it originates from enemy action in battle.
4
Analyze the exclusions
Two scenarios are explicitly not martyrdom: (a) when a Muslim's horse shies from the enemy's appearance or banners without enemy provocation, and (b) when Muslims flee and jump into a trench or from a wall, unless the enemy physically forces them. This clarifies that accidental or self-initiated death does not qualify.
The enemy's active role is essential: if the death stems from the Muslim's own action or the animal's reaction without enemy instigation, it is not martyrdom.
5
Derive the operative legal principle
From the definition and the exclusions, deduce the ruling: for martyrdom, the killing must be directly or indirectly caused by the enemy's combat action (including their animals under their control), and not a result of accidental circumstances or the Muslim's own desperate act.
This principle guides fatwa in similar cases (e.g., friendly fire, landmines, etc.).

Prayer in the Kaaba: Hanafi vs. Maliki on Obligatory Prayer and Orientation of Followers

Core Idea
Prayer inside the Kaaba is valid for fard and nafl; followers must face the same qibla as the Imam.

This lesson addresses a classic inter-madhhab dispute: can obligatory prayer be performed inside the sacred Kaaba? The Hanafi school says yes; the Maliki school restricts it to supererogatory prayer. Additionally, we examine the conditions for followers when an Imam leads prayer inside the Kaaba, a scenario that tests our understanding of qibla orientation and congregation rules.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The passage opens with 'الصلاة في الكعبة جائزة، فرضها، ونفلها' — prayer in the Kaaba is permissible, both obligatory and supererogatory. This is the ruling from the Hanafi school.
This applies to all types of prayer.
2
Identify the Maliki objection
Imam Malik is reported to restrict prayer inside the Kaaba to supererogatory (nafl) only, arguing that obligatory prayer (fard) requires facing the entire Kaaba, which is impossible when inside.
The Maliki position is based on the necessity of proper qibla orientation for fard prayers.
3

The Hanafi response is that the imperative to face the qibla is satisfied by facing any part of the Kaaba; the structure itself constitutes the qibla direction. This is supported by the general sanctity of the Kaaba (Surah Al-Imran 3:97).
Thus the Hanafi school sees no distinction between fard and nafl in this regard.
4
Derive the practical consequence
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that a Hanafi may pray fard inside the Kaaba, while a Maliki must pray fard outside and may only pray nafl inside.
This difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Ummah.
5

The passage also rules: if an Imam prays inside the Kaaba with a congregation, followers who place their backs to the Imam's back (i.e., face the same direction) have valid prayer. Those who face the opposite direction (backs to Imam's face) have invalid prayer due to incorrect orientation.
This condition applies within the Hanafi school regardless of the fard/nafl dispute.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the ruling of prayer in relation to the Kaaba. The base matn (from Al-Quduri) states two rulings: (1) If a worshipper is closer to the Kaaba than the imam but on a different side, the prayer is valid; the commentary explains that the prohibition of being ahead of the imam only applies when they are on the same side. Ruling on Praying Above the Kaaba
Ruling on prayer when above the Kaaba's height

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on prayer when above the Kaaba and identify the direction one must face.

Core Idea
When above the Kaaba, face its airspace, not the building itself.

This lesson addresses a key spatial question: what direction does a person face when praying from a position higher than the Kaaba? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that the prayer is valid as long as one faces the airspace (hawa') directly above the structure. This principle applies to any elevated platform, mountain, or building above the Kaaba's height.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Find the core legal statement: 'if one prays on a high target above it (the Kaaba), it is similarly (valid).'
The ruling is an extension of the previous discussion about prayer inside the Kaaba.
2
Understand the term 'hadaf'
Recognize that 'hadaf' refers to any elevated object—mountain, sand dune, or building—that is higher than the Kaaba.
The term is broad; the key point is vertical elevation above the Kaaba.
3
Determine the required direction
Know that for someone above the Kaaba, the qibla is the airspace (hawa') of the House, not the physical walls.
This is the distinctive Hanafi position; other schools may differ.
4
Apply the rule
In any scenario where a person is positioned above the Kaaba's height, they must intend and face the airspace directly above the Kaaba.
The prayer is valid if this condition is observed.
