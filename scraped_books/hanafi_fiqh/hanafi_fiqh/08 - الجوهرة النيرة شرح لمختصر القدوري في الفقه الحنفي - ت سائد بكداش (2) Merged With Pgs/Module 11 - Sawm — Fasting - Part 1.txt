The Definition of Sawm and Its Legal Framework
Introduction to the Book of Fasting with definitions and ordering rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal (shar'i) definition of sawm and identify its four essential specifications according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Legal sawm is not just abstaining from food: it requires specific abstention, person, time, and intention.

The Book of Fasting begins with a definition that distinguishes the linguistic meaning from the legal one. This passage also explains why the author placed fasting after zakat in the text, following the order of the Qur'an and the hadith of the five pillars. Understanding the precise legal definition is crucial, as it determines the conditions and pillars that make a fast valid or invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's rationale for chapter order
Note that the author delayed the Book of Fasting after Zakat even though both are bodily acts of worship. He followed the sequence in Qur'an 2:43 and the hadith of the five pillars. This shows deference to scriptural order.
The hadith is mentioned but not fully quoted; the reference is to the well-known hadith of the five pillars.
2
Distinguish linguistic from legal definitions
Linguistically, sawm means any abstention (imsak) at any time. The Quranic example of Maryam (19:26) illustrates this. Legally, it is a specific abstention with four restrictions.
Maryam's 'sawm' was abstention from speech, not from food.
3
List the four specifications of legal sawm
The legal definition includes: (1) abstaining from the two appetites (stomach and private parts), (2) from a person free from menstruation and post-childbirth bleeding, (3) during the time from dawn to sunset, and (4) with the intention of drawing near to Allah.
Each specification is a condition for validity in the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the definition to a scenario
Given a person who abstains from food and drink all day but eats just before sunset, or who fasts without intention from the night, determine whether the fast is valid according to this definition.
Intention from the night is required for obligatory fasts in the Hanafi school.

The Legal Definition of Fasting and Its Types
Opening of Kitab al-Sawm: definition and classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of fasting (sawm) and identify its four essential restrictions, and distinguish between obligatory and voluntary fasting.

Core Idea
Fasting in shari'a is a specific abstention with four conditions: person, time, content, and intention.

This lesson opens the Book of Fasting with the Hanafi legal definition. The author explains why he places fasting after zakah, citing Quran and hadith. He then gives the linguistic definition and the four restrictions that transform it into a legal act of worship. Understanding these restrictions is essential for valid fasting.

Guided Path
1
Understand the arrangement
The author delayed fasting after zakah even though it is a bodily worship like prayer, following the order in the Quran (Al-Baqarah 43) and the hadith of the five pillars.
This shows the Hanafi method of relying on textual order (tartib) as a guide for arrangement.
2
Distinguish linguistic vs legal definition
Linguistically, sawm means absolute abstention (imsak) from anything, anytime. Legally, it becomes a specific abstention (imsak makhsus) with four restrictions.
The Quranic verse on Maryam (19:26) shows the linguistic usage meaning abstention from speech.
3
Identify the four restrictions
The legal definition specifies: (1) content – abstaining from the two desires (eating/drinking and sexual intercourse); (2) person – must be free from menstruation and postpartum bleeding; (3) time – from dawn to sunset; (4) intention – with the purpose of drawing near to Allah.
Each restriction is necessary; missing any invalidates the fast.
4
Note the two categories
Fasting is divided into wajib (obligatory) and nafl (voluntary). The former includes Ramadan and other obligatory fasts; the latter includes supererogatory fasts.
In strict Hanafi terminology, Ramadan is fard, but the author uses 'wajib' broadly.

Obligatory Fasting: Types and Intention Timing
Legal classification of obligatory fasts and timing of intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between time-specific and dhimmah-based obligatory fasts and state the Hanafi ruling on intention timing for time-specific fasts.

Core Idea
A time-specific obligatory fast can be intended before noon; a dhimmah-based fast requires night intention.

In this passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, the commentator distinguishes two categories of obligatory fasting: those tied to a specific time (like Ramadan) and those that are liabilities in the dhimmah (like expiations). The central ruling discussed is the timing of the intention (niyyah). According to the relied-upon Hanafi position, if one fails to intend a time-specific fast at night, the intention made before noon (zawal) still renders the fast valid. This lesson will unpack this classification and the intention rule, including the difference of opinion within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of obligatory fasts
The text divides obligatory fasts into time-specific (e.g., Ramadan, specified vow) and dhimmah-based (absolute vows, expiations, qada). Note the distinction: time-specific fasts are attached to a particular time; dhimmah-based fasts are obligations in the liability.
2
State the intention requirement for time-specific fasts
According to the text, it is permissible to intend a time-specific fast at night. If one misses the night intention, an intention made before noon (zawal) suffices and the fast is valid.
This applies to Ramadan and mu'ayyan nadhr only, not to dhimmah-based fasts.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa permits the intention before noon; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) require night intention for all obligatory fasts. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's view.
The text implicitly follows Abu Hanifa as the madhhab position.
4
Contrast with dhimmah-based fasts
For dhimmah-based fasts (absolute vows, expiations, qada), intention must be made at night; there is no allowance for before noon.
This is a key practical difference.
5
Summarize the practical impact
A person who forgets to intend a time-specific fast at night can still save the fast by intending before noon. This ruling provides ease (taysir) in observing obligatory fasts tied to specific times.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on the classification of fasting. It divides obligatory fasting into two subtypes: (1) those tied to a specific time (zaman mu'ayyan) such as Ramadan and a vowed fast that has been specified to a particular day, and (2) those that are absolute liabilities in the dhimmah (fi al-dhimmah) not tied to a specific time, such as unlimited vows, expiations (kaffarat), and making up missed Ramadan (qada'). This is a common tasawwuf categorization. Finally, the text returns to the matn, repeating the two-category division and further subdividing the wajib into mu'ayyan (specific) and mursal (absolute in the dhimmah), with the ruling on intention.

النذور المطلقة (al-nudhur al-mutlaqah): A vowed fast that is not specified to a particular time; it is a liability in the dhimmah.
الزوال (al-zawal): Noon; the time when the sun passes its zenith.

The Three Spiritual Degrees of Fasting
Spiritual classification of fasting into three degrees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the three spiritual degrees of fasting (sawm al-umum, al-khusus, and khusus al-khusus) as presented in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
Fasting has three spiritual degrees: abstaining from the permissible, from sins, and from all but God.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a spiritual classification of fasting beyond the legal obligations. Common in tasawwuf literature, it outlines three levels of discipline: physical restraint, moral restraint of the limbs, and total heart-oriented devotion. While not legal categories, these degrees guide the believer toward greater taqwa. This lesson will help you internalize the progression from outer to inner fasting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three degrees
The text names three degrees: صوم العموم (fast of the general public), صوم الخصوص (fast of the elite), and صوم خصوص الخصوص (fast of the elite of the elite).
These are not legal but spiritual ranks.
2
Define each degree
The first degree is abstaining from eating, drinking, and marital relations. The second adds abstaining from all sins committed by the limbs (e.g., backbiting, looking at haram, idle talk). The third involves purifying the heart from worldly concerns and focusing entirely on Allah.
3
Notice the progression
Each degree builds on the previous: the outer actions of the first, the ethical behavior of the second, and the inner state of the third. This reflects the Islamic concept of ihsan.
4
Relate to the legal fast
A legally valid fast fulfills the first degree. The second and third degrees elevate the fast from a mere physical act to a comprehensive spiritual practice.
The legal ruling (sihha) requires only the first degree; the others are recommended.

Timing of Intention for Time-Specific Obligatory Fasts: Hanafi Position and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Classifies fasting and details intention timing for wajib with fixed time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the permissible timing of intention for time-specific obligatory fasts and explain the inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Intention for time-specific obligatory fasts can be made until before noon, not only at night.

Fasting in Ramadan is an obligation, but when exactly must we form the intention? The Hanafi school takes a facilitative approach, allowing intention to be made from night until half the day. This lesson unpacks the classification of fasting, the evidence for the timing rule, and the disagreement with the Shafi'i school. Understanding this difference is crucial for both practice and scholarly literacy.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling: intention for time-specific wajib fasts (like Ramadan) can be made from night until before noon (half the day from dawn to greater forenoon).
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that intention must be made at night before dawn, based on a hadith.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Al-Shafi'i's evidence: the hadith 'Whoever does not intend to fast before Fajr, there is no fast for him.'
This hadith is considered authentic by many.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school interprets that hadith as applying to liability fasts (dayn) or as a recommendation, not as a strict requirement for 'ain fasts due to necessity and ease.
They also cite the principle that intention must exist for most of the day.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: a Hanafi can intend upon waking before noon; a Shafi'i must intend before Fajr, even if he wakes late.
This affects validity of the fast and potential make-up.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar opens the Book of Fasting. The author first classifies fasting into obligatory and voluntary, then further divides obligatory into two categories: those tied to a specific time (like Ramadan and specified vows) and those that are a debt in the liability (like unpecified vows, expiations, and make-up fasts). This delay is allowed only for ‘ain fasts (specific-day) and not for dayn fasts (liability). It is recommended to intend at night to avoid disagreement. The passage also discusses cases of unconsciousness and intending for future days. Finally, the three degrees of fasting—general (physical), specific (limbs from sins), and specific of specific (heart from worldly concerns)—are mentioned, though they pertain more to spirituality than fiqh.

Time of Niyyah for Voluntary Fasting: Before Midday
States the Hanafi view on the permissibility of niyyah before midday and the dispute with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits niyyah before midday for voluntary fasts and state the disagreement with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
For voluntary fasts, niyyah is valid before midday; most of the day must be accompanied by intention.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah addresses the timing of niyyah for voluntary fasting, presenting the Hanafi view that intention can be made up to midday (forenoon), and contrasts it with the Shafi'i requirement of night intention. Understanding this dispute clarifies the flexibility in Hanafi fiqh for non-obligatory fasts.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling from Al-Jami' al-Saghir: niyyah before half of the day is sufficient. Define 'half of the day' as from dawn to forenoon (al-dahwah al-kubra), not noon.
The temporal definition is specific to Hanafi fiqh.
2
Understand the rational proof
The reasoning: intention must exist for the majority of the day. Therefore, if niyyah is made before midday, most of the day is performed with intention.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) supported by necessity.
3
Compare with the opposing school
Imam al-Shafi'i requires niyyah from the night (before fajr). Identify the thamarah al-khilaf: the validity of a fast when niyyah is made after fajr.
The Hanafi school allows the delay, Shafi'i does not.
4
Apply the ruling with nuance
Note that the permissibility applies only to specific (ayn) voluntary fasts, not to debt (dayn) fasts like qada of Ramadan. Also, the recommended practice is to intend at night to avoid disagreement.
The mufta bihi is the permissibility before midday, but mustahab is night intention.

Presents rulings on timing and unconsciousness for fasting niyyah

Core Idea
The niyyah for fasting must be made at night and with clear intent; unconsciousness after intention validates only the first day.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah presents several key rulings on the niyyah (intention) for fasting. The Hanafi school places great emphasis on the proper timing and specification of niyyah. Here, the author addresses three important cases: fasting after unconsciousness, intending before sunset, and the correct way to intend during the day. Understanding these conditions is essential for the validity of one's fast.

Contrast with the ruling for supererogatory fasts, where day-niyyah is allowed from the time of intention.

Commentary
This passage presents several independent rulings related to niyyah for fasting, collected as a series of legal statements. First, if one intends at night but becomes unconscious (maghma 'alayh) in the morning, his fast for that first day is valid because the intention was made, but subsequent days are invalid as he could not renew intention. Second, intending the next day's fast before sunset is invalid because the intention must be made after the time for that day begins. Third, if one intends during the day, he must intend to be fasting from the beginning of the day; if he intends only from the moment of intention, the fast is invalid. The definition of niyyah is then given as knowledge in the heart of which fast one is performing. It is sunnah to verbalize the intention, with sample phrasings for night and day. A key discussion follows on saying 'in sha Allah' (if Allah wills) in the intention: by qiyas (analogy), this would invalidate the intention because an exception (istithna') nullifies the statement, as in contracts or divorce. This distinction is recorded in Al-Dhakhirah. Finally, intending to break the fast does not break it until one actually eats or drinks, analogous to intending to speak in prayer without speaking—such intentions do not nullify the act.

The Effect of Istithna' on Intention and the Principle of Istihsan
Discusses the effect of istithna' on fasting intention and cites qiyas vs istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school validates an intention that includes 'in sha Allah' using istihsan, and apply the distinction between acts of the heart and acts of the tongue.

Core Idea
Saying 'in sha Allah' in the intention does not invalidate the fast because intention is a heart action, unaffected by mere words.

This section of the passage addresses a common practical issue: saying 'in sha Allah' (if Allah wills) when making the intention to fast. By strict analogy (qiyas), this would invalidate the intention, as it does in contracts and divorce. However, the Hanafi school applies istihsan (juristic preference) to rule that the intention remains valid. The key distinction is that intention is an act of the heart, not the tongue, and the phrase 'in sha Allah' here is a prayer for divine help, not a genuine condition. This lesson examines the reasoning and the wider principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
The issue is whether saying 'in sha Allah' in the fasting intention invalidates it. The text presents two opposing views: qiyas says it invalidates, istihsan says it does not.
2
Understand the qiyas argument
By analogy with verbal contracts (sale, divorce, manumission), where istithna' nullifies the statement, the intention should be invalidated because it involves words.
This is the default qiyas position.
3
Analyze the istihsan argument
The Hanafi school prefers istihsan because the phrase 'in sha Allah' in intention is not a genuine exception but a request for divine assistance. Moreover, intention is an act of the heart, not the tongue, so it is not affected by verbal utterances.
The text provides the distinction: istithna' is an act of the tongue that invalidates rulings of the tongue; intention is of the heart, so it remains valid.
4
Apply the ruling to other cases
The same logic applies to intending to break the fast: until one actually acts, the intention does not break the fast. Similarly, intending to speak in prayer without speaking does not invalidate prayer.

And his statement: 'Or one who has intercourse forgetfully: he does not break his fast. If he remembers and withdraws immediately, he does not break his fast.' Similarly, if he had intercourse before dawn and then at dawn he withdraws immediately. And if he had intercourse forgetfully, then remembers and remains (without withdrawing), upon him is qada (make-up) but no kaffarah. In al-Khujandi: 'If he had intercourse forgetfully, then remembered and withdrew immediately, or dawn broke while he was in contact and he withdrew: Muhammad said in both cases: he does not break his fast. The distinction for Abu Yusuf is that the end of the action is considered by its beginning; at dawn the beginning is intentional so it invalidates his fast, while in forgetfulness the beginning is with forgetfulness so it does not invalidate. Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of forgetful intercourse near dawn. It begins by stating the basic rule: if a person has intercourse forgetfully and withdraws immediately upon remembering, his fast is not broken. The same applies if he is engaged before dawn and withdraws at dawn. If he forgetfully has intercourse, remembers, but does not withdraw, then he must make up the fast (qada) but no expiation (kaffarah). If he mistakenly thinks this intercourse broke his fast and then deliberately eats, he only owes qada, not kaffarah. Then a dispute is reported from al-Khujandi: regarding the two cases (forgetful withdrawal and dawn withdrawal), Muhammad says both do not break fast; Zufar says both break; Abu Yusuf distinguishes: forgetful does not break, dawn does break. Muhammad's reasoning is that the withdrawal is a minor unavoidable act, so it is exempted. The relied-upon view is likely Muhammad's, as it is the most lenient and consistent with the principle of avoiding hardship.

Key Terms
جامع ناسياً (jaama'a naasiyan): Had sexual intercourse while forgetting he is fasting.
نزع (naza'a): Withdrew (from intercourse).
الكفارة (al-kaffaarah): Expiation for deliberately breaking a Ramadan fast (freeing a slave, etc.).
