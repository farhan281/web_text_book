Shaving Cupping Sites During Ihram: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on shaving cupping sites with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the compensation for shaving cupping sites during ihram and apply the criterion of 'prohibition from all aspects' to distinguish between acts requiring dam versus sadaqah.

Core Idea
Shaving cupping sites: Abu Hanifa requires dam; the Sahibayn require sadaqah — the distinction hinges on the extent of prohibition.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the expiation for a muhrim who shaves the area where cupping glasses are applied. Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students differ on whether the compensation is a dam (sheep) or sadaqah (charity). Understanding their reasoning — especially the concept of 'prohibition from all aspects' — is key to applying the correct ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Dispute
Determine the specific act under discussion: a muhrim shaving the sites where cupping glasses (mihjamah) are applied on the neck. Note that the dispute is about the kind of expiation required.
The sites are described as the two sides of the neck and between the shoulders.
2
Extract Each Imam's Position and Proof
State Abu Hanifa's position: dam (blood sacrifice). State the Sahibayn's position: sadaqah (charity). Identify the implicit proofs: Abu Hanifa considers the act prohibited 'from all aspects', analogous to shaving the head of a non-muhrim; the Sahibayn consider it a lesser violation.
The Karkhi commentary clarifies that acts prohibited 'from all aspects' require heavier expiation.
3
Analyze the Criterion of 'Prohibition from All Aspects'
Understand the talil: The severity of the expiation depends on whether the prohibited act is completely (min jami' al-wujuh) prohibited or only partially. Shaving hair from any part of the body during ihram is prohibited, but the extent of the prohibition determines the compensation.
Dressing a muhrim in a shirt is not prohibited from all aspects, so no expiation is required.
4
Determine the Practical Difference (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
The practical consequence: a muhrim who shaves cupping sites must decide whether to offer a sheep (dam) or give charity (sadaqah). The fatwa often follows the stronger view, but both are recorded.
If the entire neck is shaved, there is consensus that dam is required.

Cutting Nails During Ihram: Disputes on Quantity and Compensation
Rulings on cutting nails with disputes on quantity and compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for cutting nails during ihram, distinguish between sadaqah and dam based on quantity and distribution, and explain the reasoning behind the intra-Hanafi disputes.

Core Idea
Cutting nails of one full hand or foot requires a dam; fewer than five scattered nails require sadaqah.

This lesson examines the detailed rulings on a muhrim cutting their nails, a common point of confusion. We will see how the quantity of nails cut and whether they are from one limb or scattered across limbs determines the required compensation. We will also explore the differences among Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Zufar, and understand the qiyas-based reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its basic ruling
The first ruling states that cutting all nails of both hands and feet requires one dam. Cutting one hand or one foot also requires a dam, analogized to shaving a quarter of the head.
This establishes the baseline: the punishment is tied to whole limbs or quarters.
2
Note the exception for multiple sessions
If cutting is done in separate sessions, Imam Muhammad holds one dam is due unless the expiation is interrupted, while the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) require a dam per session if each session involves a full hand or foot.
The disagreement hinges on whether the act is considered one continuous violation or multiple.
3
Analyze the rule for fewer than five nails
Cutting less than five nails requires sadaqah of half a sa' per nail. If the total sadaqah reaches the value of a dam, it is reduced by half a sa'. Muhammad and Zufar have alternative views.
This shows the principle of proportional compensation for minor violations.
4
Examine the dispute over five scattered nails
For five scattered nails, the two require sadaqah, while Muhammad requires a dam, comparing it to shaving a quarter of the head. The text justifies the two's view by rejecting the majority analogy.
The core dispute: does the 'quarter rule' from head shaving apply to nails?
5
Evaluate Zufar's minority view
Zufar argues that three nails from one hand constitute a majority of that hand, and thus a dam is due. The Hanafi response is that the original obligation is for the whole hand, not a majority of nails, so the majority analogy fails.
This illustrates how qiyas can be contested when the underlying 'illa is not shared.

The Three Options for Fidyah for a Muhrim with an Excuse
States the ruling and its proof for three fidyah options

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three fidyah options for a muhrim with an excuse and apply the conditions for each option, including the intra-Hanafi difference on sadaqah.

Core Idea
Three wajib options for fidyah: slaughter, charity, or fasting — each with distinct conditions.

A muhrim who applies perfume, shaves, or wears sewn clothes due to an excuse (e.g., illness) must offer an expiation. The Quran provides a choice between slaughtering a sheep, feeding six poor persons, or fasting three days. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and the details of each option, including an intra-Hanafi dispute on how to fulfill the charity option.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Determine what the muhrim did (perfume, shaving, wearing sewn clothes) and that it was due to an excuse. The ruling is that fidyah is wajib with a choice between three options: slaughter, charity, or fasting.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verse (2:196) is the primary proof. Identify the specific words that indicate obligation and choice.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'fafidyah' indicates wujub per Hanafi usul. The disjunction 'aw' (or) between the three options establishes the choice.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow both tamlik (transfer of ownership) and ibaha (feeding) for sadaqah; Muhammad requires tamlik. Identify the mufta bihi (majority view).
5
Identify the practical consequence for slaughter
The slaughter (dam) must be performed in the Haram by consensus; after slaughter, if the meat is stolen or destroyed, no further obligation remains.

Cutting Defective Nails in Ihram: Sahibayn's Talil
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nail-cutting expiation with talil reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when a muhrim owes a dam versus sadaqah for cutting nails, based on the condition of the nail and the Sahibayn's talil.

Core Idea
Defective nails in ihram reduce the offense: comfort and beautification determine the expiation.

The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explores a nuanced ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school regarding nail-cutting in ihram. While the general rule prohibits cutting nails, the Sahibayn introduce a talil (ratio legis) based on the presence of comfort and beautification. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its practical consequences for broken or hanging nails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and general prohibition
Recognize that cutting nails is generally prohibited for a muhrim, and the default expiation is a dam. However, the Sahibayn qualify this based on the nature of the nail.
The general prohibition is rooted in hadith.
2
Understand the Sahibayn's talil
The Sahibayn argue that the completeness of the offense (requiring a dam) depends on achieving comfort and beautification. Cutting a defective nail brings no comfort and disfigures, so the offense is lessened.
This is a qiyas: asl = intact nail, far' = defective nail, illa = comfort/adornment.
3
Apply the talil to defective nails
When the nail is broken or hanging and cutting it offers no benefit, the expiation is reduced from dam to sadaqah (food for one poor person per nail), up to the value of a dam.
The sadaqah is wajib according to the Sahibayn.
4
Examine the exception for non-growing nails
If a nail is broken and has no potential for growth (e.g., it is loose and would not grow back), pulling it incurs no expiation at all. The text analogizes it to dry wood in the Haram.
If the nail could grow if left, then sadaqah is required.
5
Distinguish the two positions
Abu Hanifa's implied position is that any nail-cutting requires a dam, while the Sahibayn differentiate. The text only presents the Sahibayn's view, leaving tarjih open.
Later practice may lean toward the Sahibayn for defective nails.

Commentary
This passage addresses the situation where a pilgrim misses stoning one of the three jamarat (i.e., does not throw all seven pebbles at that jamrah). The ruling is that for each missed pebble (hasah) he owes a sadaqah, which is half a sa' of food. If the total number of missed pebbles would reach the value of a dam (i.e., the equivalent of one animal), then he is not required to give a full dam; instead, he reduces the calculation to half a sa' per pebble. The commentator explains that a full dam is not obligatory because the stoning of all three jamarat on a single day constitutes one single rite (nusk wahid), so missing only part does not trigger the full penalty.

Rulings on Leaving Stoning in Hajj and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Delay
States penalties for leaving stoning and discusses timing and ikhtilaf on delay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings for leaving stoning in Hajj and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on delaying stoning until after the days of Tashriq.

Core Idea
Leaving stoning requires blood or charity; delaying the make-up adds blood only in Abu Hanifa's view.

This lesson covers the penalties for missing the stoning ritual during Hajj, a wajib act. The text presents three clear rulings and then delves into an important intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the effect of delaying the make-up stoning. Understanding these rulings is essential for any mufti or pilgrim advising on Hajj violations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three omission scenarios and their penalties
Read the passage to list the three cases: leaving all days, leaving one day, leaving one jamrah. Note the specific penalty for each: dam (blood) for the first two, sadaqah (charity) for the third.
The penalty for all days is only one dam because the stoning is one genus.
2
Understand the exception for weakness and fear
The text states that if a person is weak, ill, or a woman fears crowding, no penalty is due. This is a concession (rukhsah) from the original requirement.
This exception applies to the obligation of stoning itself, not just the penalty.
3
Analyze the timing and the delayed make-up
The passage explains that 'leaving' is only established after sunset of the last day of stoning (the 13th). If the stoning is made up before that, no penalty. If delayed, Abu Hanifa requires blood; the Sahibayn do not.
This shows the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the ruling of delaying a missed wajib.
4
Examine the ruling for leaving one jamrah
For missing a single jamrah (e.g., one set of pebbles), the penalty is sadaqah per pebble, not a full dam. This is because the whole day's stoning is considered one ritual.
If the sadaqah reaches the value of a dam, one subtracts half a sa'.
5
Summarize the Hanafi positions and the mufta bihi
The text presents a clear ruling for the three scenarios, but on the issue of delay, there is a known ikhtilaf. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in later works often follows Abu Hanifa, requiring blood for delay.
The Sahibayn's view that no blood is required is also recorded and may be followed in cases of necessity.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on violations in Hajj by listing three rulings on leaving the stoning ritual. First, leaving stoning of all jamarat on all days requires one blood sacrifice (dam) because the genus of the act is unified. The forgetfulness or delay is considered only after sunset of the last day (the 13th of Dhul Hijjah); before that, the stoning can be made up in order. However, if one delays the stoning until after the days, Abu Hanifa holds that a blood sacrifice becomes obligatory, while the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree. Second, leaving stoning of an entire day is considered a complete ritual (nusk tamm) and thus requires a blood sacrifice. If the missed stoning is made up the same night, no penalty; but if made up the next day, Abu Hanifa requires blood, while the two say no. Third, leaving stoning of one of the three jamarat (i.e., not all pebbles) requires charity (sadaqah) for each missed pebble: half a sa' of wheat or its value. If the total reaches the value of a blood sacrifice, one subtracts half a sa' (i.e., pays less than a full dam) because the entire day's stoning is considered one ritual unit. The comment at the beginning notes an exception: if a person is weak, ill, or a woman fears crowding, no penalty.

Key Terms
دم (dam): A blood sacrifice (sheep or goat) required as compensation for omitting a wajib act in Hajj.
صدقة (sadaqah): Charity given in compensation for minor omissions in Hajj, specifically half a sa' of wheat or its value per missing pebble.
رمي (ramy): The act of throwing pebbles at the jamarat during the days of Tashriq.
