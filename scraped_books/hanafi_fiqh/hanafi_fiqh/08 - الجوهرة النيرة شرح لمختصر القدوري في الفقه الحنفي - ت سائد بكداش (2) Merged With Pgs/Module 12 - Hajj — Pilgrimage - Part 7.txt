Omitting Stoning of Jamrat al-Aqabah on Yawm al-Nahr: Graded Rulings
States ruling on omitting Jamrat al-Aqabah with gradations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the expiation for omitting stoning of Jamrat al-Aqabah on Yawm al-Nahr and distinguish between missing the entire stoning, most, or few pebbles.

Core Idea
Stoning Jamrat al-Aqabah is the defining function of Yawm al-Nahr; omission requires graded expiation.

The stoning of Jamrat al-Aqabah on the 10th of Dhul Hijjah is a distinct wajib. Unlike the three jamarat stoned on subsequent days, it is tied to the Day of Sacrifice itself. This passage details the graded consequences if one omits it partially or entirely, providing a clear framework for expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is stoning Jamrat al-Aqabah on Yawm al-Nahr. It is wajib; omitting it triggers expiation.
The ruling is based on the day's function (كل وظيفة هذا اليوم رمي).
2
Distinguish complete vs. partial omission
Complete omission requires a dam. Leaving most (the majority) also requires a dam. Leaving one, two, or three pebbles requires charity (half a sa' per pebble).
If the charity would reach the value of a dam, one reduces the amount.
3
Understand the delay ruling
Delaying the stoning from Yawm al-Nahr to the next day requires a dam.
This is separate from the omission ruling.
4
Distinguish omission on other days
If one misses stoning Jamrat al-Aqabah on a day other than Yawm al-Nahr (e.g., 11th or 12th), only charity is due.
Because the primary obligation is only on the 10th.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Delaying Halq and Tawaf al-Ziyarah
States ruling on delaying halq and tawaf with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding whether delaying halq or tawaf al-ziyarah requires a blood sacrifice, and identify which types of pilgrims are affected.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires a blood sacrifice for delay; Sahibayn do not; both restrict this ruling to mutamatti' and qarin only.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira presents a key intra-Hanafi difference on the consequences of delaying two major Hajj rituals — halq (shaving) and tawaf al-ziyarah (obligatory circumambulation) — beyond their prescribed time. It also extends the dispute to sequence violations and clarifies that the ruling hinges on whether the pilgrim is obligated to offer a sacrifice (dhabh). Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for accurate fatwa-giving in Hajj-related matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed rulings
Read the passage and note that it discusses two scenarios: delaying halq after the days of Nahr, and delaying tawaf al-ziyarah. Also note that the same difference applies to delaying stoning and to sequence violations (like shaving before stoning).
The dispute is about whether a blood sacrifice (dam) becomes wajib due to delay or sequence violation.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that if a pilgrim delays halq or tawaf al-ziyarah beyond the days of Nahr, a blood sacrifice is required. The same applies if one performs rituals out of sequence.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that no blood sacrifice is due in any of these cases. They consider the acts still valid without penalty.
The text says 'wa 'indahuma la shay'a 'alayhi fi al-wajhayn' — according to them, nothing is due in both cases.
4
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is that a muqallid (follower) of Abu Hanifa would need to offer a dam, while a muqallid of the Sahibayn would not. Both views are preserved in the school.
This difference affects the cost and ritual obligations of the pilgrim.
5
Identify the scope of the ruling
The passage explicitly states that this entire discussion applies only to the mutamatti' (one performing Umrah then Hajj) and the qarin (one combining Umrah and Hajj) because the slaughter (dhabh) is wajib upon them. The mufrid (Hajj only) is excluded because he has no slaughter obligation.
Therefore, even if a mufrid delays halq or tawaf, no dam is required according to either view.

Definition of Sayd and Ruling on Killing It in Ihram
Defines sayd and states the ruling on killing it in ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define sayd (game) according to the Hanafi school and state the ruling that killing it while in ihram requires compensation.

Core Idea
Game is a wild terrestrial animal that escapes by legs or wings; killing it in ihram demands compensation.

This lesson unpacks the precise Hanafi definition of sayd (game) used in the context of Hajj prohibitions. The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies what counts as game through a series of exclusions, and it affirms that killing any such animal while in ihram incurs a wajib compensation (jaza'). Understanding this definition is essential for correctly applying the rulings on hunting during pilgrimage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage begins by stating that if a muhrim kills game, full compensation is due. Note that the verb 'kill' is used instead of 'slaughter' because slaughtering implies ritual halal slaughter, which is not applicable here as the animal becomes carrion.
2
Parse the Core Definition
The definition lists five elements: (1) the animal must be 'unattainable' (imtina') by legs or wings; (2) it must be wild in its original creation; (3) it must be terrestrial; (4) it can be edible or inedible; (5) owned and free game are treated the same. Work through each element and its exclusion.
Each element excludes a category: e.g., 'unattainable' excludes dogs and cats.
3
Apply the Exclusions
For each element, identify which animals are excluded: (a) imtina' excludes dogs, cats; (b) by legs/wings excludes snakes, scorpions; (c) wild excludes chickens, ducks; (d) original creation excludes domestic animals that become wild; (e) terrestrial excludes sea game.
4
Note the Scope
The definition includes both edible and inedible animals, and it explicitly states that owned game (e.g., a captive deer that escaped) is treated the same as free game for the prohibition.
This means even if the animal belongs to someone, a muhrim may not kill it.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Penalty for Killing Monkey and Pig in Ihram
States rulings on predatory animals as game and intra-Hanafi dispute on monkey/pig.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Zufar regarding the penalty for killing a monkey or a pig while in ihram, and explain the rationale behind Zufar's exemption of the pig.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf requires penalty for monkey and pig; Zufar exempts pig because killing it is recommended (mandūb).

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute about which animals require a penalty (jazā') if killed by a muhrim. While all predatory animals are generally considered game, the status of the monkey and pig is contested. Abu Yusuf treats them as ordinary game, but Zufar argues that the pig is exempt because Islamic law recommends killing it due to its impurity and harm. Understanding this dispute clarifies how exceptions to general rules are derived.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage first states a general principle: all predatory animals (sibāʿ) are considered game (ṣayd). For a muhrim, killing game is prohibited and requires a penalty. Understand this baseline.
This is the default Ḥanafī position.
2
Locate the specific dispute
The author then narrows to the monkey and pig. Note that the text says 'They differed concerning the monkey and the pig' – this indicates an ikhtilāf within the school.
The dispute is between Abū Yūsuf (d. 182/798) and Zufar (d. 158/775), both senior students of Abū Ḥanīfa.
3
State each position
Abū Yūsuf holds that both the monkey and pig incur a penalty (al-jazāʾ). Zufar holds that the pig does not incur a penalty, but the monkey does. Also note that Zufar includes the ḍabb, jerboa, and barm as requiring penalty.
Abū Yūsuf’s view is that the pig is game; Zufar’s view is that it is not game because killing it is recommended (mandūb).
4
Analyze Zufar’s basis (dalīl)
Zufar argues that the pig is excluded from game because it is mandūb (recommended) to kill it. This is an exception based on the principle that harmful or impure animals may be killed even in iḥrām, and no penalty applies because such killing is not considered hunting (ṣayd).
The recommendation (mandūbiyya) overrides the general prohibition on hunting.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilāf)
The practical difference: under Abū Yūsuf’s view, a muhrim who kills a pig must pay a penalty; under Zufar’s view, no penalty is due. For the monkey, both agree penalty applies. This affects rulings for Hajj/ʿUmrah and also for general fiqh on obligations.
The conflict is real: if a muhrim kills a pig, should he/she owe compensation? The muftī must choose one opinion.

In this passage, the author explains how the penalty (jaza) for hunting while in ihram is calculated and applied. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the game is valued at the location of killing by two just persons, based on its meat value only. Compensation for Harming Game During Ihram
Listing rulings on compensation for various harms to game.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the compensation required for various forms of harm to game during ihram and explain the concept of hayyiz al-imtina'.

Core Idea
Harm to game that removes it from immunity (hayyiz al-imtina') triggers full value; partial harm triggers partial compensation.

A pilgrim in ihram must avoid hunting or harming game. But what if damage occurs accidentally? The Hanafi school provides detailed rulings on compensation (qimah) based on the extent of harm. This lesson covers key scenarios from al-Jawhara al-Nayira: plucking feathers, cutting legs, breaking eggs, milking, shearing, and causing abortion. The central concept is 'hayyiz al-imtina' (state of immunity) – once the game loses its ability to escape, full compensation is due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of harm
List the specific act: plucking feathers, cutting legs, breaking eggs, milking, shearing, striking to cause abortion. Each has a specific compensation rule.
Some acts remove the game from hayyiz al-imtina', others do not.
2
Determine if the harm removed hayyiz al-imtina'
If the game loses its ability to escape (e.g., legs cut so it cannot run, feathers plucked so it cannot fly), full value is due. Otherwise, only the value of the part lost.
Hayyiz al-imtina' is defined as the state where game can defend or escape.
3
Apply the compensation rule
Full qimah for removal from hayyiz al-imtina'; value of part (milk, wool, egg) if not removed. Exception: spoiled eggs require no compensation.
In abortion cases, pay value of fetus as alive, and mother if she dies.
4
Note the basis of compensation
Compensation is wajib because the muhrim has caused loss of property (the game belongs to no one but has sanctity during ihram). The value replaces the lost benefit.
This is a penalty, not a sale price.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Eating Game After Paying the Penalty
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether eating game after paying penalty requires extra compensation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the two disciples regarding a muhrim who eats game after paying the penalty, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires additional compensation for eating game after paying penalty; the two disciples do not, and their view is adopted for fatwa.

After a muhrim pays the penalty (jaza') for hunting game, can he eat from it? This seemingly simple question led to an intra-Hanafi dispute. Imam Abu Hanifa argued that eating requires an additional payment of the game's value, citing the Quranic verse 'that he may taste the consequence of his deed' (5:95). His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagreed, reasoning that the slaughtered animal is carrion and eating carrion imposes no extra penalty. The later Hanafi tradition prefers the disciples' view as more practical.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The issue is whether a muhrim who has already paid the penalty for game (jaza') must also pay the value (qimah) of what he eats from that game.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a disagreement with other schools.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his proof
Abu Hanifa: yes, additional qimah is due. Proof: Quran 5:95 'liyadhuqa wabala amrihi' – he must taste the consequence; if no extra payment, he would not fully taste it.
He also argues that he has already benefited from the game.
3
State the position of the two disciples (Sahibayn) and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: no extra payment. Proof: the slaughtered game is carrion (maytah); eating carrion does not incur a penalty. Analogy: if he fed it to dogs, no liability.
They consider the game's meat no different from any other carrion.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the opposing proof
Abu Hanifa counters that the verse emphasizes tasting the consequence; dropping the guarantee would nullify that. Also, the dog analogy fails because ihram prohibition is specific.
The response highlights the purpose (ta'lil) of the verse.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf and the tarjih
Practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the muhrim pays extra; under Sahibayn, he does not. The mufta bihi is the view of the two disciples due to practicality and avoiding multiplied penalties.
Al-Marghinani and later authorities prefer the disciples' view.

Series of rulings on harming game animals and the intra-Hanafi dispute on eating from the penalty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether a muhrim who pays the penalty for game and then eats from it owes additional compensation, and explain the proofs used by Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: eating from the penalty adds a new obligation; the Sahibayn: the meat is carrion, no extra liability.

After paying the penalty for hunting game, can a muhrim eat from the compensation meat without further liability? This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples on this precise question. The disagreement turns on how we interpret the Quranic purpose of the penalty and whether qiyas to feeding dogs is valid.

Guided Path
1
Extract the general rule for harming game
Identify that the passage establishes liability for any part of game (milk, wool, fetus). Understand that the muhrim owes the value of whatever he damages.
This sets the context: compensation is due for all forms of harm to game.
2
Identify the specific question and the two positions
Pinpoint the scenario: after paying the penalty (e.g., by sacrificing a sheep), the muhrim eats from it. Abu Hanifa says he must pay the value of what he ate; the Sahibayn say nothing extra is owed.
The word 'ثُمَّ أَكَلَ مِنْهُ' marks the disputed case.
3
Follow the Sahibayn's argument
Understand their reasoning: the slaughter by a muhrim produces carrion, and eating carrion does not incur a penalty. They also analogize to feeding the meat to dogs, which has no liability.
This is a qiyas jaliy: the common element is that the meat is carrion.
4
Analyse Abu Hanifa's rebuttal
Abu Hanifa cites Quran 5:95: 'that they may taste the consequence of their deed.' He argues that if the offender is not liable for eating, he would not truly 'taste' the consequence, as he would gain benefit from his own act. He rejects the analogy to dogs because dogs are not responsible beings.
The rebuttal turns on the purpose (ta'lil) of the verse.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf and the consensus on eating before paying
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa requires an additional payment; the Sahibayn do not. The passage also notes that if the muhrim eats before paying, the value is included in the penalty by consensus, limiting the dispute to after payment.
This consensus (ijma') shows the dispute is narrow.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira is a commentary (sharh) on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, covering the chapter on offenses during Hajj. It presents a series of rulings regarding damage to game animals while in ihram, followed by an intra-Hanafi dispute on eating from the compensation. The commentary opens by establishing that any part of a game animal—such as milk or wool—has value, and the muhrim must pay compensation for it. If a muhrim strikes a pregnant gazelle causing miscarriage, he pays the value of the fetus alive (since its death might be from the blow). If the mother also dies, he pays for both. If he kills a pregnant gazelle, he pays its full value as pregnant. The main dispute arises when the muhrim pays the penalty (e.g., by slaughtering a sacrificial animal) and then eats from it. Abu Hanifa rules that he must also pay the value of what he ate, citing Quran 5:95, which states that the penalty is meant for the offender to 'taste the consequence.' If he is not liable, he would not taste the consequence. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) hold that nothing further is owed, because the slaughter by a muhrim is considered carrion, and eating carrion does not incur a separate penalty; they analogize it to feeding the meat to dogs, which incurs no liability. The commentary then addresses eggs: if a muhrim cooks eggs (destroying their potential life), he owes their value; but if he eats them after being liable, no additional payment is required because the liability was for destroying the benefit of the potential chick, not for the act of eating. Finally, it notes that if the muhrim eats from the slaughtered animal before paying the penalty, there is consensus that the value of what he ate is included in the overall penalty, though some say this is also subject to disagreement.

Key Terms
جزاء الصيد (jaza' al-sayd): The penalty for hunting game while in ihram, typically a sacrificial animal or its value.
ميتة (maytah): Carrion; an animal that died without proper Islamic slaughter, considered impure and unlawful to eat.

Penalties for Killing Lice and Locusts in Ihram: Rulings and Conditions
Rulings and commentary on killing insects and small animals during Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the penalties for killing lice and locusts while in ihram, and identify the conditions that trigger or exempt a penalty.

Core Idea
Lice incur sadaqah because they arise from the body; locusts incur sadaqah because they are game.

In this lesson from al-Jawhara al-Nayra, we explore the nuanced rulings on killing insects and small animals during Hajj. While most insects carry no penalty, lice and locusts require charity (sadaqah). Understanding the conditions—such as where the louse is taken, intent, and indirect actions—is essential for a muhrim to fulfil his obligations correctly.

Guided Path
1
Classify the animal
Identify whether the creature is considered game (sayd) or non-game. Mosquitoes, fleas, ticks are non-game and incur no penalty. Lice are special due to body origin; locusts are considered game.
Game animals include monkeys, elephants, spiny-tailed lizards; they incur the full jaza' (animal sacrifice).
2
State the penalty for lice
For killing a louse taken from the body, head, or garment, charity is required. The amount depends on the number: one louse → charity as one wishes; two–three → handful of food; more than three → half a sa' of wheat.
If the louse is taken from the ground and killed, there is no penalty.
3
Identify the 'illah for lice
Lice are not game but arise from the body like hair. Killing them removes dishevelment (sha'th), which is prohibited for a muhrim. Hence, sadaqah is wajib as a compensation.
This reasoning (ta'lil) distinguishes lice from other insects.
4
Apply to indirect actions
If a muhrim hands lice to another to kill, points at them, or puts clothes in the sun/washes them with intent to kill, he incurs the penalty. If the intent is absent (e.g., drying clothes), no penalty.
The ruling follows the principle of direct causation and intent.
5
State the ruling for locusts
Killing a locust requires charity as one wishes. A date is better than a locust, based on a tradition of Umar illustrating the virtue of giving even small charity.
Locusts are considered game, so the penalty is sadaqah, not the full jaza'.

Core Idea
Original creation determines wildness; temporary domestication does not change the ruling for a muhrim.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies key terms for birds and then explains a ruling for a muhrim who kills certain animals. Understanding the distinction between original wildness and temporary domestication is essential to avoid confusion in Hajj prohibitions.

Both animals are wild in their original creation (asl al-khilqah); domestication is accidental and temporary.

Any animal wild by origin remains game even if temporarily tamed. The key is the original state, not current behavior.

Killing Game in the Haram: Penalty vs. Expiation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sacrifice for muhrim's kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the compensation for a non-muhrim and a muhrim who kills game in the Haram, and state the two narrations on the sufficiency of a sacrifice.

Core Idea
A non-muhrim pays a penalty; a muhrim's kaffarah can be fulfilled by fasting, but the sacrifice is disputed.

The Haram of Mecca has a heightened sanctity that affects rulings on hunting. This passage clarifies that the compensation differs based on whether the hunter is in ihram or not. It also presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on the form of expiation for a muhrim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage distinguishes between a non-muhrim (halal) and a muhrim (one in ihram) who kill game within the Haram boundaries. First, state the ruling for each.
Non-muhrim: penalty (gharamah). Muhrim: kaffarah.
2
Analyse the non-muhrim ruling
For the non-muhrim, the text says he must pay the value of the game and give it in charity; fasting does not suffice. Understand why: it is a financial guarantee, not a kaffarah.
The term 'gharamah' indicates a compensatory fine.
3
Analyse the muhrim ruling
For the muhrim, the kaffarah is fulfilled by fasting. Note the reasoning: the sanctity of the Haram does not add to the ihram's restrictions, so only the hunting prohibition applies.
Kaffarah for hunting while in ihram is normally fasting, feeding, or sacrifice.
4
Explore the intra-Hanafi dispute
The text mentions two narrations regarding whether a sacrifice (hadi) suffices for the muhrim's kaffarah in the Haram. One narration requires the meat's value to equal the game's value; the other presumably allows it without condition.
The dispute is within the Hanafi school, attributed to Imam Muhammad.
5
Apply the practical consequence
If the value condition is applied, the muhrim must ensure the meat of the sacrifice is worth as much as the game killed. Otherwise, fasting remains the safe option.
Later Hanafi works may prefer one view, but the text leaves it open.

Ruling on joint liability of non-muhrims for killing game in Haram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on joint liability of two non-muhrims for killing game in the Haram and explain the reason (illah) based on analogy to financial liability.

Core Idea
Two non-muhrims share one penalty for killing game in Haram because their liability is like joint financial liability.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling within the chapter of Hajj: the liability of two individuals who are not in the state of ihram (non-muhrims) when they jointly kill game within the sacred precincts (Haram). Unlike muhrims, who each bear full liability, the Hanafi school holds that non-muhrims collectively owe only one penalty. This distinction is rooted in the nature of liability, which the text compares to financial liability (damān al-amwāl). Understanding this ruling helps clarify the broader principles of compensatory obligations (damān) in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actors and the act
The passage speaks of 'two non-muhrim persons' (ḥallān) who 'share in killing' (ishtarakā fī qatl) game in the Haram. Note that they are not in ihram.
Non-muhrim means they are not observing the prohibitions of ihram; they could be residents or visitors who are not in a state of pilgrimage.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that 'upon them both is one penalty' (fa-ʿalayhimā jazāʾun wāḥid). They are jointly liable for a single expiation, not separate penalties.
This applies only when the killing is joint; each contributed to the act.
3
Analyze the reason (illah)
The text provides the rationale: 'because liability runs in the manner of financial liability' (li-anna al-ḍamān yajrī majrā ḍamān al-amwāl). In financial liability, multiple tortfeasors share the compensation; similarly here, the penalty is shared.
This analogy (qiyās) is central: the ruling is derived by comparing the liability for game to liability for property.
4
Distinguish from muhrims
In contrast, if the killers were muhrims, each would be liable for a full penalty because the sanctity of ihram adds an individual obligation. The passage implies this difference by specifying 'non-muhrims'.
This distinction is crucial for applying the correct ruling in practice.

If they killed separately, each would be liable for one penalty.
