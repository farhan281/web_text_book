Definition of Shahid and Conditions for Being Killed by Disbelievers
Defines the shahid and outlines who is considered killed by the enemy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a shahid according to the Hanafi school and determine whether a given death in a battle context qualifies as martyrdom based on the causative link to the enemy.

Core Idea
A martyr is one killed by the enemy, directly or indirectly; but death caused by one's own action in flight is not martyrdom.

This passage opens the chapter on the shahid (martyr) from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It provides the core definition — one killed by polytheists — and then extends it through analogical reasoning to include highway robbers and rebels. The commentator also clarifies that killing can be direct or indirect, and specifically addresses cases where the death might be attributed to the enemy versus cases where it is self-inflicted in fear. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for proper application of the special rulings of martyrdom (e.g., no ghusl, burial in blood-stained clothes).

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary definition
From the base text: 'The martyr: he whom the polytheists kill.' Note that this is the core definition. The commentator expands on what 'kill' means.
The definition is straightforward but serves as the foundation for the whole chapter.
2
Understand the basis for the name 'shahid'
The text gives three reasons for the name: the angels witness his death, he is testified for Paradise, and he is alive with Allah. This reinforces the high status of the martyr.
These reasons justify the special rulings that apply to him.
3
Recognize the extension to other killers
The commentator states that 'included in the meaning of polytheists are: highway robbers, and rebels.' This broadens the definition to any illegal combatant who kills in a hostile context.
This is an example of qiyas (analogy) based on the effective cause of being killed unlawfully in conflict.
4
Apply the conditions of causation
The passage then tests specific scenarios: being trampled by enemy-controlled mounts qualifies as martyrdom; but if a Muslim horse shies from fear without enemy action, or if Muslims jump into a trench fleeing, they are not martyrs. The key is that the enemy must be the direct or indirect cause of death.
The exceptions help define the boundaries: death must be attributable to the enemy's agency, not to the victim's own panic.

The Ruling on a Battlefield Body with a Wound: Shahid or Not?
Ruling on shahid status based on blood location and color.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a battlefield martyr (shahid) who does not require ghusl and a regular deceased who does, by applying the Hanafi criteria of the location and color of blood.

Core Idea
A battlefield body is a shahid only if blood flows from an unusual place like the eye or ear; blood from natural orifices means ghusl is required.

When a dead body is found on the battlefield bearing a wound, the immediate question is whether he died as a martyr (shahid) and thus does not require washing (ghusl). The Hanafi school provides a precise rule: the distinction hinges on the source of the bleeding. Not every wound grants shahid status; only those where blood emerges from an unusual site indicate a direct battlefield injury. This lesson unpacks that rule, including a subtle distinction for blood from the mouth based on its color.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Read the opening line: a body is found in the battlefield with a trace of injury (athar al-jirāḥa). The trace is defined as the oozing of blood from an unusual place like the eye or ear.
Unusual place means a location that is not a natural orifice for blood.
2
Differentiate natural vs. unnatural blood exit points
If blood comes from the nose, anus, or penis, the person must be given ghusl because these locations allow natural bleeding (e.g., nosebleed, blood in urine). This overrides the presumption of battlefield death.
Natural orifices do not indicate direct enemy action.
3
Apply the color test for mouth blood
Blood from the mouth is ambiguous. The commentator explains that the color determines its source: clear (raspberry-like) indicates head wound → shahid, no ghusl; thick dark blood indicates from the chest (internal injury) → not shahid, ghusl required.
This is a unique Hanafi diagnostic method.
4
Summarize the ruling
Compile the conditions: blood from eye/ear = shahid (no ghusl); from nose/anus/penis = not shahid (ghusl); from mouth = check color.
This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.

Loose Enemy Horse Trampling: Shahid or Not?
Presents a disagreement among Hanafi imams on whether a Muslim killed by a loose enemy horse is a shahid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf on the shahid status of a Muslim killed by a loose enemy horse, and identify the underlying principle of attribution to the enemy.

Core Idea
A battlefield death by a loose enemy horse is not automatically shahid; the key is whether the killing is attributed to the enemy's act.

In Hanafi fiqh, martyrdom (shahada) in battle has specific conditions. Not every death in the battlefield is considered shahid; the cause must be directly or indirectly attributable to the enemy. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explores a nuanced scenario: a Muslim killed by an uncontrolled enemy horse. The Hanafi imams disagree on whether this death qualifies as shahada. Let's examine the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the victim must be washed (ghusl) and is not a shahid.
This is the zahir al-riwayah position.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Abu Yusuf holds that the victim is a shahid and should not be washed.
This is a nawadir position.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Abu Yusuf argues that because the death occurred in the context of war against the enemy, it qualifies as shahada.
He focuses on the battlefield context.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that the act is not attributed to the enemy; it is merely the act of a beast (al-ʿajmāʾ), which is not considered unjust (ẓulm).
Attribution (idāfa) is key: the killing must be traceable to the enemy's action.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if shahid, no ghusl; if not, full ghusl is performed.
This affects funeral rites and inheritance categories.

Commentary
This passage records an internal ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school regarding a specific scenario: a Muslim killed by the trampling of a loose enemy horse during battle. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani rule that the victim must be washed (i.e., he is not a shahid) because the act of killing is not directly attributed to the enemy; the horse acted on its own volition. Since the horse's act is not considered unjust (zulm) in the legal sense, the death is not categorized as martyrdom. Abu Yusuf, however, rules that the victim is a shahid and should not be washed, because the death occurred in the context of warfare against the enemy. He regards the battlefield environment as sufficient to attribute the killing to the enemy, even though the horse was uncontrolled. The text does not indicate which opinion is preferred.

Key Terms

العجماء (al-ʿajmāʾ): A dumb beast, referring to an animal without human intellect; its actions are not subject to legal responsibility (not described as ẓulm).

Ruling of the Battlefield Martyr: Shroud, Prayer, No Washing
Defines the battlefield martyr and his funeral rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three funeral rulings for a battlefield martyr and distinguish the cases where martyrdom is affirmed or denied according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A martyr is not washed, is shrouded in his clothes, and is prayed upon.

The chapter on the martyr (shahid) in Hanafi fiqh establishes unique funeral rites. While the general deceased is washed and shrouded, the martyr receives special treatment: he is not washed, is buried in his own clothes, and the funeral prayer is performed. This lesson unpacks the basic rule, its exceptions (e.g., father killing son), and the intra-Hanafi dispute over killing by a heavy object.

Guided Path
1
State the three rulings for the martyr
Identify the three actions: (1) he is shrouded in his clothes (yukaffan), (2) prayer is performed over him (yusalla alayh), (3) he is not washed (la yughsal). These are the core of the passage.
These rulings apply to the battlefield martyr killed in legitimate jihad.
2
Identify the exception for killing by a father
If a father kills his son (e.g., accidentally), the son is still a martyr despite blood money being due. The text explains that qisas was initially obligatory but dropped due to shubhah, so martyrdom remains.
Contrast this with cases where blood money is obligatory from the start (accidental killing) – those are not martyrs.
3
Understand the condition ‘killed by what usually kills’
If Muslims kill a Muslim with something that does not usually kill (e.g., a light blow), the deceased is not a martyr by consensus. This limits martyrdom to effective combat deaths.
This shows that intention matters: accidental or unlikely killing does not confer martyrdom.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on killing by heavy object (muthqal)
According to Abu Hanifa, killing by a heavy object (muthqal) does not make one a martyr; according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), it does. Note the practical difference in funeral rites.
The mufta bihi (operative position) is Abu Hanifa's, though some later scholars adopt the Sahibayn's view.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on the martyr (shahid) in jihad. The general rule for a martyr killed in legitimate battle is that he is not washed (because his blood is a witness for him), he is shrouded in his own clothes (without removing them), and the funeral prayer is performed over him. The commentary then clarifies exceptions: if a father kills his son (accidentally or in a situation where qisas is not applied due to doubt), blood money is due but the killed son is still a martyr because the initial obligation of qisas was removed by shubhah, not by the act of the father. Conversely, if someone is killed unjustly and blood money is obligatory (e.g., accidental killing), or the killer is unknown, the deceased is not a martyr. A specific dispute: if Muslims kill a Muslim with a heavy object (muthqal) that usually kills, Abu Hanifa says he is not a martyr, but the Two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider him a martyr. Key Terms
شهيد (shahīd): A Muslim killed in legitimate jihad or in certain specific circumstances (e.g., killed by father with blood money) who is given special funeral rites.
مثقل (muthqal): A heavy object used as a weapon, typically one that kills by crushing or heavy impact, as opposed to a sharp cutting weapon (muhaddad).
دية (diya): Blood money, a financial compensation paid to the heirs of a victim in cases of homicide where qisas is not applicable (e.g., accidental killing or when qisas is dropped due to shubhah).
