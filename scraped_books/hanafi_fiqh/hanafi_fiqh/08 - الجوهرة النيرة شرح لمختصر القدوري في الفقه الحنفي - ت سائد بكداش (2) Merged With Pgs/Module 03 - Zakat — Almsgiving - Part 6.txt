Sadaqat al-Fitr Obligatory Despite Exemption from Fasting
Ruling that Sadaqat al-Fitr remains due on those exempt from fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling that Sadaqat al-Fitr is not dropped due to exemption from fasting, using qiyas from the case of children.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is due on every person with means, regardless of whether they actually fast.

This passage addresses a common misconception: many assume that if a person is excused from fasting, they are also excused from paying fitrah. The author clarifies that fitrah is an independent obligation tied to the person, not to the performance of the fast. The proof is a clear analogy (qiyas jali) with children, who also do not fast yet fitrah is wajib on them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The text states that Sadaqat al-Fitr is binding (lazima) on a person who is excused from fasting due to old age or illness. Note the explicit phrase 'la tasqutu 'anhu' – it is not dropped.
This is a definitive ruling in the Hanafi school.
2
Recognize the Potential Misconception
Why might someone think fitrah is dropped? Because they assume fitrah is linked to fasting. The author refutes this directly.
The misconception is common, especially among laypeople.
3
Examine the Proof: Qiyas
The proof is an analogy (qiyas): fitrah is obligatory on children (asl) despite them not fasting, so by analogy, it remains on the adult (far') who does not fast due to excuse.
The effective cause (illah) is that the absence of fasting does not remove the obligation.
4
Confirm the Legal Outcome
The ruling is that Sadaqat al-Fitr remains wajib and is not dropped. The author concludes with 'Wallahu a'lam' – affirming reliance on Allah's knowledge.
This ruling is consistent with the general Hanafi position that fitrah is wajib on every free Muslim with nisab.

ومن سقط عنه صوم رمضان ؛ لكبَر، أو مرض: فصدقة الفطر لازمةٌ له، لا تسقط عنه؛ لأنها تجب على الصغار وعنهم مع عدم الصوم منهم، فكذا لا تسقط بعدم الصوم عن البالغ، والله أعلم.
And whoever is excused from fasting Ramadan due to old age or sickness, then Sadaqat al-Fitr is binding upon him; it is not dropped from him, because it is obligatory upon children and on their behalf despite their not fasting. So likewise, it is not dropped by the absence of fasting from the adult. And Allah knows best.
Source pages p. 255

Commentary
This passage provides a ruling with proof regarding the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It states that even if a person is excused from fasting Ramadan due to old age or chronic illness (such that they cannot fast and do not have to make up), they still must pay Sadaqat al-Fitr (fitranah). The proof is by qiyas (analogy): Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib for children who do not fast, and it is paid on their behalf by their guardians. Thus, the absence of fasting does not lift the obligation. By analogy, the adult who is excused from fasting due to valid reasons is like a child in that he does not fast, so the obligation of fitrah remains. This is an example of qiyas jaliy, where the effective cause (illah) is that fitrah is linked to the person and not dependent on actually fasting.

Key Terms
صدقة الفطر (sadaqat al-fitr): The obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan, also known as fitrah.
سقط عنه (saqata 'anhu): To be dropped or removed from someone's obligation.

Feeding for Missed Prayers: Giving a Lump Sum to One Poor Person
Fatwa on giving lump sum to one poor for missed prayers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on giving a single poor person the entire amount for missed prayers and distinguish it from oath expiation.

Core Idea
For missed prayers, a lump sum to one poor suffices; not so for oath expiation.

This fatwa from a later Hanafi authority clarifies a practical scenario: a deceased person owes missed prayers and leaves a will to feed the poor on his behalf. What if the heirs give the entire amount to one poor person instead of distributing it? The ruling says it is permissible, contrasting this with oath expiation where such lump-sum giving is not valid. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the condition that makes this exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is feeding the poor on behalf of a deceased person who owed missed prayers, based on a will. The ruling is permissibility (jaza).
The ruling is from a fatwa collection (fatawa mutakhkhirin), not from the six core books (zahir al-riwayah).
2
Understand the condition: giving the entire amount to one poor person
The text states that giving the whole amount (jumlatu dhalika) to a single poor person is valid. This is specific to feeding for missed prayers.
This implies that distribution to multiple poor is not required for this case.
3
Contrast with oath expiation (kaffarah al-yamin)
The fatwa explicitly distinguishes this from oath expiation, where giving to one poor is not sufficient. Why? Because oath expiation requires feeding or clothing ten poor persons (Quran 5:89).
The difference in the basis (dalil) leads to different rules for distribution.
4
Recognize the authority weight of the ruling
This fatwa is not from the early authoritative texts (zahir al-riwayah) but from later collections (fatawa mutakhkhirin). Nonetheless, it is relied upon (mufta bihi) within the Hanafi school for this specific scenario.
The Hanafi school accepts feeding as a substitute for missed prayers with conditions, while other schools may not.

Source Text
⚠ Extracted via Advanced OCR ∼97% accuracy, minor errors may remain — always verify against original Arabic books
قال في «الفتاوى»: إذا مات وعليه صلوات، وأوصى أن يُطعموا عنه لها، فأعطوا فقيراً واحداً جملة ذلك: جاز، بخلاف كفارة اليمين.
He said in al-Fatawa: When someone dies and owes prayers, and he made a will that they feed (the poor) on his behalf for them, and they gave a single poor person the whole amount collectively: it is permissible, unlike the expiation for an oath.
Source pages p. 294

Commentary
This is a fatwa (ruling) from a later Hanafi authority. The issue is: a person dies owing missed prayers (صلوات) and has left a will (وصية) to feed the poor as expiation. If the heirs give one poor person the entire amount (جملة ذلك) in one go, it is valid (جاز). This is contrasted with كفارة اليمين (expiation for a broken oath), where giving to one poor person in lump sum is not sufficient because the oath expiation requires distribution to ten poor persons. The ruling is from "الفتاوى", likely referring to a collection of fatwas like al-Fatawa al-Hindiyya. The authority weight is from later fatawa (mutakhkhirin).

Key Terms
جملة ذلك (jumlatu dhalika): The entire quantity (of food or money) given in one lump sum to one poor person, not distributed.
كفارة اليمين (kaffarah al-yamin): Expiation for a broken oath, which according to Quran 5:89 requires feeding or clothing ten poor persons, or freeing a slave.
