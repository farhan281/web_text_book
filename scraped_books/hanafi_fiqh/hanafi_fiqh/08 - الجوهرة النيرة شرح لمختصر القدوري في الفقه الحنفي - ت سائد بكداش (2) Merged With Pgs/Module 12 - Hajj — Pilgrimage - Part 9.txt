Ruling on Marking Hady: Not Obligatory but Recommended for Time-Bound Offerings
Clarifies that marking hady is not obligatory and defines when it is recommended.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ta'rif al-hady, define it, and apply the ruling to different types of hady distinguishing the basis for recommendation.

Core Idea
Marking the hady is not wajib but recommended for time-bound sacrificial animals to ensure they are not lost.

In this passage, the commentator explains a statement from Al-Quduri's matn regarding the practice of marking the sacrificial animal (hady). While some may assume that marking is obligatory, the text clarifies it is not, and then delineates when it is encouraged. We will explore the two definitions of ta'rif, understand why it is recommended for certain types of hady, and see the reasoned distinction from expiatory offerings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on ta'rif
State clearly whether marking the hady is obligatory, recommended, or something else according to the Hanafi school. The matn says 'nor is it obligatory', so the ruling is that it is not wajib. The commentator adds that it is 'good' (hasan) for certain types, indicating it is mustahab.
The ruling is sunnah (mustahab), not fard or wajib.
2
Understand the two definitions of ta'rif
The passage gives two meanings: (1) transporting the hady to 'Arafah, and (2) placing a mark on it like a garland. Know that both are considered forms of ta'rif, and either suffices.
The first definition is literal 'taking to 'Arafah', the second is a broader 'identifying with a sign'.
3
Determine for which types of hady ta'rif is recommended
List the three categories: hady of tamattu', qiran, and voluntary (tatawwu') offerings. The text explicitly says marking these is good. Note that expiatory blood (dima' al-kaffarat) is excluded.
The recommendation applies only to hady that is part of the Hajj rituals or voluntary, not to expiatory offerings.
4

The commentator provides two reasons: (a) the recommended types are time-bound to the day of Nahr, so marking helps in case the animal is lost; (b) they are offerings of a ritual (nusk) that should be publicly known. In contrast, expiatory blood can be slaughtered earlier and is due to transgression, so concealment is more appropriate.
The 'illa is the time-sensitive nature and the ritual/public character versus the private/concealment nature of expiation.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
For hady, camels are slaughtered by nahr standing; cows and sheep by dhabh lying down.

Slaughtering sacrificial animals during Hajj (hady) follows specific methods preferred by the Hanafi school. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that camels should be slaughtered by nahr (stabbing the neck) while standing, whereas cows and sheep should be slaughtered by dhabh (cutting the throat) while lying down. The proof is drawn from Quranic verses that command the slaughter of a cow and mention the great sacrifice (dhibh) of a sheep. Understanding this distinction ensures the pilgrim follows the sunnah and maximizes reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the passage is ruling on: the method of slaughtering hady animals. Note that for camels (budn) the ruling is that nahr is preferred, while for cows and sheep, dhabh is preferred. This is a sunnah muakkadah (emphasized sunnah), not an obligation.
The ruling is about preference (al-afdal), not validity.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the Quranic evidence cited: Surah Al-Baqarah 2:67 ('Indeed, Allah commands you to slaughter a cow') and Surah Al-Saffat 37:107 ('And We ransomed him with a great sacrifice'). These establish dhabh for cows and sheep.
No hadith is cited; the author relies solely on Quranic verses.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Analyze how each verse proves the method. The command to 'slay a cow' (tadhbahū) uses the verb dhabh, indicating the method for cows. The word 'dhibh' (sacrifice) in the second verse refers to a sheep, and dhibh is derived from dhabh, indicating the method for sheep.
The word 'dhibh' is a verbal noun meaning 'slaughter' or 'sacrificial animal'.
4
Note the practical application
Understand the recommended positions: for camels, nahr is best done standing with the left foreleg tied; for cows and sheep, dhabh is best done lying down because the throat is more exposed and the slaughter is easier.

Hadi of worship is garlanded and displayed; Hadi of compensation is concealed.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on taqlid (garlanding) of sacrificial animals during Hajj. Core Idea
In vows, only precise mention of the Ka'bah, Makkah, or Maqam Ibrahim obligates; vague terms like 'al-Masjid al-Haram' are disputed.

A vow (nadhr) to perform Hajj or Umrah is a serious commitment. The exact wording matters enormously in the Hanafi school. This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira provides a clear taxonomy: five phrases that by consensus obligate a Hajj/Umrah, six that by consensus do not, and two that sparked an internal Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Identify the vow formula
All phrases begin with 'Allahu alayya' (by Allah, upon me is...). Recognize this as the standard Hanafi vow formulation that triggers analysis of the complement.

2
Classify the complement's specificity
Look at the action and destination mentioned. Is the action specific (e.g., 'mashy' = walking)? Is the destination a specific site within the Haram (Ka'bah, Makkah, Maqam Ibrahim) or vague (e.g., 'going out', 'traveling', 'al-Masjid al-Haram')?

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Vow Formulas for Hady
Rulings on verbal formulas not obligating anything in Hady

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the consensus formulas and the disputed formulas in Hady vows, and state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Six vow formulas are unanimously non-binding; two formulas split the Hanafi school between non-obligation (Abu Hanifa) and obligation of Hajj/Umrah (Sahibayn).

In this passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, we examine the legal effect of specific verbal formulas used in vows (nadhr) related to pilgrimage. The author first lists six expressions that, by scholarly consensus, create no legal obligation. He then introduces two additional expressions that sparked an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the fiqh of vows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the consensus formulas
List the six expressions that do not obligate anything by unanimous agreement (ijma). They all mention general destinations like 'House of Allah', 'Mecca', 'Safa and Marwah', 'Arafat'.
These are considered vague and not specific enough to create a binding vow.
2
Understand the basis of consensus
The six formulas lack specificity—they do not clearly indicate a particular act of worship like Hajj or Umrah. Therefore, they do not create a legal obligation.
Consensus (ijma) is a primary source in Hanafi usul, making this ruling definitive.
3
Identify the disputed formulas
Two additional expressions: 'walking to the Sacred Mosque' (al-masjid al-ḥarām) and 'walking to the Ḥaram'. These are the subject of ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and his disciples.
The dispute hinges on whether these phrases imply a pilgrimage act or remain vague.
4
State Abu Hanifa's position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that these two formulas, like the previous six, do not obligate anything. He considers them insufficiently specific.
This is the minority view within the Hanafi school on this issue.
5
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that these formulas obligate either a complete Hajj or an Umrah, as they indicate a destination associated with pilgrimage.
This is the more precautionary view and is generally relied upon for fatwa.
6
Determine the practical consequence
If someone utters 'Allah ʿalayya al-mashy ilā al-ḥaram', under the Sahibayn's view they must perform Hajj or Umrah to fulfill the vow; under Abu Hanifa they are not obligated.
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is the view of the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on the legal effect of certain verbal formulas (al-alāfāẓ) uttered as a religious commitment (nadhr) in the context of Hady (sacrificial animal of Hajj). The first set of six expressions, which mention leaving for the House of Allah, going to the House of Allah, etc., are agreed by all scholars (bil-ijmāʿ) not to create any legal obligation. These are considered vague and not specific enough to bind a person to a specific act of worship. The second set comprises two expressions: 'walking to the Sacred Mosque' and 'walking to the Haram.' According to Imam Abu Hanifa, these also do not obligate anything. However, his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybānī (al-Ṣāḥibān), hold that such an utterance obligates either a Ḥajj or an ʿUmrah. The relied-upon position (al-muftā bihi) in the Hanafi school is typically that of the Ṣāḥibān, as it is more precautionary and ensures that the vow is fulfilled. The phrase 'Allah aʿlam bi al-ṣawāb' indicates the author's humility and deference to divine knowledge.

Key Terms
الهدي (al-hady): The sacrificial animal brought to the Haram by a pilgrim performing Hajj or Umrah, or as an offering for a vow.
ألفاظ (alfāẓ): Verbal formulas or expressions used in the context of making a religious vow (nadhr) or oath relating to pilgrimage.
الإجماع (al-ijmāʿ): Scholarly consensus that the six listed formulas do not create any obligation.
