Talqin of the Dying and Positioning for Death
Commentary on prompting the dying with the shahada and positioning towards qibla

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of talqin, identify its evidence, and apply the proper method to prompt the dying.

Core Idea
Talqin is a sunnah that gently reminds the dying of the shahada before the death rattle, based on prophetic command.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira explains the recommended practice of talqin—prompting the dying person to utter the testimony of faith. The commentator expands on the matn, citing the hadith, describing the correct method, and noting the etiquettes of not repeating unless the person speaks other words. We will also see the preferred positioning of the dying person towards the qibla.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage gives two rulings: (1) talqin (prompting) of the dying with the two testimonies, and (2) positioning the dying on their back facing the qibla.
Both rulings are based on ease and prophetic guidance.
2
Locate the proof for talqin
The hadith 'Laqqinu mawtakum la ilaha illallah' is cited. This is a command from the Prophet (peace be upon him).
The command is understood as a recommendation (nadb) in Hanafi usul.
3
Determine the ruling of talqin
Apply the Hanafi principle: the imperative form indicates obligation, but corroborating evidence (ease, no punishment for leaving) shifts it to sunnah muakkadah.
The ruling is sunnah muakkadah, not wajib.
4
Understand the method of talqin
The phrases are said loudly in the dying person's hearing before the ghara (death rattle), without commanding them to say it. It is not repeated if they say it once, unless they speak other words.
Gentleness is important; do not annoy the dying.
5
Positioning the dying
The chosen position is lying on the back facing the qibla, because it makes the soul's exit easier.
This is based on ease, not a specific proof.

Immediate Post-Death Procedures: Tying the Jaw and Closing the Eyes
Ruling on recommended post-death procedures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tying the jaw and closing the eyes of the deceased and identify the hukm level as sunnah muakkadah.

Core Idea
Tying the jaw and closing the eyes are recommended, not obligatory.

When a person dies, the living are instructed to perform certain acts out of respect for the deceased. The classical Hanafi text of al-Quduri specifically mentions two immediate actions: tying the lower jaw to prevent it from falling open, and gently closing the eyes. This lesson clarifies the legal status of these acts within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their ruling
The text explicitly commands tying the jaws and closing the eyes. Determine the legal classification (hukm) of these commands according to the Hanafi school.
The imperative form (shaddu, ghammidu) does not automatically imply obligation.
2
Classify the hukm level
In Hanafi fiqh, these acts are sunnah muakkadah (emphasized recommendation), not fard or wajib. This is based on the Prophet's practice and the consensus of the Companions.
Sunnah muakkadah means omission is disliked but not sinful.
3
Determine the consequence of omission
If the jaw is not tied or the eyes not closed, the burial remains valid and no sin is incurred. The acts are meritorious but not essential for the validity of the funeral.
No compensation (fidya) or repetition is required.

Talqin in the Grave and the Questioning of the Dead
Discussion on prompting the deceased after burial and the grave questioning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on talqin after burial and explain the Hanafi belief in grave questioning, including the evidence from ijma' and the ikhtilaf regarding timing and infants.

Core Idea
Talqin after burial is a sunnah; every soul is questioned in the grave – this is a consensus of Ahl al-Sunnah.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses two related matters: the recommended practice of prompting the deceased after burial (talqin) and the reality of the grave questioning (su'al al-qabr). The author affirms both as part of the creed of Ahl al-Sunnah, citing consensus for the latter. He also resolves common questions about when the questioning occurs and whether infants are included.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main rulings
Recognize that the passage covers: (1) talqin after burial is prescribed (mustahab), and (2) the grave questioning is a reality (necessary belief).
Talqin is a recommended act; belief in questioning is obligatory.
2
Examine the evidence for talqin
The text states 'مشروع عند أهل السنة' because Allah resurrects him. This implies evidence from the practice of the Salaf, though no specific hadith is cited.
The proof for talqin is based on tradition and communal practice.
3
Examine the evidence for grave questioning
The text cites consensus (ijma') of Ahl al-Sunnah: 'بإجماع أهل السنة'. This is a definitive proof in creedal matters.
Ijma' is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala.
4
Analyze the ikhtilaf on timing
Two views: questioning before burial vs. after burial. The author chooses the view that it occurs after burial as the more famous, based on traditions.
The Hanafi position follows the more famous opinion.
5
Analyze the ikhtilaf on infants
The text confirms that every soul is questioned, including nursing infants. The angel prompts them, though some say Allah inspires them.
The Hanafi school accepts the prompting method, but both views have basis.

Commentary
This passage, from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira's commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, covers two main rulings after death: the immediate physical handling of the body and the spiritual practice of teaching (talking) the deceased in the grave. The matn states the basic sunnah acts: tightening the jaw and closing the eyes. The author then addresses the ruling on tақвин (teaching), which is legislated according to Ahl al-Sunnah because Allah revives the deceased in the grave to be questioned. The form of the teaching is given: addressing the deceased by name or as 'slave of Allah', reminding him of his monotheistic faith. A dispute is noted about the timing of the questioning: some say it begins only after burial; others say it begins immediately in the house when the earth encloses him. The first opinion is deemed more famous (ashhar) because the traditions support it. Regarding children, the author affirms that every living soul is questioned by consensus of Ahl al-Sunnah, but the angel teaches the child rather than inspiring him directly, contrary to some who analogize to Jesus in the cradle. The commentary then returns to the initial matn, providing the proof from the hadith of Abu Salamah: the Prophet closed his eyes because the sight follows the soul. The wisdom is that leaving them open leads to an ugly appearance and risk of insects. The practical method is described: the gentlest relative should gently close the eyes, tighten the jaw with a bandage, soften the joints, and position the limbs. Additional recommendations are given: informing neighbors and friends (though announcing in streets is disliked, with a more lenient view from al-Muhit), and hastening to clear debts, as the soul is suspended by debt. All these rulings are from the Hanafi school, with the relied-upon (mufta bihi) positions as described.
