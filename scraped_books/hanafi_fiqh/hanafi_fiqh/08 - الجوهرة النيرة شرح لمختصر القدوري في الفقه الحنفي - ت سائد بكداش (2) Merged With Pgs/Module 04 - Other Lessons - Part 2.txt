Ruling on Plastering and Building on Graves
Prohibition of four actions on graves with proof from hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on plastering, building, and writing on graves, and explain the proof and its classification as makruh tahrimi.

Core Idea
Plastering, building, and writing on graves are prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi) based on a clear prophetic prohibition.

In this lesson, we examine a concise ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar regarding four actions related to graves. The Hanafi school classifies these as makruh tahrimi, not haram, due to the nature of the evidence. We'll trace the proof, understand the hukm level, and see how this applies in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four prohibited actions
List the four actions mentioned: plastering with mud (tatyin), whitewashing with lime (tajsis), building structures over graves, and writing on graves. Note that the text uses the verb 'يكره' (it is disliked) for each.
All four are explicitly mentioned in the hadith cited.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites the hadith: 'لا تُجَصَّصوا القبور، ولا تبنوا عليها، ولا تقعدوا عليها، ولا تكتبوا عليها.' This is from Sahih Muslim and other collections. Note that the hadith also prohibits sitting on graves, which is another ruling.
The hadith is ahad but authentic, making its transmission zanni.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
In Hanafi usul, the imperative prohibition (nahy) indicates prohibition (tahrim) unless there is a qarinah (contextual indicator) to lower it. Here, the hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), so the ruling is lowered to karahah tahrimiyyah. The meaning is clear: these actions are to be avoided.
If the hadith were mutawatir, the ruling would be haram. The Hanafi classification reflects the epistemological weight of the evidence.
4
Understand the Hanafi classification
The ruling is makruh tahrimi. This means it is sinful to do, but the evidence does not reach the level of qat'i required for haram. The mufta bihi (operative position) is makruh tahrimi. All four schools agree on prohibition, but the legal classification varies.
The practical consequence: one who does it commits a sin and should repent; the act is not invalid (like in worship) but is avoided in funerary practice.

Sprinkling Water on Graves: The Intra-Hanafi Debate
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sprinkling water on graves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Yusuf differed on sprinkling water on graves and state the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sprinkling water on graves is permissible (mubah) in the Hanafi school despite Abu Yusuf's qiyas to prohibited tatyin.

This brief passage from a classical Hanafi text addresses a question that arises for anyone visiting graves: is it permissible to sprinkle water on the soil? The author gives the default answer with a simple justification. However, he also notes that Imam Abu Yusuf held a different view, based on an analogy to plastering graves with mud, which is prohibited. Understanding this disagreement illuminates how Hanafi scholars weigh purpose (maqsad) and analogy (qiyas).

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
Look at the first sentence: 'Wa la ba'sa bi rashsh al-ma' alayha.' The phrase 'la ba's' indicates permissibility (ibahah). The reason given is to settle the soil.
The default ruling is mubah.
2
Pinpoint the disagreement
The text then says 'Wa 'an Abi Yusuf annahu kariha al-rashsh aydan.' 'Kariha' means he disliked it. Note the word 'aydan' (also), linking it to a previous discussion about plastering.
Abu Yusuf considers sprinkling water makruh.
3
Analyze the reasoning behind the objection
Abu Yusuf draws an analogy (qiyas): sprinkling water is like tatyin (plastering with mud) because both involve applying a substance to the grave that alters its appearance and prevents easy disturbance.
The analogy is based on the effect on the soil.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
The default position rejects the analogy. Sprinkling water does not permanently change the grave; water evaporates. The purpose is practical—settling loose soil—not beautification. Thus, the prohibition of tatyin does not extend to water.
The principle of istihsan (juristic preference) may override qiyas.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is the absence of karahah. The author presents 'la ba's' first, which is the Zahir al-Riwayah position. Abu Yusuf's view is a Nawadir transmission.
Mufta bihi: sprinkling water is permissible.

Friday is a divine obligation that overrides a self-imposed i'tikaf vow.

What happens when an obligatory act clashes with a vowed act of worship? This passage addresses a classic dispute: can a person observing i'tikaf leave the mosque for Friday prayer? The Four Categories of Legal Obligation: Fard, Wajib, Sunnah, and Nafl
Defining the four categories of Islamic legal obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four Hanafi categories of legal obligation (fard, wajib, sunnah, nafl) based on the certainty of their evidence and state the practical consequence for each.

Core Idea
Fard is qat'i, wajib is zanni, sunnah is prophetic, nafl is voluntary — each has a different legal weight.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira lays the foundation for understanding how Hanafi jurists classify obligations. The distinction between fard and wajib is uniquely Hanafi and affects rulings in daily worship. Let's break down the key definitions and see how they apply.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitional criteria
The passage defines each term by the type of evidence (definitive vs. probable) and the consequence of omission. Focus on the evidence base for fard and wajib.
2
Compare fard and wajib
Both are obligatory, but fard is based on qat'i evidence (Quran, mutawatir), wajib on zanni evidence (khabar wahid). Recognize that this distinction is unique to the Hanafi school.
Other schools often treat both as synonymous.
3
Understand sunnah and nafl
Sunnah is the Prophet's way, commanded to be revived; nafl is purely voluntary, no sin for leaving.
Sunnah here refers to mu'akkadah (emphasized sunnah).

For example: the five daily prayers are fard, witr prayer is wajib, the two rak'ahs of Fajr sunnah are sunnah mu'akkadah, and the night prayer (tahajjud) is nafl.
This illustrates the practical hierarchy.
5
Recognize cross-madhhab differences
Note that other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) do not distinguish between fard and wajib; they consider both as obligatory on the same level.
This affects fatwa and emphasis in practice.

Animals Subject to Penalty and the Dispute on Monkey and Pig
List of animals with penalty and intra-Hanafi dispute on monkey and pig.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which animals incur a penalty when killed by a muhrim according to the Hanafi school, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Yusuf and Zufar regarding the pig.

Core Idea
All predators and even monkeys and pigs are game according to Abu Yusuf, but Zufar exempts the pig because it is recommended to kill it.

After defining game broadly, the commentary lists specific animals and addresses a dispute within the Hanafi school. The general principle is that all predators (siba') are game. However, for the monkey and pig, two opinions emerge: Abu Yusuf holds a penalty for both, while Zufar exempts the pig because Islamic law recommends killing pigs. Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Note the general rule: all predators are game
The text states: 'And all predators are game.' This includes lions, wolves, hyenas, etc. The commentary confirms that a muhrim is prohibited from killing them, and if he does, penalty is due.
Predators here refer to wild carnivores.
2
Examine the specific animals mentioned
Lion, hyena, wolf, wild cat (with two narrations), monkey, pig, sand lizard (dab), jerboa, baram. For most, the penalty is agreed upon.
Wild cat has two narrations; the text does not resolve.
3
Identify the dispute on monkey and pig
Abu Yusuf says penalty for both. Zufar says no penalty for the pig because it is recommended (mandub) to kill it, implying it is not protected game.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
4

Zufar's reasoning: since killing a pig is generally recommended (due to its harmful nature), it cannot be considered game that triggers a penalty for a muhrim. The muhrim is allowed to kill it without penalty.
The text does not provide Abu Yusuf's rejoinder, but his view is that general permissibility to kill does not remove the game status.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: according to Abu Yusuf, a muhrim who kills a pig must pay a penalty; according to Zufar, no penalty.
Later fatwas prefer Abu Yusuf's view, as the text does not reject it.

Conditions for Director's Liability in Game Killing
Conditions for director's liability in causing game killing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two conditions for a director (dall) to incur the penalty for game killed by another, and apply them to scenarios.

Core Idea
A director only owes penalty if the killer learned of the game solely from his direction and if the director remains in ihram until the killing occurs.

The matn says that a muhrim who directs another to game owes the penalty. However, this liability is not absolute. The commentary specifies two conditions: first, the killer must have no prior knowledge of the game; second, the director must remain in ihram until the killing. Understanding these conditions prevents misapplication of the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering act
The matn states: 'or directs to it someone who kills it, then he owes the penalty.' The director (dall) is liable.
The director is the muhrim who points out the game.
2
First condition: the killer must gain knowledge from the direction alone
If the killer already saw the game or knew about it before the direction, the director is not liable. The commentary says: 'this is when the directed person does not see the game nor know about it until the director points it out to him.'
The director must be the sole source of knowledge.
3
Second condition: director must remain in ihram until the killing
If the director exits ihram (by performing tahlul) before the killer kills the game, no penalty is due. The commentary: 'he remains in his ihram until the directed person kills it; but if he becomes free and then the directed person kills it afterwards, then nothing is on the director.'
The director's liability is tied to his state at the time of killing.
4
Apply the conditions to scenarios
Example: A muhrim points out a deer to another muhrim who shoots it. Conditions satisfied? Killer had no prior knowledge, director remains muhrim -> penalty on director. If the director had already done tahlul, no penalty.
Practice with variations.
