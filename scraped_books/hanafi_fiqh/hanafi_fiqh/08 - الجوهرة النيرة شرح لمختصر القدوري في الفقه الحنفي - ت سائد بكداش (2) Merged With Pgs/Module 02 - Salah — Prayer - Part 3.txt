Prohibition of Nafl Prayer Before and After Eid Prayer
Proves that no voluntary prayer is offered at the musalla before or after Eid prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on offering nafl prayers before and after Eid prayer at the musalla, and explain the two proofs: the hadith of 'Ali and the rational argument regarding interruption of the Eid prayer.

Core Idea
No nafl prayer at the musalla before or after Eid – the Prophet never did it, and it risks cutting the nafl or missing part of the Eid prayer.

This lesson examines why the Hanafi school prohibits any voluntary prayer in the Eid prayer ground before or after the Eid prayer. The ruling is based on a reported practice of the Prophet and a simple rational principle: the Eid prayer has no adhan or iqamah, and offering nafl at that time could lead to missing part of the obligatory prayer. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish between the Eid prayer and the daily prayers that have associated sunnah prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what exactly is being ruled upon: offering nafl (voluntary) prayer in the musalla (Eid prayer ground) before or after the Eid prayer. The ruling is not completely prohibited (haram) but prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi).
2
Examine the first proof: hadith of 'Ali
Look at the narration from 'Ali: the Prophet did not pray before or after the Eid prayer in the musalla. This indicates that it was not his practice, and therefore it is not recommended. The Hanafi school uses this as evidence that it should be avoided.
The hadith is reported in al-Karkhi.
3
Analyze the rational argument
Consider the reasoning that the Eid prayer has no adhan or iqamah, meaning it does not have the same structure as daily prayers which have sunnah prayers before and after. Moreover, if someone begins a nafl and the Imam starts the Eid prayer, the person either has to cut the nafl (which is problematic) or miss part of the Eid prayer (invalidates it). Thus, it is better not to start.
The matn says 'jaza' (possible) but the commentary explains this implies prohibition.
4

Suppose a person arrives early at the musalla and prays two rak'ahs. When the Imam begins the Eid prayer, what should he do? According to the Hanafi ruling, he should not have started the nafl, but since he did, he must either cut the nafl (which is not allowed without reason) or miss part of the Eid prayer, which is also not allowed. The lesson: do not start nafl before Eid at the musalla.
5

Note that some schools allow voluntary prayer before Eid prayer in the musalla. The Hanafi ruling is stricter due to the evidence from the Prophet's practice and the rational argument. This ikhtilaf is legitimate.
The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi for Hanafis.

Eid Prayer Takbirs: Hanafi Position and Dispute with Shafi'i/Maliki
Inter-madhhab dispute on Eid takbirs with companion evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the number of extra takbirs in Eid prayer, explain the disagreement with the Shafi'i and Maliki schools, and identify the companion-based evidence for each view.

Core Idea
Hanafi Eid prayer has no extra takbirs in first rak'ah; only three in second after recitation.

The Eid prayer is a highly emphasized sunnah. One of its distinctive features is the extra takbirs. However, the number and placement of these takbirs differ among schools. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi structure and its proof, followed by the opposing view of the Shafi'i and Maliki schools.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact number and placement of takbirs in each rak'ah according to the Hanafi school: first rak'ah has only the opening takbir and a single takbir for ruku' after recitation; second rak'ah has three supplementary takbirs after recitation, then a fourth for ruku'.
The opening takbir is not counted among the extra takbirs.
2
Identify the opposing view
State the Shafi'i/Maliki position: they prescribe seven takbirs in the first rak'ah (excluding the opening takbir) and five in the second (excluding the ruku' takbir), meaning extra takbirs are performed before recitation in both rak'ahs.
The exact numbers may vary, but the key difference is the placement of extra takbirs before or after recitation.
3
Trace the companion evidence
Recognize that each school bases its practice on a different Companion: Hanafi follows Ibn Mas'ud (no extra takbirs in first rak'ah, three in second); Shafi'i/Maliki follows Ibn Abbas (more takbirs).
Both views are transmitted, but Hanafi considers the practice of Ibn Mas'ud as more aligned with the Sunnah.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Note that the text does not provide an elaborate refutation but simply asserts that 'our saying is the madhhab of Ibn Mas'ud'. Later Hanafi works uphold this view as authentic.
The dispute is rooted in different companion practices, not in contradictory hadith.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The difference affects how the prayer is performed: In Hanafi, the imam begins recitation immediately in the first rak'ah; in Shafi'i, he first says multiple takbirs. In the second rak'ah, Hanafi recites first, then does takbirs; Shafi'i does takbirs first, then recites.
This leads to different timings for the congregation.

Latecomer to Eid Prayer and Rulings on Raising Hands
Intra-Hanafi dispute on latecomer and ruling on hand raising

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for a latecomer to Eid prayer, including when to perform extra takbirs and whether to raise hands.

Core Idea
A latecomer to Eid should prioritize catching the ruku'; extra takbirs are waived if the imam rises before completion.

The Eid prayer has unique elements including extra takbirs. When a person joins late, the imam's sequence creates questions about what the latecomer must do. Additionally, the raising of hands during the takbirs is a matter of practice. This lesson covers the Hanafi rulings for these scenarios, including an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the latecomer's scenario
The text considers a latecomer who finds the imam in ruku' during the Eid prayer. He must assess whether he can perform the extra takbirs and still catch the ruku' with the imam.
This scenario typically occurs in the second rak'ah where extra takbirs are present.
2
Determine the latecomer's obligation
He is required to say takbir al-ihram while standing. If possible, he should then perform the extra takbirs (saying each on his own) and then catch the ruku' by saying the takbir and joining.
If he cannot complete the extra takbirs before the imam rises from ruku', he faces a choice.
3
Apply the intra-Hanafi dispute
According to Abu Yusuf, if he cannot complete the extra takbirs in time, he should go directly to ruku' and engage in the glorifications (tasbih). According to Muhammad (and in another view, both Sahibayn), he should still engage in the extra takbirs even if it means missing the ruku'.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is Abu Yusuf's view, prioritizing following the imam.
4
Handle the imam rising
If the latecomer has performed some takbirs and then the imam raises his head from ruku', the latecomer must stop and follow the imam. The remaining takbirs are dropped because following the imam is an obligation.
This applies regardless of how many takbirs he has completed.
5
State the ruling on raising hands
The Hanafi position is to raise the hands for the extra takbirs of Eid (not for the takbirs of ruku'). However, there is a transmission from Abu Yusuf that he did not raise his hands. The mufta bihi is to raise them.
This ruling applies to the imam and individual alike.

Sacrificing After Prayer in One of Two Eid Locations: Istihsan vs Qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of istihsan to override strict qiyas in determining the permissibility of sacrifice after one of multiple Eid prayers.

Core Idea
Istihsan permits sacrifice after your own Eid prayer, even if other congregations haven't prayed.

This short passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a practical question: when may you sacrifice after the Eid prayer if the prayer is held in two different locations? The Hanafi school uses istihsan (juristic preference) to allow sacrifice immediately after one's own prayer, departing from strict qiyas that would require waiting for all prayers to finish. Understanding this ruling illustrates how istihsan functions as a source of law in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the two methods
Locate the phrase 'يجوز أن يضحي بعد ما صلى في أحد الموضعين؛ استحساناً'. This states that the Hanafi position (by istihsan) permits sacrificing after praying in one of two locations. Note that the ruling is contrasted with qiyas.
The passage explicitly mentions 'والقياس أن لا يجوز' – this is the strict analogy.
2
Understand the qiyas reasoning
The qiyas (analogy) starts from the original case of a single Eid prayer, where sacrifice is only valid after the prayer. The effective cause ('illa) is that the sacrifice is conditioned on the completion of the Eid prayer. In the new case of two locations, qiyas extends this condition to require both prayers to be finished.
This is a strict analogical extension, treating the entire city as one unit.
3
Understand the istihsan reasoning
Istihsan overrides the qiyas because each congregation is independent. Requiring someone who prayed early to wait for the other congregation imposes unnecessary hardship (haraj). The prayer in one location fulfills the condition for that individual.
Istihsan is not arbitrary; it is based on the principle of removing hardship and custom.
4
Note the source and authority
The passage cites 'كذا في «الخجندي»' (thus in al-Khujandi). This confirms the ruling as the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school, transmitted through the later sources.
Al-Khujandi is a Hanafi jurist; his text is part of the fatawa literature.
5
Apply the principle
Consider a city with multiple Eid prayer times. The Hanafi ruling applies: as soon as you complete your own prayer, you may sacrifice. No need to wait for other congregations. This demonstrates the practical effect of istihsan in removing hardship.
This ruling applies regardless of how many locations exist.

Commentary
This passage expands on the obligation of Takbir al-Tashriq and its conditions. The author first clarifies that the absolute statement 'wajib after fard prayers' is the view of the Sahibayn: for them, the takbir follows the fard prayer itself, so any person who prays any fard (regardless of gender, status, etc.) must say the takbir. Abu Hanifa restricts it: only men who are free, legally responsible (mukallaf), and residents (not travellers) in cities, and who pray the fard in congregation during these days, must perform the takbir. Also, those who pray with such a congregation (as followers) must follow. The text further explains that 'fard prayers' excludes witr and Eid prayer, but includes Jumu'ah because it is fard. The fatwa on this issue is on the view of the Sahibayn (as stated in the earlier passage).

Key Terms
المفروضات (al-mafrūḍāt): The obligatory (fard) prayers, as opposed to witr, nafl, or Eid prayers.
تبعية (tabaʿiyyah): Following the imam in congregation; those praying behind the imam are considered followers and must also perform the takbir after the imam, even if they themselves are not among the specified categories (e.g., a woman follower).
