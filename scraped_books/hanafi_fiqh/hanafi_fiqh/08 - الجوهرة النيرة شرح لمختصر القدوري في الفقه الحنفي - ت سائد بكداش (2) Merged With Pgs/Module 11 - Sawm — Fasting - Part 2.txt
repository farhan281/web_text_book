Wet Dream and Involuntary Emissions During Fasting
Ruling with hadith proof for wet dream not breaking fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wet dream during fasting, identify the hadith proof, and explain why involuntary acts do not nullify the fast.

Core Idea
A wet dream does not break the fast—only voluntary intentional acts invalidate it.

This lesson addresses a common concern: does a wet dream during daylight fasting break the fast? The Hanafi school, relying on a hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him), rules clearly that it does not. We will examine the ruling, its proof, and the underlying principle that involuntary events do not nullify the act of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text states: 'For if he sleeps and has a wet dream, he does not break his fast.' This is the explicit ruling. Note that the act itself (ihtilam) is involuntary and occurs during sleep.
The ruling applies to both men and women.
2
Analyze the hadith proof
The hadith cited lists three things: vomiting, cupping, and wet dream. Though the chain is weak, the Hanafi school accepts it because it is supported by other evidence and the principle that involuntary acts do not nullify the fast.
The hadith is reported by Tirmidhi and Abu Dawud.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The key principle is that only deliberate, intentional acts break the fast. Involuntary acts (e.g., sleep emission, accidental vomiting, nosebleed) do not invalidate the fast. This is consistent with the broader fiqh rule that actions beyond one's control are excused.
This principle is derived from the hadith and from qiyas on the ruling of wet dream.

Withdrawal After Dawn or Forgetfulness and Nocturnal Emission
Intra-Hanafi dispute and hadith on ihtilam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding withdrawal after dawn or after forgetfulness, and identify the ruling on nocturnal emission.

Core Idea
Withdrawal after dawn or after forgetting: Abu Yusuf distinguishes intent, Muhammad exempts triviality.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about withdrawal during fasting. We will see how three major Hanafi imams—Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and Zufar—differ on whether withdrawing from intercourse after dawn or after forgetfulness breaks the fast. Additionally, we review the clear ruling on nocturnal emission, supported by a hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage from al-Khujandi presents two cases: (1) a person forgetfully engages in intercourse, then remembers and withdraws; (2) dawn rises while he is still in contact, and he withdraws. Note the key difference: in the first, the beginning was forgetful; in the second, the beginning was intentional (since he chose to continue after dawn).
For the forgetfulness case, the withdrawal is immediate upon remembering.
2
State the three positions
Imam Muhammad says no break in either case. Imam Zufar says break in both. Imam Abu Yusuf distinguishes: no break in forgetfulness, break in dawn case. Write these positions down.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's distinction
Abu Yusuf's reasoning: 'the end of the act is considered by its beginning.' In the dawn case, the beginning is intentional (since he continued after dawn knowingly), so the act is spoiled. In the forgetfulness case, the beginning is non-intentional, so the end is also excused.
This is a usul principle: i'tibar al-akhir bi al-awwal.
4
Analyze Muhammad's response
Muhammad argues that the withdrawal itself is trivial and cannot be avoided, so it is exempted. He analogizes to a forgetful person's withdrawal: even if the beginning is intentional (dawn case), the withdrawal is a necessary small movement that does not break the fast.
Muhammad's view seems to be the relied-upon (mufta bihi) according to the commentary.
5
Apply the hadith on nocturnal emission
The hadith states that three things do not break the fast: vomiting, cupping, and wet dream (ihtilam). This is a clear dalil that nocturnal emission does not invalidate the fast, regardless of the intra-Hanafi dispute.
The hadith is ahad but accepted; it overrides general analogy that emission might break wudu.

The author of al-Jawhara does not explicitly choose, but the presentation suggests that Muhammad's view is relied upon.

Actions That Do Not Break the Fast: Looking, Oil, Cupping, Kohl
List of actions that do not break fast with rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which specific actions (looking, applying oil, cupping, applying kohl) do not break the Hanafi fast and explain the underlying rationale of lacking the form and meaning of intercourse.

Core Idea
Looking, oil, cupping, and kohl do not break the fast because they are neither form nor meaning of intercourse.

In the Hanafi school, a central principle determines whether an action breaks the fast: it must either be the actual act of intercourse (form) or its equivalent in meaning, defined as ejaculation resulting from direct physical contact. This lesson examines four common actions that fail this test and remain permissible during fasting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four actions
From the text, list: looking at a woman (even with ejaculation), applying oil, cupping, applying kohl. The ruling for each is 'lam yufṭir' (does not break fast).
Kissing is omitted here because it has a conditional ruling covered in the next lesson.
2
Understand the rationale
The commentary explains: 'because there is neither the form of intercourse nor its meaning.' Form is actual penetration; meaning is ejaculation from direct contact. These actions lack both.
This is the core legal reasoning (ta'līl) for the ruling.
3
Apply the analogy to thinking
Looking is compared to thinking (tafakkur) that leads to ejaculation – both are mental stimulation without direct contact, so they do not break the fast.
The Hanafi school uses qiyās to extend the ruling from thinking to looking.
4
Note the issue of taste
The commentary clarifies that even if the taste of oil or kohl reaches the throat, the fast is still not broken. This counters a potential misconception.
The presence of taste does not convert the action into something that breaks the fast.

Core Idea
Kissing or touching only break the fast if they cause ejaculation through direct contact without a thick barrier.

This lesson examines the conditional rulings on kissing and touching during fasting. The Principle of Form and Meaning in Fasting Invalidators
Legal principle underlying the specific rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that an action invalidates the fast only if it involves the form or meaning of intercourse, and derive rulings for cases not explicitly mentioned.

Core Idea
Only the form (intercourse) or meaning (ejaculation from direct contact) breaks the fast; otherwise the fast is valid.

This lesson crystallizes the overarching principle that governs all the specific rulings: an action only breaks the fast if it either constitutes the form (sūrah) of intercourse—penetration—or its meaning (ma‘nā), defined as ejaculation (inzāl) resulting from direct physical contact (mubāsharah). Understanding this principle allows the student to evaluate new cases logically.

Guided Path
1
Define 'form' (sūrah) and 'meaning' (ma‘nā)
Form of intercourse is actual penetration. Meaning of intercourse is ejaculation caused by direct contact. The text says: 'because there is neither the form of intercourse nor its meaning, which is ejaculation with desire by direct contact.'
These two categories exhaust the invalidators beyond eating/drinking.
2
Classify the given actions
Looking, oil, cupping, kohl → no form, no meaning → invalid. Kissing without ejaculation → no form, no meaning → valid. Kissing/touching with ejaculation → no form, but meaning present → invalid (qada). Full intercourse → both form and meaning → invalid (qada + kaffarah).
This classification maps directly to the rulings.
3

Deliberate Vomiting During Fasting: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on deliberate vomiting with internal Hanafi ikhtilaf on quantity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on deliberate vomiting (istisqa') during fasting and explain the disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding less than a mouthful.

Core Idea
Deliberate vomiting of a mouthful invalidates the fast; less than a mouthful triggers a dispute between Abu Yusuf (valid) and Muhammad (invalid).

In this passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, the commentator expands on al-Quduri's matn regarding deliberate vomiting while fasting. The base text states that inducing a mouthful requires qada. The commentary then delves into the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on less than a mouthful, contrasting the reasoning of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace Hanafi legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed ruling
Find the ruling for a mouthful: it invalidates the fast and requires qada, but no kaffara.
This is the base matn position, agreed upon by all Hanafi imams.
2
Identify the dispute
Note the case of less than a mouthful. Abu Yusuf says the fast remains valid; Muhammad says it is invalid.
The disagreement is based on different conceptualizations of 'exit'.
3
Trace each imam's dalil
Abu Yusuf: less than a mouthful is considered internal (no legal exit), analogous to not invalidating wudu. Muhammad: deliberate action (صنع) itself invalidates, regardless of quantity.
Both arguments rely on Hanafi usul principles.
4
Apply to the returning vomit scenario
If the vomit returns after the act, Abu Yusuf says it does not further invalidate because there was no prior exit; Muhammad's view is moot because the fast was already broken.
This shows how the dispute affects subsequent actions.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: a person who deliberately vomits less than a mouthful can consider their fast valid (Abu Yusuf) or invalid requiring qada (Muhammad).
Later Hanafi authorities often preferred Abu Yusuf's view for leniency.

Involuntary vomiting does not break fast; swallowing solids breaks fast but requires only qada, not kaffara.

This lesson addresses two specific cases during fasting: being overcome by vomiting involuntarily, and swallowing solid objects like pebbles or date pits. Core Idea
A mouthful of intentionally induced vomit breaks the fast; less is disputed between act and quantity.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the rules for vomiting during a fast, focusing on the internal Hanafi disagreement between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. The key issue is whether the threshold for breaking the fast is the quantity of vomit (a mouthful) or the deliberate act itself. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to track how different 'illal (legal causes) produce different rulings within the same school.

Identify the agreed ruling

Swallowing Inedible Objects: When Does a Fast Require Expiation?
Hanafi ruling and Maliki dispute on swallowing inedible objects during fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on swallowing inedible objects during fasting, explain the distinction between surat al-fitr and ma'na, and apply the ruling to specific cases like nuts, rinds, and leaves.

Core Idea
Swallowing an inedible object breaks the fast but never requires kaffarah unless the object contains a nourishing interior that is chewed.

Fasting is broken whenever anything enters the stomach, but the obligation of expiation (kaffarah) depends on the purpose of the act. This lesson delves into the Hanafi position on swallowing inedible objects like pebbles or date pits, and contrasts it with the Maliki view. Through a series of applied examples, you will learn how the school distinguishes between mere form (surat) and meaningful nourishment (ma'na).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Primary Ruling
Locate the author's statement on swallowing a pebble or iron: it breaks the fast (qada required) but no kaffarah. Note the two reasons: existence of 'surat al-fitr' and absence of 'ma'na' (purpose of satisfying hunger).
The word 'iltaqa' is used because chewing is impossible for hard objects.
2
Analyze the Mālikī Objection
Read the objection attributed to Mālik: he argues that kaffarah is due because the person deliberately breaks the fast without excuse, and his intent is only to harm the fast. Understand the opposing proof.
Mālik's reasoning is based on the gravity of deliberate harm.
3
Examine the Hanafī Rebuttal
Study the Hanafī response: lack of natural inclination (tab') toward such objects suffices as a deterrent, analogizing to the absence of hadd for drinking blood or urine (unlike wine).
The analogy draws on the idea that prohibitions target what people naturally desire.
4
Apply the Principle to Specific Objects

The key factor is whether the object is consumed as food in its usual form.
5
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilāf
Summarize the practical difference between the schools: in the Hanafī school, only qada is required for swallowing inedible objects; in the Mālikī school, both qada and kaffarah are required.
This ikhtilāf has real consequences for a fasting person's obligations.

Core Idea
Kaffarah requires the form of eating; non-oral routes break the fast but only obligate qada.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explores a set of medical actions that may break the fast. The central question is not just whether the fast becomes invalid, but whether the more severe penalty of kaffarah applies. The key legal tool is the concept of 'surah'—the external manner of ingestion. Only voluntary oral ingestion (wajur) carries kaffarah; other routes, such as enema, snuff, ear drops, or wound medicine, break the fast but require only qada, due to the absence of the form of eating.

Guided Path
1
Identify the listed actions
The passage lists five categories: wajur (oral feeding), ihtiqan (enema), isti'tat (snuff), qatr fi al-udhun (ear drops), and treatment of ja'ifa/ammah (deep body or head wounds). For each, determine whether the fast is broken and whether kaffarah applies.

The distinction highlights the two criteria: surah and ma'na.
5

For deep wounds, if moist medicine reaches the interior, it breaks the fast. There is a view that dry medicine also breaks if it reaches, but the relied-upon position is that the determining factor is actual access, not moisture. In all cases, only qada is required.
The passage clarifies that the ruling is based on access, not the form of ingestion.
