Rulings of Tamattu' Hajj and the Prohibition for Makkans
Explains tamattu' procedure and prohibition for Makkans

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings of tamattu' Hajj and explain why residents of Makkah are prohibited from performing tamattu' or qiran.

Core Idea
Tamattu' is permissible for non-Makkans with a wajib dam; Makkans may only perform ifrad.

Tamattu' is a common type of Hajj where one performs Umrah first and then, after a break, assumes ihram for Hajj. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira outlines the procedure and the key obligation of the dam, and crucially, it excludes residents of Makkah from performing tamattu' or qiran. The commentary clarifies the timing and an exception for Makkans who travel beyond the miqat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its core ruling
Determine what tamattu' is and what the basic obligation for a mutamatti' is. The passage states that upon entering Makkah, one performs Tawaf and Sa'i for Umrah, remains in ihram, and must offer a dam.
The dam is wajib for tamattu'.
2
Analyze the timing of ihram for Hajj
The matn says to assume ihram on the day of Tarwiyah (8th Dhul Hijjah), but the commentary notes this is not necessary; one may do so even on the day of Arafah. Advancing is encouraged.
Flexibility exists, but promptness is better.
3
Recognize the prohibition for Makkans
The text explicitly states that the people of Makkah cannot perform tamattu' or qiran; only ifrad is allowed for them. Understand the rationale: ihram prohibits what is permissible but does not permit what was prohibited, implying continuity of the resident state.
Makkans are considered already in the state of ihram in a sense?
4
Apply the exception for traveling Makkans
The commentary explains that if a Makkan travels out to Kufa (beyond the miqat) and performs qiran, it is valid because both Umrah and Hajj are initiated from outside. However, returning to Makkah between the acts invalidates tamattu'.
The key factor is whether the person is a resident at the time of each act.

Commentary
This passage presents the basic rulings of tamattu' (performing Umrah first then Hajj in the same journey with a break) and addresses the special status of the people of Makkah. The matn states that upon entering Makkah, the mutamatti' performs Tawaf and Sa'i for Umrah and remains in ihram until the 8th of Dhul Hijjah (yawm al-tarwiyah) when he assumes ihram for Hajj. However, the commentary clarifies that it is not a strict condition; he could assume ihram on the day of Arafah. Advancing the ihram is permissible and even better due to hastening. The mutamatti' must offer a dam (delaying sacrifice) which is accomplished by the hadi he drives. After shaving on the 10th (yawm al-nahr), he is released from both ihrams. The text then explicitly states that residents of Makkah cannot perform tamattu' or qiran; only ifrad is allowed for them. The rationale is that ihram prohibits what was permissible but does not permit what was prohibited—implying Makkans are already in the state of being residents, so they cannot combine the two acts with a break. The commentary expands by detailing an exception: if a Makkan travels to Kufa (beyond the miqat) and performs qiran, it is valid because both Umrah and Hajj are miqat-based. However, if he merely goes out to Kufa and returns to Makkah between the two acts, he is not a mutamatti' because returning to his family invalidates the tamattu' arrangement.

الإفراد (al-ifrad): A type of Hajj where the pilgrim performs Hajj first, then Umrah separately, without combining.
دم (dam): A sacrificial animal (sheep or goat) required as a penalty or obligation in Hajj.

Returning Home in Tamattu' and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Hadi
Ruling on ilimam and intra-Hanafi dispute on hadi effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on returning home after Umrah in tamattu', explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the hadi, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Returning home between Umrah and Hajj invalidates tamattu' unless a hadi is driven — but Imam Muhammad disagrees.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a detailed ruling on a specific scenario in tamattu' hajj: what happens if the pilgrim returns to his hometown after completing Umrah? The matn states the general rule, then an intra-Hanafi dispute emerges between the majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding the effect of bringing a hadi. Understanding this dispute illustrates how differences within the school arise from differing interpretations of the underlying rationale (talil).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and general ruling
The act is the pilgrim (mutamatti') returning to his hometown after completing Umrah but before starting Hajj. The general ruling: if he did not drive a hadi, his tamattu' is invalidated. Note the reason (talil): he has made a valid visit (ilimam sahih) to his family, breaking the continuity between the two rites.
This establishes the baseline rule.
2
Recognize the exception with hadi
According to the two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), if the pilgrim has driven a hadi, his visit is not considered a valid interruption, so tamattu' remains intact. The hadi symbolically maintains the connection to the sacred rites.
The hadi acts as a preservative of the pilgrim's state.
3
State Imam Muhammad's opposing view
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani argues that tamattu' is invalidated even with a hadi, because the pilgrim performed the rites in two separate journeys and visited his family. The physical return constitutes a genuine break.
His reasoning focuses on the factual separation, not the symbolic effect of the hadi.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: tamattu' is valid if a hadi is brought. This is the majority view within the school and is supported by later fuqaha.
Iftaa follows the view of the two.
5
Apply the ruling to people of the miqat
The same rule applies to residents of the miqat and those whose hometown is between the miqat and Makkah. If they return home after Umrah, they are blameworthy and must offer a blood penalty (dam jiraayah) which cannot be eaten from, and fasting does not substitute for it even if they are poor.
This distinguishes the penalty from the regular dam of tamattu'.

Invalidation of Tamattu' by Returning to Hometown
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tamattu' invalidation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition regarding returning to hometown after Umrah in tamattu' and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the effect of bringing a hadi.

Core Idea
Returning to hometown without a hadi invalidates tamattu'; with it, the school splits.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah examines a key condition for the validity of tamattu' – whether the pilgrim returns to his hometown between Umrah and Hajj. The ruling hinges on the presence of a sacrificial animal (hadi) and generates a notable intra-Hanafi disagreement. Understanding this dispute helps you apply the correct fatwa in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the default ruling
The act is tamattu' (performing Umrah then Hajj in the same year with a break). The default ruling is that it is valid (sahih). The passage specifies a condition that returning to hometown without a hadi invalidates it.
2
Understand the role of the hadi according to the Sahibayn
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, if the pilgrim brings a hadi, the return (ilmam) is not considered a valid break, so tamattu' remains valid. The hadi acts as a barrier preventing invalidation.
Muhammad dissents even with hadi
3
Analyze Muhammad's dissenting position
Muhammad holds that even with a hadi, tamattu' is invalid because the two rituals are performed in separate journeys and the pilgrim has returned to his family. He considers the hadi irrelevant to the break.
He also holds invalidation before shaving
4
Compare return to hometown vs. other places
Abu Hanifa rules that returning to a place other than one's hometown does not invalidate tamattu' – as if the pilgrim never left Makkah. The Sahibayn equate it to returning home, thus invalidating it.
Difference on intention to stay 15 days
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative position)
Later scholars (e.g., al-Marghinani) prefer the view of Abu Yusuf: tamattu' is invalid only if the return is to the hometown without a hadi. For return to a non-hometown, Abu Hanifa's view is often followed.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses two rulings related to Tamattu' in Hajj. First: the effect of a mutamatti' returning to his hometown after Umrah. If he returns without a sacrificial animal (hadi), his tamattu' becomes invalid because he has validly 'returned to his family' (ilmam sahih) between the two rituals. This is because, in the Hanafi school, ihram for Umrah is a condition that can precede the time, but the actions (tawaf, sa'y, halq) must occur within the months. Since most actions (more than three circuits) occur in the months, the ruling of the majority applies.

Umrah Before Hajj Months and Tamattu' Validity
Ruling on timing of Umrah for tamattu'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that if a person begins Umrah before the Hajj months but performs less than four tawaf circuits before the months enter, then completes the Umrah and performs Hajj, he qualifies as a mutamatti'.

Core Idea
Most actions in the months make tamattu'; four-circuit threshold defines the majority.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a person enters ihram for Umrah before the months of Hajj (Shawwal, Dhul-Qi'dah, first ten days of Dhul-Hijjah) and performs less than four circuits of tawaf. The Hanafi school rules that such a person is a mutamatti', based on the principle that the majority of the actions have the ruling of the whole. Understanding this helps clarify the timing requirements for tamattu'.

The act is performing Umrah and Hajj as tamattu'. A condition for tamattu' is that Umrah must be performed during the Hajj months. Here, the Umrah is started before the months but completed within them.
2

The passage specifies that if less than four circuits of tawaf are performed before the months, then the majority of the tawaf (seven circuits total) occurs in the months. Thus the Umrah is considered to have been performed in the months.
Four circuits is the midpoint; more than half means majority.

In the Hanafi school, ihram for Umrah can precede the months because it is a condition (shart), not a pillar. The actual performance of the actions (tawaf, sa'y, halq) must fall within the months.
This differs from schools that require ihram itself to be in the months.

Since most actions occur in the months, the person is considered a mutamatti'. He must then offer a hadi as a wajib for tamattu'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Returning to one's own country invalidates tamattu'; returning elsewhere does not, according to Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn disagree.

The ruling on tamattu' depends on when the actions of umrah are performed relative to the hajj months. An intra-Hanafi dispute arises when the mutamatti' returns to his country after completing umrah but before hajj. This lesson unpacks the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, focusing on the role of shaving (halq) and the definition of 'return'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
Read the matn: if a person performs less than four tawaf circuits for umrah before the hajj months, then completes it within the months, he is a mutamatti'. This establishes the basic condition: most of the umrah actions must occur within the hajj months.
The principle 'al-akthar lahu hukm al-kull' (the majority has the ruling of the whole) applies.
2

The commentary introduces the issue of returning (al-'awd) after finishing umrah. Abu Hanifa says returning to one's own country invalidates tamattu', but returning to any other place does not. The Sahibayn say returning to any place other than Makkah invalidates it.
This disagreement is based on the purpose of shaving (halq).
3

Abu Hanifa: shaving is obligatory (wajib) within the Haram; returning to one's country prevents proper shaving. Abu Yusuf: shaving is recommended, but returning still invalidates because the departure breaks continuity. The dispute also involves whether shaving must be done in Makkah or can be done anywhere.

Practical difference: if a mutamatti' returns to a non-home city (e.g., from Makkah to Madinah), Abu Hanifa allows tamattu' to remain; the Sahibayn invalidate it. This affects whether a sacrifice (dam) is due.
Also, according to Muhammad, if the person returns before shaving, tamattu' is invalidated even for the Sahibayn.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi
The text does not specify a mufta bihi, but later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn for caution. The condition of intending 15 days stay is also mentioned as a variant.
The principle of caution (ihtiyat) often leads to adopting the stricter view.

Ruling with proof and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for tamattu' regarding the timing of umrah tawaf, define the hajj months according to the Hanafi school, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over the inclusion of the Day of Nahr.

Core Idea
If most of the umrah tawaf occurs before the hajj months, tamattu' is invalid — the majority rules the whole.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two connected questions. First, what exactly makes a pilgrim a mutamatti'? It is not enough that the umrah and hajj occur in the same year; the majority of the umrah tawaf must take place within the hajj months. Second, what are those months? The text defines them as Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah, then opens an important intra-Hanafi debate about whether the Day of Nahr (10th) itself is included. Understanding these rulings is essential for determining when a pilgrim must offer the tamattu' sacrifice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is performing umrah tawaf (four or more circuits) before the hajj months and then performing hajj in the same year. The ruling: the pilgrim is not a mutamatti'.
2
Understand the principle
The text invokes the principle 'الأكثر له حكم الكل' (the majority has the ruling of the whole). Since four circuits out of seven is the majority, performing them before the months makes it as if the entire umrah occurred before the months.
This principle is common in Hanafi fiqh — e.g., in purification, the majority of a limb’s washing counts as washing the whole.
3
Define the hajj months
The months are Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah. The text explains that two whole months plus part of a third count as 'months' because the majority represents the whole.
4
Analyze the dispute on the Day of Nahr
Abu Yusuf argues the Day of Nahr is not included because hajj time expires at dawn and worship cannot be missed while time remains. The relied-upon Hanafi view (citing al-Wajiz and the Shaykh) holds it is included, based on the Quran calling it 'the Day of the Greater Pilgrimage' (9:3).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab one.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If the Day of Nahr is included, the hajj months extend to include the 10th; if excluded, they end at dawn of the 10th. This affects whether an umrah tawaf performed on the 10th counts as within the months.
The mufta bihi is inclusion.

A specific case arises when most of the 'umrah tawaf occurs before the months begin—the ruling hinges on the principle that the majority takes the ruling of the whole. We then explore the boundaries of the months of Hajj, including an important intra-Hanafi disagreement about whether the Day of Nahr (10th Dhu al-Hijjah) is included.

Guided Path
1
Understand the condition for tamattu'
The text states that if a person performs four or more circuits of tawaf for 'umrah before the months of Hajj, then completes 'umrah and performs Hajj the same year, he is not a mutamatti'. The key condition is that both 'umrah and Hajj must be performed entirely within the months of Hajj.

The commentary explains that the majority of tawaf (four circuits out of seven) is treated as if all tawaf occurred before the months. This is a legal principle: in rituals, the majority has the ruling of the whole. Thus, if the majority is before the months, it is as if the entire 'umrah was performed before the months.
This principle also applies to other rituals, but here it is used to determine the time of the 'umrah.
3

The text explicitly defines the months of Hajj as Shawwal, Dhu al-Qa'dah, and the first ten days of Dhu al-Hijjah. The inclusion of only ten days of the third month is justified by the majority principle: the majority of the third month (ten days out of twenty-nine or thirty) is sufficient to call it a month.
The Day of Nahr (10th Dhu al-Hijjah) is the last day of these ten.
