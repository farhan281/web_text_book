Wife's Right to Wash Husband: Two Disputed Cases
Two rulings on wife's washing right, with Zufar's opposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a wife's right to wash her deceased husband in two specific scenarios and explain Zufar's opposing view.

Core Idea
A wife's right to wash her deceased husband depends on the continuity of the marriage tie at the time of death.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two scenarios where the right of a wife to wash her deceased husband is contested within the Hanafi school. The first involves a non-Muslim wife who converts after his death; the second involves a wife who had remarried and is in iddah from a separation. In both cases, the majority Hanafi position grants the right under certain conditions, while Zufar holds a stricter view. Understanding this dispute clarifies the legal principle that the marriage tie must be intact at death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the Arabic passage and identify the two distinct cases: (1) a Zoroastrian wife whose husband converts to Islam and then dies while she remains non-Muslim, and (2) a woman who marries a second husband while still in nikah with the first, then separates from the second, and the first husband dies while she is in iddah from the separation.
The passage uses 'مثل' and 'وكذا' to introduce the examples.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each case
For case 1: The wife cannot wash at the time of death because she is non-Muslim. If she converts after his death, the right returns. For case 2: She cannot wash while in iddah of the second husband. If the iddah expires after the first husband's death, the right returns.
The phrase 'ثم يعود لها حق الغسل' indicates the return of the right.
3
Identify Zufar's position
Zufar (the Hanafi scholar) disagrees in both cases: he would hold that the right never returns, either upon conversion or upon expiration of iddah, because the marriage tie is severed permanently.
The phrase 'خلافاً لزفر' appears after each ruling.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
The Hanafi majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) base their rulings on the principle that the marriage tie (nikah) must be intact at the time of death for the wife to have the right to wash. In case 1, conversion after death retroactively validates the tie? Actually, the tie is considered to have been intact if she converts, but at death she was non-Muslim. The majority view is that the right returns because the impediment (kufr) is removed. In case 2, the iddah from the second husband indicates that she is still tied to the second, not the first; but after the first dies, the iddah from the second prevents her from washing? Actually the passage says that if the first husband dies during her iddah from the separation, she cannot wash; but if the iddah expires after his death, she can. This is because the iddah from separation ends the tie to the second husband, and then the tie to the first (which was interrupted by the second marriage) is restored? This is detailed in fiqh. The core idea: the continuity of the marriage tie is essential.
The dispute with Zufar highlights different interpretations of when the tie is considered severed.

Commentary
This passage presents two cases of intra-madhhab disagreement with Zufar regarding a wife's right to wash her deceased husband. In the first case, a Zoroastrian wife cannot wash her Muslim husband because she is not from Ahl al-Kitab, but if she converts after his death, her right returns. Zufar would allow her to wash him even before conversion as a wife. In the second case, a woman who remarried and then her first husband dies while she is in iddah from a separation, she cannot wash him because the marriage tie is disrupted. However, if her iddah expires after his death, the tie is reestablished and she may wash him. Zufar holds she cannot ever wash because the divorce severs the tie completely. The relied-upon Hanafi position is that the iddah preserves some connection.

Husband Cannot Wash Deceased Wife
Ruling with talil (reasoning by cause)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a husband cannot wash his deceased wife and explain the legal reasoning based on the severance of the marriage tie.

Core Idea
Death completely severs the marriage tie – the wife becomes a stranger.

In Hanafi fiqh, the ability to wash the deceased relies on a permissible relationship (mahramiyyah). This lesson examines a clear boundary: a husband cannot wash his deceased wife. The source presents the ruling and its rationale, allowing us to understand how the marriage tie is considered fully terminated upon death, with practical evidence from marriage prohibitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a husband washing his deceased wife. The ruling from the matn is prohibition (haram). Note that this is a ruling_statement – no proof is cited here, but the commentary provides a reason.
2
Analyze the reason (talil)
The text gives a reason: 'because the marriage tie is severed.' The severance is evidenced by the fact that the husband is immediately permitted to marry the wife's sister and to have up to four other wives. These were forbidden during marriage, so their permission proves the tie is gone.
The reasoning uses a legal indicator (amarah) – the removal of marriage prohibitions – to deduce the end of the relationship.
3
Recognize the legal consequence
Since the wife becomes a non-mahram (stranger) upon death, the husband is not permitted to wash her. Washing her would be analogous to washing any unrelated deceased woman, which is not allowed without a mahram relationship.
This ruling applies even if the husband is the only attendant; in such cases, a non-muslim or female may wash her instead, following Hanafi rules.
4
Contrast with the Shafi’i position (optional)
The Shafi’i school holds that the husband may wash his wife because the marriage tie is not fully severed – it remains for purposes like washing and burial. This difference stems from interpreting the evidence: Shafi’is prioritize the hadith where the Prophet (ﷺ) told Aisha that she could wash her husband's body, analogizing to the husband.
Understanding this ikhtilaf highlights the role of competing proofs in usul al-fiqh.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
Consider a husband in a hospital who wants to wash his wife's body after her death. According to the Hanafi ruling, he must not do so. He should seek a female relative or a professional washer. If no female mahram is available, a non-muslim woman or a male stranger (in some cases) may perform the washing under certain conditions.
This shows the practical importance of knowing the ruling and its exceptions.

And if his wife dies, he does not wash her, because the marriage tie is severed, because he is allowed to marry her sister and four others besides her.

Commentary
This ruling states that a husband cannot wash his deceased wife. The reason given is that death completely severs the marriage tie (nisah al-nikah). Evidence for this severance is that the husband is immediately permitted to marry the wife's sister (who would have been haram during marriage) and to have up to four other wives (whereas during marriage he could not add a second if he already had four). Thus, the relationship is fully terminated, and she becomes a non-mahram (a stranger) to him, making it impermissible for him to wash her. This is the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Key Terms
علقة النكاح (ʿulqat al-nikāh): The marriage tie or relationship that creates mahramiyyah and inheritance rights.

Wife's Shroud: Dispute Between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on wife's shroud obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the husband's obligation for his wife's shroud and identify the basis (qiyas vs. severance of marriage) for each view.

Core Idea
The husband's obligation to shroud his wife is disputed: Abu Yusuf analogizes to clothing; Muhammad says marriage ends at death.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between two of Imam Abu Hanifa's foremost disciples. While both agree that if the wife has wealth her shroud comes from her estate, they diverge when she is penniless. The disagreement turns on whether the marriage bond survives death for the purpose of this obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes between the wife having wealth and not having wealth. This conditional framework sets the stage for the dispute.
When she has wealth, consensus is clear: shroud from her estate.
2
State Abu Yusuf's position and his proof
Abu Yusuf holds that the husband must provide the shroud, using qiyas to the obligation of clothing (kiswah) during marriage. Since the shroud is essentially posthumous clothing, the same obligation continues.
This is an extension by analogy (qiyas) of a known obligation.
3
State Muhammad's position and his proof
Muhammad argues that the marriage bond (zawjiyyah) ends at death, so all spousal obligations—including clothing—cease. Thus, the husband is not obligated.
He focuses on the termination of the contract rather than analogy.
4
Note the area of consensus
Both imams agree that if the wife has her own wealth, the shroud comes from her estate and the husband is not liable.
This consensus limits the dispute to cases where she is destitute.
5
Recognize the practical consequence
Under Abu Yusuf, the husband bears the cost; under Muhammad, the responsibility falls on relatives or the community. The passage does not give a tarjih, so either view could be followed.
The thamarah al-khilaf is real: who pays?

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the shroud of a woman who dies without wealth. Abu Yusuf (Abu Hanifa's disciple) holds that the husband must provide her shroud, drawing an analogy (qiyas) to the wife's clothing (kiswah) during marriage, which is the husband's obligation. Since the shroud is comparable to posthumous clothing, the obligation continues. Muhammad al-Shaybani (the other leading disciple) disagrees, arguing that the marriage bond (zawjiyyah) ends at death, so the husband's obligations—including maintenance and clothing—cease. Therefore, he is not required to provide the shroud. If the wife has her own wealth, there is consensus that her shroud is taken from her own estate, and the husband is not obligated. This ikhtilaf highlights a key difference between the two imams in extending obligations beyond death.

Key Terms
الكسوة (al-kiswah): Clothing that the husband is obligated to provide for his wife during marriage.
الزوجية (al-zawjiyyah): The marital bond, which Muhammad argues ends at death.
الكفن (al-kafan): The shroud used to wrap the deceased's body for burial.

Permissibility of Marriage and Raj'ah during I'tikaf
Permissibility of contracting marriage and revoking divorce during i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a mu'takif may marry and revoke a divorce, and explain why these acts are permissible.

Core Idea
Verbal marital acts are allowed in i'tikaf because they do not require leaving the mosque.

This short ruling clarifies that while physical intimacy breaks i'tikaf, purely verbal acts like contracting marriage or revoking a divorce are fully permissible. Understanding this distinction helps you apply the rules of i'tikaf correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the Arabic text: ويجوز للمعتكف أن يتزوج، ويراجع. The key verb is يجوز (it is permissible). Identify the subject (المعتكف) and the two actions: يتزوج (marry) and يراجع (revoke divorce).
2
Understand the Scope
These are verbal contracts that can be completed without leaving the mosque. The ruling does not extend to consummation (intercourse), which invalidates i'tikaf.
Intercourse is a separate ruling not covered here.
3
Apply the Rationale
The Hanafi principle: acts that do not require exiting the mosque are permissible during i'tikaf. Marriage and raj'ah are mere utterances, so they are allowed.
This contrasts with acts like trade or medical treatment that may require moving.
4
Exclude What Is Not Permitted
Note that the text does not permit divorce or other breakers of i'tikaf. Only marriage and revocation of divorce are explicitly allowed.
Divorcing during i'tikaf is also permissible by analogy? Check other sources.

These are considered valid acts that do not invalidate i'tikaf, as they do not require leaving the mosque. The rationale is that both are verbal contracts that can be done while staying in the mosque.
