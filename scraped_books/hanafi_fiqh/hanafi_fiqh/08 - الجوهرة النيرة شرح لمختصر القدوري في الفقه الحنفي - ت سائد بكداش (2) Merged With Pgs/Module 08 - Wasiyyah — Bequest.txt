Effect of Will on Funeral Prayer Leadership
Ruling on a deceased's will appointing someone to lead the prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a will appointing a funeral prayer leader and evaluate the opposing positions of Ahmad and Malik.

Core Idea
A deceased's will does not override the guardian's right to lead the funeral prayer.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a three-way dispute on whether a will (wasiyyah) appointing a specific person to lead the janazah prayer can override the guardian's authority. The Hanafi school firmly holds that the guardian's right prevails, while Ahmad and Malik give different weights to the will. Understanding this ruling clarifies the boundaries of a deceased's wishes versus living guardianship.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the will does not take precedence over the guardian. The appointee is not presented over the guardian (لم يقدم على الولي). Some Hanafis even say the will is invalid.
This is the mufta bihi position in the madhhab.
2
Identify the opponents
Ahmad ibn Hanbal holds that the executor (wasi) is more entitled (أولى). Malik distinguishes: the executor is preferred only if his prayer is hoped to be accepted (ممن يُرجى دعاؤه).
Both differ from the Hanafi view.
3
Follow the opponents' proofs
Although the passage does not cite proofs, the opponents likely rely on the general principle of honoring a deceased's wish (wasiyyah).
For Malik, the condition of 'hoped supplication' indicates a focus on benefit to the deceased.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is based on the strength of guardianship (wilayah) over a mere request. The guardian has a pre-eminent right in funeral matters, and a wasiyyah cannot override this.
This is consistent with other Hanafi rulings where wilayah overrides recommendation.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, the guardian may ignore the will and lead himself or choose another. Under Ahmad, the executor must lead. Under Malik, the executor leads only if he is a righteous person.
This affects real funeral planning.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute: if the deceased leaves a will (wasiyyah) asking that a specific person lead his funeral prayer, does that person override the guardian's right? According to the Hanafi school, the guardian's right remains superior; the will does not bind the guardian. Some Hanafi scholars even say such a will is invalid (batil). Imam Ahmad holds that the executor (wasi, the one appointed in the will) has priority. Imam Malik distinguishes: if the executor is a righteous person whose prayer is hoped to be answered, then he is presented ahead of the guardian. The Hanafi position is based on the general principle that guardianship (wilayah) is stronger than a mere request, and funeral prayer is a collective duty where the guardian's authority takes precedence.

Key Terms
وصية (wasiyyah): A last will and testament, here regarding who should lead the funeral prayer.
وصي (wasi): The executor or the person appointed in the will.
ولي (wali): Guardian, typically the nearest male relative who has the primary right to lead the funeral prayer.
