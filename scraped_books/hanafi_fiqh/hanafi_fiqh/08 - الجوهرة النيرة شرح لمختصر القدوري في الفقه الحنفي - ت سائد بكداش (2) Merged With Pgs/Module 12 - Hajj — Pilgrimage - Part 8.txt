Killing game inside the Haram is a serious violation, and the penalty depends on the offender's state of ihram. When multiple people participate, they do not share a single penalty equally—each bears a different obligation. This lesson explains the Hanafi rulings for these joint scenarios.

For a single person: muhrim pays full value of the game; halal pays nothing if alone (unless in Haram, then half); mufrid pays one full penalty (e.g., a sheep); qarin pays two penalties.

Core Idea
Releasing game when in ihram is wajib, but ownership remains — you can reclaim it later.

This lesson examines a specific scenario during Hajj: what happens if you enter ihram while already holding game? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that balances the restrictions of ihram with property rights. Understanding this distinction between possession and ownership is essential for correctly applying the rules of ihram.

Guided Path

Identify the act and the ruling

The obligation is immediate upon realizing the ihram.
2
Determine the hukm level
The ruling is wajib (obligatory), not fard. This is because it is a requirement of the state of ihram, not a pillar of Hajj. In Hanafi usul, wajib is established by a zanni dalil, while fard is qat'i.
The text uses 'alayhi an yursilahu' (عليه أن يرسله), which indicates compulsion.

Understand the legal effect of release
Releasing the game does not extinguish ownership. The text explicitly states that ownership (milk) does not cease (lā yazūl) by the act of releasing.
Ownership is a legal construct separate from physical possession.
4
Apply the ruling to a post-ihram scenario
If after becoming halal (free from ihram), the original owner finds the game in the possession of someone else, they have the right to reclaim it (huwa awlā bihi). This is because their ownership persisted.
This ruling protects property rights even during the state of ihram.

Sending the Sacrifice and Intra-Madhhab Disputes on Tahlil
Intra-madhhab dispute on tahlil procedure and timing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the procedure for tahlil of a muhsar in the hill, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the timing of the sacrifice and the role of shaving.

Core Idea
Tahlil for a muhsar requires slaughter in the Haram; shaving is not required except in the Haram or according to Sahibayn.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira details the practical steps for a person prevented from completing Hajj (muhsar) to exit ihram by sending a sacrifice to the Haram. It then records two important intra-madhhab disputes: whether the sacrifice is tied to the Day of Nahr according to the Sahibayn, and whether shaving is necessary for tahlil. Understanding these disputes helps you navigate differing rulings within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is tahlil (exiting ihram) for a muhsar in the hill (outside the Haram). The ruling is that it requires sending a sheep (or its value) to be slaughtered in the Haram on an appointed day.
Tahlil is not valid until the slaughter occurs.
2
Trace the procedure steps
The muhsar must send a sheep or its value to the Haram, appoint a specific day with the carrier, and then he may perform tahlil after slaughter. If slaughter occurs outside the Haram or on a different day and he mistakenly thinks it is done, he must repeat the dam and remains in ihram.
A qarin requires two sacrifices.
3
Identify the intra-madhhab dispute on timing
Abu Hanifa holds that the dam of ihsar is not tied to the Day of Nahr, so the muhsar must appoint any day. The Sahibayn hold it is tied to the Day of Nahr, so no appointment is needed.
This affects validity if slaughter occurs on a different day.
4
Identify the intra-madhhab dispute on shaving
According to the Sahibayn, tahlil occurs by shaving (halq), with Abu Yusuf reportedly considering it wajib. According to Abu Hanifa, tahlil occurs by slaughter alone; shaving is recommended except when muhsar in the Haram, where it becomes wajib.
The mufta bihi follows Abu Hanifa on the primacy of slaughter.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If a muhsar sends the animal to be slaughtered on a day other than the Day of Nahr, his tahlil is valid according to Abu Hanifa but invalid according to the Sahibayn. Similarly, omitting shaving affects tahlil according to them but not according to Abu Hanifa.
Knowing these differences allows proper fatwa depending on which view is relied upon.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling for a person prevented from completing Hajj or Umrah (muhsir) while outside the Haram. Identify the act and ruling

The Timing of Slaughtering Dam al-Ihsar: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Explains the timing and place of dam al-ihsar, focusing on intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the timing of slaughtering dam al-ihsar for the muhsar of Hajj, explain the disagreement of the Sahibayn, identify their respective proofs, and evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Dam al-ihsar may be slaughtered before Yawm al-Nahr because it is an expiatory sacrifice, not tied to a specific time.

In this passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah, the author addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: may the dam al-ihsar (the sacrificial animal for one prevented from completing Hajj or Umrah) be slaughtered before Yawm al-Nahr? Abu Hanifa permits it, while his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, restrict slaughter to Yawm al-Nahr for the muhsar of Hajj. Understanding their reasoning—especially the distinction between a dam kaffarah (expiatory) and a dam nusuk (ritual)—is central to mastering Hanafi fiqh of Hajj.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify that according to Abu Hanifa, the dam al-ihsar for a muhsar of Hajj may be slaughtered before Yawm al-Nahr. This is the mufta bihi position of the school.
Abu Hanifa also permits slaughter after Yawm al-Nahr; the key is that no specific time is required.
2
Identify the opposing view (Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that slaughter for the muhsar of Hajj is only valid on Yawm al-Nahr. They analogize it to the hady of tamattu' and qiran.
For the muhsar of Umrah, all agree that slaughter can be done anytime.
3

The Sahibayn use qiyas: just as the hady of tamattu' and qiran must be slaughtered on Yawm al-Nahr, so too must the dam al-ihsar, because both involve exiting ihram via sacrifice.
They treat all Hajj-related sacrifices as similar in timing.
4

Abu Hanifa counters that dam al-ihsar is a dam kaffarah (expiatory blood), which may not be eaten, whereas the hady of tamattu' and qiran is dam nusuk (ritual blood), which may be eaten. Therefore, they belong to different categories, and the qiyas fails.
He also cites Quran 2:196, which mentions only place (mahillahu), not time.
5

The practical consequence: if a muhsar slaughters before Yawm al-Nahr, his sacrifice is valid according to Abu Hanifa and he exits ihram; if he follows the Sahibayn, he must repeat the sacrifice on Yawm al-Nahr or remain in ihram.

Muhsar's Options After Sending Hady: Three Scenarios and the Sahibayn Dispute
Matn on muhsar's options after sending hady when prevention ends

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios for the muhsar whose prevention is lifted after sending hady, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over the third scenario.

Core Idea
A muhsar who can reach Hajj but not his hady may exit by istihsan—but only according to Abu Hanifa, not the Sahibayn.

When a muhsar (prevented pilgrim) has already sent a sacrificial animal (hady) to be slaughtered on a specific day, and then the prevention is lifted, what is he to do? The answer depends on his ability to reach both the Hajj and the hady. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents three scenarios, but a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute arises over the third scenario. Understanding this ikhtilaf highlights the importance of the timing of the blood sacrifice in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The matn divides the situation into three depending on whether the muhsar can reach the hady and/or the Hajj after the prevention lifts. List them clearly.
The first two are straightforward; the third is disputed.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For scenario 1 (can reach both), he must proceed with Hajj and may not exit. For scenario 2 (can reach hady but not Hajj), he exits by slaughtering the hady. For scenario 3 (can reach Hajj but not hady), he may exit by istihsan.
Istihsan allows exit despite not sacrificing immediately.
3
Identify the ikhtilaf
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree with the third scenario. They argue that the blood sacrifice for ihsar is tied to Yawm al-Nahr, so if one can reach Hajj, he can necessarily reach the hady (since it is slaughtered on that day at Mina). Therefore, scenario 3 is impossible in their view.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the timing of the dam al-ihsar.
4

Abu Hanifa's proof: the dam al-ihsar is not tied to a specific day; thus, one can reach Hajj while missing the hady if the hady was scheduled at a different time or place. The Sahibayn's proof: the dam is tied to Yawm al-Nahr, so reaching Hajj implies reaching that day and thus the hady.
The difference rests on whether the sacrifice has a fixed time (tawqit).
5

According to Abu Hanifa, a muhsar who finds himself able to reach Hajj but not the hady may exit without sacrificing immediately (though the hady remains due). According to the Sahibayn, this situation cannot occur; if he can reach Hajj, he must sacrifice before exiting.
The practical difference: whether an exit without immediate sacrifice is ever permissible in this scenario.

Sharing a Camel or Cow for Hady: The Condition of Qurbah
Ruling on sharing a large animal for hady with condition of intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sharing a camel or cow for hady and apply the condition of qurbah to determine the validity of the sacrifice.

Core Idea
A camel or cow can serve up to seven persons only if each intends qurbah; if one intends meat, the sacrifice is invalid for all.

In the Hanafi school, large animals like camels and cows can be shared by up to seven people for the hady sacrifice. However, this sharing is conditional upon each partner intending qurbah (closeness to Allah). This lesson explains the ruling and consequence of different intentions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Recognize that a camel (badanah) or cow (baqarah) can be shared by up to seven persons for hady. This is the default ruling from the text.
2
Identify the condition
Each partner must intend qurbah (any valid form of worship). The text specifies that even if intentions differ (e.g., tamattu', qiran, tatabbu'), the condition is satisfied as long as qurbah is the goal.
Contrast with Imam Zufar's stricter view requiring identical types.
3
Recognize the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Zufar holds that all partners must intend the same category of hady. The relied-upon Hanafi position allows differing types because all seek closeness to Allah.
Analyze the basis: the unity of purpose (qurbah) overrides differences in specific rituals.
4
Apply the consequence of invalid intention
If any partner intends only meat (no qurbah), or if a non-Muslim (dhimmi) is among them, the sacrifice does not suffice for any of the partners. The entire sacrifice is invalid.
The invalidation spreads to all because the share of the faulty partner taints the whole.

Eating from the Hady of Tatabbu', Tamattu', and Qiran
Ruling on permissibility of eating from the hady.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the categories of hady from which eating is permitted and state the condition for the voluntary hady.

Core Idea
Eating from the hady is permitted only for tatabbu', tamattu', and qiran; for tatabbu', it must have reached its place.

While the hady is primarily an act of worship, there is a concession for the donor to eat from certain types. The Hanafi school specifies that eating is allowed only from voluntary hady (tatabbu'), tamattu' hady, and qiran hady. For the voluntary hady, the animal must have reached its destination (mahill). This lesson clarifies the scope and conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories
The text states that eating is permitted from three types: tatabbu' (voluntary), tamattu', and qiran. No other types, such as obligatory hady (dam) or badal, are included.
Tatabbu' is supererogatory; tamattu' and qiran are wajib due to combining Hajj and 'Umrah.
2
Note the condition for tatabbu'
For the voluntary hady, the permission is conditional upon the animal reaching the place of sacrifice (mahill). The commentator clarifies: 'يعني بالتطوع : إذا بلغ مَحِلَّه' meaning when it reaches its destination.
Mahill refers to the place where sacrifice is performed (e.g., Mina during Hajj).
3
Recognize the broader permission
Additionally, the donor may feed the wealthy from this hady. The text says 'وكذا له أن يُطعمه الغني' meaning he may also feed the rich.
This adds to the usual charity distribution; no restriction against feeding the affluent.

Timing of Slaughter: Three Categories of Dam in Hajj
Intra-Hanafi classification of hady slaughter timing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the types of dam (blood) in Hajj according to when they may be slaughtered and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding dam al-ihsar.

Core Idea
Three categories: permitted before Nahr by ijma, prohibited before Nahr by ijma, and disputed for ihsar between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

The timing of slaughtering sacrificial animals in Hajj is not uniform. The classical Hanafi texts distinguish between types of blood offerings, with some permitted before the Day of Nahr by consensus, others prohibited, and one category—dam al-ihsar—subject to an internal Hanafi dispute. This lesson unpacks that classification and the practical implications of the ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The text divides bloods into three types: (1) those that may be offered before the Day of Nahr by consensus (expiations, vows, voluntary hady), (2) those that may not be offered before the Day of Nahr by consensus (tamattu', qiran, udhiyah), and (3) dam al-ihsar, which is disputed.
The consensus (ijma) applies only to the first two categories.
2
Locate the disputed point
For dam al-ihsar, Abu Hanifa permits slaughter before the Day of Nahr, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) do not permit it. The dispute is recorded without resolution in this passage.
The text does not provide proofs; it merely states the ikhtilaf.
3
Note the additional ruling on voluntary hady
Al-Mabsut adds that voluntary hady may be slaughtered before the Day of Nahr, but slaughtering on the Day of Nahr is better (afdal). This confirms the permissibility but recommends the preferred time.
This ruling aligns with the first category.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: A pilgrim prevented from completing Hajj (muhsar) may offer his hady before the 10th of Dhul Hijjah according to Abu Hanifa, but not according to the Sahibayn. The mufta bihi in later Hanafi fatwa tends to follow the Sahibayn.
This affects when the pilgrim can be released from ihram.
