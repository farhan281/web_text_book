Ruling on Using Miswak While Fasting
States permissibility with ikhtilaf from Shafi'i and Abu Yusuf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on using miswak while fasting and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position and Abu Yusuf's view.

Core Idea
The miswak is permissible at any time for a fasting person, even if wet.

The miswak is a highly recommended act of cleanliness, but when fasting, some scholars restrict its use. This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira states the Hanafi position clearly: it is permissible morning and evening, dry or wet. It also addresses the Shafi'i objection and an intra-Hanafi difference from Abu Yusuf. By studying this, you will understand how general hadith evidence supports the Hanafi view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is using the miswak (siwak) while fasting. The Hanafi ruling is 'la ba'sa' (no harm), meaning permissible (mubah) without dislike.
2
Locate the Hanafi proof
The proof is the hadith: 'خير خلال الصائم: السواك' (The best characteristics of the fasting person is the miswak). This indicates commendation without temporal restriction.
Hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but still valid for establishing permissibility.
3
Understand the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that miswak is disliked (makruh) in the evening because it removes the mouth odor that Allah loves. The Hanafi response is that the hadith about miswak is general and the mouth odor is not detracted.
The difference is in the weight given to different ahadith.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
Abu Yusuf disliked the use of a wet miswak, fearing moisture might enter the throat. The majority of Hanafis (including Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) allow it, as the hadith is general.
The mufta bihi is that both dry and wet are permissible.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis use miswak anytime; Shafi'is avoid it after noon; Abu Yusuf avoids a wet miswak.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Chewing is only disliked if she could avoid it; necessity overrides the dislike.

A fasting woman may need to soften food for her infant by chewing it. Does this break her fast or is it simply disliked? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether she has another way to feed the child. This lesson unpacks the conditions and the underlying rationale of child welfare.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is a fasting woman chewing food for her child. The default ruling in the matn is 'yukrah' — it is disliked. Recognize that 'makruh' here means makruh tanzihi (disliked, not sinful).
In Hanafi fiqh, 'karahah' without qualification usually means tanzihi.
2
Locate the condition that changes the ruling
The text says 'idha kana laha minhu bad' — if she has an alternative from it. Understand 'bad' as another means: a different person to chew, or food that does not require chewing.
Examples given: a small child (who is not fasting), a menstruating woman (not fasting), or soft food.

Apply the condition to determine when it is not disliked
If she has no alternative (la bad laha), the ruling flips to 'la ba's' — no harm, it is not disliked. The reason is child welfare (siyanat al-walad).
The text draws an analogy: she may even break her fast if she fears for the child's safety.
4
Contrast the ruling with invalidating the fast
Merely chewing does not break the fast unless something is swallowed. This ruling only concerns the dislike, not the validity of the fast.
Common misconception: chewing implies breaking. Hanafi law requires actual ingestion to invalidate.

White cohesive gum is disliked but does not break the fast; black gum breaks it even if cohesive.

Only genuine, probable harm from fasting permits breaking; speculative fear is not enough.

This lesson examines the Hanafi criteria for a sick person to break the fast. The matn from al-Quduri states the basic ruling; the commentary from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on what constitutes valid fear, including a practical test from Abu Hanifa. Understanding these conditions ensures one does not break the fast without legitimate excuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is breaking the fast during Ramadan. The ruling is that it is permissible (mubah) but requires qada (making up) later. Determine why it is not obligatory to break even if ill (unless life is in danger).
The ruling is permission, not obligation.
2
Recognize the valid grounds for breaking
The commentary lists specific symptoms: fever worsening, eye pain, headache, or diarrhea. Also includes delayed recovery. These are signs of genuine harm.
General weakness from past illness does not count.

Apply the test of praying sitting
Abu Hanifa gives a practical criterion: if the person is so weak that he is allowed to pray sitting, then he may break the fast. This links weakness for fasting to weakness for standing in prayer.
This is a useful benchmark for borderline cases.
4
Distinguish invalid fear (mawhum)
After recovery, residual weakness and fear of relapse are not valid excuses because the fear is speculative. The text explicitly says such fear is not considered.
Fear must be based on strong probability (ghalabat al-zann), not mere imagination.

Core Idea
In Ramadan, qada is due only for days of actual ability; in a vow, the entire obligation crystallizes upon the vow.

This passage deals with a scenario where a person with a valid excuse (sickness or travel) gains the ability to fast—by recovery or becoming resident—but then dies before making up the missed fasts. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Missing Ramadan fasts may be made up in any order—together or separately—because the Quran says 'other days' without requiring consecutiveness.

Many Muslims assume that make-up fasts must be performed consecutively like the original month. However, Hanafi fiqh grants full flexibility. In this lesson, we examine the ruling from the matn of al-Quduri and the evidence from Surah al-Baqarah.

The author cites Quran 2:184: 'fa-‘iddatun min ayyāmin ukhar' (then a number of other days). This is the primary evidence.

Analyse the wajh al-dalālah
The key word is 'ukhar' (other). It is a general term that does not specify consecutiveness. Therefore, the obligation is merely to complete the count, and the manner is left to the individual's choice.

4

A person may schedule make-up fasts on any days, separate or in a block. This ruling provides ease and flexibility, especially for those with busy schedules.
Some mistakenly think qada must be consecutive like Ramadan, but the evidence shows otherwise.

Commentary's expansion by qiyas to prayers and disagreement on witr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi extension of the feeding ruling to missed prayers and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the inclusion of witr.

Core Idea
Later Hanafi scholars extend the ruling of feeding for missed fasts to missed prayers by analogy, but the inclusion of witr depends on whether it is considered wajib.

This part of the passage expands the original ruling by analogy to prayers, following a choice of later scholars (mutāʼakhkhirūn). It also records a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on the status of witr and its inclusion in this ruling. Understanding this dispute helps clarify the operative position for fatwa and the methodological principle of qiyas khafi.

Guided Path

The base ruling is the obligation to feed on behalf of the deceased for missed obligatory fasts, provided a bequest exists.

Muhammad ibn Muqatil argued that a whole day's prayers equal one day's fast, so feeding for all prayers of one day is one feeding. He retracted, and the correct view is that each obligatory prayer individually equals one day's feeding.

The principle: only obligatory acts are covered by the ruling according to the two disciples.

The mufta bihi is that each prayer individually equals one day's feeding, and witr is included per Abu Hanifa's view (since later scholars adopt his position in this context).
The text does not give explicit tarjih, but the Hanafi fatwa usually follows Abu Hanifa on witr being wajib.

Abstention for the Newly Pubescent or New Muslim in Ramadan
Intra-Hanafi dispute on wajib vs mustahabb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on abstention for a person who becomes obligated to fast mid-day and explain the intra-madhhab dispute regarding its binding force.

Core Idea
Day entry creates respect, not a valid fast – wajib or mustahabb? The Hanafi school disputes.

This lesson tackles a boundary case: a child who reaches puberty or a disbeliever who accepts Islam during a Ramadan day. The text requires them to abstain for the rest of the day. But is that abstention an obligation or merely recommended? We explore the intra-Hanafi difference between the majority view and Ibn Shuja'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and trigger event
Who are the people in question? A child reaching puberty (sabi) and a disbeliever converting to Islam. The trigger is that this occurs during a day of Ramadan, after the day has already begun.
The child was not obligated before, the disbeliever was not a Muslim.
2
State the default ruling from the matn
Al-Quduri's matn says: 'they both abstain for the remainder of their day.' The verb 'amsaka' is imperative, implying a requirement.
The matn does not specify the level of obligation.
3
Identify the dispute and its source
The commentator explicitly asks: 'Is this abstention wajib or mustahabb?' He then reports Ibn Shuja's view that it is mustahabb, implying the opposing (majority) view is that it is wajib.
Ibn Shuja' (d. 267 AH) is a known Hanafi authority, so this is an intra-madhhab difference.
4
Analyze the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If wajib: breaking the abstention is a sin and requires make-up (qada) of that day. If mustahabb: no sin, but reward for abstaining; no make-up required.
This directly affects the person's liability and recommended action.
5
Note the unresolved status and mufta bihi
The text does not give a tarjih (preference). The commentator only reports Ibn Shuja's view. The dominant Hanafi position today is that it is wajib.
Later Hanafi works generally treat this abstention as wajib out of reverence for the month.

Voluntary Fasting: Permission of Husband and Master
Rulings on voluntary fasting permission for wives and slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on voluntary fasting by a married woman without her husband's permission, identify the three exceptions, and distinguish the absolute permission required for slaves.

Core Idea
A wife must not fast voluntarily without husband's permission unless he is sick, fasting, or in ihram; a slave must always have the master's permission.

Voluntary acts of worship increase reward, but they must not conflict with the rights of others. This lesson explores how the Hanafi school balances a spouse's marital rights and a master's ownership rights against voluntary fasting. Understanding these rules helps avoid sin while fulfilling devotional goals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage rules separately for married women and slaves (including mudabbar and umm walad). Note the general rule for each.
2
Extract the permission rule for a wife
For a wife, voluntary fasting without husband's permission is disliked (makruh tahrimi). Identify the three exceptions: husband sick, fasting himself, or in ihram.
These exceptions suspend the husband's right to intimacy.
3
Extract the permission rule for a slave
For a slave, voluntary fasting without master's permission is never allowed. Note the text says 'how ever it may be' – no exceptions.
The master owns the slave's time and benefits.
4
Understand the consequences
If a wife or slave fasts without permission, the husband/master can force them to break the fast. Later, they must make up (qada) that day after permission is given or after the right expires.
For a wife: qada after husband permits or dies. For a slave: qada after master permits or emancipation.
5
Compare the underlying rationale
The husband's right is specific to marital intimacy; when he cannot exercise that right (sick, fasting, ihram), his permission is not needed. The master's right is over all benefits of the slave, so it is absolute.

Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on child/conversion restraint

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a child reaching puberty or a non-Muslim converting during Ramadan must fast the rest of that day, and distinguish between the wajib and mustahabb positions.

Core Idea
The restraint on the day of puberty/conversion is wajib, not mustahabb, per the correct Hanafi view.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira discusses a specific scenario: when a child attains puberty or a non-Muslim accepts Islam during a day in Ramadan. The key question is whether they must refrain from food and drink for the remainder of that day, and if so, whether this is obligatory or merely recommended. The commentary records an intra-Hanafi dispute between Ibn Shuja' and Imam al-Saffar, with the latter's view preferred.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling context
The act is 'imsak' (abstaining) for the rest of the day. Applicable to a child who reaches puberty during the day or a non-Muslim who converts.
The ruling applies regardless of whether the conversion/puberty occurs before or after noon.
2

Ibn Shuja' says it is mustahabb; Imam al-Saffar says it is wajib. The text states 'al-sahih annahu wajib'.
The preferred view is wajib, though not all Hanafi scholars agree.
3
Consider the timing
If after noon, or before noon after eating, mere restraint is required without intention. If before noon and before eating, the child can make it a voluntary fast by intention, but the convert cannot.
The child is eligible for voluntary worship before noon; the convert is not.

If they break the restraint, no qada is required because fasting was not obligatory on that day. They fast the remaining days of Ramadan but do not make up past days.
The day of change is not counted as a Ramadan fast for them.
5
Distinguish the two positions
Wajib: sin for not restraining. Mustahabb: no sin, but reward missed. The mufta bihi is wajib.
The practical difference is whether one incurs sin by not restraining.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If unconsciousness occurs during a Ramadan day with prior intention, no qada; if from night through Ramadan, all days are made up except the night.

This passage clarifies the rulings for someone who becomes unconscious (mughma 'alayh) during the month of Ramadan. The key distinction is between unconsciousness that begins during the day versus from the night, and the presence of intention. The text also covers insanity (junun) for the entire month.

Scenario A: unconscious during the day after having intended the fast. Scenario B: unconscious from the beginning of the night through the entire Ramadan.

Fasting Obligations for a Child Reaching Puberty or a Non-Muslim Embracing Islam During Ramadan
Ruling on child/non-Muslim fasting remainder of day

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fasting the remainder of the day for a child who reaches puberty or a non-Muslim who embraces Islam during Ramadan, and explain the reasoning based on cause, capacity, and address.

Core Idea
When obligation begins mid-day, the past is not owed—only the remainder counts.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a child who reaches puberty during a Ramadan day or a non-Muslim who embraces Islam during the day. The Hanafi school gives a detailed ruling on what they must do for that day and whether they need to make up the past days. Understanding this ruling clarifies how the obligation of fasting is tied to legal capacity and divine address.

Guided Path
1
Identify the persons and the act
The ruling applies to two categories: a child who reaches puberty during a Ramadan day, and a non-Muslim who embraces Islam during a Ramadan day. The act in question is fasting the remainder of that day.
These are cases of obligation arising mid-day.
2
State the two-part ruling
First, they must fast the remainder of the day (from the moment of obligation until sunset). Second, they do not make up the past days of Ramadan before that moment.
The ruling includes both an immediate duty and a dispensation from past missed days.
3
Analyze the proof (talil)
The ruling is based on three concepts: sabab (cause – the day of Ramadan), ahliyyah (legal capacity – present at puberty/Islam), and khitab (divine address – absent before the moment of obligation). For the remainder, both cause and capacity are present; for the past, the address was absent.
The Hanafi school uses this causal reasoning to limit the obligation.
4
Distinguish the application by timing and eating
If the refraining starts after zawal (midday) or before zawal but after having already eaten, it is merely restraint (imsaak) with no reward. If before zawal and before eating, the child may intend a supererogatory fast (nafl) and it counts; the non-Muslim’s intent does not make it supererogatory.
The difference stems from the child’s capacity for worship (ahliyyat al-ada') which the non-Muslim lacked before Islam.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) hold that the individual must make up the entire day if they were capable at some point, but the Hanafi school limits to the remainder.
This difference is rooted in how each school defines the moment of obligation.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ruling on a fasting person who breaks fast mistakenly. The author presents the Hanafi ruling: there is no sin if one had a dominant belief (zann ghalib) that the sun had set, but if one was doubtful (shakk) and broke fast, then kaffarah is required because the original presumption (asl) is the continuation of day. The reasoning is based on the legal maxim 'certainty is not overridden by doubt' (al-yaqin la yazulu bil-shakk). The author contrasts this with doubt about the rising of dawn: if one doubts whether dawn has risen and eats, no kaffarah because the original presumption is continuation of night, so the act is not considered transgressive. Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi disagrees with the ruling on doubt about sunset; he holds that no kaffarah is obligatory even in the doubtful case because the person intended to follow the sunnah of hastening to break fast. The author implicitly sides with the first view, as it is the established Hanafi position.

Key Terms
الظن الغالب (al-ẓann al-ghālib): A dominant assumption or probability that is not certainty but is strong enough to act upon.
الكفارة (al-kaffārah): A specific expiation for deliberately invalidating a fast, which in Hanafi school is to fast sixty consecutive days if unable to free a slave.

When a Person Alone Sees the Crescent of Eid: The Hanafi Ruling
Ruling on individual crescent sighting for Eid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a person alone sees the crescent of Shawwal, and explain the consequences if he nevertheless breaks his fast.

Core Idea
A personal sighting of the crescent does not permit you to break your fast — only public testimony or completion of thirty days does.

The sighting of the crescent moon marks the end of Ramadan. But what if you alone see it? This passage from al-Quduri clarifies that your personal observation is not enough to justify breaking your fast. The community's testimony or the completion of the month takes precedence. Understanding this ruling protects you from acting on incomplete evidence and clarifies the distinction between personal certainty and legal proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its overall ruling
The act is seeing the crescent of Shawwal alone. The ruling is that he does not break his fast; breaking is prohibited (haram).
The prohibition is because the sighting has not been legally established by public testimony or completion of thirty days.
2
Determine the consequence of violation
If he breaks the fast despite the prohibition, he must make up that day (qada') but there is no expiation (kaffarah) required.
The absence of kaffarah indicates that this is not a violation of a confirmed Ramadan fast.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi difference of opinion
Some Hanafi scholars (قال بعضهم) permit breaking the fast secretly if the individual is certain of his sighting. However, the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that he does not break at all.
This variant is noted but not adopted in the authoritative works.
4
Understand the underlying legal principle
The crescent must be established by public testimony (at least two upright witnesses) or by completing thirty days of Ramadan. Individual sighting, even if certain, is not sufficient legal proof.
This principle safeguards communal consistency and prevents confusion.
