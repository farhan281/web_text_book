Definition, Pillar, and Conditions of Itikaf
Defines itikaf, its rukn and shart, and evidences for fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of itikaf, identify its pillar (rukn) and conditions (shurut), and explain the Hanafi ruling on its obligation and the disputed requirement of fasting.

Core Idea
Staying in a mosque is the pillar; fasting is a condition for obligatory itikaf, but voluntary itikaf may be momentary.

This lesson unpacks the foundational rulings of itikaf as defined by the Hanafi school. You will learn what constitutes a valid itikaf, the role of intention and fasting, and the emphatic sunnah of the last ten days of Ramadan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its definition
The passage defines itikaf as 'staying in the mosque with fasting and intention of itikaf'. Note that this three-part definition immediately establishes the rukn (staying) and two shurut (fasting and intention).
The definition is quoted from al-Quduri's matn.
2
Distinguish pillar (rukn) from condition (shart)
The author explicitly states: 'As for staying, it is its pillar; as for fasting, it is its condition; and intention is a condition in all acts of worship.' Understand the difference: omission of the rukn invalidates the act entirely; omission of a condition also invalidates, but the condition must be present at the start.
In Hanafi usul, a rukn is part of the essence; a shart is external but necessary.
3
Locate the evidence for fasting as a condition
The passage cites the hadith: 'There is no itikaf except with fasting.' This is the primary proof for the condition. Note that the hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) and its dalala is also open to interpretation.
The hadith is found in Sunan Abi Dawud and others, though its authenticity is debated.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on fasting for voluntary itikaf
Two narrations from Abu Hanifa are presented: (1) From Hasan ibn Ziyad – fasting is required for all itikaf, so the minimum is one day. (2) From al-Asl (Imam Muhammad) – voluntary itikaf can be a moment without fasting, based on leniency in supererogatory acts. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the second.
The dispute affects the minimum duration and the requirement of qada' upon breaking.
5
Determine the practical consequence of the chosen position
According to the mufta bihi, a person may enter the mosque with the intention of itikaf, stay for a short moment, and leave without fasting. His itikaf is valid and he is not required to make it up if he breaks it.
This is based on the principle that acts of devotion are built on ease (al-suhulah).

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary of al-Jawharah al-Nayirah on the Mukhtasar al-Quduri, explaining the rules of itikaf. It begins by defining itikaf as staying in a mosque with fasting and intention. The author states that itikaf during the last ten days of Ramadan is an emphatic sunnah, using the Prophet's perpetual practice as evidence. He quotes al-Zuhri to emphasize its importance. Then he analyzes the components: staying (lubth) is the pillar, fasting is a condition (shart), and intention is a condition for all acts. He introduces two narrations from Abu Hanifa: one requiring fasting and a minimum of one day for obligatory itikaf (from Hasan ibn Ziyad), and another from al-Asl (the six core books) allowing a moment without fasting for voluntary itikaf, following Imam Muhammad. He also discusses the ruling on breaking itikaf: in al-Asl no qada' is required, but in Hasan's narration qada' is required because it is specified as a day. The passage then states that itikaf is only valid in a congregational mosque where all five prayers are performed with a known imam and mu'adhdhin. It ranks the best mosques: first Masjid al-Haram, then the Prophet's mosque, then Masjid Bayt al-Maqdis, then those with larger congregations. Finally, it distinguishes two types: obligatory (by vow) and voluntary (nafl). Voluntary itikaf is permissible without fasting and ends when one leaves the mosque.

Key Terms
الاعتكاف (al-iʿtikāf): Linguistically: staying in a place. Legally: staying in a mosque with fasting and intention.
اللبث (al-lubth): Staying, remaining in the mosque. It is the pillar (rukn) of itikaf.
الصوم (al-ṣawm): Fasting. In this context, it is a condition (shart) for the validity of itikaf (obligatory in one narration, voluntary in another).
النية (al-niyyah): Intention, a condition for all acts of worship, including itikaf.

Conditions of the Mosque and Virtues of Itikaf
Explains mosque requirements and preferences for itikaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid itikaf regarding the mosque and identify the preferred mosques for spiritual reward.

Core Idea
Itikaf is only valid in a congregational mosque with five daily prayers; the best is Masjid al-Haram.

This lesson focuses on the spatial and communal requirements for itikaf. You will learn why a congregational mosque is necessary, which mosques carry the greatest virtue, and the distinction between obligatory and voluntary itikaf in terms of these conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for the mosque
The passage states that itikaf is only valid in a congregational mosque (masjid jamā'ah) where all five daily prayers are performed with a known imam and mu'adhdhin. This condition applies to both obligatory and voluntary itikaf.
A mosque that does not hold regular congregational prayers is not suitable.
2
Understand the reasoning behind the condition
Since itikaf involves staying in the mosque for extended periods, the mosque must be a place where the mu'takif can attend the five daily prayers in congregation, which is a emphasized sunnah. Also, being in a mosque with a known imam and mu'adhdhin ensures the prayers are properly established.
Implicitly, this condition ensures that the mu'takif does not miss any obligatory act.
3
Rank the preferred mosques
The passage ranks mosques in order of virtue: first Masjid al-Haram (Makkah), then the Prophet's Mosque (Madinah), then Masjid al-Aqsa (Jerusalem), then mosques with larger congregations. The virtue increases with the number of worshippers.
This ranking affects the reward but not the validity; itikaf in any valid mosque is acceptable.
4
Distinguish between obligatory and voluntary itikaf
The passage reiterates that voluntary itikaf is permissible without fasting and ends upon leaving the mosque. Obligatory itikaf (by vow) has stricter rules, including the condition of fasting and a minimum of one day.
The mosque condition applies to both types.

Leaving for Friday Prayer During I'tikaf
Inter-madhhab dispute with al-Shafi'i on leaving for Friday

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on leaving for Friday prayer during i'tikaf and contrast it with the Shafi'i view, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Leaving for Friday prayer during i'tikaf is permitted because it is a necessary obligation.

This passage addresses a specific exception to the rule that a mu'takif should not leave the mosque. The Hanafi school holds that leaving for Friday prayer is permissible due to its obligatory nature, while Imam al-Shafi'i argues that it invalidates i'tikaf because the person could have chosen a mosque that holds Jumu'ah. Let's examine the reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two positions
What is the act under dispute? Leaving the mosque during i'tikaf to attend Friday prayer. State the Hanafi and Shafi'i rulings.
2
State the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school argues that attending Friday prayer is a great necessity (hajah) that is known to occur. Since it is fard 'ayn, it cannot be sacrificed for i'tikaf.
This is a rational proof based on necessity.
3
State the Shafi'i proof
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the person could have performed i'tikaf in the congregational mosque (masjid jami') where Friday prayer is held, thus eliminating the need to leave.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that although choosing a congregational mosque is preferable, once a person has started i'tikaf in a non-congregational mosque, he is not obligated to abandon it. Friday prayer is a separate obligation that takes precedence.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In the Hanafi school, one may leave and return after Jumu'ah without penalty; in the Shafi'i school, such departure invalidates i'tikaf and requires a new one.

Commentary
This passage addresses another exception to the rule that a person in i'tikaf should not leave the mosque: leaving for Friday prayer. The Hanafi position is that this is permissible because attending Friday prayer is a great necessity (hajah) and it is inevitable for those who are required to attend. The commentator adds that al-Shafi'i disagreed: in his view, leaving for Friday prayer invalidates the i'tikaf because the person could have chosen to perform his i'tikaf in the mosque where Friday prayer is held (al-masjid al-jami'), thus avoiding the need to leave. The Hanafi school, however, does not consider this a valid objection because the person may have started i'tikaf in a different mosque, and Friday prayer is a fard 'ayn that takes precedence; the i'tikaf is not invalidated as long as the person returns promptly after the prayer.

I'tikaf in Any Mosque and the Friday Prayer Obligation
Hanafi response to an objection about Friday prayer in i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that i'tikaf is valid in any mosque and explain why Friday prayer cannot be dropped for i'tikaf, based on the hierarchy of obligations.

Core Idea
A vow cannot override a divine obligation – Friday prayer takes precedence over i'tikaf.

A common objection arises: since Friday prayer can be excused by many legitimate excuses like travel or slavery, why can't it be excused for i'tikaf? The Hanafi response hinges on a fundamental legal principle: the source of obligation. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that while i'tikaf becomes obligatory by a personal vow, Friday prayer is directly commanded by Allah. The servant's vow cannot supersede Allah's command.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on i'tikaf
Identify the ruling: i'tikaf is prescribed (mashru') in any mosque, not only the one where Friday prayer is held.
2
Present the objector's argument
The objector argues that since Friday prayer is excused by many excuses (travel, slavery, etc.), it could also be excused for i'tikaf, so i'tikaf can be performed even in a mosque without Friday prayer.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response rejects this analogy because Friday prayer is a divine obligation (wajib bi amr Allah), while i'tikaf is obligatory only by a human vow (wajib bi al-nadhr). A human cannot drop a divine obligation through a personal vow.
Key distinction: divine obligation > vow obligation.

Ruling on a Chick from an Egg in Hajj
Ruling with istihsan reasoning for value of chick

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for a chick that dies after emerging from a broken egg, and explain the istihsan reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
Potential benefit creates liability: destroying an egg with life potential requires compensation for the chick's value when alive.

In this lesson, we examine a specific case from the chapter of offenses during Hajj: what happens if a muhrim (person in ihram) causes the death of a chick that emerges from an egg? The ruling is not based on strict analogy but on istihsan, because the egg had the potential to produce a live bird. Understanding this ruling illustrates how Hanafi fiqh uses juristic preference to achieve justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being judged? The act is a muhrim striking an egg, causing a chick to emerge and die. The ruling is wajib: he must pay the value of the chick when alive (qimatu-hu hayyan).
The ruling is a compensation (damān) obligation.
2
Analyze the strict qiyas (analogy)
Consider the original case (asl): if the egg had no benefit (e.g., unfertilized), destroying it incurs no compensation. By strict qiyas, this egg also had no live bird at the time of destruction, so no compensation would be due.
This would be the result if we applied simple analogy.
3
Identify the istihsan (juristic preference)
The Hanafi school departs from strict qiyas using istihsan. The effective cause (illa) is the potential benefit of the egg – the possibility that it would produce a live chick. Because this potential exists, the egg is considered valuable, and destroying it triggers compensation for the value of the chick when alive.
Istihsan is based on considering the potential (khafiyyah illa).
4
Apply the ruling to the case
Since the chick died from the blow, the muhrim is liable. The compensation is the market value of the chick as if it had lived at that moment.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
Note the secondary issue
The text also mentions: if a person ate from the slaughtered animal before paying the penalty (for hunting in ihram), the penalty includes the value of what was eaten by consensus. This shows that damān can cover multiple losses.
This reinforces the principle that compensation is comprehensive.

Slaughter Time for Muhsar in Hajj and Umrah: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
States ruling on timing of slaughter for muhsar in Hajj and Umrah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the timing of slaughter for a muhsar bi al-hajj and explain the rationale behind each position using the distinction between dam kaffarah and dam nusuk.

Core Idea
Muhsar's slaughter is expiatory, not ritual; place binds it, time does not.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra presents the classic intra-Hanafi disagreement on when a muhsar (barred pilgrim) may slaughter his hady. Understanding this dispute reveals a key principle: the nature of the sacrifice (expiatory vs. ritual) determines its temporal permissibility. The Sahibayn analogize to tamattu' and qiran, while Abu Hanifa relies on the Quranic text and the definition of dam kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
State the issue and the two positions
Identify that the dispute is about the timing of slaughter for a muhsar bi al-hajj. Abu Hanifa permits any time before tahlil as long as it is in the haram. The Sahibayn restrict it to Yawm al-Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah).
For muhsar bi al-umrah, there is consensus that slaughter is permissible at any time.
2
Identify the proofs for each position
Examine the Sahibayn's proof: analogy (qiyas) with hady al-tamattu' and hady al-qiran, which are time-specific to Yawm al-Nahr. Then examine Abu Hanifa's proof: the Quranic verse (2:196) specifies place but not time, and the classification of muhsar's hady as dam kaffarah (expiatory blood), which is place-bound but time-flexible.
Note that Abu Hanifa uses a textual proof (naqli) while the Sahibayn use qiyas.
3
Analyze the Quranic evidence used by Abu Hanifa
The verse 'until the sacrificial animal reaches its place' (2:196) is interpreted by Abu Hanifa as a spatial condition only. The word 'yablugha mahillahu' implies reaching the place (the Haram), not a specific time. This is a strong dalil because it is a direct command.
The Sahibayn do not deny this verse; they argue that the time is implied by analogy.
4
Examine the analogy used by the Sahibayn
The Sahibayn compare the muhsar's hady to the hady of tamattu' and qiran, which are 'dam nusuk' (sacrificial blood) and must be offered on the days of Nahr. They argue that the muhsar's hady is also a sacrifice tied to the pilgrimage and thus should follow the same temporal restriction.
Abu Hanifa refutes this by distinguishing the nature of the sacrifices.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is that under the Sahibayn's view, if a muhsar misses Yawm al-Nahr, his tahlil may be invalid. Under Abu Hanifa's view, he can slaughter any time in the Haram before tahlil, providing flexibility. The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's position.
This dispute matters for a pilgrim who is prevented late in Hajj and cannot slaughter on the 10th.

Qada for Muhsar: When an Extra Umrah is Needed
Ruling on mandatory compensation for a muhsar and the special case of a qarin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the qada obligations for a muhsar bi al-hajj, a muhsar bi al-umrah, and a qarin, and apply the timing conditions that affect the requirement of an additional Umrah.

Core Idea
A prevented pilgrim's makeup depends on original intention and timing — conditions matter.

When a pilgrim is prevented from completing Hajj or Umrah (ihsar), Islamic law requires a compensatory pilgrimage (qada). However, the exact makeup is not a one-size-fits-all ruling; it depends on whether the original pilgrimage was Hajj or Umrah, whether the pilgrim had combined both (qiran), and crucially, whether the makeup is performed in the same year or the following year. Let us walk through the precise Hanafi rulings as laid out in this passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of muhsar
First, determine whether the prevented pilgrim was performing Hajj only, Umrah only, or both in qiran. Each has a distinct qada obligation.
The text covers three cases: muhsar bi al-hajj, muhsar bi al-umrah, and qarin.
2
Apply the timing condition for muhsar bi al-hajj
If the muhsar bi al-hajj makes up the Hajj in the following year (min qabil), he owes both a Hajj and an Umrah. If he makes it up in the same year (min 'amihi), he owes only the Hajj — the Umrah is not required.
The rationale: in the same year, he is not considered to have 'missed' the Hajj in the same sense.
3
State the rule for muhsar bi al-umrah
A muhsar bi al-umrah owes only one Umrah as qada, without additional requirements. This is because his ihsar is directly from the Umrah itself.
No timing complication here.
4
Handle the qarin case
A qarin who is prevented owes a Hajj and two Umrahs in qada, if he does not repeat Qiran in the same year. One Umrah is the original, the second is because he exited from both ihrams. If he does repeat Qiran in the same year, the second Umrah drops.
This is parallel to the muhsar bi al-hajj case: same-year makeup reduces the Umrah obligation.

الحل (al-hill): The area outside the Haram (sacred precinct) of Makkah.
الحرم (al-haram): The sacred territory of Makkah and its surroundings.

A) Always, as part of tahlil.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school bases its ruling on the prophetic precedent at Hudaybiyya, and the shaving requirement for a muhsar depends on whether he is prevented inside or outside the Haram.
