The Five Rulings of Sadaqat al-Fitr
Lists the five aspects of Sadaqat-ul-Fitr: on whom, to whom, when, amount, from what items.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five rulings of Sadaqat al-Fitr and apply the conditions for its obligation, recipients, timing, amount, and acceptable items.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib: on every free Muslim possessing nisab, given to the poor, at dawn of Eid, half sa' wheat or one sa' dates, from four specific foods or their value.

Sadaqat al-Fitr is a wajib payment at the end of Ramadan. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah distills its rulings into five clear questions: on whom it is obligatory, to whom it is given, when, how much, and from what items. Mastering these five points ensures the correct performance of this obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify who must give
From the phrase 'على الحر، المسلم، المالك للنصاب' (on the free, Muslim, owner of nisab), list the three conditions for obligation: freedom, Islam, and possessing nisab.
Nisab here is the same as for zakat (200 dirhams of silver or equivalent).
2
Identify to whom it is given
From 'للفقراء والمساكين' (to the poor and needy), note that the recipients are the poor and the needy – the same categories as Zakat al-Mal.
Some scholars restrict it to the poor only, but the text includes both.
3
Determine when it becomes obligatory
The passage states 'بطلوع الفجر من يوم الفطر' (at the break of dawn of the day of Fitr). This means the obligation is attached to the Fajr time of Eid al-Fitr.
Hanafis: it becomes wajib at Fajr of Eid, but it is recommended to give before the Eid prayer.
4
Calculate the amount
The amount is half a sa' of wheat or one sa' of dates, barley, or similar staples. A sa' is a unit of volume. The difference reflects the value of the staple.
The wheat amount is half because it is considered superior in quality/value.
5
Determine from what items it is given
The text specifies four items: wheat, barley, dates, and raisins. For other items, only the value (qimah) equivalent to the prescribed amounts is acceptable.
In the Hanafi school, giving the value (money) is permissible when it serves the poor better.

The Five Categories of Sharia Rulings
Introductory classification of Sharia rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories of legislated matters in Sharia and list the five items under each category.

Core Idea
The entire Sharia is divided into five categories: beliefs, worship, transactions, penalties, and expiations.

Before diving into the detailed rulings of zakat, it is essential to grasp the broader framework of Sharia. This passage provides a concise map of all legislated matters, organizing them into five master categories. Memorizing this classification will help you place any fiqh topic within the proper context and see how zakat fits as a financial act of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five master categories
Read the first sentence: 'The legislated matters are five: beliefs, acts of worship, transactions, penalties, and expiations.' Commit these five terms to memory – they are the pillars of the entire fiqh classification.
In Arabic: اعتقادات، عبادات، معاملات، عقوبات، كفارات.
2
Memorize the subdivisions under beliefs
The text lists five articles of faith: belief in Allah, His angels, His books, His messengers, and the Last Day. These are the core tenets of iman in the Hanafi tradition.
These correspond to the well-known six articles of faith, but here summarized as five.
3
Memorize the five acts of worship
The text lists: prayer, fasting, zakat, hajj, and jihad. Note that zakat is placed third – it is a financial worship (ibadah maliyyah).
These five are the well-known pillars of Islam, with shahadah being part of beliefs.
4
Memorize the five transactions, penalties, and expiations
For transactions: exchanges, marriages, disputes, trusts, partnerships. For penalties: five deterrents (qisas, amputation, flogging/stoning, hadd for false accusation, execution for apostasy). For expiations: killing, zihar, breaking fast, oath, hajj violations.
These are the main chapters of Hanafi fiqh.
5
Understand the reduction of worship types
The text concludes that the five acts of worship reduce to three types: purely physical (prayer, fasting, jihad), financial (zakat), and a combined type (hajj, which is financial and physical).
This categorization helps in understanding the nature of each worship.

The Obligation of Zakat: Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma'
Ruling with proof: obligation of zakat and its three sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three sources of evidence for the obligation of zakat and explain why it is considered a fard muhkama in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Zakat is a definitive obligation proven by three independent sources: the Quran, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the Ummah.

The obligation of zakat is one of the most firmly established duties in Islam. In this passage, the author of Al-Quduri states the ruling and then provides three mutually reinforcing proofs: the Quran (with multiple verses), the mutawatir Sunnah (the hadith of the five pillars), and the continuous consensus (ijma' mutawatir). This triple basis elevates zakat to the level of fard muhkama, beyond any doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text begins with a clear statement: 'الزكاة واجبة' (Zakat is obligatory). Confirm the hukm level: fard muhkama, meaning a firmly established obligation.
In Hanafi terminology, fard is used when the evidence is qat'i (definitive), as opposed to wajib which is based on zanni evidence.
2
Locate the first proof: the Quran
The author quotes the verse 'وَأَقِيمُوا الصَّلَوَةَ وَاتُوا الزَّكَوةَ' (2:43). In Hanafi usul, the imperative (amr) indicates obligation (wujub). Additional verses (9:103, 34:39) reinforce the meaning.
The command 'ātu' (give) is a direct imperative, which in Hanafi usul is a default indicator of obligation.
3
Locate the second proof: the Sunnah
The hadith 'بني الإسلام على خمس' (Islam is built on five) is cited, which includes zakat. The author considers this hadith mutawatir (mass-transmitted) in meaning, providing certainty.
Even if the chain is technically ahad, the jurists consider it mutawatir in meaning due to widespread acceptance.
4
Locate the third proof: Ijma'
The text asserts continuous consensus from the time of the Prophet to the present. This is ijma' mutawatir, which is a definitive proof.
Ijma' here is not just a one-time agreement but a continuously transmitted consensus.
5
Synthesize: why fard muhkama?
Combine all three: each proof is qat'i in thubut (transmission) and dalala (meaning). Their convergence makes the obligation absolutely certain, a fard muhkama.
This classification has practical implications: denying zakat is kufr (if one rejects its obligation), and neglecting it without excuse is a major sin.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Zakat is an act of giving, not the wealth itself; its immediacy is disputed between Muhammad (immediate) and Abu Yusuf (delayed).

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi legal definition of zakat, emphasizing that the obligation attaches to the act of the giver, not the property. It then explores a key intra-Hanafi dispute: is the obligation to pay zakat immediate (fawr) or may it be delayed (tarakhi)? The answer affects practical matters like the validity of testimony if payment is postponed.

Guided Path
1
Parse the linguistic and legal definitions
Identify the two linguistic meanings (growth and purification) and the precise legal definition: 'payment of specified wealth in a specified amount.' Note the usul point that 'zakat' refers to the act, not the wealth, because obligation (wujub) is a quality of actions.
2

Learning Objective

Zakat is an act of the giver, not the property; obligation hinges on eight precise conditions.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Is Zakat Obligatory Immediately or Can It Be Delayed?
Discusses the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether zakat must be paid immediately or can be delayed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the positions of Muhammad al-Shaybani and Abu Yusuf on the immediacy of zakat payment and identify the practical consequence of each view.

Core Idea
Muhammad says zakat must be paid immediately; Abu Yusuf allows delay—both argue from different analogies.

The passage, after defining zakat, presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between the two leading disciples of Abu Hanifa: Muhammad al-Shaybani and Abu Yusuf. The question is whether the obligation to pay zakat is immediate (فور) or can be delayed (تراخي) without excuse. Muhammad analogizes zakat to returning a deposit—delaying harms the poor—while Abu Yusuf analogizes it to other financial obligations that can be paid at any time. This dispute has practical implications, such as the validity of testimony for someone who delays.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Muhammad holds zakat is on the spot (فور); Abu Yusuf holds it is on leisure (تراخي). Note the text does not mention Abu Hanifa's own view here.
The dispute is among the sahibayn, both authoritative.
2
Analyze Muhammad's proof
Muhammad argues that delaying harms the poor (the recipients), so it is impermissible without excuse, similar to returning a deposit (which is for the owner's right). If delayed, the giver's testimony is rejected.
He distinguishes zakat from Hajj: Hajj is a right of Allah, so delay is acceptable; zakat is a right of creation, so immediacy is required.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's proof
Abu Yusuf argues that zakat can be paid at any time during the year, unlike Hajj which has a fixed time. The risk of death before performing Hajj makes it immediate, but zakat can be paid anytime, so delay is allowed.
He reverses the analogy: Hajj is immediate, zakat is leisure.
4
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah)
According to Muhammad, if one delays without excuse, his testimony is rejected. According to Abu Yusuf, he is sinful but his testimony is accepted.
This affects social transactions like witnessing contracts.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text does not resolve the dispute. Later Hanafi fatwa often adopts Abu Yusuf's view (leisure), but both are valid within the school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on zakat for minors, the insane, and mukatabs, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding sanity as a condition of obligation versus performance.

Core Idea
Sanity for zakat is a condition of performance, not obligation—the Sahibayn's view prevails, with Abu Hanifa requiring lucidity at both year's start and end.

In this passage, the commentator of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar clarifies that zakat is not obligatory on a minor, an insane person, or a mukatab slave. While the exemption for minors and mukatabs is straightforward, the ruling for the insane involves a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about whether sanity is a condition of obligation (shart al-wujub) or performance (shart al-ada'), and how much lucidity is required during the zakat year. We will unpack these positions and their practical consequences.

Also, advancing zakat is permissible under the Sahibayn's view.

The commentator declares the Sahibayn's view (condition of performance) as correct and states Abu Hanifa's requirement of lucidity at both ends as the correct position from him. These are relied upon in fatwa.

The Effect of Encompassing Debt on Zakat Obligation
Explaining the ruling that a person with debt covering all wealth is exempt from zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on zakat when debt covers all wealth and explain the underlying reasoning (talil) of deficient ownership and original need.

Core Idea
Zakat is not owed when debt covers all wealth because the wealth is considered non‑existent – it is occupied by a primary need.

The matn of al-Quduri states a clear exception to the obligation of zakat: if a person's debt entirely covers his wealth, no zakat is due. The commentator expands on this ruling by providing two integrated reasons – deficient ownership and the classification of debt repayment as an original need – and strengthens the argument through analogies to other exempt categories. In this lesson, we will unpack the talil and see how the Hanafi school justifies this exemption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act under discussion is paying zakat. The default ruling is wajib if a Muslim owns the nisab (minimum threshold) for one lunar year. Which exception does the matn introduce?
2
State the exception from the matn
Al-Quduri states: 'Whoever has a debt that encompasses his wealth, no zakat upon him.' Identify the condition: debt covering all wealth.
3
Analyze the commentator's proof (talil)
The commentator gives two reasons: (1) deficient ownership because the wealth is claimed by the creditor; (2) the wealth is occupied with the debtor's original need (ḥājah aṣliyyah) to repay the debt to avoid harm. This makes the wealth effectively non-existent.
Original needs include what preserves honour and prevents harm, like repayment of a debt that can lead to imprisonment.
4
Examine the analogical reasoning
The commentator compares the wealth to water reserved for thirst (personal or animal) and to a service slave or dwelling house – all are exempt from zakat because they are needed for essential purposes. The same principle applies to debt repayment.
This shows that zakat is only on surplus wealth (ghinā) after fulfilling primary needs.
5
Note the ikhtilāf and authority
This ruling is a legitimate point of difference among schools. Some may not consider debt as preventing zakat. The Hanafi position is from Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah and is the muftā bihī.
Be aware that partial debt does not necessarily exempt; only encompassing debt lifts the obligation.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat obligation when sanity is intermittent

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Two rulings, one school: Abu Yusuf requires most of the year sane; Muhammad requires any occurrence.

Zakat obligation hinges on complete ownership over a full lunar year. But what if the owner was insane for part of the year? Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the two chief disciples of Abu Hanifa, differ on how much sanity is required. This lesson dissects their arguments and the underlying qiyas on fasting.

Guided Path

Abu Yusuf bases his view on the principle 'al-akthar lahukm al-kull' (the majority takes the ruling of the whole). Muhammad relies on qiyas with Ramadan fasting.

Muhammad analogizes zakat to fasting: if one regains sanity at any point in Ramadan, the entire month's fast is obligatory. Similarly, any recovery during the zakat year triggers obligation for the whole year.
The illa is that obligation attaches upon the occurrence of sanity in any part of the relevant period.

Abu Yusuf applies the maxim 'al-akthar lahukm al-kull' to treat most of the year as the whole. Muhammad does not apply this maxim here, focusing on the strict analogy.
This shows how usul principles can lead to different rulings within the same school.

Calculate the thamarah: For a person insane 10 months and sane 2 months, under Abu Yusuf no zakat (since sanity is not most of the year), but under Muhammad zakat is due.
The difference is significant for long-term unconscious patients.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on zakat discharge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf on zakat discharge when giving part of the nisab, explain the reasoning of each (shāʾiʿ vs. muzāḥamah), and identify the practical consequence of the dispute.

Core Idea
Zakat obligation is diffuse (shāʾiʿ) across the nisab, but giving part without intention may not discharge it due to competition (muzāḥamah).

This passage deals with a classic internal Hanafi disagreement: if a person who owns a full nisab gives away a portion of it in charity without specifically intending zakat, does that discharge the zakat on the given portion? Imam Muhammad argues yes because zakat is a common obligation spread over all wealth; Imam Abu Yusuf argues no because the given portion is not specified, creating competition with the remaining wealth. Understanding this dispute sharpens your appreciation of how the Hanafi school handles conflicts of obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the question
The act is giving sadaqah (voluntary charity) from part of the nisab without the intention of fulfilling zakat. The question: does the zakat on that portion drop (squṭ) or not?
2
State Imam Muhammad’s position and dalil
Muhammad holds that the zakat on the given portion falls away. His proof: the obligation is shāʾiʿ (diffuse) over the entire nisab, like a common property. Since every part carries its share of the obligation, when that part leaves ownership, its zakat is discharged.
The key concept is shāʾiʿ – an obligation that is undivided and attached to every atom of the asset.
3
State Imam Abu Yusuf’s position and dalil
Abu Yusuf holds that the zakat does not fall away. His proof: the given portion is not specified for the obligation; the remaining wealth also carries the obligation. This creates muzāḥamah (competition): the same obligatory amount could be satisfied by either portion. Without a specific intention (niyyah muʿayyanah) to designate the given portion as zakat, the obligation remains unsettled.
Contrast the case of giving all wealth: then competition vanishes, and the obligation is necessarily discharged.
4
Compare the reasoning
Muhammad’s reasoning is based on the innate diffuse nature of the obligation. Abu Yusuf’s reasoning is procedural: without specification, uncertainty persists. Both are valid Hanafi views, but they reflect different approaches to the role of intention.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Muhammad’s view, the giver owes no further zakat on the given portion; the charity counts as the zakat. Under Abu Yusuf’s view, the charity is purely voluntary (nafl) and the giver still owes the full zakat on the entire nisab.
This difference affects the mufti’s fatwa and the individual’s liability.

Transformation of Zakat upon Sale After the Haul

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Selling the zakat camel after the haul does not transfer the obligation; it remains a debt on the seller.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A sale before the haul with a court-ordered return does not interrupt the year; without court order, a new year begins.

What happens if a camel is sold during the zakat year but then returned due to a defect? Does the year continue or restart? The passage also covers gifts that are retracted. These rulings protect the continuity of the zakat year under certain conditions.

Equality of Bactrian and Arabian Camels in Legal Rulings
Defines two camel types and states their equality in five rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that Bactrian and Arabian camels are equal in zakat, riba, and udhiyah, and identify the exception for oaths based on custom.

Core Idea
Bactrian and Arabian camels are legally identical for zakat, riba, and udhiyah, but not for oaths due to custom.

In Hanafi fiqh, the obligation of zakat on camels depends on correctly identifying the type and number of camels in one's possession. This lesson clarifies that the school treats Bactrian and Arabian camels identically for most key rulings, while noting a nuanced exception in the area of oaths that reflects the role of custom in law. We will explore the definitions provided by the commentator and the scope of this equality.

Guided Path
1
Define the two camel types
Read the commentary's definitions of البخت and العراب. Note that البخت refers to mixed-breed camels (from Arab and non-Arab stock) and العراب to pure Arabian camels.
The commentator distinguishes them by origin, not by legal effect for zakat, riba, and udhiyah.
2
Identify the rulings where equality applies
From the statement 'سواء' and the commentary, list the three specific legal areas: wajib al-zakat (obligation of zakat), i'tibar al-riba (consideration of riba), and jawaz al-udhiyah (permissibility of sacrifice).
These are the only rulings explicitly mentioned; equality does not extend to all matters.
3
Recognize the exception for oaths
The commentary explicitly states that for oaths (aiman), the two types are not considered equal. If someone swears not to eat البخت, eating العراب does not break the oath.
The basis for this exception is that oaths are interpreted according to custom ('urf), and in custom the two meats are distinct.
4
Understand the legal principle behind the exception
The exception illustrates the Hanafi maxim that oaths are construed according to common usage. The default equality in legal obligations does not override customary distinctions when it comes to personal vows.
This is a key illustration of how custom can limit the scope of a general rule.
5
Apply to a practical scenario
Consider a person who owns a herd of 10 Bactrian and 10 Arabian camels. For zakat, they are counted together to determine the nisab and the number due. For an oath not to eat camel meat, specify which type is meant based on local custom.
In the absence of a specific intention, the custom of the swearer's locale governs.

Zakat Exemptions for Livestock and Collector Procedure with Denials
Lists animals exempt from zakat and procedure for collector when owner denies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which livestock categories are exempt from zakat and explain the procedure when a zakat collector faces an owner's denial, including the conditions for accepting the owner's claim with an oath.

Core Idea
No zakat on endowment, neglected horses, or blind/crippled livestock; owner's denial accepted with oath if plausible.

Zakat on livestock is obligatory only when specific conditions are met. This passage clarifies the exemptions: animals that are not fully owned (endowment) or cannot pasture independently (blind/crippled) are not subject to zakat. It then turns to a practical scenario: when the zakat collector arrives and the owner denies the obligation or claims to have paid, how is the claim evaluated? The Hanafi school provides clear guidelines that balance evidence and trust.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exempt categories
List the three groups of animals mentioned: endowment pasturing animals (سوائم الوقف), neglected horses (الخيل المسئلة), and blind or crippled livestock (المواشي العُمي ومقطوعة القوائم). For each, note the reason given: lack of ownership for the first two, and not being sa'imah (pasturing) for the third.
The term 'neglected horses' refers to horses kept for riding or work, not for breeding or fattening, so they are not considered sa'imah.
2
Understand the condition of sa'imah
Zakat on livestock is only due on animals that are sa'imah – those that graze freely in pastures for most of the year without being fed by the owner. Blind or crippled animals cannot graze independently, so they lose this status.
Imam Abu Hanifa and his students agree that sa'imah is a condition; differences exist on the exact threshold of pasturing time.
3
Recognize the owner's possible denials
The collector arrives; the owner might say: (1) 'These are not mine', (2) 'A full year has not passed', or (3) 'I have a debt covering their value'. The text states that his statement is accepted with an oath because he is denying the obligation (إنكار الوجوب).
These three claims negate the conditions of ownership, completion of hawlan, or nisab (due to debt).
4
Analyze the claim of prior payment
If the owner says he already paid zakat to another collector, the acceptance depends on the existence of that other collector. If there is another collector, the owner is believed with his oath, whether or not he brings a receipt – this is the apparent narration (ظاهر الرواية). A narrated opinion (روي) requires a receipt. If there is no other collector, the claim is not accepted.
This distinction prevents false claims: if no other collector exists, the claim is implausible.
5
Distinguish the two narrations
Compare ظاهر الرواية (the established transmitted position) and روي (a weaker narration). The apparent narration does not require a receipt; the other does. Note that the mufta bihi (operative opinion) is likely the apparent narration, but both are recorded.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the practical consequence is whether the owner must produce evidence or not.
