Commentary
This passage is from the commentary (sharh) on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, explaining rulings related to Tarawih and Witr prayers during Ramadan. The author begins by discussing the time for these prayers. The majority of Hanafi mashayikh hold that the time for Tarawih and Witr is between Isha prayer and the break of dawn (tulu' al-fajr). If one prays these prayers before Isha, they are not valid. However, praying Tarawih after Witr is considered valid, and this is the most correct position, upheld by the practice of the Salaf (the early generations). It is recommendable (mustahab) to delay Tarawih until the first third of the night, but delaying it to the middle of the night is not recommended. There is a difference of opinion: some say there is no harm in it, and the text deems this correct.

The passage then addresses the case of missed Tarawih. If one misses the time for Tarawih (i.e., the entire night passes without praying them), they are not to be made up in congregation. There is further disagreement about individual make-up. Some authorities hold that one can make them up individually as long as the month of Ramadan itself has not ended. Others hold that they are not made up at all – and this is stated as the correct view. Another opinion states that make-up is possible before the time for Tarawih of the following night comes.

A specific scenario is mentioned: if a person prayed Isha behind an imam, then prayed Tarawih behind another imam, and later discovers that the imam for Isha was without wudu, they must repeat both Isha and the Tarawih that followed, because the Isha prayer is invalid, and Tarawih depend on it (since Tarawih prayer is performed after a valid Isha). If someone misses one or two Tarawih (rak'ats) in the congregation, there are two views: either to pray Witr with the imam first and then make up the missed Tarawih afterward, or to pray the missed Tarawih first and then Witr. Both opinions are reported in al-Dhakhira.

The text then comments directly on the matn phrase "Then he leads them in Witr." This indicates to some that the time for Tarawih is after Isha and before Witr, which is the view of most mashayikh. However, the most correct view (al-ashahh) is that the time for Tarawih extends from after Isha until the end of the night, both before and after Witr, because Tarawih are sunnah prayers (nawafil) that are supererogatory, and like any nafl prayer, they can be performed at any time except the prohibited times. Abu Ali al-Nasafi adds that if one prays Tarawih before Isha, it does not count as Tarawih (since the time has not started), but if one prays them after Isha and after Witr, it is valid and counts as Tarawih.

Finally, the passage discusses Witr in congregation. The matn states that Witr is not prayed in congregation outside of Ramadan. This is because the Companions did not do so. However, in Ramadan, it is better for the imam to lead Witr in congregation, as Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) did. Yet, the text from al-Nawazil reports a permission: a Witr congregation outside Ramadan is permissible but makruh (disliked), not invalid. Accordingly, if one prays Witr with an imam outside Ramadan, it is valid but not recommended. The author concludes with "Allah knows best."

The dispute hinges on the interpretation of the Quranic verse (An-Nisa 4:102) and the weight of the Companions' practice. By studying this, you will understand how Hanafi scholars resolve conflicts between apparent textual specificity and established precedent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components of the passage
The passage has two distinct parts: a detailed description of how to perform Salat al-Khawf with two groups, and a juristic dispute between Abu Yusuf and the majority Hanafi position on whether this prayer is still prescribed after the Prophet's time.
The method assumes the congregation disagrees on having one imam; otherwise, the imam leads sequentially.
2
Describe the Hanafi method of Salat al-Khawf
The imam divides the group into two ṭā'ifas. He prays one rak'ah with the first group, then they go to face the enemy. The second group comes and prays one rak'ah with the imam. After the imam says the salam, the second group also goes to face the enemy. The first group then returns and prays their second rak'ah individually without recitation, as they are lāḥiqūn (latecomers who followed the imam's first rak'ah).
Walking to the enemy is allowed; riding would invalidate due to excessive movement. A woman joining would invalidate all prayers.
3
State Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf argued that Salat al-Khawf was only prescribed when the Prophet was present. His evidence is the verse: 'When you are among them' (An-Nisa 4:102), which he took as specific to the Prophet. He also argued that people would not have the same desire to pray behind other imams.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf — Abu Yusuf differs from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani on this point.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The majority Hanafi response is based on two arguments: (1) The Companions performed the fear prayer after the Prophet's death, establishing its continuity by practice. (2) The verse's phrase 'when you are among them' applies to the Prophet and anyone in his position (khalifa, imam), analogous to the zakat verse which is not limited to the Prophet personally.
This is a classic Hanafi use of ma fahwa al-khitab (implied meaning) and analogy (qiyas) to extend the ruling.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical difference: Abu Yusuf would consider the fear prayer invalid after the Prophet, while the majority (mufta bihi) consider it valid and prescribed. The later Hanafi consensus relies on the majority view, so the fear prayer remains a legitimate form of prayer for times of danger.
This dispute is recorded in al-Jawhara an-Nayyira but the operative fatwa follows the majority.

Ruling on Wudu of the Deceased
Explains the rules for performing wudu on the deceased, including exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wudu of the deceased and explain the modifications based on qiyas and the deceased's inability.

Core Idea
Wudu for the deceased is sunnah muakkadah, with modifications for acts requiring voluntary movement.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains the detailed rulings for performing wudu on the deceased. The Hanafi school applies qiyas to the living but adapts the procedure to suit the deceased's condition. Understanding these adjustments ensures proper funeral rites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
Determine the overall hukm level (sunnah muakkadah) and the primary proof: qiyas (analogy) to a living person before ghusl.
The text says 'because washing in life is preceded by wudu: so also after death.'
2
Note the modifications compared to living wudu
List what is omitted: wiping the head (since it doesn't contribute to cleanliness), gargling and sniffing (since they require voluntary action), and the delaying of washing the feet (as in ghusl janabah).
These changes are specific to the deceased.
3
Understand the exception for children
Recognize that a child who does not reason (does not understand wudu) is exempted because wudu was not required during life.
The text: 'except a child who does not reason'.
4
Recognize practical details
Note that intention (niyyah) is not required, and the hands are not washed separately before the main washing, unlike living wudu.
Also, a substitute for gargling is described by some scholars using a cloth-wrapped finger.

Method of Washing the Deceased After Wudu
Method description for washing the deceased after wudu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the recommended method (sunnah muakkadah) for washing the deceased's body after wudu according to the Hanafi school, including the order of washing and the use of cleansing agents.

Core Idea
Wash the deceased's head and beard with a cleanser first, then wash the sides.

Washing the deceased is a communal obligation (fard kifayah). After giving the deceased wudu like the prayer wudu, the next step is to wash the body. This lesson covers the sunnah method for that washing: starting with the head and beard using a cleansing agent, then washing the rest of the body by turning the deceased from side to side. The Hanafi school provides detailed guidance to ensure thorough purification while respecting the deceased.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall ruling for this method
The method described is sunnah muakkadah—a strongly recommended practice. It is not obligatory, but following it brings reward and completeness. The obligation itself (fard kifayah) is the basic washing.
2
Understand the order after wudu
According to Al-Khujandi, after wudu, begin by washing the deceased's head and beard with a cleansing agent. This is done before the full body wash.
The text explicitly states: 'All this is before his washing.'
3
Recognize the hierarchy of cleansing agents
The preferred cleanser is khitmi (marshmallow plant). If unavailable, soap. If unavailable, potash (hurd). If none of these, plain water suffices. This shows the school's allowance for varying circumstances.
Khitmi is preferred; water alone is sufficient if nothing else is available.
4
Learn the technique of washing the body
After cleaning the head and beard, lay the deceased on the left side and wash the right side, then on the right side and wash the left side. This ensures full coverage and modesty.
Turning the body allows thorough washing without exposing the private parts more than necessary.
5
Synthesize the complete sequence
The full sequence: 1) Give wudu like for prayer. 2) Wash head and beard with cleanser. 3) Pour water over the entire body. 4) Lay on left side, wash right side. 5) Lay on right side, wash left side.
This method is from Al-Khujandi and is the mufta bihi practice.

Permissible Minimum Shroud for Women: Three Garments and Khirqah Placement
Ruling on permissible minimum shroud for woman and composition with intra-Hanafi differences on khirqah placement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissible minimum shroud for a woman in Hanafi fiqh and identify the recommended placement of the khirqah according to different Hanafi authorities.

Core Idea
Three garments suffice for a woman's shroud, but the sunna is five; the khirqah is placed over the izār and qamis under the lifāfa.

In Hanafi fiqh, shrouding the deceased is an obligation (fard kifāyah), but the number and composition of garments can vary. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that using only three garments (izār, khimār, lifāfa) is permissible and acceptable, although the sunna is five. The commentator then delves into the proper placement of the khirqah (chest band), highlighting a difference among Hanafi authorities and providing the preferred view. Understanding these details ensures that the Muslim community fulfills the obligation of shrouding correctly, avoiding both deficiency and excess.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Find the statement of the matn: if they suffice with three garments, it is permissible. Understand that 'jāz' indicates validity but not preference.
2
Enumerate the three garments
Note the three garments mentioned: izār (lower wrap), khimār (head cover), and lifāfa (outer wrapper). The passage specifies that khimār goes above the qamis (shirt) and under the lifāfa.
3
Distinguish sunna shroud
The text calls this 'kafan al-sunnah' for a woman, which is the five-garment shroud. Understand that the three-garment shroud is a valid alternative, not the ideal.
4
Analyze the khirqah placement dispute
The commentator presents different views on the khirqah: from breasts to thighs (preferable), from chest to knees (Al-Mustasfa), tied on breasts over shrouds (Al-Khujandi), and over breasts and belly (Al-Jami' al-Saghir). Note the reconciliation: 'over the shrouds' means over the izār and qamis but under the lifāfa.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved by preferring Al-Jami' al-Saghir.
5
Synthesize the practical ruling
Combine: The permissible minimum is three garments; the preferred sunna is five. The khirqah is placed over the izār and qamis, under the lifāfa, covering from chest to knees or breasts to thighs.

These are prohibited for women, so by extension for the khuntha.

In practice, the khuntha is treated as female for shrouding, using the same fabric restrictions.
This ruling applies only when gender is ambiguous; if later determined, retroactive effects may differ.

Woman's Hair Placement in Shroud: Chest vs. Back
Ruling and dispute on hair placement in shroud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the placement of a deceased woman's hair and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using analogy and rebuttal.

Core Idea
In death, a woman's hair is placed on her chest—not her back—because adornment ceases.

This lesson examines a specific detail of the funeral shroud: where to place the hair of a deceased woman. The Hanafi school places it on the chest, braided over the dir‘, while the Shafi‘i school places it on the back. Understanding this difference reveals deeper methodological principles about analogy and the meaning of death in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
The matn states: 'Her hair is placed on her chest.' The commentary specifies two braids over the dir‘ (the inner shroud). This is the operative ruling.
The Hanafi position is that this is more secure and avoids spreading.
2
Identify the Shafi'i opposition and its analogy
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the hair should be placed on the back, using the analogy (qiyas) of the living state: a living woman's hair lies on her back, so the deceased should also be arranged that way.
This is a straightforward analogy (qiyas jali).
3
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response argues that the living arrangement is for adornment, but death is a state of grief and regret (ḥasratun wa nadāmah). Adornment ceases, so the analogy breaks down. The purpose is not to mimic life but to prepare for burial.
This rebuttal highlights the different legal cause (ʿilla).
4
Examine the turban analogy
To support their view, the Hanafis cite the practice of turbaning the deceased: the tail is placed on the face, not the back of the head, because the back was for adornment. Similarly, the hair should not be placed on the back.
This is an example of qiyas khafi (subtle analogy) or istiḥsān.
5
Note the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
In practice, Hanafis braid the hair and place it on the chest over the dir‘; Shafi‘is place it on the back. Both are valid within their schools.
The muftā bihī position is the Hanafi view.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi hierarchy of who leads the funeral prayer among relatives and apply the rules of priority, absence, and equal degree.

Core Idea
The closer relative chooses who leads the funeral prayer; the farther has no say while the closer is present.

When a Muslim dies, the funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) must be led by an imam. Among the deceased's relatives, there is a specific hierarchy of who has the right to decide the imam. Funeral Prayer Precedence When No Son and Special Cases

This passage expands on the rules of who is most entitled to lead the funeral prayer. Distinguish slave and mukatib cases
For a slave or mukatib, the master's right is prioritized over free relatives, especially in the relied-upon fatwa, despite some disagreement.

وإن صلَّى الوليُّ : لم يَجُز لأحد أن يصلي بعده . عليه، يعني إذا أراد الإعادة. - وقيد بغير السلطان: لأنه إذا صلى عليه السلطان: فلا إعادة لأحد ؛ لأنه مقدم على الولي. * قوله : ( وإن صلى عليه الولي: لم يجز أن يصلي أحد بعده)؛ لأن الفرض يتأدى بالأولى، والنفلُ بها غير مشروع. ولو صلى عليه الولي وللميت أولياء آخرون بمنزلته: ليس لهم أن يعيدوا؛ لأن ولاية الذي صلى عليه متكاملة. ولو صلى عليه الولي وأراد السلطان أن يصلي عليه: فله ذلك؛ لأنه مقدم في حق صلاة الجنازة على الولي، ولهذا لا يجوز للسلطان أن يصلي على الجنازة بالتيمم في المصر خوف الفوات؛ لأن الولاية إليه، ولا ضرورة به إلى التيمم. كذا في النهاية».
And if the guardian prayed, it is not permissible for anyone to pray after him. [Commentary: upon him, meaning if he intends repetition. And it is restricted to other than the sultan: because if the sultan prays over him, there is no repetition for anyone; because he is given precedence over the guardian. His statement: (And if the guardian prayed over him, it is not permissible for anyone to pray after him); because the obligation is fulfilled by the first, and nafl is not prescribed by it. And if the guardian prayed and the deceased has other guardians of the same rank, they may not repeat; because the guardianship of the one who prayed is complete. And if the guardian prayed and the sultan wishes to pray over him, he may do so; because he is given precedence in funeral prayer over the guardian, and for this reason it is not permissible for the sultan to pray over the funeral with tayammum in the metropolis for fear of missing; because guardianship belongs to him, and there is no necessity for him to use tayammum. Thus in al-Nihayah.]

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The text states that if the guardian (wali) of the deceased has performed the funeral prayer, no one else may pray after him. The commentator explains that this is because the obligation of funeral prayer is fulfilled by the first prayer, and performing another prayer as a supererogatory (nafl) act is not legally prescribed for the funeral prayer. The ruling is restricted to the guardian; however, if the sultan (ruler) prays, no one may repeat even if the guardian had not prayed, because the sultan has precedence. The commentator also discusses the case of multiple guardians of equal rank: the one who prays first completes the guardianship, so others may not repeat. The sultan, being higher in authority, may pray even after the guardian has prayed. An interesting detail is that the sultan is not allowed to perform tayammum for funeral prayer in the city even if he fears missing the prayer, because his guardianship gives him precedence and he has no excuse to omit wudu or ghusl.

Key Terms
الولي (al-waliy): The guardian of the deceased, the one responsible for performing the funeral prayer.
السلطان (al-sultan): The ruler or his representative who has precedence over the wali in funeral prayer.
النفل (al-nafl): Supererogatory act of worship; here the second funeral prayer is considered nafl but not valid.
