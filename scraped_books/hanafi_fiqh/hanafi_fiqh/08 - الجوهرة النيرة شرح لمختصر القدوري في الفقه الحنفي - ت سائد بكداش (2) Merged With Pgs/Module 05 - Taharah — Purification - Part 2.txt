The Ruling on Washing a Junub Martyr
Explains the exception of washing junub and child martyrs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that martyrdom prevents (manee') but does not remove (rafi') impurity to determine when a martyr must be washed.

Core Idea
Martyrdom sanctifies blood but not janabah—a junub martyr must still be washed.

The general rule is that martyrs are not washed before burial. However, this passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies an important exception: if the martyr is in a state of janabah (major impurity), washing becomes obligatory according to Imam Abu Hanifa. The key is understanding that martyrdom only prevents the martyr's blood from being najis; it does not remove other impurities, including janabah.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule and the exception
General: martyrs are not washed. Exception: junub and child martyrs are washed. Identify the exception and its scope.
2
Identify the condition for the exception
The martyr must be in a state of janabah before death, established by his own statement before killing or by his wife's testimony.
This shows the importance of evidence in fiqh rulings.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning (Manee' vs Rafi')
The text states that shahada is a preventer (manee'), not a remover (rafi'). It prevents the blood from becoming impure but does not remove pre-existing impurities like janabah.
This is the core principle governing the exception.
4
Apply the analogy (Qiyas) given in the text
If there is other impurity (e.g., urine) on the martyr's garment, it must be washed. Similarly, janabah is an impurity that must be removed. The text uses qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
The analogy reinforces that only the blood is exempted, not other impurities.
5
Cross-reference with the Shafi'i view
The Shafi'i school holds that no martyr is washed, regardless of janabah. This highlights the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between the schools.

Commentary
This passage addresses a specific case: a martyr who is in a state of janabah (major impurity, e.g., due to sexual intercourse or seminal emission). According to Abu Hanifa, such a martyr must be washed (ghusl) before burial, unlike the general rule of not washing the martyr. The same ruling applies to a child martyr. The reason is that the state of martyrdom (shahada) does not remove pre-existing impurities; it only prevents the blood of the martyr from becoming najis. The analogy is given: if there is any impurity other than blood on the martyr's garment, that impurity must be washed off. So similarly, janabah (which is a major impurity) is not lifted by shahada. The fact that the martyr is junub can be established by his own statement before death or by his wife's testimony. The commentary clarifies that Shahada is a 'manee' (preventer) not a 'rafi' (remover).

Key Terms
جنب (junub): A person in a state of major ritual impurity requiring ghusl (full body wash), typically due to sexual intercourse or seminal discharge.
مانعة (māniʿa): Preventer; shahada is described as a preventer that prevents the martyr's blood from being considered impure, but does not remove other impurities.
رافعة (rāfiʿa): Remover; shahada is not a remover of pre-existing impurities like janabah.

Washing a Junub Martyr: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ghusl for a junub martyr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding washing a junub martyr, state their proofs, and identify the practical consequence of the dispute.

Core Idea
Martyrdom prevents, not removes, janabah – so Abu Hanifa insists on ghusl; the Sahibayn see martyrdom as a replacement.

This passage presents a classical intra-Hanafi dispute about the burial rites of a martyr who was in a state of major impurity (janabah). Imam Abu Hanifa rules that such a martyr must be washed, while his two principal disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree. The debate hinges on whether martyrdom removes the obligation of ghusl or merely prevents certain impurities. We will walk through the evidence and reasoning of both sides and see how this difference affects actual funerary practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What exactly is being disputed? Is it whether a martyr in janabah should be washed (ghusl) before burial, or something else? Pinpoint the ruling in question.
The dispute is about the ghusl for janabah, not the regular ghusl of the deceased.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his evidence
Read Abu Hanifa's view: he says the junub martyr must be washed. Identify his proofs: the hadith about praying over those who say la ilaha illallah, and the analogy that martyrdom is a preventer (mani') not a remover of existing impurity.
Pay attention to the distinction between 'preventer' and 'remover'.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their evidence
Read the Sahibayn's view: they say the junub martyr is not washed. Identify their proofs: the obligation of ghusl falls with death because its cause (prayer) ends, and the analogy that martyrdom replaces ghusl just as slaughter replaces tanning.
Note the different analogies used by each side.
4
Analyze the key conceptual difference
How do the two sides view martyrdom? Is it a rafi' (remover) or a mani' (preventer)? How does this affect the remaining obligation?
This usul distinction is the root of the ikhtilaf.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
What happens in practice? According to Abu Hanifa, the body is washed before shrouding; according to the Sahibayn, it is buried without washing. Consider how this affects the funeral prayer and treatment of the body.
This difference impacts actual funeral procedures for martyrs.

Commentary
This passage addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the ruling of washing (ghusl) a martyr who was in a state of major impurity (janabah) at the time of death. The chapter (باب الشهيد) discusses two main issues: (1) whether a junub martyr should be given the ritual bath for the deceased, and (2) the ruling on washing blood from the martyr's body.

The text first presents the position of Imam Abu Hanifa: a junub martyr is to be washed (غُسل). He extends this ruling to a child (صبي) as well. Abu Hanifa's evidence includes a hadith (though weak) commanding prayer over anyone who says la ilaha illallah, implying that martyrs are even more deserving of honor through prayer, which requires prior purification. Furthermore, he argues that martyrdom is a preventer (مانعة) of certain impurities (like blood becoming impure) but not a remover of pre-existing impurity like janabah. Thus, janabah remains and must be removed by washing.

The text then contrasts with the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Sahibayn), who hold that a junub martyr is not washed. Their reasoning: the obligation of ghusl is connected to prayer, and since prayer is not obligatory on a dead person, the obligation of ghusl for janabah falls away upon death. Additionally, the specific ghusl for the deceased (غسل الميت) is abrogated by martyrdom. They use an analogy: just as slaughtering (ذكاة) an animal suffices to purify its skin without requiring tanning (دباغ), so martyrdom suffices to purify the martyr without requiring washing.

The Sahibayn also extend this to the child and the insane (مجنون) because martyrdom removes sins, and since these individuals have no sins, their death by martyrdom is like natural death, so they are not washed.

The text also clarifies the meaning of the martyr being "alive" — it refers to the afterlife, not this world.

Finally, the passage rules that the martyr's blood is not washed off, based on the Prophet's ﷺ command regarding the martyrs of Uhud. The blood is pure for the martyr himself but impure for others.

This dispute highlights a fundamental difference in how the two sides conceptualize the effect of martyrdom: Abu Hanifa views it as a preventer (مانع) that does not remove existing impurity, while the Sahibayn see it as a replacement (مقام) for the washing itself.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahīd): A martyr killed in the path of Allah in a legitimate battle.
غسل (ghusl): Ritual washing of the entire body for purification from major impurity (janabah) or as part of the funeral rites.
جنب (junub): A person in a state of major ritual impurity requiring ghusl.
مانعة (māniʿa): A preventer; in this context, martyrdom prevents certain impurities (blood from being najis) but does not remove pre-existing impurity (janabah).

Rulings on Washing and Shrouding the Martyr
Rulings on washing and shrouding the martyr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on washing and shrouding the battlefield martyr, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the rules regarding the martyr's blood and clothing.

Core Idea
The martyr is not washed because martyrdom serves as purification, preserving the sign of oppression.

This lesson explores the unique fiqh rulings for the battlefield martyr (shahid) in the Hanafi school. Unlike ordinary deceased, the martyr is not washed, his blood is not removed, and his battle attire is partially removed. These rules are rooted in specific hadith and analogical reasoning (qiyas) and distinguish the martyr's treatment from other deceased. We will examine the evidence, the intra-Hanafi dispute, and the practical applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act in question is washing (ghusl) the battlefield martyr. According to the Sahibayn, the martyr is not washed; in fact, washing him is contrary to the sunnah. This is the relied-upon position.
Abu Hanifa differed and held that the martyr must be washed like any other deceased.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The primary proof is the Prophet's command regarding the martyrs of Uhud: 'Shroud them with their blood and their wounds.' This indicates that washing is not required, as the blood and wounds are to remain.
The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear (qati' al-dalalah).
3
Understand the analogy (qiyas)
The Sahibayn draw an analogy: just as slaughtering an animal substitutes for tanning in purifying the skin, martyrdom substitutes for ghusl in purifying the deceased. Therefore, the martyr is considered ritually pure without washing.
This qiyas is used to support the ruling.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa disagreed with the Sahibayn, arguing that the general obligation to wash the deceased applies to martyrs. He considered the hadith specific to Uhud or abrogated. The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view.
The majority of other schools (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) agree with the Sahibayn.
5
Apply the rules for clothing and blood
The martyr's ordinary clothes remain, but battle-specific items (fur, khuff, stuffed garments, weapons) are removed. The blood of the martyr is pure for himself (so salah carrying him is valid) but impure for others (if it falls on another's garment, salah in that garment is invalid).
The murtatt (one brought alive from battle) is washed because he experienced life after being wounded.

Intercourse Invalidates I'tikaf Unconditionally
Ruling on invalidation of i'tikaf by intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on intercourse in i'tikaf, explain the Hanafi distinction between forgetfulness in i'tikaf versus fasting, and apply the qiyas to ihram.

Core Idea
Sexual intercourse invalidates i'tikaf unconditionally; forgetfulness is no excuse because the mosque itself is a reminder (mudhakkira).

In this lesson, we examine a critical rule from the chapter of i'tikaf: that any sexual intercourse, whether by day or night, intentional or forgetful, invalidates the seclusion. The text from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained by the commentary, clarifies that i'tikaf's environment is fundamentally different from fasting—it is a constant reminder, so forgetfulness does not exempt. This principle is derived by analogy to ihram.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is sexual intercourse (jimā') by a mu'takif. The ruling is that i'tikaf becomes batil (invalidated).
This applies day or night, intentionally or forgetfully, with or without emission.
2
Understand the scope of invalidation
Also acts in ma'na al-jimā' (like kissing or touching with lust leading to emission) invalidate i'tikaf. Without emission, i'tikaf remains valid.
Emission is the decisive factor for acts short of full intercourse.
3
Examine the exception for fasting
If the mu'takif is also fasting (e.g., in Ramadan), forgetfulness excuses the fast but not the i'tikaf. The fast remains valid.
The difference stems from i'tikaf being a mudhakkira (reminder) state.
4
Analyze the proof: qiyas to ihram
The commentary draws an analogy (qiyas jaliy) to ihram: both states are reminders (mudhakkira), so forgetfulness does not exempt.
The illa (effective cause) is the constant reminder inherent in the state.
5
Apply the ruling to borderline cases
For non-penetrative acts, check emission: if emission occurs, i'tikaf is invalid; if not, it remains valid. This applies even if the act is forgetful.
Example: kissing without emission does not invalidate i'tikaf.

The evidence that the dual has the ruling of plural is his statement (peace be upon him): 'Two and above are a congregation.' This is when he has no intention. However, if he intends i'tikaf of two days without their two nights, his intention is valid, and he is required to observe i'tikaf of two days without a night, and he has the option: if he wishes, he may do them consecutively; if he wishes, he may separate them. He enters the mosque each day before dawn and leaves after sunset. And if he obligates i'tikaf of a night, nothing is required of him because obligatory i'tikaf is only valid with fasting. And if he obligates i'tikaf of two nights and has no intention, he is required to observe them and their two days.
Source pages p. 312

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on obligatory i'tikaf (nadhr). The matn states that if one vows i'tikaf for a number of days, those days must be observed with their corresponding nights. The commentator explains that the plural 'days' includes the nights that accompany them. For a single day, the night before is not included; the mu'takif enters before dawn of that day and leaves after sunset. For two days, a dispute arises between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Abu Hanifa holds that both nights are included, so the mu'takif enters before sunset of the first day and stays through both nights until sunset of the second day. Abu Yusuf argues that the dual is distinct from the plural, so only the middle night (between the two days) is necessary for continuity. The commentary sides with Abu Hanifa, citing the hadith that 'two and above are a congregation' to prove that the dual shares the ruling of the plural. It then discusses the effect of intention: if one explicitly intends to exclude the nights, that is valid, and he may do the two days consecutively or separately. Finally, it gives two subsidiary rulings: vowing i'tikaf for a single night is not binding because obligatory i'tikaf requires fasting, and a night has no fasting. Vowing two nights, without specifying days, requires observing both nights and the intervening day.

Key Terms
المثنى (al-muthannā): The dual number (two days), debated whether it shares the same ruling as the plural regarding night inclusion.
نية (niyyah): Intention; if one intends to exclude nights, that intention is valid and he may observe the days without nights.
الاثنان (al-ithnān): Two (dual) – the subject of the dispute.

Purity in Tawaf: Condition or Not?
Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute with internal Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that purity is not a condition for tawaf, distinguish it from the Shafi'i view, and explain the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether purity is wajib or sunnah.

Core Idea
Purity is not a condition for tawaf; a pureless tawaf is valid in the Hanafi school.

The status of purity (taharah) during tawaf has been debated between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The Hanafi school holds that tawaf without purity is valid, unlike the Shafi'i school which requires it as a condition. This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah clarifies the Hanafi position, the internal disagreement among later Hanafi scholars, and the specific case of awrah exposure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
What is the specific legal question? It is whether purity (from hadath or najasah) is a condition (shart) for the validity of tawaf.
The core issue is whether tawaf without wudu or with impurity is valid.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school (indana) holds that tawaf without purity is valid (ja'iz). The text says 'كان طوافه جائزاً عندنا'.
This is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.
3
Recognize the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf
Later Hanafi scholars disagreed on whether purity is wajib or sunnah. Ibn Shuja' said sunnah; Abu Bakr al-Razi said wajib. But they agreed it is not a condition.
This internal ikhtilaf does not affect the validity of tawaf, only the merit.
4
Understand the proof
The proof is a qiyas: tawaf is a rukn of Hajj like wuquf at Arafat, which does not require purity. Therefore, purity is not a condition for tawaf.
The hadith 'al-tawafu salatun' is interpreted as referring to minimizing speech, not imposing purity.
5
Distinguish impurity on clothes from awrah exposure
Impurity on clothes (more than a dirham) is makruh but no penalty. Awrah exposure requires a dam because it is a prohibition specific to tawaf, as per the hadith 'nor naked'.
The farq (difference) is based on the reason for prohibition: general soiling vs. specific tawaf-related prohibition.

The Book of Hajj. And [the ruling on] impurity: because of his statement, peace be upon him: 'Circumambulation of the House is prayer, so speak little in it.' So if one fails to observe purity, his circumambulation is valid in our [Hanafi] view. Al-Shafi'i said: His circumambulation is not counted. Later companions of ours discussed whether purity is obligatory or sunnah. Ibn Shuja' said: Sunnah. Abu Bakr al-Razi said: Obligatory. The proof that it is not a condition for circumambulation is that circumambulation is a pillar of Hajj, so purity is not a condition for it, like the standing [at Arafat]. And if one performs circumambulation while there is impurity on his garment greater than the size of a dirham, that is disliked for him, and nothing is incumbent upon him. The difference [between impurity and nakedness] is that impurity is prohibited for no reason specific to circumambulation; rather it is prohibited because it is soiling the mosque. Not so for uncovering [nakedness]; it is prohibited for a reason specific to circumambulation, as evidence by his statement, peace be upon him: 'None shall circumambulate the House as a polytheist nor naked.' Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on purity (taharah) during tawaf, a key issue in the Hanafi school. The author begins by citing the hadith that tawaf is like prayer, implying restriction on speech, but the focus here is on purity. The Hanafi position is that purity is not a condition (shart) for tawaf; if a person performs tawaf without wudu or with najasah, the tawaf is valid (ja'iz), unlike Shafi'i who considers it invalid. The text then notes an internal Hanafi dispute among later scholars: Ibn Shuja' holds that purity is sunnah, while Abu Bakr al-Razi holds it is wajib. The proof against purity being a condition is that tawaf is a rukn of Hajj, like wuquf at Arafat, and wuquf does not require purity. The discussion then shifts to impurities on garments (more than a dirham) and exposure of awrah. For impurity on clothes, the tawaf is valid but makruh, with no penalty. For exposing awrah, the tawaf is valid but requires a dam (sacrificial animal). The author explains the legal difference (farq): impurity is prohibited because it soils the mosque, not because of a meaning specific to tawaf; awrah exposure is prohibited because it is specific to tawaf, as proven by the hadith that a naked person may not circumambulate. Therefore, awrah exposure constitutes a deficiency requiring compensation (jubran). This distinction is crucial in Hanafi fiqh for determining when a dam is required.

Key Terms
طواف (tawaf): Circumambulation around the Kaaba, a rukn of Hajj and Umrah.
شرط (shart): A condition external to the act that must be present for validity; absence invalidates.
دم (dam): A sacrificial animal required as compensation (jubran) for certain omissions or violations during Hajj or Umrah.
