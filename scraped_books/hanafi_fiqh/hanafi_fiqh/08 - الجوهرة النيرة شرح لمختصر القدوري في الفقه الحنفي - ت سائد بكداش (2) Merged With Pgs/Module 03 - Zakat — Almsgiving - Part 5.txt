Giving Large Amounts of Zakat to One Person: Cause vs. Consequence
Ruling with intra-Hanafi dispute and example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on giving 200 dirhams or more to one poor person, explain the intra-madhhab disagreement with Zufar using the cause-consequence distinction, and apply the analogy of prayer near impurity to determine validity and karaha.

Core Idea
Giving 200 dirhams or more to one poor person is valid but disliked — wealth is a consequence, not a condition.

This passage from the Jawhara al-Nayra addresses a practical zakat question: can you give a large lump sum (200 dirhams or more) to a single poor person? The text presents the majority Hanafi position and a minority view from Imam Zufar, grounded in a subtle legal distinction between the cause of payment and the effect of wealth. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how the Hanafi school treats temporal proximity in rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its level
Identify the act: giving 200 dirhams or more of zakat to one poor person. State the ruling according to the majority Hanafi view: it is valid (jaza) but makruh tanzihi (disliked). Note that the validity is affirmed first, then the karaha.
The ruling is not haram or invalid; it is a disliked but legally effective act.
2
Reconstruct Zufar's objection
Zufar argues that the payment is invalid because the recipient becomes wealthy (ghani) at the moment of payment. Since zakat cannot be given to a wealthy person, the payment fails. Identify the key premise: wealth (ghina) is simultaneous with the payment.
Zufar's view is within the Hanafi school; it is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze the majority response using cause and consequence
The majority responds that wealth is a consequence (hukm) of the payment, not a concurrent condition. The payment is the cause ('illah), and the ruling follows the cause. Therefore, the validity is determined at the time of the cause (payment), not the later effect (wealth).
This is a key usul al-fiqh concept: the ruling is attached to the cause, not the consequence.
4
Apply the analogy to prayer near impurity
The text compares this case to praying near an impurity: the prayer is valid but disliked. The proximity to impurity (or wealth in the zakat case) does not invalidate the act but makes it disliked. Recognize the common element: the act itself is sound, but the proximity to something prohibited creates karaha.
This analogy (qiyas) uses the 'illah of proximity to a disliked element.
5
Solve the boundary case from Abu Yusuf
Hisham relates a case: a man with 199 dirhams receives 2 dirhams as charity. Abu Yusuf rules that he takes only one dirham (to reach 200) and returns the other. This shows that once he reaches the nisab, he becomes wealthy and cannot keep the excess. The ruling preserves the principle that wealth is a consequence but also applies a practical restriction.
This case illustrates the nuance: when the recipient is on the threshold, he must stop at the nisab.

Zakat for Debtors and Those with Dependents: Permissibility to Exceed Nisab
Ruling with talil on permissibility of exceeding nisab for debtors and those with dependents.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which it is permissible to give zakat to a recipient that exceeds nisab, and explain the reasoning (talil) behind it.

Core Idea
Zakat can be given above nisab to a debtor or a person with dependents because the wealth effectively goes to multiple eligible recipients.

In the rules of zakat distribution, one must ensure the recipient does not end up with wealth equal to or above nisab (the minimum threshold for zakat obligation). However, this passage from al-Sarakhsi's commentary introduces an exception: if the recipient is in debt or has dependents, it is permissible to give them an amount that, after accounting for debt or distribution among dependents, leaves each with less than nisab. This ruling is based on a nuanced understanding of effective ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the baseline rule
Understand the general rule: one should not give zakat to a recipient such that the recipient's total wealth reaches or exceeds the nisab of 200 dirhams (or equivalent).
The baseline ruling is found in the preceding lines of the text.
2
Recognize the exception
The exception applies when the recipient is a debtor (madyūn) or has dependents (iyāl). In such cases, it is permissible to give an amount that, if considered for each dependent or after debt deduction, is less than nisab.
The condition is either debt OR dependents, not both.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The reasoning is that giving to a person with dependents is effectively giving to each dependent; similarly, giving to a debtor effectively goes toward paying off debts. Thus, the recipient's net ownership per person remains below nisab.
Attributed to al-Sarakhsi: 'because giving to him in effect is giving to his dependents.'
4
Apply the condition
For a debtor: you may give up to the amount of his debt, and what remains after paying the debt must be less than nisab. For a person with dependents: you may give such that if the amount were divided equally among the dependents (including the recipient), each would receive less than nisab.
The calculation ensures that no single individual effectively owns nisab.

Core Idea
Zakat to a servant is valid unless you call it compensation; to a child or insane person, it requires a guardian's receipt.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira clarifies three practical scenarios that often raise doubt: giving zakat to one's own servant, to someone who brings glad tidings, or as a gift. It also addresses the capacity of children and the insane to receive zakat. The key insight is that the intention behind the payment determines its validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three persons in the first ruling
The text mentions three recipients: a servant (who serves and fulfills needs), a bearer of good news, and a gift recipient. In each case, the zakat payment is valid.
2
Spot the exception
The ruling includes an exception: 'unless he specifies it as compensation.' If the payer explicitly says this is for the service, the payment becomes wages and does not count as zakat.
The default is validity; only explicit wording changes the ruling.

For a child (sabi) or an insane person (majnun), the zakat is valid if the guardian (wali) or caretaker takes possession on their behalf. The recipient lacks full legal capacity, so the guardian's receipt is necessary.
This ensures the zakat reaches the actual needy person.
4
Derive the underlying condition
From both rulings, we learn that the validity of zakat depends on the payer's intention and the method of delivery. The payer must not intend compensation, and the recipient (or their representative) must have the capacity to own the property.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Giving zakat to one close to nisab is disliked, but permissible if the recipient has debt or dependents.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses a nuanced issue in zakat distribution: is it permissible to give zakat to someone who already has wealth close to the nisab threshold? The Hanafi school rules that it is makruh tanzih (disliked but valid), drawing an analogy to praying near impurity. Core Idea

The Eleven Aspects of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Proofs and Conditions
Listing and explaining key aspects including hadith proof, conditions, and amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the eleven aspects of Sadaqat al-Fitr, identify its cause, conditions, and pillar, and explain why it is wajib based on the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib: half a sa' of wheat or a full sa' of barley, dates, or raisins from every free Muslim with surplus.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra systematically unpacks Sadaqat al-Fitr through eleven categories. As a ruling-with-proof passage, it not only states the legal details but anchors them in hadith evidence. By mastering these eleven aspects, you will internalise the Hanafi framework for this key obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Name the act (Sadaqat al-Fitr) and list the eleven aspects the author enumerates. Note that the passage covers only six explicitly but refers to eleven.
The author likely intended eleven; the text covers cause, description, condition, pillar, ruling, and obligated persons.
2
Extract the core ruling (hukm) and its evidence
From aspect 2 (description), identify that the ruling is wajib. Find the two hadiths cited: the Prophet's command 'Pay...' and Ibn Umar's narration that he prescribed it. Note the Hanafi principle that an imperative (amr) indicates wujub.
The hadiths are from Abu Dawud, Bukhari, and Muslim; they are ahad, giving zanni thubut, which is sufficient for wujub in Hanafi usul.
3
List the conditions (shurut) for obligation
From aspect 3, separate conditions: for the person (freedom, Islam, surplus wealth), for time (dawn of Eid), and for amount (not less than half a sa').
Conditions are external prerequisites; if missing, the obligation does not arise.
4
Identify the pillar (rukn) and the obligated persons
The pillar is the actual payment to a deserving recipient. The obligated person is any free Muslim with surplus wealth, regardless of gender or age (the hadith includes young and old, male and female).
Contrast with the cause (sabab) which the text says is 'a head one supports' – likely meaning dependents.
5
Memorise the amount and permissible staples
Wheat: half a sa'. Barley, dates, raisins: full sa'. Only these four staples are valid. Understand the measurement: a sa' is about 2.04 kg for wheat.
This specific amount is unique to Hanafi fiqh; other schools may require a full sa' for wheat as well.

Sadaqat al-Fitr: Obligation, Conditions, and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Defining the legal rulings of Sadaqat al-Fitr with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions, pillar, and ruling of sadaqat al-fitr according to the Hanafi school, explain why it is wajib not fard, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i regarding who must pay.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib on the wealthy free Muslim at dawn of Eid; the poor with only a day's surplus are not obligated.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah explains the legal framework of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It begins with the hadith evidence, then lists the conditions (shurut), pillar (rukn), and ruling (hukm). A key discussion concerns who is obligated: only the owner of nisab according to the Hanafis, while Imam al-Shafi'i extends it to those with surplus beyond daily needs. Understanding these details ensures correct practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Determine what Sadaqat al-Fitr is and its ruling level in the Hanafi school. Is it fard, wajib, or sunnah?
It is wajib, not fard, because it is proven by ahad hadith which gives zanni al-thubut.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
Find the hadith cited and analyze its indication. How does the imperative 'addu' prove obligation?
The Prophet's command 'Pay' indicates obligation (amr yufidu al-wujub) per Hanafi usul.
3
List the shurut (conditions), rukn (pillar), and hukm (ruling)
Extract the conditions for the person (freedom, Islam, wealth), for the time (dawn of Eid), and for the wajib (amount). Identify the rukn (payment) and the hukm (discharge).
These are explicitly listed in the text.
4
Note the amount and permissible grains
Specify the obligatory quantity and what grains are allowed. How does the type affect the amount?
Half sa' of wheat or full sa' of barley, dates, or raisins. The sa' varies by grain.
5
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute on who must pay
Compare the Hanafi condition of nisab with the Shafi'i condition of surplus beyond one day's food. Understand the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).
The Hanafi view is mufta bihi in the school.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition (sharh) on the chapter of Sadaqat al-Fitr from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The commentator begins by citing hadith evidence: the Prophet's command to pay a sa' of wheat or barley on behalf of every free person and slave, young or old, and the hadith of Ibn 'Umar that the Prophet obligated zakat al-fitr on all Muslims. These establish the obligation. Then the conditions (shurut) are listed: freedom, Islam, and wealth (i.e., possessing nisab) for the person, and the time being dawn of Eid day. The pillar (rukn) is the actual payment to the deserving recipients, and the ruling (hukm) is discharge of duty and reward. The obligatory amount is half a sa' of wheat or a full sa' of barley or dates; the permitted grains are four: wheat, barley, dates, and raisins. The commentator then explains that wealth means possessing the nisab (the minimum threshold for zakat). The time of obligation is dawn of Fitr, and the recommended time is before going to the Eid prayer. The place of payment is the location of the person obligated, because the obligation is on the person (dhimmah), unlike zakat which is tied to the property. This distinction is illustrated: if a slave perishes after the obligation, sadaqat al-fitr is still due from the master, but zakat would be voided if the property is destroyed. Finally, a brief intra-madhhab dispute is noted: according to al-Shafi'i, sadaqat al-fitr is also obligatory on a poor person who has surplus beyond his and his family's daily needs. The Hanafi position is that it is only on the wealthy (owner of nisab). Conditions of freedom and Islam are also emphasized to ensure valid ownership and worship.

نصاب (niṣāb): The minimum threshold of wealth that triggers obligation of sadaqat al-fitr, same as zakat nisab.
وقت الوجوب (waqt al-wujūb): The time when sadaqat al-fitr becomes obligatory: dawn of the day of Fitr.

Conditions and Rulings of Sadaqat al-Fitr in the Hanafi School
Rulings on conditions, time, place, and recipients of Sadaqat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions that trigger the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr, apply the Hanafi condition of yasar (possession of nisab surplus to basic needs), and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib on the free Muslim who possesses nisab surplus to basic needs at dawn of Eid.

Sadaqat al-Fitr is a unique charity tied to the person, not to wealth. The Hanafi school conditions its obligation on possessing nisab surplus to basic needs, derived from the hadith 'La sadaqata illa 'an zahri ghina'. This lesson unpacks the conditions, the proof, and the dispute with the Shafi'i school, helping you apply these rules in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib (obligatory) according to the Hanafi school, not fard, because the evidence is from ahad hadith.
The ruling level (wajib) affects the consequence of omission: sin but not disbelief.
2
List the conditions for obligation
The obligor must be: free, Muslim, possessor of nisab surplus to basic needs (residence, clothes, furnishings, horse, weapons, slaves for service), and free of debt. The time of obligation is dawn of Eid al-Fitr.
Basic needs are defined as items essential for daily living, not luxuries.
3
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the hadith 'لا صدقة إلا عن ظهر غنی' (No charity except from wealth surplus to need), which is a Sunnah ahad.
The hadith's chain is zanni, leading to the hukm being wajib rather than fard.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'عن ظهر غنی' indicates that charity (including Sadaqat al-Fitr) is only valid from wealth that is surplus to one's basic needs. The legal definition of 'ghani' (wealthy) in the Shariah is one who possesses nisab.
Thus, the condition of yasar (wealth) is measured by the nisab.
5
Note the ikhtilaf and evaluate tarjih
Al-Shafi'i obligates Sadaqat al-Fitr on anyone with surplus beyond one day's food for self and family. The Hanafi school rejects this because the poor person is not 'ghani' and thus not addressed by the hadith. The Hanafi view is mufta bihi.
The practical difference: a person with only one day's surplus is exempt in the Hanafi school but obligated in the Shafi'i school.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Paying cash value for Sadaqat al-Fitr is permissible and even preferable in the Hanafi school.

You may think Sadaqat al-Fitr must be given in specific grains like wheat or dates. But the Hanafi school, relying on a hadith to make the poor independent and on rational benefit, permits giving cash value. This lesson examines the proof and the preferred practice.

Identify the dispute

When Does Sadaqat al-Fitr Become Obligatory? Hanafi vs. Shafi'i

Core Idea
Fitrah becomes obligatory at dawn of Eid, not at sunset of the last day of Ramadan.

This lesson examines a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: when exactly does Sadaqat al-Fitr become due? The answer affects who must pay, especially for those who become Muslim, are born, or die during the critical night. By understanding the ruling and its rationale, you will be able to determine liability correctly according to the Hanafi school.

Timing of the Obligation: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on timing of fitrah obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr begins at fajr of Eid, contrast it with the Shafi'i position, and apply the resulting cases.

Core Idea
Hanafi: Fitrah wajib at fajr of Eid; Shafi'i: at sunset of last day of Ramadan.

This lesson examines the inter-madhhab dispute over when Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes obligatory. The timing affects many practical cases: conversion, birth, death, and slavery status. Understanding the Hanafi view and its evidence is essential for correct application.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on timing
The text says: 'ووجوب الفطرة يتعلق بطلوع الفجر الثاني من يوم الفطر' – the obligation is tied to the second dawn of Eid day.
2
State the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds the obligation begins at sunset of the last day of Ramadan.
3
Apply to cases
Work through the examples: conversion/birth on the night of Eid – Hanafi says wajib (because fajr is the condition), Shafi'i says not (because sunset already passed). Death on that night – Hanafi says not wajib, Shafi'i says wajib.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The text argues that the condition for performance is fajr; those who die before fajr never become liable. The Shafi'i view leads to inconsistency: a person who dies after sunset but before fajr would be liable, but the obligation could not have been fulfilled.
The Hanafi view ensures that only those alive at the actual time of obligation are liable.

Sadaqat al-Fitr at Dawn: When Obligation Attaches
Explains the instant of obligation and its implications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for Sadaqat al-Fitr being wajib and apply them to cases of death, conversion, and birth.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib at the exact moment of Fajr on Eid — only if the person is alive, Muslim, and wealthy at that instant.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar clarifies the precise moment at which Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes obligatory. Understanding this instant is critical because it determines the obligation for cases like death before or after dawn, conversion, and birth. The ruling is based on the principle that obligation attaches to the moment of dawn, not the day before.

Guided Path
1
Identify the time of obligation
Determine that Sadaqat al-Fitr becomes wajib at the break of dawn (Fajr) on the day of Eid. This is the 'waqt al-wujub'.
The text says: 'يدخل وقت وجوبها بطلوع الفجر'.
2
Apply the condition of being alive at dawn
If a person dies before dawn, no fitra is due because the time of obligation found him not among the living. If he dies after dawn, fitra is wajib because he reached the time as a living person.
Key phrase: 'فمن مات قبل ذلك : لم تجب فطرته'.
3
Apply the conditions of being Muslim and free at dawn
If a person becomes Muslim after dawn, no fitra is due. Similarly, if a child is born after dawn, no fitra. But if a disbeliever becomes Muslim before dawn and is wealthy at dawn, fitra is wajib.
The same logic applies: the obligation attaches only to those who meet the conditions at the moment of dawn.
4
Handle the special case of a slave freed on Eid
If a master says 'You are free on the day of Fitr', the slave is freed at dawn. Since the slave was owned at the moment of obligation (just before emancipation), the master must pay the fitra for him.
This illustrates that the obligation is determined by the state at dawn, even if the status changes immediately after.
5
Conclude that the obligation is instantaneous
The time of obligation enters and exits at dawn; there is no 'period' of obligation. Performance after dawn is still considered 'ada' (on-time) not 'qada' (make-up).
The text says: 'تخرج على الفور من غير استقرار'.

Expansion of a ruling with rational proofs and comparisons

Sadaqat al-Fitr is a personal liability that persists beyond Eid, unlike Zakat tied to wealth or Udhiyah tied to a specific time.
