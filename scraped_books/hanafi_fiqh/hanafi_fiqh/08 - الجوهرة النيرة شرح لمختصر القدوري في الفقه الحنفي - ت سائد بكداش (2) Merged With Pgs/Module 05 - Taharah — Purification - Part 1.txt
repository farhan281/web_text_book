Closing the Eyes and Binding the Jaw of the Deceased
Ruling and method for immediate care of the deceased

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sunnah rulings for closing the eyes and binding the jaw of the deceased and describe the method as per the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Closing the eyes and binding the jaw after death is sunnah, not obligatory, to maintain dignity and prevent harm.

The Prophet ﷺ himself closed the eyes of Abu Salama at the moment of death and explained the wisdom. This establishes the recommended practice for the living to care for the deceased immediately after death. In this lesson, we examine the proof and the detailed method taught by the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sunnah acts
The passage mentions two specific actions: closing the eyes (ighmad) and binding the lower jaw (shadd al-lahi). These are classified as sunnah (recommended) in the Hanafi school.
Sunnah means the Prophet did it and encouraged it, but omitting it is not sinful.
2
Understand the prophetic proof
The proof is the hadith of the Prophet entering upon Abu Salama and stating, 'Indeed, when the soul is taken, the sight follows it.' Then he closed Abu Salama's eyes. This shows the action is based on his practice and his statement explains the wisdom.
The hadith is narrated by Muslim and is considered ahad (single-chain), but sufficient for establishing a sunnah.
3
Grasp the wisdom behind the ruling
The passage gives two reasons: preventing an unsightly appearance and preventing insects from entering the eyes and mouth. These practical reasons underline the recommendation.
The wisdom relates to preserving the dignity of the deceased.
4
Learn the method step by step
The gentlest family member (child or father) should gently close the eyes, bind the lower jaw with a wide bandage tied above the head, soften the joints, and straighten the limbs in a natural position.
Flexing the joints before rigor mortis prevents stiffness.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school rules it as sunnah; the Maliki school considers it mandub (also recommended), while the Shafi'i school also considers it sunnah. All agree it is not obligatory.
No major disagreement on the substance, only on terminology.

Core Idea
Hasten burial, but for sudden death: wait until certainty.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira combines a general rule with a critical exception. The rule: prepare and bury the deceased promptly. The proof: a direct prophetic command. The exception: if death is sudden (e.g., accident), delay until death is absolutely confirmed. This prevents the tragedy of burying someone still alive.

The reason is to avoid burying a person who is still alive (apparent death).

The exception exists because sudden death may be uncertain; delaying protects against a serious error. This reflects the Hanafi principle of istihsan (juristic preference) overriding a general qiyas.

Core Idea
Wishing for death due to hardship is disliked, not forbidden; if compelled, use the Prophet's du'a.

We often face trials that make us long for relief, even through death. But Islamic teachings guide our response. In this lesson, we examine a clear hadith that prohibits outright wishing for death and provides a supplication that aligns with patience and trust in Allah's wisdom. Understanding the precise ruling—makruh tanzihi—distinguishes it from haram and shapes a believer's attitude toward hardship.

Identify the act and its ruling

The ruling is not haram; it is makruh tanzihi.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith: 'Let none of you wish for death...' from Sahih Muslim. Recognize that the hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) yet provides a clear prohibition.
The Prophet's command 'la yatamannayanna' is a prohibition.
3
Derive the exception and alternative
The hadith allows an exception: if one must wish, they should say the specific du'a. This shows the general prohibition is not absolute; the du'a transfers the desire to Allah's will.
The du'a is a taught alternative, not a permission to wish for death outright.

Hasten preparation, verify sudden death, and never wish for death due to hardship.

When a Muslim passes away, certain steps must be taken promptly. The Prophet ﷺ instructed us to hasten the preparation of the deceased, but this does not mean rushing without certainty. In cases of sudden death, one must wait until death is confirmed. Additionally, wishing for death due to worldly hardship is discouraged; instead, one should supplicate for what is best. The second ruling is based on precaution, and the third has its own hadith.
3
Analyze the imperative
The command 'عجلوا' is a plural imperative. In Hanafi usul, the default for a command is obligation (wujub), but contextual factors (like the nature of the action and the threat being for the evil dead) shift it to recommendation (sunnah).
This is a key example of usul al-fiqh applied to fiqh rulings.

Core Idea
The deceased is washed for hadath, not najasa, according to the majority Hanafi view.

When a Muslim dies, the community must wash the body. But why? Is it to remove ritual impurity (hadath) like after sleep, or because death makes the body physically impure (najasa)? This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi debate on this very question, examining the evidence from both sides.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
The passage presents two views: the majority holds that ghusl of the deceased is for hadath (ritual impurity), while a minority (Abu Abdullah al-Jurjani and others) holds it is for najasa (physical impurity).

The author presents the majority view first and it is relied upon as the mufta bihi. The minority arguments are not explicitly refuted but are considered weaker based on the principle that the human body is inherently pure and najasa from death cannot be removed by washing.
The practical difference: if for najasa, the body would be physically impure, requiring special handling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the sequential steps for washing the deceased according to the Hanafi school and identify the materials used at each stage.

Core Idea
Washing the deceased follows a precise sunnah procedure: wudu, head/beard cleaning with khitmi, then full ghusl with sidr water while turning the body.

The Hanafi school prescribes a detailed procedure for washing the deceased, combining purification (taharah) with respect. This lesson walks through each step as recorded in al-Khujandi and explained in al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah.

Guided Path
1
Understand the sequence
The procedure begins with wudu, then pre-wash of head and beard, then main ghusl with sidr water, turning the body to wash both sides, and fumigation of the bier.
2
Identify the materials
Note the hierarchy: for head/beard – khitmi, then soap, then ushnan, then plain water. For ghusl: water boiled with sidr, then ushnan, then plain water.

Rationale behind steps
Hot water is used because it removes dirt better; fumigation is done an odd number of times (max 5) for blessings; turning ensures complete coverage.

Distinguish obligation from sunnah

Missing a sunnah step does not invalidate the ghusl.
5
Apply to a scenario
If khitmi is unavailable, soap is used; if sidr is unavailable, plain water suffices. The essential requirement is that the body becomes clean.

Washing the Deceased: Procedure and Ruling on Repeating the Wash
Ruling with proof: procedure and dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the complete Hanafi procedure for washing the deceased and state the ruling when impurity emerges after washing.

Core Idea
If impurity emerges after washing the deceased, only the soiled area is washed; the full wash and wudu are not repeated.

Washing the deceased is a communal obligation (fard kifayah) in Islam, and the Hanafi school prescribes a detailed sunnah procedure to ensure thorough cleaning and respect. This lesson walks through the steps—from fumigation to turning the body—and addresses the important ruling on what to do if impurity appears after the wash. Understanding this ruling prevents unnecessary repetition and aligns with the school's principle of practicality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal obligation
Recognise that washing the deceased is fard kifayah (communal duty). The passage describes the recommended (sunnah) method, not the minimum obligation.
The minimum obligation is to wash the entire body with water once; the rest are sunnah enhancements.
2
Trace the procedure for thorough cleaning
Follow the sequence: fumigate the bier thrice, boil water with sidr/hurd, wash head and beard with khatmi, turn the body, wash sides until water reaches the underlying surface, sit up and wipe belly gently.
The purpose is to ensure water reaches every part, including the underside (taḥt).
3
Handle post-wash impurity
If any impurity (e.g., excrement) emerges after washing, only the soiled area is washed; the full ghusl and wudu are not repeated. This is the explicit Hanafi ruling from the text.
The text states: 'ولا يُعيد غسله، ولا وضوءه' (he does not repeat his wash nor his wudu).
4
Analyze the dispute
Ibn Sirin held that the entire wash must be repeated; Imam al-Shafi'i held that only wudu must be repeated. The Hanafi school differs, citing the principle of not burdening the living unnecessarily.
The practical consequence: Hanafi requires only local cleaning, Shafi'i requires wudu, Ibn Sirin requires full wash.
5
Apply the gender rule
Men wash men, women wash women, except for young children not desired (non-baligh), where the opposite gender may wash them.
This is stated at the end of the passage.

This lesson traces the distinctions laid out in Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar and its commentary in Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, focusing on the signs that distinguish a true martyr from one who died of natural causes amidst battle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key condition for exemption from washing
A martyr (shahid) of the battlefield is not washed. The primary condition is that he is found in the battlefield with a wound from which blood emerges from an unusual place like the eye or ear.
Blood from an unusual place suggests the wound caused death directly, not a natural bodily discharge.
2
Distinguish between normal and unusual blood exit points
If blood comes from the nose, anus, or penis, the deceased is washed because such blood could be due to natural causes (e.g., nosebleed, blood in urine).
These orifices are considered normal routes for blood to exit due to natural conditions.
3
Handle blood from the mouth carefully
Blood from the mouth requires examination of its origin. If it comes from the head (clear blood), it is like a wound and the person is not washed; if from the stomach (clotted blood), it indicates an internal injury and the person is also not washed. In both cases, the martyr is exempt from ghusl.
The colour of the blood distinguishes the source: clear from head, clotted from stomach.
4
Apply the ruling to determine washing obligation
Given a scenario of a deceased found in battle with a specific blood exit, use the above rules to state whether ghusl is performed.
If the condition for martyrdom is met, ghusl is not performed; otherwise, regular washing is mandatory.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Washing a Muslim Killed by a Horse in Battle
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ghusl for battlefield death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Abu Yusuf on this scenario and explain the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) regarding ghusl.

Core Idea
A Muslim killed by a loose horse in battle is washed—only if the killing is not attributed to the enemy.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement on a specific battlefield scenario. When a polytheist's horse runs loose and tramples a Muslim to death, the imams differ on whether the deceased is considered a martyr (shahid) and thus exempt from ghusl. The key is the attribution of the killing: to the enemy directly or to the animal's autonomous action.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Understand the exact case: a polytheist's horse escapes with no rider, driver, or guide, and tramples a Muslim in battle, killing him.
2
State the Position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
They rule that the slain Muslim must be washed (ghusl wajib). The killing is not attributed to the enemy but to the horse (al-'ajma'), whose action is not described as oppression (zulm).
3
State the Position of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf rules that he is not washed because he died in the fighting of the people of war (ahl al-harb), effectively making him a shahid exempt from ghusl.
4
Analyse the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa/Muhammad, the body is washed, prayed over, and buried normally. Under Abu Yusuf, it is not washed; it is treated as a martyr's body.
5
Consider the Mufta Bihi
The text does not specify which view is relied upon, but typically the Hanafi school follows the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (ghusl wajib) because the killing is not directly from the enemy.

Martyrdom Conditions: The Muthaqqal Dispute Within the Hanafi School
Intra-Hanafi dispute on martyrdom conditions and muthaqqal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for martyrdom when killed by Muslims and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on killing by a heavy blunt object (muthaqqal).

Core Idea
Being killed by a heavy blunt object (muthaqqal) is considered martyrdom by the Sahibayn, but not by Abu Hanifa — the dispute turns on what counts as a lethal weapon in battle.

The conditions for martyrdom in Hanafi jurisprudence specify that a Muslim killed unjustly by other Muslims without blood money being due is a martyr. However, the ruling differs when the killing involves a heavy blunt object (muthaqqal). This lesson explores the qualifications and the internal Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions for martyrdom
From the text, list the requirements: (1) killed by Muslims, (2) unjustly (zulman), not by a lawful punishment, (3) no blood money (diya) is mandatory due to the killing.
The condition 'no blood money' is qualified: it means no initial blood money, not when blood money follows a dropped qisas.
2
Understand the exception for 'initial blood money'
Study the father-son example: the father kills his son; qisas is due but drops due to doubt, then diya is imposed; the son is still a martyr because the diya is not initial.
This shows that the phrase 'no blood money' means no initial obligation of diya, not any diya at all.
3
Distinguish consensus from dispute on weapon type
The text states that if the killing is done with something that does not usually kill (e.g., a small stick), there is consensus that the victim is not a martyr. For a heavy blunt object (muthaqqal), there is a dispute: Abu Hanifa says not a martyr; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say he is a martyr.
The muthaqqal is a heavy weapon like a stone or club that can kill, unlike a small stick.
4
Analyse the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If Abu Hanifa's view is followed, the deceased is washed and prayed over (like an ordinary dead person). If the Sahibayn's view is followed, the deceased is not washed but only shrouded and prayed over (as a martyr).
The ruling on washing is the key practical difference.

Commentary
This passage continues the conditions for martyrdom. The condition 'killed unjustly by Muslims' excludes those killed by stoning, qisas, etc., because those are just punishments. The condition of no blood money (diya) means it must be blood money that is not preceded by a more severe obligation like qisas. For example, if a father kills his son, blood money is due but he is still a martyr because the initial obligation was qisas that fell due to doubt. Then there is a dispute: if Muslims kill him with something that does not usually kill (e.g., a small stick), he is not a martyr by consensus. If they kill him with a heavy blunt object (muthaqqal), Abu Hanifa says he is not a martyr, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say he is a martyr.
