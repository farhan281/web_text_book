Umm Walad Washing Her Deceased Master: Hanafi Position vs. Zufar
Ruling on an umm walad washing her deceased master, with Zufar's disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an umm walad does not wash her deceased master according to the Hanafi school and evaluate Zufar's contrary proof.

Core Idea
An umm walad's iddah is for emancipation, not marriage, so she cannot wash her master.

This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira addresses a specific question: may an umm walad (a slave woman who bore her master's child) wash his body after death? The Hanafi school rules that she may not, despite being in iddah. Zufar, an early Hanafi scholar, disagrees. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing the nature of her iddah from that of a wife.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling in the matn: the umm walad is forbidden from washing her master. Note that she is in iddah of emancipation (three menstrual cycles).
The ruling is unconditional: she does not have the right (lam yakun laha).
2
Identify Zufar's opposing view
Zufar holds that she may wash him. His evidence: she is in iddah from him, just like a wife in iddah is entitled to wash her husband.
Zufar was a senior Hanafi student of Abu Hanifa, so this is an intra-madhhab dispute.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response implicit in the text: her iddah is not the same as a wife's iddah. It is an iddah of istibra' (verifying the womb is empty) and emancipation, not a marital iddah. Therefore, she lacks the marital relationship that would permit washing.
The text does not elaborate further; the distinction is based on the cause of the iddah.
4
Trace the legal proof (dalil)
The Hanafi proof is based on the principle that only a spouse or a mahram may wash the deceased in general. An umm walad is not a wife; she was a slave. Her emancipation does not retroactively create a marriage.
Zufar's qiyas (analogy) to a wife is rejected because the illa (effective cause) is not shared.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: according to the mufta bihi (Hanafi position), another woman (or the husband's relatives) must wash the body. According to Zufar, the umm walad herself may do so.
The difference affects who is permitted to perform the ghusl.

Commentary
This passage addresses the case of an umm walad (a slave woman who bore a child to her master) after the master's death. Upon his death, she becomes free and must observe an iddah of three menstrual cycles (like a divorced free woman). The Hanafi position is that she may not wash him because her relationship was that of a slave, not a wife, and the iddah is for istibra (verifying no pregnancy) rather than mourning. Zufar analogizes her to a wife in iddah, arguing she should be able to wash him. The relied-upon view follows the majority of the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who has borne a child to her master.
عدة العتاق (iddat al-ʿitāq): The waiting period for a freed slave woman, set at three menstrual cycles.

Slave Women (Concubine, Mudabbara, Mukataba) Cannot Wash Master
Consensus ruling that slave women cannot wash their deceased master.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the consensus ruling that a concubine, mudabbara, and mukataba cannot wash their deceased master and explain the reasoning based on their legal status upon the master's death.

Core Idea
A slave woman who becomes free or belongs to others upon the master's death cannot wash him — consensus.

In the Hanafi school, only certain individuals are permitted to wash the deceased. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies that three categories of slave women — the concubine, the mudabbara, and the mukataba — are all prohibited from washing their deceased master by consensus. The reasoning lies in their changed legal status upon the master's death: they either become part of the estate or are on a path to freedom, severing the master's ownership. This lesson explores the ruling and its basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is washing the deceased master. The ruling is that it is not permissible (prohibited) for these three categories.
2
List the three categories of slave women
They are: the concubine (amah), the mudabbara (promised freedom after death), and the mukataba (contracting to buy freedom).
They are distinguished from umm walad.
3
Understand the consensus (ijma')
The text says 'by consensus' (bil ijma'), meaning all Hanafi scholars agree on this prohibition.
4
Analyze the reasoning
The concubine becomes property of the heirs; the mudabbara becomes free (or partially free) from the estate; the mukataba is considered a free person in some respects. None are mahram to the deceased at the time of death.
The reasoning links to the general principle that a non-mahram cannot wash a dead person.
5
Note the exception of umm walad
The passage does not mention umm walad. The common understanding is that umm walad (mother of the master's child) can wash him because she is like a wife in some respects.
This is a separate ruling.

ولو مات عن أمته، أو مدبرته، أو مكاتبته : لم تُغسله، بالإجماع; لأن الأمة صارت لغيره والمدبرة عَتَقَت من كل ماله إن خرجت من الثلث، وإن لم تخرج من الثلث : عَتَقَ ثلثها، وصارت كالمكاتبة.
And if a man dies leaving his concubine, his mudabbara, or his mukataba, she does not wash him, by consensus, because the concubine becomes property of others, and the mudabbara becomes free from all of his wealth if she comes out of one-third (of the estate); if she does not come out of one-third, one-third of her becomes free, and she becomes like a mukataba.
Source pages p. 101

Commentary
This passage states the consensus among Hanafi jurists that three categories of slave women do not wash their deceased master: a regular concubine (umm al-walad is excluded here), a mudabbara (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death), and a mukataba (a slave who has contracted to buy her freedom). The reason given is that upon the master's death, these women no longer belong to him: the concubine becomes part of the estate for heirs, the mudabbara becomes free if the estate can pay her value, and the mukataba is already working toward freedom. They are not in a marital relationship, so they cannot wash him.

Key Terms
مدبرة (mudabbara): A slave woman who has been promised freedom after her master's death.
مكاتبة (mukataba): A slave woman who has made a contract to purchase her freedom.

Key Takeaway
By consensus, a concubine, mudabbara, and mukataba cannot wash their deceased master because they are not mahram upon his death.
