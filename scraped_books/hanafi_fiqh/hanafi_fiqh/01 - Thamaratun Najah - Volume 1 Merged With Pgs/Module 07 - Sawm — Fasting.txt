Ruling on Abandoning the Fast of Ramadan
States ruling on execution for abandoning Ramadan fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a person who abandons the fast of Ramadan and explain the condition that triggers execution.

Core Idea
Mere abandonment of fasting is not apostasy; denial or belittlement is.

This lesson examines the legal consequence for a Muslim who abandons the obligatory fast of Ramadan. The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between a person who simply fails to fast while acknowledging its obligation and one who denies or belittles the fast. The proof is derived from the precedent of Abu Bakr regarding zakat, extended by analogy to fasting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act under discussion is abandoning the fast of Ramadan. Determine the default ruling: the person is not executed.
2
Locate the condition for execution
Find the two conditions that make execution applicable: denial (jahd) or belittlement (istikhfaf) of the fast.
Both are considered forms of apostasy.
3
Examine the proof (dalil)
The author cites a hadith about Abu Bakr fighting those who separated prayer and zakat. Understand how this proof is applied to fasting.
The proof is analogical (qiyas): denial of an obligatory act leads to fighting.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Identify the effective cause (illa): denial of an obligation known by necessity (ma'lum min al-din bi al-darurah). This illa is shared between zakat and fasting.
The Hanafi position is that denial of any such obligation constitutes apostasy.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Some schools may hold that intentional abandonment alone warrants execution. The Hanafi view is that execution is only for apostasy, not mere sin.
This distinction highlights the Hanafi emphasis on intention and belief.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on a person who abandons the obligatory fast of Ramadan. The author states that such a person is not subject to execution unless he denies the obligation of the fast or belittles it. The commentary explains that denial or belittlement constitutes apostasy because it implies rejection of a clear Qur'anic command. If he merely fails to fast while acknowledging its obligation, he is a sinful Muslim but not an apostate, and thus not executed. The author supports this with a hadith from Abu Bakr regarding fighting those who separate prayer and zakat, analogously showing that denial of an obligatory act leads to fighting. The hadith about beating a child for leaving prayer at ten years is also cited to indicate the seriousness of leaving obligations, but the ruling for adults is different. The Hadith from Muslim about Abu Bakr's statement is used as evidence that denial of an obligatory pillar warrants combat, which is extended to the case of fasting.

Key Terms
جحد (jahada): To deny the obligation of a religious duty, thereby rejecting its divine origin.
استخف (istakhaffa): To belittle or show contempt for a religious obligation or symbol.
مرتد (murtadd): An apostate: a Muslim who renounces Islam by word or action.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, mere abandonment of a fard act does not warrant execution; only denial or belittlement that amounts to apostasy triggers capital punishment.
