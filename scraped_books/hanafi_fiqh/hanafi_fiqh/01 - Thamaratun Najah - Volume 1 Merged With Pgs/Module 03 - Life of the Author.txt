The Teaching Methodology of a Hanafi Scholar: Mawlana Shamir al-Din Qasimi
Biographical sketch highlighting teaching methodology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the key elements of the Hanafi teaching methodology exemplified by Mawlana Shamir al-Din Qasimi, including his chain of transmission (isnad), preparation methods, and oral explanatory style.

Core Idea
Hanafi teaching is built on thorough preparation, oral transmission with proofs, and a direct chain from earlier masters.

This passage offers a rare glimpse into the life and teaching methods of a contemporary Hanafi scholar. Mawlana Shamir al-Din Qasimi, a graduate of Dar al-Ulum Deoband, devoted his career to teaching hadith and fiqh. His biography serves as a model for how Hanafi knowledge is transmitted: through meticulous study, deep respect for the chain of teachers, and a student-centered approach that combines oral explanation with textual analysis.

Guided Path
1
Trace the scholarly chain
Identify Mawlana's teachers for each of the six hadith collections (Sihah Sittah). This shows the isnad (chain) that connects him to the Prophet ﷺ through the classical compilers.
The text names teachers for Bukhari, Muslim, Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud, and Nasa'i.
2
Examine his teaching institutions
List the madaris where he taught: Patan, Anand, Munger, Manchester, Dewsbury. Note the progression from intermediate books to advanced works.
He taught Tahawi Sharif, Jalalayn, Hidayah Thalith, and major hadith collections.
3
Analyze his preparation method
The author states he never taught without studying, and his preparation was deep. How does this reflect the Hanafi emphasis on mastery?
The text says he taught many times but never without preparation.
4
Understand his oral explanation technique
He would explain rulings orally with proofs without translating the text. He would address proofs from the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) and then correlate with the text.
This method ensures students learn reasoning, not just rote memorization.
5
Appreciate the rational proof example
In Tahawi Sharif, he explained the rational proof for the recommendation of wudu before sleep, eating, etc. This shows how Hanafi fiqh uses reason alongside text.
Imam Tahawi did not mention the rational proof; Mawlana supplied it from the commentaries.

Respected Teacher: Life and Memories of Hazrat Mawlana Shamir al-Din Sahib Qasimi (May his blessings endure)
By... (Mawlana) Marghub Ahmad Lajpuri (Shaykh al-Hadith)
Resident of Dewsbury, England
Note: The writer wrote this article about Mawlana's circumstances for the publication of his treatise 'Yad-e-Watan' as a 'Dhikr al-Mu'allif', but it could not be published there.
It was the year 1407 AH (1987 CE). The writer enrolled in Madrasa Ta'lim al-Islam, Dewsbury. That year, I was to study Mishkat and other books. Upon inquiry about the teachers, it became known that the hadith teacher of Jami'a Rahmaniyya, Munger, Mawlana Mir al-Din Sahib Qasimi (may his shadow be prolonged) was coming to teach hadith this year. It was sometime in Shawwal. The writer reached the madrasa and saw a gentleman with Mawlana 'Abd al-Qadir Sahib Patni (may his shadow be prolonged) sitting in front of the library. Wheatish complexion, medium build, average height and stature, white clothes, cheerfulness on the face. I presented myself, greeted. 'Wa 'alaykum al-salam, how are you?' This was the first meeting with the respected teacher. Then the relationship of intimacy increased day by day. Once, seeing the respected teacher's literary engagement, I asked: 'Sir, what are you writing?' The answer was: 'I am compiling the history of the notables of my region.'
The matter ended. Just a few days ago, I mentioned this book in a meeting, and he said: 'That manuscript was lengthy; now I have abridged it by cutting and trimming, and I am sending it for copying.' I said: 'Mawlana! This is about the elders of your region; my heartfelt desire is that I include a brief account of you in it.' Mawlana firmly refused, but my insistence overcame his refusal, and I blackened these few pages.
Birth, Education, Teaching, and Allegiance:
Mawlana's birth occurred on 25 Muharram al-Haram 1370 AH corresponding to 4 November 1950 CE. He received his initial education in his hometown. He studied Hidaya al-Awwalin and other books at Dar al-'Ulum Chhapay, Gujarat. From Chhapay, for higher education in hadith and tafsir, he went to Azhar al-Hind Dar al-'Ulum Deoband. Staying at the alma mater for three years, he obtained a graduation certificate in 1390 AH (1970 CE) and studied the six authentic hadith collections (Sahih Sitta) from the following accomplished teachers: Sahih al-Bukhari from Hazrat Mawlana Fakhr al-Din Sahib (may Allah have mercy on him), Sunan al-Tirmidhi from Hazrat Mawlana Fakhr al-Hasan Sahib (may Allah have mercy on him), Sahih Muslim from Hazrat Mawlana Sharif Sahib (may Allah have mercy on him), Sunan Abi Dawud and Sunan al-Nasa'i from Hazrat Mawlana Muhammad Hasan Sahib al-Bahari (may Allah have mercy on him). After graduation, he stayed at Dar al-'Ulum for one year to complete Arabic literature. He also received formal education in astronomy at Dar al-'Ulum. Thereafter, he also passed the matriculation examination. His teaching career began at Madrasa Kanz al-Marghub, Patan, Gujarat, teaching intermediate books. Then he taught at Ta'lim al-Islam, Anand, where he taught various books along with Sunan Abi Dawud. Thereafter, upon the invitation of the administrators of the great institution of Bihar, Jami'a Rahmaniyya, Munger, Bihar, he went to Bihar; there too Sunan Abi Dawud and Sunan al-Tirmidhi were taught. Then, for four years he taught at the madrasa of Hazrat Mawlana Kamal Sahib (may Allah have mercy on him) in Manchester, England, teaching Sunan al-Tirmidhi, Sunan Abi Dawud, Mishkat al-Masabih, etc. After graduation, he became a disciple of Hazrat Shaykh al-Hadith Mawlana Muhammad Zakariyya Sahib (may Allah have mercy on him).
Characteristics of Teaching:
The writer studied with Mawlana in Dewsbury: Tahawi Sharif, Jalalayn Sharif, and Hidaya Thalith. Mawlana taught with great diligence. He had the privilege of teaching various books of different arts many times; however, he never taught any book without preparation. His preparation was also deep; as a result, he would immediately give a satisfactory answer to a student's question. Once, Mawlana was lecturing on 'Chapter of the person in major impurity who wishes to sleep, eat, drink, or have intercourse' in Tahawi Sharif. The writer asked: 'What is the consideration (nazar) in this chapter? Imam Tahawi (may Allah have mercy on him) did not mention a rational proof here.' Mawlana said: 'The commentators have written a rational proof: that the ruling of wudu is for honoring something, and eating, drinking, sleeping, etc., are not particularly honorable; however, due to cheerfulness, it is recommended (mustahab).' His style of explanation was excellent; he had a wonderful ability to explain even the most difficult subjects in a simple manner. The student would read the text of Hidaya; Mawlana, without translating, would explain the entire ruling with proofs orally, give satisfactory responses to the proofs of the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani), and then correlate with the text. In this style, the student would not even feel the difficulties of Hidaya.
In Tahawi Sharif as well, he would deliver a full lecture on the heading of the chapter: 'Abu Ja'far said: A group of people went (to a view), they differed in that...'

Commentary
This passage is a biographical account of the life of Mawlana Shamir al-Din Qasimi, a Hanafi scholar from the Deoband tradition. The author, Mawlana Marghub Ahmad Lajpuri, describes his personal interactions with Mawlana Shamir al-Din, providing a vivid portrait of his character and teaching style. The biography covers his birth in 1950 CE, his early education in Gujarat, his higher studies at Dar al-'Ulum Deoband (Azhar al-Hind) where he studied the six major hadith collections under distinguished teachers such as Mawlana Fakhr al-Din (Bukhari), Mawlana Fakhr al-Hasan (Tirmidhi), Mawlana Sharif (Muslim), and Mawlana Muhammad Hasan al-Bahari (Abu Dawud and Nasa'i). A specific example from Tahawi Sharif illustrates his ability to explain rational proofs (dalil 'aqli) for rulings. The passage emphasizes his deep knowledge and effective teaching style. No legal rulings are extracted here; the passage serves as a front matter biographical introduction.

The Life and Works of the Author of Thamaratun Najah
Biographical sketch of the author's life, education, and works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recount the key events in the author's life and identify his major works, especially Athmar al-Hidayah, and explain their significance in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
A scholar's biography reveals the transmission of knowledge: from teachers to student, from study to teaching, from reading to writing.

This lesson introduces you to the author of Thamaratun Najah. Understanding his scholarly journey helps you appreciate the chain of transmission that safeguards Hanafi knowledge. His teachers, his teaching method, and his writings all form a link in the living tradition of the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify his scholarly lineage
List the teachers from whom he studied the six hadith collections. This shows the isnad (chain) through which he received hadith and fiqh.
He studied under scholars like Maulana Fakhr al-Din and Maulana Muhammad Hasan al-Bihari.
2
Map his teaching career
Trace the institutions where he taught: from Gujarat to Bihar to England. This reveals the global spread of Hanafi knowledge.

3

4

5
Recognize the importance of his contributions
Understand that his works, especially the commentary, are used by later scholars to understand al-Hidayah. This is part of the living tradition of sharh (commentary) in the madhhab.

For higher education he reached the Al-Azhar of India, Darul Uloom Deoband. Once, Maulana was delivering a lecture on the chapter 'Bab al-Junub Yurid al-Nawm aw al-Akl aw al-Shurb aw al-Jima'' in Sharh Ma'ani al-Athar. The writer asked, 'What is the point in this chapter? He (may his shadow be prolonged) refuted this incident and said that my father had no journey to Egypt. And in the statements of Hazrat Shah Sahib himself, it is clarified that I only stayed in the Haramayn, I did not go to Egypt, etc. (Malfuzat Muhaddith Kashmiri, p. 258). Maulana Anzar Shah Sahib (may his shadow be prolonged) writes: 'The travel of (my father) from Hijaz to Egypt, Tripoli, Syria, and Turkey is doubtful; neither did the late himself write anything about this travel, nor has any reliable trace been found so far... The famous story in common seminaries as proof of his memorization ability, that he studied Nur al-Idah in Egypt and then preserved it after coming to India, is not correct, because Nur al-Idah has been part of the Dars Nizami for a long time; rather, India was already familiar with its commentary Maraqi al-Falah and (the commentary of) al-Tahtawi. I once inquired about the authenticity of this narration from his student Maulana Muhammad Anwari Laikpuri, and he affirmed it, but my curious nature examined the Dars Nizami curriculum from ancient times and found that Nur al-Idah was included in the curriculum from early times. Therefore, according to my knowledge and research, this seminary story is not correct.' (Naqsh Dawam, p. 137). Maulana Anzar Shah Sahib's support also comes from the fact that Allama Mulla Nizam al-Din Shahid Sahaalvi (d. 116 AH / 1747 CE) prepared a curriculum for his school which later all schools adopted, and in it the name of Nur al-Idah is also present. Similarly, Hazrat Maulana Abd al-Hayy al-Lakhnawi also studied it, which shows that in his time too, its copy was present in India. And the time of Hazrat Lakhnawi is about fifty years before Shah Sahib, because Hazrat Lakhnawi's birth year is 1264 AH and death year is 1304 AH, and Shah Sahib's birth year is 1292 AH and death year is 1352 AH. Someone asked Hazrat Hakim al-Islam Qari Muhammad Tayyib Sahib whether the story of Hazrat Shah Sahib and Nur al-Idah is correct, and he said: I do not know. (Maktubat Hakim al-Islam). The author of Nur al-Idah first wrote the matn of Nur al-Idah, then he wrote a commentary on it, naming it Imdad al-Fattah, later he also summarized Imdad al-Fattah and named it Maraqi al-Falah. Thus, these two commentaries and the summary are all works of the author of Nur al-Idah himself. A commentary on this Maraqi al-Falah was written by Hazrat Allama Qadi Ahmad bin Muhammad al-Tahtawi under the name Hashiya Tahtawi, which became very famous. (Allama Tahtawi died in 1321 AH).

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and historical discussion. It begins by refuting a widely circulated story about a scholar's travels and studies. The author presents multiple evidences: the scholar's own statements, the curriculum of Dars Nizami, the existence of the book Nur al-Idah in India before the supposed travel, and testimonies of other scholars. The second part lists sixteen commentaries and translations of Nur al-Idah, a Hanafi fiqh text on taharah and salah. The author notes that the original author of Nur al-Idah also wrote its commentaries Imdad al-Fattah and Maraqi al-Falah. The commentary by al-Tahtawi on Maraqi al-Falah is highlighted as famous. This passage serves to correct historical inaccuracies and provides a valuable bibliography for students of Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
امداد الفتاح (Imdad al-Fattah): The full commentary on Nur al-Idah written by its author, Imām Shurunbulālī.
مراقی الفلاح (Maraqi al-Falah): A summary of Imdad al-Fattah, also by the author of Nur al-Idah.
حاشیة الطحطاوی (Hashiyat al-Tahtawi): A famous gloss on Maraqi al-Falah by Allama Qadi Ahmad al-Tahtawi.
نور الایضاح (Nur al-Idah): A Hanafi fiqh text on purification and prayer, written by Imām Shurunbulālī.
