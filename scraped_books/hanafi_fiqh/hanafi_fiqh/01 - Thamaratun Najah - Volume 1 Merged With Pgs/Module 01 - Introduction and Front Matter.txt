The Pedagogical Methodology of Thamaratun Najah
Front matter listing pedagogical features

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the key pedagogical features that make Thamaratun Najah a unique Hanafi fiqh textbook.

Core Idea
Thamaratun Najah teaches fiqh through repetition, proof, and clear enumeration — a method designed for deep understanding.

This front matter introduces the pedagogical philosophy of Thamaratun Najah, a Hanafi fiqh text. Understanding these features helps you use the book effectively and appreciate the scholarly approach to teaching fiqh. The author, Maulana Samiruddin Qasmi, emphasizes accessibility, multiple proofs, and systematic presentation to ensure students grasp both rulings and their evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target audience
The book is designed for students, teachers, imams, and muftis. Note the explicit mention of these groups in feature 15.
This tells you the level of detail and depth you can expect.
2
List the evidence types for each ruling
Each ruling is supported by three hadith references (feature 1), and the source type is labelled: 'hadith' for prophetic sayings, 'qawl sahabi' for Companion sayings, 'qawl tabi'i' for Follower sayings (feature 10).
Full chapter and page numbers are given for easy verification.
3
Describe the teaching techniques
Each ruling is explained three times (feature 3), in simple Urdu (feature 4). Fard, wajib, and sunnah are enumerated at a glance (feature 5). The explanation includes the principle (usul) on which the ruling is based (feature 7).
Repetition aids memorization; categorization helps systematic learning.
4
Note the linguistic and reference features
Difficult words are researched under 'lughat' (feature 8). Both old and new weights are provided for comparison (feature 14). Hadith references include the chapter and page numbers from Beirut or Saudi editions (feature 12).
These features make the book self-contained and up-to-date.
5
Identify what is deliberately avoided
Verbal debates and objections are avoided to keep students' minds focused (feature 9). This is a deliberate choice for clarity and ease.
The book prioritizes understanding over polemics.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key methodological features of Thamaratun Najah from its preface and navigate its table of contents to locate specific chapters.

Core Idea
A Hanafi fiqh commentary's preface reveals its methodology, sources, and pedagogical approach.

Every great fiqh book has a story. The preface of Thamaratun Najah tells us how it was designed to make fiqh accessible: each ruling is backed by three hadiths, explained three ways, and stripped of unnecessary debate. The table of contents then maps out the entire Book of Purification. By learning to read this front matter, you gain the keys to unlock the entire volume.

Guided Path

The preface ends with 'ثمرة النجاح جلد ا' before the contents list.
2
List the eight features mentioned in the preface

They are: (1) three hadiths and references per ruling, (2) hadiths from sihah sittah, (3) each ruling explained three ways, (4) no unnecessary objections, (5) simple style, (6) both old and new weights, (7) enumeration of fara'id, wajibat, sunan for memorization, (8) useful for imams and muftis.
These features reveal the author's emphasis on authenticity, clarity, and practicality.
3

Table of contents listing chapters and sections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major chapters in a classical Hanafi fiqh manual and explain the logical order, starting with purification before prayer.

Core Idea
Fiqh begins with purification because worship requires purity.

This passage is the table of contents for the first volume of Thamaratun Najah, a Hanafi fiqh text. It lists the chapters in order: Purification (taharah), then Prayer (salah), and so on. Understanding this structure helps you see how Hanafi scholars organize rulings: obligations are explained before their prerequisites, and acts of worship are sequenced logically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main books
Look at the list: it starts with Kitab al-Tahara (Book of Purification) and then Kitab al-Salah (Book of Prayer). These are the two major sections.
2
Note the subsections under Purification
The TOC shows many sections (fusul) under Tahara, covering water, istinja, wudu, ghusl, tayammum, etc. This reflects the detailed treatment of purification.
3
Observe the sequence within Purification
The sections follow a logical order: general rulings of water, then specific acts like istinja, then wudu (minor impurity), then ghusl (major impurity), then tayammum (substitute), then wiping over socks, then menstruation. This order addresses increasing levels of impurity and exceptions.
4
Transition to Prayer
After purification, the Book of Prayer begins with disliked times, then adhan, conditions, pillars, obligatory acts, sunnahs, etc. This shows that once purity is established, the student learns the details of the prayer itself.
5
Recognize the pedagogical logic
Hanafi fiqh manuals start with the most fundamental act of worship (salah) and its prerequisite (taharah). This order helps beginners learn the essentials first.

The Value of Comparative Fiqh: Maulana Shu'ayb Desai's Advice
Biographical preface explaining the book's comparative fiqh scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the significance of including all four Sunni imams' positions in a fiqh commentary and identify how this approach serves both research and practice.

Core Idea
Comparative fiqh illuminates truth: including all four imams' proofs provides deeper insight and practical choice.

This preface introduces the motivation behind Thamaratun Najah. The author recounts how his teacher, Maulana Shu'ayb Desai, advised him that a commentary on al-Hidayah should not be limited to Imam Abu Hanifa's proofs alone. Instead, it should include the positions and arguments of Imams Shafi’i, Malik, and Ahmad ibn Hanbal. This advice reflects a broad-minded approach to fiqh that values comprehensive understanding and facilitates action for those who follow different schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key advice
What did Maulana Shu'ayb Desai recommend? He advised including all four majors imams' positions with proofs.
2
Understand the underlying principle
Why did he want that? Because all four imams are great and their positions/ proofs are important; the author of al-Hidayah only mentioned them occasionally.
3
Analyze the purpose
Why include all? For scholars doing research to gain insight, and for practitioners to choose what to act upon.
4
Connect to the author's response
How did the author react? He was deeply impressed and committed to this expanded work.
5
Evaluate the significance for us
As students, this shows the merit of studying comparative fiqh rather than a single school in isolation.

By the virtuous Shaykh, Hadrat Mawlana Shu'ayb Desai (may his shadow be extended)
Khatib of Masjid Umar, Sheffield, and Head Teacher of Madrasah Islamiyyah, Sheffield, England

If all the proofs and hadiths of all these personalities are in one place with references, then details will become available in very little time, and it will be a scholarly treasure for people of knowledge. Followers of each school will be able to act upon it with satisfaction. May Allah grant that Hadrat's wish be fulfilled and become a means of salvation for both. Ameen, O Lord of the worlds. Meaning of 'Nur al-Idah wa Najat al-Arwah'
Nur means light, a ray of light, that which makes objects manifest. Its plural is anwar and niran. Idah means to clear, make apparent, manifest. 'Nur al-Idah' means 'the light that makes manifest'. Arwah is the plural of ruh. Najah means deliverance. And punishment will be upon the soul. The meaning is that for one unfamiliar with the issues and method of worship, these issues are light and guidance by acting upon which a person can attain deliverance from Allah's punishment.

Popularity of Nur al-Idah
The popularity of Nur al-Idah can be gauged from the fact that many commentaries were written on it. For a long time it has been included in the curriculum of madrasas, becoming for the author a means of salvation in the afterlife. The author included only the relied-upon opinions. The author himself stated in the introduction: 'I mention only what the people of tarjih have affirmed as sound,' meaning I will transmit only that statement whose correctness the people of tarjih have trusted and relied upon. It is an excellent and comprehensive concise text for madrasa children. According to 'Allamah Kashmiri: 'It is a book on the four pillars (of Islam) that is easy to obtain.' And according to 'Allamah Lakhnawi: 'I have read from his works Nur al-Idah, a solid text in fiqh.' (Al-Fawa'id al-Bahiyyah fi Tarajim al-Hanafiyyah, p. 85)

The author, 'Allamah, wrote this book and prayed: 'I ask Allah to benefit His servants through it and perpetuate its benefit.' This prayer was so accepted that from the time of authorship until today, millions of Allah's servants have benefited from it, and its benefit is before the Ummah.

This passage is the author's own introduction (front matter) to Thamaratun Najah. It begins with a tribute to the deceased author of Nur al-Idah and the completion date of this commentary. Key Terms
نور الایضاح (Nur al-Idah): A widely used Hanafi fiqh textbook for beginners, authored by Imam al-Shurunbulali (d. 1069 AH), arranged with numbered rulings.
اثمار ہدایہ (Athmar al-Hidayah): The author's earlier commentary on al-Hidayah, which served as a model for the present work.
صحاح ستہ (Sihah Sittah): The six canonical hadith collections: Sahih al-Bukhari, Sahih Muslim, Sunan Abi Dawud, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, Sunan al-Nasa'i, Sunan Ibn Majah.
قول صحابی (Qawl Sahabi): A legal opinion or statement from a Companion of the Prophet, considered a source of law in the Hanafi school when no clear hadith is available.
قول تابعی (Qawl Tabi'i): A statement from a Successor (generation after the Companions), used as a secondary source only when no hadith or companion opinion exists.

The Author's Method: The Hanafi Proof Hierarchy in Thamarat al-Najah
Preface explains book's purpose and hadith hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchy of evidence used by the Hanafi school in deriving rulings, as exemplified by the author.

Core Idea
For every Hanafi ruling, there is a hadith, a Companion's saying, or a Successor's saying in that order.

In the preface to Thamarat al-Najah, the author explains his motivation and detailed method for citing evidence. He wants every student to know that Hanafi rulings rest on firm proofs. This lesson unpacks that method and shows how it reflects the broader Hanafi approach to sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of the book
Read the author's initial lines to understand why he wrote this commentary. He wanted to provide hadith proofs for each ruling in Nur al-Idah, thereby strengthening the student's conviction.
2
List the sources in order
The author explicitly lists his priority: first a Quranic verse, then hadith from the six canonical books, then from other collections, then Companion sayings, then Successor sayings. Write these in sequence.
This hierarchy matches the Hanafi usul: Quran, then Sunnah (with further gradations), then qawl sahabi, then qawl tabi'i.
3
Understand the rationale for the order
The author's order reflects decreasing certainty and authority. The Quran is the highest; the Sihah Sittah are the most reliable hadith collections; Companion sayings are accepted by Hanafis as proof; Successor sayings are only for support.
This is an applied example of Hanafi usul principles like al-amm yufidu al-wujub and al-udul ila al-ahadith al-sahihah.
4
Note the limitations for beginners
The author explicitly says he does not mention the positions of other schools because the book is for beginners. This shows practical pedagogy: first establish one school's proof, then later engage with ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a preface from the author of Thamarat al-Najah, explaining the book's purpose, methodology, and acknowledgments. The author begins by stating that after completing Athmar al-Hidayah (a commentary on al-Hidayah), he felt the need to do similar work for Nur al-Idah, a widely used beginner-level fiqh text. Nur al-Idah arranges rulings by numbering fara'id (obligatory acts), wajibat (necessary acts), and sunan (recommended acts). The author's aim is to provide hadith proofs for each ruling, thereby strengthening the student's conviction that every Hanafi ruling has a basis in the Sunna. The intended audience includes students with limited Arabic, so he includes simple translations and explanations. The author then describes his rigorous methodology for citing evidence: first a Quranic verse, then hadiths from the six canonical books (Bukhari, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah, Nasa'i), then from other hadith collections (Dar al-Qutni, Bayhaqi), then sayings of Companions (from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah and Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq), and finally sayings of Successors. He explicitly labels each source so readers can gauge its strength. He deliberately avoids later books to ensure authenticity. Notably, since this is a beginner's book, he does not include positions from other schools. The passage concludes with a humble request for corrections and extensive gratitude to his wife, son, and several scholars who assisted in the work. This preface sets the tone for a text that is both accessible and rooted in primary sources.

Key Terms
حدیث (hadith): A report of the sayings, actions, or approvals of the Prophet Muhammad, used as a source of Islamic law.
قول صحابی (qawl sahābi): A saying of a Companion of the Prophet, considered a proof in the Hanafi school when no explicit hadith is available.
قول تابعی (qawl tābiʿī): A saying of a Successor (a Muslim who met a Companion), used as a last resort when no hadith or qawl sahābi is found.
صحاح ستہ (ṣiḥāḥ sittah): The six canonical hadith collections: Bukhari, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah, and Nasa'i.

Introductory definition of Fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the lexical and technical definition of Fiqh, identify its subject matter (the actions of a mukallaf), and explain the purpose and virtue of studying Fiqh.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of subsidiary Shar'i rulings derived from detailed evidences, applied to the actions of a mukallaf.

This lesson lays the foundation for the entire course by defining Fiqh both lexically and technically. You will learn what makes a ruling 'subsidiary', why the subject is limited to mukallafs, and why this science is so highly regarded that the Prophet ﷺ said a faqih is more severe on Shaytan than a thousand worshippers.

Guided Path

Identify the lexical meaning

Lexical meaning helps appreciate the faqih's role.
2
Break down the technical definition
Analyze the phrase 'knowledge of the subsidiary Shar'i rulings from their detailed evidences'. Distinguish between primary (belief) and subsidiary (action) rulings, and note that detailed evidences must be known.
The definition emphasizes both ruling and proof.
3
Understand the subject and purpose
Recognize that the subject is the actions of a mukallaf (legally responsible person), excluding minors and the insane. The purpose is to achieve happiness in both worlds by acting according to Shari'ah.
The virtue of Fiqh is highlighted by two hadiths.

بسم اللہ الرحمن الرحیم
The lexical meaning of fiqh is to open up and make clear a thing. A faqih is called that scholar who clarifies Shar'i rulings, traces their realities, and clarifies ambiguous and complex issues. Technical meaning: In the terminology of the people of Shari'ah, the famous definition of fiqh is: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary Shar'i rulings derived from their detailed evidences.' Translation: Fiqh is that knowledge of subsidiary Shar'i rulings which is obtained from the detailed evidences of rulings. Explanation of each part: Al-ahkam al-shar'iyyah al-far'iyyah: Rulings are of two types: primary and subsidiary. Primary rulings: Belief-based rulings are called primary rulings, such as the Oneness of God, prophethood, the coming of messengers, knowledge of the Hereafter. These are all belief-based rulings and are called primary rulings. The science of fiqh does not conflict with this because it deals with subsidiary rulings. Subsidiary rulings: Those rulings that pertain to action, such as rulings of prayer, fasting, Hajj, buying and selling, etc. In the science of fiqh, these rulings are explained. Min adillatiha al-tafseeliyyah: The meaning of this phrase is that the detailed evidences of rulings should also be known. For example, if it is said that Hajj is obligatory, then the verse should also be known that its evidence is 'And complete the Hajj and Umrah for Allah' (Surah al-Baqarah 2:196) exists. Or if a matter is mentioned that prayer and zakat are obligatory, then this evidence should also be known that the verse 'Establish prayer and give zakat and bow with those who bow' (Surah al-Baqarah 2:43) is its evidence. In short, the science of fiqh is to apply the ruling of permissibility, prohibition, dislike, obligation, etc., to every detail of human actions and to explain the evidence for each of them. Subject matter of Fiqh: The act and action of a mukallaf (legally responsible person) from which it is discussed whether it is fard (2) or wajib (3) or sunnah mu'akkadah (4) or sunnah ghayr mu'akkadah (5) or nafl (6) or mustahab (7) or haram (8) or makruh tahrimi (9) or makruh tanzihi (10) or mubah. Discussing the above about the actions of a mukallaf and applying a ruling is the subject matter of fiqh. The restriction of mukallaf is applied because Shari'ah does not apply a ruling to the actions of a minor child or an insane person, nor are they discussed. It only discusses the actions of a mukallaf. Purpose and Goal: Attainment of happiness in both worlds: that the faqih benefits God's creation in the world, attaining high ranks, and in the Hereafter he will intercede for whomever he wishes and be honored with the vision of his Lord. Or, the purpose is to acquire the ability and faculty to act according to the Shar'i rulings by learning fiqh. Greatness of Fiqh: What greater greatness could there be than that the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him) praised the faqih in very lofty words. He said: 'From Ibn Abbas that the Messenger of Allah (may Allah send blessings and peace upon him) said: Whoever Allah intends good for, He gives him understanding of the religion.' (Tirmidhi Sharif, chapter on when Allah intends good for a servant, He gives him understanding of the religion, p. 601 no. 2645; Musnad Ahmad, Musnad Abi Hurairah, vol. 2, p. 465 no. 7153) From this hadith, it is understood that Allah gives the knowledge of fiqh ad-din to whoever He decides good for. Another hadith: From Ibn Abbas, he said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: A faqih is more severe upon Satan than a thousand worshippers. (Tirmidhi Sharif, chapter on what has come regarding the virtue of fiqh over worship, p. 608 no. 268) From this hadith, it is understood that a faqih outweighs a thousand worshippers. Therefore, learning fiqh and acting upon it is a matter of great virtue. In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful.

Commentary
This passage introduces the science of Fiqh, defining it both lexically and technically. Lexically, fiqh means to open up and clarify, indicating that the faqih makes Shar'i rulings clear and unravels complex issues. The technical definition given is the standard one in the Hanafi school: knowledge of subsidiary Shar'i rulings derived from their detailed evidences. The author then breaks down each part: 'subsidiary rulings' refers to practical, action-based rulings (as opposed to belief-based primary rulings), and 'detailed evidences' means that the faqih not only knows the ruling but also its specific proof from the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, or Qiyas. The subject matter of Fiqh is the actions of a mukallaf (a legally responsible person), categorized into the ten types of rulings: fard, wajib, sunnah mu'akkadah, sunnah ghayr mu'akkadah, nafl, mustahab, haram, makruh tahrimi, makruh tanzihi, and mubah. The restriction to mukallaf excludes minors and the insane, as their actions do not incur legal responsibility. The purpose of studying Fiqh is to attain success in both worlds—benefiting others in this world and attaining intercession and vision of Allah in the Hereafter—and to develop the faculty to act in accordance with Shari'ah. The passage concludes by citing two hadiths highlighting the virtue of Fiqh: one from Tirmidhi stating that Allah gives understanding of the religion (fiqh fi al-din) to whom He intends good, and another that a faqih is more severe on Shaytan than a thousand worshippers. Thus, learning and acting upon Fiqh is of immense virtue. This introductory material sets the foundation for the entire course.

Key Terms

مکلف (mukallaf): A legally responsible person whose actions are subject to Shar'i rulings.
فرض (fard): A ruling that is obligatory upon a mukallaf, proven by a qat'i (definitive) evidence.

The Hanafi School: Founders, Scope, and Methodology
Explains Hanafi school, its founders, and methodological approach

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why 'Hanafiyyah' refers to a collective school of three imams and identify the role of ihtiyat (precaution) in its foundational methodology.

Core Idea
Hanafiyyah is the collective school of Abu Hanifah, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad – acting on any is following the school.

This lesson introduces the structure of the Hanafi madhhab. Many assume it is solely the view of Imam Abu Hanifah, but the tradition encompasses the opinions of his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this collective nature is essential for grasping how Hanafi fiqh operates and why internal differences are a feature, not a flaw.

Guided Path
1
Define Hanafiyyah
Identify the correct scope of the term: it refers to the collective jurisprudence of Imam Abu Hanifah, Imam Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. Acting on any of their opinions is considered following the Hanafi school.
2
Distinguish the three pillars
Recognize that each of the three imams contributed to the school. Imam Abu Hanifah is the eponym, but Abu Yusuf and Muhammad are integral parts, often referred to as 'al-Sahibayn' (the two companions).
3
Understand ihtiyat in Abu Hanifah's method
Abu Hanifah frequently adopted a cautious (ihtiyat) position due to his piety and the nascent stage of fiqh compilation, making him vulnerable to criticism. He sometimes followed a Companion's saying or a Tabi'i's fatwa over a hadith if he deemed it more cautionary.
4
Recognize diversity within the school
The Sahibayn sometimes differed from their teacher, often following authentic hadith. These differences are recorded in core texts like al-Quduri and al-Hidayah, and fatwa may be given according to their views in times of need.
5
Address the rational proof misconception
The passage refutes the claim that Hanafi fiqh relies on rational proof. It asserts that every ruling is derived from Quran, hadith, Companion sayings, Tabi'i fatwas, ijma', or qiyas. Rational arguments are only used to explain wisdom, not as primary sources.

Hanafiyyah is the name of the collective [jurisprudence] of the three Imams. My respected teacher used to say that Hanafiyyah is not only the name of the school of Imam Abu Hanifah, but the name of the collective schools of Imam Abu Hanifah, Imam Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on them all). If one acts upon the school of any one of them, it will be considered acting upon the school of the Hanafiyyah. And if a fatwa is given according to the school of Imam Muhammad or Imam Abu Yusuf, it will not be considered outside the school of Hanafiyyah. This is why in important Hanafi books like Quduri and Hidayah, the schools of both these Imams are recorded, and in times of need, fatwa is also given according to them. The school of Imam Abu Hanifah is based on precaution. Imam Abu Hanifah was a very pious and cautious person. Therefore, he always gave fatwa on the side of precaution and adopted that school. On the other hand, at that time fiqh had not been compiled. Imam Abu Hanifah is the first Imam who compiled fiqh and usul al-fiqh. So if he had adopted a side other than caution, every person would have raised a finger. Therefore, he adopted a cautious school, even if it meant following a fatwa of a Tabi'i. But his distinguished disciples, Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, in the light of hadith, sometimes adopted another school, and with an open heart recorded the school with its proofs. Now the readers have the choice to adopt the school of the Greatest Imam or the school of his eminent disciples. In both cases, the virtue goes to the Greatest Imam. In the last century, the Deobandi school of thought had the honor of highlighting and disseminating the school of the Greatest Imam. They also adopted a cautious approach and generally gave fatwa on caution like the Imam. Therefore, some readers developed a doubt and began to say that the Hanafi school differs from ahadith. But perhaps they did not consider that in those issues where they did not find ahadith, the school of the two important pillars of the Hanafis differs from the Greatest Imam. And for the school they chose, there are hundred percent authentic ahadith. Moreover, at such times, the school of the Sahibayn coincides with that of Imam Shafi'i and Imam Malik. In the present book 'Thamarat al-Najah', we will see repeatedly that wherever the Sahibayn differed from the Greatest Imam, the Greatest Imam had a saying of a Sahabi or a fatwa of a Tabi'i, while the Sahibayn had ahadith. But the school of the Greatest Imam is based on caution. (1) My humble opinion is that those who raised doubts only considered the school of the Greatest Imam and, not finding ahadith in some places, strengthened their doubt about the entire Hanafiyyah. They did not pay attention to the difference of his distinguished disciples Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad and their strong ahadith, nor did they consider that they are also two important pillars of Hanafiyyah, and the dissemination of all issues is indebted to their pen. (2) They also did not consider that the school of the Greatest Imam is based on precaution, and this is the first compiled fiqh, which is why he had to adopt a cautious approach. (3) It is not correct to assume that the Hanafi school is based on rational proof. It is only later scholars who presented rational proof to explain wisdom. Otherwise, the entire Quduri, Hidayah, and Nur al-Idah that I have examined, every single issue is derived from a verse, a hadith, a saying of a Sahabi, or a saying of a Tabi'i. Or from these four, a principle was determined and from the principle the issue was extracted. The issue does not solely rely on rational proof. And wherever only rational proof is mentioned, I have recorded the principle, and principles are derived from ahadith, so those issues are also derived from ahadith. Only in the books of Iman and Qada do some issues rely on the common usage (muhawarat) of that time. Therefore, there the issue is recorded according to custom. Those issues have no hadith or Quran. Obviously, the basis of these issues is custom, so where can ahadith or verses be found for them? The schools of the Hanafis, indeed all the great Imams, are derived from (1) Quran, (2) Hadith, (3) Saying of a Sahabi, (4) Fatwa of a Tabi'i, (5) Ijma', and (6) Qiyas. Therefore, it is not correct to criticize the great Imams. Written by the humble servant: Thamir al-Din Qasimi.

Commentary
This passage is a front-matter introduction to the Hanafi school, explaining its scope and methodology. The author begins by clarifying that 'Hanafiyyah' is not merely the school of Imam Abu Hanifah alone, but the collective jurisprudence of the three foundational imams: Abu Hanifah, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Acting on any of their opinions is considered following the Hanafiyyah. This is demonstrated by the fact that authoritative Hanafi texts like Al-Quduri and Al-Hidayah include the rulings of both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. The author then explains why Imam Abu Hanifah often adopted a cautious (ihtiyati) position: his deep piety, and his role as the first to compile fiqh systematically, which made him vulnerable to criticism. In contrast, his students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, living in a later period, often followed authentic hadith even when it differed from their teacher. The author addresses a common criticism that Hanafis rely on rational argument (dalil 'aqli) rather than hadith. He refutes this by asserting that every ruling in the core Hanafi books is derived from the Quran, hadith, sayings of Companions, or fatwas of the Tabi'un. Rational argument is only used to explain wisdom, not as the basis for rulings. The only exceptions are issues of 'iman and qada, which depend on the customary usage of the time. The author also notes that the Deobandi school, which promoted the Hanafi school in later centuries, also followed the cautious approach. This passage thus serves to defend the Hanafi school against the charge of disregarding hadith and to explain the internal diversity within the school.

صاحبین (al-Sahibayn): The two companions: Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the principal students of Abu Hanifah.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Sermon. Translation: I begin with the name of Allah, who is very kind and extremely merciful. Reason for placing Bismillah: There are five reasons for writing it first. (1) When revelation first came to the Prophet (PBUH), he was told to read in the name of Allah. The verse is: 'Read in the name of your Lord who created' (Surah Al-Alaq, verse 1). (2) Prophet Sulayman (AS) wrote a letter to Bilqis and began with Bismillah. The verse is: 'Indeed, it is from Sulayman, and indeed it is in the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful' (Surah An-Naml, verse 30). (3) The Noble Quran begins with Bismillah, indeed every surah begins with Bismillah. (4) All the letters and all the sermons of the Prophet begin with Bismillah. (5) One can also infer from the indication of this hadith because the hadith contains the word 'dhikr' and Bismillah is also dhikr. The hadith is: From Abu Huraira, he said: The Messenger of Allah said: 'Every important matter that does not begin with the remembrance of Allah is cut off' (Daraqutni, Kitab al-Salah, vol. 1, p. 235, no. 873). By 'dhikr' one can mean Bismillah. Lexicology: 'Bismillah': The 'ba' is a particle (harf jar). It has many meanings. Here its meaning is attachment (ilşāq), i.e., attaching to an action. Or its meaning is seeking help (isti'ānah), i.e., I begin with the help of Allah's name. Since 'ba' is a particle, a verb must be implied to govern it. It is better to assume the verb 'abtadi' (I begin) is omitted, so the meaning is 'I begin with the name of Allah.' Then 'ba' is attached, and 'ism' is subordinate to 'Allah', so the word 'Allah' becomes foremost. In 'Iqra' bismi rabbik', the same command was given: read in the name of Allah, so the name of Allah is foremost. 'Allah': Allah has ninety-nine attributes (sifati) names, and the word 'Allah' is the personal (dhati) name. 'Allah' is derived from 'al-ilah'. 'Ilah' means worshipped, and by prefixing 'al' and 'lam', it becomes 'the specific worshipped', i.e., Allah. There are no other deities, but polytheists believe in many objects of worship, so it distinguishes, making Allah the specific worshipped. 'Ar-Rahman': on the pattern of fa'lan. It is derived from mercy (rahmah). The meaning of mercy is tenderness of heart. Tenderness of heart is impossible for Allah because He is pure of heart and soul. So when mercy is attributed to Allah, its meaning is grace and kindness, doing favor. 'Rahman' is an intensive form.

Commentary
This passage is the author's introductory khutbah, establishing the virtue and legal basis for beginning any important matter with Bismillah. The author, Thamir al-Din Qasimi, presents five proofs: (1) the first revelation commanding the Prophet to read in Allah's name; (2) Prophet Sulayman's letter to Bilqis; (3) the Quran's practice of beginning every surah with Bismillah; (4) the Prophet's own letters and sermons; (5) a hadith from Daraqutni stating that any important matter not begun with remembrance of Allah is 'cut off' (aqta'), interpreted as lacking blessing. The author then provides a linguistic analysis: the 'ba' in Bismillah is a preposition indicating attachment (ilṣāq) or seeking help (isti'ānah). An implied verb (abtadi', 'I begin') is assumed to govern the preposition. The word 'Allah' is derived from 'al-ilāh' (the specific worshipped) and is the personal name of God, not a descriptive attribute like Ar-Rahman, which is an intensive form derived from mercy (rahmah) but refers to Allah's grace, not emotional tenderness. This khutbah sets the tone for the fiqh text that follows, emphasizing the foundational role of Bismillah in Islamic practice.

Key Terms
بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم (Bismillāh al-Raḥmān al-Raḥīm): The formula 'In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful,' used to begin acts and writings.
وجه تقديم (wajh taqdīm): The reason or justification for placing something (here, Bismillah) at the beginning.
محذوف (mahdhūf): An omitted word in a sentence that must be assumed to complete the grammatical structure.

The Opening Sermon: Bismillah and Alhamdulillah
Explains the basmalah and hamd in the book's opening

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five proofs for beginning with Bismillah, explain the linguistic meanings of Ar-Rahman and Ar-Raheem, and define hamd as verbal praise for voluntary attributes.

Core Idea
Begin every important act with Allah's name and praise—this book's opening is grounded in Quran, Sunnah, and scholarly practice.

This passage is the opening sermon (khutbah) of the book. The author explains why he begins with Bismillah and Alhamdulillah, citing five proofs from revelation, prophetic practice, and hadith. He then provides a lexical analysis of the divine names and the definition of hamd. This sets the precedent for all subsequent content: every important matter should start with the remembrance and praise of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five proofs for Bismillah
The author lists five reasons: the first revelation (Iqra' bismi rabbik), the letter of Prophet Sulayman, the Quran's opening, the Prophet's letters/sermons, and the hadith 'every matter of importance not beginning with Allah's remembrance is cut off.'
These proofs establish the sunnah of beginning with basmalah.
2
Parse the linguistic components of Bismillah
Analyze the preposition 'ba' (meaning attachment or seeking help), the divine name 'Allah' (derived from al-ilah, the unique deity), and the attributes Ar-Rahman and Ar-Raheem (both from mercy, with Ar-Rahman being more intensive).
The author uses Arabic morphology to explain degrees of mercy.
3
Understand the presentation of Alhamdulillah
The author gives three reasons for beginning with hamd: the Quran starts with praise, a hadith declares speech without praise as deficient, and worldly custom of praising a king first.
Hamd is defined as verbal praise for voluntary qualities, whether or not in return for blessings.
4
Relate this to Hanafi fiqh practice
Recognize that this opening establishes the principle of beginning important matters with basmalah and hamd, which is applied in acts like writing, speech, and religious texts.
The author follows classical Hanafi methodology by grounding the opening in primary sources.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Sermon. Translation: I begin with the name of Allah, who is very merciful and extremely compassionate. Reason for presenting Bismillah: There are five reasons for writing it first. (1) The first revelation to the Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded reading in the name of Allah. The verse is: 'Read in the name of your Lord who created' (Surah Al-Alaq 96:1). (2) Prophet Solomon (peace be upon him) wrote to Bilqis beginning with Bismillah. The verse is: 'It is from Solomon, and it is: In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful' (Surah An-Naml 27:30). (3) The Quran begins with Bismillah; indeed every Surah begins with it. (4) All letters and sermons of the Prophet begin with Bismillah. (5) This hadith also indicates that: narrated Abu Huraira, the Prophet said: 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with the remembrance of Allah is cut off' (Daraqutni, Book of Prayer). The word 'remembrance' can mean Bismillah. Lexicon: Bismillah: the letter 'ba' is a preposition with many meanings; here it means attachment or seeking help. 'Allah' is the personal name derived from 'al-ilah' meaning the only deity. 'Ar-Rahman' is on the pattern of fa'lan, derived from mercy, meaning extreme mercy encompassing everything. 'Ar-Raheem' is on the pattern of fa'eel, also derived from mercy but less intensive. 'Alhamdulillahi Rabbil Alameen' means all praise is for Allah, the Lord of all worlds. The reasons for presenting hamd first are: (1) the Quran begins with praise, (2) a hadith states that speech not beginning with praise is deficient, (3) worldly custom is to praise the king first. The definition of hamd is to verbally express the voluntary qualities of the praised one, whether in return for blessings or not.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the khutbah (sermon) of the book Thamaratun Najah. It begins by explaining the divine names Ar-Rahman and Ar-Rahim, noting that both derive from mercy but Rahman has greater intensity due to more letters, and it is specific to this world while Rahim pertains to the Hereafter. The commentator then discusses the basmalah, stating that beginning with Allah's name is recommended but not obligatory; any form of dhikr suffices, though basmalah is best following the Quranic precedent. The explanation of Alhamdulillah covers linguistic and technical definitions, the reasons for praising Allah (based on Quranic opening, hadith, and customary practice), and the grammatical nuance of the definite article 'al'—whether it indicates comprehensiveness, genus, or specific praise—favored by the majority as istighraq (all-encompassing). The term Rabb al-'Alamin is explained as the gradual nourisher of the worlds, and 'Alamin as the plural of 'alam, meaning that which indicates the Creator. The final part covers the salawat: the meaning of salat (prayer for mercy from Allah, seeking forgiveness from angels, supplication from humans) and salam (peace), with Quranic evidence from Surah al-Ahzab 33:56, and the superiority of sending salawat over even the angels' prostration to Adam.

Key Terms
الرحمن (ar-Rahman): A divine name meaning 'the Most Merciful,' with greater intensity than Rahim; it relates to mercy in this world for both believers and disbelievers.
الرحيم (ar-Rahim): A divine name meaning 'the Especially Merciful,' with less intensity than Rahman; it relates to mercy in the Hereafter exclusively for believers.
الحمد (al-hamd): Verbal expression of praise for voluntary good qualities of the praised one, whether in gratitude for favors or not; here it is all-encompassing praise due to Allah.
رب (rabb): One who nurtures and gradually brings something to completion; when used alone (without idafah) it is specific to Allah; in idafah it can refer to a human master.
العالمين (al-'alamin): Plural of 'alam (world/sign); all created realms (heavens, earth, Paradise, Hell) that serve as signs of the Creator.
الصلاة (as-salah): When from Allah: mercy; from angels: seeking forgiveness; from humans: supplication. Here it denotes sending blessings upon the Prophet.
السلام (as-salam): Safety and peace; when sent upon the Prophet, it means greeting him with a prayer for his well-being.

A book's introduction reveals its purpose and the author's method; knowing it is key to understanding the text.

When you open a classical fiqh text, the first thing you encounter is the author's introduction (muqaddimah). This is not mere formality — it tells you why the book was written, for whom, and often includes essential definitions that shape the rest of the work. Al-Shurunbulali's introduction to his book on worship does exactly that, defining key terms and stating his aim to help beginners grasp the scattered rulings of larger works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and his lineage
The author identifies himself as 'Abu al-Ikhlas Hasan al-Wafa'i al-Shurunbulali al-Hanafi'. Note the nisbah 'al-Shurunbulali' refers to a place in Egypt, and 'al-Hanafi' indicates his madhhab. Recognizing the author's school and background helps you understand the perspective of the text.
His full name shows he is a Hanafi scholar from the tenth/sixteenth century.
2
State the purpose of the book
The author says that sincere friends asked him to write a concise book on acts of worship (ibadat) that would make it easier for beginners to understand the many rulings scattered in large fiqh works. This tells you the book is a teaching text, not an exhaustive reference.
The phrase 'taqrib 'ala al-mubtadi' (bring near to the beginner) is key.
3
Analyze the definition of Nabi vs Rasul
The author defines a Nabi as one who receives no new sharia, and a Rasul as one who does. He notes that a Rasul is superior but that the term Nabi includes Rasul when used out of respect. This distinction matters when reading salawat or discussions about prophethood.
Hanafi usul often uses this distinction in discussing the superiority of prophets.
4
Understand the terms 'Al' and 'Ashab'
'Al' refers to the Prophet's family, specifically those upon whom sadaqah is forbidden (e.g., Al Ali, Al Ja'far). 'Ashab' are those who saw the Prophet in faith and died in faith. The author emphasizes that all companions are included.
The Hanafi understanding of 'Al' is broader than just descendants; it includes the families of Ali, Ja'far, etc.
5
Recognize the role of the hadith in the introduction
The author includes a hadith about how to send salat upon the Prophet. This serves to provide the correct wording and demonstrate his reliance on authentic sources. It is not here to prove an obligation but to teach the proper form.
The hadith is from Muslim and Abu Dawud, showing the author's care in citing reliable texts.

Author's Purpose and Methodological Commitment
Author's preface explaining motivation, scope, and methodology.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the reason the author wrote Nur al-Idah and identify the two key methodological principles he follows: reliance on ahl al-tarjih and avoidance of verbosity.

Core Idea
A reliable beginner text omits ikhtilaf and cites only the preponderant view.

Every classical fiqh text opens with a preface that sets the author's intent and methodology. In this passage, the great Hanafi scholar al-Shurunbulali explains why he composed Nur al-Idah and what principles guided his selection of rulings. Understanding this preface helps you read the rest of the book with awareness of its scope and authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author and his self-description
The author introduces himself as 'al-ʿabd al-faqīr ilā mawlāhu al-ghanī'—a humble servant. This is a standard formula, but note his nisbah 'al-Hanafī', signaling his madhhab affiliation.
Al-Shurunbulali (d. 1659) is a leading late Hanafi authority.
2
Locate the reason for writing
He says 'some sincere friends asked me to write a preface (muqaddimah) on acts of worship.' The request came from peers seeking a concise work that gathers scattered rulings from longer books.
The scope is limited to ibadat (worship), not all fiqh.
3
Extract the methodological principle
He commits to mentioning only 'what the people of tarjīh (ahl al-tarjīh) have deemed authentic.' This means he relies on the preponderant opinion within the Hanafi school, not every variant.
Ahl al-tarjīh are the scholars who weigh evidence and pick the strongest view.
4
Note the commitment to brevity
He says 'without verbosity' (min ghayr iṭnāb). The book avoids lengthy debate to remain accessible to beginners.
This distinguishes Nur al-Idah from comprehensive works like al-Hidayah.
5
Identify the title and closing prayer
He names the work 'Nur al-Idah wa Najat al-Arwah' and prays that Allah grant benefit through it. This reflects his sincere intention (niyya).
The title 'Light of Clarification' indicates its purpose: to illuminate rulings.
