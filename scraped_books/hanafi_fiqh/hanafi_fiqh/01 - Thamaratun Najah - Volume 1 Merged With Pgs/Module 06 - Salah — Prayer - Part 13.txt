Prayer in the Ka'bah and the Follower's Direction
Ruling on validity of prayer inside Ka'bah based on muqtadi's direction to imam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios of muqtadi-imam orientation inside the Ka'bah and identify which invalidates the prayer.

Core Idea
Inside the Ka'bah, all directions are qibla, but the muqtadi must not be ahead of the imam.

This lesson covers a unique Hanafi ruling regarding congregational prayer inside or on top of the Ka'bah. While normally the muqtadi must be behind the imam, inside the Ka'bah the qibla is every direction, so the key condition becomes the relative orientation. The matn gives a concise rule, and the commentary unpacks four possible orientations, only one of which invalidates the prayer.

Guided Path
1
Understand the qibla inside the Ka'bah
Inside the Ka'bah, every direction is qibla, so orientation per se does not invalidate prayer. The ruling focuses on the muqtadi's position relative to the imam.
2
Learn the basic rule from the matn
The matn states: if the muqtadi places his back toward the imam's face, the prayer is invalid; otherwise it is valid.
This is the core memorization anchor.
3
Memorize the four scenarios
Study the four cases: (1) muqtadi's face to imam's back (valid), (2) muqtadi's face to imam's face (valid but makruh), (3) muqtadi's back to imam's back (valid), (4) muqtadi's back to imam's face (invalid).
Use the diagram in the commentary to visualize.
4
Identify the invalid scenario
Only scenario 4 invalidates because the muqtadi is considered ahead of the imam.
Aheadness is defined by having one's back to the imam's face.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling from Hanafi fiqh regarding prayer inside or on top of the Ka'bah in congregation. The matn (text) gives the basic rule: if a muqtadi (follower) places his back toward any direction other than the imam's face, his prayer is valid; but if he places his back toward the imam's face, the prayer is invalid. The commentary (sharh) explains that inside the Ka'bah, all directions are qibla, so orientation is not an issue. However, the invalidity arises because the muqtadi has moved ahead of the imam by facing away from him. The commentator then details four scenarios involving the relative directions of imam and muqtadi. The first three are valid: (1) muqtadi's face toward imam's back (normal following), (2) muqtadi's face toward imam's face (valid but makruh because it resembles facing an idol), (3) muqtadi's back toward imam's back (valid but not ideal). Only the fourth scenario—muqtadi's back toward imam's face—is invalid because the muqtadi has gone ahead of the imam. This ruling is specific to prayer inside the Ka'bah and does not apply to ordinary mosque prayer where the qibla is fixed and the imam is always in front.

امام (imam): The leader of congregational prayer.
مقتدی (muqtadi): The follower in congregational prayer.
قبلہ (qibla): The direction of prayer towards the Ka'bah.

Following an Imam Inside the Ka'bah: The Door Must Be Open
Ruling on iqtida' when imam is inside Ka'bah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on following an imam inside the Ka'bah and identify the condition that determines validity.

Core Idea
For iqtida' outside the Ka'bah to be valid, the door must be open so the muqtadi can follow the imam's actions.

This lesson examines a specific fiqh ruling: the validity of following an imam who leads prayer inside the Ka'bah while the muqtadis are outside. The Hanafi school conditions validity on the door being open, ensuring the muqtadi can track the imam's movements. We will trace the reasoning and the hadith evidence that supports this condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is iqtida' (following the imam) when the imam is inside the Ka'bah and the muqtadi is outside. The Hanafi ruling: sahih (valid) if the door is open; invalid (ghayr sahih) if the door is closed.
2
Understand the condition
The key condition is the door being open. Why? Because the muqtadi must be aware of the imam's actions. If the door is closed, the muqtadi cannot see or otherwise ascertain the imam's state, which is necessary for iqtida' to be valid.
This condition is specific to the Ka'bah; in a normal mosque, the muqtadi can see through other means.
3
Examine the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Bukhari and Muslim. In the hadith, the Prophet (peace be upon him) entered the Ka'bah with companions, closed the door, and prayed inside. Those outside did not follow him because the door was closed. This shows that when the door is closed, iqtida' is not performed.
The hadith is used as evidence that the door must be open for iqtida' to be valid.
4
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
The Hanafi ruling is mukhtalaf fih (disputed). Some other schools may hold that iqtida' is valid even when the door is closed, as long as there is some means of knowing the imam's actions (e.g., sound).
This demonstrates a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different interpretations of the hadith.

Prayer in a Circle Around the Ka'bah with Imam Outside
Ruling on validity of congregational prayer in a circle around the Ka'bah when imam is outside

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the validity of congregational prayer in a circle formation, identifying which muqtadis are invalid due to being ahead of the imam.

Core Idea
In a circular congregation, only the muqtadi nearest the Ka'bah on the imam's side is invalid; all others are valid.

When a group forms a circle around the Ka'bah for prayer with the imam standing outside, a unique question arises: does the circular formation affect the rule that a muqtadi must not be ahead of the imam? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer, distinguishing between muqtadis based on their position relative to the imam's side. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the formation
The prayer is performed in a circle around the Ka'bah. The imam is positioned outside the Ka'bah (e.g., in the Masjid al-Haram). Visualise the arrangement: worshippers form a ring, with the Ka'bah at the centre.
The Ka'bah is the qibla for all; thus, each muqtadi faces the Ka'bah.
2
Locate the imam's side
Determine which muqtadi is on the same side as the imam relative to the Ka'bah. This is the line from the imam to the Ka'bah. Only muqtadis on that line can potentially be ahead.
Muqtadis on the opposite side or perpendicular are not considered ahead.
3
Apply the ahead-of-imam rule
The muqtadi who is on the imam's side and is closer to the Ka'bah than the imam is considered ahead of the imam. This invalidates his prayer.
The key criterion is physical proximity to the qibla on the imam's directional axis.
4
Determine validity
All other muqtadis (not on the imam's side, or on his side but not ahead) have valid prayers. Only the one on the imam's side nearest the Ka'bah is invalid.
Thus, the circle formation itself is valid; only one specific position is problematic.

Congregational Prayer Around the Kaaba: The Ruling
A ruling on congregational prayer around the Kaaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for congregational prayer when the congregation circles the Kaaba with the imam outside, and identify the specific exception that invalidates the prayer.

Core Idea
A follower ahead of the imam invalidates the prayer—even around the Kaaba.

This lesson examines a unique scenario: performing congregational prayer (salat al-jama'ah) in the vicinity of the Kaaba. The Hanafi school provides a specific ruling that preserves the essential condition of following (iqtida) by ensuring no follower is physically ahead of the imam. Understanding this ruling clarifies the boundaries of valid iqtida in spatial arrangements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the setting
The matn describes a scenario where worshipers form a circle around the Kaaba, with the imam standing outside the circle. The basic ruling is that the prayer is valid for all worshipers except one specific situation.
The circle can be complete or partial; the imam must be outside the circle.
2
Identify the exception
The text states 'except for the one who is closer to it (the Kaaba) in the direction of his imam.' This means a follower who, relative to the imam, is closer to the Kaaba in the same direction as the imam is facing.
This follower is considered 'ahead' of the imam, which violates the condition of iqtida.
3
Apply the condition
Being closer to the Kaaba in the direction of the imam means the follower is ahead toward the qibla. Since the Kaaba is the qibla, any follower between the imam and the Kaaba in the imam's line of direction is effectively in front of the imam.
The Kaaba's circular arrangement can create this situation unintentionally.
4
Note the underlying principle
The condition of valid iqtida requires that the follower be behind or alongside the imam, not ahead. The exception preserves this principle in a unique spatial context.
This ruling is specific to the Hanafi school and differs from some other schools.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah is a ruling with proofs on the minimum travel distance that alters legal rulings (particularly shortening prayer). The author begins by stating the ruling: the minimum travel that changes rulings is a three-day journey using the shortest days of the year, at a moderate pace with rest. This is the relied-upon Hanafi position. The proof for three days is twofold: (1) the hadith of Abu Sa'id al-Khudri that prohibits women from traveling three days or more without a mahram, which shows that three days is considered a journey (safar) in Shari'ah; (2) the hadith of Shurayh ibn Hani about wiping over khuffs, where the Prophet set three days and nights for the traveler, reinforcing that three days is the threshold for travel rulings. He notes that modern vehicles like cars and planes were not prevalent then, so their ruling is not explicitly given. The text then converts units: one barid = 4 farsakh, one farsakh = 3 shar'i miles, so 4 barid = 16 farsakh = 48 shar'i miles, which is approximately 81 km. Proofs include the athar of Ibn Umar and Ibn Abbas shortening at 4 barid and the explicit statement of Ibn Abbas that 48 miles is the distance for qasr. The conclusion is that the Hanafi school sets the minimum travel distance at about 48 miles or 81 kilometers, measured at a moderate pace. This is a Zahir al-Riwayah ruling, as it is derived from well-known hadith and practice of the Companions.

Conditions for Valid Intention to Travel for Qasr
Three conditions for valid travel intention for qasr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions required for the validity of the intention to travel for qasr in the Hanafi school and identify when each condition is absent.

Core Idea
Three conditions must be met: independence, adulthood, and a journey of at least three days.

In the Hanafi school, shortening prayers (qasr) during travel is a concession that hinges on a valid intention to travel. This lesson unpacks the three conditions that must be present for the intention to be legally effective. Understanding these conditions helps avoid common mistakes when determining whether qasr is permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act governed by these conditions
The conditions apply to the intention to travel for the purpose of qasr (shortening prayers). If the intention is invalid, one must pray full prayers even if physically traveling.
The act is the traveler's intention, not the travel itself.
2
First condition: Independence (istiqlal)
The traveler must be independent in decision-making, not a dependent such as a slave or a wife. For dependents, the intention is made by their master or husband.
A dependent's intention does not count independently.
3
Second condition: Adulthood (bulugh)
The traveler must have reached puberty (baligh). A child's intention is not valid because the child is not fully accountable (mukallaf).
Puberty is the threshold for legal responsibility in Islamic law.
4
Third condition: Minimum travel duration (three days)
The journey must be planned to last at least three days. In Hanafi law, this corresponds to approximately 48 miles (but the standard is time-based).
If the journey is shorter, the traveler does not shorten prayers.
5
Consequence of missing any condition
If any one of the three conditions is absent, the intention to travel is not valid, and qasr is not permitted. The traveler must offer full prayers.
All three conditions must be present simultaneously.

Qasr is 'Azimah: Ruling on Completing Four Rak'ahs
Ruling on qasr being 'azimah and consequences of completing four rak'ahs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on qasr as 'azimah and determine the validity of a traveler's prayer when he completes four rak'ahs based on his sitting and intention.

Core Idea
In the Hanafi school, qasr is the original ruling ('azimah) for a traveler, not a concession — completing four rak'ahs requires the first tashahhud and proper intention, otherwise it is invalid.

This passage from Thamaratun Najah clarifies the Hanafi position that qasr (shortening the four-rak'ah prayer) is 'azimah — the determined norm — and not a mere rukhsah (concession). Consequently, if a traveler completes four rak'ahs, the validity hinges on two factors: whether he sat for the first tashahhud and whether he intended residence (iqamah) before standing. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly applying the school's rules on travel prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is being ruled upon? The act is a traveler completing four rak'ahs instead of shortening to two. The ruling is classified into three cases based on sitting and intention.
The passage states: qasr is 'azimah, so the default is to shorten.
2
Apply the condition of sitting (qu'ud awwal)
If the traveler sits for the first tashahhud and then completes four, the prayer is valid but disliked (karahah). If he does not sit, it is invalid unless he intended iqamah.
The first sitting is wajib; omitting it without iqamah makes the prayer batil.
3
Apply the condition of intention (iqamah)
If the traveler intended residence (15+ days) before standing for the third rak'ah, then completing four is valid and qasr is no longer applicable. This intention changes the ruling.
Iqamah converts the traveler to a resident, so full prayer becomes the norm.
4
Synthesize the scenarios
Combine the two conditions to determine the outcome in each case: (1) sat + no iqamah = valid with karahah; (2) no sat + no iqamah = invalid; (3) no sat + iqamah = valid without karahah; (4) sat + iqamah = also valid without karahah.
The passage only explicitly mentions the first three, but the fourth is implicit.

Core Idea
Qasr requires leaving the built-up area, intending three days, and being an independent adult.

This passage from the chapter on traveler's prayer enumerates the precise conditions that must be met for qasr to be valid. It clarifies that not every traveler may shorten prayer—children, followers whose master has not intended travel, and those intending less than three days are excluded. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly applying the ruling in daily travel scenarios.

Identify the act and its ruling
The act is qasr (shortening four-rak'ah prayers to two). In the Hanafi school, qasr is an obligatory duty ('azima) for a traveler who meets specific conditions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A follower's travel/residence status follows the master's intention when known; ignorance allows the follower to act on his own.

In Hanafi fiqh, rulings on shortening (qasr) and completing (itmam) prayer depend on intention. For dependents (wives, slaves, soldiers), does their own intention matter? This passage clarifies that the intention of the master (asl) takes precedence, but only if the follower knows it. If unknown, the follower's previous ruling continues. Let's explore the rule and its Quranic proof.

5
Derive the principle
The follower must ascertain the master's intention; in ignorance, the previous ruling (travel or residence) applies until knowledge is obtained.
This balances authority with practicality.

Core Idea
Qasr is a determined obligation (azima) for the traveler, not a concession – omitting it invalidates the prayer unless separated by tashahhud.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that shortening prayer in travel is a determined obligation ('azima), not a concession (rukhsa). We will explore the textual evidence from the Quran and Sunnah, the reasoning from Kitab al-Asl, and the practical difference between the Hanafi view and that of the Shafi'i and Maliki schools. Understanding this dispute clarifies why Hanafis require a traveler who prays four rak'ahs to sit for tashahhud after two, else the prayer is invalid.

Ruling on Traveler Praying Four Rak'ahs: Hanafi Position and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Explains Hanafi ruling on traveler's four-rak'ah prayer and refutes opposing views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a traveler prays four rak'ahs, identify the condition for validity, and evaluate the Shafi'i/Maliki counter-argument.

Core Idea
If a traveler prays four rak'ahs without sitting after two, the prayer is invalid unless he sits for tashahhud.

This passage examines a key Hanafi ruling on travel prayer: shortening is obligatory, and completing four rak'ahs is only valid if the final tashahhud separates the fard from the extra nafila. It then engages with the Shafi'i/Maliki view that completion is permissible and shortening is merely a concession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its Hanafi ruling
What is the act? A traveler praying four rak'ahs. The Hanafi ruling: if he sits after two for tashahhud, prayer is valid; if not, it is invalid. Understand the principle: fard is two, extra two are nafila.
The separation by tashahhud is key.
2
Understand the condition for validity
The final tashahhud (qa'dah akhirah) is a rukn in the two-rak'ah fard. By performing it, the traveler completes his obligatory prayer. The additional two rak'ahs become separate supererogatory prayers.
If he intends residence at the third rak'ah, he becomes resident and four are permissible.
3
Examine the Hanafi dalil
The text cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'Allah prescribed for the traveler two rak'ahs.' Also the saying of Ibn Mas'ud that one must repeat if he prays four. Recognize that the Hanafi school relies on these as primary evidence.
The hadith is sahih and explicit.
4
Analyze the opposing school's proofs
Shafi'i and Maliki cite acts of Uthman completing at Mina, Aisha completing, and the Prophet's praise of Aisha. They argue that complete is permissible and shortening is a concession like not fasting.
These actions are later and subject to interpretation.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is implicit: the Prophet always shortened in travel; Uthman's completion was likely because he considered himself resident; Aisha's case may be interpreted as her not being a traveler (dwelling). The praise is for her intention, not the act itself.
The explicit ruling of the Prophet takes precedence over later practice.

When Does a Traveler Stop Shortening? – Residency and the 15-Day Rule
Ruling with proof on duration of qasr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions that end the traveler's concession (rukhsah) of shortening and explain the evidence from the Sunnah.

Core Idea
A traveler shortens until he enters his hometown or intends to stay 15 days — intention of residency ends the travel concession.

Many assume that traveling itself automatically permits shortening prayers indefinitely, but the Hanafi school sets clear boundaries for when the travel concession ends. This passage from Thamaratun Najah explains that shortening continues until you either return to your permanent hometown or you decide to stay in a place for half a month (15 days). The decisive factor is your intention regarding the duration of stay, not the actual number of days spent. Understanding this ruling is crucial for every Muslim traveler, especially those who travel frequently for work or study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is the shortening (qasr) of the four-rak'ah prayers. The ruling is that shortening is wajib (obligatory) for a traveler and remains so until one of two conditions is met.
In Hanafi fiqh, shortening is a confirmed sunnah that becomes obligatory when traveling beyond the distance of qasr (approximately 88 km).
2
State the two conditions that end shortening
The passage states that a traveler stops shortening when he either enters his hometown (watan asli) or intends to stay for fifteen days (half a month) in any city or village.
Note that 'entering hometown' is an objective fact; 'intending 15 days' is subjective and based on the traveler's intention.
3
Examine the primary dalil
The author cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas that the Prophet ﷺ stayed in Mecca during the Conquest for 15 days and shortened the prayer. This indicates that the 15-day stay is the threshold for becoming a resident.
The hadith is from Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi, classified as hasan. Its thubut (transmission) is zanni, meaning it is acceptable for legal proof in Hanafi usul.
4
Understand the consequence of indefinite stay
If the traveler intends less than 15 days or has no fixed intention (e.g., 'I'll leave tomorrow, maybe'), he remains a musafir even if he stays for years. Shortening continues.
This is based on the hadith of Anas that the Prophet stayed 10 days in Mecca during another trip and shortened.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If a traveler goes to a city for a project and intends 20 days, he must complete prayers from day one. If he intends 10 days, he shortens. If he is unsure, he shortens until he decides on 15 or more.
The key is the intention at the time of arrival; changing intention later has rules beyond this passage.

When Does the Traveler Stop Shortening Prayers?
Matn and commentary on condition for ending qasr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions that end a traveler's qasr and identify the Hanafi proofs for the 15-day residency rule.

Core Idea
Qasr ends by entering your hometown or intending to stay 15 days — nothing else.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling: the traveler must continue shortening four-rak'ah prayers until either returning to his home city or forming a firm intention to stay fifteen days in another place. The passage provides multiple proofs from the Sunnah and addresses a common objection. Understanding this boundary is essential for correctly observing the sunnah of travel.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions that end qasr
Read the matn: the traveler continues qasr until he enters his own city (misr) or intends to stay half a month (15 days) in any town or village. These are the only factors that change his legal status from traveller to resident.
Entering a city that is not his hometown does NOT end qasr.
2
Locate the primary proof
The text cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas: the Prophet stayed in Makkah for 15 days during the conquest and shortened throughout. This shows that a stay of less than 15 days does not establish residency, so qasr continues.
Hadith from Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi.
3
Support with Companion practice
The athar from Ibn Umar confirms the rule: when he resolved to stay 15 days, he completed prayers. This reinforces the 15-day threshold.
Multiple sources, including Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
4
Examine the objection and response
The author mentions the hadith of Aisha where the Prophet praised her for completing a prayer during travel. Some might argue this permits completing anytime. The author responds by clarifying that qasr is a concession (like not fasting), and the sunnah is to shorten; completing without the conditions is not the preferred practice.
Hanafis view qasr as sunnah muakkadah, and completing without valid reason is makruh tahrimi.
5
Apply the ruling
If a traveller is in a city with no intention to stay 15 days, or on the road, he must shorten. As soon as he enters his home city or forms the 15-day intention elsewhere, he must complete (pray full 4 rak'ahs).
The mufta bihi position: shortening is required; completing without the conditions is disapproved.

Qasr When Intending Less Than 15 Days or No Intention
Commentary and proofs for continued qasr with short or no stay intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasr when a traveler intends less than fifteen days or has no fixed intention, and explain the proofs from the Sunnah and Companions' practice.

Core Idea
A traveler continues to shorten prayers indefinitely unless he forms a firm resolve to stay fifteen days.

In the Hanafi school, the key factor in determining whether a traveler must shorten prayers is not the length of stay but the intention. Even a stay of months or years does not change the traveler's status if he has not formed a firm resolve to reside for fifteen days. This lesson examines the ruling and its proofs from the hadith and athar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the prayer of a traveler who intends less than fifteen days or has no fixed intention. The ruling is that he must shorten (qasr) the four-rak'ah prayers to two rak'ahs.
This is wajib in the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author cites the hadith of Anas ibn Malik: the Prophet stayed in Makkah for ten days (less than fifteen) and shortened prayers. This shows that a stay shorter than fifteen days does not end the travel status.
Hadith from Tirmidhi and Abu Dawud.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The operative part of the hadith is the Prophet's stay of ten days with qasr. By the Hanafi principle, the absence of a fifteen-day intention means travel continues. The ten days are less than fifteen, so the traveler remains a musafir.
The opposite case (fifteen-day intention) is inferred from athar.
4
Note the ikhtilaf with Shafi'is
Shafi'is hold that a stay of four days (or more) establishes residency. The Hanafi school disagrees, requiring both a stay of at least fifteen days and a firm intention (azm).
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
5
Evaluate the additional proofs
The author also provides athar from Ibn Umar (six months in Azerbaijan shortening) and the Companions (nine months in Ramhurmuz) to show that a long stay without firm intention does not break travel status.
These strengthen the Hanafi position.

Core Idea
A traveler following a resident imam must complete four rak'ahs; after time, following is invalid.

This passage addresses a common scenario in congregational prayer: a traveler (musafir) joining a resident (muqim) imam. The Hanafi ruling distinguishes between joining within the prayer's prescribed time and joining after the time has passed. The core principle: the follower must match the imam's number of rak'ahs. We will explore the ruling, its proof, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and conditions
The act is iqtida' (following an imam). Conditions: the imam is a resident (muqim) and the follower is a traveler (musafir). The time context matters: within the prayer's prescribed time (waqt) vs. outside waqt (qada).
The traveler normally prays four-rak'ah prayers as two, but here he must follow the imam.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
Within the prayer time, the traveler's following is sahih (valid) but he must complete four rak'ahs following the imam. After the time, his following is not sahih (invalid).
This applies only to four-rak'ah prayers (Dhuhr, 'Asr, 'Isha). Fajr and Maghrib are unaffected.
3
Examine the proof (dalil)
The proof is the practice of the Companion 'Abdullah ibn 'Umar (may Allah be pleased with him). He would pray four rak'ahs when behind a resident imam and two when alone.
This shows that a traveler must conform to the imam's number of rak'ahs. The principle: the follower cannot oppose the imam.
4
Apply the principle
The sabab (cause) of the prayer time remains, so the traveler's obligation to pray four is actualized when following a resident imam. After the time, the traveler's qada is only two rak'ahs, and following a resident would create a contradiction.
The follower's intention must match the imam's prayer.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) allow the traveler to shorten even behind a resident imam if he intends qasr. The Hanafi school insists on conformity to the imam.
This difference affects the validity of the prayer in various contexts.

Traveler Following a Resident Imam: The Role of Time
Ruling on traveler praying behind resident with two conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a traveler must pray four rak‘ahs behind a resident imam and apply the ruling to scenarios within and after the prayer time.

Core Idea
Time is the key: if prayer time remains, the traveler follows the imam and prays four; if not, following is invalid.

You already know that a traveler shortens prayers. But what happens when he joins a resident (muqim) imam? Does he still pray two? The Hanafi ruling distinguishes based on time. This passage explains why the time of prayer is the determining factor and provides proof from the practice of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the ruling: the traveler following a resident imam is valid only 'within the time'. After the time, it is not valid. Determine what 'time' refers to.
Time here means the established waqt for that prayer (e.g., zuhr time).
2
Understand the mechanism
The time is the sabab (cause) for the prayer to be performed. As long as the time remains, the traveler's two-rak‘ah obligation can be overridden by following the imam's four. After time, no conversion is possible.
3
Examine the proofs
Look at the athar from Ibn Umar: when he prayed with the imam (resident), he prayed four; when alone, two. Also the saying of Shu‘ba about missed prayers. These show the principle.
These are ahad reports, but relied upon in Hanafi school.
4
Apply the principle
Derive the ruling for a new scenario: if a traveler intentionally prays two behind a resident imam within time, what is the validity? The answer: it is invalid because he opposed the imam.

Commentary
This ruling addresses one direction of the imam-muqtadi relationship: a traveler (musafir) praying behind a resident (muqim) imam. The text distinguishes two scenarios based on time. If the prayer time is still remaining (i.e., the traveler starts following the resident before the time expires), it is valid and the traveler must pray the full four rak'ahs, because following the imam requires him to not oppose the imam's act. However, if the time has already expired (e.g., he remembers a missed prayer after its time or starts qada after the time), then he cannot follow the resident imam because his owed prayer is only two rak'ahs, and the imam will pray four – the traveler would have to add extra rak'ahs which is not permissible in that state. The proofs include the practice of Ibn Umar who always completed four when behind a resident imam, and the saying of Shu'ba that the number of rak'ahs for missed prayers is determined by the state when remembered, not when missed. The Hanafi principle derived: time (waqt) is the sabab (cause) that can convert the traveler's prayer from two to four; once time is gone, no conversion occurs.

وقت (waqt): The established time for a specific prayer (e.g., zuhr time).
اقتداء (iqtida'): Following an imam in congregational prayer.

Resident Praying Behind Traveler: Validity and Recitation Rule
Ruling on resident praying behind traveler and rule of non-recitation in remaining rak'ahs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a resident praying behind a traveler imam and explain why the resident does not recite in the remaining rak'ahs.

Core Idea
A resident's prayer behind a traveler imam is valid; the resident completes four rak'ahs without recitation after the imam's salam.

We often assume that the imam and follower must pray the same number of rak'ahs. But when a traveler (who shortens) leads residents who must pray full length, does this create a problem? This lesson clarifies that the Hanafi school affirms the validity of such a prayer, with specific rules about recitation and the imam's recommendation to announce his status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a resident (muqim) praying behind a traveler (musafir) imam. Identify the hukm: the prayer is sahih (valid) in all cases, whether performed within the prayer time or after it.
The ruling does not change based on time; consistency is key.
2
Understand the recommended practice for the imam
The imam is recommended (mustahabb) to announce before the prayer that he is a traveler, so the residents know to complete their prayer after his salam.
This is a practical measure to avoid confusion.
3
Examine the textual proof (dalil naqli)
Analyze the hadith of Imran ibn Husayn: the Prophet during the Conquest of Makkah prayed two rak'ahs and instructed residents to complete. This establishes the precedent for validity and the completion method.
The hadith is ahad but accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Analyze the ruling on recitation in remaining rak'ahs
The text states the resident should NOT recite in the remaining two rak'ahs after the imam's salam. Two reasons: (1) from tahrimah perspective, the resident is still a muqtadi; (2) obligatory recitation was already fulfilled in the first two rak'ahs.
This ruling is specific to Hanafi school; Shafi'i holds recitation is required.
5
Determine the practical application
In practice, a resident following a traveler should after the imam's salam stand, complete the remaining rak'ahs silently, and not recite any Quran.
The resident's prayer is valid even if he recites unintentionally, but the recommended practice is silence.

Watan al-Iqamah and Watan as-Sukna
Defining two types of residential status for the traveler

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definitions of Watan al-Iqamah and Watan as-Sukna and apply the 15-day threshold to determine when a traveler must perform complete prayer.

Core Idea
Fifteen days or more of intended stay makes a place Watan al-Iqamah, obligating complete prayer.

The prayer of a traveler (musafir) depends on the intention to stay in a place. The Hanafi school sets a clear threshold: if you intend to stay 15 days or more, you become a resident (muqim) and must pray complete (itmam). This lesson defines the two key terms: Watan al-Iqamah (residence requiring complete prayer) and Watan as-Sukna (short stay where qasr remains valid).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of watan
The text defines Watan al-Iqamah and mentions Watan as-Sukna. Recognize that Watan al-Iqamah is the established category; Watan as-Sukna is rejected by the muhaqqiqun.
2
State the condition for each
Watan al-Iqamah: intention to stay half a month (15 days) or more. Watan as-Sukna: intention to stay less than 15 days.
3
State the legal consequence
For Watan al-Iqamah, itmam (complete prayer) becomes wajib. For Watan as-Sukna, qasr (shortening) remains permitted because the verifying scholars do not recognize it as a valid residence.
4
Note the scope of the definition
The muhaqqiqun's view is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). The category of Watan asli (original homeland) is a separate permanent residence, which always requires itmam.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement defining two key terms for the traveler's prayer: Watan al-Iqamah and Watan as-Sukna. The author first states the rule (matn) that Watan al-Iqamah is a place where one intends to stay for half a month (15 days) or more. This triggers the obligation to perform complete prayer (itmam) rather than shortening (qasr). Then he notes that the verifying scholars (muhaqqiqun) do not recognize Watan as-Sukna as a separate category affecting the ruling; this is a place where one intends to stay less than half a month. The Urdu commentary clarifies that Watan as-Sukna does not necessitate complete prayer because the stay is shorter than 15 days, and references a hadith (not quoted) as evidence. The commentary also mentions Watan Asli (original homeland) as a permanent residence like the Prophet's stay in Madinah, which is a separate category. In Hanafi fiqh, the intention to stay 15 days or more in a place makes it a 'watan iqamah' requiring complete prayer, while less than 15 days allows one to continue as a traveler (musafir) and shorten prayers. This is a fundamental rule for the prayer of the traveler.
