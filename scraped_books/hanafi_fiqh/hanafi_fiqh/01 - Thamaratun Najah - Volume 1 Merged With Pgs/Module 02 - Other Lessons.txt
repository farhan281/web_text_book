Book's Distinctive Features and Methodology
Author introduces the unique features of the commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fifteen distinctive features of Thamaratun Najah and explain how each feature aids in understanding Hanafi fiqh rulings.

Core Idea
Thamaratun Najah presents each ruling with three hadith references, three explanations, and enumerates fard/wajib/sunnah for easy memorization.

Every good fiqh book has a methodology. The author of Thamaratun Najah devotes the preface to explaining exactly how the commentary is structured—and why. These features are not decorative; they are pedagogical tools that help students (and even teachers and muftis) learn and teach Hanafi fiqh with confidence. Let us walk through them systematically.

Guided Path
1
Group the features by purpose
The fifteen points fall into four categories: pedagogical (triple hadith, triple explanation, simple language), structural (enumeration of fard/wajib/sunnah, labeling sources), evidentiary (Quran/hadith references, Sihah Sittah, full citations), and practical (old/new weights, lexicology). Identify each category.
This grouping helps you remember the list.
2
Identify the Hanafi-specific pedagogical tools
The author explicitly counts fara'id, wajibat, and sunan. In Hanafi fiqh, the distinction between fard and wajib is critical (qat'i vs zanni evidence). Understand why the author emphasizes this separation: it shapes how rulings are taught and memorized.
Other schools may not separate these categories.
3
Analyze the evidence chain
Each ruling is supported by three hadith from the Sihah Sittah with full chapter and reference numbers. This shows the author's commitment to strong textual evidence. Note that the author also labels sayings of Companions and Successors distinctly.
This teaches the hierarchy of sources.
4
Appreciate the practical additions
The book includes both old and new weights (e.g., grams) and lexicological explanations. These additions make the book usable for modern application while preserving traditional knowledge.
5
Synthesize the overall teaching method
Combine the features: three explanations + three hadith + enumeration + simple language = a method designed to build understanding, memorization, and confidence. This is the Thamaratun Najah method.

Key Terms
فرائض (fara'id): Those acts that are obligatory (fard) with definitive evidence, which must be performed.
واجبات (wajibat): Obligatory acts according to the Hanafi school, but with less than definitive evidence; neglecting them without excuse incurs sin but denial is not disbelief.
سنن (sunan): Recommended acts (sunnah) that the Prophet consistently performed; performing them brings reward, neglecting them does not incur sin but is blameworthy.
صحاح ستہ (Sihah Sittah): The six most authoritative hadith collections: Bukhari, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Nasai, and Ibn Majah.

Hanafiyyah: The Collective Madhhab of Three Imams
Definition of Hanafiyyah and explanation of intra-school differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Hanafiyyah as the combined rulings of the three founding imams and explain the methodological basis for differences between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples.

Core Idea
Hanafiyyah is not solely Imam Abu Hanifa—it includes the opinions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad as essential pillars.

Many assume the Hanafi school is only the rulings of Imam Abu Hanifa. However, as this passage clarifies, the school formally encompasses the views of his two senior students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this collective nature is crucial for navigating Hanafi texts, where differences among the three are recorded and each has authority. Moreover, the methodological contrast—Imam's caution (ihtiyat) versus the Sahibayn's adherence to hadith—shapes the school's internal diversity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Locate where the passage defines Hanafiyyah. It states that Hanafiyyah is the collection of the madhhabs of three imams, not just Imam Abu Hanifa.
The term 'Hanafiyyah' is deliberately inclusive.
2
Note the implication for fatwa
The passage says that acting on any of the three imams' rulings is considered following Hanafiyyah. A fatwa based on Abu Yusuf or Muhammad is still valid within the school.
This flexibility is important for muftis in cases of need.
3
Understand the methodological distinction
Imam Abu Hanifa preferred caution (ihtiyat), even if it meant relying on weaker proofs like qawl sahabi. The Sahibayn sometimes preferred authentic hadith over caution.
Context: Fiqh was not yet codified; Imam Abu Hanifa's cautious approach protected against error.
4
Appreciate the practical consequence
The Deobandi school generally followed the cautious approach, leading to criticism that Hanafis contradict hadith. However, the Sahibayn's positions have strong hadith support, and they are part of the same school.
Thus, the Hanafi school can accommodate both caution and hadith reliance.

Hanafiyyah is the name of the collection of three Imams. My respected teacher used to say that Hanafiyyah is not only the name of the madhhab of Imam Abu Hanifa, but it is the name of the collection of the madhhabs of Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on them). If one acts according to the madhhab of any one of them, it will be considered as acting according to the madhhab of the Hanafiyyah. And if one gives a fatwa according to the madhhab of Imam Muhammad or Imam Abu Yusuf, it will not be considered outside the madhhab of Hanafiyyah. This is why in important Hanafi books like Al-Quduri and Al-Hidayah, the madhhabs of both these Imams are recorded, and in times of need, fatwa is given according to them. The madhhab of Imam Abu Hanifa is based on caution. Imam Abu Hanifa was a very pious and God-fearing man. Therefore, he always gave fatwa with caution and adopted that madhhab. The second point is that until that time, fiqh had not been codified. Imam Abu Hanifa is the first Imam who codified fiqh and usul al-fiqh. Therefore, if he had adopted a side other than caution, everyone would have pointed fingers. So he adopted the cautious madhhab, even if it meant following the fatwa of a Tabi'i. But his brilliant disciples Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, in the light of hadith, sometimes adopted a different madhhab, and with an open heart recorded the madhhab with evidence. Now the viewers have the choice: adopt the madhhab of Imam al-A'zam or adopt the madhhab of his brilliant disciples. In both cases, the virtue goes to Imam al-A'zam. In the last century, the credit for highlighting and disseminating the madhhab of Imam al-A'zam goes to the Deobandi school of thought. They also adopted the cautious approach and generally gave fatwa with caution like Imam al-A'zam. Therefore, some viewers raised an objection and said that the madhhab of the Hanafis is different from the hadiths. But perhaps they did not reflect that in the issues where they do not find hadiths, there the madhhab of the two important pillars of the Hanafis differs from Imam al-A'zam. And for their adopted madhhab, there are one hundred percent authentic hadiths. It is another matter that on such occasions, the madhhab of the Sahibayn concurs with Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik. In this book 'Thamarat al-Najah', you will see time and again that wherever the Sahibayn differed from Imam al-A'zam, there Imam al-A'zam has the saying of a Sahabi or the fatwa of a Tabi'i, and the Sahibayn have hadiths. But the madhhab of Imam al-A'zam is based on caution. (1) My humble opinion is that those who object only considered the madhhab of Imam al-A'zam, and due to not finding hadiths in some places, they strengthened their objection against the entire Hanafiyyah. They did not pay attention to the differences of his brilliant disciples Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad and their strong hadiths, and did not consider that they too are two important pillars of Hanafiyyah, and the propagation of all issues is indebted to their pens.

Commentary
This passage defines the Hanafi school as the combined teachings of Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad. It clarifies that following any of the three imams is considered part of the Hanafi madhhab. The author then explains that Imam Abu Hanifa’s methodology was based on caution (ihtiyat), as he codified fiqh and usul al-fiqh without a precedent. His two disciples occasionally differed in the light of hadith. The Deobandi school later upheld this cautious approach. The passage addresses a common criticism that Hanafis contradict hadith, explaining that where Imam Abu Hanifa relied on the sayings of Companions or Tabi‘un, the Sahibayn had authentic hadith supporting their positions. Thus, the Hanafi school is not monolithic; it accommodates multiple valid positions within its framework.

Key Terms
حنفیت (Hanafiyyah): The Hanafi school of thought, encompassing the rulings of the three founding imams.
صاحبین (Sahibayn): The two companions of Imam Abu Hanifa: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
احتیاط (ihtiyat): Legal caution; preferring the more cautious ruling that avoids potential sin.

Obligation to Seek Water Before Tayammum
Ruling with proof on seeking water before tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which seeking water before tayammum is wajib and explain the Hanafi proof from the hadith of Ibn Umar.

Core Idea
Seeking water is wajib only up to 400 steps, provided safety and reasonable hope of finding it.

Before resorting to tayammum, a Muslim must ascertain that water is truly unavailable. But what counts as 'unavailable'? Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the end time of Maghrib prayer. The author presents the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position first: Maghrib time ends with the disappearance of the red twilight (shafaq ahmar). This is the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and the majority of scholars. The author then presents the evidence for both sides, starting with Imam Abu Hanifa's view that Maghrib time extends until the white twilight (shafaq abyad) disappears.

Imam Abu Hanifa's argument is based on four points:
1. Analogy (qiyas) with Fajr: Just as the widespread whiteness (bayad mustatir) marks the beginning of Fajr, similarly the widespread whiteness after sunset should mark the end of Maghrib, because both are similar in appearance.
2. Hadith of Abu Mas'ud: The Prophet (peace be upon him) would pray Isha when the horizon became completely black, implying that complete blackness occurs only after the white twilight disappears, so Maghrib continues until then.
3. Athar of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz: He instructed that Isha prayer be performed when the whiteness of the horizon disappears, supporting the idea that Maghrib remains until that whiteness is gone.
4. Quranic verse (17:78): 'Establish prayer at the decline of the sun until the darkness of the night' – the phrase 'darkness of the night' (ghasaq al-layl) indicates complete darkness, which occurs after the white twilight, so Isha begins then.

The Sahibayn's evidence:
1. Hadith of Ibn Umar: The Prophet (peace be upon him) said, 'The twilight is the redness; when it disappears, prayer becomes obligatory.' This directly indicates that the red twilight marks the end of Maghrib.
2. Athar from Ibn Umar: He stated that twilight is the redness.

The author then explains the physical phenomenon: after sunset, red twilight appears first due to light passing through vapor. Then, when the sun is about 12 degrees below the horizon, a faint white light (bayad mustatir) appears, which later becomes elongated (bayad mustatil). Imam Abu Hanifa considered Maghrib time to last until the end of the spread-out white light, while the Sahibayn considered the end to be at the disappearance of the red light. The mufta bihi is the view of the Sahibayn, which is also the position of the majority of scholars. This dispute has practical implications: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, Isha time begins later than according to the Sahibayn. The passage also notes that praying Maghrib at its earliest time is Sunnah, as Jibril (peace be upon him) prayed Maghrib at the same time on both days of the Imamate hadith.

Key Terms
الشفق الأحمر (ash-shafaq al-ahmar): The red twilight that appears after sunset; according to the Sahibayn, Maghrib time ends with its disappearance.
الشفق الأبيض (ash-shafaq al-abyad): The white twilight that appears after the red twilight; according to Imam Abu Hanifa, Maghrib time continues until its disappearance.
البياض المستطير (al-bayad al-mustatir): The spread-out white light after the red twilight; Imam Abu Hanifa considered this the end of Maghrib.

Imamate is Superior to Adhan
Opening the chapter on imamate with a comparative ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that imamate is more virtuous than adhan and cite the prophetic practice as evidence.

Core Idea
Leading prayer is more virtuous than calling to prayer.

This lesson opens the chapter on imamate (leading congregational prayer) with a clear comparative ruling: imamate is superior to adhan. The author provides a simple but powerful evidence from the Prophet's own practice: he led the prayer himself while appointing Bilal to give the adhan. In the Hanafi school, this establishes imamate as the more virtuous role, and it sets the tone for the detailed rules of imamate that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the first line of the source: 'Imamate is superior to adhan.' This is the core ruling of the lesson. Recognize that it is a comparative fadl (virtue) statement.
The Arabic uses the feminine 'hiya' referring to imamah.
2
Locate the Evidence
The Urdu commentary explains that the Prophet himself led prayers (imam) while Bilal was the mu'adhdhin. This shows the higher rank of imamate.
This is a practical evidence from the Prophet's sunnah.
3
Understand the Hanafi Position
In the Hanafi school, imamate is a sunnah mu'akkadah for qualified men, and it is considered more virtuous than adhan. Both are commendable, but leading prayer carries greater reward.
This position is part of the zahir al-riwayah.
4
Apply to Practice
When a qualified person has the choice to be an imam or a mu'adhdhin, he should prefer to be the imam to attain greater virtue.
This is a practical application of the ruling.

The Travel Distance for Qasr: 48 Shar'i Miles
Defines travel distance for qasr using Companions' practice and unit conversions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi travel distance for qasr in shar'i miles and modern units, and explain the derivation from the Companions' practice.

Core Idea
Qasr becomes wajib at 48 shar'i miles – a measure fixed by the Companions, not opinion.

In this lesson, we examine the distance that triggers the obligation to shorten prayer (qasr) according to the Hanafi school. The ruling is based on the practice of Ibn Umar and Ibn Abbas, who considered four barid (16 farsakh) as the travel distance. We will see how this converts to 48 shar'i miles and to modern units, and why this differs from the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
State the Hanafi position: qasr is wajib when the travel distance reaches 48 shar'i miles. This is not based on personal opinion but on the practice of the Companions.
The ruling is wajib, not fard, because the proof is from ahad hadith and the Companions' consensus.
2
Examine the dalil
The main proof is the athar of Ibn Abbas: 'Ibn Umar and Ibn Abbas used to shorten prayer and break fast at 4 barid, which is 16 farsakh.' (Bukhari, Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba). This shows the Companions fixed the distance.
The authority of the Companions in such matters is considered strong evidence (dalil).
3
Understand the unit conversions
1 barid = 4 farsakh, 1 farsakh = 3 shar'i miles. Therefore 4 barid = 16 farsakh = 48 shar'i miles. The shar'i mile is defined as 4000 cubits (2000 yards).
These conversions are taken from the text of Durr al-Mukhtar and Radd al-Muhtar.
4
Convert to modern units
Since 1 shar'i mile = 2000 yards, and 1 English mile = 1760 yards, 48 shar'i miles = 48 × (2000/1760) ≈ 54.545 English miles ≈ 87.782 km.
Modern units are not directly used; the shar'i mile is the standard.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
The Shafi'i school uses a different barid calculation, resulting in a shorter distance (about 81 km). This difference stems from how the barid was historically defined.
Both views are legitimate ijitihad.

Chapter of the prayer of the traveler. [2] The moderate pace of a man walking is considered; he generally walks 6 miles in one day, and in three days the distance becomes 48 miles. [3] If traveling in mountains, it will be seen how much a camel, man, or donkey can walk in one day; that will be considered. [4] If traveling by sea, it will be seen how much a sailing ship travels in one day with favorable wind; and in three days how much the ship travels; on that many miles one becomes a traveler. Ships run by petrol, steamships are not considered. [5] In that time, airplanes or motor cars were not common, so their ruling is not mentioned.
Calculation of farsakh, mile, and kilometer. In earlier times among Arabs, barid, farsakh, and ghalwa were prevalent; later the shar'i mile came, and now the English mile and kilometer are prevalent worldwide. So their detail is as follows.
One barid is 4 farsakh, and shortening of prayer is at 4 barid, so it is at 16 farsakh. One farsakh is 3 shar'i miles, so multiply 16 by 3 to get 48 shar'i miles for qasr. One barid being 4 farsakh is mentioned in the saying of Abdullah ibn Abbas: 'Ibn Umar and Ibn Abbas used to shorten prayer and break fast at 4 barid, which is 16 farsakh' (Bukhari, Chapter on how much to shorten prayer, p. 147, no. 1086). This athar states that 4 barid was 16 farsakh, i.e., one barid is 4 farsakh. And at 4 barid (16 farsakh) Abdullah ibn Umar and Abdullah ibn Abbas applied the ruling of travel. (2) There is also this athar: 'Ata ibn Abi Rabah said: I said to Ibn Abbas: Should I shorten prayer going to Arafa? He said: No. I said: To Marr? He said: No. I said: To Taif and Usfan? He said: Yes, and that is 48 miles, and he gestured with his hand.' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, Chapter 734 - On what distance to shorten prayer, vol. 2, p. 204, no. 8138). This athar states that at 48 miles one becomes a traveler. (3) In the footnote of Mawsua Imam Shafi'i it says that one barid is 22,176 meters, and also that shortening occurs in a travel of 81 km. (Mawsua Imam Shafi'i, Chapter on travel in which prayer is shortened without fear, vol. 2, p. 19, no. 1895). This is the same calculation being presented ahead.
And one farsakh is three miles. And one shar'i mile is 4,000 cubits (dhira'). This means the shar'i mile is 2,000 yards. Its proof is this text from Durr al-Mukhtar: 'Al-farsakh: three miles, and al-mile: four thousand cubits' (Radd al-Muhtar ala Durr al-Mukhtar, Bab Salat al-Musafir, vol. 2, p. 725). From this text it is known that a farsakh is three miles, and one shar'i mile is 4,000 cubits. And two cubits make one yard; by this calculation, one shar'i mile is 2,000 yards. Now multiply 16 farsakh by 3 miles to get 48 shar'i miles.
Difference between shar'i mile, English mile, and kilometer. The English mile is 1.13636 smaller than the shar'i mile, because the English mile is 1,760 yards, and the shar'i mile is 2,000 yards. The kilometer is 1.828 smaller than the shar'i mile, because the kilometer is only 1,093.61 yards. And the kilometer is 1.6092 percent smaller than the English mile.
By this calculation, 48 shar'i miles become 54.545 English miles and 87.782 kilometers. At this distance the ruling of qasr will be applied.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the travel distance that triggers the ruling of qasr (shortening of prayer) for a traveler in the Hanafi school. The author explains that the distance is based on a three-day journey at a moderate pace, which historically was estimated at 48 shar'i miles. He then provides the conversion between ancient units (barid, farsakh, mile) and modern units (English mile, kilometer). The proofs cited include athar (traditions) from Ibn Abbas and Ibn Umar, as well as the text of Durr al-Mukhtar. The author clarifies that the shar'i mile is 4000 cubits (or 2000 yards), which differs from the English mile (1760 yards). Consequently, the Hanafi distance for qasr (48 shar'i miles) equals approximately 54.5 English miles or 87.8 kilometers. The passage also notes that motorized vehicles (petrol boats, airplanes, cars) were not considered in classical texts, but the principle remains that the travel distance is measured by the moderate pace of a day's journey. The author emphasizes that the ruling is based on the shar'i mile, not the modern mile or kilometer, and provides the mathematical conversions to ensure correct application.

Key Terms
ميل شرعي (mil shar'i): A unit of distance equal to 4000 cubits (dhira') or 2000 yards, used in Hanafi fiqh to determine the travel distance for qasr.
برد (barid): A unit of distance equal to 4 farsakh (12 shar'i miles). Four barid is the standard journey distance for qasr.
فرسخ (farsakh): A unit of distance equal to 3 shar'i miles (12,000 cubits). Sixteen farsakh equal 48 shar'i miles.
قصر (qasr): The shortening of four-rakah prayers (Zuhr, Asr, Isha) to two rak'ahs during travel. Obligatory for the Hanafis when the travel distance is met.
