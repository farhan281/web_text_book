Purification of Dead Animal Skin by Tanning
Ruling with proof on tanning purification of dead animal skin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tanning dead animal skin, distinguish between real and legal tanning, and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
Tanning purifies dead animal skin — except pig and human — whether by astringents, soil, or sun.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position that tanning removes the impurity from the skin of a dead animal, making it permissible for use. The ruling is supported by clear hadith and the practice of the Companions. We will explore the two types of tanning recognized by the school and the exceptional cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is tanning the skin of a dead animal. The ruling is that it becomes pure (tahir).
2
Distinguish the two methods
Real tanning uses astringent substances like qaraz (gum arabic leaves). Legal tanning uses soil (tatrib) or sun (tashmis). Both remove moisture and purify.
Legal tanning is sufficient, though it is considered a lesser form.
3
Note the exceptions
Pig skin is not purified because the pig is intrinsically impure (najis al-'ayn). Human skin is also excepted due to human dignity.
4
Examine the proofs
The primary hadith is: 'اذا دبع الاهاب فقد طهر' (When the hide is tanned, it becomes purified). The athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i states that anything preventing decay is tanning.
These proofs establish the general principle.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof on the purification of dead animal skin through tanning. The matn states that the skin becomes pure by two types of tanning: real (using astringent substances like qaraz) and legal (like rubbing with soil or drying in the sun). The exception is pig skin, because the pig is intrinsically impure (najis al-'ayn) as per Quran 6:145, and human skin, due to human dignity as per Quran 17:70. The Urdu commentary explains that the impurity of the dead animal's skin is due to the unclean fluids; tanning removes these fluids, leaving only the pure hide. It likens this to the removal of blood through slaughter. Two methods are detailed: using gum arabic leaves (real tanning) and using soil or sun (legal tanning). The commentary then provides hadith proofs: the well-known hadith from Ibn Abbas (reported in Muslim and Abu Dawud) that tanning purifies the hide; the hadith from Aisha (in Abu Dawud) that the Prophet permitted using tanned hides of dead animals; and a hadith from Aisha (in Darqutni and Bayhaqi) specifying that tanning can be done with soil, ash, salt, or anything that improves the hide. Finally, an athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, narrated by Imam Muhammad in his Kitab al-Athar, states that anything which prevents decay is considered tanning. Imam Muhammad says this is the view of Abu Hanifa and the adopted position. The passage concludes with linguistic notes: qaraz is gum arabic leaf, tatrib is derived from soil, tashmis from sun.

Key Terms
دباغة (dibaghah): The process of treating animal skin to remove impurities and make it usable; can be real (with astringents) or legal (by soil/sun).
جلد الميتة (jild al-maytah): The skin of an animal that died without proper Islamic slaughter.
قرظ (qaraz): Leaves of the gum arabic tree (Acacia) used as a tanning agent.
تتريب (tatrib): Rubbing the skin with soil to absorb moisture and achieve tanning.
تشميس (tashmis): Drying the skin in the sun to remove moisture and achieve tanning.

Purification of Non-Edible Animal Skin by Slaughter
Ruling that sharia slaughter purifies the skin of non-edible animals but not the meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of sharia slaughter on the skin and meat of non-edible animals, identify the proofs, and distinguish between the mufta bihi and minority views.

Core Idea
Sharia slaughter purifies only the skin of non-edible animals, not the meat.

This lesson examines a Hanafi ruling that addresses a practical concern: if a predator is slaughtered Islamically, does its skin become pure for use, and can its meat be treated as pure? The text clarifies that while the skin is purified, the meat remains impure and cannot be eaten or carried during prayer. We explore the textual evidence and reasoning behind the mufta bihi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is sharia slaughter (dhakah) of a non-edible animal. The ruling: the skin becomes pure, the meat remains impure.
Note that 'non-edible' means animals like lions, leopards, etc.
2
Locate the proofs
The text provides two proofs: the hadith on tanning (which only mentions skin) and the impurity of saliva (which originates from meat).
3
Examine the wajh al-dalalah
How does the saliva proof work? Saliva is impure and produced from meat, so the meat must be inherently impure; slaughter does not remove that impurity.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
A minority view holds that the meat also becomes pure because slaughter removes impure blood.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon view is that only skin is pure, based on the stronger textual evidence.
The text says 'on the most correct view' (على اصح ما يفتى به).

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof on the effect of sharia slaughter on the skin and meat of non-edible animals (like lions, leopards, etc.). The matn states that sharia slaughter purifies only the skin, not the meat, and this is the most correct view (mufta bihi). The Urdu commentary explains: animals whose meat is not halal, when properly slaughtered, their skin becomes pure (usable for prayer mats, etc.), but their meat remains impure (najis) and cannot be eaten. There is a disagreement: a minority view says the meat also becomes pure (though not halal to eat), allowing it to be carried in prayer. The proofs for the majority view: (1) The hadith about tanning only mentions the hide, so slaughter similarly only purifies the hide. (2) The saliva of non-edible animals is impure, and saliva is produced from the meat, indicating that the meat retains impurity. The opposing view argues that slaughter removes the impure blood, so the meat becomes pure like the skin. The text notes that the mufta bihi view is that only the skin is purified, based on the stronger evidence.

Purity of Non-Vascular Parts After Death
Ruling on purity of non-vascular parts after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that solid, non-vascular parts of a dead animal remain pure, and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) based on blood flow.

Core Idea
Where blood does not flow, death does not impart impurity.

This ruling from the matn clarifies a practical point of taharah: not everything from a dead animal is najis. The key distinction is whether blood penetrates the part. In this lesson, we examine the scope of this ruling and its underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is determining the purity status of parts of a dead animal. The ruling is that parts in which blood does not flow remain pure (tahir).
This is a ruling about the state of objects, not an action.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): impurity is caused by blood and moisture; where they do not penetrate, no impurity occurs.
The Hanafi school considers this qati (certain) in both transmission and meaning.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The link between the cause (blood/moisture) and the ruling (impurity) is that najasa is transmitted through liquids. Solid, non-vascular parts do not absorb these liquids, so they remain pure.
This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that purity is the default state.
4
Note the examples
The text explicitly mentions hair, cut feathers, horns, hooves, and dry bones. All are parts without blood flow.
The phrase 'cut feathers' indicates attached feathers may be impure if they contain blood.
5
Identify the exception
Bones with fat or marrow become impure because the fat/marrow is considered moisture. However, if the fat is removed, the bone becomes pure again.
This shows the rule is based on the presence of moisture, not the bone itself.

Commentary
This is a ruling that clarifies which parts of a dead animal are not affected by its impurity. The matn states that anything in which blood does not flow — such as hair, cut feathers, horns, hooves, and dry bones — does not become impure (najis) by the animal's death. The Urdu commentary explains the underlying principle: impurity (najasa) is caused by blood and impure fluids. Therefore, parts that do not absorb these fluids remain pure. Examples: hair, cut feathers (as opposed to attached feathers which may have blood), horns, hooves, and bones without fat or marrow. However, bones with fat or marrow become impure because the fat/marrow is impure, but if the fat is removed, the bone becomes pure again. This ruling is based on a rational proof (istihsan/qiyas) that impurity is transmitted through liquids that seep into parts; solid parts without blood remain pure.

Tanning and Slaughter: Purification of Hides
Rulings on tanning exceptions and slaughter effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on tanning pig and human skin, and explain the effect of legal slaughter on the hide and meat of non-edible animals, citing the relevant proofs.

Core Idea
Pig skin is purified by nothing; human skin is honored; slaughter purifies hide, not meat, of non-edible animals.

The rules of tanning (dibaghah) affect what we can use from animals. In this lesson, we focus on two key exceptions: pig and human skin, which remain impure despite tanning. We also examine how legal slaughter transforms the hide of non-edible animals but leaves their meat impure according to the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two exceptions to tanning purification
Note that pig skin is not purified (najis al-ayn) and human skin is not purified due to human dignity.
These are the only two hides that tanning cannot purify in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah is an expansion on the rules of tanning (dibaghah) and related purity issues. It opens with five numbered rulings: (287) The skin of pigs and humans are not purified by tanning—pigs because they are najis al-ayn (inherently impure), and humans due to their honor. (288) When a non-edible animal (like a lion or leopard) is slaughtered according to shariah, its hide becomes pure to use, but according to the relied-upon fatwa, its meat remains impure and not permissible to eat. There is an opposing view that the meat also becomes pure, but the author sides with impurity. (289) Things in which blood does not flow—like wool, feathers, horn, and dry bones—do not become impure by death; they remain pure. However, if a bone has fat (marrow), it is impure unless the fat is removed. (290) The ruling on nerves (muscles/tendons) is disputed: some hadith imply purity, others impurity. The correct madhhab view is that they are impure, supported by the hadith forbidding benefit from the nerve of a dead animal. (291) Musk pod and civet are pure and lawful to eat and use in fragrance because they do not contain flowing blood or impure moisture. The passage connects each ruling to Quranic verses (for pig and human) and multiple ahadith (for hide purity, nerves, etc.), demonstrating the source-based reasoning of the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
دباغة (dibaghah): The process of tanning animal hides to purify them for use; in Hanafi fiqh, it renders the hide pure except for pig and human skin.
ذكاة شرعية (dhakaat shar'iyyah): Legal slaughter performed according to Islamic law, which includes cutting the throat and allowing blood to flow, thereby purifying the hide of non-edible animals but not the meat.
نجس العين (najis al-ayn): Inherently impure substance that cannot be purified by any means, such as pig and its parts.

Things Not Impure by Death: Wool, Feathers, Horn, and Bones
Rulings on parts of dead animals that remain pure and the dispute on nerves.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which animal parts do not become impure upon the animal's death, and explain the ruling on nerves (muscles/tendons) according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Blood-infused parts become impure; dry parts like wool, horn, and dry bones remain pure; nerves are impure in the correct view.

When an animal dies without being slaughtered, not everything becomes impure. The Hanafi school distinguishes parts that contain flowing blood from those that do not. In this lesson, we cover the general rule and the specific case of nerves, which has a difference of opinion within the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Learn the general rule
Anything in which blood does not flow (does not become saturated with blood) does not become impure by death.
Examples: wool, feathers, horn, hooves, and bones without fatty marrow.
2
Exception for fatty bones
If a bone contains fat (marrow), it is impure because the fat (which is impure) penetrates it. The bone becomes pure once the fat is removed.
Pure bones can be used, e.g., for tools.
3
Identify the evidence
The evidence includes multiple ahadith: e.g., the hadith of Ibn Abbas that all parts of a carcass except meat are halal and pure; the hadith of Um Salamah that wool, hair, and horns are pure after washing.
These ahadith establish that parts without blood flow remain pure.
4
Examine the ruling on nerves
Nerves (muscles/tendons) are disputed: some scholars, citing the hadith about buying a necklace of 'asab' for Fatimah, say nerves are pure. Others, citing a hadith forbidding benefit from the nerve of a carcass, say they are impure.
The correct Hanafi view is that nerves are impure (fi al-sahih).
5
Understand the tarjih
The hadith of Abdullah ibn Ukaym forbidding use of nerve is considered stronger or more specific, leading to the ruling of impurity.
The opposing hadith (Fatimah's necklace) is interpreted as before the prohibition or as referring to a different type of 'asab.

Commentary
This passage from Thamaratun Najah is an expansion on the rules of tanning (dibaghah) and related purity issues. It opens with five numbered rulings: (287) The skin of pigs and humans are not purified by tanning—pigs because they are najis al-ayn (inherently impure), and humans due to their honor. (288) When a non-edible animal (like a lion or leopard) is slaughtered according to shariah, its hide becomes pure to use, but according to the relied-upon fatwa, its meat remains impure and not permissible to eat. There is an opposing view that the meat also becomes pure, but the author sides with impurity. (289) Things in which blood does not flow—like wool, feathers, horn, and dry bones—do not become impure by death; they remain pure. However, if a bone has fat (marrow), it is impure unless the fat is removed. (290) The ruling on nerves (muscles/tendons) is disputed: some hadith imply purity, others impurity. The correct madhhab view is that they are impure, supported by the hadith forbidding benefit from the nerve of a dead animal. (291) Musk pod and civet are pure and lawful to eat and use in fragrance because they do not contain flowing blood or impure moisture. The passage connects each ruling to Quranic verses (for pig and human) and multiple ahadith (for hide purity, nerves, etc.), demonstrating the source-based reasoning of the Hanafi school.

Musk and Civet: Pure Fragrances for Worship
Ruling on purity of musk and civet.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the purity of musk and civet, and explain why they are considered pure and permissible to use in prayer.

Core Idea
Musk and civet are pure because they contain no flowing blood or impure moisture; eating musk is halal.

Natural substances used in perfumes, like musk and civet, are often questioned regarding purity. The Hanafi school rules them pure, as they do not come from flowing blood or impure secretions. This lesson clarifies their status and permissibility in worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the substances
Musk pod (nafajah) from musk deer and civet (zabad) from civet cat.
They are animal secretions but not blood or impure fluids.
2
Understand the basis for purity
They are pure because they do not involve flowing blood (dam masfuh) or any impure moisture. They are secretions that do not carry ritual impurity.
Contrast with blood or urine which are impure.
3
Learn the permissibility of eating musk
Musk can be eaten (e.g., in food or medicine) because it is a pure substance.
Civet is also pure but typically used externally.
4
Apply to worship
It is permissible to pray while wearing fragrances containing musk or civet. The fragrance itself is pure and does not nullify prayer.
This contrasts with alcohol-based perfumes in some opinions.

The Impurity of Sinew (al-Asab) from a Dead Animal
Explains the impurity of sinew with conflicting hadiths and the preferred view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the sinew of a dead animal, identify the primary dalil from the hadith of Abdullah ibn Ukaym, and explain how the prohibition indicates impurity.

Core Idea
The sinew of a dead animal is impure because the Prophet forbade benefiting from it.

The chapter on tanning addresses the purity of various parts of a dead animal. While most parts that do not contain blood are pure, the sinew (al-asab) is an exception. This lesson examines the evidence and the correct Hanafi position that sinew is impure, resolving the apparent conflict between different hadiths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed item and the two conflicting views
The sinew of a dead animal is contested: some scholars consider it pure because it lacks blood and a hadith suggests the Prophet ordered a sinew necklace for Fatima. Others consider it impure based on an explicit prohibition.
The disagreement is within the Hanafi school itself.
2
Examine the proof for purity
The argument for purity relies on the hadith of Fatima (the Prophet allowed a sinew necklace) and the principle that parts without blood do not become impure by death.
This view is held by some early Hanafis.
3
Examine the proof for impurity
The primary evidence for impurity is the hadith of Abdullah ibn Ukaym: the Prophet's letter prohibited benefiting from the hide and sinew of a dead animal. Prohibition indicates impurity because benefiting from pure things is generally permissible.
This hadith is found in Sunan Abi Dawud and Sunan al-Nasa'i.
4
Understand the Hanafi tarjih
The correct school (sahih madhhab) holds sinew as impure. The explicit prohibition overrides the other evidence because it is a direct command from the Prophet, and the ruling is confirmed by the majority of Hanafi authorities.
Thus, the mufta bihi position is impurity: sinew is najis and cannot be used.

In this lesson, we examine a fundamental condition for the validity of salah: the purity of the place where the feet, hands, knees, and forehead rest during prostration. Chapter: On the impure garment. (372) Prayer is permissible on felt whose upper surface is pure and lower impure; and on a pure garment whose lining is impure if not stitched; and on a pure edge even if the impure edge moves with its motion according to the correct narration. Explanation: These four issues are based on three principles: (1) If there is general need, the Shari'ah provides ease. (2) If the item is thick so that impurity does not touch. (3) If one does not carry the impurity. (373) If one end of a turban becomes impure, he folds it and keeps the pure part on his head, and the impure part does not move with his motion, his prayer is valid; if it moves, it is not valid. (374) One who lacks means to remove impurity prays with it and need not repeat. Similarly, one who finds nothing to cover his nakedness prays naked and need not repeat.

Commentary
This passage is a Fiqh ruling with proof. It opens with three sub-rulings concerning prayer on garments or surfaces that are partially impure. The author then explains three principles underlying these rulings: general need (umum balwa), thickness preventing transfer of impurity, and not being a carrier of impurity. The first sub-ruling permits prayer on a thick felt rug with a pure top and impure bottom because the impurity does not reach the worshipper and he does not carry it. The second permits prayer on a clean cloth placed over an impure cloth if they are not sewn together, as the clean cloth is not a carrier. The third permits prayer on a clean edge of a mat or carpet even if the impure edge moves, as the ruling follows the place of prostration. The author notes no explicit hadith for the third, but uses a hadith about praying in sheep resting places (despite impurity) to show that nearby impurity does not invalidate prayer. The next ruling (373) concerns a turban: if one end is impure and placed down so it does not move, prayer is valid; otherwise not, as the good becomes a carrier. The final ruling (374) covers extreme necessity: if no water to remove impurity, one prays as is and does not repeat; analogously, if no clothing, one prays naked. Proofs include the principle that Allah does not burden beyond capacity, and the verse of tayammum (5:6) which shows that when water is unavailable, purification is waived.

Key Terms
لِبَد (libad): Felt or thick fabric, often used as a rug.
بطانة (bitanah): Lining or inner layer of a garment.
مضرب (madrib): Stitched or attached; derived from ضرب (to strike, to sew).

Prayer with Impure Turban and When Unable to Remove Impurity
Two rulings: turban with partial impurity and prayer in unavoidable impurity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of 'not carrying impurity' to the case of a turban with an impure end and state the ruling for one who has no means to remove impurity.

Core Idea
If the impure part of a turban does not move with the head, prayer is valid; otherwise, not.

This lesson covers two additional rulings from the same chapter: the turban with one impure end and the extreme case of inability to remove impurity. These rulings further illustrate the Hanafi principle that one must not be a carrier of impurity, and that necessity (umum balwa) can ease requirements. We will also see the proof from the Qur'anic verse on tayammum.

Guided Path
1
State the turban ruling
If one end of the turban becomes impure, fold it and place the pure part on the head. If the impure end hangs down and does not move with the head's motion, prayer is valid.
Ruling (373).
2
Explain the condition for invalidity
If the impure end moves with the head's motion, the prayer is invalid because the worshipper becomes a carrier of impurity.
Movement implies the impurity is attached to the body.
3
Identify the ruling for inability to remove impurity
If one has no means to remove impurity (e.g., no water), he prays with the impurity and does not need to repeat the prayer.
Ruling (374) – includes also the case of no clothing.
4
Understand the proof from the Qur'an
The verse of tayammum (5:6) shows that when water is unavailable, purification is waived. Similarly, when unable to remove impurity, the prayer is valid without repetition.
Analogy: Allah does not burden beyond capacity.
5
Compare with the earlier rulings
Both rulings rely on the principle of necessity (umum balwa) and not being a carrier. The turban case adds the nuance of movement.
This completes the chapter's rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a Fiqh ruling with proof. It opens with three sub-rulings concerning prayer on garments or surfaces that are partially impure. The author then explains three principles underlying these rulings: general need (umum balwa), thickness preventing transfer of impurity, and not being a carrier of impurity. The first sub-ruling permits prayer on a thick felt rug with a pure top and impure bottom because the impurity does not reach the worshipper and he does not carry it. The second permits prayer on a clean cloth placed over an impure cloth if they are not sewn together, as the clean cloth is not a carrier. The third permits prayer on a clean edge of a mat or carpet even if the impure edge moves, as the ruling follows the place of prostration. The final ruling (374) covers extreme necessity: if no water to remove impurity, one prays as is and does not repeat; analogously, if no clothing, one prays naked. When a Turban's End Becomes Najis: Validity of Prayer
Ruling on prayer validity with a najis end of a turban

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for prayer validity when one end of a turban is najis and apply it to similar scenarios involving carrying impurity.

Core Idea
Prayer is valid only if the najis part is not carried by the movement of the pure part.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi school regarding prayer when a turban has one najis end. The key is whether the impurity is considered 'carried' by the worshipper. Understanding this condition helps clarify the general principle that carrying najis invalidates prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is prayer while having a turban with one najis end. The ruling is that prayer is permissible (valid) under a specific condition.
2
Identify the condition
The condition is that the pure end is on the head and the najis end is not moved when the pure end moves.
Movement indicates that the impurity is being carried by the worshipper.
3
Understand the legal principle
The underlying principle is that carrying najis (hamil al-najasah) invalidates prayer. The condition determines whether the najis is being carried.
If the najis part moves with the pure part, it is considered 'carried', invalidating prayer.
4
Apply to new scenarios
Hypothetical: If the turban is short so that moving the pure part automatically moves the najis part, prayer is invalid. If the najis part is completely detached, it is not an issue.

Ruling on One Unable to Remove Najasah or Cover Awrah
Ruling on praying with unavoidable najasah or nakedness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a person who cannot remove najasah or cover their 'awrah, and apply the principle of 'no burden beyond capacity' (la yukallifu nafsan illa wus'aha) to justify the ruling.

Core Idea
Prayer with unavoidable najasah or nakedness is valid and need not be repeated — necessity removes blame.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario: what if a person has impurity on their body or garment but no water or anything to remove it? Similarly, if a person has nothing to cover their private parts, even if that includes forbidden silk or just grass. The Shari'ah, being a religion of ease, does not burden a person beyond their capacity. Therefore, the prayer performed under such constraints is valid and does not require repetition, even if the inability is later removed. This ruling is derived from the Quranic verse on tayammum and the general principle of necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two situations covered
The text addresses two cases: (1) inability to remove najasah from body or garment, and (2) inability to cover the 'awrah. For each, the ruling is the same: pray in that condition and do not repeat later.
The inability must be genuine and not due to negligence.
2
State the ruling for each situation
For both, the prayer is valid (sahih) and there is no obligation to repeat (qada') when the inability ends. The ruling applies regardless of the type of najasah or the material used for covering (if available).
If one later finds water or clothing, the earlier prayer stands.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is that Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity (la yukallifu nafsan illa wus'aha). The person did what was possible under the circumstances; the prayer is accepted.
This principle is derived from Quran 2:286 and numerous hadith.
4
Connect to the dalil (Quran and Hadith)
The primary proof is the verse of tayammum (al-Ma'idah 5:6), which permits dry purification when water is absent. By qiyas (analogy), the same leniency applies to inability to remove najasah or cover 'awrah. Additionally, a hadith about praying where camels sit shows that proximity to najasah does not invalidate prayer if the actual place of prostration is clean.
The hadith of camels is from Abu Dawud; it indicates that some impurity in the vicinity is overlooked.
5
Confirm the absence of additional obligation
The text explicitly states 'wa la i'adah 'alayh' (no repetition on him). This is final – the prayer is complete and valid. No qada' is required, even if the person later gains the ability to remove the impurity or cover themselves.
This differs from cases where a person omits a condition deliberately; here it is due to impossibility.

'Awrah of a Free Woman in Prayer
States the 'awrah of a free woman with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which parts of a free woman's body are 'awrah in Hanafi fiqh and identify the three proofs that establish the exception of face, hands, and feet.

Core Idea
A free woman's 'awrah is her whole body except face, hands, and feet.

This lesson addresses a fundamental question in fiqh: what must a free woman cover during prayer? The Hanafi school, drawing on the Quran and authentic hadith, rules that the face, hands, and feet are not 'awrah. We will examine the evidence and the reasoning behind this position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Locate the explicit statement: 'All of the free woman's body is 'awrah except her face, her two hands, and her two feet.' This is the core ruling.
The ruling applies to prayer and also to being seen by non-mahram.
2
Examine the primary proof: Quran 24:31
The verse 'except what is apparent thereof' is explained by Ibn Abbas as the face and hands. Understand how this tafsir is used to limit the 'awrah.
The word 'kohl' and 'ring' symbolise face and hands.
3
Examine the secondary proof: Hadith of Asma
The Prophet (ﷺ) pointed to his face and hands, indicating that only those parts may be seen. This is a direct textual evidence.
The hadith is in Abu Dawud and is considered sound by Hanafi scholars.
4
Understand the role of necessity
The commentary mentions necessity (darurah) as a reason for excluding face and hands, as women need these for daily tasks.
This reinforces the ruling even if there is no explicit text.
5
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i considers the face and hands as 'awrah. The dispute arises from different interpretations of the same texts.
The practical difference: in Hanafi, a woman may pray with face uncovered; in Shafi'i, she must cover it.

(381) And all of the free woman's body is 'awrah except her face, her two hands, and her two feet. Translation: The entire body of a free woman is 'awrah except the face, both palms, and both feet. Commentary: The entire body of a free woman is 'awrah in prayer, except the face, both palms, and both feet, meaning if these parts are exposed, the prayer is not invalidated. And outside of prayer, if there is fear of desire from exposing these parts, especially the face, then covering them is necessary. Proof: (1) In the verse: 'And do not display their adornment except what is apparent thereof' (Surah An-Nur 24:31). Ibn Abbas interpreted 'what is apparent' as the kohl (face) and the ring (hands). This is reported in Sunan al-Bayhaqi. (2) There is necessity in revealing these two, so they are not 'awrah in prayer. Hadith from Aisha: The Prophet said to Asma: 'When a woman reaches puberty, it is not permissible for any part of her to be seen except this and this' and he pointed to his face and hands. (Abu Dawud). (3) Another hadith: 'The prayer of a menstruating woman is not accepted without a khimar' (Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud). This shows that covering the head is necessary for a free adult woman.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the 'awrah of a free woman in Hanafi fiqh. It states that a free woman's entire body is 'awrah except for her face, hands (palms), and feet. The commentary clarifies that in prayer, exposure of these excepted parts does not invalidate the prayer, but outside of prayer, if there is a fear of temptation, covering them becomes obligatory. The author provides several proofs: first, the verse from Surah An-Nur, with Ibn Abbas's interpretation that 'what is apparent' refers to the face and hands. Second, the hadith from Aisha where the Prophet indicates that only the face and hands may be seen. Third, a hadith stating that the prayer of a menstruating woman (i.e., an adult woman) is not accepted without a head covering (khimar), which implies covering the head is obligatory. These evidences establish that the face, hands, and feet are not 'awrah, while the rest of the body must be covered in prayer. In the Hanafi madhhab, this ruling is consistent with the principle that uncovering a quarter of a limb invalidates prayer, but these excepted parts are not considered 'awrah.

Key Terms
عورة ('awrah): Parts of the body that must be covered in prayer and before others.
حرة (ḥurrah): A free woman, as opposed to a slave woman.

Sunan of Tashahhud: Sitting and Pointing Finger
Listing sunan of salah: sitting posture for women and pointing finger in tashahhud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two sunnah acts (sitting tawarruk for women and pointing the index finger once during tashahhud) and identify the hadith proofs that establish them as sunnah muakkadah.

Core Idea
Women sit on the left side; everyone points once with the index finger at the testimony.

In this passage, the author presents two sunnah acts within prayer: the recommended sitting posture for women and the specific method of pointing the index finger during tashahhud. While these acts are not obligatory, they are strongly emphasized and rooted in the practice of the Prophet ﷺ as transmitted through multiple hadith. Understanding their proofs and details helps you perform salah in closer conformity to the sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sunnah acts
Read the ruling statement: the woman sits on her left side (tawarruk) and the worshipper points with the index finger at the testimony.
The text begins with (434) and (435) signaling separate rulings.
2
Locate the proofs for each act
For the sitting, an athar from Ata ibn Abi Rabah is cited. For pointing, multiple hadith from Wail ibn Hujr, Asim ibn Kulaib, Ibn Mas'ud, and Ibn Umar are quoted.
The hadith describe the Prophet's action (fi'l) – a primary source for sunnah.
3
Extract the specific details
From the text: the finger is raised at 'la ilaha' (negation) and lowered at 'illa Allah' (affirmation), and it is done only once without repeated motion.
The author notes that the exact timing is not found in hadith but is the preferred view of the Hanafi fuqaha.
4
Note the cross-madhhab variation
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) point repeatedly or keep the finger raised throughout. The Hanafi position restricts to a single point.
This difference stems from varying interpretations of the hadith about moving the finger.

Commentary

The author first states the ruling that women should sit tawarruk (on the left side) and points to an athar (trace from a companion/tabi'i) where Ata was asked about a woman's sitting during tashahhud. Wiping over Khuffs: Permissible Without Necessity
Ruling with hadith proof on wiping over khuffs without necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wiping over khuffs without necessity and cite the hadith proof.

Core Idea
The Prophet regularly wiped over khuffs without compulsion, making it a sunna and a valid purification method.

This lesson addresses a common misconception: that wiping over leather socks (khuffs) is only allowed in emergencies. The Hanafi school holds that it is a regular sunna, even without necessity. The proof comes from authentic hadiths of the Prophet’s continuous practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is wiping over khuffs during wudu. The ruling in this passage is that it is permissible without any compulsion (بغیر مجبوری).
The term 'طهارة الملة' indicates it is a complete means of purification.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites two hadiths from al-Mughirah ibn Shu'bah in Abu Dawud. The first states the Prophet used to wipe over his khuffs, forelock, and turban. The second mentions the Prophet wiping after putting feet into clean khuffs.
Both hadiths show the practice happened in normal circumstances.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The continuous imperfect verb 'كان يمسح' indicates a habitual action, establishing that wiping without necessity is a sunna. The hadith does not mention any condition of necessity.
This is the key point: the Prophet's practice without compulsion proves permissibility even when not required.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools require wiping only during travel or due to necessity. The Hanafi school differs, as evidenced by these hadiths.
The practical difference: Hanafis allow wiping even at home, others may restrict it.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The passage concludes that since wiping is a valid purification (طهارة الملة), the person who wipes can lead congregational prayer.
This shows the ruling has implications for imamate.

The operative phrase 'la yatakallam' (he should not speak) is interpreted broadly to cover any speech act, including tilawah (Quran recitation), because the legal impediment (hadath) renders all speech out of prayer impermissible except for the necessities of wudhu.
Thus, reciting Quran during the break violates the prohibition and invalidates the prayer.

Impurity on a Riding Animal is Excused in Prayer
Ruling with proof: impurity on riding animal excused

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that impurity on a riding animal is excused during prayer and explain the hadith proof.

Core Idea
Impurity on a riding animal does not invalidate prayer because the Prophet prayed while riding.

When you pray while riding an animal, it is almost certain that the animal or its equipment will have some impurity from urine or feces. The Hanafi school rules that such unavoidable impurity is excused and does not affect the validity of your prayer. This relaxation is based on the practice of the Prophet ﷺ, who performed voluntary prayers while riding. In this lesson, we will examine the ruling, its scope, and the proof from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is praying while riding an animal (dābba). The ruling is that impurity present on the animal, its saddle, or stirrups does not invalidate the prayer. The prayer remains valid (sahih) and permissible (mubah) despite the impurity.
The ruling applies to both obligatory and voluntary prayers, as indicated by the text 'صلوة الفرض' (obligatory prayer).
2
Understand the scope of the excused impurity
The impurity is excused only when it is on the animal itself or its equipment (saddle, stirrups). It does not extend to the rider's clothing or body. The impurity must be of the type normally produced by the animal (urine, feces) and should be minor and unavoidable.
The text explicitly mentions 'ولو كانت في السرج والركابين' (even if on the saddle and stirrups).
3
Locate the proof (dalil)
The author cites the hadith of Jabir ibn Abdullah that the Prophet ﷺ used to pray voluntary prayers while riding, facing other than the qibla. This hadith is reported by Bukhari and Muslim.
The hadith is ahad (single narration), so its thubut is zanni, but it is strong enough to derive a ruling.
4
Explain the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof supports the ruling)
Since the Prophet prayed while riding, it is certain that the animal had some impurity. If such impurity invalidated prayer, the Prophet would not have done it. Therefore, the impurity is considered excused (afw) and does not harm the prayer's validity.
The Hanafi principle of 'necessity (darura) and ease (taysir)' also supports this.
5
Note the ikhtilaf and the Hanafi tarjih
Some other schools may require the animal to be clean for prayer. However, the Hanafi school takes the correct view ('ala al-ashall') that the impurity is excused, as it is unavoidable when riding.
The text says 'على الأصح' (according to the correct opinion), indicating the preferred Hanafi position.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof concerning the validity of prayer on an animal despite the presence of impurity. The author states that impurity on the animal, the saddle, or the stirrups does not prevent the prayer from being valid. This is based on the principle that such impurity is excused due to necessity, as the hadith of Jabir confirms that the Prophet (peace be upon him) performed voluntary prayers while riding. The commentary explains that it is inevitable for riding animals to have urine and feces, and this would naturally affect the saddle and stirrups. Therefore, the Hanafi school holds that such impurity is overlooked. The key legal point is that only the minimum unavoidable impurity is excused; the ruling is specific to the animal and its equipage.
