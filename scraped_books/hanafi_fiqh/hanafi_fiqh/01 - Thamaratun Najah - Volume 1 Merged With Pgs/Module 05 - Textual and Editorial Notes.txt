Definition of Fiqh: Linguistic and Terminological
Defines fiqh lexically and terminologically, explains each part

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the lexical and terminological definitions of fiqh, identify its components (ahkam shar'iyya far'iyya and adillatiha tafseeliyya), and distinguish primary from subsidiary rulings.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of subsidiary sharia rulings derived from detailed evidences — not just rulings, but their proofs.

Before diving into specific rulings, we must understand what fiqh itself is. This passage gives the classical Hanafi definition, breaking it into its essential parts. By studying this, you will see that fiqh is not mere memorization of rules but a discipline grounded in evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the lexical and terminological definitions
Read the lexical meaning (to open/clarify) and the terminological definition: 'knowledge of subsidiary sharia rulings derived from detailed evidences.' Note how the faqih is described.
The lexical basis is 'a comprehending, opening.'
2
Break down the terminological definition into components
Isolate two parts: 'ahkam shar'iyya far'iyya' (subsidiary sharia rulings) and 'min adillatiha al-tafseeliyya' (from their detailed evidences). Understand each.
These two components are the subject matter and the method.
3
Distinguish primary from subsidiary rulings
The passage divides rulings into primary (beliefs like tawhid) and subsidiary (actions like prayer, zakat). Fiqh deals only with the latter.
This clarifies that fiqh is about 'amal (actions), not 'aqidah (creed).
4
Recognize the requirement of detailed evidence
The definition requires that each ruling be linked to its specific proof from the Quran, Sunnah, etc. The examples of Hajj (2:196) and prayer/zakat (2:43) illustrate this.
Knowing the ruling without evidence is not complete fiqh.

The Knowledge of Fiqh. Lexical meaning of fiqh: Lexically, fiqh means to open and clarify something. An expert jurist (faqih) is a scholar who clarifies sharia rulings, traces their realities, and explains ambiguous and complex issues. Terminological meaning: In the terminology of the people of sharia, the famous definition of fiqh is: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary sharia rulings derived from their detailed evidences.' Translation: Fiqh is called that knowledge of subsidiary sharia rulings which is obtained from the detailed evidences of the rulings. Explanation of each component: 'Al-ahkam al-shar'iyya al-far'iyya': Rulings are of two types: primary and subsidiary. Primary rulings: Belief rulings are called primary rulings, such as the Oneness of the Holy Lord, prophethood, the coming of the Messenger, knowledge of the Hereafter. These are all belief rulings and are called primary rulings. The science of fiqh does not conflict with this because it explains subsidiary rulings. Subsidiary rulings: rulings related to actions, such as rulings of prayer, fasting, Hajj, buying and selling, etc. These are the rulings explained in the science of fiqh. 'Min adillatiha al-tafseeliyya': The meaning of this phrase is that the detailed evidences of the rulings should also be known. For example, if one says that Hajj is obligatory, then the verse should also be known that its evidence is 'And complete the Hajj and Umrah for Allah' (Quran 2:196). Or if a ruling is stated that prayer and Zakat are obligatory, then this evidence should also be known that the verse 'Establish prayer and give Zakat and bow with those who bow' (Quran 2:43) is its evidence. The conclusion is that applying a ruling of permissibility, prohibition, dislike, obligation, etc. to every single act of humans, and stating the evidence for each, is the knowledge of fiqh.

Terminologically, fiqh is knowledge of subsidiary sharia rulings derived from detailed evidences. The author then explains each part: 'ahkam shar'iyya far'iyya' are rulings related to actions (not beliefs), and 'adillatiha tafseeliyya' means knowing the detailed proofs from Quran, Hadith, etc. for each ruling. Two Quranic examples are given: for Hajj (2:196) and for prayer/zakat (2:43). This passage emphasizes that fiqh is not just knowing rulings but also their evidence. This is the classical Hanafi approach as seen in usul al-fiqh texts.

Key Terms
فقہ (fiqh): Knowledge of subsidiary sharia rulings derived from detailed evidences.
فقیہ (faqih): A scholar who clarifies sharia rulings, traces their realities, and explains ambiguous issues.
احکام شرعیہ فرعیہ (ahkam shar'iyya far'iyya): Subsidiary sharia rulings pertaining to actions (e.g., prayer, fasting, transactions).

Linguistic Analysis of Alhamdulillah: The Meanings of the Definite Article
Explains the meanings of alif lam in al-hamd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three possible meanings of the definite article in 'al-hamd' and state which interpretation the majority (jumhur) adopt.

Core Idea
The 'al' in al-hamd is not mere grammar—it locks all praise exclusively to Allah.

When we say 'Alhamdulillah', we are performing an act of worship that carries deep linguistic meaning. The definite article 'al' attached to 'hamd' is interpreted by scholars in three ways, each reinforcing the oneness of Allah. This lesson unpacks those meanings so you understand not just the phrase but its theological weight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its components
The phrase 'Al-hamdu lillah' consists of 'al' (definite article) + 'hamd' (praise) + 'lillah' (for Allah). Focus on the function of 'al'.
The definite article is the key to interpretation.
2
Recognize the three possible functions of the definite article
The text explains three meanings: istighraq (comprehensiveness), jins (genus), and 'ahd (specific reference). Understand each definition.
These are standard linguistic categories in Arabic grammar.
3
Note the majority (jumhur) position
The majority of scholars hold that the alif lam in 'al-hamd' indicates istighraq, meaning all praises that can occur belong to Allah.
This is the relied-upon view.
4
Understand the theological implication
The istighraq interpretation establishes that every possible expression of praise is exclusively due to Allah, reinforcing the concept of tauhid.
This connects grammar to belief.

The Linguistic Nuance of Rabb al-'Alameen
Linguistic and theological definition of 'Rabb' and ''alameen'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dual usage of 'Rabb' (absolute vs. construct) and derive the meaning of 'al-'alameen' from its Arabic root, citing Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
‘Rabb’ alone is exclusive to Allah; in construct it can refer to a human master, as shown in Surah Yusuf.

This lesson unpacks the Quranic epithet 'Rabb al-'Alameen' through a linguistic lens. Understanding the precise meaning of 'Rabb' and ''alameen' is essential for grasping the tawhidic foundation of the Hanafi creed and avoiding common theological pitfalls. We will examine how a single verse from Surah Yusuf clarifies the grammatical condition under which 'Rabb' may refer to a human, reinforcing the principle that Allah's lordship is unique and absolute.

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the Phrase
Identify the two components: 'Rabb' and 'al-'alameen'. Note that the phrase is in construct (idafah) but here it is combined with a definite plural, establishing a relationship of ownership or lordship.
The definite article 'al-' on 'alameen' makes it specific: 'the worlds'.
2
Analyze 'Rabb' Alone vs. in Construct
Study the rule: when 'Rabb' is used without idafah (absolute), it is exclusively for Allah. When it appears in a possessive construct (e.g., 'rabbuka'), it can refer to a human master. This is established by the context of the Quranic verse cited (Surah Yusuf 12:42).
The verse: 'Remember me in the presence of your lord' uses 'rabbuka' (your lord) to refer to the king of Egypt.
3
Examine the Derivation of 'Alameen
Break down ''alameen' as plural of ''alam' (world). It is derived from ''alamah' (sign) because creation is a sign of the Creator. Note its morphological pattern: it follows the form of an instrument noun (ism al-alah), like 'khatam' (seal).
The pattern is fa'al, though it functions as an active noun (ism fa'il).
4
Confirm with the Dalil
Apply the Quranic proof from Surah Yusuf. Observe that in the verse, 'rabb' is used in idafah to a person (Yusuf), and the referent is a human (the king). This grammatical usage is consistent with the rule: construct allows limited application to others.
The verse is a dalil naqli (textual evidence) with qati' thubut (certain transmission) but zanni dalala (speculative indication) due to context.
5
Synthesize the Theological Implication
Combine the two analyses: the absolute usage of 'Rabb' is reserved for Allah alone, which upholds tawhid. The construct usage does not diminish Allah's exclusive right to the title when unqualified. The term ''alameen' points to the universe as a collection of signs, reinforcing the Creator's oneness.
This understanding prevents confusion when reading other texts where 'rabb' appears for humans.
