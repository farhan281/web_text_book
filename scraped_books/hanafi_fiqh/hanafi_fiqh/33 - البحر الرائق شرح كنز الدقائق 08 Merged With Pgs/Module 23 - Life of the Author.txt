Musaqah Terminates with Death: The Hanafi Ruling and Istihsan Exception
Matn and extensive commentary on death in musaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of death in musaqah, explain the istihsan exception for unripe fruit, and identify the three options available to the heirs when the landowner dies.

Core Idea
Musaqah is void upon death, but istihsan allows the worker to continue to prevent loss and waste of unripe fruit.

This passage from al-Hidayah and its commentary addresses a critical question: does the death of either party in a musaqah contract immediately void the agreement? The matn states the basic rule—the contract becomes void by death—because musaqah is analogous to ijarah. However, the commentator immediately introduces an istihsan exception: if the fruit is still unripe, the worker may continue tending it until ripening. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the reasoning behind the exception (qiyas and istihsan), the specific options granted to the heirs of the deceased landowner, and an intra-Hanafi dispute about the amount the heirs can recover from the worker's share. Understanding this ruling requires navigating between legal principle (qiyas) and equitable consideration (istihsan), a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and its qiyas
First, locate the matn's statement: 'وتبطل بالموت' (and it becomes void by death). The commentator explains the reason: 'لأنها في معنى الإجارة' (because it is in the meaning of ijarah). This is a clear qiyas jaliy: musaqah is analogized to ijarah, so the same rule applies—death voids the contract. Note that this is the general principle.
Qiyas jaliy is the strong analogy where the 'illa is explicit and obvious.
2
Apply the istihsan exception
Now look at the exception: if the landowner dies and the fruit is unripe (busr), the worker may continue as before until the fruit ripens. The commentator says this is by istihsan, because preventing him would cause him harm (darar) and there is no harm to the heirs. This is a departure from qiyas to prevent loss. Identify the two elements: qiyas (void) and istihsan (continuation).
Istihsan overrides qiyas here based on the principle of darar (harm).
3
Analyze the three options for the landowner's heirs
The passage states that if the worker chooses to continue (or if the worker dies, his heirs have options), the heirs of the landowner have three choices: (1) divide the unripe fruit according to the contractual share, (2) give the worker the value of his share of the unripe fruit, or (3) spend on the fruit until it ripens and then recover from the worker's share. Understand each option and its rationale.
The options balance the interests of both parties.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on recovery amount
The commentator notes an issue (إشكال) about the recourse: if the heirs choose option 3, do they recover all expenses from the worker's share or only half? Some say all expenses (as the work was entirely on the worker), but al-Nasafi and al-Hakim al-Shahid say half (since both parties benefit from the fruit). This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school. Note that the text does not give a definitive mufta bihi.
This dispute is also present in mazar'a (sharecropping).
5
Compare with mazar'a at expiry of term
The passage ends with a comparison: when the term of musaqah expires and the fruit is unripe, the worker may remain until it ripens, but he does not owe rent for the tree because trees cannot be rented. In contrast, in mazar'a (land sharecropping), the worker owes rent for the land after the term ends because land can be rented. This highlights a key distinction based on the nature of the capital.
The difference stems from the permissibility of renting land vs. trees.

Commentary
This long passage deals with the termination of musaqah due to death. The matn states that musaqah becomes void (mawt) upon death of either party, because it is analogous to ijarah. However, the commentary introduces istihsan: if the landowner dies and the fruit is still unripe (busr), the worker may continue tending until the fruit ripens to avoid loss. The heirs cannot prevent this. If the worker dies, his heirs have similar rights. There is a discussion of whether the heirs can force the worker to continue, and the options available to the heirs: they can divide the unripe fruit immediately, or pay the worker's share's value, or spend on the fruit and then recover from the worker's share. A dispute arises about the amount they can recover: some say they recover all expenses from the worker's share, but others (like al-Nasafi) say only half. The passage then discusses the expiry of the term: if the term ends and the fruit is unripe, the worker may remain until it ripens, but unlike mazar'a, the worker does not owe rent for the tree because trees cannot be rented, but he still does all the work. There is a comparison with mazar'a where the worker owes rent for the land after the term ends.

Key Terms
بسر (busr): Unripe dates; fruit that has not yet ripened.
استحسان (istihsan): Juridical equity; departure from qiyas for a stronger reason.
مزارعة (mazar'a): Sharecropping contract for land, analogous to musaqah but for crops.
