Introduction to Coercion (Ikrah): Definitions and Classification
Opening definitions and classification of coercion in Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mulji' and ghayr mulji' coercion and state the effect of each on different types of acts in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Coercion changes the ruling of an act from prohibited to permissible, but only mulji' coercion fully transfers responsibility to the coercer.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi fiqh of coercion (ikrah). You will learn the two types—mulji' and ghayr mulji'—and how each affects legal responsibility. The source passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq outlines the key definitions, proof from Quran and Sunnah, and the general ruling that coercion can negate consent but not always choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
State the Arabic root and what ikrah conveys: forcing someone to do something they dislike.
This is the foundation for the technical definition.
2
Extract the technical definition
According to Hanafi jurists, ikrah is impelling another to an act they would not willingly do, under threat of harm.
The technical definition builds on the linguistic but includes the element of threat.
3
Classify coercion into two types
Based on threat level: mulji' (compelling) involves threat to life or limb; ghayr mulji' (non-compelling) involves lesser harm like beating or imprisonment.
This distinction determines legal effects.
4
Determine the effect of each type
Mulji' coercion removes consent and free choice, affecting all acts and attributing the act to the coercer. Ghayr mulji' removes consent but not choice, affecting only transactions that require consent.
The key difference is whether free choice remains.
5
Analyze the evidence
The Quranic verse (16:106) excepts the coerced from faith-related sin, and the hadith of Safwan shows the Prophet upheld a divorce under coercion, indicating that coercion does not automatically invalidate such acts.
These proofs support the Hanafi position that coercion changes the ruling but does not nullify every act.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Coercion (Ikrah) by explaining its placement after the Book of Wala, as both involve a change from prohibition to permissibility. The author then outlines six aspects of the topic: linguistic meaning, technical definition, pillar, evidence, conditions, and ruling. Linguistically, ikrah means forcing someone to do something they dislike. Its pillar is the utterance expressing coercion. Evidence includes the Quranic verse ('except one who is coerced', 16:106) and the hadith about Safwan who divorced under threat of death—the Prophet upheld the divorce, showing that coercion does not invalidate such acts in all cases. The ruling is that if harm occurs, the coercer (mukrih) bears responsibility as if he acted directly, with the coerced person being his instrument. Coercion is divided into two types: mulji' (compelling) and ghayr mulji' (non-compelling). Mulji' involves threat to life or limb, which removes consent and free choice entirely, affecting all actions. Ghayr mulji' involves lesser threats like beating or imprisonment; it removes consent but does not destroy choice, so it only affects transactions that require consent (sale, lease, acknowledgment). The passage ends by noting that the coerced person, when acting under mulji' coercion, is like a tool of the coercer for acts like speech and eating, though the text breaks off.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrāh): Compelling someone to do something they dislike, either physically or psychologically.
الإكراه الملجئ (al-ikrāh al-mulji’): Coercion that involves threat to life or limb, eliminating free choice and removing consent.
الإكراه غير الملجئ (al-ikrāh ghayr al-mulji’): Coercion involving lesser harm like beating or imprisonment, removing consent but not free choice.

The Ten Topics of the Book of Pledge (Kitab al-Rahn)
Lists and explains the ten topics of the book of pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the ten topics of the book of pledge and explain the Hanafi view on each.

Core Idea
A pledge (rahn) is a contract of security; it requires possession to bind and is valid only for property.

This lesson introduces the structure of the Book of Pledge by outlining ten key topics that will be covered. Understanding this framework helps you navigate the detailed discussions to come. The passage begins by linking the pledge to the previous book on hunting, as both are means of acquiring wealth, then systematically presents each topic with a brief explanation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ten topics
Read through the list of ten topics. They are: linguistic meaning, proof, pillar, condition of binding, condition of validity, ruling, cause, description, legal definition, and benefits.
These form the outline for the entire book of pledge.
2
Analyze the linguistic meaning and proof
Note that 'rahn' linguistically means 'confinement,' as shown by Quran 74:38. The proof for the pledge is from Quran 2:283, which commands taking a pledge during debt.
The proof is a Quranic command.
3
Understand the pillar and conditions
The pillar (rukn) is the offer (ijab); acceptance is a condition because the pledge is a gratuitous contract. The condition for binding (luzum) is possession (qabd). The condition for validity (jawaz) includes that the item be separated, free of others' rights, and capable of satisfying a debt.
Possession is crucial for the contract to become binding in the Hanafi school.
4
Examine the ruling and cause
The ruling gives the pledgee the right to retain the pawn until the debt is paid, and priority over other creditors upon the pledgor's death. The cause is the need for security in lending.
The pledgee does not own the item but has a right of retention.
5
Review description, definition, and benefits
The description notes that the majority consider the pledgee as guarantor of the item. The legal definition is deferred. The benefits include relieving the pledgor from hardship and giving the pledgee confidence. A condition that voids the pledge (e.g., exempting loss) is invalid.
Any condition contrary to the contract's purpose is void.

كتاب الرهن
وجه مناسبة كتاب الرهن لكتاب الصيد من حيث إن كل واحد من الرهن والصيد سبب لتحصيل المال والكلام في الرهن يقع في مواضع : الأول في معناه لغة. والثاني في دليله. والثالث في ركنه والرابع في شرط لزومه والخامس في شرط جوازه والسادس في حكمه. والسابع في سببه . والثامن في صفته والتاسع في معناه عند الفقهاء. والعاشر في محاسنه. أما معناه لغة فهو عبارة عن الحبس بأي شيء كان قال الله تعالى كل نفس بما كتسبت رهينة ﴾ [المدثر : ۳۸] أي محبوسة بما كسبت من المعاصي. يقال رهنت الشيء وارتهنته والجمع رهن ورهون ورهان والرهن المرهون تسمية بالمصدر وأما دليله فقوله تعالى فرهن مقبوضة ﴾ [البقرة : ۲۸۳] أمر يأخذ الرهن وقبضه حال المداينة وأما ركنه فهو الإيجاب وهو قول الراهن رهنت عندك هذا الشيء بمالك على من الدين أو خذه والقبول شرط له لأن الرهن عقد تبرع لأنه لم يستوجب الرهن بذاته شيئاً والتبرع يتم بالإيجاب من غير قبول حتى لو حلف لا يرهن فرهن ولم يقبل الآخر يحنث . وأما الرابع وهو شرط اللزوم وهو القبض. وأما الخامس وهو شرط الجواز فكونه مقسوماً مفرزاً فارغاً عن الشغل بحق الغير، وأن يكون الرهن بحيث يمكن الاستيفاء منه كالدين حتى لا يصح الرهن بما ليس بمال كالحدود والقصاص والعتق . وأما حكمه فملك المرتهن المرهون في حق الحبس حتى يكون أحق بإمساكه إلى وقت ايفاء الدين في حال الحياة، وأما إذا مات الراهن فهو أحق به من سائر الغرماء فيستوفي منه دينه وما فضل فهو للغرماء. وأما سببه فهو الحاجة إليه لأن الإنسان قد لا يقرضه مجاناً من غير رهن أو يصبر عليه بغير رهن. وأما صفته قال عامة العلماء بأن الرهن مضمون على المرتهن كما سيأتي بيانه. وأما التاسع وهو تفسيره شرعاً فسيتكلم عليه المؤلف . وأما العاشر وهو محاسنه فهو فك عسرة الطلب عن الراهن ووثوق لا يجد من قلب المرتهن بما يحصل ماله ولو ارتهن على أنه إن ضاع بغير شيء وأجاز الرهن وبطل الشرط لأنه تغيير لعقد موضوع بحكم مشروع وتبديل المشروع لا يجوز والمقبوض بحكم الرهن الفاسد مضمون.
Book of Pledge. The aspect of appropriateness of the Book of Pledge to the Book of Hunting is that each of pledge and hunting is a means of acquiring wealth. Discussion of pledge occurs in ten places: first, its linguistic meaning; second, its proof; third, its pillar; fourth, the condition of its binding; fifth, the condition of its validity; sixth, its ruling; seventh, its cause; eighth, its description; ninth, its meaning according to jurists; tenth, its benefits. As for its linguistic meaning, it is expressed as confinement by any means. Allah the Exalted said: 'Every soul is held in pledge for what it has earned' (74:38), i.e., confined by what it earned of sins. It is said 'rahantu al-shay' and 'irtahantuhu'. The plural is rihan, ruhun, and rihan. The term 'al-rahn' (the pawned item) is named by the infinitive. As for its proof, His statement: 'Then a pledged possession taken' (2:283) is a command to take the pledge and its possession at the time of debt. As for its pillar, it is the offer (ijab), which is the pledgor's saying 'I pledge this thing with you for your right against me' or 'take it', and acceptance is a condition for it because the pledge is a gratuitous contract since it does not obligate any benefit by itself, and a gratuitous contract is completed by offer without acceptance; even if one swears not to pledge and then pledges without acceptance by the other, he breaks his oath. As for the fourth, the condition of binding, it is possession. As for the fifth, the condition of validity, it is that the thing be partitioned, separated, free from encumbrance by another's right, and that the pledge be such that satisfaction can be obtained from it like a debt, so a pledge is not valid for something that is not property like hudud, qisas, and manumission. As for its ruling, the pledgee has ownership of the pawned item in terms of the right of retention, so he has priority to hold it until the debt is paid during the pledgor's life; if the pledgor dies, the pledgee has priority over other creditors to satisfy his debt from it, and what remains is for the creditors. As for its cause, it is the need for it because a person may not lend without a pledge or may not wait without a pledge. As for its description, the majority of scholars said that the pledge is a guarantee upon the pledgee as will come. As for the ninth, its legal definition, the author will speak on it. As for the tenth, its benefits, it is relieving the pledgor of the hardship of seeking and confidence so that the pledgee's heart is at ease about obtaining his property; if he pawned on condition that if it is lost without anything... and the pledge is valid and the condition is void because it changes a contract established by a legitimate ruling, and changing the legitimate is not permissible; what is taken under an invalid pledge is guaranteed.

Commentary
This passage introduces the Book of Pledge (Kitab al-Rahn) and enumerates ten topics that will be discussed. It begins by drawing an analogy to the Book of Hunting, as both involve acquiring wealth. The ten topics are: linguistic meaning, proof, pillar, conditions of binding and validity, ruling, cause, description, legal definition, and benefits. The text then elaborates on each. The linguistic meaning of rahn is 'confinement,' supported by Quran 74:38. The proof is from Quran 2:283, which commands taking a pledge during debt. The pillar is the offer (ijab); acceptance is a condition because it is a gratuitous contract. The condition for binding is possession (qabd). For validity, the pledged item must be separated, free of others' rights, and capable of satisfying the debt. The ruling grants the pledgee the right to hold it until payment, and priority over other creditors upon the pledgor's death. The cause is the need for security in lending. The description: majority view holds the pledge is guaranteed by the pledgee. The legal definition is deferred to the author's later statement. The benefits include ease for both parties; a condition that nullifies the contract (e.g., exempting loss) is void, and such an invalid pledge renders the pledgee a guarantor.

Key Terms
الرهن (al-rahn): A contract whereby property is held as security for a debt.
الحبس (al-habs): Confinement or retention of the pawned item.
القبض (al-qabd): Possession; a condition for the binding nature of the pledge.

Navigating the Volume: Understanding the Table of Contents (الفهرس)
Index listing books and chapters with page numbers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major fiqh books in this volume and state their order as given in the index.

Core Idea
The table of contents is your roadmap to the book's structure and sequence of topics.

Every serious fiqh text includes a comprehensive index (fihris) to help students and scholars navigate its contents. This volume's table of contents lists the major books (kutub) and chapters (abwab) with corresponding page numbers. Understanding this index allows you to quickly locate rulings on specific subjects, appreciate the logical progression of topics, and see how the Hanafi tradition organizes fiqh. In this lesson, we will walk through the index and its hierarchical structure.

Guided Path
