Debt Distribution of an Authorized Slave Owned by Partners: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Rulings on debts of an authorized slave owned by partners with different partnership types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi dispute regarding debt distribution for an authorized slave owned by partners to different partnership types and distinguish between Imam Abu Hanifa's 'awl method and the Sahibayn's munaza'a method.

Core Idea
Debt distribution from an authorized slave's assets differs by partnership type and by whether Imām Abū Ḥanīfa's 'awl or the Sāḥibayn's munāzaʿa is used.

This passage from al-Baḥr al-Rāʾiq presents a series of scenarios involving two partners who authorize their slave (madhūn) to trade. The slave incurs debts to the partners and to an outsider, but his assets are insufficient to cover all debts. The distribution of the slave's assets becomes a matter of intra-Hanafi dispute: Imām Abū Ḥanīfa applies the principle of ʿawl (proportional reduction) analogous to inheritance insolvency, while the Sāḥibayn (Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad al-Shaybānī) adopt a method of munāzaʿa (dispute resolution) that treats claims competitively. Understanding this ikhtilāf is key to mastering Hanafi partnership law (shirkah).

Guided Path
1
Identify partnership types and their legal characteristics
List the three partnership forms mentioned: shirkat al-milk (co-ownership without trade partnership), mufawada (full equal partnership), and 'inan (limited partnership). Understand how each affects ownership of the slave and liability.
Shirkat al-milk means the slave is jointly owned but not part of a trade partnership; mufawada and 'inan are trade partnerships.
2
Analyze the first scenario: shirkat al-milk
Each partner advances 100 dirhams to the slave, and an outsider advances 100. The slave's total asset is 100. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, half goes to the outsider and half between the partners ('awl). According to the Sahibayn, division is by munaza'a (dispute), resulting in a different allocation.
This illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf: different numerical distributions.
3
Trace the dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Imam Abu Hanifa applies 'awl (proportional reduction of all claims to match assets). The Sahibayn consider the claims as competing and allocate via dispute resolution, prioritizing certain claims. Note the text defers full explanation to the Book of Wills.
'Awl is a method from inheritance law; munaza'a is distinct.
4
Apply to scenarios with trade partnerships (mufawada or 'inan)
When the slave is not part of the partnership asset (i.e., owned separately), and one partner lends from partnership funds and an outsider lends, the distribution differs. The text gives two views: one-third to the partners vs. two-thirds to outsider (Imam's view) or a different division into eighths. Also handle case where slave is part of partnership asset.
In the last scenario, all goes to the partnership because the slave and debt are both partnership property.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
For each scenario, compute the actual shares under each method. Notice that the choice of method changes the amount each creditor receives, especially the outsider's share.
This ikhtilaf is essential for a mufti to know which method to follow.

Commentary
This passage deals with the debts of a slave authorized by two partners. The distribution of the slave's proceeds when he has multiple creditors depends on the type of partnership (ownership, mufawada, 'inan) and whether the slave is considered part of the partnership asset or not. Imam Abu Hanifa uses the principle of 'awl (proportional reduction) to divide the proceeds, while the Sahibayn use a different method (munaza'a, dispute resolution). The text presents several scenarios and notes the difference between Imam and Sahibayn, indicating an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. The details are complex and reference al-Mabsut for full reasoning.

Key Terms
شركة ملك (shirkat al-milk): Partnership in ownership without partnership in trade (co-ownership).
مفاوضة (mufawada): Full partnership where partners equally share capital, profit, and liability.
عنان ('inan): Limited partnership where partners contribute capital unequally and share profits proportionally.
عول ('awl): Proportional reduction of shares when debts exceed assets.
منازعة (munaza'a): Dispute; here, a method of allocating assets by considering competing claims rather than proportional across-the-board reduction.

Partner Plants without Permission
Ruling on a partner who plants joint land without consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a partner planting joint land without permission and identify the condition that makes a subsequent agreement valid.

Core Idea
An agreement to share the crop after it grows is valid; an agreement before growth is not, due to uncertainty.

Partnership in land requires consent for any use. When one partner plants without permission, a subsequent agreement can validate the arrangement, but only after the crop has appeared. This lesson examines the precise ruling from Abu Yusuf as recorded in al-Muntaqa and al-Nawadir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the case involves two partners in an undivided land (mushā‘), where one partner plants without the other’s permission.
The land is jointly owned (mushā‘), not partitioned.
2
Identify the Subsequent Agreement
The partners agree that the non-planter gives half the seed, and the entire crop is shared equally. Note that the agreement involves a mutual financial arrangement.
3
Apply the Timing Condition
Determine when the agreement was made relative to the crop growth. If after the crop appears, it is valid; if before, it is not valid.
The text explicitly states this condition.
4
Analyze the Ruling
Understand the reason: before growth, the crop is nonexistent, so the agreement resembles a sale of non-existent goods (gharar). After growth, the crop is known and existent, making the agreement sound.
This ties to the principle of valid salam (sale of future goods) only when conditions are met.
5
Consider the Non-Planter's Option
If the non-planter wishes to uproot the crop after growth, the land is divided equally, and the planter compensates for any damage to the land caused by cultivation.
This shows that the non-planter has the right to reclaim his land share and receive compensation.

If the non-planter wants to remove the crop, the land is divided, and the non-planter gets what he removes, while the planter compensates for damage to the soil.

Introduction to Partition (Qisma): Definition, Proof, and Compulsion
Definition, legal proof, conceptual analysis of partition, and ruling on compulsion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of qisma, identify its proof from Quran and Sunnah, distinguish between ifraz and mubadala, and apply the ruling on compulsory partition for homogeneous versus non-homogeneous property.

Core Idea
Partition is either pure identification or exchange—the right to compel depends on homogeneity and property type.

Partition (qisma) is a fundamental chapter in Hanafi muamalat, dealing with the separation of jointly owned shares. This lesson unpacks its legal definition, proofs from the primary sources, and the critical distinction between identification (ifraz) and exchange (mubadala) that determines the rules for fungible and non-fungible goods. We will also explore when a partner can be compelled to partition and a notable intra-Hanafi difference regarding the division of wine.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the legal definition and dual nature of qisma
Understand the author's definition: 'gathering a common share into a specific portion'. Recognise that partition always involves both ifraz (identification) and mubadala (exchange), though one may predominate.
The matn explicitly states this duality is the basis for the subsequent rulings.
2
Identify the proof of legitimacy (dalil al-mashru'iyya)
Locate the Quranic verses (water division and she-camel), the Sunnah (Prophet's division of Khaybar), and the consensus of the ummah. Understand how these establish the permissibility and institutional nature of partition.
The dalil block in the toolbox lists the exact verses and their indication.
3
Analyse the distinction between mithli and ghayr mithli
Note that in fungible goods (mithli) identification predominates, allowing a partner to take his share even in the absence of the other. In non-fungible goods, exchange predominates, so presence or agreement is required.
The commentary illustrates this with examples like grain vs. houses.
4
Apply the ruling on compulsion (jabr)
The text states that a reluctant partner is compelled to partition only when the property is homogeneous (mutahid al-jins). If heterogeneous, no compulsion. This applies whether the property is mithli or not.
The passage also lists three types of partition based on compulsion.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi difference on partition of wine
See how Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani permits partition of wine by a Muslim executor as pure ifraz, while other mashayikh deem it makruh. This shows the practical effect of the theoretical analysis.
This ikhtilaf is a legitimate difference within the school.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the Book of Partition (Kitab al-Qisma) from a Hanafi fiqh commentary. The author first defines partition linguistically and legally: it is the process of turning a common undivided share into a specific property. He then notes that partition inherently involves two aspects: ifraz (identification/separation) and mubadala (exchange). Which aspect dominates depends on the type of property. In fungible goods (mithli), such as wheat or oil, each partner's share is exactly equal to his right, so if one partner is absent, the other can take his share because the goods are interchangeable. This is pure ifraz. In non-fungible goods (ghayr mithli), like real estate or animals, each portion is unique and cannot be exactly equal, so the absent partner cannot take his share without his presence because it involves mubadala (exchange). The passage then provides the proofs for the legality of partition: the Quranic verses about the division of water and the she-camel, the Prophet's division of Khaybar, and the consensus of the ummah. The various components of partition are listed: its rukn (the act by which separation occurs), its shart (the property must not lose utility by division), its hukm (each partner becomes exclusive owner of his share), its sabab (the request for individual enjoyment), and its sifa (it is wajib upon the judge to perform it when requested). The commentator then discusses a subtle issue: the partition of wine by a Muslim executor. The matn suggests that partition of wine is makruh, but the commentator reconciles two views: Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani permits it as pure ifraz (since wine is mithli?), while other mashayikh dislike it because of the semblance of exchange. The commentator leans toward the idea that all partition involves both ifraz and mubadala to some extent, but the preponderance differs. Finally, the passage discusses compulsion in partition. If the property is homogeneous (e.g., all wheat), a partner can be compelled to partition, whether it is mithli or not. If heterogeneous (e.g., goats and camels), no one is compelled. The passage ends by listing three types of partition based on compulsion and three types of options (khiyar: stipulation, sight, defect) that apply depending on the type of property. The text breaks off in the middle of discussing options in non-fungibles.

Key Terms
القسمة (al-qisma): Legal partition of jointly owned property to assign specific portions to each owner, combining elements of identification (ifraz) and exchange (mubadala).
الإفراز (al-ifraz): The aspect of partition where each partner receives exactly what is his right without any exchange.
المبادلة (al-mubadala): The aspect of partition where each partner receives a different portion as exchange for what is owned by the other.
المثلي (al-mithli): Fungible goods that have exact equivalents in the market, such as grains and oils, where one can substitute without loss.
غير المثلي (ghayr al-mithli): Non-fungible goods that vary individually, such as real estate, animals, and clothes, where exchange is necessary.
الشفعة (al-shuf'a): The right of pre-emption in property, related to qisma because both arise from common ownership.
خيار العيب (khiyar al-'ayb): The option to rescind a contract due to a defect in the subject matter.
خيار الرؤية (khiyar al-ru'ya): The option to rescind a contract upon seeing the object if it was unseen.
خيار الشرط (khiyar al-shart): The option to rescind a contract within a stipulated period.

Partner in Muafada Cancelling the Pawn
Ruling on a partner in a muafada partnership cancelling a pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a partner cancelling a pledge in muafada and inan partnerships, and apply the liability rules when the pledged property perishes.

Core Idea
In muafada partnership, one partner's cancellation of a pledge is valid and binding on both.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the distinction between two types of partnerships: muafada (full agency) and inan (limited). The key is that in muafada, each partner's disposition is like that of both, so a unilateral cancellation of a pledge is valid. In inan, it is not. The passage also explains the liability when the pledged item perishes after cancellation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the partnership type
Determine whether the partnership is muafada or inan. In muafada, partners have equal and full authority; in inan, authority is limited.
2
Apply the cancellation ruling
In muafada, one partner's cancellation of a pledge is valid because his act is treated as the act of both. In inan, one partner cannot cancel alone because his authority is not co-extensive with his partner's.
3
Analyse the liability scenario
If a muafada partner cancels the pledge, takes possession, and the item perishes, the pledgee becomes liable for the share of the non-cancelling partner. The cancelling partner must compensate the pledgee for half the value (or the other partner's share) and then has recourse against the pledgee.
This is because the pledgee was deceived by the apparent authority of the cancelling partner.
4
Understand the objection and response
Isa objected that the cancelling partner cannot recover from the pledgee without proof of agency. The response is that the partnership itself provides apparent agency, making the pledgee a victim of deception (gharar), thus the cancelling partner can recover.
An alternative interpretation is provided in al-Mustawda'.

Commentary
This passage discusses the cancellation of a pledge by a partner in different types of partnerships. In an inan (limited) partnership, one partner cannot cancel alone.
