Rulings on Coitus Interruptus ('Azl) with a Slave Woman and a Free Wife
This passage explains the Hanafi ruling on coitus interruptus with a slave woman and a free wife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'azl with one's slave woman and one's free wife, explain the evidence, and apply the conditions correctly.

Core Idea
‘Azl with a slave woman is permissible without permission; with a free wife it requires her permission because she has a right to children.

This passage addresses a sensitive issue: when a man may practice coitus interruptus to avoid pregnancy. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a man's own slave woman and his free wife based on the rights each has over intercourse and offspring. Understanding this distinction is crucial for fulfilling marital and ownership rights properly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Cases and Their Rulings
Two separate scenarios: ‘azl with one's own slave woman (amah) and with one's free wife (zawjah). The ruling for the slave woman is permissible without her permission; for the wife, permissible only with her permission.
Note the phrase 'bila idhniha' vs 'bi idhniha'.
2
Understand the Evidence for Each Case
For the free wife, the evidence is the hadith that the Prophet forbade ‘azl with a free woman except with her permission. For the slave woman, the absence of such a restriction and the lack of right to intercourse on her part.
The hadith is a key dalil.
3
Analyze the Rationale
A free wife has a right to intercourse and to bear children; she may even demand it in court. A slave woman has no such right because her purpose is service, not childbearing, and the master's right over her is for pleasure and service.
This justification is based on the legal maxim that rights depend on the nature of ownership.
4
Note the Exception for a Married Slave Woman
The passage distinguishes between a slave woman owned by the man (where permission is not needed) and a slave woman who is married to someone else (where permission is from her master). This is to avoid repetition with the earlier ruling in the nikah chapter.
The author clarifies that the earlier reference to 'idhn al-sayyid' is about a married slave woman.
5
Apply the Ruling in Practice
In a real situation, a man who wishes to practice ‘azl must first determine the status of the woman: if she is his slave, he may do so without asking; if she is his free wife, he must obtain her consent. Failure to do so is a sin.
The consequence of not obtaining permission from a free wife is that the act becomes haram.

Commentary
This passage covers two rulings. First, the ruling that a female slave's own male slave (i.e., a slave owned by a female slave) is legally like a foreign man (ajnabi) in terms of hijab. This means the female slave must observe hijab in front of him as she would with any non-mahram male. The Hanafi proof is that he is not a mahram (no prohibitive kinship) nor a husband, and desire is present. They reject the analogy to 'what your right hands possess' from Quran 4:3 (which the Shafi'is and Malikis use to say such a slave is like a mahram) because that verse refers to female slaves, not male slaves. The reasoning is that a free wife has a right to intercourse and to bear children, whereas a slave woman has no such right. The passage distinguishes between a married slave woman (where 'azl permission is from her master) and a slave woman owned by the man himself.

Slave of a Free Woman is Like a Foreign Man for Hijab
Inter-madhhab dispute on slave-hijab ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a woman's male slave is like a foreign man regarding hijab, explain the Hanafi proof using qiyas and the tafsir of the Quranic verse, and identify the practical difference with the Shafi'i/Maliki position.

Core Idea
A woman's male slave is not a mahram; she must observe hijab from him as from any foreign man.

This lesson examines a classical inter-madhhab dispute: Is a woman's male slave considered a mahram for her in terms of hijab? The Hanafi school rules that he is like a foreign man (ajnabi), so she must cover in front of him and cannot display her adornment. The Shafi'i and Maliki schools consider him a mahram based on a general interpretation of the Quranic verse. We will unpack the proofs and the Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what the Hanafi school rules regarding a woman's male slave and hijab. The matn says 'her slave is like a foreign man from the men'.
This is the starting point: the slave is not a mahram.
2
Identify the opposing school and their argument
State which school(s) disagree and what proof they cite. Here, Malik and Shafi'i use the Quranic verse 'aw ma malakat aymanukum' to include male slaves as mahrams.
The opponent's dalil is the general wording of the verse.
3
Follow the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof consists of qiyas (the slave is neither husband nor mahram, desire is present, need is limited) and tafsir (the verse was revealed concerning female slaves only, as reported by tabi'un).
The rational and textual elements combine.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the opponent
The Hanafi response is that the verse is specific to female slaves, not general. The reports from Ibn Jubayr, Ibn al-Musayyib, and al-Hasan confirm this specificity.
The Hanafis restrict the general meaning (takhsis) with revealed context.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, the free woman must cover from her slave and cannot travel with him without a mahram. Under Shafi'i, travel is allowed and hijab is relaxed.
This shows the real-world impact of the ikhtilaf.

Ruling on 'Azl with Slave Woman and Wife
Stating the ruling on 'azl with proof and addressing a potential objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'azl with a slave woman and a free wife, identify the prophetic evidence, and explain the rationale for the distinction.

Core Idea
A man may perform 'azl with his slave woman without permission but not with his free wife without her consent.

This lesson addresses the permissibility of coitus interruptus ('azl) in two distinct relationships: with a slave woman whom the man owns, and with his free wife. The Hanafi ruling hinges on the rights each woman has over intercourse. We will examine the prophetic proof and the underlying logic that differentiates the two cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage discusses 'azl with two categories of women: one's own slave woman (amah) and one's free wife (zawjah). Note the distinction from a married slave woman (covered separately in Nikah).
The slave woman here is the object of intercourse by right of ownership (milk al-yamin), not through marriage.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
With his own slave woman, 'azl is permissible without her permission. With his free wife, it is only permissible with her permission; without it, the act is makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked).
The ruling for the wife is based on a hadith.
3
Analyze the proof
The author cites a hadith: the Prophet forbade 'azl with a free woman except with her permission. This prohibition (nahy) indicates tahrim unless the exception applies. The authority of the hadith is zanni in transmission and meaning.
The wajh al-dalalah: the explicit exception (illa bi-idhniha) shows that without permission, the act falls under the prohibition.
4
Understand the rationale
A free wife has a right to intercourse for satisfaction of desire and procreation; she may even demand it in court (e.g., in cases of impotence). A slave woman, being owned property, has no such right. The text also refutes an objection: the wife's right is not merely to the act itself but to the potential for ejaculation if the husband is capable.
If the husband is incapable of ejaculation (e.g., no water), the wife cannot complain, but if he has the ability, withholding through 'azl violates her right.

Divorcing a Wife Who Neglects Prayer
Stating the ruling on divorcing a non-praying wife with a financial exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling regarding divorcing a wife who does not pray and identify the exception when divorce is discouraged due to inability to pay mahr.

Core Idea
A man should divorce a wife who neglects prayer, unless he cannot pay her mahr.

This passage from the branches of fiqh deals with a serious marital situation: a wife who does not pray. The Hanafi ruling directs the husband to divorce her to avoid companionship with a sinner. However, a critical exception is made: if the husband cannot afford to pay the mahr (dower) that becomes due upon divorce, it is better not to divorce because an unpaid mahr is a grave debt before Allah. Imam Abu Ja'far al-Kabir's statement underscores the heavy responsibility of both actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
State that a man should divorce his wife if she does not pray. The Arabic verb 'yutalliquha' indicates a strong recommendation (not obligation).
The ruling applies when the wife neglects salah without valid excuse.
2
Identify the exception
If the husband does not have the means to pay the mahr upon divorce, it is better (al-awla) not to divorce. The mahr is a debt that must be settled.
The exception prevents exchanging one sin (companionship with non-prayer) for another (unpaid debt).
3
Understand the reasoning
Imam Abu Ja'far's statement shows that he considers unpaid mahr a very serious liability, but he would rather face Allah with that than have intercourse with a non-praying woman. This implies both are major sins; the ruling balances them.
The weight of avoiding intercourse with a non-prayer is given priority in the primary ruling.
4
Memorize the ruling and exception
Fix the two scenarios in mind: (1) able to pay mahr → divorce; (2) unable to pay mahr → better not to divorce.
This is a memorization anchor for the branches (furu') of nikah.

Ruling on Having Two Sister-Slave-Girls: Combining Prohibited
Ruling on the prohibition of intimate relations with two sister-slave-girls simultaneously and its exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on combining two sister-slave-girls, explain the three scenarios of kissing, and identify which actions remove the prohibition.

Core Idea
Kissing both with desire prohibits both; relief only through ownership transfer, marriage, or emancipation of one.

This lesson addresses a specific case in Hanafi fiqh: a man who owns two slave-girls who are sisters. The Quran prohibits combining sisters in marriage (4:23), and the Hanafi school extends this prohibition to sexual relations through slave ownership. We will examine the precise conditions under which such relations become prohibited, the proof from the Quran, and how the prohibition may be lifted through lawful means.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is having intimate relations (intercourse or its means) with two sister-slave-girls. The ruling is haram when both relations are combined.
The prohibition applies only when there is a combination; if he only ever touches one, no issue.
2
Understand the three scenarios of kissing
Al-Inaya clarifies: if he kisses neither, he may choose either; if he kisses one with desire, only that one remains lawful; if he kisses both with desire, both become prohibited.
The key condition is 'with desire' – without desire, the action is not considered.
3
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is Quran 4:23 forbidding combining sisters, which is given precedence over 4:24 (permissibility of what right hands possess). The statement of Ali (RA) supports this: the prohibitor overrides the permissive.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle of giving priority to prohibitory texts over permissive ones when there is apparent conflict.
4
Recognize the prohibition of means (dawa'i)
Actions like touching, looking, or kissing with desire are treated like intercourse itself, as they lead to it. The Hanafi maxim 'al-wasa'il laha hukm al-maqasid' applies here.
Thus, these means also trigger the prohibition when done with both sisters.
5

The prohibition is lifted only when one sister's vulva becomes unlawful (haram al-farj) through: full ownership transfer (sale, gift, etc.), valid marriage to another, or full emancipation. Partial actions like tamlik al-ba'd, kitabah, and itq ba'd also suffice; but hiring out, tadbir, and rahn do not.
The effect of partial actions differs between Imam Abu Hanifa (divisible) and the Sahibayn (not divisible), but all agree it prohibits intercourse.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A single upright report of a wife's apostasy after marriage may be accepted, but before marriage it is not—and even this is contested within the school.

This lesson examines a nuanced issue in Hanafi fiqh: when can a husband act on a report that his wife has apostatized? The passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq reveals an internal dispute between two narrations from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani: one from Kitab al-Istihsan and one from al-Siyar al-Kabir. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to handle ikhtilaf within the school and highlights the role of ihtiyat (caution) in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario

Extract the two narrations
Kitab al-Istihsan accepts a single upright report for the woman's apostasy after marriage; al-Siyar al-Kabir requires two witnesses for any apostasy of either spouse.

The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is immediate permissibility vs. delay until proof.

Core Idea
A single witness's report is accepted after marriage but not before, because it overrides the weaker presumption of continuity.

This distinction hinges on the relative strength of khabar al-wahid versus the presumption of validity (istishab al-hal). The proof is that the decisive new information (al-qati' al-tari') overrides the earlier presumption. The initial act of marriage does not prove the absence of the defect, so the single report is stronger than istishab.

This shows the methodology of weighing conflicting evidences in Hanafi fiqh.

Liability of a Borrower Who Misuses Before Pawning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi majority position on when a musta'ir who misuses before pawning becomes free from liability, and explain the opposing view with its proof.

Core Idea
A borrower who misuses a pawned item remains liable until the owner receives possession, according to the majority.

This lesson examines a nuanced case within the Hanafi school: a person borrows an item to pawn it but uses it first, committing misuse (ta'addi). After later pawning it, does his liability for the misuse automatically end? The text presents a dispute among Hanafi authorities, with the majority insisting on actual return to the owner. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of the principles governing trust (amanah) and liability (daman) in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the initial ruling
The borrower (musta'ir) uses the borrowed item before pawning it. This is ta'addi (misuse), which transforms him from an amin (trustee) into a damin (guarantor).
The text begins by stating pawning is permissible and removes the misuse.
2
Locate the dispute over liability clearance
The majority (عامة المشايخ) and the author of al-Hidayah hold that liability remains until the item is returned to the owner. A minority (al-Karkhi, al-Halwani, al-Hindwani) holds that pawning alone clears liability.
Note the opposing scholars: Shams al-Aimma al-Karkhi, Shams al-Aimma (al-Halwani), and Shaykh al-Islam (al-Hindwani).
3
Analyze the dalil of the majority
The majority argues that the musta'ir's hand is 'his own hand' (يد نفسه); misuse made him a guarantor, and removing misuse does not restore trust unless the owner regains possession. The pawner's hand is considered the owner's hand only for the pawn's purpose, not for the borrower's liability.
This is based on the principle that an amin's breach creates a permanent liability until actual return.
4
Analyze the dalil of the minority
The minority analogizes to wadi'a (deposit): if a depositary misuses but then returns to the trust (by repawning), his liability ceases. They argue that pawning effectively returns the item to the owner's sphere, so the borrower becomes an amin again.
Shaykh al-Islam supports this with a case: if the borrower is bankrupt, the pawner cannot prevent sale because the pawn benefits both parties.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if the pawn perishes after pawning but before return, the majority holds the musta'ir liable for the value; the minority holds him not liable.
The text does not explicitly choose, but later fatwa works tend to follow the majority.

Interpreting 'Jabran an al-Rahin' in al-Hidayah
Critical refinement of a disputed phrase in al-Hidayah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the major Hanafi interpretations of 'jabran an al-rahin' and state the preferred view of Ibn Nujaym in al-Bahr al-Raiq.

Core Idea
The phrase 'jabran an al-rahin' in al-Hidayah is ambiguous; the preferred reading is 'as a substitute on behalf of the pawner' (niyabah).

This lesson delves into a dense intra-Hanafi dispute over a single phrase in al-Hidayah. The ruling itself is clear: when a pawn's value equals the debt, the mu'ir (the lender of the pawned item) may redeem the pawn by paying the debt, and the murtahin (creditor) cannot refuse. But what does 'jabran an al-rahin' mean? Is the mu'ir acting by force against the pawner? Compensating the murtahin? Or acting as the pawner's substitute? The commentator Ibn Nujaym analyzes the various interpretations and selects the most coherent one, demonstrating the rigorous linguistic and legal methodology of Hanafi scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
First, locate the ruling in al-Hidayah: when the pawn's value equals the debt, the mu'ir can redeem the pawn 'jabran an al-rahin', and the murtahin cannot prevent him after payment. Note that this is the starting point for all interpretations.
The condition 'value equals debt' is incidental, not exclusive.
2
List the competing interpretations
The commentator mentions several views: (a) 'when the pawner becomes insolvent' (a reported scribal variant), (b) 'by force without the pawner's consent', (c) 'as a substitute on behalf of the pawner, by force against the murtahin', (d) 'compensation for what the murtahin loses'. List each with its proponent.
The scribal variant is attributed to Imam Muhammad's original wording.
3
Analyze the linguistic critique
Ibn Nujaym critiques the phrase 'jabran an' itself. He argues that the expression is not standard Arabic whether derived from jabr (compulsion) or jubran (compensation). The difficulty lies in the preposition 'an'. He uses this to dismiss some interpretations.
Focus on the syntactic role of 'an' in the phrase.
4
Evaluate the preferred interpretation
Ibn Nujaym favors the view of al-Kifayah: 'niyabatan an al-rahin jabran an al-murtahin' (as a substitute on behalf of the pawner, by force against the murtahin). He finds this syntactically coherent and avoids the issues of other readings.
This interpretation harmonizes the phrase without requiring textual emendation.
5
Consider the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: under the 'force against pawner' view, the mu'ir can override the pawner's objection; under the 'substitution' view, the mu'ir acts on behalf of the pawner, and the murtahin is forced to accept. The mu'ir's action is not against the pawner but for him.
The practical effect on the pawner's consent differs.

The author of al-Inayah said: His saying 'redeem it jabran an al-rahin' is said to mean 'without his consent', but that is not apparent; it is also said to mean 'as a substitute' and perhaps it is from jubran, meaning compensation for what the murtahin missed if he pays his debt and [the text goes on]. I say: There is discussion in it. Firstly because the meaning he chose does not apply when he wants to redeem before the term, as the pawner has not failed to pay himself at that time. Secondly, the Arabic expression 'jabra an' is not known whether from jabr (compulsion) or jubran (compensation). The difficulty in the author's phrasing is the preposition 'an' upon the pawn; on the meaning he chose, 'an' has no visible object unless we assume a missing part. This is very far-fetched. So why commit to that when the purpose can be achieved by taking 'niyaba' (substitution) alone as the author of al-Kifayah did? And it became clear from what we presented that the author of al-Hidayah's condition 'like the debt' is an accidental qualification, not exclusive.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement from the commentator (Ibn Nujaym, author of al-Bahr al-Raiq) on a disputed phrasing in al-Hidayah. The Hidayah states that when the pawn's value equals the debt, the mu'ir (lender of the item for pawning) can redeem the pawn from the murtahin 'jabran an al-rahin' (جبراً عن الراهن), and the murtahin cannot refuse once the mu'ir pays the debt. The commentator examines multiple interpretations of 'jabran an al-rahin'. Some early scholars like Sarakhsi and Bazdawi noted that the original wording of Imam Muhammad (in the Mabsoot) was 'when the pawner becomes insolvent', suggesting the phrase 'jabran an al-rahin' might be a scribal error. Others interpret it as 'by force against the pawner' (بدون رضاه). Al-Kifayah interprets it as 'on behalf of the pawner, by force against the murtahin' (نيابة عن الراهن جبراً عن المرتهن). Al-Inayah suggests it might be from 'jubran' (compensation) for what the murtahin loses if the mu'ir pays the debt, as if the mu'ir is compensating for the murtahin's loss. The commentator (Ibn Nujaym) critiques these views: he points out that if the redemption occurs before the debt's term, the pawner has not failed to pay, so the 'compensation' interpretation fails. He also notes that the expression 'جبر عنه' is not standard Arabic whether from jabr or jubran. He concludes that the difficulty lies in the preposition 'عَن' (an) and that the interpretation of al-Kifayah (taking it as 'نيابة') is the most plausible. Finally, he clarifies that the condition of value equal to debt in al-Hidayah is incidental, not exclusive—the ruling applies even if the value is different.

Key Terms
جبر (jabr): Ambiguous term in the passage; possibly 'jabr' (compulsion) or 'jubran' (compensation).
افتكاك (iftikak): Redeeming the pawn by paying off the debt.
نيابة (niyabah): Acting on behalf of another; substitution.

Liability for Injury to and by the Pawn
Ruling with proof via qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on liability when the pawner or pledgee injures the pawn, and when the pawn injures a third party, and apply the qiyas to determine compensation.

Core Idea
Both pawner and pledgee have respected rights; injury to the pawn is compensable, and liability for the pawn's acts splits between them.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies the intricate liability rules when pawned property is damaged or causes damage. The Hanafi school balances the owner's and pledgee's rights, using qiyas to the slave with bequeathed services. We will walk through the two main scenarios: injury upon the pawn and injury by the pawn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, note that the text covers: (1) injury inflicted upon the pawn by the pawner or pledgee, and (2) injury caused by the pawn to a free third party. Separate these two cases.
2
Analyse the first scenario: compensation for injuring the pawn
The pawner and pledgee both have a respected right in the pawn. If one injures it, he must compensate the other for the loss. Understand the analogy: like a slave whose services are bequeathed, if heirs destroy him, they pay value to replace him.
The compensation taken by the pledgee becomes part of the security; if the debt is due and of same type, he takes it; if deferred, he keeps it as pledge.
3
Analyse the second scenario: pawn injures a third party
When the pawned slave kills a free man by mistake, the liability depends on the slave's value relative to the debt. The pawner and pledgee are jointly addressed with the option to surrender (daf') or ransom (fida'). If both surrender, debt is void. If only one wants ransom, the pledgee bears the entire ransom because he benefits from preserving the security.
If the pawner pays ransom, it counts as repayment of the debt to that extent.
4
Trace the qiyas
The analogy to a slave with bequeathed services is used: both cases involve a right attached to the slave's person (raqabah) beyond mere ownership. The person who destroys must compensate the other right-holder.
This is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) because the illa (effective cause) is the shared right in the slave's physical body.
5
Determine the practical consequences
Memorise the outcomes: (a) surrender by both voids debt; (b) pledgee alone ransoms bears full cost and cannot recover from pawner; (c) pawner ransoms reduces debt pro rata. In injury to pawn, compensation is paid to the other party.
These rulings highlight the interplay between ownership and security interest in Hanafi fiqh.

Because the pawner is the owner and the pledgee has a right, so if one destroys, he compensates the other. The pledgee's right is binding and respected, and its likes attach to property, so the owner is made like a stranger in terms of compensation, like the slave whose services are bequeathed: if the heirs destroy him, they pay his value to buy a slave to take the place of the first. If his value equals the debt, then the pawner and the pledgee are both addressed with surrendering the slave or ransoming him, because each of them has a right resembling ownership, so they become opponents, and their joining is required in the address of surrender or ransom. If both surrender him, the debt is void because the slave is removed from the pawner's ownership due to being in the pledgee's hand and under his liability, so it is as if he died a natural death, and thus the claim is fixed. If one chooses surrender and the other refuses, it is not surrendered because if the pawner chooses surrender, he aims to remove his own ownership without his consent, so he is prevented. If he chooses ransom, the entire ransom is on the pledgee because the ransom is to save the slave from destruction and to revive his right by purifying him from the injury, like taking medicine to prevent destruction and the cost of medicine is on him because the destruction would be on him; so similarly the ransom. And it is like the usurped slave: if he commits an injury, the injury is against the usurper because the destruction is on him. And he does not recover the ransom from the pawner because he paid an obligatory right. Commentary
This passage covers two distinct areas of liability concerning pawned property. First, the ruling on injury inflicted upon the pawn by the pawner (rahin) or the pledgee (murtahin). The text states that such injury is compensable (مضمونة), because each party has a respected right: the pawner as owner, the pledgee as holder of a security interest. If one injures the pawn, he must compensate the other for the loss. The compensation taken by the pledgee becomes part of the security; if the debt is due and of the same type, he takes it; if deferred, he keeps it as pledge. The analogy is given of a slave whose services are willed — if the heirs destroy him, they pay value to replace him. This also connects to restrictions on a terminally ill person's donations (which cannot exceed one-third of the estate). Second, the passage addresses the case where the pawned slave injures a free third party (e.g., kills by mistake). The author of al-Mabsut (Imam Muhammad) is quoted: the liability depends on the slave's value relative to the debt. If value equals debt, both the pawner and pledgee are addressed with the option to either surrender the slave to the victim's family or pay the ransom (fida'). They must act jointly; if one surrenders and the other refuses, surrender is not valid. If they both surrender, the debt is void (since the slave is lost). If one chooses ransom, the pledgee bears the full ransom because he benefits from preserving the slave as security; he cannot recover it from the pawner. If the pawner pays the ransom himself, it counts as repayment of the debt to the extent of the ransom, reducing the pawn accordingly. The ruling highlights the interplay between ownership and security interests.

Key Terms
جناية (jinayah): Injury or tort; in this context, harm inflicted upon the pawn or by the pawn.
دفع (daf'): Surrendering the slave to the victim's family (in case of homicide).
فداء (fida'): Ransom payment to free the slave from liability for injury.
