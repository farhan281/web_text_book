Ruling on a Mukatab Paying Wine for Freedom
Explaining the ruling when a mukatab pays wine before the judge's annulment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a mukatab pays wine and explain the reasoning (istihsan) and distinction from carcass and blood.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, paying wine in kitabah frees the slave but he must pay its value.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra’iq clarifies a nuanced ruling: if a mukatab pays wine before the judge annuls the invalid contract, he becomes free but must compensate the value. The text presents the Hanafi position, internal disputes, and the distinction from carcass and blood.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and Hanafi ruling
Read the opening: 'فإن أدى الخمر عتق'. State the ruling: the slave becomes free and must pay the value.
The ruling applies before the judge's annulment.
2
Recognize the proof: istihsan
The proof is that wine is considered property in general (mal fi al-jumla), so the contract, though invalid, can produce effects like freedom. This is based on istihsan.
3
Compare with Zufar's position
Zufar says he does not become free unless he pays the value, because wine is not property. Understand the difference in reasoning.
4
Distinguish between wine/pork and carcass/blood
The text differentiates: wine and pork are property in general, so the contract is fasid; carcass and blood are not property at all, so the contract is batil and no freedom occurs.
This distinction is central to the Hanafi proof.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical outcome
The relied-upon position is that the slave is freed and must pay the value. Identify the authority and the practical consequence for fatwa.

Ibn Farishta said: This is if the master is Muslim; because if a disbeliever makes kitabah with his disbelieving slave then becomes Muslim, he does not become free by paying wine by agreement. I say: the Muslim does not become free by paying wine, because since wine is not property in his case, the apparent from his situation is that he intended suspension upon payment, so he becomes free by the payment. As for the disbeliever, since wine is property in his case, the apparent is that suspension is absent in his case; rather he intended the consideration. By Islam, its being consideration is negated and the suspension is absent, so he does not become free by paying the value of the wine.' Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the first part of the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding the ruling of a mukatab (slave under a contract of manumission) who pays wine (khamr) as the consideration. The passage is a ruling with proof. The author, Imam al-Marghinani, begins by explaining that if the mukatab pays wine before the judge annuls the contract due to its invalidity, he becomes free. This is because the contract, although invalid (fasid), takes effect and can produce results before annulment. The slave must then work to pay the value (qimah) of the wine, as he becomes free but must compensate for the invalidity. The text then presents several points: (1) Zufar's opposition: Zufar holds that the slave does not become free unless he pays the value of the wine, not the wine itself, because wine is not property (mal) in Islamic law. (2) A difference within the Hanafi school: According to Abu Hanifa, if the master said 'If you pay, you are free' (ta'liq), the slave becomes free by paying wine; if he did not say that, the slave is not freed. The apparent narration (Zahir al-Riwayah) supports unconditional freedom upon paying wine. (3) The distinction between wine/pork and carcass/blood: wine and pork are considered property in general (mal fi al-jumla), so the contract is fasid but can produce effects; carcass and blood are not property at all, so the contract is void (batil) and does not produce freedom unless by explicit suspension. (4) The case of a disbelieving master: if he converts to Islam after the kitabah, the ruling changes because wine is not property for Muslims. The Hanafi position is that the slave is freed and must pay the value, and this is mufta bihi.

Key Terms
المكاتب (al-mukatab): A slave who has entered into a contract of kitabah with his master, agreeing to pay a sum for his freedom.
الخمر (al-khamr): Wine; considered property in general (mal fi al-jumla) for the purpose of kitabah, though not for other contracts.
القيمة (al-qimah): The monetary value that the slave must pay after being freed by paying wine, equivalent to the value of the wine.

Wala' of Child When Freed Woman Marries a Non-Arab
Ruling on wala' of child of freed woman married to non-Arab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the wala' of a child born to a freed woman married to a non-Arab with muwalat, and explain the internal ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
For wala', actual manumission beats contractual muwalat.

In this passage, the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a ruling on who inherits the wala' of a child when a freed woman marries a non-Arab man who has muwalat (clientage by agreement). While the initial statement seems straightforward, the subsequent commentary reveals an internal Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold one view, while Abu Yusuf dissents. The key is the relative strength of manumission versus muwalat. Let's explore the categories of husbands and the reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
What exactly is being disputed? The wala' of a child when the mother is a freed woman (mu'taqah) and the father is a non-Arab (ajami) who has wala' of muwalat.
Muwalat is a contractual agreement of clientage, not actual manumission.
2
State the main positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the child's wala' remains with the mother's patron. Abu Yusuf: if the father has muwalat, his patron gets the wala'.
3
Examine the five categories of husbands
The text categorizes husbands into five types: slave, mukatab, freedman (mu'taq), one with muwalat, or Arab/non-Arab. For each, determine whether the child's wala' goes to mother's or father's patron, noting where the ikhtilaf occurs.
Slave and mukatab: all imams agree child's wala' goes to mother's patron. For other three, the dispute exists.
4
Analyze the basis of ikhtilaf
Why does Abu Yusuf differ? He considers muwalat a cause of wala' equivalent to manumission, so the father's patron is like a manumitter. The majority view holds that muwalat is weaker and only actual manumission creates the strong wala' that attracts the child.
The hadith 'al-wala' li man a'taqa' (wala' belongs to the one who frees) is cited implicitly.
5
Apply the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as stated in the matn and most commentaries. Understand the practical consequence: the child's inheritance and 'aql go to the mother's patron, not the father's muwalat patron.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute between the Hanafi imams regarding the wala' of a child born to a freed woman married to a non-Arab (ajami) who has wala' of muwalat (clientage by agreement). According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), the child's wala' remains with the mother's patrons, even if the father has muwalat, because muwalat is a weaker form of wala' compared to actual manumission. Abu Yusuf argues that muwalat is a cause of wala' just like actual manumission, so if the father has muwalat, he attracts the child's wala' to his own patrons (i.e., the patron of his muwalat). The text also enumerates five categories of husbands for a freed woman: slave, mukatab, freedman (mu'taq), one with muwalat, and Arab/non-Arab. In the first two categories (slave and mukatab), all agree the child's wala' goes to mother's patrons because the father lacks legal capacity. In the remaining three categories (freedman, muwalat, Arab/non-Arab), Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold the child's wala' remains with mother's patrons, while Abu Yusuf holds it goes to the father's patrons if the father has a wala' connection (either by manumission or muwalat). The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as it is the majority view and the text of Kanz al-Daqa'iq follows it. The passage illustrates the Hanafi internal ikhtilaf on the weight of muwalat relative to manumission, with Abu Yusuf giving it equal status, while the other two imams consider it secondary.

Key Terms
الموالاة (al-muwalat): A contractual relationship where a free person (without known patrons) agrees to be a client of another, with mutual inheritance rights.
عجمي (ajami): A non-Arab, specifically non-Qurashi and non-Arab, used here as an example of a free person who may have muwalat.
المعتق (al-mu'taq): A freed slave (by manumission), as opposed to a free-born Arab or one with muwalat.

Coercion and the Validity of Freeing a Slave and Divorce
Ruling that coercion does not invalidate freeing or divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that coercion does not invalidate the acts of freeing a slave (itq) and divorce (talaq), and distinguish these from contracts that require consent.

Core Idea
Freeing slaves and divorce are valid even under coercion because they do not require consent.

This lesson examines a distinctive Hanafi ruling: acts that do not require the doer's consent, such as freeing a slave (itq) and divorce (talaq), remain legally effective even when performed under coercion. The passage contrasts these with contracts like sale, which require mutual consent and are thus invalidated by coercion. It then applies the same principle to marriage under coercion, clarifying the consequences for dower and the rights of the coerced party.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their ruling
Recognize that the passage addresses two specific acts: freeing a slave (itq) and divorce (talaq). The ruling is that they take effect (يقع) even when performed under coercion.
2
Locate the rationale
The author explains that coercion does not nullify legal capacity (أهلية). The invalidity of contracts like sale under coercion stems from the requirement of consent (رضا), which is absent here. For itq and talaq, consent is not a condition, so they are valid.
Compare with the example of jest (هزل): these acts also take effect in jest, confirming that intention is not required.
3
Extend to marriage under coercion
The passage applies the same logic to marriage: coercion does not invalidate the contract. However, adjustments are made regarding the dower (mahr). The dower must be proper (mahr al-mithl); any excess is void. The coercer is not liable for compensation.
The text distinguishes between a man coerced to marry and a woman coerced into marriage, with different rules for the husband and the coercer.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
There is a disagreement among the Hanafi masters regarding the completion of the dower when a woman is coerced into marriage. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the guardian has the right to complete the proper dower, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree.
This is a secondary detail; the main ruling that coercion does not invalidate the marriage itself is agreed upon.
5
Apply to modern contexts
Consider how this ruling applies today: divorce pronouncements made under duress (e.g., threats) are valid in Hanafi law, which differs from other schools that require free choice. Coerced contractual agreements (e.g., forced sale) remain void.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when the coercer is liable for half mahr (or mut'ah) in a coerced divorce before consummation, and explain the role of the husband's intention (insha vs. ikhbar) in determining liability.

Core Idea
The coercer pays half mahr because his coercion destroyed the husband's chance to avoid payment.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq: when a husband is forced to divorce his wife before consummation, who bears the financial burden of half the mahr? The answer depends on the husband's intention at the moment of utterance and on whether consummation occurred. We will explore the legal reasoning (talil) behind the coercer's liability and the conditions that limit it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
The passage states: if a man is coerced to divorce before consummation, the coercer guarantees half the mahr. Start by noting this core ruling and its scope: it applies only before consummation.

The author provides a legal reasoning: the husband's obligation to pay half mahr was on the verge of falling away due to a cause from the wife's side (e.g., apostasy). The husband's recourse against the coercer depends on his intent. If he intended a real divorce (insha), the coercer pays. The husband's claim of ikhbar is not accepted in court, but it affects the religious ruling and liability.

If no mahr was specified, the coercer pays mut'ah (consolation gift). If the husband is coerced to both free a slave and divorce, he recovers the lesser of the slave's value or half mahr. Note the principle: harm is repelled by the lesser.

If coercion occurs after consummation, the coercer is not liable for anything because the full mahr was already owed by the husband. No loss was inflicted.
After dukhul, the entire mahr is due, so the husband suffered no new financial burden from the divorce.

Manumission and Expiation Under Coercion
Rulings on coerced future manumission, suspended manumission, and expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between different scenarios of coerced manumission and expiation, and determine liability and validity.

Core Idea
Coerced manumission may be valid but liability depends on whether the act was avoidable; expiation under coercion does not count as valid.

This lesson deals with complex scenarios where coercion interacts with manumission (freeing slaves) and expiation (kaffara). The rulings depend on the type of act: future manumission, suspended manumission on an unavoidable act, and coercion to perform expiation. The key is whether the coerced person had an alternative and whether the act is considered a paid freeing or a pure act of worship.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the three scenarios
Identify three cases: (1) future manumission statement, (2) suspended manumission on an unavoidable act, (3) coerced expiation or freeing a slave for expiation.
Each has a different ruling regarding validity and liability.
2
Apply the rule for future manumission
If coerced to say 'every slave I own in the future is free', it is valid and no liability on coercer because it is considered the owner's own act.
The coercion was in making the statement, not in the subsequent acquisition.
3
Apply the rule for suspended manumission on unavoidable acts
If coerced to suspend freeing on an act he must do (e.g., pray, eat), the slave is freed and the coercer pays value because the person had no choice.
The act is unavoidable, so coercion directly causes the loss.
4
Apply the rule for coerced expiation
If coerced to perform expiation (kaffara), he cannot recover from coercer. If coerced to free a slave for expiation, the freeing is valid but does not count as expiation; coercer pays value.
Expiation is a religious duty; coercion to do it is not actionable. Freeing for expiation under coercion is like a sale, so expiation invalid.

Liability for Half Mahr When Compelling Divorce Before Consummation
Ruling on liability of the one who compels divorce before consummation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on compulsion to divorce, distinguishing when the compeller is liable for half the mahr and when not.

Core Idea
If a man is compelled to divorce his wife before intercourse, the compeller must pay half the mahr because his compulsion fixed the obligation.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses a nuanced scenario: a man is forced to divorce his wife before consummation. The Hanafi school holds the compeller financially liable for half the mahr, reasoning that the compulsion turned a potential loss into a fixed liability. The passage also explores conditions like the husband's intent, unnamed mahr, and multiple compulsion options.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is compulsion (ikrah) to divorce before consummation. The ruling is that the one who compels (mukrih) must pay half the mahr (dower) to the wife, as a liability.
This liability applies only to the half mahr that becomes due upon divorce before consummation.
2
Extract the legal reasoning (talil)
The husband's mahr obligation was on the verge of dropping by separation from the wife's side through sin (like apostasy). The divorce confirmed that obligation, so the compeller caused the fixation (taqrir) of that property, making him liable as a destroyer of it.
The reasoning is rooted in causation: the compeller's act directly caused the loss of the half mahr.
3
Note the condition of the husband's intention
If the husband intended to create the divorce immediately as demanded (inmaa), the compeller pays. If he intended false reporting (ikhbar kadhiban), the divorce is effective in court but not religiously, and the compeller does not pay because the husband disobeyed.
The husband's inner intent affects the compeller's liability in the religious (diyaanatan) sense.
4
Extend to unnamed mahr and multiple options
If the mahr was not specified, the compeller pays the mut'ah (a fair gift). If forced to choose between freeing a slave or divorcing, the compeller pays the lesser of the slave's value or half the mahr, because harm is repelled by the lesser amount.
This shows the principle of minimizing harm (darar) in liability.
5
Apply the cutoff: after consummation
If the divorce occurs after consummation, the compeller pays nothing because the full mahr is already due to the wife, and no property is destroyed from the husband's side.
Consummation changes the liability since the wife is entitled to full mahr.

The reasoning is that the husband's financial obligation (the mahr) was about to drop due to separation from the wife's side by sin (like apostasy), but the divorce fixed that obligation, so the one whose compulsion caused that fixing is liable. However, this liability applies only if the husband intended to create the divorce immediately as demanded. If he intended false reporting (i.e., he said the divorce but intended to report something else), the divorce takes effect in court but not religiously, and he is not compensated because he disobeyed the compeller. If the mahr was not specified, the compeller pays the mut'ah. If one is compelled to either free a slave or divorce his wife, he recovers the lesser of the slave's value or half the mahr because the harm is repelled by the lesser. After consummation, no recovery is due because no property was destroyed.

The Child of a Usurped Slave-Girl: Cross-Madhhab Dispute
Cross-madhhab dispute on the status of a child born to a usurped slave-girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that the child of a usurped slave-girl is not guaranteed and contrast it with the Shafi'i view, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on the option for birth defect.

Core Idea
The child of a usurped slave-girl is not guaranteed in Hanafi law, unlike the Shafi'i school.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a key cross-madhhab dispute regarding the status of a child born to a usurped slave-girl. According to the Hanafi school, the child is not guaranteed (ghayr madmun) — it is considered separate property of the usurper. Imam al-Shafi'i, however, holds that the child is guaranteed like the mother. The passage also notes an intra-Hanafi variance: Zufar denies the owner the option to claim compensation for the defect of childbirth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
The central issue: is the child born to a usurped slave-girl considered part of the usurped property?
2
State the Hanafi position
According to the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad), the child is not guaranteed. It is not owned by the original owner.
The text says: 'الولد عندنا غير مضمون'.
3
State the Shafi'i position
Al-Shafi'i holds that the child is guaranteed, meaning it must be returned or compensated.
The text says: 'وعند الشافعي مضمون'.
4
Note the consensus and intra-Hanafi difference
If the usurper destroys the child, there is consensus (ijma') that he must pay its value. However, Hanafis differ on whether the owner has the option to claim for the defect of birth: the general Hanafi view allows it, but Zufar does not.
The text says: 'ويتخير بنقصان الولادة عندنا، وعند زفر لا يتخير'.

Pregnancy from Zina in Usurped Slave-Girl: Istihsan vs Qiyas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on compensating overlapping defects from zina and pregnancy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi positions on compensating defects from zina and pregnancy in a usurped slave-girl, distinguishing the istihsan of Abu Yusuf from the qiyas of Muhammad, and apply the ruling to various outcomes.

Core Idea
On compensating two defects from zina and pregnancy, Abu Yusuf's istihsan (guarantee only the greater) is mufta bihi over Muhammad's qiyas (guarantee both).

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute regarding compensation for two overlapping defects when a usurped slave-girl becomes pregnant from zina. The key disagreement is between Abu Yusuf, who applies istihsan to guarantee only the greater of the two defects (zina defect or pregnancy defect), and Muhammad, who holds by qiyas that both defects must be compensated. The text further explores scenarios after birth, death in childbirth, and the effect of the child's value.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
A usurper has intercourse (zina) with a usurped slave-girl, who becomes pregnant. The usurper wishes to return her to the owner. She now has two defects: the defect of zina (loss of value due to being an adulteress) and the defect of pregnancy (temporary reduction in value).
2
Define the two defects
The arsh (compensation) for zina is the decrease in value because the slave-girl is now known to have committed zina. The arsh for pregnancy is the decrease in value due to her being pregnant.
These are separate defects.
3
Present Abu Yusuf's istihsan
Abu Yusuf adopts istihsan: the usurper guarantees only the greater of the two defects. The lesser is included in the greater, so no double payment.
The text says: 'وهذا استحسان أخذ به أبو يوسف'.
4
Present Muhammad's qiyas
Muhammad holds by qiyas: the usurper must guarantee both defects fully. Since they are separate, each must be compensated.
The text says: 'والقياس أن يضمن الأمرين جميعاً وهو قول محمد'.
5
Apply to various outcomes
After birth, the defect of pregnancy disappears, leaving only the zina defect. If the slave-girl dies in childbirth, Abu Hanifa holds the usurper guarantees full value, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad limit to the pregnancy defect. If the child dies, the value of the child may offset the defect.
These applications show the practical implications of the positions.

رهن (rahn): A pledge or collateral in a contract of mortgage.

Checkpoint
If the mortgagee destroys a pledge worth $1000 when the debt is $500 and the debt is deferred, what happens according to the Hanafi school?
A) The mortgagee must pay $1000 immediately and the debt is cancelled.
B) The mortgagee pays $500 (value at time of contract) and it becomes a new pledge until the debt is due.
C) The mortgagee pays $1000 and the debt remains.
D) The mortgagee is not liable because the pledge was destroyed by his action.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, the mortgagee's destruction of the pledge obligates him to pay its value at the time of the contract, which then stands as a new pledge; a third party's destruction triggers liability at the time of destruction.
