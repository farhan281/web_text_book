Rulings on Hajj and Ihram Penalties for an Interdicted Person
Rulings on hajj, umrah, and penalties for violations by an interdicted person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules for an interdicted person performing obligatory vs voluntary hajj and identify which ihram violations require immediate fasting versus delayed monetary expiation.

Core Idea
Obligatory hajj cannot be prevented; monetary penalties for ihram violations are suspended until financial soundness.

This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the delicate balance between the right to perform hajj and the protection of creditors' rights. The Hanafi school ensures that obligatory worship is not obstructed, while delaying monetary penalties until the person regains fiscal responsibility. The text also compares the interdicted person to a slave, providing a clear legal analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling status
Determine whether the intended hajj is obligatory (hajj al-Islam) or voluntary. Obligatory hajj cannot be prevented because it is a duty from Allah; voluntary hajj is allowed with restrictions.
The same applies to umrah: it is obligatory and cannot be blocked.
2
Categorize the ihram violation
For each type of violation (hunting, shaving, wearing stitched clothes, applying perfume, intercourse, etc.), identify whether the penalty involves fasting or monetary payment (e.g., badanah, sheep).
If the penalty is fasting, the interdicted person can perform it immediately; if monetary, it is delayed.
3
Apply the suspension rule for monetary penalties
Monetary penalties (e.g., badanah for intercourse after Arafah before tawaf) are not paid until the person becomes 'sound' (yuslih) — i.e., no longer interdicted.
This mirrors the rule for a slave who commits ihram violations with his master's permission.
4
Distinguish between completion scenarios
If the person returns home without completing tawaf al-ziyarah, expenses for returning are provided. But if they performed tawaf while junub, no return expenses are given, and additional penalties apply.
This shows attention to the severity of performing tawaf in a state of major impurity.

Commentary
This passage addresses the rules for an interdicted person performing hajj. He cannot be prevented from performing the obligatory hajj (hajj al-Islam) because it is a duty imposed by Allah directly; he has no role in making it obligatory. The same applies to umrah. If he commits ihram violations (like hunting, shaving, wearing stitched clothes, applying perfume), the penalty depends on the type: if the penalty is fasting (e.g., for shaving due to need), he can fast immediately. If the penalty involves monetary payment (e.g., badanah for intercourse, sheep for certain violations), it is postponed until he becomes 'sound' (i.e., no longer interdicted). He is compared to a slave: when a slave commits ihram violations with his master's permission, monetary penalties are delayed. The passage also discusses the scenario where he completes most of hajj but neglects certain tawafs: traveling back to complete tawaf ziyarah is allowed and expenses are provided, but if he performed tawaf while junub, he is penalized and no return expenses are given. For voluntary hajj, expenses are given modestly; he may walk to save money. If he has extra funds from the allowance, he may use it for better transportation, as long as it is not extravagance. For ihsar (being prevented from completing hajj), the rules vary: for obligatory hajj, a sacrificial animal is sent to release him; for voluntary hajj, he does not send an animal unless absolutely necessary. Importantly, the judge does not hand over the entire expense amount to the interdicted person directly but gives it to a trustee, to prevent waste. The trustee spends up to one-third of the interdicted person's wealth on travel if he has such wealth.

Definition and Conditions of Udhiya (Sacrifice) in Hanafi Fiqh
Opening of Kitab al-Udhiya with definition, conditions, and classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define udhiya according to the Hanafi school, state its conditions of obligation and performance, and identify the animals eligible for sacrifice.

Core Idea
Udhiya is a wajib sacrifice of a specific animal (camel, cow, sheep, goat) performed with intention on Eid days; its conditions include residence, wealth, and Islam.

This passage from Bahr al-Raiq opens the Book of Sacrifice by explaining its placement and then providing the linguistic and technical definition of udhiya. It distinguishes between conditions of obligation and conditions of performance, and clarifies the unique nature of udhiya as combining both destruction (bloodshed) and charity (meat distribution). We will unpack the Hanafi framework for this act of worship.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the structure
The passage begins with a discussion of why Kitab al-Udhiya follows Kitab al-Dhaba'ih, then gives linguistic and technical definitions, then lists conditions and types.
Note the logical flow from general (slaughter) to specific (sacrifice).
2
Extract the technical definition
Identify the four types of animals (camel, cow, sheep, goat) and the specific age requirement (jadha' from sheep).
The definition includes intention (qurbah) and specific days.
3
Identify the conditions of obligation
Note: resident, wealthy, Muslim. Note that puberty and sanity are not conditions.
This means even minors may have udhiya performed from their wealth.
4
Identify the conditions of performance
Time (after Eid prayer for urban dwellers), location (place of sacrifice), cause (dawn of 10th Dhul Hijjah).
The cause (sabab) triggers the obligation, while time and location regulate performance.
5
Understand the dual nature
Udhiya combines destruction (bloodshed) and transfer of ownership (charity), distinguishing it from other financial acts of worship.
This explains why it is considered a unique form of qurbah maliyyah.

The Ruling on a Muhrim Directing a Non-Muhrim to Game: Two Hanafi Narrations
Presents two narrations on whether a Muhrim can direct a non-Muhrim to game

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions within the Hanafi school on a Muhrim directing a non-Muhrim to game, explain the proof for each, and identify the authority weight of each narration.

Core Idea
A Muhrim who directs a Halal person to game—between two Hanafi texts: one permits, one prohibits based on the hadith of Qatada.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf concerning a Muhrim who points out game to a non-Muhrim. Does the prohibition of hunting for the Muhrim extend to indirect assistance? Two narrations are cited: one from al-Ziyadat (Zahir al-Riwayah) permits it, while another from al-Muntaqa (nawadir) from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad prohibits it, grounding their view in the hadith of Qatada. Understanding this difference is key to appreciating how later Hanafi authorities weigh conflicting transmissions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and legal question
The act: a Muhrim (person in ihram) directs (dalla) a halal person (non-Muhrim) to game. The non-Muhrim kills the game. Can the Muhrim eat the meat? The question is whether the Muhrim's indirect role makes the game prohibited for him.
The ruling concerns the Muhrim's permissibility to eat, not the validity of the hunt itself.
2
State the first narration and its reasoning
According to al-Ziyadat, the game is halal for the Muhrim. The reasoning: the killing was done by the halal person, not by the Muhrim's direction. The cause (sabab) of slaughter is the act of the halal person, not the guidance of the Muhrim.
Al-Ziyadat is among the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah, giving this narration strong authority.
3
State the second narration and its proof
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad in al-Muntaqa, the game is not halal. They cite the hadith of Qatada: the Prophet asked companions if they helped or pointed, and they said no, after which he permitted them to eat. The hadith links permissibility to the absence of assistance. Pointing is a form of assistance, so the Muhrim's direction negates permissibility.
Al-Muntaqa is a collection of nawadir (non-standard narrations), so this position has lower authority but is still transmitted.
4
Compare authority weight and identify thamarah al-khilaf
The first narration (halal) is from Zahir al-Riwayah, which is the most authoritative layer. The second (haram) is from nawadir. The passage does not specify which is mufta bihi. The practical difference: a Muhrim who directs game should avoid eating it according to one position, but may eat it according to the other.
In later fatwa practice, the position of al-Ziyadat is often preferred due to its stronger authority, but caution may incline towards prohibition.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute. The first narration from al-Ziyadat (one of the six core books of Zahir al-Riwayah) states that if a person in ihram (Muhrim) points out or directs a non-Muhrim to game, and the non-Muhrim kills it, it is halal for the Muhrim to eat. The reasoning is that the actual killing was done by the halal person, and the Muhrim's direction did not directly cause the death; it was just guidance. However, in al-Muntaqa, a narration from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad states that it is not halal. They cite the hadith of Qatada: when the Prophet asked the companions if they helped or pointed, and they said no, he permitted them to eat. The hadith links permissibility (ibahah) to the absence of assistance (i'ana). Since pointing (dalala) is a form of assistance, the Muhrim's direction makes the game haram for him. Thus, there are two positions within the Hanafi school: one allowing it (from al-Ziyadat) and one prohibiting it (from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). The text does not explicitly state which is relied upon.

حلال (halal (here)): A person who is not in Ihram, thus permitted to hunt.
إعانة (i'ana): Assistance; in this context, any form of help in hunting, including pointing.

Ruling on the Validity of Hunting by Different Persons
Prohibition of hunting by Majusi, idolater, apostate, and Muhrim; permissibility by Kitabi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether the game hunted by a Majusi, idolater, apostate, Muhrim, and Kitabi is permissible according to the Hanafi school, and explain the qiyas reasoning behind the ruling.

Core Idea
Only those valid for voluntary slaughter are valid for forced slaughter (hunting).

This passage establishes a clear principle in Hanafi fiqh: the permissibility of eating game hunted by a non-Muslim depends on whether that person is eligible to perform valid slaughter (dhabh) in normal circumstances. The author applies this to four categories: Majusi, idolater, apostate, Muhrim (in ihram), and Kitabi. Understanding this qiyas-based ruling helps clarify the boundaries of lawful food from hunting.

1
Identify the four categories of hunters
The passage lists: Majusi (Zoroastrian), idolater (wathani), apostate (murtadd), and Muhrim (in ihram). These are prohibited. Then it mentions Kitabi (Jew/Christian) as permitted.
Note that Muhrim is temporarily prohibited due to ihram, not permanent.
2
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is that only those who are 'ahl al-dhakāt' (people valid for slaughter) in the case of choice (ikhtiyār) are considered valid in the case of necessity (idtirār) through hunting. This is a qiyas (analogy) between voluntary slaughter and forced slaughter (hunting).
'Forced slaughter' here means killing by any means (e.g., arrow, trap) as opposed to throat-cutting.
3
Apply the principle to each category
Majusi, idolater, and apostate are not ahl al-dhakāt because their slaughter is invalid entirely. Muhrim is also not ahl al-dhakāt regarding game because he is prohibited from killing game voluntarily. Kitabi is ahl al-dhakāt, so his hunting is valid.
Apostates are treated as non-Muslims whose slaughter is invalid.
4
Derive the ruling for each
Thus, the game is haram for the first four and halal for the Kitabi. The ruling is based solely on the status of the hunter, not the method of killing.
The ruling applies regardless of the hunter's intention or recitation.

He (the author) said: And the game (hunted) by a Majusi, an idolater, and an apostate is prohibited, because they are not among the people valid for slaughter in the case of choice, so likewise in the case of necessity. And the game of a Kitabi (Scriptury) is eaten, because he is among the people of voluntary slaughter, so likewise forced slaughter.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling on who may lawfully hunt game (catch and kill) for Hanafis. The principle is: only those who are valid to perform voluntary slaughter (dhabh ikhtiyari) are valid to perform forced slaughter (dhabh idtirari) through hunting. A Majusi (Zoroastrian), an idolater (wathani), and an apostate (murtadd) are not considered people of the book and their slaughter is invalid, whether voluntary or forced. Therefore, any game they hunt is not halal. A Muhrim (one in the state of ihram for Hajj/Umrah) is also prohibited from hunting game; his hunting is invalid because he is not allowed to kill game voluntarily, so he cannot perform forced slaughter either. In contrast, a Kitabi (Jew or Christian) is valid for voluntary slaughter, so his forced slaughter (hunting) is also valid, and the game he hunts is halal. This ruling is based on qiyas: the capacity for voluntary slaughter implies capacity for forced slaughter, and vice versa.

Key Terms
المجوسي (al-majūsī): A Zoroastrian (fire-worshipper); their slaughter is invalid.
المحرم (al-muḥrim): A person in the state of ihram for pilgrimage; prohibited from hunting.
الذكاة (al-dhakāt): The Islamic method of slaughter; validity requires the slaughterer to be Muslim or Kitabi.
