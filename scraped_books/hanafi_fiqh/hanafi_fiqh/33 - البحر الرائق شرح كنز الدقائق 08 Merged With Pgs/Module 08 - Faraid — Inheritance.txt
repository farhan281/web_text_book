The Layered Kitaba Case of al-Ziyadat: Iqrar, Payment, and Wala'
Case study of layered kitaba and iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for sequential and simultaneous payment in a multi-tiered kitaba chain involving a person of unknown lineage, and identify who gains wala' in each scenario.

Core Idea
A confession of slavery by a free person only binds themselves, not third parties, leading to intricate ownership and patronage outcomes.

This passage from al-Ziyadat presents a layered case of kitaba and iqrar that tests the boundaries of Hanafi rulings on wala' and ownership when a person of unknown lineage confesses slavery. Understanding this case requires tracking the effects of iqrar and payment sequences across tiers. The rulings demonstrate how Hanafi principles maintain consistency in complex hypotheticals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial chain
A man of unknown lineage (MNL) buys a slave and makes him a mukatab (higher mukatab). That mukatab then buys a female slave and makes her a mukataba (lower mukataba). Note the tiers: MNL → higher mukatab → lower mukataba.
MNL's status as free is presumed until iqrar.
2
Understand the iqrar and its effect
MNL confesses that he is the slave of the higher mukatab. The passage states this iqrar is valid against himself but does not affect the rights of the lower mukatab. So the higher mukatab remains free vis-à-vis the lower mukatab and remains a mukatab for a free man.
Key principle: iqrar against self is valid; iqrar against others is not.
3
Analyze sequential payment scenarios
The case considers two possible sequences: higher pays first then lower, or lower pays first then higher. The first one to pay becomes free with no wala' for the other (since the other is still a slave/mukatab and not eligible for wala'). The second to pay becomes free and his wala' goes to the first because the first became free and thus eligible for wala'.
Wala' follows freedom; timing matters.
4
Analyze simultaneous payment
If both pay at the same moment, both become free simultaneously. Since neither was free at the moment the other was freed, neither has wala' over the other.
Simultaneity cancels wala' eligibility.
5
Analyze default scenarios
If one defaults, he becomes the property of the other. If the higher defaults, he becomes owned by the lower mukataba (since he is part of MNL's earnings which became hers via iqrar). If the lower defaults, she becomes the property of the higher, and MNL becomes joint property. If both default, the lower becomes free (she never paid but her status changes?), and MNL and the higher become her slaves.
Default leads to ownership inversion; careful with 'if both default' ruling.

Two Masters: One Frees, Other Makes Mudabbir – Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of tadbir after freeing by another master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the validity of tadbir after a co-owner frees the slave, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Emancipation is indivisible: the Sahibayn invalidate tadbir after freeing; the first master does not guarantee.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute. When a jointly-owned slave is freed by one master, can the other master still make him a mudabbir? The answer depends on a fundamental principle: can emancipation be partial? Let's examine the positions of the founding imams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act: one co-owner frees the slave, then the other makes him a mudabbir. The dispute: Is the tadbir valid? Does the first master guarantee anything?
The first master's act is complete freeing; the second attempts tadbir.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa holds the tadbir valid and the first master does not guarantee. His proof: emancipation can be partial in joint ownership; the first master frees only his share, leaving the other share owned, so tadbir is possible.
Illa: ownership is divisible.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold the tadbir invalid and the second master must guarantee the slave's value if solvent. Their proof: emancipation is indivisible (la yatajazzā'); freeing by one master frees the entire slave, so no ownership remains for tadbir.
Illa: indivisibility of emancipation.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the slave becomes free upon the second master's death (as mudabbir). Under the Sahibayn, the slave is free immediately upon the first freeing, and the second master must pay the value.
The guarantee is wajib only if solvent.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's position, as it is more cautious in protecting the slave's freedom and consistent with the principle of indivisibility of emancipation.
Tarjih: caution and consistency with other rulings.

Commentary

Death of Mukatab: Contract Not Dissolved
Inter-madhhab dispute on kitaba dissolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the effect of a mukatab's death on the kitaba contract and explain the key point of dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
A mukatab's death does not dissolve the contract; his heirs may complete the payment and secure his freedom.

When a mukatab (a slave under a contract of manumission) dies, what happens to the kitaba contract? The Hanafi school, following Ibn Mas'ud, holds that the contract remains valid and the heirs may complete the payment to free the deceased. The Shafi'i school, following Zaid bin Thabit, argues that death dissolves the contract. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind each position and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the kitaba contract is not dissolved upon the mukatab's death. The contract continues, and the heirs may pay the remaining kitaba from his estate.
The source phrase: 'وإن مات وله مال لم تفسخ'
2
Identify the opposing position
Recognize that Zaid bin Thabit and Imam al-Shafi'i hold that the kitaba is dissolved by death.
Opposing view based on the impossibility of freeing a corpse.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The opponent argues that freedom cannot be established before death (no payment) or after death (dead body cannot receive manumission). Therefore, the contract's purpose becomes impossible.
Key concept: 'محل لنزول العتق' – a valid locus for emancipation.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds that the master can effect manumission after death (as in tadbir) and the heirs stand in the place of the mukatab. The contract's purpose (freedom for the deceased's estate) remains achievable.
The text cuts off but implies continuation: the master is an agent of manumission, not the subject.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Hanafi law, heirs pay the kitaba, freeing the deceased, and the estate is inherited by them. Under Shafi'i law, the contract dissolves, and the estate returns to the master.
The thamarah al-khilaf directly affects inheritance.

Inheritance of Children of a Mukatab
Case rulings on inheritance of mukatab's children

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the inheritance rules for children of a mukatab, distinguishing between children born during kitaba and free children, and apply the rule that a child who dies before payment does not inherit from the father.

Core Idea
Children born during kitaba do not inherit from the father if they die before the kitaba is paid; only free children inherit.

This section from Nawadir and other sources presents specific case rulings on inheritance when a mukatab dies and leaves children. The key principle is that a child's right to inherit depends on being free at the father's death. Children born during kitaba are slaves until the kitaba is paid, so they cannot inherit if they predecease the father or die before payment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of children
Children can be born during kitaba (still slaves) or born before and free. Also, children can be co-mukatabs if included in the contract.
Distinguish between free sons and slave children.
2
Apply the rule for a child who dies before payment
If a child born during kitaba dies before the father pays the kitaba, that child does not inherit from the father. His own estate goes to his mother and siblings.
The child's inheritance is determined at the time of his death, not the father's.
3
Handle the case of a co-mukatab child
If a child was a co-mukatab (included in the contract) and dies after the father but before kitaba is paid, he does not inherit from the father because he was a slave at the father's death.
Co-mukatab children are also considered slaves until payment.
4
Apply the principle to multiple heirs
When there are free children and slave children, only free children inherit. Slave children may inherit from each other if they are of the same status.
The case from al-Muntaqa illustrates distribution between a free son and a child born during kitaba.

Debt Priority with Reasoning from al-Muhit
Detailed ranking of debts with legal reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reproduce the detailed order of debt priority from al-Muhit and explain the reasoning (talil) for each ranking based on the strength of the obligation.

Core Idea
Debts are paid in order of strength: outsider debt, then injury compensation, then kitaba, then unauthorised dower, with reasoning for each.

In al-Muhit, we find the most detailed ranking of claims on a mukatab's estate. Each debt is assigned a strength based on its nature: whether it is compensation for something (عوض من كل وجه or من وجه), whether it can be nullified by inability, and whether it was contracted with the master's permission. This reasoning (talil) is essential for understanding Hanafi jurisprudence.

1
List the four categories in order
From the passage: first outsider debt, then injury compensation (dayn al-jinaya), then kitaba (badal al-kitaba), then dower of a wife married without permission (mahr).
Also note the master's debt if the mukatab owes the master.
2
Analyze the reasoning for outsider debt vs injury
Outsider debt is stronger because it is compensation from every aspect (عوض من كل وجه). Injury debt is compensation from one aspect because its substitute is not property (the injury itself is not property).
Thus outsider debt takes precedence.
3
Compare injury debt to kitaba
Injury debt is stronger than kitaba because it is not nullified by inability (لا يسقط بالعجز). The kitaba falls if the mukatab becomes unable to pay.
So injury debt is paid before kitaba.
4
Compare kitaba to unauthorised dower
Kitaba is stronger because it was contracted with the master's permission, whereas the dower was contracted by a person under interdiction (محجور عليه).
Hence kitaba is paid before dower.

Key Terms
دين الجناية (dayn al-jināyah): Debt arising from injury caused by the mukatab.
بدل الجناية (badal al-jināyah): Compensation for injury.
محجور عليه (maḥjūr ʿalayhi): A person under legal interdiction, such as a slave or a minor, who cannot contract independently.

Key Takeaway
Debt priority is determined by the strength of the obligation: pure compensation debts are strongest, followed by partial compensation, then debts subject to nullification, then debts from unauthorized contracts.

Jointly Owned Slave Made Mukatab: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on jointly owned slave made mukatab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether a jointly owned slave made a mukatab becomes fully a mukatab or only half, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
According to Abu Hanifa, only half of a jointly owned slave becomes a mukatab; the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say all of him becomes a mukatab.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) on the status of a slave jointly owned by two masters when one makes him a mukatab without the other's permission. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that only the share of the master who made the contract becomes a mukatab, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sāḥibayn) hold that the entire slave becomes a mukatab. This has significant implications for the slave's earnings and the rights of the other master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and actions
Two masters jointly own a slave. One master makes the slave a mukatab without the other's permission.
The slave then dies leaving earnings (kasb).
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa says the slave died in a state of inability (عاجز) because only half of him became a mukatab. Neither master has a claim on his earnings.
The slave's earnings are not subject to kitaba payment because the kitaba was only partial? Actually he died unable, so the kitaba is null.
3
State the position of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)
The two say the entire slave becomes a mukatab. All his earnings are his property. He pays the kitaba from his earnings, and he must guarantee half of his value to the non-consenting master.
Thus, the contract is effective for the whole slave, but the other master must be compensated.
4
Determine the practical difference
Under Abu Hanifa, the slave dies in slavery and his earnings go to the masters? Actually he says 'la sabila li-wahidin minhuma 'ala kasbihi' – neither has a way to his earnings. So earnings are lost? Under the two, the slave pays kitaba and the other master gets half his value.
The difference affects the rights of the masters and the slave's freedom.

Wala' Is Not Annulled: The Qiyas with 'Asaba
Ruling with proof via qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that wala' is not annulled but superseded, using the qiyas with 'asaba to justify the ruling.

Core Idea
Wala' is like 'asaba: never annulled, only superseded by a stronger right.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq establishes the foundational rule for the wala' of children born to a freed female slave. The author uses a powerful analogy: just as a brother's 'asaba status is not voided when a son appears but is merely superseded, so too the mother's patrons' wala' is not annulled when a stronger wala' arises. Understanding this principle is key to grasping the Hanafi position on patronage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Base Ruling
State the ruling: the wala' of a child born to a freed female slave belongs to her patrons (manumitters). Note that this holds even if the father has wala' al-muwalat, because wala' al-'itaq is stronger.
The base ruling is the starting point for the proof.
2
Recognize the Proof: Qiyas with 'Asaba
Identify the analogy: just as lineage (nasab) is not annulled after establishment, wala' is not annulled. More specifically, just as a brother remains an 'asaba even when a son appears (though the son takes priority), so the mother's patrons' wala' remains even when a stronger wala' appears.
The text explicitly draws this parallel using 'asaba.
3
Extract the Illa (Effective Cause)
Determine the common element: both wala' and 'asaba are fixed rights that cannot be annulled but can be superseded by a stronger right. This illa connects the original case (brother's 'asaba) to the new case (mother's patrons' wala').
The illa is 'fixed, non-annullable right that is superseded by a stronger right.'
4
Analyze the Objection
The opponent argues: if the mother's patrons' wala' is not annulled, they should inherit when the stronger patron is absent after transfer. The Hanafi response: the transfer of wala' is absolute, so the mother's patrons lose their claim; they no longer have a connection. The wala' is not annulled but transferred entirely.
This objection tests the analogy.
5
State the Mufta Bihi Position
The relied-upon Hanafi position is that the mother's patrons have wala' initially, and it is not annulled; a stronger wala' (e.g., father's wala' al-'itaq) takes priority but does not void it. The mother's patrons do not inherit after a complete transfer.
This is the zahir al-riwayah position per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Subcases of Wala': Timing, Marriage to a Slave, and Apostasy
Expansion of sub-rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether wala' transfers to the father's patrons based on the mother's 'idda status, birth timing, father's servitude, and apostasy.

Core Idea
Wala' transfer depends on when the child is born relative to the mother's freedom and relationship with the father.

The previous lesson established that wala' is not annulled but can be superseded. This lesson delves into the specific conditions under which wala' actually transfers from the mother's patrons to the father's patrons, including cases of 'idda, marriage to a slave, and apostasy. Understanding these subcases is essential for applying the correct ruling in complex family situations.

Guided Path
1
Consider the Mother's 'Idda Status
If the mother is not in 'idda, the general rule applies: wala' goes to her patrons. If she is in 'idda from a previous marriage, the child's existence at the time of emancipation determines who holds wala'.
Idda is a waiting period after divorce or death.
2
Analyze Birth Timing Relative to Emancipation and Separation
For a child born during 'idda: if born more than 6 months after emancipation and less than 2 years from separation, wala' stays with mother's patrons. If born after 2 years, it goes to father's patron.
These timeframes are key.
3
Evaluate Marriage to a Slave and Later Emancipation of Father
If the mother marries a slave and has children, the 'aql for their crimes is on mother's patrons. If the father later is freed, he draws the wala' to himself, but the mother's patrons are not reimbursed, unlike the li'an case.
The distinction is based on the time of liability.
4
Handle Apostasy Scenarios
If a woman apostatizes after freeing a slave, her wala' is null (like death). If both master and slave apostatize then the slave re-embraces Islam while the master persists, the wala' remains with the master.
Apostasy does not automatically void wala' if the slave remains Muslim.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq, deals with the rules of wala' (patronage/clientage) arising from emancipation (wala' al-'itaq). The ruling is that when a freed female slave (mu'taqah) gives birth to a child, the wala' of that child goes to her patrons (her manumitter or his heirs), even if the father has wala' al-muwalat (patronage by covenant). This is because wala' al-'itaq is stronger and does not admit of annulment, while wala' al-muwalat does. The author uses an analogy: just as lineage (nasab) is not annulled once established, so wala' is not annulled. The response is that the transfer of wala' is not absolute; in the case of a child born during the mother's 'idda (waiting period) from a prior marriage, the child's wala' stays with the mother's patrons because the child existed at the time of emancipation. Several subcases are discussed from the Tatarkhaniyya and al-Jami' al-Saghir, including scenarios where the father is a slave or later emancipated, the difference between wala' and li'an (accusation of adultery), and a case involving apostasy and capture. Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Wala' of a Child When Father is a Non-Arab
Intra-Hanafi dispute on wala' when the father is a non-Arab married to a freed slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf on the wala' of a child born to a freed female slave when the father is a non-Arab, and apply the rule to determine inheritance and 'aql liability.

Core Idea
When a non-Arab father has wala' al-muwalat, the child's wala' goes to the mother's patrons because wala' al-'itaq is stronger than contractual wala'.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the wala' of a child when the father is a non-Arab (ajami) who may have either wala' al-muwalat or a father in Islam. The matn (Kanz al-Daqa'iq) states the child's wala' belongs to the mother's patrons, following Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. However, Abu Yusuf disagrees, holding that the father's side takes precedence under certain conditions. By understanding the proofs and tarjih, you will be able to apply the correct ruling in inheritance and liability cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The dispute revolves around two key scenarios: (1) the father has wala' al-muwalat (patronage by covenant), and (2) the father is a non-Arab with a father in Islam. Note that the mother is always a freed female slave (mu'taqa).
The 'ajami father is a non-Arab, and his wala' status differs from an Arab father.
2
State the positions of the three imams
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the child's wala' belongs to the mother's patrons in both scenarios. Abu Yusuf holds that if the father has a father in Islam, the child's wala' goes to the father's patrons (or to dhawu al-arham if no patron).
This is the core of the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze the proofs
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that the mother's wala' al-'itaq is strong and cannot be annulled, whereas the father's wala' al-muwalat is weak and can be annulled. Abu Yusuf argues that the father's side (whether by 'itaq or lineage) should prevail because of paternal preference.
The strength of wala' al-'itaq is a key principle: it is qati' (certain) vs zanni (speculative).
4
Identify the tarjih (relied-upon position)
The majority of mashayikh hold that the child's wala' is to the mother's patron, and this is the mufta bihi (operative) position. The grandfather (the father's father) does not draw wala'.
Tarjih is based on the strength of wala' al-'itaq and the fact that muwalat can be annulled.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: If the child dies, inheritance and 'aql liability differ. According to Abu Hanifa/Muhammad, inheritance goes to mother's patrons and 'aql is on her 'aqila. According to Abu Yusuf, inheritance goes to dhawu al-arham (if no patron) and 'aql is on father's 'aqila.
This shows how a theoretical ikhtilaf has concrete effects on property and liability.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq covers several detailed rulings about claims of wala (clientage) and the distribution of inheritance when a freed slave dies. The passage type is a series of ruling statements with proofs (ruling_with_proof). The author addresses various scenarios: initial claims of emancipation requiring witnesses to state that the deceased owned the slave, had no other known heir, etc. When someone takes property claiming to be an heir, the property remains with him pending proof because his possession is presumed valid. Women's Capacity in Wala
States the limited circumstances under which women can have wala.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on women's wala and identify the specific actions through which a woman can acquire wala.

Core Idea
Women can have wala only through their own emancipation or chains they initiate.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on women's capacity in the institution of wala (clientage). While wala typically follows the emancipator, women are not excluded entirely. The matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, supported by a specific hadith, outlines the limited circumstances under which a woman can be a wala holder. Understanding these nuances is crucial for inheritance calculations involving freed slaves.

Wala al-Muwalat: Definition, Evidence, and Pillar
Defines wala al-muwalat with evidence and pillar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of wala al-muwalat, identify its evidence from the Sunna, and explain its pillar (rukn) and how it differs from wala al-'itaqah.

Core Idea
Wala al-muwalat is a valid but weaker clientage contract, proven by explicit hadith, but not as strong as emancipation-based wala.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, quoting al-Hidayah, introduces wala al-muwalat – a contract of clientage established between a new Muslim and his sponsor. The author contrasts it with wala al-'itaqah (emancipation clientage), giving four reasons why the latter is stronger. The discussion is structured into five aspects: evidence, pillar, meaning, conditions, and ruling. In this lesson, we will explore the first three aspects in detail, grounding our understanding in the textual proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal institution
Recognize that wala al-muwalat is a contract of clientage by agreement, not by emancipation. It applies when a Muslim without agnatic relatives ('asaba) enters into an arrangement with a sponsor.
The term 'muwalat' implies mutual alliance.
2
Compare with wala al-'itaqah
Note the four reasons given for why wala al-'itaqah is stronger: it is permanent and non-transferable, involves legal revival (ihya' hukmi), is agreed upon as a cause of inheritance, and takes precedence over Dhawi al-Arham.
This comparison clarifies the weaker status of muwalat.
3
Locate the evidence (dalil)
Examine the two hadith cited: the general statement 'Huwa ahaqq al-nas bi-mahyahu wa-mamatihi' and the specific hadith of Tamim al-Dari where the Prophet (s) explicitly says 'He is your brother and mawla, you bear his crimes and inherit from him.'
These ahadith are the primary textual proofs.
4
Define the pillar (rukn)
Understand that the rukn of wala al-muwalat is the verbal declaration (e.g., 'You are my mawla for such and such'). No other actions are necessary.
This is a verbal contract.
5
Grasp the technical definition
Learn the technical definition as given in al-Dhakhira: a contract where a new Muslim says to his sponsor 'I am your client on condition that if I die, my inheritance is yours, and if I commit a crime, my blood money is on you.'
This definition encapsulates the rights and obligations.

Women only acquire wala' from their own acts of freeing slaves or similar contracts.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies that women are not excluded from wala' (clientage) in Hanafi law. Rather, they acquire wala' specifically through actions they directly perform, such as freeing a slave, entering into a kitaba contract, or making a mudabbar. The rule is grounded in a hadith and ensures that women's legal capacity mirrors that of men in this area of inheritance law.

The hadith 'ليس للنساء من الولاء إلا ما أعتقن' is cited as proof. Recognize that it is a single-chain (ahad) hadith, yet it is relied upon. The phrase 'except what they emancipated' specifies the condition.

Wala' al-Muwalat: Conditions, Proof, and the Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute
Detailed discussion of wala al-muwalat: proof, conditions, rulings, and inter-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi proof for wala al-muwalat, list its three conditions, and explain why the Hanafi school accepts it while the Maliki and Shafi'i schools reject it.

Core Idea
Wala al-muwalat allows a convert of unknown lineage to secure inheritance and blood-wit through a contract, but only Hanafis recognize it.

The institution of wala (clientage) is central to Islamic inheritance law, creating rights of inheritance and blood-wit responsibility. In this passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq, the commentator examines a specific type: wala al-muwalat, the clientage by mutual agreement. This lesson unpacks its proof from hadith, the three conditions required for validity, and the intra-Hanafi debate over stipulations, as well as the inter-madhhab dispute with the Maliki and Shafi'i schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and Hanafi position
Recognize that the passage centers on wala al-muwalat – a contract between a person of unknown lineage and a patron. The Hanafi school holds it valid and a cause of inheritance and blood-wit.
Wala al-muwalat is distinguished from wala al-itaq (manumission) which is stronger and unanimous.
2
Examine the proof (dalil) from hadith
The author cites two hadiths: the Prophet's statement that a patron is 'most entitled to his life and death' (i.e., inheritance), and the explicit hadith of Tamim al-Dari: 'He is your brother and your mawla; you pay his blood-wit and inherit from him.' These establish the validity by command.
The dalil is ahad (single-narrator), thus zanni al-thubut, but still sufficient for obligation in Hanafi usul.
3
Break down the three conditions
The text lists three conditions for the client: (1) unknown lineage, (2) no existing wala (by manumission or prior muwalat), (3) not an Arab. Understand why each condition is necessary.
The condition of non-Arab status is unique; the commentator notes it excludes Arabs from making such contracts.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on stipulation
Some early Hanafis (al-Hakim al-Shahid) held that stipulating inheritance and blood-wit is not required; the contract itself implies them. The commentator prefers this view, while others require explicit stipulation.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah sides with implicit inclusion, and this is the mufta bihi.
5
Contrast with the opposing schools
Malik and al-Shafi'i reject wala al-muwalat entirely. The Hanafi response is that the hadith provides clear proof, while the opponents require wala to be only by manumission. Understand the practical consequence: without Hanafi acceptance, a convert of unknown lineage would have no contractual heir and his estate goes to the public treasury.
This is an example of thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) where the ruling directly affects inheritance outcomes.

Wala as Residuary Heir and Resolving Conflicting Claims
Ruling statement on wala inheritance and conflict resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the position of wala among dhawil arham heirs and apply the rule that the later claimant of wala al-muwalat is preferred.

Core Idea
Wala al-muwalat is rescisable; the later claimant supersedes the earlier, unlike wala al-itaq.

This lesson unpacks two key rules from the Hanafi faraid tradition: first, that wala (clientship) from al-muwalat ranks last among the distant kindred (dhawil arham) for inheritance; second, when two claimants each present evidence for wala al-muwalat over a deceased, the later claimant is given priority. This rule hinges on the rescisable nature of the contractual clientship, contrasting with the binding clientship from manumission (wala al-itaq).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Rulings
Read the passage to extract two distinct legal rulings: (1) the place of wala in the inheritance hierarchy, and (2) the resolution of conflicting claims for wala al-muwalat.
The first ruling is from the matn; the second is from al-Muhit.
2
Understand the Inheritance Hierarchy
Note that wala is the last category among dhawil arham. If the deceased leaves no other heirs except distant kindred, then the wala heir inherits. This establishes the priority order.
Dhawil arham are non-Quranic, non-Asabah relatives.
3
Analyze the Conflict Resolution
When two claimants provide evidence for wala al-muwalat, the later claimant wins because the contract can be rescinded (faskh). The later contract supersedes the earlier, assuming the earlier was validly rescinded.
The reasoning: since wala al-muwalat is non-binding, the later valid contract overrides the earlier.
4

Wala al-itaq is binding and cannot be rescinded; hence the rule differs. For wala al-itaq, the earlier claimant would be preferred if both provide evidence.
This contrast highlights the unique legal character of wala al-muwalat.

Tree Planting on Another's Land: Right to Uproot
Answers from al-Tatimmah and al-Khujandi on tree planting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether a planter can uproot a tree from another's land and the conditions that restrict that right.

Core Idea
The planter may uproot his tree unless it harms the land; the owner cannot force him to accept a payment instead.

When someone plants a tree on another's land without permission, a conflict arises between the planter's desire to reclaim his property and the landowner's interest in preserving the land. This lesson, drawn from al-Tatimmah and al-Khujandi, clarifies the legal options and their conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is planting a tree (ghars) on another's land without permission. The dispute is whether the planter must accept the landowner's offer to pay for the tree or can insist on uprooting it.
2
State the primary ruling
The planter has the right to uproot the tree (la-hu an yalqa'ahu). The landowner cannot force him to accept a payment; the only option the owner has is to demand removal (al-amr bi al-qal' faḥsab).
This is from al-Tatimmah.
3
Add the condition for harm
If uprooting would damage the land (naqṣ), the planter must compensate for the damage, but still has the right to uproot. Ali ibn Ahmad adds that the planter receives the value of the branches at the time of planting if removal harms the land.
4
Apply al-Khujandi's condition
Al-Khujandi adds that the planter may uproot only if the land does not decrease in value (lam tanquṣ al-arḍ). In other words, if removal would cause a decrease, the planter cannot uproot? But the text says he may uproot if it does not decrease – so the right is conditional on no harm.
This seems to limit the right: if harm, then no uprooting? Actually, earlier it said he may uproot and compensate for harm. Al-Khujandi appears to say he may uproot if no harm; otherwise, he may not. This is a nuance.
5
Summarize the operative ruling
The mufta bihi appears to be that the planter can uproot only if no harm to the land; if harm, he must leave the tree and take compensation. However, the text from al-Tatimmah suggests he can uproot and pay for damage. There is ikhtilaf.
For fatwa, the later position often restricts the planter's right to avoid waste.

Successive Sowings: Ownership of Crops
Fatwa on multiple sowings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for determining ownership when two people sow seeds on the same land successively.

Core Idea
The first sower owns the crop if his seed sprouted first; otherwise the second sower may own it with compensation.

When two people sow seeds on the same land without agreement, the ownership of the resulting crop depends on the timing of actions and whether the first crop had rooted. This lesson from a fatwa in Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq details the intricate rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial scenario
A man sows his own land. Another person comes and throws his seed onto the same land and either turns the soil or not, and waters it until the seed grows.
The second sower is a usurper (ghasib) since he uses the land without permission.
2
Ruling if only the second seed grows
According to Abu Hanifa, the resulting crop belongs to the second sower. He owes the first sower (landowner) compensation: the value of his seed as if sown in his own land (i.e., the difference in land value with and without the seed).
The calculation: value of land with seed minus value without seed = the seed's value as placed.
3
Ruling if the landowner sows again and both seeds grow
If the landowner sows again (after the second sower) and both seeds grow, all the crop belongs to the landowner. The landowner must pay the second sower the value of his seed (but now calculated as if sown in another's land).
The second sower gets his seed value, but the landowner gets all crops.
4
Ruling if the first sower's crop was already rooted
If the landowner's crop had already sprouted (nābit) before the second sower sows, and the second sower sows and waters without turning the soil, then if the rooted crop can regrow after being turned, the second sower's crop does not affect ownership; if it cannot regrow, the second sower's crop takes over and he must pay the landowner the value of his standing crop.
This is a complex condition based on whether the first crop is perennial or annual.
5
Compensation rule
In all cases, the second sower (usurper) guarantees to the landowner the value of his seed as sown, and the landowner guarantees to the second sower the value of his seed, but each calculation depends on whose land it is and whether the crop is standing.
The 'value as sown' is determined by the market price of the seed plus the benefit of being placed in land.

Mixed Grains and Usurpation
Rulings from al-Zahiriyya and Abu al-Layth on mixed grains

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on mixed crops when a usurper sows a different grain and the options available to the landowner.

Core Idea
If a usurper sows barley on another's wheat field, the landowner may either take the crop and pay compensation or demand removal.

When someone sows a different grain on another's land without permission, a conflict of ownership arises. This lesson, drawn from al-Zahiriyya and Abu al-Layth, clarifies the landowner's choices based on whether he approves of the sowing.

Guided Path
1
State the initial scenario
A man sows wheat on his own land. Another person comes and sows barley on the same land without permission.
2
Ruling if the landowner is satisfied
If the landowner is satisfied (raḍiya) with the barley sowing, the possessor of the barley (the usurper) owes the value of his seed as sown (mabdhūran).
This is the ruling reported by Muhammad ibn Samā'ah from Muhammad ibn al-Husayn.
3
Ruling if the landowner is not satisfied
If the landowner is not satisfied, he has the choice: either let the barley grow and then take it by uprooting (i.e., demand removal), or he may absolve the usurper of liability (ibrā') and let the crop remain.
From Abu al-Layth al-Samarqandi.
4
After harvest
If both grains are harvested, the yield is shared between them according to the proportion of each person's share (i.e., based on the land input).
The landowner's share is the land itself; the usurper's share is his labor and seed.
5
Compensation details
The usurper pays the value of his seed 'as sown' in the landowner's land. The landowner pays nothing unless he chooses to take the standing crop.
The valuation is at the time of sowing, not harvest.

Sharecropping (Mu'amala) and Grape Consumption
Question to Abu Ja'far on mu'amala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the liability of a sharecropper's family when they eat grapes from the vineyard without permission.

Core Idea
Family members who eat grapes from a sharecropped vineyard without permission are liable for the worker's share, not the owner.

In a sharecropping contract for a vineyard (mu'amala), the fruits are jointly owned. When the owner's family eats grapes without the worker's consent, issues of liability arise. This lesson from a question to Abu Ja'far clarifies who is responsible.

Guided Path
1
Understand the setup
A man gives his vineyard as a mu'amala (sharecropping) contract. The owner and his family enter the vineyard, eat grapes, and carry them away, while the worker (the sharecropper) rarely enters.
The key point is whether the owner authorized the consumption.
2
Case 1: Eating without owner's permission
If the family eats without the owner's permission, the owner is not liable; the family members themselves are liable for the value of what they consumed.
The liability is on the direct agents.
3
Case 2: Eating with owner's permission
If the family eats with the owner's permission, then we look at whether the owner is obligated to support them (nafaqa). If they are dependents, the owner is liable for the worker's share because he effectively consumed it himself.
The owner is considered to have consumed the worker's share if he authorized his dependents.
4
Case 3: Non-dependents
If the family members are not dependents (e.g., guests), the owner is not liable; he is considered to have merely pointed out the property of another.
The owner's act is similar to indicating someone else's property for consumption.
5
Practical implication
The sharecropper can claim compensation only from those who ate without authorization, or from the owner only if the owner authorized dependents.
This protects the worker from losing his share.

Customary Land Share and Building on Another's Land
Questions to Shaykh 'Ata' and a ruling on building

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for determining land rent by custom and for building a wall on another's land.

Core Idea
If custom sets a land share (e.g., one-third), it is binding; and a builder's wall made of the landowner's soil becomes the owner's property.

This lesson covers two distinct rulings: first, when someone sows another's land without permission, the customary share of the produce for land rent is enforceable; second, if a usurper builds a wall using the landowner's soil, the wall belongs to the landowner. Both illustrate the role of custom and property rights.

Guided Path
1
First scenario: Customary land share
A person sows another's land with his own seed without permission. The landowner claims a share of the harvest (e.g., one-third, one-fourth). The ruling: if the custom (ʿurf) in that region is to give a specific fraction (e.g., shāʿiʿ, a common share), then the landowner is entitled to that share.
This is from Shaykh ʿAṭā' ibn Ḥamza.
2
Evidence for custom
It was asked: 'Is there a transmitted narration (riwāya) for this?' He replied: Yes: the case of a man who usurped land and built a wall with the landowner's soil.
The analogue: just as the wall from the land's soil belongs to the owner, the land's share belongs to the owner by custom.
3
Second scenario: Building a wall
If a usurper builds a wall on another's land using soil from that same land, he cannot dismantle it; the wall becomes the property of the landowner.
If he uses soil from elsewhere, he can dismantle it.
4
Reasoning
Using the land's own soil means the material comes from the owner's property, so the structure is an accession to the land and belongs to the owner. If the soil is imported, the builder retains ownership of the material.
5

Dividing Real Estate Based on Heirs' Admission: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on proof requirement for real estate division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding division of real estate based on heirs' admission and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of each position.

Core Idea
Real estate division requires proof of death; mutual admission alone is insufficient according to Imam Abu Hanifa.

In Hanafi inheritance practice, a critical question arises: may the judge divide the deceased's real estate among the heirs based solely on their mutual admission that they are the rightful heirs? Or must they present formal proof of death and the number of heirs? This passage presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). Understanding this dispute reveals deeper principles about the nature of the estate before division and the role of judicial proof.

Guided Path
1
State the core ruling and identify the imams
The passage opens with a ruling: 'The judge does not divide real estate among the heirs based on their admission until they prove death and number of heirs.' Note that this is attributed to 'the Imam' (Abu Hanifa). Then the Sahibayn's opposing view is introduced: division is allowed based on acknowledgment.
The phrase 'ولا يقسم' is a negative command, indicating prohibition according to Imam.
2
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn argue that possession (yad) is evidence of ownership and admission (iqrar) is evidence of truthfulness. They analogize to movable property and purchased real estate. They also note there is no denier, so evidence is unnecessary.
Their reasoning is based on the absence of a contesting party and the strength of mutual admission.
3
Analyze Imam Abu Hanifa's proof and response
Imam argues that division is a form of judgment against the deceased (qada' ala al-mayyit) because the estate remains in the deceased's ownership until division, with bequests still effective. Therefore, evidence is required just as in any claim against an absent person. He addresses the objection that no clear plaintiff/defendant exists by stating the judge can designate one heir as litigant against the deceased.
The key point: the estate is not yet the heirs' property; it is still the deceased's.
4
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
The text mentions that if division is based only on admission, it does not conclusively prove death. Consequently, the deceased's ummahāt al-awlād (concubine-mothers of children) and mudabbars (slaves promised freedom after death) are not automatically freed. Under the Imam's view requiring proof, death is established and these effects follow.
This shows the real-world consequence of the procedural difference.
5
Determine the relied-upon position
The passage does not explicitly state 'wa alayhi al-fatwa' but the structure defends Imam Abu Hanifa's view and responds to objections. In later Hanafi works, the Imam's position is often preferred to protect the deceased's rights.
The mufta bihi is likely the Imam's view requiring proof.

Understanding this distinction ensures that creditors' rights are protected and that the judge's partition is final and irreversible.

State the ruling for ghayr mustaghriq
The judge withholds an amount equal to the debt and partitions the remainder among the heirs. The withheld portion is not distributed to avoid having to revoke the judgment if the creditor appears.
The rationale: the estate is owned by the heirs subject to the debt, but partitioning the debt portion would require revisiting the partition.
4
State the ruling for mustaghriq
If the debt covers the entire estate, the judge does not partition at all because the heirs have no ownership in the estate.
Ownership only vests in heirs after debts are settled; a mustaghriq debt extinguishes heir ownership.
5
Understand the rational proof
The ruling is based on the principle that heirs own the estate net of debts, but partition of the debt portion is prohibited to preserve finality of the judgment. This is a rational (ma'qul) proof.
The text says 'li-anna al-tarikat milk li-l-warithi...' (because the estate is owned by the heirs).

Commentary
This is a ruling from the Hanafi school on how to handle partition of a deceased's estate when there is a debt owed to an absentee creditor. If the debt is less than the total estate (ghayr mustaghriq), the estate belongs to the heirs subject to the debt. The judge must secure the amount of the debt by withholding it from partition. He then partitions only the remaining surplus among the heirs. The reason for not partitioning the debt portion is to avoid having to revoke a judgment later if the creditor appears. If the debt covers the entire estate (mustaghriq), the heirs have no actual ownership, so no partition occurs.

Key Terms
مستغرق (mustaghriq): A debt that covers the entire estate, leaving nothing for heirs
قسمة (qisma): Partition or division of the estate among heirs
غائب (gha'ib): An absent person, specifically the creditor who is not present to claim the debt

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school requires the judge to withhold the exact debt amount from partition when the debtor is absent and the debt is less than the estate, to ensure the creditor's right and preserve the finality of the partition.
