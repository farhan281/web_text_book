The General Principle: Coercion Does Not Permit Killing Another
Ruling that killing another under coercion is not allowed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing another under coercion and explain the underlying qiyas argument.

Core Idea
Killing another under coercion is not permitted; both lives are equal, so no preference.

This passage addresses a critical ethical and legal dilemma: if someone is forced to kill another person or be killed himself, what should he do? The Hanafi school rules decisively: he must not kill, even at the cost of his own life. The reasoning is based on the fundamental equality of human lives and the principle that killing a Muslim without right is never permissible, even for necessity.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Explicitly state the Hanafi position: killing another under coercion is not permitted (lā yurakhkhas). The act remains haram.
The text opens with 'Iā yurakhkhas' meaning no concession is granted.
2
Identify the proof
The passage gives two proofs: (1) the basis for concession is fear of destruction, but both parties are equal, so the victim's life cannot be sacrificed; (2) killing a Muslim without right is not permissible for any necessity, so coercion does not change that.
These are qiyas and general principles.
3
Analyze the qiyas
The qiyas compares the coerced killer and the victim: both are Muslims with sacred lives. The same prohibition applies to both. Therefore, one cannot be preferred over the other.
The equality of lives is the core illa (effective cause).
4
Understand the dispute
The text notes there is a dispute (niza’) on this ruling. Some scholars may permit killing in extreme necessity, but the Hanafi position is that it is not permitted.
This ikhtilaf is acknowledged; the mufta bihi is the prohibition.

He said, may Allah have mercy on him: (And regarding killing another under threat of death, it is not permitted). Meaning: if one is coerced to kill another under threat of death, killing is not permitted for him to save his own life, because the basis for concession is fear of destruction, and the coerced and the one coerced upon are equal in that, so the [life of the] coerced falls away. And because killing a Muslim without right is not made permissible for any necessity, so too with coercion. And this is disputed. And he used the general term 'another' which includes free and slave, his own slave and another's slave. And in al-Muhit: if he is coerced by threat of death to kill his own slave or cut off his hand, it is not allowed for him; if he kills, he sins, and the coercer is executed for the killing and he (the coerced) pays half the value because his blood is sacred by original nature. And if he is coerced by threat of death to kill his father or son, and kills him, he is not deprived of inheritance; but if the coercer is the father of the killed or his son, he is deprived of inheritance because the direct killer is the coerced. And if he is coerced by threat of death to strike a man with an iron, and he strikes him and then repeats without coercion, and he dies, both are responsible for the killing because one strike is without coercion so it is attributed to him, and the other is attributed to the coercer. And if one of the two strikes was with a stick, the 'aagila of each is liable for half the blood money over three years. And if the coercion is by imprisonment or shackling, then liability is on the striker, whether as retaliation or blood money, because coercion by imprisonment is not considered coercion in these rulings. And in it also: if one is coerced by threat of death to cause a person to kill his slave, it is not permitted, and if he kills, he sins, and retaliation is only against the coercer.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on coercion to kill another person. The author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq states that killing another under threat of death is not permitted, even to save one's own life. The commentary explains the reasoning: the license (rukhsa) to commit normally prohibited acts under coercion is based on the fear of loss of life, but here both the coerced person and the intended victim face equal danger, so the victim's life cannot be sacrificed. Additionally, killing a Muslim without right is never permissible, even for necessity. The text then discusses specific cases: if coerced to kill one's own slave, that is not allowed; if one does kill, he sins, the coercer is executed, and the coerced pays half the slave's value. Killing a father or son under coercion does not deprive the killer of inheritance, but if the coercer is the father/son of the victim, they are deprived. Cases with multiple strikes and variable degrees of coercion are examined, concluding that only direct killing under actual death threat is considered coercion in this context.

Key Terms
لا يرخص (lā yurakhkhas): It is not permitted; the license (rukhsa) is not granted.
مباشر (mubāshir): The direct perpetrator; the person who physically commits the act.
عاقلة (ʿāqila): The collective responsible for paying blood money; usually the male relatives.

Implicit vs Explicit Threat in Coercion (Ikrah)
Definition and expansion of 'threat of death' in coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a credible threat of death in coercion and distinguish between explicit and implicit threats.

Core Idea
A threat of death in coercion can be implicit if circumstances create a strong presumption of death.

The author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies a key point in the law of ikrah (coercion): what constitutes a threat of death? While an express verbal threat is clear, the Hanafi school also recognizes implied threats based on the situation. This lesson unpacks that definition and its consequences for qisas (retaliation).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal significance
Determine what act is being defined and its role in fiqh. The passage defines what counts as a credible threat of death (al-qatl) in the context of coercion (ikrah). This definition determines whether a person is considered coerced and whether liability transfers to the coercer.
Coercion (ikrah) can affect the validity of acts and criminal liability.
2
Distinguish explicit vs implicit threat
The passage states the threat can be explicit (verbal statement like 'I will kill you') or implicit where the situation (dall al-hal) creates a strong presumption (ghalaba ʿalā ẓannihi) that death will occur, even without words.
Explicit is clear; implicit requires interpretation of circumstances.
3
Understand the standard of proof
The key term is 'ghalaba ʿalā ẓannihi' – it must be overwhelming assumption, not mere suspicion or fear. This is an objective standard based on what a reasonable person would conclude.
The Hanafi school requires a high probability, not certainty.
4
Apply the ruling to a case
If a credible threat (explicit or implicit) is established, then the coerced person who kills under duress is not liable; instead, qisas (retaliation) is against the coercer.
This ruling is cited from Jami' al-Fatawa and is mufta bihi.
5
Note the authority and ikhtilaf
The source is Jami' al-Fatawa, a later Hanafi compilation. There is intra-madhhab difference: some early Hanafis may have required explicit threat, but the relied-upon position is that implicit suffices.
This shows the evolution of Hanafi fatwa practice.

Sin and Retaliation in Coercion to Kill
Ruling that the coerced killer sins and retaliation is only on coercer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on sin and retaliation in cases of coercion to kill, and compare the views of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad with Zufar.

Core Idea
The coerced killer sins, but retaliation is only against the coercer.

Coercion (ikrah) is a legal factor that can shift liability but does not erase the moral sin of the direct actor. This passage from Sharh Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents two key rulings: first, that the coerced killer commits a sin because the prohibition remains, and second, that retaliation (qisas) is executed on the coercer alone. The text also draws a parallel to coercion in zina, distinguishing between men and women based on consequences. Within the Hanafi school, Zufar disagrees on the retaliation ruling, insisting on the direct perpetrator's liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings in the passage
The author states two separate rulings: (1) the coerced killer sins, and (2) retaliation is only on the coercer. Note the evidence and analogy provided for each.
The sin ruling is supported by the principle that sin attaches to the direct actor; the retaliation ruling relies on causation.
2
Analyze the analogy to zina
The text compares male coercion to kill with coercion to zina. For a man, zina under coercion is not permitted because of grave consequences (illegitimate child, disruption of lineage). For a woman, it is permitted under overwhelming coercion because her lineage is not affected. This distinction highlights the role of practical effects (mafasid) in determining rulings.
This is a qiyas jaliy based on the shared illa of prevention of harm.
3
Map the intra-madhhab dispute
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that retaliation is on the coercer only. Zufar argues retaliation should be on the coerced because he is the direct killer. Identify the evidence each side uses: the majority rely on legal causation (the coercer is the true cause), while Zufar relies on the physical act.
This is a dispute over whether the act is attributed to the tool (the coerced) or the principal (the coercer).
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The text indicates that the view of Imam and Muhammad is the relied-upon position. Zufar's opinion is not followed. Note the practical consequence: the coercer faces qisas, the coerced does not (though he still sins).
The mufta bihi is the majority Hanafi view, as stated in the tarjih.

Commentary
This passage contains two close rulings. First, the author states that if a person kills under coercion, he commits a sin (ithm) because the prohibition of killing remains, and sin follows the direct perpetrator. The text then analogizes to zina: a man coerced to zina is not permitted because of the severe consequences (illegitimate child, disruption of lineage), whereas a woman can be forced because lineage is not compromised. This distinction is based on the different effects. Second, the author rules that retaliation (qiṣāṣ) is only against the coercer, not the coerced killer. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl disagrees, arguing that retaliation should be on the coerced because he is the direct killer and sin attaches to him. The Hanafi majority view is based on the principle that the coercer is the true cause (mutasabbib) and the coerced is merely a tool; thus legal responsibility shifts to the coercer. However, sin remains on the direct perpetrator because it is a personal moral liability not transferred.

إكراه قاصر (ikrāh qāṣir): Minor coercion; a lower level of duress that averts punishment but does not make the act permissible.

Retaliation Under Coercion: Only the Coercer Is Liable
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mufta bihi position on qisas under coercion, list the three intra-Hanafi opinions, and explain the Hanafi argument that the coerced acts as an instrument.

Core Idea
The coerced killer is an instrument; the coercer bears the qisas.

The question of who is liable for murder when one person forces another to kill has produced nuanced debate within the Hanafi school. The matn states clearly that only the coercer (al-mukrih) is executed. But the other disciples of Abu Hanifa — Zufar and Abu Yusuf — took different positions, and even Imam al-Shafi'i weighed in. In this lesson, we examine the arguments and the school’s preferred ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn's ruling
The matn states 'ويقتص من المكره فقط' — retaliation is only against the coercer. This is the view of Imam Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
2
List all positions
Zufar: qisas on the coerced. Al-Shafi'i: on both. Abu Yusuf: on neither.
Opposing views within and outside the Hanafi school.
3
Understand each proof
Zufar: the coerced is the direct killer and directly acts, so he should be liable. Al-Shafi'i: both contribute. Abu Yusuf: the coerced is not fully responsible.
Each has a different conception of causality.

Liability in Coerced Murder: The Muffered's Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas in murder under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas liability for a coerced killer, distinguish the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and apply the exception when the commander is a child or madman.

Core Idea
The coerced killer bears qisas; the coercer bears sin but not execution.

When a person is forced to kill another under threat of death, who is liable for retaliation? The Hanafi school navigates a complex interplay of intention, instrumentality, and responsibility. This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi debate and the relied-upon ruling, including a critical exception.

Guided Path

This is based on istihsan, not pure qiyas.
2
Identify the opposing views
Al-Shafi'i holds qisas on both. Abu Yusuf (within Hanafi) holds qisas on neither. Muhammad al-Shaybani agrees with the Zahir al-Riwayah position (qisas on muffered only).
Abu Yusuf's view is a minority within the school.
3

The muffered's act is attributed to him because he acted voluntarily in choosing to kill to save himself. He becomes an 'instrument' (آلة) for the coercer in the destruction, but the instrument's liability is on the user only for sin, not for the act itself.
Key concept: separation of worldly liability (qisas) from sin (ithm).
4
Examine the exception for child/madman commander
According to Abi al-Yusr and others, if the commander is a child or insane, the muffered is not liable because the actual perpetrator is the child/madman (who lacks legal capacity). The muffered is merely an instrument in their hands.
This is a critique of the absolute rule in al-Nihayah.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practically, if the muffered is executed, the coercer lives. If the muffered cannot be executed (e.g., minor or dies), the coercer may be liable for diyah in some views.

Ruling on Cutting Limbs Under Coercion
Ruling on cutting limbs under coercion and legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting a limb under coercion, identify the exception when threatened with death, and distinguish between cutting another's limb and one's own limb.

Core Idea
Coercion does not automatically justify harm; only a greater harm (death) permits cutting another's limb.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses the delicate issue of committing physical harm under duress. The general principle is that limbs have sanctity like life, so cutting another's limb is normally prohibited and incurs qisas. However, when the alternative is death, the ruling changes, and the liability shifts to the coercer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
State the general rule: cutting another's limb under coercion is not permitted, and the coercer bears qisas (if free) or diyat (half value if slave).
The text begins with 'وحرمة طرف الإنسان كحرمة'.
2
Locate the exception and its condition
Find the exception from Qadikhan: if threatened with death, cutting is permissible. Note the condition: the threatened harm must be greater than the cutting.
The exception uses 'وسعه أن يقطع'.
3
Analyze the talil: comparing harms
Understand the logic: if the threatened harm is more severe (death), cutting is allowed; if equal (cut for cut), it is not.
The text states 'إذا كان أحدهما أغلظ من القطع وسعه وإن كان قطع بقطع لا يسعه'.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on qisas liability
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, qisas is on the commander (the one giving the order). Some other Hanafi scholars hold it is on the coercer (the one who threatened).
The text says 'على الأمر القصاص على قول الإمام'.
5
Apply to self-harm
Cutting one's own limb under coercion is permissible (like sacrificing property to save life), unlike killing oneself.
The text contrasts cutting own limb with killing oneself: 'ولو أكره على قطع طرف نفسه حل له قطعه بخلاف ما إذا أكره على قتل نفسه'.

And the sanctity of a human limb is like sanctity [of life], such that if he is coerced to cut a person's hand, he cuts his hand, it is not permitted for him. If he does that, he sins and qisas is obligatory upon the coercer if he is free, and he guarantees half the value if he is a slave. This does not contradict what Qadikhan narrated: if a man says to a man, 'Cut the hand of this person, otherwise I will kill you,' it is permissible for him to cut, and if he cuts, the qisas is upon the commander according to the Imam. And in al-Tatarkhaniyyah: if he says, 'If you do not cut your hand, I will cut it,' he [the coerced] does not cut his own hand. End quote. So it became clear from what we narrated that if one of them is more severe than cutting, it is permissible; but if it is cutting in exchange for cutting, it is not permissible. And if he is coerced to cut his own limb, it is permissible for him to cut it, contrary to if he is coerced to kill himself.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on performing a physical harm under coercion. The general principle is that a person's limb has sanctity like life, so cutting another's limb under coercion is not permissible; the coercer bears the qisas or financial liability. However, the text reconciles a narration from Qadikhan that permits cutting when the alternative is death (threat of killing). The distinction is that if the threatened harm is greater than the cutting (e.g., death), it is permissible, but if the harm is equivalent (cut for cut), it is not. Cutting one's own limb under coercion is permissible, unlike killing oneself, because limbs are treated like property; one may sacrifice a limb to save one's life.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retributive justice in kind for bodily harm

Intra-Hanafi Disputes on Coercion Leading to Death and Marriage
Intra-Hanafi disputes on liability for coerced suicide and coerced marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish and apply the three Hanafi positions on liability for coerced suicide and the two positions on coerced marriage with excessive dowry disparity.

Core Idea
Coercion does not automatically transfer liability; the method of killing determines qisas, diyah, or no liability.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents two distinct areas of intra-Hanafi dispute: liability for death resulting from coerced self-harm, and the guardian's right to annul a coerced marriage. The first set of scenarios reveals a three-way ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on whether the coercer faces qisas, diyah on his 'aqilah, or diyah from his own wealth. The second scenario shows disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on whether a guardian can separate a marriage after the woman's consent, when she was coerced with a deficient mahr. Understanding these nuanced positions is essential for distinguishing causation and consent in Hanafi criminal and family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the coercion-to-death scenarios
Locate the two scenarios: (1) coerced to throw self into fire, (2) coerced to jump from mountain under threat of sword. Note the different outcomes: fire leads to qisas on coercer (Imam); mountain leads to three differing views.
The fire scenario mentions a 'leniency' (takhfif) giving the victim choice.
2
Trace the talil (reasoning) for each position
Examine why Abu Hanifa distinguishes fire from mountain: in the mountain case, the coercer's threat is direct killing by a heavy object (sword?), so analogy leads to diyah on 'aqilah. Abu Yusuf shifts liability to coercer's wealth; Muhammad insists on qisas. Understand the criterion: directness of killing.
The text uses 'because if he directly killed' to justify the analogy.
3
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
For each scenario, determine the outcome: qisas (execution), diyah on 'aqilah (tribal compensation), or diyah in wealth (personal payment). This affects who pays and whether the coercer faces capital punishment.
4
Analyze the coerced marriage case
Note the conditions: woman coerced into marriage with mahr fahish (excessive underpayment). When coercion ends, she consents but guardian does not. Abu Hanifa grants guardian right to separate or demand mahr al-mithl; Sahibayn deny this because mahr is woman's exclusive right.
The dispute centers on whether the guardian's right overrides the woman's consent regarding mahr.
5
Synthesize the underlying legal principle
The unifying theme is the effect of coercion on liability and consent. In criminal law, the coercer's liability depends on whether the victim's act is categorized as direct killing (qisas) or indirect (diyah). In marriage, the guardian's authority may persist even after the woman's consent if the initial coercion tainted the contract.
Note that the passage does not specify a mufta bihi; later Hanafi works may favor Abu Yusuf's view on diyah in wealth.

ولو أكره على أن يلقي نفسه في النار أو على الإلقاء من الجبل بالقتل وكان الإلقاء بحيث لا ينجو ولكن فيه نوع تخفيف فله الخيار إن شاء فعل وإن شاء لم يفعل عند الإمام، فلو ألقى نفسه في النار فاحترق فعلى المكره القصاص، وعندهما لا يصبر ولا يفعل. ولو قال له لتلقين نفسك من رأس الجبل أو لأقتلنك بالسيف فألقى نفسه فمات فعند أبي حنيفة تجب الدية على عاقلة المكره لأنه لو باشر لا يجب عليه القصاص لأنه قتل بالمثقل بل فيه الدية على العاقلة فكذا إذا أكره عليه. وعند الثاني تجب الدية على المكره في ماله. وعند محمد يجب القصاص. ولو قال لآخر اقتلني فقتله تجب الدية في ماله الصحيح. ولو أكرهت المرأة على التزويج بمهر فيه غبن فاحش ثم زال الأكراه فرضيت المرأة ولم يرض الولي فللولي الفراق عند أبي حنيفة أو يتم مهر المثل. وقالا : ليس له ذلك لأن المهر خالص حقها حتى تملك إسقاطه والله تعالى أعلم بالصواب.
And if he is coerced to throw himself into fire or to throw from a mountain with killing, and the throwing is such that he cannot escape but there is some leniency, he has the choice: if he wishes he may do it, if he wishes he may not, according to Imam. If he threw himself into fire and burned, then the qisas is upon the coercer. According to the two, he is not patient and does not do it. Commentary
This passage covers several scenarios of coercion leading to death and one on coerced marriage. The first scenario: a person is coerced to throw himself into fire. Imam Abu Hanifa gives him a choice; if he does it, the coercer is liable for qisas. The Sahibayn say he should not do it. The second scenario: threatened with death by sword if he does not jump from a mountain. Here Imam holds the 'aqilah (tribal heirs) of the coercer must pay diyah (blood money), because direct killing with a heavy object (throwing) does not incur qisas. The second (Abu Yusuf) holds the coercer pays from his own wealth, while Muhammad holds qisas is obligatory. The third scenario: asking someone to kill oneself; the killer must pay diyah. Finally, coerced marriage: if a woman is coerced into marriage with an excessively low mahr and later consents, the guardian can seek annulment (Imam) or the husband must pay proper mahr. The Sahibayn say the guardian has no such right because the mahr is the woman's exclusive right.

Key Terms
دية (diyah): Blood money paid to heirs of victim; in the passage, it varies in payer: 'aqilah (tribal group) or personal wealth of coercer.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation, usually execution for intentional murder; Muhammad holds that coercer faces qisas.
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): Proper dowry equivalent to that of women of similar status; Abu Hanifa allows guardian to demand this or separate.
غبن فاحش (ghubn fahish): Excessive disparity in the dowry, indicating exploitation; triggers guardian's right to annul.
عاقلة ('aqilah): The tribal group or 'asabah of the killer who pay diyah for accidental/quasi-intentional killing.
