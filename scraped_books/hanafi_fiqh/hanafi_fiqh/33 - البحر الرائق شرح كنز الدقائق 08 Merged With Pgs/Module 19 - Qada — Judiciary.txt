Interdiction of the Immoral and Heedless: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Hanafi rebuttal of Shafi'i position on interdiction of fasiq and ghaafil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on interdiction of a fasiq and a ghaafil, and evaluate the opposing proofs from the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Immorality (fisq) does not equal financial incompetence; Hanafis reject interdiction unless wealth is actually mismanaged.

This passage presents a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on whether a judge can interdict an immoral person (fasiq) or a heedless person (ghaafil). The Hanafi school, particularly Imam Abu Hanifa, distinguishes between religious sin and financial capacity, while the Shafi'i school and the Sahibayn hold that both conditions warrant interdiction to protect wealth. We will examine the textual proofs and the Hanafi rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage discusses interdiction for a fasiq (immoral) and a ghaafil (heedless). State the Hanafi position for each according to Imam Abu Hanifa.
Abu Hanifa says no interdiction for either.
2
Compare with opposing views
Note that Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and Imam al-Shafi'i hold that both should be interdicted.
This creates an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf as well.
3
Analyze the Quranic proof
The opposing side cites Quran 4:6 ('If you perceive in them rushd...'). Explain how the Hanafis interpret 'rushd' as financial competence.
The word is indefinite, so it is general, but Hanafis restrict based on context (qarain).
4
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith of Munqidh is used by the opponents. Show how Abu Hanifa's response turns it into his own proof.
The Prophet did not actually interdict, only said 'La khilabah'.
5
Assess the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the fasiq and ghaafil can transact freely; under the opposing view, they are restricted.
Later Hanafi fatwa may prefer the Sahibayn for ghaafil.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute and also includes an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. It addresses two types of potential interdiction: (1) the fasiq (immoral person) and (2) the ghaafil (heedless person). Imam Shafi'i and the Sahibayn differ from Imam Abu Hanifa. The core issue is whether interdiction (hajr) is for punishment/protection or only for protection of wealth. The Sahibayn and Shafi'i argue that the fasiq is like the safih and should be interdicted, but Abu Hanifa distinguishes: the safih is interdicted to protect his wealth because he mismanages it, whereas the fasiq may be immoral in religious matters but still manages his wealth properly. The Quranic verse about 'rushd' is interpreted by the Hanafis as referring to financial competence, not religious piety. For the ghaafil (one who is not intentionally wasteful but lacks business acumen), Imam Abu Hanifa says no interdiction, because he is not deliberately harming his wealth. The Sahibayn and Shafi'i say he should be interdicted like a safih, citing the hadith of Munqidh. The Hanafi response clarifies that the hadith actually supports Abu Hanifa, as the Prophet did not actually order interdiction but only said 'La khilabah' (no deception). The passage demonstrates how Hanafi usul distinguishes between different legal causes for interdiction and prioritizes textual evidence over apparent analogy.

Key Terms
فسق (fisq): Immorality; sinfulness that does not necessarily affect financial competence.
غفلة (ghaflah): Heedlessness; lack of business acumen without intentional waste.
سفيه (safih): One who is foolish with money, spends wastefully, and is subject to interdiction by consensus.

Interdiction Due to Debt: Imam vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on interdiction for debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on interdiction for debt, explain their proofs, and apply the mufta bihi ruling to a scenario.

Core Idea
The judge can interdict and sell the debtor's property to pay creditors — the adopted fatwa, despite Imam's opposition.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a qadi can interdict a debtor at creditors' request. Imam Abu Hanifa forbids it, viewing interdiction as destroying legal capacity (ahliyyah). The Sahibayn allow it for creditor protection, citing the Prophet's sale of Mu'adh's property. The fatwa follows the Sahibayn, enabling judges to directly liquidate assets.

Guided Path
1
State the positions
Identify what each party (Imam vs. Sahibayn) holds regarding interdiction for debt.
Imam says no interdiction; Sahibayn say yes.
2
Analyze the proofs
Examine the dalil each side uses: Imam's argument from harm to ahliyyah; Sahibayn's reliance on the Mu'adh hadith and maslahah.
The text directly quotes the Mu'adh narration.
3
Compare the practical outcomes
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf: under Imam, only imprisonment; under Sahibayn, direct sale by judge.
This difference affects creditors' ability to recover debts.
4
Identify the tarjih and its basis
Note that the author states 'al-fatwa 'ala qawlihima' — the fatwa is on Sahibayn's view. Consider why later scholars preferred this.
The tarjih is explicit in the text.
5
Apply the mufta bihi
Use the Sahibayn's ruling to determine the judge's powers in a given scenario.
Include the scope: interdiction covers existing assets, not new acquisitions.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding interdiction of a debtor. Imam Abu Hanifa opposes interdiction for debt, viewing it as a severe deprivation of legal capacity (ahliyyah) that reduces the person to the level of animals (بهايم). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) permit it based on maslahah for creditors and the precedent of the Prophet selling Mu'adh's property. The fatwa follows the Sahibayn. The passage then details various subsidiary rulings: the interdiction only affects wealth existing at the time of hajr, not later acquisitions; the interdicted person's transactions on new property are valid. Knowledge of the interdiction is necessary for it to be effective. The judge is recommended to testify to the hajr. The text also discusses imprisonment: Abu Hanifa allows imprisonment to compel payment but not direct sale; Sahibayn allow the judge to sell the debtor's property if he refuses, citing the Mu'adh hadith. The fatwa here again is on Sahibayn. The passage clarifies that for an insolvent person, alternatives like hiring out or marriage for dowry are not valid methods of debt recovery; the only valid routes are those that directly target the debtor's existing assets or voluntary payment.

Key Terms
حجر (hajr): Judicial interdiction restricting a person's legal capacity to transact.
إفلاس (iflas): Bankruptcy; state of having debts exceeding assets.
مماطلة (mumatālah): Procrastination in paying debt despite ability.

Judge's Sale of Debtor's Assets: Same vs. Different Currency and Order of Sale
Rules for judge selling debtor's property when assets match debt type or differ

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a judge sells a debtor's assets of the same or different type as the debt, distinguish Imam Abu Hanifa's view from the Sahibayn regarding movable goods and real estate, and apply the preferred order of sale for debt collection.

Core Idea
A judge may sell matching cash directly; mismatched cash by istihsan; movables and real estate only if needed (Sahibayn's view is mufta bihi).

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq details the judge's authority in selling a debtor's property. It addresses three scenarios: when the debtor's cash matches the debt type, when it differs, and when the debtor owns movable goods or real estate. You will learn the consensus and ikhtilaf among Hanafi imams, and the practical order of sale that a judge must follow to balance the creditor's right with the debtor's basic needs.

Guided Path
1
Classify the asset-debt relationship
Determine whether the debtor's wealth and the debt are in the same type (dirhams/dirhams or dinars/dinars), different types (dirhams/dinars or vice versa), or involve non-cash assets like movable goods ('urud) or real estate ('iqar).
This classification triggers different rules.
2
Apply the rule for same-type cash
If both are dirhams or both dinars, the judge pays directly without the debtor's consent, because the creditor can seize the same type himself. The judge merely assists.
No dispute among Hanafis here.
3
Understand the istihsan for different-type cash
When cash differs (e.g., dirhams owed but only dinars held), the judge sells by consensus. Imam Abu Hanifa uses istihsan: dirhams and dinars share the attribute of thimaniyyah (standard of value) and maliyyah, so the judge can sell despite literal difference. Qiyas would bar it, but istihsan overrides.
The istihsan is based on combining both aspects: unity (thimaniyyah) gives the judge authority; difference gives the creditor the right to take.
4
Distinguish positions on movable goods and real estate
Imam Abu Hanifa prohibits selling 'urud and 'iqar during the debtor's lifetime (allowed after death by consensus). The Sahibayn permit sale during life, with a regulated order. Later fatwa adopts the Sahibayn's view.
Two narrations from Sahibayn exist on the precise order.
5
Apply the mufta bihi order of sale
According to the relied-upon opinion, the judge sells cash first, then movable goods (starting with those that may perish), and finally real estate. The debtor keeps necessary clothing (one or two sets) and a basic dwelling. If the debtor has surplus assets, they are sold.
The judge must not sell items needed for basic living (e.g., clothes, home) unless the debtor can manage without them.

Evidentiary Rules in Usurpation Disputes
Rules for resolving disputes in usurpation cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for resolving disputes over the identity, description, value, and return of usurped property, and apply them to multi-party and conflicting evidence scenarios.

Core Idea
The defendant's statement is the default, but claims of exemption require proof, and conflicting evidence is reconciled when possible.

In property disputes arising from usurpation, the burden of proof follows general principles of Islamic law: the plaintiff must prove his claim, and the defendant's denial is accepted with an oath. However, when the underlying liability is admitted, a claim of exemption shifts the burden. This lesson presents a series of cases from al-Muhit and al-Nawadir that illustrate how these principles are applied in practice, including multi-party disagreements and conflicting evidence.

Guided Path
1
Basic Rule on Identity, Description, and Value
Identify the default rule: when owner and usurper disagree about the specific property, its description, or its value at the time of usurpation, the usurper's statement is accepted because he is the defendant denying an increase in liability.
This applies when the owner claims a higher amount or better quality.
2
Three-Party Disagreement with Guarantor
Analyze the case where owner, usurper, and guarantor differ on value. The guarantor's statement is accepted against the owner, but the usurper's admission of a higher amount binds only himself, not the guarantor.
The guarantor is the defendant vis-à-vis the owner.
3
Claim of Return After Admitted Usurpation
If the usurper claims he returned the property and the owner denies it, the owner's statement is accepted because the usurper admitted the cause of liability (usurpation) and now claims an exemption; he must bring proof.
Analogous to taking property with permission: the taker's claim of permission is not accepted without evidence.
4
Reconciling Conflicting Evidence
If both parties present evidence that can be harmonized (e.g., usurper proves return, owner proves subsequent damage by usurper), both are acted upon by treating the damage as a fresh usurpation.
The two pieces of evidence are not contradictory; they describe separate events.
5
Preference When Evidence Conflicts on Death Location
If owner proves death at usurper's and usurper proves death at owner's, the usurper's evidence is stronger because it implies return, which negates liability for the loss.
Evidence of death alone (without usurpation) is irrelevant; the key is whether return is established.
6
Partial Admission with Added Value
When the defendant admits to usurping only part of a composite item (e.g., outer lining of a garment) but denies the rest, his statement is accepted with oath, and he must pay the value of the part he admitted, including the added value from stitching that made it subordinate to the larger whole.
The larger part (inner lining and stuffing) is considered the principal, and the smaller part becomes subordinate.

Reconciling Evidence of Return and Later Damage

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Conflicting evidence can be reconciled if both can be true, potentially imposing liability for a fresh usurpation.

Preferring Evidence: Death at Usurper's vs. Owner's Custody
Resolving contradictory testimony: death in different places.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the tarjih (preference) rule when the owner proves death in the usurper's custody and the usurper proves death in the owner's custody.

Core Idea
When both parties present evidence of death in each other's custody, the usurper's evidence is preferred because it implies return.

Here both sides have seemingly direct evidence: the owner says the animal died in the usurper's custody, and the usurper says it died in the owner's custody. Hanafi law must prefer one. The ruling hinges on what each piece of evidence effectively proves, not just the literal words.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the owner's evidence
The owner's witnesses testify that the animal died in the usurper's custody. This proves death at a location, but usurpation is already admitted (the usurper confessed to taking it). The owner's evidence does not add new information about liability.
Liability is based on usurpation, not death per se.
2
Analyze the usurper's evidence
The usurper's witnesses testify that the animal died in the owner's custody. This implies that the animal was returned to the owner before death, i.e., the usurper had returned it.
Return is a key factor that can nullify liability.
3
Apply the tarjih rule
Since the owner's evidence establishes death but not liability (usurpation is already proven by confession), and the usurper's evidence establishes return (by showing death in owner's custody), the usurper's evidence is more relevant and thus preferred.
The preference is based on legal effect: return affects liability; location of death does not.
4
Conclude: usurper's evidence preferred
Therefore, the court accepts the usurper's evidence, meaning the animal is deemed to have been returned, so no liability for death after return.
This does not mean the owner's evidence is false; it is simply outweighed in legal consequence.

Rejecting Irrelevant Evidence: Death Before Usurpation
Resolving contradictory testimony: death before usurpation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when evidence is legally irrelevant and should be rejected in a usurpation case.

Core Idea
Evidence that only negates usurpation without establishing return is irrelevant and rejected – owner's positive evidence of usurpation prevails.

The usurper attempts to prove the animal died in the owner's possession before the alleged usurpation. This would negate the act of usurpation itself. However, the owner already has strong evidence of the usurpation. Hanafi law evaluates whether the usurper's evidence serves any legal purpose beyond denial.

Guided Path
1
Examine the usurper's evidence
The usurper brings witnesses that the animal died in the owner's possession before the usurpation occurred. This, if true, would mean there was no usurpation at all (the animal was already dead).
But the usurper has already confessed to usurpation? Or the owner has established it? The text says 'beyyinat al-mawla tuthbit al-ghasb' – the owner's evidence proves usurpation. So usurpation is established.
2
Determine legal effect of this evidence
The usurper's evidence does not prove return (which could reduce liability). It only denies the core fact of usurpation, which is already proven by the owner's evidence.
Denial alone is not a valid defense if usurpation is proven.
3
Apply the rule of legal relevance
Since the usurper's evidence merely negates usurpation without offering an alternative legal outcome (like return), it is considered to have no legal effect (hukm). The owner's evidence is stronger because it positively proves usurpation and liability.
Thus, the usurper's evidence is rejected.
4
Conclude: owner's evidence preferred
The court accepts the owner's evidence of usurpation and liability, rejecting the usurper's evidence as irrelevant.
This does not mean the usurper's witnesses are liars; their testimony is simply not legally beneficial.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Evidence that the animal was in a specific place at a specific time is irrelevant if it does not affect the usurpation claim.

This final scenario from al-Nawadir presents a case where the owner proves the animal was at Mecca on the day of sacrifice. The purpose is to imply the usurper could not have had it, thus negating usurpation. However, Hanafi law rules this evidence irrelevant because it does not directly disprove usurpation or establish a defense.

Understand the owner's evidence

If the owner brought evidence that the usurped property died in the usurper's custody, and the usurper brought evidence that it died in the owner's custody, then the usurper's evidence is preferred, because the owner's evidence established death, not usurpation, as usurpation is established by the usurper's confession, and liability is due to usurpation, not death, so establishing evidence on death is of no avail. The usurper's evidence establishes return because it establishes death in the owner's custody, and return is associated with it, so it is preferred. If the usurper brought witnesses that it died in the possession of its owner before the usurpation, this testimony is not accepted, because its death in the owner's custody before the usurpation carries no legal effect, as it does not prove return; it only proves negation of usurpation, while the owner's evidence proves usurpation and liability, so it is preferred. Commentary
This passage presents a series of hypothetical cases of conflicting evidence (beyyinah) in usurpation disputes. The general approach is to try to reconcile both pieces of evidence if possible, or to prefer the evidence that is more directly related to liability. For example, if the usurper proves return and the owner proves later damage, both can be true: return followed by a fresh usurpation. If the owner proves death at the usurper's place, and the usurper proves death at the owner's place, the usurper's evidence is preferred because it implies return. Delay After Establishing Pre-Emption: Does It Invalidate?
Combines evidentiary rules for proving demand and the effect of delay on pre-emption right.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on delay after establishing pre-emption and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Once pre-emption right is established by witnessing, delay does not invalidate it — the right is fixed.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) is established through a series of demands. After the right is confirmed by witnesses, the pre-emptor must take possession. But what if he delays? The passage presents the majority view that delay after establishment does not invalidate, along with significant intra-Hanafi disagreement. Let's examine the rulings, the proofs, and what the mufta bihi position is.

Guided Path
1
Understand the three demands of pre-emption
Pre-emption requires three sequential demands: immediate knowledge demand (talab al-muwāṣafah), witnessing demand (talab al-ishhad), and taking demand (talab al-akhdh). This lesson focuses on the third demand and the effect of delay.
The third demand is the actual taking of possession.
2
State the majority Hanafi ruling on delay
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf in the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwaya), delaying the third demand after the right is established does not invalidate the pre-emption right. The right remains until the pre-emptor takes or abandons.
This is the mufta bihi position as per al-‘Ayni.
3
Identify the opposing intra-Hanafi positions
Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that a delay of one month without excuse invalidates. Another narration from Abu Yusuf says abandoning litigation in one court session without excuse voids the right.
These opposing views are based on potential harm to the buyer.
4
Analyze the reasoning for the majority view
The majority argues that the right is already established (thābit) and does not fall by mere delay. The buyer's harm can be remedied by taking the matter to a judge, who will compel the pre-emptor to choose immediately.
The absence of the pre-emptor does not cause forfeiture either.
5
Determine the operative (mufta bihi) ruling
The fatwa is on the saying of Abu Hanifa: delay does not invalidate. This is the relied-upon position, as cited from al-‘Ayni. However, the presence of an excuse (illness, imprisonment, or absence of a judge) strengthens the majority view and is agreed upon by consensus.
When there is no judge in the town, delay does not invalidate by consensus.

Definition, Proofs, and Ruling of Qisma (Division)
Defining qisma, its proofs, pillar, condition, ruling, and attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of qisma, identify its proofs, list its pillar and condition, and apply the ruling that it becomes wajib upon the judge at a partner's request.

Core Idea
Qisma is wajib on the judge when a partner demands it—even without the other's consent.

Division of jointly owned property (qisma) is a fundamental transaction in Hanafi fiqh, rooted in Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma. This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unpacks its definition, proofs, and conditions, and clarifies that a judge must enforce division upon request. Understanding qisma is essential for resolving disputes in shared ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition of qisma
Find the author's definition: 'جمع نصيب شائع في معين' (collecting a common share into a specific portion). Note that it includes segregation (ifraz) and exchange (mubadala).
The definition implies that division transfers ownership by exchange of shares.
2
Locate the proofs of legitimacy
Three proofs are cited: Quran (54:28, 26:155), Sunnah (the Prophet divided Khaybar among the combatants), and Ijma of the Ummah.
The Quranic verses show division of resources among partners.
3
Extract the pillar (rukn) and condition (shart)
The pillar is the act of separation (الفعل الذي يقع به الإقرار). The condition is that the property's benefit does not change or get lost by division.
The condition ensures that division does not destroy the property's utility.
4
Determine the ruling and cause
The ruling is the determination of each party's share in ownership and usufruct. The cause is the request of each partner to benefit individually.
The ruling is wajib upon the judge, as stated: 'وصفتها فهي واجبة على الحاكم عند طلب بعض الشركاء'.
5
Apply the rule to fungible vs. non-fungible goods
In fungible goods (mithli), a partner may take his share even in the absence of the other; in non-fungibles, this is not allowed without presence.
This distinction protects the absent partner's interests in unique items.

Partition with Absent and Minor Co-Owners: Buyer's Right
Ruling on partition with absent/minor co-owners with qiyas proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a buyer demanding partition when co-owners are absent or minor, and apply the qiyas reasoning from the seller's right.

Core Idea
The buyer acquires the seller's right to demand partition, even if other co-owners cannot act.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies a practical scenario in property partition. A buyer purchases a share and wants to divide the property, but some co-owners are absent or minors. The judge does not wait for all; instead, he appoints the present partner as agent. The reasoning is a clear analogy (qiyas) to the seller's right to demand partition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario

2
Isolate the ruling
The judge orders the present partner to conduct the partition and appoints him as agent for the absent and minor. The buyer's demand triggers this even without the absent/minor's presence.
3
Extract the legal substitution (qiyam maqam)
The buyer stands in the place of the seller. The seller had the right to demand partition from his co-owner. Therefore, the buyer has the same right.
This is a qiyas jali (clear analogy): the illa is qiyam maqam.
4
Apply the qiyas to derive the ruling
Since the seller could demand partition, the buyer (substitute) can also demand. The judge facilitates by making the present partner an agent.
The ruling ensures the buyer's right is not blocked by absence or minority.

Core Idea

In shared property disputes, a co-owner has the right to demand partition. If the other co-owner claims to have sold his share after the demand, his evidence is not accepted. Judge never compels harmful partition; even with consent, he may refuse to officiate.

Testimony of Partitioners (al-Qasimayn)
Ruling on accepting testimony of partitioners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting the testimony of two partitioners regarding receipt of shares and distinguish the majority position from Muhammad al-Shaybani's objection.

Core Idea
The testimony of two partitioners about a partner's receipt is accepted because they testify about another's action, not their own.

In disputes after property division, partitioners may be called to testify whether a partner received his share. The Hanafi majority accepts their testimony, while Muhammad al-Shaybani objects. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the key distinction between testifying about one's own action versus another's.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Partners have divided property; some partners deny receiving their allocated shares. Two partitioners (qasimayn) testify that the denying partner did receive his share.
The partitioners may be appointed by the court or by the parties themselves.
2
State the Hanafi majority ruling
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the testimony of two partitioners is accepted if they testify that a partner received his share.
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
Follow Muhammad al-Shaybani's objection
Muhammad argues that partitioners are testifying about their own action (the act of division), which is not acceptable. He analogizes to a case where someone conditions emancipation on an action and the person whose action is the condition testifies about it.
This view is also held by al-Shafi'i.
4
Analyze the majority's response
The majority respond that partitioners testify about the partner's receipt, which is an action of someone else. Their own act (tamyiz) is already agreed upon by the parties, so there is no need to testify about it. The dispute is only about receipt, so no self-interest taints their testimony.
The distinction is between testifying about one's own act vs. another's act.
5
Consider the exception (rent division)
Al-Tahawi notes that if partitioners are hired and divide rent, their testimony about fulfillment is not accepted by consensus, because they claim performance of their own contract.
This exception protects against self-serving testimony.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Testimony of partitioners on receipt is accepted because they testify about another's act, not their own.

After a joint property is divided by two appointed partitioners, a partner may deny receiving his share. The partitioners then testify that he did receive it. Is their testimony accepted? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on this precise point. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the proofs, and the practical consequence of the difference between the imams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and legal question
Recognize the factual situation: after division (qisma), some partners deny taking their share. The partitioners (al-qasimān) come forward to testify. The legal question is whether their testimony is accepted to prove receipt.
The partitioners' own act is demarcation (tamayyuz), not receipt (istifā'). The dispute is only about receipt.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
According to the majority Hanafi opinion (Abu Hanifa and the final view of Abu Yusuf), the testimony of the two partitioners is accepted, regardless of whether they were appointed by the judge or not. Their testimony is about the partner's act of receiving, which is an act of someone else.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
3

Imam Muhammad argues that the testimony is not accepted because the partitioners are testifying about their own action (demarcation). He analogizes to a case where a person conditions an act and then testifies about it—such testimony is rejected.
Early Abu Yusuf also held this view before retracting.
4

The majority responds that the opponents agree on the demarcation; the only disagreement is about receipt, which is the partner's action. Therefore, the partitioners have no personal stake in the outcome, and the suspicion of self-interest is removed. Their testimony is thus free from taint.
This reasoning also addresses the sub-rulings: one partitioner's testimony is rejected, and a trustee's testimony is accepted only for his own delivery, not for obligating another.

Dispute Over an Item Claimed as Pawn by an Absent Mortgagee
Ruling on third-party claim to pawned item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule when a claimant asserts that an item in another's hand is his pawn, and the possessor claims ownership.

Core Idea
If a pawn claimant and possessor both claim ownership, the claimant wins and the item is held for the absent mortgagee.

This lesson covers a scenario where a person claims that an object in someone else's possession is his own pawn to an absent person. The possessor also claims ownership. The Hanafi rule gives judgment to the claimant and places the object with a trustee until the absent mortgagee appears.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
There is a claimant (who says the item is his pawn to an absent person), a possessor (who claims the item is his own), and an absent mortgagee.
2
Analyze the possessor's claim
If the possessor claims ownership for himself, he becomes a defendant (khasm) to the claimant's suit.
The text says: 'wa dhu al-yad yaqul huwa 'abdi, yuqda li al-mudda'i'.
3
Apply the rule
The claimant's evidence is preferred because he is establishing his right, and the possessor's claim is self-interested.
4
Determine the disposition
The item is placed in the hands of a trustworthy person (yadu 'adl) until the absent mortgagee appears and can claim his right.
5
Contrast with another scenario
If the possessor acknowledges that the item belongs to the absent person, he has no right to hold it, so the case is different.
The text says: 'bikhilaf ma law amara bi al-milk li al-gha'ib'.

Mortgagee Claims Return of Pawn: Burden of Proof and Comparison with Usurper
Burden of proof in disputed return of pawn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi rule on burden of proof when the mortgagee claims he returned the pawn versus a similar claim by a usurper.

Core Idea
The pawner must prove non-return of the pawn; the mortgagee's liability is not established before destruction.

When the mortgagee claims he took the money and returned the pawn, but the pawner denies the return, who bears the burden? This lesson explains the Hanafi rule and contrasts it with the case of a usurper who claims return of usurped property.

Guided Path
1
State the specific dispute
Mortgagee says: 'I took the money and returned the pawn.' Pawner denies the return.
2
Apply the burden of proof rule
The burden is on the pawner (to prove that the mortgagee did not return the pawn).
Text: 'fa al-bayyinah li al-rahin' because the pawner's evidence would establish the mortgagee's liability.
3
Explain the rationale
The mortgagee's liability for the pawn was not established before destruction; his possession was only a right to hold (habs), not ownership. After destruction, the pawner's claim that the mortgagee is liable requires evidence.
The text: 'li'anna binat al-rahin tuthbit al-daman 'ala al-murtahin'.
4
Contrast with the usurper case
If a usurper (ghasib) claims he returned the property and the owner claims it was destroyed, the burden is on the usurper to prove return because his liability was established from the moment of usurpation.

5

The key difference is when the liability arose. In pawn, liability for destruction arises only after the pawn is destroyed; in usurpation, liability for return exists from the act of usurpation.

Agent's Confession in Pawn: Binding Effect and Consequences
Ruling on agent's confession in pawn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that an agent's confession of pawning binds the principal, and determine when the agent can recover his payment.

Core Idea
An agent's confession of pawning is binding; he cannot later deny it and lose his money.

If an agent is given an item to pawn for a principal, but he pawns it to himself or in a different manner, and the item is destroyed, what are the consequences? This lesson covers the Hanafi rule on the agent's confession and its effect on liability.

Guided Path
1
Set up the scenario
A principal gives a heart (metal) to an agent to pawn for 10 units. The heart weighs 20. The agent pawns it for 10 from himself (his own money) and says 'I pawned it', without specifying to whom. The heart is destroyed.
2
Case 1: Both parties confirm the pawn
If the principal and agent agree that the agent pawned it, the agent is a trustee (amin) for the heart and can recover his 10 from the principal because the pawn debt is deemed satisfied by destruction.
Text: 'in tasadaqa yarji' bi al-asharah wa kana aminan fi al-qalb'.
3
Case 2: The agent denies the pawn after confessing
If the principal says 'you confessed you pawned it', the agent's earlier confession is binding. He cannot deny the pawn to avoid liability. He loses his 10 because the pawn is deemed valid and the heart destroyed.
Text: 'fa la shay' lahu, yuqbal qawluhu ba'da an yahlif'.
4
Explain the oath
The agent must swear that he does not know that he held the heart wrongfully (i.e., that he did not misappropriate it).
The oath is to confirm his trustworthiness.
5
Reason for the ruling
The agent's confession is like any other confession (iqrar); he cannot retract it. Therefore, the pawn is established, and the principal is deemed to have paid the debt through the destruction of the pawn.
The text compares to other confessions: 'kama fi sa'ir al-aqarir'.

Liability on a pledge returns with possession: repossession reinstates the mortgagee's risk.

This passage from Bahr al-Raiq explains a key rule in the law of pledge (rahn): the mortgagee's liability is not permanent but depends on possession. When the pledge returns to his hand, his responsibility is restored, and if it perishes, the debt is considered paid. The commentary also clarifies the mortgagee's right to retake possession and his priority over other creditors if the mortgagor dies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the matn: 'And by its return his liability is restored.' Understand that the ruling states that when the pledge comes back to the mortgagee's possession, his liability (daman) is reinstated.
2
Understand the basis
The liability returns because the possession (qabd) that necessitates liability has returned. This possession gives the mortgagee control and thus responsibility for the pledge.
Possession is the legal cause (sabab) of liability.
3
Explore the mortgagee's right
The mortgagee has the right to retake possession because the pledge contract remains valid. He is not forced to leave it with the mortgagor permanently.
The loan (ariyah) arrangement is not binding.
4
Examine the edge case
If the mortgagor dies before the mortgagee retakes possession, the mortgagee has priority over other creditors. This is because his hand via loan is not binding, but his original right as mortgagee persists.
This shows that the mortgagee's right is superior to unsecured creditors.
5
Analyze the offspring example
The commentator notes that the offspring of a pledge is itself a pledge but not guaranteed by the mortgagee. This illustrates that liability is not an inherent quality of a pledge; it depends on possession.
The offspring, even though part of the pledge, is not in the mortgagee's possession, so no liability.

Each Party's Right to Return the Pledge
Matn and commentary on right of each to return pledge after temporary removal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that the pledge contract remains intact after temporary removal (loan/deposit) and identify which actions (sale, lease, gift) invalidate it.

Core Idea
The pledge contract persists after temporary non-possessory actions, but permanent transfers void it.

This lesson builds on the previous discussion of pledge possession. Here we explore the principle that the pledge contract is not automatically voided when the item leaves the mortgagee's hands for a temporary, non-binding purpose. The text from Kanz al-Daqaiq and its commentary in Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the rights of both parties to return the pledge and the consequences of various actions, including permission to use and disputes about timing of destruction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Ruling
State that each party (mortgagor and mortgagee) may return the pledge to its original status because the contract of rahn remains intact. This is the basic ruling.
The phrase 'ولكل أن يرده رهناً' is the matn.
2
Distinguish Actions That Preserve vs. Invalidate
Contrast temporary non-binding actions (loan, deposit) with permanent transfers (sale, lease, gift). The former do not invalidate the contract; the latter require a new contract to re-pledge.
The commentary explicitly lists the invalidating acts.
3
Analyze the Effect of Permission to Use
When the mortgagor permits the mortgagee to use or lend the pledge for work, determine liability based on when destruction occurs: before work → debt extinguished; after work → trust (amanah); during work → trust.
The distinction hinges on whether the hand of trust (amanah) is present.
4
Handle Disputes About Time of Destruction
If the parties disagree on when the pledge perished, the mortgagee's statement is accepted because he denies liability (he is the defendant), and the mortgagor must provide evidence.
This is a procedural rule based on the principle that the defendant's oath is preferred.

He said: (And each may return it as a pledge) meaning each of the mortgagor and mortgagee has a right in the pledge, so he may return it as a pledge in its place because the pledge contract remains, unlike if one of them leases it, sells it, or gifts it to the mortgagee or a third party before re-pledging it, for it does not return as a pledge except by a new contract. If the mortgagor dies, the mortgagee is equal to other creditors because these transactions invalidate the pledge, unlike loan (ariyah) and deposit (wadi'ah) because they are non-binding. If the mortgagor permits the mortgagee to use the pledge or to lend it for work, and it perishes before the work begins, it perishes against the debt (i.e., the debt is extinguished) based on the foundation of the pledge contract, and similarly if it perishes after the work is finished due to the removal of the hand of trust. If it perishes during the work, it perishes as a trust (amanah). If they disagree about the time of destruction, the mortgagee's statement is accepted because he is denying, and the evidence is on the mortgagor because he is claiming.

Commentary
This passage explains that after the pledge has been temporarily removed from the mortgagee's possession (e.g., by loan), either the mortgagor or the mortgagee can return it to its original status as a pledge. This is because the contract of pledge (rahn) remains intact. The commentary contrasts this with other acts like leasing, selling, or giving the pledge as a gift; these invalidate the pledge contract, so the item cannot be returned as a pledge without a new contract. The commentary also details scenarios where the mortgagor grants the mortgagee permission to use the pledge or lend it for work: if it perishes before work begins, the debt is considered paid; if after work, the loss is on the mortgagee as an amanah (trust); if during work, it is also an amanah. Key Terms
أمانة (amanah): Trust; the pledge is held as a trust when the mortgagee is permitted to use it or lend it for work.

Executor's Duties Upon Death of the Pawner
Rules for executor selling pawn, judge appointing executor, and prohibition of favoring creditors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the executor's obligatory duties regarding a pawn after the pawner's death and identify when pawning to a creditor is prohibited due to preference (ithar).

Core Idea
The executor must sell the pawn to pay the debt; favoring one creditor by pawning is forbidden as it is like paying them preferentially.

When a pawner dies, the executor steps in to manage the estate. The primary duty is to sell the pawn and settle the secured debt, whether the executor is appointed by will or by a judge. However, the executor cannot use the estate to give preference to one creditor over others by pawning assets to them—this is analogous to direct payment and is prohibited during deathly illness. This lesson unpacks these rules and their reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the executor's primary duty
Upon the pawner's death, the executor must sell the pawn and pay the debt because the executor stands in the place of the testator and has the same authority to sell.
The obligation applies whether the executor was appointed by will or by a judge.
2
Recognize the role of the judge
If the deceased had no executor, the judge appoints one to handle the sale. The judge is the supervisor of Muslims' rights when they cannot manage themselves.
The judge's appointment ensures the debt is paid and the estate is administered fairly.
3
Understand the prohibition of ithar (favoring creditors)
The executor cannot pawn part of the estate to one creditor if there are other creditors, because this gives preference (ithar) and is like paying that creditor constructively (ifa' hukmi).
This prohibition mirrors the rule that a person in deathly illness cannot favor one creditor over others.
4
Apply the analogy to actual payment
The text uses qiyas jaliy: pawning to a creditor is analogous to actual payment (ifa' haqiqi). The common factor (jami') is that both annul the rights of other creditors.
Thus, the same prohibition applies during marad al-mawt (deathly illness).
5
Note the exceptions
If the executor pays off all creditors before the pawning is rejected, or if there is only one creditor, the pawning becomes valid because the obstacle is removed or no other creditor's right is harmed.
Additionally, if the executor accepts a pawn for a debt owed to the deceased, it is permissible as it is collecting a right.

He (the author) said: (And if the pawner dies, his executor sells the pawn and pays the debt, because the executor stands in the place of the testator, and just as he could sell the pawn, so can the executor.) He said: (And if he has no executor, the judge appoints an executor for him and orders its sale.) And that is the judge's function because the judge is appointed as a supervisor of the rights of Muslims, when they are unable to manage themselves. He has determined the share of the executor to fulfill obligations to others and collect his rights from others. And if the deceased had a debt and the executor pawned some of the estate to one of his creditors, it is not permissible. Commentary
This passage covers several rules regarding the estate of a deceased pawner. Firstly, the executor is obligated to sell the pawned property and use the proceeds to pay off the debt secured by the pawn, because the executor acts as the legal representative of the deceased. If no executor is appointed, the judge must appoint one to handle the sale. Secondly, the executor is prohibited from giving preference to one creditor over others by pawning part of the estate to that creditor, because this is equivalent to paying that creditor first, which is forbidden during deathly illness (marad al-mawt) if there are other creditors. However, if the executor pays off all creditors before any objection, the pawning becomes valid. If there is only one creditor, pawning is allowed. Thirdly, the executor may accept a pawn for a debt owed to the deceased, as that is collecting a right. These rules ensure fair treatment of all creditors and proper administration of the estate.

Key Terms
وصي (wasi): Executor of the deceased's estate, appointed by will or by judge.
إيثار (ithar): Giving preference to a creditor over others, which is prohibited during deathly illness.
إيفاء حكمي (ifa' hukmi): Constructive payment, like pawning to a creditor, which is treated like actual payment.
