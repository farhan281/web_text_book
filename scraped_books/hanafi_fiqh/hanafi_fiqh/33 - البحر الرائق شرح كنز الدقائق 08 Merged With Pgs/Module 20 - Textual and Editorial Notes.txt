The Master's Taking of Dharibat al-Mithl from the Ma'dhun
Ruling on master's taking of dharibat al-mithl by istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the master's taking of the ma'dhun's earnings and explain the istihsan reasoning that permits taking only the equivalent of service value.

Core Idea
The master may take only the equivalent of service value (dharibat al-mithl); excess is invalid and must go to creditors.

This lesson examines a key ruling from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq concerning the master's right to the earnings of a ma'dhun (slave permitted to trade). The Hanafi school distinguishes between taking the equivalent of the slave's labor value, which is valid by istihsan, and taking any excess, which is batil. We will unpack the reasoning behind this ruling and its practical implications for creditor rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is the master taking earnings from the ma'dhun while the slave has debts. The condition of indebtedness makes the earnings generally encumbered.
Without debts, the master could take all earnings, but with debts, creditors have priority.
2
State the default ruling by qiyas
By strict analogy (qiyas), any taking by the master would be invalid because the earnings are tied to creditors' rights.
Qiyas would require the master to return all taken earnings to the creditors.
3
Explain the istihsan exception
The master has the right to the slave's labor (since benefits remain master's property). Taking dharibat al-mithl is compensation for waiving that labor right. Hence, it is valid by istihsan.
Istihsan prefers this equitable reasoning over strict qiyas.
4
Identify the limit of validity
Only the equivalent of service value (dharibat al-mithl) is valid. Any excess is taken without compensation and is batil.
The master cannot take more than what he would earn by hiring out the slave.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the sharh of Kanz al-Daqaiq on the subject of the ma'dhun (slave permitted to trade). It addresses two main issues: (1) the master's right to take the slave's earnings when the slave has debts, and (2) the effect of a creditor's acknowledgment (iqrar) or release (ibra) on the master's taking. The author explains that if the master takes only the equivalent of the slave's service value (dharibat mithlihi), it is valid by istihsan because the master sacrifices his right to the slave's labor. Any excess must be returned to the creditors. The passage then examines scenarios with two consecutive debts: if the master takes the slave's earnings and later another debt arises, the master must return what he took so that both creditors can share proportionally. If a creditor releases the slave from his debt before the second debt arises, the master's taking is valid; if after, it is not, because the slave's earnings were already encumbered by the creditors' rights. The author also distinguishes between a creditor's false denial of his debt (which confirms the slave's acknowledgment was false and validates the master's taking) and a genuine release (which does not retroactively clear the encumbrance). Finally, if the master believes his slave's acknowledgment of a debt, he effectively acknowledges that the slave's earnings are shared among creditors, and cannot later claim exclusive ownership.

Key Terms
ضريبة المثل (dharībat al-mithl): The equivalent of the slave's service value; the amount the master would earn by hiring out the slave.
المأذون (al-ma'dhūn): A slave permitted by the master to trade, with capacity to contract debts and be sued.
استحسان (istihsān): Juristic preference, departing from strict analogy for a stronger reason.

Effects of Creditor's Acknowledgment and Release on the Ma'dhun's Earnings
Distinction between ibra and false iqrar in validating master's taking

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the legal effects of a creditor's release (ibra) and false denial (iqrar) on the master's right to the ma'dhun's earnings.

Core Idea
Release after creditor rights attach does not validate master's taking; false denial voids the slave's acknowledgment and validates the taking.

This lesson continues from the previous one, focusing on two scenarios involving creditors: when a creditor releases the slave from his debt (ibra), and when a creditor falsely denies the debt (iqrar). The Hanafi school draws a critical distinction: release does not retroactively clear encumbrance, but a false denial effectively nullifies the slave's acknowledgment, freeing the earnings for the master. Understanding this distinction is essential for determining the master's rights and creditor priority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two creditor actions
The passage examines two actions by the first creditor: (1) releasing the slave from the debt (ibra), and (2) falsely stating that the debt never existed (false iqrar).
Both actions occur after a second debt has arisen.
2
Analyze the effect of release (ibra)
If release occurs after the second debt, the master's taking is not valid because release is an isqat (dropping) after obligation; it does not change the fact that the earnings were encumbered when the master took them.
If release occurs before the second debt, the master's taking is valid.
3
Analyze the effect of false denial (false iqrar)
If the first creditor falsely says 'I had no debt on the slave,' this makes the slave's earlier acknowledgment void (since the slave's iqrar is contradicted by the creditor's statement). The debt is deemed never to have existed, so the master's taking is valid.
The false denial is considered a repudiation of the debt from the root.
4
Contrast the two outcomes
Release only drops the debt from the time of release forward; it does not retroactively clear prior encumbrance. False denial, by nullifying the slave's iqrar, retroactively shows no debt existed, so the master's taking is valid.
The key difference: ibra is isqat (dropping) after wujub; false iqrar is takdhib (declaring false) of the basis.

Ajr al-Mithl for Extended Land Use in Muzara'ah
Explaining the obligation of fair rent on the tenant when the lease period ends before the crop is ready.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ajr al-mithl when the muzara'ah period ends before the crop ripens, and explain the distinguishing argument from regular ijarah.

Core Idea
In muzara'ah, the wage is delivered with possession of the seed, so the tenant owes ajr al-mithl for continued land use.

Imagine a sharecropping contract where the agreed period ends, but the crop is not yet ready. Must the tenant pay extra? The Hanafi school says yes, based on fairness and preventing waste. In this lesson, we unpack the ruling and the clever analogy-defeating response to an objection from al-'Inayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the opening statement: 'If the period passes and the crop has not ripened, then upon the tenant is the rental equivalent.' This is the core ruling.
Ajr al-mithl means fair market rent for similar land.
2
Recognize the objection
The commentator cites al-'Inayah: The objector analogizes to ijarah where the leased object is destroyed before delivery – in that case, the lessee owes ajr al-mithl. So why not here?
The objector thinks the named wage (crop share) is lost, just like the object in ijarah.
3
Analyze the distinction
The answer: In muzara'ah, the wage (the seed or its equivalent) was delivered to the tenant at the start. Possession of the seed is possession of its produce. So the wage is not lost before delivery; it was delivered.
This breaks the analogy (qiyas) because the crucial condition 'loss before delivery' is absent.
4
Apply the ruling
Since the tenant benefited from the land after the contract ended, and the original wage covered only the contract period, ajr al-mithl is wajib for the extra period by istihsan (to avoid waste and injustice).
The land yields no rent if the tenant does not pay; the crop is saved for the tenant, so fairness demands compensation.
5
Note the cross-madhhab view
The Shafi'i school holds that the contract terminates immediately, and the tenant must remove the crop even if unripe, with no rent for extra time.
This shows how different usul positions lead to different practical rulings.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the matn of Kanz al-Daqaiq along with its commentary from al-Bahr al-Raiq. The ruling states that if the fixed period of a muzara'ah contract ends before the crop is ripe enough to harvest, the tenant (al-zari') must pay the landowner ajr al-mithl (fair rental value) for the land for the additional period until the crop ripens. The commentator explains that the land is 'released' from the contract when ajr al-mithl becomes due, but even if not formally released, the obligation remains. An objection from al-'Inayah is then raised: In a regular ijarah (lease) where the leased object is destroyed before delivery, the lessee owes ajr al-mithl. By analogy, in muzara'ah, since the contract was valid and the named wage (the share of crop) is destroyed because no crop emerged, ajr al-mithl should be owed. The answer distinguishes: In muzara'ah, the wage (the seed or the share) is destroyed after delivery because the tenant took possession of the seed, which is the origin of the crop; taking possession of the origin is like taking possession of its branches. In the ijarah case, the specific object was destroyed before delivery, so nothing is owed. Thus, in muzara'ah, the tenant owes ajr al-mithl for the additional use of the land.

استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; departing from strict analogy for a stronger reason (fairness, necessity).

The Placement of Istibra' and the Mutlaq/Muqayyad Principle in Fiqh Ordering
Explaining why istibra' is placed after 'azl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that the restricted (muqayyad) follows the unrestricted (mutlaq) in the arrangement of fiqh topics, and apply it to the placement of istibra' after 'azl.

Core Idea
The restricted (muqayyad) follows the unrestricted (mutlaq) in Hanafi fiqh arrangement.

In Hanafi fiqh manuals, the order of topics is not arbitrary but reflects a logical progression. This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains why the section on istibra' (the waiting period for a slave woman) is placed after the discussion of 'azl (coitus interruptus). The commentator reveals the underlying principle: matters related to restricted intercourse (istibra') follow those related to unrestricted intercourse ('azl). Understanding this ordering principle helps students appreciate the systematic reasoning in Hanafi legal writing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two topics
Note that the previous chapter deals with 'azl, which involves intercourse ('wat' mutlaq) without temporal restriction. The current section is on istibra', which is a precautionary waiting period for a female slave after acquisition, and is considered 'wat' muqayyad because it is linked to a specific time (one menstrual cycle).
The terms 'mutlaq' and 'muqayyad' are used both for the act of intercourse and for the ruling itself.
2
Understand the ordering principle
The commentator states that istibra' is delayed because it deals with a restricted ownership (muqayyad), and the restricted comes after the unrestricted (mutlaq). This is a general principle in arranging fiqh topics.
The principle is that the general or unconditional precedes the specific or conditional.
3
Recognize the criticism
Some scholars questioned why there was no explicit mention of precaution against unrestricted intercourse in the previous chapter. They thought the heading was only about 'azl, not a general treatment of intercourse.
The critic assumes the previous chapter's heading does not cover the concept of 'unrestricted intercourse'.
4
Follow the commentator's resolution
The commentator refutes the criticism by clarifying that the 'unrestricted intercourse' refers to the act of intercourse inherent in the issue of 'azl itself, while 'restricted intercourse' refers to the intercourse that necessitates istibra'. Thus, istibra' (restricted) is correctly placed after 'azl (unrestricted).
The existence of the restricted implies the existence of the unrestricted, as the compound includes the simple.
5
Apply the principle
This ordering is consistent with the rule that the compound (muqayyad) follows the simple (mutlaq). You can see similar ordering in other chapters: general rules of purity before menstruation, general contract rules before specific transactions.
This principle helps in anticipating and understanding the structure of Hanafi texts.

Commentary
This passage is a front_matter methodological discussion. It explains why the author of Kanz al-Daqaiq delayed the section on Istibra' (the waiting period for a slave woman to ensure she is not pregnant from a previous owner) until after the discussion of 'azl in the previous chapter. The commentator (sharih) states the principle: istibra' is a precaution concerning a restricted ownership (i.e., a slave woman whose pregnancy is to be avoided), and the restricted (muqayyad) is placed after the unrestricted (mutlaq) in the arrangement. He then responds to a criticism raised by 'some virtuous ones'. The critic questions where in the previous chapter is the precaution against unrestricted intercourse, since the chapter heading was about 'azl, not a general discussion of intercourse. The commentator initially seems to reject the question and answer, but then clarifies: the 'unrestricted intercourse' referred to is the act of intercourse itself mentioned in the context of 'azl, while 'restricted intercourse' here is the specific intercourse that requires istibra' to avoid mixing lineages. Istibra' is time-restricted (one menstrual cycle), while 'azl is not time-restricted. The ordering is consistent with the principle that the compound comes after the simple. This discussion shows the scholarly attention to the logical arrangement of fiqh topics.

الاستبراء (al-istibra'): The waiting period for a female slave after acquisition to ensure she is not pregnant from her previous owner; usually one menstrual cycle.
الوطء المطلق (al-wat' al-mutlaq): Unrestricted intercourse; here it refers to the act of intercourse in general, as in the issue of 'azl.
الوطء المقيد (al-wat' al-muqayyad): Restricted intercourse; here it refers to the specific act of intercourse that requires istibra' to avoid uncertain paternity.

Why Istibra' Follows 'Azl: The Logic of Chapter Ordering
Meta-commentary on chapter arrangement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi jurists placed the istibra' section after the 'azl section, applying the principle that restricted matters follow absolute ones.

Core Idea
The restricted (istibra') follows the absolute ('azl) because the compound presupposes the simple.

In Hanafi fiqh texts, the order of chapters is not arbitrary but follows a logical sequence. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains why istibra'—a precaution for slave women after intercourse—is placed after 'azl (coitus interruptus). The commentator clarifies that 'azl deals with absolute intercourse, while istibra' deals with intercourse restricted by time; since the restricted presupposes the absolute, it comes later. Understanding this order helps you navigate the text and grasp the conceptual framework of Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two topics
Recognise that 'azl (coitus interruptus) and istibra' (waiting period for slave women) are both related to intercourse but differ: 'azl is about absolute intercourse, istibra' about intercourse restricted by time (after which the woman's womb must be cleared).
These are distinct rulings, but the order reflects a conceptual link.
2
Understand the 'restricted after absolute' principle
The commentator argues that the restricted (muqayyad) comes after the absolute (mutlaq) because the restricted incorporates the absolute plus an additional condition. Istibra' is restricted by time, so it logically follows 'azl which is unrestricted.
This is a common organizational principle in Hanafi texts.
3
Reconstruct the objection and response
An imagined objection asks: 'Where was the precaution regarding absolute intercourse earlier?' The commentator shows that the answer—that the previous chapter's title 'touching' implies intercourse—is flawed. He then gives the correct explanation: the precaution regarding restricted intercourse (istibra') is placed after the precaution regarding absolute intercourse ('azl) in the natural order.
Follow the dialectic: objection → flawed answer → corrected reasoning.
4
Apply the principle to this text
See that the order is based on the logical dependence: restricted intercourse (istibra') can only occur if absolute intercourse exists; therefore the absolute is discussed first.
This illustrates how fiqh texts are structured for pedagogical clarity.

The key distinction is between absolute intercourse (without restriction) and restricted intercourse (with temporal restriction). The commentator engages with an objection that the precaution regarding absolute intercourse should have preceded, and argues that the restricted comes after the absolute in the natural order, and that the concept of restricted intercourse presupposes absolute intercourse. The discussion also clarifies that the title of the previous chapter using 'touching' (al-mass) implicitly includes intercourse.

مطلق (mutlaq): Absolute, unrestricted; in this context, intercourse without temporal restriction.

Critique of the Phrase 'جبر عنه' and the Nature of Qualification 'مثل الدين'
Commentator critiques a previous interpretation of the term 'جبر' in the context of the pledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the phrase 'جبر عنه' is problematic in Arabic and distinguish between an accidental (ittifaqi) and restrictive (ihtirazi) qualification in Hanafi legal texts.

Core Idea
The phrase 'جبر عنه' is not standard Arabic; the qualification 'مثل الدين' is accidental, not restrictive.

This lesson examines a key textual critique from Al-Bahr al-Raiq where the commentator refines the understanding of a phrase in the matn about pledges. Understanding such linguistic and interpretive precision is essential for correctly applying Hanafi rulings. We will dissect the commentator's arguments and learn the usul distinction between accidental and restrictive qualifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase under critique
Locate the term 'جبر عنه' in the passage and understand the proposed meaning by previous interpreters (compensation from him).
The commentator argues this phrase is not found in classical Arabic.
2
Examine the linguistic argument
The commentator states that 'جبر عنه' is not heard in Arabic, whether from 'jabr' (force) or 'jubran' (compensation). This is a linguistic critique.
Thus, the construction is linguistically invalid.
3
Understand the logical problem
Even if the phrase were valid, it fails in cases where the pledgor redeems before maturity without a corresponding 'payment itself'.
This shows the interpretation is not comprehensive.
4
Learn the alternative interpretation
The commentator suggests that the word 'عن' can simply take the implied object 'نيابة' (substitution), as done in al-Kafiyya, avoiding the need for 'جبر عنه'.
This is more straightforward and conforms to Arabic usage.
5
Distinguish qayd ittifaqi vs ihtirazi
The phrase 'مثل الدين' in al-Hidayah is an accidental qualification (ittifaqi), meaning it describes a common scenario but does not restrict the ruling to only that scenario. A restrictive qualification (ihtirazi) would limit the ruling to cases where the debt is similar.
This distinction affects the scope of application.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) by the commentator on the wording of the matn and its interpretation. The text begins by quoting a portion of the matn: 'The mortgagee if he repays his debt, and the injury of the pledgor and the mortgagee upon the pledge is guaranteed, and the injury of the pledge upon them both.' Then the commentator says 'End' and begins his critique. He points out that the previous interpretation of the word 'جبر' (compensation) is problematic both logically and linguistically. First, it fails to cover the case where the pledgor redeems the pledge before the debt's maturity. Second, the phrase 'جبر عنه' is not authentically Arabic. He argues that the real point is that the word 'عن' is part of the construction, and the intended meaning can be achieved simply by specifying the object of 'عن' as 'نيابة' (substitution), as al-Kafiyya did. He concludes that the phrase 'مثل الدين' in the Hidayah is an accidental (ittifaqi) qualifier, not a restrictive (ihtirazi) one, meaning it describes a common case but does not limit the ruling.

احتزازي (ihtirazi): A restrictive qualification that limits the ruling to the described state.
