Kitabah of a Kafir Master with His Kafir Slave on Wine: Validity of Wine as Consideration
Explaining validity of kitabah between kafirs on wine

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kitabah between a kafir master and his kafir slave on wine and identify the legal basis for its validity.

Core Idea
Wine is valid consideration in kitabah between kafirs because it is property for them.

The Hanafi school recognizes that non-Muslims have their own concepts of valuable property. This lesson examines a specific case: a kafir master contracting kitabah (a manumission agreement) with his kafir slave on wine. Although wine is not property for Muslims, it is considered valuable (mal mutaqawwim) among kafirs, making the contract valid. We will explore the ruling, its rationale, and how it applies to different categories of kafirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is kitabah (contract of manumission) between a kafir master and his kafir slave, with wine as the consideration. The ruling is that the contract is valid (sahih) and the slave becomes free upon payment.
2
Locate the legal basis (dalil)
The basis is the principle that wine is valuable property (mal mutaqawwim) among kafirs. The text compares it to juice (عصير) for Muslims—both are valid considerations in their respective contexts.
This is a talil (reasoning) rather than a transmitted proof.
3
Trace the scope of the ruling
The ruling applies to all categories of kafirs: dhimmi, musta'min, and harbi. For dhimmi, it applies whether in dar al-Islam or dar al-harb (if not emigrated). For musta'min, it applies while in dar al-Islam.
The exception is if the slave is Muslim—then the contract is invalid and the value must be paid.
4
Consider the edge case of harbi in dar al-harb
If a harbi master makes kitabah with his Muslim slave on wine or pork in dar al-harb, and the slave pays, the slave becomes free. This is based on the principle that Muslims may use stratagems to take the property of harbis.
This shows flexibility in rules of war and property.

Ruling on a Harbi Slave Who Becomes Muslim
Ruling on harbi slave's status upon Islam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a harbi slave who becomes Muslim and distinguish the outcomes based on permission (musta'min) vs. defiance (muraghim).

Core Idea
A harbi slave entering Muslim land with permission remains property; if defiant, becomes free.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq expands on a key branch of the law of jihad: the status of a slave belonging to a harbi (enemy combatant) who enters Muslim territory and embraces Islam. The ruling hinges on the nature of the entry—whether with the master's permission (as a musta'min trader) or as a fugitive (muraghim). Understanding this distinction is essential for applying the Islamic law of war and slavery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
Read the passage and note the two distinct cases: (1) slave enters as musta'min for trade, (2) slave enters as muraghim (fugitive).
Also note the third scenario: slave becomes Muslim in dar al-harb and comes out as trader, which follows the first case.
2
Determine legal consequences
For the musta'min case: imam sells the slave and keeps the price for the harbi master. For the muraghim case: the slave becomes free and may contract wala with whomever he wishes.
Exception: if the treasury has paid blood-wit for the muraghim slave, wala goes to the treasury.
3
Identify the ratio legis (illa)
The key distinction is the master's permission. If the slave came out with permission, he remains property because the master's rights are preserved. Without permission (defiance), the slave attains freedom.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that permission indicates consent to the slave's presence in Muslim lands.
4
Analyze the exception
In the muraghim case, if the treasury has paid blood-wit (aql) on behalf of the slave, the right of wala (patronage) transfers to the treasury. This prevents the slave from choosing a patron freely.
The treasury's payment creates a claim similar to that of a master.
5
Consider cross-madhhab differences
The Shafi'i school may view any harbi who enters Muslim land and becomes Muslim as automatically free, regardless of permission. This is a mukhtalaf fih issue.
The Hanafi position protects the property rights of the harbi master during the period of aman (safe conduct).

(فروع) عبد الحربي خرج مستأمناً في تجارة لمولاه فأسلم يبيعه الإمام ويمسك ثمنه على مولاه وكذا لو أسلم العبد في دار الحرب وخرج تاجراً لمولاه لأنه لم يعتق عليه لما خرج بإذن المولى، وإن خرج مراغماً فهو حر ويوالي من شاء إلا إذا عقل عنه بيت المال اهـ. والله أعلم بالصواب.

Likewise, if the slave becomes Muslim in dar al-harb and comes out as a trader for his master; because he is not manumitted for him since he came out with the master's permission. And if he comes out as a mughami (fugitive/defiant), he becomes free and may contract wala with whomever he wishes, unless the treasury (bayt al-mal) has paid blood-wit for him.

Commentary
This 'branches' (furu') section covers the case of a slave belonging to a harbi (non-Muslim enemy) who enters the Muslim land under safe conduct (musta'min) for trade for his master and then becomes Muslim. The imam (ruler) sells him and retains the price for his harbi master, because the slave did not become free by entering Muslim territory as he came with his master's permission. Similarly, if the slave becomes Muslim in dar al-harb and later comes out as a trader for his master, the same ruling applies. However, if he comes out fleeing (muraghim), meaning he rebels against his master and enters Muslim land without permission, he becomes free and can choose his own patron (yuwali man sha) unless the treasury has paid blood-wit (aql) on his behalf, in which case wala goes to the treasury.

Key Terms
مستأمناً (musta'min): A non-Muslim entering Muslim territory under safe conduct (aman) for temporary purposes.
مراغماً (muraghiman): Fleeing from the master to Muslim territory with defiance, not permission.

Master's Liability for Jinaaya of a Ma'dhun Slave: The General Rule
General rule of master's liability for slave's jinaaya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the master's liability for jinaaya of a ma'dhun slave and explain the reasoning based on the master's ownership of the slave's person.

Core Idea
The master is liable to surrender or ransom the ma'dhun slave for his jinaaya because the slave's person belongs to the master.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq establishes the foundational rule: when a ma'dhun slave commits a crime, the master is the one against whom the claim is made. The reason is that the slave's person is an earning of the master, not the slave himself. Understanding this principle is essential before we examine how debts or multiple crimes affect the liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is jinaaya (crime like homicide) committed by a ma'dhun slave. The ruling: the master is demanded to either surrender the slave (daf‘) or pay ransom (fidā’).
2
Determine the legal basis
The text states: 'because the opponent concerning the person of the ma'dhun slave is the master'. The slave's person is considered the master's property (kasp al-mawlā), so the claim is against the master.
3
Analyze the exception of inability to surrender
If the master has already transferred ownership or done something that makes surrender impossible (i‘jāz), then he becomes liable for the value. Otherwise, he can always surrender.
The text says: 'Any transaction that the owner makes regarding the slave that does not disable him from surrender does not make him liable.'
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi‘is) may hold the slave personally liable if the value exceeds his worth. Hanafis maintain the master's liability as primary.
This ikhtilaf is mentioned in the analytical toolbox.

The passage also discusses multiple crimes and confessions, illustrating the Hanafi approach to determining liability based on the sequence of events. The distinction between inability to surrender (i'jaz) and liability is emphasized: a master who retains ability to surrender is not automatically liable.

الفداء (al-fidā’): Ransom paid by the master to redeem the slave from the crime victims.

Debt and Multiple Crimes: Priority and Master's Liability
Detailed scenarios of debt and multiple crimes affecting master's liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine how pre-existing debt affects the master's liability for a ma'dhun slave's crimes and calculate the distribution of the slave's value among creditors and crime victims.

Core Idea
The timing of debt and crime determines whether the master is liable for full value, half, or nothing.

The previous lesson established the master's general liability. This lesson dives into the nuanced scenarios where debts and multiple crimes interplay. The passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq provides a detailed analysis of how priority is determined by the sequence of events. Understanding these priority rules is crucial for correctly allocating the slave's value between creditors and crime victims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the relevant timelines
The passage presents three key scenarios: (a) crime before debt, (b) crime after debt, (c) multiple crimes with debts in between. Identify which timeline is being discussed.
2
Apply the general rule for each timeline
For crime before debt: victims have right on unencumbered slave → master liable for value. For crime after debt: victims receive encumbered slave → master not liable. For multiple crimes and debts: the order determines shares.
3
Analyze the reasoning for the half-value case
When one crime before debt and another after, the later crime victim gets half from the master. Why? Because the first crime victim takes half from the slave, and the second gets half from the master after debt is satisfied.
The text says 'رجع صاحب الجناية الأخيرة على مولاه بنصف قيمته حصته من الدين الباقي'.
4
Understand the implication of confession
If the master confesses to two crimes and surrenders the slave half to each, the first victims may still claim half from the master if the second confession is deemed an admission of transfer that is invalid.

Because if both debts were due before the crime, the crime victims would not recover anything from the master. If both debts attached between the crimes, the crime victims recover from the master the entire value of the slave. And if one attached before the crime and the other after, each debt has its own ruling. He confessed against his slave for a crime then another crime; he surrendered him to both halves, then the victim of the first crime recovers from the master half of his value if the guardians contradict, because the confession to the second crime is a confession of transferring ownership of the slave to the guardians of the crime. Commentary
This passage addresses the complex scenario when a ma'dhun slave commits a crime (jinaaya) and also owes debts. The key principle: the master is liable for the slave's crime (to surrender or pay ransom) because the slave's person belongs to the master. However, the debt is attached to the slave's earning capacity, not to the master. The timing of debt and crime matters for distributing the slave's value. If the debt existed before the crime, the crime victims get a slave already burdened; they cannot claim from the master beyond what the slave's sale yields. If the crime precedes the debt, the master is liable for the full value of the slave to the crime victims because their right was established on an unencumbered slave. The passage also discusses multiple crimes and confessions, illustrating the Hanafi approach to determining liability based on the sequence of events. The distinction between inability to surrender (i'jaz) and liability is emphasized: a master who retains ability to surrender is not automatically liable.

Key Terms
الجناية (al-jināyah): A crime committed by the slave, triggering the master's liability.
الدين (al-dayn): A debt that attaches to the slave's earning capacity or person.
الضمان (al-ḍamān): Guarantee; the master becomes a guarantor when he encumbers the slave after the crime victims' right attached.

Teaching the Quran to a Non-Muslim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on teaching the Quran to a non-Muslim and identify the condition regarding touching the mushaf.

Core Idea
Teaching the Quran to a disbeliever is permissible, but physical contact with the mushaf is prohibited.

This passage from al-Dhakhira addresses a practical da'wah scenario: a non-Muslim requests Quranic instruction. The ruling clarifies that there is no harm in teaching both dhimmi and harbi, with an important restriction. Understanding this ruling helps balance the sanctity of the Quran with the goal of inviting others to Islam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is teaching the Quran to a non-Muslim. The ruling is 'la ba'sa' (no harm), meaning permissible (mubah) in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Distinguish the two categories of non-Muslim
The passage mentions 'ahl al-harb' (enemy combatants) and 'ahl al-dhimma' (protected citizens). Both are included; the ruling applies equally.
3
Recognize the exception (istithna)
Qadi 'Ali al-Sufdi adds 'except that he should not touch.' This restricts physical contact with the mushaf.
The exception implies a condition for the permissibility.
4
Understand the rationale
The exception is based on the requirement of ritual purity (tahara) for touching the mushaf, which a non-Muslim cannot fulfill without converting. Therefore, teaching is allowed verbally or by listening, not through handling the text.

Liability for Destroying or Freeing a Pledged Slave
Rulings on destruction/freeing of pledged slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings when a mortgagee destroys or a mortgagor frees a pledged slave, and identify when the slave must work (sa'ayah).

Core Idea
The mortgagee guarantees the pledge's value if he destroys it; the mortgagor cannot free a slave without the slave working off the debt.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq examines the legal consequences when a pledged slave is destroyed or freed. We will see that the mortgagee's liability is tied to possession, and the mortgagor's attempt to free the slave does not extinguish the creditor's right—the slave must work to pay off the debt. The text also highlights an intra-Hanafi disagreement with Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what action is being considered (destruction or freeing of the pledge) and the resulting legal obligation (guarantee of value or sa'ayah).
The mortgagee's destruction triggers a guarantee; the mortgagor's freeing triggers the slave's obligation to work.
2
Distinguish the two parties' actions
Separate the scenario where the mortgagee destroys the pledge from where the mortgagor frees it. Each has a different consequence.
Mortgagee: guarantees value and it remains a pledge. Mortgagor: the slave must work (sa'ayah) unless the mortgagor is wealthy.
3
Apply the conditions for sa'ayah
Identify when the slave must work: if the mortgagor confesses to the pledge and then frees the slave, and the mortgagor is insolvent.
If the mortgagor is wealthy, he pays the value directly; sa'ayah only applies if he is insolvent.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Recognize that Zufar holds the slave is not obligated to work if the master confesses to the pledge and then frees him, contrary to Abu Hanifa.
The mufta bihi is the position of Abu Hanifa.
5
Extend to mudabbar and umm walad
Apply the same principle to a mudabbar (slave promised freedom after master's death) or umm walad (slave mother of master's child): they only work for the value, not more.
Their earnings after freedom belong to them, so they only pay up to the value of the debt.
