Validity of a Will by an Interdicted Person: Qiyas vs Istihsan
Ruling on validity of will by interdicted person for pious purposes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a will for pious purposes by an interdicted person is valid by istihsan and invalid by qiyas, and identify the illa for both.

Core Idea
Interdiction protects during life; after death, a pious will is valid by istihsan.

The Hanafi school balances textual reasoning (qiyas) with juristic preference (istihsan). In this passage, we see how the rule of interdiction is relaxed after death for pious bequests, demonstrating the school's pragmatic flexibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a will (wasiyyah) by an interdicted person (mahjur 'alayh) for pious purposes. The ruling: valid by istihsan.
2
Locate the qiyas
Qiyas would invalidate the will, just as lifetime gratuitous transfers (e.g., gifts, charity) are invalid for the interdicted person.
Qiyas: asl = lifetime transfers; hukm = invalid; illa = risk of waste.
3
Compare the illa
The illa (effective cause) for interdiction is preventing waste and dependency on others. This applies only during life, not after death.
After death, the interdicted person no longer needs wealth and is not at risk of waste.
4
Apply istihsan
Istihsan overrides qiyas because the purpose of interdiction (protection) is already fulfilled by the time the will takes effect. Pious bequests are especially encouraged.
Istihsan considers the maslahah (benefit) and the absence of harm after death.
5
Determine the scope
Only bequests for pious purposes (e.g., hajj, charity, mosques, bridges) are valid. Bequests for non-pious purposes remain invalid.
The will is limited to one-third of the estate, as per standard wasiyyah rules.

Four Differences Between an Interdicted Person and a Minor
Four differences between an interdicted person and a minor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four differences between a mahjur 'alayh and a minor according to Imam Muhammad and explain the underlying reason for each.

Core Idea
The interdicted person retains capacity for personal acts; the minor's acts are void.

Imam Muhammad provides a concise comparison showing that despite similar financial restrictions, the mahjur 'alayh has greater legal capacity in key areas, reflecting the temporary nature of interdiction versus the minor's complete lack of capacity.

Guided Path
1
First difference: Executor's authority
For a minor, the executor can validly manage the minor's wealth. For a mahjur 'alayh, the executor's intervention is void; the mahjur retains control.
The text: 'تصرف الوصي في مال الصبي جائز وفي مال المحجور عليه باطل'.
2
Second difference: Personal acts (emancipation, tadbir, divorce, marriage)
These acts are valid from the mahjur but not from a minor. A minor lacks any legal capacity for such acts.
The text lists emancipation, tadbir, divorce, and marriage as valid from mahjur.
3
Third difference: Tadbir details
If the mahjur declares tadbir, it is valid. The slave does not work for deficiency during the mahjur's life. After death, the slave works for his value as mudabbar, unless the community considers the tadbir as extravagance; if it is a good custom, no work is required.
From Al-Muhit: the ruling depends on whether the tadbir is seen as wasteful.
4
Fourth difference: Cause of interdiction
Interdiction can be due to fisq (immorality) as well as safah (foolishness). The text mentions that the interdiction is 'because of fisq'.
The text cuts off, but this appears to be one of the four distinctions: the cause of interdiction matters.

Bequests of an Interdicted Person for Pious Causes
Ruling on validity of pious bequests by an interdicted person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequests by an interdicted person (mahjur) and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas in this case.

Core Idea
A mahjur's bequest for pious causes is valid by istihsan, though qiyas would reject it.

This passage from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a key tension between qiyas and istihsan in Hanafi law. A person under interdiction (mahjur) due to foolishness cannot make voluntary donations during life. But what about after death? The author rules that if the bequest is for pious acts, it is valid from the one-third by istihsan, because the reason for interdiction ceases after death. This lesson explores the rationale and limits of this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a bequest (wasiyyah) by a mahjur (interdicted person). The ruling is that it is valid if for pious causes (qurb) by istihsan.
The text specifies that the bequest is executed from the one-third of the estate.
2
Understand the qiyas argument
By analogy with his invalid lifetime donations, the bequest should also be invalid. This is the default qiyas.
The text explicitly says 'al-qiyas an la tajuz...' indicating the analogical reasoning.
3
Analyze the istihsan rationale
The purpose of interdiction is to protect property during life so he does not become a burden. After death, the one-third is surplus, so the reason for prohibition vanishes.
Istihsan is based on the change of circumstances between life and death.
4
Distinguish the scope
The exception applies only to bequests for recognized pious acts (e.g., Hajj, mosque building, charity). For non-pious bequests, the qiyas remains and the bequest is invalid.
This is a nuanced position unique to the Hanafi school.
5
Note the thamarah (practical effect)
A mahjur can still benefit the community through charitable bequests, and the estate is not wasted on non-pious purposes.
The ruling balances protection of the mahjur's property with enabling good deeds after death.

The rationale is that interdiction protects his property from waste during his lifetime, but after death, the one-third of his estate is no longer needed for his maintenance, so there is no harm in using it for good causes. Gift of a Slave in Death Illness to a Creditor: The One-Third Rule
Ruling on gifting a slave to his creditor during death illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two outcomes of gifting a slave to his creditor during marad al-mawt, depending on heirs' consent, and apply the one-third rule.

Core Idea
A death-illness gift to a creditor is a bequest: heirs' consent passes the whole; refusal limits it to one-third.

Death illness gifts are treated as bequests in Hanafi law, limited to one-third of the estate unless the heirs allow more. This passage examines a specific case: the testator gives his slave to a person who is also a creditor of that slave. The ruling distinguishes between heirs' acceptance and rejection, showing how the creditor's debt interacts with the part ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Recognize the testator, the slave, and the creditor (donee). Note that the donee is a creditor of the slave, meaning the slave owes a debt to him.
The slave is both subject of the gift and debtor.
2
Determine the nature of the gift
The gift is made during marad al-mawt (death illness). In Hanafi fiqh, such a gift is treated as a bequest (wasiyyah), subject to the one-third rule: it cannot exceed one-third of the estate without heirs' consent.
3
Analyze the first scenario: heirs consent
If the heirs approve (ajazat al-warathah), the entire slave goes to the creditor, and his debt against the slave is dropped (saqata daynuhu).
Heirs' consent removes the one-third limit.
4
Analyze the second scenario: heirs refuse
If the heirs refuse (abat), the creditor receives only one-third of the slave, and the remaining two-thirds stay with the heirs. The text adds 'wa la dayna lahu ghayr al-abdi bi-ghayr daynihi', which is interpreted that the creditor's debt on the slave is also affected, being reduced proportionally due to partial ownership.
The phrase is ambiguous; commentary explains proportional reduction of debt.
5
Synthesize the principle
The one-third limitation applies to the value of the slave. If the creditor's debt exceeds one-third, he may not recover the full amount, but the partial ownership affects the debt.

Rulings for Udhiyah by Vow and Bequest
Rulings for udhiyah obligatory by vow or will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for udhiyah made obligatory by vow (nadhr) and by bequest (wasiyyah), and identify the practical differences including the intra-Hanafi difference on the rich-after-sacrifice scenario.

Core Idea
A vowed udhiyah is entirely charity; a bequested udhiyah is limited to one third of the estate.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the unique fiqh rulings when an udhiyah becomes obligatory through a vow or a will. Unlike a voluntary udhiyah, a vowed udhiyah cannot benefit the owner at all. The passage also addresses a practical intra-Hanafi dispute: if a poor person sacrifices a vowed udhiyah and later becomes rich during the festival days, must he sacrifice another? Finally, it outlines the execution of a bequest for udhiyah from the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of obligatory udhiyah
The passage discusses two scenarios that make udhiyah obligatory: (1) by a vow (nadhr), (2) by a bequest (wasiyyah). Understand the distinct rulings for each.
Voluntary udhiyah is not obligatory; these two create obligation.
2
Analyze the ruling for vowed udhiyah
Note that the owner may not eat from the animal nor feed its meat to the rich. The animal is treated as pure charity (sadaqah). This applies whether the person who made the vow is rich or poor.
The prohibition on eating is absolute; the meat must be given to the poor.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
A special case: a poor person buys and sacrifices a sheep for vowed udhiyah, then becomes rich during the days of sacrifice. Some Hanafi scholars say he must sacrifice another; the adopted view (mufta bihi) is that he does not.
The adopted view is from Al-Atabiyyah: 'wa huwa al-mukhtar'.
4
Understand the rules for a bequested udhiyah
If someone bequeaths (wasiyyah) that an udhiyah be offered for them without specifying the animal, it is understood as a sheep. If heirs do not approve, the bequest is limited to one third of the estate.
The bequest is executed from one third; if the specified animal exceeds the third, the amount available is used.
5
Synthesize the practical implications
Apply: a vowed udhiyah must be given entirely in charity; a bequested udhiyah is executed from one third of the estate, with a default of a sheep if unspecified.

وإذا لم تكن واجبة وإنما وجبت بالنذر فليس لصاحبها أن يأكل منها شيئاً ولا أن يطعم غيره من الأغنياء، سواء كان الناذر غنياً أو فقيراً لأن سبيلها التصدق وليس للمتصدق أن يأكل من صدقته ولا أن يطعم الأغنياء وفي الظهيرية اشترى شاة للأضحية وهو فقير فضحى بها ثم أيسر في أيام النحر قال بعضهم عليه غيرها، وقال بعضهم ليس عليه غيرها وبه تأخذ. في العتابية : وهو المختار ولو أوصى بأن يضحي عنه ولم يسم ينصرف إلى الشاة. أوصى بأن يشتري بماله أضحية ولم تجز الورثة فالوصية جائزة في الثلث ويشتري به شاة يضحي بها، ولو أوصى بأن يشتري بقرة بعشرين درهماً ويضحي ولم يبلغ ثلث ماله ذلك فإنه يشتري بقدر ما بلغ، وكذا لو لم يعين قدراً يشتري بقدر الثلث ا هـ.
And if it is not obligatory and only becomes obligatory by a vow (nadhr), then the owner may not eat from it at all nor feed other rich people, whether the one who made the vow is rich or poor, because its path is charity, and the one who gives charity may not eat from his charity nor feed the rich. In al-Zahiriyyah: He bought a sheep for udhiyah while poor and sacrificed it, then became rich during the days of sacrifice. Some said he must offer another, and some said he does not, and this is what is adopted. In al-Atabiyyah: This is the chosen view. If he bequeaths that an udhiyah be offered for him and does not specify, it is understood as a sheep. If he bequeaths that an udhiyah be bought with his wealth and the heirs do not permit, the bequest is valid in one third, and a sheep is bought for sacrifice. If he bequeaths that a cow be bought for twenty dirhams and sacrificed, and the one third of his wealth does not reach that, then he buys as much as it reaches. Similarly, if he did not specify an amount, he buys as much as one third covers.

Commentary
This passage distinguishes between a voluntary udhiyah and one made obligatory by a vow (nadhr). For an udhiyah by nadhr, the owner cannot eat any of it or feed it to the wealthy, as it is entirely charity. A fatwa is cited: if a poor person buys an udhiyah and sacrifices it, then becomes rich during the festival days, some say he must offer another; the adopted view is that he does not. Regarding a bequest (wasiyyah): if someone wills that a sacrifice be made for them without specifying an animal, a sheep is understood. If the bequest is to buy an animal but the heirs do not approve, it is executed from one third of the estate. If the bequest specifies a cow of a certain price but the one third is insufficient, the amount available is used. If no price is specified, one third is used.

Key Terms
النذر (al-nadhr): A vow that makes an otherwise voluntary udhiyah obligatory; the animal becomes entirely charity.
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A bequest for an udhiyah from one's estate; limited to one third unless heirs approve.
