Harim of a Well: Livestock vs. Irrigation – Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on harim distances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the harim distance for livestock and irrigation wells, and state the operative ruling.

Core Idea
The harim of a well varies by use: 40 cubits for livestock, 60 for irrigation – the Sahibayn's view is the mufta bihi.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the harim (inviolable zone) around a well. While the matn states that one who digs in the harim may be prevented, the commentary reveals a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students regarding the distance based on the well's purpose. We will analyze their proofs and see which view is adopted for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the common ground
Both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree that a well has a harim and that digging within it is prohibited. The dispute is only about the distance.
2
Identify the point of disagreement
Does the purpose of the well affect the harim distance? Abu Hanifa says no (same distance for all wells), while the Sahibayn say yes (livestock: 40 cubits, irrigation: 60 cubits).
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa relies on a general principle and a version of the hadith without differentiation. He argues that need is equal and that the mention of livestock in the hadith is for preponderance, not restriction.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn cite an explicit hadith differentiating the distances and argue that irrigation wells require more space for the animal and rope. This is a specific proof that takes precedence over a general principle.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text states 'and with it fatwa is given' (به يفتى), indicating the Sahibayn's view is the operative ruling. Thus, 40 cubits for livestock well, 60 cubits for irrigation well.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the harim (inviolable zone) around a well. The matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq states that one who digs within the harim may be prevented. The commentary then clarifies the distance for the harim of a well. Abu Hanifa holds that there is no difference between a well used for livestock watering (bi'r al-'atn) and one used for irrigation (bi'r al-nadih); both have the same harim distance. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differentiate: livestock well harim is 40 cubits (dhira'), and irrigation well harim is 60 cubits. They support this with a hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) specifically mentioning these measurements. Their reasoning is that the need for space is greater for an irrigation well because the animal (usually a camel) needs room to move and the rope may be long. Abu Hanifa's evidence is the same hadith but without the distinction, and he argues that the general principle (that harim is determined by need) is agreed upon, while the specific differentiation is disputed. He also uses analogy: just as zakat applies to all produce from the earth regardless of irrigation method, the harim should be uniform. The fatwa (operative ruling) is according to the two companions, giving 60 cubits for irrigation wells.

بئر العطن (bi'r al-'atn): A well used for watering livestock, where water is drawn by hand.
بئر الناضح (bi'r al-nadih): A well used for irrigation, where water is drawn using an animal (nadih).

Harim of a Spring (Al-'Ayn): 500 Cubits per Side
Fixing the harim of a spring by text and reason

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the harim of a spring, explain the proof from revelation and reason, and apply the measurement correctly in a given scenario.

Core Idea
The harim of a spring is 500 cubits on each side — a fixed legal measure beyond human opinion.

This lesson examines the fixed legal zone (harim) around a natural spring (al-'ayn). The Hanafi school holds that it must be 500 cubits on each side, based on a prophetic tradition and the practical need for water collection and flow. There is an intra-school dispute about whether the 500 cubits applies total or per side, but the correct view is per side. We will walk through the proof and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The 'act' here is the establishment of the harim (protected zone) around a spring. The ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) to keep this zone free from encroachment. Violation is prohibited.
The hukm level is derived from the text's authority; the hadith is ahad but the meaning is explicit.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the hadith reported by the author: 'العين خمسمائة ذراع' (the spring has five hundred cubits). Additionally, a rational argument (qiyas talili) is given: a spring needs a basin for water to settle and a channel to flow, so a fixed space is necessary.
The hadith is not quoted verbatim in this passage but referenced as 'روينا' (we narrated).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit number 'خمسمائة' (500) indicates the fixed limit. In Hanafi usul, such a clear textual number is qat'i in meaning (dalala qat'iyya) even if the transmission is zanni.
The rule: 'لا مدخل للرأي في المقادير' – no room for human opinion in such fixed measures.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi scholars (قيل) said the 500 cubits is total, thus 150 cubits per side. But the author declares the correct view (الأصح) to be 500 cubits from each side.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the disagreement is over the distribution, not the total.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The author gives tarjih (preference) explicitly: 'الأصح' (the most sound view) is 500 per side. This is the mufta bihi position.
The basis is the apparent meaning of the hadith and the principle that fixed measures are taken literally.

Digging in the Harim: Liability and Compensation
Rulings on preventing digging in harim and liability for damages caused.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on digging in another's harim, explain the measure of compensation, and apply the qiyas with the demolition of a wall.

Core Idea
Digging in another's harim is transgression; compensation is for decrease in value, not profit.

This lesson from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the legal ruling when someone digs or builds within the harim of another's well. The harim is considered part of the property by necessity. The passage also discusses the measure of compensation and the role of the Imam's permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is digging or building in the harim of another's well. The ruling is that it is prohibited (haram) because it is transgression (ta'addi) into another's property.
The harim is considered property by necessity for the well owner's benefit.
2
Determine the basis (dalil)
The ruling is derived by qiyas (analogy) with demolishing another's wall. In both cases, the act is transgression causing damage to property.
The effective cause (illa) is transgression causing loss.
3
Analyse the intra-Hanafi dispute on compensation
There are two views: some say the transgressor must give his profit (like placing something in another's property), while the correct view is compensation for the decrease in value of the first well.
The correct view is supported by analogy: just as the demolisher pays value, not rebuilding.
4
Evaluate the chosen position
The text states 'this is correct' (وهو الصحيح) for compensation of decrease. The first owner also removes the excavation himself, like the wall owner does not rebuild.
This is the mufta bihi position.
5
Apply to a scenario
If a person digs a second well in the harim of the first, causing the first well's water to decrease or its value to drop, the second is liable for that decrease.
If the water simply moves without damage, no liability (water is not owned).

Harim of a Qanat: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qanat harim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad regarding the harim of a qanat and apply the ruling based on whether water appears on the surface.

Core Idea
The harim of a qanat depends on surface water appearance, with internal Hanafi disagreement on analogy.

The harim (protected zone) of a qanat (underground channel) is not explicitly prescribed in the primary texts. The matn states it is determined by need, but the commentary reveals a disagreement between the two primary students of Abu Hanifa: Imam Muhammad likens it to a well, while Abu Hanifa distinguishes based on whether water appears on the surface. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal object
A qanat is an underground channel for conveying water. The legal question is: what is its harim (zone that cannot be obstructed)?
Harim is a protective buffer to prevent damage.
2
State Muhammad's position
In the Zahir al-Riwayah, Imam Muhammad holds that a qanat is like a well: it has a fixed harim (typically 40 cubits for a well).
This position may also be attributed to both Sahibayn.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa says: a qanat only has a harim if its water appears on the surface; otherwise it is like a river (which for him has no harim). If water appears and flows like a spring, then the harim is 500 cubits.
This is based on the qanat being 'essentially a river' — i.e., a flowing watercourse.
4
Apply to a scenario
If water gushes from the qanat opening, treat it as a spring with 500 cubit harim. If no water appears, some (Abu Hanifa) say no harim; others (Muhammad) still give it a well-like harim.
The mufta bihi is not specified; the passage leaves it case-by-case.

Euphrates Overflow: Dead Land or Not
Determining when land adjacent to the Euphrates is considered dead (mawat) and can be revived.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions that determine when land separated from a river is considered mawat and when it is not, and apply the ruling to a new case.

Core Idea
Land that cannot receive river water again is dead; land that can is public property.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies a practical issue: when a river changes course or a branch dries up, does the exposed land become mawat (dead land) that can be claimed by reviving it? The answer depends on whether the water can ever return. Understanding this distinction is crucial for land ownership rights near public waterways.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Find in the text the two possibilities regarding the river's return: one where it cannot return, and one where it can.
The text uses 'does not bear returning' and 'bears returning'.
2
Determine the ruling for each scenario
Classify the land as mawat or not mawat in each case.
The ruling is explicit: when no return, it is mawat; when return possible, it is not.
3

Learning Objective

Guided Path

Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue is whether a river (nahr) dug in dead land has a harim – a reserved strip of land for maintenance. Abu Hanifa says no; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say yes, both sides.

The principle: nass (text) is exception; qiyas is not allowed where there is a text to the contrary.

The Sahibayn analogize rivers to wells and springs because the owner needs a pathway and a place to deposit mud. This need justifies a harim. They also note that without a harim, the owner cannot effectively manage the river.
They consider the 'illa (effective cause) to be need, which is common.

If there is no harim, the river owner cannot prevent others from building up to the edge. If there is a harim, the owner has a legal right to that strip, and encroachment is forbidden.
This affects litigation over land adjacent to rivers.

The Legal Definition of Shurb (Water Right)
Defines shurb with its legal components and proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the linguistic and legal definition of shurb, identify its pillar and condition, and state the dalil for its legality.

Core Idea
Shurb is the right to a share of water for land, with water as its pillar and irrigation as its ruling.

This lesson unpacks the first part of the passage, where the author from al-Muhit breaks down the concept of shurb into five components: legality, definition, pillar, condition, and ruling. You will learn the hadith proof for water rights and distinguish between the linguistic and legal meanings. This foundational understanding is essential for the subsequent rules on using large rivers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five components of shurb
List the five elements: mashru'iyyah (legality), tafsir (definition), rukn (pillar), shart (condition), hukm (ruling). The text explicitly enumerates them.
These are standard fiqh analytical categories for defining a right or obligation.
2
Locate the dalil for legality
Find the hadith: 'When the valley reaches the ankles, the upper people may not withhold it from the lower people.' Understand that this establishes a right to use excess water.
The hadith is about preventing harm, not ownership.
3
Understand the definition of shurb
Linguistically, shurb means a share of water (from Quran 54:28 and 26:155). Legally, it is the share of water for lands, not for other purposes.
The legal definition restricts the right to irrigation of land.
4
Distinguish rukn, shart, and hukm
Rukn (pillar) is water itself—shurb is established by water. Shart (condition) is that one has a portion of shurb (i.e., land needing irrigation). Hukm (ruling) is irrigation—the purpose is to water the land.
In Hanafi fiqh, rukn is an essential element without which the thing does not exist; shart is a prerequisite for it to be valid.
5
Apply the components to a scenario
Given a piece of land, the shurb right exists if there is water (rukn), the land has a share (shart), and the effect is irrigation (hukm). The legality is proven by the hadith.

Large rivers are common property; use is allowed only if it does not harm the public.

This lesson dives into the second part of the passage, which addresses the legal status of large rivers like the Tigris and Euphrates. Based on the hadith 'People are partners in three: water, fire, and grass,' the Hanafi school rules that these rivers are not owned by anyone because they cannot be possessed. The distinction is between common (mubah) and private (milk).

Hanafi Definition of Khamr and Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Definition of khamr and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Imam Abu Hanifa's definition of khamr and explain the difference with the Sahibayn's definition.

Core Idea
Khamr is defined as raw grape juice that ferments and throws foam; Sahibayn hold foaming is not required.

The Book of Beverages begins by defining the key term 'sharab' as intoxicating liquids. Among the four prohibited drinks, khamr (wine) is the first. Its precise definition is a matter of intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf. Imam Abu Hanifa sets a strict criterion, while his two companions adopt a broader view. Understanding this difference is essential for applying rulings on impurity, punishment, and transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Khamr is one of the four prohibited drinks. Its ruling is that it is haram, and both small and large quantities are unlawful. The text immediately states: 'حرم قليلها وكثيرها'.
This indicates that even a drop is forbidden.
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa's definition
Imam defines khamr as the raw juice of grapes (ni' min ma' al-'inab) when it ferments (ghala), becomes strong (ishtadda), and throws foam (qadhafa bi al-zabad). All three conditions must be met.
The phrase 'وقذف بالزبد' is the key condition.
3
Note the Sahibayn's alternative
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that once the grape juice becomes strong (ishtadda), it is khamr, regardless of whether it throws foam. Their reasoning is that the enjoyment (ladhdha) is achieved at that point, and the negative consequences (enmity, distraction from prayer) are present.
They consider foaming as the completion (kamaal) not a condition (shart).
4
Recognize the mufta bihi position
The author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq presents Imam's definition as the primary one, and it is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school. The ten consequences that follow are based on this definition.
This affects whether a beverage is considered khamr for hadd punishment and najasah.

Does 'Khamr' Include All Intoxicants? The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Inter-madhhab dispute over the definition of khamr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to present the Hanafi position that khamr is only grape juice and evaluate the Shafi'i argument using the hadith 'kullu muskar khamr'.

Core Idea
Shafi'i says all intoxicants are khamr; Hanafi says only grape juice, and the hadith is metaphorical or legal, not linguistic.

A central controversy in fiqh al-ashriba is whether the term 'khamr' includes all intoxicating beverages or is restricted to wine from grapes. The Shafi'i school, relying on the hadith 'Every intoxicant is khamr', treats all muskirat as khamr. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the linguistic meaning and the legal ruling, restricting khamr to grape juice while still prohibiting all intoxicants through other evidence. This lesson unpacks the arguments and the practical consequences of each view.

Guided Path
1
State the Shafi'i claim
The Shafi'i school and some Hanbalis hold that every intoxicating drink is called khamr in the legal sense. Their primary evidence is the hadith 'kullu muskar khamr' (every intoxicant is khamr). They also argue that the word 'khamr' is derived from 'mukhamara' (covering), and all intoxicants cover the intellect.
This claim is based on non-linguistic reasoning.
2
Identify the Hanafi linguistic argument
The Hanafi school relies on the consensus of Arabic linguists (ijma' ahl al-lugha) that the real (haqiqi) meaning of khamr is the fermented juice of grapes. Applying the term to other intoxicants is metaphorical (majaz).
Linguistic consensus is a strong dalil in Hanafi usul.
3
Interpret the hadith 'kullu muskar khamr'
The Hanafi response is that the hadith is not a definition but a declaration of legal ruling. The Prophet (peace be upon him) was sent to clarify rulings, not to redefine the Arabic language. Thus, the hadith means that all intoxicants have the same legal status as khamr (i.e., haram), not that they are linguistically khamr.
This is a classic usul al-fiqh distinction between haqiqah shar'iyyah and haqiqah lughawiyyah.
4
Examine additional Hanafi evidence
The Hanafi also cite the hadith 'al-khamru min hadhatayn al-nakhlati wa al-'anabati' (khamr is from these two: the date palm and the grape vine). This shows the origin of khamr from specific sources, not all intoxicants.
This hadith is used to support the restriction to dates and grapes.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah)
The practical difference is that according to Hanafi, khamr has unique rulings such as being najis al-ayn (physically impure) and incurring hadd even if not intoxicated. Other intoxicants are also impure and carry hadd, but the definitional difference can affect issues like selling, compensation for destruction, and the status of cooked beverages.
Both schools agree all intoxicants are haram; the ikhtilaf is mainly terminological.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the opening of the Book of Beverages from Kanz al-Daqaiq. It begins by explaining why the chapter follows the previous one, then defines the term 'sharab' in the context of jurists: it refers specifically to intoxicating beverages. The author then discusses the first of the four prohibited drinks: khamr. The Hanafi position, according to Imam Abu Hanifa, defines khamr strictly as raw grape juice (ni' min ma' al-'inab) that ferments (ghala) and becomes strong (ishtadda) and throws foam (qadhafa bi al-zabad). The text then presents the inter-madhhab dispute: some scholars (Imam al-Shafi'i and others) argue that all intoxicants are khamr based on the hadith "Every intoxicant is khamr." The Hanafi response is that the linguistic definition restricts khamr to grape juice, and the hadith is interpreted as a declaration of legal ruling (hukm) not a linguistic definition. The definition of Imam Abu Hanifa is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi). The passage then enumerates ten legal consequences of khamr: (1) its essence is haram, not contingent on intoxication unlike other beverages; (2) it is physically impure (najis al-'ayn) like urine and stool; (3) declaring it permissible is disbelief; (4) no compensation for destroying it; (5) sale is prohibited; (6) drinking it incurs the fixed punishment (hadd) even if not intoxicated; (7) cooking does not remove its prohibition; (8) it may be turned into vinegar; (9) other rulings like prohibition of using it in dough or eye-liner. Defining Tilā' and Its Legal Rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tilā', state its impurity ruling in the apparent riwāyah, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding its sale.

Core Idea
Tilā' becomes haram only when it ferments and foams; its impurity is light, and its sale is disputed among Hanafis.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rā'iq defines tilā'—grape juice cooked until two-thirds evaporates—and clarifies its legal status. You will learn the precise definition based on cooking reduction, the nature of its impurity, and a key disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two companions on the permissibility of selling it. The passage also explains when tilā' becomes haram: the foam criterion, and which opinion is relied upon for fatwa.

1
Identify the substance and definition
Find the exact definition: tilā' is grape juice cooked until at least two-thirds evaporates, leaving one-third. Note the name's origin from Umar's saying about camel tar.

2
Determine the impurity classification
The text states two opinions: severe (mughallazah) or light (mukhaffafah). Identify which is the apparent riwāyah (zahir al-riwāyah).
The apparent riwāyah is that impurity is light.
3
Analyze the sale ruling dispute
Abu Hanifa allows sale of bādhaq, munsif, muskir, and naqī' al-zabīb. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad prohibit it. Determine the mufta bihi (relied upon) position.
Fatwā is on the view of the two companions (prohibition).
4
Understand when tilā' becomes haram
If the juice boils and becomes strong with foam (ghalā wa ishtadda bi al-zabad), it is haram. If it strengthens without foam, there is ikhtilaf between Imam and Sahibayn.
The foam indicates fermentation and intoxication.
5
Recognize the mufta bihi
Consolidate: the relied-upon ruling is that impurity is light, sale is prohibited (qawluhuma), and haram only upon fermentation with foam.

Sukr, Fadīkh, and Naqī' al-Zabīb: Types and Rulings
Defines three prohibited drinks and debates their gravity relative to khamr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three non-khamr intoxicants, state their Hanafi rulings on impurity, hadd, and takfīr, and explain why the consensus on their prohibition is not qat'ī.

Core Idea
Non-grape intoxicants are haram but lighter than khamr: no hadd, no takfīr, light impurity.

In this passage from al-Bahr al-Rā'iq, the author defines three specific intoxicating drinks that are prohibited but distinguished from khamr (wine). The Hanafi school treats them with a lighter ruling: they are not najāsah mughallazah, their consumer is not excommunicated, and the fixed penalty (hadd) does not apply. The passage also addresses a key objection: if there is consensus on their prohibition, why no takfīr? The Hanafi response hinges on the epistemological weight of the consensus transmission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three drinks and their definitions
List the drinks: sukr (raw date juice), fadīkh (raw unripe date juice), naqī' al-zabīb (raisin infusion). Note the condition for prohibition: they must ferment, boil, and throw foam.
Sukr is from fresh dates (rutab), fadīkh from unripe dates (busr), naqī' al-zabīb from raisins soaked in water.
2
Determine the trigger for prohibition
The drinks become haram only when they 'become strong and throw foam' (qadhāfat bil-zabad). Before that, they are halal.
Shuraik ibn Abdullah held that even after foaming they remain halal, citing Surah al-Nahl 16:67.
3
Distinguish their rulings from khamr
The Hanafi school states: no hadd for drinking, consumer is not a kāfir, impurity is light (najāsah khafīfah), and destroying them requires compensation (according to Abu Hanifa).
These distinctions are unique to Hanafis; Shafi'is treat all intoxicants as khamr.
4
Understand the objection about consensus and Hanafi response
An objector claims that consensus of the Companions on prohibition of sukr should make its consumer a kāfir. The Hanafi response: the consensus was transmitted through āhād (isolated) reports, which are not qat'ī, so takfīr does not follow.
This illustrates the usul principle that only qat'ī evidence can lead to takfīr.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on sale
Abu Yusuf permits sale of these drinks if more than half is cooked away (e.g., made into vinegar). Abu Hanifa likely does not permit sale as long as they remain intoxicating.
The mufta bihi is the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) as per earlier passages.

The Fifteen Conditions for Permissible Hunting

Core Idea
Hunting is halal only if fifteen conditions are met: five for the hunter, five for the dog, and five for the prey.

The Ruling on the Guarantee of the Pawn (Rahn) in Hanafi Fiqh
Core ruling with proofs and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a pawn is guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt, and explain the Hanafi evidence against the Shafi'i position that the pawn is a complete trust.

Core Idea
The pawn is guaranteed to the extent of the debt; surplus is a trust.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on what happens when a pawned item perishes in the hands of the pledgee. Unlike the Shafi'i school, which views the pawn as a complete trust, the Hanafi school holds that the pawn is a hybrid: guaranteed to cover the debt up to its value, with any surplus held as a trust. We will explore the evidence from hadith and consensus, and the practical consequences of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core Hanafi rule
The pawn is guaranteed (madhmun) by the lesser of its value and the debt. This means the pledgee's right to repayment attaches to the pawn itself.
2
Examine the three scenarios
If the pawn perishes with the pledgee: (1) value equals debt → debt satisfied; (2) value exceeds debt → debt satisfied, surplus is trust; (3) value less than debt → debt partially satisfied, pledgee claims remainder.
The surplus is amanah, not guaranteed.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds the entire pawn is a trust (amanah), so destruction does not reduce the debt at all.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proofs
Two hadith: 'His right is lost' and 'If the pawn perishes, the debt perishes.' Plus consensus (ijma) of Companions and Successors.
The hadith are zanni in transmission but support the ruling.
5
Apply the ruling to a practical example
A pawn worth 100 for a debt of 80 is destroyed. Per Hanafi: debt satisfied (80), surplus 20 is trust. Pledgee must return the 20.
This shows the hybrid nature: guaranteed for debt, trust for excess.

The pledgee is (the pawn) guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt. If it perishes and its value equals the debt, he becomes a satisfier of his debt. If it is more than his debt, the surplus is a trust, and to the extent of the debt he becomes a satisfier of his debt. If it is less, he becomes a satisfier to the extent of his debt, and the pledgee has recourse for the surplus. The author, may Allah have mercy on him, said: (It is guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt; if it perishes and its value equals the debt, he becomes a satisfier of his debt; if it is more than his debt, the surplus is a trust, and to the extent of the debt he becomes a satisfier of his debt; if it is less, he becomes a satisfier to the extent of his debt and the pledgee has recourse for the surplus.) Imam al-Shafi'i, may Allah be pleased with him, said: The entire pawn is a trust, so nothing of the debt falls due by its destruction. Our proof is his saying, peace be upon him, to the pledgee whose horse perished: 'His right is lost,' and his saying, peace be upon him: 'If the pawn perishes, the debt perishes.' And its meaning is such, and the Companions and Successors agreed upon that. The clarification of the two proofs from both sides is in the lengthy works. In al-Kafi its explanation: If he pawns a garment worth ten for ten, and it perishes with the pledgee, his debt falls. If the value of the garment is five, the pledgee claims another five from the pawner. If its value is fifteen, the surplus is a trust according to us. In al-Yanabi': The pawn is guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt. The benefit of this appears in issues such as: if he pawns a slave for one thousand dirhams and his value is two thousand, and he runs away and a man returns him from a distance of three days' journey, then the reward is half on the pawner and half on the pledgee, because the slave is half guaranteed by the debt and half a trust, so the reward is between them proportionally. And including medical treatment of illnesses and wounds, because that is divided between the guaranteed portion and the trust portion proportionally; what befalls the guaranteed portion is upon the pledgee, and what befalls the trust portion is upon the pawner. And if he said: 'It is guaranteed by the lesser of the value of the guaranteed and the debt' it would be more appropriate to include the case where the value of the pawn is more than the debt in origin. The invalid pawn is that which is not contracted at all, like the invalid sale, and the defective is that which is contracted but with the attribute of defect, and what is exchanged for it is guaranteed wealth. In every place where the pawn is not wealth and what is exchanged for it is not guaranteed, the pawn is not contracted at all, and that is the invalid. The value of the pawn is considered on the day of possession. The author did not mention the rulings of water overwhelming the pawned property. He said in al-Muhit: Land that is pawned and overwhelmed by water is like a slave who absconds, because it is such that the water descends from it, so the land is benefited from, so the debt is not nullified due to the possibility of return, like the absconder. And if a slave becomes halal-blooded or stolen with the pawner and then cut with the pledgee, that is from the guarantee of the pawner, and nothing of the debt goes, and he remains pawned for the entire debt according to Imam Abu Hanifa. According to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), theft is a defect and he is valued as a thief and halal-blooded and as non-thief and non-halal-blooded, so there falls from the debt an amount equivalent to his value as halal-blooded and cut, and he remains pawned for the share of his value likewise. And if there becomes obligatory upon him the hadd punishment for slander or fornication with the pledgee, or a defect enters him, there falls from the debt to that extent. He pawned a garment worth five dirhams and, for example, gold equal to ten with five dirhams; the gold perishes but not the garment until the rag: he is liable for the value of the garment; he calculates that as one and two-thirds dirhams, because by the gold perishing two-thirds of the debt go, that is three and one-third dirhams of the value. He said: because corresponding to the gold is one-third of the debt, and corresponding to the garment is one-third of it. So when the gold perishes and the garment is consumed, by the garment perishing one-third of the debt goes, and he guarantees the mithqal of gold, so it remains pawned with him for three and one-third dirhams. The author, may Allah have mercy on him, mentioned the ruling of the destruction of the pawned item in the hand of the pledgee, and he did not mention the ruling of its deficiency. In al-Khulasa: if the pawned item decreases in the hand of the pledgee, if the decrease is in its substance, there falls from the debt to that extent. Qadikhan said: the debt is to that extent. A man owes a debt to another and has a guarantor. The claimant took a pawn from the guarantor and a pawn from the principal, each one being the other, and each is sufficient for the debt. Then one of the two pawns perishes with the pledgee. Zufar, may Allah have mercy on him, said: whichever perishes (falls) with the entire debt. Imam Abu Yusuf, may Allah have mercy on him, said: if the second pawn perishes; if the pawner knew of the first pawn, then the second perishes with half the debt; if he did not know, it perishes with the entire debt. It is mentioned in the Book of Pawn that the second perishes with half the debt, and knowledge and ignorance are not mentioned, and that is correct because each one is held for the entire debt, so the second pawn is made an addition to the first pawn. If their values are equal, the debt is divided between them. So if the second perishes, it perishes with half the debt. And they said: if he stipulated that if it is lost it is gratuitous, the stipulation is void and it perishes with the debt.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the Hanafi ruling that a pawned item (marhun) is guaranteed (madhmun) by the lesser of its value and the debt. The passage begins by stating the core ruling: if the pawn perishes in the hands of the pledgee (murtahin), the outcome depends on the relationship between its value and the debt. If the value equals the debt, the debt is considered satisfied. If the value exceeds the debt, the excess is a trust (amanah) in the hands of the pledgee, and the debt is satisfied to the extent of the debt. If the value is less than the debt, the debt is satisfied only to the extent of the pawn's value, and the pledgee retains the right to claim the remainder from the pawner. The author then quotes the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, which repeats this ruling. He presents the inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, who holds that the entire pawn is a trust (amanah), so its destruction does not extinguish any part of the debt. The Hanafi response cites two hadith: (1) the Prophet's statement to the pledgee whose horse perished: 'His right is lost' (indicating the debt is lost unless the pawn's value covers it), and (2) 'If the pawn perishes, the debt perishes.' Additionally, consensus (ijma) of the Companions and Successors is claimed. Practical examples from al-Kafi and al-Yanabi' illustrate the rule, followed by subsidiary issues: the reward for returning an absconded pawn, medical expenses for the pawn, the distinction between invalid (batil) and defective (fasid) pawn contracts, the valuation date, water damage to pawned land, changes to the pawn's condition (theft, hadd punishments), partial destruction, and cases involving two pawns for the same debt from different parties (with differences between Zufar and Abu Yusuf). Throughout, the Hanafi position is consistently that the pawn is guaranteed by the lesser of value and debt, with the surplus held as a trust.

Key Terms
مضمون (madhmun): Guaranteed; the pawn is subject to guarantee so that if it perishes, the debt is satisfied to its value.
أمانة (amanah): Trust; the portion of the pawn's value exceeding the debt is held by the pledgee as a trust, not guaranteed.
مستوفٍ لدينه (mustawfin li-daynihi): One who has fully recovered his debt; when the pawn's value equals the debt, the pledgee is considered to have received his debt.
