Invalidity of Pledging Carcass Skin and Mortgagee's Claim for Tanning
Ruling on carcass skin pledge and mortgagee's compensation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on pledging a carcass skin and explain why the mortgagee can claim the value added by tanning.

Core Idea
A pledge is invalid if the subject is not property at formation, but the mortgagee may claim compensation for improvements made.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a dhimmi pledges a carcass skin, which is not property (مال) at the time of contract, rendering the pledge void. However, if the mortgagee subsequently tans the skin, adding value, he is entitled to compensation for the increase. The ruling hinges on the fundamental condition that a pledge requires a valid subject matter, and it is illustrated by analogy to dyeing another's garment and to wine turning into vinegar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its condition
The act is a pledge (رهن) of a carcass skin (جلد ميتة). Identify that the subject must be property (مال) at the moment of contract.
Carcass skin is not مال in Hanafi fiqh before tanning.
2
Apply the condition to obtain the ruling
Since the skin is not property, the contract was not concluded (لم ينعقد) due to absence of subject matter. Therefore the pledge is invalid (batil).
Pledge invalid for فوات المحل.
3
Consider the mortgagee's tanning action
If the mortgagee tans the skin with something of value, the increase is a separate right. The mortgagee can claim the value added (قيمة ما زاد الدباغ) from the pledger.
This is not based on the pledge contract but on the principle of compensation for improvement.
4
Examine the analogy to dyeing
The text likens this to a person dyeing another's garment: the dyer is entitled to compensation for the increase. Similarly, the mortgagee here is entitled to أجرة الدباغة.
Qiyas: both involve adding value to another's property with effort.

Revival of Pledge After Death of Pledged Sheep and Debt Reduction
Ruling on revival of pledge after death and debt reduction calculation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the revised debt when a pledged sheep dies and its tanned skin revives part of the pledge, and distinguish this from a sale scenario.

Core Idea
If a pledged sheep dies, the pledge is frustrated but revives to the extent of the tanned skin's value, proportionally reducing the debt.

This passage explores a nuance: a live sheep is validly pledged, but it dies. The skin is tanned and has value. Does the pledge disappear entirely? The Hanafi ruling is that the pledge is frustrated (يتعذر) by destruction, but revives partially when the skin is tanned. The extent of revival equals the skin's value at the time of pledge. Consequently, the debt is reduced proportionally. The ruling is contrasted with a sale scenario where the animal dies before delivery: there, the contract is annulled completely.

Guided Path
1
Establish the base case
A sheep worth 10 dirhams is pledged for a debt of 10 dirhams. The sheep dies, then its skin is tanned. Determine the skin's value at the time of pledge.
Value is assessed at the time of pledge, not at death or tanning.
2
Apply the rule of frustration and revival
The death frustrates the pledge (يتعذر بالهلاك). However, when the skin is tanned, some of the subject matter is 'revived'. The ruling returns to the extent of that revival.
Revival is partial: only the skin's value is considered.
3
Calculate debt reduction
The skin's value (e.g., 1 dirham) determines the portion of the pledge that remains. Consequently, the debt is reduced by the value of the destroyed portion (the meat and other parts).
The text gives examples: if sheep value is 10 and skin is worth 1, debt reduces to 9? Actually, it says the skin is a pledge for 1 dirham, meaning the debt is reduced by the value of the lost portion. Careful: the debt itself is not reduced automatically; the pledge is now only for 1 dirham of the debt. The debtor still owes the full debt but only 1 dirham is secured. However, the text later says 'سقط من الدين أربعة' in another example. So the debt is reduced proportionally by the value of the part that is permanently lost.
4
Contrast with sale
In a sale, if the animal dies before delivery, the contract is annulled entirely, and the skin does not revive the sale. This highlights the difference: a pledge can be frustrated and revived, but a sale is absolutely annulled.
Some mashayikh hold that sale also revives partially, but the relied-upon view is that it does not.

Effect of Mortgagee's Improvement: Retention Rights and Ikhtilaf
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on effect of mortgagee's improvement on original pledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the two Hanafi opinions on whether the mortgagee's improvement (tanning with value) voids the original pledge and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
When the mortgagee improves the pledged property, does that improvement establish a new pledge that supersedes the original? Two opinions exist; the relied-upon view is that the original pledge remains stronger.

This passage addresses a subtle intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: if the mortgagee tans the pledged skin using materials of value, he gains a right to retain the skin for the cost of tanning (زيادة). The question is whether this new right of retention replaces the original pledge right, effectively voiding the original pledge. The text presents two opinions: one that the original pledge is voided, and another that it remains because the improvement is subordinate to the original subject. The latter is preferred, with support from al-Mabsut, which also details the proportional calculations when the sheep's value differs from the debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario triggering the ikhtilaf
The mortgagee tans the pledged skin using materials that have value (شيء له قيمة). This gives him a separate right to retain the skin for the value of the increase.
Previously, tanning with valueless materials (e.g., salt) did not create this conflict.
2
Understand the first opinion
Some scholars say the original pledge is voided (يبطل الرهن). The reason: the skin is now held for the new right (increase), so it exits being a pledge for the original debt. Analogous to repledging the same item for a different debt.
This view emphasizes that a thing cannot be a pledge for two debts simultaneously.
3
Understand the second opinion (preferred)
Other scholars say the original pledge does not void. The increase (value of tanning) is subordinate to the skin, like a description follows the substance. The original pledge is stronger because it is for the original debt, which is an independent right. The second right is dependent on the skin.
The principle: 'a thing is voided only by something equivalent or superior, not inferior.'
4
Examine the tarjih and practical application
The text cites al-Mabsut as supporting the latter view. The mufta bihi is that the original pledge remains, and the mortgagee may retain the skin for both the original debt and the improvement cost, but the pledge is still for the original debt. When the pledger pays the debt, he also pays the improvement value.
The improvement is treated as a lien on the skin, but it does not replace the pledge.
5
Calculate in complex cases (if time permits)
Al-Mabsut provides calculation methods when the sheep's value exceeds or falls short of the debt, determining how much of the skin is a pledge and how much is a trust. This is an extension of the basic ruling.
These calculations reinforce that the original pledge is not voided but continues proportionally.

Division of Debt Between Original Pawn and Its Increase
Ruling with proof: dividing debt between pawn and its increase.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dividing a debt between a pawn and its increase and apply it to a scenario where the increase perishes before redemption.

Core Idea
The increase of a pawn only secures its share of the debt at redemption, not before.

When a pawned item produces an increase, such as a cow giving birth to a calf, how is the debt divided? This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling that the original pawn secures the debt from the time of the pledge, while the increase only becomes part of the security at redemption. Understanding this temporal distinction is essential for determining liability if the increase is lost before redemption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the division of debt between the original pawn and its increase. The ruling is that the debt is divided proportionally based on the value of each at their respective relevant times: the original at the time of taking possession, and the increase at redemption.
2
Locate the dalil
The text provides a reasoning: 'because the offspring obtains a share at the day of redemption, and the mother entered into liability at the time of taking possession.' This ta'lil is the proof.
The proof is not from Quran or Sunnah but from the nature of rahn.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The difference in timing means the increase is not part of the security until redemption. Hence, if the increase perishes before redemption, it does not cancel any part of the debt.
4
Note the practical consequence
If the increase dies before redemption, the debtor still owes the full debt; no reduction occurs.
This preserves the creditor's security until the debtor redeems.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the Hanafi ruling on how to divide the debt when a pawned item produces an offspring or increase. The commentator opens by quoting the matn: the debt is divided based on the value of the original at the time of taking possession, and the offspring's share is redeemed by its value at redemption. The reasoning is that the offspring (al-walad) only becomes a part of the security at the moment of redemption, while the mother (al-umm) entered the surety at the time of the pledge contract. Therefore, the value of each is considered at different times. If the offspring dies after the mother but before redemption, it dies without any corresponding portion of the debt, confirming that no part of the debt attaches to it before redemption. The passage then discusses the scenario where the pledgor allows the mortgagee to consume the increase (e.g., fruits, milk, wages). Such permission is a pure license (ibaha), not a transfer of ownership, so the mortgagee is not liable, and no part of the debt is considered paid. If the pawn subsequently perishes, the debt is divided between the original and the consumed increase based on their respective values at the time of consumption. What corresponds to the original is cancelled, and what corresponds to the increase is a debt owed by the mortgagee to the pledgor, since the increase was destroyed by the mortgagee with the pledgor's permission, making it as if the pledgor destroyed it himself.

Permission to Consume the Increase of the Pawn
Ruling with proof: permission to consume increase and subsequent division of debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a mortgagee consuming the increase of a pawn with the pledgor's permission and determine the effect on the debt when the pawn subsequently perishes.

Core Idea
Permission to consume increase is a license (ibaha), not ownership, and its effect on debt is only realized if the pawn perishes.

What happens if the pledgor explicitly allows the mortgagee to consume the fruits or offspring of the pawn? This lesson covers the Hanafi ruling that such permission is a pure license (ibaha), not a transfer of ownership. The mortgagee is not liable for consuming the increase, but if the pawn later perishes, the value of the consumed increase is deducted from the debt in a specific way. We will explore the reasoning behind this division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal classification
The act is the pledgor giving permission to the mortgagee to consume the increase of the pawn. This is classified as ibaha (permission, license) not tamlik (ownership transfer).
The distinction matters because ibaha allows consumption without liability, while tamlik would transfer ownership.
2
State the ruling on liability
If the mortgagee consumes the increase with permission, there is no liability (daman) on him, and no part of the debt is cancelled.
This is because the consumption is authorized by the owner.
3
Analyze the scenario when the pawn perishes later
If the pawn (the original) perishes in the hands of the mortgagee before redemption, the debt is divided between the value of the consumed increase and the value of the original at the time of consumption. The share of the original is cancelled, and the mortgagee must pay the share of the increase to the pledgor.
The increase is considered to have been destroyed by the mortgagee with the pledgor's authorization, so it is as if the pledgor destroyed it himself.
4
Understand the ta'lil (reasoning)
The increase perished while owned by the pledgor due to the mortgagee's action with the pledgor's authorization. Therefore, the mortgagee is not liable for the consumption itself, but when the pawn perishes, the division ensures that the increase's value is not lost to the mortgagee.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Debt division fixes original pawn's value at possession; increase's value assessed at redemption for offspring, at possession for other additions.

In Hanafi pawn (rahn) law, when a pawn generates an increase (e.g., a cow gives birth to a calf), the debt is divided proportionally between them. But what happens if their values change due to market shifts or defects? The Hanafi school provides detailed rulings based on the timing of value assessment. This lesson explores those rulings as recorded in Fatawa Qadikhan, al-Muhit, and al-Muntaqa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components
Determine the original pawn (al-asl) and its increase (al-ziyada). Note their respective values at key times: the original at possession (qabd), the increase at redemption (fakka) for offspring, or at possession for other types like new growth.
The passage distinguishes between offspring and other increases due to different attachment rulings.
2
Fix the original's value
The original pawn's value for debt division is taken at the time of possession. Subsequent price changes or defects do not alter its assessed value for this purpose.
This is the key Hanafi principle found in the first scenario: even if the mother's price drops, division remains half.
3
Assess the increase's value
For offspring (walad), value is assessed at redemption. For other increases (e.g., new palm trees), value is assessed at possession. Defects or price changes in the increase adjust its assessed value accordingly.
The passage shows that if the offspring's value drops, the division shifts to thirds.
4
Calculate proportional division and handle special cases
Divide the total debt in proportion to the fixed values. Special rules apply when a component is destroyed or becomes defective: the share of the affected item is partially or fully cancelled.
The example of the one-eyed mother shows that half of her share is cancelled, resulting in cancellation of a quarter of total debt.

Distinguishing Offspring from Additional Pledges in Rahn
Expansion on the Hanafi rule for debt division with additional pledges and the unique treatment of offspring.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for dividing debt between an original pawn and an additional pledge, and distinguish the valuation timing for offspring versus other increases.

Core Idea
The offspring of a pawned item is valued at redemption, not at deposit, because it is a dependent branch of the original.

In Hanafi muamalat, when a pawn (rahn) is already held and the pledgor adds more collateral or the pawn gives birth, the debt must be apportioned between the original and the increase. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the critical distinction: additional items voluntarily pledged are valued at the time they are taken into possession, whereas offspring born to a pawned female slave are valued at the time of redemption. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly calculating how much of the debt each part of the pawn secures.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of increase
Determine whether the increase is an extra item voluntarily pledged (ziyada fi al-rahn) or offspring (walad) automatically attaching to the pawn. This classification decides valuation timing.
Offspring is a natural increase; extra pledges are contractual.
2
Determine valuation timing
For extra pledges, use value at the time of qabd (taking possession). For offspring, use value at the time of fikak (redemption).
The rationale: offspring is a 'branch' attached to the mother, so its valuation follows the mother's release.
3
Apply the division formula
Divide the remaining debt proportionally between the original pawn and the increase based on their respective values at the relevant valuation moments. Account for any defects or partial repayments.
For an original that decreased in value, use its current value at division time; for an added pledge, use its value at deposit.
4
Work through the examples
Follow the one-eyed slave + added slave scenario (division into thirds), then the female slave giving birth scenario (division into halves with subsequent adjustment).
The examples illustrate the mathematical application of the rule.
5
Resolve the partial increase scenario
Analyze the al-Muntaqa case where both parties increase: Abu Hanifa's ruling splits the validity of the pledge proportionally.
This shows that increasing the debt along with the pawn is not categorically permitted.

Adding a Pledge After Partial Payment
Ruling on allocation of security when adding a pledge after partial payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on adding a pledge after partial debt payment and calculate the allocation of security among multiple pledged items.

Core Idea
A new pledge added after partial payment secures only the remaining debt, proportionally to the value of the existing encumbered property.

When a debtor pledges an item for a debt and later adds another pledge after making a partial payment, the new pledge does not secure the entire original debt but only the unpaid balance. The allocation depends on the values of the items and the nature of the payment. This lesson explores the Hanafi rule through key examples from the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial pledge and debt
Determine the original pledged item, its value, and the total debt amount at the time of the contract.
Example: slave girl worth 1000 for debt 1000.
2
Note the partial payment and addition of new pledge
Record the amount paid and the value of the new item added as an additional pledge after the payment.
Payment of 500, then addition of a male slave worth 1000.
3
Apply the principle: increase valid only for remaining debt
The new pledge attaches only to the outstanding debt (the part not yet paid or fallen), not to the paid portion.
Remaining debt = original debt - payment = 500.
4
Compute the allocation proportionally
Divide the remaining debt over the total encumbered value (value of new pledge plus the portion of the original pledge still encumbered by the paid amount).
Total encumbered value = value of new pledge (1000) + half the original pledge's value (500) = 1500; new pledge secures (1000/1500)*500 = 2/3 of 500.
5
Examine variations based on payment type
If payment is with a different genus (e.g., good coin), the debt remains wholly outstanding, and both pledges secure the full amount. If payment is with bad coins later returned, the allocation adjusts.
Different genus: both secure full 1000; bad coins returned: original secures 1000, new secures 500.

Division of Debt Among Surviving Pledged Items (Inqisam al-Dayn)
Detailed arithmetic variations on multi-item pledge division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the division of a debt among surviving pledged items when some items perish, applying the Hanafi rule of proportional allocation based on values.

Core Idea
When pledged items perish, the remaining items absorb the debt proportionally to their values, not equally.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq completes a complex case of multiple pledges: two slave girls, a male slave, and a child. The author works through various scenarios of deaths and value changes to demonstrate how the debt is divided among the surviving items. This lesson will guide you through the method of inqisam al-dayn (division of the debt) using fractions, a key principle in Hanafi muamalat.

Ruling on post-defect increase allocation

Core Idea
An increase after a defect is treated as if it existed before the defect, restoring lost debt value.

In Hanafi law, when a pledged item becomes defective, the debt it secures is reduced proportionally. However, if an increase (such as offspring or an additional pledge) occurs after the defect, that increase is legally treated as if it existed before the defect. This principle allows some of the lost debt value to be recovered.
