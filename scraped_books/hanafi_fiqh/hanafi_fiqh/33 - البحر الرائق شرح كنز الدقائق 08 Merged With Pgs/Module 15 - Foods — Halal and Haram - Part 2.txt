Accepting Reports: The Four Types and the Condition of Uprightness
Report acceptance rules with condition of uprightness and ownership removal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four types of reports and explain why the report of a slave or child is accepted in property matters while the report of a free upright person is not if it removes ownership.

Core Idea
A report is accepted if no removal of ownership; otherwise testimony is required.

In this lesson, we examine the rules for accepting reports from trustworthy individuals. The Hanafi school distinguishes between religious matters and property rights, with different conditions. We also explore an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the acceptance of reports in rights of people.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering scenario
Recognize the initial scenario: a single upright person reports meat as Magian slaughter while others say halal. The ruling follows the majority if upright.
2
Locate the removal of ownership condition
Al-Sighnaqi states: a report of an upright person is not accepted if it results in removal of ownership; testimony (shahada) is then required. Understand why this condition exists.
3
Analyze the objection and response
Question: Why is the report of a slave or child accepted despite removal of ownership? Answer: Because ownership of the neck (milk al-raqaba) is of lower status than ownership of marriage (milk al-nikah), which requires testimony.
This is a key talil (causative reasoning) in the text.
4
Recognize the four-fold classification
The text summarizes four types of reports: (1) messenger's report without punishment; (2) with punishment; (3) rights of people with partial obligation; (4) signs. Each has different conditions.
5
Understand the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa requires either number or uprightness for reports in rights of people, while the Sahibayn accept any discerning person's report. Note the mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq deals with the acceptance of reports (khabar) from trustworthy informants, particularly when there is a conflict of reports. The ruling is introduced with a scenario: a trustworthy Muslim reports that an animal is the slaughter of a Magian (which is impermissible), but others say it is halal and they are upright. The text says to accept the statement of the majority if they are all upright, but if there are two conflicting reports, the number or quality of reporters is considered. This leads to a broader discussion on the types of reports. Al-Sighnaqi is quoted stating that in religious matters, the report of an upright person is not accepted if it results in removal of ownership (zawal al-milk); rather testimony (shahada) is required. This is contrasted with the case of a slave or a child, whose report can be accepted even with removal of ownership. The reason given is that ownership of a slave (milk al-raqaba) is of lower status than ownership in marriage (milk al-nikah), which requires testimony. The passage then provides a summary (hasil) dividing reports into four types: (1) the report of a messenger in matters not involving punishment, where uprightness is sufficient; (2) his report in matters involving punishment, where the same rule applies according to al-Jassas, but al-Karkhi requires recompense; (3) the rights of people in matters where obligation is involved from one aspect, where either number or uprightness is required, contrary to the Sahibayn who accept any discerning person's report; and (4) signs, whose ruling has been explained. The Tatarkhaniyya adds that the reporter must be Muslim, but al-Hakim al-Shahid did not mention this condition, so it is not essential. The main matn then restates the ruling and provides detailed scenarios: if two upright persons report, the truth is preferred by number, but if they are accused, one upright person's report is taken. In case of conflict between permissibility and prohibition, prohibition is given precedence as potentially abrogating. The number and status (free vs slave, male vs female) affect preference. Subsequent rulings cover cases of slave girls, food, usurpation, apostasy, divorce, and manumission. The overall principle is that a single trustworthy report can create doubt but not certainty; hence one must investigate (taharrī) when possible, and refrain (tanzzuh) if there is any suspicion.

Key Terms
خبر (khabar): A report or piece of news from a single person, as opposed to shahada (testimony) which requires specific conditions.
عدالة (ʿadāla): The quality of being upright (ʿadl), meaning one who avoids major sins and does not persist in minor sins, and is trustworthy in religion.
شهادة (shahāda): Formal testimony, which requires multiple conditions including number, uprightness, and sometimes gender, as opposed to khabar which is less formal.
تحري (taharrī): Investigation or exertion of opinion to determine which of two conflicting reports is more likely true.
ملك الرقبة (milk al-raqaba): Ownership of the neck, i.e., full ownership of a slave.

Resolving Conflicting Reports: Tarjih by Number, Status, and Investigation
Detailed tarjih rules for conflicting reports from trustworthy informants.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of tarjih (preference) when reports conflict, including investigation (taharrī) and consideration of number, freedom, and gender.

Core Idea
When reports conflict, prefer by number, freedom, and gender; if still unsure, investigate or refrain.

This lesson covers the practical scenarios of conflicting reports from trustworthy persons. The text provides detailed rules for preferring one report over another based on the number of reporters, their status (free vs slave, male vs female), and the content (prohibition vs permissibility). We also see the method of taharrī when no clear preference exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for multiple reports
If two upright persons report, truth is preferred by increase in number. However, if the reporters are accused (not trustworthy), accept the single upright person's report.
Number alone is not absolute; uprightness matters.
2
Understand the role of taharrī
If two upright persons give conflicting reports (one halal, one haram), you must investigate (taharrī) to determine which is more likely. If you cannot decide, it is permissible to eat.
Taharrī is an exertion of opinion, not a fixed rule.
3
Apply preference by number and status
When reporters differ in number, freedom, or gender, preference is given to free over slave, male over female, and more over fewer, with freedom and masculinity often outweighing number.
The text gives specific examples: 2 free men vs 4 slaves, etc.
4
Handle the rule of prohibition as abrogating
If one reports prohibition and another permissibility, the prohibition is given precedence as potentially abrogating the permissibility (hamlan ʿala al-naskh).
This is a default preference when no other factors differentiate.
5
Apply tarjih in special cases
Scenarios involving slave girls, usurpation, apostasy, divorce, and manumission all follow the same principles: single trustworthy report creates doubt but not certainty, so one may refrain (tanzzuh) or act based on strongest opinion.
The text ends with practical applications demonstrating the rules.

Effect of Long Time Elapsed After Dog Ate
Additional ruling from mashayikh on time factor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the temporal qualification of Abu Hanifa's ruling on preys caught before a dog ate, and apply it to scenarios involving recent vs. distant catches.

Core Idea
Long time lapse after a dog eats makes earlier preys halal due to possible forgetfulness.

In the previous lesson, you learned about the intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa says preys caught before a dog ate are prohibited, while the Sahibayn say they are not. This passage introduces a temporal refinement by some later mashayikh: the dispute only applies when the eating incident is recent. If a long period has passed, all imams agree the earlier preys are halal, because the dog may have forgotten its training.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original dispute
Recall that according to Abu Hanifa, if a trained dog eats from a prey, any preys caught earlier by the same dog are prohibited. The Sahibayn disagree and say the earlier preys remain halal.
This dispute is recorded in the Zahir al-Riwayah.
2

The mashayikh state that Abu Hanifa's prohibition applies only when the time between the earlier catches and the eating incident is short (al-'ahd qarib). If a long period has passed (tatawul al-'ahd), such as a month or more, then Abu Hanifa's prohibition does not apply, and the earlier preys are halal according to all imams.
The rationale is that after a long time, the dog may have forgotten its training, so we cannot conclude it was untrained at the time of the earlier catches.

Consider two scenarios: (1) The dog ate yesterday, and the owner has hares caught last week. Since the time is short, the dispute applies: if following Abu Hanifa, those hares are haram; if following the Sahibayn, they are halal. (2) The dog ate yesterday, but the hares were caught two months ago. Now, due to the long period, all scholars agree the hares are halal.
The mashayikh's ruling is practical and removes uncertainty.
4
Understand the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi today is the view of the mashayikh: if more than a month has passed, the earlier preys are halal. This is because it is the most practical and takes into account the possibility of forgetfulness.
This view is from the later authorities (fatawa mutakhkhirin).

Core Idea
If the dog eats before you take the prey, it's unlawful; if after, it's permissible.

This lesson examines a key condition for lawful hunting with a trained dog: the dog must not eat from the prey during the hunt. The Hanafi school derives this from the hadith that the dog should 'hold for you.' We'll explore the precise scenarios and the reasoning behind the rulings.

Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hunting with a trained dog. The ruling on the prey depends on whether the dog eats from it. If the dog eats before the hunter takes possession, the prey is unlawful (haram). If after, it is lawful (halal).

The primary proof is the hadith of Adi ibn Hatim (Bukhari and Muslim): 'When you send your dog, mention the name of Allah. If it catches for you and you find it alive, slaughter it. If it kills and does not eat, eat it.'

The phrase 'catches for you' indicates the dog is holding the prey on behalf of the hunter. If the dog eats from the prey, it shows the dog is acting for itself (جاهل), not for the hunter. Thus the condition of 'not eating' is derived from this indication.
This is a Hanafi derivation; other schools may interpret differently.

Some scholars (e.g., Shafi'i) may not consider eating from the prey as invalidating the trained status of the dog. The Hanafi school is emphatic that eating during the hunt indicates the dog is not trained (جاهل).
The difference stems from how strictly the 'holding for you' condition is interpreted.

Ruling When Hunter Reaches the Prey Alive
Sets the requirement to slaughter game if found alive, and exceptions when unable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when game is reached alive and apply the exception for inability to slaughter.

Core Idea
Slaughter is required if you can; if you cannot, the catch suffices.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the obligation to slaughter game that is found alive after being caught by a trained dog, falcon, or arrow. The hadith of Adi ibn Hatim provides the proof, but the Hanafi school recognizes an exception when the hunter is unable to slaughter due to lack of tool or time. Understanding this nuance prevents ruling on hunting cases incorrectly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on the game (صيد) when the hunter reaches it alive (أدركه حياً). The ruling is that he must slaughter it (ذكاه). The hukm level is wajib.
The obligation is derived from the imperative in the hadith.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Adi ibn Hatim: 'When you send your dog, mention the name of Allah; if it catches for you and you reach it alive, then slaughter it.' Narrated by Bukhari and Muslim. This is a Sunnah ahad.
The hadith is the primary textual evidence.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'فاذبحه' (then slaughter it) is a command (amr). In Hanafi usul, al-amr yufid al-wujub (the imperative indicates obligation). Thus the hadith imposes obligation to slaughter when reaching alive.
This is a zanni al-dalala proof, but sufficient for wujub.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The passage records a difference: some scholars (e.g., in Al-Muhit) hold that slaughter is obligatory even if unable. However, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say: if the hunter cannot manage to slaughter (e.g., no knife, time constraint, animal in final breath), the meat is permissible without slaughter.
Al-Karkhi's Mukhtasar supports the exception.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: if unable, it is permissible. This is the choice of some mashayikh and recorded in the zahir al-riwayah.
Cross-madhhab: Shafi'i requires slaughter in all cases if any life remains.

Birds of Prey and Cheetahs in Hunting: Analogy to Dogs
Analogy of baz/fahd to dog in hunting rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the Hanafi school treats trained birds of prey and cheetahs analogously to dogs for hunting, and explain the consequence for game found alive.

Core Idea
Baz and fahd are like dogs: they perform emergency slaughter; game found alive must be manually slaughtered.

In this passage, the commentator explains that the legal rulings governing hunting with trained dogs also apply to trained falcons (baz) and cheetahs (fahd). This is based on the common effective cause: each delivers an emergency slaughter (tadhkiyah idtirariyyah). If the hunter finds the game alive after the animal has caught it, he must complete the slaughter manually; otherwise, the meat becomes unlawful.

If the hunter finds the game alive from such a catch, he must slaughter it (tadhkiyah). If he fails to do so, the meat becomes forbidden. This matches the rule for dogs.

Ruling on Game Lost After Being Shot: Continued Pursuit vs Giving Up
Reconciliation of hadiths on lost game

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling for game that disappears after being shot, distinguishing between when the hunter continues pursuit and when he gives up, and explain the reconciliation of the two hadiths.

Core Idea
Game found dead after disappearing is permissible only if the hunter did not give up pursuit; giving up renders it haram due to doubt.

The commentator addresses a common scenario in hunting: an arrow strikes game, which then struggles and disappears from view. When the hunter later finds it dead, is it permissible to eat? Two hadiths seem conflicting: one allows consumption after three days, another expresses dislike. The commentator reconciles them by linking the ruling to the hunter's effort. If he continues searching, it is permissible because the probability of death by the arrow is high. If he gives up, it is forbidden because other causes become likely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two hadiths
The hadith of Abu Tha'laba: eat after three days unless putrid. The other hadith: the Prophet disliked eating game that disappeared, saying insects may have killed it. Recognize the apparent conflict.
2
Understand the Hanafi reconciliation
The first hadith applies when the hunter does not give up pursuit (the likelihood of the arrow causing death remains high). The second applies when he gives up (other causes become probable). Note the key phrase: 'lacul lisa al-arḍ qatalathu' (perhaps earth's insects killed it).
3
Apply the principle of doubt
When avoidance of doubt is possible (by not giving up), the doubt must be avoided. When impossible (due to the nature of hunting), the doubt is overlooked. This is based on the legal maxim: 'al-mawhum fi al-muharramat ka al-mutahaqqaq' (the imagined in prohibitions is like the confirmed), but it is dropped when avoidance is impossible due to necessity.
4
Determine the consequence
If the hunter gave up, the game is not permissible. If he did not give up, it is permissible provided it has not putrefied.

Commentary
This passage presents multiple intra-Hanafi disputes regarding hunting scenarios where two shooters or projectiles interfere. The first scenario involves a halal shooter and a muhrim (one in ihram). If the muhrim's arrow hits the halal arrow and imparts additional force, causing the game to be killed, the game is not permissible to eat. This is because the muhrim's involvement is deemed to have contributed to the kill, and since muhrim cannot hunt, the game becomes unlawful. The second scenario involves two Muslim shooters. If the first arrow is known to have reached the game without the second, but the second added force, the game is for the first shooter and is halal. The author notes that the original text (Kitab) did not address the case of doubt, but according to later shaykhs, it should still be for the first. In the third scenario, a Magian's arrow interacts with a Muslim's arrow. If the Muslim's arrow would not have reached without the Magian, the game is for the Magian and is not halal. If the Muslim's arrow would have reached anyway, but the Magian's arrow added force, then by qiyas (analogy) it is halal, but by istihsan (juristic preference) it is not. This reflects a classic Hanafi internal dispute: qiyas would allow it because the Magian's arrow only aided, but istihsan prohibits it to prevent any appearance of Magian involvement in hunting. The remaining scenarios involve Magians shooting arrows or sending dogs/birds, then a Muslim shooting after the Magian's projectiles have landed. The key distinction is whether the Muslim's action occurs after the Magian's projectiles have come to rest (then it is halal) or while they are still in motion (then it is not, because the Muslim is deemed to have assisted Magian hunting). An untrained dog chasing game is analogized to the Magian's dog: if the Muslim shoots after the dog has stopped, it is halal; if while it is still following, it is not.

Key Terms
محرم (muhrim): A person in the state of ihram (consecration for Hajj/Umrah) who is prohibited from hunting.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning: applying the ruling of the original case (Muslim's arrow alone) to the case where Magian's arrow adds force, because the effective cause (death by arrow) is present.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference: departing from qiyas due to a more relevant consideration, here to avoid the appearance of Magian involvement in halal hunting.

Ruling on Game Falling on Ground or Other Surfaces After Being Shot
Rulings on game shot and falling on various surfaces

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on game that falls after being shot, classify surfaces as harmless or dangerous, and apply the principle of al-taharrus to determine whether the death is attributed to the shot or the fall.

Core Idea
If fall is avoidable and could cause death, game is haram; apparent cause (zahir) outweighs conjectural (mawhum).

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq discusses a common scenario in hunting: the game falls on a surface after being shot. The ruling depends on whether the fall was unavoidable and whether it could have caused death. The text distinguishes between harmless surfaces (ground, rope, flat brick) where the death is attributed to the shot, and dangerous surfaces (sharp rocks, edges, heights) where death may have been caused by the fall. A key intra-Hanafi dispute about a game whose head splits on a rock is reconciled by prioritizing the apparent cause over a conjectural one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hunting game with a projectile. The ruling on eating the game depends on the circumstances of its death. The general principle: if the game falls on the ground initially, it is halal because the fall is unavoidable and could not have been prevented.
Initial ground fall is considered harmless by default.
2
Classify the surface and determine avoidance
Determine whether the surface is 'harmless' (like ground, rope, flat brick) or 'dangerous' (like a sharp rock, edge, or height that can cause fatal injury). Also, assess whether the fall was avoidable: if the shooter could have aimed to prevent the fall onto a dangerous surface, avoidance is possible.
Avoidable + dangerous = haram; unavoidable or harmless = halal.
3
Apply the concept of al-taharrus
Al-taharrus is the ability to prevent the fall. If the shooter cannot avoid the fall (e.g., it lands on ground), the fall is disregarded. If he can avoid it (e.g., by not shooting in a certain direction), the fall is considered a potential alternate cause of death.
If unavoidable, death is attributed to the shot; if avoidable, the fall is a potential cause and the game is not halal.
4
Reconcile conflicting apparent and conjectural causes
When the game's head splits or belly tears on a rock, death from the rock is apparent (zahir), while death from the arrow is conjectural (mawhum). The apparent cause takes priority, so the game is not halal. This reconciles the general ruling (initial ground fall is halal) with the specific case in al-Muntaqa.
Apparent cause: visible fatal injury from the rock. Conjectural: possibility of death by arrow despite the split.
5
Extend to waterfowl
If a water bird is shot and the wound is immersed in water, the water may cause death by drowning or increased pain, so it is not eaten. If the wound stays above water, the death is attributed to the shot and it is halal.
The same principle: if an alternate cause (water) is possible, it takes precedence over the shot.

Hunting with Mirad, Bullet, and Other Weapons: Wound vs. Weight

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that hunting is halal only if death results from a wound (jarh) that draws blood, and state the rulings for mirad, bullet, stone, and other weapons.

Core Idea
Hunting requires a wound that draws blood; killing by weight or blunt force is haram.

This lesson examines a fundamental rule of Halal hunting in Hanafi fiqh: the animal must be killed by a wound that causes blood to flow. The hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim provides a clear distinction between piercing and blunt impact. We will explore how this principle applies to various weapons, from the mirad (a flat-headed arrow) to bullets and stones, and understand the reasoning behind the rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on hunting with weapons that kill by wound vs. weight. The general principle: if death is by wound (jarh), it is halal; if by weight (thiqal) or doubt, it is haram.
This is the core legal principle.

Locate the dalil
Two hadith are cited: the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim about the mirad, and the hadith prohibiting al-hadhf (pelting). Both are from Bukhari, Muslim, and Ahmad.

In the mirad hadith, the Prophet explicitly distinguishes piercing (fakhzaqa) from hitting with the broad side. This directly shows that only piercing (wounding) makes the meat halal. The prohibition of al-hadhf shows that killing without cutting is not hunting.
The evidence is clear and decisive.

The text mentions a difference of opinion when the wound is not bloody. Some Hanafi scholars consider it not halal because the blood has not flowed, which is necessary for dhabh.
This difference does not affect the main principle.

The text applies the rule to: knife/sword (halal if cuts), stone (halal only if light and has an edge that cuts), iron hammerstone (haram if crushes), stick (haram unless it cuts). The determining factor is whether the weapon causes a wound.
This extends the principle to modern weapons.

And if he shoots it with a stone, if it is heavy, it is not eaten, because of the possibility that it killed it by its weight. And if the stone is light and has an edge and wounds, then it is certain death by the stone at that time. And if he makes the stone long like an arrow and it is light and has an edge, and he shoots game with it, then if it wounds, it is halal because of killing by wound. And if he shoots it with an iron hammerstone (marwah) and it does not make a cut, it is not halal because it killed it by crushing. Likewise, if he shoots it with it and it cuts its jugular veins and severs its head, then the veins may be cut by the weight, so doubt arises, and it is possible that it died before the cutting of the veins. And if he shoots it with a stick like a staff and the like, it is not halal because it killed it by weight, not wound; unless it has an edge that cuts, then it is like a sword or spear. And the principle in this entire class of issues is that if death occurs by wound, it is definitely halal; if it occurs by weight, or there is doubt about it, then it is not halal, definitively or out of precaution. And if he wounded it and it died, if the wound was bloody, it is halal by agreement; if it was not bloody, they differed: some said it is not halal because the meaning of dhabaha (slaughter) is absent, which is the extraction of impure blood, and the Prophet (peace be upon him) made extraction a condition.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on hunting weapons that do not have a sharp edge, such as the mirad (a type of arrow with a flat iron head) and the bullet. The key principle is that the game must be killed by a wound that causes blood to flow; if it is killed by blunt force or weight, it is not halal. The primary evidence is the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim where the Prophet distinguished between the mirad piercing (halal) and hitting with its broad side (haram). Another hadith prohibits al-hadhf (pelting with stones) because it kills by breaking without cutting. The same applies to bullets (bunduqah) which kill by impact, not cutting. The discussion extends to other weapons: knives and swords are halal if they cut; stones are halal only if they are light and have an edge that cuts; heavy stones are haram because death is attributed to weight. An iron hammerstone (marwah) that crushes is haram. Even if it cuts the jugular veins, if the cutting could have been caused by the weight, it is haram due to doubt. A stick without an edge is haram. The general principle is established: death by wound = halal; death by weight or doubt = haram. Finally, the passage discusses the importance of blood flow: if the wound is bloody, there is agreement that it is halal; if the wound is not bloody, there is a difference of opinion. The Hanafi position is that the extraction of blood (the 'blood of the beast') is a necessary condition for the meat to be permissible, based on the Prophetic tradition.

Key Terms
معراض (mi'rad): A type of arrow with a flat iron head, no feathers, no blade; it has a sharp edge that can pierce and a broad side that can hit without cutting.
بندقة (bunduqah): A bullet or pellet that kills by impact, not by cutting.
جرح (jarh): A wound that causes blood to flow, necessary for halal hunting.
