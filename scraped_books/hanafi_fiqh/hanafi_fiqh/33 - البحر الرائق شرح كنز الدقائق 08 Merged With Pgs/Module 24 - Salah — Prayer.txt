Timing of Udhiyah: City Dwellers Must Wait for Eid Prayer
Explains timing of udhiyah with hadith evidence and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the timing of udhiyah for city dwellers and villagers, and evaluate the inter-madhhab dispute with evidence.

Core Idea
City dwellers must sacrifice after Eid prayer; villagers may sacrifice after dawn.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a key practical question: When exactly can one slaughter the udhiyah? The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction based on one's residence and obligation to attend the Eid prayer. As you read, note how the author uses two prophetic hadiths to establish that the prayer is a condition (shart) for the sacrifice for city dwellers, while villagers are free from this condition. The inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik illustrates a classic difference in interpreting the scope of the hadith.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two categories: city dwellers and villagers. The Hanafi ruling forbids city dwellers from sacrificing before the Eid prayer, but permits villagers after dawn prayer.
The key distinction is the obligation to attend the congregational Eid prayer.
2
Identify the proof (dalil)
Locate the two hadiths cited. The first commands repetition for those who slaughter before the imam's prayer; the second states that the first rite is prayer then sacrifice.
The author of al-Nihaya clarifies that the issue is the absence of the condition (prayer), not the time.
3
Identify the opposing school view
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik hold that no one may sacrifice before the imam sacrifices, even if they prayed individually.
The text explicitly says 'this is an argument against al-Shafi'i and Malik'.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school restricts the hadith to those obligated to pray the Eid prayer (city dwellers). Villagers are not included; they may sacrifice after their own dawn prayer.
The second hadith 'first our rite is the prayer' is interpreted as applying only to those who pray the Eid prayer.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Hanafi law, villagers may sacrifice early on Eid morning, while under Shafi'i/Maliki, they must wait for the imam's sacrifice.
This difference can affect the entire community if they follow different madhhabs.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq is a commentary on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. The author clarifies the ruling that city dwellers (ahl al-misr) may not slaughter their udhiyah before performing the Eid prayer, while villagers and Bedouins may slaughter after the dawn prayer (fajr) even before the imam has performed the Eid prayer. The proof is two hadiths: first, the Prophet's command to repeat the sacrifice if one slaughters before the imam's prayer, and second, his statement that the first rite is prayer, then sacrifice. The commentator notes that the condition for validity is the performance of the prayer, not the specific time. He then presents this as a proof against Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik, who held that even after the individual's own prayer, one cannot sacrifice before the imam's sacrifice. The Hanafi position based on these ahadith is that for non-city dwellers, sacrifice is valid from dawn, as the restriction applies only to those who are obligated to attend the congregational Eid prayer (the city dwellers).

Key Terms
مصري (masri): In this context, a city dweller (ahl al-misr), i.e., a resident of a town or city where the Eid prayer is established.
أضحية (udhiyah): The sacrificial animal offered during the days of Eid al-Adha.
قروي (qarawi): Villager – a person who does not reside in a city and is not obligated to attend the congregational Eid prayer.

Eid Prayer and Udhiyah: Scenarios and Rulings
Details multiple scenarios linking Eid prayer and udhiyah validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for various scenarios where the Eid prayer impacts the validity of udhiyah and apply the correct ruling to each case.

Core Idea
The Eid prayer is a condition for udhiyah, but numerous exceptions accommodate practical realities.

This lesson examines the detailed rulings from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq on how the Eid prayer affects the validity of udhiyah. The passage presents several specific scenarios involving timing, congregation, the imam's state of purity, and unusual circumstances. Understanding these nuances is crucial for correct application of the Hanafi position, especially in contemporary situations where prayer arrangements may vary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base rule
Recognize that for city dwellers, the Eid prayer is a condition for the udhiyah to be valid. The sacrifice must occur after the prayer.
This is the default ruling from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Analyze the first exception
If the people of the mosque have prayed but the people of the musalla have not yet prayed, sacrificing after the mosque prayer is valid by istihsan.
Istihsan considers the prayer as valid even if not the entire congregation.
3
Examine the tashahhud dispute
There is ikhtilaf on sacrificing after the imam sits for tashahhud but before salam. The preferred Hanafi view is that it is not permissible.
Contrary to al-Hasan and some sources that allow it.
4
Navigate the imam's impurity scenario
If the imam prayed without wudu and the congregation was unaware until after they sacrificed, the sacrifice is valid. If they knew before dispersing, it must be repeated (preferred view).
Knowledge at the time of sacrifice affects validity.
5
Consider the imam's delay or absence
If the imam does not pray on the first day, sacrifice is deferred to noon; if delayed to the second day, sacrifice before the qada prayer is valid. In a land without a leader, sacrifice after dawn suffices.
Practical concessions for disrupted situations.

Commentary
This rich passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses numerous specific scenarios regarding the relationship between the Eid prayer and the validity of the udhiyah. The commentator first discusses the case where different groups pray at different places (mosque vs. musalla): if one sacrifices after the people of the mosque have prayed but before the people of the musalla have prayed, it is valid by istihsan, because the sacrifice is tied to the performance of a valid prayer, even if not the entire congregation. There is a difference of opinion regarding whether the sacrifice is valid if one slaughters after the imam has sat for tashahhud but before he says salam. The passage presents various views: according to al-Hasan it is invalid, but in other sources it is valid, and the most correct view is that it is invalid. The text then discusses cases where the imam prays without wudu: if the congregation did not know and sacrificed, it is valid; if they knew before dispersing, the sacrifice should be repeated (according to the preferred view). If the imam later finds out and calls for repeating the prayer, anyone who sacrificed before knowing of the imam's impurity is excused, but if they sacrificed after knowledge and before noon, it is invalid; after noon it becomes valid. Moreover, if the imam fails to pray the Eid prayer on the first day, the sacrifice is deferred to noon, and if the imam prays only after noon, the sacrifice done before that is insufficient. However, if the sacrifice is postponed to the next day, it may be done before the prayer because the first day's time has lapsed. Finally, in a land of lawlessness where no leader is available to lead the Eid prayer, the people may sacrifice immediately after dawn, and it is valid. These rulings highlight the Hanafi approach of considering both the condition of a valid prayer and the practical circumstances of the community.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; a method of departing from strict analogy in favor of a ruling that is more equitable or practical.
الجبانة (al-jabbanah): The open area outside the city where the Eid prayer is performed (musalla).
التشهد (al-tashahhud): The testimony of faith recited while sitting in prayer, usually during the last rak'ah.

Key Takeaway
The Eid prayer is a condition for udhiyah in cities, but the Hanafi school allows numerous exceptions based on istihsan, necessity, and the timing of knowledge.

When Is the Udhiyah Valid? Timing Relative to the Eid Prayer
Rulings on the timing of Udhiyah relative to the Eid prayer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when the sacrifice becomes valid relative to the Eid prayer and identify exceptions for missed, invalid, or delayed prayers.

Core Idea
The sacrifice is valid only after a valid Eid prayer; exceptions exist for unknown invalidity or when no imam is present.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq unfolds the intricate Hanafi rulings on the timing of the udhiyah in relation to the Eid prayer. The base rule is that the sacrifice becomes valid only after the prayer has been performed correctly. However, the jurists delve into numerous scenarios: what if the imam prayed without wudu? What if he has not yet completed the salam? What if the prayer was not performed at all? This lesson will guide you through these detailed rulings to ensure your sacrifice is compliant with the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base rule
The sacrifice is valid only after a valid Eid prayer has been performed. This is the foundational condition.
The text begins by stating that sacrificing after the mosque people have prayed suffices, even if those in the cemetery have not yet prayed.
2
Analyze the imam's actions during prayer
If the imam is in the tashahhud but has not said salam, the sacrifice is not valid according to the preferred view. However, if the imam prayed without wudu and the people sacrificed before knowing, it is valid.
The text distinguishes between knowledge before and after the sacrifice.
3
Examine cases of missed or delayed prayer
If the imam did not pray the Eid prayer on the first day, the sacrifice is delayed to midday. If the prayer is made up the next day, sacrificing before that make-up prayer is valid.
The next day's prayer is qada', not ada', so the timing condition is relaxed.
4
Address mistaken dates
If the imam prays on what turns out to be the day of 'Arafah but it was thought to be Eid, the prayer and sacrifice are valid if testimony was given; otherwise, they must be repeated.
The ruling hinges on whether the mistake was avoidable.
5
Summarize the days and nights of sacrifice
The days of sacrifice are three, with the first being best. Slaughtering at night is allowed but disliked due to possible error in darkness.
Sacrifice is better than giving its value in charity because it is time-bound.

After completing the discussion on issues of clothing, and presenting it due to the severe need for it, he mentioned after it the issues of looking because they occur more frequently than other matters of istibra' (seeking purity/investigation), so he presented them first. The issues of looking are four categories: a man looking at a woman, a woman looking at a man, a man looking at a man, and a woman looking at a woman. The first category is divided into four subcategories: a man looking at a non-mahram woman, his looking at his wife and slave-woman, his looking at his mahram relatives, and his looking at another person's slave-woman. The evidence for the permissibility of looking is what was narrated that Asma' bint Abi Bakr entered upon the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) wearing thin clothes, and the Messenger turned away from her and said: 'O Asma', when a woman reaches the age of menstruation, it is not proper that anything be seen of her except this and this' – and he pointed to his face and his hands. He said (may Allah have mercy on him): 'He should not look at anything other than the face of a free woman and her palms.' The commentator said: 'This statement has a flaw because it leads to the meaning that he does not look at anything except the face of a free woman and her palms, thus it would be an incitement to look at these two parts and to abandon looking at everything else besides them.' But it is not hidden from a thoughtful person that this flaw does not exist because the particle 'ila' is a substitute for the preposition 'min' (from) which indicates the starting point, so it is in the sense of the expressed, so the implied meaning is: 'He is not allowed to look from the woman to other than the face and palms.' This gives the meaning of forbidding looking at other than the face and palms, not incitement. The commentator supported the permissibility of looking at what was mentioned with the saying of Allah: 'And let them not display their adornment except what appears thereof' [24:31] – 'Ali and Ibn 'Abbas said: what appears is the kohl and the ring, not the entire face and hand, so it does not support the claim. The basis is that the woman is an 'awrah that must be covered, due to his saying 'The woman is an 'awrah that is covered except what the law has exempted, which are two limbs.' Also because the woman cannot avoid dealing with strangers, so she must show her face to be recognized, to demand the price, and to return (reject?) the exit (transaction?) ...

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on looking (nazar) and touching (lams). The author explains that after clothing, looking issues are more frequent. He categorizes looking into four types: man→woman, woman→man, man→man, woman→woman. For man→woman, there are four subcases: to a non-mahram (ajnabiyyah), to his wife/slave, to mahram relatives, and to another's slave. He provides the primary evidence: the hadith of Asma' bint Abi Bakr, where the Prophet turned away from her thin clothing and stated that after puberty, only the face and hands may be seen. The matn states: 'لا ينظر إلى غير وجه الحرة وكفيها' — he must not look at anything other than the face and palms of a free woman. Then the commentator (sharh) notes a potential grammatical flaw: if read as 'he does not look except at face and palms', it might encourage looking at those parts. But the sharh clarifies that the Arabic particle 'ila' here serves as a substitute for 'min' (from), yielding 'he may not look from the woman to anything other than face and palms', i.e., he may look only at those. The sharh also cites the Quranic verse 'ولا يبدين زينتهن إلا ما ظهر منها' and reports that Ali and Ibn Abbas interpreted 'what appears' as kohl and ring, not entire face/hands, so it doesn't support the ruling. Instead, the ruling is based on the hadith and the principle: woman is 'awrah, except two limbs (face and hands) out of necessity for transactions. Thus, the Hanafi position: it is permissible to look at the face and hands of a non-mahram free woman without desire; looking at other parts is haram.

Travel of Female Slaves Without a Mahram: Principle and Temporal Qualification
A ruling on female slave travel with a temporal caveat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the original Hanafi ruling on female slaves traveling without a mahram and explain why later scholars made it impermissible due to changing social conditions.

Core Idea
A ruling may change when society changes: what was permissible in righteous times becomes impermissible in corrupt times.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq addresses a specific question: can a female slave (amah) or a mother of a master's child (umm walad) travel without a mahram? The author provides the original permissive ruling based on the slave's status, but then introduces a crucial qualification from later Hanafi authorities: this permission was conditional on a society dominated by righteous people. In later times, when corruption prevailed, the ruling was reversed. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the temporal shift.

Guided Path
1
Extract the original ruling and its rationale
Identify the permissive ruling for female slaves and the reasoning: the female slave is like a mahram with respect to looking and touching, so travel without a mahram is allowed.
2
List the categories included
Note that the ruling extends to umm walad, mudabbarah, mukatabah, and, according to Imam Abu Hanifa, even a partially freed woman (mu'taqatu al-ba'd).
The common link is the presence of slavery (رق).
3
Identify the temporal qualification
Find the critical statement from al-Kafi: this ruling was only for earlier times of righteous people; in the author's time (and ours), it is not permissible due to the prevalence of corrupt people.
This shows the Hanafi method of considering societal conditions (al-‘urf wa al-‘ādah) in applying rulings.
4
Apply the ruling to contemporary scenarios
Recognize that while female slavery is nearly extinct, the principle of societal condition affecting permissibility remains relevant in analogous cases.
This is a model for how fiqh adapts to changing social realities without discarding the original evidence.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on travel for a female slave (amah) and umm walad (the mother of a master's child) without a mahram. The author states that it is permissible for them to travel without a mahram because their status with regard to looking and touching by other men is akin to that of a mahram—meaning that they are not considered to have the same degree of modesty restrictions as free women. This applies equally to umm walad, mudabbarah, mukatabah, and according to Imam Abu Hanifa, even a partially freed woman (mu'taqatu al-ba'd). However, a critical qualification is added from al-Kafi: this ruling was applicable in the earlier age of righteousness (ghalabat ahl al-salah). In the author's time (and ours), due to the prevalence of corrupt people, it is no longer permissible. This shows the Hanafi school's responsiveness to changing social conditions in applying rulings related to travel and gender interaction.

Key Terms
أمة (amah): A female slave.
أم الولد (umm walad): A female slave who has born a child to her master.
محرم (mahram): A close relative whom one is permanently prohibited from marrying.
مكاتبة (mukatabah): A female slave under a contract of emancipation (kitabah).
مدبرة (mudabbarah): A female slave promised freedom upon her master's death.
معتقة البعض (mu'taqatu al-ba'd): A woman who has been partially freed (e.g., half free, half slave).

The Obligation to Slaughter and the Hidden Life Dispute
Rulings on death before slaughter with hidden life dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a hunted animal dies without slaughter and explain the intra-madhhab dispute regarding hidden life (al-hayah al-khafiyyah).

Core Idea
If able to slaughter, failure makes it carrion; hidden life only suffices according to Abu Hanifa.

This passage from Bahr al-Raiq addresses a common hunting scenario: the animal is reached alive but the hunter does not slaughter it in time. The ruling depends on whether the hunter was able to slaughter and, if not, the degree of life remaining. The madhhab contains a significant internal disagreement on what constitutes viable life for valid slaughter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Note that if the hunter reaches the animal alive and fails to slaughter it, it becomes carrion and is haram. This assumes ability to slaughter.
Ability means having a knife and being able to perform the cut.
2
Distinguish the exception
If the hunter is unable to slaughter and the animal only has the convulsive life of a slaughtered animal, it is halal by consensus (or according to the Sahibayn).
The convulsive life is not considered real life; the animal is legally dead.
3
Understand the hidden life dispute
Abu Hanifa holds that hidden life (al-hayah al-khafiyyah) is sufficient for valid slaughter, so if the animal has any life (even minimal), slaughter can make it halal. The Sahibayn require apparent life (clear signs of vitality).
This affects cases like mutaraddiyah, natihah, etc.
4
Apply the proof from Quran and hadith
The verse 'وما أكل السبع إلا ما ذكيتم' makes an absolute exception for slaughtered animals. The hadith to 'Adi ibn Hatim commands slaughter if reached alive. These proofs require slaughter whenever the hunter has control over the animal.
Quran 5:3 is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalalah; the hadith is sahih.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Al-Shafi'i distinguishes based on the reason for inability: if no tool, haram; if time insufficient, halal. The Hanafi response is that the proofs are absolute and such distinctions are unsupported.

Commentary
This is a comprehensive ruling covering multiple scenarios where the animal becomes forbidden (haram). The matn states that if the animal dies without being slaughtered, is choked without a wound, or if an untrained dog, a Magian dog, or a dog over which tasmiyah was intentionally omitted participates, the meat is forbidden. Abu Hanifa holds that hidden life is sufficient for slaughter to be valid, so the animal can be halal if slaughtered while still alive even minimally. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) require apparent life, meaning the animal must show clear signs of life beyond the convulsions of a slaughtered animal. The text then discusses the proof from Quran and Sunnah for requiring slaughter when possible. It contrasts the Shafi'i view and rebuts it. Then it covers the prohibition when an untrained dog, Magian dog, or one without tasmiyah participates, citing the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim. It also addresses the case where the dog returns the animal without wounding it, and the ruling of dislike (karahah) with two opinions: tanzihi (disliked but not sinful) and tahrimi (close to haram). The passage emphasizes that the wound (جرح) from a trained dog is essential, and choking or breaking bones does not suffice because blood must flow. The overall message is that the validity of hunting depends on the dog being trained, the hunter reciting Bismillah, and the dog wounding the animal. Any deviation renders the meat unlawful.

Conditions for Hunting: Trained Dog, Tasmiyah, and Wounding
Conditions for valid hunting with dogs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions for a valid hunt using a dog and apply the ruling when these conditions are violated.

Core Idea
The dog must be trained, the hunter must recite Bismillah, and the dog must wound the animal—missing any makes it haram.

This passage from Bahr al-Raiq continues the discussion of hunting, focusing on the conditions required for the animal to be halal when caught by a dog. It emphasizes the necessity of tasmiyah (mentioning Allah's name), the dog being trained (mu'allam), and the dog wounding the animal. Violation of any of these renders the meat forbidden. The text provides detailed evidence from the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim and discusses nuanced cases like participation of an untrained dog or a Magian's dog.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
The passage states that training (ta'lim), tasmiyah, and wounding (jarh) are necessary. Without these, the catch is haram.
Training means the dog responds to commands and does not eat the prey.
2
Understand the implication of choking without wounding
If the dog chokes the animal without wounding it (breaking bones, etc.), it is haram because blood must flow out.
Choking does not cause blood to flow, so it does not fulfill the wounding requirement.
3
Analyze the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim
The hadith explicitly states that if the hunter sends his dog with tasmiyah and it catches and kills, he may eat. But if another dog joins, he must not eat because he only named over his own dog.
This hadith is used as proof against participation of untrained/other dogs.
4
Apply the principle of halal and haram conflicting
The maxim: 'When halal and haram come together, the haram prevails.' So if a trained dog and an untrained dog both catch the animal, it is haram.
This is a general usul principle.
5
Examine the ruling when the dog returns the animal without wounding
If the dog returns the animal (e.g., retrieves it) without wounding it further, the meat is disliked (makruh). There are two opinions: tanzihi (preferred to avoid) and tahrimi (close to haram).
Al-Hulwani chose tahrimi due to participation from one aspect.

Inter-Madhhab Dispute on Eating a Limb Cut from a Live Animal
Hanafi response to Shafi'i on permissibility of a severed limb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the prohibition of a severed limb and explain why the Shafi'i analogy fails, using the hadith proof.

Core Idea
A limb cut from a live animal is carrion—no slaughter took place, even if the animal later dies.

This lesson examines a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: if an animal is shot and a limb is severed, and the animal dies from the wound, can the severed limb be eaten? The ruling affects how we apply the concept of 'forced slaughter' (dhabh al-idtirar) and the distinction between life in reality and life in legal ruling. By studying this, you will understand why the Hanafi school holds the limb to be haram.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that a limb severed from a live animal is carrion (haram), based on the hadith 'What is cut from an animal while it is alive is carrion.'
2
Identify the opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that if the animal dies from the wound, the limb is halal because it is separated by 'forced slaughter' (dhabh al-idtirar), analogous to voluntary slaughter.
3
Examine the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i proof analogizes forced slaughter (hunting wound) to voluntary slaughter: if death results, the separation is considered a valid slaughter, making the limb halal.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes between life in reality (haqiqatan) and life in ruling (hukman). At the moment of separation, the animal is still alive both ways; slaughter only occurs at death, not at separation. The hadith explicitly declares such parts carrion.
The hadith (Ibn Majah) is the primary evidence; it is ahad but clear in meaning.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf): Hanafis prohibit eating the severed limb; Shafi'is permit it if the animal dies from the wound. This affects what a hunter may consume.

Complete Severance vs. Partial Cutting of Limbs
Rulings on complete and partial severance during slaughter and hunting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between cases where the entire animal becomes halal versus where only the uncut part is halal, based on the principle of conceivable survival.

Core Idea
If survival is conceivable after cutting a limb, the limb becomes carrion but the rest remains halal.

This lesson examines the critical distinction between a complete separation of the head or body (where survival is impossible) and partial cutting of limbs (where survival is still possible). The key is the concept of 'conceivable survival' (tawahhum al-hayah). We will apply this to both domestic animals during slaughter and game during hunting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the extent of the cut
Determine if the cut is complete (head or body in half) or partial (limb or portion).
Complete severance includes cutting the head entirely or cutting the body in half.
2
Assess conceivable survival
If the cut is complete, survival is impossible; if partial, survival is possible.
The criterion is whether the remaining part could logically support life.
3
Apply the ruling
For complete separation, whole animal halal; for partial, the cut part is haram (carrion) and the rest is halal.
If a limb is cut but hangs by skin and healing is impossible, it is considered effectively separated.
4
Note the exception
In the case of a limb cut but hanging by skin, if healing is impossible (i.e., effective separation), the limb is carrion.
Legal rulings follow the underlying reality, not mere external appearance.

Commentary
This passage enumerates subsidiary rulings (furu') related to cutting parts of an animal during slaughter or hunting. It builds on the previous principle: the key is whether the animal's survival is conceivable after the wound. The overall principle is that legal rulings follow the underlying meanings and reality, not mere external forms.

Neck Strikes and Game Limb Cases
Rulings on neck strikes from front and back, and on game limbs cut but attached.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the halal status of an animal when struck from the front or back of the neck, and when a game limb is cut but remains attached.

Core Idea
The halal status depends on whether the jugulars are cut while the animal is still alive, regardless of the direction of the strike.

This lesson covers two specific scenarios: striking an animal's neck from the front or back during slaughter, and hunting a game animal where a limb is cut but does not fully separate until death. In both cases, the critical factor is whether the animal's blood vessels (awdāj) are cut while it is still alive, ensuring a valid slaughter.

Distinguish front strike from back strike

2
Check timing of jugular cut
If the animal dies before the jugulars are cut, it is not halal (like mawqudhah). If it remains alive long enough for the jugulars to be cut, it becomes halal.
The key is that the cut to the jugulars must occur while the animal is still alive.
3
Assess limb cut in game
If a game animal's limb is cut but remains attached by skin, check if healing is conceivable.
If healing is conceivable, the whole animal is halal; if not, the limb is carrion.
4
Apply the meaning-based rule
If the limb is effectively separated (ibānah ma'nan), even if physically attached, it is treated as carrion.
The ruling follows the reality, not just the external form.

Two Hunters and One Game: Ownership and Permissibility
Ownership and permissibility when two persons shoot the same game

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between disabling (ithkhan) and mere wounding in hunting, state the Hanafi ruling on ownership and permissibility for each case, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the significance of short-term life.

Core Idea
Ownership of game hinges on disabling (ithkhan), not just wounding; the first to disable owns it, and the second's kill is haram.

When two hunters shoot the same game, who owns it? Is the meat halal? This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the Hanafi position, distinguishing between a mere wound and a disabling shot. It also introduces a subtle intra-madhhab difference between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the legal significance of a short lifespan. Understanding this ruling sharpens your grasp of ownership transfer and the concept of hayyiz al-imtina' (realm of inaccessibility).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main scenarios
The matn presents two cases: first shot does not disable (lam yuthkhinhu) vs. first shot disables (athkhanahu). Note the different rulings for ownership and permissibility.
The hadith 'al-sayd liman akhadhahu' is cited.
2
Apply the hadith
The hadith 'Game belongs to the one who takes it' attributes ownership to the actual taker/killer. Understand that this applies fully when the game is still in hayyiz al-imtina'.
3
Analyze the concept of hayyiz al-imtina'
Once the game is disabled, it leaves the realm of inaccessibility. The first hunter becomes capable of voluntary slaughter and is obliged to perform it. Failure makes the meat haram.
Disabling (ithkhan) means the animal cannot escape.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad: If the first shot leaves the game with only a day of life, Abu Yusuf considers that life negligible (second's kill is halal), while Muhammad considers it significant (second's kill is haram).
Both views are in al-Asl.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and compensation
The precautionary view (Muhammad's) is often relied upon. Also note the liability: if the second kills after the first disabled, the second must pay the value minus the decrease from the first wound.
Compensation is analogous to destroying a sick slave.

Commentary
This passage discusses the scenario where two hunters shoot the same game. The matn gives two cases: (1) If the first shot merely wounds without disabling (lam yuthkhinhu), the second hunter who kills it owns it and it is halal. The proof is the hadith 'Game belongs to the one who takes it.' The reasoning: the game was still in a state of inaccessibility (himyan), so the second's kill constitutes forced slaughter. (2) If the first shot disables the game (athkhanahu), it becomes owned by the first hunter, and the game is prohibited for the second. The first is now capable of voluntary slaughter and obliged to perform it; failing that, the second's killing is not a valid slaughter but mere killing, making the meat haram. An intra-Hanafi dispute arises regarding the degree of life after the first shot. Abu Yusuf holds that if the game has only a short time to live (like a day or less), the second's shot does not make it haram because that level of life is not considered. Muhammad al-Shaybani disagrees, saying such life is considered, so the second's shot does make it haram. The relied-upon position may vary. The passage also covers liability: if the second kills the game after the first disabled it, the second must pay the first the full value of the game minus the decrease caused by the first's wound, because the first owned it by disabling it. The value is assessed at the time of destruction (i.e., after the first wound). This is analogous to destroying a sick slave: the compensator pays the value as is.

أثخنه (athkhanahu): To wound so severely that the animal cannot escape or effectively is disabled.
حيز الامتناع (hayyiz al-imtinā'): The realm of inaccessibility; the animal is not yet under control.
