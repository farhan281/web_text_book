Ruling on a Male Mukatab Committing Zina: Hadd and 'Aqr
Ruling from al-Asl on mukatab zina, hadd, shubha, and 'aqr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a male mukatab owes the 'aqr (mahr) when he commits zina, and distinguish between cases of shubha, compliance, and affirmation.

Core Idea
A mukatab's hadd for zina is replaced by financial 'aqr only where a valid shubha exists; immediate payment depends on the woman's resistance.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a ruling from Imam Muhammad's al-Asl regarding a mukatab (a slave under kitaba) who commits zina. It clarifies that while hadd is the default, if the mukatab claims a shubha (e.g., he believed the woman was lawful to him), the hadd is dropped and replaced by an obligation to pay the 'aqr (full mahr). The key nuance is that this 'aqr is due immediately unless the woman was compliant (mutawā'iah). Additionally, if the woman confirms the mukatab's claim of marriage, the immediate payment is also waived. The text draws an analogy with a madman to illustrate the principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is a male mukatab committing zina. The default ruling is that hadd (the fixed punishment) applies. This is stated as 'apparent' in the text.
2
Identify the condition that changes the ruling
The condition is the mukatab claiming a shubha (a semblance or doubt of legality). If he claims a shubha, the hadd is dropped.
Shubha can be, e.g., thinking she is his slave or having a disputed marriage.
3
Trace the consequence of the condition
When hadd is dropped, the 'aqr (dower) becomes obligatory, just as in the case of a free man. This 'aqr is taken from the mukatab immediately, not deferred until emancipation.
'Aqr is the full mahr for a free woman.
4
Note the qualifications on immediacy
The immediate payment applies only if the woman was coerced (mukraha). If she was compliant (mutawā'iah), the 'aqr is not taken immediately. Also, if the woman affirms the mukatab's claim of marriage (tasdiq), immediate payment is also waived, regardless of coercion or compliance.
Affirmation creates a mutual shubha.
5
Observe the qiyas (analogy) used
The text analogizes the mukatab's case to that of a madman (majnūn). For a madman, if the woman was coerced, mahr is wajib; if compliant, no mahr. This confirms the same ruling for the mukatab.
The shared attribute is the woman's lack of consent triggering immediate financial liability.

Hadd for Wine Drinking Under Duress and Types of Duress
Determining whether hadd is applied for wine drinking under duress and classifying duress types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hadd for wine drinking under duress, explain why it is averted using the principle of shubha, and distinguish the three types of duress on sins according to Al-Mabsut.

Core Idea
Doubt (shubha) averts hadd: even mild duress creates enough doubt to drop the fixed punishment for wine drinking.

We have previously learned that drinking wine is a major sin carrying a fixed hadd punishment of eighty lashes. But what if a person is forced to drink under duress? Does the punishment still apply? This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq cites two authoritative Hanafi sources—Al-Muhit and Al-Mabsut—to clarify that duress, even of a milder degree, creates a doubt (shubha) about permissibility, and such doubt is sufficient to avert the hadd. The passage also provides a systematic classification of duress on sins, helping us understand when a coerced act becomes permissible and when it remains forbidden.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Find the specific ruling: Al-Muhit states that if someone drinks wine under duress, he is not subject to hadd. Note that this applies regardless of the severity of duress, as long as it creates a doubt.
The ruling is about the application of hadd, not about the permissibility of wine itself.
2
Examine the proof from Al-Muhit
The proof is the principle that 'doubt suffices to avert hadd punishments.' The passage explicitly states that even the milder form of duress establishes a doubt (shubha) of permissibility, and this doubt is sufficient to drop the hadd.
This principle is based on the hadith 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubahat' (Avert the fixed punishments by doubts).
3
Analyze the classification of duress in Al-Mabsut
Al-Mabsut divides duress on sins into three types: (1) actions that become permissible under duress (e.g., uttering kufr), (2) actions that remain haram even under duress (e.g., killing an innocent), and (3) actions that are permissible in the sense that one is not sinful for doing them but is rewarded for restraint (e.g., drinking wine).
The third category is intermediate: the act is still prohibited in origin, but duress creates a shubha that averts punishment.
4
Locate wine drinking in the typology
Wine drinking under duress falls into the third category. The passage explains that with the severer of two types of duress, actual permissibility is established (as in necessity), but with milder duress, only a doubt is established. Since doubt suffices, no hadd is applied.
The 'severer' type likely refers to life-threatening duress that would make the act actually permissible under necessity; the milder type is non-life-threatening duress.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is that a person coerced into drinking wine is not flogged. This differs from some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) that may require stronger duress to drop the hadd. The Hanafi position is broad: any credible duress creating doubt is enough.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate and based on different interpretations of the principle of shubha.

Dispute on whether hadd is imposed on one compelled to zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on hadd for compelled zina and identify the mufta bihi view with its basis.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school ultimately drops the hadd for anyone compelled to zina, regardless of the compeller.

This lesson explores a nuanced point in Hanafi criminal law: whether the hadd punishment for zina applies when the act is committed under compulsion. While zina is haram, the Hanafi masters debated the effect of coercion. The passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq reveals an intra-madhhab dispute where Imam Abu Hanifa changed his view, and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) held a consistent position. Understanding this dispute illustrates how Hanafi fiqh balances textual rulings with considerations of coercion and intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the ruling
Determine what act and punishment are at issue: the hadd (fixed corporal punishment) for zina when committed under compulsion (ikrah).
The act itself (zina) remains haram, but the question is whether the penalty applies.
2
Trace Imam Abu Hanifa's evolving view
Note his first opinion: hadd is wajib. Then his retraction: no hadd if compelled by the sultan, but hadd if compelled by a non-sultan.
Zufar agreed with the first opinion.
3
State the Sahibayn's consistent position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad held that no hadd applies in either case (sultan or non-sultan compeller).
This is referred to in the text as the view of 'the two' (Qala).
4
Understand the basis for each view
The Imam's distinction may rest on the different nature of compulsion by a ruler versus a private individual. The Sahibayn likely considered that any compulsion negates the voluntary intent required for hadd.
The text does not elaborate, but the principle of compulsion removing liability is central.

Later authorities (fatawa mutakhkhirin) adopt the Sahibayn's view as the relied position. The practical difference: under the mufta bihi, no hadd in any case; under the Imam's final view, hadd only if compelled by a non-sultan.

Hadd for Coerced Zina: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hadd for coerced zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on hadd for coerced zina, explain the reasoning behind the relied-upon view, and apply the ruling to a scenario.

Core Idea
Hadd is not imposed on a coerced person because coercion negates the consent necessary for criminal liability.

In Hanafi law, the hadd penalty for zina is a fixed right of Allah, but its application requires full legal capacity and free will. This passage examines a classic intra-madhhab dispute: should a person coerced into zina still be subject to hadd? The early view of Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar held that hadd applies except when the ruler is the coercer, but the later relied-upon view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) is that hadd is never imposed under coercion. Understanding this dispute requires analyzing the role of consent and the distinction between different types of coercers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Initial Hanafi Position
Start with the first view attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa and Zufar: hadd is obligatory if the person commits zina under coercion. Note the exception mentioned.
This is the earlier position before retraction.
2
Trace Imam Abu Hanifa's Retraction
Abu Hanifa later distinguished between coercion by the ruler (sultan) and coercion by others. For ruler coercion, no hadd; for others, hadd remains.
The distinction may be based on the ruler's ability to enforce justice or the severity of threat.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad held that hadd is not obligatory in either case of coercion, whether by the ruler or by someone else.
This is the view that became mufta bihi.
4
Analyze the Rationale (Ta'lil)
The Sahibayn's view is based on the principle that coercion removes the element of free choice, which is essential for criminal liability for hadd. Coercion creates a shubha (doubt) that blocks the hadd.
Hanafi usul: hadd is suspended by shubhat.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
In practice, a person coerced into zina incurs no hadd under the relied-upon view, while the earlier view would apply hadd unless the coercer was the ruler.
Also note: the mahr (dower) may be dropped or obligatory depending on the case, as mentioned in the passage.

Iqrar and Capacity: Minors, Insane, and Slaves
Ruling statement on iqrar of minors, insane, and slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the capacity requirements for a valid iqrar in Hanafi fiqh and distinguish the rulings for minors, insane persons, and slaves regarding acknowledgment of property, hadd, and qisas.

Core Idea
Iqrar requires full legal capacity; minors/insane lack it, while a slave's iqrar binds him for hadd/qisas immediately, for property only after freedom.

In Hanafi law, iqrar (acknowledgment) is a powerful tool that can create obligations against oneself. However, its validity hinges on the capacity (ahliyyah) of the declarant. This lesson examines the rules for those with deficient capacity—minors, the insane, and slaves—and explains how their acknowledgments are treated differently depending on the subject matter. We will work through the basic rule, then explore nuanced scenarios from the commentary in Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.

Guided Path
1
Identify the capacity requirement for iqrar
Iqrar is a legal statement that creates liability; it is only effective if the declarant possesses ahliyyat al-ada' (capacity to perform legal acts). Minors and the insane lack this capacity entirely.
Even if a minor's statement entails a hadd penalty, it is not treated as iqrar but as itlaf (destruction) warranting liability.
2
Apply to slaves: property iqrar
A slave's acknowledgment regarding property (e.g., debt) is not immediately binding because what the slave possesses belongs to the master. It becomes binding only after emancipation when the impediment (master's ownership) is removed.
This is because iqrar against another (the master) is not accepted.
3
Apply to slaves: hadd and qisas iqrar
For hadd (fixed penalties) and qisas (retaliation), a slave's iqrar is immediately binding. These pertain to his humanity and are not subject to the master's ownership; the slave is treated as originally free in these matters.
The master's iqrar against the slave is invalid for the same reason.
4
Examine nuanced scenarios from the commentary
The commentary adds scenarios: a slave acknowledging consuming a trust vs. killing, and a person with deficient capacity who later recovers and makes contradictory statements. Note how the ruling differs based on subject matter and timing.
For trust consumption, the acknowledgment after recovery is not binding if the initial consumption was not witnessed; for killing, it binds immediately.

Liability for Defects in Usurped Slave Girl: Istihsan vs Qiyas
Intra-Hanafi liability rules for defects in usurped slave girl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi positions on compensation for defects when a usurped slave girl becomes pregnant from zina and apply the rule of 'lesser included in greater' to calculate liability.

Core Idea
When a usurped slave girl becomes pregnant from zina, the usurper pays only the greater defect — the lesser is absorbed (istihsan) instead of paying both (qiyas).

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores a nuanced scenario: a usurper (ghasib) returns a slave girl who became pregnant from zina while in his possession. The key legal question is how to compensate the owner for the decrease in her value. The child itself is not considered a separate defect (contra Shafi'i). The intra-Hanafi dispute centers on methodology: should the usurper compensate both the defect of zina (permanent character flaw) and the defect of pregnancy (temporary swelling), or only the greater of the two? Abu Yusuf adopts istihsan (juristic preference) to avoid double compensation, while Muhammad follows qiyas (strict analogy) requiring compensation for each harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Liability Regime
We are dealing with civil liability (daman) for defects caused by the usurper. The act is returning the usurped slave girl after she became pregnant from zina.
2
State the Cross-Madhhab Disagreement
Hanafis hold that the child is not guaranteed (ghayr maddmun) as a separate property, unlike al-Shafi'i who treats the child as a guarantee. Understand the basis: offspring follows the mother in Hanafi law.
3
Trace the Intra-Hanafi Positions
Identify the three imams: Abu Hanifa (full value if she dies in childbirth with child survivor), Abu Yusuf (only the greater defect, istihsan), and Muhammad (both defects, qiyas).
Also note that on the method of payment, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on including the lesser defect.
4
Apply the 'Lesser Included in Greater' Principle
In each scenario, calculate the greater of the two defects (zina vs. pregnancy) and the usurper pays only that amount. The lesser is absorbed. When the child's value offsets the decrease, no payment is due.
This principle (al-akthar yadummu al-aqall) is a form of istihsan.
5
Recognize the Role of Istihsan
Understand why Abu Yusuf deviates from qiyas: to avoid double recovery and achieve equity. Istihsan is a distinct Hanafi tool that balances strict analogy with practical justice.

Core Idea
Return of a usurped item requires its original safety; a fatal defect invalidates the return, making the usurper liable for full value.

The key is whether the return was valid despite the pregnancy. Abu Hanifa argues that safety is a condition for valid return; since the defect led to death, the return was void, and full value is owed. The Sahibayn hold the return was valid and only the depreciation for pregnancy is due. Let's dissect the arguments and the underlying principles.

Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
He uses the condition of safety (salama) for valid return. Since the slave-girl was returned with a fatal defect (pregnancy leading to death), the return is invalid, so the usurper remains fully liable.

They analogize to cases of fever and flogging after return, where only the deficiency at return is owed. They also analogize to sale where delivery is valid despite defects.

This distinction is crucial: in sale, the contract transfers ownership; in usurpation, the item must be restored exactly as taken.

Under Abu Hanifa, owner gets full value; under Sahibayn, only depreciation for pregnancy. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view as per the text.
The tarjih is based on the strength of the condition of safety in usurpation.

Since the slave-girl now has a fatal defect (pregnancy leading to death), the return is invalid, so the usurper remains as if he never returned her and is liable for her full value. The free woman is not subject to liability because free persons are not guaranteed as property. The discussion concludes with a principle: fever causes death through loss of strength, which is not directly caused by the usurper's action, so only the deficiency at the time of return is guaranteed.

Ruling on Dregs of Wine and the Prescribed Punishment
Ruling on drinking dregs of wine and the prescribed punishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on drinking dregs of wine, identify when the hadd penalty applies, and explain the school's reasoning as distinguished from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Dregs of wine are prohibited because they contain wine, but the hadd penalty only applies if intoxication occurs.

This lesson examines a nuanced fiqh issue: the status of wine dregs (dirdi al-khamr). The Hanafi school distinguishes between the inherent impurity and prohibition of the substance and the application of the prescribed punishment. You will explore the reasoning behind the ruling and understand why the Shafi'i position differs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Determine the act being discussed: drinking dregs of wine. The matn says it is 'makruh' (disliked), but the commentary clarifies it is actually prohibited (haram) because it contains wine. Note the level of prohibition: makruh tahrimi, which is sin but not as severe as pure wine.
The text uses 'kariha' but explains it is due to containing wine, so it is effectively haram.
2
Understand the Extent of Prohibition
The prohibition extends to any use: combing, medicinal application, feeding to dhimmis, children, or animals. The rationale is that benefiting from an impure prohibited substance is forbidden.
This is consistent with general Hanafi principles on najasah.
3
Analyze the Dispute on Hadd
The Hanafi position: no hadd unless the drinker becomes intoxicated. The Shafi'i position: hadd applies for any amount, even a drop. Locate both proofs in the passage.
The Hanafi proof uses qiyas to other non-wine drinks.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi Dalil
The Hanafi argument is based on the purpose of hadd: deterrence ('zajr') for things the soul desires. Dregs are not desired, so the deterrence need is weaker. Hence, punishment is only for the effect (intoxication), not the substance itself.
This is a talil (rationale) that limits the punishment.
5
Note Ancillary Rulings
The passage also covers: carrying wine to turn into vinegar (permissible only if the goal is to spoil it), dregs in vinegar (permissible), enema with wine (disliked unless medical), and feeding wine to sheep (meat permissible unless smell affected).
These show consistent application of prohibition and impurity.
