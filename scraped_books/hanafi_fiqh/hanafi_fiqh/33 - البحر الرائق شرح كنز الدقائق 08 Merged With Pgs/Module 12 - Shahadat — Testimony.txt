Ruling on Dispute over Wala' as Judgment of Incapacity
Ruling that a dispute over wala' decided in favor of the mother's master constitutes a judgment of incapacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a judgment awarding a child's wala' to the mother's master automatically dissolves the kitabah of the father and identify the Hanafi principle that protects judgments from being nullified.

Core Idea
A judgment of wala' for the mother's master necessarily implies the father's incapacity, dissolving the kitabah.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explores a crucial intersection of patronage and slave contracts. When the death of a makatib triggers a dispute over the wala' of his child, the judge's ruling for the mother's master carries a hidden consequence: it retroactively establishes the makatib's incapacity, voiding the kitabah. Understanding this ruling reveals how Hanafi fiqh preserves the integrity of judicial decisions while navigating competing property rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Recognize that after the makatib's death, his free child's wala' is disputed between the masters of the mother and father. The judgment goes to the mother's master.
The dispute occurs because the father died as a makatib (slave under contract), so his child's wala' could potentially belong to either master.
2
Understand the necessary consequence
Realize that wala' from the father's side is only impossible if the kitabah is dissolved. If the kitabah remained, the father might be freed posthumously and wala' could attach to his master. Therefore, to award wala' to the mother's master, the judge must have effectively ruled the makatib incapable ('ajiz).
The judgment itself becomes a determination of incapacity.
3
Apply the principle of protection of judgment
Note that the consensus is that protecting a judgment from being nullified (ṣiyānat al-qaḍāʾ ʿan al-intiqāḍ) is obligatory. Hence, the judgment's effects are absolute; even if later funds appear, they cannot revive the kitabah because that would contradict the judgment.
This principle gives the judgment finality.
4
Contrast with pre-judgment scenarios
Understand that before a judgment, if wealth appears, it could be used to pay the kitabah and save it. After the judgment, incapacity is established absolutely, so any payment does not count as kitabah but becomes inheritance for the master.
The judgment changes the legal status retroactively.
5
Analyze the al-Muhit scenario
Consider the case where a deposit is found after the makatib's death. The depositor claims it belongs to the makatib and that wala' shifted to the father's side. His claim about ownership is accepted (as it is against himself), but his claim about wala' shifting is rejected because it affects third parties and contradicts the judgment.
This reinforces that the judgment's determination of incapacity is definitive.

Commentary
This passage explains that if there is a dispute between the parent's masters over the patronage (wala') of a child and the judge rules in favor of the mother's master, that judgment automatically implies the incapacity ('ajz) of the makatib (the father). The reason is that patronage from the mother's side can only be established if it is impossible to establish it from the father's side; that impossibility arises only if the kitabah is dissolved (since if the kitabah were intact, the father might eventually be freed and patronage would attach to his side). Therefore, the judgment itself dissolves the kitabah. If later the debt is collected and paid, it becomes the property of the master (since the slave is still a slave) and does not count as a kitabah payment because the judgment has already established 'ajz. The passage then cites al-Muhit for a scenario where a deposit is found after the makatib's death: if the depositor claims the deposit belongs to the makatib and that patronage shifted, his claim about ownership is accepted as it affirms his own liability, but his claim about patronage shifting to the father's side is not accepted because that would affect third parties who do not confirm it.

Key Terms
عجز (ʿajz): Incapacity of the makatib to pay the kitabah; once established by court judgment, the kitabah dissolves and the child's patronage goes to the mother's side.
ولاء (walā'): Patronage right that transfers inheritance and loyalty; here it determines which master inherits from the child.
فسخ (faskh): Dissolution or annulment of the kitabah contract due to the makatib's incapacity.

Priority of Debts by Strength of Witness Testimony
Rulings on prioritizing debt claims based on witness testimony strength

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for prioritizing debt claims based on the strength of legal proof (hujja) as determined by witnesses' religion and the parties involved.

Core Idea
The strength of legal proof determines debt priority: Muslim witnesses prove against both slave and master; non-Muslim witnesses prove only against the slave.

Imagine a non-Muslim slave, authorized to trade, owes debts to multiple creditors. Each creditor presents witnesses of different religious statuses. How does the Hanafi school decide who gets paid first? This lesson unpacks the detailed rules from al-Muhit, showing how the strength of legal proof (hujja) governs priority. You will learn to analyze witness testimony's scope and apply it to various creditor combinations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
Locate the statement: 'Muslim witnesses are a proof (hujja) against both the slave and his Muslim master; non-Muslim witnesses are only a proof against the slave.' This is the key.
The master's religion matters because he is ultimately responsible for the slave's debts.
2
Apply to the basic scenario
Consider two creditors, both Muslim: one with Muslim witnesses, the other with dhimmi witnesses. Determine who gets paid first and why. Trace the reasoning in the text.
3
Extend to the three-creditor case
Analyze the scenario with three creditors: one Muslim with Muslim witnesses, one Muslim with dhimmi witnesses, and one non-Muslim with Muslim witnesses. Identify the priority order.
Notice that when proof strength is equal (both are only against the slave), creditors share proportionally.
4
Include the harbi slave scenario
Examine the case where the slave is a harbi under aman and creditors include a harbi, a dhimmi, and a Muslim. Understand why the dhimmi gets priority over the Muslim in some cases.
The harbi's evidence is not a proof against the Muslim or dhimmi; the dhimmi's evidence is stronger than a Muslim's against certain parties.
5
Synthesize the general rule
Formulate the overarching rule: priority depends on how many parties the evidence is valid against. If one proof is valid against more parties, that debt is paid first. If equal, they share.

The Relative Validity of Testimony: Muslim, Dhimmi, and Harbi Witnesses
Rules for dividing a slave's price when multiple creditors of different religions present testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy of testimony validity to allocate shares in multi-party claims against a trading slave.

Core Idea
Testimony's validity is relative: Muslim binds all, dhimmi binds dhimmis/harbis, harbi binds only harbis.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq explores how the religious category of witnesses and parties determines the probative value of testimony in claims against a trading slave. The key insight is that testimony is not universally binding; its effect depends on the witness's and opponent's status. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correctly resolving mixed-religion disputes in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and testimonies
List the creditors (Muslim, dhimmi, harbi) and their respective witnesses. Note the religious category of each witness and the defendant (the slave, who is harbi).
All scenarios involve a harbi slave because the slave is the asset being claimed.
2
Determine relative validity of each testimony
Apply the hierarchy: Muslim testimony is valid against all; dhimmi testimony is valid against dhimmis and harbis but not Muslims; harbi testimony is valid only against harbis.
If a testimony is not valid against the opponent, it is as if no evidence was presented.
3
Compare the strength of proofs
When both sides have valid testimonies, they are equal in proof and shares are divided equally. If one side's testimony is invalid, the other side takes the entire amount.
Precedence is given to the party whose testimony is valid against the opponent.
4
Calculate the shares
Based on the validity assessment, allocate the slave's price proportionally. Remember that a party can only benefit from testimony that is valid against the opposing party.
The rules ensure that no one profits from testimony that does not bind their adversary.

The Usurper's Statement and Oath: Evidence and Burden of Proof
Procedural ruling with proof and intra-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whose word is accepted regarding the value of a usurped object and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute over the acceptance of the usurper's evidence.

Core Idea
The usurper's word with oath is accepted; his evidence is not, because evidence for negation is not accepted and he is not like a depositary.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies a procedural rule in usurpation (ghasb) disputes: when the owner and usurper disagree on the value of the object, the usurper's statement about the value is taken with his oath, while the owner must provide evidence for a higher claim. However, there is an internal Hanafi debate on whether the usurper can also present evidence to prove a lower value. The commentator analyzes the reasoning and distinguishes the usurper from the depositary (mudi‘), settling on the prevailing view.

Guided Path
1

State the baseline ruling: the usurper's statement about the value is accepted, but he must swear an oath; the owner must produce evidence if he claims a higher value.
The matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq says: 'al-qawl fi al-qimah lil-ghasib ma‘a yaminihi wa al-bayyinah lil-malik.'
2

The usurper is the denier (munkir) of a higher value; the owner is the claimant (mudda‘i). In Hanafi procedure, the denier's statement with oath is accepted, and the claimant must bring evidence. Evidence for negation (nafy) is not accepted.
The text explicitly says: 'li-anna al-ghasib munkir wa al-malik mudda‘in.'

Some later Hanafi scholars (mashāyikh) analogized the usurper to the depositary (mudi‘) and argued that the usurper's evidence should be accepted. Abu Ali al-Nasafi found the issue problematic.

The depositary, when he claims return, produces evidence to drop the oath completely, ending the dispute. The usurper, however, still owes the value; evidence only drops the oath but not the payment. Thus, the analogy fails.
The text in al-Inayah states: 'al-mūdi‘ laysa ‘alayhi illā al-yamīn, wa bi-iqāmat al-bayyinah asqatahā, fa-irtafa‘at al-khuṣūmah. Wa ammā al-ghāṣib fa-‘alayhi al-yamīn wa al-qīmah, wa bi-iqāmat al-bayyinah lam yusqiṭ illā al-yamīn, fa-lā yakūn fī ma‘nā al-mūdi‘.'
5

The relied-upon view is that the usurper's evidence is not accepted, and he must settle with his oath. This is the position affirmed by al-Inayah and is the operative ruling for fatwa.
The commentator favors this view, as indicated by 'wa huwa al-ṣaḥīḥ' in the text.

Conflicting Reports and Preponderance (Tarjih) in Hanafi Fiqh
Rules for conflicting reports and preponderance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for preponderance when conflicting reports are received, considering number, freedom, and masculinity.

Core Idea
When reports conflict, preponderance is given to the side with greater number of upright reporters, or to free persons over slaves, or to males over females.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq details a series of scenarios where a person receives conflicting reports about a matter (e.g., whether meat is halal or from a Magian slaughter, ownership of a slave-girl, or a wife's apostasy). Hanafi scholars have established a system of tarjih (preponderance) based on the number of reporters, their freedom, gender, and reliability. This lesson explores these rules and their application, including the role of tahrri (investigation) when no clear preponderance exists.

Guided Path
1
Examine the principle of tarjih by number
The passage states that if two upright persons inform, truth is preponderated by increase in number. Understand that unlike witnessing, testimony is not about number but about the reporter's credibility.
Example: if two say halal and one says haram, the halal report preponderates.
2
Analyze tarjih by freedom and masculinity
When free persons and slaves or males and females conflict, the side with free/male reporters is preferred. The passage provides examples: two free persons over two slaves, two free persons over four slaves, one man over two women.
Freedom and masculinity are given weight over sheer number in some cases (e.g., two free vs. four slaves: free preponderates).
3
Explore the role of tahrri (investigation)
When one reporter is upright and the other is not, or when there is no clear preponderance, the person must investigate and form an opinion. If no opinion, he may act on any side or avoid.
The passage uses 'يترجح' and 'يتحرى'.
4
Identify the exception: reports involving punishment
The passage alludes to a dispute between al-Jassas and al-Karkhi about whether two witnesses are needed for punishment matters. Note that the month of Ramadan is treated as a matter without punishment.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
5
Apply the rules to the cases of usurped property and apostasy
A single trustworthy report about usurpation does not nullify ownership but should lead to caution (refraining). Similarly, a report of apostasy allows remarriage for the husband.
These demonstrate the practical outcomes of the tarjih rules.

The commentator said: The word of one whose probity is unknown [mustur] is not accepted in religious matters in the apparent narration, and from Abu Hanifa that it is accepted, and the word of a slave and female slave is accepted in religious matters if they are upright due to the preponderance of truth in their report. Agency is from transactions, and permission in trade and everything that does not involve obligation nor indicates dispute is from transactions; if it contains any of that, the report of a single person is not accepted. And from religious matters are permissibility and prohibition when there is no removal of ownership. He said: 'al-tathabbatu, yusma'u al-mu'amalat.' That it is a Magian slaughter and the rest said rather lawful and they are upright, he acts on their saying. And whoever is invited to a wedding feast... al-Sighnaqi: The report of an upright person is not accepted in religious matters if it involves removal of ownership, such that if an upright man or woman informs the two spouses that they were breastfed by a certain woman, it is not accepted; rather witnessing is necessary; end. If you say: Why is the condition of no removal of ownership stipulated in accepting the report of an upright person but not stipulated in accepting the report of a child or slave, such that if a child or slave says 'my master gifted you this slave-girl', his word is accepted though it involves removal of ownership, even though the slave is of lower status than the free upright person? We say: Because the ownership of the individual [?] is of a lower status than the ownership from marriage, evidenced by the stipulation of witnessing in ownership from marriage but not in ownership of the individual; therefore it is stipulated in the report of the free person what is mentioned, not in the report of the child; ponder; end. The result is that reports are of types: First, the report of a messenger in matters involving no punishment, in which only probity is required. Second, his report in matters involving punishment, and it is like the first according to the second [opinion] and it is the choice of al-Jassas, contrary to Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi who stipulates there the presence of two [witnesses] according to him, and the month of Ramadan is from the first category. Third, the rights of servants in matters involving obligation in one aspect but not another, so one of the two conditions of witnessing is stipulated: either number or probity, contrary to the two of them [Abu Hanifa and others?] who accept in it the report of every discerning person. Fourth, signs and we have explained its ruling; end. And in al-Tatarkhaniyyah: The condition is that the reporter be upright and Muslim; al-Hakim al-Shahid mentioned in the abridgement probity and did not mention Islam, and the situation is made clear by what was mentioned, and that mentioning Islam is incidental and not a condition; end. The author said: (And if a trustworthy Muslim informs, free or slave, male or female, that it is a Magian slaughter and the rest said rather lawful and they are upright, he acts on their saying.) Likewise, if two upright persons inform, truth is preponderated by increase in number of reporters, unlike witnessing. If they are accused, he acts on the word of the one, because it is not permissible to nullify the report of an upright person by their report. If among them there is one upright, he investigates, as if two upright persons inform him, one of permissibility and the other of prohibition, he must preponderate one by investigation; if he has no opinion and they are equal in his view, then there is no harm in his eating, unlike if one reported a narration of prohibition and one a narration of permissibility, the prohibition is preponderated over permissibility by considering prohibition as abrogating. If two inform him of permissibility and one of prohibition, there is no harm in his eating. If two free persons inform him of prohibition and two slaves of permissibility, the report of the two free persons is preponderated regarding prohibition. If two free upright persons inform him of permissibility and four slaves of prohibition, or a man of permissibility and two women of prohibition, preponderance is given [to the side with] masculinity and freedom. Whoever buys a slave-girl and a trustworthy Muslim informs him that she is originally free or his foster-sister, he may have intercourse with her, though it is good to refrain, because the testimony of a single person does not nullify ownership nor establish the prohibition of fosterage. If he owns food or a slave-girl through a cause, and a trustworthy Muslim testifies that the grantor had usurped it from so-and-so, he should refrain from eating her and having intercourse with her. If an upright person informs him that it is a Magian slaughter and the butcher informs him that it is a Muslim slaughter, and the butcher is upright, he should refrain from that; if he does it, there is nothing upon him. If he knows a slave-girl belongs to Zayd and sees her in the hand of someone else, it is not permissible for him to buy her unless he knows that she is the property of the one in whose hand she is, or that he is permitted to sell her. A man married a woman and did not consummate with her, then he was absent from her, and a trustworthy person, free or slave or one who had been punished for slander, informed him that she apostatized from Islam; it is permissible for him to marry four others besides her, if he is older.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the types of news and the conditions for their acceptance in Hanafi fiqh. The commentator begins by discussing the 'mustur' (person of unknown probity) and reports from slaves and female slaves in religious matters (diyanat). The apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) rejects the mustur's report, but Abu Hanifa reportedly accepts it. Then the text classifies reports into four categories: (1) news from a messenger in matters without punishment (only probity required); (2) news with punishment (like the first according to al-Jassas, but al-Karkhi requires two witnesses); (3) rights of servants (huquq al-'ibad) with partial obligation, requiring either number or probity; (4) signs (al-'alamat). The passage then addresses the issue of conflicting reports, e.g., when two trustworthy persons give contradictory information about food being Magian or halal; one must investigate and preponderate based on number, freedom, masculinity, etc. Specific examples include: a purchased slave-girl reported to be free or foster-sister (intercourse allowed, but refraining is better); a usurped property; and a case of a wife's apostasy allowing the husband to remarry if he receives a report from a trustworthy person. Throughout, the Hanafi school emphasizes the role of tahrri and the varying standards for reports depending on the nature of the matter.

Key Terms
المستور (al-mustur): A person whose probity ('adala) is unknown, i.e., neither known to be upright nor fasiq.
خبر العدل (khabr al-'adl): Report of a just, upright person ('adl). In Hanafi fiqh, accepted in many matters but conditions vary.
زوال الملك (zawal al-mulk): Removal of ownership, e.g., from marriage or slavery.
العلامات (al-'alamat): Signs or indicators that lead to knowledge, such as a woman's menstruation indicating non-pregnancy.
