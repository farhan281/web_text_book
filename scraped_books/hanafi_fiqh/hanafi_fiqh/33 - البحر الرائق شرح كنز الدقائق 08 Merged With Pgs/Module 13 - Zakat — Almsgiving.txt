Charity Paid by Makatib Becomes Lawful for Master if He Defaults
Ruling with proof on charity of a makatib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that charity given by a makatib becomes lawful for his master upon default, and identify the proofs, justifications, and condition for this ruling.

Core Idea
When a makatib defaults, the master's prior ownership overrides, making the charity lawful for him.

In the Hanafi school, a makatib (a slave under a contract of manumission) has the capacity to own earnings and give charity. However, if he fails to complete the kitabah, his ownership ceases and the master's right re-emerges. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning, the multiple justifications provided by the commentators, and the evidence from the Hadith of Barirah.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its scope
Identify the exact ruling: the charity (sadaqah) paid by the makatib becomes lawful (tāba) for his master if the makatib later becomes incapable (ajz) of fulfilling the kitabah. Note that this applies when no prior judgment of ajz has been made.
The ruling is specific to charity given by the makatib from his earnings, not other forms of property.
2
Analyze the justifications (ta'lil)
The text gives three explanations: (a) the master had weak ownership over the charity because the makatib's hand acts like the master's; (b) the master has the right to revoke the makatib's gift; (c) ownership reverts to the master upon default. Understand each and note how they harmonize.
These explanations are not contradictory; they highlight different angles of the same principle.
3
Address the objection
Consider the objection: if the master already owns the makatib's person (milk al-raqaba), how can the makatib own anything? The answer is that milk al-raqaba is subdued (maghlūb) compared to the makatib's ownership of his hand (milk al-yadd). Upon default, the hand-ownership vanishes and the person-ownership resurfaces.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh regarding kitabah.
4
Examine the dalil
The Hadith of Barirah is cited: the Prophet (peace be upon him) said about Barirah, a makatibah, that the charity given to her was charity for her (i.e., lawful for her) but a gift to the Companions was lawful for them. Hanafis analogize that the charity becomes the makatib's property, but because of the master's overriding right, it reverts upon default.
The dalil is an ahad hadith but its implication is clear (qati al-dalala) for the ruling.
5
Note the condition
The ruling applies only if there has been no prior judgment of the makatib's incapability. If a qadi has already ruled that he is incapable, the makatib is under interdiction (hajr) and his transactions are invalid. In that case, the charity does not become lawful for the master.
This condition shows the importance of judicial determination in defining the makatib's status.

Commentary
This ruling states that if a makatib gives charity (sadaqah) from his earnings and later becomes unable to complete the kitabah (defaults), the charity becomes lawful (tāba) for his master. The commentator gives several explanations: (1) The master had weak ownership over the charity because the makatib's hand acts like the master's. When the makatib defaults, his ownership disappears, leaving only the master's ownership. (2) The master had the right to revoke the makatib's gift (hiba), and if he does so, it becomes his lawful property. (3) More comprehensively: the makatib may give charity from his kitabah wealth because he owns his earnings, but the master owns his person. Charity is a transfer: when paid, ownership goes to the recipient, but if default occurs, the ownership reverts to the master. A question arises: How can ownership change when the master already owned the slave's person? The answer: The master's ownership of the person is subdued (maghlūb) compared to the makatib's ownership of his hand (i.e., his earnings). When default occurs, the hand-ownership vanishes and the person-ownership resurfaces. The Hadith of Barirah is cited: the Prophet said about Barirah, a makatibah, that the charity given to her is charity for her (so it is lawful for her to take) but a gift for us (when it was given to him as a gift). This Hadith analogically supports that once charity is given to the makatib, it becomes his property, and if he defaults, the master can take it back.

Key Terms
صدقة (ṣadaqa): Charity given voluntarily; in this context, it is the charity paid by the makatib from his earnings.
طاب (ṭāba): Becomes lawful (ṭāba) for the master; i.e., he may take it without religious or legal prohibition.
ملك الرقبة (milk al-raqaba): Ownership of the physical person (the slave's body); the master's primary right over the makatib.

Sadaqat Paid by Mukatab Becomes Permissible to Master Upon Incapacity
Explains that sadaqat given by a mukatab become lawful for the master if the mukatab becomes incapacitated

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that sadaqat given to a mukatab become permissible for his master upon his incapacity, cite the hadith of Barīrah as proof, and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement between Muhammad and Abu Yusuf on the timing of permissibility.

Core Idea
Sadaqat given to a mukatab becomes lawful for his master upon incapacity because ownership changes, akin to the Prophet's statement about Barīrah.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Rāʾiq addresses a specific scenario: a mukatab (slave contracted for manumission) receives sadaqat (charity) and then becomes unable to pay the kitaba. What happens to that charity? The author clarifies that it becomes lawful for the master, using a key principle of ownership transfer and citing the Prophet's statement about Barīrah. We will examine the proof, the analogies, and an internal Hanafi disagreement on the matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
State the ruling: sadaqat given to a mukatab become permissible (ṭāb) for his master if the mukatab becomes incapacitated (عجز). Note that this is a conditional ruling triggered by the mukatab's inability to pay the kitaba.
The term 'ṭāb' means legally pure and permissible.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The text cites the hadith of Barīrah: when she, a mukātibah, received sadaqah and then gifted it to the Prophet ﷺ, he said: 'It is sadaqah for her, and a gift for us.' This shows that a change in ownership (from sadaqah to gift) alters the ruling.
The hadith is used to illustrate the principle that change of ownership can remove the prohibition of sadaqah for those who cannot take it initially.
3
Follow the analogies presented
The text draws analogies: a poor person who dies leaving sadaqah makes it permissible for a wealthy heir; a poor person who becomes wealthy may keep sadaqah taken before wealth; a wayfarer who reaches his destination may keep sadaqah. In each case, the prohibition was on the initial taking, not on continued possession after a change of status.
The underlying principle is that the bar is on the act of taking while in a state of need, not on subsequent ownership.
4
Analyze the objection and response
The objection: the master always owned the slave's person (raqaba), so how can ownership change? The response: the master's ownership of the person is lesser (مغلوب) compared to the mukatab's ownership of his earnings (yad). Upon incapacity, the master's full ownership reasserts, causing a real change akin to changing the substance.
This is a subtle legal argument about dual ownership (milk al-raqaba vs milk al-yad).
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
If the mukatab becomes incapacitated before paying the sadaqat to the master, Muhammad holds it becomes permissible because the master acquires new ownership (ملك مبتدأ). Abu Yusuf holds it does not because no new ownership is created—only a pre-existing ownership is strengthened. The author states the correct view is permissibility by consensus.
This difference turns on whether the acquisition is considered a new ownership or an intensification of an existing one.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the permissibility of sadaqat (charity) given to a mukatab. If a mukatab receives sadaqat (which is lawful for him as a slave) and then becomes incapacitated (unable to pay the kitaba), that sadaqat becomes permissible (lawful) for his master. The reason is that the ownership of the property changes: originally the mukatab owned it (though subject to the master's ownership in the person), but upon incapacity, the master's full ownership reasserts, causing a change akin to a change in substance. The text cites the hadith of Barīrah, a mukātibah, to whom sadaqah was given, and then she gifted it to the Prophet ﷺ, who said it is sadaqah for her but a gift for us. This illustrates that change of ownership can remove the prohibition. The commentary then addresses objections and analogies. It also notes an intra-Hanafi difference: Muhammad holds that it becomes permissible because the master acquires new ownership, while Abu Yusuf holds it does not, as no new ownership is created. The correct view is that it is permissible by consensus, because the prohibition was on the initial act of taking, which the master did not do.

صدقات (ṣadaqāt): Voluntary charity; in this context, it includes zakāt and other charitable gifts.
طاب (ṭāba): Becomes lawful (ḥalāl) and free from legal impurity (khubth).

Zakat and Nafaqa for the Interdicted Person: The Crucial Role of Intention
Rulings on zakat and nafaqa for an interdicted person, focusing on the role of intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why zakat requires the safih's own intention while nafaqa can be handled by a trustee, and apply this ruling to a scenario involving an interdicted person.

Core Idea
Zakat is worship requiring intention; nafaqa is a financial right that does not — the safih must give zakat personally but a trustee may manage nafaqa.

This lesson examines two crucial obligations that remain upon a person even after interdiction: zakat and nafaqa. The Hanafi school identifies a key distinction between them: zakat is an act of worship (ibadah) that requires the giver's intention (niyyah), while nafaqa is a financial right that does not. Consequently, the judge must ensure the safih himself performs the zakat distribution, whereas a trustee can handle maintenance payments. This ruling highlights how the Shari'ah balances the protection of the interdicted person's wealth with fulfillment of essential duties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Zakat and nafaqa are both wajib (obligatory) on the safih despite interdiction. The text states that foolishness (safah) does not nullify rights of Allah or people.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the principle that zakat is an ibadah requiring niyyah, as expressed: 'الواجب عليه الإيتاء وهو عبارة عن فعل يفعله وهو عبادة ولا يحصل ذلك إلا بنيته'. Nafaqa, by contrast, does not need intention.
The text does not give a separate dalil for nafaqa; it implies that intention is not required because it is a financial right.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'ita'' (giving) is defined as an act performed by the person himself, and it is worship (ibadah). Worship requires intention by scholarly consensus. Therefore, the safih must personally perform the giving.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is no intra-Hanafi dispute on this point; the ruling is consistent. However, the text earlier mentions a difference on waqf by a safih (Abu Bakr al-Balkhi vs. Abu al-Qasim), which is not the focus here.
5
Evaluate the thamarah
The practical consequence: the judge gives the zakat amount to the safih to distribute, but may appoint a trustee for nafaqa to prevent misdirection.
The judge also appoints a trustee for zakat if he fears misappropriation, but the safih must still perform the actual distribution.

Mentioning Others Alongside Allah during Slaughter
Expansion on the ruling of mentioning others alongside Allah during slaughter.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mentioning another name with Allah's name during slaughter is only haram if it implies partnership; otherwise it is disliked or permitted.

The sacred act of slaughter requires the sole dedication of the animal to Allah. Mentioning any other being alongside Allah's name can corrupt this dedication. The Valid Slaughter Zone: Between the Throat and the Collarbone
Ruling on the correct location for slaughter: between the throat (halq) and the base of the neck (labbah).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the required anatomical boundaries for valid Hanafi slaughter and explain the evidence from hadith and rational reasoning.

Core Idea
Slaughter is valid only between the larynx and the chest hollow; cutting elsewhere turns the animal into carrion.

This lesson examines the first part of the passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, which sets the precise limits for dhabh. The matn declares that slaughter must be between the halqah (larynx) and labbah (collarbone hollow). The commentator explains the evidence—the hadith 'al-dhakaat fi al-halq' and the rational reasoning that the throat is where vital passages converge—and deduces the ruling that slaughter above or below is invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is dhabh (slaughter). The ruling is that it must be performed between the halq (larynx) and labbah (base of neck at collarbone). If done outside this area, the meat is haram (carrion).
2
Locate the evidence (dalil)
Two proofs are offered: 1) The hadith 'al-dhakaat fi al-halq' (the slaughter is in the throat). 2) Rational reason: the throat is where the windpipe, esophagus, and blood vessels meet, so cutting there ensures swift bleeding.
The hadith is ahad but accepted in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'al-halq' in the hadith is interpreted broadly as the throat area, not just the larynx. The rational proof reinforces that the entire zone from larynx to collarbone base is effective for bleeding.
Hanafis extend the meaning to the whole throat region.
4
Note the practical consequence
If one slaughters above the larynx (towards the head) or below the labbah (into the chest), the animal is not considered properly slaughtered; it becomes carrion.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf from the boundaries.

He said: This is the speech of common people and it is not considered. It is permissible to eat it, whether the knot is on the side of the chest or on the side of the head. He said: Because what is considered with us is the cutting of the jugular veins (awdaj).
Source pages p. 309

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the correct anatomical area for slaughter in the Hanafi school. The matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq states: 'And the slaughter is between the halq (throat) and labbah (the hollow of the collarbone).' The commentator explains that 'halq' here refers to the larynx and 'labbah' is the base of the neck at the top of the chest. He clarifies from al-Jami‘ al-Saghir that any part of the throat is acceptable – top, middle, or bottom. The evidence is the hadith 'Al-dhakaat fi al-halq' (Slaughter is in the throat) and the rational reason that the throat is where the windpipe, esophagus, and blood vessels converge, so cutting there ensures rapid bleeding and death with minimal suffering. If one slaughters above the larynx (towards the head) or below the labbah (into the chest), the slaughter is invalid because it is not in the designated place. However, the commentator then quotes Imam al-Rustughfani in Fatawa al-Samarqandi that if a knot of the larynx remains (i.e., the cut is not completely through the larynx), as long as the jugular veins (awdaj) are severed, the meat is halal. The common people's notion that the knot must be towards the head is not considered; what matters is cutting the main blood vessels. Thus the Hanafi school focuses on severing the awdaj (four jugular veins) for valid slaughter, and the precise location within the throat is flexible as long as the cut is between the designated boundaries.

Rulings on Men's Rings: Permissible Materials and Placement
Explaining the exceptions for silver rings, belt fittings, and sword ornaments, and the prohibition of gold, iron, and brass rings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for men's rings made of silver, gold, iron, brass, and stone, and identify the conditions for permissibility of silver and the exception for gold nails.

Core Idea
Gold, iron, and brass rings are haram for men; silver is permissible but discouraged without need.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the detailed rulings on what materials men may use for rings. While the general rule is that men cannot adorn themselves with gold or silver, the Prophet's sunnah creates exceptions for silver rings, belt fittings, and sword ornaments. We will examine the proofs and the conditions that govern these rulings, including the intra-Hanafi dispute over stone rings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wearing a ring (takhattum). The general ruling is that men may not adorn themselves with gold or silver, but exceptions are made for a silver ring, silver belt (minṭaqa), and silver sword ornament. Identify the specific materials that are haram (gold, iron, brass, stone) and the conditions for silver.
The passage also discusses placement and other details.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proofs are hadiths: the Prophet's own silver ring (indicating permissibility), and his statements about a yellow ring ('I find from you the smell of idols') and an iron ring ('adornment of the people of Hell'). Note the chain and authenticity.
These are ahad hadiths, so thubut is zanni, but dalala is qati due to explicit prohibition.
3
Trace wajh al-dalalah
From the Prophet's action, we derive that silver is permissible. The explicit phrases 'smell of idols' and 'adornment of Hellfire' indicate prohibition (tahrim) for gold and iron. Brass is included by analogy (qiyas) to iron or by specific mention in other narrations.
The 'illa for gold is its association with idolatry, for iron its association with Hell.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is a dispute within the Hanafi school regarding stone rings. Some scholars allow them, but the majority prohibit them except for a stone called yashb (which is not considered a true stone). Identify the basis of each view.
The text also mentions a dispute about the permissibility of gold nails in the stone; the relied-upon view allows it if under one mithqal.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The mufta bihi (operative position) is: gold, iron, brass, and stone (except yashb) rings are haram; silver rings are mubah but makruh tanzihi without a legitimate need (e.g., for a judge or ruler). The prohibition of stone rings is based on imitation of non-Muslims and lack of prophetic precedent.
The text also mentions the sunnah of leaving the ring altogether if not needed.

قال رحمه الله : (ولا يتحلى الرجل بالذهب والفضة إلا بالخاتم والمنطقة وحلية السيف من الفضة) لما روينا غير أن الخاتم وما ذكر مستثنى تحقيقاً لمعنى النموذج والفضة لأنهما من جنس واحد وكان للنبي صلى الله عليه وسلم خاتم من فضة وكان في يده إلى أن توفي ثم في يد أبي بكر إلى أن توفي ثم في يد عمر إلى أن توفي ثم في يد عثمان إلى أن وقع في البئر فأنفق مالاً عظيماً في طلبه فلم يجده، ووقع الخلاف بين الصحابة والتشويش من ذلك الوقت إلى أن استشهد. والسنة في ترك التختم وحرم التختم بالحجر والحديد والصفر والذهب وحل مسمار الذهب يجعل في حجر الفص وشد السن بالفضة وكره إلباس ذهب وحرير صبياً كالخرقة لوضوء أو مخاط والرتم . . حق الرجل أن يجعل فص الخاتم في باطن كفه وفي حق المرأة أن تجعله في ظاهر كفها لأنها تزین به دون الرجل ولا بأس بالتختم بالفضة إذا كان له حاجة إليه كالقاضي والسلطان ولغير ذلك مكروه لما روي أنه عليه الصلاة والسلام رأى في يد رجل خاتماً أصفر فقال : مالي أجد منك رائحة الأصنام. ورأى في يد آخر خاتم حديد فقال : ما لي أرى عليك حلية أهل النار. وروي عن ابن عمر أن رجلاً جلس إلى النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم وعليه خاتم ذهب فأعرض عنه . والتختم بالذهب حرام ومن الناس من أطلق التختم بحجر يقال له يشب لأنه ليس بحجر إذ ليس له ثقل الحجر والحلقة هي معتبرة لأن قوام الخاتم بها ولا يعتبر بالفص لأنه يجوز من الحجر والأولى أن لا يتختم إذا كان لا يحتاج إليه ولا بأس بمسمار الذهب يجعل في حجر الفص يعني في ثقبه لأنه تابع كالعلم فلا يعد لابساً ولا يزيد وزنه على مثقال لقوله عليه الصلاة والسلام اتخذه من ورق ولا تزده على مثقال ورد النص بجواز التختم بالعقيق وقال عليه الصلاة والسلام تختموا بالعقيق فإنه مبارك الحديث. وفي الحاوي : ولا بأس أن يتخذ الرجل خاتم فضة، فإن جعل فصه من عقيق أو ياقوت أو فيروزج أو زمرد فلا بأس به، وإن نقش عليه اسمه أو اسم أبيه أو اسماً من أسماء الله فلا بأس به، ولا ينبغي أن ينقش عليه تماثيل من طير أو هوام الأرض ولا بأس بأن يشرب من كفه وفي خنصره خاتم ذهب، ولا بأس بمسمار الذهب يجعل في الفضة وفي الينابيع : كان يتختم باليمين وأبو بكر وعمر بالشمال وفي الفتاوى : وينبغي أن يلبس الخاتم في خنصره اليسرى دون سائر أصابعه ولا ينبغي أن يخضب يد الصغير أو رجله.
He (the author) said: (A man should not adorn himself with gold and silver except with the ring, the belt, and the sword ornament of silver). Based on what we narrated, except that the ring and what is mentioned are exempted to realize the meaning of the model and silver because they are of one type. The Prophet had a silver ring on his hand until he died, then in Abu Bakr's hand until he died, then in Umar's hand until he died, then in Uthman's hand until it fell into a well; he spent great wealth searching for it but did not find it, and disagreement occurred among the companions, and confusion from that time until he was martyred. The sunnah is to leave wearing a ring. And wearing a ring of stone, iron, brass, and gold is prohibited. It is permissible to put a gold nail in the stone of the bezel. And fastening a tooth with silver is allowed. And it is disliked to dress a child in gold and silk. Like the cloth for ablution or mucus and the handkerchief. It is the right of a man to place the bezel of the ring on the inside of his palm, and for a woman to place it on the outer part because she adorns with it, unlike a man. There is no harm in wearing a silver ring if there is a need for it, like a judge or ruler; otherwise it is disliked. Based on the narration that the Prophet saw on a man's hand a yellow ring and said: 'I find from you the smell of idols.' And he saw on another's hand an iron ring and said: 'I see on you the adornment of the people of Hell.' And it is narrated from Ibn Umar that a man sat with the Prophet wearing a gold ring, so he turned away from him. Wearing a gold ring is prohibited. Among people, some allowed wearing a ring of a stone called yashb because it is not a stone, as it has no weight of stone. The ring itself (the band) is considered because the strength of the ring is by it, and the bezel is not considered because it is permissible from stone. It is better not to wear a ring if there is no need. There is no harm in putting a gold nail in the stone of the bezel, i.e., in its hole, because it is a subordinate, like a mark; so it is not considered as wearing it. And its weight should not exceed a mithqal due to his saying: 'Take it of silver and do not increase it beyond a mithqal.' The text permitting wearing an agate ring has come, and he said: 'Wear agate rings, for it is blessed.' In al-Hawi: There is no harm for a man to take a silver ring; if he makes its bezel of agate, ruby, turquoise, or emerald, there is no harm. If he engraves on it his name or his father's name or a name from Allah's names, there is no harm. He should not engrave on it images of birds or crawling creatures. There is no harm for him to drink from his hand while having a gold ring on his little finger. There is no harm in putting a gold nail in silver. In al-Yanabi': He used to wear the ring on the right hand, and Abu Bakr and Umar wore it on the left. In al-Fatawa: He should wear the ring on his left little finger, not on other fingers. He should not dye the hand or foot of a child.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the commentator on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It addresses the general prohibition of men adorning themselves with gold and silver, and then states the exceptions: a silver ring, a silver belt (or buckle), and a silver ornament on the sword. The proof is the established sunnah of the Prophet (peace be upon him) who wore a silver ring, and the practice of the Rightly-Guided Caliphs. The commentary then details the rulings on rings: the prohibition of gold, iron, and brass rings; the permissibility of a silver ring; and the recommendation for non-rulers to avoid wearing a ring altogether. For those who need a ring (like a judge or ruler), it is permissible. The placement of the ring is on the left little finger for men, with the bezel facing the palm, while women wear it on the outer part of the hand. The passage also discusses the permissibility of using a gold nail to fix the stone, provided the weight does not exceed one mithqal, as it is a subordinate part. The hadith about the Prophet turning away from a man wearing a gold ring and the hadith about iron rings being the adornment of Hellfire are cited as proofs. The commentary also mentions a difference of opinion regarding a stone called yashb, with some scholars allowing it because it is not a true stone. Engraving names or images is discussed: permissible to engrave names, but not images of living creatures. The overall ruling is that for men, gold rings are haram; silver rings are mubah but makruh tanzihi without need; and iron/brass rings are also haram. The relied-upon position is the prohibition of gold, iron, brass, and stone rings (except yashb).

Key Terms
التختم (al-takhattum): The act of wearing a ring (on the finger).
الخاتم (al-khatam): The ring itself, consisting of the band (halqa) and possible stone (fass).
الفص (al-fass): The stone or gem set in the ring.
المثقال (al-mithqal): A unit of weight approximately 4.25 grams; the maximum weight for a gold part of the ring (nail).

Key Takeaway
Men's rings are haram if made of gold, iron, brass, or stone (except yashb); silver rings are permissible but discouraged without a legitimate need, and a gold nail in the stone is allowed if under one mithqal.
