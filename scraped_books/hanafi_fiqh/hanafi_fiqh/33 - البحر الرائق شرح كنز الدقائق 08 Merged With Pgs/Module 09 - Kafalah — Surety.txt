Prohibited Gratuitous Acts for a Mukatab
Listing and explaining prohibited gratuitous acts for a mukatab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on a mukatab's marriage, gifts, suretyship, and loans, and identify the exception for trivial gifts.

Core Idea
A mukatab cannot perform gratuitous acts without permission; only trivial gifts are allowed.

This lesson examines the restrictions placed on a mukatab (a slave in a contract of manumission) regarding certain financial and personal acts. The Hanafi school prohibits him from marrying without the master's permission, giving or gifting more than a negligible amount, and entering into suretyship or loans. These are all considered tabarru' (gratuitous) and conflict with his primary purpose of earning to fulfill his kitabah. Small charitable gifts, however, are recognized as necessities for social interaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited acts
List the four acts discussed: marriage without permission, gifts/charity beyond a little, suretyship (kafalah), and loans (iqrad).
All are classified as tabarru'.
2
Understand the rationale
These acts are prohibited because they are gratuitous and do not directly help the mukatab earn money for his freedom. Marriage encumbers him with dower and maintenance; gifts, suretyship, and loans deplete his assets.
The master's permission for marriage is allowed because it does not contradict the purpose of earning.
3
Recognize the exception
Small gifts and charity (e.g., a fals, a loaf, less than a dirham) are permitted as necessities of trade, such as hospitality or small gifts to maintain social ties.
Al-Dhakhirah specifies the amount.
4
Identify consequences
If a mukatab violates these rules, he may be held liable after emancipation, similar to a full slave.
Suretyship is invalid even with permission.

Commentary
This passage lists actions a mukatab (slave contracted for freedom) cannot perform without the master's permission. These include marriage (due to financial obligations), gifts and charity beyond a negligible amount, suretyship (kafalah), and loans (iqrad). The reasoning is that these are gratuitous transfers (tabarru') that do not directly aid the mukatab in earning money for his kitabah. The Hanafi school allows a small amount of charity and gift (e.g., a loaf of bread) as necessary for social interaction. The commentary cites al-Dhakhirah for specific amounts. Regarding marriage, the mukatab may marry only with the master's permission, and he cannot have intercourse with his own slave-woman. Suretyship and loans are completely prohibited, even with the master's permission, as they are pure gratuitous acts. The passage also discusses the consequences if the mukatab violates these rules, such as being held liable after emancipation.

Key Terms

كفالة (kafalah): Suretyship; a contract guaranteeing another's debt or presence.
يسير (yasir): A small amount, specified as less than a dirham or a loaf.

The Owner's Right of Choice When the Usurped Object Appears with Increased Value
Rulings on khiyar when object appears after compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the owner's option (khiyar) when the usurped object appears after compensation, and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the matn versus Zahir al-Riwayah.

Core Idea
The owner has no choice if he consented to the compensation, but choice exists when consent was incomplete—and the preferred view gives him choice in all cases.

In this passage, the commentator explains the rulings when a usurped object is found after the usurper has already paid compensation. The key question is whether the owner can reclaim the object and return the compensation, or must accept the payment. The answer depends on the basis of liability and the owner's consent. We will examine the scenarios and the intra-Hanafi dispute on this issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of liability
Determine how the usurper became liable: by the owner's own statement, by evidence, by the usurper's refusal to swear (nukul), or by the usurper's oath.
The basis affects whether consent is deemed complete.
2
Apply the consent rule
If liability was established by owner's statement/evidence/nukul, the owner's consent is complete—no choice. If by usurper's oath, consent is incomplete—owner has choice.
Consent is key: رضي به (he consented) vs. عدم تمام رضاه (lack of complete consent).
3
Analyze the ikhtilaf on equal/less value
The matn says no choice when value is equal or less, but Zahir al-Riwayah gives choice, and this is الأصح (most correct). Understand why: the owner did not get what he claimed, so consent is absent.
The commentator sides with the stronger transmission.
4
State the mufta bihi
The relied-upon view in fatwa is that the owner has the choice (khiyar) in all cases where the object appears after compensation, even if value is higher, according to the more correct view.
This is based on the principle of فوات الرضا (loss of consent).

Commentary
This passage covers multiple rulings related to the situation where a usurped object is found after the usurper has been made to pay compensation, and the object's value has increased. The author (commentator) first presents the ruling from the matn: if the object appears with higher value and the usurper was made liable by the owner's own statement, by evidence, or by his refusal to swear (nukul), then the object belongs to the usurper and the owner has no choice (لا خيار). The rationale is that the owner consented by his claim. However, if the liability was established by the usurper's oath (i.e., the usurper swore to a lower value and paid), then the owner has the choice to either accept the compensation or take the object and return the compensation, because his consent was not complete—he only accepted due to lack of proof. Regarding the amount of increase, the text notes that even a small increase (a daniq) triggers these rulings. Then the passage discusses the case where the object's value is equal to or less than the compensation paid; in that case, according to the matn, the owner has no choice, but according to Zahir al-Riwayah (the authentic transmission), he does have choice, and this is the more correct view. This is because the owner did not get what he claimed, so consent is absent. The passage then gives the usurper the right to detain the object until he recovers the value paid, as it is an exchange. Finally, a dispute scenario: if they disagree about the object, its description, or its value at the time of usurpation, the word is the usurper's (with oath). A specific case: a garment, a guarantor (kafil) for the value; the guarantor says ten, usurper says twenty, owner says thirty. The word is the guarantor's, and the usurper is held to his own admission—he must pay an extra ten (to the owner?) but not the guarantor. The passage ends mid-sentence about return.

Key Terms
الخيار (al-khiyār): The right of the owner to choose between taking the object or accepting compensation.
النكول (al-nukūl): The refusal of the defendant (the usurper) to swear an oath, which results in the claimant's claim being accepted.

Dispute Resolution and Guarantor Liability in Usurpation Cases
Dispute resolution rules and guarantor liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the word is given to the denier in disputes about the object of usurpation, and identify the liabilities of the guarantor and the usurper when they disagree on the value.

Core Idea
In disputes about the usurped object, the word is the usurper's; when a guarantor is involved, his word prevails over both the usurper and the owner.

This passage from Al-Muhit addresses procedural disputes that arise after a usurpation: who is believed when they disagree about the object or its value? And how does a guarantor's liability work when multiple parties claim different amounts? These rules are essential for settling cases in court and understanding the interplay between liability and testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputants and their claims
In the general dispute, the owner claims an increase in value or liability; the usurper denies. In the guarantor scenario, three parties: owner, usurper, and guarantor, each with different value claims.
Principle: البينة على المدعي واليمين على من أنكر (but here it's about the word of the denier with oath).
2
Apply the rule 'القول للغاصب'
If they disagree about the object, its description, or its value at the time of usurpation, the word is the usurper's (with his oath) because he is the denier.
This is based on the presumption of innocence: the owner is claiming an additional liability.
3
Analyze the guarantor scenario
Guarantor says 10, usurper says 20, owner says 30. The word is the guarantor's because he is the denier against the owner's claim. The usurper's confession (20) binds him, but not the guarantor.
The usurper's confession of 20 is valid against himself but not against others.
4
Determine the consequences
The guarantor pays 10 (his admission). The usurper pays an additional? The text says he is liable for another ten (likely to the owner) but not the guarantor. The scenario is incomplete but the principle is clear.
The confession of one party does not affect others.

The author (commentator) first presents the ruling from the matn: if the object appears with higher value and the usurper was made liable by the owner's own statement, by evidence, or by his refusal to swear (nukul), then the object belongs to the usurper and the owner has no choice (لا خيار). The rationale is that the owner consented by his claim. However, if the liability was established by the usurper's oath (i.e., the usurper swore to a lower value and paid), then the owner has the choice to either accept the compensation or take the object and return the compensation, because his consent was not complete—he only accepted due to lack of proof. Regarding the amount of increase, the text notes that even a small increase (a daniq) triggers these rulings. Then the passage discusses the case where the object's value is equal to or less than the compensation paid; in that case, according to the matn, the owner has no choice, but according to Zahir al-Riwayah (the authentic transmission), he does have choice, and this is the more correct view. This is because the owner did not get what he claimed, so consent is absent. The passage then gives the usurper the right to detain the object until he recovers the value paid, as it is an exchange. Finally, a dispute scenario: if they disagree about the object, its description, or its value at the time of usurpation, the word is the usurper's (with oath). A specific case: a garment, a guarantor (kafil) for the value; the guarantor says ten, usurper says twenty, owner says thirty. The word is the guarantor's, and the usurper is held to his own admission—he must pay an extra ten (to the owner?) but not the guarantor. The passage ends mid-sentence about return.

الكفيل (al-kafīl): A guarantor who undertakes to pay the value of the usurped object.

Liability Drops When Pledge Lent with Permission
Matn and commentary on lending a pledge to a non-party with both owners' consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that the mortgagee's liability for a pledge is removed when both the mortgagor and mortgagee consent to lending it to a third party, and identify the condition that triggers this removal.

Core Idea
Mutual permission to lend a pledge lifts the mortgagee's liability.

This short but decisive ruling clarifies a common situation in pledge (rahn) contracts: what happens if both the pledgor and pledgee agree to let a third party borrow the pledged item? The answer simplifies liability—when both consent, the mortgagee's responsibility ends. Let's see why and how this ruling applies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the transaction
Recognize that there are two owners: the mortgagor (pledgor) and the mortgagee (pledgee). They lend the pledge to an ajnabi (third party) with both of their permissions.
Ajnabi means someone not party to the original pledge contract.
2
State the ruling from the matn
The matn says: 'If one of them lends it to a third party with the permission of the other, the liability falls away.' This means the mortgagee is no longer liable for loss or damage.
The ruling is explicit and unanimous among the Hanafis.
3
Trace the reason (sabab) for the ruling
The mortgagee's liability was based on his exclusive possession over the pledge. When both owners consent to lending it to a third party, that exclusive possession is relinquished; therefore, the liability is lifted.
The commentary refers back to the earlier explanation about the basis of liability.
4
Apply the ruling to a modern scenario
For example, a pawnbroker holds a pledged watch. The owner and pawnbroker both agree to lend it to a friend. If the friend loses it, the pawnbroker is not liable (the friend is).
Contrast this with lending without permission: then the mortgagee remains liable.
