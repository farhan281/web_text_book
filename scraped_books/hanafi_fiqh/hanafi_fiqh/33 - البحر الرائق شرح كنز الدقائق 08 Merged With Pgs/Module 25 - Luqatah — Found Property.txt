Ownership of Fruit from a Flowing River and Scattered Items
Ruling on taking fruit from river and ownership by possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking fruit from a flowing river and apply the principle of ihraz to determine ownership of scattered items.

Core Idea
Taking fruit from a river is permissible to prevent water spoilage; ownership of scattered items depends on whether original owner took possession.

This lesson addresses two seemingly unrelated scenarios: fruit floating in a river and sugar falling into someone's lap. Both are governed by a single principle—possession (ihraz). The Hanafi school uses qiyas to extend the ruling from the fruit case to the sugar and rainwater examples, teaching us how intent and action create ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on fruit from a river
Read the first part: 'It is permissible to take fruits from a flowing river and eat them even if many.' Note the reason given: because leaving them would spoil the water, so permission is implicit.
The hukm is jā'iz (permissible) due to darar (harm) prevention.
2
Analyze the sugar scattering scenario
The text says: a man scatters sugar; it falls into another's lap; a third person takes it. The ruling depends on whether the lap owner intended to catch it (ihraz). If yes, the taker steals; if no, the taker owns it.
The key factor is intent + preparation = possession (ihraz).
3
Apply the same principle to the basin case
A man places a basin on a roof; rainwater collects. If he placed it for collection, he owns the water; if not, the taker owns it. This is the analogue (nazir) to the sugar case.
The 'illa (effective cause) is whether the original owner exerted control (ihraz).
4
Generalize the rule
Ownership of unowned or unattended items follows ihraz: one must physically secure the item with intent. Without ihraz, the item remains mubah (free for anyone to take).
This is a core principle in Hanafi property law (kitab al-luqatah).

And it is permissible to take fruits from a flowing river and eat them, even if many, because it spoils the water if left, so one is implicitly permitted to take it. As an indication: a man scattered sugar, and it fell into the lap of a man; another man took it from him. If the second man's lap was intended for the sugar to fall into, it is not permissible (for the taker) because he had already taken possession of it; otherwise, it is permissible because he had not taken possession. And its analogue: a man placed a basin on a roof, and rainwater collected in it; another man came and took it. If the owner placed it for that purpose, it belongs to him; if he did not place it for that purpose, it belongs to the taker because he did not take possession of it.

Commentary
This passage deals with ownership of found or accessible items. First, fruit that falls or is present in a flowing river is permissible to take and eat, even in large quantities, because if left, it would spoil the water and cause harm, so the law implicitly permits removal. The text then gives analogous cases to clarify possession. If someone scatters sugar and it falls into another's lap, the sugar becomes owned by the person in whose lap it falls if he intended to receive it (his lap was prepared for that purpose). If another person takes it, he is stealing. But if the lap was not intended for catching the sugar, then the sugar is not yet possessed by the lap owner, so a taker may claim it. Similarly, if a person places a basin on a roof intending to collect rainwater, the water belongs to him. If he did not intend collection, then whoever takes the water becomes its owner, as the original owner had not taken possession.

Key Terms
الإحراز (al-ihraz): Taking possession or securing an item, thereby establishing ownership.
الدلالة (al-dilalah): Indication or evidence from the scenario.
