Locusts: Permissible Without Slaughter
Ruling and proofs for eating locusts without dhabh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating locusts, identify the two hadith proofs, and explain why no dhabh is required.

Core Idea
Locusts are halal dead or alive; they require no slaughter, like fish.

This passage from the Book of Hunting and Slaughtered Animals establishes a unique exception in Islamic dietary law: locusts are permissible without ritual slaughter. We will examine the two key hadith proofs that support this ruling and understand why the Hanafi school treats locusts like fish in this regard.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the Hanafi ruling on locusts as stated in the text: it is permissible to eat locusts and no slaughter is required.
2
Examine the first hadith proof
Look at the hadith from Ibn Abi Awfa: the Prophet and companions ate locusts during expeditions. This indicates permission by practice.
The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Examine the second hadith proof
The hadith from Ibn Umar: 'Two dead animals are lawful for us: fish and locusts.' This explicitly includes dead locusts, removing any need for slaughter.
4
Understand the Hanafi principle
The text explains that locusts are like fish in not requiring slaughter, but unlike fish, there is no condition on how they died — even a dead locust is halal.
Fish that die naturally and float (tafi) are not halal, but locusts have no such exception.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Book of Hunting and Slaughtered Animals. The author states the Hanafi ruling: it is permissible to eat locusts, and they require no slaughter (dhabh). Two proofs are provided: (1) the hadith of Ibn Abi Awfa, who reports that the Prophet and his companions ate locusts during expeditions; this indicates permissibility. (2) The hadith of Ibn Umar that two dead animals are permissible: fish and locusts. This means that even if the locust is found dead, it is halal. The principle (usul) clarifies that for fish, only those that die from an external cause (like getting stranded or beaten) are halal; those that die naturally and float (tafi) are not. For locusts, there is no such condition; all locusts are halal regardless of how they die, and no slaughter is needed. This aligns with the Hanafi position that locusts are like fish in not requiring slaughter, but with a broader permissibility.

Key Terms
الْجَرَاد (al-jarad): Locusts, an insect that is halal to eat according to the Hanafi school without the need for slaughter.
الذَّكَاة (al-dhakah): The ritual slaughter (dhabh) required for animals to become halal.
الْحُوت (al-hut): Fish, which is also halal without slaughter, but with condition regarding how it died.

Conditions for Permissibility of Eating Carrion Under Coercion
Ruling on when coercion makes eating haram permissible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions under which eating carrion becomes permissible due to coercion and apply the distinction between severe and mild threats.

Core Idea
Only a threat to life or limb lifts the prohibition of carrion; mild coercion does not suffice.

The sacred law forbids carrion, but the Quran itself carves out an exception for dire necessity. This passage from al-Quduri lays down the exact threshold: when does coercion turn haram into halal? We explore the textual proofs and the Hanafi principle that only a threat to life or a limb can justify consumption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is eating carrion or drinking wine, generally haram. Understand the baseline prohibition before considering exceptions.
The ruling applies to any prohibited food/drink under coercion.
2
Recognize the exception: severe coercion
The text states that mild coercion (beating, imprisonment, shackling) does not permit consumption. Only when the threat involves fear for one's own life or a limb does it become permissible.
This is the Hanafi definition of darurah: fear of death or loss of limb.
3
Locate the dalil and its wajh al-dalalah
The author cites Quran 2:173 and 5:3. The phrase 'man udturra' (whoever is compelled) includes coercion. The condition 'ghayra baghin wa la 'adin' is interpreted by Hanafis as not seeking pleasure or exceeding necessity.
The dalil is qati al-thubut but zanni al-dalalah, giving room for interpretive conditions.
4
Understand the consequence of enduring without eating
If the person refuses to eat and suffers the threatened harm, he is sinful because he unnecessarily exposes himself to destruction, as per Quran 2:195.
This establishes a duty to preserve life and limb when the prohibition is lifted.
5
Apply the Hanafi threshold in practice
Identify if the threat is severe (life/limb) or mild. Only severe coercion changes the ruling from haram to mubah.
Other schools (Shafi'i, Maliki) may restrict to threat of death only.

Commentary
This passage details the conditions under which eating carrion or drinking wine becomes permissible under coercion. The general rule: mild coercion (beating, imprisonment, shackling) does not make these unlawful acts permissible. Only when there is a threat to life or limb does it become licit to consume them. However, if the person endures the punishment and does not consume, he is sinful because he unnecessarily exposes himself to destruction, as per the verse forbidding self-harm. The Urdu usul principles reinforce that only severe threats (such as danger to life or limb) justify violating the prohibition; mere inconvenience does not. The ruling is based on Quranic verses that allow eating carrion only in dire necessity (idtirar).

Key Terms
ضرورة (ḍarūrah): State of extreme necessity that permits what is normally prohibited, here fear of death or loss of limb.
ميتة (maytah): Carrion, an animal that died without proper Islamic slaughter.

Rulings on Geldings, Castration, and Mule Breeding
Rulings on using geldings, castrating animals, and crossing donkeys with horses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on using geldings, castrating animals, and breeding mules, and explain the proofs from the Prophet's sacrifice and the statements of the Companions and Successors.

Core Idea
Castration of animals is permissible; using geldings is disliked; mule breeding is permissible despite a contrary hadith requiring harmonization.

This passage addresses three related issues regarding treatment of animals: the use of castrated animals (geldings), the act of castration itself, and the crossbreeding of donkeys and horses to produce mules. The Hanafi school provides clear rulings supported by textual evidence, though one of the proofs (the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas) appears to contradict the permissibility of mule breeding. As we examine the passage, we will learn how the Hanafi jurists harmonize the proofs and why the school adopts the position of permissibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rulings
Read the opening line and list each ruling: using geldings, castrating animals, and mating donkeys with horses. Note the level of each ruling: makruh (disliked) for geldings, and la ba'sa (no harm/permissible) for the other two.
The term 'al-hasyān' (geldings) is ambiguous; some commentaries interpret it as 'al-khisyan' meaning castrated animals.
2
Examine the first proof for castration
Read the hadith of Jabir about the Prophet sacrificing two castrated rams. Consider why this is evidence for the permissibility of castration: if castration were impermissible, the Prophet would not have used them for sacrifice.
This hadith is narrated by Abu Dawud and is considered sahih (authentic).
3
Examine the second proof for castration
Read the statement of al-Hasan al-Basri (a tabi'i) that there is no harm in castrating beasts. This shows the practice of the early Muslims.
The Musannaf of Ibn Abi Shaybah records this as a proof for those who permitted estray.
4
Analyze the third proof and identify the tension
Read the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas where the Prophet forbade mating a donkey with a horse. This seems to contradict the ruling that mule breeding is permissible. Ask: How can the Hanafi school use this hadith as a proof for permissibility?
The hadith lists three exceptions for the Prophet's family; some scholars interpret the prohibition as specific to the Banu Hashim, not the general community.
5
Synthesize the harmonization
Understand the Hanafi position: The hadith of Ibn 'Abbas is understood as a prohibition for the Prophet's household (as a special honor), not for all Muslims. Therefore, mule breeding is permissible for others. This is supported by the general principle of permissibility in such transactions.
Later Hanafi scholars confirm that the mufta bihi position is permissibility.

The wording is confusing. The Hanafi position usually permits castration of animals for benefit, and some scholars permit mule breeding. This passage reflects that complexity. The teacher should note that the proofs may require harmonization.
