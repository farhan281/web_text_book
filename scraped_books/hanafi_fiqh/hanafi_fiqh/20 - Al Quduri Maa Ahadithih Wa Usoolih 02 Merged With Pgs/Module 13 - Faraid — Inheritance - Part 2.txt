Munasakhat: Correcting Shares When an Heir Dies Before Distribution
Procedure for correcting shares when an heir dies before the estate is divided.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of tashih in cases of munasakhat (successive deaths) by adjusting the inheritance of the first deceased and combining the two cases.

Core Idea
When an heir dies before distribution, calculate their share from the first estate, then apply tashih to the second deceased's inheritance and combine.

In some cases, an heir dies after the entitlement but before the estate is actually divided. This creates a new inheritance scenario (munasakhat) where the share of the deceased heir becomes part of the second estate. The Hanafi method involves first calculating the shares for the original heirs, then treating the deceased heir's share as an estate for their own heirs, and finally combining the two inheritance cases appropriately.

Guided Path
1
Calculate the first deceased's estate and the deceased heir's share
Determine the original inheritance (first case) and compute the share that the deceased heir would have received from the first deceased's estate.
This share becomes the 'estate' for the second deceased.
2
Check if the share of the second deceased divides evenly among their heirs
If it does, the case is correct, and no further correction is needed for the second deceased. If not, apply tashih to the second case to get a corrected base.
Use the same tashih rules as before.
3
Combine the two cases
Multiply the corrected base of the second case (or its original base if no correction needed) by the base of the first case to get a combined corrected base. If the shares of the second case agree with the first case's base, use wafq to avoid overmultiplying.
Alternatively, if the second case's shares are consonant with the first case's corrected base, multiply by wafq.
4
Determine final shares for all heirs
Each heir from the first case takes their original share multiplied by the appropriate factor (either full second base or wafq). Each heir from the second case takes their share from the second case multiplied by the appropriate factor (from the first case or its wafq).
The final combined base is the product of the two corrected bases (or adjusted by wafq).

The Four Principles of Numerical Relations in Faraid Tashih
Introduction to four numerical relationships used in faraid tashih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the numerical relationship between two numbers in a faraid case (tamathul, tadaakhul, tawaafuq, tabaayun) and apply the correct tashih multiplier.

Core Idea
The right multiplier saves the inheritance: know whether your numbers mate, nest, share, or stand apart.

In faraid, after dividing the estate, we often encounter groups of heirs whose shares do not split evenly into the base denominator. The correction process (tashih) requires multiplying the base by a specific number. Which number? That depends on the numerical relationship between the numbers of heirs in each group. This lesson introduces the four Hanafi principles—tamathul, tadaakhul, tawaafuq, tabaayun—that determine the multiplier efficiently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two numbers
Take the numbers of the two groups of heirs (e.g., number of wives and number of sisters).
2
Check for tamathul (equality)
If the two numbers are equal, then multiply the base by that number.
E.g., 4 and 4: multiply by 4.
3
Check for tadaakhul (interlocking)
If the smaller number divides the larger exactly, multiply the base by the larger number.
E.g., 4 and 12: 4 divides 12, multiply by 12.
4
Check for tawaafuq (consonance)
If the numbers share a common factor greater than 1 (but neither divides the other), divide the larger number by the common factor and multiply the base by this result.
E.g., 8 and 10: common factor 2, multiplier = 10 ÷ 2 = 5, multiply by 5.
5
If none of the above, it is tabaayun (disparity)
Multiply the base by the product of the two numbers.
E.g., 9 and 10: multiply by 90.

Principles of Tamathul: two numbers are the same, like four and four, or ten and ten. In such a case, multiplying by one number in the base of the case suffices.
Principle of Tadaakhul: the smaller number is included in the larger, like four and twelve. In such a case, multiplying by the larger number in the base suffices for tashih.

Commentary
This Urdu section explains the four key numerical relationships used in tashih when shares of different heir categories do not divide evenly. Tamathul (equality): when the numbers of two groups are identical, multiply the base by that number. Tadaakhul (interlocking): when one number is a multiple of the other, multiply by the larger. Tawaafuq (consonance): when the numbers share a common factor (but neither divides the other), multiply the base by the number of one group after dividing by the common factor (wafq). Tabaayun (disparity): when the numbers have no common factor, multiply the base by the product of the numbers. These principles streamline the tashih process.

Key Terms
تماثل (tamathul): When the numbers of two groups of heirs are equal, e.g., 4 and 4.
تداخل (tadaakhul): When one number is a multiple of the other, e.g., 4 and 12.
توافق (tawaafuq): When two numbers share a common divisor greater than 1, e.g., 8 and 10 (common divisor 2).
تباين (tabaayun): When two numbers have no common divisor other than 1, e.g., 9 and 10.

Correcting Munasakhah: Multiplying Estates and Using Wafq
Ruling on procedure for combining two inheritance estates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method for combining two inheritance problems in munasakhah, distinguishing when to multiply the full estate and when to multiply only the proportional part (wafq).

Core Idea
In munasakhah, multiply full estates if shares are incompatible; multiply only the wafq if compatible.

When an heir dies before receiving their share, the inheritance problem mutates into a combined calculation across two estates. Al-Quduri lays out the precise arithmetic: if the second deceased's shares do not align with his corrected estate, multiply the entire second problem into the first. If they do align (tawafuq), multiply only the proportional part (wafq). This ensures each heir receives the correct portion. Finally, to express shares in practical units, divide the corrected base by 48 to get danaqs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two inheritance problems
You have two estates: the first deceased (mutawaffa al-awwal) and the second deceased (mutawaffa al-thani) who was an heir from the first but died before distribution. Each has its own base number (asl al-mas'alah) and share distribution.
The second problem arises because the second deceased's own heirs now inherit what he would have received from the first.
2
Check compatibility of shares
Examine the shares (siham) of the second deceased relative to the base number of his own corrected estate. If there is a common factor (tawafuq), you use the wafq; if not (tabayun), you use the full number.
Compatibility here means the shares and the base share a common divisor greater than 1.
3
Calculate the wafq if compatible
If compatible, divide the base number of the second estate by the common factor to get the wafq (proportional part). If not compatible, the wafq is the whole base.
The wafq is the share of the second estate that will be multiplied into the first.
4
Multiply to get the common base
If incompatible, multiply the entire corrected base of the second estate into the first. If compatible, multiply only the wafq of the second into the first. The result is the new base number (sahhah) from which both estates are distributed.
Each heir from the first estate gets his original share multiplied by the wafq (or full base) of the second; each heir from the second gets his share multiplied by the wafq of the second's estate.
5
Convert to danaqs (optional)
If you want to express shares in subunits of a dirham (danaqs), divide the final base number by 48. The quotient is the number of danaqs per share; multiply by each heir's share count to get their danaq amount.
48 danaqs = 1 dirham in this context.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri explains the method for correcting the inheritance problem when there is a sequence of deaths (munasakhah) — i.e., when an heir dies before receiving their share from the first deceased, so their own estate must be combined with the first. The author gives two cases: if the shares of the second deceased do not harmonize with the base number of his own estate, you multiply the entire second (corrected) estate by the first. Then each heir from the first estate gets his share multiplied by the wafq of the second, and each heir from the second gets his share multiplied by the wafq of the second's estate. Finally, to express shares in danaqs (forty-eighths of a dirham), divide the corrected base by 48 and assign the quotient to each heir's share count.

Key Terms
المناسخة (al-munasakhah): The case where an heir dies before distribution, requiring the combination of two inheritance problems.
وفق (wafq): The proportional part of a number after dividing by a common factor; used when shares are compatible.
حبة (habbah): A danaq (1/6 of a dirham) or in this context 1/48 of a dirham; a unit for fractional shares.
