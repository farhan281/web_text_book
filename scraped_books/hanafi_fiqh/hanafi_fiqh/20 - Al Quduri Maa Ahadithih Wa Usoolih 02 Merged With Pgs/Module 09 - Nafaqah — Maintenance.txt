The Obligation of Wife's Maintenance (Nafaqah)
Ruling on wife's maintenance with Quran and Hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wife's maintenance (nafaqah) and identify the condition of surrender and the scriptural proofs that establish it.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is wajib on the husband upon the wife's surrender, regardless of faith or wealth.

The financial support of a wife is a central obligation in marriage. This passage from Al-Quduri presents the Hanafi ruling that nafaqah, clothing, and housing are wajib upon the husband once the wife places herself under his authority. Through Quranic verses and prophetic hadith, we see the binding nature of this duty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Name the act: maintenance (nafaqah), clothing (kiswah), and housing (suknah) for the wife. State the hukm: wajib (obligatory) upon the husband.
The ruling is absolute, not contingent on wealth or religion.
2
Locate the condition
Find the phrase 'idhā sallamat nafsahā fī manzilihi' (when she surrenders herself in his house). This is the key condition triggering the obligation.
Surrender (taslim) means the wife makes herself available in the marital home, not necessarily consummation.
3
Note the scope
Observe that the obligation applies whether the wife is Muslim or non-Muslim, and whether the husband is wealthy or poor (mūsiran kāna al-zawju aw mu‘siran).
The Hanafi school does not condition nafaqah on the husband's capacity.
4
Examine the proofs
Review the cited dalil: two Quranic verses (65:6-7, 2:233), a hadith, and the saying of ‘Aṭā’. Note how each establishes the obligation.
The hadith of Hind further supports the wife's right to take reasonably even from a stingy husband.
5
Understand the legal basis
Recognize that nafaqah is compensation (badal) for the wife's iḥtibās (restriction to the marriage). Thus, it is not a gift but a reciprocal right.
This principle appears at the end of the passage in Urdu.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof establishing the obligation of nafaqah (maintenance) for the wife upon her husband. The ruling states: Nafaqah, clothing (kiswah), and housing (sukna) are wajib (obligatory) on the husband for his wife, regardless of whether she is Muslim or disbeliever, and regardless of whether the husband is wealthy or poor. The condition is that the wife surrenders herself (sallamat nafsaha) to the husband's house, i.e., makes herself available for marital life. The author then provides multiple proofs: (1) The Quranic verse from Surah At-Talaq (65:6-7) explicitly commands spending on wives, especially pregnant ones. (2) Another verse from Surah Al-Baqarah (2:233) stating that the father must provide food and clothing for the mother according to custom. (3) The hadith from the Farewell Pilgrimage where the Prophet commanded: 'And for them (the wives) upon you is their provision and clothing with goodness.' (4) The saying of the Tabi'i 'Ata' that a wife has no maintenance until consummation (this is a minority view; the Hanafi position is that maintenance is due after surrender, not necessarily consummation). (5) The hadith of Hind, where the Prophet allowed her to take from her husband's wealth what is reasonable for herself and her children, indicating the obligation of maintenance. The bottom Usul points clarify that maintenance is compensation (badal) for the wife's restriction (ihtibas) to the marriage, and it is owed to specific relatives: wife, divorced woman, children, parents, and kin.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance; the financial support a husband must provide for his wife, including food, clothing, and housing.
كسوة (kiswah): Clothing that the husband must provide for his wife as part of nafaqah.
سكنى (suknā): Housing that the husband must provide for his wife.
احتباس (iḥtibās): The wife's confinement to the husband's house; it is the legal basis for nafaqah.

Maintenance for Wife Who Refuses Submission Until Mahr is Paid
States that refusal for mahr does not constitute nushuz and maintenance is due.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a wife who withholds herself for prompt mahr retains her right to maintenance.

Core Idea
Withholding for mahr is not nushuz; maintenance continues.

This lesson addresses a common concern: does a wife lose her maintenance if she refuses to live with her husband because he hasn't paid the prompt mahr? The Hanafi school clarifies that this is a legitimate right, not disobedience, so maintenance remains due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The husband has not paid the prompt mahr, and the wife withholds herself (refuses to submit) until it is paid.
This only applies when the mahr is prompt (mu'ajjal), not deferred.
2
Recognize that this is not nushuz
Nushuz (disobedience) requires leaving the home without right or refusing marital duties without legal excuse. Withholding for unpaid mahr is a legal excuse.
The wife is exercising her right to demand the mahr before consummation.
3
State the ruling
She has maintenance (nafaqah) during this period. The husband must provide food, clothing, and shelter despite her refusal.
Maintenance is an obligation of the husband regardless of her withholding for mahr.

Commentary
This is a ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri in the Book of Maintenance. It covers two situations. First, if a wife withholds herself from her husband because he has not paid the prompt mahr, she is not considered disobedient (nashizah) and she is still entitled to maintenance. Second, if she is disobedient (nushuz) by leaving the home without right or refusing marital relations without cause, she loses her right to maintenance until she returns to obedience.

Key Terms

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Nushuz suspends maintenance until obedience resumes.

This lesson covers the opposite case: a wife who is disobedient (nashizah) by leaving the home without right or refusing marital duties without excuse loses her maintenance. Maintenance is conditional upon obedience, and nushuz breaks that condition.

The Hanafi definition is narrower than some other schools.

Identify the ruling
The nashizah wife has no right to maintenance during her period of disobedience.
3
Condition for restoration of maintenance
Maintenance resumes when she returns to obedience, specifically when she returns to the marital home and ceases the disobedient behavior.

Maintenance for a Minor Wife and a Minor Husband
States two rulings on maintenance for minor spouses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two rulings regarding maintenance when one spouse is a minor and identify the key condition (capability of sexual intercourse) that determines entitlement.

Core Idea
Maintenance is tied to marital enjoyment; a minor wife gets none, but a minor husband must pay.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents two clear rulings on maintenance (nafaqah) when one spouse is a minor. The underlying principle is that maintenance is linked to the wife's ability to submit to marital relations (tamkin). When the wife is too young to be enjoyed, no maintenance is due even if she physically submits. However, if the husband is too young, he still must provide maintenance because the marriage contract obligates him.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and note the two cases: (1) wife is a minor, (2) husband is a minor. Observe the asymmetry in the rulings.
2
Identify the ruling for each scenario
For case 1: no maintenance even if she submits. For case 2: maintenance is due to the adult wife from the minor husband's wealth.
3
Identify the underlying condition
The key condition is the wife's capability of being sexually enjoyed. If she cannot, maintenance is not obligated. The husband's incapacity does not relieve him because his obligation arises from the marriage contract.

This ruling from Al-Quduri covers two scenarios. First, if the wife is a minor and not yet capable of sexual intercourse, she is not entitled to maintenance even if she submits to her husband, because the purpose of maintenance (tamkin) is not fulfilled. Second, if the husband is a minor and unable to have intercourse while the wife is an adult, she is entitled to maintenance from his wealth, because the impediment is on his side and she is ready for marital life.

Maintenance During Iddah: Three Categories of Women
States three rulings on maintenance: divorcee, widow, and separation by wife's fault.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi rulings on maintenance during iddah and identify which women are entitled or not entitled.

Core Idea
Maintenance is due for all divorces during iddah, but not for widows or women whose separation is caused by their own sin.

This short but powerful passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays down the essential rules of maintenance (nafaqah) during the waiting period (iddah). It covers three distinct cases: the divorced woman, the widow, and the woman who separates due to her own disobedience. Understanding these categories is fundamental for anyone dealing with family law in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of women
Read the passage carefully and list the three types of women mentioned: (1) the divorced woman, (2) the widow, and (3) the woman who separates from her husband due to her own disobedience.
The passage uses clear Arabic terms: المطلقة, المتوفى عنها زوجها, and المفارقة بمعصية.
2
State the maintenance ruling for each category
For each category, extract whether the woman is entitled to maintenance (nafaqah) and housing (sukna) during iddah. The passage explicitly states the ruling for each.
Divorcee: maintenance and housing are due. Widow: no maintenance. Disobedient separation: no maintenance.
3
Note the key exception for divorce type
Observe that the passage explicitly says maintenance is due whether the divorce is raj'i (revocable) or ba'in (irrevocable). This is a distinctive Hanafi position.
Some might think only raj'i divorce entitles maintenance; the text clarifies otherwise.
4
Understand the rationale behind the rulings
Consider why a widow is not entitled: she inherits from her husband's estate, which takes precedence over maintenance. For the disobedient wife, she forfeits her right because the separation is due to her own fault.
These rulings reflect the principle that maintenance is compensation for the wife's availability within the marriage bond.

Source pages p. 117

Commentary
This passage presents three distinct rulings from Al-Quduri. First, a divorced woman is entitled to both maintenance (nafaqah) and housing (sukna) during her iddah, regardless of whether the divorce is revocable (raj'i) or irrevocable (ba'in). Second, a widow whose husband dies has no maintenance during her iddah (which is four months and ten days), because the inheritance takes precedence. Third, if a separation occurs due to the wife's wrongdoing (e.g., khul' initiated by her fault or talaq caused by her nushuz), she loses her right to maintenance.

Key Terms
رَجْعِيًّا (raj'iyyan): A revocable divorce where the husband can take back his wife during iddah without a new marriage contract.
بَائِنَا (ba'inan): An irrevocable divorce that ends the marriage immediately, requiring a new contract for remarriage.
نَفَقَة (nafaqah): Maintenance: food, clothing, housing, and other necessities that a husband must provide to his wife during marriage and iddah under certain conditions.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Suspension of Maintenance: Imprisonment, Abduction, and Hajj without Mahram
Ruling on suspension of maintenance in three specific cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three situations in which a wife's maintenance is suspended according to Hanafi fiqh, and apply the rule to new scenarios involving absence.

Core Idea
A wife loses maintenance if her absence is caused by her own debt, abduction, or unlawful Hajj – not if she is sick at home.

In this lesson, we examine a short but important passage from al-Quduri's matn on nafaqah. Core Idea

This lesson examines a key ruling from Al-Quduri on the effect of a husband’s financial hardship on the marriage bond. The Hanafi school holds that the marriage remains intact even if the husband cannot pay maintenance, while the Shafi‘i school permits judicial separation. We will study the textual evidence from the tabi‘in and the practical consequence of this dispute.

Rules of Child Custody (Hadānah) in Hanafi Fiqh
Commentary on Al-Quduri's rules of child custody with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules of child custody, identify the effect of marriage on custody rights, and explain the duration of custody for mother/grandmother with proofs from hadith.

Core Idea
The mother's right to custody is continuous until the child is independent (boy) or menstruates (girl); marriage generally nullifies it except for the grandmother marrying the grandfather.

This lesson takes you through the Hanafi rules of child custody (hadanah) as laid out by Imam al-Quduri and illuminated by the commentator's proofs. You will learn the order of eligible female relatives, the critical exception for the grandmother, and the limits of custody duration based on the child's development. We will also address the Hanafi position on whether the child can choose between parents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the order of female relatives entitled to custody
The passage states that maternal aunts take precedence over paternal aunts, and they descend in the same order as in inheritance (like sisters). This gives a hierarchy: mother, grandmother, maternal aunts, then paternal aunts.
This order is based on closeness of blood relation and the principle that females from the mother's side are preferred.
2
Delineate the exception to the rule that marriage nullifies custody
The general rule is that if a female custodian marries someone other than the child's close relative, her right falls. However, the grandmother is an exception: if she marries the child's grandfather, she retains her right because she does not bring a stranger into the household.
The evidence: the hadith 'You are more entitled to him as long as you do not marry' and the practice of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq.
3
Determine the duration of custody for mother and grandmother
For a boy, custody lasts until he can independently perform basic tasks: eating, drinking, dressing, and cleaning himself after relieving himself. For a girl, custody lasts until she menstruates. This indicates physical independence, not a fixed age.
The Hanafi school rejects the idea of child choice at a certain age; the mother's right is continuous until these developmental milestones.
4
Contrast the treatment of free vs. enslaved women
A female slave or umm walad has no right to custody before being freed. After emancipation, they become like free women and are eligible for custody according to the same rules.
Slavery prevents independent authority over the child; freedom restores that right.
5
Apply the ruling for a dhimmi mother of a child from a Muslim father
A non-Muslim (dhimmi) mother retains custody of her child from a Muslim husband as long as the child is too young to understand religions and there is a risk of the child becoming accustomed to kufr. Once the child can discern religious differences and is safe from being influenced, custody may be transferred to the Muslim father.
The underlying principle is the child's welfare and protection from corrupting influences.

Commentary
This passage from the Kitab al-Nafaqat (Book of Maintenance) of Al-Quduri, with its commentary providing proofs, establishes the detailed rules of child custody (hadanah) in the Hanafi madhhab. The author first states the order of female relatives entitled to custody: maternal aunts come before paternal aunts, and they follow the same order of closeness as in inheritance (like sisters). Custody of a Dhimmi Woman over Her Child from a Muslim Husband
Ruling on custody of a non-Muslim mother over her child from a Muslim father

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for a dhimmi mother's custody and apply the hadith to determine when custody transfers to the Muslim father.

Core Idea
A dhimmi mother retains custody of her child from a Muslim husband only until the child can understand religion—thereafter, the risk of adopting disbelief overrides her preference.

In this ruling, the Hanafi school balances a mother's natural right to custody with the paramount concern of protecting the child's Islamic faith. The key is the child's ability to comprehend religious differences, at which point the mother's non-Muslim environment becomes a disqualifying factor. The Prophet's (PBUH) handling of a similar case provides the proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
The passage states that the dhimmi woman is more entitled (ahaqq) to her child from her Muslim husband, but only as long as two conditions are met: the child has not yet comprehended religions (ya'qilu al-adyān) and there is fear that the child may become accustomed to disbelief (ya'lafa al-kufr).
The condition is stated as a single phrase: 'mā lam ya'qil al-adyān wa yukhāf 'alayhi an ya'lafa al-kufr'.
2
Locate the hadith proof
The author cites the hadith of Rafi' ibn Sinan, in which the Prophet (PBUH) gave the child a choice. The child initially chose the non-Muslim mother, but after the Prophet's supplication ('O Allah, guide her'), the child chose the Muslim father.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud and is of zanni thubut.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The Hanafi school interprets the Prophet's supplication as showing that the child's natural inclination towards the non-Muslim mother could lead to misguidance. Therefore, the ruling focuses on preventing the child from being exposed to disbelief, even if the mother is otherwise entitled.
This is a key point of ijtihad: the prevention of harm (mafsadah) takes priority over the mother's right.
4
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Whereas the Hanafi school gives the mother custody only until the child can comprehend religion, the Shafi'i school grants the child a choice at age 7-8 between the parents, even if the mother is non-Muslim.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that under Shafi'i, a child who chooses the non-Muslim mother may remain with her, while Hanafi transfers custody to the father.

Maintenance for Parents and Grandparents Despite Religious Difference

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Maintenance for ascendants and descendants is wajib even if they are non-Muslim—lineage overrides religion.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses a key question: does religious difference eliminate the duty of financial support for family members? The text establishes that for close lineal relatives (parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren), the obligation remains. The general rule—that maintenance is not obligatory when there is a difference of religion—is set aside for these specific relatives. Let us examine the ruling and its evidences.

Guided Path

The act is maintenance (nafaqah) for parents, grandparents, and grandchildren. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) when they are poor.

The text states: 'Maintenance is not obligatory with difference of religion except for the wife, parents, grandparents, children, and grandchildren.' So the general rule has a specific exception for close lineal relatives.
The exception covers both ascending (parents, grandparents) and descending (children, grandchildren) lines.
3
Examine the first proof from Quran
The text cites Quran 31:14, which commands kindness to parents even if they strive to make you commit shirk. The wajh al-dalalah is that good treatment includes financial support when needed.
The verse proves that religious difference does not negate the duty to parents.
4
Examine the hadith proofs
Two hadiths are cited: one by Kulayb ibn Manfa'ah emphasizing honoring mother, father, and relatives; another by Tariq al-Muharibi ordering to start spending on those you support: mother, father, sister, brother, then the nearest.
These hadiths establish priority and obligation of spending on parents.
5

The exception is limited to the named relatives: spouse, ascendants, and descendants. Other relatives (siblings, uncles, aunts, etc.) are not included; for them, religious difference negates the obligation.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position.

Maintenance for Mahram Relatives: Conditions and Proofs
Ruling with proof: maintenance obligation for mahram relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for obligatory maintenance of mahram relatives and explain the Hanafi principle of proportionality to inheritance shares.

Core Idea
Maintenance for mahram relatives is wajib only for poor, helpless recipients, and the amount is proportional to inheritance.

This passage from Al-Quduri establishes a key Hanafi ruling on family maintenance (nafaqah). While the obligation to support one's parents falls solely on the child, other mahram relatives become entitled only under specific conditions of poverty and helplessness. The Hanafi school uniquely ties the amount of maintenance to the recipient's potential inheritance share, a method grounded in the Quranic verse about the heir's obligation. Let's unpack the ruling, its conditions, and the proofs that support it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The passage states that maintenance is obligatory (wajib) for every mahram relative who meets certain conditions. First, note that no one shares the obligation of a child's maintenance of parents. Then identify the categories of recipients.
Mahram means a relative with whom marriage is permanently prohibited (e.g., parents, siblings, uncles/aunts).
2
List the conditions for the recipient
The text mentions five types of eligible recipients: a poor minor (any gender), an adult poor woman, a disabled (zamān) poor male, a blind poor male, or an insane poor male. Common element: they are poor and helpless (cannot earn or are not obliged to earn).
The Urdu principle adds three conditions for the giver: must be well-off (able to pay), and the recipient must be deserving and helpless.
3
Understand the method of calculating maintenance
The obligation is 'according to the proportion of inheritance' (ʿalā qadr al-mīrāth). This means the amount each mahram relative is entitled to is based on what they would inherit from the recipient if the recipient were to die (assuming no other impediments).
This is a unique Hanafi method; other schools may base it on need alone.
4
Examine the primary proof (Quran)
The author cites the verse 'And upon the heir is an obligation like that' (al-Baqarah 2:233). The word 'heir' (al-wārith) is interpreted by al-Dahhak as referring to the child inheriting from the parent. This verse is used as evidence that the heir owes maintenance to the deceased's dependents.
The proof is considered zanni al-dalāla because the term 'heir' is open to interpretation. Hanafi usul classifies this as 'amm (general) that is binding.
5
Review additional proofs
Two hadiths and a saying of the tabi‘i al-Dahhak reinforce the obligation. The hadith from Kulayb ibn Manfa‘ah lists relatives in order of priority for kindness/obligation. These support the general duty toward close relatives.
The combination of proofs elevates the ruling from recommended to wajib.

Maintenance for Mahram Relatives
States the rule of nafaqa for certain needy relatives and its basis on inheritance shares.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for obligatory nafaqah on mahram relatives and apply the 'ala qadr al-mirath principle to determine its amount.

Core Idea
Maintenance for poor mahram relatives is wajib, measured by inheritance shares, but not across religions.

This lesson examines al-Quduri's concise statement on nafaqah for relatives. You will learn the precise categories of recipients, the key condition of poverty, the unique Hanafi rule linking maintenance to inheritance shares, and the bar of different religion. Mastering this ruling equips you to advise on family maintenance obligations in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the recipient categories
List the five types of mahram relatives entitled to nafaqah: poor minor, poor adult woman, poor disabled man, poor blind man, poor insane man. Note that 'poor' (faqir) is a common condition.
These are specified in the source text; they all share the attribute of being unable to earn their living.
2
Understand the 'ala qadr al-mirath principle
The obligation is proportional to what each relative would inherit from the payer if the payer died. This ties maintenance to the inheritance system and prevents unequal burdens.
For a single relative, the amount is derived from the inheritance share, e.g., a sister inherits 1/2, 1/6, etc., depending on other heirs.
3
Recognize the exception for difference of religion
Maintenance is not obligatory if the payer and recipient have different religions (Islam vs. kufr). This is a preventer (mani') that nullifies the obligation.
This rule applies regardless of the recipient's poverty or incapacity.
4
Note the special rule for parents
The passage begins by stating that no one shares with the child in maintaining his parents. This means the obligation to maintain parents falls entirely on the child, even if siblings exist.
This is a distinct rule for parents; for other mahram relatives, multiple heirs may share the obligation proportionally.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two rulings on imprisonment for maintenance (wife vs child) and cite the Hanafi proof from the hadith and Quran.

Core Idea
A father is not imprisoned for his child's debt, but is imprisoned for refusing to provide maintenance to his child.

This passage presents two interconnected rulings about imprisonment to enforce maintenance obligations. The first concerns a husband's duty to his wife; the second addresses a father's duty to his child, with a critical distinction between a child's debt and the father's own obligation to spend. The proofs combine prophetic hadith and Quranic verses to establish the legal boundaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main rulings
Read the first sentence: a man is imprisoned for his wife's maintenance, but a father is not imprisoned for his child's debt. Then note the exception: he is imprisoned if he refuses to spend on the child.
The distinction hinges on the source of the financial obligation: debt vs. maintenance.
2
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father' shows that the father has a right over the child's property, so he cannot be imprisoned for a debt the child owes.
This hadith is ahad, so its authority is zanni al-thubut.
3
Examine the Quranic proofs
Three verses are cited: (1) obligation to treat parents kindly, (2) the father must provide for the mother/child, (3) wages for nursing. These establish the father's duty to spend, so if he refuses, he can be imprisoned.
The verses are qat'i al-thubut but their dalalah is zanni for imprisonment.
4
Understand the legal distinction
The key difference: a father is not liable for a debt incurred by his child (child's own responsibility), but he is liable for maintenance of his child (his own obligation). Thus imprisonment applies only to the latter.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that one is not punished for another's debt, but one can be compelled to fulfill one's own obligation.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
Consider a father who has a son with a credit card debt. The father cannot be jailed for the son's debt. But if the father refuses to provide food and clothing for the son, he may be jailed.
Modern courts would require a demand and proof of ability to pay.
