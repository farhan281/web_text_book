Classification of Arab Lands as 'Ushr and Sawad as Kharaj
Classifies lands into 'ushr and kharaj with geographical boundaries and proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi classification of lands into 'ushr and kharaj and identify the primary evidence for each.

Core Idea
Arabia is 'ushr land; Sawad is kharaj land — ownership remains with the people.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on land classification. The Arabian Peninsula is entirely 'ushr (tithe) land, while the Sawad (Iraq) is kharaj (tax) land. The author provides textual proofs from hadith and the practice of the Companions, emphasizing that Sawad lands are owned by their inhabitants and can be bought and sold.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two land categories
Recognize that the text divides lands into 'ushr (tithe) and kharaj (tax). 'Ushr land is all of Arabia; kharaj land is Sawad (Iraq).
The categories determine the tax obligation on produce.
2
Understand the geographical definitions
Note the boundaries: Arabia from al-'Udhayb to Yemen and Syria; Sawad from al-'Udhayb to Hulwan and from al-'Alth to 'Abbadan.
These boundaries are based on historical conquests.
3
Recognize the legal implication for Sawad
The text states that Sawad lands are owned by their inhabitants; they can sell and dispose of them freely.
This is a key Hanafi position — conquered land not distributed as spoils.
4
Examine the primary evidence for Arabia
Hadith: 'Expel the polytheists from the Arabian Peninsula.' This implies Arabia is an exclusive Islamic land where only 'ushr applies.
Bukhari no. 3053.
5
Examine the evidence for Sawad
The consultation of 'Umar with 'Ali and the Companions: 'Ali advised leaving Sawad inhabitants as a resource for Muslims, imposing kharaj.
This established the Hanafi ruling that Sawad is kharaj land with retained ownership.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling (fasl) from Kitab al-Siyar that classifies lands into two categories: 'ushr (tithed) land and kharaj (taxed) land. The author states that all of the Arabian Peninsula is 'ushr land, defined geographically from al-'Udhayb to the farthest Yemen, Mahrah to Syria. All of Sawad (Iraq) is kharaj land, defined between al-'Udhayb, Hulwan, al-'Alth, and 'Abbadan. Importantly, the land of Sawad is owned by its inhabitants; they can sell and dispose of it. The author then provides evidences: first, a hadith from Ibn Abbas commanding expulsion of polytheists from the Arabian Peninsula, indicating it is a land of Islam where no other religion is allowed, hence only 'ushr applies. Second, a hadith from 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz quoting the Prophet ﷺ that two religions cannot remain in the Arab land. Then he quotes Sa'id ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz and al-Asma'i defining the Arabian Peninsula geographically. For Sawad, he quotes Ibn Sirin stating that parts of Sawad were conquered by force (and thus belong to the Muslims) and parts by peace treaty (so property remains with the inhabitants). Finally, a hadith about 'Umar's consultation with the Companions, where 'Ali advised against dividing Sawad among Muslims but to keep the inhabitants as a resource, imposing a kharaj tax. The Urdu note summarizes: Arab land is 'ushr; kharaj is only where disbelievers live; thus in Arabia only one religion permitted, unlike Iraq where kharaj lands exist.

Key Terms
أرض عشر (ard 'ushr): Land on which 'ushr (tithe) is payable on produce; typically Muslim-owned or in Islamic heartlands.
أرض خراج (ard kharaj): Land on which kharaj (land tax) is levied; usually conquered lands remaining in non-Muslim ownership.
السواد (al-sawad): The fertile agricultural region of Iraq, conquered by Muslims early on.

Taxes Collected by Rebels: Hanafi Ruling
States that the Imam does not collect taxes again if rebels collected and spent them correctly

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that taxes paid to rebels are not collected again by the Imam, identify the two scenarios based on how the rebels spent the funds, and apply this ruling to a modern case of zakat payment under a non-legitimate authority.

Core Idea
Taxes paid to rebels are valid; the Imam cannot recollect them if the rebels spent them correctly.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a pressing issue: what happens to taxes (kharaj, ushr, zakat) when rebels collect them from people under their control? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that protects the people from double payment, provided the rebels spent the funds appropriately. This lesson will walk through the ruling, its conditions, and the evidence from the Quran, hadith, and companion practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the collection of taxes (kharaj, ushr, zakat) by rebels from lands they control. The ruling is that the legitimate Imam does not collect these taxes a second time.
2
Identify the two scenarios
The passage distinguishes two cases: (1) if the rebels spent the collected funds correctly (in their rightful places), then the payment suffices and is not repeated; (2) if they did not spend correctly, the people are advised to make it up between themselves and Allah.
The phrase 'في حقه' means in its rightful place, i.e., on legitimate public expenditures or the prescribed categories for zakat.
3
Locate the dalil
The passage cites three proofs: a hadith from Abu Dharr about obeying rulers (implying validity of taxes paid to rulers); companion statements (athar) where Ibn Umar and others told people to pay zakat to rebel rulers; and the Quranic verse on zakat categories (9:60) to clarify that if zakat is spent on those categories, it is valid.
The hadith on obedience supports the general principle that payments to those in de facto authority are valid.
4
Understand the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that the payment is valid and not repeated because the obligation of zakat/ushr is discharged when the money reaches the legitimate recipients, even if the collector is a rebel. The Imam has no right to demand it again.
This is a mercy to the people.
5
Note the dispute with other schools
Some schools may argue that payment to rebels is invalid and must be paid again to the legitimate Imam. The Hanafi position is based on the principle of 'obedience to rulers' and the practical difficulty of recollection.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri addresses the question of what happens to taxes (kharaj and ushr) collected by rebels (ahl al-baghy) from lands they control. The passage type is a ruling with proof. The ruling states that the legitimate Imam should not collect these taxes a second time from the people. If the rebels spent the collected funds correctly (i.e., on legitimate public interests), then the payment is considered sufficient for the taxpayer. If they spent it incorrectly, the people are advised to make it up between themselves and Allah (i.e., pay again). The proofs include: a hadith from Abu Dharr about obeying rulers even if they are harsh (implying that taxes paid to them are valid), and athar (companion statements) where companions like Ibn Umar and Abu Huraira instructed people to pay zakat to the rebel rulers, indicating that the payment is valid. The Quranic verse on the categories of zakat recipients (9:60) is cited for the principle that zakat must reach specific recipients. The Urdu notes clarify two principles: (1) taxes paid to rebels are not collected again by the Imam; (2) in ushr and kharaj, ownership transfer to the poor is not required, whereas in zakat it is essential. The passage emphasizes the practical legal validity of payments made under rebel rule.

Validity of Tax Collection by Rebel Rulers (Ahl al-Baghy)
Ruling with four proofs on validity of rebel tax collection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of zakat and kharaj collected by rebel rulers and explain the proofs from hadith and sahabi sayings.

Core Idea
Rebels' tax collection is valid; the Imam cannot impose double taxation.

When a rebel group seizes control of a region, a common concern arises: must the people pay their zakat and land taxes again to the legitimate Imam? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling based on textual evidence. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proofs from the Prophetic hadith, Companion fatwas, and the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the opening sentence: what ahl al-baghy collect from kharaj and 'ushr, the Imam does not take a second time. This establishes the validity of the rebels’ collection.
2
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith of Abu Dharr commands obedience to rulers even if they are imperfect. Understand how this applies to accepting the rebels' collection as binding.
The hadith is general (amm) in Hanafi usul, indicating obligation within the scope of non-sinful commands.
3
Analyze the sahabi sayings
The reports from Ibn 'Umar, Abu Huraira, and others advised paying zakat to unjust rulers, including rebels. Note the strength of these as qawl sahabi.
These reports show the Companions' practice in times of fitna.
4
Consider the Quranic verse
Verse 9:60 lists the recipients of zakat. It is cited to show that once the funds are given to those in authority (even rebels), the obligation is discharged.
The verse does not condition the payer's obligation on the ruler's piety.
5
Distinguish proper vs. improper spending
If rebels spent the funds correctly, it suffices; if not, the payer is advised to repeat voluntarily, but the Imam cannot legally recollect.
This distinction is based on the principle that the Imam does not take twice.

What the people of rebellion (ahl al-baghy) collect from the lands they have overpowered, from kharaj and 'ushr, the Imam does not take a second time. If they spent it in its rightful place, it suffices for the one from whom it was taken. And if they did not spend it in its rightful place, the people were given a fatwa among themselves and towards Allah the Exalted that they should repeat that. (Proof 1: Hadith for establishing 'What ahl al-baghy collect from lands' from Abu Dharr who said: My friend (the Prophet) advised me to listen and obey even if the ruler is a slave with limbs cut off. (Muslim, Bab Wujub Ta'at al-Umara', no. 1837/Sunan al-Bayhaqi, Bab Ahl al-Baghy, no. 16768) Proof 2: Sahabi saying for establishing 'What ahl al-baghy collect from lands': I asked Sa'id, Ibn 'Umar, Abu Hurayra, and Abu Sa'id, saying: I have wealth and I want to give its zakat but I find no place for it, and these (rebels) are doing what you see with it? They all ordered me to pay it to them. (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, no. 10189) Proof 3: Sahabi saying: I asked Ibn 'Umar, and he said: 'Pay it to them even if they eat with the dogs.' When they returned to him, he said: 'Pay it to them, even if they eat with a price.' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, no. 10192) Proof 4: Verse for establishing 'If they did not spend it in its rightful place': 'Zakat expenditures are only for the poor, the needy, the workers on it, those whose hearts are to be reconciled, for freeing slaves, for debtors, in the cause of Allah, and for the wayfarer, an obligation from Allah. And Allah is Knowing, Wise.' (Surah al-Tawbah, 9:60) Principles: If the group of rebels took control and collected zakat and 'ushr from the people, then the Imam of the Muslims does not have the right to take it again. Principle: In 'ushr and kharaj, it is not necessary to make the poor owners; however, in zakat it is necessary to make the poor owners, otherwise zakat is not fulfilled. Glossary: jabahu = to collect, sarafuhu = to spend, yahmuhum = to protect.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on the validity of taxes collected by a rebel group (ahl al-baghy) who have seized control of a region. The ruling is that whatever they collect from kharaj (land tax) and 'ushr (tithe) is considered valid and sufficient; the legitimate Imam cannot demand these amounts again from the people. If the rebels spent the collected funds in the proper channels (e.g., on the poor), then the payer is discharged from his obligation. If they misappropriated the funds, the people are advised to repeat the payment out of piety, but legally the Imam cannot re-collect. The author supports this ruling with four proofs: first, a hadith from Abu Dharr emphasizing obedience to rulers even if they are oppressive; second, sayings of Companions (including Ibn 'Umar, Abu Huraira, etc.) who advised paying zakat to unjust rulers; third, another similar report; and fourth, the Quranic verse on zakat distribution (9:60) which implies that once the funds are given to those authorized (even rebels), the obligation is fulfilled. The Urdu commentary adds two principles: (1) the Imam cannot re-collect from the people; (2) in 'ushr and kharaj, ownership by the poor is not required, but in zakat it is necessary for the validity of the payment.
