Jizyah: Types, Rates, and Exemptions in Hanafi Fiqh
Explains the two types of jizyah, its rates, who pays, and who is exempt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of jizyah, apply the graded rates based on wealth, and identify who is exempt from jizyah according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Jizyah is graded by wealth; the poor who cannot work are exempt.

This passage from Kitab al-Siyar of al-Quduri explains the Islamic ruling on jizyah, the poll tax on non-Muslim citizens. It distinguishes between jizyah agreed by treaty and jizyah imposed by the Imam after conquest. You will learn the graded amounts (48, 24, 12 dirhams annually) based on the practice of Umar, and the important exemptions that reflect the principle that jizyah is in lieu of military service.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of jizyah
The text opens by stating jizyah is of two types: one placed by mutual agreement (treaty) and one imposed by the Imam when he conquers a territory. Distinguish their legal basis.
The first type is flexible, the second is fixed by the Imam based on wealth.
2
Note the graded rates for imposed jizyah
For the imposed type, the Imam sets rates: wealthy 48 dirhams/year, middle 24, poor worker 12. Observe the monthly breakdown (4, 2, 1 dirhams).
This grading follows Umar's practice, not a fixed amount for all.
3
Recognize who pays jizyah
The text states jizyah is taken from People of the Book, Magians, and non-Arab idolaters. Note that Arab idolaters are excluded (they are fought until Islam or death).
The 'idolaters of the non-Arabs' means non-Arab polytheists, e.g., Persian or Indian pagans.
4
List the exemptions
Jizyah is not taken from women, children, the chronically ill, blind, poor who cannot work, and secluded monks. Understand the rationale: lack of capability to fight.
Exemptions protect those who cannot bear arms or earn a living.
5
Examine the proofs and the Hanafi response to Shafi'i view
The passage cites the treaty of Najran (hadith), Quran 9:29, and Umar's practice. It also mentions the Shafi'i proof (Mu'adh's hadith of one dinar). The Hanafi school uses the same hadith to limit jizyah to adult males, thus exempting women and children.
The Hanafi position is that the hadith indicates jizyah is on 'every adult male,' not a fixed amount regardless of wealth.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Siyar of al-Quduri covers the rulings of jizyah (poll tax). It begins by stating that jizyah is of two types: one agreed upon by treaty, where the amount is mutually decided; and the other imposed by the Imam when he conquers a territory and leaves the people in possession of their properties. For the second type, the Imam sets a graded amount based on wealth: the rich pay 48 dirhams annually (4 per month), the middle-class pay 24 (2 per month), and the poor who are able to work pay 12 (1 per month). This grading is based on the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab (radiAllahu anhu). Jizyah is taken from People of the Book, Magians, and non-Arab idol-worshippers. The text then provides various proofs: the treaty of Najran (hadith), the Quranic verse (9:29), and the action of Umar. It also mentions the Shafi'i evidence from Mu'adh's hadith that jizyah is a dinar from every adult male, but the Hanafi school uses this very hadith to limit jizyah to free adult males, thus exempting women and children. The passage then discusses exceptions: jizyah is not taken from Arab idolaters, apostates, women, children, the chronically ill, the blind, the poor who cannot work, and secluded monks. Proofs include Quranic verses about fighting polytheists (9:5, 8:39) and the hadith on apostasy (whoever changes his religion, kill him). The Urdu marginal note explains that jizyah is on persons while kharaj is on land. The overall teaching is that jizyah is a substitute for fighting and is only levied on those capable of bearing arms, hence exemptions for those unable to fight or support themselves.

Key Terms
جِزْيَة (jizyah): A poll tax imposed on non-Muslim subjects (dhimmis) in an Islamic state as a substitute for military service and in return for protection.
خَرَاج (kharāj): Land tax imposed on agricultural land in conquered territories, as distinguished from jizyah which is on persons.
مُعْتَمِل (muʿtamil): A poor person who is able to work and earn a living; such a person is subject to the lowest jizyah rate of 12 dirhams.
ذِمِّي (dhimmi): A non-Muslim citizen living under the protection of an Islamic state, subject to jizyah.
