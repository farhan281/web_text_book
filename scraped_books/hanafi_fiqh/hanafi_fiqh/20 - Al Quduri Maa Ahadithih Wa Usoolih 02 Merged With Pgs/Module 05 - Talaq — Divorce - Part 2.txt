Conditional Compensation in Divorce: The 'Ala' Wording
Intra-Hanafi dispute on 'ala' wording and delegation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation when the wife offers money for three divorces using the particle 'ala' and the husband gives fewer divorces, and identify the intra-Hanafi difference with Imam Muhammad's view.

Core Idea
With 'ala', a condition is created; if unfulfilled, no compensation is due.

This passage examines a subtle but crucial distinction in Hanafi divorce law: the preposition 'ala' versus 'bi' when a wife offers compensation for divorce. The ruling depends on whether the offer is interpreted as a conditional transaction or a divisible one. Here, the contrast between Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad (via al-Thawri) reveals how a single word can change the legal outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The wife says: 'Divorce me three times upon a thousand' (using 'ala'). The husband divorces her only once. What is owed?
Focus on the particle 'ala' – it signals a condition.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, nothing is due from the wife because the condition (three divorces) is not fulfilled. The compensation is conditional on the entire set.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
3
Contrast with Imam Muhammad's view
Imam Muhammad (transmitted via Sufyan al-Thawri) holds that the husband is entitled to three thousand dirhams (three times the per-divorce rate) and the single divorce becomes bain (irrevocable).
This view sees the compensation as divisible per divorce.
4
Analyse the linguistic basis
'Ala' implies a condition (shart) – the transaction is a single conditional deal. 'Bi' implies a direct exchange for the act itself, allowing proportionality.
This linguistic distinction is the core of the ikhtilaf.
5
Apply to the delegation case
If the husband delegates divorce to the wife saying 'divorce yourself three times upon a thousand' and she divorces once, nothing occurs, because the delegation is conditional on three.
Consistent with Abu Hanifa's condition-based approach.

Mubara'ah and Khul': Dropping All Rights
Intra-Hanafi dispute on rights after khul' and mubara'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position that mubara'ah and khul' drop all marital rights and evaluate Imam Muhammad's counterargument regarding maintenance.

Core Idea
In khul' and mubara'ah, the wife forfeits all rights including maintenance; Abu Hanifa's view is mufta bihi.

In this lesson, we examine a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the consequences of khul' and mubara'ah. According to Abu Hanifa, these forms of separation drop all rights between spouses—including maintenance, housing, and inheritance—because the wife's compensation replaces them. Imam Muhammad, relying on a narration from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, holds that the wife retains housing and maintenance during her 'iddah. We will analyze the proofs and understand why the Hanafi school ultimately adopts Abu Hanifa's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
State what Abu Hanifa rules regarding khul' and mubara'ah. Which rights are dropped? The text says they drop 'kulla haqqin' (all rights) related to marriage.
2
Examine the supporting narrations
Look at the athar from Qatada and al-Sha'bi. How do they support Abu Hanifa? Qatada says no maintenance for the mukhtali'ah and mubari'ah. Al-Sha'bi uses reasoning: 'How can he spend on her while he takes from her?'
These are the proofs for Abu Hanifa.
3
Consider the opposing view
What did Ibrahim al-Nakha'i say? He said the mukhtali'ah retains housing and maintenance. This is the basis for Imam Muhammad's position.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference? Under Abu Hanifa, the woman gets nothing during 'iddah; under Imam Muhammad, she gets housing and maintenance.
This affects actual rulings in marriage dissolution.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi
Which view is relied upon? The matn of al-Quduri presents Abu Hanifa's view as the mufta bihi. The intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is noted but the operative position is that all rights are dropped.
This is the standard Hanafi fatwa.

Conditional Khul' and Rights Dropped: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khul' and mubara'ah rights with proofs from tabi'in and usul principles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional khul' and distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and Imam Muhammad's positions on the scope of rights dropped in khul' and mubara'ah.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, mubara'ah drops all rights; khul' drops only specified rights—a crucial distinction.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal effects of khul' (wife-initiated divorce for compensation) and mubara'ah (mutual release). While Abu Hanifa considered both to drop all mutual rights, Imam Muhammad—supported by later usul principles—limited khul' to only the explicitly agreed rights. Understanding this difference is essential for correctly determining post-divorce entitlements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional khul' ruling
State the scenario: wife offers payment for three divorces, husband gives only one. According to Abu Hanifa, the husband gets nothing because the condition was not fulfilled.
This ruling is based on the principle that the consideration is tied to the full condition.
2
Locate the proof for conditional khul'
Reference the tabi'i al-Thawri's statement: the consideration is tied to the full condition. Al-Thawri's ruling supports that if the full number of divorces is not given, no payment is due.
The proof is a dalil naqli from a tabi'i.
3
State Abu Hanifa's general principle on rights
Abu Hanifa holds that both khul' and mubara'ah drop all rights between spouses. This is supported by Qatada and al-Sha'bi, who deny maintenance for both the mukhtali'ah and mubari'ah.
Qatada: 'The mukhtali'ah and mubari'ah have no maintenance.' Al-Sha'bi: 'How can he spend on her while he takes from her?'
4
Contrast with Imam Muhammad's position
Imam Muhammad cites Ibrahim al-Nakhai that the mukhtali'ah gets maintenance and residence, implying khul' does not drop all rights. This creates an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
Ibrahim al-Nakhai: 'For the mukhtali'ah, there is residence and maintenance.'
5
Apply the usul principles to resolve
The usul principles clarify: the particle 'ba' indicates exchange, and a condition does not distribute. Mubara'ah implies mutual release, so all rights drop. Khul' only drops specified rights because it does not entail mutual release. This adopts Imam Muhammad's view as the mufta bihi.
Thus, in khul' only explicitly agreed rights are dropped; in mubara'ah all rights are dropped.

States the ruling that li'an becomes obligatory on the husband who accuses his wife of zina or denies paternity, and provides four proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which li'an becomes wajib and identify the proofs from the Quran and Sunnah that establish this ruling.

Core Idea
Li'an is the husband's wajib recourse when he accuses his wife of zina without witnesses, replacing the hadd penalty for qadhf.

Al-Quduri presents the ruling on li'an, a unique procedure in Islamic law that balances the gravity of false accusation with the need for accountability. When a husband accuses his wife of zina or denies her child, and she demands legal action, the husband must perform li'an. This lesson examines the conditions and proofs for this obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its conditions
Locate the main ruling in al-Quduri's text: li'an becomes wajib on the husband who accuses his wife of zina or denies paternity, provided the wife demands the consequence. Note the prerequisite conditions: both spouses must be ahl al-shahada, and the wife must be muhsana (her accuser would be subject to hadd).
The condition 'ahl al-shahada' means free, adult, Muslim, of upright character.
2
Examine the Quranic proof
Analyze Surah al-Nur (24:6-7). The imperative 'fashahadatu' indicates a command; in Hanafi usul, commands imply obligation (wujub) unless a contrary indicator exists. The context shows this is a legal duty when there are no witnesses.
The verse prescribes the exact wording of oaths and curses for li'an.
3
Analyze the hadith proofs
Review the three hadith: (a) the incident of 'Uwaymir al-'Ajlani, which is the primary sunnah establishing li'an; (b) the hadith listing four women for whom li'an is not applicable, defining its scope; (c) the hadith of Ibn 'Umar showing that li'an results in separation and the child is attached to the mother.
The second hadith (four women) is used to further define the conditions—li'an only applies in valid marriages where both are eligible.
4
Synthesize the Hanafi position
Understand why li'an is wajib (not fard): the evidence is zanni al-dalala (the imperative yields obligation but not absolute certainty). The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that li'an is wajib upon the wife's demand. Note the intra-madhhab agreement.
The Hanafi position is consistent across the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad).
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Construct a practical application: A husband accuses his wife of zina, he has no witnesses, she is Muslim, free, chaste, demands legal action. The husband must perform li'an. If he refuses, he faces hadd qadhf.
The procedure involves the husband swearing four oaths that he is truthful, and a fifth curse, followed by the wife's similar response.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from al-Quduri on the obligation of li'an. The author states that if a husband accuses his wife of zina or denies the paternity of her child, and the wife demands the legal consequence (i.e., either hadd for the husband or li'an), then li'an becomes obligatory on the husband. This is conditioned on both spouses being eligible for testimony (free, adult, Muslim, of upright character) and the wife being such that her accuser would be liable for hadd (i.e., she is muhsana: free, Muslim, chaste). The author provides four proofs: first, the Qur'anic verses from Surah al-Nur (24:6-7) which outline the procedure of li'an. Second, the well-known hadith of Uwaymir al-'Ajlani, which is the primary sunnah establishing li'an. Third, a hadith specifying four women for whom li'an is not applicable (Christian, Jew, free under slave, slave under free), implying that li'an is only for valid marriages where both are eligible. Fourth, another hadith showing the Prophet ﷺ performed li'an between a man and his wife when he denied her child, and the child was then attached to the mother. These proofs collectively establish the obligation of li'an under the specified conditions. The Hanafi position is that li'an is wajib upon demand by the wife when the husband accuses her. Note that the hadith of the four women is used here to define the scope of li'an, not to restrict it.

Key Terms
اللَّعَان (al-liʿān): A procedure of mutual imprecation between husband and wife when the husband accuses her of zina or denies paternity, consisting of sworn testimonies and curses.
قَذْف (qadhf): Accusation of zina against a muhsan person. It is a crime punishable by 80 lashes if not proven.
أَهْل الشَّهَادَة (ahl al-shahādah): Those eligible to give testimony in court: free, adult, Muslim, of upright character ('adl).

Imprisonment of Husband if He Refuses Li'an
States the ruling that if the husband refuses li'an, the judge imprisons him until he either performs li'an or retracts his accusation and receives hadd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a husband refuses li'an and explain the proof from the hadith of Hilal ibn Umayyah.

Core Idea
Refusal to perform li'an leads to imprisonment until compliance or acceptance of hadd.

This lesson addresses a critical point in the procedure of li'an: what happens when the husband, after accusing his wife of adultery, refuses to undergo the mutual imprecation. The Hanafi ruling provides a clear coercive measure to ensure justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the husband's refusal to perform li'an after accusing his wife. The ruling: the judge imprisons him.
Imprisonment is not a punishment but a coercive measure.
2
Understand the two possible outcomes
The husband can either perform li'an (mutual imprecation) or retract his accusation, which leads to hadd (80 lashes for qadhf).
The imprisonment continues until one of these outcomes occurs.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is the hadith of Hilal ibn Umayyah, where the Prophet ﷺ said: either bring proof (four witnesses) or face hadd.
This hadith is found in Sunan Abi Dawud.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'lā al-bayyinata aw ḥaddun fī ẓahrika' indicates that the husband must produce evidence or accept punishment. Imprisonment is the means to compel this choice.
5
Note the Hanafi position on coercion
Hanafis permit imprisonment to enforce a legal duty when no alternative exists.
Other schools may allow less coercive measures.

Wife's Obligation in Li'an and Consequences of Refusal
Ruling statement on the wife's obligation in li'an and coercive measure for refusal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the wife's obligation to perform li'an after the husband's li'an and explain the judge's coercive measure if she refuses.

Core Idea
Li'an is mutual: if the husband performs it, the wife must; her refusal leads to imprisonment until she complies or confirms.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar completes the li'an procedure. Once the husband has performed his four oaths and fifth curse, the wife is obligated to respond with her own li'an. If she refuses, the judge does not immediately apply the hadd for zina but instead imprisons her to compel her to perform li'an or admit the accusation. Only then is the marriage dissolved. This ruling highlights the Hanafi emphasis on mutual participation in li'an.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Trigger
The husband's performance of li'an (four oaths and fifth curse) is the cause that triggers the wife's obligation.
Li'an is initiated by the husband; the wife does not begin it.
2
Recognize the Wife's Obligation
After the husband's li'an, li'an becomes wajib (obligatory) upon the wife. She must respond with her own oaths and fifth curse.
This is based on Quran 24:6-9, which implies mutuality.
3
Understand the Consequence of Refusal
If the wife refuses to perform li'an, the judge imprisons her until she either performs li'an or confirms the husband's accusation (i.e., admits to zina).
Imprisonment is a coercive measure, not a punishment for zina.
4
Determine the Final Outcome
Once she performs li'an or confirms the accusation, the marriage dissolves permanently (tahillu).
After li'an, the couple is separated forever (tahrim mu'abbad).

When Li'an is Not Applicable: Hadd for the Husband
States the ruling that husbands who are slaves, non-Muslims, or previously punished for qadhf cannot do li'an and must receive hadd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of husbands who cannot perform li'an and state the hadd punishment that applies instead.

Core Idea
A slave, non-Muslim, or previously punished husband cannot do li'an; instead he is flogged for qadhf.

Li'an is a special procedure that allows a husband to avoid hadd by swearing oaths, but it is only for those who qualify as 'ahl al-shahada'. This lesson examines three categories of husbands who are permanently barred from li'an: slaves, non-Muslims, and those who have already received the hadd penalty for false accusation. Understanding these exceptions helps clarify the boundaries of li'an and the default rule of hadd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a husband accusing his wife of zina. Determine the ruling: normally li'an is an option, but for these three categories, li'an is prohibited and hadd becomes wajib.
The hadd here is the punishment for qadhf: 80 lashes for a free man, 40 for a slave.
2
List the three ineligible categories
The text mentions 'abd (slave), kafir (non-Muslim), and mahdud fi qadhf (one already punished for qadhf). Understand why each is excluded: slaves lack full legal capacity, non-Muslims are not 'ahl al-shahada', and a mahdud has lost credibility as a witness.
A Muslim free man who has not been punished for qadhf is eligible.
3
Determine the proof for each category
The passage cites three proofs: (1) hadith listing four types of women with whom li'an is not performed, (2) the general qadhf verse (Surah al-Nur 24:4), and (3) the practice of Ali (RA) punishing a slave with 40 lashes. Identify how each proof supports the ruling.
The hadith is used analogically: if li'an is not done for certain wives, then by extension a husband of certain status also cannot do it.
4
Derive the practical consequence
Since li'an is invalid, the husband must receive the hadd penalty unless he can produce four witnesses. This renders the accusation of zina as a false accusation (qadhf) without the escape of li'an.
The wife is not subject to punishment; she is the victim of defamation.

Li'an separation is a ba'in divorce, not a permanent prohibition—the majority Hanafi view.

Identify the legal issue

This ruling from al-Quduri addresses a specific scenario: a husband accuses his wife of adultery, but the wife is a minor or insane. The normal consequences—hadd on the husband or li'an between them—do not apply. Why? Because the wife lacks the legal capacity to take oaths or be subject to the accusation in a meaningful way. Denial of Paternity After Acknowledgment: The Hanafi Position and Intra-School Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on denial after acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on denial of paternity after acknowledgment and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding denial during nifas.

Core Idea
Once paternity is acknowledged, it cannot be denied except through li'an, but lineage remains established.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents a pivotal ruling in Hanafi family law: if a husband acknowledges paternity (by word or action) and later denies it, he must perform li'an, but the child's lineage remains tied to him. The Sahibayn offer a limited exception during the postpartum period. Understanding this ruling requires appreciating the interplay between acknowledgment, the hadith 'al-waladu lil-firash', and the function of li'an as evidence rather than negation of lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the husband's denial of paternity after having acknowledged it (explicitly or by silence at birth). The ruling: he must perform li'an, but the lineage remains established.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that if the denial occurs during the postpartum period (nifas), the husband may deny without li'an, effectively removing lineage. This is a well-known ikhtilaf.
The text says 'lahu an yanfiyahu' indicating a valid denial without li'an.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The hadith 'al-waladu lil-firash' establishes the child's default attribution to the husband. Acknowledgment confirms this; later denial is inconsistent and requires li'an but does not sever lineage.
The practice of Umar and Shurayh supports that once acknowledged, denial is ineffective for lineage.
4

Imam al-Shafi'i holds that li'an always severs lineage, based on the hadith of Sahl bin Sa'd where the child was called by his mother's name after li'an.
The Hanafi response: that case involved no prior acknowledgment; the general rule from 'al-waladu lil-firash' governs here.
5

Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school. The Sahibayn's view is a minority exception known but not operative for fatwa.
Al-Quduri presents Abu Hanifa's view first, implying its authority.

Twin Births and Li'an: The Hanafi Ruling on Acknowledgment and Denial
Specific ruling on twin births in li'an

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Twins from one pregnancy are treated as one unit: acknowledgment of one establishes lineage for both.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: when a woman gives birth to twins and the husband's response differs for each. The Hanafi school has a clear ruling that preserves the child's lineage and punishes the husband for slander if he inconsistently denies one and acknowledges the other. This rule is an application of the principle that acknowledgment of one is acknowledgment of all.

The text assumes twins born from the same pregnancy (same batn). The husband can either deny the first and acknowledge the second, or acknowledge the first and deny the second.

Both children are attributed to him, the husband is flogged for slandering his wife (by denying the first), and no li'an is required because his acknowledgment of the second is inconsistent with denying the first.

Both children are attributed to him, but now he must perform li'an to deny the second. The lineage of both remains established.

Acknowledgment of one twin implies acknowledgment of both because they come from the same pregnancy and the same father. Inconsistent behavior is not credible; the husband cannot pick and choose.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the denial of paternity after acknowledgment and the ruling on twins in li'an. The author presents the ruling: if a husband acknowledges paternity (e.g., by not denying at birth or by explicit word) and later denies, he must perform li'an and the lineage remains established. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ: they allow denial during the postpartum period (nifas) even after implied acknowledgment. Then a case of twins: if the husband denies the first twin but acknowledges the second, both children are attributed to him, he is flogged for slander (qadhf) of the wife, and no li'an is needed because his denial of the first is inconsistent. If he acknowledges the first and denies the second, both are attributed and he must perform li'an to deny the second. The proofs cited include the famous hadith 'al-waladu lil-firash' (the child belongs to the bed), which is the core Hanafi proof. The opposing Shafi'i proof is the hadith of Sahl bin Sa'd about the mutala'in couple where the child was attributed to the mother after li'an. The Hanafi response is not stated explicitly here but is implied by the hadith and the practice of Umar. The principles at the end summarize: if there was no practical denial at birth, later denial requires li'an but lineage is established; acknowledgment of one twin is acknowledgment of both. The authority weight: the main ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's view is a minority within the school.

نسب (nasab): Lineage or paternity, established by valid marriage or acknowledgment.

Definition of Quru' and the 'Iddah of Free Women
Defines 'iddah and the meaning of quru' with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of quru' and explain why it determines the 'iddah for a free menstruating woman.

Core Idea
Quru' means menstruation, not purity – the entire 'iddah calculation hinges on this definition.

This passage from Al-Quduri opens the Book of 'Iddah with a clear legal definition. The waiting period after divorce is three quru', but what exactly is a qur'? The Hanafi school answers decisively: it is menstruation. This single definition affects every 'iddah calculation and triggers a well-known difference with the Shafi'i school. We will examine the Quranic proof, the supporting hadith, and the evidence of the opposing view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Find the exact statement: the 'iddah of a free menstruating woman is three quru'. Note the conditions: free, menstruating, divorced (or separated).
The ruling applies whether divorce is raj'i or ba'in.
2
Define the key term: al-aqra'
The author explicitly defines al-aqra' as al-hayd (menstruation). This is the Hanafi position. Understand why this definition is crucial – it determines when the 'iddah begins and ends.
The word qur' is ambiguous in Arabic; the school resolves it with hadith.
3
Examine the primary proof (Quran and Hadith)
Read the Quranic verse (2:228) and the two hadiths cited. The hadith of Umm Habibah uses 'aqra' to mean menstruation. The hadith of Aisha states that a slave woman's qur' is two menstruations, linking qur' to menstruation.
These proofs are dalil naqli (textual evidence).
4
Understand the inter-madhhab dispute
The passage presents the Shafi'i proof: Aisha said 'al-aqra' al-athar' (the quru' are purities). Compare the two interpretations. The Hanafi response relies on the stronger explicit hadith.
The practical difference (thamarah) is whether the 'iddah counts menstruations or purity intervals.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete scenario
Consider a free woman divorced during her purity period. Under Hanafi, her first qur' is the next menstruation. Under Shafi'i, the current purity counts as the first qur'. Trace the difference.
This shows why the definition matters in real life.

Guided Path

The act is a divorced woman in iddah whose former husband dies during the terminal illness that prompted the divorce. The ruling: her iddah becomes 'ab'aad al-ajalayn'—the longer of the divorce iddah and the widow's iddah.

The operative phrase is 'ista'nafat 'iddat al-mutawaffa 'anha' (she begins anew the iddah of the deceased). This indicates that the widow's iddah overrides the remaining portion of the divorce iddah, implying that the longer period is observed.

The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that in the opposing view, the woman would complete her original iddah and then be free to remarry, while in Hanafi fiqh she must wait longer.

The Hanafi school adopts the longer period as mufta bihi, relying on the principle that when obligations coincide, the heavier obligation is observed. The statement of 'Ikrimah serves as supporting evidence.
This position is consistent with the Hanafi principle of ihtiyat (caution) in matters of iddah.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The iddah of an umm walad is three menses, based on the practice of 'Amr ibn al-'As endorsed by 'Umar.

The iddah of a slave-concubine (umm walad) upon her master's death or emancipation is a disputed matter. The Hanafi school holds it is three menstrual cycles, citing the practice of the Companion 'Amr ibn al-'As and the approval of Caliph 'Umar. The Shafi'i school holds it is only one cycle, relying on statements of al-Hasan and Ibn 'Umar. Understanding these proofs and the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is essential for accurately applying this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
Recognise that the issue is the duration of the iddah for an umm walad when her master dies or frees her. The two positions are three cycles (Hanafi) vs. one cycle (Shafi'i).
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi school says the iddah is three menstrual cycles. The proof is the action of 'Amr ibn al-'As, who ordered an umm walad freed to observe three menses, and his letter to 'Umar, who approved it. This is a practice of a Companion endorsed by the Caliph.
In Hanafi usul, the statement or practice of a Companion (qawl al-sahabi) is a source of law when no contradicting proof exists.
3
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
The Shafi'i school says the iddah is one menstruation. Their proof is the statement of al-Hasan and Ibn 'Umar, who reportedly said the iddah is one menses. These are statements of Tabi'i (follower) and a Companion respectively.
The Hanafi response would note that the practice endorsed by 'Umar carries more weight than an isolated statement from Ibn 'Umar, especially when the action of 'Amr was a judicial or administrative practice.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi, the woman waits three cycles before remarriage; under Shafi'i, she waits only one. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school is three cycles.

Iddah of a Woman in Iddah Who is Approached with Shubha
Ruling on additional iddah when a woman in iddah is approached with shubha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a woman in iddah is approached with shubha, explain the sequential iddah requirement, and cite the proofs from the Sahaba.

Core Idea
A woman in iddah who has intercourse with shubha must complete her first iddah then observe a separate full iddah.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario: a woman already observing iddah from a previous marriage is then subjected to intercourse under a mistaken belief of lawfulness (shubha), such as an invalid marriage during her iddah. The Hanafi school has a clear ruling on how she must observe her iddahs, supported by the practice of the Sahaba. We will examine the ruling, the proofs, and a differing opinion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Understand that a woman is currently in iddah from a prior marriage and then has intercourse with shubha (e.g., a marriage that is later found invalid). This triggers a second iddah.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The ruling is that she must complete the remainder of her first iddah, then observe a separate, full new iddah for the second relationship.
3
Examine the proofs
The proofs come from the actions of 'Ali and 'Umar (radi Allahu anhum). Read the narrated reports: 'Ali ordered her to complete the first then a new iddah; 'Umar also ruled for iddah from both.
These are considered qawl al-sahabi, a source of evidence in Hanafi usul.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Al-Sha'bi (a Tabi'i) held a different order: she should observe iddah from the second first, then complete the remainder of the first. Recognize that this is a legitimate difference.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafi says complete first iddah first, then second; Sha'bi says start with the new iddah, then finish the old. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi.

Ruling on 'Idda for a Widow with Pregnancy Appearing After Death
Ruling with Quranic proof on 'idda for pregnant widow

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the 'idda of a widow when pregnancy is discovered after the husband's death and explain the Quranic evidence for that ruling.

Core Idea
For a widow, 'idda is fixed at 4 months 10 days even if pregnancy appears after death; lineage is not established.

This lesson focuses on a specific scenario in family law: a widow whose pregnancy becomes known only after her husband's death. The Hanafi school, relying on the clear text of the Quran, rules that her waiting period remains the standard four months and ten days, and the child is not attributed to the deceased. Understanding this ruling requires distinguishing it from the case where pregnancy is known at the time of death, which shortens the 'idda to delivery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Subject and the Ruling
First, note the subject: a widow whose pregnancy is discovered after her husband's death. The ruling has two parts: (1) her 'idda is four months and ten days, and (2) the child's lineage is not established from the deceased.
This is a specific sub-case of the general widow's 'idda.
2
Locate the Quranic Proof
The author cites Surah al-Baqarah (2:234) as evidence. Read the verse: 'And those who are taken in death among you and leave wives behind, they shall wait concerning themselves four months and ten days.'
The verse is general and does not mention pregnancy; the Hanafi jurists apply it here because the pregnancy was not known at death.
3
Understand the Legal Reasoning
The key point: since the pregnancy occurred after death (or was unknown), the widow is treated as non-pregnant for the purpose of 'idda. The verse's imperative indicates obligation, and the specified duration is binding.
The child's lineage is not established because the pregnancy is not attributed to the deceased husband; it is considered a new pregnancy from an unknown father (or not from the deceased).
4
Contrast with the Known Pregnancy Case
If the widow is known to be pregnant at the time of death, her 'idda ends with delivery (based on another Quranic verse, al-Baqarah 2:233 and hadith). This lesson only covers the case where pregnancy appears after death.
This distinction is crucial for correct fatwa.
5
Memorize the Ruling and its Proof
As a memorization anchor, internalize: 'For a widow with pregnancy after death, 'idda = 4 months 10 days, lineage not established.' The proof is the clear command of the Quran.
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah and is the mufta bihi position.

Divorce During Menses: The Current Menstruation is Not Counted
Ruling with Quran and Hadith proof on counting 'idda cycles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on counting 'idda when divorce occurs during menses and explain the evidence from the Quran and the Companion's statement.

Core Idea
A menses in which divorce occurs is not counted toward the 'idda.

The 'idda is a waiting period for divorced women. But what if the divorce happens during menstruation? The Hanafi school rules that the current menses does not count. This lesson examines the ruling and its proofs from the Quran and from Ibn 'Umar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is divorce during menstruation. The ruling is that the menstruation in which the divorce occurs is not counted as part of the 'idda.
This is a specific sub-ruling within the broader obligation of 'idda.
2
Locate the primary proof (Quran)
The Quranic verse 2:228 states: 'And divorced women shall wait concerning themselves for three quru'.' The Hanafis interpret quru' as menstrual cycles.
The verse alone does not specify whether the current menses counts; it must be understood with additional evidence.
3
Locate the secondary proof (Companion statement)
Ibn 'Umar said: 'If he divorces her while she is menstruating, she does not count that menstruation.' This explicit statement clarifies the ruling.
The statement of a Companion is considered a proof in Hanafi usul when it interprets a general text.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If a woman is divorced on day 1 of her period, she completes that period, then observes three full menstrual cycles after it. The initial period is excluded.
This ensures the 'idda consists of three complete menses after the divorce.
5
Note the inter-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i counts the current menses because he interprets quru' as purity periods. The practical difference: the 'idda may end earlier or later depending on the school.
Both interpretations are based on valid linguistic considerations.
