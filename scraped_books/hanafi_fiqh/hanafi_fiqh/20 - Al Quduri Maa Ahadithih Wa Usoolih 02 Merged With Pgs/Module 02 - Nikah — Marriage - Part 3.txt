The Child's Religion When Parents Differ
Ruling with proof on child's religion when parents differ.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the religion of a child when one parent is Muslim and explain the proof from the hadith.

Core Idea
A minor child follows the Muslim parent's religion automatically — Islam prevails.

This lesson addresses a critical family law issue: what religion does a child follow when parents have different faiths? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule derived from a prophetic incident. You will examine the primary text from al-Quduri and the hadith of Rafi' bin Sinan, and understand the reasoning behind the child being considered Muslim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is determining the religion of a child when one parent is Muslim. The ruling is that the child follows the Muslim parent's religion.
The child's religion is not left to choice.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Sunan Abi Dawud about Rafi' bin Sinan and his wife.
The hadith is the primary evidence.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith shows that after the Prophet's supplication, the girl inclined to her Muslim father, and the Prophet let him take her. This indicates the child goes to the Muslim parent, implying religious following.
The Prophet's du'a guided the outcome.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools may give the child a choice at the age of discernment; Hanafi does not.
The difference is legitimate.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is based on the superiority of Islam and the guardianship of the Muslim parent.
The child is considered Muslim automatically.

This passage addresses the religion of children when parents differ in religion. It is a ruling with proof. The first ruling: If one spouse is Muslim and the other is not, the child follows the religion of the Muslim parent. Validity of Pre-Islamic Marriages After Conversion
Ruling on validity of pre-conversion marriages

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marriages contracted by disbelievers and apply the condition that they must be valid in their religion and not violate Islamic limits.

Core Idea
A marriage valid in its own religion is upheld after conversion, subject to Islamic limits like the maximum of four wives.

When a non-Muslim embraces Islam, questions arise about the validity of marriages contracted before conversion. The Hanafi school takes a pragmatic approach, recognizing prior contracts as valid provided they were lawful in the person's former religion and do not contravene clear Islamic prohibitions. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its evidence from the Sunnah, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Locate the core ruling: a disbeliever's marriage without witnesses or during iddah, if valid in their religion, is confirmed upon conversion. This applies to both spouses entering Islam.
Note that the ruling covers marriages that would be defective by Islamic standards but valid in the couple's prior faith.
2
Examine the primary proof
Read the hadith of Wahb al-Asadi: he had eight wives upon conversion; the Prophet ordered him to keep only four. This implies the original marriages were valid, but Islam imposes the limit of four.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud (no. 2241) and is ahad (single narration) but accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The operative phrase is 'Choose four of them.' The Prophet did not invalidate the marriages entirely; he allowed retention of up to four, confirming the original validity.
This indicates that the pre-Islamic marriages are presumed valid unless they violate a fundamental Islamic principle.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require witnesses even for non-Muslim marriages, arguing that Islamic marriage conditions apply retroactively. Hanafis give weight to the couple's prior religious law.
This difference affects how converts are advised to regularise their marriages.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is based on the narration of 'Ata that the Prophet left people of Jahiliyyah upon their marriages and divorces. This is the relied-upon view in the school.
The ruling is mufta bihi: confirmation is the default unless a clear prohibition exists.

Commentary
This passage deals with the validity of marriages contracted by disbelievers according to their own religious laws before embracing Islam. It is a ruling with proof. The ruling: If a disbeliever contracts a marriage without witnesses or during a waiting period, and this is valid in his religion, then when both he and his wife embrace Islam, the marriage is recognized as valid in Islam (they are confirmed upon it). This is because Islamic law respects prior contracts that were valid according to the parties' own religion, as long as they do not violate clear Islamic principles. The proofs include the hadith of Wahb al-Asadi, who had eight wives when he became Muslim, and the Prophet ordered him to keep only four, implying that the original marriages were valid but Islam limited the number. Additionally, the statement of 'Ata confirms that the Prophet left the pre-Islamic people's marriages and divorces as they were. The key point is that marriages of disbelievers are not ignored upon conversion; they are validated if they would be acceptable in Islam, subject to Islamic limits (e.g., maximum four wives).

Key Terms
الشُّهُودُ (al-shuhud): Witnesses to the marriage contract, required for validity in Islam but not necessarily in other religions.
الْعِدَّةُ (al-iddah): Waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of husband before remarrying.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a minor child follows the Muslim parent upon conversion and identify the hadith evidence that supports it.

Core Idea
A minor child's religion automatically follows the Muslim parent — no choice until puberty.

In families where one parent becomes Muslim, a critical question arises: what is the child's religion? The Hanafi school answers with clarity: the minor child follows the Muslim parent. This lesson unpacks the ruling from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar and the hadith evidence that grounds it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the passage is ruling on: a minor child when one parent is Muslim or converts. The ruling is that the child becomes Muslim automatically.
The act is not a physical action but a status determination.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the evidence cited. The passage cites a hadith from Sunan Abi Dawud (no. 2244) about Rafi' bin Sinan.
The Quranic verse (4:137) is also mentioned but is less direct.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah

This is a legitimate scholarly difference.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih

Separation When a Fire-Worshipper Marries a Mahram and Converts
Ruling on separation when a fire-worshipper marries close relatives and converts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a Majusi marries his mother or daughter and then embraces Islam, and explain why the marriage is annulled absolutely.

Core Idea
A marriage to a mahram is batil from the start; conversion does not cure it.

Imagine a fire-worshipper whose religion permits marrying close relatives, then he and his wife embrace Islam. Is the marriage automatically valid now? The Hanafi school says no: because the marriage itself was forbidden from the Islamic perspective from day one. In this lesson, we'll explore the ruling and the evidence, including a key Quranic verse that establishes the prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
What exactly is the act? A Majusi (fire-worshipper) marries his mother or daughter. Then either he or she (or both) embrace Islam. The ruling: they must be separated (farq).
The act is a marriage between a mahram (unmarriageable kin) and the ruling is immediate separation.
2
Locate the Primary Dalil
The author cites Quran 4:23, which explicitly prohibits marrying mothers and daughters. This is a qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala text.
The hadiths cited (Abu Dawud 2133 and Bukhari 5213) seem mismatched to this ruling; they likely belong to other topics. Always verify hadith citations.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The verse says 'Hurrimat 'alaykum ummahatukum...' — the word 'hurrimat' indicates a permanent, inviolable prohibition. Marriage to a mahram is thus haram and the contract is batil (void from inception).
No condition can make such a marriage valid; the prohibition is absolute.
4
Note the Cross-Madhhab Ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that if a non-Muslim marries a mahram and then converts, the marriage is annulled only if the original religion allowed it. The Hanafi school annuls it absolutely.
The practical difference: in Shafi'i, if the Majusi religion considered such marriages permissible, the marriage might be valid after conversion. Hanafi says never.
5
Understand the Hanafi Tarjih
The Hanafi position is based on the absolute prohibition in Quran 4:23. There is no room for considering the original faith's permission because the marriage contravenes a clear nass. This is the mufta bihi position.
Later Hanafi masters (al-Haskafi, Ibn Abidin) confirm this ruling.

Little and Much Breastfeeding Within the Period Establish Prohibition
Ruling with proofs and rebuttal of Shafi'i's position requiring five known sucklings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the amount of breastfeeding that establishes marriage prohibition and evaluate the evidence used by both Hanafis and Shafi'is.

Core Idea
Any amount of breastfeeding within the period establishes marriage prohibition—even one sip suffices.

This lesson presents the Hanafi position on the quantity of breastfeeding required to create foster relationships that prohibit marriage. The school holds that little and much, even a single sip, within the defined breastfeeding period (approximately two and a half years) establish the same marital prohibitions as blood relations. We will examine the proofs from the Quran, hadith, and sayings of companions, and then analyze the Shafi'i counterargument which requires five known sucklings. By understanding the evidence and the Hanafi response, you will appreciate how the school derives rulings from general texts and handles conflicting narrations.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that any amount of breastfeeding within the period (muddat al-rada') establishes the prohibition of marriage. This includes even one or two sips.
The period is defined as approximately 2.5 years (30 months) from birth, based on Quran 46:15 and 2:233.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school requires five known, separate sucklings within the first two years to establish prohibition. They rely on specific hadiths limiting the effect to five or more feedings.
3
Follow the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'is cite two hadiths from Aisha: (1) Revelation of ten sucklings abrogated to five, and (2) the Prophet saying 'One or two sips do not cause prohibition.'
These are found in Muslim and Abu Dawud.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that the five-suckling hadith is not authentic or is abrogated; the general evidence of the Quran and authentic hadith (e.g., 'breastfeeding prohibits what birth prohibits') takes precedence. They also rely on the qawl sahabi of Ali and Ibn Mas'ud.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi, marriage is prohibited after any breastfeeding within the period; under Shafi'i, only after five known sucklings. This affects many cases where limited breastfeeding occurs.
The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi (relied upon) in the school.

Fosterage from a Virgin's Milk
Ruling that a virgin's milk creates fosterage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a virgin's milk establishes fosterage and identify the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
A virgin's milk, if used to breastfeed, triggers the same fosterage prohibitions as any woman's milk.

The passage begins by addressing the unusual case of a virgin producing milk. This ruling clarifies that the source of milk is not limited to women who have given birth. Even a spontaneous lactation in a woman who has never married or had intercourse creates the legal ties of radāʿa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
The text specifies a virgin (bikr) who produces milk and breastfeeds a child. Note that this is an extraordinary scenario – a woman who has not had intercourse or given birth.
The term 'bikr' can refer to a woman who has never been married or has never given birth.
2
State the ruling
The ruling: prohibition attaches (taʿallaqa bihi al-taḥrīm). The fosterage prohibition becomes effective, making the child a mahram to the woman.
3
Locate the proof
The proof is the Quranic verse: 'And your mothers who breastfed you' (Surah al-Nisa' 4:23). The feminine plural pronoun 'allatī' indicates that the milk providers are women, implying any woman, including a virgin.
The proof is qatʿī al-thubūt (Quran) and qatʿī al-dalāla (the pronoun clearly refers to females).
4
Understand the implication
The ruling establishes that the condition for fosterage is that the milk comes from a female human, regardless of her maternal or marital status. Virginity or previous birth is irrelevant.
This is the muftā bihī position in the Hanafi school.

No Fosterage from a Man's Milk
Ruling that a man's milk does not create fosterage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a man's milk does not create fosterage and identify the proof from the tabiʿī statement.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, male milk has no legal effect in establishing fosterage prohibitions.

The second part of the passage addresses the exceptional case of a man producing milk. Such cases are rare but possible due to medical conditions. The ruling is clear: no fosterage prohibition arises, regardless of the milk's quantity or quality.

The text describes a man who produces milk and breastfeeds a child. This is an extraordinary scenario where a male lactates.
The term used is 'رَجُل' (rajul). The proof refers to 'لبن الفحل' (laban al-faḥl), i.e., milk of the male.
2
State the ruling
The ruling: no prohibition attaches (lam yataʿallaq bihi taḥrīm). The milk of a man does not create any fosterage relationship.
3
Locate the proof
The proof is the statement of the tābiʿī ʿĀmir al-Shaʿbī (transmitted by Jābir ibn Zayd) that they considered 'the milk of the male' (laban al-faḥl) to be nothing (lā yarayāni laban al-faḥli shay’an).
This is a statement of a tābiʿī, which is a source of evidence in Hanafi madhhab, though not as strong as Quran or mutawatir hadith.
4
Understand the rationale
The underlying principle: fosterage is tied to female milk; male milk does not create the same nutritional and familial connection. The term 'laban al-faḥl' literally means 'milk of the stallion', a metaphor for the man.
In Arabic, 'al-faḥl' is the male animal, used here for a man.
5
Note the authority weight
The proof is not from the Quran or mutawatir hadith but from the practice of early tabiʿīn. Despite being ẓannī, the ruling is unanimously accepted in the Hanafi school.
There is no known Hanafi dissenting opinion on this matter.

Ruling on Breastfeeding by a Co-Wife
Detailed scenario: a man marries a minor and an adult; the adult breastfeeds the minor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a co-wife breastfeeds the other, and apply the Hanafi rule on intentional harm (ta'ammud al-fasad) to determine financial liability.

Core Idea
Intentional fosterage by a co-wife nullifies both marriages and makes her liable for the minor's mahr.

This lesson presents a detailed scenario from al-Quduri's matn: a man married to both a minor girl and an adult woman. If the adult breastfeeds the minor, both become prohibited to him. The ruling then addresses the mahr consequences, which hinge on the adult's intent. This illustrates how Hanafi fiqh combines family law with principles of compensation for deliberate harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
Recognize the three individuals: the husband, the adult wife, and the minor wife. The critical act is the adult breastfeeding the minor.
2
Determine the legal effect of breastfeeding
Both marriages become forbidden (haram) because the minor becomes a foster child of the adult, and the adult becomes a foster mother; thus, both are now mahram to the husband.
3
Analyze financial consequences if no consummation with adult
If the husband has not consummated with the adult, she receives no mahr because dissolution occurred before consummation. The minor is entitled to half the mahr.
Half mahr is standard for divorce or dissolution before consummation.
4
Apply the principle of intentional harm (ta'ammud al-fasad)
The husband can recover the half mahr paid to the minor from the adult only if she intentionally caused the prohibition (i.e., deliberately breastfed to nullify marriages). If unintentional, she is not liable.
Intent is determined by the adult's state of mind; external evidence may be considered.
5
Summarize the ruling structure
Prohibition is unconditional; financial liability for the adult is conditional on intent. This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on intention in compensatory obligations.

This lesson covers the foundational rulings from the Book of Rada' in the Quduri text. The passage presents two essential principles: what constitutes rada' (human milk) and how it is proven in court (specific testimony combinations). Understanding these is crucial for correctly applying laws of fostership and marriage prohibition.

The Hanafi Ruling on Khul': Divorce by Redemption
States the definition, validity, and effect of khul' with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khul', identify its four proofs, and explain why it results in a ba'in (irrevocable) divorce rather than an annulment.

Core Idea
Khul' is a valid divorce initiated by the wife in return for compensation, resulting in one irrevocable divorce.

In this passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, we encounter the foundational ruling on khul' — a form of divorce where the wife initiates separation by paying compensation. The author concisely states that khul' results in a single ba'in (irrevocable) divorce, and then provides four distinct evidences, from the Qur'an to the practice of companions. Understanding this ruling requires not only knowing its effect but also grasping the proofs that ground it in the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the initial statement: khul' is a divorce initiated by the wife's ransom (iftida') when discord is feared. The act is valid; its effect is one ba'in divorce and binding compensation.
The ruling has both a condition (fear of transgression) and an effect (ba'in divorce).
2
Locate the dalil
The commentator cites four proofs: (1) Quran 2:229, (2) the hadith of Thabit ibn Qays' wife, (3) a hadith that the Prophet declared khul' a ba'in divorce, and (4) the practice of a companion.
Each proof reinforces both the legitimacy and the legal nature of khul'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
For the Quran, the phrase 'fima iftadat bihi' (that by which she ransoms herself) directly permits the exchange. The hadiths show actual application and the specific ruling of ba'in.
The hadith of Ibn Abbas explicitly states 'ja'ala al-khul'a tatliqatan ba'inatan'.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Hanafi position differs from the Shafi'i school, which considers khul' to be faskh (annulment) rather than talaq. This affects whether the husband can remarry without a new mahr.
Hanafi: ba'in divorce requires a new contract. Shafi'i: annulment meaning it is as if no marriage occurred.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is based on multiple narrations and is the mufta bihi (operative opinion) in the school. The hadith specifying 'tatliqatan ba'inatan' gives direct textual authority.
No intra-Hanafi dispute; the three imams agree.

Ruling When Discord Originates from the Wife
States the ruling on taking excess mahr in khul'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the husband taking more than the mahr in khul' and explain why it is valid judicially but disliked.

Core Idea
Taking more than the mahr in khul' is valid in court but morally disliked.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in khul' divorce: when the discord originates from the wife (nushuz). The husband is compensated for the mahr he gave, but the text warns against profiting beyond that. It distinguishes between judicial validity (sihha) and moral permissibility (hillah), a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh. We will explore the ruling, its proof, and the scholarly disagreement surrounding it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the husband taking compensation in khul' when the wife is at fault. The ruling is that it is disliked (makruh) to take more than the mahr he gave. However, if he does so, the khul' remains valid in court (jaza fi al-qada').
The ruling splits into moral and judicial dimensions.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Jamilah bint Salul (from Sunan Ibn Majah), where the Prophet ordered the husband to take back his garden (the mahr) and 'not to increase'.
The hadith is ahad (single-narrator) and thus zanni al-thubut, which influences the strength of the ruling.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'la yazdad' (do not increase) is a prohibition. In Hanafi usul, a prohibition indicates dislike (karaha) unless there is evidence for impermissibility. Since the Prophet did not nullify the khul' when an increase occurred, the prohibition is understood as tanzihi (disliked), not tahrimi (forbidden).
This shows the Hanafi principle that a prohibition does not necessarily invalidate the contract.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars hold that taking more than the mahr invalidates the khul' because the prohibition is interpreted as a condition for validity. The Hanafi school disagrees, maintaining that the contract is valid despite the disliked excess.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the broader framework of ijtihad.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa) and is based on the Zahir al-Riwayah. The key distinction is between the contract's validity (sihha) and its moral status (hillah).
The hadith's context and the absence of contract nullification support the Hanafi view.

Divorce for Compensation: Valid and Invalid Consideration
Ruling on divorce for compensation with valid and invalid consideration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on divorce for compensation, distinguishing between valid and invalid consideration, and between khul' and talaq.

Core Idea
Invalid compensation in khul' does not affect the ba'in nature of divorce; in talaq it turns the divorce into raj'i.

This passage addresses divorce (talaq) and khul' where a woman pays compensation to her husband. The key distinction is between valid and invalid compensation (e.g., wine, pig, carrion). The Hanafi school rules that invalid compensation in khul' still results in an irrevocable divorce, while in talaq it makes the divorce revocable. Additionally, any item valid as mahr can serve as compensation in khul'.

If the invalid compensation is in a regular talaq (not khul'), the divorce becomes raj'i (revocable).
This shows a key difference between khul' and talaq.

The rulings depend on the phrasing: if she says 'what is in my hand' and has nothing, no compensation is due; if she says 'wealth' (mal) and has none, she must return her mahr; if she says 'dirhams' (plural) and has none, she owes three dirhams. The applied usul principle: the plural form (jam') yields a minimum of three.

Identify the ambiguous phrases
The passage presents three levels of ambiguity: (1) 'what is in my hand' (absolute), (2) 'what is in my hand of wealth' (generic), (3) 'dirhams' (specified but plural).
Each has a different default ruling.
2
Rule for absolute ambiguity
If she says 'khul' for what is in my hand' and has nothing, the husband gets nothing.
This is because 'what is in my hand' is understood to refer to something possessed; if nothing, no compensation.
3

If she says 'of wealth' (mal) and has none, she must return her mahr.
The term 'wealth' implies something of value, so the default becomes the known mahr.

If she says 'dirhams' (plural) and has none, he gets three dirhams.
The plural form in Arabic implies at least three, so that is the minimum payable.
5

If she asks for three divorces for a thousand, but he only gives one, she owes one-third.
This is proportional to the number of divorces actually given.

Establishing Lineage of a Child from Divorced and Widowed Women
Rulings on establishing paternity based on timeframes after divorce or death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi timeframes for establishing a child's lineage after divorce or death, and identify the effect of the woman's admission of complete 'iddah.

Core Idea
Lineage is based on the marriage bed (firash) and the maximum pregnancy period of two years; beyond that, the husband must claim the child.

In this lesson, we examine the rules for establishing paternity (nasab) when a woman gives birth after divorce or the death of her husband. The Hanafi school relies on the hadith 'al-waladu lil-firash' and the maximum pregnancy period of two years, reported from Aisha. We will walk through each category: revocably divorced, irrevocably divorced, widowed, and a woman who admits her 'iddah had ended. Understanding these timeframes is essential for determining the child's legal parentage and the rights that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Categories of Women
List the four types: revocably divorced (raj'iyyah), irrevocably divorced (mabtutah), widowed (mutawaffa 'anha zawjuha), and a woman in 'iddah who admits expiry of her 'iddah.
Each has a specific timeframe for lineage establishment.
2
Note the Timeframes
For all three main categories, the maximum period from divorce/death is two years. For the woman who admits 'iddah expiry, the period is six months from the admission.
Two years is the Hanafi maximum pregnancy period.
3
Understand the Effect of Admission
For a revocably divorced woman, if she admits her 'iddah has expired before giving birth, that affects lineage establishment. For a woman in 'iddah who admits expiry, birth within six months establishes lineage; at six months or later, it does not.
Admission of 'iddah expiry ends the firash (marriage bed) effect.
4
Examine the Proofs
The key proof is the hadith 'al-waladu lil-firash', and the saying of Aisha that a woman does not exceed two years in pregnancy. Also the incident of Umar and Ali establishing minimum pregnancy as six months from Quranic verses.
These proofs are cited in the text.
5
Apply the Principle
The firash governs lineage unless the maximum period is exceeded, the woman admits 'iddah expiry, or for irrevocably divorced, the husband disclaims paternity at the exact two-year mark.
The ruling varies by the woman's status and timing.

Chapter on establishing lineage. The lineage of the child of a revocably divorced woman is established if she gives birth within two years or more as long as she does not admit the expiry of her 'iddah. If she gives birth before two years, she becomes separated (bainuna), and if she gives birth after two years, his lineage is established and she remains revocable. For the irrevocably divorced woman, the lineage of her child is established if she gives birth before two years; if she gives birth at the completion of two years from the day of separation, his lineage is not established unless he claims it. The lineage of a woman whose husband has died is established between the death and two years. And if a woman in 'iddah acknowledges the expiry of her 'iddah, then gives birth to a child in less than six months, his lineage is established; if she gives birth at six months, it is not established due to the possibility that it occurred after the 'iddah. For evidence: (1) The hadith for establishing the lineage of the child of a revocably divorced woman: From 'Aisha ... he said, 'The child belongs to the bed, and for the fornicator is the stone. And veil yourself from him, O Sawda bint Zam'a.' (2) The saying of the female Companion for establishing: 'If she gives birth before two years, she becomes separated from him': From 'Aisha, she said, 'A woman does not exceed two years in pregnancy, not even the turning of the shadow of a spindle stick.' (3) The saying of the female Companion for establishing: 'If she gives birth at the completion of two years from the day of separation': From 'Aisha, she said, 'A woman does not exceed two years in pregnancy, not even the turning of the shadow of a spindle stick.' Commentary
This passage is a series of rulings from the matn of Al-Quduri on the establishment of a child's lineage (nasab) from women whose marriages have ended. The author presents the timeframe rules for revocably divorced (raj'iyyah), irrevocably divorced (mabtutah), and widowed women, followed by a rule for a woman who admits her 'iddah is over then gives birth. The foundational principle is the hadith 'al-waladu lil-firash' (the child belongs to the bed), meaning that during the marriage or 'iddah period, the husband's bed is the basis for lineage unless clear evidence contradicts. The maximum pregnancy period is set at two years, based on the report of 'Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her). For the revocably divorced woman, if she gives birth within two years from the divorce, the child's lineage is established to the husband and she remains in 'iddah (revocable). If she gives birth after two years, the child is also attributed to the husband but she is considered to have completed her 'iddah at the time of birth, so she is fully separated. For the irrevocably divorced woman, the child's lineage is established only if born before two years; if born at exactly two years, it is not established unless the ex-husband acknowledges it. For the widow, the child's lineage is established if born within two years of the husband's death. Regarding the woman in 'iddah who admits her 'iddah is over: if she gives birth within six months of that admission, the child is attributed to the ex-husband (since conception could have occurred during 'iddah); if at exactly six months, it is not attributed because of the possibility that conception occurred after 'iddah. The proofs include the famous hadith of the child belonging to the bed, the report of 'Aisha on maximum pregnancy, and the incident of 'Umar and 'Ali on minimum pregnancy (six months) derived from Quranic verses on suckling and pregnancy duration. Witness Requirements for Birth in 'Iddah: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on birth witness requirements

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Birth in 'iddah: Abu Hanifa requires two men or one man and two women; the Sahibayn accept one woman's testimony for ease.

This passage from al-Quduri presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on how much evidence is needed to prove that a woman in 'iddah has given birth, which then establishes the child's lineage. Abu Hanifa insists on the standard two-male or one-male-two-female witness rule, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that the private nature of childbirth necessitates accepting a single midwife's testimony. Identify the act and its legal consequence
The act is a woman in 'iddah giving birth. The legal consequence is establishing the child's lineage to the former husband.

Abu Hanifa requires two men or one man and two women to witness the birth. He cites Quran 2:282 and a statement of 'Ali.

Later Hanafi jurists prefer the Sahibayn's view for ease and practicality, especially in matters of lineage and inheritance.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the evidence required to prove that a woman in 'iddah has given birth, which in turn establishes the child's lineage to the former husband (if within the 'iddah period). According to Abu Hanifa, the default rule is that the birth of a child from a woman in 'iddah must be witnessed by either two men or one man and two women, similar to the requirement for financial transactions (based on Quran 2:282). However, if the pregnancy was externally apparent (haml zahir) or if the husband acknowledges the child, then no testimony is needed; the lineage is established automatically. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold a different view: the testimony of a single woman (e.g., a midwife) is sufficient for establishing the birth, because matters of birth are typically witnessed only by women, and requiring two male witnesses would cause undue hardship. This is based on the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) who accepted the testimony of a midwife (qabilah). The passage then provides a separate ruling: if a man marries a woman and she gives birth in less than six months from the marriage date, the child is not attributed to him (since the minimum period of pregnancy is six months per Hanafi view, derived from the incident of 'Umar and 'Ali). If she gives birth at six months or more, the child's lineage is established provided the husband acknowledges it (because the child could be from him, but the husband must confirm paternity). The supporting evidences include the hadith of the midwife (from Hudhayfah), the practice of the Companions, the verse on witnesses, and the hadith of the woman who claimed suckling (showing acceptance of a single female testimony in sensitive matters). The Urdu usul (principle) restates the minimum pregnancy duration of six months.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the evidence required to prove that a woman in 'iddah has given birth, which in turn establishes the child's lineage to the former husband (if within the 'iddah period). According to Abu Hanifa, the default rule is that the birth of a child from a woman in 'iddah must be witnessed by either two men or one man and two women, similar to the requirement for financial transactions (based on Quran 2:282). However, if the pregnancy was externally apparent (haml zahir) or if the husband acknowledges the child, then no testimony is needed; the lineage is established automatically. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold a different view: the testimony of a single woman (e.g., a midwife) is sufficient for establishing the birth, because matters of birth are typically witnessed only by women, and requiring two male witnesses would cause undue hardship. This is based on the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) who accepted the testimony of a midwife (qabilah). The passage then provides a separate ruling: if a man marries a woman and she gives birth in less than six months from the marriage date, the child is not attributed to him (since the minimum period of pregnancy is six months per Hanafi view, derived from the incident of 'Umar and 'Ali). If she gives birth at six months or more, the child's lineage is established provided the husband acknowledges it (because the child could be from him, but the husband must confirm paternity). The supporting evidences include the hadith of the midwife (from Hudhayfah), the practice of the Companions, the verse on witnesses, and the hadith of the woman who claimed suckling (showing acceptance of a single female testimony in sensitive matters). The Urdu usul (principle) restates the minimum pregnancy duration of six months.

The Shafi'i Objection and Hanafi Response on Scaling Maintenance by Wealth
Inter-madhhab dispute on maintenance scaling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Shafi'i objection that maintenance should be scaled by wealth and evaluate the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school holds maintenance fixed regardless of wealth; the Shafi'i scaling argument is rebutted by the principle that maintenance is badal for confinement.

After presenting the Hanafi proofs, the commentator anticipates the Shafi'i counter-argument. Imam al-Shafi'i cites the hadith 'Feed them from what you eat' and the verse 'Let the wealthy spend...' to argue that maintenance is proportional to the husband's means. The Hanafi response is that these texts indicate reasonableness (bi al-ma'ruf), not a scaling of the obligation itself.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Hanafi position
Maintenance is wajib regardless of the husband's wealth, established by the four proofs.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i school argues that maintenance should be scaled according to the husband's financial status (mu'tabar bi-halihima).
3
Follow the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i evidence includes the hadith 'أطعموهن مما تأكلون واكسوهن مما تكتسون' and the verse 'لينفق ذو سعة من سعته' (65:7).
These texts appear to tie the amount to the husband's capacity.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school interprets these as indicating a standard of reasonableness (bi al-ma'ruf), not a condition of wealth. The obligation remains fixed because it is compensation for ihtibas.
The principle of ihtibas is key: the compensation is for the wife's submission, not for the husband's ability.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In Hanafi, a poor husband must still provide maintenance; in Shafi'i, the amount is reduced. This affects the wife's options for separation.
The practical difference is significant in cases of persistent poverty.

Maintenance When Wife Refuses, Rebels, or Is Minor
Fiqh ruling with dalil naqli

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios affecting a wife's maintenance and apply the Hanafi rule that maintenance depends on ihtibas (confinement).

Core Idea
Maintenance is due only when the wife is confined (ihtibas); refusal for dower preserves it, rebellion cuts it.

This passage from al-Quduri’s matn, with its accompanying commentary, clarifies three special situations that alter the wife’s right to maintenance. Understanding these nuances is essential to correctly determining when a husband must provide nafaqah. The central principle is ihtibas: the wife’s availability for marital intimacy. The passage then provides proof texts to cement the rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base principle
Recognize that Hanafi maintenance is compensation for ihtibas (confinement). Without ihtibas, no obligation.
This principle appears at the end of the commentary: 'If a woman's ihtibas ceases, maintenance will not remain.'
2
Analyze the first scenario: refusal for dower
The wife refuses submission until dower is paid. This refusal is not nushuz because she is exercising a right. She still has maintenance.
Contrast this with unconditional refusal – here she has a valid reason.
3
Analyze the second scenario: nushuz (rebellion)
When the wife rebels (e.g., leaves the home without permission), she breaks ihtibas. Maintenance stops until she returns and submits.
The hadith of Fatimah bint Qays is cited as proof.
4
Analyze the third scenario: minor wife
If the wife is so young that intercourse is impossible, no ihtibas is achieved even if she submits. Hence no maintenance.
The saying of 'Ata' confirms this.
5
Examine the proofs
The commentator adduces the hadith of Fatimah bint Qays for nushuz, and sayings of Sulayman ibn Yasar, al-Sha'bi, and 'Ata' for the other rulings. These are dalil naqli that support the matn.
All proofs are from authoritative sources for the Hanafi school.

Divorce and Manumission Under Coercion Are Effective
Ruling that divorce or manumission under coercion still takes effect, with proof from hadith and a tabi'i saying.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divorce and manumission under coercion and explain the proof from the hadith of the three binding matters.

Core Idea
Divorce and freeing slaves are binding even under coercion because their seriousness exceeds jest.

In the previous lesson, we learned that coerced acts like killing or destroying property do not take effect. But divorce and manumission are different: they are so weighty that even jest makes them binding. This lesson explains why coercion does not invalidate these solemn contracts, and provides the scriptural evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and the ruling
What are the two acts mentioned? Divorce (talaq) and manumission (itq). What is the ruling? They take effect (waqa'a) even under coercion.
Notice the contrast with the previous rulings on destruction of property.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'Three matters, their seriousness is serious and their jest is serious: marriage, divorce, and taking back [the wife].' This is from Abu Dawud and Tirmidhi. It is a hadith ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear.
The hadith explicitly includes divorce; manumission is added by qiyas because of its similar gravity.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith states that even jest binds these three acts. Coercion is more serious than jest (since jest is voluntary). If jest cannot negate, coercion certainly cannot. This is an a fortiori argument (mafhūm al-muwāfaqa).
The Hanafis use this reasoning: what is true for the lesser (jest) is true for the greater (coercion).
4
Consider the tabi'i statement
Ibrahim al-Nakha'i said: 'It is valid; it is only something by which he ransomed himself.' This confirms that the coerced person intended to save his life, but the legal effect still occurs.
The term 'aftada' (ransomed) implies he chose the lesser evil, but the act itself is not void.
5
Summarize the principle
The Urdu note concludes: 'Nikah, talaq, raj'a, and 'itq – in whatever state they occur, upon coming out of the tongue they take effect.' This is a general rule: these contracts are so serious that the outward utterance is sufficient, regardless of intention or duress.
This distinguishes them from ordinary contracts like sale, where coercion may invalidate.

Permissible Looking at Mahram Women and Another's Slave Woman
Ruling on permissible looking at mahram women and another's female slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissible body parts a man may look at from his mahram women and from another's female slave, and explain the Hanafi proof for this ruling.

Core Idea
A man may see the face, head, chest, legs, and arms of his mahram women and of another's slave woman — but not the back, belly, or awrah.

This passage from al-Quduri specifies the limits of permissible looking between a man and his mahram women (those forever prohibited in marriage), as well as between a man and a female slave owned by another. The ruling defines which body parts may be seen and touched, and is supported by a hadith from Ibn Abbas regarding the purchase of a slave woman. Understanding these boundaries is essential for maintaining proper modesty in daily interactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is looking at mahram women and at another's slave woman. The default ruling is mubah (permissible) within specified limits.
The ruling applies only when there is no sexual desire; with desire it becomes haram.
2
List the permissible and prohibited parts for mahram women
From mahram women, a man may look at the face, head, chest, shanks, and upper arms. He may not look at the back or belly.
These are the explicit limits in the matn.
3
Extend the ruling to another's slave woman
A man may look at a female slave owned by another man to the same extent as he looks at his own mahram women.
The hadith supports this by allowing looking at a slave woman for purchase except her awrah (knee to navel).
4
Analyze the hadith proof and its application
The hadith specifies the awrah of a slave woman as from knee to navel, implying the rest is permissible to see. Hanafis use qiyas to extend this permission to mahram women, since both categories involve a lesser degree of modesty than non-mahram free women.
The hadith is ahad (zanni thubut) but its meaning is clear (qati dalala) for the ruling.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi and cross-madhhab differences
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad restrict mahram looking to face and head only, but Abu Hanifa's broader view is relied upon. The Shafi'i school restricts mahram looking to face and hands.
The thamarah al-khilaf is the extent of permissible physical contact and sight in family settings.

Ruling on 'Azl: Slaves and Wives
Ruling differentiating 'azl permission for slave woman vs. wife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi ruling on 'azl by identifying when permission is required and apply the evidence from the Companions and the Prophet's teachings.

Core Idea
A wife's consent is necessary for 'azl; a slave woman's is not.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in marriage law: the permissibility of coitus interruptus ('azl). Al-Quduri concisely states that a man may practice 'azl with his slave woman without her permission, but with his wife only with her permission. The distinction rests on the different rights each woman holds. We will explore the proofs from the practice of the Companions and explicit statements of the Salaf that underpin this Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
State the two rulings
Identify the ruling for the slave woman (permissible without permission) and for the wife (permissible only with permission).
2
Examine the first proof (Companion practice)
Consider the hadith of Jabir: 'We used to practice 'azl while the Qur'an was being revealed.' This shows the general permissibility of 'azl during the Prophet's lifetime, implying it was known and not prohibited.
The Hanafis use this as evidence that 'azl is permissible in principle.
3
Examine the second proof (Ibn Abbas)
Ibn Abbas explicitly differentiates: 'The free woman is asked for permission; the slave woman is not asked.' This directly supports al-Quduri's ruling.
This statement is considered a qawl sahabi in Hanafi usul, a valid source of law.
4
Examine the proofs for the wife's right
The hadith on the rights of the wife (body, eyes, spouse) and the command to be truthful and not rush her indicate the wife's right to full sexual satisfaction. Withdrawing without her consent would compromise that right.
These hadiths establish the obligation to consider the wife's needs.
5
Understand the underlying principle
A slave woman's rights are limited; she does not have the same claim to offspring or full enjoyment as a free wife. Hence, the husband does not need her permission.
This distinction is based on the status of the woman (free vs. slave).
