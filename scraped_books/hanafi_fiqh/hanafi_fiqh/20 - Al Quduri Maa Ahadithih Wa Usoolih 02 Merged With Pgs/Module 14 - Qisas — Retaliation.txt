Intentional Killing ('Amd): Ruling and Proof
Defines 'amd and its immediate consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for intentional killing ('amd) and identify the Quranic proof for qisas.

Core Idea
Intentional killing requires qisas and bears sin; no kaffarah in the Hanafi school.

The Hanafi school categorizes homicide into three types, with 'amd being the most severe. This lesson examines the definition, consequence, and primary evidence for intentional killing from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for 'amd
Read the opening statement: 'And the consequence of that is sin, and qisas, and there is no kaffarah in intentional killing according to us, unless the heirs pardon.' Note the three elements: sin, qisas, no kaffarah.
The phrase 'indana' indicates this is the Hanafi position.
2
Locate the Quranic evidence
The text cites Surah al-Baqarah 2:178, which commands qisas for homicide. Examine the verse and identify how it establishes qisas as an obligation.
The imperative 'kutiba' indicates obligation in Hanafi usul.
3
Determine the thamarah
Understand that qisas is the default, but the heirs may pardon and accept blood money. No kaffarah is required; this distinguishes the Hanafi view.
Other schools may require kaffarah for 'amd.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Jinayat in Al-Quduri presents the Hanafi classification of homicide into three categories: intentional killing ('amd), semi-intentional killing (shibh al-'amd), and killing by mistake (khata'). It states the legal consequence (mujab) for each, along with intra-madhhab differences. The author begins with the ruling for 'amd: the consequence is sin (ma'tham) and qisas (retaliation), and there is no kaffarah (expiation) according to the Hanafi school (indana) unless the heirs pardon. Then the text defines shibh al-'amd. According to Abu Hanifa, it is striking intentionally with something that is not a weapon (silah) nor something that runs the course of a weapon (ma ajriya majra al-silah), i.e., an instrument that does not usually kill. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it is striking intentionally with something that does not usually cause death (ma la yaqtulu ghaliban). Both views agree that the consequence is sin (ma'tham), but they differ on whether kaffarah is required. The text then provides proofs: for the definition of qisas, it cites Surah al-Baqarah 2:178; for Abu Hanifa's definition of shibh al-'amd, it cites the hadith 'Everything is a mistake except the sword' from al-Nu'man ibn Bashir; for the Sahibayn's definition, it cites the statement of 'Ali: 'The killed by whip and stick is shibh 'amd.' Further proofs for the consequence of sin are given from Surah al-Nisa' 4:93 (for 'amd) and the hadith of the two women from Hudhayl. The second part of the passage covers killing by mistake (khata'). It is defined as having two types: mistake in intention (khata' fi al-qasd) — aiming at a person thinking it is game, and mistake in action (khata' fi al-fi'l) — aiming at a target but hitting a person. The consequence is kaffarah (freeing a slave or fasting) and blood money (diyah) on the 'aqilah (tribal guarantors), with no sin. Proofs include the statement of 'Abdullah ibn Mas'ud and 'Ali regarding the division of blood money, and the verse Surah al-Nisa' 4:92. This passage highlights the Hanafi methodology of using both textual evidence and rational distinctions to classify homicide and determine penalties.

Semi-Intentional Killing (Shibh al-'Amd): Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Defines shibh al-'amd with two Hanafi views and their proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi internal dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the definition of shibh al-'amd and identify the practical consequence of this difference.

Core Idea
Shibh al-'amd: striking intentionally with a non-lethal instrument; sin and diyah mughallazah on 'aqilah, but no qisas.

The category of shibh al-'amd (semi-intentional killing) is unique to Hanafi fiqh and involves a disagreement within the school. Abu Hanifa and his two students define it differently, leading to a difference in whether kaffarah is required. This lesson unpacks the definitions, proofs, and practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Define shibh al-'amd according to Abu Hanifa
Read: 'Shibh al-'amd according to Abu Hanifa is that he intentionally strikes him with something that is not a weapon and not that which runs the course of a weapon.' Identify the key elements: intent + instrument not normally lethal.
Weapons include swords, knives, guns; non-weapons include sticks, whips, fists.
2
Define shibh al-'amd according to the Sahibayn
Read: 'Shibh al-'amd according to them is that he intentionally strikes him with what does not usually kill.' Note the different standard: not the nature of the instrument but the usual outcome.
Sahibayn's definition is broader; it includes any act that is not normally fatal.
3
Identify the proofs for each view
Abu Hanifa cites the hadith: 'Everything is a mistake except the sword.' The Sahibayn cite the statement of 'Ali: 'The killed by whip and stick is shibh 'amd.'
Both proofs are used to support the respective definitions within the Hanafi school.
4
Understand the thamarah (practical consequence)
Both views agree on sin and diyah mughallazah on 'aqilah, but differ on kaffarah: Abu Hanifa says no kaffarah; Sahibayn say kaffarah is wajib.
No qisas in shibh al-'amd according to all.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Jinayat in Al-Quduri presents the Hanafi classification of homicide into three categories: intentional killing ('amd), semi-intentional killing (shibh al-'amd), and killing by mistake (khata'). It states the legal consequence (mujab) for each, along with intra-madhhab differences. The author begins with the ruling for 'amd: the consequence is sin (ma'tham) and qisas (retaliation), and there is no kaffarah (expiation) according to the Hanafi school (indana) unless the heirs pardon. Then the text defines shibh al-'amd. According to Abu Hanifa, it is striking intentionally with something that is not a weapon (silah) nor something that runs the course of a weapon (ma ajriya majra al-silah), i.e., an instrument that does not usually kill. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it is striking intentionally with something that does not usually cause death (ma la yaqtulu ghaliban). Both views agree that the consequence is sin (ma'tham), but they differ on whether kaffarah is required. The text then provides proofs: for the definition of qisas, it cites Surah al-Baqarah 2:178; for Abu Hanifa's definition of shibh al-'amd, it cites the hadith 'Everything is a mistake except the sword' from al-Nu'man ibn Bashir; for the Sahibayn's definition, it cites the statement of 'Ali: 'The killed by whip and stick is shibh 'amd.' Further proofs for the consequence of sin are given from Surah al-Nisa' 4:93 (for 'amd) and the hadith of the two women from Hudhayl. The second part of the passage covers killing by mistake (khata'). It is defined as having two types: mistake in intention (khata' fi al-qasd) — aiming at a person thinking it is game, and mistake in action (khata' fi al-fi'l) — aiming at a target but hitting a person. The consequence is kaffarah (freeing a slave or fasting) and blood money (diyah) on the 'aqilah (tribal guarantors), with no sin. Proofs include the statement of 'Abdullah ibn Mas'ud and 'Ali regarding the division of blood money, and the verse Surah al-Nisa' 4:92. This passage highlights the Hanafi methodology of using both textual evidence and rational distinctions to classify homicide and determine penalties.

Killing by Mistake (Khata'): Definition and Ruling
Defines khata' and its two subtypes with rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of killing by mistake (khata' fi al-qasd and khata' fi al-fi'l) and state the legal consequences (kaffarah, diyah on 'aqilah, no sin).

Core Idea
Killing by mistake carries no sin but requires kaffarah and diyah on the 'aqilah; qisas is not applicable.

The third category of homicide is killing by mistake (khata'). This lesson covers its two subtypes—mistake in intention and mistake in action—and the legal obligations that follow: expiation (kaffarah) and blood money (diyah) on the tribal guarantors ('aqilah). The evidence from Quran and Sunnah is provided.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of khata'
Read: 'Killing by mistake is of two types: mistake in intention (khata' fi al-qasd) and mistake in action (khata' fi al-fi'l).' Understand the distinction: the first involves intending an act on the wrong subject; the second involves an unintended outcome of a lawful act.
Examples: shooting at a deer that turns out to be a person (mistake in intention); shooting at a target but hitting a person (mistake in action).
2
Identify the consequences of khata'
The text states: 'And the consequence of that is kaffarah, and the blood money on the 'aqilah, and no sin in it.' Note three elements: kaffarah (expiation), diyah (blood money) on the 'aqilah (not the killer), and no sin.
No qisas because there was no intent.
3
Locate the evidence for khata'
The text cites Surah al-Nisa' 4:92 for the obligation of kaffarah and diyah, and the hadith of the two women from Hudhayl (Bukhari) for diyah on 'aqilah.
Also statements of 'Abdullah ibn Mas'ud and 'Ali on the division of diyah.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Jinayat in Al-Quduri presents the Hanafi classification of homicide into three categories: intentional killing ('amd), semi-intentional killing (shibh al-'amd), and killing by mistake (khata'). It states the legal consequence (mujab) for each, along with intra-madhhab differences. The author begins with the ruling for 'amd: the consequence is sin (ma'tham) and qisas (retaliation), and there is no kaffarah (expiation) according to the Hanafi school (indana) unless the heirs pardon. Then the text defines shibh al-'amd. According to Abu Hanifa, it is striking intentionally with something that is not a weapon (silah) nor something that runs the course of a weapon (ma ajriya majra al-silah), i.e., an instrument that does not usually kill. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it is striking intentionally with something that does not usually cause death (ma la yaqtulu ghaliban). Both views agree that the consequence is sin (ma'tham), but they differ on whether kaffarah is required. The text then provides proofs: for the definition of qisas, it cites Surah al-Baqarah 2:178; for Abu Hanifa's definition of shibh al-'amd, it cites the hadith 'Everything is a mistake except the sword' from al-Nu'man ibn Bashir; for the Sahibayn's definition, it cites the statement of 'Ali: 'The killed by whip and stick is shibh 'amd.' Further proofs for the consequence of sin are given from Surah al-Nisa' 4:93 (for 'amd) and the hadith of the two women from Hudhayl. The second part of the passage covers killing by mistake (khata'). It is defined as having two types: mistake in intention (khata' fi al-qasd) — aiming at a person thinking it is game, and mistake in action (khata' fi al-fi'l) — aiming at a target but hitting a person. The consequence is kaffarah (freeing a slave or fasting) and blood money (diyah) on the 'aqilah (tribal guarantors), with no sin. Proofs include the statement of 'Abdullah ibn Mas'ud and 'Ali regarding the division of blood money, and the verse Surah al-Nisa' 4:92. This passage highlights the Hanafi methodology of using both textual evidence and rational distinctions to classify homicide and determine penalties.

Key Terms
الخطأ (al-khata'): Killing by mistake, divided into mistake in intention and mistake in action.
العاقلة (al-'aqilah): The paternal male relatives (or modern equivalent) who pay blood money on behalf of the killer.

Qisas Between Free, Slave, Muslim and Dhimmi
Hanafi ruling with opposing evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas across categories (free/slave, Muslim/dhimmi) and evaluate the evidence used to rebut the Shafi'i objection.

Core Idea
A free person is executed for killing a slave; a Muslim for a dhimmi—sanctity of life is equal.

This passage addresses a critical point of disagreement between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: the scope of qisas when killer and victim are not of the same legal category. The Hanafi school holds that a free person can be executed for murdering a slave, and a Muslim for murdering a dhimmi, based on specific hadiths. The Shafi'i school restricts qisas to equals in freedom and religion, citing the general Quranic verse. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi method of balancing general and specific texts.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact rulings given: free person killed for slave, and Muslim killed for dhimmi. Note that qisas is for the master of the slave, and the slave can also be killed for the free.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection and their proofs
Shafi'i argues that qisas requires equality (mumathala) between killer and victim. They cite Quran 2:178 ('free for free, slave for slave') and a hadith from Ibn Abbas: 'A free person is not killed for a slave.'
Shafi'i considers these texts clear and general.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to each proof
Hanafis respond: the Quranic verse is general, but specific hadith (Samurah, Ibn Umar) specify that a slave's life is protected by qisas. The hadith of Ibn Abbas is deemed weak, and they prefer the hadith of Ibn Umar where the Prophet executed a Muslim for a dhimmi. They also cite the practice of Ali (ra).
Hanafis weigh the hadith of Samurah as explicit and strong.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: In Hanafi law, killing a slave or dhimmi intentionally incurs qisas; in Shafi'i law, only diyah (blood money) applies unless the victim is a free Muslim.
This affects sentencing and compensation in criminal cases.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preference)
The Hanafi position is relied upon for fatwa. The tarjih is based on the authenticity and specificity of the supporting hadiths, and the weakness of the opposing hadith according to Hanafi hadith criticism.
This shows Hanafi usul: specific ('amm) can be specified (takhsis) by a hadith.

Qisas Across Social and Religious Categories: The Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute
Inter-madhhab dispute on qisas categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas for killing across free/slave and Muslim/dhimmi lines, explain the Hanafi hadith evidence, and identify the Shafi'i counter-argument and its rebuttal.

Core Idea
A free man is killed for a slave, and a Muslim for a dhimmi — the Hanafi school prioritises prophetic practice over a literal reading of Quran 2:178.

This lesson examines one of the most well-known inter-madhhab disputes in the law of qisas (retaliation). The Hanafi school holds that a free man may be executed for killing a slave, and a Muslim for killing a dhimmi. The Shafi'i school disagrees, relying on the apparent meaning of Quran 2:178 and a hadith from Ibn Abbas. The passage presents the evidence on both sides and shows how the Hanafi school evaluates and weighs the proofs.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the cases mentioned in the matn: free for free, free for slave (qisas to the master), slave for free, slave for slave, and Muslim for dhimmi.
Note that qisas for a slave goes to the master, not to the state.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Shafi'i school argues that qisas only applies within the same category: free for free, slave for slave, female for female.
They deny qisas between a free man and a slave, and between a Muslim and a dhimmi.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i cite Quran 2:178 and the hadith from Ibn Abbas: 'A free man is not killed for a slave.' Understand why these texts seem to support their view.
The Quranic verse is read restrictively, and the Ibn Abbas hadith is taken as a general principle.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds by: (a) arguing that the Quranic verse is general and does not prohibit cross-category qisas, (b) treating the Ibn Abbas hadith as weak (da'if), and (c) presenting stronger hadiths from Samurah and Ibn Umar that explicitly allow cross-category qisas.
The hadith from Ibn Umar reports that the Prophet himself killed a Muslim for a mu'ahid (dhimmi).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Hanafi law, qisas is applied in these cross-category cases; under Shafi'i law, only diyah (blood money) is due.
This affects the life of the offender and the rights of the victim's family.

Qisas: Musta'min and Equality Across Gender, Age, and Health
Rulings on qisas for musta'min and equality in qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on qisas regarding the musta'min and the equality between genders, ages, and health conditions, and cite the Quranic and hadith evidence.

Qisas Exceptions: Father for Son and Master for Slave
Rulings that a father is not killed for his son nor a master for his slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five exceptions to qisas for a father and master, cite the three hadith proofs, and explain the underlying rationale based on familial authority and ownership.

Core Idea
A father's authority and a master's ownership bar qisas, requiring diyah instead.

Qisas (retributive justice) is the norm for intentional murder, but Shari'ah creates exceptions based on the elevated status of fatherhood and the master's ownership over his slave. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that a man is not killed for his son, nor for his slave, mudabbir, mukatab, or the slave of his child. The proofs from Sunnah and underlying principles (like 'you and your property belong to your father') show that these relationships modify the usual qisas obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Exceptions
Read the matn: 'The man is not killed for his son, nor for his slave, nor for his mudabbir, nor for his mukatab, nor for the slave of his child.' List these five cases. Note that the man here refers to the father or master as the killer.
The repetition 'nor for his slave' in the Arabic is likely a scribal error; one occurrence suffices.
2
Analyze the First Hadith Proof
Examine the hadith from Ibn Abbas: 'The father is not killed for the son.' This is a direct prohibition. Note its chain (Tirmidhi, Ibn Majah) and classify it as ahad. Determine that it establishes the father-son exception explicitly.
3
Analyze the Second Hadith Proof
The hadith of Amr ibn Shu'ayb: a man killed his slave intentionally. The Prophet did not apply qisas but flogged, exiled, and required freeing a slave. This shows the master-slave exception. Note that qisas is replaced by ta'zir and diyah.
4
Analyze the Third Hadith Proof
The hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father' and 'your children are among your best earnings' indicates the father's ownership-like authority over his son's life and property. This underpins the exception: the father is not killed because he has a right over the son.
5
State the Ruling and Rationale
Combine the proofs: qisas is not wajib for these cases; instead diyah (blood money) is wajib. The rationale is the high status of fatherhood (parental authority) and the master's complete ownership over the slave, making execution contradictory to that relationship.
The victim must have 'ismat al-dar' (sanctity of life) for qisas generally, but here the killer's relationship overrides it.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Qisas is not due when a musta'min is killed; the Quran prescribes diyah and expiation.

Why does the Hanafi school exempt the killer of a musta'min from retaliatory execution? The ruling is rooted in Allah's command in Surah An-Nisa, which treats the killing of a person from a treaty community differently from killing a believer. This lesson unpacks that verse and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Read the opening statement: 'A Muslim is not killed for a musta'min.' The act is killing a musta'min; the ruling is that qisas (retaliatory execution) is not wajib; instead diyah is wajib.

The text cites Surah An-Nisa 4:92. Read the verse carefully. It addresses unintentional killing of a believer and then mentions a person from a treaty people (qawm baynakum wa baynahum mithaq).
The verse is from the Quran, which is qat'i al-thubut.
3
Analyze the content of the verse
Identify what the verse prescribes: diyah (blood money) and freeing a believing slave, or fasting if unable. There is no mention of qisas.
The absence of qisas is significant.
4
Derive the ruling from the verse
The Hanafi school applies the principle that when the Quran specifies a penalty without mentioning qisas, qisas is not required. Since the verse mentions only diyah for a treaty person, the same applies to a musta'min by extension (they share the protected status).
This is an istinbat (derivation) based on the text's silence on qisas.
5
Apply to the ruling
Conclusion: The killer must pay diyah and perform kaffarah (expiation) but is not executed. This is the mufta bihi (operative) position.
The Shafi'i school disagrees; that will be covered in the next lesson.

Hadith evidence and cross-madhhab dispute

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to cite the hadith evidence for the Hanafi position and explain the Shafi'i disagreement on qisas for a musta'min.

Core Idea
The hadith 'A Muslim is not killed for a disbeliever' is the direct sunna proof for no qisas.

In addition to the Quranic verse, the Prophet ﷺ explicitly stated that a Muslim is not executed for a disbeliever. This lesson examines that hadith and the cross-madhhab dispute over its application to the musta'min.

Read the hadith evidence
The author cites the hadith: 'A Muslim is not killed for a disbeliever.' This is a general statement from the Prophet ﷺ recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sunan Abi Dawud.
The hadith is authentic (sahih) and used as direct evidence.

The hadith explicitly prohibits executing a Muslim for killing a disbeliever. Since a musta'min is a disbeliever (kafir), he falls under this general rule.
The Hanafi school takes this hadith as 'amm (general) and binding.
3
Note the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i school holds that a Muslim can be killed for a musta'min. They argue that the hadith may be specific to certain disbelievers or abrogated, or they give priority to other texts.
The source does not detail the Shafi'i proofs, but the disagreement is recorded.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
Hanafis maintain that the hadith is general and not abrogated. They also rely on the Quranic verse (4:92) which prescribes diyah, reinforcing the hadith.
The Hanafi position is that both proofs are consistent.
5
Practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In a Hanafi court, the killer of a musta'min pays diyah. In a Shafi'i court, he would be executed. This difference affects real cases.
Understanding ikhtilaf helps in legal interpretation.

Inequality of parties prevents qisas for wounds below the soul; only diyah is due.

This passage from Kitab al-Hinayat addresses a key point of legal dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is regarding retaliation for non-fatal wounds. The Hanafi school holds that qisas cannot apply when the parties are not equal in gender or legal status, while Shafi'is argue the general Quranic verse mandates qisas regardless. Qisas for Limbs Between Muslim and Non-Muslim
Obligates qisas for limbs between Muslim and non-Muslim with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that qisas for limbs is wajib between Muslim and non-Muslim and identify the primary proofs (action of Umar and the hadith from Bukhari) that support this ruling.

Core Idea
Qisas for limbs is wajib between Muslim and non-Muslim—the practice of Umar and the hadith on killing prove it.

This lesson examines a distinctive Hanafi ruling from the Book of Jinayat in Al-Quduri: that qisas (retaliation) for limbs applies not only between Muslims but also between a Muslim and a non-Muslim. While other schools restrict qisas to cases with equal faith status, the Hanafi school, relying on the practice of the Caliph Umar and the implications of a well-known hadith, holds that a Muslim who injures a non-Muslim's limb must submit to the same injury in return. We will explore the ruling, its proofs, and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage states that qisas is wajib for limbs between a Muslim and a non-Muslim. What exactly is being ruled? The act: deliberate injury to a limb. The ruling: qisas (retaliation in kind) is obligatory.
2
Locate the first proof
The action of Umar ibn al-Khattab: he ordered a Muslim (Ubadah ibn al-Samit) to submit to qisas for wounding a dhimmi. This is a qawl sahabi (Companion's statement/action) that serves as evidence.
The full story is in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, no. 27869.
3
Locate the second proof
The hadith from Bukhari (no. 6915): 'A Muslim is not killed for a non-Muslim.' The Hanafi school uses its negative implication (mafhum): since the hadith only negates qisas for killing, it implies that for injuries less than killing (limbs), qisas does apply.
This is an ahad hadith, so it is zanni al-thubut.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Al-Shafi'i holds that qisas is not wajib between Muslim and non-Muslim for limbs; the Hanafi view is the mufta bihi. The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is that in a Hanafi jurisdiction, a Muslim who injures a non-Muslim's limb must face qisas, whereas in a Shafi'i jurisdiction, only diyah (blood money) is due.
5
Evaluate the strength of evidence
Both proofs are zanni in transmission and meaning, but together they establish the ruling as wajib. The practice of Umar is a strong precedent and the hadith's mafhum supports it. The Hanafi school gives weight to sahabi practice and mafhum al-muwafaqah.

Option When Offender's Hand is Defective: Qisas or Arsh?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the victim's option when the offender's hand is defective and explain the legal basis using qawl al-tabi'i.

Core Idea
When exact retaliation is impossible due to defect, the victim chooses between defective qisas or full arsh.

This passage addresses a nuanced situation in qisas: the offender's limb is defective, making exact retaliation unequal. The Hanafi school grants the victim a choice – either execute qisas on the defective limb or demand full monetary compensation. The ruling is anchored in statements from the Tabi'i Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, establishing that when qisas is not possible, the obligation shifts to the offender's wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the specific scenario
The act is cutting a hand from half the forearm or a penetrating wound. The scenario involves a healthy victim and an offender with a defective hand (paralyzed or missing fingers). The ruling also applies to jai'fah wounds.
The defect is in the offender's limb, not the victim's.
2
State the victim's option
The victim has two choices: (1) cut the defective hand and receive nothing extra, or (2) take full arsh (compensation) without any qisas.
The option is the victim's right; the offender must submit to either.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites two statements from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i: (1) any intentional wound where qisas is impossible obligates payment from the offender's wealth, not the aqila; (2) the arsh for a broken limb that heals without deficiency is 180 dirhams.
These are qawl al-tabi'i, considered zanni al-thubut but relied upon in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Understand the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
The first statement establishes the principle: when exact retaliation is impossible, compensation replaces qisas. The second provides a benchmark for full arsh, confirming that the victim can demand the complete amount even if the offender's limb is defective.
This avoids injustice: the victim should not suffer loss due to the offender's defect.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools require only monetary compensation when qisas is impossible; they do not allow qisas on a defective limb. Hanafi permits it to give the victim full satisfaction.
The practical consequence: the Hanafi option includes both defective qisas and full arsh.

Settlement and Forgiveness in Qisas: The Binding Effect of One Heir's Action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on financial settlement in qisas and explain why forgiveness by one joint heir drops qisas for all, citing the hadith and athar as proofs.

Core Idea
Qisas is a single joint right; one heir's forgiveness drops it entirely, converting to diyah.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents two related rulings on qisas. First, if the killer and all heirs reach a financial settlement, qisas drops and the agreed sum becomes wajib. Second, if there are multiple heirs and one of them forgives or settles from his share, qisas drops for all remaining heirs, though they still receive their share of diyah. This second ruling is based on the principle that the right to qisas is indivisible. We will examine the textual proofs and the disagreement with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the Arabic carefully: the first line covers settlement by all heirs; the second covers forgiveness or settlement by one heir among multiple. State each ruling concisely.

2
Understand the legal basis for the second ruling
The Hanafi school treats the right to qisas as a single, indivisible right (haqq wahid). If one co-heir waives his part, the right is no longer complete, so it drops entirely and transforms into a claim for diyah.
This is opposed to the Shafi'i view that each heir has an individual share.

This is a qawl sahabi, also zanni, but reinforces the hadith.

Al-Shafi'i argues by qiyas that each heir owns an independent share of qisas, so one forgiving only drops his share. The Hanafi response is that the hadith and athar show a different nature of the right, supported by the principle of jointness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a deliberate shot that kills two, distinguishing between intentional and accidental killing in the liability for qisas and diyah.

Core Idea
Intentional killing demands qisas; accidental killing demands diyah on the 'aqilah — one act can yield both.

This lesson examines a classic scenario from al-Quduri's matn: a single deliberate arrow kills two people. The first victim is killed intentionally, the second accidentally. You will learn how the Hanafi school categorizes each death and assigns liability — qisas for the intentional victim, and diyah paid by the 'aqilah for the accidental victim. The proof from the Sunnah solidifies this distinction.

Guided Path
1

Read the matn: someone deliberately shoots at one man, and the arrow passes through him to kill another. Both die. Recognize that there is one actor, one arrow, but two separate deaths.

2

For the first victim, the death is qatl 'amd (intentional murder) because the shooter aimed at him. For the second victim, the death is qatl khata' (accidental killing) because the shooter did not intend to hit him.
The classification determines the type of liability.

For intentional killing, the ruler imposes qisas (retributive execution) on the killer. For accidental killing, the killer's 'aqilah (male paternal relatives) must pay diyah (blood money) to the victim's heirs.
The 'aqilah are liable because the killing was not deliberate; the community shares the burden.

The hadith of the two women from Hudhayl (Bukhari and Muslim) is cited: one woman threw a stone killing another and her fetus. The Prophet ruled diyah for the fetus (a slave) and diyah for the woman on the thrower's 'aqilah. This supports the principle that accidental killing requires diyah on the 'aqilah, not qisas.
The qiyas: the arrow passing through is analogous to the stone that kills two.

Delay of Qisas for Wounds Until Healing
Matn ruling on postponing qisas until victim's wound heals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the timing of qisas for wounds and identify the underlying rationale based on the hadith of Jabir.

Core Idea
Qisas for a wound must wait until the wound heals to ensure just equivalence.

This lesson presents a fundamental procedural rule in the law of qisas (retaliation) for wounds. The matn of al-Quduri states that the injurer cannot be subjected to retaliatory wounding until the victim's wound has fully healed. This precaution ensures that the retribution is precisely equivalent to the original injury. The ruling is supported by a hadith from Jabir ibn Abdullah where the Prophet ﷺ delayed qisas until the wound healed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wounding another person (jarh). The ruling is that qisas (retaliatory wound) is not performed until the victim's wound heals (yabra'a).
2
Understand the reason for delay
The delay is necessary to determine the exact nature and extent of the injury. If the wound worsens or the victim dies, the case changes from wound qisas to homicide liability.
The rationale is 'like for like' precision.
3
Recall the supporting hadith
The proof is a hadith from Jabir where a man stabbed another, and the Prophet ﷺ delayed the qisas until the victim healed. This establishes the Sunnah basis.
Although the Arabic text of the hadith is not in the matn, it is cited in the commentary.
4
Memorize the matn
Learn the Arabic phrase: 'wa man jaraha rajulan jirahatan lam yuqtass minhu hatta yabra'a' as a concise rule.
This is a memorization anchor.
