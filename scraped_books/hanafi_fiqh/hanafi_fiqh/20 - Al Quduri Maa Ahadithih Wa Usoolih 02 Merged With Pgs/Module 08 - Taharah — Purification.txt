Place of Iddah and Ihdad for Widow and Divorcee
Ruling with proof on iddah residence and ihdad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on where a woman in iddah must reside and what ihdad entails, identify the Quranic and hadith proofs, and apply the exceptions for necessity.

Core Idea
A woman in iddah must stay in the marital home unless necessity forces her out; ihdad is wajib for widows only.

This lesson examines the obligations of a woman during her iddah period after divorce or death of her husband. The Hanafi school holds that she must remain in the home where she was residing at the time of separation, and a widow must also observe ihdad (mourning) by abstaining from perfume and adornment. The proofs include clear Quranic commands and prophetic instructions, with limited exceptions for genuine hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two obligations
Recognize that the passage addresses both the place of iddah (where to stay) and ihdad (mourning practices).
These are separate but related obligations for a widow; a divorcee only has the place obligation.
2
State the default ruling
The woman must observe iddah in the house attributed to her by residence at the time of separation or death.
This applies to both widow and divorcee.
3
Identify the exception
She may leave only if there is a valid excuse (e.g., the house is insufficient or dangerous).
The passage gives the example of her share being insufficient and heirs expelling her.
4
Define ihdad
Ihdad is the obligation for a widow to abandon perfume and adornment during her iddah.
This is only for widows, not divorcees.
5
Examine the proofs
The proofs are Quranic verses (2:234, 65:1), the hadith of Zaynab bint Ka'b, and the explanation of Aisha regarding Fatima bint Qays.
These establish the binding nature of the obligation.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof from the Kitab al-Idda. It states two obligations: (1) the iddah must be observed in the house where the woman was living when the marriage ended, whether by divorce or death; (2) ihdad (mourning) for a widow requires abstaining from perfume and adornment. The general rule is that she cannot leave the house except for a valid excuse such as an emergency or necessity. The author then provides proofs: Quranic verses from Surat al-Baqarah (2:234) for the widow's iddah and from Surat al-Talaq (65:1) for not expelling her; the hadith of Zaynab bint Ka'b that teaches a widow must stay in her home even if she lacks housing and maintenance; and the saying of Ibn Umar that a widow or irrevocably divorced woman must stay in her house. An exception is then made: if her share of the deceased's house is insufficient and the heirs expel her from their share, she may move. This is supported by Aisha's explanation of the concession given to Fatima bint Qays, who was in a dangerous location, and the practice of Umar who moved Umm Kulthum from the governor's house. The Hanafi position is that staying in the original home is wajib (obligatory) for both divorced and widowed women during iddah, and moving is only permitted for genuine hardship.

Key Terms
العِدَة (al-iddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of her husband before remarrying.
الإحْدَاد (al-ihdad): The mourning practice for a widow, which includes abstaining from perfume, adornment, and beautification.

Rulings on Found Bodies and Qasamah in the Hanafi School
Rulings on qasamah: bodies found in water or on land, accusations, and testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on liability for a found body based on location (floating vs. stuck) and explain the effect of the guardian's accusation on qasamah.

Core Idea
A body found floating carries no liability; a body stuck on the bank ties liability to the nearest village, and qasamah hinges on the guardian's accusation.

In the Hanafi school, the discovery of a slain body triggers a specific legal process called qasamah (oaths of compurgation). The location of the body and the identity of the accused determine who is liable and whether oaths are required. This passage from Al-Quduri's matn sets out the key rules, supported by hadith from Bayhaqi and Abu Dawud. Understanding these rulings is essential for applying the law of homicide and blood money.

Guided Path
1
Identify location scenarios
Read the first two rulings: if the body is found floating in flowing water, it is of no consequence (hader). If it is stuck on the bank, the nearest village is liable for diyah.
The key distinction is whether the water carries the body or not.
2
Analyze the effect of the guardian's accusation
If the guardian (wali) accuses a specific person from the locality, qasamah is not dropped from the entire locality; they must all swear. If he accuses an outsider, qasamah and diyah are dropped from the locals.
This protects the locality from false accusations.
3
Understand the oath wording
If the person required to swear says 'X killed him,' he must swear by Allah that he did not kill and that he only knows X as the killer.
This oath fulfills the requirement without admitting guilt.
4
Recognize the testimony rule
Two witnesses from the locality testifying against an outsider are not accepted due to suspicion of partiality.
This prevents misuse of local testimony.
5
Connect to hadith proofs
The hadith about measuring distance to determine the nearest community supports the ruling for a stuck body. The hadith of Sahl b. Abi Hathma supports the procedure when the guardian accuses a specific person.
These hadith are ahad but are accepted as proof in this context.

Chapter of Qasamah. If the body is found in the middle of the Euphrates with water passing over it, it is of no consequence. If it is stuck on the bank, then it is upon the nearest village from that place. If the guardian claims the killing against a specific person from the locality, the qasamah is not dropped from them; if he claims against someone else, the qasamah is dropped from them, and the diyah (is also dropped?). Commentary
This passage is from the chapter on qasamah (oaths of compurgation) in Al-Quduri's matn. It presents several rulings regarding the discovery of a slain body and how liability is determined. First, if a body is found floating in a flowing river (like the middle of the Euphrates), no one is liable because the water carries it; it is considered 'Hader' (forfeit). Second, if the body is stuck on the riverbank, the nearest village is responsible for paying the diyah (blood money), based on a hadith where the Prophet measured the distance to determine which community was closer. Third, if the guardian (wali) accuses a specific individual from that locality, the oath (qasamah) is not dropped from all the inhabitants—they must still swear that neither they nor their accused killed the victim. However, if he accuses someone outside the locality, the inhabitants are absolved of qasamah and potentially diyah. Fourth, if the person required to swear says 'X killed him,' he must swear that he himself did not kill and that he only knows X as the killer. Fifth, if two witnesses from the locality testify against a man from outside the locality, their testimony is not accepted due to suspicion (tuhmah) of partiality or enmity. The commentary includes hadith from Bayhaqi and Abu Dawud supporting these rulings, and Urdu principles summarizing the key points.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasāmah): Oaths of compurgation; a legal procedure where inhabitants of a locality swear that they did not kill a found victim, thereby avoiding liability for diyah.
الدية (ad-diyah): Blood money; monetary compensation paid for homicide or bodily harm, mandated in cases of accidental killing or quasi-intentional killing.
المحلة (al-mahallah): The locality or quarter where the victim is found; the inhabitants may be subject to qasamah and diyah if the body is found in their area.

Conditions and Nisaab for Amputation in Theft
Ruling statement with proofs from Qur'an and Hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for the hadd of amputation and identify the nisaab value from the proofs.

Core Idea
Amputation requires a mature, sane thief, a nisaab of ten dirhams, a secure place, and no ambiguity.

The Hanafi school prescribes the hadd of amputation for theft, but not for every theft. It is a severe punishment that requires precise conditions. This lesson examines the ruling from Al-Quduri's Matn, including the nisaab (minimum value) and the essential prerequisites, supported by Qur'an and Sunnah proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is theft (sariqah) meeting specific conditions; the ruling is the obligation of amputation (wajib al-qat').
The ruling is not general; it only becomes wajib when all conditions are satisfied.
2
List the conditions
From the matn: the thief must be baligh (mature) and 'aqil (sane); the stolen property must be worth ten dirhams or its equivalent; it must be taken from a hirz (properly secured place); and there must be no shubha (legal ambiguity) in the property. The ruling applies equally to free persons and slaves.
These conditions ensure the punishment is not applied hastily; each is derived from the Sunnah.
3
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is Qur'an 5:38: 'And the thief, male and female, amputate their hands.' This is supplemented by hadiths: Ibn Abbas narrates the Prophet cut for a shield worth a dinar or ten dirhams; another Ibn Abbas statement fixes nisaab as the price of a shield (ten dirhams); and Rafi' reports the Prophet said no amputation for fruit or palm pith (trivial items).
The Qur'an provides the obligation in general; the Sunnah specifies the nisaab and conditions.
4
Understand the dalala (indication)
The imperative 'faqta'u' in the verse indicates obligation (wujub) per Hanafi usul. The verse does not detail nisaab or conditions, so the Sunnah clarifies that the nisaab is ten dirhams and that the theft must be from hirz and without shubha.
This is a classic example of the Qur'an being mujmal (general) and the Sunnah providing bayan (explanation).
5
Note cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
While the Hanafi school sets nisaab at ten dirhams, Imam al-Shafi'i sets it at quarter dinar (approx. 1.125 dirhams) and Imam Malik at three dirhams. The practical effect is that more thefts qualify for amputation in other schools. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi based on the stronger evidenced nisaab.
Understanding this ikhtilaf helps in comparative fiqh and when advising in multi-madhhab contexts.

The Nisaab for Amputation: Inter-Madhhab Proofs
Presents the proofs of Shafi'i and Maliki on the nisaab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanafi positions on the nisaab for amputation and explain the Hanafi evidence that establishes ten dirhams as the threshold.

Core Idea
The nisaab is ten dirhams in Hanafi law, based on stronger hadith evidence.

In the previous lesson, we learned that the Hanafi school sets the nisaab for amputation at ten dirhams. Now we examine the proofs of the other schools. Imam al-Shafi'i relies on a hadith from Aisha that sets the threshold at a quarter dinar, while Imam Malik relies on a hadith from Ibn Umar that mentions three dirhams. The Hanafi response, though not fully detailed in this passage, is rooted in the hadith of Ibn Abbas and the practice of the Companions. Understanding this dispute is crucial for correctly applying the hadd punishment.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Recall the Hanafi ruling: the nisaab for amputation is ten dirhams (or equivalent value). This is based on the hadith of Ibn Abbas and the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs.
This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i school cites the hadith of Aisha: 'The hand is cut for a quarter dinar and above.' Identify the text and understand that quarter dinar is roughly 2.5 dirhams at the time.
The hadith is considered sahih by many scholars.
3
Identify the Maliki proof
Imam Malik relies on the hadith of Ibn Umar: the Prophet cut for a shield worth three dirhams. This sets a lower threshold than the Hanafi one.
The shield (junnah) was a common item in transactions.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school counters that the hadith of Ibn Abbas (shield worth a dinar or ten dirhams) is more explicit and supported by the consensus of the Companions. The Shafi'i hadith is interpreted in the context of the time when a dinar equaled ten dirhams, so quarter dinar equals 2.5 dirhams, but the settled nisaab remained ten. The Maliki hadith may be abrogated or refer to a specific shield.
The Hanafi response prioritizes the hadith of Ibn Abbas and the established practice of the Companions.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: a theft of 4 dirhams triggers amputation in Shafi'i law (since 4 > 2.5) and possibly in Maliki law (since 4 > 3), but in Hanafi law it falls short of ten dirhams, so only ta'zir (discretionary punishment) applies.
This difference affects actual sentencing and underscores the importance of knowing the correct nisaab.

Establishing the Hadd of Theft: Confession and Testimony
States that amputation is established by one confession or two witnesses, with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two methods of proving theft for amputation and identify the proofs from the Sunnah and the practice of the Companions.

Core Idea
A single confession or two just witnesses suffices for the hadd of theft.

In this passage, the great Hanafi jurist Al-Quduri succinctly states the foundational ruling on how the ḥadd penalty for theft becomes obligatory. He then anchors it with two authoritative proofs: the direct practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the implementation by Caliph ʿAlī (may Allah be pleased with him). This lesson will walk you through the ruling, its two methods of proof, and the underlying evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its two bases
State that amputation (al-qaṭʿ) is obligatory (yajibu) when proven. Note the two pillars of proof: (1) the thief's own confession, and (2) the testimony of two male witnesses.
The confession must be voluntary; the witnesses must be male, free, and upright (ʿadl).
2
Analyze the first proof – the Prophetic hadith
Read the hadith of Muhammad ibn ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn Thawbān: a thief was brought to the Prophet ﷺ, he confessed once, and the Prophet ordered amputation. This directly shows that a single confession is sufficient.
The hadith is clear and not contradicted by any stronger evidence in the Hanafi view.
3
Analyze the second proof – the practice of ʿAlī
Examine the narration from ʿAlī (r.a.): two men brought a thief to him and testified, so he carried out the cut. This illustrates that testimony of two witnesses is also sufficient and was the practice of a rightly-guided caliph.
The Hanafi school accepts qawl al-ṣaḥābī (the statement of a Companion) as a source of law when not contrary to stronger evidence.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi position
Recall that Imām Abū Ḥanīfa and Imām Muḥammad hold that one confession suffices. Some reports attribute to Imām Abū Yūsuf a requirement of two confessions, but the relied-upon (muftā bihī) position is that a single confession is sufficient.
The text explicitly says 'one time' (مرة واحدة), establishing the dominant view.
5
Appreciate the cross-madhhab difference
Outside the Ḥanafī school, some scholars require two confessions or two witnesses from the same method. The Ḥanafīs rely on the literal proofs and the principle that confession is conclusive evidence in criminal cases.
This difference affects the practical application of ḥadd in mixed jurisdictions.

Joint Theft and Exempt Items in Hadd al-Sariqa
Axiomatic rulings on joint theft and exempt items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for amputation in joint theft and identify items exempt from hadd according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Each thief must individually reach ten dirhams; common or worthless items incur no amputation.

In Hanafi fiqh, the punishment of hand amputation is reserved for theft of significant, protected wealth. This lesson clarifies two key rules: first, when multiple thieves cooperate, each is judged individually; second, certain items—either because they are considered trifling or because they are freely available—do not trigger the hadd penalty. Understanding these exceptions prevents misapplication of the law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its primary ruling
The passage first discusses joint theft (sariqa mushtaraka). Determine the ruling on amputation for each participant when they cooperate.
The ruling is conditional: amputation occurs only if each thief's share is ten dirhams or more.
2
Examine the individual nisaab condition
Focus on the phrase 'each one of them obtains ten dirhams'. The Hanafi school requires that each thief individually must receive the minimum threshold (nisaab) of ten dirhams. It is not enough that the total stolen amount is ten dirhams if divided.
This is a key difference from some other schools that consider total value.
3
Identify the exempt categories
The passage lists items for which there is no amputation: wood, grass, reeds, fish, game, birds. These are characterized as tāfih (trifling/abundant) and mubāh (permissible to take).
The Hanafis consider these either not proper property (mal mutaqawwim) or of negligible value.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Take a concrete example: three thieves steal 24 dirhams. Divide equally: each gets 8, less than 10. Therefore, no amputation. If they steal 30 dirhams equally, each gets 10, so all three are amputated.
Verify that each share meets or exceeds ten dirhams.

Commentary

The underlying principle is that hadd is only for theft of significant wealth that is legally protected (mal mutaqawwim). These items are either not owned by anyone specifically or are so common that they lack the requisite value.

مباح (mubāh): Permissible to take, i.e., not owned by anyone; taking them is not theft in the legal sense.

Theft from Any Place with Owner Present and Exception for Bathhouse
Ruling on theft with owner presence and bathhouse exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that owner presence makes any place hirz for theft, identify the exception for bathhouses, and explain the evidence from the hadith of Safwan.

Core Idea
Owner presence makes any place hirz; bathhouses are excepted unless the owner is guarding.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on when cutting the hand is obligatory for theft. The key principle is that the owner's presence and guarding can substitute for a physical secure place. However, there is an exception: theft from a bathhouse or a public house generally does not incur cutting. We explore the ruling and its proofs from hadith and companion sayings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Find the statement that cutting is wajib if theft occurs from any place—whether hirz or not—provided the owner is present and guarding the property.
2
Note the exception
Look for the exception: no cutting for theft from a bathhouse or a house that people are permitted to enter.
3
Examine the proof for owner presence
Analyze the hadith of Safwan ibn Umayyah: theft from a mosque (not a hirz) resulted in cutting because the owner was present and guarding his cloak.
4
Examine the proof for the exception
Read the saying of Abu al-Darda' and the hadith about bathhouse theft—these show that unsecured items like birds in a bathhouse do not incur cutting.
5
Understand the limit of the exception
Note that if the stolen item has been secured (e.g., taken to a dovecote) and meets the nisab, cutting applies; otherwise only a fine and punishment.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from Al-Quduri. It presents the general rule for theft: if a person steals anything—whether from a secure place (hirz) like a locked house, or from a non-secure place like an open field—but the owner is present and guarding it, then the hadd penalty of cutting the hand is obligatory. The key principle is that the presence of the owner who is guarding the property makes even a non-secure place effectively secure for theft. The text then provides an exception: no cutting for theft from a bathhouse or a house that people are generally permitted to enter, because such places are not considered secure for the owner's property when the owner is not present. However, if the owner is present and guarding, the general rule applies. The commentary (wajh) provides three proofs: first, the hadith of Safwan ibn Umayyah who was sleeping in the mosque with a cloak; when it was stolen, the Prophet cut the thief's hand. This shows that even a mosque, which is a public place, becomes a secure place when the owner is present. Second, the saying of Abu al-Darda' that there is no cutting for a thief from a bathhouse, but this is understood to refer to birds or pigeons that are not secured. Third, a hadith specifying that if someone steals a secured pigeon from its dovecote and the value reaches the threshold, cutting applies; otherwise, there is a double-value fine. This confirms that the exception is limited to unsecured items.

Key Terms
الحرز (al-hirz): A secure place where property is typically kept safe, such as a locked house or a guarded location. In this context, the owner's presence can also make a place effectively secure.
القطع (al-qat'): The legal punishment of cutting the hand of a thief who meets all conditions, applied as a hadd penalty.
الحمام (al-hammam): Bathhouse; a place where people enter freely, thus not considered a secure place unless the owner is present and guarding specific items.

The Slaughtered Animal of a Muslim and Kitabi is Halal: Conditions and Proofs
Ruling with proof on the identity of the slaughterer and general conditions for halal slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a halal dhabiha and identify the proof from Quran 5:5 for the permissibility of Ahl al-Kitab's slaughtered animals.

Core Idea
The slaughtered animal of a Muslim or Kitabi is halal; of a Majusi, apostate, or idolater is not.

This lesson examines the first ruling in the passage: who can slaughter and whose slaughtered animal is lawful to eat. The Hanafi school establishes clear conditions based on the identity of the slaughterer and the act of slaughter itself. We will dissect the primary proof from the Quran and the related conditions that ensure the animal is halal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rulings on slaughterers
The passage states that the slaughtered animal of a Muslim and a Kitabi (Jew/Christian) is halal, while that of a Magian (Zoroastrian), an apostate, and an idolater is not. Note the categories.
The term 'Kitabi' refers to those who follow a divinely revealed scripture before Islam.
2
Locate the primary Quranic proof
The author cites Surah al-Ma'idah 5:5 as the main evidence for the permissibility of Ahl al-Kitab's food, which includes slaughtered animals.
The word 'طعام' (food) in the verse encompasses their slaughtered animals.
3
Examine supporting proofs
Statements of Ibn Abbas (Companion) and az-Zuhri (Tabi'i) confirm the permissibility. Umar's view shows a minority position limiting 'Kitabi' to non-Arab Jews/Christians.
The Hanafi school adopts the broader interpretation that all People of the Book are included.
4
List the general conditions for halal slaughter
The passage provides five conditions in Urdu: tasmiya, blood flow, slaughterer is Muslim/Kitabi, animal is halal meat, and not in ihram or Haram.
These conditions are derived from various proofs and are essential for the validity of the slaughter.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school agrees on the permissibility of Kitabi's slaughter but differs on the requirement of tasmiya. This will be explored in the next lesson.
Here the focus is on the slaughterer's identity; the tasmiya ruling is separate.

Book of Hunting and Slaughtered Animals. Section on the Slaughtered Animal. The slaughtered animal of a Muslim and of a Kitabi (People of the Book) is lawful. The slaughtered animal of a Magian, an apostate, and an idolater is not eaten. Proof (1): The verse to establish that the slaughtered animal of a Muslim and Kitabi is lawful: 'Today I have made permissible for you good things, and the food of those who were given the Scripture is lawful for you and your food is lawful for them' (Surah al-Ma'idah, verse 5). Proof (1): Statement of a Companion: Ibn Abbas said: 'Their food is their slaughtered animals' (Bukhari, chapter on slaughtered animals of Ahl al-Kitab...). Proof (2): Statement of a Tabi'i: Az-Zuhri said: 'There is no harm in the slaughtered animal of Arab Christians, and if you hear him invoking other than Allah, do not eat; if you do not hear, then Allah has permitted it while knowing their disbelief...' Proof (3): Statement of a Companion: Umar ibn al-Khattab said: 'The Arab Christians are not People of the Book, and their slaughtered animals are not lawful for us...' Proof (a): The hadith to establish that the slaughtered animal of a Magian is not eaten: from al-Hasan ibn Muhammad ibn al-Hanafiyyah: 'The Messenger of Allah wrote to the Magians of Hajar inviting them to Islam; whoever accepted, he accepted from him, and whoever refused, the jizyah was imposed on them on condition that their slaughtered animals would not be eaten and their women would not be married...' This is mursal and the consensus of the majority of the Ummah confirms it. Proof (2): The verse to establish that the slaughtered animal of a Magian is not eaten: 'And forbidden to you is game from the land while you are in ihram' (Surah al-Ma'idah, verse 96). Proof (3): The verse: 'O you who believe, do not kill game while you are in ihram' (Surah al-Ma'idah, verse 95). Principles: Conditions for the lawfulness of a slaughtered animal: (1) The name of Allah has been pronounced over the animal, (2) Blood has flowed from the slaughter, (3) The slaughterer is a Muslim or a Kitabi, (4) The animal is of permissible meat, (5) The hunting did not occur in the state of ihram or within the boundaries of the Haram.

And if the slaughterer deliberately leaves out the tasmiya (saying Bismillah), then the slaughtered animal is carrion, it is not permissible to eat it. And if he leaves it out forgetfully, it is eaten. Proof (4): Statement of a Tabi'i: I asked al-Thawri about a pilgrim who slaughters game, is it lawful for others to eat? Commentary

The Effect of Omitting Tasmiya: Deliberate vs Forgetful
Ruling with proof on the effect of omitting tasmiya, distinguishing deliberate and forgetful omission, with a cross-madhhab comparison to the Shafi'i view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on omitting tasmiya deliberately versus forgetfully and contrast it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Deliberate omission of tasmiya makes the animal carrion; forgetful omission leaves it halal.

This lesson drills into the second major ruling in the passage: the impact of tasmiya (saying Bismillah) at the time of slaughter. The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction between intentional and unintentional omission, backed by specific Quranic verses and hadith. The Shafi'i school differs, considering tasmiya as sunnah, not wajib, so omission does not affect permissibility. We will walk through the proofs and the practical consequence of this difference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on deliberate omission
The passage clearly says: 'وَإِنْ تَرَكَ الذَّابِحُ التَّسْمِيَةَ عَمْدًا فَالذَّبِيحَةُ مَيْتَةٌ لَا يَحِلُّ أَكْلُهَا' – if the slaughterer deliberately leaves out tasmiya, the animal is carrion and cannot be eaten.
This is a strict ruling: it becomes like an animal that died naturally.
2
State the Hanafi ruling on forgetful omission
Then it says: 'وَإِنْ تَرَكَهَا نَاسِيًا أُكِلَتْ' – if he leaves it out forgetfully, the animal is eaten (halal).
Forgetfulness is excused, based on hadith and the principle of raf' al-qalam.
3
Examine the Quranic proof against eating without tasmiya
The passage cites Surah al-An'am 6:121: 'وَلَا تَأْكُلُوا مِمَّا لَمْ يُذْكَرِ اسْمُ اللَّهِ عَلَيْهِ' – which is a general prohibition. Hanafis interpret this as applying to deliberate omission only.
The Shafi'i school interprets this verse differently, as will be seen.
4
Analyze the hadith evidence for forgetfulness
The hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'الْمُسْلِمُ يَكْفِيهِ اسْمُهُ فَإِنْ نَسِيَ أَنْ يُسَمِّيَ حِينَ يَذْبَحُ فَلْيُسَمَ وَلْيَذْكُرِ اسْمَ اللَّهِ ثُمَّ لِيَأْكُل' indicates that forgetfulness is overlooked.
Also the hadith from Aisha: 'سَمُّوا عَلَيْهِ أَنْتُمْ وَكُلُوهُ' – when in doubt, you say the name and eat.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i position
The passage includes a Shafi'i proof (via Hakam ibn Utaybah) that there is no harm in eating even if tasmiya is omitted. The Shafi'i school considers tasmiya as sunnah, so omission does not affect permissibility.
The practical difference (thamarah) is significant: for Hanafis, halal meat requires intentional tasmiya; for Shafi'is, it does not.

The third hadith uses the example of a cracked cup repaired with silver, indicating permissibility of using small amounts of silver in items. Finally, the hadith for women from Abu Musa is referenced, with a note that using silver in small quantities is permissible as a principle.

Ruling on Boys Wearing Gold, Silver, and Silk
Ruling on boys wearing gold, silver, and silk with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a boy wearing gold, silver, or silk, and identify the evidence from the Companions' practice that supports this ruling.

Core Idea
A boy wearing gold, silver, or silk is makruh tahrimi, following the general prohibition for men.

In this passage, Imam al-Quduri addresses the dress code for children, specifically boys. While the prohibition of gold and silk for adult men is well-known, the text extends this to young boys. The ruling is supported by the practice of the Companions, who removed silk garments from boys but allowed them for girls. We will examine the textual evidence and the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Locate the phrase 'يُكْرَهُ أَنْ يَلْبَسَ الصَّبِيُّ الذَّهَبَ وَالْفِضَّةَ وَالْخَرِيرَ'. Note that the ruling is 'makruh' (disliked). Determine the level of 'makruh' in Hanafi: makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) due to analogy with adult men.
The term 'makruh' can be either tanzihi or tahrimi; here it is tahrimi.
2
Locate the Evidence
Find the hadith and companion statements cited: the hadith from Jabir (we removed it from boys, left it on girls) and the incident of Hudhayfah removing silk from his sons but not daughters. These are the main proofs.
The hadith from Jabir is in Sunan Abi Dawud no. 4059.
3
Analyze the Companion Practice
Understand that the action of the Companions (Jabir and Hudhayfah) is considered authoritative (qawl al-sahabi) in Hanafi usul. Their consistent removal of silk from boys indicates strong disapproval.
Hanafi gives weight to companion statements when no stronger evidence exists.
4
Understand the Legal Reasoning
The ruling is derived by analogy (qiyas) to adult men: since silk and gold are haram for men, they are makruh tahrimi for boys, as they are being trained to avoid these items. The prohibition is not absolute because the evidence is circumstantial.
The principle: what is haram for adults is makruh tahrimi for children.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Khatr wa al-Ibahah addresses two additional rulings. First, it is makruh (disliked) for a boy (sabi) to wear gold, silver, or silk (حرير). The term makruh here is understood as makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) because of the association with the general prohibition for men. The second ruling states that using gold and silver vessels for eating, drinking, applying oil, or perfume is not permissible (la yajuz) for both men and women, meaning it is haram. Then it mentions that there is no harm in using vessels made of glass, lead, crystal, or agate, indicating they are permissible. The passage provides several hadith proofs. The first hadith (from Abu Musa) is repeated to reinforce the prohibition of gold and silk for males. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa permits using silver in vessels and furnishings based on the Prophet's own practice.

In this lesson, we examine a ruling from al-Quduri's matn where Abu Hanifa declares it permissible to drink from a silver vessel, ride a silver saddle, and sit on a silver chair. The proofs come from two authentic hadith: one about the Prophet's cup repaired with silver, and another about his sword having a silver pommel. Understanding how Abu Hanifa uses these actions to derive permissibility will sharpen your skill in evaluating dalil from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the Arabic matn: Abu Hanifa permits three specific acts: drinking from a silver vessel, riding a silver saddle, and sitting on a silver chair. Note that the ruling is attributed to Abu Hanifa alone, implying a known ikhtilaf.
The term 'al-tharb' means drinking. The phrase 'ind Abi Hanifa' limits the ruling to his view.
2
Examine the first hadith proof
The first hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari describes the Prophet's cup that cracked and was repaired with silver. Anas ibn Malik states that he gave the Prophet drink from that cup many times. This shows the Prophet used a vessel containing silver, establishing permissibility through his action.
Even though the cup was primarily made of another material (nusar), the silver repair made it 'mufaddad' (silver-inlaid).
3
Examine the second hadith proof
The second hadith from Sunan Abi Dawud states that the pommel of the Prophet's sword was silver. This extends the permissibility beyond vessels to other items like saddles and chairs, which are analogous to the sword's adornment.
The sword is not a vessel, so this proof supports the permissibility of non-vessel items.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah (legal implication)
For both hadith, the Prophet's action (fi'l) and tacit approval (taqrir) constitute a Sunnah that indicates permissibility (ibahah). The Hanafi usul principle holds that the Prophet's actions, unless proven otherwise, indicate permissibility, not obligation.
Hanafi usul distinguishes between actions done as worship (ibadah) and those done as custom (adah); these actions fall under adah.
5
Note the ikhtilaf and the authority weight
The text attributes the ruling to Abu Hanifa, indicating that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) may have held a different view. The proofs are from ahad hadith, which are zanni al-dalala but qati al-thubut. The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah as the ruling appears in al-Quduri.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Adding marks and dots to the Quran is makruh in the Hanafi school, based on the practice of the Companions and Followers.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a specific act: marking tens (ta'shir) and adding dots (naqt) to the Quran. Identify the act and its ruling

Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The disapproval expressed by these early authorities indicates that the act is disliked. Their action is considered evidence because they were knowledgeable about the preservation of the Quran.
The Hanafi school considers such statements as valid proofs when no contradictory evidence exists.

Some later scholars allowed ta'shir and naqt for practical benefit (e.g., to aid recitation). This does not override the original ruling but provides a concession based on necessity.
The original ruling remains makruh, but istihsan may permit it when needed.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi position is that the act is disliked. The proofs from the Salaf are clear and not overridden by weaker considerations.
The weight of evidence lies with the disapproval of the Companions and Followers.

Commentary
This passage gives a ruling from al-Quduri that marking every ten verses (ta'shir) and adding dots (nuqt) to the Quran is makruh (disliked). The proofs are from the practices of the Salaf: 'Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (a companion) disliked ta'shir; Muhammad ibn Sirin (a tabi'i) disliked marking with letters like qaf and kaf; 'Ata' ibn Abi Rabah (a tabi'i) also disliked it. The ruling is based on the idea that the Quran should remain as it was revealed without human additions that might distract or lead to innovation. However, later scholars allowed some markings for necessity, but the original ruling is makruh.

The Hanafi Ruling on Adorning the Quran and Mosques with Gold
Ruling on decorating Quran and mosques with gold and inscriptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on adorning the Quran and decorating mosques with gold and identify the proofs used to support this ruling.

Core Idea
Adorning the Quran and mosques with gold is permissible (mubah) provided it does not lead to extravagance.

This passage addresses a practical question about beautifying religious objects. While the act is permissible, the proofs balance between permissibility and warnings against excess. Understanding this ruling requires examining the hadith and companion statements cited.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling phrase
The text begins 'wa la ba'sa' (and there is no harm). This is the default expression of permissibility (ibahah) in Hanafi fiqh.
La ba'sa is weaker than 'halal' but indicates no legal prohibition.
2
Locate the primary proof
The first evidence cited is the action of Caliph 'Uthman in expanding the Prophet's mosque and decorating it with engraved stones and plaster. This is a precedent from the righteous caliphs.
The action of a caliph is considered a form of sunnah (practice of the rightly guided).
3
Examine the secondary proofs
The second proof is a direct statement from Muhammad (al-Shaybani or a narrator) saying 'la ba'sa an yuhalla al-mushaf' (no harm in adorning the Quran). The third is a hadith where the Prophet said he was not commanded to build up mosques, which Ibn 'Abbas interpreted as a warning against excessive decoration.
The hadith 'ma umirtu bi tashyid al-masajid' is not a prohibition but a statement of what was not commanded.
4
Analyze the counter-evidence
The fourth proof is a statement from Abu Dharr condemning adornment and warning of destruction. This represents a stricter view.
The Hanafi school does not adopt Abu Dharr's absolute condemnation.
5
Conclude the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school reconciles the proofs by permitting moderate adornment (following 'Uthman's practice) while discouraging extravagance (following the warning of Ibn 'Abbas and Abu Dharr).
The mufta bihi is permissibility, but with the condition that it does not lead to waste or distraction.

Rulings on Looking and Touching Female Slaves and Mahrams
Compilation of rulings on permissible looking and touching, with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for looking at and touching female slaves (one's own and another's) and for male slaves and eunuchs, and identify the proofs for each ruling.

Core Idea
A female slave's 'awrah is from knee to navel; a eunuch is like any man; a male slave is a stranger to his mistress.

This lesson brings together a set of interrelated rulings from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar on the permissibility of looking and touching in contexts involving female slaves, mahram relatives, eunuchs, and male slaves. Each ruling is supported by clear proofs from the Quran, Sunnah, and statements of the Companions and Followers.

Guided Path
1
Identify each ruling
Locate the four main rulings: (1) looking at a female slave for purchase, (2) looking at another's female slave, (3) touching a female slave for purchase, (4) ruling on eunuchs, (5) ruling on male slaves looking at their mistress.
2
Locate the proof for each
For ruling (1), find the hadith from Ibn 'Abbas defining her 'awrah. For (2), find the athar about the slave-girl praying without headcover. For (3), the same hadith from Ibn 'Abbas. For (4), the athar from Ibn 'Abbas on castration as mutilation. For (5), the athar from al-Dahhak and Ibrahim, and the Shafi'i evidence.
3
Understand the 'awrah of a female slave
Note that a free woman's 'awrah is entire body except face and hands, but a female slave's 'awrah is only from knee to waist-wrapper. This difference underlies the permissibility of looking at more of a slave.
4
Apply the eunuch and male slave rules
A eunuch has the same restrictions as any man—no special permission. A male slave is treated as a non-mahram stranger regarding his free mistress: he may only see her face and hands.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, expanded with proofs (hadith and athar), covers a set of interrelated rulings on the permissibility of looking at and touching female slaves and mahram relatives. The passage is a compilation of explicit rulings followed by evidentiary support.

The first ruling prohibits looking at the back and belly of a female slave one intends to purchase, but allows touching what is permissible to look at. The proof from Ibn 'Abbas defines her 'awrah as from knee to waist-wrapper, thus back and belly are 'awrah and not to be looked at. The third proof from Abu Hurayra warns against zina of the eyes, supporting restriction.

Next, a man may look at another person's female slave to the same extent he may look at his own mahram women (e.g., mother, sister). Two proofs show that the slave-girl's hair is not considered 'awrah in the same way as a free woman's (she prays without headcover), thus more is permissible to look at.

Touching is permissible when intending to purchase, even if he fears desire, as per the hadith where the Prophet allowed turning over the slave-girl. The second proof reminds that hands can sin, but intent matters.

A eunuch (castrated male) is ruled like a fertile male in the permissibility of looking at non-mahram women; his lack of desire does not change the ruling. The proof from Ibn 'Abbas prohibits castration itself, but the ruling follows the general principle that eunuchs retain full male legal status.

Finally, a male slave cannot look at his mistress except as a stranger would (i.e., only at face and hands). Two athar from al-Dahhak and Ibrahim support this. The Shafi'i evidence from the hadith of Fatima is cited, where the Prophet allowed her slave to see her because the slave was like a son (the Prophet called him 'your boy'), but Hanafis restrict this to the slave being a mahram by being like a son? Actually the Hanafi position is that a male slave is not a mahram; the hadith is interpreted as specific to that situation due to closeness. The Hanafi ruling is stricter: a male slave cannot look at his mistress's hair or body beyond what a stranger can, because she is free and he is not a mahram.

The Urdu principles at the end summarize: for mahram relatives, touching what is permissible to look at is allowed when necessary, and a eunuch is treated as a full male in all rulings including looking.

Ruling with proofs on permissibility of touching a slave for purchase

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Touching a slave for purchase is permissible because the need for examination overrides the general prohibition of touching non-mahram.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a specific case: a man intending to buy a slave woman. The ruling is that it is permissible for him to touch and examine her, except for her 'awrah. The author provides two hadith proofs, one explicitly permitting physical inspection for purchase, and the other distinguishing between sinful touch and non-sinful touch. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that necessity (hajah) can transform a prohibited act into permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? (Touching a slave woman when intending to buy). What is the hukm level? (Mubah, permissibility).
The Arabic phrase 'la ba'sa' directly indicates no harm, i.e., permissibility.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The first hadith from Ibn Abbas (Bayhaqi) explicitly mentions 'flipping the slave girl' and looking except for awrah. This is the direct proof.
The hadith is ahad but considered sufficient for this ruling.

Analyze the wording of the dalil
The phrase 'la ba'sa' (no harm) is used for permission. The definition of awrah is given: between knee and waist, indicating what cannot be exposed even for examination.
The explicit exception (exclusion of awrah) shows that some touching is allowed.
4
Understand the second dalil
The hadith on zina of the limbs implies that touching without desire (since the heart and limbs are accountable) is not sinful if done for a legitimate purpose like purchase.
This reinforces that the permissibility is limited to necessary, non-desirous touch.
5
Note cross-madhhab implication
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may restrict touching to a minimum or only allow looking. The Hanafi position is that the explicit hadith gives broad permission.
The disagreement is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different usul.

Apply the ruling to a scenario

The first proof cites Ibn Abbas who condemns castration of animals as a change of Allah's creation, indicating that a eunuch is not a different category from a man. The second proof from al-Dahhak actually concerns a male slave looking at his mistress, but it is used here analogically to show that eunuchs, like male slaves, are not excused from the normal rulings of looking. The principle stated in Urdu confirms that in all legal matters (prayer, inheritance, etc.), a eunuch is considered fully male, so the same restrictions on looking apply. Thus, a eunuch is not permitted to look at non-mahram women beyond what is allowed for any other man.

Male Slave's Look at His Mistress: Non-Mahram Limit
Inter-madhhab dispute with dalil analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a male slave looking at his mistress, explain the proofs from the tabi'un, analyze the Shafi'i counter-proof from the hadith of Fatima, and apply the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A male slave is like a non-mahram; only face and hands are permissible to see.

This passage addresses a practical question of modesty in a household with slaves: may a male slave see more of his mistress than a non-mahram? The Hanafi school answers with a clear restriction, citing early authorities. The Shafi'i school uses a hadith about Fatima to allow more. We will examine the proofs and the Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling
State the ruling as given: a male slave may only see what a non-mahram can see—face and hands.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof from tabi'un
Read the statements of al-Dahhak and Ibrahim: they indicate a woman must conceal from her slave. Note that these are qawl sahabi-derived evidence.
These are not direct prophetic hadith but accepted in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i counter-proof
Read the hadith of Fatima: the Prophet said 'no harm' when her slave saw her while her garment was short. Understand how al-Shafi'i uses this to allow more.
This is a sahih hadith from Abu Dawud.
4
Understand the Hanafi response to the hadith
The Hanafi reply: the permissibility was due to necessity (poverty, short garment) and does not establish a general rule. The default is restriction.
Hanafi distinguishes between rukhsah (concession) and azimah (original ruling).
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view: slave is like non-mahram. Only face and hands are allowed.
This is from al-Quduri and later Hanafi works.

Commentary
This passage contains an inter-madhhab dispute. The Hanafi ruling states that a male slave is not permitted to look at his mistress except as a non-mahram (i.e., face and hands). The first two proofs are statements from tabi'un (al-Dahhak and Ibrahim) that a woman should cover from her male slave, indicating the slave is not like a mahram. The third proof is the dalil of Imam al-Shafi'i, who uses the hadith of Fatima to argue that a slave may see his mistress more freely, since the Prophet said 'there is no harm' even when her garment was short. The Hanafi response is that this hadith indicates permissibility only when necessary (due to poverty or lack of covering), but the general ruling is restriction. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) Hanafi position is that a male slave is like a non-mahram, so only face and hands may be seen.
