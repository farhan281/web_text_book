Basic Ruling on Wills: Not Obligatory, Recommended, and Invalid for Heirs
Opening of Kitab al-Wasaya stating fundamental rulings on wills with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the legal status of a will (wasiyyah) and explain why a bequest to an heir is invalid without consent of other heirs.

Core Idea
A will is not obligatory but highly recommended; leaving a bequest to an heir is invalid unless all other heirs agree.

This lesson opens the Book of Wills (Kitab al-Wasaya) from al-Quduri's matn. You will learn two foundational rulings: the recommended nature of making a will, and the prohibition of bequeathing to an heir without unanimous consent of all other heirs. The passage presents strong proofs from the Quran and Sunnah, and introduces key terminology such as al-musi (testator) and al-wasi (executor).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings in the matn
Read the matn carefully: the will is 'ghayr wajibah' (not obligatory) and 'mustahabbah' (recommended). Also, 'la tajuz al-wasiyyah li al-warith' (it is not permissible to make a will for an heir) unless the other heirs consent.
These are the core legal statements from which the rest of the proofs follow.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof (2:180)
The verse uses the imperative 'kutiba alaykum' (prescribed for you). In Hanafi usul, the imperative indicates obligation in principle, but the author uses other proofs to show it remains recommended, not binding. Notice how the same verse is also used as proof for the second ruling regarding heirs.
The verse is cited twice: once for the recommendation of wills, and once for the prohibition of bequests to heirs (through abrogation).
3
Examine the hadith proofs
Four hadith are cited: (1) The Prophet ﷺ did not leave a written will, showing non-obligation. (2) Allah gifted one-third of wealth for charity at death, showing permissibility. (3) A Muslim should have a written will if he has something to bequeath. (4) The hadith from Ibn Abbas explaining the abrogation of the verse regarding heirs.
These hadith collectively shift the ruling from obligation to recommendation.
4
Understand the abrogation (naskh) for the heir bequest ruling
The verse originally allowed wills for parents and relatives (who were heirs at that time). After the inheritance verses (e.g., Surah al-Nisa) were revealed, the right of heirs was fixed, and the general permission for wills to heirs was abrogated. Thus, a bequest to an heir is invalid unless all other heirs consent.
This is a classic example of naskh (abrogation) in the Hanafi tradition.
5
Memorize the key terminology
The passage ends with definitions: al-musi (testator), al-musa lahu (legatee), al-musa bihi (bequeathed property), and al-wasi (executor). These terms are essential for understanding any discussion of wills.
These terms will recur throughout Kitab al-Wasaya.

(4) Hadith urging Muslims to have their wills written. Will Exceeding One-Third Requires Heirs' Consent
Ruling with two hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wills exceeding one-third and identify the two hadith proofs that establish this limit.

Core Idea
A will beyond one-third is invalid unless heirs consent — the Prophet's limit protects their rights.

This passage from al-Quduri presents a foundational ruling in Islamic inheritance law: a will that disposes of more than one-third of the estate is not valid unless the heirs give their permission after the testator's death. The author then supports this ruling with two prophetic hadith that establish the one-third limit. Understanding this rule is essential for drafting valid wills and protecting the rights of heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
Read the opening sentence: 'A will by what exceeds one-third is not valid unless the heirs permit it.' Note that the ruling is about the validity of the will itself, not about its prohibition.
The ruling is conditional: the excess portion is invalid except with consent.
2
Examine the first hadith (Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas)
The hadith shows the Prophet limiting Sa'd's bequest to one-third, indicating that one-third is the maximum without heirs' permission. The Prophet's statement 'one-third is much' emphasizes the limit.
This hadith is the primary proof for the one-third limit.
3
Examine the second hadith (Ibn Abbas)
The hadith reports the Prophet saying 'one-third, and one-third is much,' implying that even one-third is considerable, so more than that is excessive without consent.
This hadith reinforces the first and shows the Prophet's emphasis.
4
Understand the legal deduction
The combination of these hadiths establishes the rule: a will beyond one-third is invalid unless heirs consent. The consent after death makes the will valid for the excess.
Hanafi school treats the consent as a new gift from the heirs.
5
Note the practical application
In practice, if no consent is given, the excess portion is distributed to the heirs according to inheritance shares, and only up to one-third goes to the beneficiary of the will.
This protects heirs from being dispossessed of their rightful shares.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the matn of al-Quduri: a will that exceeds one-third of the estate is not valid unless the heirs give their permission after the testator's death. The author then provides two hadith proofs. The first hadith is the famous story of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (radiyallahu 'anhu) who fell ill and asked the Prophet (sallallahu 'alayhi wa sallam) whether he could bequeath all his wealth. The Prophet limited it to one-third, and Sa'd considered even that much. The second hadith from Ibn Abbas states that the Prophet said 'One-third, and one-third is much,' indicating that even one-third is a large amount. Together, these hadith establish the principle that no will beyond one-third is valid without the consent of the heirs. This is a foundational rule of Islamic inheritance law.

Will to an Heir Invalid Without Other Heirs' Consent
Ruling with two hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wills to heirs and explain the two hadith proofs that support it.

Core Idea
A will for an heir is invalid unless all other heirs consent after death.

This lesson examines a fundamental ruling in Islamic inheritance law: a will cannot be made in favor of an heir without the consent of other heirs. The source passage states this rule and supports it with two authentic hadiths. Understanding this rule is crucial for estate planning and ensuring just distribution according to divine law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
The text states that a will to an heir is not valid unless the other heirs permit it. This is the core ruling: invalidity as default, conditional validity upon consent.
Condition applies only after the testator's death; consent during lifetime is revocable.
2
Examine the first hadith proof
The hadith from Abu Umamah states: 'Indeed Allah has given every rightful person his right, so no will for an heir.' The phrase 'no will for an heir' is a clear prohibition indicating invalidity in Hanafi usul.
This hadith establishes the general prohibition, leaving no room for a will to an heir without exception.
3
Examine the second hadith proof
The hadith from Ibn Abbas states: 'A will for an heir is not valid unless the other heirs wish it.' This explicitly makes validity conditional on the consent of the other heirs.
The second hadith clarifies the exception: consent after death transforms the invalid will into a valid one.
4
Understand the underlying wisdom
The prohibition protects the fixed inheritance shares (fara'id) that Allah has ordained. A will to an heir would disturb this divine distribution unless all affected heirs voluntarily agree.
This wisdom is derived from the first hadith: each heir has a pre-assigned right.
5
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a testator who leaves a will giving his house to his eldest son (an heir). After death, the other two children do not consent. The will is invalid; the estate is divided according to inheritance shares.
If consent were given, the will would be valid and the son would take the house.

Core Idea
A killer cannot benefit from his victim's will—the hadith 'No will for a killer' prevents rewarding the criminal.

This lesson examines the Islamic ruling on wills that benefit someone who kills the testator. The Hanafi school, relying on a hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him), holds that such a will is void. We will explore the textual proof and its legal implications.

The Hanafi school restricts the ruling to intentional, unlawful killing. Accidental killing does not bar the beneficiary from receiving the will.
The common misunderstanding is that all killing bars; only deliberate murder does.

In practice, if a beneficiary intentionally and unjustly kills the testator, the will is void and the killer cannot take anything under it.
This deters criminals from profiting from their crime.

Permissibility of Muslim Willing to Non-Muslim
Ruling on permissibility of interfaith bequests with two proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muslim's will to a non-Muslim and explain the evidence from the Quran and companion practice.

Core Idea
A Muslim may will to a non-Muslim; the Quranic exception 'except that you may do kindness' permits it.

Many assume that Islamic law prohibits a Muslim from leaving anything to a non-Muslim. This lesson examines a clear ruling from the Hanafi school that a Muslim may validly make a will in favor of a non-Muslim, and a non-Muslim may make a will for a Muslim. We will explore the textual proofs: a Quranic verse that explicitly allows a 'kindness' to non-heirs, and the action of the Prophet's wife Safiyyah who bequeathed to her Jewish relatives. Understanding this ruling is essential for correct estate planning and interfaith family relations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the opening sentence: 'It is permissible for a Muslim to make a will for a non-Muslim, and for a non-Muslim for a Muslim.' Recognize that the hukm (legal ruling) is ja'iz (permissible).
2
Locate the first dalil
The author cites Surah al-Ahzab 33:6. Focus on the phrase 'except that you may do to your close associates a kindness' (إلا أن تفعلوا إلى أوليائكم معروفا). This exception permits bequests to non-heirs, including non-Muslim relatives.
The verse primarily discusses inheritance, but the exception opens the door for wills.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
How does this verse prove the ruling? The exception clause allows doing 'ma'ruf' (kindness) to 'awliya' (close associates). Scholars interpret this as permitting wasiyyah to non-Muslim relatives who are not legal heirs.
The Hanafi school considers this a clear textual permission.
4
Locate the second dalil
The author cites the action of Safiyyah bint Huyayy, the wife of the Prophet, who bequeathed a great amount of wealth to her relatives. As a companion, her practice is a source of law (qawl sahabi).
Safiyyah herself was a Muslim but her relatives were Jewish, confirming the permissibility.
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Shafi'i school holds that a Muslim cannot will to a non-Muslim because the non-Muslim is not an heir and difference of religion is a bar. The Hanafi school disagrees, relying on the explicit Quranic exception.
The practical difference: Hanafi allows, Shafi'i prohibits.

Acceptance of Will Occurs After Death
Ruling with three proofs from hadith and athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that acceptance of a will is only valid after the testator's death and explain the three proofs that support this ruling.

Core Idea
Acceptance or rejection of a will before death is void; the beneficiary decides only after the testator dies.

A will is a suspended disposition that becomes effective only upon death. Therefore, the legatee's acceptance or rejection during the testator's life has no legal weight. The Hanafi school bases this on the principle that the testator can revoke until death, and the beneficiary's right arises only at death. This lesson examines the ruling and three proofs from the Companions and the Prophet (sallallahu 'alayhi wa sallam).

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the core hukm: acceptance of a will is after death; acceptance or rejection during life is invalid (batil).
The key is that the will is mu'allaq (suspended) on death.
2
Understand the legal reasoning
A will becomes effective only upon death. Until then, the testator can revoke, and the legatee has no vested right. Thus, any declaration before death is legally irrelevant.
This is a foundational usul principle: al-wasiyyah tata'allaqu bi al-mawt.
3
Analyze the first proof (Umar)
Umar ibn al-Khattab ruled that when a man makes two conflicting wills, the latter is binding. This shows the testator can change his mind until death, so acceptance during life cannot be final.
The athar from Ibn Abi Shaybah supports the testator's continued authority.
4
Analyze the second proof (Shurayh)
Shurayh stated that if a testator seeks permission to will more than one-third and heirs consent, they can revoke after death. This indicates that the legatee's acceptance during life is not determinative.
Heirs are free to decide after death; similarly the legatee is free after death.
5
Analyze the third proof (Prophet)
The Prophet (sallallahu 'alayhi wa sallam) returned al-Bara' ibn Ma'rur's bequest to his children after his death, showing that he did not consider acceptance during life binding.
This hadith directly illustrates that acceptance occurs only after death.

Recommendation with two hadith proofs

A will for less than one-third is recommended; even one-third is considered a large share.

This lesson presents a straightforward ruling from al-Quduri: it is mustahabb (recommended) to bequeath less than one-third of one's estate. Ownership of Bequest by Acceptance
Ruling that bequest is owned by acceptance with one exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a bequest is owned by acceptance, identify the one exception, and cite the proofs for both the rule and the exception.

Core Idea
A bequest is not owned upon death; the legatee must accept it, except when he dies before accepting — then his heirs inherit the right.

This lesson presents a fundamental rule of Islamic inheritance (wasiyyah) in the Hanafi school: ownership of a bequest transfers not at the testator's death but at the moment of the legatee's acceptance. One exception protects the legatee's rights if he dies before accepting. We will examine the rule, the exception, and the proofs from the Tabi'un and the Companion Ali (RA).

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The rule is: الموصى به يملك بالقبول — the bequest is owned by acceptance. This means ownership transfers only when the legatee accepts, explicitly or implicitly.
Acceptance can be explicit (e.g., saying 'I accept') or implicit (e.g., taking possession).
2
State the one exception
If the testator dies first, then the legatee dies before accepting, the bequest does not revert to the testator's heirs. Instead, it enters into the ownership of the legatee's heirs.
This is because the right to accept is inheritable.
3
Identify the proof for the rule
The rule is supported by two statements of the Tabi'un: (1) Amr ordered Ibn Awn to accept an executorship, showing acceptance is needed; (2) Abi Qilabah said: 'There is no bequest for a dead person' — meaning the legatee must be alive to accept.
These proofs are recorded in Mushannaf Ibn Abi Shayba.
4
Identify the proof for the exception
The exception is proven by the explicit ruling of Ali (RA): if a man bequeaths to a man and the legatee dies before acceptance, the bequest belongs to the legatee's heirs.
This is recorded in Mushannaf Ibn Abi Shayba and Mushannaf Abd al-Razzaq.
5
Understand the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that ownership transfers at the testator's death, not by acceptance. So if the legatee dies before acceptance, the bequest reverts to the testator's heirs.
This difference has practical consequences in inheritance distribution.

Core Idea
A will to your own slave is invalid if it harms adult heirs — impartiality is impossible.

This passage from Quduri's matn addresses a specific scenario: appointing one's own slave as executor. The ruling hinges on whether there are adult heirs who could be harmed. Judge Appoints Co-Executor for Incapable Executor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling and its dalil when an appointed executor is incapable of executing the will.

Core Idea
An incapable executor is not removed; the judge must appoint a co-executor to safeguard the estate.

When a testator appoints an executor who later becomes unable to fulfil the duties, Islamic law ensures the will is still executed properly. The Hanafi school gives the judge a specific responsibility: not to remove the incapable executor but to add a capable person alongside. This ruling is rooted in the statement of the early tabi'i 'Amir al-Sha'bi, equating the executor to a parent who would be aided if unable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling

2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the statement of 'Amir al-Sha'bi: 'The executor is like a parent; if the executor is orphaned (unable), he is removed or another is placed with him.' This is a qawl al-tabi'i, considered a type of dalil in Hanafi usul.

Some schools allow the judge to remove the executor entirely. The Hanafi position is to add a co-executor, not remove, unless the executor is completely incapable to the point of being unfit.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi view prioritises preserving the testator's choice.

Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that the judge must join a capable person to the incapable executor. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is the standard Hanafi fatwa.
The ruling applies even if the testator did not anticipate incapacity.

Bequest of One-Third and One-Sixth – Proportional Division
Ruling on proportional division of bequests

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the proportional division of a bequest when multiple legatees are named and the total exceeds one-third, applying the Hanafi rule of proportionate shares.

Core Idea
When bequests exceed one-third, divide the available third proportionally.

In this passage, al-Quduri extends the previous ruling on multiple bequests. When one legatee is given one-third and another one-sixth (totaling half), the available one-third is not split equally but proportionally based on the bequest amounts. This ensures fairness according to the testator's intent, as the heirs cannot be forced to give more than the legal limit. The proof from al-Hasan al-Basri confirms proportional sharing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the bequest fractions
Note the fractions given to each legatee. In this case: one-third (1/3) and one-sixth (1/6).
The total exceeds one-third (1/3 + 1/6 = 1/2).
2
Find the ratio of the bequests
Combine the fractions into a common denominator to express the ratio. 1/3 : 1/6 = 2/6 : 1/6 = 2:1.
The ratio of one-third to one-sixth is 2:1.
3
Apply the ratio to the available one-third
Divide the available third (1/3 of the estate) into parts equal to the sum of the ratio (2+1=3). Give each legatee their proportion: the one-third legatee gets 2 parts, the one-sixth legatee gets 1 part.
Thus, from the one-third, the first gets 2/3 of that third (i.e., 2/9 of the estate) and the second gets 1/3 of that third (i.e., 1/9 of the estate).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When bequests exceed one-third, division among legatees differs: Abu Hanifa halves, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad use a 3:1 ratio.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about how to distribute the permissible one-third when two legatees are given conflicting amounts. The matn presents the two positions, and later school relies on the Sahibayn's view.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
The testator bequeaths all his wealth to one person and one-third of his wealth to another person. The total exceeds one-third and the heirs do not permit the excess.
The one-third is the maximum that can be given to non-heirs.
2
Identify the two Hanafi opinions
Abu Hanifa holds that the one-third is divided equally between the two legatees (halves). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that it is divided into four shares: the 'all wealth' legatee gets three shares and the other gets one share (3:1 ratio).
The Sahibayn's method gives a larger portion to the one who was given all wealth.

Under Abu Hanifa, each legatee gets 1/2 of the one-third = 1/6 of the entire estate. Under the Sahibayn, the 'all wealth' legatee gets 3/4 of the one-third = 1/4 of the entire estate, and the other gets 1/4 of the one-third = 1/12 of the entire estate.
4
Identify the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Haskafi, Ibn Abidin) prefer the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Additional Wasiyyah Rulings: Debts, Shares, and Deathbed Transactions
Multiple wasiyyah rulings including debt, share wording, and deathbed transactions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on bequests when debts exceed assets, distinguish valid and void bequests of a son's share, and apply the priority rules for deathbed transactions in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A will is void if debts exceed assets; bequeathing 'the share of my son' is void but 'the like' is valid; deathbed gifts compete from one-third with priority varying between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

This passage covers important boundary conditions in wills: the effect of overwhelming debt, the precise wording for bequeathing a son's share, and the competition among deathbed transactions. The last part includes another intra-Hanafi dispute on priority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the effect of overwhelming debt
If the testator's debts exceed his assets, the will is not executed unless the creditors forgive the debts. This is because creditors' rights take precedence over bequests.
2
Understand the wording distinction for a son's share
Bequeathing 'the share of my son' is void because it is vague and refers to a specific heir. Bequeathing 'the like of the share of my son' is valid because it is a quantifiable amount.
3
Apply the rule when two sons exist
If the testator has two sons, and he bequeaths 'the like of the share of my son', the legatee receives one-third of the estate (because the sons each get half, and the like of one share is half, but limited to one-third).
The one-third cap applies.
4
Recognize deathbed transactions as bequests
Freeing a slave, selling at an undervalue (muhabah), or giving a gift during death illness are all considered bequests and come from the one-third. They compete with other bequests.
5
Analyze the priority dispute
If multiple deathbed transactions occur, their order matters according to Abu Hanifa: if muhabah precedes freeing, muhabah takes precedence; if freeing precedes muhabah, they are equal. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, freeing always takes precedence regardless of order.
The Sahibayn's view is that freeing is more meritorious and thus prioritized.

Ruling on bequest with exception and intra-Hanafi dispute on distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequest of a slave girl with an exception of pregnancy and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding distribution when the bequest exceeds one-third.

Core Idea
A bequest of a pregnant slave includes the child unless excepted; within Hanafi school, dispute on proportional vs. prioritized distribution.

This passage from al-Quduri presents a specific ruling on bequests involving a slave girl and her unborn child. It illustrates the Hanafi principle that an exception (istithna) is valid, and then details a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on how to distribute the bequest when its value exceeds the permissible one-third of the estate. Understanding this intra-madhhab difference sharpens your appreciation of how usul principles like 'the child follows the mother' are applied differently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common ground
Both parties agree that the bequest of a slave girl with an exception of her pregnancy is valid. The exception is effective, so the child remains with the estate.
This is the starting point agreed upon by all Hanafi imams.
2
State the scenario for the dispute
The bequest is of a slave girl who gives birth after the testator's death, and the legatee accepts. The combined value of mother and child exceeds one-third of the estate.

State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the legatee takes a proportionate share from both the mother and the child. The value of the mother and child are pooled, and the legatee gets a fraction equal to (one-third of estate)/total value.
This treats both as equally subject to the bequest.
4
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the legatee takes the full value of the mother first. If the one-third is enough for the mother, nothing goes to the child. If something remains, it goes to the child.
They prioritize the mother as the primary object of the bequest.

The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the legatee always gets a share from both. Under the Sahibayn, if the mother's value alone does not exceed the third, the legatee gets the mother fully, and the child goes to the heirs; if the mother's value exceeds the third, the legatee gets a proportion from the mother and possibly some from the child.

Bequest to Named Persons: Wording Matters and Bequest with No Wealth
Rulings on bequest wording and timing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi rulings when a testator uses 'to' versus 'between' in a bequest, and apply the rule that a bequest of a third is valid even if the testator has no property at the time of the will, provided he later acquires wealth before death.

Core Idea
The wording 'to' vs 'between' changes distribution; a bequest is effective based on estate at death, not at time of will.

In this passage, Al-Quduri presents two important rulings on bequests (wasiyyah). First, the interpretation of the testator's wording—whether he says 'to Zayd and Amr' or 'between Zayd and Amr'—determines how the bequest is distributed when one legatee predeceases the testator. Second, a bequest of a third of one's wealth is valid even if the testator owns nothing at the time of the will; if he later acquires wealth before death, the legatee is entitled to one-third of the estate at death. The proof is drawn from Quran 4:12, which orders the execution of bequests after death.

2
Parse the wording
Focus on the prepositions used: 'li' (to) vs 'bayn' (between). Understand that in Hanafi law, 'li' implies joint ownership with survivorship, while 'bayn' indicates separate shares.
This is a parsing_exercise: the legal effect hinges on the precise wording.
