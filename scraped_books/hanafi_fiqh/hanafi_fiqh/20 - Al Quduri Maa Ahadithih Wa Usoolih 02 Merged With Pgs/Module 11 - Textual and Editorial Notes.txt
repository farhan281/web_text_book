Emancipation When Buying One's Son in Partnership
Ruling on purchasing the son of one partner with hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when two men jointly buy the son of one of them and apply the hadith proof to determine the father's liability and the partner's options.

Core Idea
Ownership of a mahram relative automatically frees the owner's share, and the partner has a choice.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in slavery law: two partners purchase a slave who is the son of one partner. The hadith 'Whoever possesses a mahram relative is free' triggers automatic emancipation of the father's share. The father bears no liability, and the other partner may either free his share or have the slave earn its value. This illustrates how a direct textual proof (dalil naqli) overrides the normal rules of joint ownership.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the ruling
Identify that two men buy the son of one. The father's share is freed automatically; no liability on him. The partner has the option to free his share or have the slave earn.
2
Identify the proof (dalil)
The hadith 'Whoever possesses a mahram relative is free' is cited. Classify it as a hadith ahad, with zanni thubut and dalala.
Reference: Sunan Abi Dawud and Tirmidhi.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'فهو حر' gives a direct ruling of freedom upon ownership. This is not conditioned on choice or payment.
4
Apply the principle to the case
Because the father's share became free by operation of law, he is not liable for the value of the partner's share. The partner retains ownership of his share.
This is the Hanafi position.
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah)
The partner may either free his share (iqal) or have the slave earn (sa'ayah) to pay for his share. The father is not required to pay anything.

Commentary
This lesson deals with a specific scenario: two individuals jointly purchase a slave who is the son of one of them. According to the hadith 'Whoever comes into possession of a close relative (mahram) becomes free,' the father's share automatically emancipates without any liability on him, because the right of kinship overrides ownership. The other partner then has a choice: he may free his own share (iqal) or have the slave earn the value of his share through sa'aya. The same ruling applies if they inherit the slave rather than purchase. The principle stated at the end confirms that the father is not liable for the value of the other partner's share because his share was freed by operation of law (due to mahram relationship), not by his own act. This is consistent with the Hanafi position that ownership of a mahram relative automatically triggers emancipation.

Key Terms
ذو رحم محرم (dhū raḥim maḥram): A blood relative within the prohibited degrees of marriage, such as a son, father, brother, etc.
سعاية (saʿāyah): The slave works to earn money to pay for the partner's share.

Mutual Testimony and the Slave's Earning
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mutual testimony for emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when each co-owner testifies the other freed the slave, and differentiate the Sahibayn's position based on solvency.

Core Idea
Slave earns both shares under Abu Hanifa; Sahibayn conditionally exempt if partners are wealthy.

This lesson addresses a unique intra-Hanafi dispute: when each co-owner testifies that the other freed the jointly owned slave, what is the slave's obligation? Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the slave must earn for both partners regardless of their financial status. His two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differentiate based on the solvency of the co-owners. Understanding this dispute clarifies the interplay between testimony, denial, and the institution of sa'aya (earning) in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
You are presented with a case where two partners each testify that the other freed the slave. The slave is jointly owned. No one admits to freeing. Determine the legal problem.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
Abu Hanifa holds that the slave must earn for each partner in his respective share, irrespective of whether the partners are wealthy or insolvent. The rationale is that the partner's denial prevents imposing a guarantee, so the slave's earning is the only recourse.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differentiate: if both partners are wealthy, the slave does not earn (presumably he becomes free); if both are insolvent, he earns for both; if one is wealthy and the other insolvent, he earns only for the wealthy.
The Sahibayn rely on the hadith of freeing a jointly-owned slave: if wealthy, the slave is assessed and freed; if insolvent, he earns.
4
Examine the evidence
Both sides cite the same hadith and the statement of Hammad. Abu Hanifa interprets the hadith as a general rule that does not apply to mutual testimony; the Sahibayn extend it by analogy.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence for the slave: under Abu Hanifa, he always owes work for both shares; under the Sahibayn, if both partners are wealthy, he is free without earning.
This difference affects the slave's legal status and economic obligation.

When each of the two partners testifies against the other that he freed the slave, the slave earns for each of them in his share, whether they are wealthy or insolvent according to Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: If both are wealthy, there is no earning; if both are insolvent, he earns for both; if one is wealthy and the other insolvent, he earns for the wealthy and does not earn for the insolvent. Proof (1): Hadith from Salim from his father from the Prophet: 'Whoever frees a slave between two, then if he is wealthy, it is assessed for him, then he is freed.' Proof (2): Statement of the Tabi'i Hammad regarding a slave between two men: one testified against the other that he freed him and the other denied. He said: 'If the one testified against is insolvent, the slave earns for him; if wealthy, the slave earns for both.' Principle: Because of the partner's denial, guarantee cannot be imposed on him; the final solution is earning.

Commentary
This lesson addresses a unique scenario: each co-owner testifies that the other has freed the slave, but neither admits to freeing. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, regardless of solvency, the slave must earn the value of each share for its owner, since each claim is unconfirmed. The Sahibayn differentiate: if both are wealthy, no earning is required because each could pay the value but the testimony is against each other; if both insolvent, the slave earns for both; if one is wealthy and one insolvent, the slave earns only for the wealthy partner because the wealthy partner could pay but denied, so the slave earns for him, while the insolvent partner cannot pay so no earning for him. The hadith and the statement of Hammad are cited as evidences. The final principle clarifies that since the partner (the defendant) denies the testimony, he cannot be forced to pay a guarantee (daman), so the only recourse is sa'aya (earning) for the claimant.

Key Terms
شهادة (shahādah): Testimony; in this context, each partner testifies against the other.
سعاية (saʿāyah): Earning; the slave works to pay for the share.
موسر (mūsir): Wealthy; having sufficient wealth to pay the value.
معسر (muʿsir): Insolvent; lacking wealth.

Validity of Emancipation Despite Intention, Compulsion, or Intoxication
Rulings on emancipation with different intentions, compulsion, and drunkenness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on emancipation when performed for different intentions, under compulsion, or while intoxicated, and cite the textual proofs.

Core Idea
Emancipation is valid and binding regardless of intention, compulsion, or intoxication — the pronouncement alone suffices.

In this lesson, we examine a key Hanafi principle: certain legal acts, once uttered, become effective irrespective of the speaker's inner state or external pressure. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar covers emancipation under various intentions (even for an idol), under compulsion, and while intoxicated. We will explore the textual proofs from hadith and companion statements that establish this binding effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that emancipation is valid (عتق) in all three scenarios: for the sake of Allah, for the devil, or for an idol. Also, the emancipation of one who is compelled (mukrah) or drunk (sakran) is valid.
The ruling is unconditional — the legal effect is tied to the utterance, not the intention.
2
Examine the First Proof
The hadith of Abu Huraira: When he came with his slave, he said, 'I make you witness that he is for Allah.' This shows that even a declaration intended for Allah is valid emancipation.
This proof establishes that the intention (even if good) does not affect validity; the word itself is effective.
3
Examine the Second Proof
The hadith: 'Three things, their seriousness is serious and their jest is serious: marriage, divorce, and returning (to wife).' By analogy (qiyas), emancipation is included because it is a similar serious act that cannot be played with.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud and is used to show that jest does not negate legal effect.
4
Examine the Third Proof
The statement of Abu Darda: 'Three are not to be played with: marriage, emancipation, and divorce.' This explicitly includes emancipation and reinforces the principle.
This is a companion statement (qawl al-sahabi) which is a valid source in Hanafi usul.
5
Understand the Scope
The principle extends to compulsion (ikrah) and drunkenness (sukr) — both do not invalidate emancipation. The key is that the utterance is clear and intentional at some level, even if the person is under pressure or intoxicated.
Only if the person is completely unconscious would the pronouncement be invalid, but this is not in the passage.

Commentary
This lesson asserts the validity of emancipation regardless of the intention behind it, whether for God, the devil, or an idol. The key point is that the utterance of emancipation is effective in all cases, because the legal effect is tied to the words, not the inner intention. Similarly, emancipation under compulsion (ikrah) or while intoxicated (sukr) is valid and takes effect. The text provides proofs from the practice of Abu Huraira, who declared his slave free for Allah, and from the hadith stating that marriage, divorce, and return (raj'a) are serious even if said in jest, analogously for emancipation. The statement of Abu Darda includes emancipation among these three. Note: This is consistent with the Hanafi position that the pronouncement of certain legal acts (like marriage, divorce, or emancipation) has binding effect even if said in jest or under compulsion, because they are categorized as 'serious matters that cannot be played with.'

سكران (sukrān): Drunken person; one who has lost control due to intoxication.
عتق (ʿitq): Emancipation; freeing a slave.
