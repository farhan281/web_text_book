Marriage of Muslim to Dhimmi with Dhimmi Witnesses
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witness requirements

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the validity of marriage with dhimmi witnesses and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow; Muhammad and Zufar require Muslim witnesses.

This lesson examines a specific intra-Hanafi dispute from Kitab al-Nikah. The scenario is a Muslim man marrying a dhimmi (protected non-Muslim) woman, with two dhimmi men as witnesses. The question is whether such a marriage is valid. The school splits: the founders Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule it permissible, while Imam Muhammad and Imam Zufar disagree. Understanding this dispute refines your grasp of how witness qualifications operate in marriage contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling scenario
Read the source text: a Muslim marries a dhimmi woman with two dhimmi witnesses. Note that the wife and witnesses are both from the dhimmi community.
The act in question is the marriage (nikah) itself.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say it is permissible (ja'iz). Muhammad and Zufar say it is not permissible (la yajuz). Recognize that the dispute is within the Hanafi school.
The positions are recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah as two valid riwayat.
3
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if such a marriage occurs, according to Abu Hanifa it is valid and remains intact; according to Muhammad it is void (batil) and must be dissolved.
This affects the marital rights, inheritance, and legitimacy of children.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The operative position for fatwa is the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, as it is the majority view recorded in the core texts and relied upon by later Hanafi authorities.
Note: even within the school, tarjih favours the more flexible position.

Permanently Prohibited Women in Marriage (Mahramat Mu'abbada)
Listing of permanently prohibited women by blood and marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list all women who are permanently prohibited for marriage (mahramat mu'abbada) and identify the Quranic proof for each category.

Core Idea
Marriage to blood relatives, certain in-laws, and foster relatives is permanently forbidden—these prohibitions are absolute and agreed upon.

The foundation of lawful marriage in Islam requires knowing whom you may not marry. This lesson presents the complete list of women who are permanently prohibited (mahramat mu'abbada) from the Hanafi text of Al-Quduri. The prohibitions are drawn directly from the Qur'an (Surah an-Nisa 4:23) and are unanimous among the schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sources of prohibition
Understand that prohibitions arise from blood relation (nasab), marriage (musahara), and suckling (radaa'). This passage covers the first two; suckling is mentioned in the Quranic verse.
Suckling prohibitions mirror blood prohibitions in many cases.
2
List the blood relatives prohibited
Enumerate: mother, grandmothers (both paternal and maternal), daughter, granddaughters, sister, brother's daughters, sister's daughters, paternal aunt, maternal aunt. Note that daughters of siblings and grandchildren are included even if descended.
The wording 'even if descended' (wa in sifalna) means all female descendants count.
3
List the relatives by marriage prohibited
Enumerate: mother-in-law (whether or not consummated with her daughter), step-daughter (only if consummated with her mother), wife of one's father/grandfather, wife of one's son/grandson.
Step-daughter is only prohibited after consummation with her mother; otherwise permissible.
4
Confirm the Quranic proof and Hanafi consensus
All prohibitions are directly from Surah an-Nisa 4:23 (qat'iy al-thubut wa al-dalala). The Hanafi school unanimously holds these as haram, and any marriage contract with them is batil.
No differences among the four schools on these core prohibitions.

Prohibition of Marrying Mother-in-Law and Step-Daughter
Ruling with proofs from Quran and Hadith on in-law marriage prohibitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marrying the mother-in-law and step-daughter, identify the conditions for each prohibition, and cite the Quranic and hadith proofs.

Core Idea
Marrying your wife's mother is always haram; marrying your step-daughter is haram only if you consummated with her mother, regardless of custody.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents two key prohibitions in marital relations due to the marriage relationship (musaharah): the mother-in-law and the step-daughter. The Hanafi school distinguishes between them based on the condition of consummation and clarifies that the step-daughter's custody is irrelevant. In this lesson, we will unpack the ruling, examine the proofs from the Quran and Sunna, and understand the Hanafi reasoning that gives the ruling its precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prohibited persons and their conditions
Read the matn: it states that the mother-in-law is prohibited whether or not consummation with her daughter occurred, and the step-daughter is prohibited only if he consummated with her mother. Note that custody ('fī ḥujūrikum') is not a condition.
The condition for step-daughter is dukhūl (consummation) with the mother.
2
Locate the hadith proof for the mother-in-law ruling
The hadith from 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb explicitly says: any man who marries a woman, whether he consummates or not, it is not permissible for him to marry her mother. This establishes the absolute prohibition.
The hadith covers both rulings but the mother-in-law part is unconditional.
3
Locate the Quranic proof for the step-daughter ruling
Quran 4:23 mentions step-daughters (rabāʾib) who are in the husband's custody from wives he has consummated with. The Hanafi school reads the phrase 'fī ḥujūrikum' as descriptive of the common situation, not a restrictive condition.
The hadith also confirms the condition of consummation for step-daughter.
4
Conclude the Hanafi position
Mother-in-law: haram absolutely. Step-daughter: haram only if consummation with her mother occurred, regardless of whether she is in the husband's custody or not.
The ḥadīth and āyah together give complete clarity.

A father's wife is permanently forbidden to his son — the marriage contract itself creates the prohibition, not consummation.

This demonstrates the gravity of the sin and confirms the Quranic ruling.
4
Determine the scope
The prohibition is unconditional: it applies whether the father's marriage was consummated or not, and whether the father is living or deceased. Grandfathers are explicitly included.
There is no exception after the revelation (excluded what preceded).
5
Confirm scholarly consensus
All four Sunni schools agree on this ruling. There is no ikhtilaf on its prohibition.
Cross-madhhab verification strengthens certainty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a master marrying his female slave or his male slave's wife, identify the proof from the Companion 'Umar, and explain the legal reasoning behind the prohibition.

Core Idea
A master cannot marry his female slave or his male slave's wife — ownership and marriage cannot combine.

This passage from al-Quduri presents a clear prohibition: a master cannot marry his own female slave nor the wife of his male slave. The ruling is supported by an action of the Companion 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, who punished a woman for marrying her slave. In this lesson, we will unpack the ruling and the proof, and understand why this combination is prohibited in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What exactly is being prohibited? The master marrying his female slave, and the master marrying the wife of his male slave.
Two distinct scenarios are covered.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
What proof does al-Quduri cite? He cites the action of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab (a mursal report) as the wajh (basis).
Wajh here means the evidence or argument.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof indicates the ruling)
How does 'Umar's action prove prohibition? 'Umar separated the woman from her slave and punished her, showing that such a marriage is invalid and punishable.
The report also says he forbade husbands to her as further punishment.
4
Examine the authority of the dalil
The report is mursal (missing a companion in the chain). However, two mursal reports strengthen each other, and the Hanafi school accepts mursal reports of a Companion as a proof.
Thubut is zanni (non-definitive transmission) but dalala is qati (definitive indication).
5
Understand the rationale (hikmah) of the prohibition
Why is this prohibited? Combining ownership and marriage in one person creates a conflict of interest — the master would have full authority over the slave and also be her husband, undermining the equality and consent required in marriage.
This is a maslaha-based reasoning to protect the institution of marriage.

Prohibition of Marrying Majus and Idolaters
Ruling with hadith and Qur'anic proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marrying Majusiyyat and idolatresses, and cite the hadith and Qur'anic proof for the prohibition.

Core Idea
Marriage to a polytheist woman is absolutely prohibited in the Hanafi school.

This lesson examines a clear-cut prohibition in Islamic family law: the marriage of Muslim men to women from polytheistic faiths, specifically Zoroastrians (Majus) and idol worshippers. The passage provides two key proofs: a prophetic letter and a Qur'anic verse. Understanding this ruling helps delineate permissible marriage partners and reinforces the importance of textual evidence in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling (hukm)
Read the opening statement: 'la yajuz' — it is not permissible. This establishes the hukm level as haram. Note that the statement is general, covering both Majusiyyat and wathaniyyat.
The ruling is unconditional.
2
Locate the hadith proof
The first proof (wajh) is a hadith from the Prophet's letter to the Majus of Hajar. Identify the operative clause: 'wa la tunkaḥ minhum imra'ah' (their women are not to be married). This explicit prohibition (nahy) indicates haram.
The hadith is from Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq; its transmission is ahad (zanni al-thubut), but its meaning is clear (qati al-dalala).
3
Analyze the dalal of the hadith
The phrase 'la tunkaḥ' is a prohibition (nahy) in the passive form. In Hanafi usul, a direct prohibition (nahy) indicates that the act is haram unless there is evidence to the contrary. Here, no such evidence exists.
This step confirms the hukm level from the hadith.
4
Locate the Qur'anic proof
The second proof is Quran 2:221: 'Wa la tankiḥu al-mushrikati hatta yu'minn' (And do not marry polytheist women until they believe). The verse uses a prohibition (la tankiḥu) which again signifies haram. The term 'mushrikat' includes both Majus and idol worshippers.
The verse is general; it covers all polytheists, including those not from the People of the Book.
5
Understand the scope and implications
Note that Majus are treated as ahl al-kitab for jizyah but not for marriage. The prohibition applies to any woman who does not belong to the People of the Book (Jews/Christians) or a recognized monotheistic faith. If the woman converts to Islam, marriage becomes permissible.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school.

Marriage During Ihram: Hanafi Position
Ruling with conflicting hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marriage during ihram and explain how the Hanafi school reconciles conflicting hadith evidence.

Core Idea
The Prophet's action overrides the prohibition hadith: marriage in ihram is valid.

This passage from al-Quduri presents the Hanafi ruling that a person in the state of ihram can validly marry. The ruling is based on the authentic report that the Prophet (ﷺ) himself married while in ihram, despite a contrary hadith prohibiting it. The Hanafi school prioritizes the actual practice of the Prophet over the general prohibition, which they consider either abrogated or interpretively overridden.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states 'yajuz' (it is permissible). Note that the Hanafi school considers the marriage contract valid and not prohibited, though some later scholars consider it makruh tanzihan.
The ruling is for both male and female muhrim.
2
List the Three Hadith Proofs
Three wajh (proofs) are cited: (1) The Prophet married while muhrim. (2) A hadith prohibiting marriage by a muhrim. (3) Maimuna's report that the Prophet married her while halal (not in ihram).
These represent the conflicting evidence that the Hanafi school must reconcile.
3
Understand the Hanafi Reconciliation
The Hanafi school takes the action of the Prophet (marrying while muhrim) as the primary evidence, considering it stronger than the prohibition hadith. The prohibition may be abrogated or interpreted as non-binding (e.g., makruh rather than haram). The third hadith clarifies the actual state of the Prophet at the time of marriage.
This is a classic example of preferring 'fi'l' (action) over 'qawl' (saying) when direct conflict exists.
4
Note the Cross-Madhhab Conflict
Other schools, particularly the Shafi'i school, prohibit marriage during ihram based on the explicit prohibition hadith. The practical difference is the validity of the contract.
The Hanafi view is mukhtalaf fih, but their evidence is considered sound within their usul.

Free Woman's Marriage: The Hanafi Ruling and Its Evidence
Ruling with proofs on the validity of marriage contracted by a free adult woman without a guardian.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a free adult sane woman may contract her own marriage without a guardian, and identify the primary textual evidence (Quran 2:232 and the hadith on consent) used to support this ruling.

Core Idea
A woman's consent is the essential element; the guardian's presence is not required for validity.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi position on marriage without a guardian. Based on the Quranic prohibition of preventing women from marrying and the Prophet's emphasis on seeking a woman's consent, the Hanafi school holds that a free, adult, sane woman can contract her own marriage. We will examine the primary evidence and note an internal difference among Hanafi scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
The ruling applies to a free woman who has reached puberty and is sane. According to Abu Hanifa and Zufar, her marriage contract is valid by her consent alone, even without a guardian.
2

The verse prohibits guardians from preventing women from marrying when the couple agrees. This implies that women have the right to choose their spouse, and the guardian cannot veto that choice.
The address is to guardians, indicating that the woman's decision is paramount.

The Prophet said that a previously married woman must be consulted, and a virgin must give permission. This shows that her consent is necessary, and if it is sufficient for validity, then she can act on her own.
The hadith does not mention a guardian, implying that consent alone is enough.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad require a guardian for the contract to be valid, but only with the woman's consent. Abu Hanifa and Zufar do not require a guardian at all.
The mufta bihi (reliable position) is Abu Hanifa's view.

The Guardian Requirement: Hanafi vs Shafi'i Perspectives
Inter-madhhab dispute over the requirement of a guardian in marriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the necessity of a guardian in marriage, analyze the key hadith used by each school, and explain the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
The same hadiths are interpreted differently; the Hanafi school prioritizes consent over guardian authority, while the Shafi'i school requires the guardian's involvement for validity.

This lesson focuses on the well-known dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the role of the wali (guardian) in a woman's marriage. We will examine the Shafi'i proof—especially the hadith 'Any woman married without the permission of her guardian, her marriage is invalid'—and the Hanafi response to it. Understanding this dispute deepens your appreciation of how different usul principles lead to different rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
A free adult sane woman can contract her own marriage without a guardian; it is valid (sahih) by her consent alone.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Zufar, and is mufta bihi.
2
State the Shafi'i position
A guardian is a condition of validity; if a woman contracts her own marriage without a guardian, the marriage is invalid (batil).
This is the standard Shafi'i view.
3

The Prophet said: 'Any woman married without the permission of her guardian, her marriage is invalid.' This seems to directly contradict the Hanafi view.
This hadith is cited by the Shafi'i school as a clear dalil.
4

Hanafis interpret this hadith as referring to a woman who lacks capacity (e.g., a minor) or as a recommendation (not a condition). They argue that the earlier Quranic verse (2:232) and the hadith on consent take precedence, or that this hadith is abrogated or weaker in transmission.
The Hanafi school prioritizes the stronger evidence and the general principle of consent.
5
Determine the practical difference
In a Hanafi context, a woman marrying without guardian is valid; in a Shafi'i context, it is invalid unless the ruler performs the marriage if there is no guardian.
This has significant implications for marriage registration and family law.

Guardian Cannot Compel a Mature Woman into Marriage
Ruling prohibiting guardian compulsion with hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a guardian compelling a mature woman into marriage and explain the hadith evidence that establishes the requirement of consent.

Core Idea
A mature woman's consent is essential; no guardian may compel her into marriage.

This lesson examines a foundational ruling in Hanafi marriage law: the absolute prohibition of a guardian compelling a mature woman into wedlock. The text, drawn from Al-Quduri's matn, is followed by two hadith proofs that establish the necessity of the woman's consent, whether she is a virgin or previously married. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi position on women's legal capacity in marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the matn: 'It is not permissible for the guardian to force a mature woman into marriage, whether she is a virgin or a non-virgin.' Note the operative verb 'yajuz' (permissible) negated, indicating prohibition.
The ruling is general: it applies to all guardians, not just fathers.
2
Examine the first proof
Read the hadith of Ibn Abbas about a virgin girl whose father married her against her will. The Prophet gave her the choice to annul or accept. What does this show about consent?
The hadith is a clear precedent that even a father cannot force his virgin daughter.
3
Examine the second proof
Read the hadith of Abu Hurairah: 'A woman who has been previously married is not to be married until she is consulted.' This explicitly requires consultation (istishar) for a thayyib, implying that compulsion is invalid.
The word 'la tunka' (not to be married) indicates prohibition.
4
Understand the evidence relationship
Both hadith are ahad (single-narrator) in transmission, so their thubut is zanni. However, their meaning (dalala) is clear and definitive in prohibiting compulsion. Hanafi usul accepts ahad hadith for establishing prohibitions when the meaning is qat'i.
The dalala is qat'i because the imperative to consent is unambiguous.
5
Apply the ruling to cases
Consider: a father arranges a marriage for his 20-year-old daughter without asking her. She is both balighah and bikr. According to Hanafi law, the marriage is suspended (mawquf) until she confirms acceptance. If she rejects, it is annulled.
For a thayyib, even silence is not enough; she must explicitly consent verbally.

Consent in Marriage: Silence for Virgin, Speech for Thayyib
Ruling with hadith proof on modes of consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for obtaining consent from a virgin and a thayyib, and apply the hadith evidence to differentiate between the two.

Core Idea
A virgin's silence is consent; a thayyib must speak.

In Islamic marriage, the woman's consent is essential. But how is it expressed? The Hanafi school, guided by prophetic hadith, differentiates between a virgin (who may be shy) and a previously married woman (thayyib). This lesson examines the ruling and its evidence, highlighting the practical distinction and addressing a common cross-madhhab difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage rules on how consent is given: for a virgin, silence or laughter is sufficient; for a thayyib, explicit verbal consent is required.
2
Locate the dalil
Two hadith are cited: one from Aisha emphasizing the necessity of guardian's permission, and one from Adi ibn Adi explicitly stating that 'the thayyib speaks for herself, and the virgin's consent is her silence.'
The second hadith is the key proof for the Hanafi distinction.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The first hadith establishes that marriage without permission is invalid, implying consent is required. The second hadith specifies the modes of consent, differentiating between virgin and thayyib based on natural modesty.
The operative phrase is 'al-bikru riḍāhā ṣamtuhā' (the virgin's consent is her silence).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that a virgin must also verbally consent, arguing that silence is ambiguous. The Hanafi response relies on the explicit hadith and the principle of taking modesty into account.
This ikhtilaf has practical implications for marriage validity.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa) because it is directly based on the prophetic hadith and considers the natural shyness of a virgin. It is the relied-upon view in the school.
The tarjih authority here is the explicit text of the hadith.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khiyar al-bulugh and distinguish the situations where the option applies or does not apply based on the type of guardian.

Core Idea
When a non-father/grandfather marries a minor, the minor has the option to annul upon puberty.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on khiyar al-bulugh (option of puberty) for children married by a guardian other than their father or paternal grandfather. The author presents the ruling and supports it with two primary proofs: the action of Anas ibn Malik (a Companion) and the decree of Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz (a Tabi'i). A third proof is cited for an opposing view attributed to Abu Yusuf. Understanding this ruling clarifies the scope of guardianship authority in Hanafi marriage law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the opening sentence: 'If someone other than the father and grandfather marries them, then each has the option when they attain puberty.' Note that the ruling applies to both male and female minors.
The exception: father and grandfather do NOT give this option.
2
Analyze the first proof (Qawl al-Sahabi)
Examine the hadith where Anas ibn Malik acts as guardian for his mother Umm Sulaym. The Prophet's approval establishes that a non-father relative can marry a minor, and by implication, the minor retains the right to choose upon maturity.
This proof carries weight as a Companion's practice (qawl al-sahabi).
3
Analyze the second proof (Tabi'i decree)
Consider the decree of Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, who wrote regarding orphans that they have the option when married young. This shows the practice of the early Muslim community.
A Tabi'i's statement is a valid proof in Hanafi usul when not contradicted.
4
Evaluate the opposing proof
Read the third proof attributed to Abu Yusuf: the statement of Hammad that the marriage is valid with no option. Recognize this as a dissenting view within the Hanafi school.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position overrides this.
5
Confirm the authoritative Hanafi position
Conclude that both minors have the option upon puberty when married by a non-father/grandfather. The option is a right, not an obligation.
If the option is not exercised, the marriage remains valid and binding.

Guardianship Restrictions and Non-Asabah Guardians
Ruling with proofs on guardianship restrictions and non-asabah guardians.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state who cannot be a guardian in marriage, explain Abu Hanifa's position on non-asabah guardians, and identify the proofs for each ruling.

Core Idea
No guardianship for minors, slaves, the insane, or non-Muslims over Muslim women; non-asabah relatives may act as guardians.

This lesson examines two interrelated rulings: the absolute prohibition of certain individuals from acting as marriage guardians, and Abu Hanifa's unique position allowing non-asabah relatives (such as maternal uncles) to give a woman in marriage. Through Quranic verse, prophetic hadith, and companion statements, you will see how these rulings are derived and why Abu Hanifa's view is relied upon in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify prohibited guardians
List the four categories of people who cannot be guardians: minors, slaves, the insane, and non-Muslims over Muslim women.
The phrase 'لا ولاية' indicates a definitive prohibition.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof
Examine verse 4:141 which denies non-Muslims authority over believers, establishing the prohibition of non-Muslim guardians.
The verse is qat'i in both transmission and meaning.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position
Recognise that non-asabah relatives (e.g., maternal uncle) may validly give a woman in marriage. This is the permissibility ruling.
Non-asabah are relatives not connected through male agnates.
4
Trace the hadith proof
Consider the hadith of Aisha arranging a marriage for a relative, showing prophetic approval of non-asabah involvement.
The hadith is a proof that the Prophet did not object.
5
Note the intra-madhhab dispute
Understand that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) restrict guardianship to asabah. The mufta bihi position is Abu Hanifa's view.

Guardianship When No Wali and Absent Wali
Ruling on marriage by ex-owner and distant guardian when wali absent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios in which a woman may be given in marriage by a non-asabah guardian and identify the condition for a distant guardian to act (ghaybah muntaqi'ah).

Core Idea
A woman without a wali can be married by her former master; if the nearest wali is absent beyond reach for a year, the next wali may act.

This lesson addresses two situations where a woman may be married without her agnatic guardian. First, a woman who has no wali at all can be given in marriage by her former master (mawla al-itaq). Second, if the nearest wali is absent in a place so remote that communication is possible only once a year, the next nearest wali may contract the marriage. These rulings from Imam al-Quduri provide practical solutions to avoid undue hardship. We will also note a difference of opinion with Imam Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first scenario
Determine who acts as guardian when a woman has no wali (e.g., a freed slave with no agnatic relatives). The text states that her ex-master (mawla al-itaq) may marry her.
2
Identify the second scenario
When the nearest wali is absent, the next wali may act only if his absence is 'muntaqi'ah' (interrupted). This means contact is impossible except once a year.
This condition prevents hasty actions when the nearest wali can be reached in a reasonable time.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Zufar (a Hanafi) held that the marriage should be delayed until the absent wali can be contacted, rather than transferring authority immediately.
The mufta bihi position follows the majority Hanafi view allowing the distant guardian to act.
4
Define ghaybah muntaqi'ah
Understand the definition: being in a country where caravans travel only once a year, making timely communication impossible.
This definition is key to applying the rule correctly.

Kafa'ah (Compatibility) in Marriage
Ruling with hadith proofs on kafa'ah (compatibility) in marriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three aspects of kafa'ah in the Hanafi school and explain how each hadith proof supports the ruling.

Core Idea
Compatibility in marriage is a condition proven by multiple hadith, considered in lineage, religion, and wealth (ability to pay mahr and nafaqah).

In Hanafi fiqh, kafa'ah is not about equality in all things but about social and religious harmony. This passage from al-Quduri presents the ruling that kafa'ah is required and supports it with four hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him). We will see how each hadith contributes to the proof and what the practical implications are for marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What is the central ruling about kafa'ah in marriage? The text says it is 'considered' (mu'tabarah). It is a condition that gives guardians the right to annul if absent.
The ruling is that kafa'ah is a condition for marriage; non-compliance allows guardians to seek separation (firqah).
2
List the three aspects
The text states kafa'ah is in lineage (nasab), religion (din), and wealth (mal). Wealth is specifically defined as ability to pay mahr and nafaqah.
Each aspect is proven by a hadith. Note that the hadith on lineage includes exceptions (weaver, cupper).
3
Analyze the hadith proofs
The passage cites four hadith: (1) 'Choose for your sperm...' (general command); (2) 'Three things do not delay...' (urgency); (3) Story of the girl whose father married her to a non-kuf' (guardians' right); (4) Arabs are compatible with each other except certain professions (lineage specification).
The first two hadith are general proofs; the third shows guardians' right; the fourth specifies lineage.
4
Understand the consequence
If a woman marries a non-kuf', her wali (guardian) has the right to demand annulment. This protects the family's honor and the woman's status.
The marriage is valid until annulled. The guardian must act promptly or lose the right.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi ruling on khalwa sahiha (valid seclusion). The default rule is that valid seclusion with no physical or legal barrier to intercourse triggers the same consequences as actual intercourse: the wife becomes entitled to the full mahr and must observe iddah upon divorce. Exceptions are provided where seclusion is not considered valid: if either spouse is sick, fasting in Ramadan, in ihram for hajj/umrah, or the woman is menstruating. In these cases, seclusion has no legal effect. An important sub-ruling concerns the eunuch (majbub): according to Abu Hanifa, even a eunuch's seclusion obligates the full mahr because the hadith about uncovering a woman's veil indicates that privacy alone, regardless of consummation ability, makes the dower obligatory. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ, holding that only half is due since consummation is impossible. However, the text here adopts Abu Hanifa's view. Finally, mut'ah is recommended (mustahabb) for every divorced woman except one specific case: a woman divorced before consummation whose mahr was named. In that case, she gets half the named mahr, not mut'ah. The proofs include: (1) a mursal hadith about uncovering a woman's veil obligating the dower, (2) Umar's ruling that closing the door and drawing the curtain makes the dower and iddah obligatory, (3) Ibn Abbas's contrary view that dower is only obligatory with actual intercourse (used to highlight the Hanafi position via istihsan), (4) repetition of the mursal hadith for the eunuch's case, (5) Quran 2:236 for mut'ah, and (6) Ibn Umar's statement specifying the exception to mut'ah. The Urdu principle notes that if despite valid seclusion the man cannot consummate due to his own inability, the mahr may be reduced (referring to the eunuch ikhtilaf).

States that mut'ah is recommended for all divorced women except one specific case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mut'ah for divorced women and identify the one exception where it becomes wajib, citing the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Mut'ah is mustahabb for all divorced women, but wajib for the one divorced before consummation with no mahr.

Mut'ah (consolation gift) is a financial compensation given to a divorced woman. According to the Hanafi school, it is generally recommended (mustahabb), but there is one specific case where it becomes obligatory (wajib): when a woman is divorced before consummation (dukhul) and no mahr was specified in the marriage contract. This ruling is based on the Quranic command in Surah al-Baqarah 2:236 and is supported by a statement of Ibn Umar. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the evidence, and the cross-madhhab dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
What is the default legal classification (hukm) of mut'ah for a divorced woman? The text states it is mustahabb (recommended).
Mustahabb means performing it is rewarded but not sinful to omit.
2
Isolate the exception
Which divorced woman is excluded from the general mustahabb ruling? She is the one divorced before consummation and for whom no mahr was specified.
For her, mut'ah becomes wajib, not just mustahabb.
3
Examine the Quranic proof
Read Surah al-Baqarah 2:236. The imperative 'matte'uhunna' (give them mut'ah) is a command. How does the Hanafi school interpret it?
The imperative indicates obligation (wujub) in principle, but other evidences limit its application to this specific case.
4
Understand the Hanafi interpretation
Why is the command not applied to all divorced women? Because other Quranic verses (e.g., 2:237 about half mahr) and the saying of Ibn Umar show that mut'ah is not obligatory when a mahr has been specified or after consummation.
Thus the general command is narrowed (takhsis) to the exception.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
How do other schools, such as the Shafi'is, differ? They hold that the command is general and mut'ah is wajib for all divorced women.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is whether a husband can be compelled to give mut'ah in cases beyond the Hanafi exception.

Shighar (Exchange Marriage): Validity and Mahr
Rulings on exchange marriage: both contracts valid, each woman gets mahr al-mithl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shighar, explain the reconciliation of the prohibition hadith, and apply the rule that mahr al-mithl is wajib.

Core Idea
Shighar marriage is valid; the prohibition targets the condition of no mahr, not the contract.

Shighar, or exchange marriage, where two men marry each other's daughters or sisters without specifying a mahr, appears to be forbidden by a clear hadith. Yet the Hanafi school holds the marriage contracts valid. How can this be? This lesson unpacks the classical Hanafi reasoning: the prohibition is directed at the sinful condition, not the marriage itself, so each woman must receive her proper mahr (mahr al-mithl).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is shighar (exchange marriage). The ruling stated in the text is that both contracts are jā'iz (valid) and each woman gets mahr al-mithl.
Jā'iz here means sahih (valid), not merely permissible.
2
Analyze the proofs (adillah)
The author provides three proofs: (1) Ibn Abbas's ruling that marriage is valid despite a void condition; (2) 'Ata's statement that both marriages stand and sadaq is due; (3) the hadith of prohibition from Ibn Umar. Note the apparent conflict between the prohibition and the ruling of validity.
The hadith is sahih (qat'i thubut) but its meaning (dalala) is zanni.
3
Understand the Hanafi reconciliation
The Hanafi school reconciles the hadith by interpreting the prohibition as directed at the condition of no mahr (a void condition), not at the marriage contract itself. The contract is valid; the condition is void and makruh.
This is a classic application of the principle: 'The marriage is valid; the condition is void.'
4
Identify the operative ruling (mufta bihi)
Imam Abu Hanifa's view is that mahr al-mithl is wajib in shighar. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
The Urdu note confirms 'mahr al-mithl is obligatory according to Imam Abu Hanifa.'
5
Contrast with opposing school
The Shafi'i school holds shighar invalid because the Prophet forbade it, and the condition is integral to the contract. The Hanafi response is that the prohibition does not affect the validity of the contract itself.
This ikhtilaf arises from different usul principles on how to treat a prohibition in a contract.

Shighar Marriage: Validity and Mahr al-Mithl
Ruling on shighar marriage with three proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shighar marriage and explain the school's reconciliation of the prophetic prohibition with the validity of the marriage.

Core Idea
Shighar marriage is valid, but each wife must receive mahr al-mithl.

Shighar—a marriage where two men exchange female relatives without a dower—raises questions about validity and fairness. The Hanafi school upholds the validity of such marriages, requiring each woman to receive a dower appropriate to her status (mahr al-mithl). This lesson unpacks the ruling and the three proofs Al-Quduri cites to support it.

Guided Path
1
Define shighar
Understand the definition: two men marry each other's female relatives, making each contract the consideration for the other, with no specified dower.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The contracts are valid (ja'iz), and each woman is entitled to mahr al-mithl (dower of her equivalent).
This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Examine the Sahabi proof
Ibn Abbas ruled that a marriage condition (like a penalty) does not invalidate the marriage; the marriage is valid and the condition is void. This supports the validity of shighar despite the condition of exchange.
Sunan al-Bayhaqi, Bab al-Shurut fi al-Nikah.
4
Examine the Tabi'i proof
Ata stated that in shighar, the marriages are confirmed and each woman takes a dower. This directly supports the Hanafi position.
Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
5
Examine the Hadith proof and Hanafi interpretation
The Prophet forbade shighar when there is no dower. The Hanafis apply this prohibition to cases without any dower; if mahr al-mithl is given, the marriage is valid, thus reconciling the hadith with the ruling.
Sahih al-Bukhari, Bab al-Shighar.

Shighar is defined as two men marrying each other's female relatives (daughters or sisters) without an agreed dower, making each contract the consideration for the other. According to the Hanafi school, such marriages are valid, but each woman is entitled to mahr al-mithl (the dower of a woman of her status) rather than a specified dower.
