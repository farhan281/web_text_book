The Permanent Rejection of Testimony after Hadd al-Qadhf
Ruling on testimony of qadhif with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of a Muslim flogged for qadhf, explain the Quranic proof, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument.

Core Idea
The Quranic 'abadan' makes the rejection of testimony permanent—repentance does not restore it.

The chapter on hadd al-qadhf addresses not only the physical punishment but also its legal consequences. Among the most significant is the permanent disqualification of the qadhif as a witness. This lesson examines the Quranic basis for this ruling and the inter-madhhab debate surrounding it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states: 'If a Muslim is flogged for qadhf, his testimony falls, even if he repents.' Identify the exact ruling and its scope.
The ruling applies to a Muslim who has been subjected to hadd al-qadhf.
2
Locate the Quranic Proof
The author cites Surah an-Nur 24:4. Find the verse in the source and note the key word 'abadan' (ever).
The verse commands 'never accept their testimony'.
3
Analyze 'Abadan'
Explain why 'abadan' indicates perpetuity in Hanafi usul. The word is explicit in denying acceptance forever.
Hanafis hold that 'abadan' is 'qat'i' in dalala.
4
Understand the Shafi'i Objection
The Shafi'i school argues based on the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab who accepted testimony after repentance. Identify this counterargument.
Umar's action is a sunnah, but the Hanafis respond that the Quranic text is unconditional.
5
Review the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response is that the Quranic text is general and cannot be overridden by a practice that may have occurred before the verse's finality.
Also, a hadith states that testimony of one flogged with a hadd is not accepted.

Commentary
This passage is from the chapter on Hadd al-Qadhf (the fixed corporal punishment for false accusation of adultery) in Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with added hadith proofs and usul principles. It contains several rulings: First, it reports that Abu Yusuf holds that ta'zir (discretionary punishment) can reach up to seventy-five lashes, whereas the majority of the Hanafi school (including Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) set the maximum at forty lashes based on a hadith that flogging above ten lashes is only for hudud. Second, the imam may add imprisonment to flogging in ta'zir if he deems it necessary, drawing analogy from the hadd of the unmarried fornicator which includes exile. Third, the severity of beating: ta'zir is the severest, followed by hadd for zina, then hadd for drinking, then hadd for qadhf—this is based on the practice of the Companions where the clothes are removed for the zani but not for the qadhif or drinker. Fourth, if a person dies as a result of the imam's execution of hadd or ta'zir, no blood money is due; the blood is 'hadar' (of no consequence). This is the Hanafi position: there is no guarantee (daman) on the imam or the public treasury. Imam Shafi'i holds that if the death occurs from a hadd that has no fixed text (like drinking), the treasury must pay diya. Fifth, a Muslim who is flogged for qadhf has his testimony permanently rejected, even if he repents. The proof is the Quranic verse (24:4) which commands 'never accept their testimony'—the word 'abadan' indicates perpetuity. The Hanafis also use the hadith that the testimony of one who has been flogged with a hadd is not accepted. The opposing view, attributed to Shafi'i, is that repentance restores testimony, based on the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab who accepted the testimony of Abu Bakra and others after they repented. The Hanafi response is that the Quranic text is general and unconditional. Sixth, if a non-Muslim (dhimmi) is flogged for qadhf and then accepts Islam, his testimony becomes acceptable after Islam, because Islam erases previous sins. This is based on the principle that 'Islam destroys what came before it.' In contrast, if a slave is flogged for qadhf and then freed, his testimony does not become valid, since slavery does not have the same effect as Islam. The passage concludes with two usul principles: (1) Ta'zir is proportionate to the crime; the severity of punishment is based on the nature of the offense. (2) According to Imam Abu Hanifa, there is no liability for death resulting from hadd; Shafi'i holds the treasury is liable. (3) Islam forgives prior sins.

Key Terms
حد القذف (hadd al-qadhf): The fixed corporal punishment of 80 lashes for falsely accusing a chaste person of zina.
شهادة (shahadah): Testimony given in a court of law; in this context, legal testimony of a witness.
أبداً (abadan): Ever, forever; used in the Quran to indicate perpetual rejection of testimony.

Subsidiary Rulings: Ta'zir Severity, Death Liability, and Special Cases
Subsidiary rulings on ta'zir, death from hadd, and testimony of non-Muslim/slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the maximum ta'zir, the imam's discretion to add imprisonment, the absence of liability for death from hadd, and the acceptance of testimony from a non-Muslim qadhif after Islam.

Core Idea
Ta'zir is flexible but capped at 40 lashes; death from hadd carries no diya; Islam erases prior sin but slavery does not.

In addition to the central ruling on testimony, the passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar covers several important subsidiary rulings: the limits of ta'zir, the imam's discretion to combine flogging with imprisonment, the severity hierarchy of beatings, the question of liability if the offender dies during punishment, and the treatment of non-Muslims and slaves who are flogged for qadhf. These rulings complete the Hanafi penal framework for qadhf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rulings on Ta'zir
The passage mentions that Abu Yusuf allows ta'zir up to 75 lashes, but the majority set the maximum at 40. Also, the imam may add imprisonment. Identify each sub-ruling.
The hadith 'no flogging above ten lashes except in a hadd' restricts ta'zir to under 40.
2
Understand the Severity Hierarchy
The passage states the order of severity: ta'zir, then hadd zina, then hadd shurb, then hadd qadhf. The proof is from the practice of Qatada regarding removing clothes.
Ta'zir is considered severest because it can be repeated or combined with other punishments.
3
Analyze the Ruling on Death from Hadd
The ruling: whoever the imam punishes with hadd or ta'zir and dies, his blood is in vain. The Hanafi position is no liability. The Shafi'i position differs.
Proof from a hadith where 'Alqama said he would not pay diya except for wine.
4
Examine Special Cases: Non-Muslim and Slave
The passage states that if a non-Muslim is flogged for qadhf and then accepts Islam, his testimony becomes valid. But a slave flogged and freed does not regain testimony. Understand the rationale.
Islam destroys what came before; slavery does not have that effect.
5
Review the Usul Principles
The passage ends with two usul points: (1) Ta'zir is proportionate to the crime, (2) No liability for death in hadd according to Abu Hanifa, (3) Islam forgives prior sins. Note these.
These principles guide the derivation of rulings.

Commentary
This passage is from the chapter on Hadd al-Qadhf (the fixed corporal punishment for false accusation of adultery) in Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with added hadith proofs and usul principles. It contains several rulings: First, it reports that Abu Yusuf holds that ta'zir (discretionary punishment) can reach up to seventy-five lashes, whereas the majority of the Hanafi school (including Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) set the maximum at forty lashes based on a hadith that flogging above ten lashes is only for hudud. Second, the imam may add imprisonment to flogging in ta'zir if he deems it necessary, drawing analogy from the hadd of the unmarried fornicator which includes exile. Third, the severity of beating: ta'zir is the severest, followed by hadd for zina, then hadd for drinking, then hadd for qadhf—this is based on the practice of the Companions where the clothes are removed for the zani but not for the qadhif or drinker. Fourth, if a person dies as a result of the imam's execution of hadd or ta'zir, no blood money is due; the blood is 'hadar' (of no consequence). This is the Hanafi position: there is no guarantee (daman) on the imam or the public treasury. Imam Shafi'i holds that if the death occurs from a hadd that has no fixed text (like drinking), the treasury must pay diya. Fifth, a Muslim who is flogged for qadhf has his testimony permanently rejected, even if he repents. The proof is the Quranic verse (24:4) which commands 'never accept their testimony'—the word 'abadan' indicates perpetuity. The Hanafis also use the hadith that the testimony of one who has been flogged with a hadd is not accepted. The opposing view, attributed to Shafi'i, is that repentance restores testimony, based on the practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab who accepted the testimony of Abu Bakra and others after they repented. The Hanafi response is that the Quranic text is general and unconditional. Sixth, if a non-Muslim (dhimmi) is flogged for qadhf and then accepts Islam, his testimony becomes acceptable after Islam, because Islam erases previous sins. This is based on the principle that 'Islam destroys what came before it.' In contrast, if a slave is flogged for qadhf and then freed, his testimony does not become valid, since slavery does not have the same effect as Islam. The passage concludes with two usul principles: (1) Ta'zir is proportionate to the crime; the severity of punishment is based on the nature of the offense. (2) According to Imam Abu Hanifa, there is no liability for death resulting from hadd; Shafi'i holds the treasury is liable. (3) Islam forgives prior sins.

Key Terms
تعزير (ta'zir): A discretionary punishment imposed by the judge for offenses that do not have a fixed hadd or for which the hadd is not applicable.
هدَر (hadar): Vain, of no consequence; here, blood shed without liability for blood money or qisas.
الخَبْس (al-khabs): Imprisonment; the imam may add imprisonment to flogging in ta'zir.

Items Exempt from Hand Cutting in Theft
Lists items exempt from hand cutting with implied proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories of items for which the hand is not cut in Hanafi law, and explain the legal rationale for each category.

Core Idea
The hand is not cut for stealing items that are not considered protected property (mal mutaqawwim).

This lesson examines a foundational ruling from the Hanafi school on hudud: the items that exempt a thief from the hadd of hand cutting. Al-Quduri enumerates five categories in his matn. Understanding these exceptions is crucial because they delineate the boundaries of mal mutaqawwim required for applying the hadd. We will explore each category and the evidence supporting the exemptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall ruling
The passage states that there is no cutting for the listed items. Recognize that this is a negative ruling exempting these items from the hadd.
The hadd is a fixed punishment; exemptions narrow its application.
2
List each category and its specific items
Go through the passage: perishables (fruits, milk, meat, etc.), intoxicating drinks, musical instruments (tunbur, duff, mizmar), the mushaf (even with ornamentation), cross of gold/silver, chess and backgammon.
Note that each category shares a common characteristic that removes the hadd.
3
Determine the legal reason (illah) for each exemption
For perishables: they decay quickly and are not stored securely (hirz). For intoxicants and musical instruments: they are impure or sinful objects. For the mushaf: it is sacred and not considered property. For the cross: objects of disbelief. For chess/backgammon: instruments of gambling.
The illah connects the ruling to a broader principle.
4
Connect to the broader principle: mal mutaqawwim
The hadd requires the stolen property to be mal mutaqawwim (valuable, permissible property kept in a hirz). These items fail that condition because of their nature.
If an item is not mal mutaqawwim, the hadd cannot be applied.
5
Note inter-madhhab differences
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may apply hadd for the ornament on a mushaf or for a gold cross. Recognize the Hanafi stance and the evidence.
The difference stems from whether the material value or the primary nature governs.

Commentary
This passage lists categories of items for which the hand of a thief is not cut, according to the Hanafi school. The ruling is a statement from the matn of al-Quduri. The passage type is a ruling statement supported by hadith evidence (provided in the surrounding commentary). The first category is perishable items: fresh fruits, milk, meat, watermelon, fruit on the tree, and unharvested crops. These are not considered wealth that is preserved (mutamawwal) and are not typically kept in a secure place (hirz), so the hadd is not applied. The second category is intoxicating drinks and musical instruments (tunbur, duff, mizmar) – these are ritually impure (najis) or forbidden objects, thus not considered valuable property (mal mutaqawwim). The third category is the mushaf (codex of the Qur'an) – even if it has silver or gold ornamentation, the hand is not cut because the primary value is the divine word, not the material. The fourth is a cross of gold or silver – this is an object of disbelief and not considered property. The fifth is chess and backgammon – these are instruments of gambling and frivolity, not considered property. The author then provides various hadith and sayings of the Companions as evidence for these rulings. For perishables, the hadith of Rafi' and the practice of Uthman are cited. For intoxicants and instruments, the hadith on wine and the prohibition of musical instruments are used. For the mushaf, the story of Safwan ibn Umayyah is referenced. For the cross, the verse condemning idle talk (Quran 31:6) is invoked. For chess and backgammon, a hadith equating playing backgammon with dipping the hand in pig's blood is cited. Exempt Items from Hand-Cutting for Theft
Rulings and hadith proofs on items exempt from hand-cutting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on which items are exempt from hand-cutting for theft and identify the textual proofs (dalil) for each exemption.

Core Idea
Dogs, musical instruments, and ledgers (except account books) are not considered valuable property, so their theft does not warrant amputation.

Not all stolen items trigger the hadd punishment of hand amputation. The Hanafi school specifies that the stolen property must have inherent value in the Shari'ah. Items like dogs, musical instruments, and certain ledgers are not considered valuable and thus exempt. This lesson examines these exempt items and the proofs from the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify each exempt item
Read the list: dogs, cheetahs, tambourine, drum, flute, and all ledgers except account ledgers. Also note that valuable woods (teak, bamboo, ebony, sandalwood) are not exempt.
The exemption applies to items that are not considered lawful property (mal) in the Shari'ah.
2
Classify the type of exemption
Distinguish between items that are inherently non-valuable (dogs, musical instruments) and items that become valuable after transformation (wood into vessels/doors).
3
Examine the hadith proofs for dogs
Four hadith are cited: (1) the order to kill dogs, indicating they are not valuable; (2) prohibition of eating fanged beasts; (3) prohibition of wine and gambling; (4) a prophecy about people making lawful silk and musical instruments. The first two directly show dogs are not treated as property.
The Hanafi school uses these to establish that dogs have no monetary value.
4
Examine the hadith proofs for musical instruments
The hadith about kubba (drum) and ma'azif (musical instruments) indicate they are unlawful to use, thus not considered valuable property subject to hadd.
The prohibition on use negates their status as tradeable property.
5

Note that account ledgers are not exempt because they contain financial records that are valuable. Similarly, valuable woods and crafted wooden items are subject to hadd.
The underlying principle: the item must have recognized Shari'ah value (e.g., usable, permissible, tradeable).

Exempt Persons and Shared Property from Hand-Cutting
Rulings and proofs on exempt persons and shared property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the categories of persons (khain, nabbash, muntahib, mukhtalis) and cases of shared property (public treasury, parents/children) for which hadd amputation is not applied, and explain the underlying rationale.

Core Idea
Hadd amputation is not applied when the thief has a right in the property (as in shared funds) or when the taking lacks secrecy (as in plunder) or trust (as in embezzlement).

Not all takings of another's property constitute theft (sariqa) subject to hadd. The Hanafi school exempts certain persons due to the nature of their act: traitors (who betray trust), grave robbers (whose taking is not from a guarded place), and those who take openly (plunderers, snatchers). Additionally, theft from property in which the thief has a share, like the public treasury or parents' property, does not trigger amputation. This lesson explores these exemptions and their proofs.

Guided Path
1
List the exempt persons
Identify the five types: khain (traitor), nabbash (grave robber), muntahib (plunderer), mukhtalis (snatcher). Note that khain and nabbash have specific definitions.
Khain had lawful access to the property; nabbash steals from a grave which is not a guarded place.
2
Understand the rationale for each
For khain: no secrecy, property came lawfully. For nabbash: grave is not hirz. For muntahib and mukhtalis: taking is open or with force, not stealthy.
The essence of sariqa is taking secretly from a guarded place.
3
Examine the proofs
The hadith 'no cutting for a traitor, plunderer, or snatcher' directly supports these three. For grave robber, companion and follower statements indicate no cutting, though there is difference.
The proofs include hadith, companion sayings, and a practice of early caliphs.
4
Apply to shared property cases
The public treasury (bayt al-mal) is owned by all Muslims collectively, so the thief has a share. Similarly, parents' property is shared with children (and vice versa). The principle: 'your property, some of it stole from some' -- i.e., the thief is taking his own share.
This creates a shubha (doubt) that bars hadd.
5
Memorize the Urdu principles
Two principles: (1) If the thief took property in exchange for his own goods, no cutting. (2) If he took back his own dirham, no cutting.
These summarize the idea that the thief must have no right whatsoever in the stolen property.

Book of Theft and Highway Robbery. There is no amputation for ledgers all except account ledgers. There is no amputation for theft of a dog, nor a cheetah, nor a tambourine, nor a drum, nor a flute. Amputation applies for teak, bamboo, ebony, sandalwood. And if one makes vessels or doors from wood, amputation applies for them. There is no amputation upon a traitor male or female, nor a grave robber, nor a plunderer, nor a snatcher. 

Proof (1) hadith for establishing 'no amputation for theft of a dog': from Jabir: the Prophet ordered killing dogs... (Sunan Abi Dawud). Proof (2)... from Abu Tha'laba: the Prophet forbade eating every fanged beast (Bukhari). Proof (3)... from Ibn Abbas: God forbade wine, gambling, and kubba (drum) (Abu Dawud). Proof (4)... from Abu 'Amir: the Prophet said there will be people who make lawful silk, wine, and musical instruments (Bukhari).

Proof (1) for 'no amputation upon a traitor': from Jabir: the Prophet said there is no cutting for a traitor, plunderer, or snatcher (Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud). Proof (2) companion's saying for 'no grave robber': Ibn Abbas said there is no cutting for a grave robber (Ibn Abi Shayba). Proof (3) follower's saying: al-Zuhri reported that in Mu'awiya's time they did not cut the hand of a grave robber but beat and paraded him (Ibn Abi Shayba). Proof (4) follower's saying: from Ma'mar that 'Umar ibn 'Abd al-'Aziz cut off a grave robber's hand (Ibn Abi Shayba).

Proof (1) companion's saying for 'the thief from the public treasury is not cut': from Ibn Abbas: a slave from the fifth booty stole from it and the Prophet did not cut him, saying 'God's property, some of it stole from some' (Ibn Majah). Proof (2) from 'Ali: he used to say there is no cutting for one who steals from the public treasury (Bayhaqi). Proof (3) from al-Qasim: Sa'd wrote to 'Umar about a man who stole from the treasury; 'Umar wrote back that there is no cutting, he has a share in it (Ibn Abi Shayba).

Proof (1) companion's saying for 'one who steals from his parents or his child': Ibn Mas'ud was asked about a slave stealing the cloak of another slave; he said 'your property, some of it stole from some, no cutting upon him' and this is also the saying of Ibn Abbas (Bayhaqi, Ibn Abi Shayba).

Principles: If one takes someone's property in exchange for his own goods, then there is no cutting for theft. If one takes back his own dirham, then the ruling of cutting does not apply.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's Kitab al-Sariqa presents a series of rulings on when the hadd punishment of amputation (qat') is not applied for theft, along with the textual proofs. The author first lists items for which cutting is not applied: all types of ledgers except account ledgers (because they are not considered valuable property in the same way), dogs (considered filthy), cheetahs (similarly, though not dogs), and musical instruments (drums and flutes) which are not considered property of value in the Shari'a). For precious woods like teak, bamboo, ebony, and sandalwood, cutting is applied because they are valuable. If wood is made into vessels or doors, cutting applies because they become usable property. Then the author lists persons exempted: a traitor (khain) who embezzles entrusted property, a grave robber (nabbash), a plunderer (muntahib), and a snatcher (mukhtalis)—these are not considered thieves in the full sense because of the nature of their taking. Four hadith are cited for the dog ruling, showing it is not treated as valuable property. For the exempt persons, a hadith from Jabir states no cutting for a traitor, plunderer, or snatcher. For grave robbers, companion and follower statements show difference of opinion; the relied position is no cutting. Next, the text addresses theft from the public treasury (bayt al-mal) and from property where the thief has a share, including theft from parents or children. The principle is that if the thief has a share in the property, cutting is not applied because he is taking what is partly his. Proofs from Ibn Abbas, Ali, Umar, and Ibn Mas'ud support this. Finally, two Urdu principles summarize: if one takes someone's property in exchange for his own goods (e.g., as a replacement), no cutting; if one takes back his own dirham (reclaiming his money), no cutting. These rulings are built on the concept that the stolen property must be wholly owned by another person, with no right of the thief in it.

Key Terms
خَائِن (khā'in): One who betrays a trust by embezzling property entrusted to him.
نَبَّاش (nabbāsh): Grave robber; one who steals from graves.
مُنْتَهِب (muntahib): Plunderer; one who takes property openly by force.
مُخْتَلِس (mukhtalis): Snatcher; one who takes property quickly, often in public.
بَيْت الْمَال (bayt al-mal): Public treasury; public funds belonging to the Muslim community.

Exemptions from Hand-Cutting for Theft in Hanafi Fiqh
Compilation of cases where the hand of the thief is not cut according to Hanafi school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of thieves who are exempt from hand-cutting according to the Hanafi school and identify the underlying rationale (shared ownership, implied permission, or lack of stealth).

Core Idea
The hand is not cut when the thief has a share in the property or a special relationship with the owner.

In this lesson, we examine a classical Hanafi matn from al-Quduri that lists several categories of thieves who are not subject to the hadd punishment of hand amputation. These exemptions are based on the principle that the theft is not considered a complete violation of exclusive ownership. The passage is supported by proofs from the Prophet ﷺ, Companions, and later scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and the exceptions
Read the matn carefully. It begins with 'The thief is not cut from the treasury...' and then lists several categories: theft from bayt al-mal, property with shared ownership, grave robbery, plundering, snatching, theft from parents/children/close relatives, spouses, slaves from masters, and theft from spoils.
These are exceptions to the general hadd for theft.
2
Analyze the rationale for each exemption
For each category, determine the legal cause: shared ownership (bayt al-mal, spoils), implied permission (parents, children, close relatives, spouses), or lack of stealth (grave robbery, plundering, snatching).
The hadd requires complete violation of another's exclusive right.
3
Examine the primary proof (dalil)
The Prophet ﷺ did not cut the hand of a slave who stole from the khums, saying: 'مَالُ اللَّهِ عز وجل، سَرَقَ بَعْضُهُ بَعْضًا' (Allah's property, one part stole from another). This indicates that when the thief has a share, the punishment is not applied.
This hadith is used for several exemptions.
4
Consider the additional proofs from Companions and Tabi'in
The commentary provides sayings from Ibn Abbas, Ali, and Umar, reinforcing the exemptions. Note that some Tabi'in disagreed, but the majority Hanafi position is as stated.
These are considered supporting evidence (shawahid).
5
Evaluate the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school may cut the hand for theft from a parent if the nisab is reached and no shared ownership, while the Hanafi school exempts due to implied permission. Recognize the practical difference.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different usul principles.

The Two Types of Hirz (Secure Place) for Theft
Definition of the two types of hirz with a hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the two types of hirz and determine whether a given location qualifies as a secure place for the hadd of theft.

Core Idea
A place is secure either by its nature (house) or by a guardian; both satisfy the condition for cutting the thief's hand.

This passage from al-Quduri defines the key condition of hirz for the hadd of theft. Understanding hirz is essential because if the stolen item was not from a secure place, the hand is not cut. The author states the two categories and provides a prophetic precedent. Let's unpack them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal function of hirz
Hirz is a condition (shart) for the hadd punishment. Without a valid hirz, the hand is not cut.
Hirz is one of several conditions for the hadd of theft, along with nisab and ownership.
2
Define the first type: hirz by meaning
Hirz li-ma'nan fihi refers to places that are inherently secure due to their structure and typical use, such as houses, shops, and locked rooms.
Even an unlocked house is considered hirz because it is a dwelling.
3
Define the second type: hirz by guardian
Hirz bi-l-hafiz refers to places that become secure due to the presence of a watchman or guard, such as a guarded field or tent.
The guard must be present at the time of theft.
4
Connect the hadith proof
The hadith of the shield stolen from the women's chamber shows that the place was considered hirz by meaning, and the hand was cut. This establishes that chambers within houses are secure places.
The hadith also confirms the nisab of three dirhams.

Theft from a Mosque with Owner Present
Ruling on theft from a mosque with owner present.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on theft from a mosque and identify the condition that makes it subject to the hadd penalty.

Core Idea
A mosque becomes a secure place (hirz) for theft if the owner is present.

You already know that the hadd penalty for theft requires the property to be taken from a secure place (hirz). But what about a mosque — a place open to all? In this lesson, we examine Al-Quduri's clear ruling: if the owner is present in the mosque, that presence makes it a secure place, and the thief's hand is cut. The proof comes directly from the incident of Safwan ibn Umayyah, whose cloak was stolen while he slept in the mosque.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
Read the first sentence: 'Whoever steals merchandise from the mosque while its owner is with him is cut.' Note that the ruling applies specifically when the owner is present. The hukm is wajib — the cutting is obligatory.
The term 'quti'a' implies the hadd penalty for theft.
2
Locate the Dalil
The author cites the hadith of Safwan ibn Umayyah. While sleeping in the mosque, his cloak worth thirty dirhams was stolen. The Prophet ordered the thief's hand to be cut.
This dalil is a hadith ahad, so its thubut is zanni, but it is accepted in establishing the ruling.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The hadith directly applies to the case: theft from a mosque resulted in cutting. The fact that Safwan was present (sleeping in the mosque) establishes that the mosque was a secure place because the owner was guarding his property.
The owner's presence is the operative factor (sabab) for making the mosque a hirz.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
Some schools argue that a mosque is never considered a secure place because it is open to all. The Hanafi school insists that the owner's presence changes the status, and the hadith is the proof.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf, but the Hanafi view is mufta bihi.
5
Apply the Ruling
Now consider a scenario: a man enters a mosque, takes a bag from a worshipper who is right there praying, and leaves. According to this ruling, the thief's hand should be cut if the value reaches nisab.
The condition of owner presence is crucial; without it, the mosque is not a hirz.

Core Idea

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a subtle point in hudud: what happens when a thief, after being amputated for stealing an item, later steals the same item after it has been transformed? The Hanafi school holds that transformation (istihalah) creates a new property, so a new theft occurs, requiring a new amputation. After the hadd of cutting, the thief owes nothing—if the property exists, return it; if destroyed, no compensation.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario: after a thief's hand is cut, what happens to the stolen item? The ruling is supported by the prophetic hadith 'La gharam' and the practice of the Tabi'i Ata. Understanding this ruling prevents confusion between criminal punishment and civil liability.

Parse the ruling

Ata' describes a case of doubt where the hadd is dropped; the property is returned without punishment. This supports the idea that after hadd, the original property is returned to the owner.

The hadith 'There is no liability (gharam) upon the thief after cutting his right hand' explicitly negates any financial obligation after the hadd. This is the primary evidence.

Core Idea
A mere claim of ownership by the thief creates sufficient doubt to avert the hadd — no evidence required.

This passage from the matn delivers a crucial Hanafi principle in hudud: if the thief claims the stolen property is his, the hadd (hand-cutting) is dropped even if he provides no proof. The reason is that his claim introduces a doubt (shubhah) about his ownership, and according to the maxim 'al-hudud tudra'u bi al-shubhat' (hadd punishments are averted by doubts), the hadd cannot be applied. The commentary supports this with a statement from Ata' and a prophetic hadith, illustrating that protecting the integrity of the severe punishment takes priority over strict evidentiary requirements.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Highway robbers are only amputated if they actually take property of nisab; otherwise, they are imprisoned until repentance.

Highway robbery (qat‘ al-tariq) is a distinct crime in Hanafi law with punishments ranging from imprisonment to amputation. The key factor is whether property was taken before capture. This lesson unpacks the two scenarios from Al-Quduri and the proofs that support the Hanafi position, including the Quranic verse, the practice of Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, and the hadith of the Ukl tribe.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage explicitly distinguishes between robbers captured before taking property or killing, and those captured after taking property of at least nisab. Recognize that the ruling differs.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
If captured before taking property: the Imam imprisons them until repentance. If captured after taking property meeting the nisab: the Imam cuts their right hand and left foot from opposite sides.
Nisab is 10 dirhams or equivalent value per robber.
3
Locate the primary proofs
The passage cites three proofs: (1) Athar of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz showing amputation requires actual removal from the house; (2) Quran 5:33 specifying 'cutting from opposite sides' as punishment for muharibah; (3) Hadith of the Ukl tribe where the Prophet (ﷺ) amputated robbers who killed and stole.
The Hanafi school uses these to condition the hadd on actual taking of property.
4

From the Quran: the phrase 'or that their hands and feet be cut off from opposite sides' is interpreted by the Hanafis to apply when property is taken, as supported by the context of 'corruption' and the prophetic practice. The athar of Umar indicates that amputation is not for attempts but for completed theft.
5

Verify that the robbers are mumtani‘un (capable of resistance), the property belongs to a Muslim or dhimmi, and each robber's share reaches nisab. Without these, the hadd is not applied.
The condition of 'opposite sides' (right hand and left foot) is uniquely Hanafi.

Highway Robbery with Killing: Hadd, Discretion, and Exceptions
Rulings on punishments for highway robbers who kill, with proofs from Quran and Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the mandatory hadd execution for highway robbers who kill without taking property from the Imam's discretionary choices when they also take property, and identify the exception that reverts the case to qisas.

Core Idea
When highway robbers kill without taking property, hadd execution is mandatory and beyond family pardon; with property, the Imam chooses among three punishments.

Highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) is a severe crime that can invoke the harshest hudud punishments. However, the specific penalty depends on two factors: whether the robbers killed and whether they took property. This passage from al-Quduri lays out three distinct scenarios, each with its own ruling and evidentiary basis. It also introduces a critical exception that protects minors, the insane, and close relatives from the hadd framework, returning the case to private retaliation (qisas). Understanding these distinctions is essential for applying the correct penalty in cases of armed robbery with homicide.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the first scenario: killing without taking property
Read the first ruling: if the robbers kill but do not take any property, the Imam must execute them as a hadd punishment, and the pardon of the victim's heirs is ineffective. Identify the proof cited: Quran 5:45 (life for life). Understand why this is a hadd (right of Allah) not subject to private waiver.
The proof is the general retaliation verse, but here it is applied as a fixed penalty because the act is part of hirabah.
2
Examine the second scenario: killing with taking property
Read the ruling: if they both kill and take property, the Imam has a choice among three punishments: (1) cut hands and feet then crucify, (2) kill only, (3) crucify alive (then kill by spear). Note the proof: Quran 5:33 which lists these options. Recognize that the Imam's discretion is based on the word 'aw (or) in the verse, indicating choice.
Some schools interpret the verse as sequential, but Hanafis see it as optional.
3
Understand the method of crucifixion and its limits
The text details the procedure: they are crucified alive, then their bellies are stabbed with a spear until they die, and they are not left on the cross for more than three days. This is a unique Hanafi specification.
The purpose is to deter and punish, but with some limits to avoid excessive cruelty.
4
Identify the exception: minors, insane, or mahram
Read the exception: if among the robbers there is a minor, an insane person, or a close blood relative (mahram) of the victim (the one on whom the cutting is done), then the entire hadd for the group falls. The case reverts to qisas: the heirs decide whether to kill or pardon. The proof cited is Quran 5:45 continuing (the tooth for tooth, wounds qisas), implying that when the killer is not fully liable for hadd, the private right of retaliation applies.
This is a critical example of how hudud are suspended when certain conditions are not met; the matter becomes a private right.
5
Synthesize the three rulings into a coherent framework
Now step back and see the overall structure: the severity of punishment depends on the presence of property taking and the status of the perpetrators. The Imam's role is strongest when property is taken; when no property, the hadd is fixed; when an exception applies, the state's role recedes and private parties take over.
This shows the Hanafi school's nuanced balancing of God's rights and private rights.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sariqah wa Qutta' al-Tariq (Book of Theft and Highway Robbery) is a ruling with proof. It begins with the matn of al-Quduri stating the punishments for highway robbers (muharibun). The first ruling: if the robbers kill but do not take property, the imam executes them as a fixed penalty (hadd). The pardon of the victim's family (awliya') is ineffective because this is a hadd right of Allah, not a personal right. The second ruling: if they kill and take property, the imam has a choice among three punishments: (a) cut off hands and feet from opposite sides and kill them crucified, (b) kill them without mutilation, or (c) crucify them alive. Crucifixion is done by leaving them alive, then piercing their bellies with spears until death, and the crucifixion cannot exceed three days. The third ruling: if among the group there is a minor (sabi), an insane person (majnun), or a mahram relative of the victim (a close relative by blood or marriage such that marriage is permanently forbidden), then the hadd is dropped for all the remaining robbers, and the case becomes qisas (retaliation) for the heirs. They may execute any of them or pardon. This is because the presence of such a person creates a doubt (shubha) that prevents the hadd. The proof (dalil) for these rulings includes Quranic verses (al-Ma'idah 5:45 for the first case, al-Ma'idah 5:33 for the second case), the hadith of Anas (Bukhari) where the Prophet cut hands and feet of the 'Uraynah tribe, sayings of the Tabi'in (al-Zuhri, 'Ata), and a principle from al-Thawri regarding mahram. The passage also includes two principles in Urdu: (1) Hadd penalties drop due to shubha (doubt); so if it drops for one robber, it drops for all. (2) In highway robbery, if one robber directly commits the killing, that act is attributed to all, so all are liable for execution (as per the earlier ruling that the hadd is applied to all if one directly commits the act). This is consistent with the Hanafi position that the act of one in a group is considered the act of all for retaliation.

Key Terms
قُطَّاع الطَّرِيق (qutta' al-tariq): Highway robbers who ambush travelers and commit theft, violence, or murder. In this text, they are subject to the hadd of hirabah as per Quran 5:33.
حَدّ (hadd): A fixed, specific punishment prescribed by Allah for certain crimes (e.g., theft, adultery, hirabah). It cannot be increased, decreased, or pardoned by the victim or judge.
صَلْب (salb): Crucifixion; a punishment prescribed for muharibun in Quran 5:33. In this text, it involves fixing the convict alive on a cross and piercing the belly with a spear until death, not exceeding three days.

Effect of Doubt (Shubha) from Presence of Minor, Insane, or Mahram, and Joint Liability in Highway Robbery
Ruling with proof on doubt-based exemption and joint liability in highway robbery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the hadd punishment is dropped for all robbers when a minor, insane person, or mahram relative is among them, and state the rule that the act of one robber is attributed to all.

Core Idea
Hadd penalties drop due to shubha (doubt); if one robber is not subject to hadd, all are exempt and the case becomes qisas.

The hadd punishments for highway robbery are stringent, but the law is sensitive to legal doubts (shubuhat). This lesson explores two ancillary rules: first, the presence of a minor, insane person, or mahram relative among the robbers creates a doubt that voids the hadd for the entire group, turning the case into qisas for the heirs. Second, if only one robber directly kills, the act is attributed to all, so all are liable for execution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third scenario
Read the matn: when among the robbers there is a minor (sabi), an insane person (majnun), or a mahram relative of the victim (dhu rahim mahram), the hadd is dropped for all the remaining robbers.
This is based on the principle that hadd is waived by doubt.
2
Understand the reason for dropping the hadd
The presence of such a person creates a legal doubt (shubha) that prevents the application of the fixed hadd. When one member is not liable for hadd, the entire group's hadd is dropped, and the case reverts to qisas (retaliation) for the heirs.
The Urdu principle states: 'Hadd penalties drop due to doubt; therefore when it drops for one or some, it drops for the remaining ones as well.'
3
Analyze the joint liability rule
The matn also states: if one robber directly commits the act (killing), the hadd is applied to all of them. This means that in a group, the act of one is considered the act of all, and all are equally liable for the hadd if conditions are met.
This is a Hanafi principle in hudud and qisas cases.
4
Examine the proofs
The proofs for the third scenario include Quran 5:45 (the tooth for tooth verse) and a saying of al-Thawri about mahram theft. The joint liability rule is based on general principles of participation.
The proofs are provided in the source text.
5
Apply the rules to a group scenario
Practice combining the two rules: If a group includes a minor, the hadd is dropped for all; if no minor/insane/mahram, and one kills, all are executed as hadd.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sariqah wa Qutta' al-Tariq (Book of Theft and Highway Robbery) is a ruling with proof. It begins with the matn of al-Quduri stating the punishments for highway robbers (muharibun). The first ruling: if the robbers kill but do not take property, the imam executes them as a fixed penalty (hadd). The pardon of the victim's family (awliya') is ineffective because this is a hadd right of Allah, not a personal right. The second ruling: if they kill and take property, the imam has a choice among three punishments: (a) cut off hands and feet from opposite sides and kill them crucified, (b) kill them without mutilation, or (c) crucify them alive. Crucifixion is done by leaving them alive, then piercing their bellies with spears until death, and the crucifixion cannot exceed three days. The third ruling: if among the group there is a minor (sabi), an insane person (majnun), or a mahram relative of the victim (a close relative by blood or marriage such that marriage is permanently forbidden), then the hadd is dropped for all the remaining robbers, and the case becomes qisas (retaliation) for the heirs. They may execute any of them or pardon. This is because the presence of such a person creates a doubt (shubha) that prevents the hadd. The proof (dalil) for these rulings includes Quranic verses (al-Ma'idah 5:45 for the first case, al-Ma'idah 5:33 for the second case), the hadith of Anas (Bukhari) where the Prophet cut hands and feet of the 'Uraynah tribe, sayings of the Tabi'in (al-Zuhri, 'Ata), and a principle from al-Thawri regarding mahram. The passage also includes two principles in Urdu: (1) Hadd penalties drop due to shubha (doubt); so if it drops for one robber, it drops for all. (2) In highway robbery, if one robber directly commits the killing, that act is attributed to all, so all are liable for execution (as per the earlier ruling that the hadd is applied to all if one directly commits the act). This is consistent with the Hanafi position that the act of one in a group is considered the act of all for retaliation.

Key Terms
شُبْهَة (shubha): Legal doubt or uncertainty that prevents the application of a hadd punishment. In highway robbery, the presence of a minor, insane person, or mahram relative creates a shubha that voids the hadd for the entire group.
قِصَاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind; a punishment where the victim or heirs have the right to demand execution of the perpetrator. Unlike hadd, qisas can be pardoned by the heirs.
مُبَاشَرَة (mubasharah): Direct commission of an act; in the context of joint liability, if one robber directly kills, his act is considered to be performed by all, so all are liable for the hadd.

Hadd on All Highway Robbers When One Kills
Ruling statement: hadd on all when one kills, with two underlying usul principles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that when one highway robber kills, all participants are liable for hadd punishment, and explain the underlying principles of attribution and doubt.

Core Idea
In highway robbery, if one kills, all are punished because the act is attributed to the group and hadd cannot be divided.

You might think that only the person who actually kills should face the death penalty. However, the Hanafi school treats highway robbery (hirabah) as a collective crime with special rules. This lesson explains why all members of the group become liable for hadd when one of them commits murder, and how the principle of doubt (shubha) affects the application of punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act is highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) where a group uses force to steal. The specific scenario is when one of the robbers kills someone.
Highway robbery is a distinct crime in Islamic law with severe penalties.
2
State the ruling from the matn
The matn says: 'If one of them perpetrated the act, the hadd is executed on all of them.' So the ruling is that all participants receive the hadd punishment, not just the killer.
The hadd for highway robbery with murder is qisas (death) according to most Hanafi authorities.
3
Understand the two principles
First principle: In highway robbery, the act of one is attributed to all because they are considered a single entity. Second principle: Hadd punishments are dropped by doubt (shubha); if one member has a doubt that drops the hadd for that person, it drops for all because the punishment cannot be divided.
These principles ensure consistency in the application of hadd.

Ruling on Coercion to Commit Zina
States the Hanafi ruling on coercion and zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a person coerced to commit zina is liable to hadd, and explain the exception for sultanic coercion.

Core Idea
Coercion does not remove hadd for zina unless the sultan compels.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on coercion (ikrah) in the context of zina. According to Abu Hanifa, even if a person is forced into zina, they are still subject to the hadd punishment, with one notable exception: if the sultan (ruler) is the coercer, the person is excused. The lesson explores the proofs cited from the Quran and the practice of Umar, and addresses the apparent tension between the ruling and the proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal issue
The matn states: 'if he coerces him to commit zina, the hadd is obligatory upon him according to Abu Hanifa, unless the sultan coerces him.' What is the general ruling and what is the exception?
2
Examine the first proof: Quranic verse
The verse (24:33) about slave girls being forced into prostitution is cited. How does this verse support the ruling? It indicates that the coerced are forgiven by Allah, but the Hanafi reasoning is that the legal punishment (hadd) still applies as a deterrence, because the act itself remains haram.
The verse uses 'ghafur rahim' for the coerced, but this is forgiveness from sin, not removal of legal penalty.
3
Examine the second proof: Action of Umar
The hadith reports that Umar flogged and exiled a slave who forced a slave girl into zina, but did not flog the girl. This shows that the perpetrator (the one who commits the act, even if coerced) receives hadd, while the victim (the woman) does not. The ruling in the matn applies to the person coerced into committing the act (the perpetrator), not the victim.
This clarifies that the coerced person in the matn is the one forced to perform zina (e.g., a man forced to penetrate), not the one forced to submit.
4
Understand the exception: sultanic coercion
The exception for the sultan indicates that when the ruler uses his authority to compel, the individual may be overpowered to the point of excusable duress. Hanafi scholars differentiate between coercion by a private individual and by the state.
This exception is based on the principle that the sultan's command is effectively irresistible.
5
Recap the Hanafi position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that hadd is obligatory on the coerced perpetrator. The Shafi'i and others may hold that coercion removes liability. The Hanafi distinction is based on the severity of the crime and the requirement to resist even under threat.
The practical consequence: in most cases, if a man is forced by a bandit to commit zina, he still faces hadd unless the bandit is the sultan.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's matn, with its sharh, presents the Hanafi rulings on apostasy. The passage type is a composite of ruling statements and proofs. The author first addresses the female apostate (murtaddah): she is not killed but imprisoned indefinitely until she returns to Islam. This is based on the practice of the Companions, as narrated from Ibn Abbas. The Hanafi school distinguishes between male and female apostates in punishment, contrary to the Shafi'i school which holds that both are killed. Then the passage discusses the male apostate's property: his ownership in his properties ceases immediately upon apostasy, but if he returns to Islam, ownership returns as if it never ceased. This is supported by the hadith where the Prophet sent someone to execute a man who married his father's wife and take his property, showing that apostasy results in loss of property. The Male Apostate's Property: Cessation and Return
Ruling on male apostate's property cessation and return

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the cessation of a male apostate's property and when it returns upon repentance.

Core Idea
Upon apostasy, a male loses ownership of his property immediately, but if he returns to Islam, all property is restored as if nothing happened.

The passage now turns to the male apostate (murtadd). Unlike the female, his ruling involves property consequences. His ownership in all his properties ceases from the moment of apostasy. However, if he repents and returns to Islam, his ownership is reinstated retroactively. This ruling is based on a Companion's precedent and the principle that apostasy is like death, but with the possibility of return. Let's unpack the details.

Guided Path
1
Locate the core ruling
Find the sentence that states the immediate effect of apostasy on a male's property. It says: 'وَيَزُولُ مِلْكُ الْمُرْتَدِ عَنْ أَمْلَاكِهِ بِرِدَّتِهِ' – his ownership ceases.
The key term is 'يَزُولُ مِلْكُ' (ownership ceases).
2
Note the exception for return
The passage immediately adds: 'فَإِنْ أَسْلَمَ عَادَتْ أَمْلَاكُهُ عَلَى حَالِهَا' – if he becomes Muslim, his properties return to their original state. This is a suspension, not permanent loss.
This shows that the cessation is conditional on his remaining in apostasy.
3
Identify the proof
The author cites a report about Mustawrid al-'Ijli: he apostatized, was executed, and his inheritance was given to his Muslim heirs. This implies that property ceased upon apostasy and was already considered transferred.
The proof is again a qawl al-sahabi (action of the Companion 'Aliq).
4
Understand the legal principle
The cessation is similar to death: the apostate is treated as though dead regarding his wealth, but because return is possible, the property is restored if he repents.
This is reflected in the Urdu usul: 'مرتد هو گيا كأنه مات لكن أمل العودة'.

Post-Death Distribution: Muslim-Acquired vs. Apostasy-Acquired Property
Rulings on distribution of property and debts after apostate's death or joining dar al-harb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between property acquired during Islam and during apostasy, and state the rules for inheritance, fay', debts, and the status of mudabbar and umm walad when the apostate joins dar al-harb.

Core Idea
At death, the apostate's Muslim-earned property goes to Muslim heirs; post-apostasy earnings become fay' for the treasury.

The passage now covers what happens when an apostate dies or is killed, and the more complex scenario of joining dar al-harb. The key distinction is between property acquired while he was a Muslim and property acquired after apostasy. Debts are also classified accordingly. Additionally, if he joins enemy territory, certain slaves are freed and property is transferred to his Muslim heirs. Let's piece together these interconnected rulings.

Guided Path
1
Separate the two types of property
The passage distinguishes 'ما اكْتَسَبَهُ فِي حَالِ إِسْلَامِهِ' (acquired during Islam) and 'ما اكْتَسَبَهُ فِي حَالِ رِدَّتِهِ' (acquired during apostasy). Understand the different destinations.
Muslim-acquired property goes to Muslim heirs; apostasy-acquired property becomes fay'.
2
Describe the death scenario
If the apostate dies (or is killed) on apostasy, his Muslim-acquired property is inherited by his Muslim heirs; his post-apostasy acquisitions go to the public treasury as fay'.
This is straightforward distribution.
3
Analyze the dar al-harb scenario
If he joins enemy territory as an apostate and a judge rules on his joining, his mudabbar and umm walad are freed immediately. His Muslim-acquired property is transferred to Muslim heirs.
The freeing of these slaves is a direct effect of joining dar al-harb, not death.
4
Address debts
Debts incurred during Islam are paid from Muslim-acquired property; debts incurred during apostasy are paid from apostasy-acquired property. This is separate from inheritance.
This ensures that creditors of each period are satisfied from the appropriate fund.

وَإِنْ قُتِلَ أَوْ مَاتَ عَلَى رِدَّتِهِ انْتَقَلَ مَا اكْتَسَبَهُ فِي حَالِ إِسْلَامِهِ إِلَى وَرَثَتِهِ الْمُسْلِمِينَ، وَكَانَ مَا اكْتَسَبَهُ فِي حَالِ رِدَّتِهِ فَيْئًا. وَإِنْ لَحَقَ بِدَارِ الْحَرْبِ مُرْتَدًّا وَحَكَمَ الْحَاكِمُ بِلَحَاقِهِ عَتَقَ مُدَبَّرُهُ وَأُمَّهَاتُ أَوْلَادِهِ، وَنُقِلَ مَا اكْتَسَبَهُ فِي حَالِ الْإِسْلَامِ إِلَى وَرَثَتِهِ مِنَ الْمُسْلِمِينَ. وَتُقْضَى الدُّيُونُ الَّتِي لَزِمَتْهُ فِي حَالِ الْإِسْلَامِ مِمَّا اكْتَسَبَهُ فِي حَالِ الْإِسْلَامِ، وَمَا لَزِمَهُ مِنَ الدُّيُونِ فِي حَالِ رِدَّتِهِ مِمَّا اكْتَسَبَهُ فِي حَالِ رِدَّتِهِ.
أُصُولٌ: عَوْرَتٌ مُرْتَدَّةٌ هُوَ جَائِزٌ لَا يُقْتَلُ الْبَتَّةُ حَتَّى تُسْلِمَ قِيدَ رِكَاظٍ. بَرْخِلَافِ شَافِعِيٍّ. أُصُولٌ: مُرْتَدٌّ نَظَرًا حَالِ إِسْلَامٍ فِيمَا كَسَبَهُ بَعْدَ الْمَوْتِ وَارِثُهُ دَوْرُ ثَابِتٍ يَأْوِي إِلَى. أُصُولٌ: مُرْتَدٌّ هُوَ ذَهَبَ كَأَنَّهُ مَاتَ لَكِنَّ أَمَلَ الْعَوْدَةِ إِلَى الْإِسْلَامِ نَظَرًا.
Book of Siyar: As for the female apostate, she is not killed but is imprisoned until she becomes Muslim. Evidence (1): Statement of the Companion to establish that: 'Women are not killed when they apostatize from Islam, but are imprisoned and called to Islam and compelled to it.' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah). And the ownership of the male apostate ceases from his properties upon his apostasy; if he becomes Muslim, his properties return to their original state. Evidence (1): Statement of the Companion: From 'Aliq: that he brought Mustawrid al-'Ijli who had apostatized, and presented Islam to him but he refused; he said: so he killed him and made his inheritance among his Muslim heirs. (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah). And if he is killed or dies upon his apostasy, what he acquired during his state of Islam passes to his Muslim heirs, and what he acquired during his apostasy becomes booty (fay'). And if he joins the Abode of War as an apostate and the judge rules on his joining, his mudabbar and umm walad become free, and what he acquired during his state of Islam is transferred to his Muslim heirs. And the debts that were binding upon him during his state of Islam are paid from what he acquired during his state of Islam, and what became binding upon him of debts during his apostasy from what he acquired during his apostasy. Principles: A female apostate is not killed absolutely but is imprisoned until she becomes Muslim, contrary to the Shafi'is. Principles: For an apostate, considering his state of Islam in what he earned after death, his heir is established to take. Principles: An apostate is considered as if he died, but with the hope of return to Islam.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's matn, with its sharh, presents the Hanafi rulings on apostasy. The passage type is a composite of ruling statements and proofs. The author first addresses the female apostate (murtaddah): she is not killed but imprisoned indefinitely until she returns to Islam. This is based on the practice of the Companions, as narrated from Ibn Abbas. The Hanafi school distinguishes between male and female apostates in punishment, contrary to the Shafi'i school which holds that both are killed. Then the passage discusses the male apostate's property: his ownership in his properties ceases immediately upon apostasy, but if he returns to Islam, ownership returns as if it never ceased. This is supported by the hadith where the Prophet sent someone to execute a man who married his father's wife and take his property, showing that apostasy results in loss of property. If the apostate dies or is killed on apostasy, the property he acquired while a Muslim goes to his Muslim heirs, while property acquired after apostasy becomes fay' (booty for the public treasury). If he joins dar al-harb (enemy territory) as an apostate, and the judge rules that he has joined, his mudabbar (slave whose freedom is deferred) and umm walad (slave-concubine who has borne a child) become free immediately, and his Muslim-acquired property is distributed to his Muslim heirs. Debts incurred while Muslim are paid from his Muslim-acquired property; debts incurred after apostasy are paid from his post-apostasy acquisitions. The Urdu usul at the end summarize: a female apostate is not killed but imprisoned; an apostate's property from his Muslim period goes to his Muslim heirs after death; an apostate is treated as dead from the moment of apostasy but with the hope of return to Islam, so matters are suspended.

Key Terms
فَيْء (fay'): Booty or public property; in this context, property acquired by an apostate during apostasy that goes to the treasury.
مُدَبَّر (mudabbar): A slave whose freedom is promised upon the master's death.
أُمَّهَاتُ الْأَوْلَاد (ummahāt al-awlād): Slave-concubines who have borne a child to the master.
