Surety for Plaintiff's Witness and Its Proofs
Ruling on requiring surety with two proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on requiring a surety when a plaintiff claims to have a present witness and explain the hadith and athar evidence for this ruling.

Core Idea
A defendant must provide a surety for himself or face imprisonment if a plaintiff claims a present witness.

In the Book of Claims, al-Quduri presents a procedural ruling that balances the plaintiff's right to present evidence with the need to ensure the defendant's presence. This lesson examines the ruling, its exception for travelers, and the two proofs from the Sunnah and the practice of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
When a plaintiff says 'I have a present witness,' the defendant must give a surety for his own person for three days. If he fails, he is imprisoned. Identify this as the core ruling.
The surety is for the defendant, not the witness.
2
Understand the exception
If the plaintiff is a traveler, the defendant does not provide a surety but instead stays with the plaintiff for the duration of the judge's sitting. Note the difference.
3
Examine the first proof (hadith)
The hadith from Abu Umamah includes the phrase 'al-za'imu gharim' (the guarantor is liable). This supports the validity of taking a surety as a legal mechanism.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud and others; its transmission is zanni (single-chain).
4
Examine the second proof (athar)
The practice of Umar ibn al-Khattab, as reported by Hamza al-Aslami, shows that a surety was taken from a man involved in a crime until the matter was brought before Umar.
This athar is from a Tabi'i and indicates Companion practice.
5
Conclude the Hanafi position
The ruling is wajib (obligatory) based on these proofs. The Hanafi school follows this; some other schools may differ.
Epistemologically, the ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from Kitab al-Da'wa (Book of Claims) concerning the procedure when a plaintiff declares he has a witness present. According to the Hanafi school, the defendant is given a three-day period to produce a surety (kafil) for his own person. If he fails, the judge orders his imprisonment, unless the plaintiff is a traveler, in which case the defendant remains with him for the duration of the judge's sitting. The commentator then provides two proofs: first, a hadith from Abu Umamah indicating that the guarantor (za'im) is liable, which supports the concept of kafalah. Second, an athar from the Tabi'i Hamzah showing that Umar ibn al-Khattab allowed taking a surety in a similar context.

Key Terms
بَيِّنَة (bayyinah): A witness or evidence that the plaintiff claims to have present in court.
كَفِيل (kafil): A surety or guarantor for the defendant's presence, ensuring he does not abscond.
