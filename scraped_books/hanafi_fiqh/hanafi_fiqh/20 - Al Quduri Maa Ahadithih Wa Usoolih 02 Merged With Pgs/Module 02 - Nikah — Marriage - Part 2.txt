Marriage on Condition of Service or Teaching Quran
Ruling with three proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when marriage is contracted on a condition of service or teaching as dower, and explain why the wife receives mahr al-mithl.

Core Idea
Marriage is valid even if the dower condition is invalid; the wife gets mahr al-mithl.

In this passage from al-Quduri, we encounter a scenario where a man marries a woman on the condition that he will serve her for a year or teach her the Quran as her dower. How does the Hanafi school treat such a condition? The text rules that the marriage is sahih, but the condition does not constitute a valid dower; instead, the wife receives the mahr al-mithl — the dower of a woman of her status. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning and the three proofs cited.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a marriage contract with a condition of service or teaching as dower. The ruling: marriage is valid (sahih), but the wife gets mahr al-mithl, not the promised condition.
The condition is void, but the marriage stands.
2
Analyze the hadith proof
The hadith of Sahl ibn Sa'd: the Prophet married a man to a woman for the Quran he knew. Hanafis interpret this as a specific case where the dower was the already-known surahs (a form of property), not future teaching; hence it does not validate teaching as a general dower.
The hadith is distinguished by its context.
3
Analyze the Quranic proof
The verse (28:27) about Musa's marriage for service. Hanafis argue that 'ajr' here refers to hire for herding, not dower; the marriage contract was separate from the service contract.
The verse is not about dower but about a separate agreement.
4
Analyze the Sahabi proof
Ali's statement that dower is whatever spouses agree is general. Hanafis qualify that the agreed item must be lawful property (mal mutaqawwim). Service and teaching are not property.
General statements are restricted by other principles.
5
Summarize the practical consequence
The wife is entitled to mahr al-mithl — the dower of a woman of her standing. The invalid condition does not affect the validity of the marriage.
Mahr al-mithl is determined by social status, not the void condition.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the marriage guardianship (wilayah) of an insane woman. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (Sahibayn), the son is the wali. According to Muhammad al-Shaybani, the father is the wali. Identify the authority of each view.

The thamarah is the validity of the marriage contract depending on who acts as wali.

Identify the mufta bihi
In the Hanafi school, the position of the Sahibayn is generally considered stronger and is relied upon for fatwa. Why might that be? Consider the general order of guardianship: the son is closer in the inheritance hierarchy.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute; both views are within the school, but the preferred view is that of Sahibayn.

A slave's marriage without the master's permission is akin to fornication – invalid unless the master consents.

This lesson presents a clear ruling from al-Quduri's matn, supported directly by a prophetic hadith. The ruling establishes that a slave, male or female, cannot contract a valid marriage without the owner's consent. Effect of Fasid Conditions in Marriage on Mahr
Ruling on mahr when marriage contains fasid conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mahr when a marriage contains a fasid condition regarding relocation or remarriage, and apply it to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
A fasid condition in marriage does not invalidate it; breach converts mahr to mahr al-mithl.

Marriage contracts sometimes include conditions intended to restrict the husband's future actions, such as not moving the wife out of town or not taking a second wife. How are these conditions treated in Hanafi fiqh? This lesson examines the ruling: the marriage remains valid, but if the condition is breached, the wife's mahr changes from the stipulated amount to the mahr of her peers (mahr al-mithl).

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions mentioned
The passage mentions two conditions: (1) not taking the wife out of the town, and (2) not marrying another woman. Recognize that these are conditions placed on the husband after marriage.
These conditions are fasid (defective) in the contract because they restrict a right that the husband would otherwise have.
2
Determine the legal effect of fulfilling the condition
If the husband fulfills the condition, the marriage continues with the stipulated mahr (musamma). This shows that a condition can be voluntarily honored.
The condition is not enforceable; it is merely a promise.
3
Determine the legal effect of breaching the condition
If the husband breaches (by moving her or marrying another), the marriage stays valid, but the wife is entitled to mahr al-mithl instead of the stipulated amount.
The breach does not invalidate the marriage; it only changes the financial obligation.
4
Distinguish between mahr musamma and mahr al-mithl
Mahr musamma is the amount agreed upon. Mahr al-mithl is determined by the mahr of women similar to the wife in family, age, beauty, wealth, and locality.
Mahr al-mithl ensures the wife receives a fair dower when the agreed amount is forfeited due to the condition breach.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn of al-Quduri, followed by proofs. The first ruling: Nikah al-mut'ah (temporary marriage for pleasure) and nikah al-muwaqqat (marriage with a fixed term) are batil (absolutely void). The Hanafi position is that these contracts are invalid from their inception; there is no possibility of them becoming valid. The proof is from Quran 23:6, which permits sexual relations only with wives or slave women, implying that any marriage with a time limit is not a true marriage. The second proof is a hadith from Muslim where the Prophet forbade mut'ah permanently until the Day of Resurrection. The second ruling: a slave's marriage without the master's permission is mawquf (suspended). The master may either ratify it or reject it; if ratified, it becomes valid, otherwise it is void. The same applies to a free woman married without her consent. The proof is a hadith from Ibn Umar. The third ruling: it is permissible for a man to marry his paternal cousin (ibn al-'amm) directly, meaning he can act as her wali and marry her to himself. Two hadiths are cited: the first from Aisha about her marriage to the Prophet, and the second from Sahl ibn Sa'd about a woman offering herself. The fourth ruling: if a woman authorizes a man to marry her to himself, and he contracts in the presence of two witnesses, it is valid. The proof is from Aisha. The Urdu note explains the definitions of mut'ah and muwaqqat, emphasizing that both are void with no remaining validity.

Rulings on Mahr Guarantee, Separation in Fasid Marriage, and Effects of Consummation
Matn with proofs on three marriage rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a guardian's guarantee of mahr, explain when mahr is due in a fasid marriage, and identify the conditions that trigger iddah and nasab.

Core Idea
In fasid marriage, mahr is only due after consummation; khalwa alone does not obligate mahr.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents three key rulings in marriage law: the validity of a guardian guaranteeing the mahr, the consequences of a fasid (defective) marriage before and after consummation, and the establishment of iddah and lineage. The text is a matn (core text) and is accompanied by proofs from hadith and sayings of the tabi'un. Understanding these rulings is essential for distinguishing between valid and defective marriages and knowing the financial and familial obligations in each case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rulings
Read the matn and list the three separate legal rulings: (1) guardian's guarantee of mahr, (2) mahr in fasid marriage before/after consummation, (3) iddah and nasab after consummation in fasid marriage.
The text begins with 'wa idha damina al-waliyyu al-mahra' and continues with three distinct rules.
2
Analyze the proof for guardian's guarantee
Examine the hadith of Abu Qatadah guaranteeing the debt of the deceased. Notice how the Prophet accepted the guarantee and considered the deceased free of the debt. This establishes the validity of a third-party guarantee (damān). Apply this ruling to the guardian's guarantee of mahr.
The hadith is cited from Sunan al-Bayhaqi. The key phrase is 'haqq al-gharīm' (the creditor's right is secured).
3
Analyze the proofs for fasid marriage rulings
Read the reports from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i and Ata. Ibrahim states that in every fasid marriage (like marrying during iddah), if consummated, she gets the mahr. Ata confirms: 'She gets her dowry because he had intercourse with her.' Note that these are sayings of tabi'un, used as supporting evidence.
The text also includes a report from Ali ibn Abi Talib about a woman married during iddah: he ordered iddah and gave her a choice afterward.
4
Understand the Hanafi position on khalwa
The matn explicitly says 'wa kadhālika ba‘da al-khalwa' – after khalwa (seclusion) no mahr is due. Contrast this with a valid marriage where khalwa triggers full mahr. This is a distinctive Hanafi position; the Shafi'i school holds that khalwa in a fasid marriage also obligates mahr.
The thamarah: if a fasid marriage is annulled after seclusion but before consummation, the Hanafi says no mahr; Shafi'i says full mahr.
5
Apply the rulings to a scenario
Consider a woman who marries during her iddah (fasid). Before consummation, the judge annuls. No mahr. If consummated, she gets mahr al-mithl (not exceeding specified), completes a full iddah, and any child is legitimate. The guardian's guarantee allows her to claim mahr from either him or the husband.
The Urdu note confirms that marrying a woman in iddah is a fasid marriage.

Commentary
This passage continues the matn of Quduri on marriage rulings. First ruling: If a guardian (wali) guarantees payment of the mahr (dower), the guarantee is valid, and the wife has the option to demand the mahr from either her husband or the guardian. Second ruling: In a fasid (defective) marriage, if the judge separates the couple before consummation (dukhul), the woman is not entitled to any mahr; the same applies if separation occurs after khalwa (valid seclusion). This is because a fasid marriage does not establish a valid marriage bond until consummation. Third ruling: If consummation takes place in a fasid marriage, the woman receives mahr al-mithl (the proper mahr of a woman of her status), but this amount cannot exceed the mahr specified in the contract. She must also observe the iddah (waiting period), and the lineage of any child born is established (i.e., the child is attributed to the husband). The proofs come from hadith and reports from tabi'un (successors). The hadith about Abu Qatadah guaranteeing the debt supports the validity of guarantee. The reports from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i and Ata establish the mahr in fasid marriage after consummation. The report from Ata and the practice of Ali (RA) confirm the iddah and nasab. Core Idea
Marrying a slave woman is permissible, but never over a free woman—hierarchy matters.

Core Idea
A free man may marry only four wives — the Quran, the Prophet's practice, and Ibn Abbas affirm this limit.

This passage from al-Quduri lays out the foundational ruling on polygyny: a free man cannot exceed four wives. The author supports this with three distinct proofs from revelation and early practice. Understanding these proofs is essential to seeing how the Hanafi school derives a clear, binding limit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling Statement
Read the matn: 'And for the free man, he may marry four from free women and slave women, and it is not for him to marry more than that.' This is the core ruling — a clear numerical limit.
The ruling applies to free men only; slaves have a different limit.
2
Recognize the First Proof (Quran)
The verse (4:3) says 'marry what seems good to you of women, two, three, or four.' The word 'muthna' (two), 'thulatha' (three), 'ruba' (four) indicates the maximum is four.
The verse continues with 'if you fear you cannot be just, then one' — but that does not affect the upper limit.
3
Recognize the Second Proof (Hadith)
Wahb al-Asadi converted to Islam with eight wives. The Prophet (peace be upon him) told him to choose four. This shows the Prophet enforced the four-wife limit even in a special case.
This hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud.
4
Recognize the Third Proof (Qawl Sahabi)
Ibn Abbas said: 'The free man should not marry from slave women except one.' This shows that among the four wives, only one can be a slave woman.
This is based on the principle of preventing slavery from affecting lineage.
5
Synthesize the Ruling
Combine the three proofs: a free man may marry up to four women, but his total cannot exceed four, and among them, at most one can be a slave woman.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A slave may only marry two women — the Sahaba's consensus limits his nikkah to half of a free man's.

This lesson presents a clear ruling from the Hanafi school: a male slave cannot marry more than two women. The matn of al-Quduri states this as a categorical limit, and the author immediately provides two proofs: the explicit saying of Umar ibn al-Khattab and the consensus of the Companions. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the half-rights principle that underlies many slave-free distinctions in Hanafi fiqh.

Identify the act and the ruling

Examine the second proof: Ijma of the Sahaba
The author reports from al-Hakam that the Companions unanimously agreed that a slave cannot combine more than two women. This ijma is the second proof.
Ijma of the Sahaba is a strong source in Hanafi jurisprudence.

While the Hanafi school holds this limit, some other schools allow a slave to marry up to four, analogizing to a free man. Recognise that this ikhtilaf exists.

Marrying After Ba'in Divorce: Waiting for Iddah
Ruling with two proofs from qawl sahabi and hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marrying a fifth wife after a ba'in divorce and identify the two proofs from the Sahaba.

Core Idea
After a ba'in divorce, iddah must expire before marrying another—the divorced wife still counts.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Quduri regarding the prohibition of immediately replacing a divorced wife with another when the divorce is ba'in (final). The Hanafi school relies on the practice of the Sahaba to establish this waiting period. We will explore the proofs and the underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a free man divorcing one of his four wives with a ba'in divorce. The ruling: he cannot marry a fifth (i.e., another woman) until the iddah of the divorced wife expires.
The text uses 'fourth' to mean 'fifth' relative to the original four.
2
Locate the first proof
The first proof is the saying (qawl) of Ali ibn Abi Talib: 'He should not marry a fifth until the iddah of the divorced woman expires.'
This is a qawl sahabi, a saying of a Companion, which Hanafi usul accepts as a proof.
3
Analyze the second proof
The second proof is a hadith from Amr ibn Shu'ayb: a man divorced a woman and then married her sister. Ibn Abbas ordered separation until the iddah of the divorced woman expired.
This shows the practice of a Companion, acting as a secondary proof.
4
Identify the legal implication
During her iddah, the divorced wife is still considered a wife for the purpose of counting the maximum of four. Thus marrying another would result in five wives concurrently, which is forbidden.
The iddah is a legal waiting period that maintains some marital effects.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) allow immediate marriage after a ba'in divorce, but the Hanafi school follows the Sahaba's practice as stronger evidence.
The difference arises from whether a qawl sahabi is considered binding.

The Option of a Freed Slave Woman: Khiyar al-`Itq
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute on khiyar al-`itq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on khiyar al-`itq, explain the dispute with the Shafi'i school, and identify the dalil used by each side.

Core Idea
A freed slave woman keeps her option to leave regardless of her husband's status – the hadith of Barirah proves it.

This passage from al-Quduri presents a clear ruling: if a slave woman is freed after being married, she has the option (khiyar) to either remain with her husband or leave, whether her husband is free or a slave. The same applies to a mukatabah (a slave with a manumission contract). While this seems straightforward, the proof from the hadith of Barirah sparked a scholarly interchange with Imam al-Shafi'i. In this lesson, we will examine the Hanafi proof, the Shafi'i counter-proof, and the legal outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a slave woman being married and then freed. The ruling is that she has the option (khiyar) to stay or leave.
The text explicitly says 'whether her husband is free or a slave' – this is key to the Hanafi position.
2
Follow the Hanafi proof from the hadith of Barirah
The Hanafi proof is that the Prophet gave Barirah the option when her husband was a slave. The phrase 'if he had been free he would not have given her choice' is interpreted to mean the option is not contingent on the husband's status.
Read the Arabic phrase: 'wa law kana hurran lam yukhayyirha'.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i counter-proof
The Shafi'i school cites another version of the same story stating that Barirah's husband was free, and she was still given the option. This would imply the option exists even with a free husband.
Both sides agree on the outcome but differ in nuance of proof.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school accepts both reports but relies on the explicit wording of the first hadith. The option is established in both scenarios, and the ikhtilaf does not affect the practical ruling.
The thamarah al-khilaf here is minimal – both schools give the option, but the Hanafi reasoning is more straightforward.
5
Apply the usul principle about khiyar and time
The Urdu commentary clarifies that the option only applies if the marriage was contracted before freedom. If the marriage occurred after freedom, no option exists.
This is an important condition: the timing of the marriage relative to freedom.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Emancipation cures the defect of missing permission, leaving the marriage valid and irrevocable.

This passage presents a specific scenario in marriage contracts: a slave woman marries without her master's permission, then later gains freedom. The Hanafi school rules that the marriage becomes valid upon emancipation, and she has no right to annul it. This ruling is based on a careful distinction from the well-known hadith of Barirah, where a similar option was given. Understanding this distinction clarifies the limits of khiyar (option to annul) in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? What is the hukm? Here, the act is a slave woman marrying without permission, then being freed. The ruling: the marriage is valid (sahih) and she has no khiyar.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof is cited? The hadith of Barirah from Aisha (Abu Dawud no. 2235). In that hadith, Barirah was given an option upon freedom because her husband was a slave. The Hanafi school uses this to show that the option only arises when the husband is still a slave at the time of freedom.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but relied upon for this ruling.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the ruling? The Hanafi interpretation: the option in Barirah's case was due to the husband's slave status, not her own lack of permission. In our scenario, the husband is free (or assumed free), so the option does not apply. The marriage becomes valid upon freedom because the impediment (lack of permission) is removed.
This is an example of distinguishing a ruling based on a specific cause (illa).
4
Identify the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school holds that the marriage is suspended on the master's permission; if permission is not given, the marriage is invalid. Emancipation does not automatically validate it. The Hanafi school disagrees, considering emancipation as a cure.
The practical difference: in Hanafi, the marriage is valid from the moment of freedom; in Shafi'i, it would remain invalid unless the master (or now the woman herself) ratifies it.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa) and is based on the principle that freedom removes previous disabilities. The proof from the Barirah hadith is interpreted narrowly to restrict khiyar.
This ruling is found in Zahir al-Riwayah and is relied upon.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A corrupt condition is void, but the marriage itself remains sahih.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi principle that distinguishes marriage from commercial contracts. In Hanafi fiqh, if a marriage contract includes a condition that contradicts the Shariah (shart fasad), the condition is invalid and unenforceable, but the marriage contract itself remains fully valid. This ensures the stability of marriage even if improper conditions are attached.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is the marriage contract (nikah). The condition (shart) is any stipulation that goes against the rulings of Shariah, such as a condition that the wife will not receive maintenance or that the husband will not provide housing.
Not every condition is corrupt; only those contradicting the essence or objectives of marriage.
2
State the ruling on the marriage
The marriage itself remains sahih (valid). The presence of a corrupt condition does not affect its validity.
This applies regardless of whether the condition is fulfilled or not.
3
State the ruling on the condition
The corrupt condition is batil (void) and has no legal effect. It is as if the condition was never made.
The condition is ignored; parties are not bound by it.
4
Contrast with commercial contracts
In commercial contracts (e.g., sale), a corrupt condition can render the entire contract fasid (voidable). Marriage is treated differently to safeguard its sanctity and permanence.
This distinction is specific to Hanafi fiqh.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An impotent husband gets one year to consummate; if he fails, the wife can request a bain talaq with full mahr.

This passage addresses a critical situation in marriage: impotence. Hanafi fiqh grants a wife the right to seek separation after giving her husband one full year to overcome the issue. The ruling is supported by the practice of the Companions and a hadith. We will examine the conditions, procedure, and legal effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Determine when the reprieve is granted and its duration (one full year).
The judge must grant the reprieve; it is not automatic upon discovery.
2
Recognize the judge's role
The judge grants the reprieve and, if impotence persists and wife requests, orders separation.
The wife's request is a condition for separation.
3
Understand the wife's option
The separation occurs only if the wife requests it; she may also choose to remain married.
The reprieve is for the husband to attempt consummation.

The separation is a talaq baynuna (irrevocable divorce), not an annulment (faskh).
This means it counts as a divorce and requires iddah.

Khalwa is valid seclusion in private with no impediment to intercourse.

Castration: Immediate Separation
Ruling on castrated husband: immediate separation without reprieve

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a castrated husband (majbub) and identify the proofs cited in the text.

Core Idea
A castrated husband is immediately separated without waiting, unlike an impotent man who gets a year.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn addresses the unique case of a castrated husband (majbub). Unlike impotence, which may be curable, castration is permanent and leaves no hope of marital consummation. Therefore, the judge pronounces immediate separation without any reprieve. The commentary provides two proofs to support this ruling, though they are indirect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What is the ruling for a majbub husband? The matn states that the judge separates them immediately and does not give a reprieve.
The ruling is wajib (obligatory) separation.
2
Locate the proofs
The text cites a hadith from Muhammad ibn Abd al-Rahman ibn Thawban and a saying of 'Ali ibn Abi Talib. Identify the exact words.
The hadith is about the obligation of dower upon looking or uncovering; the saying of 'Ali is about seclusion and seeing nakedness.
3
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah
How do these proofs support immediate separation? Both indicate that the dower becomes obligatory when marital intimacy is expected or initiated. Since a castrated man cannot consummate, separation is warranted and the dower remains due.
The proofs are indirect; they establish that a valid marriage exists and consummation is expected.
4
Distinguish from impotence
Why no reprieve for the majbub? Impotence may be curable, so the wife must wait one year. Castration is permanent and incurable, so separation is immediate.
This distinction is crucial in Hanafi fiqh.

Eunuch's Reprieve: Analogy to Impotence
Ruling on eunuch's reprieve by analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a eunuch's reprieve and distinguish it from the case of a completely cut man (majbub).

Core Idea
A eunuch (khasi) is given a one-year reprieve like an impotent man.

This brief passage from al-Quduri extends the established ruling on impotence to a eunuch—a man whose testicles are removed. The key point is that castration does not necessarily render a man completely incapable of intercourse, so rather than immediate annulment, the wife must wait a year. This lesson clarifies the legal treatment of the khasi and the reasoning behind the analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Subject and Ruling
Read the Arabic text 'والخصي يؤجل كما يؤجل العنين'. Note that 'الخصي' is the eunuch and 'يؤجل' means 'is given a reprieve'. The ruling is that the eunuch is given a reprieve just like the impotent man.
The term 'عنين' refers to a man unable to achieve or maintain an erection.
2
Understand the Analogy
The reprieve for the impotent is a one-year waiting period to see if the husband can consummate the marriage; the same applies to the eunuch. The basis is that removal of testicles does not necessarily remove all ability to perform intercourse.
The Hanafi school uses qiyas (analogy) to extend the impotence ruling to the eunuch.
3
Distinguish from the Completely Cut Man (Majbub)
A man whose penis is cut (majbub) is considered permanently incapable, and no reprieve is given; the wife may seek annulment immediately.
This distinction is crucial: the khasi still has the organ but lacks testicles.
4
Recognize the Practical Consequence
A wife married to a khasi must wait one year before she can seek an annulment (faskh) from a judge. This protects the marriage from hasty dissolution.
If the husband successfully consummates during the year, the marriage continues and the wife's option lapses.

كِتَابُ النِّكَاحِ: فَإِنْ كَانَ الزَّوْجُ هُوَ الْمُرْتَدُّ وَقَدْ دَخَلَ بِهَا فَلَهَا الْمَهْرُ، وَإِنْ لَمْ يَدْخُلْ بِهَا فَلَهَا النِّصْفُ. وَإِنْ كَانَتْ هِيَ الْمُرْتَدَّةُ قَبْلَ الدُّخُولِ فَلَا مَهْرَ لَهَا، وَإِنْ كَانَتِ ارْتَدَّتْ بَعْدَ الدُّخُولِ فَلَهَا جَمِيعُ الْمَهْرِ. وَإِنِ ارْتَدَّا مَعًا ثُمَّ أَسْلَمَا مَعًا فَهُمَا عَلَى نِكَاحِهِمَا. وَلَا يَجُوزُ أَنْ يَتَزَوَّجَ الْمُرْتَدُّ مُسْلِمَةً وَلَا كَافِرَةً وَلَا مُرْتَدَّةً. وَكَذَلِكَ الْمُرْتَدَّةُ لَا يَتَزَوَّجُهَا مُسْلِمٌ وَلَا كَافِرٌ وَلَا مُرْتَدٌّ.

أَسْلَمَتِ النَّصْرَانِيَّةُ قَبْلَ زَوْجِهَا بِسَاعَةٍ حَرُمَتْ عَلَيْهِ. (بخاري شريف: باب إذا أسلمت المشركة أو النصرانية تحت الذمي، رقم 5288)

وَعَنْ عَطَاءٍ فِي النَّصْرَانِيَّةِ تُسْلِمُ تَحْتَ زَوْجِهَا قَالَ: يُفَرَّقُ بَيْنَهُمَا. (مصنف ابن أبي شيبة، باب ما قالوا في المرأة تسلم قبل زوجها، رقم 18299)

وَعَنِ الثَّوْرِيِّ قَالَ: إِذَا ارْتَدَّتِ الْمَرْأَةُ وَلَهَا زَوْجٌ وَلَمْ يَدْخُلْ بِهَا فَلَا صَدَاقَ لَهَا، وَقَدِ انْقَطَعَ مَا بَيْنَهُمَا، فَإِنْ كَانَ قَدْ دَخَلَ بِهَا فَلَهَا الصَّدَاقُ كَامِلًا. (مصنف عبد الرزاق، باب المرتدين، رقم 12618)

وَعَنْ عِكْرِمَةَ قَالَ: أُتِيَ عَلِيٌّ بِزَنَادِقَةٍ فَأَحْرَقَهُمْ، فَبَلَغَ ذَلِكَ ابْنَ عَبَّاسٍ فَقَالَ: لَوْ كُنْتُ أَنَا لَمْ أُحَرِّقْهُمْ لِنَهْيِ رَسُولِ اللَّهِ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ، وَلَقَتَلْتُهُمْ لِقَوْلِ رَسُولِ اللَّهِ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ: مَنْ بَدَّلَ دِينَهُ فَاقْتُلُوهُ. (بخاري شريف: باب حكم المرتد والمرتدة، رقم 6922)

وَعَنْ أَبِي مُوسَى قَالَ: أَقْبَلْتُ إِلَى النَّبِيِّ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَسَلَّمَ وَمَعِي رَجُلَانِ مِنَ الْأَشْعَرِيِّينَ... فَأَمَرَ بِهِ فَقُتِلَ. (بخاري شريف: باب حكم المرتد والمرتدة، رقم 6923)

اصول: میاں بیوی کا ایک ساتھ دین تبدیل ہو تو نکاح باقی رہے گا لہذا اگر دونوں ایک ساتھ مرتد ہو کر مسلمان ہوئے تو دونوں کا نکاح باقی رہے گا۔
Book of Marriage: If the husband is the apostate and he had consummated with her, then she gets the mahr; if he did not consummate, she gets half. If she is the apostate before consummation, then no mahr for her; if she apostatized after consummation, she gets all of the mahr. If they both apostatize together then both become Muslim together, they remain upon their marriage. And it is not permissible for an apostate to marry a Muslim woman, nor a disbeliever, nor an apostate woman. Similarly, an apostate woman cannot be married by a Muslim, nor a disbeliever, nor an apostate. A Christian woman who converted to Islam an hour before her husband became forbidden to him. (Bukhari: Chapter on if a polytheist or Christian woman under a dhimmi converts, no. 5288) And from 'Ata regarding a Christian woman who converts under her husband, he said: They are separated. (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, Chapter on what they said about a woman converting before her husband, no. 18299) And from al-Thawri he said: If a woman apostatizes while she has a husband and he has not consummated with her, then no bridal gift for her, and what is between them is cut off; if he had consummated with her, then she gets the full bridal gift. (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, Chapter on apostates, no. 12618) And from 'Ikrima he said: 'Ali was brought some heretics and he burned them. That reached Ibn 'Abbas, who said: Had I been [in his place], I would not have burned them due to the prohibition of the Messenger of Allah, and I would have killed them due to the saying of the Messenger of Allah: 'Whoever changes his religion, kill him.' (Bukhari: Chapter on the ruling of the male and female apostate, no. 6922) And from Abu Musa he said: I came to the Prophet with two men from the Ash'aris... he ordered him to be killed. (Bukhari: Chapter on the ruling of the male and female apostate, no. 6923) Principle: If a husband and wife change their religion together, the marriage remains; therefore if both apostatize together and then become Muslim, their marriage remains.

Commentary
This passage presents Hanafi rulings on the effect of apostasy (irtidad) on marriage and mahr. The type is a ruling with proof. The author, al-Quduri, first states the core rules: When the husband apostatizes, if consummation occurred, the wife is entitled to the full mahr; if not, she gets half. This is because the husband's apostasy invalidates the marriage, but the wife retains her right to mahr based on consummation or the marriage contract. If the wife apostatizes before consummation, she forfeits all mahr because the contract was valid but she nullified it by her apostasy. If after consummation, she gets full mahr because consummation entitles her to it, and her apostasy does not retroactively void that right. If both apostatize together and then return to Islam together, the marriage is preserved, as the apostasy of both simultaneously does not sever the marriage bond, following the principle that when both change religion, the marriage continues. Then it is stated that an apostate man cannot marry any woman—Muslim, disbeliever, or apostate—because his apostasy makes him beyond the pale of Islam and marriage to a Muslim is unlawful, and marriage to a disbeliever or apostate is also prohibited as the apostate is considered more severe in disbelief. Similarly, an apostate woman cannot be married by a Muslim, a disbeliever, or an apostate. The proofs include hadith from Bukhari about a Christian woman converting to Islam before her husband, which separates them; the statement of 'Ata that they are separated; the statement of al-Thawri regarding the effect of apostasy on mahr; and two hadith about the killing of apostates, establishing the severity of apostasy. The Urdu note reiterates the principle that if both spouses change religion together, the marriage remains valid.

Key Terms
الْمُرْتَدُّ (al-murtadd): A person who leaves Islam by word or deed after being a Muslim.
الْمَهْرُ (al-mahr): The bridal gift due to the wife from the husband by marriage contract or consummation.
الدُّخُولُ (al-dukhul): Actual sexual intercourse between spouses; it triggers full mahr obligation.
