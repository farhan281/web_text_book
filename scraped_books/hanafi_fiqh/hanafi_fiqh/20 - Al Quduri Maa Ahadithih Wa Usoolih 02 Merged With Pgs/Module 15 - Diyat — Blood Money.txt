The Ruling on Shibh 'Amd (Quasi-Intentional Killing)
Defines shibh 'amd and prescribes mughallazah diyah on 'aqilah and kaffarah on killer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for shibh 'amd, explain the evidence, and distinguish it from the Shafi'i view on the camel allocation for mughallazah diyah.

Core Idea
Shibh 'amd requires mughallazah diyah on the 'aqilah and kaffarah on the killer, with a madhhab dispute on the exact breakdown of camels.

This passage from al-Quduri's chapter on Diyat defines shibh 'amd and outlines its legal consequences. You will learn the basic ruling, the proof from Quran and hadith, and how the Hanafi school differs from al-Shafi'i in the specific allocation of camels for the heavy diyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is shibh 'amd? What are the obligations? (diyah on 'aqilah, kaffarah on killer)
2
Locate the Hanafi proof
What evidence does the Hanafi school cite? (Quran 4:92, hadith of two women, athar of Ibn Mas'ud)
3
Analyze the opposing view
What is the Shafi'i position and its proof? (different camel allocation based on athar of Uthman and Zayd)
4
Determine the practical difference
What is the thamarah al-khilaf? (different number/type of camels in actual payment)

Book of Diyat. The author said: When a man kills a man in shibh 'amd (quasi-intentional), then on his 'aqilah is a heavy diyah, and on him is the kaffarah. The diyah of shibh 'amd according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf is one hundred camels in quarters (twenty-five each of four types), etc., and the heavy (mughallazah) is only established in camels specifically; if he judges with diyah from other than camels, it is not heavy. (Then proofs: Quran verse 4:92, hadith of the two women, and athar from Abdullah ibn Mas'ud for the Hanafi view; and athar from Uthman and Zayd for the view of al-Shafi'i.) Principles: The compensation in wealth for accidental killing, shibh 'amd, and wounds is called diyah. For shibh 'amd, the killer's family pays mughallazah diyah, and the killer himself must pay kaffarah.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's chapter on Diyat defines the ruling for shibh 'amd (quasi-intentional killing). The author first states the basic ruling: if a person kills another in a manner that is intentional in the act but not in the intent (e.g., striking with something not ordinarily lethal but it kills), then the killer's 'aqilah must pay a mughallazah (heavy) diyah, and the killer himself must observe kaffarah (expiation, which is freeing a slave or fasting two months). According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the diyah for shibh 'amd is one hundred camels divided into four categories of twenty-five each: hiqqah (three-year-old she-camels), jadha'ah (four-year-old), bint labun (two-year-old), and bint makhad (one-year-old). The heaviness (taghliz) only applies when the diyah is paid in camels; if paid in other forms (gold, silver, etc.), it is not heavy. The passage then provides evidence: Quran 4:92 (which mentions diyah and kaffarah for accidental killing, and qiyas extends it to shibh 'amd), the hadith of the two women from Hudhayl (where the Prophet placed diyah on the 'aqilah for what is effectively shibh 'amd), and a saying of Ibn Mas'ud specifying the breakdown for shibh 'amd. Then it cites a narration from Uthman and Zayd that differs, which is used by al-Shafi'i as proof for a different allocation. This shows an inter-madhhab disagreement: the Hanafi school relies on the athar of Ibn Mas'ud, while al-Shafi'i relies on the athar of Uthman and Zayd. The passage concludes with Urdu commentary reiterating that diyah is the term for financial compensation in killing and wounding, and that shibh 'amd requires mughallazah diyah on the 'aqilah and kaffarah on the killer.

Key Terms
شبه عمد (shibh 'amd): Quasi-intentional killing: an intentional act (e.g., striking with a stick) that is not intended to kill but results in death due to its severity.
دية مغلظة (diyah mughallazah): Heavy blood money for shibh 'amd, which is 100 camels or equivalent in other forms (but taghliz only applies when paid in camels).
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation for unintentional or quasi-intentional killing: freeing a believing slave, or fasting two consecutive months if unable.

Accidental Killing Diyah: Obligation and Property Types
States ruling on accidental killing and intra-Hanafi dispute on diyah property types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accidental killing, identify who pays diyah and kaffarah, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the types of property from which diyah can be paid.

Core Idea
Diyah for accidental killing is wajib on the 'aqilah; property types are debated within the Hanafi school.

This passage from Al-Quduri introduces the foundational rules of blood money (diyah) in accidental homicide. It establishes the obligation of diyah on the 'aqilah (agnate group) and expiation (kaffarah) on the killer. A key intra-Hanafi dispute is then presented: Imam Abu Hanifah limits the property types for diyah payment to camels, gold, and silver, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) add cattle, sheep, and garments based on the practice of Caliph 'Umar. Understanding this difference is essential for applying the ruling in various economic contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on accidental killing
Read the matn: 'قتل الخطأ يجب فيه الدية على العاقلة والكفارة على القاتل'. Note the obligation of diyah on the 'aqilah and kaffarah on the killer.
This is the core ruling, supported by hadith and athar.
2
Locate the base diyah amounts
Find the basic amounts: 100 camels (in fifths), 1,000 gold dinars, or 10,000 silver dirhams. These are the three types acknowledged by Abu Hanifah.
These amounts are fixed by the Prophet's practice.
3
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Compare Abu Hanifah's restriction (three types) with the Sahibayn's expansion (adding cows, sheep, garments). Understand that both views are within the Hanafi school.
The dispute is about the types of property, not the obligation itself.
4
Analyze the proof from Umar's practice
Review the athar from 'Umar cited in the passage, where he fixed diyah amounts for cows, sheep, and garments. This is the primary evidence for the Sahibayn's position.
The Sahibayn prioritize 'Umar's ijtihad as a source of practical guidance.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
Conclude that the later Hanafi school relies on the Sahibayn's view due to practicality and the strength of 'Umar's precedent.
This is the operative position for fatwa today.

وَقَتْلُ الْخَطَا يَجِبُ فِيهِ الدِّيَةُ عَلَى الْعَاقِلَةِ، وَالْكَفَّارَةُ عَلَى الْقَاتِل، وَالدِّيَةُ فِي الْخَطَا مِائَةٌ مِنَ الْإِبِلِ أَخْمَاسًا إِلَى آخِرِهِ. وَمِنَ الْعَيْنِ أَلْفُ دِينَارٍ وَمِنَ الْوَرِقِ عَشَرَةُ آلَافٍ وَلَا تَثْبُتُ الدِّيَةُ إِلَّا مِنْ هَذِهِ الْأَنْوَاعِ الثَّلَاثَةِ عِنْدَ أَبِي حَنِيفَةَ. وَقَالَ أَبُو يُوسُفَ وَمُحَمَّدٌ: وَمِنَ الْبَقَرِ مِائَتَا بَقَرَةٍ وَمِنَ الْغَنَمِ أَلْفَا شَاةٍ، وَمِنَ الْحُلَلِ مِائَتَا حُلَّةٍ كُلُّ حُلَّةِ ثَوْبَانِ.
وجه: (ا) الحديث لثبوت وَقَتْلُ الْخَطَأَ يَجِبُ فِيهِ الدِّيَةُ عَلَى الْعَاقِلَةِ: عَنْ جَابِرِ بْنِ عَبْدِ اللَّهِ، أَنَّ امْرَأَتَيْنِ مِنْ هُذَيْلٍ قَتَلَتْ إِحْدَاهُمَا الْأُخْرَى وَلِكُلِّ وَاحِدَةٍ مِنْهُمَا زَوْجٌ وَوَلَدٌ، فَجَعَلَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ دِيَةَ الْمَقْتُولَةِ عَلَى عَاقِلَةِ الْقَاتِلَةِ، وَبَرَّأَ زَوْجَهَا وَوَلَدَهَا، قَالَ: فَقَالَ عَاقِلَةُ الْمَقْتُولَةِ: مِيرَاثُهَا لَنَا؟ قَالَ: فَقَالَ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ صلى الله عليه وسلم: «لا، مِيرَاثُهَا لِزَوْجِهَا وَوَلَدِهَا» (سنن أبي داود، باب دية الجنين، رقم 4575/ بخاري شريف، باب جنين المرأة وأن العقل على الوالد وعصبة الوالد لا على الولد، غير 6909)

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Blood Money (Kitab al-Diyat) in Al-Quduri, presenting the core rulings on accidental killing (qatl al-khata). The author first states that the diyah (blood money) is due on the 'aqilah (the agnate group responsible for payment), while the kaffarah (expiation) is due on the killer himself. The diyah amount is one hundred camels in five categories, or their equivalent in gold (1,000 dinars) or silver (10,000 dirhams). A key intra-Hanafi dispute follows: Imam Abu Hanifah restricts the property types for diyah to camels, gold, and silver only, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) add cattle (200 cows), sheep (2,000 sheep), and garments (200 suits). The passage then provides multiple proofs: first, a hadith from Jabir where the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) placed the diyah on the 'aqilah of the killer, excluding the husband and children. Second, a saying of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab that the 'aqilah does not bear responsibility for intentional killing, slave, reconciliation, or confession. Then for the camel categories, there is a hadith from Ibn Mas'ud specifying the five categories (twenty each of hiqqah, jadh'ah, bint makhad, bint labun, and bani makhad). Further proofs from the practice of 'Umar show the valuation adjustments. The Urdu note at the end clarifies that in intentional killing, the killer faces qisas (retaliation) not diyah. This passage thus establishes the foundational rules for diyah in accidental homicide, with the scholarly difference on additional property types noted but the mufta bihi position being the expansion to include cattle, sheep, and garments as per the Sahibayn and later practice.

Key Terms
الدية (al-diyah): Blood money or financial compensation paid to the heirs of a victim in cases of homicide or bodily injury.
العاقلة (al-'aqilah): The agnate relatives (asabah) of the killer on the father's side, who collectively bear the diyah obligation.
الخطأ (al-khata'): Accidental killing where the killer had no intent to kill and no intention to harm.

Core Idea
The diyah of a dhimmi equals that of a free Muslim in Hanafi law, based on the practice of the Rightly Guided Caliphs.

This lesson addresses a point of intra-madhhab and inter-madhhab dispute: whether the blood money (diyah) for a non-Muslim citizen (dhimmi) is the same as for a Muslim. The Hanafi school, relying on the practice of Abu Bakr and 'Umar, rules it equal. Commentary

The author then provides proofs from hadith, sayings of the Companions (Abu Bakr), and a Tabi'i (Mujahid). The key principle is that when a wound cannot be subject to qisas (retaliation) due to the risk of error, diyah is prescribed or the matter is referred to a just arbitrator (hukumat), but here the amounts are fixed by nass.

Diyat for Fingers and Palm
Finger diyat ruling with proofs and principle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for diyat of the five fingers of one hand and apply the principle for wounds where qisas is not possible.

Core Idea
The five fingers of one hand carry half diyah; the palm adds no extra.

In Hanafi fiqh, the diyat for loss of the five fingers of one hand is fixed at half the full diyah. This ruling is based on a prophetic hadith and the practice of Caliph Umar. Moreover, if the fingers are cut along with the palm, the diyah remains half because the palm is not a separate member with a fixed amount. This lesson examines the textual proofs and the underlying principle that when qisas is not feasible, diyah or arbitration applies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for fingers alone
The text states that for the five fingers of the hand, the diyah is half of the full diyah. This is the primary ruling.
Full diyah for a person is 100 camels; half is 50 camels.
2
Note the ruling when cut with the palm
If the fingers are cut together with the palm, the diyah remains half. The palm does not add any extra amount.
The palm is considered part of the hand and has no separate fixed diyah.
3
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith from Abu Musa quotes the Prophet: 'The fingers are equal, ten ten camels.' This establishes 10 camels per finger, so five fingers make 50 camels = half diyah.
This is a sound hadith (zanni thubut and dalala).
4
Examine the companion's ruling
The text also cites a ruling of Umar that assigns different amounts to each finger, but the total still equals half diyah. This serves as supplementary evidence.
Hanafi school prefers the hadith over Umar's differentiation, maintaining equality.
5
Understand the general principle
For any wound where qisas (retaliation) is not possible due to risk of excess death, no qisas is taken; instead diyah or a just arbitrator's judgment (hukumat) applies.
This principle governs cases where limbs or wounds have no fixed diyah.

Diyat for Forearm and Extra Finger: Fixed and Assessed Compensation
Ruling with proof on diyat for forearm and extra finger

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for cutting the hand with part of the forearm and for an extra finger, and explain why hukumat applies when no fixed diyah exists.

Core Idea
Body parts without a fixed diyah are compensated through hukumat, assessed by just arbitrators.

In Hanafi fiqh, the diyah (blood money) for certain injuries is fixed, but not all parts have a prescribed amount. This lesson examines two such cases: when the cut includes the forearm along with the hand, and when an extra (supernumerary) finger is severed. The rule is clear: the fingers and palm carry half the full diyah, but the forearm and extra finger require hukumat, an estimate by two just arbitrators. The evidence comes from the sayings of early authorities, illustrating the Hanafi reliance on precedent and analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct injury cases: (1) cutting the hand with half the forearm, and (2) cutting an extra finger. Recognize each scenario and the compensation assigned.
The first scenario combines fixed and assessed parts.
2
Distinguish fixed diyah from hukumat
The fingers and palm receive half diyah because the hand has a fixed diyah (half the full diyah). The forearm and extra finger have no fixed amount, so hukumat applies. Understand the principle: fixed diyah is only for parts with explicit textual prescription.
Hukumat means an assessment by two just arbitrators based on the value of the lost part relative to the whole body.
3
Analyze the proof for the forearm ruling
The text cites Ibrahim al-Nakha'i: when the palm is cut at the joint, full diyah for the hand; if after that something is cut, hukumat. This supports that only the hand itself has a fixed diyah when cut at the wrist joint; the forearm beyond does not.
The proof is a qawl Tabi'i (saying of a successor), which is zanni in transmission but relied upon in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Analyze the proof for the extra finger
The text cites Sufyan al-Thawri: 'In the extra finger, a ruling (hukm).' This indicates that the extra finger is not like a normal finger; it has no fixed diyah, so hukumat is used. Understand that extra fingers are considered abnormal limbs.
Other schools may assign a fixed diyah (e.g., ten camels) but Hanafi treats it as a non-standard part.
5
Apply the principle to similar cases
The principle: any body part without a specified diyah in the Quran, Sunnah, or ijma receives hukumat. This includes parts like the forearm, upper arm, thigh, and supernumerary digits. Practice applying this to a hypothetical injury.
Hukumat is calculated by experts who estimate the value of the lost part relative to the full diyah.

Core Idea
Liability follows control: rider liable for front, driver for all, leader for front only; hind leg kicks are excused.

In this lesson, we examine the detailed Hanafi rulings on who is liable when a ridden, driven, or led animal causes injury. The general principle from the hadith 'al-ʿajmāʼu jurḥuhā jubār' seems to absolve all animal owners, but the Hanafi school, relying on the practice of the salaf, qualifies this based on the degree of control the handler has over the part of the animal that caused the harm. These distinctions are essential for justly assigning responsibility in diyat (blood money) cases and remain relevant in modern contexts involving animal transport and recreation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three roles and their liability
First, note the three roles: rider (rākib), driver (sāʼiq), and leader (qāʼid). The rider is liable for injuries caused by the animal's forelegs (stepping or knocking) but not for kicks from hind legs or tail, nor for excretions while moving. The driver is liable for both forelegs and hind legs. The leader is liable only for forelegs. If someone leads a string (qiṭār), he is liable for what the lead animal steps on; if a driver also is present, both share liability.
Memorise the pattern: rider = front only; driver = both; leader = front only.
2
Understand the underlying principle of control
The basis for these distinctions is the degree of control. The rider controls the front via the reins, but the hind legs are out of his effective control. The driver, walking behind, can control both ends. The leader only controls the front. When control is impossible, damage is excused (jubār).
This is the key to remembering the rulings.
3

The primary proof is the hadith: 'al-ʿajmāʼu jurḥuhā jubārun, wal-biʼru jubārun, wal-maʻdinu jubārun...' (The injury of an animal is a lost cause, a well is a lost cause, a mine is a lost cause...). Hanafis restrict this to cases where no person is in direct control; when a person is riding, driving, or leading, the person is not 'al-ʻajmā' (unowned), so liability applies to the extent of control.
The word jubbār means no compensation is due; it does not apply when the injury is caused by a part under the handler's control.

The matn lists statements from Ibn Sīrīn, Ḥammād, Shurayḥ, and Ibrāhīm that distinguish between front and hind leg injuries. For example, Ibn Sīrīn said they did not hold liable for a kick (nafḥah) but held liable for the head of the rein. These corroborate the Hanafi position.

Commentary

This passage from al-Quduri's Kitab al-Diyat presents the Hanafi rulings on the master's liability for his slave's accidental crimes. The key is the master's option (takhyir) to either surrender the slave or pay the arsh. The proofs from the Companions clarify that the 'aqila does not bear liability for a slave's crime. This lesson will break down the scenarios and the underlying legal reasoning.

A master who knowingly sells or frees a criminal slave must pay the full compensation — knowledge triggers liability.

Liability for Mudabbar and Umm Walad's Crimes
Ruling with proof from Tabi'i practice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on master's liability for crimes committed by a mudabbar or umm walad, and explain the proof based on the practice of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz.

Core Idea
For a mudabbar or umm walad, the master pays the lesser of the slave's value or the crime's arsh.

This lesson introduces a special rule in Hanafi fiqh regarding crimes committed by slaves with unique status: the mudabbar (promised freedom upon master's death) and umm walad (female slave who bears her master's child). The master's liability is capped at the slave's value, protecting him from paying full compensation. We examine the ruling and its evidence from the practice of the Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states: when a mudabbar or umm walad commits a crime, the master guarantees the lesser of the slave's value and the arsh (compensation). This is a limitation of liability.
The ruling is specific to these two categories of slaves due to their partial freedom.
2
Locate the Dalil
The proof cited is a practice of the Tabi'i Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, narrated by Bashir al-Mukattib, who ruled that the mistress pays the value of the slave-girl, not the full arsh.
This is a Qawl Sahabi (statement of a Companion, though Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz is a Tabi'i; Hanafis accept such practice as evidence).
3
Analyze the Wajh al-Dalalah
The Caliph's ruling limited payment to the value of the slave, implying that liability does not exceed the slave's value. The Hanafi school derives that the master pays the lesser of the two amounts.
This interpretation is consistent with the principle of protecting the master from excessive loss due to the slave's unique status.
4
Determine the Hukm Level
The ruling is wajib on the master: the payment is an obligatory financial liability.
It is not fard because the proof is zanni in both thubut and dalala.
5
Note Cross-Madhhab Difference
Some other schools may hold the master liable for the full arsh regardless of the slave's value. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi due to the strength of the practice evidence.
The practical consequence is that Hanafi masters pay less when the arsh exceeds the slave's value.

Diyat for a Female Slave and a Slave's Hand
States rulings on diyat for a female slave and a male slave's hand with proofs from hadith and qawl sahabi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on diyat for a female slave and a male slave's hand, and identify the proofs from hadith and qawl sahabi.

Core Idea
A female slave's diyah is capped at 5,000 dirhams; a male slave's hand diyah is 4,995 dirhams.

In this lesson, we examine two specific rulings from the Book of Diyat: the blood-money (diyah) for a female slave and for a male slave's hand. These rulings demonstrate how the Hanafi school derives fixed monetary amounts from prophetic hadith and the practice of the Companions, and how they differ from other schools. By the end, you will not only know the numbers but also understand the legal reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the first two sentences: the diyah for a female slave when her value exceeds the diyah is 5,000 dirhams; the diyah for a male slave's hand is 5,000 dirhams minus 5 dirhams (4,995 dirhams).
The amounts are fixed even if the slave's market value is higher.
2
Understand the general principle
The next sentence states: 'Everything that is measured from the diyah of a free person is measured from the value of the male slave.' This means the proportion of a free person's diyah for a body part applies to the slave's value, not a fixed amount.
For example, if a free person's hand diyah is half of full diyah (5,000 out of 10,000), then a slave's hand diyah is half of his market value.
3
Trace the proof for the female slave ruling
The author cites two proofs: (1) hadith of Mu'adh ibn Jabal: 'The diyah of a woman is half of the diyah of a man' – this sets the maximum for a free woman at 5,000 dirhams, and by analogy for a female slave when her value exceeds that. (2) 'Ali's statement: 'The injuries of women are half of the diyah of a man, little or much.'
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), but accepted here.
4
Note the opposing proof and Hanafi response
A hadith from 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb says a woman's diyah equals a man's until one-third. The Shafi'is use this to argue no cap. The Hanafis interpret it as applying only up to one-third, after which the woman's diyah reduces to half.
The Hanafis rely on the practice of the caliphs (e.g., 'Umar) to maintain the cap.
5
Examine the proof for the slave's hand ruling
The author cites 'Umar's statement: 'The blood-money of a male slave is in his price, like the blood-money of a free person in his blood-money.' This establishes the proportionality principle.
Thus, the fixed amount of 4,995 dirhams for the hand assumes a full slave value of 10,000 dirhams (half of 10,000 is 5,000, minus 5 as a minor adjustment).

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Diyat provides rulings on the blood-money (diyah) for slaves, along with their proofs from hadith and sayings of the Companions. The first ruling states that if a female slave's market value exceeds the standard diyah of 5,000 dirhams, her diyah is capped at 5,000 dirhams—it does not increase with her value. This is because her diyah is tied to her price as property, not to the free person's diyah scale. The second ruling specifies that the diyah for a male slave's hand is 5,000 dirhams minus 5 dirhams, i.e., 4,995 dirhams. The general principle follows: whatever is fixed from the diyah of a free person is proportionally fixed from the value of the slave. For example, if a free person's hand diyah is half of full diyah (2,500 out of 5,000), then a slave's hand diyah is half of his value. The proofs cited include: (1) the hadith that a woman's diyah is half a man's, establishing the norm for female slaves as well; (2) 'Ali's statement applying half-rates to women's injuries; (3) a hadith from 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb indicating that a woman's diyah equals a man's until one-third, which the Hanafis interpret as up to a third but then the woman's diyah reduces; (4) 'Umar's statement equating a slave's blood-money to his price. These evidences show the Hanafi reliance on both primary texts and Companion opinions (qawl sahabi) as valid sources when no explicit Quran or mutawatir hadith exists.

أَمَة (amah): A female slave.
عَبْد ('abd): A male slave.
قِيمَة (qimah): Market value of the slave at the time of the injury.

Diyah for Miscarriage and Fetus of a Slave Woman
Specifies various miscarriage scenarios and diyat for a slave woman's fetus with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on diyah for a miscarried fetus, differentiate scenarios based on the mother's death timing, and calculate the compensation for a slave woman's fetus.

Core Idea
The diyah for a dead fetus is a ghurrah; timing of the mother's death determines whether it is owed.

This passage from the Book of Diyat details the compensation for causing a miscarriage. The Hanafi school, relying on the hadith of the two Hudhayl women and statements of the tabi'in, establishes distinct rulings for a free woman's fetus and a slave woman's fetus, as well as the inheritance of the compensation and the absence of atonement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling for a free woman's miscarriage
If a man strikes a free woman's belly and she miscarries a dead fetus, he must pay a ghurrah: a male or female slave worth half a tenth of the full diyah (500 dirhams).
The hadith of Abu Hurayrah is the primary proof.
2
Distinguish scenarios involving the mother's death
If the fetus is expelled dead, then the mother dies, the perpetrator owes full diyah for the mother AND a ghurrah for the fetus. If the mother dies first and then delivers a dead fetus, nothing is due for the fetus because its death is consequential.
The fetus's death is not an independent injury in the second scenario.
3
Understand the inheritance of the fetus's diyah
Whatever compensation is fixed for the fetus is inherited by its heirs (e.g., the father) – not by the mother's tribe or the killer.
The hadith explicitly states that the inheritance goes to the husband and children.
4
Apply the ruling for a slave woman's fetus
For a slave woman's fetus: if male, half a tenth of the slave's market value; if female, a tenth of her value (as if alive). This is derived from the practice of early scholars like Sufyan and Ibrahim.
The slave's fetus has no independent diyah; its value is a fraction of the mother's price.
5
Recognize the absence of atonement (kaffarah)
No kaffarah is required for causing a miscarriage because this is killing by cause (qatl sabab), not direct accidental killing (qatl khata').
Kaffarah is only wajib for direct accidental killing.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The diyat for a miscarried fetus is a ghurrah, inherited by its heirs, with no kaffarah required.

This lesson from al-Quduri's Kitab al-Diyat presents the Hanafi position on compensation (diyat) for causing a pregnant woman to miscarry. We will examine the different scenarios—whether the woman dies before or after the miscarriage, and whether the child is born alive or dead—and the evidence from the Sunnah that establishes these rulings. Additionally, the rule for a slave-girl's fetus and the absence of expiation are discussed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Read the first part of the text to identify the conditions: (1) miscarriage alive then mother dies, (2) miscarriage dead then mother dies, (3) mother dies then miscarriage dead.

2
Determine the compensation for each scenario
For the first two scenarios, the text says 'upon him is diyah and a ghurrah'—meaning full blood money for the woman and a ghurrah for the fetus. For the third, 'nothing is due for the fetus'.
Ghurrah is a male or female slave of minimum value.
3
Understand inheritance of the ghurrah
The text states that what is obligatory for the fetus is inherited from it. The hadith from Abu Dawud confirms that the diyat of the fetus goes to the husband and children, not the mother's tribe.
This overrides the default inheritance rules for a living person's diyah.
4
Examine the ruling for a slave-girl's fetus
For a slave-girl, the diyat is a fraction of her market value: half a tenth (1/20) if male, a tenth (1/10) if female. This is based on statements of Sufyan and Ibrahim.
The higher value for a female fetus reflects the greater value of a female slave.

Core Idea
For accidental and quasi-intentional killing, the expiation is freeing a believing slave or fasting two consecutive months — feeding is not an option.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses the expiation (kaffarah) for two categories of unintentional killing: shibh al-'amd (quasi-intentional) and khata' (accidental). The ruling is based on Quran 4:92 and establishes a specific sequence: first freeing a believing slave, and if unable, fasting two consecutive months. Notably, feeding the poor — a common alternative in other expiations — is explicitly excluded here. Understanding this ruling requires attention to the precise order and the reason for exclusion.

The primary expiation is freeing a believing slave (raqabah mu'minah). This is the first option mentioned in the text and derived from the Quranic imperative.
The slave must be a believer (mu'min).

If one cannot free a slave (la yajid), the alternative is fasting two consecutive months (siyam shahrayn mutatabi'ayn). This is sequential, not optional.
Fasting must be consecutive; breaking the sequence invalidates it.

Note the exclusion of feeding
The text explicitly states that feeding (it'am) is not sufficient (la yajzi). This distinguishes this kaffarah from others (e.g., oath expiation) where feeding is allowed.
This is based on the absence of feeding in the verse and Hanafi usul principles.

The verse also includes diyah (blood money) but that is separate from kaffarah.

Qasamah: The Hanafi Procedure for Unknown Homicide
States the Hanafi ruling on qasamah and contrasts with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah and explain why the Hanafi school differs from the Shafi'i school on this issue.

Core Idea
In unknown homicide, fifty oaths from the locality impose diyah—the Prophet's practice is our proof.

The chapter on qasamah addresses a difficult situation: a slain person found in a locality with no known killer. The Hanafi school holds that oaths from the locality suffice to impose blood money, while Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees. Understanding why the Hanafi school relies on prophetic practice is key to grasping this distinctive institution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is qasamah? It is a legal procedure where fifty men from the locality swear they did not kill and do not know the killer. The ruling: diyah (blood money) becomes wajib upon the locality after these oaths.
The hukm level is 'wajib' not 'fard' because it is established by ahad hadith and ijtihad.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that when the killer is unknown, the wali (guardian of the victim) selects fifty upright men from the locality. They swear fifty oaths, and upon their oath, diyah is imposed on the locality (on the 'aqilah).
The wali has the right to choose the fifty; this is part of the procedure.
3
Identify the opposing school and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that diyah is not obligatory with these oaths. Instead, the wali swears (presumably fifty oaths) and then receives the blood money directly.
The Shafi'i position does not involve the locality's oath.
4
Follow the Hanafi proof
The text cites five hadith where the Prophet (ﷺ) imposed diyah after oaths from the locality (or from the Jews) in cases of unknown homicide. The key evidence is that the Prophet did not require the wali to swear but rather the locality.
All hadith are ahad (zanni al-thubut), but multiple chains strengthen the practice.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In Hanafi practice, the financial burden falls on the locality's 'aqilah (tribe/neighbourhood). In Shafi'i practice, the wali receives the diyah and the locality is not burdened collectively.
This difference affects who ultimately pays and how the case is adjudicated.

Conditions of Traces and Special Cases in Qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a trace of injury justifies qasamah and identify when diyah falls on the driver's 'aqilah instead of the locality.

Core Idea
Trace of injury required for qasamah; blood from eyes/ears counts, but not from nose/mouth/back.

In qasamah (oath-taking for homicide), not every dead body triggers liability. The Hanafi school requires a physical trace of violence on the body to presume homicidal death. This lesson clarifies which bodily marks count as valid traces and also covers a special scenario: when a body is found on a mount driven by a specific person, the diyah shifts from the local community to that person's 'aqilah.

State the general rule for qasamah
If a body is found with no trace of injury, there is no qasamah and no diyah on the community. The trace must be a sign of violence (wound, bruise, blood from unnatural orifices).
This is the default rule.

Blood flowing from the nose, back, or mouth is not a valid trace because it can occur naturally. Blood from the eyes or ears is considered a valid trace, making the person a presumed homicide victim.
The school relies on the likelihood of foul play.
3
Apply the special case of a mounted slain
If the slain is found on a mount that a man is driving, the diyah falls on that man's 'aqilah (paternal relatives), not on the people of the locality where the body was found.
The driver's association with the mount shifts liability.

Al-Thawri's statement is used as a dalil: 'If a slain is found among a people and there is a trace, the blood money is upon them; if no trace, nothing unless evidence is established.' This supports the condition of trace.
The proof is a statement of a tabi'i, considered a valid authority in this context.

If he is found dead with no trace of injury, there is no qasamah and no diyah. Likewise, if blood flows from his nose, back, or mouth. But if blood comes out from his eyes or ears, then he is (considered) a slain person. And if the slain is found on a mount that a man is driving, the diyah is on his 'aqilah, not on the people of the locality. The proofs: from al-Thawri: 'If a slain is found among a people and there is a trace of injury, the blood money is upon them; if there is no trace, nothing is upon the 'aqilah unless evidence is established against someone.' (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq). From Shuraih: 'Regarding a man found dead on a shop at the door of a people with no trace, he made the people of the house swear.' (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq). Principle: Whoever appears as the killer by an evident sign, diyah and qasamah are upon him.

Commentary
This passage provides rulings on two conditions in qasamah. First, the presence of a trace of injury (athar) on the body is essential for qasamah and diyah. If no trace is found, or if blood flows from natural orifices (nose, back, mouth) where it could be due to natural causes, qasamah is not applied. However, if blood comes from eyes or ears, it is considered a sign of homicide and qasamah applies. The distinction is that blood from eyes or ears is not typical of natural death. Second, if the slain is found on a mount driven by a specific man, the diyah is on that man's 'aqilah (paternal relatives who pay blood money), not on the inhabitants of the locality, because the driver has direct association with the mount. The proofs are from al-Thawri and Shuraih. The Urdu note explains that the person indicated by an evident sign is liable for diyah and qasamah.

Key Terms
أَثَر (athar): A physical trace of violence on the body, such as a wound, bruise, or blood from unnatural orifices.
دَابَّة (dābbah): A riding animal, such as a horse or camel, that is being ridden or driven.
عَاقِلَة (ʿāqilah): The paternal relatives of a person who are liable to pay diyah on their behalf.

Diyah on the 'Aqilah: Collective Responsibility for Unintentional Killing
Ruling with multiple hadith proofs for diyah liability on the 'aqilah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that diyah for quasi-intentional and accidental killing is wajib upon the 'aqilah, define the 'aqilah as the people of the diwan or the tribe, and explain the primary hadith proof.

Core Idea
Diyah for accidental killing is collective—on the 'aqilah, not the killer alone.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Kitab al-Ma'aqil establishes a foundational ruling in Hanafi fiqh: blood money (diyah) for unintentional killings is a collective responsibility, not an individual one. The author defines the responsible party (al-'aqilah) and provides multiple proofs from the Sunnah and early practice. By studying this, you will understand how the Hanafi school anchors an important financial obligation in prophetic precedent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the opening sentence: 'Kullu diyatin wajabat bi-nafs al-qatl' and note that the ruling applies to both shibh al-amd (quasi-intentional) and khat' (accidental) killing. The diyah becomes obligatory because of the killing itself, not as a punishment.
This distinguishes it from intentional murder (amd) which leads to qisas.
2
Define the 'aqilah
The text defines the 'aqilah as 'ahl al-diwan' — those registered in the state military payroll system, representing the tribe or clan. Understand that this is the Hanafi specific definition based on the practice of 'Umar.
Other schools define 'aqilah differently (e.g., male relatives).
3
Analyze the primary hadith proof
Examine the proof from Al-Shafi'i quoting the Prophet's judgment: diyah of 100 camels for accidental killing is upon the 'aqilah, paid over three years. Note the chain of transmission and how it establishes both the party and the method.
The hadith is ahad but its meaning (dalalah) is clear and acted upon.
4
Examine supporting secondary proofs
The passage includes additional proofs: the Prophet assigning blood money on every tribe (from Jabir), the document of 'Umar quoting the Prophet's pact, statements of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, and the practice of 'Umar establishing diwans. Each supports the definition of 'aqilah as the tribe or diwan.
These strengthen the evidence and show early consensus.
5
Understand the practical implementation
The second part details how the diyah is collected: from stipends if on diwan, else from tribe members over three years with a cap of four dirhams per person per year. The killer himself also contributes as one of the 'aqilah.
The three-year instalment is based on prophetic practice and Sunnah.

Book of Blood Money. He (the author, may Allah have mercy on him) said: The diyah for quasi-intentional killing and accidental killing, and every diyah that becomes obligatory due to the killing itself, is upon the 'aqilah, and the 'aqilah are the people of the military register (diwan). (Bukhari and Muslim) Proof 2: The hadith establishing that the 'aqilah are the people of the diwan: that he heard Jabir ibn Abdullah say: The Prophet (peace be upon him) assigned blood money upon every tribe. (Muslim) ... (Additional proofs from the prophetic tradition and statements of Companions including a document of Umar, statements of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, and the practice of Umar establishing registers.) [Principle]: Ma'aqil is derived from 'aql, meaning to prevent, to come to one's senses. When the family pays the diyah for accidental or quasi-intentional killing, they repeatedly prevent the killer from such mistakes by rebuking him. If the killer is among the people of the diwan, the diyah is taken from their stipends over three years. If the stipends are distributed over more or less than three years, the collection follows that schedule. Whoever is not from the people of the diwan, his tribe is his 'aqilah, and the diyah is divided among them over three years, with no individual paying more than four dirhams per year (one dirham and two daniqs), and the amount may be reduced. If the tribe cannot bear it, the nearest tribe is added. The killer himself enters into the 'aqilah and pays like one of them. Proof 1: The hadith establishing that if the killer is from the diwan... (al-Shafi'i narrated that the Prophet (peace be upon him) judged accidental killing of a free Muslim against a free man as one hundred camels upon the 'aqilah of the offender, and that it is in three years, one-third each year.) Proof 2: Statement of Yahya ibn Sa'id that it is from the Sunnah to pay diyah in instalments over three years. Proof 3: Hadith establishing that for those not from the diwan, blood money is upon the male relatives. (Bukhari and Muslim) Proof 4: Hadith establishing that the killer enters into the 'aqilah: Abu Hurairah related the story of two women from Hudhayl, and the Prophet (peace be upon him) ruled diyah of the woman upon her 'aqilah. (Bukhari) [Principle]: Whether the killer is one or many, in both cases, a grace period of three years is given for payment of the diyah.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Ma'aqil of Al-Quduri presents the Hanafi ruling on who bears the diyah (blood money) for accidental (khat') and quasi-intentional (shibh al-amd) homicides, along with extensive textual proofs. The author first states the core ruling: every diyah that becomes obligatory due to killing itself (i.e., as a compensation for the act of killing, not as a punishment for murder) is upon the 'aqilah. The 'aqilah are defined as the people of the diwan—that is, those registered in the state military payroll system, representing the tribe or clan. This is the Hanafi position, distinguishing the 'aqilah from the killer himself. In accidental killings, the diyah is not paid by the killer alone but by his 'aqila (male paternal relatives). Book of 'Aqila: And the 'aqila of a freed slave is the tribe of his master. And the client of muwalaat (contractual patronage) – his master and his tribe pay on his behalf. And the 'aqila does not bear less than half a tenth of the diyah (1/20), and bears half a tenth and above. And what is less than that is in the property of the offender. And the 'aqila does not bear the injury of a slave. (Then proof texts) ... Principle: If the blood money is less than one twentieth of the full diyah, then the killer will pay it, not his 'aqila.
Source pages p. 198

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri presents three additional rulings regarding the 'aqila. First, for a freed slave (mu'taq), his 'aqila is the tribe of his master (the one who freed him). This is because the freed slave's 'asabah are his master's tribe through wala' (patronage). For a client of muwalaat (mawla al-muwalaat), meaning a person who enters a contract of loyalty with another (e.g., a non-Muslim who becomes Muslim at his hands), both his master and his original tribe are responsible. Second, the 'aqila only bears responsibility for diyah amounts that are at least half a tenth (1/20) of the full diyah. This threshold corresponds to the diyah for a fetus (ghurrah), which is five hundred dirhams (or fifty dinars), half a tenth of the full diyah of ten thousand dirhams (or one thousand dinars). If the amount is less than that, the offender himself pays. Third, the 'aqila does not bear liability for crimes committed by a slave. The slave's owner is responsible for his slave's torts, not the 'aqila. The principle (usul) at the end summarizes: for amounts less than one-twentieth of the full diyah, only the killer pays. The proofs include the hadith 'the freed slave belongs to the tribe of his master' and the hadith of the two women of Hudhayl, which establishes the ghurrah amount as half a tenth.

Key Terms
عاقلة المعتق (ʿāqilat al-muʿtaq): The 'aqila (responsible group) of a freed slave is the tribe of the master who freed him.
مولى الموالاة (mawlā al-muwālāt): A person who enters a contract of mutual loyalty and inheritance with another free person, often upon conversion to Islam.
نصف عشر الدية (niṣf ʿushr al-diyah): Half a tenth of the full diyah, i.e., 1/20 of the full amount (500 dirhams or 50 dinars for a complete diyah of 10,000 dirhams or 1,000 dinars).
جناية العبد (jināyat al-ʿabd): Any injury or crime committed by a slave; the 'aqila is not liable for it.
