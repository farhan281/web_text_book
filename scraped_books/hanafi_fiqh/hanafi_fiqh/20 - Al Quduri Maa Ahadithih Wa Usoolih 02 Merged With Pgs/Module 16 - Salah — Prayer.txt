Ruling on Leaving Tasmiyah During Slaughter
States ruling and proofs for tasmiyah omission in slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on omitting tasmiyah during slaughter (intentional vs forgetful), identify the supporting proofs, and explain why the ruling differs from the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Intentional omission of tasmiyah makes meat carrion; forgetfulness does not—a distinction based on the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas and istihsan.

This passage from Kitab al-Sayd wa al-Dhaba'ih addresses a key fiqh question: what happens if the slaughterer forgets to say Bismillah? The Hanafi school draws a clear line between intentional omission—which renders the meat haram—and forgetful omission, which is excused. The author, Imam al-Quduri, states the ruling concisely and then provides multiple proofs from the Quran and Sunnah, as well as opinions of the Salaf. In this lesson, we will unpack the ruling, trace its evidence, and understand the underlying usul principles that justify the exception for forgetfulness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is omitting tasmiyah during slaughter. The ruling: if intentional, meat is carrion (haram); if forgetful, it is permissible to eat.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the Quranic verse (6:121), the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim, and the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas. Identify each proof.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The general verse prohibits eating meat without tasmiyah; but the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas specifically excuses forgetfulness. How do the Hanafis reconcile? They say the verse applies to intentional omission only.
The hadith of Ibn 'Abbas: 'The Muslim is sufficient with his name; if he forgets...'
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that both intentional and forgetful omission forbid the meat because the verse is general with no exception. Recognize the opposing view.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school gives weight to the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas (marfu') and the Salaf's practice, applying istihsan to avoid hardship. This is the mufta bihi position.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sayd wa al-Dhaba'ih presents the Hanafi ruling on the obligation of tasmiyah (saying Bismillah) during slaughter, along with its proofs. The ruling states: if the slaughterer intentionally omits tasmiyah, the meat is carrion (maytah) and strictly forbidden; but if he forgets, it is permissible to eat. The author then provides multiple proofs: the Quranic verse (Surah al-An'am 6:121) prohibiting eating meat over which Allah's name is not mentioned, which supports the prohibition of intentional omission. The hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim about hunting with the mi'rad and dogs shows the importance of tasmiyah at the time of releasing the dog or throwing the weapon. For the forgetful case, the hadith of Ibn 'Abbas reports that the Prophet said the Muslim is sufficient with his name, meaning his inherent faith, and if he forgets, he can still say the tasmiyah retroactively and eat. The hadith of 'A'ishah further supports that if there is doubt whether tasmiyah was said, one can say it themselves and eat. The Hanafi school takes the middle position: tasmiyah is wajib (obligatory), but forgetfulness does not render the meat impermissible, unlike the Shafi'i view that intentional and forgetful omission both forbid the meat, and unlike a strict literalist view that would forbid it in all cases. The proofs from the Companions (Ibn 'Abbas) and Tabi'un (al-Hakam) are cited as supporting evidence.

Key Terms
التَّسْمِيَة (al-tasmiyah): Uttering 'Bismillah Allahu Akbar' at the time of slaughter.
الذَّبِيحَة (al-dhabihah): The animal that is slaughtered.
مَيْتَة (maytah): Carrion; an animal that died without proper Islamic slaughter, hence haram.

Required Veins for Slaughter and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on number of veins to cut in slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four veins required for valid slaughter in the Hanafi school, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding how many must be cut, and identify the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: cutting most of the four veins suffices; the Sahibayn require the trachea, esophagus, and at least one jugular.

In this passage from al-Quduri, we learn the precise location for slaughter and the four veins that must be cut. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that cutting most of these four is sufficient, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, demand specific veins. This lesson unpacks that dispute and its practical impact on halal slaughter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its location
Locate the ruling: slaughter (dhabh) must be performed between the throat (al-khalq) and the collar bone (al-layyah). This is the accepted site.
The site ensures proper cutting of the major vessels.
2
List the four veins
Memorize the four cut vessels: the trachea (al-khalqum), the esophagus (al-mari'), and the two jugular veins (al-wadajan).
These are the essential passages and blood vessels.
3
Contrast the two positions
State Abu Hanifa's view: cutting most of the four suffices. Then state the Sahibayn's view: at least the trachea, esophagus, and one jugular must be cut.
The difference is in strictness: Abu Hanifa's is more facilitative.
4
Analyze the proofs
The primary proof for Abu Hanifa is the general hadith 'Eat what causes blood to flow' – as long as blood flows, validity is achieved. The Sahibayn rely on qiyas stressing the necessity of cutting the vital passages.
The Hanafi response defends the general hadith over narrow qiyas.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Note that the relied-upon position in the school is Abu Hanifa's, due to facilitation and the strength of the general evidence.
This affects fatwa: if most veins are cut, meat is halal.

Commentary
This passage addresses two related issues: the location and method of valid slaughter, and the tools that may be used. First, the proper place for slaughter is between the throat (khalq) and the collar bone (layyah). The veins to be cut are four: the trachea (khalqum), the esophagus (mari'), and the two jugular veins (wadajan). All four are cut. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that if most of these four are cut, the slaughter is valid and the meat is halal. His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, require that at least the trachea, esophagus, and one jugular vein be cut—meaning at least three of the four, including the two major ones. This is a stricter view. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is the view of Abu Hanifa, as it is more facilitative. Secondly, the tool for slaughter must be sharp and cause blood to flow (anhara al-dam). Any such tool is permissible except for a tooth or nail that are attached (qa'imah), because these are considered cruel and do not properly cut. The proofs: hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij quotes the Prophet permitting what causes blood to flow except tooth and nail. The hadith of Ibn 'Abbas specifies the location as throat and collar bone. The prohibition of cutting the spinal cord (nakh') from Ibn 'Umar shows that the animal should be allowed to die from the cut, not from severing the spine. This passage combines a ruling, a proof for the tool, and an intra-madhhab dispute.

Key Terms
الْخَلْقُوم (al-khalqum): The trachea or windpipe.
الْمَرِيء (al-mari'): The esophagus or food pipe.
الْوَدَجَان (al-wadajan): The two jugular veins on either side of the neck.

The Hanafi Ruling on Eating Crows: Crop Crow vs. Speckled Crow
States two rulings on crows and provides scriptural evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating crows and explain how the diet of the bird determines its permissibility.

Core Idea
Crows are not all the same — crop crows are halal, carrion-eaters are haram.

In this lesson, we examine a concise ruling from Al-Quduri's matn on the permissibility of eating crows. The Hanafi school makes a crucial distinction based on the bird's diet. We will explore the two rulings and the four proofs that support them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
State the Hanafi ruling for the crop crow (ghurab al-zar') and the speckled crow (abqa'). The matn says: 'There is no harm in crop crow' and 'the speckled one that eats carrion is not eaten'.
The crop crow is permissible; the speckled crow is forbidden.
2
Locate the proofs
The commentary lists four pieces of evidence: (1) a statement of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, (2) a hadith from Ibn Umar about jallalah, (3) a statement of Tawus, and (4) a hadith from Aisha about four foul animals.
Each proof supports the ruling that birds eating carrion are impermissible.
3
Analyse the jallalah hadith
The hadith from Ibn Umar forbids eating the jallalah (animal that eats filth) and its milk. By qiyas, the speckled crow, which eats carrion, is analogous to the jallalah and thus prohibited.
Crop crow does not eat carrion, so it is not included in this prohibition.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school considers all crows haram based on the hadith listing the crow as one of the four 'fasiq' animals. The Hanafi school restricts that to crows that eat carrion.
The distinction rests on the diet and the legal maxim 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by their purposes).

Core Idea

Women and Mushaf in Military Expeditions: Large Army vs Small Detachment
Ruling on taking women and mushaf to battle with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taking women and copies of the Quran on military expeditions and explain why the ruling differs between a large army and a small detachment.

Core Idea
Safety determines permissibility: taking women and mushaf is mubah with a large army but makruh in a small raiding party.

In this lesson, we examine the first part of a passage from al-Quduri's Kitab al-Siyar. The author addresses the permissibility of women and mushaf accompanying Muslim forces. The ruling hinges on the size of the force and the level of security. We will also look at the hadith proof cited.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage contrasts two military contexts: a large army (عسكر عظيم) where safety is assured, and a small detachment (سرية) where safety is not assured.
The term 'sariyyah' refers to a small raiding party sent out from the main army.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For a large army, the ruling is 'لا بأس' (no harm, i.e., permissible/mubah). For a small detachment, it is 'يكره' (disliked, makruh tanzihi).
The ruling is the same for both women and copies of the Quran (مصاحف).
3
Understand the reasoning
The permissibility is based on safety. In a large army, women and mushaf are secure from the enemy; in a small detachment, there is risk of capture and desecration.
The Shari'ah aims to prevent harm to vulnerable individuals and sacred objects.
4
Analyze the hadith proof
The hadith from Ibn Umar states that the Prophet forbade traveling with the Quran to enemy lands due to fear of desecration. By implication, when fear is absent (large army), the prohibition is lifted, making it permissible.
This is an example of 'mafhum al-mukhalafah' (inverse implication) in Hanafi usul.

Permission Required for Women and Slaves to Fight, Except in Sudden Attack
Ruling on permission for women and slaves to fight, with an exception for sudden attack

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when women and slaves may fight without permission and apply the exception of sudden enemy attack.

Core Idea
Women need husband's permission, slaves need master's permission to fight, unless the enemy attacks suddenly.

This lesson covers the second ruling from the same passage: the conditions under which women and slaves may participate in combat. The general rule is that they require permission, but an important exception is made for defensive warfare when the enemy launches a surprise attack.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage states that a woman may not fight except with her husband's permission, and a slave may not fight except with his master's permission.
This applies to offensive jihad; the slave hadith illustrates this.
2
Identify the exception
The exception is 'إلا أن يهجم العدو' (unless the enemy attacks suddenly). In that case, permission is not required.
The exception covers defensive situations where fighting becomes a personal obligation (fard 'ayn).
3
Analyze the hadith proofs
The hadith of the slave shows that the Prophet required permission from the mistress. The hadith of the man with elderly parents shows that prior obligations (like caring for parents) can supersede the call to jihad, analogously for slaves and wives.
The second hadith is used to support the principle that permission from those with authority over the individual is needed.
4
Derive the ruling
In normal circumstances, women and slaves may not fight without permission. However, in a sudden enemy attack, defense becomes obligatory, and permission is waived.
This is based on the maxim 'الضرورة تبيح المحظورات' (necessity permits prohibitions).

Prohibited Targets in Jihad: Women, Children, and Non-Combatants
Rulings on avoiding treachery and not killing non-combatants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling prohibiting the killing of specified non-combatants in jihad, identify the exceptions, and cite the primary hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Killing non-combatants in jihad is haram unless they directly participate in warfare.

This lesson examines the ethical boundaries of warfare in Hanafi fiqh based on the clear teachings of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The matn of al-Quduri lists categories of people—women, children, the insane, the elderly, the blind, the crippled—who must not be killed, with a crucial exception: those who contribute to the war effort. We will unpack the ruling and its proofs from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited acts and categories
List the specific actions forbidden (ghadr, ghulul, tathl) and the categories of people not to be killed: women, boys, insane, elderly, blind, crippled.
The prohibition includes both direct killing and treachery.
2
Locate the exceptions
Find the clause 'إِلَّا أَنْ يَكُونَ أَحَدُ هَؤُلاءِ مِمَنْ لَهُ رَأْيِّ فِي الْحَرْبِ أَوْ تَكُونُ الْمَرْأَةُ مِلْكَهُ' – exceptions for combatant role or if the woman is a slave.
The exception transforms the ruling from absolute to conditional.
3
Analyze the primary proof (dalil)
Examine the hadith from Buraidah: 'اغزوا ولا تغلوا ولا تغدروا ولا تقتلوا وليدا' – the imperative 'لا تقتلوا' indicates prohibition.
The term 'وليدا' (child) is interpreted as a general category for all non-combatant minors.
4
Resolve the apparent conflict with other hadith
Reconcile the hadith from Samurah ('اقتلوا شيوخ المشركين') by noting that the command to kill elderly applies only when they are combatants (advisors/strategists).
The exception clause in al-Quduri ('له رأي في الحرب') harmonizes the texts.
5
Derive the final ruling
State the mufta bihi: killing any non-combatant is haram unless they directly participate in war (e.g., giving strategic advice, fighting, or being an enemy soldier).
This is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.

Prohibition of Killing Non-Combatants in War
Ruling with hadith evidence on prohibitions in war.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which non-combatants are protected from killing in war according to the Hanafi school, identify the exceptions, and cite the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Islam forbids killing non-combatants; only those with a direct role in war may be targeted.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational ruling from Kitab al-Siyar: the protection of women, children, the elderly, and other non-combatants during armed conflict. The text lists prohibited acts and provides multiple prophetic hadith as evidence. Understanding these rules is essential for a proper Hanafi understanding of just warfare.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited acts
Recognize the four prohibitions: treachery, embezzlement, mutilation, and killing non-combatants. Note that the first three are also mentioned in the hadith.
Treachery (ghadr) refers to breaking treaties; embezzlement (ghulul) is stealing from spoils before distribution; mutilation (tathil) is disfiguring the dead.
2
List the protected categories
From the passage: woman, child, insane person, decrepit old man, blind person, and cripple. These are protected from being killed unless an exception applies.
The insane are separately emphasized: 'and they should not kill an insane person'.
3
Understand the exceptions
Two exceptions: (1) if any of these persons has a 'role in war' (ra'y fi al-harb), such as advising or strategizing, and (2) if the woman is the property (slave) of a Muslim.
The exception for property women indicates that a female slave is not considered a free non-combatant; however, killing her may still be subject to other rules.
4
Analyze the hadith evidence
The author cites four hadith evidence chains. Extract the common prohibitions and note the apparent contradiction in the hadith of Samura (commanding to kill old men). Understand how it is harmonized: 'old men' here means those capable of fighting, not the decrepit.
The hadith from Samura is interpreted in light of other stronger prohibitions, showing the Hanafi method of tawfiq (harmonization).
5
Apply the Hanafi principle
The prohibitions (nahy) in the hadith indicate haram (strict prohibition) per Hanafi usul unless evidence suggests otherwise. The exceptions are explicitly stated, so they are narrowly construed.
The principle 'al-asl fi al-nahy al-tahrim' (the default in a prohibition is that it indicates unlawfulness) applies.

Commentary
This passage from the Kitab al-Siyar of Al-Quduri states a ruling with multiple hadith proofs. The ruling: Muslims must avoid treachery, embezzlement of spoils, mutilation, and killing specific non-combatants: women, children, insane, very old, blind, and crippled. The exception: if such a person actively contributes to warfare (e.g., by advising or fighting), they may be killed. Also, a woman captured as a slave is considered property, not a free combatant, so killing her is not permitted under this rule. The author then cites several hadith: from Sulayman ibn Burayda (prohibiting treachery and killing children), from Ibn Umar (the Prophet forbade killing women and children), from Anas (prohibiting killing the elderly, children, women), and from Samura ibn Jundub (commanding to kill the old men of the polytheists and spare their youth). The Imam's Discretion with Captives: Kill, Enslave, or Release
States imam's three options and provides proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three options the Hanafi school grants the imam for captives and explain the proof for each option from the Quran and sunna.

Core Idea
The imam has full discretion to kill, enslave, or release captives as dhimmis, based on Quran and multiple hadith.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents the foundational Hanafi ruling on captives of war. The imam is not restricted to a single course of action; rather, he may choose among three options based on public interest. The text then marshals several hadith and a Quranic verse to establish the legality of each option, highlighting the school's reliance on sunna beyond the explicit Quranic text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its nature
Recognize that the matn states the imam has choice (khiyar). This is a discretionary (mubah) ruling, not an obligation.
2
List the three options
Kill, enslave, or release as dhimmis. Note the condition: release must be as dhimmis, not absolute freedom.
3
Examine the Quranic proof
Read Surah Muhammad 47:4, which mentions mann (release) and fida' (ransom). The Hanafi school uses this to support the option of release but adds other options via hadith.
The verse does not explicitly forbid other options.
4
Examine the hadith proofs
For killing: hadith of Ibn Khatal and Banu Qurayza. For enslaving: Banu Qurayza. For releasing: Thumama ibn Uthal. Note each hadith's authenticity.
The Khaibar hadith relates to land but is cited as additional proof.
5
Understand the thamarah (practical result)
The imam is not limited to ransom/release; he can choose based on public interest, which differs from other schools.

Prohibition of Returning Captives and Disposition of Livestock
Forbids returning captives to enemy territory and gives rules for livestock when the army returns

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that returning captives to dar al-harb is impermissible and explain the conditions under which livestock may be destroyed, citing the Quranic and hadith proofs.

Core Idea
Captives may not be returned to enemy territory; livestock may be slaughtered and burned if unable to transport, but hamstringing is forbidden.

This lesson presents two important rulings from the Hanafi fiqh of siyar (military law). The first prohibits returning captives to dar al-harb, supported by multiple Quranic verses. The second gives the imam permission to destroy captured livestock to prevent enemy use, with the condition that it is done humanely through slaughter, not torture. We will examine the legal proofs (dalil) for each ruling and understand the rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage contains two distinct rulings: (1) it is not permissible to return captives to dar al-harb, and (2) when the imam cannot transport livestock to dar al-Islam, he may slaughter and burn them, but not hamstring or leave them.
Both are part of the law of war (siyar).
2
Analyze the proofs for the first ruling
The author cites three Quranic verses: 8:67 (criticizing taking captives before subduing the land), 9:5 (command to kill polytheists wherever found), and 47:4 (option of release or ransom, implying captives are under Muslim control). These collectively show that captives should not be returned to enemy territory.
3
Analyze the proofs for the second ruling
The author uses verse 59:5 (permission to cut palms), the hadith of the Prophet burning Banu al-Nadir's palms, the hadith prohibiting making living things targets, and the practice of Abu Bakr instructing not to slaughter except for food. These justify destroying livestock to deny enemy benefit, but prohibit cruelty.
The prohibition of hamstringing comes from the hadith 'Do not make anything with a soul a target'.
4
Understand the conditions and limits
The permission to destroy livestock is conditional on inability to transport them to Muslim territory. The destruction must be by slaughter (dhabh) and burning, not by hamstringing (which is torture). Leaving them for the enemy is also prohibited as it would strengthen them.
5
Summarize the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that captives must not be returned to dar al-harb; they are subject to the imam's discretion (execution, enslavement, ransom, or release) but not return. Livestock that cannot be transported may be slaughtered and burned, but never hamstrung.
This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Only One Horse Receives a Share in Spoils
States the ruling and provides hadith proofs on limiting horse shares.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the number of horses eligible for shares in spoils and explain the intra-madhhab disagreement between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A single horse per fighter gets a share in booty; Abu Yusuf allows two.

The distribution of spoils (ghanimah) in jihad follows precise rules set by prophetic practice. Among them is the allocation of shares for horses used in combat. While the horseman himself receives one share, his horse may also receive a share—but how many horses count? This lesson examines a dispute within the Hanafi school on whether a fighter can receive shares for more than one horse, based on two hadiths presented by the commentator of al-Quduri's matn.

Guided Path
1
State the general Hanafi position
Identify the ruling in the matn: 'no share except for one horse' (ولا يسهم إلا لفرس واحد). Note that this is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
The matn is the authoritative text; the commentary expands with proofs.
2
Analyze the first proof (for one horse)
Examine the hadith from al-Zubayr: the Prophet gave two shares for one horse at Khaybar. Determine how this supports the single-horse view.
The phrase 'two shares for his horse' implies a single horse received both the rider's and horse's increments.
3
Analyze the second proof (for two horses)
Examine Abu Yusuf's hadith from Makhul: 'No share from horses except for two horses.' Understand that this is the basis for his allowance of two horses.
This hadith is weaker in transmission according to the preferred view.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Contrast the two positions: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (one horse) vs. Abu Yusuf (two horses). Note that the madhhab's relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is one horse.
The commentator does not explicitly refute Abu Yusuf but the matn favors one horse.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider a fighter who brings two horses to battle. Under the dominant view, he gets shares for only one horse; under Abu Yusuf, he gets shares for both.
This affects the distribution of spoils among warriors.

No Share for Riding Camels or Mules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shares for non-horse mounts and explain the Quranic proof limiting shares to horses.

Core Idea
Only horses qualify for additional shares in spoils; other mounts are excluded by Quranic specification.

In the distribution of spoils, the Hanafi school restricts additional shares to horses alone. This lesson examines the ruling that riding camels and mules do not entitle their rider to an extra share, supported by the Quranic verse on preparing 'ribat al-khayl' (steeds of war).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The matn states that no share (sahm) is given for a riding camel (raahilah) or a mule (baghl). This is a prohibition: additional shares are limited to horses.
The ruling applies to extra shares for mounts, not to the basic share of the fighter.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The Quranic verse (8:60) commands preparation of 'ribat al-khayl' (steeds of war). The term 'khayl' specifically refers to horses, excluding camels, mules, and donkeys.
The verse is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala regarding which animals qualify.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The specific mention of 'khayl' indicates that only horses are included in the command to prepare mounts. This implies that only horses are entitled to the associated share in spoils.
Hanafi usul: al-takhsis bi al-dhikr – specification by mention limits the ruling.
4
Address the apparent contradiction in the hadith
The hadith from Makhul states that the early Muslims used to give shares for mules. This seems to contradict the matn. The commentator reconciles by noting that the hadith likely refers to a different practice or is weak; the stronger evidence is the Quranic specification.
This highlights the principle of preferring Quran over a weak or ambiguous narration.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi school holds that only horses qualify. Some earlier scholars permitted shares for mules, but the Hanafis rely on the Quranic text.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the practical consequence is different share distributions.

The Dhimma Covenant: Actions That Do Not Nullify It

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that certain major offenses do not break the dhimma contract, identify the evidence from the Najran treaty, and differentiate this from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
A dhimmi's covenant survives even murder, blasphemy, and adultery with a Muslim woman — the Hanafi dhimma is not broken by these acts.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents a distinctive Hanafi ruling on the inviolability of the dhimma contract. While the Islamic state imposes conditions on dhimmis (distinction in dress, prohibition of horse-riding and weapon-carrying), their protected status is not automatically terminated by serious offenses like murder, insulting the Prophet, or adultery. Instead, they are punished for the crime while remaining under the covenant. Let us examine the text and its proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligations and the listed offenses
The passage first states that dhimmis must be distinguished from Muslims and prohibits them from riding horses and carrying weapons. Then it lists four actions that some might think break the covenant: refusing to pay jizyah, killing a Muslim, reviling the Prophet (peace be upon him), and committing adultery with a Muslim woman.
These are not trivial matters; they include capital crimes and blasphemy.
2
State the core ruling
For each of these four actions, the text says 'lam yantaqid 'ahduhu' — 'his covenant is not broken.' This is the explicit Hanafi position. The covenant remains intact despite the offense.
The ruling is negative (not broken) rather than positive (still valid). This has practical implications.
3

The author cites the hadith of the treaty of Najran, where the Prophet (peace be upon him) stipulated that churches would not be destroyed and priests not expelled 'as long as they do not commit a new offense (hadath) or consume usury.' The Hanafi interpretation understands that the covenant is only broken by specific new acts (like usury) as per the treaty, and the four listed offenses are not among them.
The hadith is a Sunan Abi Dawud narration; its chain is ahad, so it is zanni al-thubut.
4
Understand the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) hold that apostasy or insulting the Prophet breaks the covenant. The Hanafi school distinguishes: while the dhimmi is punished (e.g., executed for blasphemy), his dhimma status remains for other legal purposes (e.g., inheritance from his family).
The practical consequence (thamarah) is whether his marriage and estate are treated as Muslim or dhimmi after execution.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents a set of rulings concerning the treatment of dhimmis (non-Muslim subjects of the Islamic state). It is a ruling statement with proofs (dalil). The author first states that if old churches and synagogues are destroyed, dhimmis are allowed to rebuild them. They are required to distinguish themselves from Muslims in dress, mounts, saddles, and garments, and they are prohibited from riding horses and carrying weapons. Then, crucially, the passage states that even if a dhimmi refuses to pay jizya, kills a Muslim, reviles the Prophet (peace be upon him), or commits adultery with a Muslim woman, his covenant (dhimma) is NOT broken according to the Hanafi school. This is a distinctive ruling; other schools may differ. The author then provides supporting evidence: (1) the hadith of the treaty with Najran, which allowed them to keep their churches and not be expelled unless they committed a new offense (this hadith also shows that the covenant is not broken by past actions), (2) the hadith about not greeting them first, indicating a lowered status, (3) the practice of Umar stamping their necks, and (4) the detailed conditions of the treaty of Sham. The Urdu note summarizes that dhimmis may not propagate their religion excessively and that insulting the Prophet makes one liable for execution. Note: The Hanafi position is that these actions do not automatically nullify the covenant; rather, the dhimmi may be punished (e.g., for murder) but remains under the covenant.

Key Terms
ذمة (dhimma): The covenant of protection granted to non-Muslims living under Islamic rule, entailing certain rights and obligations.
جزية (jizyah): The poll tax imposed on adult male dhimmis in exchange for protection and exemption from military service.
حدث (hadath): A new offense; in the context of the Najran treaty, it refers to an act that violates the peace agreement (e.g., consuming usury).

Conditions That Break the Dhimma Covenant
States when the covenant with dhimmis is broken: joining dar al-harb or fighting Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two specific conditions that break the dhimma covenant according to the Hanafi school and explain the prophetic evidence that supports this.

Core Idea
The dhimmi's covenant breaks only by joining dar al-harb or actively fighting Muslims — not by insults or lesser treason.

This lesson examines the precise legal conditions for nullifying the dhimma contract. The author of Al-Quduri specifies that the covenant is not voided by every breach but only by two specific acts: physically joining enemy territory (dar al-harb) or taking up arms against Muslims. This position is supported by the Prophet's treatment of the Jewish tribes of Medina and an incident involving a Jewish woman who insulted the Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling
Read the opening line: 'وَلَا يَنْتَقِضُ الْعَهْدُ إلَّا أَنْ يَلْحَقُوا بِدَارِ الْحَرْبِ أَوْ يَغْلِبُوا عَلَى مَوْضِعٍ فَيُحَارِبُونَا'. This clearly states that the covenant is not broken unless one of two actions occurs.
The ruling is negative: the covenant remains intact except in these two cases.
2
Understand the two exceptions
The two exceptions are: (1) joining dar al-harb (enemy territory) and (2) overpowering a place and fighting Muslims. Note that 'fighting' implies active combat, not mere hostility.
Dar al-harb is land under non-Muslim rule where Islamic law does not prevail.
3
Analyze the first evidence: Banu Qurayzah
The Prophet initially expelled Banu al-Nadir for treachery but allowed Banu Qurayzah to remain. Only when Qurayzah later fought alongside the enemy in the Battle of the Trench did the Prophet punish them. This shows that fighting, not earlier hostility, broke the covenant.
4
Analyze the second evidence: the Jewish woman
A Jewish woman repeatedly insulted the Prophet. A man strangled her. The Prophet did not enforce retaliation or blood money, implying her covenant was intact – insults alone do not break it.
5
Contrast with other schools
Some schools (e.g., Shafi‘i) consider insulting the Prophet as a breach. The Hanafi position relies on the Qurayzah precedent: even persistent hostility did not break the covenant until actual fighting occurred.
This ikhtilaf has practical consequences for how dhimmis are treated.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on the dhimma covenant, specifying the conditions under which it is broken. The author states that the covenant is not broken by the previously mentioned actions (refusing jizya, murder, etc.) but only if the dhimmi physically joins the enemy territory (dar al-harb) or overpowers a place and fights Muslims. Ruling on Silk for Men and Dispute on Using It as a Pillow
Intra-Hanafi dispute with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on silk for men and women, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on using silk as a pillow by citing the proofs for each position.

Core Idea
Silk is haram for men to wear but halal for women; the dispute on pillowing it hinges on whether the prohibition extends beyond wearing.

This passage presents a clear foundational ruling: silk is prohibited for men's clothing and permitted for women. Yet it opens a nuanced intra-Hanafi debate about a secondary use—using silk as a pillow. The matn records the positions of Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples, and the text supplies hadith proofs that ground each view. We will walk through the ruling, the disagreement, and the evidence, sharpening our ability to trace how a single prohibition can be analogically extended or restricted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State the undisputed part: silk clothing is haram for men and halal for women. Note that this is agreed within the Hanafi school.
This is the foundation; the dispute builds on it.
2
Locate the point of disagreement
The text adds: 'la ba'sa bi-tawassudihi 'inda Abi Hanifah wa qala Abu Yusuf wa Muhammad yukrahu tawassuduhu.' Identify that the dispute is about using silk as a pillow, not wearing it.
Tawassud = using as a pillow or leaning on.
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
The hadith from 'Ali states silk and gold are 'halal for their females.' Abu Hanifa argues that the prohibition is specifically on clothing and adornment; passive use like a pillow is not included.
The operative phrase is 'haram 'ala dhukuri ummati, hillun li'inathihim.'
4
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
They cite the hadith that forbids drinking from gold/silver vessels and wearing silk. By qiyas, using silk as a pillow is a form of use analogous to wearing, thus makruh.
They extend the prohibition via 'illa of adornment or luxury.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Abu Hanifa, a man may use a silk pillow; according to the Sahibayn, it is better to avoid it. The mufta bihi is not settled in this text; both views are transmitted.
In practice, many later Hanafi fatwas lean towards caution (makruh).

(صحيح البخاري، باب الحرير للنساء، رقم 5840) وجه (٣) الحديث لثبوت لا يحل للرجال لبس الحرير ويحل للنساء أنه سمع علي بن أبي طالب، يقول: إن نبي الله : أخذ حريرًا فجعله في يمينه، وأخذ ذهبًا فجعله في شماله ثم قال: «إن هذين حرام على ذكور أمتي» (سنن أبي داود، باب في الحرير للنساء، رقم 4057) وجه (١) الحديث لثبوت لا بأس بتوسده عند أبي حنيفة وقال أبو يوسف ومحمد يكره توسده سمعت علي بن أبي طالب يقول: أخذ رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم حريرًا بشماله، وذهبًا بيمينه، ثم رفع بهما يديه، فقال: «إن هذين حرام على ذكور أمتي، حل لإناثهم» (سنن ابن ماجه، باب لبس الحرير والذهب للنساء، رقم 3595/ سنن أبي داود، باب في الحرير للنساء، رقم 4057) وجه (١) دليل الصاحبين الحديث لثبوت لا بأس بتوسده عند أبي حنيفة وقال أبو يوسف ومحمد يكره توسده عن حذيفة الله قال: «نهانا النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم أن نشرب في آنية الذهب والفضة، وأن نأكل فيها وعن لبس الحرير، (بخاري شريف، باب افتراش الحرير وقال عبيدة هو كلبسه، رقم 5837/مسلم شریف، باب : تحريم استعمال إناء الذهب والفضة على الرجال والنساء، وخاتم الذهب والحرير على الرجل، وإباحته للنساء. ،رقم2067) اصول: سونا کی طرح ریشم بھی مردوں پر حرام ہے، مگر یہ کہ تین یا چار انگلی ریشم ہو تو جائز ہے۔ لغات توسد : ٹیک لگانا، تکیہ لگانا۔
Wearing silk is not permissible for men, and it is permissible for women. There is no harm in using it as a pillow according to Abu Hanifa, and Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said it is disliked (makruh) to use it as a pillow. (Proof 1: Hadith for establishing 'Wearing silk is not permissible for men and is permissible for women' from Hudhayfa who said: The Messenger of Allah forbade wearing silk and gold, and said: 'It is for them in this world, and for us in the Hereafter.' (Sunan Ibn Majah, Bab Karahiyat Labis al-Harir, no. 3590) Proof 2: Hadith from 'Ali who said: The Prophet clothed me in a silk garment, so I went out in it, and I saw anger in his face, so I tore it apart among my women. (Sahih al-Bukhari, Bab al-Harir li al-Nisa', no. 5840) Proof 3: Hadith from 'Ali: The Prophet of Allah took silk in his right hand and gold in his left hand, then said: 'These two are haram for the males of my Ummah.' (Sunan Abi Dawud, Bab fi al-Harir li al-Nisa', no. 4057) Proof 4 (for Abu Hanifa's view on pillow): Hadith from 'Ali: The Messenger of Allah took silk in his left hand and gold in his right hand, then raised both hands and said: 'These two are haram for the males of my Ummah, halal for their females.' (Sunan Ibn Majah, no. 3595/ Sunan Abi Dawud, no. 4057) Proof 5 (proof of the two companions for their view): Hadith from Hudhayfa: The Prophet forbade us from drinking in vessels of gold and silver, and from eating in them, and from wearing silk. (Sahih al-Bukhari, Bab Iftirash al-Harir wa Qala 'Ubayda huwa ka lubsihi, no. 5837/Muslim, Bab Tahrim Istikhdam Ina' al-Dhahab wa al-Fiddah, no. 2067) Principles: Just like gold, silk is also haram for men, except if it is three or four finger-widths (of silk), then it is permissible. Glossary: tawassud = to lean on, to use as a pillow.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on silk for men and women, and an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding using silk as a pillow. The matn (base text) states that wearing silk is haram for men and halal for women. Additionally, it mentions the disagreement: Abu Hanifa sees no problem in using silk as a pillow, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) consider it makruh (disliked). The author then provides hadith proofs for each part. For the prohibition on men, three hadith are cited: from Hudhayfa, from 'Ali, and another from 'Ali. For Abu Hanifa's permissive view on the pillow, a hadith from 'Ali that silk is 'halal for females' implies that using it as a pillow is not covered by the prohibition. For the Sahibayn's view, a hadith from Hudhayfa that forbids wearing silk is extended by qiyas to using it as a pillow, since it is a form of use akin to wearing. The Urdu commentary adds that silk is like gold in being haram for men, except if the silk content is only three or four finger-breadths (e.g., in mixed fabrics).
