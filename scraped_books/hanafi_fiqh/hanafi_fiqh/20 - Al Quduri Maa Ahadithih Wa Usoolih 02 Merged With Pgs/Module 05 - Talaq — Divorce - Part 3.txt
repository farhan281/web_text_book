Intercourse by Shubha During 'Idda: Second 'Idda Required
Ruling with Companion proofs on 'idda after shubha intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a woman in 'idda has intercourse by shubha, cite the Companion proofs, and explain the principle of tadakhul (interlocking of 'iddas).

Core Idea
Intercourse by shubha during 'idda creates a separate 'idda; the two interlock (tadakhul).

This lesson addresses a scenario where a woman already observing an 'idda (from divorce or death) engages in sexual intercourse under the mistaken belief of legality (shubha). The Hanafi school, relying on the judgments of 'Ali and 'Umar, holds that a new 'idda becomes obligatory. However, does she serve two separate 'iddas consecutively, or do they run concurrently? The Hanafi principle of tadakhul resolves this practically.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
Recognize that the woman is already in an 'idda (from divorce or death) and then has intercourse by shubha. The act is the sexual relation under mistaken belief.
2
State the ruling (hukm) from the text
The text says 'another 'idda is obligatory upon her.' This is wajib. Understand that the ruling is clear: a second 'idda must be observed.
3
Analyze the proofs from the Companions
Two proofs are given: (1) 'Ali ordered separation, completion of the first 'idda, then a new full 'idda. (2) 'Umar ruled both 'iddas run together. These actions are authoritative for Hanafis as qawl al-sahabi.
The difference between 'Ali and 'Umar indicates an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf on the method.
4
Understand the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf and tarjih
Some Hanafis (based on 'Ali) say consecutive: finish first, then second. Others (based on 'Umar) say concurrent (tadakhul). The relied-upon position is tadakhul, meaning the same menstrual periods count for both 'iddas.
Tarjih: later Hanafi authorities prefer tadakhul for practicality.
5
Apply the principle of tadakhul to a concrete scenario
If a woman is in a 3-month 'idda and after 1 month has shubha intercourse, her second 'idda also starts. The remaining 2 months serve for both 'iddas simultaneously. She completes both when the first period set ends.

Interlocking of Two 'Iddas and Immediate Commencement
Ruling with proof on tadakhul and commencement of 'idda

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on tadakhul (interlocking) of two 'iddas and explain that the 'idda begins immediately upon divorce or death, regardless of the woman's knowledge.

Core Idea
Two concurrent 'iddas can overlap; the 'idda begins immediately upon divorce or death, regardless of knowledge.

This lesson addresses two important rulings about the 'idda (waiting period) for women. First, if a woman is subject to two 'iddas at the same time, the same menstrual periods count for both (tadakhul). Second, the 'idda always begins immediately after the cause—divorce or death—even if she does not know about it. The proof is the statement of Ibn 'Umar (radi Allahu anhu). Understanding these rules clarifies many practical scenarios in Muslim family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The text first mentions tadakhul: when two 'iddas coincide, the menstrual periods are counted for both. Then it states when the first 'idda ends and she is not pregnant from the second, she must complete the second 'idda. Finally, it gives the general rule on commencement.
Rulings 19 and 20 in the text.
2
Understand tadakhul
If a woman is divorced and later in her 'idda has intercourse by shubha (ambiguous) that creates a second 'idda, the same menstrual periods fulfill both obligations. This shortens the total waiting period.
This is the Hanafi position.
3
Note the commencement rule
The 'idda begins immediately upon the occurrence of divorce or death, not upon knowledge. This means if a woman only learns later that the event happened, she does not add extra time if the original period has expired.
Opposing view: some say upon knowledge.
4
Examine the dalil
The evidence is a statement from Ibn 'Umar (radi Allahu anhu) recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah: 'Her 'idda begins from the day he divorces her or dies.' This shows immediate commencement.
This is a hadith of a Companion, considered strong in Hanafi usul.

Overlapping of Two Waiting Periods
States the ruling on when two 'iddas coincide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on tadākhul al-ʿiddatayn and apply it to a scenario where a woman experiences two marital dissolutions in succession.

Core Idea
When two 'iddas overlap, the same menstrual cycles count for both; the total waiting period is not shortened.

This lesson addresses a common situation: a woman experiencing two marital dissolutions in succession, such as a divorce followed by the husband's death during the first 'idda. The Hanafi school provides a clear rule to avoid double counting and ensure the waiting period is observed correctly. We will examine the ruling, its proof from a companion, and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two 'iddas and the principle of overlap
Understand that if a woman is in two simultaneous 'iddas (e.g., from divorce and death), the Hanafi rule allows the same menstrual cycles to count for both. This is called tadākhul.
The principle applies only when the 'iddas are of the same type (both based on menstrual cycles or both on months).
2
Analyze the counting mechanism
The passage states: 'what she sees of menstruation is counted for both together.' This means the woman does not need to observe separate waiting periods; the cycles she experiences fulfill both obligations simultaneously.
3
Examine the edge case when the first 'idda ends first
If the first 'idda (e.g., from divorce) ends before the second (e.g., from death), and she is not pregnant, she must complete the remainder of the second 'idda. The total time may be longer than either individual 'idda.
This prevents the woman from being considered free before the longer 'idda is fulfilled.
4
Study the proof from Ibn Umar
The hadith: 'Her waiting period is from the day he divorced her and from the day he dies.' This indicates that the 'idda begins from the event, and if two events occur, the later event's 'idda must be completed if it extends beyond the earlier one.
This is a qawl sahabi (companion's statement) used as proof by the Hanafi school.
5
Contrast with other schools
Unlike the Hanafi school, the Shafi'i school generally does not allow overlapping of 'iddas; each 'idda must be observed separately. Understand the practical difference.
This ikhtilaf is based on different interpretations of the evidence.

When Does the Waiting Period Begin?
Ruling on commencement of 'idda and the effect of ignorance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for when 'idda begins and explain why a woman's ignorance does not delay its completion.

Core Idea
'Idda starts at the event, not at knowledge — time alone suffices.

This short passage from Al-Quduri's Matn settles a critical question: when does the waiting period ('idda) for a divorced or widowed woman actually begin? The text gives a crisp rule and then addresses a common situation — what if she doesn't even know? The answer may surprise you and has important practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Triggers
The passage mentions two events that start an 'idda: talaq (divorce) and wafah (death). Each has its own beginning point stated right after the trigger word.
The phrase 'ʿaqība' means immediately after, without any gap.
2
Apply the Timing Rule
For divorce, the 'idda begins the moment the husband says the divorce words. For death, it begins at the moment of death. There is no waiting for delivery of news or for the woman to become aware.
This is a fixed objective rule — time starts ticking from the event, not from awareness.
3
Consider the Ignorance Scenario
The passage then asks: what if she learns of the divorce or death only after the 'idda period has already passed? The answer is clear: her 'idda has finished. She does not need to restart or make up the period.
The completion depends on calendar time, not on her knowledge.
4
Extract the Legal Principle
The underlying principle is that 'idda is a temporal obligation (ʿibāda waqtiyya). Once the time window has elapsed, the obligation is fulfilled. Knowledge is not a condition (shart) for the passage of time.
This contrasts with acts like salat, where knowledge of the time is generally required.

In a fasid marriage, 'idda begins at separation or when the man decides to stop intercourse.

This shows the importance of intention (niyya) in Hanafi fiqh.

In a valid marriage, 'idda begins at divorce or death, not at separation or resolve. The fasid marriage 'idda has a broader trigger.
This difference arises because the fasid marriage is not fully valid.

Common scenario: couple separates but no divorce; man resolves to quit intercourse.

Core Idea
Ihdad is wajib for widow and irrevocably divorced woman during 'idda.

This passage from al-Quduri establishes a clear ruling: certain women must observe ihdad (mourning) during their waiting period. The text specifies who is obligated, the conditions that must be met, and what actions are forbidden. Understanding this ruling is essential for correctly applying the fiqh of 'idda.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligated persons
The passage mentions two categories: the mabtutah (irrevocably divorced woman) and the widow (whose husband died). Note that the revocably divorced woman is not included.
The term mabtutah refers specifically to a woman divorced with a ba'in divorce.
2
Note the conditions of obligation
The obligation depends on three conditions: the woman must be adult (balighah), sane (aqilah), and Muslim. If any condition is missing, ihdad is not required.
3
Define the acts of ihdad
The passage defines ihdad as leaving (i.e., refraining from) perfume, adornment, kohl, and oil. These are specific forms of beautification that must be avoided.
Adornment includes things like wearing jewelry, colorful clothes, or applying makeup.
4
Recognize the exception
The exception 'except for necessity' (illa min 'udhr) allows a woman to use these items if there is a genuine need, such as medical treatment for the eyes using kohl.
Necessity is judged by the 'urf or medical opinion.
5
Determine the ruling's basis
Although not quoted here, the ruling is based on sound hadith, e.g., the Prophet's command to the widow to avoid perfume and adornment during her 'idda.
This is why the ruling is wajib, not merely recommended.

Explicit and Indirect Proposals During 'Iddah
Presents the ruling on proposals during iddah with Quranic proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between explicit and indirect proposals during iddah and cite the Quranic evidence for the permissibility of ta'rid.

Core Idea
Explicit proposal to a woman in iddah is impermissible, but indirect hints (ta'rid) are allowed.

The waiting period is a time of sanctity; a woman should not be approached for marriage directly. However, the Quran permits alluding to the intention to marry her after the iddah ends. This lesson clarifies the distinction between explicit and indirect proposals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on proposals
The text states: 'وَلَا يَنْبَغِي أَنْ تُخْطَبَ الْمُعْتَدَّةُ' (it is not proper to propose explicitly) and 'وَلَا بَأْسَ بِالتَّعْرِيضِ فِي الْخِطْبَةِ' (no harm in indirect proposal). Note the difference.
'لا ينبغي' in Hanafi fiqh often indicates tahrim (prohibition) or at least karahah tahrimiyyah.
2
Locate the Quranic evidence
The text cites Surah al-Baqarah 2:235 which permits ta'rid by removing sin ('لا جناح') for what you hint at or conceal in your hearts.
The verse explicitly mentions 'ta'rid' and 'aknantum' (concealed intention), showing that only indirect expression is allowed.
3
Understand the difference between explicit and indirect
Explicit proposal (khatbah) uses clear language like 'I want to marry you.' Indirect proposal (ta'rid) uses hints like 'I see no one better than you' or 'I desire a woman like you.'
The Hanafi school uses the verse's implication: permission for ta'rid implies prohibition for explicit proposals (mafhum al-mukhalafah).
4
Apply to different types of iddah
The ruling applies to all women in iddah (divorced or widowed). However, for a revocably divorced woman (raj'iyyah), explicit proposal is even more restricted because her husband can take her back.
The text does not differentiate, but other sources make this distinction.

Residence During 'Iddah: Staying Home and Exceptions
Presents Hanafi rulings on residence during iddah with Quranic and hadith proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the residence rules for divorced women and widows during iddah, and identify when leaving the home is permitted.

Core Idea
A divorced woman must stay home day and night; a widow may go out by day but must return home at night.

Where a woman spends her iddah is a critical legal issue. The general rule is that she must remain in the marital home. But the text makes a distinction between divorce and widowhood, and allows leaving for necessity. This lesson examines the evidence from Quran, hadith, and companion statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The text distinguishes between a revocably or irrevocably divorced woman (al-mutallaqah al-raj'iyyah wa al-mabtutah) and a widow (al-mutawaffa 'anha zawjuha). Note they have different rules.
Divorced woman: cannot leave at all. Widow: can go out by day but not stay overnight.
2
Examine the Quranic evidence
Surah al-Talaq 65:1 commands 'do not turn them out of their houses, nor shall they leave, except if they commit clear immorality.' This establishes the prohibition of leaving for divorced women.
The phrase 'لا يخرجن' (they shall not leave) is a strong prohibition.
3
Examine the evidence for widows
Surah al-Baqarah 2:234 specifies the waiting period but not residence. The hadith of Zaynab bint Ka'b commands her to 'stay in your house' and the statement of Ibn 'Umar says widows should not spend the night elsewhere.
The widow's permission to go out by day is derived from the fact that she may need to earn a living or attend to affairs.
4
Note the exceptions
The text says 'except due to an excuse' (إلا من عذر). Both categories may leave for genuine necessity, such as fear for life, fire, or the need to relocate when the house is not suitable.
The excuse must be legitimate; frivolous outings are not allowed.

Core Idea
A bain woman's remarriage during iddah is valid; the dispute is whether the second divorce resets iddah and mahr.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the consequences of a second divorce before consummation when a woman in bain iddah remarries her former husband. The key issue is whether the second marriage creates new obligations for full mahr and a fresh iddah, or whether the woman simply completes her original iddah and receives half mahr. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi approach to valid but anomalous marriages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The case is a bain divorced woman who remarries her ex-husband during her iddah, then is divorced again before consummation. Determine the legal status of the second marriage: it is valid but at risk of different rulings.
The marriage is valid because she is a bain woman; she can remarry after her divorce subject to iddah completion, but here she remarries during iddah, which is permissible with conditions.

Imam Muhammad: half mahr + complete the first iddah. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: full mahr + a new full iddah. Clearly articulate each.

Imam Muhammad's evidence: a tabi'i statement (al-Hasan) that half mahr and no new iddah are due. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rely on the principle that a valid new marriage creates full mahr and a new iddah (by qiyas on a normal marriage).
Both evidences are zanni in thubut and dalalah.

Analyze the underlying usul principles
Imam Muhammad prioritizes transmitted precedent (athar). The two shaykhs prioritize general qiyas (and possibly istihsan) – a valid marriage triggers full rights. Determine which principle is stronger according to the school.
The text indicates the school accepts the two shaykhs' view as mufta bihi.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical effect
The relied-upon view is full mahr and new iddah. The thamarah al-khilaf: in practice, the woman gets the full agreed mahr and begins a new iddah from the second divorce.
This dispute only arises if the second marriage is valid; if invalid, different rules apply.

(2) The statement of the companion for proof: 'The woman's pregnancy does not exceed two years, nor even the amount of the movement of the shadow of a spindle stick.' (3) The statement of the companion for proof that if she gives birth at six months it is not established: that Umar was brought a woman who gave birth at six months and intended to stone her. Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with its commentary including hadith proofs, covers the rules for establishing the lineage (nasab) of a child born to a divorced or widowed woman. The key principle is the hadith 'al-walad li'l-firash' — the child belongs to the marriage bed. For a revocably divorced woman (raj'iyyah), if she gives birth within (or, according to the text, even after) two years from the divorce, the child is attributed to the husband, provided she does not admit that her iddah has expired. If she gives birth before two years, she becomes bain (irrevocably divorced) because the iddah ends with delivery. If she gives birth after two years, lineage is established but the woman becomes revocable (i.e., the husband can take her back) because it indicates the iddah is not yet complete. For the irrevocably divorced (battutah), lineage is only established if she gives birth within two years; at the completion of two years, it is not established unless the man claims the child. For a widow, lineage is established within two years of the husband's death. An important nuance: if the woman acknowledges the end of her iddah and then gives birth within six months, lineage is established (since the minimum pregnancy is six months, meaning conception occurred during the iddah). But if she gives birth at six months exactly, it is not established because the child could have been conceived after the iddah ended. The proof for the two-year maximum pregnancy is the statement of Aisha (ra), and the proof for the minimum six months is the incident with Umar and Ali, who combined the verses of two years breastfeeding and thirty months total gestation and weaning. The Urdu usul at the end summarizes: as long as the woman does not admit the iddah has ended, the husband's bed (firash) remains intact for lineage purposes.

Commentary
This passage is a section (fasl) from the Quduri matn on the law of custody (hadanah) when parents separate. The ruling establishes a hierarchy of who is most entitled to the physical custody of a young child. The mother is first, but her right is lost if she marries another man. Then the maternal grandmother (umm al-umm) takes precedence over the paternal grandmother (umm al-ab). If no grandmother exists, then sisters are given preference over aunts, with full sisters (from both parents) prioritized over maternal half-sisters, then paternal half-sisters. The proofs are three: (1) The famous hadith from the woman who described her motherly care, where the Prophet (ﷺ) ruled that she is more entitled as long as she does not remarry. (2) A narration from al-Bara' where the Prophet (ﷺ) gave custody to the maternal aunt, equating her to the mother. (3) The practice of Abu Bakr (رضي الله عنه) in the case of 'Umar's divorce, where the grandmother was given custody. The Urdu principle at the bottom states: 'A minor child's upbringing: first the mother and maternal grandmother are most entitled; in their absence, the paternal grandmother is entitled.'

Custody Rights: Order of Entitlement and Conditions
Ruling with multiple proofs from hadith and athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi order of custody entitlement among females and male 'asabah, and identify the conditions that affect the right (remarriage, grandmother exception, child's independence).

Core Idea
Custody passes in a fixed order among female relatives; remarriage forfeits it unless the woman is the grandmother married to the grandfather.

This passage from Al-Quduri's matn, with commentary, lays out the Hanafi hierarchy for who is entitled to physically care for a minor child after parental separation. The ruling is supported by multiple proofs from the sunnah and practice of the Companions. You will learn not only the order but also the critical conditions that can shift the right from one relative to another.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational rule
Locate the statement that the mother is most entitled as long as she does not marry. This is the default right and its primary condition.
The mother's right is conditional; her remarriage to a non-mahram transfers the right.
2
Trace the female hierarchy
Follow the order given: mother → maternal grandmother → paternal grandmother → full sister → maternal sister → paternal sister → maternal aunt → paternal aunt. Note which relatives are preferred over others.
The maternal grandmother is preferred over the paternal grandmother because she is closer to the mother.
3
Recognize the remarriage exception
Note that all women lose their right upon remarrying a stranger, except the grandmother if she marries the child's paternal grandfather. This is a key exception.
Marrying a mahram (like the grandfather) does not forfeit the right because the child remains within the family.
4
Examine the male 'asabah order
When no eligible female relative exists, the closest male 'asabah (agnate relative) is entitled, following the inheritance order.
This is based on the principle that custody is a right of the 'asabah after the maternal line.
5
Determine the duration of custody
The mother and grandmother retain custody until the child can independently perform self-care tasks: eating, drinking, dressing, and cleaning after using the toilet. This is typically around age seven.
The hadith about commanding prayer at seven is used as evidence for this age criterion.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the rules of custody (hadanah) from the Hanafi school, presented by the commentator as an explanation of Al-Quduri's matn. The passage opens with the foundational ruling: when separation occurs between parents, the mother is most entitled to the custody of a minor child, provided she does not remarry. If she remarries, her right is forfeited, and the right passes to her mother (maternal grandmother), then to the paternal grandmother, then to full sisters (from both parents), then maternal sisters, then paternal sisters, then maternal aunts, then paternal aunts. This order is based on the principle that women are preferred for custody due to their nurturing nature. The commentator then clarifies an exception: if a grandmother marries the child's own paternal grandfather (i.e., her husband is the child's father's father), her right remains intact because she is still within the family. If there are no eligible women, then the male relatives ('asabah) compete for custody, with the closest in 'asabah winning (following the same order as inheritance). The mother and grandmother's custody lasts until the child can independently perform basic self-care tasks: eating, drinking, dressing, and cleaning himself after using the toilet. This age is typically around 7 years for boys and 9 for girls in Hanafi fiqh, but the criterion here is functional independence, not a fixed age. The proofs include the well-known hadith: 'You are more entitled to him as long as you do not marry' (Sunan Abi Dawud), and the ruling of Abu Bakr for the grandmother when the mother had remarried. The principle of 'khalah (maternal aunt) is in the position of the mother' establishes the precedence of maternal aunts over paternal aunts. The passage also cites al-Dahhak's interpretation of the Quranic verse (2:233) to support the 'asabah's obligation when there is no female relative. Finally, the usul note reiterates: if a woman entitled to custody marries a stranger (ajnabi), her right falls. This is a Zahir al-Riwayah ruling and is consistently upheld in the Hanafi school.

حَضَانَة (hadanah): The right and duty to physically care for and raise a minor child, including custody of the child's person.
العَصَبَة (al-'asabah): Male agnatic relatives who inherit residually; in custody, the closest male 'asabah gets the right when no eligible woman exists.
خَالَة (khalah): Maternal aunt; she is given precedence over paternal aunt in custody because she is considered in the position of the mother.

The Divorced Mother and Relocation of the Child: A Hanafi Ruling
Ruling on divorced mother relocating with child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a divorced mother moving with her child out of the city, identify the sole exception, and explain the Quranic evidence used.

Core Idea
A divorced mother cannot move the child out of the city unless returning to her pre-marriage hometown.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Al-Quduri's matn that balances the mother's custodial rights with the father's right of access. The passage presents a clear prohibition with one exception, grounded in the Quranic principle of avoiding harm. Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to the condition that allows relocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a divorced mother taking her child out of the city where they currently reside. The ruling is that she does not have that right (laysa laha dhalik), i.e., it is prohibited (haram) without the exception.
The wording 'laysa laha dhalik' indicates a strong prohibition in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the exception and its condition
The exception is when she takes the child to her 'watan' (hometown), but only if the husband married her in that hometown. The condition is that the destination must be the place where the marriage contract took place and which she considers her original home.
The watan is defined as the place where the wife lived before marriage and where the husband married her.
3
Examine the Quranic evidence
The author cites Quran 2:233: 'And a mother should not be harmed through her child, nor a father through his child.' This verse establishes the principle that neither parent may use the child to harm the other. Moving the child far away would harm the father's right to visitation and nurture the child.
The verse is qati al-thubut and qati al-dalala in this context.
4
Understand the wisdom (hikma)
The ruling protects the father's right to maintain a relationship with his child and prevents disruption to the child's stability. The exception for returning to the watan acknowledges the mother's attachment to her own home and family, as long as it does not unduly harm the father's access.
This is a classic Hanafi balancing of interests: the child's welfare and both parents' rights.

Commentary
This passage contains a ruling from Al-Quduri's matn on the divorced mother's right to relocate with her child. The ruling states that she cannot move the child out of the city (the city of residence after divorce) unless she is returning to her own hometown—the place where the husband originally married her. The harm principle from the Quran (Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:233) is cited: neither parent should cause harm to the other through the child. Moving the child far away could harm the father's visitation rights. The second citation is the same ayah, reiterating the father's obligation to provide maintenance. In Hanafi fiqh, this ruling protects the father's right to see his child and ensures the child's stability.

Key Terms
وَطَن (waṭan): The pre-marriage hometown of the wife, where the husband married her.

Ruling on Coercion to Divorce or Free a Slave
Ruling with proofs and cross-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced divorce and freeing, identify the opposing Shafi'i position, and apply the compensation rule.

Core Idea
Coerced divorce and freeing are valid in Hanafi school; the coercer must compensate.

This lesson addresses a classic inter-madhhab dispute: is a divorce or manumission performed under duress valid? The Hanafi school holds that such acts are effective upon utterance, while the Shafi'i school considers them void. We will examine the proofs from both sides and the practical consequences, including the compensation owed by the coercer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the Hanafi position: coerced divorce and freeing are valid and take effect, and the coercer must pay compensation (half mahr if before consummation, full value of slave).
This is the core of the passage.
2
Examine the Hanafi Proof
Read the hadith: 'Three things are serious in earnest and in jest: marriage, divorce, and revocation.' Understand how it shows that intent is irrelevant for these acts.
Also note the statement of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i as supporting evidence.
3
Examine the Shafi'i Proof
Read the hadith: 'No divorce and no freeing in a locked state' and the hadith: 'Allah has forgiven my nation for error, forgetfulness, and what they are coerced into.' Understand how these seem to invalidate coerced acts.
The passage presents these as the opposing school's evidence.
4
Note the Practical Consequence
Under Hanafi law, the divorce/freeing is valid, so the coercer must compensate. Under Shafi'i law, the act is invalid, so no compensation is due. This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
The compensation rules are detailed: half mahr if before consummation, full slave value.
5
Apply to a Scenario
Given a case where a man is threatened into divorcing his wife before consummation, state the Hanafi ruling and the compensation.
Use the template from the practice tasks.

And if he is coerced to divorce his wife or free his slave, and he does that, then what he was coerced to do takes effect. And he recovers from the one who coerced him the value of the slave, and he recovers half the dowry of the woman if it was before consummation. Proof: (1) the hadith for establishing that if he is coerced to divorce his wife: from Abu Hurairah that the Prophet (ﷺ) said: 'Three things are serious in earnest and serious in jest: marriage, divorce, and revocation.' (Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi). Proof (2) the statement of the Tabi'i for establishing that if he is coerced to divorce his wife: from Ibrahim that he said: 'It is permissible; it is only something by which he ransomed himself.' (Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah, Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq). Proof: (1) the proof of al-Shafi'i, the hadith for establishing that if he is coerced to divorce his wife: I heard Aisha say: I heard the Prophet (ﷺ) say: 'No divorce and no freeing in a locked state.' (Abu Dawud, Ibn Majah). Proof: (2) the hadith for establishing that if he is coerced to divorce his wife: from Abu Dharr al-Ghifari that the Prophet (ﷺ) said: 'Allah has forgiven my nation for error, forgetfulness, and what they are coerced into.' (Ibn Majah, Bukhari). Proof: (1) the hadith for establishing that he recovers from the one who coerced him the value of the slave: I heard Abu Hurairah say: 'The free commander is killed, and the slave is not killed.' (Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq). Proof: (2) the hadith for establishing that he recovers from the one who coerced him the value of the slave: from Abdullah ibn Umar that the Prophet (ﷺ) said: 'Whoever frees a share he has in a slave, and he has wealth that reaches the value of the slave, the slave is valued at a fair price, then he gives his partners their shares, and the slave is freed. Otherwise, only what has been freed is freed.' (Bukhari). Principle: Marriage, divorce, revocation, and freeing, whatever the situation, when uttered, take effect. Principle: Once he has consummated with his wife, he has taken possession of the vulva, and from taking possession of the vulva, the mahr becomes obligatory.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling that if a person is forced to divorce his wife or free his slave, the act is valid and takes effect, contrary to the Shafi'i school which holds that coerced divorce is invalid. The text then provides Hanafi proofs: the hadith 'Three things are serious in earnest and serious in jest: marriage, divorce, and revocation' – indicating that these acts are effective regardless of intent; and the statement of Ibrahim al-Nakha'i that it is permissible because the person ransomed himself. Then the Shafi'i proofs: the hadith 'No divorce and no freeing in a locked state' and the hadith 'Allah has forgiven my nation for error, forgetfulness, and what they are coerced into.' The text then gives proofs for the compensation part: the hadith of Abu Hurairah regarding the free commander (used analogically to show the coercer pays value) and the hadith of Ibn Umar about freeing a share. The Urdu principles state that marriage, divorce, and freeing take effect as soon as uttered, and that the mahr becomes obligatory upon consummation. This clarifies the basis for the compensation rules.

Key Terms
إكراه (ikrah): Compelling someone to do an act against their will under threat
طلاق (talaq): Divorce; dissolution of marriage by the husband's pronouncement
عتق (itq): Freeing a slave
