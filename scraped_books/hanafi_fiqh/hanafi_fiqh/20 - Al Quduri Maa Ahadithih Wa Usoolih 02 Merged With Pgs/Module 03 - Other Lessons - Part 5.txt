Bequest of a Third of Clothes When Part Is Destroyed
Ruling on bequest of a third of clothes when two-thirds are destroyed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on calculating a bequest of a fraction of a specific asset when part of that asset is destroyed, and apply it to similar cases.

Core Idea
A bequest of a fraction of a specific item gives the legatee that fraction of what remains, not the entire remaining item.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in bequests (wasiyya) where the testator bequeaths a fraction of a specific category of property, such as one-third of his clothes. If a portion of those clothes is destroyed before distribution, the legatee's share is proportionally reduced. The ruling clarifies that the legatee is entitled to one-third of the remaining clothes, not all of them, and this share is taken from the one-third of the total estate permissible for bequests. The proof comes from the Tabi'i Ata, who established the principle of proportional sharing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a bequest of one-third of clothes. The ruling: if two-thirds of the clothes are destroyed, the legatee is entitled only to one-third of the remaining clothes.
The remaining clothes are one-third of the original, so the legatee gets one-ninth of the original.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the statement of the Tabi'i Ata, as recorded in Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah. He was asked about a man who bequeathed one-third of his property and left a house worth exactly one-third of the estate: should the legatee get the entire house? Ata said no, the legatee gets a share of both the house and the other property.
Ata's principle is that a fractional bequest entitles the legatee to a proportionate share of each asset, not the entire asset.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Ata's statement implies that a bequest of a fraction of the estate applies proportionally to each component. By analogy, a bequest of a fraction of a specific asset applies to that asset proportionally, taking into account any destruction.
The 'illa (effective cause) is the proportional allocation of bequests to avoid unfairness to heirs.
4
Apply the principle to the case
Here, the clothes are a specific asset. The bequest is of one-third of the clothes. Since two-thirds are destroyed, only one-third remains. The legatee gets one-third of that remaining portion, i.e., one-third of one-third = one-ninth of the original clothes.
This share is then taken from the one-third of the total estate allowed for bequests.
5
Contrast with possible misunderstanding
One might think the legatee should take all remaining clothes because the bequest was one-third and only one-third remains. However, the fraction is applied to the asset as it exists at death, not to the original. So the legatee gets only one-third of the remaining one-third.
This ensures consistency: the legatee always gets exactly the fraction bequeathed of the asset as it stands.

Bequest from Mixed Estate: Cash and Debt
Ruling on bequest of a fixed sum when the estate includes both cash and debts receivable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for paying a fixed bequest when the estate consists of both cash and debts receivable, determining the immediate and deferred portions.

Core Idea
If cash covers the bequest from its one-third, pay fully; otherwise, pay one-third of cash immediately and share debt collections.

When a testator leaves both cash ('ayn) and debts (dayn), the executor must distribute a bequest proportionally. The Hanafi school provides a clear method: first check if the cash alone can satisfy the bequest from the one-third share allowed. If not, the legatee gets an immediate partial payment from cash, and later receives a share of each debt as it is collected. This ruling balances the rights of the legatee with the need to collect debts over time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the asset categories
Note the two types of assets: 'māl 'ayn' (tangible cash/property) and 'dayn' (debts receivable). The ruling applies only when both exist.
If only one type, the rule simplifies.
2
Check the condition on cash
Determine if the bequest amount (e.g., 1000 dirhams) can be fully paid from one-third of the cash. If yes, the entire bequest is paid immediately from cash.
One-third of cash = cash / 3.
3
Apply the partial payment rule
If cash is insufficient, pay one-third of the cash immediately. Then, as each debt is collected, pay one-third of that collection to the legatee until the bequest is fully satisfied.
The legatee gets a share of each debt payment proportionally.
4
Work through an example
Test with numbers: bequest 1000, cash 600, debt 2400. One-third of cash = 200 (insufficient). So legatee gets 200 now. When debt collected, e.g., 1200 collected, he gets 400 additional, etc.
Total collected from debts must sum to 800 to complete the bequest.

Bequest to an Unborn Child (Haml)
Ruling on bequest to unborn child with condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bequests to an unborn child and explain the condition that the child must be born within six months of the bequest.

Core Idea
A bequest to an unborn child is valid only if the child is born within six months of the bequest.

This lesson addresses a specific mu'amala ruling: bequests to or by an unborn child. The Hanafi school permits such bequests under a strict condition that ensures the child existed at the time the bequest was made. Understanding this condition is essential for drafting valid wills that include unborn beneficiaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a bequest (wasiyya) made to an unborn child (haml). The Hanafi ruling is that it is valid (jā'iz), but subject to a condition.
The text uses 'تجوز' indicating permissibility/jawāz.
2
Identify the condition
The condition is 'if it (the child) is delivered less than six months from the day of the bequest.' This restricts validity to a clear timeframe.
The six-month period is the minimum normal gestational age in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the legal basis
The six-month limit ensures that the child was conceived (i.e., in existence) at the time of the bequest. If born after six months, it could be from a later conception, making the bequest invalid due to uncertainty (gharar).
This is derived from the Qur'ānic guidance on gestation (Sūrat al-Aḥqāf 46:15 and Sūrat Luqmān 31:14) interpreted by the Hanafīs to set a minimum of six months for viability.
4
Apply the condition
If the child is born less than six months from the bequest date, the bequest is effective. If born at six months or later, or if stillborn, the bequest is invalid.
The counting begins from the day the bequest is made, not from the testator's death.
5
Note the cross-madhhab disagreement
Other schools (e.g., Shāfiʿī) may require nine months or allow the child to be specifically named. The Ḥanafī position is based on textual evidence and qiyās.
This ikhtilāf has practical effects in estate planning across different jurisdictions.

Bequest Wording: 'Walad' vs 'Warathah'
Two rules on bequest wording with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the distribution rule when a bequest uses 'walad' (child) versus 'warathah' (heirs) and cite the evidence from Quran and hadith.

Core Idea
Terminology dictates distribution: 'walad' gives equal shares; 'warathah' gives male twice female.

In Islamic inheritance planning, the words chosen in a bequest determine how the gift is divided. This lesson examines two common phrasings: bequeathing to 'the child of so-and-so' versus 'the heirs of so-and-so'. Each triggers a different distribution rule, supported by clear proofs from the Quran and the practice of the Salaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the first two sentences: one on bequest to 'walad' (child) and one on 'warathah' (heirs). Note the different outcomes.
The key difference is the distribution ratio.
2
Analyse the term 'walad'
Recognise that 'walad' in Arabic is gender-neutral when used absolutely. The Hanafi school applies this by dividing the bequest equally among male and female children.
This ruling is based on the generality of the term.
3
Analyse the term 'warathah'
The term 'warathah' (heirs) evokes the inheritance system of the Quran (4:11), which gives the male twice the share of the female. Hence the bequest follows that ratio.
This is consistent with the principle that bequests to heirs follow inheritance rules.
4
Examine the proofs
The passage cites statements from al-Hasan al-Basri and Quran 4:12. Understand how these proofs support each ruling.
Al-Hasan's statement confirms equal distribution for 'sons' (walad); Quran 4:11 (implicitly) supports the heir ratio.
