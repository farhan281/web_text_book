Purchasing Relatives and Freeing Half a Slave for Kaffarah
Rulings with hadith proofs on purchasing relatives and freeing half a slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on purchasing a parent or child for kaffarah and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding freeing half of a jointly-owned slave, including the condition of completing before intercourse.

Core Idea
Purchasing a relative for kaffarah is valid; freeing half a slave requires full completion before intercourse.

This lesson covers two specific scenarios related to kaffarah of zihar. First, the permissibility of purchasing one's father or son with the intention of freeing them to fulfill the expiation. Second, the detailed intra-Hanafi debate on whether freeing half of a jointly-owned slave, along with a guarantee to free the other half, suffices for kaffarah. We will examine the hadith proofs and the differing views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani).

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
Two distinct rulings are presented: (1) purchasing a father or son for kaffarah is permissible; (2) freeing half of a jointly-owned slave for kaffarah is invalid according to Abu Hanifa. Note the conditions for each.
Pay attention to the role of intention (niyya) in the first case and the requirement of full ownership in the second.
2
Examine the proofs for purchasing relatives
Two hadiths are cited: from Abu Huraira (the child can only repay a parent by purchasing and freeing him) and from Samura (owning a mahram relative frees him automatically). Analyze how these support the Hanafi position.
The hadith from Abu Huraira establishes an exception to the general inability to repay a parent.
3
Analyze the case of freeing half a jointly-owned slave
Understand the scenario: the slave is owned jointly, the person frees his half, guarantees the other half's value, then frees the remaining half. Determine why this is problematic for Abu Hanifa.
The key is that at the moment of freeing the first half, the entire slave is not fully owned by the person.
4
Trace the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa holds the freeing is invalid for kaffarah. The Sahibayn argue it is valid based on the hadith 'Whoever frees a share...' which they interpret as making the whole slave free immediately. Compare the evidences.
The conflicting interpretations hinge on whether the hadith implies immediate full emancipation or requires completion.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: according to Abu Hanifa, if one has intercourse before completing the freeing, the kaffarah is invalid. According to the Sahibayn, it remains valid as long as the intention and guarantee are present. Note the importance of order.
The requirement that kaffarah be completed before intercourse is a key principle in zihar expiation.

Commentary
This passage continues the chapter on kaffarah of zihar, presenting several specific cases with proofs. First: If a man buys his own father or son with the intention of freeing them for his kaffarah, it is permissible according to the Hanafis. This is supported by two hadith: one from Abu Huraira stating that a child can only repay a parent by finding them enslaved, purchasing, and freeing them; and another from Samura stating that owning a mahram relative results in automatic freedom. The purchase is thus a means to fulfill the kaffarah. Second: If a man frees half of a slave that is jointly owned with another person, with the intention of it counting for kaffarah, and he guarantees to pay the value of the other half and then frees that half, this is not acceptable according to Abu Hanifa. Why? Because at the moment of freeing the first half, the slave was not fully owned by him, so the kaffarah is not complete until the entire slave is free, but the partial freeing cannot count because the kaffarah requires full ownership at the time of the act. However, if he owns the entire slave and frees half, then later frees the other half, both from the same kaffarah intention, it is acceptable, provided the second freeing occurs before intercourse. But if he frees half, then has intercourse with his wife (which requires the kaffarah to be completed first), then frees the other half, the kaffarah is invalid according to Abu Hanifa because the act of intercourse before completing the freeing nullifies the partial expiation. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they hold that freeing half a jointly-owned slave with guarantee of the other half is valid for kaffarah, based on the same hadith from Abu Huraira about freeing a share, which they interpret as making the entire slave free through the first person's action. The hadith proofs from Ibn Umar and Abu Huraira are cited to support the general rule. The Urdu summary clarifies that if one frees a slave in two installments, the kaffarah is valid as long as it is completed before intercourse.

Key Terms
مشترك (mushtarak): Jointly-owned; in this context, a slave owned by two or more people. Freeing one's share does not fully free the slave unless the other shares are also freed.
ضمن القيمة (damana al-qima): To guarantee the value of the remaining share of a jointly-owned slave, i.e., to assume liability to pay the other owner(s) their portion so that the slave becomes fully freed.
جامع (jama'a): To have marital intercourse. In this context, it nullifies the partial expiation if done before completing the freeing.

Definition and Conditions of Kitabah Contract
Opening rulings on the contract of kitabah and its validity conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of kitabah, identify its conditions (payment terms, minor's capacity), and explain the legal effect (master's control vs ownership).

Core Idea
Kitabah is a binding contract: the slave gains freedom of movement but remains owned until the full price is paid.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn opens the chapter on the mukatab slave. It defines the kitabah contract, its essential conditions, and its immediate legal effect. The proofs from Quran and Sunnah are cited to establish its legitimacy. Understanding these fundamentals is crucial before exploring the detailed rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the kitabah contract between master and slave. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) and recommended when the slave has good qualities.
The ruling is not obligatory; it is conditioned on the master's judgment of khayr (good).
2
List the conditions for validity
The master stipulates a sum, the slave accepts, payment may be immediate, deferred, or in installments. If the slave is a minor, he must understand buying and selling.
The minor's understanding is key; the hadith of the lifted pen is cited as proof.
3
Identify the legal effect
After valid kitabah, the mukatab leaves the master's hand (control) but does not leave his ownership (milkiyyah).
The slave gains freedom to work and travel but remains owned until full payment.
4
Examine the proofs
Quran 24:33 commands kitabah if good is seen; hadith of Barirah shows practice with deferred payment; hadith about lifted pen shows minor's capacity.
The hadith of Barirah also implies permissibility of lump-sum or installment payment.
5
Note the Hanafi position
Kitabah is a binding contract once valid; the minor's capacity is based on transactional understanding, not age alone.
This view uses istihsan based on the hadith of the lifted pen.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on mukatab (kitabah contract) from al-Quduri's matn, followed by proofs. The ruling: a master may contract kitabah with his slave (male or female) for a stipulated sum. The slave must accept, becoming a mukatab. The payment may be immediate, deferred, or in installments (munajjaman). Even a minor slave who can comprehend trade can be a mukatab. After a valid contract, the mukatab gains freedom of movement (leaves the master's hand) but remains in ownership (milkiyyah) until full payment. The proofs: Quran 24:33 commands kitabah upon request if good is seen in the slave; the hadith of Barirah shows kitabah in practice; the hadith about the pen being lifted explains that a minor who understands transactions can contract. The Urdu principles summarize: kitabah is an agreement to earn freedom by paying a sum; after valid kitabah, the mukatab is free to travel and trade.

Key Terms
مكاتبة (mukātabah): Contract between master and slave for the slave's freedom in exchange for a stipulated payment.
منجماً (munajjaman): Payment in installments over time.
صغير يعقل البيع (saghīr yaʿqil al-bayʿ): A minor who has the intellectual capacity to understand buying and selling transactions.

Core Idea
The mukatab gains financial autonomy but remains a slave until full payment; marriage and guarantees require permission.

This passage from Al-Quduri clarifies the rights and restrictions of a mukatab (slave on a contract of manumission). Once the kitabah is valid, the mukatab can conduct business and travel to earn money for his freedom, but he cannot marry, give charity beyond a small amount, or act as a guarantor without the master's permission. The Prophet ﷺ's hadith underscores that partial payment does not free him until the full amount is paid.

Guided Path

Identify the legal status

The hadith from Amr ibn Shu'ayb shows that even if a slave pays all but ten ounces, he remains a slave. This proves the master's ownership continues until full payment.
The hadith is used as dalil to defend the rule that partial payment does not change status.

Today, although slavery is abolished, the principles of restricted autonomy and permission apply analogously to similar contracts (e.g., installment purchase with work). However, the specific rules remain classical.
Modern application is limited, but the legal reasoning is important for understanding Hanafi contract theory.

Core Idea
Khamr is only from grapes; other intoxicants are haram but not khamr.

This passage from the Kitab al-Ashribah defines the prohibited drinks, focusing on khamr (wine). The Hanafi school defines khamr strictly as grape juice that ferments. Understanding this dispute is crucial for knowing which drinks carry the hadd punishment versus ta'zir.

The act is drinking khamr. The ruling is haram. Also, other intoxicants are haram but not classified as khamr.

The proof includes Quran 5:90, hadith 'from grapes is khamr, and I forbid every intoxicant', and Ibn Abbas's statement distinguishing khamr from other intoxicants.

Ruling on Cooked Grape Juice: The Two-Thirds Condition
States the Hanafi ruling on grape juice reduced to one-third and cites a Companion's practice as proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cooked grape juice and identify the condition that determines its permissibility.

Core Idea
Cooked grape juice is halal only when two-thirds is removed; less cooking does not remove prohibition.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from al-Quduri's matn on food and drink. The passage establishes that grape juice, a potential intoxicant, becomes permissible if cooked sufficiently to remove two-thirds of its volume. The proof comes from the practice of Caliph 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, as reported by Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib. Understanding this condition is crucial to avoid confusion between permissible and prohibited drinks.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the first line: 'And grape juice, when cooked until two-thirds goes and one-third remains, is permissible.' Note the condition attached to the ruling.
The act is drinking cooked grape juice, and the hukm is halal (mubah).
2
Locate the condition
The permissibility depends on the degree of cooking: exactly two-thirds must evaporate. Less than that leaves the juice prohibited.
The Urdu principle restates: if two-thirds boiled away, halal; otherwise, not.
3
Identify the dalil
The proof is a saying (qawl) of the Companion 'Umar, transmitted by Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib. It describes the drink 'tila'' as that which has been cooked until two-thirds is gone.
Referenced from Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba (no. 23988).
4
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The dalil explicitly ties permission to the condition of two-thirds evaporation. 'Umar's sanction of this specific drink indicates that the ruling is based on this physical transformation.
The Hanafi school accepts qawl sahabi as a proof when not contradicted.
5
Apply the precautionary principle
The Urdu passage adds: when permissibility and prohibition conflict, give precedence to prohibition. Thus, if the cooking level is uncertain, the juice remains haram.
This reflects the Hanafi maxim: al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk.

Ruling on Making Nabidh in Specific Vessels
States permissibility of making nabidh in certain vessels after abrogation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on making nabidh in the previously prohibited vessels and cite the evidence that the prohibition was abrogated.

Core Idea
Vessels do not make drinks halal or haram; only intoxication is prohibited.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a well-known issue in Hanafi fiqh: the use of certain vessels for preparing nabidh (a non-intoxicating infused drink). The Prophet ﷺ initially forbade specific containers, but later abrogated that ruling. The matn states clearly that there is no harm in using these vessels, and the attached proofs demonstrate the basis for this permissibility. Understanding this abrogation is key to applying the correct ruling today.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial prohibition
Recognise that the Prophet ﷺ once forbade making nabidh in dubba', hantam, muzaffat, and naqir. This is recorded in multiple hadith collections.
This was a precautionary measure to prevent intoxication, as these vessels could speed up fermentation.
2
Understand the abrogation (naskh)
The later narrations show that the Prophet ﷺ allowed these vessels after the Companions expressed need. The prohibition was abrogated, and only intoxication itself remains haram.
Abrogation is established by the later hadith, and the Hanafi school follows the final ruling.
3
Analyse the three hadith proofs
Evaluate each of the three proofs cited: (1) Ibn Abbas hadith showing prohibition but later abrogation, (2) Jabir hadith where the Prophet allowed due to need, and (3) Ibn Burayda's explicit statement that vessels do not render anything halal or haram.
The common thread: the cause of haram is intoxication, not the vessel.
4
Conclude the ruling
Apply the Hanafi position: making nabidh in any vessel is permissible (mubah) as long as the drink does not become intoxicating. The earlier prohibition is abrogated.
This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the Hanafi school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wine that turns to vinegar, identify the proofs, and explain the difference with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Wine that turns into vinegar becomes pure and permissible, even if intentionally made.

This lesson examines a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: what happens to the ruling and purity of wine when it transforms into vinegar? The Hanafi school permits its use and even the act of making it, while Imam al-Shafi'i prohibits intentional transformation. We will explore the proofs each side relies upon and understand the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
First, identify the exact Hanafi ruling: wine that turns to vinegar is halal and pure, whether the change happens naturally or by adding something, and it is not disliked to intentionally make it into vinegar.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opposing school's view
Next, understand the Shafi'i position: intentionally making vinegar from wine is forbidden (haram); only natural transformation is acceptable. This is based on a hadith from Anas.
The hadith is found in Muslim and Abu Dawood.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the hadith: Anas reported that the Prophet was asked about making vinegar from wine and replied 'No' (Muslim, no. 1983). The Shafi'is use this as a prohibition.
The hadith is sahih, but its interpretation is disputed.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Now study the Hanafi rebuttal: they argue the hadith of Anas is abrogated or applies to a specific context. They rely on the practice of Aisha (who permitted it) and the action of 'Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz (who ordered wine to be made into vinegar), and the general hadith praising vinegar as a condiment.
The Hanafis also consider the principle that transformation changes the ruling.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Finally, note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference): Hanafis consider vinegar from wine halal and pure, so it can be used in food and the container becomes pure. Shafi'is consider it impure and haram if intentionally made, so they must avoid it.

Commentary
This passage deals with the status of wine that turns into vinegar. The ruling states that if wine changes into vinegar, it becomes permissible to consume and use, whether the change occurs naturally or by adding something (like salt or onions). Recommended Slaughter Methods: Dhabh and Nahr
Explains mustahabb slaughter methods for domestic animals with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the recommended (mustahabb) method of slaughter for cattle/sheep and camels, and explain the ruling (jaa'iz makruh) when the alternative method is used.

Core Idea
For cattle and sheep, dhabh is mustahabb; for camels, nahr is mustahabb; the opposite is valid but disliked.

In this passage from the Book of Hunting and Slaughterings, al-Quduri presents the Hanafi ruling on the preferred method of slaughter for common livestock. He distinguishes between dhabh (throat-cutting) and nahr (neck-stabbing) and provides a wealth of textual evidence. Understanding this ruling is essential for performing slaughter in accordance with the Sunnah while knowing that deviations are still permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the recommended method for each animal
State that for cattle and sheep, the recommended (mustahabb) method is dhabh; for camels, it is nahr.
This is the core ruling of the passage.
2
Note the ruling for the alternative method
If one performs nahr on cattle/sheep or dhabh on camels, the meat is permissible (jaa'iz) but disliked (makruh tanzihan).
The dislike is not sinful but leaves the optimal practice.
3
Examine the proofs for dhabh in cattle/sheep
Al-Quduri cites the hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim (command to slaughter), the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij (runaway camel analogy), the Qur'anic verse 'wanhar' (Surah al-Kawthar), the Prophet's own sacrifice of a ram, and the statement of al-Zuhri and Qatada.
These collectively establish dhabh as the sunnah for non-camel animals.
4
Examine the proofs for nahr in camels
He cites the Qur'anic reference to slaughtering a cow (Surah al-Baqarah), the hadith of Jabir about sharing a cow or camel, the Prophet's sacrifice of a ram (showing dhabh for small cattle), and 'Aishah's report of the Prophet performing nahr for his wives.
These establish nahr as the preferred method for camels.
5
Understand the Hanafi reasoning and cross-madhhab context
The Hanafi school distinguishes based on the Prophet's practice and the nature of the animals. Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may consider both methods equally valid without dislike.
The disagreement is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is the Hanafi distinction.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Hunting and Slaughterings opens with a general rule: tame game animals are slaughtered by dhabh (cutting the throat), while wild domestic animals (like a runaway camel) are killed by wounding ('aqr). Then the author, al-Quduri, states the recommended (mustahabb) method for common livestock. For cattle and sheep, the Sunnah is dhabh—cutting the throat at the upper chest. If one instead performs nahr (stabbing the hollow of the neck, which is the method for camels), the meat is permissible (halal) but disliked (makruh tanzihan). For camels, the Sunnah is nahr; if one does dhabh, it is also permissible but disliked. The author then provides extensive proofs from the Qur'an, hadith, and sayings of the Tabi'un. The Qur'anic verse 'Fasalli li rabbika wanhar' (So pray to your Lord and sacrifice) is cited as evidence for dhabh in cattle/sheep, interpreting 'wanhar' as the general command to sacrifice. However, the Hanafi understanding is that nahr is specifically for camels based on action of the Prophet. The hadith of 'Adi ibn Hatim shows that for game found alive, dhabh is required. Core Idea
A dead fetus is not halal; fanged beasts and clawed birds are haram — each ruling is supported by distinct proofs.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses two important food-related rulings. The first determines the status of a fetus found dead after its mother is slaughtered. Guided Path

Read the passage and note that it contains two separate rulings: one about a dead fetus in a slaughtered animal, and another about fanged beasts and clawed birds.

Defendant's Claims Against an Absent Person
Rulings on defendant's claims of absent ownership with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a defendant attributes property to an absent person and identify when the case is dismissed or proceeds, applying the burden of proof principle.

Core Idea
If defendant proves deposit, pawn, or usurpation by absent owner, case dismissed; if he claims purchase, he remains a litigant.

A common scenario in litigation is when the defendant says the disputed property actually belongs to a third person who is absent. The Hanafi school distinguishes between claims that transfer liability away from the defendant (like deposit) and those that assert the defendant's own ownership (like purchase). This lesson unpacks the precise conditions under which the case is dismissed or continues, supported by a Prophetic hadith and usul principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defendant's claim type
Note whether the defendant says the property is a deposit (وديعة), pawn (رهن), or that the plaintiff usurped (غصب) it from the absent owner, OR says he bought (ابتياع) it from the absent person.
Deposit/pawn/usurpation imply the defendant is not the owner; purchase implies he claims ownership.
2
Determine the procedural effect
If the defendant brings proof (بينة) for deposit/pawn/usurpation, the case is dismissed (لا خصومة). If he claims purchase, he becomes a defendant (خصيم) and the case proceeds.
The key difference: does the defendant admit the property belongs to someone else or claim it for himself?
3
Special scenario: plaintiff claims theft
If the plaintiff says the property was stolen and brings proof, while the defendant says it was deposited by an absent person and brings proof, the case is not dismissed (لم تندفع الخصومة). Both claims contradict each other, so the court cannot dismiss.
Theft and deposit are incompatible; the judge must hear both sides.
4
Link to the hadith proof
The hadith (Kindī and Ḥaḍramī) establishes that the burden of proof is on the plaintiff. The Prophet asked the Ḥaḍramī for proof. This supports the principle that when the defendant proves his claim, the plaintiff's case fails.
The hadith is cited as the basis for the rule that the defendant's proof can dismiss the case.
5
Apply the usul principles
The Urdu usul notes: (1) A person accused of usurpation becomes a defendant (خصم) because he is charged. (2) If both parties agree the property is another's, the defendant is not a criminal (i.e., not liable). These principles explain why the case is dismissed when the defendant proves the property belongs to an absent owner.
The defendant is not a true opponent (خصيم) if he is merely a custodian, not a claimant.

Claim of Paternity for Child of Sold Slave-Girl
Ruling on when seller claims paternity after slave-girl gives birth to child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules on paternity claims for a child born to a sold slave-girl, distinguish the timing conditions, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on compensation.

Core Idea
If a child is born within six months, it belongs to seller; if mother dies, Abu Hanifa returns full price, Sahibayn only child's share.

This lesson from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a common scenario in classical Islamic law: a man sells his slave-girl, she later gives birth, and the seller claims the child. The ruling hinges on the timing of birth relative to the sale, and a key intra-Hanafi dispute arises when the mother dies before the child is claimed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the time window for birth
Distinguish between birth within less than six months and birth between six months and two years. This determines the legal presumption.
Six months is the minimum pregnancy period in Hanafi fiqh; two years is the maximum.
2
Determine the legal effect on paternity and mother's status
If within six months, child is seller's, mother becomes umm walad, sale annulled for child. If between six months and two years, seller's claim requires buyer's confirmation.
The umm walad status means she becomes free upon master's death and cannot be sold.
3
Examine priority of claims
If both seller and buyer claim, seller's claim is prior when birth is within six months.
4
Handle special cases: death of child or mother
If child dies before claim, istilad not established for mother. If mother dies but child lives, lineage is established and seller takes child, but compensation dispute arises.
Istilad requires the child to be alive at time of claim for mother to become umm walad.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on compensation
Abu Hanifa returns entire price; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad return only child's share. Understand the reasoning and the mufta bihi.
The tarjih (preferred view) is that of the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar deals with a specific dispute: a man sells his slave-girl, she later gives birth, and the seller claims the child as his own. The ruling distinguishes based on the time elapsed since sale. If the child is born less than six months from the sale, the child is attributed to the seller because the minimum period of pregnancy is six months. The slave-girl becomes an umm walad (mother of the child) for the seller, meaning the sale is annulled for the child and the price refunded. If the buyer also claims the child, the seller's claim is stronger (because of the short period). If the child is born between six months and two years (maximum pregnancy), the seller's claim is not accepted unless the buyer confirms. This is because the child could be from the buyer. If the child dies before being claimed, the seller cannot establish umm walad status for the mother because the child is gone. If the mother dies but the child lives, the seller takes the child but must pay compensation. Abu Hanifa says he must return the entire price (since the mother was sold with the child implicitly), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say he returns only the child's share (a proportionate part of the price). This is an intra-Hanafi dispute. Finally, if the seller claims only one of twins, both are attributed to him because twins come from the same pregnancy.

Lineage Claims for a Child Born to a Sold Slave-Girl
Rulings on lineage claims with an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on lineage claims for a child born to a sold slave-girl, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on the refund amount, and apply the six-month minimum pregnancy rule derived from the Quran via the Companions.

Core Idea
The seller's claim to the child takes priority over the buyer's, but only if the child is born within six months to two years.

This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa deals with a complex scenario: when a slave-girl is sold and later gives birth to a child whose lineage is claimed by either seller or buyer. The rulings depend critically on the timing of birth and whether the mother or child has died. There is also a notable intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on the financial compensation when the mother dies. A hadith from Ali (ra) establishes the minimum pregnancy period of six months, a key principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic rule of priority
The text states that if both buyer and seller claim the child, the seller's claim is preferred. Understand the general principle: the seller is the original owner, and the child is treated as part of the sold property unless established otherwise.
2
Apply the timing condition
If the child is born after more than six months and less than two years from the sale, the seller's claim is not accepted unless the buyer confirms it. This is because the minimum pregnancy is six months; a birth before six months would be too early for the seller to be the father, and a birth after two years would be beyond the maximum.
The six-month minimum is derived from the Quranic verses combined by Ali (ra).
3
Analyze the scenarios when the child or mother dies
If the child dies before the seller claims it and the birth was before six months, the mother does not become an umm walad. If the mother dies and the child was born before six months, the child's lineage is established (the seller takes the child), but there is a dispute on the refund amount.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on refund
Abu Hanifa holds that the seller must return the entire price. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the seller returns only the portion of the price attributable to the child, not the mother. Consider why each position might be argued.
The text does not provide the reasoning for each, but you can infer: Abu Hanifa may view the mother's share as part of the sale that must be unwound; the Sahibayn may view the mother's share as consumed.
5
Understand the twin rule
If someone claims the lineage of one twin, the lineage of both is established because they come from the same pregnancy. This is a practical application of the principle that twins share the same origin.

Appointment of a Qasim and His Conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on appointing a qasim, list the required qualities of a qasim, and identify the evidence for each from Quran, hadith, and athar.

Core Idea
The Imam is recommended to appoint a just, trustworthy, and knowledgeable divider; if no state salary exists, a paid divider is permissible.

In this passage from Al-Quduri, we learn about the appointment of a divider (qasim) for dividing commonly owned property. The text explains the Imam's duty and the qualities the divider must possess. We will also examine the proofs from the Quran, Sunnah, and practice of the Companions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The divider's fee is either per head or per share – the Sahibayn's proportional view is the mufta bihi.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses two practical issues in property division. First, the judge should not force all people to use a specific divider; parties may choose. Second, and more contested, how should the divider's fee be split among co-owners? Imam Abu Hanifa argues for equal distribution per head, while his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, argue for proportional distribution according to each person's share. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to handle financial disputes and recognize how Hanafi tarjih works.

Guided Path
1
State the first ruling
Read the sentence: 'ولا يخبر القاضي الناس على قاسم واحد' – what does it prohibit? The judge must not dictate a single divider; people can choose their own.

2
Identify the dispute on fee distribution
Locate the two positions: Abu Hanifa says 'على عدد الرؤوس' (by number of heads), the Sahibayn say 'على قدر الأنصباء' (by proportion of shares).
The disagreement is on the basis for splitting the cost, not on the permissibility of charging a fee.

Examine the proof cited
The author provides a narration (وجه) from Musa ibn Tarif – Ali ibn Abi Talib said to a man charging for calculation, 'You are only consuming ill-gotten gain.' How does this support the ruling?
This hadith is cited as evidence against unjust fees, but the Hanafi school allows a reasonable fee for division when necessary.

Abu Hanifa's illa: the divider's effort benefits all equally regardless of share size. The Sahibayn's illa: the benefit received is proportional to one's share, so cost should follow benefit.
Think about modern examples: a real estate agent dividing a jointly owned property – does the work change if one party owns 60% vs 30%?
5
Apply the tarjih (preponderance)
The text does not state the tarjih explicitly, but later Hanafi authorities (e.g., Al-Hidayah, Radd al-Muhtar) give preference to the Sahibayn's view. Why? Because proportional distribution is more just and avoids dispute.
This is a classic example of later Hanafi practice (mufta bihi) following the Sahibayn.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses when a partner can force division of jointly owned property. The general rule is that if each partner benefits, any one can demand division. But if one partner is harmed by a tiny share, only the one with the larger share can request it—protecting the smaller from loss.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partition when a co-owner has an unstated easement and derive the proof from the Quran.

Core Idea
A partition that forces one partner to bear another's easement is unjust; if diversion is impossible, the division is rescinded.

When jointly owned property is divided, pre-existing rights like easements can become problematic if not stipulated. This lesson examines the Hanafi rule for such situations, which balances justice and property rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Partition (qismah) between co-owners where one has an easement (right of way or water channel) in the other's share, but no condition was made in the division.
2
Note the two outcomes
If the easement can be physically diverted away from the other's share, the right-holder must do so and cannot continue using the other's share. If diversion is impossible, the division is rescinded (faskh) and shares revert to common ownership.
The ruling hinges on possibility of diversion.
3
Understand the rationale
The ruling prevents harm (darar) to the co-owner whose share is burdened. Hanafi law protects property rights from unjust encumbrance.
4
Connect to the proof
The Quranic verse (al-An'am 6:136) condemns unjust allocation among partners. Analogously, a partition forcing an easement on one partner without compensation is unjust, warranting rescission if diversion is impossible.
The verse is used as a dalil for the ruling.

When floors are not equal, divide by value not area — each unit must be valued separately.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on partitioning a building where partners own different floors that are not symmetric. The text from Al-Quduri states that each floor must be valued independently and division must be based on monetary value, not physical area. In any co-ownership of floors with unequal values, each floor must be appraised and the shares distributed based on that appraisal.

Testimony of Partitioners and Claims of Error
Rulings on accepting partitioners' testimony and handling claims of mistake

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting partitioners' testimony and identify the evidentiary burden required for a partner claiming error after division.

Core Idea
A completed division is final; a claim of error requires clear evidence, not just a bare assertion.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario after property division: when partners disagree after the division, the testimony of the neutral partitioners is accepted. However, if one partner later claims that he made an error or that his property went to the other, despite having acknowledged receipt in front of witnesses, his claim is rejected unless he produces strong evidence. This prevents frivolous reopening of settled divisions and upholds the finality of the process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
After division, partners disagree. The two partitioners (qāsimān) testify about the division. The ruling is that their testimony is accepted (تُقْبَلُ شَهَادَتُهُمَا).
This is based on the neutrality and expertise of the partitioners.
2
Analyze the claim of error
One partner claims an error (الْغَلَط) and alleges that some of his property ended up with the other partner. However, he had previously acknowledged receipt of his full share (أَشْهَدَ عَلَى نَفْسِهِ بِالِاسْتِيفَاءِ).
The acknowledgment is a binding admission against his own interest.
3
Apply the evidentiary rule
The claim is not accepted unless he brings evidence (بَيِّنَة). The burden is on him because the original division and his own acknowledgment stand against his claim.
This follows the general principle: 'The burden is on the claimant, the oath on the defendant.'
4
Connect to the supporting hadiths
The author cites two hadiths from Bukhari: (1) The Prophet (SAW) ruled that the oath is on the defendant (الْيَمِينُ عَلَى الْمُدَّعَى عَلَيْهِ). (2) The Prophet said: 'Your two witnesses or his oath.' These establish that the partner claiming error must produce evidence or else the other partner's denial (supported by the original acknowledgment) suffices.
Hadith 2668 and 2673 provide the textual basis for the rule.
5
Consider the practical consequence
This ruling protects the finality of divisions and prevents partners from reopening settled matters based on unsubstantiated claims. The only exception is if the claimant brings clear evidence (bayyinah) such as two witnesses or a written document.
The rule applies even if the claim seems plausible; mere assertion is not enough.

Contradictory Claims and Rescission of Division
Ruling on two cases of contradictory claims after partition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for two scenarios of contradictory claims regarding a division of property and identify when the division is rescinded.

Core Idea
A claimant who contradicts himself loses credibility; the opponent's word with oath is accepted, and in some cases the division is rescinded.

This passage from Al-Quduri presents two specific cases where partners disagree about receipt of shares after a partition. The rulings illustrate the Hanafi principle of preferring the opponent's statement when the claimant's narrative is inconsistent or unacknowledged. Understanding these rules helps maintain finality in property divisions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the Arabic passage and note the two distinct cases: (1) a claimant first says he received his full share, then says the other took part; (2) a claimant says his property ended up in a certain place (the other's share) but was not handed over, and he never acknowledged receipt.
The first scenario involves self-contradiction; the second involves an unacknowledged claim.
2
Determine the outcome for each
For the first scenario, the opponent's word with oath is taken. For the second, the partners are considered contradictory and the division is rescinded (faskh).
The key difference is the presence of acknowledgment.
3
Identify the condition for rescission
Rescission only occurs when the claimant never acknowledged receipt (no shahada 'ala nafsihi bil-istifa') and the partner denies the claim.
If the claimant had acknowledged receipt, his later denial would be contradictory and his word would not be accepted.
4
Memorize the key Arabic phrase
The first ruling is encapsulated in: 'فَالْقَوْلُ قَوْلُ خَصْمِهِ مَعَ يَمِينِهِ' (the saying is the saying of his opponent with his oath). Commit this phrase to memory.
This is a standard Hanafi rule in cases of contradictory claims.

Third Party Claim on Specific Share and Recourse
Ruling when a third party proves entitlement to one partner's specific share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a third party proves ownership of a partner's specific share after division and explain the mechanism of recourse.

Core Idea
A valid division is not rescinded by a third party claim; recourse is taken from the partner's share.

In this passage, Imam al-Quduri presents a key ruling on the effect of a third party's successful claim (istihqaq) after a property division (qisma). According to Abu Hanifa, the division remains valid, and the affected partner compensates by taking from his partner's share. This principle preserves the stability of contracts while ensuring justice between partners. We will analyze the ruling, its basis, and the intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The passage states: 'a third party proves entitlement to the specific share of one of them.' This is a post-division claim on a specific portion of one partner's allotted share.
The claim is on a specific share (bi 'aynihi), not on the undivided whole.
2
State the ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the division is not rescinded (lam tufsakh al-qismah). The division remains valid.
Contrast with other scenarios where the division might be rescinded.
3
Explain the remedy
The affected partner 'has recourse for that portion from the share of his partner' (yarji'u bihissati dhalika min nasibi sharikihi). This means he takes compensation from the other partner's property proportional to the lost value.
Recourse is a form of compensation, not rescission.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani differ; they likely favour rescinding the division entirely. This disagreement affects whether the division stands or is undone.
The mufta bihi (operative view) is Abu Hanifa's, as upheld in al-Hidayah.
5
Consider the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, the division is stable; the affected partner gets compensation. Under the Sahibayn, the division would be rescinded, and the property returned to undivided co-ownership, requiring a new division including the third party.
This illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) of the dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced contracts and identify the conditions under which the coerced party retains the option to rescind.

Core Idea
A coerced contract is valid but not binding — the victim retains the option (khiyar) to confirm or cancel.

This lesson examines contracts executed under duress (ikrah). The Hanafi school uniquely holds that such contracts are sahih (valid) but ghayr lazim (non-binding), giving the coerced party the right to rescind. We will explore the specific acts included, the conditions for ratification, and the proofs from the Quran and the practice of the tabi'i Shurayh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the coerced acts
List the types of contracts explicitly mentioned: sale of property, purchase of a commodity, confession of debt, and lease of a house. Note that these are all transactional or declaratory acts.
The list is illustrative, not exhaustive.
2
Understand the ruling: valid but non-binding
For each coerced act, the contract is sahih (valid) but the coerced party has khiyar (option) – they can either confirm (imda') or rescind (faskh). The contract is not void (batil) because the basic elements of offer, acceptance, and subject matter exist.
This differs from the Shafi'i view that coerced contracts are batil from the outset.
3

If the coerced party later accepts the price (or benefit) willingly (taw'an), it constitutes ratification (ijazah) and the contract becomes binding. If they accept while still coerced (mukrahan), it is not ratification and they must return the item if still in hand.
The key is the presence or absence of duress at the time of acceptance.
4

First proof: Quran 4:29 – 'Do not consume wealth unjustly but only by mutual consent.' Coercion negates mutual consent. Second proof: The statement of Shurayh (a tabi'i) that imprisonment, beating, shackling, and threats constitute coercion.
Shurayh's statement is accepted as a reliable precedent (qawl al-tabi'i).
5
Note the usool principle
The Urdu usool states: 'A contract that requires mutual consent, if coercion is used, the contract will be valid but not binding.' This reflects the Hanafi principle that ikrah affects the binding nature (luzum) but not the validity (sihhah) of the contract.
This principle is derived from the proofs and distinguishes Hanafi from other schools.

The coerced buyer is liable for the price due to possession, but can seek recourse from the coercer.

In a coerced sale, the buyer is compelled to purchase an item. If that item perishes while in the buyer's possession, who bears the loss? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position: the buyer is liable to the seller for the price, but can then claim that amount from the coercer. The ruling is derived from Quranic and hadith evidence that establish liability for loss caused by one's action or possession.

Guided Path

The hadith of Ibn 'Umar (Bukhari) about freeing a co-owned slave is used to support the method of valuation (fair price) and the idea of liability for one's share. The hadith shows that when a person has a share in property, he must compensate the other owners fairly.
The ḥadīth is cited to reinforce that valuation and payment are required when one causes loss or takes possession.
4

After the buyer pays the seller, the buyer (the coerced party) has the option to claim the same amount from the coercer, because the coercer is the ultimate cause of the transaction and loss.
This is a right of recourse (rujūʿ) that the buyer may exercise or waive.

Liability of a Coerced Buyer: Ruling and Recourse
Ruling on buyer's liability and recourse against coercer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a coerced buyer's liability when purchased goods perish and explain the buyer's right of recourse against the coercer, citing the Quranic and hadith proofs used.

Core Idea
A coerced sale is valid in Hanafi law; the buyer pays the seller but recovers from the coercer.

Imagine you are forced under threat to buy an item. You take possession, and it perishes accidentally. Who bears the loss? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling from al-Quduri, where the buyer is liable to the seller but can claim from the coercer. The text also provides two proofs: a Quranic verse on liability for accidental harm and a hadith on valuing a freed slave share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the first part: the buyer is liable to the seller for the value if the item perishes in his hand. Note the condition 'while he is not coerced' in the first clause—this sets a baseline for non-coerced buyers. The second clause gives the coerced buyer the right to recover from the coercer. So the ruling covers both scenarios: a non-coerced buyer is fully liable; a coerced buyer is liable but has recourse.
2
Understand the Hanafi Position on Coercion
In Hanafi fiqh, a contract entered under coercion (ikrah) is valid (sahih) but the coerced party has options. Here, the buyer must fully pay the seller because the contract is effective and possession transferred. The coercion does not invalidate the sale.
Contrast with Shafi'i view where coerced sale is batil.
3
Analyze the Buyer's Liability
The buyer's liability is based on the principle that possession transfers risk. The seller has no responsibility once the goods are delivered. So the buyer must pay the value (qimah) to the seller.
4
Examine the Recourse Right
The buyer may choose to recover the paid amount from the coercer. This is a separate claim based on tort (the coercer caused the loss). The buyer can seek reimbursement from the coercer, but this does not affect his liability to the seller.
5
Evaluate the Proofs
The text cites a Quran verse (4:92) about compensation for accidental killing. The connection is analogical: just as the killer must pay blood money, the buyer must pay value for goods that perish in his possession. A hadith (Bukhari 2522) on freeing a slave partner establishes a principle of valuation and payment where a person must pay for a share. This reinforces the buyer's obligation.

Peace Treaties with the Enemy: Ruling and Conditions
Ruling on making peace treaties and their annulment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on peace treaties with the enemy, identify the conditions for their validity and annulment, and cite the Quranic and Prophetic proofs.

Core Idea
Peace treaties are permissible when beneficial, but must be honored or terminated with clear notice – treachery is forbidden.

This passage from the Kitab al-Siyar addresses the Imam's authority to make peace treaties (sulh) with the enemy. The Hanafi school considers peace permissible if there is maslahah (overall benefit for Muslims). The passage also explains the proper procedure for breaking a treaty: the Imam must give explicit notice, not attack suddenly. We will examine the ruling and its proofs from the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the Imam making a peace treaty (sulh) with the enemy (ahl al-harb) or a group of them. The ruling is: permissible (la ba'sa bihi) when there is maslahah for the Muslims.
Note that the ruling is mubah, not wajib. It becomes recommended if the benefit is clear.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites two Quranic verses and two hadiths. Quran 8:61 commands inclining to peace if the enemy inclines. Quran 8:58 commands throwing back the treaty equally if treachery is feared. The Prophet's treaty of Hudaybiyyah (10-year truce) and the hadith of Amr ibn Abasah emphasize honoring treaties until term or notice.
These proofs establish the permissibility and the procedure for termination.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
From Quran 8:61, the imperative 'fajnah laha' indicates permission (amr yufidu al-ibahah in this context, not obligation). From Quran 8:58, the imperative 'anbidh ilayhim' indicates obligation to give notice before breaking. The hadith of Amr ibn Abasah explicitly forbids attacking without notice.
This shows the proof for the requirement of notice (nabadha).
4
Note the conditions
The treaty is valid when: (1) the Imam judges it to involve maslahah, (2) it may be for a fixed period (though not required), and (3) if breaking, the Imam must give clear notice (nabadha ilayhim) before fighting.
Breaking without notice is treachery (ghadr), which is haram.
5
Apply to modern scenarios
Consider a modern ceasefire or peace agreement. The Hanafi ruling applies: the Muslim ruler should only enter such agreements when beneficial, and if withdrawal is needed, formal notice must be given before resuming hostilities.
This prevents accusations of perfidy and upholds Islamic ethics of treaties.

وجه (۲) قول التابعى لثبوت وَيُكْرَهُ بَيْعُ السَلَاحَ فِي أَيَّامِ الْفِتْنَةِ عَنِ الْحَسَنِ، وَابْنِ سِيرِينَ أَنَّهُمَا كَرِهَا بَيْعَ السَّلَاح فِي الْفِتْنَةِ (مصنف ابن ابي شيبه ، مَا يُكْرَهُ أَنْ يُحْمَلَ إِلَى الْعَدُقِ فَيَتَقَوَّى به، نمبر 33370)
And it is disliked to sell weapons during days of strife. Aspect (1): The hadith for proving 'It is disliked to sell weapons during days of strife': from Imran ibn Husayn, he said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) forbade selling weapons during strife. Aspect (2): The saying of the followers for proving 'It is disliked to sell weapons during days of strife': from al-Hasan and Ibn Sirin that they disliked selling weapons during strife.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The matn states that selling weapons (al-silah) during times of fitnah (civil strife, rebellion, or widespread disorder) is makruh (disliked). The first proof is a hadith from Imran ibn Husayn: the Prophet explicitly prohibited selling weapons during fitnah. This indicates that the ruling is close to prohibition (makruh tahrimi) as the hadith uses 'nahi' (prohibition). The second proof is the practice of the tabi'is al-Hasan al-Basri and Ibn Sirin, who both disliked such sales. The reason is that selling weapons during strife facilitates violence, injustice, and bloodshed. In the Hanafi school, this ruling applies regardless of who the buyer is, as the default is that the weapon may be used to harm others during the chaos.

Key Terms
فتنة (fitnah): Times of civil strife, rebellion, or widespread disorder and conflict among Muslims.
سلاح (silah): Weapons, arms, instruments of fighting.
يكره (yukrahu): It is disliked (makruh). In Hanafi fiqh, this can be either makruh tahrimi or tanzih depending on context.

Selling Grape Juice to a Known Wine-Maker: Ruling and Proofs
Ruling on selling grape juice to a known wine-maker with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling grape juice to a known wine-maker and identify the proofs used.

Core Idea
Selling juice to a wine-maker is valid but disliked; you are not responsible for the buyer's misuse.

This passage from Al-Quduri addresses a tricky question: can you sell grape juice to someone you know will turn it into wine? The answer, using Hanafi principles and evidence, is that the sale itself is valid (not forbidden), but it carries moral blame. The key distinction is between the direct sale of wine and the sale of its raw material.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is being sold? Grape juice. To whom? A known wine-maker. The ruling in the matn is 'la ba'sa' (no harm), indicating permissibility.
Note that 'la ba'sa' often implies permissibility with some dislike when associated with sin.
2
Examine the first evidence
Ayyub al-Sakhtiyani's statement: selling a defective item to a known deceiver is allowed. This implies the seller is not responsible for the buyer's misuse, supporting the permissibility of selling juice to a wine-maker.
This is a qawl sahabi, a saying of a companion, which is a weak but valid source in Hanafi usul.
3
Examine the second evidence
The hadith cursing ten categories of people involved with wine, including the seller. At first glance, this seems to prohibit the sale, but the Hanafi school reconciles it by noting that the curse applies to the seller of wine itself, not the seller of the raw material.
The hadith is authentic (Sunan Abi Dawud). It is used by the Shafi'i school to argue that any facilitation of wine is haram.
4
Understand the Hanafi reconciliation
The sale of grape juice is valid because the object (juice) is pure and beneficial. The buyer's sinful intention does not invalidate the contract. The seller is not a direct participant in the sin.
This is based on the principle that the contract is judged by its own subject matter, not the buyer's future use.
5
Note the practical ruling
The sale is valid (sahih) but makruh tanzih (disliked) because it facilitates sin indirectly. It is better to avoid such sales if one knows the buyer's intention.
The Urdu note adds that the sin is proportional to the level of help. The seller sins to the extent of his facilitation.

And there is no harm in selling grape juice to someone whom you know will make it into wine. Aspect (1): The hadith for proving 'There is no harm in selling grape juice to someone whom you know will make it into wine': Ma'mar informed us, he said: I said to Ayyub: Should I sell a defective commodity to someone I know will act dishonestly with that defect? He said: Do you only want to sell to the pious? Aspect (2): The hadith for proving 'There is no harm in selling grape juice to someone whom you know will make it into wine': from Anas ibn Malik, he said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) cursed ten concerning wine: the presser, the one for whom it is pressed, the drinker, the carrier, the one to whom it is carried, the server, the seller, the eater of its price, the buyer, and the one for whom it is bought. Principle: In buying and selling, the right to fix prices belongs to the owner; the king, the Commander of the Faithful, or anyone else has no right to interfere. Principle: Regarding a matter of sin, if one helps from a distance, there is scope for permissibility, but to the extent of the help, there will be sin.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The matn declares that there is no harm (la ba'sa) in selling grape juice (asir) to someone whom the seller knows will use it to make wine. This is a surprising ruling because the sale facilitates sin. The first evidence is a statement from Ayyub al-Sakhtiyani (a tabi'i) indicating that it is permissible to sell to a known dishonest person; the seller is not responsible for the buyer's misuse. The second evidence is the famous hadith about the ten cursed categories regarding wine, which includes the seller. This seems contradictory. The Hanafi reconciliation is that the hadith curse applies to the seller of wine, not the seller of the raw material (grape juice). The sale of the juice is valid, but it is makruh tanzih (disliked) because it enables sin indirectly. The Urdu principles highlight two broader legal maxims: (1) The owner has the right to set prices; the state should not interfere. (2) Indirect assistance in sin is permitted to some extent, but the level of assistance carries sin proportionate to it. This means that selling the juice is a mild form of assistance, and while the transaction is valid, it is not without moral blame. In the Hanafi school, the sale is considered valid (sahih) because the object (juice) is pure and beneficial in itself; the buyer's intention does not invalidate the contract. However, it is recommended to avoid such sales if one knows the buyer will use it for haram.

Key Terms
عصير (asir): Juice, specifically grape juice or any fruit juice that can be made into wine.
خمر (khamr): Intoxicating wine made from grapes or other substances.
لا بأس (la ba'sa): There is no harm; indicates permissibility but often carries an element of dislike when associated with sin.
