Renting Property for Non-Muslim Worship: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on renting property for non-Muslim worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on renting property to dhimmis for religious purposes, explain the basis of the dispute, and apply the location-based ruling to new cases.

Core Idea
Renting a house for a church is permissible in rural dhimmī areas according to Abu Hanifa, but prohibited in cities and Islamic-dominated areas per the relied-upon view.

In this lesson, we explore a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding renting property to non-Muslims for places of worship and wine selling. The ruling hinges on location and the nature of the contract. We will follow the arguments of Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, and understand how this dispute is resolved in later fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
What is the act? Renting a house to be used as a church, fire temple, or for selling wine. Who disagrees? Abu Hanifa permits it; Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) prohibit it.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi, not cross-madhhab.
2
Understand the location condition
The permissibility is restricted to the sawad (rural countryside, especially around Kufa) because in cities (amsar) it is unanimously prohibited. The reason: Islamic dominance in cities prevents open non-Muslim worship.
The term 'sawad' is important: it refers to areas where dhimmis may be numerous.
3
Follow Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the contract of hire is for the benefit of the house, which is permissible. The rent becomes obligatory upon delivery. The sin of using it for worship or wine is the lessee's free choice; the lessor is not responsible. Analogy: selling a slave girl to someone who might misuse her – the sale is valid despite potential misuse.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on separating the contract from the lessee's actions.
4
Analyze Sahibayn's objection
Sahibayn say renting for such purposes is not allowed because it is assistance in sin (i'ānah 'alā al-ma'siyah). The lessor knowingly enables the sinful act, so he shares responsibility.
Their view is based on the principle of blocking means to sin (sadd al-dharā'i').
5
Note the tarjih for our sawad
The text states that in 'our sawad' (areas where Islam is dominant, like Iraq), the view of Sahibayn is taken in the most correct view (fī al-aṣaḥḥ). This is because Islamic signs are apparent, and allowing churches or wine sales would undermine them.
Thus the mufta bihi for most Muslim lands today is prohibition.

Commentary
This passage discusses renting a house in rural areas (sawad) to non-Muslims for purposes like churches or wine selling. The matn states that Abu Hanifa permits this because the sin is attributable to the lessee's free choice, not the lessor's. The Sahibayn prohibit it as assistance in sin. The text notes that this ruling is limited to rural areas because in major cities it is unanimously disallowed. Then there is a further qualification: in 'our sawad' (i.e., areas like Iraq where Islam is dominant), the stricter view (Sahibayn) is taken to prevent public display of non-Islamic practices. The commentary provides analogy: it is like selling a slave girl to someone who might abuse her; the sale is valid despite potential misuse. Abu Hanifa argues that the contract is for the benefit of the house, which is permissible; the lessee's sinful use is separate. The key distinction is that in cities (amsar) it is prohibited because of the clear manifestation of Islam.

Key Terms
سواد (sawad): The rural countryside, especially the area around Kufa; lands where dhimmis are numerous.
بيت نار (bayt nar): Fire temple; place of worship for Zoroastrians.
إعانة على المعصية (i'ānah 'alā al-ma'siyah): Assistance in sin; a principle that prohibits helping others commit sinful acts.

Leasing in Sawad: Ruling and Scope
Ruling on leasing for sinful use in sawad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between sawad and amsar to determine the permissibility of leasing property for potentially sinful purposes in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Leasing for sinful use is mubah in sawad, haram in amsar; the sawad itself is now Islamic, changing the ruling.

This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi ruling on leasing a house in the countryside (sawad) for uses like a fire temple, church, or wine shop. The key is the location: in cities (amsar) it is unanimously prohibited, but in sawad the ruling differs between Abu Hanifa and his disciples. We will explore the geographical and historical context that determines when the lessor is not accountable for the lessee's sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its location
What is the leased property used for? Where is it located? The passage specifies 'sawad' (countryside) vs 'amsar' (cities).
Sawad here originally refers to rural areas of Kufa.
2
State the three positions
Abu Hanifa says mubah, Sahibayn say not permissible, and in 'our sawad' (where Islam is dominant) the most correct view is prohibition.
3
Understand the condition 'sawad'
Why is it restricted to sawad? Because in amsar, Islamic rituals are public, so allowing sinful acts openly is prohibited by consensus.
In sawad of Kufa, most inhabitants were dhimmis, so different ruling.
4
Apply the mufta bihi
Today, in most regions where Islam is dominant, the Sahibayn's view applies: it is not permissible.
Al-Marghinani states 'in our sawad they are not allowed to do so in the most correct view.'

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi ruling on leasing a house in the countryside (sawad) for purposes that involve sin, such as using it as a fire temple, church, synagogue, or for the sale of wine. The text first states the ruling: this is permissible according to Abu Hanifa because the sin is solely the act of the tenant, who is a free agent. The two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn), hold that it is not permissible because it constitutes assistance to sin. The restriction to the countryside is significant: in cities, where Islamic rites are manifest, such leasing is prohibited by consensus because it would be openly flouting the law. In the countryside of Kufa, where most inhabitants were dhimmis, Abu Hanifa's view applied; but in other sawads where Islam is dominant, the prohibition applies in the most correct view. The proof offered is that the lease transfers ownership of the benefit of the house, which is permissible; the sin of the tenant is his own free choice, disconnected from the landlord. This is likened to selling a slave girl to someone who might not observe her 'iddah or who might commit a sin with her, or selling a boy for intercourse. The analogy is that the contract itself is valid and the sin is the buyer's. The hadith about the curse on the carrier of wine is interpreted as applying only when the carrying is accompanied by the intent to sin, which is not the case in ordinary carriage.

Key Terms
سواد (sawād): Countryside, rural areas outside cities; here specifically the rural areas of Kufa and other lands.
أمصار (amsār): Cities or urban centers where Islamic rites are openly practiced.
فاعل مختار (fā‘il mukhtār): Free agent; an individual with free will and capacity to choose his actions.

Proof: The Free Agent and the Analogy
Proof and rebuttal for the ruling on leasing in sawad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Abu Hanifa's qiyas (analogy) for the permissibility of leasing in sawad and evaluate the Sahibayn's counterargument.

Core Idea
The lessor is not responsible for the tenant's sin because the tenant is a free agent; analogy to selling a slave girl for possible sinful use.

This lesson delves into the proof structure behind Abu Hanifa's ruling. The central argument is that the sin is solely the act of the free agent (al-fa'il al-mukhtar), and the lease contract only conveys the benefit of the property, which is inherently permissible. The Sahibayn counter that such leasing constitutes assistance to sin. Abu Hanifa responds with a powerful analogy: it is like selling a slave girl to someone who might commit a sin with her, which is valid. We will unpack this qiyas and understand the distinction between assisting sin and providing a neutral object.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core proof
Abu Hanifa's proof: the lease is on the benefit, which is permissible; the sin belongs to the tenant, a free agent.
2
Analyze the qiyas analogy
The text likens this to selling a slave girl to someone who might not observe 'iddah or commit a sin with her. In both cases, the seller's act is valid; the sin is the buyer's choice.
The analogy is qiyas jaliy; the illa is the free agency of the buyer/lessee.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's counter
They argue it is assistance to sin (i'ana 'ala al-ma'siya). The text records their objection and Abu Hanifa's response.
4
Evaluate the hadith interpretation
The hadith about the curse on the carrier of wine is interpreted as applying only when carrying is accompanied by intent to sin, which is absent in carriage for permissible purposes.
This shows usul al-fiqh: treating the hadith as muqayyad (conditional) on intent.
