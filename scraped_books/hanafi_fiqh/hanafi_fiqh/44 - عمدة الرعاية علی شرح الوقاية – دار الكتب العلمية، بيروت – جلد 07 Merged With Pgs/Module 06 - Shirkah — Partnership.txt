Istilad of a Jointly-Owned Mukatabah: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on a complex case of istilad and kitabah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on istilad of a jointly-owned mukatabah and identify the practical differences between the rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Istilad by a part-owner converts the entire woman into his umm walad due to the indivisibility of the status.

The status of umm walad is indivisible in Hanafi law: if a part-owner begets a child from a female slave, she becomes his umm walad entirely, not just his share. But when that slave is also a mukatabah (under kitabah), and she later becomes incapable, complex questions arise about the interaction of part-ownership, kitabah, and istilad. This passage presents the views of Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on how to resolve the situation.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
A female mukatabah is owned jointly by two partners. One partner (first) has intercourse with her (istilad). She later becomes incapable of paying the kitabah installments. The passage analyzes the legal consequences for both partners and the children born.
2
Identify the core legal principle
In Hanafi fiqh, the status of umm walad is indivisible. When a part-owner causes istilad, the entire slave becomes his umm walad, not just his share. This triggers a chain of effects on ownership, kitabah, and liability.
This indivisibility is agreed upon by both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, but they differ on the timing and consequences.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the istilad by the first partner makes the entire woman his umm walad, but only upon her incapacity (ʿajz). Before that, the kitabah remains in force and the woman is still partially owned by the second partner. The first partner must guarantee half her value to the second. The second partner's child, if he also had intercourse earlier, is free with value (walad maghrur) because he acted under apparent ownership (shubhah). He must pay the full dower (ʿaqr) but no hadd.
The key: umm walad status attaches only after ʿajz, not before.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) agree that istilad is not divisible, but they hold that it takes effect immediately, before incapacity. The kitabah is rescinded for the partner's share because it does not harm the mukatabah (actually benefits her). Thus, the second partner's share transfers to the first automatically. The second partner's child is not free (no walad maghrur). The first partner's guarantee differs: Abu Yusuf says guarantee half value as a mukatabah; Muhammad says guarantee the lesser of half value or half the remaining kitabah debt.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under the Sahibayn, the second partner's child is a slave; under Abu Hanifa, it is free with value.
5
Synthesize the practical consequences
Compare the outcomes for the second partner's child, the amount of guarantee, and the timing of ownership transfer. Recognize that both views are reported within the Hanafi school and each has its own internal logic.
The passage does not declare a mufta bihi; these are both valid positions.

Commentary
This passage addresses a complex intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a female slave under kitabah (mukatabah) who is jointly owned by two partners. The core question is what happens when one partner (the first) impregnates her (istilad), and then she becomes incapable (cajizah) of paying the kitabah installments. The passage presents the opinion of Imam Abu Hanifa first, then the opinion of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), followed by detailed glosses explaining each point.

According to Abu Hanifa: The istilad (begetting a child) by the first partner is not divisible (ghayr mutajazzi), meaning that once one partner causes istilad, the entire woman becomes his umm walad (mother of his child), even though he only owns half. This is because the status of umm walad is an indivisible attribute. Consequently, the second partner's share is effectively transferred to the first, but only upon the woman's incapacity. The first partner must guarantee (pay) half the value of the woman to the second partner, because he acquires that half via the istilad. However, the child born to the second partner (if he had intercourse before the istilad) is considered a 'child of a deceived person' (walad maghrur) — the second partner acted under the belief that his ownership was complete, so the child is free upon payment of its value (qimmah). The second partner must also pay the full dower (mahr al-mithl) for his intercourse, but he is not subject to the hadd punishment because of the doubt (shubhah) arising from his apparent ownership.

According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): The istilad by the first partner is not divisible either, but they reach a different conclusion. Before the woman's incapacity, she becomes umm walad for the first partner entirely, and the second partner's share transfers to the first automatically by the rescission (faskh) of the kitabah contract. The kitabah is rescinded only in matters that do not harm the mukatab (i.e., the benefit of becoming umm walad outweighs any harm). The second partner's intercourse then occurs without ownership, so his child does not establish lineage from him, is not free with value, and he must pay the full dower but no hadd. The first partner guarantees to the second half of her value as a mukatabah according to Abu Yusuf (since the guarantee is for owning the share), but Muhammad says the guarantee is the lesser of half her value or half the remaining kitabah debt, because the partner's right is uncertain between the value and the debt.

The glosses clarify terms like 'apparent ownership', the possibility of valuing an umm walad, and the logic behind the different guarantees. The passage ultimately shows the subtlety of Hanafi reasoning on the intersection of part-ownership, kitabah, and istilad.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has entered a contract of kitabah with his master, agreeing to pay a sum in installments in exchange for freedom.
استيلاد (istilad): Begetting a child from one's female slave; the act that confers the status of umm walad upon her.
أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master; she cannot be sold, exchanged, or given as gift, and is freed automatically upon the master's death.
عجز ('ajz): Inability of a mukatab to pay the kitabah installments, leading to rescission of the contract and reversion to full slave status.
ولد مغرور (walad maghrur): A child born from sexual intercourse that occurred under a mistaken belief of lawful ownership, where the child is free but must be ransomed by the father at its value.
عقر ('aqr): Dower or compensation paid for sexual intercourse with a slave woman, equivalent to the mahr al-mithl of a free woman.

Authorized Slave's Permitted Contracts and the Self-Hire Dispute
Lists additional transactions permitted for the authorized slave with a Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the contracts permitted for an authorized slave in Hanafi fiqh and explain the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i objection regarding self-hire.

Core Idea
An authorized slave may hire himself out because his labor is capital, unless it defeats the master's purpose.

This passage continues the enumeration of transactions an authorized slave (ma'dhun) may undertake. After covering sale and debt, it now addresses partnerships, mudarabah, and hiring—including the controversial question of whether the slave can hire himself out. The text clarifies the types of partnership that fall within the permission and presents a clear dispute with the Shafi'i school over self-hire.

Guided Path
1
Context of the authorized slave's capacity
Recall that an authorized slave is permitted to trade within the scope of his master's permission. This passage lists additional specific contracts that are included in that general permission.
Focus on the acts: buying seeds, shirkah, mudarabah, hiring things, and hiring himself.
2
Identify the disputed transaction
The key dispute concerns 'yu'ajjir nafsah' (hiring himself out). The Hanafi school rules it valid; al-Shafi'i holds it invalid.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in rulings.
3
State the Shafi'i objection and its basis
Al-Shafi'i argues that since the slave does not own his person (nafs), he cannot contract on his person or its benefits. He analogizes to self-sale, gift, or pawn which are prohibited.
The objection is based on the slave's lack of full ownership over himself.
4
Explain the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi response: 'His self is his capital' (nafsuhu ra's maluhu). The slave's labor is analogous to capital in trade, which he may dispose of. This is a qiyas on the permissibility of using capital in business.
Exception: contracts that would nullify the master's permission, like sale or pawn, are excluded.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis hold the self-hire contract valid, entitling the slave to wages; Shafi'is declare it void.
This difference affects the enforceability of such contracts in Hanafi jurisdictions.

Commentary
This passage continues the list of transactions permissible for an authorized slave (ma'dhūn). The slave may buy seeds for cultivation, enter into a limited partnership (shirkat al-'inan) but not a universal partnership (shirkat al-mufawada) because the latter involves suretyship (kafala), which is beyond the scope of permission. The slave may also give and receive capital for mudarabah (profit-sharing venture), and hire things like workers or property. The main point of contention with al-Shafi'i is whether the slave can hire himself out (yu'ajjir nafsah). The Hanafi position is that the slave owns his own labor (nafs) as capital, so he can contract for it, except for transactions that would defeat the master's purpose, like selling himself (which would end his slavery) or pawning himself (which would detain him). Al-Shafi'i argues that since the slave does not own his person, he cannot contract on his person or its benefits. The Hanafi response analogizes the slave's labor to capital, which he may dispose of within limits.

Key Terms
شركة العنان (shirkat al-'inan): A limited partnership where partners contribute capital for a specific trade; it does not require mixing capital and allows unequal contributions and profit shares.
شركة المفاوضة (shirkat al-mufāwaḍah): A universal partnership where partners are equal in capital, freedom, and religion, and which includes mutual agency and suretyship.
المضاربة (al-muḍārabah): A profit-sharing contract where one party provides capital and the other provides labor.
إجارة النفس (ijārat al-nafs): Hiring oneself out for labor in exchange for wages.

Muzara'ah: Termination, Expenses, and Invalid Conditions
Commentary on multiple rulings in muzara'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on termination, expense allocation, and invalid conditions in a muzara'ah contract, and distinguish between legal and religious obligations.

Core Idea
In muzara'ah, the worker's right attaches to the produce, not land; early termination gives no legal claim but owner should compensate religiously.

This passage from the Hanafi tradition lays down several crucial rulings for muzara'ah (sharecropping) contracts. It covers what happens when the owner refuses after the worker has prepared the land, how debt affects the contract, expense sharing after the term ends, and when a stipulated condition invalidates the whole agreement. Understanding these rules is essential to avoid invalid contracts and to know when a worker has a right to compensation.

Guided Path

Identify the six distinct rulings

كتاب المزارعة

ولو أبى رب البذر والأرض وقد كرب العامل، فلا شيء له حكماً، ويسترضى ديانة. وتبطل بموت أحدهما، وتفسخ بدين محوج إلى بيعها. هذا قبل أن ينبت الزرع لكن يجب ديانة أن يسترضي إذا عمل العامل، أما إذا نبت الزرع ولم يستحصد لا يباع الأرض لتعلق حق المزارع. فإن مضت المدة ولم يدرك الزرع فعلى العامل أجر مثل نصيبه من الأرض حتى يدرك: أي أجر مثل ما فيه نصيبه، ونفقة الزرع عليهما بالحصص. قلنا: نعم؛ إلا أن في إلزام موجب العقد إياه ضرر فيما يتناوله العقد؛ لأن البذر ليس بمعقود عليه، بخلاف العمل. هذا زبدة ما في العناية والكفاية.

قوله: وقد كرب؛ أي والحال أنه أي العامل قد قلب الأرض للزرع، كما فهم من الصحاح.

قوله: وتفسخ بدين محوج إلى بيعها...الخ؛ قال في الهداية: فإذا فسخت المزارعة بدين قادح لحق صاحب الأرض فاحتاج إلى بيعها فباع جاز، كما في الإجارة، وليس للعامل أن يطالبه بما كرب الأرض وحفر الأنهار بشيء؛ لأن المنافع إنما تتقوم بالعقد، وهو إنما قوم بالخارج، فإذا انعدم الخارج لم يجب شيء، ولو ثبت الزرع ولم يستحصد. (١) لأن المزارع استوفى منفعة بعض الأرض لتربية حصته فيها إلى وقت الإدراك. ينظر: درر الحكام (٢ : ٣٢٧). (٢) العناية (٩ : ٤٧٣). (٣) الكفاية (٨ : ٣٩٤ - ٣٩٥).

ثم قال: فإن شرط على العامل فسدت. كأجر الحصاد، والرفاع، والدوس، والتذرية فإن شرط على العامل فسدت؛ لأنَّهُ شرط مخالف لمقتضى العقد، فإنَّ ثم بيع الأرض في الدِّين حتى يستحصد الزرعُ ويخرجه القاضي من الحبس إن كان حبسه بالدين؛ لأنه لما امتنع بيع الأرض لم يكن هو ظالماً، والحبس جزاء الظلم. انتهى.

وأشار الشارح له إلى هذا التفصيل بقوله: هذا قبل أن يثبت الزرع... الخ.

قوله: يكون عليهما ...الخ؛ أي بقدر ملكهما بعد انقضاء مدة المزارعة؛ لأنه عمل مشترك، حيث انتهى العقد باعتبار المدة، واستحقاق العمل على العامل إنما كان في المدة بالعقد، ولم يبق بخلاف ما إذا مات رب الأرض، والزرع بقي بحيث يكون العمل فيه على العامل؛ لأن العقد فيه ثمة يبقى في مدته. وهذا معنى قول صاحب الهداية: «لأن هناك نفس العقد في مدته، والعقد يستدعي العمل على العامل»، وبهذا ينكشف أن قول صدر الشريعة له بعيد هذا. فالحاصل: إن كل عمل قبل الإدراك فهو على العامل، محمول على ما إذا كان قبل مضي مدة المزارعة؛ ليتصور بقاء العقد، واستحقاق العمل على العامل، إذ لو مضت فلا عقد ولا استحقاق، فلا منافاة بين قوله: يكون عليهما، وبين قوله: فهو على العامل، فليتأمل. قاله الجلبي.

قوله: كأجر الحصاد...الخ؛ الحصاد قطع الزرع بعد إدراكها، والرفاع بفتح الراء المهملة وكسرها: أن يحمل الزرع إلى البيدر، والدوس وطء الزرع لإخراج الحبوب من غلافها، والتذرية: تميز الحب من التبن بالريح. كذا في معتبرات كتب اللغة. قاله الجلبي.

قوله: فإن شرط؛ أي العمل الذي يكون بعد انتهاء الزرع كالحصاد وأخواته. وبيان الحكم: أنه إن شرط على العامل هذه الأعمال فسدت المزارعة؛ لأنه شرط مخالف لمقتضى العقد، والشرط إذا كان مخالفاً لمقتضى العقد يفسد العقد. وقد سبق بيانه في الإجارة
Chapter of Muzara'ah. If the owner of the seed and land refuses after the worker has tilled, there is nothing legally for him, and he is appeased religiously. It becomes void by the death of one of them, and is rescinded by a debt that necessitates selling it. This applies before the crop sprouts, but religiously it is obligatory to appease if the worker has worked. If the crop has sprouted but not ripened, the land is not sold due to the attachment of the cultivator's right. If the term has passed and the crop has not ripened, then on the worker is the equivalent rent of his share of the land until it ripens: i.e., the equivalent of what his share is in it, and the expenses of the crop are on both according to shares. We said: Yes, except that obligating the effect of the contract upon him causes harm to what the contract includes, because the seed is not contracted upon, unlike the work. This is the essence of what is in al-Inaya and al-Kifaya. He said: 'And he had tilled': i.e., the situation that the worker has turned the land for cultivation. He said: 'And it is rescinded by a debt necessitating its sale...' al-Hidaya said: When the muzara'ah is rescinded due to a harmful debt that befalls the landowner and he needs to sell it, he sells it, as in ijara. The worker cannot claim anything for tilling the land or digging canals, because benefits are only valued by the contract, and it is valued by the produce, so when the produce is absent, nothing is obligatory. He said: 'Then if he stipulates on the worker, it becomes invalid. Like the wages of harvesting, carrying, threshing, and winnowing, if stipulates on the worker, then it becomes invalid; because it is a condition contrary to the requirement of the contract, because then the land is sold for debt until the crop ripens and the judge removes it from detention if it was detained for debt; because since the sale of the land was prevented, he is not an oppressor, and detention is the penalty for oppression.' He indicated this detail by saying: 'This is before the crop becomes established...' He said: 'It is on both...' i.e., according to their ownership after the end of the muzara'ah term; because it is shared work, since the contract ends by the term, and the entitlement to work from the worker was during the term by contract, and does not remain, unlike when the landowner dies and the crop remains such that work on it is on the worker; because the contract there remains during its term. The conclusion: every work before ripening is on the worker, interpreted as when it is before the expiration of the muzara'ah term, so that the contract and entitlement to work remain. Once expired, no contract or entitlement, so no contradiction between his statement 'it is on both' and his statement 'it is on the worker'. He said: 'Like wages of harvesting...' Harvesting is cutting the crop after ripening; carrying is transporting the crop to the threshing floor; threshing is treading the crop to extract grains from husks; winnowing is separating grain from chaff by wind. He said: 'Then if stipulates' i.e., the work that is after the end of the crop like harvesting and its counterparts. Explanation of the ruling: if he stipulates these works on the worker, the muzara'ah becomes invalid; because it is a condition contrary to the requirement of the contract, and a condition contrary to the requirement of the contract invalidates the contract, as previously explained in ijara.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from the Hanafi tradition on the chapter of muzara'ah (sharecropping). It covers several key rulings. First, if the landowner refuses to proceed after the worker has prepared the land (tilled it), the worker has no legal claim to compensation, but the landowner is religiously advised to compensate him. This distinction between legal and religious obligation is typical in Hanafi fiqh. Second, the contract is voided by the death of either party. It is also rescinded if the landowner incurs a debt that requires selling the land, but only before the crop sprouts; after sprouting, the land cannot be sold because the worker's right attaches. Third, if the contractual period ends before the crop ripens, the worker must pay an equivalent rent for his share of the land until ripening, and expenses are shared proportionally according to ownership shares. Fourth, if the landowner stipulates that the worker perform certain post-harvest tasks like harvesting, carrying, threshing, or winnowing, the contract becomes invalid because such a condition contradicts the nature of the muzara'ah contract, where the worker's share is for cultivation work, not post-harvest processing. The commentary also addresses an apparent contradiction: why the worker is responsible for post-period expenses on the land but not considered to have a claim for pre-ripening work; the resolution is that the worker's entitlement to work only exists during the contract term, and after the term ends, any work on the worker's share of the land is his own responsibility. The passage relies on texts from al-Hidaya, al-Inaya, and al-Kifaya, reflecting the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
مزارعة (muzāraʿah): A contract of sharecropping where one party provides land and seed, and the other provides labor, and they share the produce.
ديانة (diyānatan): Religiously or morally, as opposed to legally (qaḍā'an). An obligation that is not enforceable in court but binding upon the individual's conscience.
مقتضى العقد (muqtaḍā al-ʿaqd): The inherent requirement or nature of a contract; the essential purpose and obligations that arise from the contract itself without additional stipulations.
