Interdiction of Bankrupt and Licentious Mufti: Public Harm Exception
Ruling on interdiction with intra-madhhab clarification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on interdiction of a bankrupt debtor and identify the three exceptional cases where a licentious person may be interdicted due to public harm.

Core Idea
A bankrupt is interdicted upon creditors' request; a fasiq is not, unless his harm reaches the public.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya presents a clear ruling on the bankrupt debtor and then navigates a manuscript issue to clarify the Hanafi position on interdiction of a fasiq. The key distinction is between private harm and public harm. We will unpack the ruling, correct a common misreading, and understand the exceptional three categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling on bankrupt debtor
The judge places the bankrupt under interdiction when creditors request it, preventing sale and acknowledgment of new debts. This is the mufta bihi position.
This applies to a free, adult, sane bankrupt.
2
Detect the manuscript error regarding Sahibayn
A corrupt manuscript reading suggested that Sahibayn and al-Shafi'i allow interdiction of a fasiq. The commentator denies this, citing the Mufti of the Two Worlds.
The correct position is that Sahibayn do not allow interdiction for mere licentiousness.
3
State the corrected Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa: no interdiction for prodigality, debt, licentiousness, or negligence. Sahibayn: no interdiction for licentiousness (but allow for others). Al-Shafi'i: interdiction for all.
The passage focuses on the Hanafi view that a fasiq is not interdicted.
4
List the three exceptional categories for interdiction
Despite the general rule, three individuals are interdicted because their harm spreads: ignorant physician, licentious mufti, bankrupt carrier.
This is an exception for public protection.
5
Define 'majin' and specify the harm of the licentious mufti
Al-majin is reckless and unconcerned. The licentious mufti teaches invalid legal tricks (hiyal batila) that cause widespread harm, such as teaching apostasy to circumvent obligations.
His harm is not limited to himself.

Commentary
This passage provides two key rulings. First, regarding a bankrupt debtor (mufallis): if creditors request interdiction, the judge places him under interdiction and prevents him from selling or making acknowledgments (i.e., no new transactions that could harm creditors). This is a clear ruling and agreed upon by the Hanafi school and al-Shafi'i. Second, the passage addresses a manuscript error where a phrase was misattributed to Sahibayn and al-Shafi'i regarding interdiction of a licentious person (fasiq). The commentator clarifies that such a reading is corrupt because it would imply Sahibayn differed on this point, whereas they actually agree that interdiction is not allowed for mere licentiousness. The Mufti of the Two Worlds (likely Ibn Abidin or a similar authority) clarifies the full position: According to Abu Hanifa, no free, sane adult is interdicted due to prodigality, debt, licentiousness, or negligence. According to Sahibayn, interdiction is allowed for all except licentiousness. According to al-Shafi'i, it is allowed for all. However, an exception is made for three specific professions that cause public harm: an ignorant physician (mutatabbib jahil), a licentious mufti (mufti majin), and a bankrupt carrier (mukari mufallis). These three can be interdicted even under Abu Hanifa because their harm is widespread. The term 'majin' is defined as someone reckless and unconcerned about consequences. The 'licentious mufti' specifically teaches invalid legal tricks (hiyal batila) that cause harm to the public, such as teaching apostasy to circumvent marriage or zakat obligations. Therefore, such a person's harm is not limited to himself but extends to society, justifying interdiction.

Key Terms
المفلس (al-mufallis): A bankrupt debtor whose debts exceed his assets and creditors request interdiction.
المفتي الماجن (al-mufti al-majin): A licentious mufti who teaches invalid legal tricks (hiyal batila) that harm the public.
الفاسق (al-fasiq): A licentious person who openly commits sins.

Imprisonment of the Usurper Who Claims Destruction
Explains the procedure when the usurper claims destruction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for a usurper claiming destruction and apply the qiyas to a bankrupt debtor scenario.

Core Idea
A usurper who claims destruction is imprisoned until the claim is verified, then must pay the value.

When a usurper claims the item perished, the judge must balance the owner's right to compensation and the usurper's claim. This lesson explains the imprisonment procedure, its discretionary nature, and the logical analogy to a bankrupt debtor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim
Recognize that the usurper claims the usurped item has perished (hālik). This triggers a specific legal procedure.
2
Understand the Imprisonment
The judge imprisons the usurper to compel verification, but only if the owner does not agree to a value ruling. The imprisonment has no fixed term, like for a debtor claiming bankruptcy.
This is at the judge's discretion (tafwīḍ ilā ra'y al-qāḍī).
3
Trace the Qiyas
The text analogizes to a debtor who claims bankruptcy while owing the price of goods (asl). In both cases, the claim goes against the apparent situation, so the claimant is not believed without evidence.
The effective cause (ʿilla) is that the claim contradicts the apparent.
4
Determine the Outcome
Once the claim is verified, the obligation to return the original item drops, and the usurper must pay the substitute (value). This is because the original duty was to return the ʿayn, and destruction is an unforeseen event.
The value is assessed by agreement or by owner's proof.

Commentary
This passage covers two aspects: the procedure when the usurper claims the item perished, and the condition that the item must be movable. The ruler or judge can imprison the usurper who claims destruction until it becomes clear that if the item had survived he would have produced it. This imprisonment has no fixed duration; it is at the judge's discretion, similar to imprisoning a debtor. After imprisonment, the judge orders the usurper to pay the substitute (value). This is because the original obligation was to return the item, and destruction is a claim that contradicts the apparent situation (the item is assumed to exist until proven otherwise). The analogy is made with a bankrupt debtor who claims inability to pay. Once the destruction is confirmed, the obligation shifts to paying the value. The second part states the condition that the usurped item must be movable (manqūl). If it is immovable (ʿiqār), the act of usurpation (ghaṣb) does not apply in its true sense because usurpation is defined as removing the owner's control by physically moving the item. An immovable item (like land) cannot be physically taken; although the owner's control can be prevented, the technical definition of ghaṣb requires transfer. Thus, the ruling of guarantee (ḍamān) for usurpation applies only to movable property. However, if someone takes over immovable property, the ruling regarding guarantee may be different (e.g., penalty for preventing benefit).

Key Terms
حبس (ḥabs): Imprisonment of the usurper by the judge to compel verification of his claim of destruction.

Condition of Movability for Usurpation
Explains the condition that the usurped item must be movable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school restricts the full usurpation (ghaṣb) guarantee to movable property and identify what constitutes a movable vs. immovable item.

Core Idea
Usurpation (ghaṣb) applies only to movable property because its definition requires physical transfer.

The previous lesson noted that the item must be movable for the full ghaṣb ruling to apply. This lesson unpacks that condition: why immovable property (ʿiqār) is not subject to the same guarantee, and how Hanafi jurists define movability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Condition
The passage states: 'And its condition: that the usurped item is movable (naqlī).' This is a condition for the full gamut of ghaṣb liability, including the procedure of imprisonment.
2
Understand the Rationale
The definition of ghaṣb is 'removing the owner's hand by taking and moving' (izālat al-yad bi al-naql). Since immovable property cannot be physically moved, the act of taking it is not technically ghaṣb.
The commentary mentions moving 'like the transference of eggplants'.
3
Distinguish Movable and Immovable
Movable (naqlī) can be transferred; immovable (ʿiqār) has a foundation (aṣl), like land and buildings. The guarantee for taking immovable property may fall under other rules (e.g., usurpation of benefit).
The text cites al-ʿInāyah for this definition.
4
Apply the Condition
If someone takes over immovable property, the full ghaṣb liability (return of ʿayn or value) does not apply. Instead, the owner may seek legal recovery and compensation for loss of use.
This is a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh.

The Qadi's Sequential Inquiry in a Shuf'a Claim
Expansion on the qadi's procedure for shuf'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sequential steps a Hanafi qadi follows when a shafi' claims pre-emption and explain the rationale for each step.

Core Idea
The qadi must verify the shafi's ownership of the adjacent property before asking about the sale.

This passage details the meticulous procedure a Hanafi qadi follows when adjudicating a shuf'a (pre-emption) claim. It shows that the qadi does not simply ask about the sale; he first establishes the shafi's ownership of the adjoining property, then the fact of the sale, using evidence and oaths. Understanding this sequence is essential for anyone studying the mechanics of Islamic judicial practice in property disputes.

Guided Path
1
Initial claim
The shafi' appears before the qadi, claims a sale has occurred, and requests pre-emption. The qadi begins by questioning the opponent (the buyer).
The qadi does not automatically grant the claim; he initiates a structured inquiry.
2
First inquiry: ownership of adjacent property
The qadi asks the buyer whether the shafi' owns the adjacent property by which he claims pre-emption. This is the foundation of the shafi's right.
Mere possession (yad) is insufficient because it could be due to loan or lease.
3
Proving or disputing ownership
If the buyer admits ownership or the shafi' provides evidence (bayyinah), ownership is established. If the shafi' fails to provide evidence, the qadi administers an oath to the buyer that he does not know the shafi' owns it. If the buyer refuses, ownership is proven.
Istihlaf (oath) is used to resolve lack of evidence.
4
Second inquiry: the sale
Only after ownership and adjacency are proven does the qadi ask the buyer whether he purchased the property. If the buyer denies, the shafi' must prove the sale with evidence; otherwise, the buyer swears an oath denying the purchase or the shafi's right.
The qadi distinguishes between the cause (sabab: the sale) and the outcome (hasil: the shuf'a right).

Core Idea
Compulsion to partition only applies to separation (ifraz) of same-genus goods, not to exchange (mubadala).

In Hanafi fiqh, partition (qismah) is a legal process to divide jointly owned property. This passage defines two methods: pure separation (ifraz) and exchange (mubadala). Understanding when a partner can be compelled to accept partition is crucial for fulfilling the right to benefit from one's share. The passage also discusses the appointment of a state-funded partitioner (qasim) to ensure equitable division.

Guided Path

Conditions for Division of Inherited vs Purchased Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on proof requirement for dividing inherited immovable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the requirement of proof for dividing inherited immovable property and apply the ruling to distinguish between movable and immovable, and between inheritance and purchase.

Core Idea
For inherited immovable property, Abu Hanifa requires proof of death and heirs, while the Sahibayn accept possession as sufficient evidence.

This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding judicial division of jointly owned property. The central question is whether partners claiming inherited immovable property must provide formal evidence of the testator's death and heirship before the judge can divide it. We will explore the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and understand how the type of property (movable vs immovable) and the mode of acquisition (purchase vs inheritance) affect the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Partners appear before a judge with property in their hands and request division. The property may be movable or immovable, and they may claim purchase, absolute ownership, or inheritance. Determine which combination triggers the dispute.
The dispute only arises for inherited immovable property.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that inherited immovable cannot be divided without proof (bayyinah) of the testator's death and the number of heirs. His evidence: the ownership of the deceased remains, so division is a judgment against the deceased, requiring evidence. Also, immovable is inherently preserved, so division for preservation is unnecessary.
This contrasts with movable inherited property, where division is allowed without proof because it aids preservation.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that inherited immovable can be divided based on the partners' admission and possession alone. Their evidence: possession is a strong indicator of ownership, and there is no third-party claimant to challenge the division.
They equate inherited immovable with purchased immovable and all movables in this regard.
4
Compare the reasoning and identify key assumptions
Abu Hanifa assumes the deceased's ownership persists until formal transfer, making division a qada against the deceased. The Sahibayn assume that possession is sufficient evidence of ownership, especially when there is no contesting party.
The difference hinges on the evidentiary value of possession and the need to protect the deceased's estate.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, a judge must demand proof before dividing inherited land; under the Sahibayn, the judge can proceed immediately. The text indicates that the Sahibayn's view is relied upon, though the cautious requirement of proof may still be observed in certain narrations.
The later Hanafi authorities (mufta bihi) prefer the easier position of the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah deals with the conditions under which a judge may divide property among partners who present themselves claiming joint ownership. Water Channel and Building Excess in Share Division
Hanafi ruling on easements and compensation in property division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rule: if a water channel or pathway of one share falls into another's share during division, it must be redirected if possible, otherwise the division is rescinded; and explain that compensation for a building excess must be from the same type of property, not from outside dirhams.

Core Idea
Division must achieve complete separation; a shared channel defeats it, and compensation must be from the same asset class.

When dividing jointly owned property, each share must become fully independent. If a water channel or pathway from one share ends up on another's share, the division is incomplete unless the channel can be rerouted. Similarly, if one share has a building excess, compensation must come from the estate itself, not from external dirhams. This lesson unpacks these two connected principles from the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
Focus on the scenario: after division, a water channel or pathway belonging to one share lies on another share, and no condition was made in the division contract to allow it.
2
Apply the primary ruling
If redirection is possible without harm, it must be done. If impossible, the division is rescinded (faskh) because the purpose of division—cutting partnership—is not achieved.
3
Distinguish the building excess rule
When one share has an excess (e.g., a building), compensation must be from the same type of property, not from dirhams outside the estate, because division is a right of partnership and there is no partnership in dirhams.
4
Analyze the vestibule example
In the example of a room whose door and water channel open onto a vestibule, after division the door and channel must be turned to the owner's own share to realize complete separation. If impossible, the division is rescinded.

Claim of Error After Admitting Receipt in Division
States the ruling on claim of error after admitting receipt in division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on the admissibility of a claim of error after admitting full receipt in division and explain the basis for the preferred position.

Core Idea
Admission of receipt bars later claims of error without clear proof.

This passage deals with a common scenario in Islamic property division: one party admits he received his full share, then later claims that due to a mistake, part of his share actually ended up with the other. The Hanafi school generally rejects such a claim unless the party provides strong evidence (bayyina), because the claim contradicts his earlier admission and attempts to rescind the division. However, there is also a minority view within the school that might accept the claim under certain conditions. This lesson will help you understand the main ruling and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and ruling
The passage states that if a co-divisor admits istifa' (full receipt), then claims error, he is not believed except with proof. Note the effect: the claim is effectively a rescission of the division.
2

The claim is to rescind the division (faskh al-qisma), which contradicts the admission. The Hidayah says it should not be accepted due to contradiction (al-tanaqud).
3

Some sources (matn, Mabsut, Fatawa Qadi Khan) suggest that if the error becomes apparent after deep reflection, the admission might be reconsidered. A reconciliation is offered: it depends on who performed the division (third party vs. self).

Evaluate the preferred position
Ibn Abidin notes that the majority of matns follow the author of al-Hidayah, and the fatwa is that the claim is not accepted without proof. The contradictory claim is not relied upon.
5

In practice, a party who admits receipt cannot later claim error unless he brings clear evidence (bayyina). Even then, strong evidence is required to overcome the contradiction.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Partitioners testify about the co-owner's receipt, not their own work — that distinction saves their testimony.

This passage presents a key procedural dispute in Hanafi law: when a co-owner denies receiving his share after division, are the two partitioners who conducted the division credible witnesses? The discussion pits Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (accept) against Muhammad al-Shaybani and Imam al-Shafi'i (reject), with the decisive point being whether the testimony concerns the partitioners' own act or the co-owner's act. We will follow the reasoning to see how the Hanafi school settles the issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
After a property division, a co-owner denies taking his share. The partitioners testify that he did. Understand the factual setting: the dispute is about the admissibility of that testimony.
The division itself is admitted by all.
2
State the Positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two shaykhs) accept the testimony; Muhammad and al-Shafi'i reject it. Identify each side.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf as well as cross-madhhab.
3
Analyze the Opposing Dalil
Muhammad uses qiyas: a person cannot testify about his own act; partitioners are testifying about their distinguishing of shares, so it is inadmissible. Understand the analogy.
The analogy is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Examine the Hanafi Response
The two shaykhs counter that the partitioners' act of distinguishing is not what is being testified; they testify about the co-owner's receipt (istifa'), which is an act of the co-owner. Hence no self-interest.
They also note that the work of distinguishing is conceded by both parties, so no need to testify about it.
5

If the partitioners were paid for their work, their testimony is not accepted by consensus because they have a financial stake in claiming performance.
This exception is mentioned by al-Tahawi and supported by some later scholars.

Core Idea
One heir cannot represent another in qisas because the right is by representation, not inheritance.

When multiple heirs share a right, can one of them litigate on behalf of all? The answer depends on whether the right is inherited property or a right that arises by representation (khilafah), as in qisas. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute: what happens when one heir has already presented evidence for qisas and an absent heir later appears? The ruling hinges on the nature of the right, and the two companions differ from Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle of representation among heirs
The passage first states a general rule: when heirs possess property through inheritance, any one heir can represent the others in litigation. This is because the right is jointly owned. But when the right is not inherited, such as in qisas, one heir cannot automatically represent others.
Key distinction: inheritance (wirathah) vs. representation (khilafah).
2

Abu Hanifa holds that if one heir presents evidence for intentional killing (qisas) while another heir is absent, the absent heir must re-present his own evidence when he appears. The first evidence only established the right of the present heir.
Abu Hanifa's dalil: qisas right is khilafah, not wirathah.
3
State the Sahibayn's opposing ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the absent heir does not need to re-present evidence; the first evidence suffices for all heirs. They analogize qisas to property claims where the right is common.
Sahibayn's dalil: the right is common among heirs, like money.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, a new trial is required when the absent heir appears; under the Sahibayn, the original trial's evidence is sufficient, saving time and resources.
Later Hanafi works favor the Sahibayn as mufta bihi.
5
Apply the distinction to accidental killing and debt
The passage clarifies that for accidental killing (khat'a) and debt, the right is property-based and inherited. Thus, one heir's evidence suffices for all, and no re-presentation is needed, aligning with the general principle.

Defines 'Aqila and refutes Shafi'i clan-based definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'Aqila, identify the Shafi'i counter-position, and explain the Hanafi proof based on 'Umar's practice and the principle of nasrah.

Core Idea
The 'Aqila are defined by effective assistance (nasrah), not fixed kinship—adapting to social organization.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi definition of al-'Aqila—the collective responsible for paying blood money (diyah). The Hanafi school defines it by the diwan (military register) for those registered, while Imam al-Shafi'i insists on clan (al-'ashira). Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafis apply the underlying principle (ma'na) over the specific historical form.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafis define al-'Aqila as the people of the diwan (military register) for those registered. This is the default ruling for those in the army.
Based on the practice of 'Umar (ra).
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the 'Aqila should be the clan (al-'ashira), because that was the practice in the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and no abrogation occurred.
3
Examine the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i position relies on the precedent of the Prophet's era, which they consider unchanged and binding.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that 'Umar's action was not abrogation but a fresh ijtihad applying the effective cause (illa) of assistance (nasrah). The Companions' silent consensus (ijma sukuti) validates this.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: a person in the diwan is liable with fellow diwan members; a clan member may not be liable if clan ties are weak. In modern contexts, the 'Aqila can be defined by employer, union, or other support network.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of Kitab al-Ma'aqil, defining the term 'Aqila (the collective responsible for paying blood money). The Hanafi position is that the 'Aqila are the people of the diwan—the military register—for those registered in it. Al-Shafi'i argues it should be the clan (al-'ashira) because that was the practice at the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and no abrogation is known. The Hanafi response: 'Umar's action in instituting the diwan, done in the presence of the Companions with no objection, did not abrogate a divine ruling but rather implemented the underlying principle: responsibility falls on those who provide assistance (nasrah). In the Prophet's time, assistance came through kinship and alliances; in 'Umar's time, it came through the diwan. Therefore, the ruling adapts to changing means of support. The same principle applies today: if people assist each other by craft, the 'Aqila are the people of that craft; if by alliance, then the people of that alliance. This is based on the principle of ma'na (effective cause) rather than mere form.

Key Terms
العاقلة (al-‘āqilah): The group responsible for paying the blood money (diyah) on behalf of a killer from among them.
الديوان (al-dīwān): The military register where names of soldiers receiving stipends are recorded.
أهل النصرة (ahl al-nuṣrah): Those who provide assistance and support to each other, which is the effective cause for 'aqila responsibility.

Payment of Diyah from 'Aqila Stipends Over Three Years
Ruling on collecting diyah from 'Aqila stipends in installments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the period and method of collecting diyah from the 'Aqila, identify the per-person cap, and distinguish the Shafi'i position on immediate payment.

Core Idea
Diyah is collected from the 'Aqila's annual stipends over three years; even when the killer pays personally, the installments remain.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies how diyah is collected from the 'Aqila (the support group of the killer). The Hanafi school structures payment over three years to ease the burden, using the annual stipends ('ata') of registered soldiers in the diwan. For those not in the diwan, the 'Aqila are the people of the same craft, and each member pays a capped amount over three years. The passage also addresses a key dispute with al-Shafi'i regarding when the killer pays from his own wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what is being ruled upon: the payment of diyah from the 'Aqila's stipends. It is an obligation (wajib) but structured to ease hardship.
2
Locate the payment method
The diyah is taken from the annual stipends of the 'Aqila over three years. This is the default method for those registered in the diwan.
3
Determine the per-person cap
For those not in the diwan, each member pays a total of three or four dirhams over three years. The most correct (asahh) statement is four dirhams total, i.e., one dirham and a third per year.
This cap prevents overburdening the 'Aqila's individual members.
4
Recognize the cross-madhhab dispute
When the killer pays from his own property (e.g., father killing son), the Hanafis maintain the three-year installment period, while al-Shafi'i requires immediate payment.
This shows the Hanafi principle of giving time even in direct liability.
5
Note the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf
Al-Quduri's narration limits each person to four dirhams per year, but the asahh position is four dirhams total over three years—a lower annual burden.
The tarjih is by the author of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah.

Commentary
This passage details the method of collecting diyah from the 'Aqila. The diyah is taken from the annual stipends ('ata') of the 'Aqila over a period of three years. If the total diyah is more or less than the total of three years' stipends, the payment is adjusted accordingly. For persons not registered in the diwan, each individual pays a fixed amount: three dirhams or four dirhams total over three years, meaning one dirham per year or one dirham plus a third per year. The author states that the latter (four dirhams total) is the most correct (asahh), citing that al-Quduri's narration does not allow exceeding four dirhams per person per year? Actually the text is ambiguous. The passage then repeats the principle that the 'Aqila are the people of the diwan, or if the assistance is by craft, then by craft. It also clarifies a dispute with al-Shafi'i: when the father kills his son, the diyah is upon the father's own property (not the 'Aqila), and the Hanafis say it is payable in three years, while al-Shafi'i says it is immediate. This is an important nuance: the payment period for diyah (three years) applies even when the killer pays from his own wealth, except that the Shafi'i school demands immediate payment. The text ends abruptly with a note that al-Quduri's narration limits the amount to four dirhams per person per year? The meaning is that each member of the 'Aqila (outside the diwan) should not be asked to pay more than four dirhams annually.

Key Terms
العطاء (al-‘aṭā’): The annual stipend given to soldiers registered in the diwan, from which diyah is deducted in installments.
ثلاث سنين (thalāth sinīn): Three years: the period over which diyah is paid in installments, whether by the 'Aqila or by the killer himself.
