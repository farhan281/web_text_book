Compensation for the Coerced Person in Manumission and Divorce
Ruling with proof (talil) on compensation for coercion in manumission and divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for a coerced person in manumission and divorce, and explain the reasoning based on the distinction between act (fi'l) and speech (qawl).

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a coerced person can recover financial loss from the coercer by treating the coerced act as destruction attributed to the coercer, but only for acts, not speech.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explores a nuanced area of Hanafi fiqh: when a person is coerced into an action that causes financial loss, who bears the liability? The answer distinguishes between physical acts (like manumitting a slave) and verbal acts (like pronouncing divorce). Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to causation in financial obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The passage covers two distinct cases: coerced manumission of a slave and coerced divorce before consummation.
Both involve a loss that falls on the coerced person, but the reasoning differs.
2
State the Ruling for Each
In manumission, the coerced person recovers the full value of the slave from the coercer. In divorce, if no consummation occurred, he recovers half the dower.
The amount recovered matches the financial loss incurred.
3
Understand the Reasoning for Manumission
Manumission is a physical act (fi'l) that destroys property (the slave's value). Since the act is attributed to the coercer by treating the coerced person as an instrument, the loss is attributed to the coercer.
This follows the principle that causation (itlaf) creates liability.
4
Understand the Reasoning for Divorce
Divorce is speech (qawl), which cannot be attributed to the coercer directly. However, half the dower was already vulnerable to being lost if separation came from the wife. By coercing the divorce, the coercer effectively 'destroys' the wife's right to that half, so it is treated as destruction attributed to him.
The half dower becomes confirmed by the divorce, and this confirmation is the loss.
5
Note the Implicit Distinction
The passage highlights a key difference: acts (fi'l) can be directly attributed to the causer; speech (qawl) cannot, but if it triggers a financial consequence that was already imperiled, that consequence can be attributed.
This is a unique Hanafi reasoning method that may differ from other schools.

Three Scenarios of Coerced Kufr and Their Rulings
Explaining three scenarios of coerced kufr and their rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three scenarios of coerced utterance of kufr and state the ruling on the marriage in each scenario.

Core Idea
Coercion does not automatically nullify marriage – inner conviction matters before Allah, while judges rule by the apparent.

The ruling on coerced apostasy is nuanced in Hanafi fiqh. The commentator Akhi Jalabi explains that whether a coerced utterance of disbelief affects the marriage depends on the person's inner state and awareness. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya lays out three distinct scenarios, each with a different outcome both judicially and religiously.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage divides coerced kufr into three cases based on the person's intent and awareness. List them in order.
The key distinction is whether the person intended the meaning of kufr or not.
2
Analyze the first scenario (no intent)
The person uttered without any thought of the meaning, seeking an escape from coercion. Ruling: not an apostate, marriage intact both qada'an and diyanatan.
Condition: heart is at ease with faith (mutma'inn bi al-iman).
3
Analyze the second scenario (intended the kufr)
The person intended the disbelief and could have chosen otherwise – he is like a voluntary apostate. Ruling: apostate both qada'an and diyanatan, marriage ends.
This scenario applies when he knew he could have endured but chose to utter contemptuously.
4
Analyze the third scenario (dispute over intention)
The person claims he was only reporting past words, but the judge goes by the apparent. Ruling: apostate qada'an (judge separates them) but not diyanatan.
The husband's word is accepted religiously because his claim is plausible; the qadi does not believe him judicially.
5
Understand the underlying principle
Apostasy is about belief, and coercion creates doubt. Therefore, separation is not established by doubt, and the husband's claim is accepted by istihsan.
Contrast with coercion to Islam, where the benefit of the doubt is given to Islam.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the ruling that a coerced utterance of disbelief does not cause separation from one's wife. The commentator, Akhi Jalabi, clarifies three distinct scenarios based on the inner state of the coerced person. First, if the person utters disbelief without any intention whatsoever, and his heart remains firm in faith, he is not considered an apostate in either the judicial or religious sense, and his marriage remains intact. Second, if he intends the disbelief and knows he could have endured the coercion but chose to utter contemptuously, he becomes an apostate in both senses and his wife is irrevocably separated. Third, if he claims afterward that he was only reporting past words and not originating disbelief, the qadi rules by the apparent—he is judged an apostate and separated judicially—but religiously he is not an apostate because his claim is plausible. The ruling is based on the principle that apostasy concerns inner belief, and coercion creates doubt about true intent. Consequently, when there is a dispute between spouses, the husband's word is accepted due to istihsan, since the utterance does not necessarily indicate actual disbelief. The passage also contrasts this with coercion to accept Islam, where the benefit of the doubt is given to Islam because of its superiority.

Confession of a Restricted Slave
Ruling on slave's confession regarding money and homicide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the confession of a restricted slave and explain why its effect differs for monetary vs. bodily claims using the rational proof of the slave's retained humanity.

Core Idea
A slave's confession binds his own person immediately for hadd/qawad, but is deferred for money until freedom.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya addresses a unique aspect of legal capacity in slavery: the restricted slave (al-`abd al-mahjur) retains partial humanity for bodily matters. While his master owns his property, his person remains his own. Hence, his confession regarding his own body is effective immediately, but regarding money, it is suspended until emancipation. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the Hanafi view of legal subjectivity in a state of dependency.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the Two Object Categories
Identify the two types of claims mentioned: monetary (e.g., debt) and bodily (hudud punishments, qawad – blood-money). The ruling treats them differently.
Bodily claims relate to the slave's person (dam), while monetary claims affect his master's property.
2
Understand the Basis of the Ruling
The master's ownership over the slave's property is the reason for deferral of monetary confessions. The slave's person, however, remains under the original ruling of humanity (asl al-adamiyya), so confessions about bodily matters are effective immediately.
The proof is rational: 'because regarding his blood, he remains on the original state of humanity.'
3
Apply the Distinction to the Two Cases
For a debt confession: deferred to after emancipation (ukhira ila 'itqihi). For a hadd or qawad confession: immediate execution (ajjil).
4
Contrast with the Master's Confession
The master cannot confess regarding the slave's bodily rights (hadd/qawad) because these are exclusive to the slave's person. This reinforces the idea that the slave retains independent legal capacity for these matters.
5
Examine the Rational Proof (Dalil `Aqli)
The proof is based on the inherent humanity of the slave, which is not extinguished by slavery. The original human status (asl al-adamiyya) serves as the operative cause ('illa) for immediate effect of confessions on the person.
This rational proof distinguishes Hanafi thought from schools that treat the slave as entirely devoid of capacity.

Immediate vs Deferred Dispositions: Definitions and Rulings
Definitions and general rulings for immediate and deferred dispositions in illness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between immediate (munajjaz) and deferred (mudaf) dispositions and state the rule for each in health and terminal illness, including exceptions for acknowledgment of debt and marriage.

Core Idea
Immediate dispositions are from the whole estate in health, from one-third in illness; deferred dispositions are always from one-third.

This opening part of the chapter on manumission in illness introduces two key categories of legal dispositions: those that take effect immediately and those contingent upon death. Understanding these definitions is essential for applying the correct limitation of one-third, protecting the rights of heirs while allowing certain acts of charity.

Guided Path
1
Define the two terms
Identify the Arabic terms al-munajjaz and al-mudaf ila al-mawt. Munajjaz takes effect immediately; mudaf takes effect after death.
Note the key distinction: timing of effect.
2
Apply the general rule for immediate dispositions
If the person is healthy at the time, it's from the whole estate; if in marad al-mawt (terminal illness), it's from one-third.
This is the default rule.
3
Note the exceptions
Acknowledgment of debt (iqrar) and marriage (nikah) with a proper dower are not limited to one-third even in illness because they are not purely gratuitous.
These are considered transfers of right rather than charity.
4
Apply the rule for deferred dispositions
Deferred dispositions are always from one-third, whether made in health or illness.
They are essentially wills (wasaya).
5
Understand the effect of recovery
If the person recovers from the illness, the sickness is treated like health, so prior immediate dispositions become valid from the whole estate.
This applies only to immediate dispositions; deferred remain from one-third.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the chapter of manumission during terminal illness (marad al-mawt). It opens by distinguishing two types of dispositions: immediate (munajjaz) and deferred to death (mudaf). For immediate dispositions, the ruling depends on the state of health at the time of the contract. If the person is healthy, the disposition takes effect from the entire estate; if in terminal illness, it is limited to one-third of the estate, like a will. The key exception is that acknowledgment of debt (iqrar) and marriage with a proper dower (mahr al-mithl) are executed from the entire estate even during illness because they are not purely gratuitous. For deferred dispositions (e.g., 'you are free after my death'), the ruling is always from one-third, regardless of whether the person was healthy or ill when making the statement. Then the commentary discusses the scenario where a person recovers from the illness: such a sickness is considered like health, so prior dispositions become valid from the entire estate. It then lists transactions that are treated like wills (manumission, preferential sale, gift, guarantee). Finally, it provides an example of conflict between a preferential sale (muhaba) and a subsequent manumission. In the case where he makes a preferential sale first and then manumits, the preferential sale is given priority (the third is used for compensation to the buyer), and the manumitted slave must work to earn his freedom. It opens by distinguishing two types of dispositions: immediate (munajjaz) and deferred to death (mudaf).
