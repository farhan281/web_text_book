Zakah Given by Mukatab to Master: Lawfulness After Incapacity
Ruling on lawfulness of zakah given to master by mukatab after incapacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that zakah transferred via kitaba becomes lawful for a wealthy master, and explain the reasoning using the hadith of Barirah.

Core Idea
Zakah taken by a mukatab becomes lawful for his master when paid as kitaba price, even if the master is wealthy.

This lesson examines a nuanced scenario: a mukatab (a slave under a contract of emancipation) receives zakah as a qualified recipient, then pays it to his master as part of the kitaba installments. Later, the mukatab becomes incapacitated, revealing that the wealthy master indirectly received zakah. The Hanafi school rules that the master may keep the money lawfully, based on a prophetic principle and sound reasoning. We will unpack the proof and the subtle differences between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
Recognize that the mukatab is eligible for zakah (needy), the master is wealthy and not a zakah recipient. The payment is for the kitaba price.
The mukatab is considered poor enough for zakah in the Hanafi school.
2
Recognize the apparent problem
At first glance, a wealthy person receiving zakah is prohibited. Here, the master received zakah indirectly through the mukatab.
The prohibition of giving zakah to the wealthy is established.
3
Apply the hadith of Barirah
The Prophet said of Barirah's charity: 'It is charity for her, but for us it is a gift.' This shows that the same item can change legal status when ownership transfers legitimately.
Hadith reference: Bukhari and Muslim.
4
Distinguish the master's taking
The master took the money as a substitute ('iwad) for the emancipation, not as charity. The impurity (khubth) is in the act of receiving charity, which the master did not perform.
The master never accepted the money as a charity recipient.
5
Understand the intra-Hanafi difference
Imam Muhammad holds that incapacity converts ownership (making the master's taking retroactively valid). Abu Yusuf holds that the master's ownership is confirmed from the start; both agree on lawfulness.
The difference is jurisprudential, not practical.

And what he gave to his master from charity becomes lawful, even if he becomes incapacitated: meaning, if the master is not a recipient of zakah, and the mukatab takes zakah because he is among the recipients, then pays it to the master as part of the kitaba payment, then becomes incapacitated, it becomes apparent that the master took zakah while wealthy, yet it is lawful for him, because he took it as compensation for freedom at the time of taking, and the slave had taken it as charity, and the Prophet said: 'For her it is charity, for us it is a gift.' Commentary
This passage deals with a scenario where a mukatab (a slave under kitaba) takes zakah because he is eligible (being in need), and then pays that amount to his master as part of his kitaba installment. Later, the mukatab becomes incapacitated (fails to pay and the kitaba is rescinded). At that point, it is revealed that the master, who is wealthy, received zakah indirectly. The ruling is that the master may keep the money lawfully. The reasoning is that the master took it as a substitute for the emancipation (the kitaba payment), not as zakah. The Prophet's hadith about Barirah's meat supports the principle that when an item changes from charity to gift, it becomes lawful for the recipient. Intellectually, the impurity lies in the act of the poor person receiving charity (due to the sense of degradation), not in the item itself. Since the master did not take it as a recipient of charity, it is permissible. This is compared to a traveler who receives charity and then reaches home, or a poor man who becomes rich—they may keep the charity because the prohibition of takings applies only at the time of receipt. The difference between Imam Muhammad and Abu Yusuf lies in the effect of incapacity on ownership: Muhammad holds that ownership converts at incapacity, while Abu Yusuf holds that the master's ownership is confirmed retroactively, but both agree on the lawfulness.

Key Terms
زكاة (zakah): Obligatory alms specifically; here it is the zakah that the mukatab receives as a qualified recipient.
حيلة (hila): Not explicitly stated, but the scenario is a permissible means for the poor slave to pay his kitaba using zakah.

Key Takeaway
Zakah paid by a mukatab to his master as part of kitaba remains lawful for the master if the mukatab becomes incapacitated, because the master took it as a substitute for emancipation, supported by the hadith of Barirah.

Permissibility of Locusts and Fish Without Slaughter
General exemption ruling for fish and locusts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that fish and locusts are halal without ritual slaughter and identify the scope of this exception.

Core Idea
Fish and locusts are halal without dhabh – they are exceptions to the slaughter requirement.

In this lesson, we examine a clear ruling from the matn that fish and locusts do not require ritual slaughter to be permissible. This is a well-known Hanafi position based on the principle that certain creatures are exempt from dhabh. Understanding this exception helps avoid confusion about dietary laws.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subjects and the condition
The text mentions locusts (الجراد) and all types of fish (أنواع السمك) and states they are permissible without slaughter (بلا ذكاة).
This applies to all fish, not just certain species.
2
Recognize the general rule
Land animals require ritual slaughter (dhabh) to be halal. Fish and locusts are exceptions.
The exception is based on hadith and practice.
3
Understand the legal basis
The Hanafi school derives this from the principle that sea creatures and locusts are not subject to the same requirements as land animals.
This is a ruling from the zahir al-riwayah.
4
Note the scope
The ruling covers all fish and locusts regardless of how they die, as long as they are not found dead floating with belly up (a separate ruling).
This matches the commentary that mentions the exception.

Kaffarah for Accidental Killing: Components and Conditions
Ruling on expiation for manslaughter with proofs and validity conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the components of kaffarah for accidental killing according to the Hanafi school, distinguish between valid and invalid slaves for expiation, and cite the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for accidental killing is limited to freeing a believing slave or fasting two consecutive months; feeding is not an option.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on expiation for accidental killing. The text from the commentary clarifies the obligatory components—freeing a believing slave, or fasting if unable—and establishes that no other alternatives, like feeding the poor, are valid. It also addresses which slaves qualify, including an infant of Muslim parents, but excluding a fetus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognise that accidental killing requires kaffarah, which is wajib (obligatory) upon the killer.
The passage begins with 'وكفارتهما' referring to the expiation for two types of killing, presumably accidental.
2
List the components in order
The primary component is freeing a believing slave. If unable, then fasting two consecutive months. No feeding is permitted.
The sequence is important: slave first, then fasting.
3
Analyse the proof from Quran
The verse 4:92 is cited: 'fatahriru raqabatin mu'minah' – freeing a believing slave. The command indicates obligation.
The verse also mentions diyah, but that is separate from kaffarah.
4
Examine the condition for the slave
The slave must be believing (mu'min) and alive. An infant of Muslim parents qualifies because it follows the better parent in faith. A fetus does not qualify because its life and soundness are unknown.
The text uses the analogy of a limb to explain why a fetus is not a valid slave.
5
Understand the exclusion of feeding
The commentary argues that feeding is not included because no textual evidence supports it, and legal measures are known only by divine prescription (tawqif).
The analogy with conditional statements reinforces that if feeding were intended, it would have been mentioned.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh explains the ruling on expiation (kaffarah) for accidental killing. The text states that the expiation consists of freeing a believing slave. If one cannot afford it, then fasting two consecutive months. There is no option for feeding the poor, because no textual evidence supports it, and legal measures are known only by divine prescription. The author argues using analogy with conditional statements: if a condition includes a consequence, nothing else is implied. The passage also addresses the validity of freeing a suckling infant in expiation: it is valid if one parent is Muslim because the infant follows the better parent in faith. However, freeing a fetus is not valid because its life and soundness are unknown, and it is considered a limb in some respects, so it does not fall under the absolute text that requires a 'slave'. The commentary cites Quranic verses as proofs.

Key Terms
كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation for accidental killing, consisting of freeing a slave or fasting.
عتق (itq): Freeing a slave; the primary means of expiation in this context.
ولاء (wala'): Consecutive (two months of fasting without interruption).
