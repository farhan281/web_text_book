Eating Forbidden Food Under Coercion: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Sin and Permission
Ruling on coercion to eat carrion, blood, pork, or drink wine

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when coercion permits consuming haram food and explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding sin.

Core Idea
Only threat of death or amputation permits haram food; lesser coercion does not remove prohibition.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains a key Hanafi ruling on coercion (ikrah) to consume prohibited items. It distinguishes between compelling and non-compelling coercion and introduces an important intra-madhhab debate: if a person refuses and is killed, do they sin? The answer affects how we understand necessity and concession in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Classify the coercion
Identify whether the coercion is mulji' (threat of death or amputation) or ghayr mulji' (imprisonment, beating, shackling). This determines permissibility.
2
State the ruling for each type
For ghayr mulji', consumption remains haram; for mulji', it becomes permissible (even wajib to preserve life).
3
Identify the intra-madhhab dispute
Both imams agree on permissibility under compelling coercion, but differ on the consequence of refusal. Abu Hanifa: sins if refuses and dies; Abu Yusuf: no sin.
4
Trace the proofs
Abu Hanifa: analogy to starvation (must eat to live; refusing makes one an accomplice). Abu Yusuf: Quran 2:173 removes sin for the one in necessity.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa, as per the commentary.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed Hanafi ruling on consuming prohibited items (carrion, blood, pork, wine) under coercion. It distinguishes between types of coercion: imprisonment, beating, or shackling do not make consumption permissible, whereas the threat of death or amputation does. The reasoning is that these items are only excepted from prohibition in genuine necessity, and non-compelling coercion does not rise to that level. The commentary also addresses a retroactive ownership issue in guarantee cases. A key point is the intra-madhhab dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that if a person refuses to eat despite the threat of death and is killed, they sin because they are aiding in their own destruction. Abu Yusuf disagrees, considering it a concession (rukhsah) where the prohibition remains but sin is removed. The passage cites the Quranic verse (2:173) to support both views. The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa, as per the text.

Key Terms
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion that forces a person to act against their will.
ضرورة (darurah): Necessity that permits otherwise prohibited acts, such as fear of death or loss of limb.
رخصة (rukhsah): A concession that removes sin while the prohibition remains in effect.

The Meaning of Tayyib in Farming: Prayer and Ethics
Ethical exhortation on the meaning of 'tayyib' for farmers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi understanding of 'tayyib' in the context of farming and list the recommended practices at sowing time.

Core Idea
Your food's blessing depends on your prayer – delaying salah for farming makes your crop not 'tayyib'.

This passage from al-Zahidi explains the deeper meaning of 'halalan tayyiban' (lawful and good) in the Quran. It teaches that the spiritual quality of one's earnings is tied to fulfilling religious obligations and ethical dealings, not just the source of the food. We will explore what makes a farmer's produce truly 'tayyib' and the sunnah acts that bring blessing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Key Quranic Verse
Locate the verse 'كُلُوا مِمَّا فِي الْأَرْضِ حَلَالًا طَيِّبًا' (Eat of what is in the earth, lawful and good). Understand that the passage interprets 'tayyib' beyond external halal.
The verse is from Surah al-Baqarah 2:168.
2
List Actions That Make Crop Not Tayyib
From the passage, note the actions that negate 'tayyib': delaying prayer for farming, cultivating without purity, withholding wages, delaying debt payment, paying without consent.
These all relate to neglecting religious duties or injustice.
3
Recognize the Recommended (Mustahabb) Acts
Identify the sunnah acts at sowing: being in a state of purity, praying two rak'ahs, making a supplication, and sending blessings upon the Prophet.
These are recommended, not obligatory.
4
Understand the Promise of Blessing
Note the outcome: if one performs these acts, Allah protects the crop from afflictions and blesses it. This is a spiritual incentive.
The promise is from al-Jalabi's commentary.

Al-Zahidi explains that the goodness (tayyib) of one's crop is connected to fulfilling religious obligations, especially performing prayers on time in congregation. Timing of Udhiyah Slaughter: After the Eid Prayer in Cities
Stipulates the earliest permissible time for udhiyah slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the earliest permissible time for udhiyah slaughter according to the Hanafi school, cite the hadith proof, and explain why the rule differs for rural dwellers.

Core Idea
The udhiyah slaughter in cities is valid only after the Eid prayer; before prayer it must be repeated.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies a key timing condition for the udhiyah sacrifice. While many assume the sacrifice can begin at dawn of Eid, the Hanafi school strictly stipulates that in cities (amsar) the slaughter must occur after the Eid prayer. The proof is a clear prophetic command. Understanding this nuance ensures your sacrifice is valid and fulfills the sunnah correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Find the exact condition stated: the earliest time for udhiyah slaughter is after the prayer if slaughtered in a city (misr). Note that 'after the prayer' means after the Eid prayer on Yawm al-Nahr.
The prayer referred to is the Eid al-Adha prayer.
2
Locate the proof
The passage cites two hadith from Bara' ibn 'Azib: one commands repeating the slaughter if done before the prayer, and the other establishes the order: first prayer, then sacrifice.
These hadith are in Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Analyze the legal implication
The imperative 'falyu'id' (let him repeat) indicates that slaughter before prayer is invalid (batil) and must be repeated. The Hanafi position is that this timing is a condition (shart) for validity in cities.
The ruling is wajib in the sense of being an obligatory condition.
4
Note the exception for rural areas
The commentary explains that the timing condition applies only to city dwellers (ahl al-misr) who are obligated to pray Eid. Rural dwellers (ahl al-sawad) are not bound by this delay because they may not have access to the congregational prayer.
For villagers, slaughter before Eid prayer is permissible.
5
Apply the ruling
A city dweller who slaughters before the Eid prayer must repeat the sacrifice after the prayer. A rural dweller may slaughter earlier, but the preferred time is still after the prayer if possible.
The fatwa is according to the matn: after prayer in cities.

Time and Substitution in Udhiyah: Hanafi Rulings and Proofs
Rulings on udhiyah time and meat substitution with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the beginning and end times for udhiyah according to the Hanafi school and explain the ruling on substituting the meat of a minor's sacrifice.

Core Idea
Udhiyah time begins after Eid prayer for city dwellers; substitution of meat must be for benefitting in kind, not cash.

This passage from a commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah clarifies the temporal boundaries of the udhiyah sacrifice and the conditions for handling the meat when the sacrifice is on behalf of a minor. It also addresses a key inter-madhhab dispute with Malik and Shafi'i regarding whether the sacrifice may be performed before the imam's sacrifice. The discussion is grounded in hadith and qiyas, showing how Hanafi jurists derive practical rulings from revealed sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main issues
The passage addresses (a) the timing of udhiyah—when does the period for sacrifice begin and end—and (b) what is permissible to do with the meat when the sacrifice is for a minor (i.e., substitution).
These are separate rulings but linked by the discussion of the minor's property.
2
Analyze the timing ruling
Note the distinction between city dweller (after Eid prayer) and rural dweller (after dawn on the 10th). The proof is the hadith of Bara' ibn 'Azib: 'Whoever sacrifices before the prayer should repeat…' which indicates that the valid time starts after prayer.
The end time is before sunset of the third day of Tashreeq (12th Dhul-Hijjah).
3
Understand the substitution ruling
The text uses qiyas: just as the skin of the sacrificed animal can be exchanged for something beneficial in kind (e.g., a bag), the meat can also be exchanged for something beneficial in kind. Exchanging for cash (dirhams) is not allowed because that would be acquiring wealth and is akin to selling.
The obligation on the minor is the shedding of blood, which is completed; any charity from the meat would be a voluntary act from the minor's property, which is not permitted.
4
Examine the inter-madhhab dispute
Malik and Shafi'i hold that the sacrifice must be after the imam's sacrifice. The Hanafi position is that the hadith is general and does not require that. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that a sacrifice performed before the imam's sacrifice is valid in the Hanafi school but invalid according to Shafi'i.
The Hanafi response relies on the apparent meaning of the hadith and the practice of the Companions.
5
Consider additional nuances
The passage also discusses how changes in wealth, birth, or death during the days of Tashreeq affect obligation. Exception cases: if there is no imam due to fitnah, sacrifice after dawn before Eid prayer suffices; if the imam prayed on what turned out to be 'Arafah, the sacrifice is still valid.
These show the flexibility of Hanafi rulings in unusual circumstances.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah covering the rulings of Udhiyah (sacrificial animal). It opens by discussing the permissibility of substituting the meat of the sacrifice for a minor. The author explains that it is allowed to exchange the meat for something that can be benefited from in itself, by analogy with the skin which can be made into a bag. The substitute takes the ruling of the original. However, exchanging for dirhams is not allowed because that is wealth and would be like selling. The fatwa is on what is in the matn (text). Then the beginning time of sacrifice is given: after the Eid prayer on the day of Nahr for city dwellers. The evidence is the hadith from Bara' ibn 'Azib that the Prophet ﷺ said whoever sacrifices before the prayer should repeat, and that the first ritual is prayer then sacrifice. For rural areas where there is no Eid prayer, the time begins at dawn on the day of Nahr. The end time is just before sunset on the third day of Tashreeq. The passage also discusses how status changes affect obligation: if a person becomes poor during the days, the sacrifice is not obligatory; if a rich person becomes poor, it is not required; if a child is born on the last day, it becomes obligatory; if a person dies on the last day, it is not obligatory on his estate. The author also addresses a dispute with Malik and Shafi'i: they hold that sacrifice is not valid after the prayer before the imam's sacrifice. The Hanafi position is that it is valid, and the hadith proof is presented. Additionally, it mentions that if the imam prayed without wudu, the prayer is repeated but not the sacrifice. If there is no imam due to fitnah, sacrificing after dawn before Eid prayer suffices. If the imam was mistaken about the day (thinking it was Eid but it was 'Arafah), both prayer and sacrifice are valid.

أيام التشريق (ayyam al-tashreeq): The three days following Eid al-Adha (11th, 12th, 13th Dhul-Hijjah) during which slaughter is allowed.
ذبح (dhabh): The act of slaughtering the animal in the prescribed manner.
تموّل (tamawwul): Acquiring wealth or property; used here to describe the prohibition of exchanging meat for cash as it would turn the sacrifice into a commercial transaction.

Ruling on Attending Gatherings with Forbidden Entertainment
Rules for attending feasts where forbidden play or singing occurs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on attending a gathering with forbidden entertainment, distinguishing between a role model and a non-role model, and apply the conditions to a given scenario.

Core Idea
A sunnah (accepting invitation) is not abandoned for a bid'ah, except that a role model must leave to avoid being imitated in sin.

When invited to a feast, responding is a stressed sunnah. But what if the feast includes forbidden amusement like singing or musical instruments? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling based on whether you knew beforehand, whether you can stop it, and whether others look up to you. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains the conditions and proofs.

Guided Path
1
Determine ruling by prior knowledge
If you knew of the forbidden entertainment before attending, attendance is not permissible (haram). This is the first principle stated in the text.
The text says 'إن عَلِمَ قبل الحضور أنَّ هناك لهوا لا يجوز الحضور' – if he knew beforehand, attendance is not allowed.
2
Assess ability to prevent
If you discover the entertainment only after arriving, and you are able to stop it, you must do so. If unable, then check your social standing.
The passage states: 'فإن كان قادراً على المنع يمنع' – if able, he prevents.
3
Evaluate role model status
If you are a role model (muqtada – someone whose actions are followed), you must leave so that others do not imitate you. If you are not a role model, you may sit and eat because the sunnah of accepting the invitation overrides the presence of bid'ah.
The text distinguishes: 'إن كان الرجل مقتدى يخرج؛ لئلا يقتدي الناس به، وإن لم يكن مقتدى، فإن قعد وأكل جاز'.
4
Examine the proof (dalil)
The text cites the hadith: 'Whoever does not answer the invitation has disobeyed Allah and His Messenger.' This shows that answering is a strong sunnah, almost wajib. The commentator defends the permissibility even though the act is a sunnah, not fard, by noting the severe warning.
The hadith is from Sahih Muslim, Kitab al-Nikah. The wajh al-dalalah is the threat of disobedience, indicating importance.
5
Understand the exception and inward condition
Not all play is haram – the Prophet exempted three activities. Also, even when sitting is permissible, one must remain inwardly detached, not deriving pleasure, and denounce the sin in the heart. If the entertainment is in the same room as the food, a non-role model should also not sit.
The text mentions the three exceptions and Abu Hanifa's sabr (patience) meaning he turned away internally.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on disliked matters deals with the ruling of attending a gathering (such as a wedding feast) where there is forbidden entertainment like singing or playing. The author begins by stating a principle: if a person knows beforehand that there will be forbidden amusement, he must not attend. If he discovers it only after arriving, then if he is able to stop it, he must do so. If he cannot stop it, then his further action depends on his social status: if he is a person whose actions are followed (muttabi'), he must leave so that others do not imitate him and think it permissible. If he is not such a role model, he may sit and eat, because responding to the invitation is a sunnah that should not be abandoned simply because of an innovation (bid'ah) present. The author compares this to a funeral prayer (salat al-janaza) where wailing (niyahah) occurs: one still prays because the prayer is a farḍ, while here the invitation is a sunnah. However, a commentator notes that this analogy is not perfect because the prayer is obligatory while the invitation is merely sunnah, but he responds that this particular sunnah is so emphasized that it has the force of an obligation (wajib) due to the prophetic threat: 'Whoever does not answer the invitation has disobeyed Abu al-Qasim.' The passage then narrates an incident from Abu Hanifa: he was once tested with such a situation and 'was patient.' This statement indicates the prohibition of the amusement itself (it is haram). Some object: how could Abu Hanifa sit amidst haram? The answer is that his patience was internal: he remained physically present but turned away from the forbidden, denounced it in his heart, and derived no pleasure from it. This shows that for a common person who is not a role model, sitting and eating is permissible provided he maintains inward detachment. The text also clarifies that not all play is haram: the Quranic verse about worldly life being 'play and diversion' is descriptive, not prohibitive. And the Prophet exempted three forms of play: training a horse, archery, and playing with one's family. Thus, the blanket prohibition applies only to specifically forbidden forms of amusement, such as musical instruments and singing that lead to sin.

Key Terms

Ruling on Silk Mixtures in Garments
Explanation of the three types of silk mixtures and their rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of silk mixtures and state the Hanafi ruling for each, explaining the principle of attributing the ruling to the weft.

Core Idea
In mixed silk garments, the weft decides the ruling—the last attribute controls the legal effect.

The permissibility of silk for men is a nuanced issue in Hanafi fiqh. This passage addresses mixed fabrics where silk is combined with other fibers. The key principle is that the legal ruling follows the effective cause (ʿilla), and when a cause has multiple attributes, it attaches to the last one to occur. In weaving, the weft (liḥma) is the final component, so it determines whether the garment is considered silk or not. This leads to different rulings for three types of mixtures, with an exception for war due to necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three mixture types
List the three possible combinations: (1) warp silk, weft other; (2) warp other, weft silk; (3) both silk. The passage focuses on the first two.
Type 1 is the one explicitly described; type 3 is the opposite.
2
Understand the ʿilla principle
The ruling attaches to the ʿilla (effective cause). When the ʿilla has two attributes (warp and weft), the attribute that occurs last in existence (the weft) is decisive. This is a Hanafi usul principle.
The weft is the final thread woven; it gives the fabric its identity.
3

Since the weft is not silk, the ʿilla for silk prohibition is absent. The garment is mubah (permissible) for men in all situations—even outside war.

4

Here the weft is silk, so the ʿilla for silk prohibition is present. For men, wearing such a garment is makruh tahrimi except in war. In war, it becomes mubah due to necessity (instilling awe in the enemy).
Necessity (ḍarūra) overrides the prohibition.
5
Contrast with other schools
The Shafiʿi school generally prohibits any silk garment for men, regardless of warp/weft distinction. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different usul methods.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi within the school.

This passage from the Hanafi sharh clarifies the rulings of looking for women by analogy to men, grounded in customary practice (‘urf) and necessity (darura). The commentator uses the widespread custom of women's bathhouses as proof that such looking is permissible. The necessity (darura) of women needing to bathe and adorn themselves serves as a supporting rationale, especially since men have alternative bathing options.

Ruling on female slave travel with qiyas and temporal restriction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a female slave traveling without a mahram, identify the qiyas used to justify it, and explain the temporal condition that later restricted the ruling.

Core Idea
A female slave is legally equivalent to a mahram for travel because touching her is like touching a mahram.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah presents the Hanafi ruling that a female slave (amah) and umm walad may travel without a mahram. The justification is a qiyas (analogy): since looking at and touching a female slave is permissible for non-related men in the same way as a mahram, she is legally treated as a mahram for travel. However, the text adds a critical temporal condition: this ruling applied in the early time of righteousness, but in later times of widespread corruption, it is no longer permissible. Understanding this ruling requires grasping both the qiyas and the socio-legal condition that modifies its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Scope
Read the passage and determine the default ruling for a female slave (amah) and umm walad traveling without a mahram. Note that the ruling extends to mukataba, mudabbara, and partially freed slaves according to Imam Abu Hanifa.
The passage begins with a general statement that touching their limbs is like touching mahrams, implying permissibility.
2
Trace the Proof: Qiyas
Identify the analogy: the female slave is legally equivalent to a mahram in matters of looking and touching. Since touching a mahram is permissible, the same applies to the female slave. This analogy justifies travel without a mahram.
The qiyas is based on the equality of permissibility of touch, which is the 'illa (effective cause).
3
Analyze the Temporal Condition
Note the condition from al-Kafi and al-Nihayah: the ruling applied in early times when righteous people were dominant, but in later times of prevailing corruption, it is impermissible. Understand that this is a sociological condition affecting the application of the qiyas.
This condition shows that Hanafi rulings can change based on societal context, reflecting the principle of 'taghayyur al-ahkam bi taghayyur al-zaman.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi
Identify the operative ruling for fatwa today: the later prohibition due to corruption is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) in later times. The passage attributes this to Shaykh al-Islam.
The temporal condition is not a part of the original qiyas but a later restriction, showing the evolution of fatwa.
5
Summarize the Legal Status
Conclude: In principle, a female slave is like a mahram for travel based on qiyas, but the ruling is conditioned by the moral state of society. In our time, the default is impermissibility unless the environment is overwhelmingly righteous.
This illustrates the Hanafi use of istihsan (juristic preference) to override qiyas when circumstances change.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between immediate (munjaz) and deferred (muḍāf) gratuitous transactions in the context of death illness, and state the correct source of payment (all wealth vs. one-third).

Core Idea
An immediate gift in fatal illness is limited to one-third; a deferred gift always is; debt acknowledgment and proper marriage are from all wealth.

This passage opens a new chapter on manumission during illness ('itq fi al-marḍ). The Hanafi school lays down a nuanced rule: the source of payment depends on whether the transaction is immediate or deferred to death, and whether it is truly a gift. We will trace the general rule, its exceptions, and the reasoning behind the classification.

Guided Path
1
Categorize the transaction
Identify whether the transaction is immediate (munjaz – effective now) or deferred to death (muḍāf ilā al-mawt – effective after death, e.g., 'you are free after I die'). This determines the baseline rule.
The author also mentions that deferred acts are always treated as bequests (waṣiyyah).
2
Apply the health/illness condition for immediate acts
For immediate acts, check the person's state at the time of the transaction: if healthy (not in death illness), the act draws from the entire estate; if in death illness (marad al-mawt), it draws only from one-third of the estate.
Death illness is an illness from which the person is expected to die and does die within a year.
3

Deferred-to-death acts always draw from one-third of the estate, regardless of whether they were made in health or illness. This is because they are essentially bequests taking effect after death.
This is stricter than immediate acts, but consistent with the intent of limiting posthumous gifts.

Not all immediate transactions in illness are limited to one-third. Two key exceptions: (1) acknowledgment of debt (iqrār bi al-dayn) – because it is a form of testimony, not a gift; (2) marriage (nikāḥ) with a proper dower (mahr al-mithl) – because marriage is not purely a gift. These are valid from the entire estate.
The common element: both lack the pure gratuitous (tabarru‘) nature. They involve rights or obligations beyond mere donation.
5

The core rule: any gratuitous transaction (tabarru‘) that is immediate in death illness is limited to one-third; deferred acts always are. Non-gratuitous acts (like iqrār and nikāḥ) are unaffected. This distinction prevents injustice to heirs while honoring the sick person's intentions.
This rule is unique in balancing the right of heirs and the freedom of the dying. It is operative (muftā bihī) in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Hidayah deals with the rulings for a khuntha (hermaphrodite/intersex person) in two main parts: first, how to distinguish whether the khuntha is male or female based on signs and the dispute between Abu Hanifah and the two companions regarding frequency of urination; second, the prayer rulings for the muskil (ambiguous) khuntha.

The first ruling is about identifying the gender of a khuntha: if he urinates from both organs, the ruling is based on which organ he urinates from first (asbaq). If both are equal in timing, then the khuntha is considered muskil (ambiguous). Here, Abu Hanifah holds that frequency of urination is not considered; only precedence matters. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say frequency should be considered, because more frequent urination indicates the strength of that organ and its being the original channel. Abu Hanifah's counterargument is that frequency does not necessarily indicate strength; it could be due to one channel being wider and the other narrower. If urine emerges equally from both, there is consensus that the person is muskil.

After puberty, certain signs definitively indicate gender: growing a beard, having a wet dream, or being able to have intercourse with a woman indicates the person is male. Developing breasts, having soft breasts, menstruating, becoming pregnant, or being penetrated (passively) indicates the person is female. If both sets of signs appear together, or none appear, the person remains muskil.

The passage then gives the ruling for the muskil khuntha in prayer: he must stand between the rows of men and women to avoid corrupting either gender's prayer by potential close proximity to the opposite gender. If he mistakenly stands in the women's row, he must repeat his prayer (since he might be a man). If he stands in the men's row, those men adjacent to him and directly behind him must repeat their prayers (since they might have been in close proximity to a woman). He also prays with a veil (qina') like a woman.

Throughout, the principle is precaution (ihtiyat) - where there is doubt about a ruling, one takes the safer option to ensure validity.

The author also mentions a discussion about a newborn with no external genitalia at all (only an opening in the navel), citing a statement from Ash-Sha'bi that such a person gets half the inheritance of a male and half of a female, similar to a known khuntha.

Key Terms
الخنثى (al-khuntha): A person born with ambiguous genitalia – either having both male and female organs or neither.
الأسبق (al-asbaq): The earlier in time; referring to which organ the person first urinated from.
الكثرة (al-kathrah): Frequency of urination from a particular organ.

Prayer Rulings for the Ambiguous Khuntha (al-Mushkil)
Rulings on prayer for the ambiguous khuntha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the precautionary prayer rules for a muskil khuntha, including standing position, repetition requirements for oneself and others, and use of a veil.

Core Idea
The ambiguous khuntha prays between the rows of men and women to avoid corrupting anyone's prayer.

This passage explains the practical prayer rulings for a khuntha whose gender remains ambiguous (mushkil) even after puberty and the appearance of signs. The guiding principle is precaution (ihtiyat): wherever there is doubt, one takes the safest option. The muskil khuntha must stand between the men's and women's rows to avoid the risk of invalidating his own or others' prayers through proximity to the opposite gender.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling scenario
The khuntha is muskil (ambiguous) – either no signs appeared or both male and female signs appeared together, leaving gender undetermined.
If gender becomes clear after puberty, he is no longer muskil and prays as a man or woman.
2
Rule 1: Standing position in congregational prayer
The muskil khuntha must stand between the men's row and women's row, because if he were in the men's row, he might be a woman and thus corrupt the prayer of men near him (by being in close proximity to a non-mahram). Similarly, if he were in the women's row, he might be a man and corrupt the women's prayer.
The principle: 'احتمال أنه امرأة فلا يتخلّل الرجال كيلا تفسد صلاتهم' (probability that he is a woman, so he should not intermingle with men lest their prayer be corrupted).
3
Rule 2: Consequence if he stands in the wrong row
If he stands in the women's row, he must repeat his prayer (because he might be a man and his prayer might be invalid due to proximity). If he stands in the men's row, the men directly beside him and behind him opposite must repeat their prayers, because they might have been praying next to a woman.
The khuntha himself does not repeat if he stands in the men's row; only the adjacent men repeat.
4
Rule 3: He prays with a veil (qina')
The muskil khuntha prays with a veil like a woman, as a precaution in case he is female, covering her hair during prayer.
This is an additional precaution to ensure the prayer's validity.
5
Understand the underlying principle
All these rules are built on the principle of precaution (الاحتياط) in matters of worship where doubt exists. The text says: 'الأصلُ فيه أن يؤخذ فيه بالأحوط والأوثق في أمور الدين' (the principle is to take the precautionary and most reliable course in religious matters).
This principle also applies to other rulings for the muskil, like inheritance and dress.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Hidayah deals with the rulings for a khuntha (hermaphrodite/intersex person) in two main parts: first, how to distinguish whether the khuntha is male or female based on signs and the dispute between Abu Hanifah and the two companions regarding frequency of urination; second, the prayer rulings for the muskil (ambiguous) khuntha.

The first ruling is about identifying the gender of a khuntha: if he urinates from both organs, the ruling is based on which organ he urinates from first (asbaq). If both are equal in timing, then the khuntha is considered muskil (ambiguous). Here, Abu Hanifah holds that frequency of urination is not considered; only precedence matters. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say frequency should be considered, because more frequent urination indicates the strength of that organ and its being the original channel. Abu Hanifah's counterargument is that frequency does not necessarily indicate strength; it could be due to one channel being wider and the other narrower. If urine emerges equally from both, there is consensus that the person is muskil.

After puberty, certain signs definitively indicate gender: growing a beard, having a wet dream, or being able to have intercourse with a woman indicates the person is male. Developing breasts, having soft breasts, menstruating, becoming pregnant, or being penetrated (passively) indicates the person is female. If both sets of signs appear together, or none appear, the person remains muskil.

The passage then gives the ruling for the muskil khuntha in prayer: he must stand between the rows of men and women to avoid corrupting either gender's prayer by potential close proximity to the opposite gender. If he mistakenly stands in the women's row, he must repeat his prayer (since he might be a man). If he stands in the men's row, those men adjacent to him and directly behind him must repeat their prayers (since they might have been in close proximity to a woman). He also prays with a veil (qina') like a woman.

Throughout, the principle is precaution (ihtiyat) - where there is doubt about a ruling, one takes the safer option to ensure validity.

The author also mentions a discussion about a newborn with no external genitalia at all (only an opening in the navel), citing a statement from Ash-Sha'bi that such a person gets half the inheritance of a male and half of a female, similar to a known khuntha.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The khuntha mushkil follows the stricter rule in every situation because his true sex is unknown.

When a person's sex remains ambiguous after puberty, Islamic law cannot simply ignore the question. The Hanafi school classifies such a person as a khuntha mushkil (ambiguous hermaphrodite) and applies a consistent principle: take the most cautious ruling in every matter where sex matters. This lesson unpacks that principle and shows how it governs prayer, dress, circumcision, and even funeral rites.

2
Grasp the principle of ihtiyat
The foundational rule for the khuntha mushkil is legal precaution (ihtiyat): apply the stricter ruling for each action to avoid certainty of sin.
This is not a positive choice of gender but a method to ensure the actual obligation is not violated.
3
Work through the prayer rulings

Each ruling assumes the sex that would make the action forbidden or obligatory.

Order of Placement in the Funeral Prayer for Multiple Deceased Including a Hermaphrodite
Third ruling on funeral prayer placement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of placement for a man, hermaphrodite, and woman in the funeral prayer and explain the analogy to the living state that underpins it.

Core Idea
In the funeral prayer, the hermaphrodite is placed between men and women, mirroring their living position between rows.

The third ruling in the passage addresses the arrangement when multiple bodies are prayed over simultaneously: the male is placed closest to the imam, then the hermaphrodite, then the female. This order is based on the living practice where the hermaphrodite stands between the rows of men and women during prayer. The ruling underscores how death does not erase the need for modest separation and order based on gender.

Guided Path
1
Identify the order
The text states: 'ويوضع الرجل بقرب الإمام، ثم هو ثم المرأة' – the man is placed near the imam, then the hermaphrodite, then the woman.
'ثم هو' – 'then he' refers to the hermaphrodite.
2
Understand the reason (illah)
The commentary says: 'اعتباراً لحالة الحياة' – out of consideration for the state of life. The hermaphrodite, in life, stands between the rows of men and women, so the same placement is applied in death.
This is a qiyas (analogy) from the living state to the dead state.
3
Analyze the structure of the analogy
Asl (original case): The hermaphrodite in life stands between men and women rows during prayer. Hukm: The hermaphrodite is placed between them. Far' (new case): In death, when bodies are arranged for funeral prayer, the hermaphrodite should also be between the male and female bodies. Illa: The need to maintain modest separation and proper order based on gender.
This analogy is simple and direct.
4
Consider the conditional
The passage adds 'إذا صلي عليهم' – 'if they are prayed over' – indicating the order applies only when all three are present and prayed upon together.
If any is missing, the order may change.
