The Hanafi Internal Dispute on Coercion and Hadd for Zina
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of coercion from non-sultan on hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on coercion by a non-sultan and evaluate the Sahibayn's counter-position, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence of the disagreement).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: Only the sultan's coercion drops hadd; the Sahibayn: any real coercion suffices. The disagreement hinges on the definition of legally effective coercion.

This lesson tackles a debate between the founder of the Hanafi school and his two leading disciples. The issue is whether a person coerced into zina by someone other than the ruler should be punished with hadd. The answer depends on what counts as 'true coercion' in Islamic law. By understanding this internal disagreement, you will see how a single principle—coercion negates punishment—can be interpreted differently based on assumptions about power and free will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Positions
State the ruling of Abu Hanifa and that of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding hadd for zina under coercion. Note the exception: only sultan coercion drops hadd for Abu Hanifa; for the Sahibayn, all genuine coercion drops it.
The base text says 'unless the sultan coerced him' – this is Abu Hanifa's view.
2
Understand the Underlying Usul
The key is the definition of legally effective coercion (ikrah). Abu Hanifa requires the coercer to have overwhelming power (like the sultan); others cannot truly compel. The Sahibayn consider only the threat's severity, not the source.
This is a talil (rationale) debate: what makes coercion 'true'?
3
Compare the Dalil for Each
Abu Hanifa's dalil: Only the sultan can exercise complete control; others leave the victim a choice. The Sahibayn's dalil: Coercion is about the threat (death, etc.), which anyone can make.
The text does not quote explicit proofs, but the reasoning is inferential.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Practical outcome: Under Abu Hanifa, a person coerced by a kidnapper to commit zina is liable for hadd; under the Sahibayn, he is not. This directly affects the application of punishment.
This is the most concrete result of the disagreement.
5
Note the Tarjih in Later Fatwa
The text notes that later Hanafi fatwa often adopts the Sahibayn's view for leniency, though Abu Hanifa's view is the base ruling.
Knowing the mufta bihi helps in practical fatwa issuance.

Coercion and Hadd for Zina: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ikrah and hadd for zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether coercion from a non-sultan removes the hadd punishment for zina, and distinguish between the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Coercion drops hadd, but Abu Hanifa says only the sultan can truly coerce; the Sahibayn say any credible threat suffices.

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of coercion (ikrah) on the fixed punishment (hadd) for zina. While all Hanafi imams agree that true coercion removes the hadd, they differ on what constitutes legally effective coercion. Abu Hanifa restricts it to coercion by the sultan, whereas his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, extend it to any credible threat. The passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies this nuanced debate.

Guided Path
1
State the agreed principle
Recognize that all Hanafi imams agree: if a person is truly coerced (ikrah), the hadd punishment is dropped. The dispute is not on the principle but on its application.
The passage begins: 'كون الإكراه اه مسقطاً للحد متفق عليه فيما بينهم' – coercion dropping hadd is agreed upon.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that only coercion by the sultan (the ruler with executive power) qualifies as true coercion. A threat from a private individual is not legally effective, so the hadd remains.
He says: 'الإكراه لا يتحقق من غير السلطان' – coercion is not realized from other than the sultan.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that coercion is realized from both the sultan and private individuals. Therefore, if any credible threat forces zina, the hadd is dropped.
They say: 'الإكراه يتحقق من السلطان وغيره' – coercion is realized from the sultan and other than him.
4
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
If a person is coerced by a private individual (e.g., a gang leader) into committing zina, Abu Hanifa would still apply the hadd, while the Sahibayn would not.
This directly affects the application of hudud in cases involving duress from non-state actors.
5
Consider the tarjih (preferred view)
The text does not explicitly state which view is mufta bihi (the operative position for fatwa). Both are recorded within the school, indicating that the later jurists may have given weight to either depending on circumstances.
Later Hanafi works often treat the Sahibayn's view as stronger in cases of genuine duress, but the school preserves both.

Intercourse with the Ma'dhun's Female Slave: Ruling and Shubha
Critical refinement of the ma'dhun's sexual limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a ma'dhun having intercourse with his female slave even with the master's explicit permission, and explain why the hadd is dropped while the act remains haram.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun's intercourse with his female slave is pure zina even with the master's permission; the hadd is averted by shubha, not permissibility.

This lesson refines a common misconception: that explicit permission from the master could make the ma'dhun's sexual relationship with his female slave permissible. The Hanafi position is categorical: it remains zina and haram. The only concession is the dropping of the fixed penalty due to the presence of shubha (doubt). We will examine the reasoning behind this strict ruling through the lens of talil (legal reasoning).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Who is the ma'dhun? What is the ma'dhun doing with his female slave? State the hukm: is it halal, haram, or something else?
The text says the act is 'zina mahd' and 'haram sirfan' — pure fornication and absolutely unlawful.
2
Examine the master's involvement
The text considers a scenario where the master explicitly gives the female slave to the ma'dhun as a gift and permits intercourse. Does this change the ruling?
Even with explicit permission, the ruling remains unchanged. The ma'dhun's authority is limited to trade, not ownership of a female slave for sexual purposes.
3
Understand the role of shubha
Why is the hadd (fixed punishment) not applied? Identify the shubha and its effect on the penalty, not on the act itself.
The shubha arises from the master's explicit permission and the ma'dhun's possession of the female slave. It only drops the hadd, not the sinfulness.
4
Differentiate between 'haram' and 'hadd'
The act remains haram and sinful. The dropping of hadd is a separate legal consequence. Clarify that absence of worldly punishment does not imply permissibility.
This distinction is crucial: hadd is a specific punishment for certain crimes; shubha can avert it, but the act is still prohibited.

Dirdi al-Khamr: Prohibition Without Hadd – The Hanafi Position
Hanafi ruling on dirdi and absence of hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on drinking dirdi and explain why the hadd penalty is not applied without intoxication, and distinguish this from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Drinking wine dregs is haram, but hadd applies only if intoxication occurs.

This passage from Al-Hidayah deals with a subtle point: wine dregs (dirdi) are prohibited because they contain parts of wine, yet the fixed penalty (hadd) for drinking wine does not automatically apply. The Hanafi school bases this on the effective cause (illa) of intoxication. The Shafi'i school disagrees, applying hadd to any part of wine. We will examine the reasoning and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling on dirdi
What is the hukm of drinking dirdi? The text says 'makruh' but explains it means haram because it contains parts of wine.
The use of 'makruh' instead of 'haram' is due to the absence of a definitive text.
2
Determine the condition for hadd
When does the hadd penalty apply? The text states 'ولا يحد شاربه بلا سكر' – only if intoxication occurs. A small amount of wine triggers hadd because it calls to more; dirdi does not.
The effective cause (illa) is the property of leading to intoxication.
3
State the Shafi'i opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that hadd applies to any part of wine, including dirdi, regardless of intoxication. Why? Because it is part of the prohibited substance.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafis argue that dirdi is naturally repulsive and does not provoke desire for more; its dominant component is sediment, so it resembles other non-intoxicating drinks. Therefore, the illa of intoxication is absent.
Analogy to water overcoming wine: if water overwhelms wine, hadd is not applied without intoxication.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Hanafi law, a person who drinks dirdi without intoxication is not subject to hadd; under Shafi'i law, he would receive the fixed penalty.
This difference affects judicial application of hudud.

Multiple Injuries and Compensation for Healed Wounds
Ruling on multiple injuries and healed wound compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for multiple injuries of different legal types (intentional and negligent) and apply the principle that a completely healed wound without a trace is not compensable, while distinguishing the minority view of Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A completely healed wound without trace is ignored in compensation—only the fatal injury counts, but ta'zir may apply.

This passage addresses a critical nuance in the law of qisas and diyat: what happens when an offender inflicts multiple injuries with different mental states (intentional vs negligent)? The text also explores a key intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether compensation (hukm al-adl) is due for wounds that have healed and left no trace. By working through the example of 100 lashes, you will see how the school distinguishes between compensable and non-compensable harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios and their rulings
The passage first presents two scenarios: (1) intentional cutting then negligent killing, and (2) negligent cutting then intentional killing. In each, the compensation (qisas or diyah) is determined separately for each act based on its legal nature.
The key is that each injury is treated independently because the legal categories differ.
2
Understand the reasoning: difference of the two injuries
The ruling is based on 'ikhtilaf al-jinaayatayni' (difference of the two injuries). Because one act is intentional and the other is negligent, they cannot be merged; each must be compensated according to its own rules.

Core Idea
Forgiveness of a cutting does not automatically forgive death by serayah—a separate legal act triggers new liability.

This lesson examines a nuanced case where a hand is cut, the victim forgives, but then dies. The Hanafi school splits: Abu Hanifa holds the cutter liable for diyah, while the Sahibayn say nothing is due. Understanding the reasoning hinges on the legal effect of serayah (spreading) and the scope of forgiveness.

The key distinction: the act of cutting and the resulting death are legally separate.

The Sahibayn argue that forgiveness of an act implies forgiveness of its necessary consequences. Since cutting can lead to death, forgiving the cutting includes forgiving the death. Therefore, nothing further is owed.
They view the cause as inclusive of all natural outcomes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

The Shafi'i school restricts the half rule to injuries exceeding one-third of the full diyah. We will analyse the proofs and the Hanafi refutation, culminating in the adopted position for fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why the Hanafi school distinguishes between hudud and qisas regarding a mute's gesture and apply the distinction to a scenario involving a mute's confession or accusation.

Core Idea
Hudud are dropped by doubt; a mute's gesture is doubtful, so it cannot establish hadd, but it can establish qisas.

This lesson explores a critical application of the Hanafi principle that hudud are repelled by shubha (doubt). The passage contrasts the mute's gesture in hudud offenses—where it is rejected—with its acceptance in qisas. Understanding this distinction reveals the deeper logic behind the categories of Allah's rights versus human rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling
Find the key statement: 'لا يحد' (he is not penalized). This applies to hudud when a mute confesses or accuses by gesture or kinayah.
The ruling is explicit: gesture does not establish hadd.
2
Understand the rationale for the distinction
The author explains that hudud are pure rights of Allah, legislated as deterrents (zawajir), and are dropped by any doubt (shubha). In contrast, qisas has a compensatory element (ma'na al-'awadiyyah) like financial transactions, so it can be established with less certain evidence.
This is the core proof: qisas is like a right of the slave; hudud are rights of Allah.
3
Note the dispute about mu'taqil al-lisan
For a person with a tied tongue, Hanafis require the condition to persist (a year or until death) and his gestures to be known before treating him like a mute. Shafi'is treat him as a mute immediately.
The practical difference: a temporary impairment does not grant the same legal capacity as a permanent mute.
4
Consider the internal objection
A later scholar (Mawlana Muhammad Abdul Halim) notes that al-Hidayah itself states elsewhere that qisas is also forestalled by doubts, questioning the consistency of the distinction.
This is a methodological critique; the student should reflect on whether the compensatory analogy fully holds.

Commentary
This passage from Umdat al-Ri'ayah discusses the legal effect of a mute person's gestures and writing in establishing hudud penalties versus qisas retaliation. The passage type is a ruling with proof, comparing the two categories. The author first states that a mute person who confesses to a hadd offense or accuses someone via gesture is not subject to hadd punishment. This is because hudud are pure rights of Allah, legislated as deterrents, and are dropped by any doubt (shubha). The gesture or writing lacks the certainty of an explicit verbal declaration, thus it is akin to a kinayah (indirect expression) which requires intention or contextual evidence. The author then distinguishes qisas from hudud: qisas has a compensatory nature (ma'na al-'awadiyyah) like financial transactions, so it can be established by less explicit means, including a mute's gesture or writing. However, the author notes an objection from a later scholar (Mawlana Muhammad Abdul Halim) that this contradicts statements earlier in al-Hidayah that qisas also is forestalled by doubts. The passage then addresses a person with a speech impediment (mu'taqil al-lisan): if the condition persists (usually a year or until death) and his gestures are understood, he is treated like a mute; otherwise not. Finally, an example of mixed sheep (slaughtered with a dead one) is given where the mute's gesture is accepted if customary. The key ruling: hudud require explicit proof due to their severity and divine nature, while qisas can be established with lesser proof due to its compensatory aspect. This aligns with the Hanafi principle that hudud are repelled by shubhat (doubt).

Key Terms
الأخرس (al-akhras): A mute person who cannot speak; his gestures and writing are considered for legal declarations except in hudud.
معتقل اللسان (mu'taqil al-lisan): A person who has a speech impediment that may be temporary or permanent; Hanafis require it to be extended (at least a year or until death) and his gestures known to be treated like a mute.
الحدود (al-hudud): Fixed corporal punishments for crimes like zina, theft, drinking, etc., considered as rights of Allah and dropped by doubts.
