Ruling on Unlawful Slaughter and Exceptions for Fish and Locusts
Commentary on the ruling that an unslaughtered animal is unlawful, with proof from Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that tadhkiyah (slaughter) is a condition for halal meat of land animals and identify fish and locusts as exceptions, citing Quran 5:3 as proof.

Core Idea
All edible land animals require slaughter; fish and locusts are exceptions needing no slaughter.

This lesson establishes the foundational Hanafi ruling that an animal must be properly slaughtered (tadhkiyah) to be lawful to eat. The proof is taken from the Quranic verse 5:3, which exempts properly slaughtered animals from the prohibition of carrion. A common question arises about fish and locusts – they are lawful without slaughter because they are not customarily slaughtered. The commentary also distinguishes between voluntary and emergency slaughter and critically examines the wording of the earlier text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is eating an animal that has not been properly slaughtered. The ruling is haram (unlawful). The passage states: 'An animal that has not been slaughtered is unlawful.'
This applies only to animals that are customarily slaughtered.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is from Quran 5:3: 'إِلَّا مَا ذَكَيْتُمْ' (except what you slaughter). This is an exception after the prohibition of carrion, indicating that properly slaughtered animals are lawful.
The verse establishes tadhkiyah as a condition for halal meat.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The exception implies that tadhkiyah (slaughter) is necessary to make the animal permissible. Without it, the animal remains haram, just like carrion.
The Hanafi school holds that the exception is a condition (shart) for permissibility.
4
Note the exception for fish and locusts
Fish and locusts are not customarily slaughtered; they are consumed without tadhkiyah. The commentator clarifies that 'dhabiha' (slaughtered animal) in the ruling is understood metaphorically to mean an animal that is normally slaughtered, thus excluding fish and locusts.
This avoids the apparent conflict with the general ruling.
5
Distinguish voluntary vs. emergency slaughter
Voluntary (ikhtiyari) slaughter is the ideal method, cutting the throat between the lubbah and jawbones. Emergency (idtirari) slaughter is a wound anywhere on the body and is a substitute when voluntary is impossible. Both make the animal halal if performed correctly.
Emergency slaughter is a mercy and reflects the principle of ease in the law.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from 'Umdat al-Riayah on Sharh al-Wiqayah, explaining the ruling that an animal is unlawful if not properly slaughtered. The author first defines terms: 'dhabiha' refers to an animal that is customarily slaughtered, and slaughter (dhabh) involves three elements: the slaughterer (must be Muslim or Kitabi), the animal (must be of edible meat), and the tool (anything that severs the jugular veins). The ruling is established by Quran 5:3, which excludes properly slaughtered animals from the prohibition of carrion. Thus, tadhkiyah (slaughter) is a condition for permissibility and also renders the meat pure. The text then addresses an objection: if the ruling is general, fish and locusts would be included, but they are lawful without slaughter. The commentator explains that 'dhabiha' is used metaphorically to mean an animal that is normally slaughtered, excluding fish and locusts. He also discusses voluntary slaughter (between throat and collar bone) and emergency slaughter (any part of the body) as alternatives. The passage then critiques the commentator's wording, offering three points: first, the literal meaning of 'dhabiha' suffices to exclude fish and locusts; second, the phrasing should be adjusted for clarity; third, the term 'shaan' (custom) needs specification whether linguistic or legal. The passage ends mid-sentence discussing the linguistic interpretation.

Key Terms
ذبيحة (dhabīḥah): In this text, it refers to an animal that is customarily slaughtered, excluding fish and locusts which are not normally slaughtered.
ذكاة (tadhkiyah): The process of slaughtering by cutting the jugular veins, making the animal lawful to eat. It includes both voluntary (ikhtiyari) and emergency (idtirari) methods.
اختيارية (ikhtiyāriyyah): Voluntary or ideal method of slaughter, performed by cutting the throat between the lubbah (throat pit) and the jawbones.

The Hanafi Position on Intentional Omission of Tasmiyah and the Dispute with al-Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute with Shafi'i on tasmiyah omission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on intentional omission of tasmiyah, explain the Hanafi proof from Quran 6:121, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument from Quran 6:145.

Core Idea
Intentional omission of tasmiyah invalidates slaughter; forgetfulness does not – a distinction rooted in Quranic context.

This passage from the Book of Slaughter presents a crucial inter-madhhab dispute. While the Hanafi school holds that deliberately omitting 'Bismillah' makes the meat haram, Imam al-Shafi'i argues otherwise. Understanding this difference requires careful analysis of two Quranic verses and the principle of contextual interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
The passage states that slaughter by one who intentionally omits tasmiyah is not lawful. This is the ruling according to the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school cites Quran 6:121: 'And do not eat of that upon which the name of Allah has not been mentioned.' The verse is unconditional in its prohibition.
3
Identify the opposing Shafi'i proof
Imam al-Shafi'i uses Quran 6:145, which lists forbidden items and does not include meat without tasmiyah unless it is dedicated to other than Allah.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school interprets the prohibition in 6:121 by connecting it to the phrase 'and indeed it is sinful' in the same verse, arguing that the context reinforces the prohibition. They also argue that 6:145 does not override 6:121 because it is a general list, not an exclusive one.
The Hanafi interpretation uses the principle of 'qarinah' (contextual evidence).
5
Determine the practical difference
According to Hanafis, meat from intentional omission is haram; according to Shafi'is, it is halal (unless dedicated to other than Allah). This difference affects daily consumption for followers of each school.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Slaughter (Kitab al-Dhaba'ih) presents the Hanafi position on who may perform a valid slaughter and the ruling on omitting the tasmiyah (saying 'Bismillah'). The passage is an inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i. The ruling begins by stating that slaughter by Jews and Christians is lawful, even if the slaughterer is insane, a woman, a boy who understands and controls the animal, uncircumcised, or mute. The evidence is Quran 5:5, which permits the food of the People of the Book, including their slaughtered meat, as they mention Allah's name. However, slaughter by an idol worshipper, fire-worshipper, apostate, or one who intentionally omits tasmiyah is not lawful. The Hanafi proof is Quran 6:121, which forbids eating meat upon which Allah's name has not been mentioned. Al-Shafi'i counters with Quran 6:145, which lists forbidden things but does not include meat without tasmiyah unless it is slaughtered for other than Allah. The Hanafis interpret the prohibition in 6:121 as referring specifically to meat slaughtered for other than Allah, based on the context of 'fisq' (sinfulness) in the following verse. If such a context is absent, the default permissibility applies. The passage then addresses forgetfulness: if one forgets to say tasmiyah, the meat is lawful due to the excuse of forgetfulness, supported by Quran 2:286 and the prophetic saying that the name of Allah is in every Muslim's heart, applied to forgetfulness. This nuance distinguishes the Hanafi position: the ruling is strict for deliberate omission but lenient for forgetfulness.

الذبيحة (al-dhabihah): An animal that is slaughtered according to Islamic law.

Prohibited Land Animals and Bird Dietary Classification
List of prohibited land animals and bird dietary classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which land animals are prohibited and classify birds by diet to determine permissibility according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Land animals like hyena and elephant are haram; birds are judged by diet.

When selecting meat, we must know which animals are permissible. This passage from a classical Hanafi text lists specific prohibited land animals and provides a dietary classification for birds, with a notable difference of opinion on certain birds.

Guided Path
1
Survey the prohibited land animals
Read the list: horses, hyena, wasp, turtle, piebald carrion-eater, ghudhaf, elephant, jerboa, weasel. All are haram to eat according to the Hanafi school.
This list is from the matn; specific proofs are not given here.
2
Distinguish the three bird categories
The passage divides birds into three types: (a) those that eat only carrion, (b) birds of prey, (c) those that mix grains and carrion.
3
Note the consensus rulings
Types (a) and (b) are haram by consensus (ittifaq) among Hanafis.
4
Examine the ikhtilaf on the third type
The third type, exemplified by the ‘aq‘aq, is halal according to the majority (al-a‘zam), though some consider it makruh. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) is halal.
5
Identify the ‘aq‘aq bird
The text describes it as long-tailed, black and white, also called field crow, small, red-footed, eating only grains (disputed). This helps in practical identification.
The description aids in distinguishing it from pure carrion-eaters.

Legal Categories of Eating and the Case of Donkey Milk and Camel Urine
Ruling on eating categories and two specific substances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five legal categories of eating, distinguish the Hanafi rulings on donkey milk and camel urine, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on camel urine using the hadith of 'Uraniyin.

Core Idea
Eating is fard, rewarded, mubah, or haram depending on intention; donkey milk is makruh; camel urine is disputed among Hanafi imams.

This passage from Sharh al-Wiqayah addresses a fundamental question: when does a normal act like eating become a legal obligation, and when does it become sinful? It then tackles two controversial substances: donkey milk and camel urine. The ruling on camel urine reveals a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf where the Sahibayn differ from Abu Hanifa, relying on a specific hadith for medical treatment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five categories of eating
The text lists fard, rewarded, mubah, haram, and an exception for haram beyond satiety. Trace each condition: fard when life-threatening, rewarded when enabling worship, mubah up to satiety for strength, haram beyond except for valid reasons.
Note that the reward (ma'jur) is not a separate category but a merit added to mubah.
2
Apply the rule to donkey milk
The text says its ruling follows its meat. Recall that Hanafi school holds donkey meat as makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked). Therefore, donkey milk is also makruh tahrimi.
Makruh tahrimi is near haram; the text says 'karaha' (disliked), which in this context likely means makruh tahrimi.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute on camel urine
Identify the positions: Abu Hanifa says haram; Abu Yusuf permits it for treatment; Muhammad permits it absolutely. This is an ikhtilaf among the three leading Hanafi imams.
The dispute is based on different interpretations of evidence.
4
Analyze the evidence for permissibility
The hadith of 'Uraniyin is cited, where the Prophet ﷺ permitted drinking camel urine for treatment. This is a dalil naqli (textual evidence). The Sahibayn use istihsan (juristic preference) to prioritize this hadith over qiyas on impurity.
The hadith is ahad (solitary) but accepted for action; Abu Hanifa may have considered it abrogated or specific to that context.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
In medical contexts, the permissive view (Abu Yusuf) is often followed in later fatwas. Abu Hanifa's view prohibits even medicinal use. The mufta bihi (operative opinion) leans toward permissibility under necessity.
Later Hanafi jurists like Ibn Abidin often adopt Abu Yusuf's view for treatment.

When Causes Conflict: Precedence of Prohibition in Hunting
Explains rulings when Muslim sends dog and Magian calls, or vice versa, and when no sender but Muslim calls

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of tarjih al-hurmah when causes of prohibition and permissibility coincide in hunting scenarios, and distinguish rulings based on the timing of the Magian's call.

Core Idea
When prohibition and permission conflict and avoidance is possible, prohibition prevails.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling when a Muslim sends a trained dog and a Magian calls it. The commentary from al-Hidayah introduces a key principle: when the causes of prohibition and permissibility coincide, and it is possible to avoid the cause of prohibition, the prohibition takes precedence. However, the timing of the Magian's call matters: if during pursuit, the prey is halal; if after the dog stops, it is haram. Understanding this distinction requires careful analysis of the source of the sending and the nature of the call.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general legal principle
The principle is: when hurmah and hill causes meet, and avoidance of hurmah is possible, hurmah is preferred; if unavoidable, the cause is ignored. This is cited from al-Hidayah.
The principle applies to various fiqh chapters, not only hunting.
2
Apply the principle to the first scenario
In the case where a Muslim sends his dog and a Magian calls it, determine if the call occurs during the dog's pursuit or after it stops.
The ruling depends on this timing distinction.
3
Analyze the timing distinction
If the Magian calls during the chase, it merely reinforces the valid sending and does not introduce a new, invalid irsal. If the dog has stopped, the call initiates a new sending from an non‑Muslim, which is invalid.
Shams al-A'imma explains this in his commentary on Kitab al-Sayd.

The ruling is from al-Hidayah and Takmilat al-Bahr, reflecting the Zahir al-Riwayah position.

Core Idea
A valid send stays valid despite later calls; an invalid send cannot be fixed by a Muslim's call.

Similarly, if a Muslim deliberately omits tasmiyah when sending, a later Muslim call with tasmiyah does not fix it. These rulings are grounded in the principle that the original action's validity is decisive and irreversible. The passage also covers cases where there is no sending and only a call is made.

Ruling on a Hunting Dog Catching Game Other Than Its Target
States Hanafi ruling and rebuts Maliki view on accidental catch.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a hunting dog catches game other than its specified target and explain the role of istihsan in overriding qiyas.

Core Idea
A hunting dog's catch is halal even if it differs from the intended target — because training such precision is impossible and istihsan overrides qiyas.

When a hunter sends his dog after a specific game animal, but the dog catches something else, a legal question arises: is that accidental catch halal? The Hanafi school answers with a clear yes, while Imam Malik takes a stricter view. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, showing how istihsan (juristic preference) allows a pragmatic ruling that aligns with the realities of hunting with trained dogs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Scenario and the Hanafi Ruling
The scenario: a hunter sends his trained dog at a specific game animal, but the dog catches a different one. The Hanafi matn states clearly that the catch is eaten (halal).
2
Recognize the Opposing View (Malik and Ibn Abi Layla)
Imam Malik holds that if the hunter specified a target, the catch of a different animal is not halal. Ibn Abi Layla similarly restricts, but allows if no specification was made. Understand their basis: the sending and tasmiyah (naming of Allah) are tied to the specified prey.
3
Understand the Qiyas Argument
Strict qiyas would forbid the catch: the dog's sending is considered a form of dhabh (slaughter) under necessity, so if the sending was for a different animal, the dhabh is absent, making the catch unlawful.
This is the opposing school's analogical reasoning.
4
Grasp the Istihsan Reasoning
The Hanafi response uses istihsan: the dog's training cannot achieve such selectivity, and the hunter's call (zajr) when the dog is not directly sent becomes a new sending. The sending and tasmiyah cover the act of hunting, not each individual prey. Therefore, the catch is halal.
Istihsan overrides qiyas because strict analogy would lead to hardship.
5
Appreciate the Practical Outcome
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi (relied upon for fatwa). Specification (ta'yin) is not a condition for validity, and the hunter is not required to do the impossible. Any game caught by the dog under normal hunting conditions is halal.
This ruling provides ease and practicality for hunters.

Imam Malik disagrees, arguing that the sending and tasmiyah were for a specific animal, so catching another is like slaughtering a different animal without proper tasmiyah. The Hanafis respond with istihsan (juristic preference): the dog's call, in the absence of a specific sending, becomes a new sending. The principle is that the dog's action is a form of dhabh under necessity, and the tasmiyah covers the act of sending itself. The passage also notes the intermediate view of Ibn Abi Layla, who holds that if the hunter specifies a target, the dog's catch is restricted, but if no specification is made, any catch is permissible. The Hanafi final position is that specification is not a condition, nor does it limit the catch, because it is beyond the hunter's control. The practical consequence is that according to Hanafis, the game is always halal unless the dog was deliberately trained to fetch only a specific item (which is impossible).

Key Terms
تعيين (ta'yin): Specifying a particular game animal before sending the dog.
ذكاة (dhabh): The Islamic method of slaughter; here extended to hunting with a trained dog under necessity.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that overrides strict analogy (qiyas) in favor of ease or custom.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning that would render the catch impermissible if strictly applied.

Eating Meat with Doubt: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i on Ghalabah
Inter-madhhab dispute on eating meat with doubt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on eating meat when the majority is presumed halal, distinguish the Shafi'i counter-argument, and apply the principle of ghalabah to market food.

Core Idea
When the majority is halal, the doubt is ignored; the rule of the many governs the market.

This passage addresses a common scenario: you are in a market where most meat comes from proper slaughter, but some may be carrion. Can you eat without investigating each piece? The Hanafi school says yes, relying on the predominant (ghalabah), while Imam al-Shafi'i insists on certainty or necessity. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind both positions and the practical consequence for daily life.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify exactly what the Hanafi school permits: eating meat from a market where the majority is from slaughtered animals, without further investigation.
The ruling applies only in normal circumstances (ikhtiyar), not in necessity where carrion itself is halal.
2
Identify the opponent and their argument
State Imam al-Shafi'i's position: investigation (taharrī) is only for necessity; without necessity, one must abstain from doubtful meat.
The Shafi'i position is stricter, requiring certainty or a valid excuse.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the Shafi'i dalil: taharrī is a proof of necessity (dalīl ḍarūrī), and there is no necessity here since the person can choose other food.
This is a methodological difference: Shafi'i restricts taharrī to necessity, Hanafi extends it to hardship.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal: taharrī is used to remove hardship (ḥaraj), not only necessity. The market inevitably contains some haram, yet Muslims eat based on the majority. The small amount cannot be avoided, so it is disregarded.
The key maxim: al-ghālib yuʿāmalu ka al-mutaḥaqqiq (the predominant is treated as certain).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical difference: Hanafis eat freely in Muslim markets without inquiry; Shafi'is would investigate or avoid unless compelled by necessity.
This illustrates how a difference in usul leads to a difference in daily practice.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding the ruling on eating meat when there is doubt whether it is from a properly slaughtered animal (dhakiyya) or carrion (mayta). The Hanafi position is that if the meat available is predominantly from slaughtered animals (i.e., more than half), one may eat it without investigation. If the majority is carrion or they are equal, then one must investigate or abstain. This applies in normal circumstances (ikhtiyar); in necessity, carrion itself becomes permissible. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that investigation (taharrī) is only a proof of necessity, and since there is no necessity here, it is not allowed to resort to it. The Hanafi response is that investigation is used to remove hardship (haraj), not only necessity. The existence of some prohibited items in markets is inevitable, yet Muslims eat based on the predominant (ghalabah) without checking every piece. The passage also compares with the ruling on a garment: for a garment with mixed purity and impurity, one always investigates if they are equal because the ruling is lighter. For food, it is stricter, so investigation is only needed when the impure is predominant or equal. The predominant takes the place of necessity in granting permissibility, as seen in market practices. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the Hanafi view, and the text cites al-Hidayah and al-Kifaya for support.

Key Terms
تحري (taharrī): Investigation to determine the legal status of something when it is ambiguous.
الغالب (al-ghālib): The predominant or majority case, which is given legal weight in the absence of certainty.
ضرورة (ḍarūrah): State of necessity that permits otherwise prohibited actions.
حرج (ḥaraj): Hardship or difficulty that the Law aims to remove.
