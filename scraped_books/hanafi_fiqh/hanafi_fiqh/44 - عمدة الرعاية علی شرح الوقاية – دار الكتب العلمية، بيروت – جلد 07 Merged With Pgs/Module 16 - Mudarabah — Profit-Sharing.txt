Definition and Ruling of Musaqah (Irrigation Contract)
Definition, intra-Hanafi dispute, and conditions of musaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of musaqah, identify the intra-madhhab dispute over its validity, and explain how its conditions mirror those of muzara'ah.

Core Idea
Musaqah is valid by fatwa, even though Abu Hanifa held it invalid; it mirrors muzara'ah in ruling, dispute, and conditions.

This lesson begins the Book of Musaqah by defining the contract and immediately highlighting its deep connection to muzara'ah. You will see how the Hanafi school resolved an internal difference of opinion, and how the contract's conditions are derived from its sharecropping counterpart. The passage also touches on why al-Shafi'i permits musaqah but restricts muzara'ah, revealing a methodological divergence.

Guided Path
1
Define musaqah
Read the definition: 'handing over trees to someone who tends them for a portion of the fruit.' Note that the subject matter is trees, not land, distinguishing it from muzara'ah.
The definition focuses on the object (trees) and the consideration (share of fruit).
2
Identify the Hanafi dispute
The passage states that the fatwa is on the validity of musaqah, but Abu Hanifa considered it invalid, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad held it valid. This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved by adopting the Sahibayn's view.
Zahir al-Riwayah follows the fatwa; Abu Hanifa's view is marjuh.
3
List the shared conditions
The conditions of musaqah are like those of muzara'ah wherever applicable: capacity of parties, specification of the worker's share, vacating the trees, and partnership in the produce. Note that seed specification is impossible in musaqah.
Conditions that cannot exist in musaqah (e.g., seed) are omitted.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i position
Al-Shafi'i holds musaqah valid and only permits muzara'ah within the framework of musaqah. His argument: the origin is mudarabah, and musaqah is more similar to it because both involve partnership in profit, not land.
This is an inter-madhhab dispute: Shafi'i's qiyas differs from the Hanafi analogy.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the Hanafi fatwa, musaqah is a valid independent contract. Under Abu Hanifa's original view, it would be invalid. The Shafi'i view also validates musaqah but restricts muzara'ah's independence.
The dispute affects whether a standalone irrigation contract is enforceable.

Scope of Musaqah: First Fruit and Herbs
Explains the first ruling of Musaqah on fruit and herbs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the temporal scope of a Musaqah contract for tree fruit and herbs, and explain the role of custom in determining its termination.

Core Idea
Musaqah covers only the first fruit of the season; if no fruit appears, the contract is void.

This passage clarifies an essential feature of the Musaqah contract: it attaches to the first fruit that appears after the agreement. For herbs like ratbah, the contract lasts until the seeds ripen. Custom (urf) fills the gap when no explicit duration is stated. Understanding this scope is vital for determining the validity and end of the contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject matter
What type of contract is being discussed? Musaqah is a contract to tend trees in return for a share of the fruit. The passage focuses on its temporal scope.
2
Understand the rule for tree fruit
The contract applies only to the first fruit of the season. This means the worker's labor is compensated from the first produce that appears after the contract is made.
No fixed period is needed; the fruit's natural ripening determines the end.
3
Understand the rule for ratbah
Ratbah (fresh greens) is treated differently: the contract continues until the seeds of the herb ripen, because those seeds are analogous to fruit. If seeds are not the primary object, the contract ends after the first year.
Custom determines the ripening period, as herbs are harvested multiple times.
4
Recognize the role of custom (urf)
The Hanafi school uses custom to determine the time of fruit ripening when no duration is specified. This is a secondary source (dalil) for the ruling.
Custom is zanni (probable) but legally effective here.
5
Note the consequence of no fruit
If no fruit appears in the first season, the contract is void (invalid). This is because the contract is limited to that specific season's fruit.
The worker receives nothing, as the contract is treated as if it never existed.

Effect of Time Period on Musaqah Validity
Explains when time period invalidates Musaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the validity of a Musaqah contract based on the time period specified, and identify when the worker is entitled to an equivalent wage (ujrat al-mithl).

Core Idea
Impossible time period invalidates Musaqah; if fruit may appear, contract stands, and natural calamity does not trigger wage.

In Musaqah, specifying a time period is permissible, but its legal effect depends on whether fruit can realistically appear within that period. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on when such a stipulation renders the contract invalid (fasid) or valid (sahih), and the consequences for the worker's compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
Recognize that Musaqah is a contract where a worker tends trees in exchange for a share of fruit. The ruling here concerns the validity of specifying a fixed time period.
The act is the stipulation of a time period in the contract.
2
Understand the Condition of the Period
If the period is so short that fruit cannot possibly appear (e.g., one year for a new grapevine), the contract is invalid (fasid). If fruit may appear (even if unlikely), the contract is valid (sahih).
Custom (urf) determines normal ripening time; custom is like law.
3
Apply Qiyas to Impossible Periods
The impossible period case is analogous to any contract with an impossible condition—qiyas jali (clear analogy) renders the contract fasid because the object (fruit) is unattainable.
The illa is impossibility of achieving the object.
4
Distinguish Non-Appearance Causes
If fruit does not appear due to a natural calamity (afah samawiyyah), the contract remains valid because the period was possible—the calamity does not retroactively prove impossibility. If the period was clearly impossible from the start, the contract is fasid.
The distinction is crucial for the worker's compensation.
5
Determine Worker's Compensation
If the contract is fasid due to clear impossibility, the worker receives ujrat al-mithl for the work already done. If the contract is valid but no fruit appears due to calamity, the worker gets nothing—neither party claims against the other.
Ujrat al-mithl is for past work, not future work.

Commentary
This passage covers the second ruling of Musaqah regarding the specification of a time period. If the parties agree on a period within which it is impossible for fruit to appear (e.g., one year for a vine that takes three years), the contract is invalid (fasid) because the object—fruit—is unattainable. However, if the period is such that fruit may or may not appear (e.g., a mature tree within a year), the contract is valid. If fruit does appear, they share as per agreement. If it does not appear due to a natural event (like a blight), the contract remains valid but no fruit means no claim for either party. If it becomes clear that the period was miscalculated (the fruit could never have appeared in that time), the contract becomes invalid from the start, and the worker is entitled to an equivalent wage (ujrat al-mithl) for his past labor. The commentators clarify that the wage is for work already done, not for future work, because the contract is void.

آفة سماوية (afah samawiyyah): A natural calamity beyond human control, like drought or blight.
مدة (muddah): A specified time period in the contract.

Musaqah is valid for any perennial plant that repeatedly yields produce, not only grains or annual crops.

Musaqah (sharecropping on trees) is usually associated with date palms and grapevines. But the Hanafi school extends it to a broad category: any plant with a permanent root structure that yields produce year after year, including vegetables and herbs. Commentary
This final passage lists the types of plants for which a Musaqah contract is valid according to the school. The list includes: the vine (grapevine), fruit trees generally, ratab (fresh greens or herbs, such as leek and spinach), roots of eggplant (meaning perennial plants whose edible parts are under the ground but regrow), and date palms. The commentator Al-Jalabi clarifies that ratab refers to vegetables as commonly understood. The phrase 'even if' suggests the continuation would mention something like 'even if the fruit is not harvested immediately' or similar, but the text is incomplete.

Key Terms
الكرم (al-karm): The grapevine.
الرطاب (al-ratab): Fresh greens, vegetables, herbs.
أصول الباذنجان (usul al-badhinjan): Roots/stems of eggplant, remaining in the ground and regrowing.
