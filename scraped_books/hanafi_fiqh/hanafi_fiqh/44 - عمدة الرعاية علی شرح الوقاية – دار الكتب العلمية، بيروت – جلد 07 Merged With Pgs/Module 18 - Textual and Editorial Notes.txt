Linguistic Refinement on the Ruling of Men Kissing and Embracing
Linguistic analysis of the terms used in the ruling on men kissing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the linguistic refinement in the Hanafi ruling on men kissing and embracing, distinguishing between the scope of 'kissing' and 'embracing in one garment'.

Core Idea
The garment restriction applies only to embracing, not kissing — a linguistic refinement that narrows the disliked act.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah provides a critical linguistic analysis of the ruling that it is disliked for a man to kiss and embrace another man. The commentator clarifies that the phrase 'in one garment' specifically modifies embracing, not kissing, and explains why the author chose the singular form 'inaq' over the reciprocal 'mu'anaqa'. Understanding this refinement prevents a common misunderstanding that the dislike applies equally to both acts in that context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Identify 'taqbil' (kissing) and 'inaq' (embracing) in the ruling. Note the lexicographical equivalence of 'inaq' and 'mu'anaqa' as stated by Al-Jawhari.
The commentator uses 'inaq' for brevity, though both mean the same.
2
Understand the lexical change
The author of the sharh changed from the reciprocal form 'mu'anaqa' (used in Al-Hidayah) to the simple form 'inaq'. The reason is for brevity (ikhtisar) when adding the preposition to 'al-rajul'.
This change does not affect the legal meaning.
3
Analyze the syntactic attachment
The adverbial phrase 'in one garment' (fi izār wāḥid) is argued to attach only to 'embracing', not to 'kissing'. This is inferred from the flow of the text and the statement in Al-Hidayah that separates the two acts.
The commentator provides evidence from Al-Hidayah's wording.
4
Distinguish the scope of the ruling
The dislike (karāhah) applies to kissing generally, but the specific condition of 'in one garment' is limited to embracing. The disagreement (khilāf) mentioned later is specifically about embracing in one garment.
This prevents misunderstanding that the garment condition applies equally to kissing.
5
Apply the refinement
When stating the Hanafi position, one should specify that embracing is disliked when done in one garment, while kissing is disliked without that condition. The common phrasing can be refined accordingly.
This is important for precise fatwa and teaching.

The Meaning of 'Min' in Hanafi Fiqh: Tab'eed vs. Bayan
Critical commentary on the meaning of 'min' in a legal ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the meaning of 'min' in a legal context concerning debt and apply the principle that a mushtarak has no generality.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'min' is a mushtarak; it cannot indicate both partiality and clarification at once—it must be interpreted contextually.

This passage from 'Husn al-Dirayah' illustrates the crucial role of linguistic precision in Hanafi fiqh. The author critically refines a previous interpretation of the preposition 'min' in a ruling about the destruction of a pledged item. Understanding whether 'min' indicates partiality (tab'eed) or clarification (bayan) directly affects the legal ruling. The analysis also invokes a key usul principle: a common term (mushtarak) has no inherent generality (umum), a distinctive Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal context
The passage deals with a case where a debtor's property (e.g., a pledged item) is destroyed, and the compensation is determined by weight. The ruling states: '...its destruction is... of the debt from it.' The word 'min' is the point of contention.
2
Recognize the two possible meanings of 'min'
The author considers two interpretations: tab'eed (partiality, meaning 'part of') and bayan (clarification, meaning 'which is'). The previous commentator argued that 'min' could be for bayan to cover all cases, but the author refutes this.
In Hanafi usul, when a word is homonymous (mushtarak), it cannot indicate more than one meaning in a single usage.
3
Understand the objection
If 'min' is for tab'eed, the ruling fails in cases of equality (weight = debt) because partiality is impossible. If 'min' is for bayan, it would require the word to have generality, but since 'min' is mushtarak, it cannot have umum. Even if one attempts to combine, the case where weight exceeds debt (e.g., 15 weight vs 10 debt) makes no sense: 'fifteen of ten' is absurd.
The objection highlights the logical inconsistency of assigning both meanings to the same occurrence.
4
See the author's resolution
The author concludes that 'min' is solely for tab'eed. The aim of the ruling is to show that destruction is considered by weight, not value, only when the debt is greater. The expression is not intended to cover all possible cases (equality or excess) but merely to illustrate the principle. Those cases are understood by analogy.
This is a classic Hanafi move: the text is taken as a pointer to the underlying principle (weight consideration), not as an exhaustive statement.
5
Relate to the usul principle of mushtarak
The footnote explicitly states that a common term (mushtarak) like 'min' has no generality according to the Hanafi school. Therefore, it cannot be interpreted to have multiple meanings simultaneously or to cover all instances. This principle is foundational in Hanafi textual interpretation.
Compare with the Shafi'i view that context can give a mushtarak general applicability.

Commentary
This passage is a super-commentary (hashiyah) from 'Husn al-Dirayah' on the latter part of 'Sharh al-Wiqayah'. The author is critically refining a previous commentator's interpretation of the word 'min' in a legal ruling about the destruction of a pledged item in relation to debt. The ruling concerns a case where a debtor's property is destroyed and the amount owed is determined by weight. The key question is whether 'min' indicates partiality (tab'id) or clarification (bayan). The author argues that 'min' must be for partiality, not clarification, based on the context: the aim is to show that compensation is by weight, not value. He addresses objections that this interpretation fails in cases of equality or excess weight. The footnote clarifies the principle that a common term (mushtarak) like 'min' has no generality (umum), which is a Hanafi position in usul al-fiqh. The author concludes that even if the expression does not cover all cases, it is sufficient for the intended purpose.

Key Terms
من (min): The preposition 'min' in this passage is analyzed as potentially meaning partiality (tab'id) or clarification (bayan); the author argues it is for partiality.
التبعيض (al-tab'eed): The meaning of partiality, i.e., indicating a part of something.
البيان (al-bayan): Clarification or explanation, i.e., specifying the nature of something.
المشترك (al-mushtarak): A homonym; a word that can apply to different meanings alternately. In this passage, 'min' is considered mushtarak between tab'id and bayan.
