Ruling on Coercion to Disbelief: When Taqiyya is Permitted
Ruling with proof on the limits of taqiyya under threat of death or mutilation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for permitted taqiyya and apply them to distinguish valid from invalid coercion in cases of kufr.

Core Idea
Only threats of death or amputation permit outward kufr; lesser threats do not, and one must endure even unto death.

In the previous lesson, we saw that eating carrion is permitted under dire necessity. The same principle of darurah applies to being coerced into disbelief, but with a stricter threshold because kufr is the most severe prohibition. This lesson examines the exact conditions under which a Muslim may outwardly comply with a demand to utter kufr while keeping faith in the heart, and why lesser threats demand steadfastness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its gravity
Being coerced to utter kufr is the act under consideration. Understand that kufr is the most severe sin in Islam, so the allowance for it is extremely restricted.
The text compares kufr's prohibition to that of wine, stating kufr is even more severe.
2
Distinguish valid from invalid coercion
The text explicitly states that only threats of killing (qatl) or amputation (qat') constitute valid coercion for kufr. Imprisonment, beating, or other harms do not permit outward kufr.
This is a key Hanafi restriction; other schools may be more lenient.
3
Locate the proof (dalil)
The text refers to the story of Khubayb ibn Adiyy, a Companion who chose death over renouncing faith. This story illustrates the virtue of steadfastness but also forms the basis for the ruling: since Khubayb endured, the allowance is only for the most severe threats.
The dalil is a hadith ahad, so it yields zanni thubut, but it is sufficient for the ruling.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a hypothetical captive threatened with death for refusing to blaspheme, the Hanafi ruling is that he may outwardly comply while keeping faith. If threatened only with beating, he must not comply.
The consequence of non-compliance under valid threat is sin, analogous to starvation.
5
Contrast with the opposing view
Some scholars permit outward kufr under any form of duress. The Hanafi school rejects this because it trivialises the severity of kufr.
This ikhtilaf is a practical one: it affects whether a person is considered sinful for yielding to lesser threats.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on being coerced to utter words of disbelief. It parallels the earlier ruling on eating haram: only threats of death or amputation allow one to outwardly comply while maintaining faith in the heart. Imprisonment or beating are not valid coercion for kufr because the gravity of kufr exceeds even that of wine. The commentator emphasizes that if a person endures the threat and is killed, they sin – analogously to the case of starvation – because they are aiding their own destruction when the act was permitted. The proof is the story of Khubayb (may Allah be pleased with him), who was captured by polytheists. Although the text is cut off, the point is that Khubayb did not renounce his faith despite threats, showing the virtue of steadfastness. However, the ruling allows dissimulation (taqiyya) when the threat is severe enough.

Key Terms

تقيّة (taqiyya): Dissimulation of faith when under threat of death or severe harm.
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion; forcing someone to do something against their will.

The Two Types of Error in Killing
Defines two types of error and their shared ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between error in action and error in intention, and state the rulings of diya and kaffāra for each.

Core Idea
Error in killing is either in action or intention; both require diya on the 'āqila and kaffāra.

The passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah first clarifies that diya is due even for killing a slave by error. It then defines two distinct types of error in killing: error in action (e.g., aiming at a target but hitting a person) and error in intention (e.g., shooting at someone thinking he is game). Both types share the same ruling: diya on the 'āqila and kaffāra, but the killer is not sinful for intentional murder, only for negligence. The proof is the hadith of relief from error.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? Erroneous killing (al-khaṭa'). The ruling: diya upon the 'āqila and kaffāra upon the killer.
Even killing a slave by error requires diya, despite the slave being property.
2
Classify the two types of error
The text divides error into: (1) error in action – intending an action but another results (e.g., shooting a target but hitting a person); (2) error in intention – intending a permissible target but mistaken about its identity (e.g., shooting a Muslim thinking he is a combatant).
In error in action, the intention is correct but the execution is flawed; in error in intention, the intention itself is mistaken.
3
Locate the dalil
The hadith 'رُفِعَ عَنْ أُمَّتِي الْخَطَأُ وَالنِّسْيَانُ' (My community is relieved of error and forgetfulness) is cited. It proves that there is no sin of murder in error, but the legislation of kaffāra indicates some sin (negligence).
The hadith is aḥad (ẓannī al-thubūt) but its meaning is clear.
4
Note the scope of the ruling
The ruling applies to both types of error, and even to killing a slave. The 'āqila (agnatic relatives) are liable for the diya, not the killer's own property.
The 'āqila are the 'aṣaba (male relatives through the father's side).
5
Identify the consequence of omission
If the 'āqila fail to pay diya, it becomes a debt on them. Kaffāra is an act of worship, so it is an individual obligation upon the killer.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āyah discusses the rulings on erroneous killing. The author first clarifies that even killing a slave by error requires diya on the 'āqila, because although the slave is property, the diya is for the person. Error is divided into two types: error in action (e.g., aiming at a target but hitting a person) and error in intention (e.g., shooting at a person thinking he is game or an enemy). In both types, the killer is not sinful for intentional murder, but may be sinful for negligence. The proof is the hadith: 'My community is relieved of error and forgetfulness.' Then the text addresses killing by indirect cause, such as placing a stone or digging a well on non-owned land, causing death. According to the Hanafi school, this requires diya on the 'āqila but no expiation and no deprivation of inheritance (except for the killer's own inheritance from the victim). Imam al-Shāfi'ī analogizes this to direct error and requires expiation and inheritance deprivation. The Hanafi response is that causal killing is not actual killing (the act does not directly cause death), so it is treated like error only in matters of financial liability (diya), not in matters of worship (kaffāra) or legal status (inheritance). The commentary further clarifies that the sin in error is the sin of negligence, not of murder, and that expiation is required only for the sin of killing itself.

خطأ (khaṭa’): Error: unintended killing due to mistake in intention or action.
عاقلة (‘āqila): The male agnatic relatives who bear diya for erroneous killing.
دية (diya): Blood money paid to the victim's heirs.
كفارة (kaffāra): Expiation required for erroneous killing (freeing a slave or fasting).

Killing by Cause: The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Compares causal killing with direct error and presents the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on killing by cause, compare it to the Shafi'i position, and explain the Hanafi response based on the principle of limiting analogy to liability only.

Core Idea
Causal killing is treated like error only for diya, not for kaffāra or inheritance – the analogy stops at financial liability.

The passage presents a key inter-madhhab dispute: whether killing by indirect cause (like placing a stone or digging a well) should be treated identically to direct erroneous killing. The Hanafi school restricts the analogy to financial compensation (diya) because the killing is not actual; the Shafi'i school extends it to kaffāra and inheritance deprivation. The text presents both views and the Hanafi justification using the principle that causation is only relevant for liability (ḍamān), not for acts of worship or legal status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case of killing by cause
The text gives examples: placing a stone on non-owned land that causes someone to trip and die, or digging a well on non-owned land that someone falls into. These are cases of indirect causation (qatl bi-sabab).
The act itself is not lethal; it creates a hazard.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
Hanafi: diya is wājib on the 'āqila, but there is no kaffāra, and the killer does not lose inheritance rights (except in some cases? The text says 'lā irth illā hunā' meaning the killer may inherit if he is an heir).
The phrase 'لا إرث إلا هنا' indicates that in causal killing, the inheritor (if the killer is an heir) does inherit, unlike other types of killing where the killer is deprived.
3
Present the Shafi'i position
Shafi'i: causal killing is analogized (ilhāq) to error in all its rulings, so kaffāra is wājib and the killer is deprived of inheritance.
The Shafi'i argument uses the principle that the Lawgiver treats the cause (musabbib) like the direct agent (mubāshir) in liability, and thus extends it to all aspects.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafi: 'al-qatl ma'dūm ḥaqīqatan' – the killing is non-existent in reality; the causal act does not directly kill. The analogy (ilhāq) is valid only for liability (ḍamān) because that is based on causation of harm. For kaffāra (an act of worship) and inheritance (a legal status), the original principle (aṣl) remains: no obligation without a direct act of killing.
The key distinction: financial liability can be based on causation, but spiritual and legal consequences require direct agency.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
In a case of causal killing, a Hanafi mufti would rule: diya on 'āqila, no kaffāra, the killer inherits from the victim if he is an heir. A Shafi'i mufti would rule: diya, kaffāra, and the killer is deprived of inheritance.
The tarjīḥ (preferred view) is the Hanafi position and is muftā bihi.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'āyah discusses the rulings on erroneous killing. The author first clarifies that even killing a slave by error requires diya on the 'āqila, because although the slave is property, the diya is for the person. Error is divided into two types: error in action (e.g., aiming at a target but hitting a person) and error in intention (e.g., shooting at a person thinking he is game or an enemy). In both types, the killer is not sinful for intentional murder, but may be sinful for negligence. The proof is the hadith: 'My community is relieved of error and forgetfulness.' Then the text addresses killing by indirect cause, such as placing a stone or digging a well on non-owned land, causing death. According to the Hanafi school, this requires diya on the 'āqila but no expiation and no deprivation of inheritance (except for the killer's own inheritance from the victim). Imam al-Shāfi'ī analogizes this to direct error and requires expiation and inheritance deprivation. The Hanafi response is that causal killing is not actual killing (the act does not directly cause death), so it is treated like error only in matters of financial liability (diya), not in matters of worship (kaffāra) or legal status (inheritance). The commentary further clarifies that the sin in error is the sin of negligence, not of murder, and that expiation is required only for the sin of killing itself.

Equality of Free and Slave in Qisas: Hanafi Proofs and Rationale
Hanafi proof for qisas across free and slave status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas between free and slave, and explain the proofs and rebuttal of the opposing view using the concepts of amm and khass.

Core Idea
A slave is killed for a free person and vice versa because both share equal 'ismah as humans.

This passage tackles a key dispute in qisas: does the verse 'the free for the free' restrict retaliation to cases where both parties are free? The Hanafi school answers no, based on general Quranic commands and a deeper understanding of 'ismah. The author presents the proofs and rebuts the opposing interpretation by contextual analysis and historical background, establishing that the slave's humanity overrides any status-based distinction.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify clearly what the Hanafi school holds: a free person can be killed in retaliation for killing a slave, and vice versa. This is the mufta bihi position.
Contrast with Shafi'i view that a free person is not killed for a slave.
2
Identify the general proofs (dalil)
Locate the Quranic verses cited: 'the soul for the soul' (5:45), 'prescribed for you is retaliation for the killed' (2:178), and the hadith 'intentional killing is retaliation'. Note that these are unrestricted (mutlaq) and cover all protected lives.
Hanafis consider these amm (general) texts which are qat'i in their coverage until specific restriction (takhsis) is proven.
3
Analyze the rebuttal of the opponent's proof
The opponent relies on the verse 'the free for the free, the slave for the slave' to argue like-for-like only. The Hanafi rebuttal: this verse does not negate the general, because (a) it only mentions some instances, (b) the lam in 'al-hurr' is for a specific historical covenant (ahd), not for defining the species (jins), as per Ibn Abbas' report. Thus it does not restrict the general command.
This is a classic usul argument: amm remains unless definitive takhsis is proven.
4
Understand the rationale: 'ismah based on humanity and abode
The underlying cause (illah) for qisas is equality in 'ismah (inviolability). Hanafis hold 'ismah is based on being human and residing in the abode of Islam (dar), not on religion or status. Both free and slave are human and protected; therefore qisas applies.
If 'ismah were based on religion alone (as Shafi'is hold), then a Muslim slave would have higher protection than a non-Muslim free person, leading to inconsistency.
5
Note the practical consequence
The ruling ensures deterrence (zajr) and cuts off corruption (fasad). A slave's life is not undervalued; he is not mere property. This reflects the Islamic principle of the sanctity of life.
The slave's disability or apostasy (if he was a Muslim slave who apostatizes) does not remove 'ismah because it is a juridical effect, not real.

No Qisas for Killing a Muslim Mistaken for a Polytheist in Battle
Ruling and proof for mistaken killing in battle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mistaken killing in battle and identify the Quranic proof for kaffarah and diyah.

Core Idea
Killing a Muslim by mistake in battle incurs kaffarah and diyah, not qisas, based on Quran 4:92.

In the chaos of battle, a Muslim soldier may mistakenly kill a fellow Muslim, thinking he is an enemy. Islamic law distinguishes this from intentional murder. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling and the Quranic evidence that exempts such a killing from qisas and instead requires expiation (kaffarah) and blood-money (diyah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a Muslim killing another Muslim in battle under the mistaken belief that the victim is a polytheist. The ruling is that there is no qisas; instead, the killer must perform kaffarah and pay diyah.
This applies when the victim is a protected Muslim (ismah) and the killing is unintentional.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is Quran 4:92, which explicitly commands kaffarah and diyah for mistaken killing of a believer.
The verse is quoted in the passage: 'Wa man qatala mu'minan khata'an...'
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The verse directly prescribes kaffarah (freeing a slave) and diyah (blood-money) for mistaken killing. The case in battle fits this category, so the ruling applies.
The text clarifies that this is 'one of the two types of mistake', thus falling under the general rule of khata'.
4
Note the exception
If the deceased Muslim was fighting in the enemy's ranks, his inviolability (ismah) is lost because he increased the enemy's numbers. In that case, no diyah is due.
The hadith 'Whoever increases the numbers of a people is one of them' is cited as proof.
5
Evaluate the proof
The Quranic verse is qat'i al-thubut (certain transmission) and qat'i al-dalalah (clear meaning), so the ruling is firmly established. The hadith provides an exception grounded in Sunna.
There is no ikhtilaf among the major schools on this basic ruling; it is muttafaq alayh.

Four Scenarios of Testimony on Pardon: Diya Distribution and the Role of Istihsan
Expansion of Al-Hidayah's ruling on testimony of pardon leading to four rational divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios of testimony on pardon in qisas and apply the Hanafi ruling of istihsan to determine diya shares.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas when a brother's confirmation of testimony shifts liability for diya.

This passage from Husn al-Dirayah expands on a ruling in Al-Hidayah concerning two witnesses who testify that the victim's brother has pardoned the killer. The commentary identifies four possible scenarios of confirmation or denial by the killer and the brother, and explains how diya is distributed in each. At the heart of the discussion is a dispute between qiyas and istihsan: whether the brother, by confirming the witnesses, becomes liable to pay them one-third of the diya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
Work through the four rational divisions of responses (confirmation/denial) by the killer and the brother (the person testified against). List the diya outcome for each scenario as stated in the text.
The text explicitly divides them: both confirm, both deny, killer alone confirms, brother alone confirms.
2
Understand the qiyas position
Recognize the qiyas argument: if the brother confirms the witnesses, his confirmation is inconsistent with the killer's denial; therefore, the brother should owe nothing because the witnesses' claim against the killer is unproven.
Qiyas relies on the principle that the killer's confession for the brother is invalidated by the brother's denial of the witnesses.
3
Analyze the istihsan reasoning
Trace the istihsan argument: the killer by denying the witnesses implicitly confessed that the brother's share of qisas turned into money; the brother's confirmation of the witnesses then becomes an iqrar (confession) in favor of the witnesses for that money, making him liable.
The analogy with a man confessing a debt and the debtor redirecting it to a third party illustrates how iqrar can transfer liability.
4
Compare the practical consequences
Under qiyas, the brother retains his full diya share; under istihsan, he must pay one-third to the witnesses. This shows the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) of the dispute.
The commentator affirms istihsan as the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).

Commentary
This passage from 'Husn al-Dirayah' is a commentary expanding on a ruling from Al-Hidayah regarding the distribution of blood money (diya) when two witnesses testify that the victim's brother has pardoned the killer, thereby dropping qisas. The commentary critiques the Hidayah's mention of only three scenarios, insisting there are four rational divisions. It explains that the ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference) as opposed to strict qiyas. The four scenarios are: (1) both killer and brother confirm the witnesses – then the witnesses get two-thirds of diya; (2) both deny – then the witnesses get nothing, the brother gets one-third; (3) killer alone confirms – each of the three gets one-third; (4) brother alone confirms – brother gets one-third. The commentary provides the legal reasoning for each, emphasizing that when the brother confirms the witnesses, he effectively admits that the witnesses' share of qisas has become money for them, and his confirmation is treated like a valid iqrar (confession) for something already acknowledged by the killer. The overarching principle is that the witnesses' testimony about the brother's pardon cannot be used to benefit themselves directly, but when the killer or brother confirms it, the rights are adjusted accordingly.

Rider's Liability: Distinguishing Avoidable and Unavoidable Harm
Ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A rider is liable for harms he can control, but not for unpredictable kicks from hindquarters.

This chapter addresses injuries caused by animals and the liability upon the rider. The central question is whether the rider is responsible for every harm caused by his mount. The Hanafi school draws a clear line based on controllability: the rider is liable for actions he can reasonably prevent, but not for sudden, unavoidable kicks. This principle leads to a disagreement with Imam al-Shafi‘i, who holds the rider liable for all actions attributed to him.

(And in the gouging out of both eyes of a slave, the master may hand him over and take his value, or keep him without taking the decrease): That is, if the master wishes, he may hand the slave over to the offender and take the full value, and if he wishes, he may keep him without taking any compensation for the decrease. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said: He has the option between handing over and keeping with compensation for the decrease. And al-Shafi'i said: He guarantees the full value and keeps the blind body, for he makes the guarantee in exchange for what is lost, so the remainder remains his property, as when one eye is gouged out. They (the two companions) said: Financial value is considered with respect to limbs, and it is only dropped with respect to the essence [of the slave] itself. And the ruling of property is as we mentioned, like the case of a large tear. Abu Hanifa said: If financial value is considered, then humanity is not disregarded. So acting on both similarities necessitated what we mentioned. [1] His statement: 'what we mentioned' etc.: That is, if he wishes, he keeps the property and takes what it decreased, and if he wishes, he hands over the property and takes its value. [2] His statement: 'like the case of a large tear': That is, one who tears another's garment a large tear: if the owner wishes, he may hand over his garment to him and hold him liable for its value, and if he wishes, he may keep the garment and hold him liable for the decrease. [3] His statement: 'humanity is not disregarded': That is, with respect to the essence, and also with respect to limbs. Do you not see that if a slave cuts off the hand of another slave, the master is ordered to hand over or ransom? This is from the rulings of humanity, because the requirement for injury to property is that his neck be sold for it. Then, from the rulings of humanity, it cannot be divided into parts, nor can the offender own it. From the rulings of property, it can be divided and owned as a thousand pieces. So acting on both similarities necessitated what we mentioned. That is, by consideration of humanity, liability should not be distributed, but only in exchange for what is lost, and by consideration of property, he cannot take the entire substitute for the thing by keeping the body, just as he cannot do that with property. What they said is an elimination of the aspect of humanity, as they made it like a torn garment. What al-Shafi'i said is a total elimination of the aspect of property, as he made it like a free person whose eyes are gouged out. So we gave each similarity its due and said: If the master wishes, he may hand over his slave and take his value, by consideration of property; and if he wishes, he may keep him and get nothing, by consideration of humanity. Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute (with mention of al-Shafi'i) regarding the remedy when a slave's eyes are gouged out. The base matn gives two options: the master may either hand over the slave to the offender and take the full value, or keep the slave without taking any compensation for the decrease. The commentator explains that this is Abu Hanifa's position. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) disagree: they say the master has the option between handing over and keeping while also taking compensation for the decrease. Al-Shafi'i holds that the master must be given the full value and keeps the slave (the blind body) because the guarantee is in exchange for what is lost. The commentator then presents the reasoning: The Sahibayn argue that financial value is considered for limbs, only dropped for the essence; thus the master should get the decrease if he keeps the slave. Abu Hanifa argues that if financial value is considered, then humanity is not disregarded. The slave has both human and property aspects. By the human aspect, liability cannot be distributed across parts; by the property aspect, he cannot take the entire value while keeping the body. So Abu Hanifa gives the master two pure options: either treat it like property (hand over and take full value) or treat it like a free person (keep and get nothing). The Sahibayn's view treats it like a torn garment (property), and al-Shafi'i's view treats it like a free person (human). The Hanafi position thus balances both aspects.

Key Terms
فقء (faq'): Gouging out (of eyes); causing loss of eyesight.
أرش (arsh): Compensation for injury to a limb, measured as a fraction of diyah.
قيمة (qimah): Market value (of the slave) at the time of injury.
