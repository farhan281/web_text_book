Joint Amputation: Hanafi vs Shafi‘i on Two Hands for One Hand
Dispute between Hanafi and Shafi'i on qisas for two hands cut by one stroke

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi and Shafi‘i positions on joint amputation of a hand, state the Hanafi proof based on divisibility, and explain the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
When two cut one hand: Hanafi says diyah, Shafi‘i says qisas—divisibility of limb matters.

This lesson explores a classic inter-madhhab dispute about joint criminal acts. How should Islamic law treat two people who together cut off one hand? The Hanafi school argues that the hand is divisible, so each cutter is only partially responsible—full qisas (retaliation) cannot apply. The Shafi‘i school analogizes to murder: if two kill one, both are executed, so if two cut one hand, both hands should be cut. We will examine the legal reasoning behind each position and the practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
No hand is cut; both jointly guarantee diyah for one hand. This is the position of the Hanafi school as stated in the matn.
2
Identify the Shafi‘i objection
Imam al-Shafi‘i argues by analogy (qiyas) with murder: if two kill one, both are executed, so if two cut one hand, both should lose their hands.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi response is that the hand is divisible (mutajazzi'), so the cutting can be attributed partially to each person. The soul (nafs) is indivisible, so the analogy fails.
Key concept: mutajazzi' (divisible) vs ghayr mutajazzi' (indivisible).
4
Apply the analogy
Because the hand is divisible, each cutter is only responsible for a part of the injury, and qisas cannot be applied partially. Instead, they share the diyah.
5
Consider the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafi imposes diyah only; Shafi‘i would impose qisas (cutting both hands). This affects actual sentencing in a joint amputation case.

The Validity of a Slave's Confession to Qisas
Hanafi proof for slave's confession to qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school validates a slave's confession to qisas and distinguish the underlying proof from Zufar's property analogy.

Core Idea
A slave's confession to qisas is valid because in blood he is free, and he is not suspect.

This passage from a hashiyah on al-Hidayah deals with a key intra-Hanafi dispute: whether a slave's confession to intentional homicide leading to qisas is valid. The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) holds that it is, while Zufar disagrees, drawing an analogy to property. We will unpack the rational proof (dalil 'aqli) that establishes the Hanafi position and see how blood (dam) is treated differently from property (mal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a slave confessing to intentional killing. The ruling is that the confession is valid and the slave is subjected to qisas.
This is the Hanafi position ('inda).
2
Understand the Hanafi proof
Two reasons are given: (1) the slave is not suspect because the confession harms himself, (2) in blood matters the slave is considered free (mubqa 'ala asl al-hurriyyah).
The proof is rational (dalil 'aqli), not textual.
3
Analyze Zufar's objection
Zufar analogizes to property: a slave's confession in property matters is invalid because it harms the master's right. He extends this to blood.
Zufar is a Hanafi scholar, so this is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
The response distinguishes blood from property: in blood, the master has no right, and the slave is treated as a free person. The analogy to creditors in an estate (gharimayn) shows that competing rights can coexist even when limited.
The text cites al-Hidayah and al-Inayah for elaboration.
5
Apply the rule broadly
The same principle applies to hudud (fixed punishments): a slave's confession to a hadd offense is valid, and the master cannot confess on behalf of the slave.
The commentary extends the ruling beyond qisas.

Ruling on Shooting That Kills Two: First Intentional, Second Accidental
Hanafi ruling and proof for two deaths from one shot

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a single shot killing two persons—qisas for the first and diyah on the 'aqila for the second—and explain the talil that a single act is multiplied by its effects.

Core Idea
One shot, two deaths: the first is intentional (qisas), the second is accidental (diyah) because the act is multiplied by its effects.

This passage from a sharh work presents a specific hanafi ruling with its proof. It addresses the scenario where a single intentional shot kills two persons: the first victim is intentionally targeted, the second is an unintended victim. The Hanafi school handles this by treating each death according to its own legal character—qisas for the intentional killing, and diyah (blood money) on the shooter's 'aqila for the accidental killing. The proof is rooted in a powerful legal principle: 'al-fi'lu yata'addadu bita'addudi al-athar'—the act is multiplied by the multiplicity of its effects. This principle not only justifies the ruling but also answers objections and distinguishes the Hanafi position from that of al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and the Hanafi Ruling
Read the passage and extract the factual scenario: a person shoots at a man intentionally; the arrow passes through and kills a second person. State the Hanafi ruling: qisas (retaliation) for the first victim, and diyah (blood money) on the 'aqila for the second victim. Note that the Hanafi school makes no distinction between whether the deaths occur sequentially or simultaneously.
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Recognize the Shafi'i Disagreement
The passage mentions that Imam al-Shafi'i distinguishes between sequential deaths (qisas only for the first) and simultaneous deaths (casting lots to determine which victim gets qisas). Identify the difference and understand its practical consequence.
The Shafi'i reliance on casting lots arises from the physical impossibility of cutting one hand for two claimants at the same time.
3
Grasp the Hanafi Proof: Multiplicity of Effects
The Hanafi proof is a talil: the act of shooting is one physical act, but it produces multiple effects (two deaths). The law treats each effect as a separate legal act. Therefore, the first death is intentional (the shooter aimed at that person) and the second is accidental (the shooter did not intend to kill the second). This principle is supported by everyday examples: the same arrow that kills may also break a pot, each being a different legal act.
The principle is 'al-fi'lu yata'addadu bita'addudi al-athar'.
4
Apply the Principle to Repel the Objection
The passage ends by repelling an objection: 'How can one shot become two different acts?' The answer is that the legal identity of the act follows its effects. Each effect generates its own ruling. This is the core of the Hanafi reasoning.
This objection might arise from a literal view of the physical act.
5
Identify the Legal Classification of the Second Death
The second death is classified as khata' (accidental), specifically one of the two types of mistake: a mistake in the act (e.g., aiming at game but hitting a human). This classification removes qisas and imposes diyah on the 'aqila.
Khata' can be mistake in act or mistake in intent; here it is mistake in act.

Eight Scenarios: Cutting Hand Then Killing – The Complete vs Deficient Equivalent
Intra-Hanafi dispute with eight scenarios and uṣūl principle of complete vs deficient equivalent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the eight scenarios for two successive acts (cutting hand then killing), state the Hanafi positions (Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn) on whether both acts require qisas, and apply the uṣūl principle of complete vs deficient equivalent.

Core Idea
The complete equivalent (cutting then killing) is preferred over the deficient (killing only), but the Sahibayn hold that the lesser penalty merges into the greater.

This passage from the chapter on two successive acts sets out eight scenarios by varying the intent (intentional or mistaken) of each act and whether the wound healed before death. It then focuses on the case where both acts are intentional and no healing occurs, revealing a disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The dispute hinges on whether cutting then killing is the 'complete equivalent' (al-mithl al-kāmil) or whether the penalty for cutting is absorbed into the penalty for killing. Understanding this requires knowing the uṣūl principle that the complete equivalent is taken when possible, and the deficient only when the complete is impossible.

Guided Path
1

Identify the binary factors: cutting (intentional/mistake) × killing (intentional/mistake) × healing (yes/no) = 8. State each combination from the text.
The text explicitly says 'eight cases' and gives the counts.

Extract the ruling for each scenario
From the matn: if both intentional and healing occurred, qisas for both cutting and killing. If no healing, the matn says 'kadhā' (thus) according to Abu Hanifa, but the Sahibayn disagree. For mixed intents and mistaken cases, the matn mentions diya (blood money) suffices if no healing.
The commentary expands on the disagreement.
3

When both acts are intentional and no healing: Abu Hanifa requires qisas for both cutting and killing. The Sahibayn require only qisas for killing, not cutting. Identify the reasoning for each side.
The key phrase: 'وعندهما يقتل ولا يقطع' (according to the two, he is killed and not cut).
4
Analyze the uṣūl principle
The complete equivalent (cutting then killing) is preferred over the deficient (killing only). Abu Hanifa says the complete is possible; the Sahibayn say it is not because cutting a dead body is not valid retaliation. The uṣūl rule: the deficient is only taken when the complete is impossible.
This is referenced in the passage as 'تحقيقه في أصول الفقه'.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
According to Abu Hanifa, the heir (wali) has the option to demand both cutting and killing (the complete) or just killing (the deficient). According to the Sahibayn, the heir must demand only killing; the cutting is forfeited.
Later Hanafi fatwa often adopts the Sahibayn's view.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Qisas: Cutting Hand Then Killing
Presents eight scenarios and the intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas for combined acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the combination of qisas for cut and kill, and explain the rationale of sirayah underlying the Sahibayn's view.

Core Idea
When an intentional cut leads to death without recovery, the Sahibayn treat the two acts as one killing; Abu Hanifa allows separate or combined retribution.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya presents eight scenarios of cutting then killing, with a key intra-Hanafi dispute. When both acts are intentional and there is no recovery, Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples disagree on whether the heir may exact retribution for both the cut and the kill, or only for the killing. The disagreement hinges on the concept of sirayah (transmission of injury) and the nature of qisas as a rational likeness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the eight-case framework
Understand that the scenarios are based on intentionality (amd or khata) of cut and kill, and presence or absence of recovery (bur'). This gives 2x2x2 = 8 cases.
The matn outlines these eight cases as the foundation for the rulings.
2
Focus on the contested case: both intentional, no recovery
This is the case where Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ. Both agree on the rulings for the other seven cases.
The other cases involve different combinations of intentionality and recovery, resulting in either multiple qisas or multiple diyah.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Abu Hanifa argues that the cut and kill are two separate intentional acts, each warranting its own qisas. The heir has the option to exact both (cut then kill) as a complete likeness (mithl kamil) or just kill.
The cut and kill together form a perfect counterpart to the victim's injuries in form and meaning.
4

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that the killing completes the effect of the cut (sirayah), making the two acts a single homicide. Thus, only qisas for killing is due; the cut is subsumed.
This is analogous to multiple blows that lead to death; the cumulative act is treated as one offense.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, the heir can choose to cut the offender's hand before killing him, or just kill. Under the Sahibayn, only death penalty is allowed; no cutting.
This affects the severity of punishment and the possibility of the offender surviving the cut if the heir chooses only killing.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute discussing the ruling when a person cuts off another's hand and then kills the same person. The base text (matn) states that there are eight possible scenarios based on whether the cutting and killing are intentional or accidental, and whether there was recovery (bur') between the two acts. The commentary expands on these, focusing on the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Sahibayn). For the case where both acts are intentional and there is no recovery, Abu Hanifa holds that the heir of the victim has the option to exact retribution for both the cut and the kill (i.e., cut the offender's hand then kill him) or to kill only. This is because the cut followed by the kill is a complete likeness (mithl kamil) in form and meaning. The Sahibayn, however, rule that the heir can only kill; the cut is subsumed into the killing because the killing completes the effect of the cut, making the two acts a single offense. The commentary from 'al-Talwih' reinforces the Sahibayn's view by analogizing to sirayah (transmission of injury) from the act of cutting to the killing. For accidental acts, if recovery occurs, both blood moneys (for cut and kill) are due; if no recovery, only the blood money for the killing suffices, because blood money (diyah) is not a rational likeness (mithl ghayr ma'qul) and is only due when the effect of the cut is consolidated (i.e., it is known that it did not lead to death). The commentary also notes that the difference in treatment between intentional and accidental cases lies in the nature of the recompense: retaliation (qisas) is a rational likeness, while blood money is not.

Two Accidental Injuries with and without Healing
Ruling on two accidental injuries and effect of healing between them

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah for two accidental injuries and apply the concept of bar' (healing) to determine whether one or two diyat are due.

Core Idea
Healing between two accidental injuries separates them; no healing merges them into one act requiring one diyah.

This passage from the commentary on al-Wiqayah addresses a subtle scenario: when a person is first accidentally cut and later accidentally killed, the key factor is whether the cut healed before death. The Hanafi ruling hinges on this healing (bar'), which determines whether the cut's effect transferred (saraya) into the death. The passage provides a rational proof and notes an intra-madhhab difference regarding the guardian's options.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage deals with two accidental injuries: a cut and a killing. State the basic ruling: if healing occurred between them, both diyat are due; if not, only the diyah of killing is due.
2
Locate the key condition (bar')
Bar' (healing) is the decisive factor. It indicates that the cut did not transfer to the death, so the cut is an independent injury requiring its own diyah.
3
Understand the proof (dalil aqli)
The text uses a rational proof: diyah is not a like-for-like (mithl) compensation, so its obligation is not automatic; it requires evidence that the act is independent. Qisas is like-for-like, so it is due by default. Here, siraayah (transfer) is negated by healing.
The passage contrasts with intentional acts where qisas applies due to like-for-like nature.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), if the cut heals and then the person dies accidentally, the guardian cannot demand qisas for the cut (only for the killing). This implies the cut is subsumed into the death for qisas purposes, though for diyah the cut remains separate.
The text says 'according to them (the two)'. Abu Hanifa's view is not explicitly stated here.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that if healing occurred, both diyat are due; the guardian may only seek qisas for the killing (i.e., cannot cut for the wound).
The commentary presents this as the operative ruling.

Core Idea
Forgiveness of a specific injury does not automatically forgive the resulting death.

This lesson unpacks an intra-Hanafi dispute: if a man cuts off another's hand, the victim forgives the cutting, but then dies from that injury, is the cutter liable for compensation? Abu Hanifa and his two main students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree. We will examine each position, the reasoning behind it, and the relied-upon ruling for fatwa.

Identify the fact pattern and the legal issue

The concept of saraya is crucial: the injury spreads from the hand to cause death. This shows that the initial act was actually a killing, not just a cutting. Abu Hanifa uses this to argue that the forgiveness, which was for cutting, does not apply to the killing that became evident later.
The Sahibayn respond that the forgiveness was for the cause, which is the same regardless of the outcome.

Liability for Death Caused by Limb Retaliation: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability when retaliation for a limb causes death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on liability when limb retaliation causes death and distinguish it from the views of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
Executing a limb retaliation does not waive the right to qisas if death results.

This lesson explores a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: what happens when a person lawfully retaliates for a limb (e.g., cuts off a hand) but the injury spreads and kills the offender? Does the executor face any further liability? Imam Abu Hanifa holds that he is killed for murder and his 'aqlah pays diyah, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue for exemption. We will examine the proofs, responses, and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa: the executor is killed (qisas for murder) and his 'aqlah must pay the full diyah of the victim, because his right was only to the limb, not to cause death.
The diyah is on the 'aqlah because the killing is treated as quasi-error (khata').
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Abu Yusuf (on qisas) and the Sahibayn (on diyah) hold that the executor incurs no liability: Abu Yusuf argues the lawful cut exempts further claims, while both Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that executing one's right precludes any compensation for death.
The opposing view is not from outside the Hanafi school but from within.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine their reasoning: Abu Yusuf says 'because when he proceeded to cut in retaliation, he exempted the other from what lies beyond.' The Sahibayn say 'because he executed his right' – the right to cut covers the entire act.
The opponents analogize to cases where a lawful act (like an imam's cutting of a thief's hand) carries no liability for accidental death.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa's response distinguishes between the right to the limb and the right to life. Executing the cut does not waive the right to qisas for death; the death is a separate wrong. The analogies to the imam and cupper are rejected because those actors are obligated or contracted, whereas in qisas the guardian is encouraged to pardon, so the act is merely permissible and permissibility is conditional on safety.
The Hanafi response uses the principle that obligations (like the imam's duty) are not conditioned on safety, but permissibility (like hunting) is.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the executor is subjected to qisas (killed) and his 'aqlah pays diyah. Under Abu Yusuf/Sahibayn, he faces no penalty. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's.
This ruling prevents the misuse of limb qisas as a means to cause death with impunity.

Core Idea
Cutting beyond your right triggers compensation, even if you had the right to kill—shubhah saves you from qisas but not from diyah.

The key is that the guardian exceeded his right (ghayru haqqihi) because his right was to kill, not to cut. However, qisas for the cut is precluded by a legal doubt (shubhah) since the cut could be seen as a step toward exercising his right to kill. Cutting the Hand of a Killer: Liability for Diyah after Pardon (Intra-Hanafi Dispute)
Ruling on diyah for cutting hand of a person who owes qisas for murder and is later pardoned

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the liability for diyah when a wali cuts the killer's hand and then pardons, and explain the reasoning behind each position.

Core Idea
If you have the right to kill, you do not automatically have the right to cut; but if you cut, you may be liable for diyah — this dispute turns on whether the limb is subordinate to the life.

This lesson examines a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute about the scope of qisas rights. The case: a wali cuts off the killer's hand before pardoning him from death. Is the wali liable for the diyah of the hand? Abu Hanifa says yes, because his right was only to take life. The Sahibayn say no, because the right to take life includes the right to destroy parts. Understanding this disagreement sharpens your ability to parse the boundaries of legal rights and the effect of shubha.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the rights involved
Recognize that the wali has a right to qisas (death) for the murder, and the question is whether cutting a limb is within that right or an excess.
The act of cutting occurs before the pardon.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the wali is liable for the diyah because his right is specifically to take life, not to cut limbs. Cutting is an act beyond his right.
The shubha (doubt) drops qisas for the hand but does not drop financial liability.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the wali is not liable because his right to kill includes the right to destroy all parts of the body. Cutting the hand was an exercise of that right.
The pardon is considered a waiver of the remaining right, not a retroactive invalidation of the cutting.
4
Analyze the effect of shubha
Explain how shubha affects the case: it prevents qisas for the cut, but according to Abu Hanifa, it does not prevent financial liability. For the Sahibayn, the shubha extends to the entire act, eliminating both qisas and diyah.
This is where the core disagreement on the nature of subsidiary rights appears.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the wali must pay diyah for the hand; under the Sahibayn, he pays nothing. This difference matters in judicial rulings.
The text does not give a preferred view, so both are potentially operative.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a specific case: a person (the wali of the slain) has the right to qisas (retaliation) for the murder of his relative. Instead of killing the murderer, he cuts off the murderer's hand, and then later pardons the killer from the death penalty. The question is whether he is liable to pay the diyah (blood money) for the hand. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that he must pay the diyah for the hand. His reasoning is that the wali's right was to take the life of the killer, not to cut off a limb. When he cut the hand, he exercised a right that was not his, so he is liable. However, the cutting does not trigger qisas for the wound (i.e., cutting off the hand) because there is a shubha (ambiguity) that he might have intended to take the life by cutting. The cut is a wound that could lead to death, but qisas for the wound is dropped due to shubha, and instead he owes the diyah. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) disagree. They say he owes nothing. Their argument is that his right to take the life includes the right to destroy all parts of the body. By cutting off the hand, he was exercising part of that right. When he later pardons the death, it is a pardon for whatever remains of the body beyond the hand, so he is not liable for the hand. The text then discusses various analogies: like the Imam cutting a thief's hand, the cupper doing his job, etc., but the commentators note that those cases are different because there is either an obligation or a contract, whereas here the wali is merely encouraged to pardon, and his act is one of absolute permissibility (ibaha). The glosses clarify the linguistic and legal points. The passage concludes by noting that the ruling differs depending on whether the wound spreads and causes death, and what the timing of the pardon is. This is a delicate ruling that highlights the Hanafi methodology of balancing rights, shubha, and the underlying intentions.

Key Terms
أرش (arsh): The diyah (blood money) specifically for wounds or loss of limbs, as opposed to diyah for the entire life.
قود (qisas): Retaliation in kind for murder or wounding.
شبهة (shubha): A legal ambiguity or doubt that prevents the application of the full penalty, such as here where cutting the hand could be seen as part of taking life.
سراية (sirayah): The spreading of a wound from the initial site to the rest of the body, causing death.
عفو (afw): Pardon; the waiver of the right to qisas by the victim or his heirs.

Liability for Limb Cutting Before Pardon of Qisas: Sahibayn vs Abu Hanifa
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for limb after pardon

Core Idea
Parts subordinate to the whole: if you have right to life, limbs are included; pardon only affects what remains.

This passage examines a nuanced question in qisas law: if someone entitled to kill another instead cuts a limb and later pardons the qisas, is he liable for the limb? The Hanafi school splits: Sahibayn argue no liability because the right to life encompasses the limbs, while Abu Hanifa likely holds liability. The reasoning hinges on the relationship between the whole and its parts, and the effect of pardon on already executed acts.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a person who has a right to qisas (retaliatory killing) against someone but instead of killing him, he cuts a limb of that person. The question is whether he becomes liable for damages (arsh) for the limb, especially after he pardons the qisas. The reasoning is that had he actually executed the qisas (killed him), his right in the limbs would have been subordinate; but since he did not execute, his right in the limb never manifested. Mode of Establishing Qisas for Heirs: Khilafa vs Wiratha
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the nature of qisas establishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the establishment of qisas for heirs and explain the practical differences (thamarah al-khilaf) between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Qisas is an act, not property — heirs receive it directly via khilafa, not by inheritance.

This passage from the chapter on testimony in killing addresses a foundational intra-Hanafi dispute: how does the right to qisas (retaliatory execution) pass from the victim to the heirs? Imam Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is established directly for the heirs by way of succession (khilafa), because the deceased cannot own an act. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) consider it inherited like property. Understanding this difference is crucial for procedural rules in qisas claims.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Identify what Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn each say about how qisas is established for the heirs.
Abu Hanifa: ibtida'an bi tariq al-khilafa. Sahibayn: bi tariq al-wiratha.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof
Analyze the Quranic verse (17:33) and the analogy of a slave accepting a gift that goes to the master.
Quran: 'We have given his heir authority' — the sultan is given to the heir directly, not inherited.
3
Understand the rationale
Why can't the deceased own qisas? Because qisas is an act (fi'l), not property (mal). The deceased has no capacity to own an act.
Compare with diyah: property can be owned by deceased, hence inherited.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
What practical differences result from this dispute? Focus on evidence by one heir and validity of pardon.
According to Abu Hanifa, one heir's testimony does not benefit absent heirs; each must prove. According to Sahibayn, it does.
5
Distinguish qisas from diyah
When qisas is converted to diyah (e.g., by pardon), the right becomes property, so inheritance rules apply.
This shows the consistency of Abu Hanifa's position: the mode follows the nature of the right.

Commentary
The passage opens with a chapter heading on testimony in homicide cases, specifically addressing how the right of retaliation (qisas) vests in the heirs. The central issue is an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, qisas is established for the heirs initially through succession (khilafah), meaning the right arises directly for them, not passing through the deceased. His students, Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, hold that it vests through inheritance (wirathah), meaning the deceased first owns the right then it transfers to the heirs.

The commentator explains the practical significance: since qisas is an act of physical retaliation, the deceased cannot own an action. Hence, the Quranic verse (17:33) giving 'authority to the heir' supports Abu Hanifa's view. The verse explicitly addresses the wali (heir) directly, indicating the right belongs to him from the outset. In contrast, property rights like diyah (blood money) or debts can be owned by the deceased because property is beneficial for his needs (e.g., covering funeral expenses). An analogy is given: if a person sets a net before death and game is caught after death, the deceased owns it—this is not possible with an action.

A key consequence is in legal representation. Under Abu Hanifa, one heir cannot represent the others in court to prove qisas without specific agency, because the right belongs to each initially by khilafah. Had it been inheritance (as per the Sahibayn), one heir could represent the others. The commentator gives a concrete example: if one brother (present) brings evidence that his father was killed while another brother is absent, the evidence does not establish qisas regarding the absent brother. When the absent brother returns, he must present the evidence again himself or through an agent. However, the present brother may be imprisoned on suspicion until the absent one appears.

The passage then clarifies the difference between khilafah and wirathah: wirathah requires prior ownership of the deceased, then transfer; khilafah does not. In khilafah, the successor (the heir) takes the right directly, similar to a slave receiving a gift where ownership goes to the master directly. Thus, khilafah is more general than wirathah.

Overall, the text establishes that the method of vesting qisas directly affects procedural rules in litigation, specifically the capacity of one heir to represent others. The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is often Abu Hanifa's view, though the text presents both and applies the consequences accordingly.

Core Idea
Qisas is a personal right; each heir must prove their own claim according to Abu Hanifa.

In intentional killing (qisas), Abu Hanifa holds that the absent heir must re-establish evidence. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, holding that re-establishment is not needed. Understand the reasoning: qisas is a personal right that each heir may exercise or waive individually.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.

In accidental killing (khatā) and debt (dayn), there is no need for re-establishment by consensus. The reason is that the obligation is monetary (diya or inheritance), and money is established for all heirs collectively through inheritance, not as personal rights.

The commentary adds that the rule applies when the same judge presides. If the judge differs, or if the present heir only proved his own share, the absent heir must re-establish. Also, for real estate there is disagreement, though the preferred view is no repetition.
These conditions refine the scope of the ruling.

Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If Abu Hanifa's view is followed, the absent heir's case will be dismissed unless he re-establishes evidence. According to the Sahibayn, the existing evidence suffices and the absent heir may benefit from it without repetition.
This difference impacts court procedure and the burden on heirs.

The Void Testimony of Interested Heirs and Its Effect as Acknowledgment
Ruling with proof on void testimony and its legal consequences in qisas/diyat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a testimony tainted by personal interest is void, and analyze its treatment as an acknowledgment (iqrār) that binds the testifiers in the context of qisas and diya.

Core Idea
Self-interested testimony is void, but it stands as the testifiers' own forgiveness, converting their share of qisas to diya.

This lesson examines a case where two out of three heirs of a murder victim testify that the third heir has forgiven the killer. At first glance, this seems like straightforward testimony, but a deeper analysis reveals that the testifiers stand to benefit—they gain the possibility of receiving diya instead of qisas. The Hanafi school rules that such self-serving testimony is void. However, the claim has legal effect: it is treated as an acknowledgment (iqrār) from the testifiers that they themselves have forgiven their own shares. This leads to a nuanced set of consequences depending on whether the killer and the absent heir confirm or deny the testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their rights
The victim has three sons (walīyān al-qawad). The killer is the defendant. Two sons testify that the third son forgave the killer. Each son has a share in the right to qisas (retaliation) or diya (blood money).
In Hanafi law, the right to qisas belongs to the heirs collectively; forgiveness by one heir only extinguishes his share.
2
Understand the ruling on the testimony
The testimony is ruled void (bāṭil) because the testifiers draw profit (magnam, māl, nafʿ) to themselves: if the testimony were accepted, they would benefit by converting qisas into diya, which they can inherit.
The principle is that no one can testify in their own favor. The ʿilla (effective cause) is self-interest.
3
Distinguish between effect on self vs. effect on others
While the testimony is void as evidence against the third brother, it is treated as an acknowledgment (iqrār) by the testifiers that they themselves have forgiven their shares. Thus, their right to qisas is extinguished and replaced with a right to diya.
A person's acknowledgment is binding on themselves but not on others. The testifiers' statement cannot bind the third heir without his own confirmation.
4
Analyze the sub-cases based on confirmation/denial
The passage presents three main sub-cases: (i) The killer confirms the testimony, the third denies; (ii) both killer and third deny; (iii) the third confirms but the killer denies. Each leads to a different distribution of diya.
The distribution hinges on who is bound by the acknowledgment and whether the killer's admission (if any) creates liability.
5
Formulate the general principle
The key legal principle is that a testimony laden with personal interest is void as proof, but it may operate as an acknowledgment that binds only the testifiers. Claims against a third party require independent evidence or the third party's own admission.
This illustrates the Hanafi insistence on the disinterestedness of witnesses and the strict treatment of iqrār.

Pardon Report Scenarios and Istihsan in Qisas
Pardon report scenarios and istihsan vs qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios of pardon report by relatives and apply the Hanafi istihsan ruling to determine the killer's liability.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas when the killer's denial implies an acknowledgment that transforms the brother's share into property.

In qisas, when two relatives inform that a third relative has pardoned the killer, the liability depends on who affirms or denies the report. This lesson unpacks four scenarios, focusing on the Hanafi use of istihsan in the fourth case where only the brother affirms. Understanding this reveals how Hanafi jurists prioritize equitable outcomes over strict analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
List the four combinations based on whether the killer and the brother affirm or deny the informers' report of pardon. The matn gives: (1) both affirm, (2) both deny, (3) only killer affirms, (4) only brother affirms.
The scenarios are derived from the informers' claim that the brother pardoned.
2
Understand the default qiyas ruling
Under qiyas, in scenario 4 (brother alone affirms), the killer owes nothing because the informers' claim is unproven (killer denies) and the killer's acknowledgment to the brother is nullified by the brother's denial (since he affirms the informers, he denies their claim against him).
Qiyas is the general principle that a claim must be established by evidence.
3
Apply the istihsan reasoning
Istihsan treats the killer's denial of the informers as an implied acknowledgment that the brother's share of retaliation has transformed into property (diyah). Then, the brother's affirmation of the informers makes him acknowledge their share. Thus, the killer must pay one-third diyah to the brother.
The analogy is a man acknowledging a debt then specifying it belongs to another.
4
Note the conflict and resolution
The conflict is between qiyas (no liability) and istihsan (one-third diyah to brother). The Hanafi school adopts istihsan as the mufta bihi position, as recorded in al-Hidayah.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf resolved by preference for istihsan.
5
Extract the mufta bihi ruling
In practice, when only the brother affirms the pardon report, the killer pays one-third of the diyah to the brother. The informers receive nothing in this scenario.
In scenario 3 (only killer affirms), each gets one-third; in scenario 2 (both deny), brother gets one-third.

Commentary
This passage from the book of jinayat covers two distinct legal issues: the first deals with a scenario where two guardians of retaliation (the victim's brothers) inform that a third brother has pardoned the killer. The text presents four cases based on whether the killer and that brother affirm or deny the report. The second issue deals with contradictions in the testimony of two witnesses regarding the killing, specifically in time, place, or instrument, and how that affects the obligation of qisas or diyah. 

**First Issue (the four cases):** 
The matn lists four possibilities after two informers report that a third brother pardoned the killer: 
1. Both killer and brother affirm the report → the killer has no liability, and the two informers get two-thirds of the diyah. 
2. Both deny → the informers get nothing, but the brother (the one reported to have pardoned) gets one-third. 
3. Only the killer affirms → each of the three (two informers and the brother) gets one-third. 
4. Only the brother affirms → that brother gets one-third. 

The commentary explains the reasoning. In case 2, since the informers' testimony is against their own benefit (they are claiming the brother pardoned, which would eliminate their right to retaliation but give them property), it is not accepted because they draw benefit (their right transfers to property). Istihsan is then applied: despite the denial, the killer's denial implies an acknowledgment that the brother's share turned to property, and the brother's affirmation of the informers makes him acknowledge their share. This is analogous to a man acknowledging a debt then specifying it belongs to another. 

**Second Issue (contradictory witnesses):** 
If two witnesses disagree on the time, place, or instrument of the killing, their testimony is void (laghw) because each killing is considered a separate event. For example, one says 'with a stick' and the other says 'I don't know the instrument'—the testimony is invalid, as absolute and restricted differ in rulings (absolute: diyah from his property; restricted with stick: diyah on the 'aqila). However, if both agree on the killing but are ignorant of the instrument, diyah is obligatory (as the killing is established but not intentional). 

The commentary further explains that disagreement on time/place/instrument makes the testimony invalid because killing is not repeated; each specification constitutes a different case. The Hanafi position prefers istihsan in case 4, which is the mufta bihi position, while qiyas would exempt the killer. This intra-Hanafi distinction is noted in al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): A method of legal reasoning that adopts an exceptional ruling based on public good or necessity, overriding qiyas.
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation in kind for murder or injury, subject to strict conditions.
الدية (al-diyah): Blood-money paid as compensation to the victim's heirs in cases where qisas is not applicable or is waived.

Witness Contradictions: When Testimony is Void in Qisas
Witness contradictions in qisas testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contradictions in witness testimony (time, place, instrument) that void shahadah in qisas and distinguish cases where ignorance of the instrument still leads to diyah obligation.

Core Idea
Disagreement on time, place, or instrument voids testimony because each killing is unique and cannot be repeated.

The second part of the passage addresses contradictions in witness testimony regarding qisas. When two witnesses disagree on the time, place, or instrument of the killing, their testimony is void (laghw). However, if they agree on the killing but are both ignorant of the instrument, diyah is still obligatory. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi conditions for valid testimony and the reasoning behind each ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of contradiction
The text lists three specific contradictions: time, place, and instrument. Also, one witness may specify the instrument while the other claims ignorance. Each type renders testimony void.
These are material details; disagreement means the witnesses are testifying about different events.
2
Understand the rationale for voiding testimony
Killing is not an act that can be repeated or multiplied. A killing at one time or place is distinct from another. Similarly, a killing with a weapon (intentional) differs from one with a stick (quasi-intentional). Therefore, each killing requires individual testimony.
The Hanafi position is that testimony must be identical on all material facts to establish qisas.
3
Distinguish between absolute and restricted instruments
If one witness says 'killed with a stick' and the other says 'I don't know the instrument', the testimony is void because the absolute (unknown instrument) obliges diyah from the killer's own property, while the restricted (stick) obliges diyah on the 'aqila. They are different legal consequences.
This distinction is based on the different rulings for intentional and quasi-intentional killing.
4
Note the exception: both ignorant of instrument
If both witnesses agree on the killing but both say 'we do not know the instrument', then diyah is obligatory because the killing is established but the lack of intentionality means qisas is not applicable.
This is not a contradiction; ignorance on a detail that does not affect the core fact is acceptable.
5
Derive the ruling
The ruling: contradiction on time, place, or instrument voids the whole testimony, so neither qisas nor diyah is imposed based on that testimony. However, if both are ignorant of the instrument, diyah is still obligatory because the killing is confirmed.
The testimony must be consistent on all substantive aspects to be accepted.

However, if both agree on the killing but are ignorant of the instrument, diyah is obligatory (as the killing is established but not intentional). 

The commentary further explains that disagreement on time/place/instrument makes the testimony invalid because killing is not repeated; each specification constitutes a different case. The Hanafi position prefers istihsan in case 4, which is the mufta bihi position, while qiyas would exempt the killer. This intra-Hanafi distinction is noted in al-Hidayah.

Effect of Pardon When Jointly-Owned Slave Kills Relative

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Pardon by one joint-owner voids the entire claim (Abu Hanifa) or only half (Sahibayn), due to the unspecified nature of the original right to retaliation.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on the interaction of co-ownership and pardon in homicide cases. When two men jointly own a slave and that slave kills a relative of both owners, each owner initially has a right to retaliation (qisas) in half of the slave. If one owner pardons, that right converts to a monetary claim. The critical question is: does the pardon extinguish only the pardoning owner's share, or the entire claim? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on this point. Understanding their reasoning sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists handle shared rights and uncertainty in claims.

Guided Path
1
State the case and initial right
The case involves two co-owners of a slave. The slave kills a relative of both. Initially, each owner has a right to retaliation (qisas) in half of the slave's body. This right is joint and unspecified (mushāʿ).
2
Identify the event triggering the dispute
One owner pardons (ʿafā). By Hanafi law, pardon converts the right to retaliation into a financial claim for blood money (diyah).
3
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the entire claim is void (baṭala al-kull). Because the original qisas was shared without specification, when it converts to money, there are three possibilities: it attaches entirely to the other's share, entirely to his own share, or jointly to both. Due to this uncertainty, the claim cannot be established — the principle that what is established with certainty is not removed by doubt applies.
4
State the Sahibayn's ruling and reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the pardon extinguishes only the pardoning owner's share of the monetary claim. The non-pardoning owner's right remains, but half of that belongs to the pardoning owner (who cannot claim against his own slave). Therefore, the non-pardoning owner is entitled to one quarter of the full value. The pardoning owner must either give half of his share (quarter of the value) to the other or ransom it with a quarter of the full blood money.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the non-pardoning owner receives nothing. Under the Sahibayn, he receives one-quarter of the blood money or the equivalent value of the slave.
This is the key practical difference.

This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the consequence of one master's pardon when a jointly-owned slave kills a relative of both masters. The base text states that if both masters own the slave and the victim is related to both, and one master pardons, the entire claim is void according to Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) dissent: the pardoning master must either give half of his share of the slave to the other master or ransom it with a quarter of the full blood money. The commentator then explains Abu Hanifa's reasoning: since the right to retaliation (qisas) was originally shared without specification between the two masters, when the claim converts to a monetary right upon pardon, there is uncertainty about which half it attaches to. Because of this doubt, the monetary claim cannot be established (a principle: what is established with certainty is not removed by doubt). The Sahibayn's reasoning is that the non-pardoning master's half of the claim (which was originally the right to retaliation) becomes money in his ownership, but half of that money belongs to the pardoning master. The pardoning master cannot claim money from his own slave, so that portion drops, leaving only the quarter (half of the half) that belongs entirely to the non-pardoning master. This dispute is purely within the Hanafi school, and the commentary presents both positions without explicitly preferring one.

Definition and Rulings of Qasamah (Compurgation)
Definition, cause, pillar, conditions, and ruling of Qasamah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Qasamah, identify its cause, pillar, conditions, and ruling (wajib diyah) in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Qasamah protects the accused from qisas and prevents blood from being wasted.

This passage introduces the chapter on Qasamah (compurgation) in the Hanafi school of fiqh. Qasamah is a unique legal institution invoked when a dead body is found with signs of violence in a locality and the killer is unknown. It requires fifty men from that locality to swear oaths of innocence, thereby shifting liability from any individual to the community. Understanding its elements—cause, pillar, conditions, and ruling—is essential for correctly applying this procedure in contemporary settings.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and legal definition
Read the definition: Qasamah is oaths taken by fifty men from a locality where a dead body is found with signs of violence, swearing they did not kill nor know the killer.
Linguistically derived from 'qasm' meaning oath.
2
Identify the cause (sabab)
The cause is the existence of a killing with wounds, beating, or strangulation, and the killer being unknown.
The cause triggers the obligation of Qasamah.
3
Distinguish the pillar (rukn) from conditions (shurut)
The pillar is the administration of the oath on the tongue of each swearer. Conditions include majority, sanity, freedom, and completion of fifty oaths (with repetition if fewer men).
Conditions are external; the pillar is integral to the act.
4

After the oath, the judge decrees diyah (blood-money) from the locality. If they refuse and the guardian claims intent, they are imprisoned until they swear; if the guardian claims error, diyah is decreed upon default.
The ruling differs based on the guardian's claim.
5

Qasamah preserves blood from being wasted and rescues the accused from qisas. The number fifty is established by well-known hadith.
The institution is a mercy from the Shariah.

Liability of the 'Aqila: What They Bear and What They Do Not
Explains the liability of the 'Aqila for blood money and its exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five categories for which the 'Aqila does not bear diyah, identify the minimum amount they bear, and explain the exception for a slave's value using the hadith and Hanafi reasoning.

Core Idea
The 'Aqila bears diyah only for unintentional killing, at or above arsh al-mudihah, never for amd, sulh, iqrar, or small amounts.

The 'Aqila—the group of agnatic relatives—collectively pays blood money for accidental killings. But their liability is not absolute. This lesson unpacks a key hadith that defines the scope: they bear only for amounts that cause significant hardship. We will also resolve an apparent exception regarding the killing of a slave.

Guided Path
1
State the basic rule of 'Aqila liability
Identify the general principle: the 'Aqila bears what is due by the act of killing itself, i.e., diyah for unintentional homicide (khata' and shibh al-'amd).
The minimum amount is arsh al-mudihah (half of one-tenth of full diyah).
2
Locate the primary proof (dalil)
Examine the hadith of Ibn Abbas (marfu'): 'The 'Aqila does not bear for intentional killing, nor for a slave, nor for a settlement, nor for a confession, nor for what is less than arsh al-mudihah.'
The hadith is ahad but relied upon in this context.
3
List the five exceptions
From the hadith, five categories are excluded: intentional killing (amd), killing of a slave, settlement (sulh), confession (iqrar), and amounts below arsh al-mudihah.
Each category has its own reason, as explained in the text.
4
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
The 'Aqila's liability is to relieve the killer from heavy hardship; small amounts cause no such hardship, so they are not borne. The decisive limit (arsh al-mudihah) is fixed by revelation.
This is a principle of 'removal of hardship' (raf' al-haraj).
5
Resolve the objection about the slave
The hadith excludes 'slave' from 'Aqila liability for injuries. But for the full value (qimah) of a slave killed by mistake, the value is equivalent to diyah for a free person, so the 'Aqila bears it even if below arsh al-mudihah.
This is because the hadith's exclusion applies to amounts less than the full diyah for a slave, not to the full value.

قوله: أو عمد... الخ; أي في النفس أو الطرف, فإن العمد لا يوجب التخفيف بتحمل العاقلة, فوجب القود به, قال في «الأشباه»: لا تعقل العاقلة العمد إلّا في مسألة ما إذا عفا بعض الأولياء أو صالح, فإن نصيب الباقين ينقلب مالاً, وتتحمله العاقلة. أقول: وقد قدمناه في باب القود فيما دون النفس عن العلامة قاسم: إنه خلاف الرواية, ولم يقل به أحد, والذي في سائر الكتب أنه في مال القاتل, فتنبه. كذا في «رد المحتار».
قال: فيه إشكال, وهو أن من قتل عبد غيره خطأ, وقيمته أقل من أرش الموضحة فإن العاقلة تتحمل.
والجواب عن هذا الإشكال: إن المراد منه فيما دون النفس, فأما في النفس فالنص ورد بوجوب الدية على العاقلة, وهذا دية; لأن القيمة في العبد قائمة مقام الدية في الحر. كذا في «الكفاية».
Book of 'Aqila: Explanation of the 'Aqila
And for the client of mutual patronage (mawla al-muwalat) is his patron and his clan (hayy). And the 'Aqila bears what is due by the act of killing itself, and the amount of the blood money of al-mudihah (crushing wound) and above. Al-Nasa'i from Ibn Abi Rafi', the freed slave of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him), said: 'The patron (mawla) of a people is from among themselves.' As in al-Hidayah and some of its margins.
His statement: 'And for the client of mutual patronage': he is the ally (halif), so his patron with whom he made the contract of support and his 'Aqila bear blood money for him, and that is what is meant by 'his clan (hayy)'; i.e., the clan of his patron with whom he contracted, because the Arabs support each other through it, so it resembles the patronage of emancipation (wala' al-'itaqa). As in al-Zayla'i.
His statement: 'his patron and his clan': there is a disagreement with al-Shafi'i regarding it. It has passed in the chapter of patronage that al-Shafi'i says: Mutual patronage (muwalat) is nothing, because it nullifies the right of the public treasury (bayt al-mal). As in some margins of al-Hidayah.
His statement: 'And the 'Aqila bears what is due by the act of killing itself, and the amount of the blood money of al-mudihah, which is half of one-tenth (of the full blood money) and above.' The basis for this is the hadith of Ibn Abbas, narrated as a stop (mawquf) and raised (marfu') to the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him): 'The 'Aqila does not bear blood money for intentional killing, nor for a slave, nor for a settlement (sulh), nor for a confession (i'tiraf), nor for what is less than the blood money of al-mudihah.' And the blood money of al-mudihah is half of one-tenth of the blood money for the soul. And because the bearing (tahammul) is for the removal of hardship (ijhaf), and there is no hardship in a small amount, but rather in a large amount, and the decisive limit is known through revelation (sam'), so they do not bear less than half of one-tenth of the blood money.
Not (does the 'Aqila bear) what is due by a settlement (sulh), or a confession (iqrar) that the 'Aqila did not confirm, or intentional killing (amd) whose retaliation (qawad) fell due to a doubt (shubha).
His statement: 'Not': i.e., the 'Aqila does not bear what is due by a settlement or a confession, because the settlement and confession are not binding on the 'Aqila due to the deficiency of authority over them. However, in confession, the blood money is due in three years, and in settlement for intentional killing, the wealth is due immediately unless a term is stipulated in the settlement, then it is deferred. As in al-'Inayah.
His statement: 'that the 'Aqila did not confirm': he only qualified it because if they confirm, they bear it, because it is established by their mutual affirmation, and the prohibition was for their right, and they have authority over themselves. As in al-Hidayah.
His statement: 'or intentional killing... etc.': i.e., regarding the soul or limb. Indeed, intentional killing does not necessitate leniency by the 'Aqila bearing it, so retaliation (qawad) is obligatory. He said in al-Ashbah: The 'Aqila does not bear intentional killing except in the case when some of the heirs pardon or settle, then the share of the remaining heirs converts to wealth, and the 'Aqila bears it. I say: We have presented this in the chapter of retaliation for less than the soul from 'Allamah Qasim: it is contrary to the reliable transmission (riwayah), and no one has said it. What is in the other books is that it is in the wealth of the killer. So be mindful. As in Radd al-Muhtar.
He said: There is an objection, which is that someone who kills another's slave by mistake, and its value is less than the blood money of al-mudihah, the 'Aqila bears it.
The answer to this objection: What is meant (by the hadith) is regarding (what is) less than the soul (nafs). As for the soul, the text (nass) came with the obligation of blood money on the 'Aqila, and this is blood money (diyah) because the value (qimah) of the slave stands in place of the blood money for the free person. As in al-Kifayah.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains the rules of the 'Aqila (the group responsible for paying blood money) in Hanafi fiqh. The 'Aqila consists of the agnatic male relatives and, for a client of mutual patronage (mawla al-muwalat), includes the patron and his clan. The 'Aqila bears only the blood money due from the act of killing itself (i.e., unintentional killing such as khata' or shibh al-'amd) and only when the amount is at least the blood money of al-mudihah (a wound exposing bone), which is half of one-tenth of the full blood money (i.e., 5 camels or equivalent). The hadith of Ibn Abbas (the key proof) explicitly excludes intentional killing, killing of a slave, settlement, confession, and amounts less than arsh al-mudihah. The reasoning is that the 'Aqila's liability is to relieve the killer of heavy financial burden; small amounts do not constitute hardship. Exceptions: The 'Aqila does not bear amounts due from a settlement (sulh) or confession (iqrar) that they did not confirm, because these transactions lack authority over them. However, if they confirm, they bear it. Intentional killing (amd) is never borne by the 'Aqila unless some heirs pardon or settle, turning the share of the remaining heirs into wealth—but this is a weak opinion contrary to the reliable transmission. An objection arises: if someone kills a slave by mistake and the slave's value is less than arsh al-mudihah, the 'Aqila still bears it because the value of the slave is equivalent to diyah for a free person, and the hadith's exclusion of 'less than arsh al-mudihah' applies only to amounts less than soul (i.e., for injuries). Thus, for the soul of a free person or the full value of a slave, the 'Aqila bears even if below arsh al-mudihah.

Key Terms
العاقلة (al-'Aqila): The group of agnatic male relatives (and in some cases the clan of a client) who collectively pay blood money (diyah) on behalf of a member who has committed unintentional homicide.
أرش الموضحة (arsh al-mudihah): The blood money for a wound that exposes the bone (mudihah). It is fixed as half of one-tenth of the full diyah (i.e., 5 camels or equivalent).
الموالاة (al-muwalat): A contract of mutual patronage between a non-Arab Muslim and an Arab tribe or individual, establishing inheritance and 'Aqila relations in the absence of blood ties.
