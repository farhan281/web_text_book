Sick Master Writes Kitaba for Double Value: Slave's Options
Explains the options for slave and heirs when a sick master writes kitaba for double value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the slave's options when a sick master writes kitaba for double value, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
When a sick master writes kitaba for double value, the slave must either pay two-thirds of the named price immediately or be re-enslaved—the dispute is over the basis of the immediate payment.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the consequences of a kitaba contract made by a terminally ill person. The core question: how much must the slave pay immediately to avoid re-enslavement? Two views emerge, each grounded in a different analysis of the heirs' rights over the consideration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling trigger
The master is terminally ill (marad al-mawt) and writes a kitaba contract for double the slave's value, with deferred payment. The master dies. The heirs reject the deferment. What options does the slave have?
The ruling is within the context of the one-third rule for transactions of the sick.
2
State the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the slave chooses between paying two-thirds of the named consideration immediately and the rest deferred, or being re-enslaved.
Named consideration is the full amount specified in the kitaba, e.g., 2000 dirhams.
3
State the ruling of Muhammad al-Shaybani
According to Muhammad, the slave chooses between paying two-thirds of the actual value of the slave immediately and the rest up to the full named consideration deferred, or being re-enslaved.
Actual value is the market price, e.g., 1000 dirhams.
4
Analyze the dalil of each side
Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf argue that the entire named consideration is the price of the slave's freedom (badal al-raqaba), so the heirs' right attaches to the whole amount, thus only one-third can be deferred. Muhammad argues that the sick person cannot defer payment on the portion equal to the value (which belongs to the heirs), but can defer the excess because he could have abandoned it altogether.
The distinction hinges on whether the excess is a gift or part of the price.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical difference is the immediate payment amount: under the mufta bihi (Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf), the slave pays two-thirds of the named consideration; under Muhammad, he pays two-thirds of the actual value. The mufta bihi is the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
In the example, named consideration 2000, value 1000: immediate payment 1333.33 vs 666.67.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling_statement and an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a sick person (marid) who writes a kitaba contract for his slave for double the slave's value, with payment deferred. The master dies. The passage first states the rule: the slave's freedom is established for one-third of his value (from the deceased's estate) apparently. When the heirs reject the contract (i.e., do not ratify the deferment), the slave has options. According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the slave chooses either to pay two-thirds of the named consideration immediately and the rest deferred, or to refuse and become a slave again. According to Imam Muhammad, the slave chooses either to pay two-thirds of the actual value (not the named consideration) immediately and the rest up to the full named consideration deferred, or to be re-enslaved. The reasoning: The sick person can only affect the validity of the contract in one-third of his property; the two-thirds belong to the heirs. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf view the entire named consideration as the price of the slave's freedom (badal al-raqaba), so the heirs' right attaches to the whole. Thus, deferment beyond one-third is invalid because it harms the heirs. Muhammad argues that the sick person cannot defer payment beyond two-thirds of the value because that portion is tied to the heirs' right, but the excess over the value is merely a gift that the sick person could have abandoned entirely, so deferring that portion is valid. The commentator draws a distinction from khul' (divorce for compensation), where the consideration is not tied to wealth and thus deferment is fully valid.

Key Terms
مريض (marid): A person who is terminally ill (marad al-mawt), whose transactions are subject to the one-third rule from the estate.
بدل الرقبة (badal al-raqaba): The consideration for the slave's freedom; the entire amount specified in the kitaba contract.
استحقاق حرية الثلث (istihqaq hurriyyat al-thuluth): The slave is entitled to freedom for one-third of his value from the deceased's estate, as a bequest.

Types of Nasabi Asabah in Hanafi Faraid
Definition and classification of lineage-based residuary heirs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of nasabi asabah (asabah bi-nafsihi, bi-ghayrihi, ma'a ghayrihi) and state the conditions for each.

Core Idea
A residuary heir (asabah) is either male by himself, female with a male, or female with another female.

In Hanafi inheritance law, the residual estate goes to asabah. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya classifies lineage-based asabah into three distinct categories. Understanding these categories is crucial for correctly distributing the residue after the Quranic shares are allocated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main types of asabah
Note that asabah are divided into nasabi (lineage) and sababi (causal, due to wala). This lesson focuses on the nasabi type.
Sababi asabah are not covered here.
2
List the three subtypes of nasabi asabah
From the passage, there are three: asabah bi-nafsihi, asabah bi-ghayrihi, and asabah ma'a ghayrihi.
Memorise the Arabic terms.
3
Analyze the conditions for asabah bi-nafsihi
A self-asabah is a male with no prescribed share and no female in his lineage to the deceased. Examples: son, father, brother, paternal uncle.
Maternal relatives like maternal uncle are excluded because a female (the mother) enters the lineage.
4
Distinguish asabah bi-ghayrihi from asabah ma'a ghayrihi
Asabah bi-ghayrihi is a female made asabah by a male of equal degree (e.g., daughter with son). Asabah ma'a ghayrihi is a female made asabah by another female (e.g., full sister with daughter). The Hanafi school uniquely recognizes the third type.
This difference affects inheritance distribution across madhhabs.
5
Identify non-asabah relatives
The passage notes that relatives with a female in their lineage (e.g., children of the mother, maternal grandfather) are not asabah; they may be Quranic share-holders or dhawi al-arham.
This prevents misclassification.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of the types of lineage-based asabah (residuaries) in inheritance. The author states that asabah are of two kinds: nasabi (by blood relation) and sababi (by cause, i.e., wala of emancipation). Here he explains nasabi asabah. They are three: (1) Asabah bi-nafsihi (by himself): a male who has no fixed share and in whose lineage to the deceased no female intervenes. Examples are son, father, brother, paternal uncle. A female cannot be a self-asabah. (2) Asabah bi-ghayrihi (by another): a female who becomes an asabah due to the presence of a male of equal degree, like a daughter with a son, or a sister with a brother. (3) Asabah ma'a ghayrihi (with another): a female who becomes an asabah due to the presence of another female, like a full or consanguine sister with a daughter. The author distinguishes these from those who are not asabah because a female enters their lineage (e.g., children of the mother—uterine siblings) or those who are dhawi al-arham (distant kindred). This classification is essential for determining who inherits the residue after the Quranic shares are given.

Key Terms
عصبة (asabah): A residuary heir who inherits the remainder of the estate after the Quranic shares are allocated, or the entire estate if no share-holders exist.
عصبة بنفسه (asabah bi-nafsihi): A male agnate relative who has no prescribed share and no female intervenes in his lineage to the deceased; he inherits by himself.
عصبة بغيره (asabah bi-ghayrihi): A female relative who becomes an asabah due to the presence of a male relative of equal degree, e.g., daughter with son, sister with brother.
عصبة مع غيره (asabah ma'a ghayrihi): A female relative who becomes an asabah due to the presence of another female, e.g., full sister with a daughter.

Priority of Nasabi Asabah over Manumitter
States the priority order: all nasabi asabah precede the manumitter, who precedes dhawi al-arham.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the priority order of residuary heirs in Hanafi inheritance law and apply it to determine who inherits the residue.

Core Idea
All lineage asabah precede the manumitter, who precedes dhawi al-arham.

In Hanafi faraid, the residue after fixed shares is distributed among residuary heirs in a strict order. Understanding this priority is crucial for correct distribution. This passage clarifies that the manumitter (mu'tiq) does not inherit if any lineage-based asabah exists, and that dhawi al-arham only inherit in default of all asabah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the priority order: nasabi asabah > manumitter > dhawi al-arham.
The rule is absolute; all lineage asabah, even distant, take precedence.
2
Examine the proof
The hadith: 'He did not leave an heir.' The Hanafis interpret 'heir' here as an heir who is an asabah.
The manumitter inherited only because no asabah existed.
3
Support from companion practice
The ruling is based on the practice of Zayd ibn Thabit, a leading sahabi in inheritance matters.
Zayd's authority is strong in Hanafi faraid.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Given a deceased with a full brother (asabah) and a manumitter, the brother takes the residue, excluding the manumitter.
If also a maternal uncle (dhawi al-arham), he gets nothing if any asabah exists.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Shafi'is may prioritize closer asabah over all lineage asabah, potentially letting the manumitter inherit sooner.
The practical difference depends on the closeness of the asabah.

Commentary
This passage establishes the priority order among residuary heirs. The author explains that all lineage-based asabah (of any of the three types) are given precedence over the manumitter (the former master who freed the slave). The justification is that the manumitter is an asabah by cause (sababi), but he is the last of the asabah. This is based on a narration from Zayd ibn Thabit and the hadith of the man who died leaving only a slave he freed, where the Prophet gave the inheritance to the freed slave (the manumitter) only because there was no other heir who was an asabah. The phrase 'he did not leave an heir' is interpreted to mean he left no heir who is an asabah, not that he left no heir at all. This is supported by the incident of Hamza's daughter, where the inheritance went to other relatives. Therefore, the manumitter is postponed behind any lineage asabah but takes precedence over dhawi al-arham (uterine relatives who are not asabah and not share-holders). The passage also defines dhawi al-arham as those who have no fixed share and have a female in their lineage to the deceased; they are not asabah and take the entire estate only when no asabah exists.

Categories of Asabah and Precedence over the Manumitter
States ruling on precedence of asabah over manumitter and defines four categories of asabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of asabah bi-anfusihim and explain the order of precedence among the manumitter, asabah, and dhawi al-arham, citing the Hanafi proof.

Core Idea
Blood asabah always outrank the manumitter, who is the lowest asabah and merely above dhawi al-arham.

In Hanafi inheritance law, not all heirs receive fixed shares; some inherit as residuaries (asabah). This lesson focuses on the categories of asabah by themselves, who are all male agnatic relatives, and how they relate to the manumitter (mu'tiq) and distant kin (dhawi al-arham). Understanding this order is essential for correctly distributing an estate when multiple asabah are present.

Guided Path
1
List the four categories of asabah
From the passage, identify each category: descendant of the deceased, root of the deceased, descendant of the father, descendant of the grandfather. Provide an example for each.
The categories are ordered by proximity to the deceased.
2
State the order of precedence
All blood asabah take precedence over the manumitter. The manumitter takes precedence over dhawi al-arham (relatives through females). Within blood asabah, the nearest category excludes the farther.
The manumitter is the last among asabah.
3
Identify the proof
The Hanafi proof is the practice of Zayd ibn Thabit (رضي الله عنه) and the hadith of the daughter of Hamza (رضي الله عنه), which show that the manumitter is delayed by any blood asabah.
The dalil is ahad, but relied upon in faraid.
4
Explain the logic
The manumitter is considered an asabah only when no blood asabah exist; his right is suspended in their presence. Dhawi al-arham are not asabah at all.
This is based on the interpretation of 'he left no heir' as 'no blood asabah'.
5
Apply the rule to a scenario
Given a set of heirs (e.g., son, brother, manumitter), determine who inherits and why, using the precedence order.
Practice builds fluency in applying the rule.

Wala' al-Muwalat: Contract, Conditions, and Proof
Explaining the contract of wala' al-muwalat, its conditions, and the Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition and conditions of wala' al-muwalat, explain the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute over its validity, and cite the Hanafi proofs from Quran and hadith.

Core Idea
A valid contract of mutual alliance creates inheritance and blood-money liability, but requires non-Arab, freedman, and explicit stipulation.

Wala' al-muwalat is a pre-Islamic practice retained in Islamic law, allowing a person of unknown lineage to secure inheritance and diyah support through a contract. The Hanafi school validates this contract based on explicit textual evidence, while Imam al-Shafi'i rejects it. In this lesson, we will unpack the contract's definition, essential conditions, and the proof structure that sustains the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Define the contract and its parties
Identify the core mechanism: a person of unknown lineage (client, asfal) makes a pact with a patron (a'la) that the patron will inherit from him and pay his blood money. Acceptance creates the legal relationship.
The client must be a non-Arab freedman; the patron can be either the one at whose hands he converted or another.
2
Identify the conditions for validity
List the four (or five) conditions stated in the text: (i) client is non-Arab, (ii) client is a freedman, (iii) explicit stipulation of inheritance and blood money, (iv) client has not already transferred this patronage to another, and (v) some require conversion at patron's hands, but the correct view excludes this.
The condition of conversion is not a condition; the text explicitly rejects it as such.
3
Understand the intra-Hanafi difference
Note that some Hanafi scholars required the client to have embraced Islam at the patron's hands, but the relied-upon (sahih) position rejects this. The contract is valid without it.
This is an internal ikhtilaf resolved by later authorities.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i objection and Hanafi response
Al-Shafi'i argues that wala' al-muwalat nullifies the right of the public treasury (bayt al-mal) and is therefore invalid. Hanafis respond with Quran 4:33 and a hadith, asserting that the textual evidence overrides the treasury's default right.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is that in Shafi'i law, such a contract is void and the estate goes to bayt al-mal; in Hanafi law, it is valid and creates the stipulated rights.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a hypothetical case of a non-Arab freeman converting and making a valid wala' al-muwalat contract, determine that the patron inherits and pays diyah. If the same case were judged by Shafi'i rules, the contract would be void and the estate would go to the treasury.
This shows the real-world difference between the schools.

The conditions are enumerated: (1) the client must be a non-Arab, as Arabs have strong tribal support; (2) the client must be a freedman (not a slave); (3) both parties must explicitly stipulate inheritance and blood money; (4) the client must not have already transferred this alliance to another; (5) some require the client to have embraced Islam at the patron's hands, but the correct (sahih) view is that this is not a condition. Commentary

The Sin of Digging vs. Sin of Killing in Inheritance Deprivation
Objection and response regarding inheritance deprivation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between the sin of digging and the sin of killing in inheritance deprivation, and apply it to evaluate similar objections.

Core Idea
Deprivation of inheritance attaches only to the sin directly from killing, not from a separate sinful act like digging.

The Hanafi rule that a killer is deprived of inheritance applies even to erroneous killing. But what if death results from a separate sin—like digging a pit in another's land? An objector argues that since the digging is sinful, deprivation should follow. The Hanafi response sharpens our understanding: the sin must be directly causal to the death, not merely a prior sinful act. This lesson unpacks that crucial distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the first part of the passage: the objector claims that because digging in another's property is sinful, and the death results from that sin, the digger should be deprived of inheritance—just as in erroneous killing.
The objector is drawing an analogy (qiyas) between the two situations.
2
Understand the Hanafi response
The Hanafi reply concedes that the digging is sinful, but holds that inheritance deprivation is tied specifically to the sin of killing itself, not to any unrelated sin. The digger's sin is 'sin of digging', not 'sin of killing'.
The key phrase: 'Deprivation of inheritance is only attached to the sin resulting from the act of killing itself.'
3
Analyze the distinction: direct vs. indirect sin
In erroneous killing, the sin (though less than intentional) is directly connected to causing death. In the digging case, the sin is the unauthorized act; death is incidental, not intended, and the sin is not the cause of death in the same legal sense.
The Hanafi focus is on the legal causation (sababiyya) of the killing, not just any sinful act.
4
Apply the distinction to a parallel scenario
Consider: a person builds a wall on someone else's land without permission, the wall collapses and kills someone. According to this ruling, the builder's sin is trespass, not killing, so inheritance deprivation does not apply.
This reinforces that the principle is narrow: only the sin that is integral to the act of killing triggers deprivation.

Debt vs. Deposit Priority: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute with critical refinement of attribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether a deposit or a debt takes priority in an estate, and evaluate the correction made by later scholars regarding the attribution of these positions.

Core Idea
The relative strength of deposit versus debt in an estate was disputed; later scholars corrected a misattribution in al-Hidayah.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the priority of a deposit (wadi'ah) versus a debt (dayn) claim when only one heir acknowledges both. The discussion is rooted in an analogy to the treatment of debt and emancipation during terminal illness. However, the teacher of the author, Muhammad Abd al-Halim, points out that the attribution of views in al-Hidayah is reversed compared to what the great early authorities reported. We will explore the two positions, the analogical reasoning, and the critical correction.

Guided Path
1
State the hypothetical scenario
A man dies leaving 1000 dirhams. Two claimants: one claims a debt of 1000 dirhams, the other claims a deposit (trust) of 1000 dirhams. A single heir acknowledges both claims as true.
The heir is the only heir, so there is no other claimant to the estate.
2
Identify the positions as reported in al-Hidayah
According to al-Hidayah, Abu Hanifa holds that the deposit is stronger (i.e., the depositor recovers the full 1000 dirhams first), while the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that both claims are equal and share the estate proportionally.
This is the version that the teacher corrects.
3
Understand the correction by the teacher
The teacher, citing many early authorities (al-Hakim, Abu al-Layth, al-Quduri, etc.), states that the opposite is correct: according to the two (Sahibayn) the deposit is stronger, and according to Abu Hanifa they are equal.
This is a critical refinement of the transmitted ruling.
4
Trace the analogical reasoning
The dispute is based on an analogy to the principle that during terminal illness (marad al-mawt), acknowledgment of debt is valid from the entire estate, while acknowledgment of emancipation is limited to one-third. The analogy suggests that a deposit, being an amanah (trust), is more deserving of full return than a debt.
The stronger the claim (amanah vs. financial obligation), the higher the priority.
5
Apply the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If deposit is stronger: the depositor takes the full 1000 dirhams; the debt claimant receives nothing (since estate is exhausted). If equal: each claimant gets 500 dirhams. The difference is significant.

Learning Objective

When a hermaphrodite inherits alongside a son, the share depends on whether we presume female (1:2 ratio) or average both possibilities (3:4 ratio).

The matn gives a simple rule: treat the hermaphrodite as a daughter, giving her one share to the son's two. But Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, following al-Sha'bi, argue for a proportional share that averages the possibilities of male and female. Understanding this ikhtilaf deepens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists handle ambiguous cases and demonstrates the flexibility of faraid reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on how to calculate the inheritance share of a khuntha (hermaphrodite). The text presents three positions: Abu Hanifa's principle of 'the lesser of the two shares' (aql al-nasibayn), and the specific methods of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani for applying this principle in particular cases. The author explains that for a khuntha whose gender is unknown, we determine the share on both assumptions (male and female) and give the smaller amount. This is based on the principle of certainty (yaqin) in Islamic jurisprudence: what is certain is not removed by doubt. The text contrasts the methods of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad by comparing fractions: in a case where the khuntha inherits with a son, Abu Yusuf's method gives 3/7 (about 0.4286) while Muhammad's gives 5/12 (about 0.4167), showing that Abu Yusuf's method is more beneficial for the khuntha. The passage also quotes Al-Sha'bi's view that the khuntha gets half of the combined shares (nisf al-nasibayn), which Abu Yusuf interprets as 3/7. A concrete example is given: if a khuntha dies leaving a husband, grandmother, paternal brother, and mother (also a khuntha), the shares are computed on two assumptions, and the lesser is taken. The commentary emphasizes that the reasoning is analogical (qiyas) to the case of a debt claim where the minimum number is certain.

Inheritance of the Khuntha with a Son: Abu Yusuf vs Muhammad
Explains the calculation of khuntha's share comparing Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance share of a khuntha when inheriting with a son, applying the Hanafi method of resolving uncertainty, and compare the outcomes according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
When a khuntha inherits, the share is determined by the certain minimum plus half the disputed surplus between male and female scenarios.

This passage tackles a delicate faraid problem: a khuntha (person with ambiguous gender) inherits alongside a son. The sex is unknown, so we consider both possibilities. The Hanafi school resolves the uncertainty by giving the khuntha the minimum guaranteed share (if female) and splitting the difference with the larger share (if male). The passage illustrates two calculation methods both yielding 5/12, then compares with Abu Yusuf's 3/7, showing the subtle but real difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two possible scenarios
The khuntha may be male or female. If male, estate split equally between khuntha and son (1/2 each). If female, khuntha gets 1/3 (as a daughter with a son), son gets 2/3.
2
Determine the certain minimum share
The lower of the two shares (1/3) is guaranteed to the khuntha regardless of gender.
This is the mutayaqqan.
3
Compute the disputed surplus and halve it
The difference between the male share (1/2) and female share (1/3) is 1/6. Since the surplus is in doubt, only half is given to the khuntha.
Surplus halved = 1/12.
4
Add the certain and half-surplus to get the share
1/3 = 4/12 plus 1/12 = 5/12 for the khuntha. Son gets the remainder: 7/12.
This is Muhammad's method.
5
Compare with Abu Yusuf's view
The passage notes that Abu Yusuf's method yields 3/7. By converting to a common denominator (84), we see 3/7 = 36/84, slightly larger than 5/12 = 35/84.
Difference is 1/84.
