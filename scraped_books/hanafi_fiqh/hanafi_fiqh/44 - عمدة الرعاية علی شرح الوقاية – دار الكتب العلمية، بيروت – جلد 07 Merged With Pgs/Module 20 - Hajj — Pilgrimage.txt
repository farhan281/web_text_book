Hunting Rulings: Permissibility and Multiple Hunter Liability
Ruling on hunting permissibility and liability for multiple hunters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hunting non-edible animals and apply the liability rules when two hunters wound the same animal.

Core Idea
Hunting is permissible for all animals; shared liability is apportioned based on each hunter's contribution to death.

Hunting is a common practice with detailed fiqh rulings. This passage establishes that hunting is permissible for both edible and non-edible animals, and then explores a nuanced scenario: when two hunters wound the same game, who is liable for what? The proof from the Quran and linguistic analysis supports the general permissibility, while qiyas and the works of al-Hidayah and al-Ziyadat provide the liability framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Ruling
Read the first line: 'ويصاد ما يؤكل لحمه، وما لا يؤكل...' This states that hunting applies to both edible and non-edible animals. For non-edible ones, hunting purifies the meat and skin. Extract the hukm level (mubah) and note the proof: the Quranic imperative in 5:2 and linguistic generality.
The Quran verse is quoted: 'وَإِذَا حَلَلْتُمْ فَأَصْطَادُوا'.
2
Understand the Liability Scenario
The passage then discusses two hunters wounding the same game. Three sub-cases are presented: (1) the second hunter kills the animal after a non-fatal wound by the first, (2) death results from both wounds, or (3) it is uncertain. Identify which sub-case is detailed by al-Hidayah and which by al-Ziyadat.
Al-Hidayah covers the first sub-case; al-Ziyadat covers the second and third.
3
Analyze the Qiyas
The commentator uses qiyas: the second hunter killing a wounded animal is analogized to killing a sick slave owned by another. In both, the killer guarantees the deficiency but not the full value because the victim was already diminished. Note the asl (sick slave), far' (second hunter), illa (death after prior injury), and hukm (partial guarantee).
This qiyas is jaliyyah with tahqiq al-manat.
4
Apply the Three-Part Ruling from al-Ziyadat
When death results from both wounds or is uncertain, the second hunter must pay: (a) the deficiency caused by his wound, (b) half the value of the animal wounded by both, and (c) half the value of the meat. Trace why each component is owed: (a) for the injury, (b) for being a co-destroyer, (c) for spoiling the meat that could have been lawfully eaten.
The first hunter is liable only for his initial wound deficiency.
5
Connect to the Proof
Finally, the passage returns to the general proof: the Quranic imperative and linguistic scope of 'sayd'. This reinforces that hunting is not restricted to edible animals. Note the authority (Quran, language, and the Hanafi reliance on general commands).

Liability for Hunting: When the Arrow Strikes After Ihram Ends or Begins
Ruling on two hunting scenarios with state change

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that liability for hunting in ihram depends on the shooter's state at the moment of shooting, not the moment the arrow hits.

Core Idea
Liability for hunting in ihram attaches at the act, not the outcome.

This passage presents a matched pair of cases that test the boundaries of hunting liability during ihram. At first glance, one might think the decisive moment is when the arrow strikes the game. The Hanafi school, however, teaches that the ruling is determined by the shooter's state at the time of shooting. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly assigning jaza' (compensation) in boundaries scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two pairs
The passage describes two cases: (1) a muhrim shoots game, then leaves ihram before the arrow hits; (2) a halal person shoots, then enters ihram before the arrow hits. Identify these clearly.
2
State the ruling for each
Case 1: the muhrim must pay jaza'. Case 2: the halal owes nothing. Note the different outcomes.
3
Pinpoint the decisive factor
The commentary explains: 'because liability is only due to transgression'—meaning the shooting itself must be a violation. The decisive factor is the state (muhrim or halal) at the moment of shooting, not at the moment the arrow hits.
This is the key analytical move.
4
Generalise the principle
The underlying rule: liability (damān) for prohibited hunting attaches to the act of shooting when it occurs during ihram. If the shooter is halal at the moment of shooting, no transgression, even if the result occurs in ihram.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Consider: a muhrim shoots an arrow, misses, then becomes halal, and later hits the game with a second arrow (now halal). Is he liable? He is liable only for the first shot (if it hits) because it was done in ihram; the second shot is halal. If the first shot never hits, no liability.
Practice consistency.

Dispute on Hajj from Estate when Allocated Funds Are Lost
Dispute among Hanafi imams on performing hajj from balance of estate after allocated funds lost

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad regarding the performance of Hajj from the estate when allocated funds are lost, and explain the legal reasoning behind each view.

Core Idea
Funds set aside for Hajj are not securely dedicated until delivery; loss before delivery triggers disagreement among Hanafi imams.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about what to do when funds allocated for Hajj from a deceased's estate are lost before the Hajj is performed. The three leading imams—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani—each offer a distinct ruling based on different conceptualizations of the executor's role and the nature of allocation. Understanding this dispute clarifies how estate bequests are treated when unforeseen loss occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Understand the factual situation: an executor (waṣī) sets aside a portion of the estate for Hajj, but the funds are lost before the Hajj is performed. The question is whether Hajj should be performed from the remaining estate and, if so, how much.
The bequest is from the permissible one-third of the estate.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that division (qisma) is only complete upon delivery to the intended purpose. Since the funds were not delivered to Hajj, their loss is like destruction before division. Therefore, one-third of whatever remains of the estate should be used, and if those funds are also lost, the process repeats indefinitely.
Key phrase: 'تمام القسمة بالتسليم إلى الجهة المسماة' (completion of division by delivery to the named direction).
3
State Abu Yusuf's position and reasoning
Abu Yusuf focuses on the original one-third of the total estate. If the allocated funds were exactly one-third, nothing further is taken. If they were less, the shortfall is made up from the remaining one-third of the total estate. However, if the replacement funds are lost, no further payment is made.
Underlying principle: the bequest's place is one-third; if that place remains partially intact, the bequest is executed up to that amount.
4
State Muhammad's position and reasoning
Muhammad argues that the executor's setting aside is exactly analogous to the deceased's own setting aside. If the deceased had set aside funds and they were lost after death, the bequest would become void. Similarly, the executor's allocation is void upon loss, so no Hajj should be performed from the balance.
Key analogy: 'إفراز الوصي كإفراز الميت' (the executor's setting aside is like the deceased's setting aside).
5
Compare the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
The dispute has real-world impact: under Abu Hanifa, multiple Hajj performances could be funded from the estate until the one-third is exhausted; under Abu Yusuf, at most one replacement; under Muhammad, no replacement at all. The passage does not specify a preferred view, leaving the ikhtilaf unresolved in this text.
No tarjih is given, so the fatwa authority may depend on later Hanafi practice.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the proper course of action when funds allocated for Hajj from the estate are lost before the Hajj is performed. The case arises when an executor (wasi) or someone designated to perform Hajj on behalf of the deceased sets aside a portion of the estate for that purpose, but the funds are stolen or lost. The three leading Hanafi imams differ on whether the Hajj should still be performed from whatever remains of the estate or not. (We follow the author’s order). First, Abu Hanifa’s position: he holds that since the division (qisma) is not complete until the funds are actually delivered to the specific purpose (the Hajj), the loss of unspent funds is like their destruction before division. Therefore, the Hajj must be performed from one-third of what remains of the estate, and if the new funds are also lost, the process repeats indefinitely. Third, Muhammad’s position: he draws a direct analogy between the executor and the deceased. Just as the deceased’s own allocation would be void if the funds were lost after his death, the executor’s allocation is similarly void; hence no Hajj is performed from the balance. The commentary then elaborates the reasoning behind each view, citing al-‘Inayah and al-Hidayah. The dispute boils down to whether the executor’s act is equivalent to the deceased’s own act (Muhammad), whether the bequest’s connection to the third is resilient (Abu Yusuf), or whether the purpose of the bequest (Hajj) takes precedence over the form of allocation (Abu Hanifa).
