Definition and Proofs of Shuf'a (Pre-emption)
Defines shuf'a and presents Hadith proofs for its legality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shuf'a and identify the Sunnah proofs that establish it as a legal right.

Core Idea
Shuf'a is a co-owner's right to forcibly buy a sold share -- established by multiple authentic hadith.

Shuf'a, or pre-emption, is a unique institution in Islamic property law that protects co-owners from unwanted third-party partners. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah defines shuf'a and lays out the primary textual proofs from the Sunnah, then clarifies key terms such as 'obligation' and 'establishment' that are critical to understanding the right.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the Definition
Read the opening: 'It is ownership of immovable property.' Note that shuf'a compels the buyer to sell to the pre-emptor. The term is derived from 'joining' (shaf').
The definition excludes movables and uncompensated transfers.
2
Identify the Dalil
The author cites four hadith: from Bukhari ('the neighbor has more right'), Muslim ('decreed pre-emption in every undivided share'), Abu Dawud, and the four Sunan. These are the basis for the Hanafi position.
All are ahad hadith, so thubut is zanni, but dalala is qati' according to Hanafis.
3
Understand 'Obligation' and 'Establishment'
The text says 'it becomes obligatory after the sale' – the commentator clarifies that 'obligation' here means 'establishment' (thubut), not a religious duty. Shuf'a is a right, not an obligation.
This avoids the misconception that leaving shuf'a is sinful.
4
Note the Role of Ishhad
The right is unstable before witnesses are called (ishhad). Once the pre-emptor bears witness, the right stabilizes and is not lost by delay.
Ishhad is necessary to prevent the buyer from denying the demand.
5
Recognize the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Immediacy
The majority (general mashayikh) require immediate demand (fawr). Hisham's narration from Muhammad allows deliberation until the end of the sitting, which al-Karkhi and al-Tajrid prefer as more correct.
The thamarah: if the pre-emptor delays without ishhad, his right is lost according to the majority.

Commentary
This passage is from the beginning of the Book of Pre-emption (Kitab al-Shuf'a) in the commentary 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah. The author defines shuf'a (pre-emption) as the ownership of immovable property (tamlik 'iqar) that is compelled upon the buyer. The term 'shuf'a' is derived from 'shaf' (joining), because it joins the purchased property to the pre-emptor's property. The author then provides the textual proofs (usul) for shuf'a from the Sunnah, citing hadith from Bukhari, Muslim, Abu Dawud, and the four Sunan. These hadith establish that the neighbor or partner has a right of pre-emption in undivided shares of immovable property.

The commentary then explains the statement from the text: 'Upon his buyer by compulsion for the like of its price, and it becomes obligatory after the sale, and it becomes established by bearing witness.' The commentator clarifies that 'obligation' here means 'establishment' (thubut), not a religious obligation that incurs sin if omitted. The cause (sabab) of shuf'a is the connection (ittisal) of ownership through purchase. The right is initially unstable (mutazalzil) before bearing witness; if the pre-emptor delays in demanding (talab) without bearing witness, the right can be lost. Once he bears witness (ishhad), the right becomes stable and does not lapse by delay.

The passage also specifies that shuf'a applies only to purchases (buyu') and analogous transactions like sulh (compromise) for property and gift with compensation (hiba bi 'iwad). It does not apply to property obtained without compensation, such as gift, inheritance, charity, or as dower, hire, or khul'. The mention of 'compulsion' (jabran) indicates that the buyer does not willingly accept the pre-emptor's taking.

Finally, the author discusses the requirement of immediate demand (talab al-fawr) for shuf'a. The majority of mashayikh hold that the demand must be immediate, but a minority, citing Hisham's narration from Imam Muhammad, allow deliberation (ta'ammul) until the end of the sitting, analogous to an option (khiyar). This is the preferred view according to al-Tajrid.

Key Terms
الشفعة (al-shuf'a): The right of a co-owner to forcibly purchase the share of a co-owner that was sold to a third party, at the same price.
عقار (iqar): Immovable property such as land, house, vineyard, and fixed structures like wells and mills.
الإشهاد (al-ishhad): Bearing witness before witnesses that one has demanded shuf'a, to prevent the buyer from denying the demand.

Scope of the Prohibition: 'Not Mentioning Allah's Name' in Slaughter
Ruling and proof regarding the scope of 6:121

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school restricts the prohibition of 'eating over which Allah's name was not mentioned' to animals that are actually slaughtered, and distinguish it from carrion.

Core Idea
The Quranic prohibition applies only to animals actually slaughtered; carrion is haram for a different reason.

This passage delves into the precise scope of Quran 6:121. The author argues that the phrase 'over which Allah's name was not mentioned' must be interpreted as applying only to animals that are properly slaughtered. It does not cover animals that die by falling or goring, nor does it, through its contrary implication, make predators lawful if slaughtered. The discussion engages with the principle of mafhum al-mukhalaf and demonstrates its limits when leading to absurd results.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central ruling and its source
Pinpoint the Quranic verse (6:121) and the key phrase 'mimma lam yudhkar'. State the Hanafi ruling: eating meat from an animal slaughtered without naming Allah is haram, but this does not include carrion.
The verse is the primary proof; its interpretation is contested.
2
Understand the two refutations of a broader interpretation
The author presents two arguments against those who claim the verse includes all animals not mentioned upon (including non-slaughtered ones). First, animals like al-mutaraddiyah are not by nature subject to slaughter. Second, unqualified application of mafhum al-mukhalaf would absurdly permit eating dogs and lions if slaughtered with tasmiyah.
The first refutation is based on the animal's state; the second on logical consequence.
3
Analyze the absurd consequence of unqualified mafhum al-mukhalaf
The contrary implication (mafhum al-mukhalaf) of the prohibition suggests that mentioning Allah's name makes the animal lawful. If applied without restriction, it would include predators, which are unanimously forbidden.
This shows that the contrary implication must be qualified by the animal's nature (edibility).
4
Examine the resolution: slaughterability tied to edibility
Some commentators restrict 'slaughterability' to edible animals. The author questions this because the Law also commands slaughter of inedible animals for purification (e.g., dog for tanning). Thus, the restriction is seen as arbitrary, and the safer position is that the verse only applies to animals actually slaughtered.
The author leans toward the stricter interpretation, limiting the verse to slaughtered animals regardless of edibility.
5
Recognize the subtlety and the final position
The passage concludes that the prohibition is specific to the absence of proper slaughter (tadhkiyah). The Hanafi position is that the verse does not cover carrion; carrion is haram by separate evidence.
This guards against misapplying the verse to animals that die without human intervention.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof discussing the scope of the Qur'anic prohibition of eating meat over which Allah's name was not mentioned (Quran 6:121). The author argues that this prohibition only applies to animals that are actually slaughtered (i.e., have the potential to be slaughtered with naming), not to those that die without slaughter such as an animal that fell into a well (al-mutaraddiyah) or was gored (al-natihah). The reasoning is that such animals are not by nature the subject of slaughter; before death they are living animals, after death they are carrion. The contrary implication (mafhum al-mukhalafah) of the phrase 'if he did not mention Allah's name upon it' would imply that an animal upon which Allah's name is mentioned becomes lawful, but this would absurdly include lions and dogs which are unanimously unlawful even if slaughtered. Therefore, a qualification is needed: the animal must be of the type that is meant to be slaughtered (i.e., edible). The commentators of al-Mukhtasar restrict slaughterability to edible meat animals, excluding predators and non-meat animals. However, the author notes that this specification is arbitrary because the Law also commands slaughter of inedible animals for purification (e.g., dogs for making containers or tanning). So the stricter position is that the prohibition of not mentioning Allah's name applies only to animals that are actually slaughtered, not to those that die otherwise. The text guards against a misapplication of the concept of mafhum al-mukhalafah.

Key Terms
المتردية (al-mutaraddiyah): An animal that falls into a well or from a mountain and dies from the fall.
المفهوم المخالف (al-mafhum al-mukhalaf): The contrary implication of a conditional or restrictive phrase, i.e., that the ruling applies only when the condition is met and its opposite when not.
ذكية (dhakiyyah): Proper ritual slaughter according to Islamic law, resulting in the animal being lawful (halal) and pure (tahir).

Definition and Place of Tadhkiyah (Slaughter)
Defining tadhkiyah and its two types, with a Hanafi intra-madhhab resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-tadhkiyah and distinguish between optional slaughter and slaughter of necessity, identifying the valid location of the cut according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Tadhkiyah is a legal purification that requires cutting the throat between the collarbone and jawbones for optional slaughter.

In this passage, the author clarifies the meaning of tadhkiyah, the Islamic method of slaughter that renders an animal lawful and pure. He explains its linguistic roots in sharpness/swiftness and purification, and then details the two categories: necessary slaughter (a wound anywhere) and optional slaughter (cut between throat and collarbone). A key intra-Hanafi discussion about the exact location is resolved by adopting the broader view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of tadhkiyah
Distinguish between tadhkiyah al-darurah (necessity: a wound anywhere on the body) and tadhkiyah al-ikhtiyar (optional: a cut between the throat and collarbone).
Necessity occurs when normal slaughter is impossible, e.g., the animal is wounded and cannot be controlled.
2
Understand the linguistic basis of the term
Note that tadhkiyah linguistically means sharpness/swiftness or purity, both applicable to slaughter because it causes swift death and purifies the animal from impure blood.
The hadith 'Dibagh al-adim dhakatuhu' uses tadhkiyah to mean purification via tanning.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the exact location of the cut
Al-Quduri's narration restricts the cut to between the throat and collarbone, but Al-Jami' al-Saghir allows any part of the throat within the area between collarbone and jawbones.
This is a legitimate difference within the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the principle: the entire throat area is valid
The principle 'al-dhakatu ma bayna al-labbati wa al-lahiyayn' (the slaughter is between the collarbone and jawbones) is interpreted to include the whole throat, as it contains the main veins for maximum blood flow.

Clothing Children in Gold or Silk
Ruling on clothing boys in gold/silk

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dressing a child (boy) in gold or silk and explain the analogy used.

Core Idea
It is disliked to clothe boys in gold or silk, just as giving wine to drink is disliked.

This lesson covers the ruling on clothing young boys in gold ornaments or silk garments. The Hanafi school considers it makruh (disliked), drawing an analogy with the prohibition of giving wine to drink.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is clothing a boy (sabi) in gold or silk. The ruling is 'kariha' (disliked), not haram.
The text says 'kariha ilbasa al-sabi dhahaban aw hariran'.
2
Understand the analogy
The commentator compares this to drinking wine: just as giving wine to drink is haram (because drinking is haram), so making someone wear gold/silk is makruh (because wearing is makruh). The analogy is based on the principle that the ruling of the act extends to causing it.
The analogy is tashbih, not qiyas, because the rulings differ in degree.
3
Differentiate between children and adults
For adult men, wearing gold and silk is haram. For boys, it is only makruh because they are not yet obligated and there is hope of them learning to refrain.
Some scholars say it is haram for boys as well, but the Hanafi position is karahah.
4
Apply the ruling
Parents should avoid dressing their sons in gold or silk garments. If done, it is makruh but not sinful in the same way as for adults.
The ruling applies to boys; for girls, gold and silk are permissible.

Ruling on ratam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ratam (tying a thread on the finger to remember something) and distinguish it from frivolous thread-tying.

Core Idea
Ratam is permissible because it serves a valid purpose; other thread-tying for no reason is disliked.

This lesson examines the practice of tying a thread around the finger as a reminder. The Hanafi school considers this permissible (not disliked) because it has a legitimate purpose, unlike pointless thread-tying on limbs which is considered frivolous and disliked.

Guided Path
1
Define ratam
Ratam is a thread tied on the finger to help remember something. It is a practical mnemonic device.
The text defines it explicitly.
2
Identify the ruling
The base text says 'wa la al-ratam' meaning it is not disliked. The commentator confirms that tying it is not disliked because it is not pointless.
The negation 'la' indicates it is not included in the previous category of disliked actions.
3
Understand the reason
The action has a valid purpose (tadhakkur). Therefore, it is not considered 'عبث' (frivolity). The Islamic legal maxim is that actions are judged by intentions.
Purposeful actions are generally permissible unless specifically prohibited.
4
Distinguish from other thread-tying
The commentator notes that some people tie threads on limbs or wear chains for no reason; that is pure frivolity and is disliked. Ratam is different because it serves a need.
The distinction is crucial to avoid misunderstanding.

Commentary

(4) Ratam (tying a thread on the finger to remember something): This is not disliked because it has a valid purpose (remembrance). The commentator distinguishes it from other thread-tying on limbs that is purely frivolous (عبث) and thus makruh. The key legal principle here is that actions are judged by their intentions and purposes.

Rulings on Looking at Mahrams and Non-Mahrams
Expands rulings on permissible and impermissible looking and touching

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on permissible looking at mahram and non-mahram women, explain the intra-Hanafi difference on the foot, and evaluate the inter-madhhab dispute on the male slave.

Core Idea
Looking at a mahram is limited to head, face, chest, shank, upper arm; non-mahram only face and hands.

This passage from the commentary on al-Wiqaya provides detailed rules for permissible looking and touching between men and women, covering mahrams, non-mahrams, female slaves, and the special case of the male slave. It resolves an intra-Hanafi difference on whether a non-mahram's foot is 'awra and rejects the Maliki/Shafi'i view that a male slave is a mahram.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of women
The passage discusses mahram (permanently forbidden in marriage), ajnabiyyah (non-mahram), and female slave (of another man). Also a male slave (mamluk) and his female owner (sayyidah).
Mahram includes mother, sister, daughter, etc.
2
Determine the permissible body parts for each category
For a mahram: head, face, chest, shank, upper arm if safe from desire; not back, stomach, thigh. For a non-mahram: only face and hands (relied view). For a female slave: like mahram.
The condition of safety from desire is crucial.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the foot
Abu Hanifa held that a non-mahram's foot is permissible to look at. The relied view (zahir al-riwaya) restricts to face and hands, arguing that necessity only permits face and hands.
The foot is not 'awra in prayer due to necessity, but no necessity in looking.
4
Examine the inter-madhhab dispute on the male slave
Malik and one Shafi'i view consider a male slave a mahram to his female owner based on Quran 24:31. The Hanafi school disagrees, interpreting 'what their right hands possess' as female slaves only.
Hanafi evidence includes early tafsir and the possibility of marriage after manumission.
5
Apply the tarjih (preference) and understand the practical effect
The relied Hanafi position is that a male slave is not a mahram, so the female owner must cover her 'awra except face and hands in front of him. The opposing view allows more exposure.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is in hijab requirements.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Wiqaya deals with the rules of looking at and touching various categories of women. It opens with the ruling on a mahram woman (one permanently unmarriageable, such as mother, sister, etc.). According to the Hanafi school, a man may look at his mahram's head, face, chest, shank, and upper arm provided he is safe from desire; if he fears desire, it is not permissible. However, he may not look at the back, stomach, and thigh, just as he may not look at these parts of another man's female slave. The ruling for another's female slave is like that of a mahram because she is seen in work clothes out of necessity. The text then states a general principle: whatever is permissible to look at is also permissible to touch. When intending to purchase a slave woman, a man may look and touch even if he fears desire, because of necessity; but touching with desire is not allowed. A baligh slave girl should not be displayed for sale in only a single garment covering from navel to knee, as that would expose her back. Regarding a non-mahram woman (ajnabiyyah), the relied-upon (zahir al-riwaya) position is that only her face and hands may be looked at. There is a narration from Abu Hanifa that looking at her foot is also permissible, but the commentator refutes this: the foot is not 'awra in prayer due to necessity, but for a non-mahram there is no such necessity. The owner of a male slave (sayyidah) is treated like a non-mahram in regard to looking at her feet. If a man fears desire, he must not look even at the face of a non-mahram woman except for genuine needs: a judge, a witness, one intending marriage, one buying a slave woman, or a doctor. These may look to the extent of necessity. Finally, the passage addresses the status of a male slave (mamluk). It rejects the view of Malik and one opinion of al-Shafi'i that a male slave is like a mahram based on Quran 24:31 ('or what their right hands possess'), arguing that 'right hands possess' refers only to female slaves, as stated by early authorities. A male slave is not a mahram; he is a non-mahram man because desire is possible (he can marry after manumission) and his need to enter the house is limited (he usually works outside).

Key Terms
محرم (mahram): A person with whom marriage is permanently prohibited due to blood, marriage, or fosterage (e.g., mother, sister, daughter).
أجنبية (ajnabiyyah): A woman who is not a mahram and not one's wife.
مملوك (mamluk): A male slave; in this context, a male slave owned by a woman.

Numbering and Dotting the Quran for Non-Arabs
Ruling on ta'shir and nuqt with exception for non-Arabs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ta'shir and nuqt of the Mushaf and explain why the ruling differs for Arabs and non-Arabs.

Core Idea
Numbering and dotting the Quran is commendable for non-Arabs but disliked for Arabs because need changes the ruling.

In this lesson, we examine a ruling from al-Wiqayah regarding two practical aids for Quran reading: marking every ten verses (ta'shir) and dotting letters (nuqt). The Hanafi school differentiates based on the reader's linguistic ability, reflecting the principle that necessity can transform a disliked act into a commendable one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for Arabs
The matn implies that for Arabs, ta'shir and nuqt are disliked (makruh). The exception clause 'illa li al-'ajam' shows the default is dislike.
The default ruling is based on preserving the original script of the Quran.
2
Identify the exception for non-Arabs
For non-Arabs (al-'ajam), the ruling changes to 'hasan' (commendable). The reason is stated: 'due to their inability to learn except by it.'
The exception is based on need (hajah).
3
Analyze the basis of the exception
The commentator explains that non-Arabs cannot learn to recite correctly without these aids. This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on necessity.
The Hanafi school uses 'urf and need to modify rulings.
4
Apply the same reasoning to related matters
The commentator extends the ruling to writing surah names and verse numbers, citing al-Jalabi. The same principle of need applies.
This shows analogical extension (qiyas) within the madhhab.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position: ta'shir and nuqt are permissible for non-Arabs when needed, but discouraged for Arabs. This is a fatwa from later Hanafi authorities.
The authority weight is fatawa mutakhkhirin.

Definition and Prohibition of Khamr
Opening of the Book of Beverages with definition and prohibition of khamr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of khamr, identify the condition for its prohibition according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and explain why not every intoxicant is called khamr.

Core Idea
Khamr is specifically grape juice that becomes strong and foams; not every intoxicant is khamr by linguistic consensus.

This lesson explores the opening of the Book of Beverages, where the author defines khamr and establishes its prohibition. The commentary delves into the precise conditions for khamr, highlighting an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the requirement of foaming. It also addresses a cross-madhhab dispute about whether the term 'khamr' extends to all intoxicants, settling the matter with a linguistic consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage begins by stating that khamr is prohibited (haram). Identify the act as consuming khamr, a specific beverage, and note that the ruling is absolute prohibition.
The prohibition is established by consensus and textual evidence.
2
Define khamr according to the text
Locate the definition: khamr is the juice of grapes that has boiled, become strong (fermented), and thrown foam. Note the three conditions: grape origin, boiling, and foaming (the latter is disputed).
The text emphasizes that the name khamr is specifically for this beverage by consensus of linguists.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on foaming
Imam Abu Hanifa requires the juice to throw foam (qadhf al-zabad) for it to be considered khamr. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that once it becomes strong (ishtadda), it is intoxicating and thus khamr, regardless of foaming.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of the Sahibayn, as intoxication is the effective cause.
4
Analyze the cross-madhhab dispute on extension
Some scholars (e.g., Shafi'i) argue by qiyas that every intoxicant is khamr because khamr intoxicates. The Hanafi response rejects qiyas in language, citing consensus of linguists that the term is restricted to grape wine.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that Hanafis apply the hadd punishment only for drinking khamr, not for other intoxicants.
5
Derive the practical ruling and its implications
Khamr is haram, najis (heavy impurity), and its drinker is subject to hadd punishment. Considering it lawful is kufr. The same rulings do not automatically apply to other intoxicants.
This affects daily life: a beverage made from dates that intoxicates is prohibited but not classified as khamr.

The Hanafi Ruling on Wine Vinegar: A Dispute with Shafi'is
Hanafi ruling on permissibility of wine vinegar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on wine vinegar, distinguish it from the Shafi'i position, and identify the evidence used by both schools.

Core Idea
Wine vinegar is halal in the Hanafi school, even if hastened by treatment, because the intoxicating property is removed.

This passage tackles a well-known inter-madhhab dispute: the permissibility of vinegar derived from wine. The Hanafi school takes a strong position of absolute permissibility, while Imam al-Shafi'i imposes conditions based on the method of conversion. The disagreement hinges on whether the prohibition of wine carries over to its transformed product and whether intentional conversion constitutes a prohibited approach to wine. Let us explore the legal reasoning on both sides.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what the Hanafi school says about vinegar from wine, especially when treatment (adding a substance) is used. Note the phrase 'halal mutlaqan' (absolutely permissible).
The text explicitly says: 'خل الخمر حلال مطلقاً عندنا' (vinegar from wine is absolutely permissible in our school).
2
Identify the Shafi'i position
Understand that Shafi'i distinguishes between vinegar made by treatment (impermissible) and without treatment (two opinions). The key is the method of conversion.
The text mentions that if by treatment, it is impermissible 'قولاً واحداً'; if without, two opinions.
3
Examine the Hanafi evidence
Look at the dalil cited: the hadith 'نعم الإدام الخل' (Vinegar is an excellent condiment) and the analogy (qiyas) to spontaneous vinegar and tanning. The reasoning: the corrupting quality (wine's intoxication) is removed by conversion.
The hadith shows vinegar is generally commendable; the removal of the intoxicating property lifts the prohibition.
4
Examine the Shafi'i evidence
The Shafi'i argument is that intentionally converting wine to vinegar involves 'approach to wine on a profit-making basis', contradicting the command to abstain from wine.
The text says: 'في التخليل اقتراباً من الخمر على وجه التموّل، والأمر بالاجتناب ينافيه'.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis counter that the hadith directly indicates vinegar's value, and the corrupting quality is gone, so the prohibition no longer applies. The underlying cause ('illa) of prohibition – intoxication – has ceased.
This is a classic application of istihsan (juristic preference) based on the change of substance.
6
Determine the practical difference
Note the thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis may consume, sell, or use wine vinegar freely; Shafi'is must inquire about the method of conversion and restrict accordingly.
This affects not only consumption but also trade and use in food products.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of al-Wiqayah, expanding on four invalidating factors in hunting. First: If the hunter intentionally leaves out the tasmiyah (saying 'Bismillah') until the animal dies without slaughtering it, the game is unlawful. The same applies if a Magian (fire-worshipper) sends his dog to hunt, and then a Muslim shouts to incite it—since the shouting is subordinate to the sending, it does not make the game lawful; the Magian's sending taints the process. Second: For a sheep that becomes ill, the fatwa is that even minimal life at the time of slaughter is sufficient; as long as it can be slaughtered while alive, it is permissible based on Quran 5:3 'except what you slaughter.' Third: Killing with a mi'rad (a blunt arrow without feathers) hitting with its broad side is unlawful unless it has a sharp edge and hits with that edge, because the hadith of Adi ibn Hatim (Bukhari) distinguishes: if it strikes with its sharp edge, eat; if with its broad side, do not eat. The underlying principle is that death must be from a wound (jarh), not from the weight of the object. Fourth: If a hunter shoots game and it falls into water, onto a roof, or off a cliff, the game is unlawful because the cause of death is uncertain—it could be the water or the fall. However, if it falls directly to the ground (without any intervening hazard), it is lawful because avoidance of such fall is not possible. The commentator summarizes the general principle: if death is definitely from the wound, the game is halal; if definitely from weight or impact, it is haram; if doubtful, it is haram as a precaution.

Determining Gender of a Hermaphrodite (Khuntha): Definition, Evidence, and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Opening of Kitab al-Khuntha with definition and gender determination rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi criteria for determining the gender of a khuntha and explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on precedence vs. quantity.

Core Idea
The gender of a hermaphrodite is determined by the organ from which urine first exits — a rule rooted in the Prophet's guidance.

How does Islamic law classify a person born with both male and female genitals? The Hanafi school addresses this rare but important case in Kitab al-Khuntha. This lesson introduces the definition of khuntha, the primary criterion of urination based on a Prophetic hadith, and the internal dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his disciples on whether to consider precedence or quantity of urine.

Guided Path
1
Define the khuntha and the legal problem
Identify what a khuntha is and why fiqh needed to address this rare condition. Note that the original assumption is that each person has one pair of genitals; the khuntha is an exception.
2
Identify the primary criterion: urination
State the default ruling: if urine exits from the male organ, the person is male; if from the vulva, female. Understand the proof (dalil) from the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the Companion Ali.
This criterion is based on the primary function of the genital organs at birth.
3
Analyze the case of urine from both organs
When urine exits from both organs, determine which Hanafi view to apply: precedence (al-sabq) according to Abu Hanifa, or quantity (al-kathrah) according to the Sahibayn.
The mufta bihi view is that of Abu Hanifa: precedence is decisive.
4
Examine the reasoning behind each position
Abu Hanifa argues that precedence indicates the original urinary passage, while later exit may be due to a defect. The Sahibayn argue that greater quantity indicates strength and originality. Understand the rebuttal: quantity may be due to width, not strength.
5
Apply secondary signs of puberty
If the case remains ambiguous, use signs of puberty: beard, wet dream, or cohabitation indicate male; breast development or milk indicate female. This resolves the dispute when urination criteria are inconclusive.
These are also mentioned in the passage.

This passage opens the Book of the Hermaphrodite (Kitab al-Khuntha) and establishes the foundational ruling for determining the gender of a person born with both male and female genitals. The text defines a khuntha as a person possessing both a vulva and a male organ. The initial ruling is based on urination: if urine exits from the male organ, the person is considered male; if from the vulva, female; if from both, further examination is needed. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Permissibility in markets rests on the predominant condition; small unavoidable impurities are excused to prevent hardship.

In daily life, Muslims interact with markets where some items may be forbidden (stolen, usurped, carrion). The Hanafi school, relying on juristic preference (istihsan), rules that consumption is permitted when halal is predominant, because complete avoidance of small, scattered impurities is impractical.
