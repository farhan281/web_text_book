Reading a Hanafi Fiqh Bibliography
List of sources cited in the work

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of sources in a Hanafi fiqh bibliography and explain the significance of each category.

Core Idea
A fiqh bibliography is a map of the scholarly tradition — every source reveals the school's roots.

A bibliography (fihris al-maraji') is not just a list; it is a window into the scholarly foundation of a work. This page lists the references that underpin the commentary you are studying. Understanding what kind of sources are cited—biographies, Quranic exegesis, usul works, later fatwa collections—helps you grasp the breadth of Hanafi learning.

Guided Path
1
Scan the list
Read through the titles quickly to get a sense of the range: classical and modern, specific to Abu Hanifa or general fiqh.
Notice the variety of authors and time periods.
2
Categorize entries
Group the books into categories: biographies of Abu Hanifa, works on usul al-fiqh, collections of ahkam (rulings), works on hadith, and later commentaries.
For example, 'Akhbar Abi Hanifa' is biographical; 'Ahkam al-Qur'an' is tafsir/fiqh.
3
Identify key figures
Note the authors and their significance in the Hanafi school: Siddiq Hasan Khan (modern), al-Laknawi (classic), Muhammad Abu Zahra (contemporary).
This indicates the school's continuity.
4
Relate to Hanafi fiqh
Think why each work is relevant to Hanafi fiqh: some are direct sources of rulings, others are methodological or biographical context.
For instance, al-Jassas' 'Ahkam al-Qur'an' is a key Hanafi tafsir.

Navigating the Hanafi Bibliography
List of reference sources used in the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse a Hanafi scholarly bibliography entry and identify the author, title, and publication details, and explain the significance of such references in Hanafi legal scholarship.

Core Idea
A bibliography is the gateway to verifying sources and understanding the scholarly chain of Hanafi works.

Scholarly works in the Hanafi tradition are often cited by title and author, with the author's death date serving as a chronological anchor. The bibliography at the end of a book like 'Umdat al-Ri'aya provides essential information for tracing the sources used. This lesson will equip you to read such entries efficiently, recognizing the common format and the range of subjects covered.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the entry number and title
Each entry begins with a sequential number. The title is usually enclosed in guillemets or quotation marks. Note the full title as it is the primary identifier of the work.
Titles often start with 'Tuhfat', 'Tahrir', 'Ta'liq', etc.
2
2. Locate the author and his death date
After the title, the author's name appears in parentheses with the death year (hijri). This is crucial for placing the work in its historical context and assessing its authority.
e.g., 'Muhammad Amin Ibn Abidin (d. 1252 AH)'
3
3. Extract publication details
The entry includes the editor (t:), publisher, place, and edition year (hijri or Gregorian). These details help verify the edition used and its reliability.
Look for 'Dar' (publishing house) and edition number.
4
4. Determine the subject area
From the title and author's known specialization, infer the book's subject (fiqh, hadith, usul, etc.). This helps understand why the work is cited in the main text.
e.g., 'Tuhfat al-Fuqaha' is a Hanafi fiqh work.
5
5. Assess the work's relevance to Hanafi fiqh
Given that the book is a Hanafi commentary, note whether the cited work is a standard Hanafi reference (e.g., by al-Samarqandi, Ibn Abidin) or a source from other disciplines used for evidence.
Works by Abd al-Hayy al-Laknawi appear frequently.
