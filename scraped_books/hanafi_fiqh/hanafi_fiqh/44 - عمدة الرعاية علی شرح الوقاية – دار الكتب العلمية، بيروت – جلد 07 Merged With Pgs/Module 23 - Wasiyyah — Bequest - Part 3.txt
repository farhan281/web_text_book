Powers of Multiple Executors: Joint vs. Individual Action
Ruling on when two executors may act alone

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for when one of two executors may act alone, list the specific exceptions, explain the reasoning (talil) behind each exception, and compare the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad with Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Two executors must act together, except in urgent matters where delay causes harm or no deliberation is needed.

When a testator appoints two executors, the default rule in the Hanafi school is that they must act jointly. However, necessity and practicality carve out exceptions: certain matters are so urgent or straightforward that individual action is permitted. This passage from Al-Hidayah lists those exceptions and records an important intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Yusuf would allow individual action in all matters, while Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad restrict it to this list. Understanding the scope of these exceptions is crucial for both executors and judges overseeing estates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
The passage begins by stating the general principle: when two executors are appointed, neither may act alone. Note that this is the default position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
The default promotes careful management and prevents one executor from overreaching.
2
List and classify the exceptions
The passage then lists the specific matters where individual action is permitted. Classify each exception into two categories: (a) matters where delay would cause harm (e.g., shroud, burial, child's necessities, perishable goods), and (b) matters that require no personal judgment (ra'y) (e.g., returning a specific deposit, freeing a specified slave).
The commentary in the source provides the rationale for each exception.
3
Analyze the reasoning (talil)
For each exception, identify the illa (effective cause) that permits individual action. The two main illas are: (1) risk of immediate harm if joint action is required (e.g., body decomposition, child starvation, spoilage), and (2) lack of need for deliberation (e.g., the act is already specified by the testator).
This reasoning is key to applying the rule to new cases.
4
Compare the intra-Hanafi dispute
Note the opposing view of Abu Yusuf: he permits individual action in all matters based on the executor's general authority (wilayah 'ammah). Contrast this with the majority view that limits exceptions to necessity.
The mufta bihi (operative position) is the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as it is more detailed and cautious.

Commentary
This passage addresses the rules governing multiple executors (wasis) appointed in a will. It begins with the case of a single executor who is incapable: the judge must appoint another to assist him, but if the executor is trustworthy and capable, he cannot be removed; retaining him takes precedence because the deceased chose him. When two executors are appointed, the default rule is that they must act jointly. However, certain urgent matters are excepted, allowing individual action: purchasing the shroud and burial preparations (due to risk of decay), litigation, debt payment and collection, buying necessities for a child (fear of starvation), accepting gifts for the minor, freeing a specified slave (no need for deliberation), returning a specific deposit, executing a specified will, gathering scattered assets, and selling perishable goods. The passage then records an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad restrict individual action to these listed matters, while Abu Yusuf holds that each executor may act alone in all affairs. The commentary in the text explains each exception with its rationale, emphasizing practicality and necessity.

Listing and explaining executors' solo actions

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding when a single executor may act alone, and apply the correct ruling to given scenarios.

Core Idea
An executor may act alone in urgent or simple matters; joint action in litigation is discord.

When multiple executors are appointed, must they always act together? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced answer. This lesson explores the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad versus Abu Yusuf on the scope of individual executor authority. Understanding this dispute helps in estate administration and litigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Determine the precise legal question: Can a single executor act without consulting co-executors?
2
List matters allowed by the majority
Compile the nine matters from the passage where Abu Hanifa and Muhammad allow solo action.
Includes litigation, debt payment, purchasing child's needs, accepting gifts, manumitting designated slave, returning specific trust, executing specific bequest, collecting lost property, selling perishable goods.
3
Understand the rationale
Analyze why each matter is permitted: some need no opinion (e.g., returning a specific trust), others are urgent (e.g., selling spoiling goods), and litigation is inherently discordant.
4
Contrast with Abu Yusuf's view
Note that Abu Yusuf allows solo action in all matters, not just the listed ones.
The text mentions this difference but does not detail his proof.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider a concrete scenario: selling a non-urgent property. Under the majority, joint action is required; under Abu Yusuf, a single executor may proceed.
This practical consequence guides estate administration.

The Authority of a Successor Executor Over Dual Estates
Explaining the authority of an executor's appointee over dual estates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the scope of a successor executor's authority and explain the qiyas that supports it.

Core Idea
A successor executor inherits authority over both estates by analogy to a grandfather's guardianship.

This passage explains a key principle in Hanafi wasiyyah law: when an executor appoints a successor, that successor automatically gains authority over both the original testator's estate and the first executor's own estate. The reasoning is based on the nature of executorship as a transferred authority, analogous to the grandfather's replacement of the father. Understanding this rule is crucial for correctly structuring wills and executorships in Hanafi practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage states that the second executor (waṣī al-waṣī) becomes executor for both estates (the original testator's and the first executor's) even if only one was explicitly mentioned in the appointment.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Parse the three scenarios of appointment
The text discusses: (a) appointment only in the first executor's own property, (b) only in the testator's property, (c) both explicitly. In all three, the second executor becomes executor for both.
The commentary clarifies that the apparent implication of the matn is not upheld; the aṣaḥḥ view overrides.
3
Follow the qiyas argument
The author analogizes the executor to a grandfather. Just as a grandfather inherits guardianship from the father and can delegate, the executor inherits authority from the deceased and can delegate to a successor.
The ʿillah is that the authority is transferred (wilāyah muntaqilah), enabling delegation.
4
Contrast with the agent (wakīl)
An agent cannot delegate because the principal is alive and capable of acting. The executor, facing mortality, has implied permission to appoint a successor.
This distinction reinforces the qiyas.

Commentary
This passage explains that when an original executor (al-wasi al-awwal) appoints another person as his own executor (wasi al-wasi), that second person becomes executor over both the original testator's estate and the first executor's own estate, regardless of whether the appointment specified one or both. Since the first executor had authority over both estates (his own and the testator's) at the moment of the original testator's death, his appointment of a successor effectively delegates that dual authority. Unlike a living principal who can act himself, the executor anticipates his own death and thus his appointment of another is considered an implied acceptance of delegation. The text distinguishes this from an agent (wakil) who cannot delegate because the principal is alive and capable.

Executor's Authority to Appoint and Estate Division with Legatee
Commentary on executor's powers and division rules.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an executor appointing a successor and apply the conditions for a valid division of the estate with the legatee.

Core Idea
The executor's authority is like a grandfather's, not an agent's, enabling delegation; division binds only when legatee is present or judge acts.

In this lesson, we examine two related rulings from the Book of Wills. First, the authority of an executor to appoint a successor executor, even without explicit permission, based on a key analogy to the grandfather's guardianship. Second, the rules governing division of the estate: when the executor divides in the legatee's presence it is binding, but if the legatee is absent the division is not binding and the legatee may recover; however, a judge's division is always valid. These distinctions hinge on whether the person receiving the share is a true successor of the deceased or a new owner via bequest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage covers (1) the validity of an executor appointing a successor executor, and (2) the validity of estate division by the executor or judge in relation to the legatee.
Distinguish the two parts in the Arabic text: beginning at 'wa wasi al-wasi' and then at 'ma'a al-mawsa lahu'.
2
Analyze the analogy for appointment
The Hanafi position uses qiyas: the executor is like a grandfather who can appoint a guardian. Identify the asl (grandfather), far' (executor), and illa (transferred authority). Note the objection from those who analogize to an agent and how the Hanafi response distinguishes based on the principal's death.
This is a qiyas khafi – the illa must be extracted by tanqih al-manat.
3
Apply the ruling on division
Read the second part: the executor's division is valid and binding if done in the presence of the legatee. If the legatee is absent, the division is not binding and the legatee may claim one-third of what remains. But the judge's division is valid even in absence.
The difference stems from the legatee not being a successor of the testator in all respects – he owns by a new cause.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
Other schools may require explicit permission for the executor to delegate. The practical difference is when an executor delegates without permission – Hanafi deems it valid, Shafi'i may nullify.
This illustrates the importance of usul al-fiqh in generating concrete fiqh differences.
5
Consolidate with modern application
In contemporary estate administration, an executor can appoint a co-executor or delegate tasks like accounting unless the will restricts. Division of shares to identified beneficiaries must be done carefully – absentee rights are protected unless a court oversees.
The classical paradigm remains relevant in Islamic will settlements.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from the Book of Wills explaining two related rulings. The first ruling: If an executor (the first executor) appoints another person (the second) as executor – either for his own property, or for the property of the original testator, or for both – the second becomes executor in all matters unless the appointment was explicitly limited. The commentator clarifies that the most correct view is that the second becomes executor in both the first executor's property and the original testator's property. The reasoning is that the first executor holds transferred authority from the testator, enabling him to delegate further – analogous to a grandfather who can appoint a guardian. This is distinguished from an agent (wakil) whose principal is alive and can act himself, so an agent cannot appoint a substitute without permission. Therefore, the second executor lawfully takes the place of the first. The second ruling concerns division of the estate by the executor or judge. When the executor divides the estate in the presence of the legatee (al-mawsa lahu), it is valid and binding; if the legatee's share is lost after delivery, the legatee has no recourse against the heirs. But if the legatee is absent, the division made by the executor for him with the heirs is not valid – if that share is lost, the absent legatee can recover one-third of the remaining estate. This is because the executor is not the representative of an absent legatee, unlike the heir for whom the executor acts. However, if the judge divides the estate, even in the legatee's absence, it is valid because the judge exercises general authority. An example clarifies: A man bequeaths 3000 dirhams; the heirs pay it to the judge who distributes it while the legatee is absent – this is allowed because the bequest itself is valid.

وصي (wasi): A person appointed by the testator (or by a previous executor) to manage the estate after death.
الموصى له (al-mawsa lahu): The legatee – the person who receives a bequest.

Division by Executor with Legatee: Validity and Recourse
Ruling on executor's division with legatee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the validity of an executor's division with a legatee and identify when the heirs have recourse against the legatee.

Core Idea
Executor's division with legatee is valid; heirs cannot reclaim lost share from legatee.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah commentary on al-Wiqayah clarifies a nuanced rule: when an executor divides the estate together with a legatee (i.e., they act jointly to separate the heirs' shares), the division is valid. However, the legatee is not a representative of the deceased in the same way as the heir or executor. This distinction has practical consequences: if the executor loses the heirs' share, the heirs cannot pursue the legatee; but if the legatee's own share is lost, he retains a claim on the estate. Let's unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, the passage presents the ruling when the executor divides with the legatee regarding the heirs' share: it is valid. Second, it distinguishes the case where the executor acts alone for the legatee: that division is not valid unless the legatee accepts.
2
Analyze the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The validity hinges on representation (khilafah). The heir is a representative of the deceased, and so is the executor. Thus the executor can represent absent heirs. But the legatee is not a full representative; he acquires by a new cause, so he does not inherit defects, etc.
3
Determine the consequence
If the executor loses the heirs' share after taking possession, the heirs cannot recourse against the legatee. But if the legatee's designated share from the executor is lost, the legatee can claim one-third of what remains of the estate because the division did not bind him.

Executor's Invalid Division for the Absent Legatee
Ruling on invalidity of executor's division for absent legatee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the executor's division for an absent legatee, explain the proof, and distinguish the case of a present legatee with or without permission.

Core Idea
The executor has no authority over the absent legatee's share; division without permission is invalid and the legatee recoups one-third of what remains.

This passage clarifies the limits of an executor's authority when distributing a bequest to an absent legatee. Unlike heirs, the legatee's right arises from a new cause, not from representation of the deceased. Therefore, the executor cannot unilaterally deal with the legatee's share. The ruling also contrasts the situation of a present legatee who may authorize the executor as an agent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the executor dividing the estate on behalf of an absent legatee along with the adult heirs present. The ruling is that such division is invalid (la tasihh).
The ruling applies specifically when the legatee is absent and has not given permission.
2
Locate the implicit proof (dalil)
The reasoning is that the legatee is not a representative (khalifa) of the deceased in the same way as an heir. The legatee's ownership is by a new cause (sabab jadid), i.e., the bequest itself, not by inheritance.
The text does not cite an explicit verse or hadith but uses this rational proof.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (indication of meaning)
Because the legatee's right is independent of the executor's representative capacity, the executor cannot act for the legatee without authorization. Hence, taking possession of the legatee's share without permission is an invalid act.
This distinguishes the legatee from an heir, whose right flows directly from the deceased's estate.
4
Note the distinction for a present legatee
If the legatee is present and has authorized the executor to take possession, the executor acts as an agent (wakil) and the division is valid. If the legatee is present but did not authorize, the legatee has recourse (ruju').
Presence implies the possibility of authorization; absence precludes it.
5
Determine the consequence of loss
If the executor takes the absent legatee's share without permission and it is lost, the legatee recoups one-third of what remains of the estate (thuluth ma baqi). This is a specific remedy.
The measure 'one-third of what remains' is a legal estimate, likely based on the typical portion of a bequest (maximum one-third of the estate).

Judge's Division for Absent Legatee and Hajj Bequest
Ruling on validity of judge's division for absent legatee and executor's division for Hajj bequest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a judge's division of an estate for an absent legatee is valid, and explain why an executor's division for a Hajj bequest is limited to one-third of the remaining estate.

Core Idea
Judge's division valid for measurable/weighable goods; executor's Hajj division limited to one-third if lost.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya addresses two practical issues in inheritance and bequests. First, when a legatee is absent, the judge can validly divide the estate and take possession of the legatee's share—but only if the property is measurable or weighable. Second, if an executor divides assets for a Hajj bequest and the money is lost, the Hajj must be performed from one-third of what remains. The key distinction lies in the authority of the judge versus the executor and the nature of division (tamyiz vs mubadala).

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and rulings
The passage gives two rulings: (1) judge's division for absent legatee is valid (sahih), and (2) if executor divides for Hajj bequest and money is lost, Hajj is performed from one-third of remaining estate.
Both rulings arise from the same principle of authority over absent persons.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof for the judge's authority is a general principle: 'al-qadi nusiba naziran li-umur al-muslimin' (the judge is appointed as overseer for Muslims' affairs). This gives him the power to act for absentees.
This is a zanni dalil based on the judge's role.
3
Trace the distinction between judge and executor
The judge has authority over the absent legatee's interests, including the power to sell perishable property. The executor has no such authority; therefore his division of the legatee's share is not valid. For the Hajj bequest, the executor's division is only effective for one-third of what remains after loss.
Ghayat al-Bayan highlights this fundamental difference.
4
Note the condition on property type
Al-Inaya adds a condition: the judge's division is valid only for property that is measured (makil) or weighed (mawzun). This is because division of such goods is tamyiz (separation without exchange), not mubadala (exchange). For non-measurable/non-weighable items, division is like a sale, which the judge cannot do for an absentee.
Dirhams are weighed, so the example falls within this condition.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Hajj Bequest After Loss of Property
Intra-Hanafi dispute on Hajj from remainder after loss of bequest property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi positions on whether Hajj must be performed from the remaining one-third of the estate after the bequest property is lost, and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between them.

Core Idea
When bequest property for Hajj is lost, the Hanafi imams differ on whether to take from the remaining one-third.

In this passage, we encounter an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) regarding a bequest of Hajj. The scenario involves an executor who divided the estate for a Hajj bequest, but the money was stolen before the Hajj was performed. The three eponymous imams—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani—offer distinct rulings based on their underlying principles. The practical consequence is whether the Hajj must still be carried out and at what cost.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal question
The deceased bequeaths 1000 dirhams for Hajj from his 4000 dirham estate. The money is stolen after being set aside but before Hajj. The question: must Hajj still be performed from the remaining estate?
The executor divided with the heirs (qismah) and set aside the money (ifraz) before the loss.
2
State the position of each Hanafi imam
Abu Hanifa: take one-third of the remaining estate repeatedly if theft recurs. Abu Yusuf: take only if the amount set aside was less than one-third of the original estate; take up to complete one-third of the original, once. Muhammad: take nothing in either case; the bequest is void.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil) of each imam
Abu Hanifa: the division (qismah) is not complete until delivery to the designated purpose (Hajj); without delivery, the loss is like destruction before division, so the remaining one-third must be used. Abu Yusuf: the bequest attaches to one-third of the estate; if part of that one-third remains, it must be executed; if the entire one-third was set aside and lost, nothing further is owed. Muhammad: the executor's ifraz is like the deceased's own ifraz; if the deceased's set-aside is lost, the bequest fails, so the executor's loss also voids it.
The key conceptual difference is the legal effect of the executor's ifraz compared to the deceased's.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The amount taken and whether repeated theft requires further payment. Under Abu Hanifa, every theft triggers a new obligation from the remaining one-third. Under Abu Yusuf, only one additional taking is required if the original set-aside was less than one-third of the original estate. Under Muhammad, no further payment is ever required.
5
Consider the tarjih (preference)
The passage does not specify which position is mufta bihi. Later Hanafi works may prefer Abu Hanifa's view because it enforces the bequest more strictly. Students should note that all three positions are transmitted in the Hanafi corpus as legitimate ikhtilaf.
In practice, a mufti would need to consult later tarjih sources.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) regarding a specific scenario in the chapter of bequests (Kitab al-Wasaya). The case: A man dies leaving 4000 dirhams and bequeaths that Hajj be performed for him, which costs 1000 dirhams. For Abu Yusuf, the bequest attaches to one-third of the estate; if what remains of that one-third is insufficient, no further obligation arises. For Muhammad, the executor stands in the place of the deceased, so the loss is attributed to the deceased's estate, and the bequest fails. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here; the text merely reports the differences.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on recoupment after reclaimed sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the executor's recoupment when a slave sold per the will is reclaimed and explain the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
The executor recoups from the entire estate; Muhammad limits recoupment to one-third, treating the sale as an execution of the will.

This passage addresses a scenario where an executor sells a slave from the estate as instructed by the will, the price is lost, and then the slave is reclaimed from the buyer. The executor must return the price to the buyer, but can he recoup from the estate? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement: Abu Hanifa's final view allows recoupment from the entire estate, while Muhammad restricts it to one-third. Understanding the reasoning behind each position clarifies the principles of agency, deception, and the nature of wills in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The executor sells a slave per the will, the price perishes, then the slave is reclaimed. The executor is liable to the buyer, then recoups from the estate.
This is the baseline ruling presented in the matn.
2
Understand the basis for validity of sale
The sale is valid even without creditors present because their right is attached to the value, not the specific form. The sale transforms the asset into its price, fulfilling their right.
Contrast with the case of an indebted authorized slave where creditors have a right to earnings.
3
Recognize the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa initially held no recoupment, then retracted to recoupment from the entire estate. Muhammad al-Shaybani held recoupment only from one-third of the estate.
The dispute is over the scope of recoupment, not the validity of the sale or liability.
4

Abu Hanifa: the deceased deceived the executor by claiming the slave was his, creating a debt on the estate. Debts are paid from the entire estate. Muhammad: the sale was to execute the will, and wills are limited to one-third.
The key difference is whether the executor's role is as agent (deception) or will executor.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If the estate is large, under Abu Hanifa the executor recoups all; under Muhammad recoupment is capped at one-third. If the one-third is insufficient, the executor bears the loss.
The practical difference depends on the size of the estate relative to the loss.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a ruling from the matn regarding the executor's (wasīy) sale of a slave from the estate of the deceased. The opening line of the matn states: 'The sale by the executor of a slave from the estate in the absence of the creditors is valid. And the executor who sold what he was instructed to sell becomes liable. He gave its price in charity, then the slave was reclaimed after the price perished with him, and he recoups from the estate.' The commentary then explains each part. First, it affirms the validity of the executor's sale in the creditors' absence, drawing an analogy to the testator himself selling during his death illness. The reason is that the creditors' right is attached to the value, not the specific asset, and the sale transforms the asset into its price, thus preserving value. This is contrasted with the case of an indebted authorized slave (ʿabd maʾdhūn madīn), where creditors have a right to his earnings (istisʿāʾ), and selling him would nullify that right; hence the sale is invalid without their presence. Second, when the executor sells a slave per the will's instruction, receives the price, the price is lost, and then the slave is reclaimed from the buyer, the executor is liable to return the price to the buyer because the buyer did not receive the sold item. This liability is on the executor as the contracting party. The executor then recoups from the estate because he acted as an agent (ʿāmil) for the deceased. The commentary also discusses a similar case with a child's inheritance: an executor sells a slave that fell to the child's share, the price perishes, then the slave is reclaimed; the executor recoups from the child's property, and the child recoups from the other heirs. The passage then records an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the executor's recoupment: Abu Hanifa initially held that there is no recoupment because the executor became liable by taking possession (ḍamina bi-qabḍihi). However, he later retracted and ruled that the executor recoups from the entire estate (jamīʿ al-tarika). Muhammad al-Shaybani held that the executor only recoups from one-third of the estate (al-thulth), because the recoupment is based on the will (waṣiyya) and the subject of a will is limited to one-third. The commentary supports Abu Hanifa's final view with the reasoning that the executor was deceived (maghrūr) by the deceased, who claimed the slave was his, and the liability becomes a debt on the deceased, which is paid from the entire estate.

Key Terms
الوصي (al-waṣī): The executor appointed by the testator to carry out the will and manage the estate; he stands in the place of the testator in legal acts.
الغرماء (al-ghuramāʾ): The creditors of the deceased whose debts are to be paid from the estate.
الاستسعاء (al-istisʿāʾ): The right of creditors of an indebted authorized slave (ʿabd maʾdhūn madīn) to seek repayment from his earnings; if the slave is sold, this right is lost.

Executor's Trading: Only Slight Fraud Permitted
Ruling on executor's trading with slight vs gross fraud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an executor's sale and purchase on behalf of an orphan, distinguish between slight and gross fraud, and explain the rationale.

Core Idea
An executor may only transact with slight fraud; gross fraud is prohibited because it wastes the orphan's property.

An executor appointed by the father or judge has a fiduciary duty to protect the orphan's wealth. This lesson explains the fundamental principle: the executor may buy and sell only within the range of price fluctuation that people normally overlook (slight fraud), but not with gross fraud. The distinction between the two is based on appraisers' evaluations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the executor is doing (selling or buying) and the hukm: permissible with slight fraud, prohibited with gross fraud.
The ruling is from al-Hidayah based on Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Define slight and gross fraud
Slight fraud is a price difference that falls within the range of appraisers' estimates; gross fraud exceeds that range.
This definition is from an-Nihayah and is the most sound according to the text.
3
Analyze the rationale
Slight fraud is unavoidable in commerce, so it is tolerated. Gross fraud destroys the orphan's wealth and violates the executor's duty of guardianship.
The text contrasts the executor with authorized individuals who act by their own capacity.
4
Note the scope
This ruling applies to executor's transactions with strangers, not to self-purchase, which has additional conditions.
Self-purchase is covered in a later lesson.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah, addresses the rules governing an executor's (wasī) sale and purchase of property on behalf of an orphan. The central ruling is that an executor may only transact with slight market fluctuation (ghaban yasīr) — meaning fractional differences that fall within the range of normal appraisers' estimates — and not with gross fraud (ghaban fāḥish), which is beyond that range. The reason is that an executor acts as a legal guardian (niyāba shar'iyya) and is obligated to protect the orphan's wealth; gross fraud nullifies this purpose. 

The passage then clarifies that the authorized minor (ṣaghīr ma'dhūn), authorized slave ('abd ma'dhūn), and mukātab (slave with a contract of manumission) may transact with gross fraud according to Imam Abu Hanifa, because they act by their own legal capacity ('ahliyya), not as representatives. The two companions (Sahibayn, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, holding that such transactions are invalid because they amount to gratuitous acts (tabarru'), which these individuals are not permitted to perform without necessity. 

A key distinction is made between an executor appointed by the father (waṣī al-ab) and one appointed by the judge (waṣī al-qāḍī): only the former may buy from himself for the orphan, provided there is an apparent benefit (manfa'a ẓāhira). The apparent benefit is defined as selling something worth 15 for 10 or buying 10 for 15, i.e., a clear advantage for the orphan. 

For immovable property ('iqār), the earlier scholars (mutaqaddimūn) permitted sale at market value. The later scholars (muta'akhkhirūn) imposed stricter conditions, as listed by Qadhi Khan: the property must be sold for double its value to a willing buyer, or the orphan needs the money for maintenance, or to pay a debt of the deceased, or to execute a bequest, or if the property is burdensome or dilapidated. Even then, sale must be at market value or with slight fraud, never with gross fraud.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah, addresses the rules governing an executor's (wasī) sale and purchase of property on behalf of an orphan. The central ruling is that an executor may only transact with slight market fluctuation (ghaban yasīr) — meaning fractional differences that fall within the range of normal appraisers' estimates — and not with gross fraud (ghaban fāḥish), which is beyond that range. The reason is that an executor acts as a legal guardian (niyāba shar'iyya) and is obligated to protect the orphan's wealth; gross fraud nullifies this purpose. 

The passage then clarifies that the authorized minor (ṣaghīr ma'dhūn), authorized slave ('abd ma'dhūn), and mukātab (slave with a contract of manumission) may transact with gross fraud according to Imam Abu Hanifa, because they act by their own legal capacity ('ahliyya), not as representatives. The two companions (Sahibayn, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, holding that such transactions are invalid because they amount to gratuitous acts (tabarru'), which these individuals are not permitted to perform without necessity. 

A key distinction is made between an executor appointed by the father (waṣī al-ab) and one appointed by the judge (waṣī al-qāḍī): only the former may buy from himself for the orphan, provided there is an apparent benefit (manfa'a ẓāhira). The apparent benefit is defined as selling something worth 15 for 10 or buying 10 for 15, i.e., a clear advantage for the orphan. 

For immovable property ('iqār), the earlier scholars (mutaqaddimūn) permitted sale at market value. The later scholars (muta'akhkhirūn) imposed stricter conditions, as listed by Qadhi Khan: the property must be sold for double its value to a willing buyer, or the orphan needs the money for maintenance, or to pay a debt of the deceased, or to execute a bequest, or if the property is burdensome or dilapidated. Even then, sale must be at market value or with slight fraud, never with gross fraud.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah, addresses the rules governing an executor's (wasī) sale and purchase of property on behalf of an orphan. The central ruling is that an executor may only transact with slight market fluctuation (ghaban yasīr) — meaning fractional differences that fall within the range of normal appraisers' estimates — and not with gross fraud (ghaban fāḥish), which is beyond that range. The reason is that an executor acts as a legal guardian (niyāba shar'iyya) and is obligated to protect the orphan's wealth; gross fraud nullifies this purpose. 

The passage then clarifies that the authorized minor (ṣaghīr ma'dhūn), authorized slave ('abd ma'dhūn), and mukātab (slave with a contract of manumission) may transact with gross fraud according to Imam Abu Hanifa, because they act by their own legal capacity ('ahliyya), not as representatives. The two companions (Sahibayn, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, holding that such transactions are invalid because they amount to gratuitous acts (tabarru'), which these individuals are not permitted to perform without necessity. 

A key distinction is made between an executor appointed by the father (waṣī al-ab) and one appointed by the judge (waṣī al-qāḍī): only the former may buy from himself for the orphan, provided there is an apparent benefit (manfa'a ẓāhira). The apparent benefit is defined as selling something worth 15 for 10 or buying 10 for 15, i.e., a clear advantage for the orphan. 

For immovable property ('iqār), the earlier scholars (mutaqaddimūn) permitted sale at market value. The later scholars (muta'akhkhirūn) imposed stricter conditions, as listed by Qadhi Khan: the property must be sold for double its value to a willing buyer, or the orphan needs the money for maintenance, or to pay a debt of the deceased, or to execute a bequest, or if the property is burdensome or dilapidated. Even then, sale must be at market value or with slight fraud, never with gross fraud.

Key Terms
منفعة ظاهرة (manfa'a ẓāhira): An apparent benefit for the orphan, defined as selling something worth 15 for 10 or buying 10 for 15.
وصي الأب (waṣī al-ab): An executor appointed by the father in his will to manage the orphan's property.
وصي القاضي (waṣī al-qāḍī): An executor appointed by a judge after the father's death or in the absence of a paternal executor.
المتأخرون (al-muta'akhkhirūn): The later Hanafi scholars, typically from the 6th century AH onwards, who refined and sometimes restricted earlier rulings.

Commentary

Executor's Restrictions on Hawala, Lending, and Sale to Absent Major
Explains executor's duties regarding debt collection, lending, and selling property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings for an executor regarding hawala, lending, and sale of property, and apply the conditions to hypothetical scenarios.

Core Idea
An executor must protect the orphan's wealth: hawala only to the wealthy, no lending, no sale of real estate to absent majors.

This lesson examines the specific limits placed on an executor (wasi) when managing the estate of an orphan or an absent major heir. The matn lays out three clear restrictions: debt transfers must go to the wealthiest debtor, lending is absolutely prohibited, and only movable property can be sold for an absent major. The commentary unpacks the rationale behind each rule, grounded in the executor's fundamental duty of preservation (hifz), not profit-making.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
Read the matn phrase by phrase: hawala (debt transfer), lending, and sale. For each, determine the ruling (permissible/obligatory/prohibited) and the scope (e.g., to whom? for whom?).
The matn uses short imperatives: 'resorts to the wealthy, does not lend, sells except real estate.'
2
Understand the rationale of preservation
The commentary states that the executor's job is preservation, not investment. Lending puts the orphan's money at risk and is therefore prohibited. Similarly, hawala to an insolvent debtor would endanger the debt.
This is a rational (aqli) proof: lending contradicts the duty of hifz.
3
Distinguish between minor and absent major heirs
For a minor, the executor may sell real estate (early view) or only under strict conditions (later view). For an absent major, the executor can sell movables but not real estate. Note why: presence of the heir means the executor loses authority.
The text says 'restricted to absent major' because if present, the heirs handle their own affairs.
4
Examine the exceptions and intra-Hanafi disputes
The father has broader powers than an executor or agent. The judge's sale of one minor's property to another is disputed. Understand the 'khilaf' and the later conditions for selling real estate (buyer desires it, high price, etc.).
The commentary references 'Radd al-Muhtar' and 'al-’Inayah' for these details.

Regarding the judge's sale of that, there is a dispute. And if the father appoints a man to sell his property to his child or to buy from him, it is not permissible unless the father is present. And it is not permissible for the judge to sell the orphan's property to himself, and its opposite because the judge's permissibility is by way of judgment, and his judgment for himself is not permissible, unlike what he buys from his executor or sells to the orphan, and his executor accepts, then it is permissible. And if [the executor is appointed] by this judge... etc., summarized. So in 'Radd al-Muhtar'. [1] His saying: 'yabhtal' means he accepts hawala in collecting the debt that was owed to the orphan from another. [2] His saying: 'ala al-amla' means upon the one most capable of paying the debt from the transferor who is the debtor. [3] His saying: 'wa la yuqrid' means the executor does not lend the orphan's money, because he is entrusted with preservation, and lending is not preservation, rather it harms the orphan, so it is not permissible. [4] His saying: 'illa al-'iqar' means it is permissible for the executor to sell for the absent major everything except real estate. He restricted to the absent major because if they are present, the executor has no disposal of the estate at all, but he collects the debts of the deceased, receives his rights, and pays to the heirs, unless the deceased has a debt or made a will and the heirs did not pay the debts nor execute the will from their own wealth, then he sells the estate. Commentary
This passage discusses the executor's authority regarding hawala (debt transfer), lending, and selling on behalf of an absent major heir. The matn states: 'And he resorts to the wealthy for hawala, not to the insolvent, and he does not lend, and he sells for the absent major except real estate.' The commentary explains each phrase: 'yabhtal' means accepting hawala (transfer of debt) to collect what is owed to the orphan from another debtor. 'Al-amla' refers to the wealthiest debtor, i.e., the one most capable of paying. The executor must transfer the debt to the wealthy debtor, not to the insolvent one, to protect the orphan's interests. 'Wa la yuqrid' means the executor must not lend the orphan's money, as guardianship is about preservation, and lending is not preservation and may harm the orphan. 'Illa al-'iqar' restricts sale for the absent major: the executor may sell any property except real estate for an absent major heir. For a minor heir, the executor may sell real estate as well, according to the early scholars (salaf). The later scholars (mutakakhirun) add conditions for selling real estate, such as the buyer desiring it at a high price. The commentary also addresses the father's exclusive right to sell property between his two minors, and the judge's limitations. If the father appoints an agent, it is permissible only if the father is present. The judge cannot sell orphan's property to himself, but can buy from the executor or sell to the orphan if the executor accepts. The passage is summarized from 'Radd al-Muhtar' and 'al-’Inayah'.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-ḥawāla): Transfer of a debt from one debtor to another; the executor must transfer to the wealthiest debtor.
الأملأ (al-amla'): The wealthiest or most capable debtor; the one most able to pay the debt.
العقار (al-'iqār): Real estate, immovable property. The executor cannot sell it for an absent major heir, but can for a minor.

Key Takeaway
The executor's duty is preservation, not profit; hence hawala to the wealthy, no lending, and restricted sale of real estate.
