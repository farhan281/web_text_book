The Validity of a Minor's Acknowledgment in Inheritance: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on minor's iqrar in inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on a minor's iqrar in inheritance, identify the basis of each, and apply the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
A minor with permission becomes like an adult; his acknowledgment is valid in both earnings and inheritance, according to Zahir al-Riwaya.

This passage examines a disagreement within the Hanafi school: does a minor who has been given permission to trade also have the capacity to make a binding acknowledgment (iqrar) regarding property inherited from a deceased relative? The Zahir al-Riwaya answers yes, while a narration from Abu Hanifa (via Hasan ibn Ziyad) restricts validity to earnings. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of how the madhhab balances textual authority and underlying rationale (ta'lil).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the Zahir al-Riwaya position and the alternative narration from Abu Hanifa. One says iqrar is valid in both inheritance and earnings; the other says it is valid only in earnings.
2
Trace the legal reasoning of the alternative view
Why does the alternative view limit iqrar to earnings? Because the need for iqrar arises only in trade to facilitate transactions; inheritance does not generate the same need.
This reasoning is based on the purpose (hikma) of permission.
3
Examine the Zahir al-Riwaya response
How does the relied-upon position justify validity in inheritance? Permission removes hajr and makes the minor like an adult. Both funds are his property free from others' claims. The guardian's permission covers ancillaries of trade, including iqrar.
A rebuttal is given: the need for iqrar exists in inheritance because otherwise people would not deal with the minor.
4
Understand the tarjih mechanism
How does the author signal the relied-upon position? By qualifying with 'zahir al-riwaya', indicating that this is the well-known authoritative transmission, despite the conflicting narration.
This shows how the madhhab prioritizes some transmissions over others.
5
Apply the mufta bihi ruling
In a scenario, determine whether a minor's iqrar on inherited property is binding. For example, a minor with permission admits that a portion of his inherited house belongs to his sister. The iqrar is binding according to Zahir al-Riwaya.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether a minor who has been given permission to trade (al-madhun) can make a valid acknowledgment (iqrar) regarding inherited property, in addition to earnings. The text first states the position of the Zahir al-Riwaya (the six core books of Imam Muhammad): that since permission lifts the legal restriction (hajr) and the minor becomes like an adult, his acknowledgment is valid in inheritance as well. It then reports a narration from Abu Hanifa that it is not valid in inheritance, because the need for acknowledgment only exists in trade to facilitate transactions; inheritance does not generate the same need. A question is raised: the guardian does not have authority to make an acknowledgment on the minor, so how can the minor's own acknowledgment be effective? The answer is that the acknowledgment is an ancillary aspect of trade, and the guardian's permission covers trade and its ancillaries. The commentator then clarifies that the qualification 'in the apparent narration' is used because Hasan ibn Ziyad narrated from Abu Hanifa that the minor's acknowledgment in inherited property is not valid, relying on the rationale of necessity limited to trade. The preferred view (zahir al-riwaya) is that the minor, with the guardian's consent, is treated as an adult, and since he owns both types of property free from claims of others, his acknowledgment is valid in both. This is supported by Abu Hanifa's ruling that after permission, the minor may trade even with a grossly unfair price (ghubn fahish), just as an adult may.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment or confession by a person that leads to a legal consequence, such as admitting a debt or ownership.
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwaya): The apparent or well-known transmitted rulings from the six core books of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.
حجر (hajr): Legal restriction on a person's capacity to transact, e.g., minority or insanity.
ولاية (wilaya): Guardianship; the authority of a guardian (wali) over a minor's property and affairs.
غبن فاحش (ghubn fahish): Gross fraud or deception in a transaction where the price is extremely unfair.

Testimony of Dividers: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on accepting testimony of dividers regarding receipt of shares

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf accept the testimony of dividers while Muhammad al-Shaybani rejects it, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Dividers testify about another's receipt, not their own act; thus their testimony is accepted.

In property partitions, a dispute may arise when a partner denies receiving his share. The dividers (qasimayn) can testify to confirm receipt, but their own involvement raises questions of self-interest. This lesson explores the intra-Hanafi difference on the admissibility of such testimony and the reasoning behind the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two shaykhs), the testimony of dividers is accepted in court regarding receipt of shares. This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani (and Imam al-Shafi'i) reject this testimony. They argue it is testimony about the dividers' own act of division.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; Imam Muhammad is a disciple of Abu Hanifa.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Muhammad likens it to a person who testifies about creating a condition for freeing a slave – it is testimony about one's own action, which is inadmissible.
The analogy emphasizes the dividers' direct involvement.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The two shaykhs differentiate: the dividers' act is simply marking boundaries (tamyiz), which the disputants agree upon. The testimony is about the partner's act of receipt, not their own. Self-interest is absent because the dispute is only about receipt, not the division work.
Al-Tahawi adds an exception for hired dividers, where self-interest exists.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: in Hanafi courts according to the two shaykhs, dividers' testimony is accepted; in Shafi'i courts it is rejected. The mufta bihi is acceptance, except for hired dividers by consensus.
This difference affects litigation outcomes in property disputes.

Commentary
This passage discusses the admissibility of the testimony of the two individuals appointed to divide property (qasimun). According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two shaykhs), their testimony that a partner has received his share is accepted in court after partition, whether the dividers were appointed by the judge or privately. Their reasoning is that they are testifying about the act of the partner (receipt), not their own act; their own act is merely marking boundaries. Imam Muhammad and Imam al-Shafi'i reject such testimony, arguing that it is essentially testimony about their own act (the division), which is impermissible as no one can testify in his own favor. Al-Tahawi adds that if the dividers were hired for the job, there is consensus that their testimony is not accepted because they have a direct interest (they claim fulfillment of their contract). Some later scholars prefer this view. However, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the acceptance of their testimony, since the dispute is about receipt, not about their work, and no benefit returns to them.

شهادة (shahadah): Testimony given in court; here specifically regarding property division.
استيفاء (istifa'): Receipt or taking possession; the act of receiving one's share.
تميّز (tamyiz): Distinguishing or marking boundaries; the act of the dividers in partition.
تهمة (tuhma): Accusation of self-interest or bias that renders testimony inadmissible.

Acceptance of Testimony in Transactions vs Religious Matters
Explains the Hanafi ruling on accepting testimony from various individuals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi conditions for accepting testimony in mu'amalat and diyanat, and identify the relied-upon position in the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the mastur.

Core Idea
In transactions, the word of any discerning person is accepted; in religious matters, only a just Muslim's word suffices.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya discusses a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh: the acceptance of a person's statement (qawl) varies depending on the context. In worldly transactions (mu'amalat), the law eases conditions due to necessity, while in religious matters (diyanat) stricter requirements apply. The passage also reveals a subtle internal disagreement among Hanafi authorities regarding the 'mastur'—a person whose moral character is unknown. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly applying rulings in everyday dealings such as buying meat or accepting agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Contexts
Recognize that the passage distinguishes between mu'amalat (transactions) and diyanat (religious matters). The ruling on accepting a person's word differs fundamentally between them.
Mu'amalat includes sales, agency, gifts, etc. Diyanat includes reports about halal/haram, purity, etc.
2
State the General Principle for Each Context
For mu'amalat: the word of any discerning person (mumayyiz) is accepted, regardless of faith, gender, or status. For diyanat: only the word of a just Muslim ('adl) is accepted.
This is due to the frequency of transactions and the necessity for ease.
3
Understand the Implicit vs Explicit Distinction
The passage notes that in a statement about buying meat, the primary information is the transaction (purchase), and the halal/haram ruling is only implicit. Something can be established implicitly even if not explicitly, like endowment of movable property with immovable.
This explains why a disbeliever's word about meat is accepted in transactions.
4
Analyze the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Mastur
The passage presents a disagreement: In the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya), the word of a mastur (person of unknown status) is not accepted, equating him with a fasiq. Imam Abu Hanifa (according to one report) accepts his word.
The relied-upon position is the apparent narration.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi Position
Based on the majority of later authorities (e.g., al-Hidayah), the position of the apparent narration is adopted: the mastur is treated like a fasiq and his word is not accepted in any context.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya, a commentary on Sharh al-Wiqaya, discusses the Hanafi ruling on the acceptance of a person's statement (qawl) in two contexts: transactions (mu'amalat) and religious matters (diyanat). The author begins with a specific example: a disbeliever claims to have bought meat from a Muslim or Kitabi (making it halal) or from a Majusi (making it haram). The ruling is that the disbeliever's word is accepted in this transaction because transactions are frequent and require ease. The commentary then addresses a potential objection: since the statement involves declaring meat halal or haram, which is a religious matter, why should a disbeliever's word be accepted? The response clarifies that the primary information is about the purchase (a transaction), and the halal/haram determination is merely implicit (dhimni). It is a well-established principle that something can be established implicitly even if it cannot be established explicitly, like the endowment of movable property as an implicit part of immovable property. The text then expands to a general principle: in transactions, the report of any discerning person (mumayyiz) is accepted, regardless of being free or slave, Muslim or disbeliever, adult or minor, due to the widespread necessity. People rarely find someone who meets all conditions of 'adala (justice) to witness or handle their affairs. Moreover, transactions involve no compulsion, so requiring justice would create hardship. This acceptance extends to examples like agency, gifts from a slave or child, and permission to trade. In contrast, religious matters (such as reporting the impurity of water for tayammum) require the word of a just Muslim ('adl) because these matters are less frequent and involve higher stakes. The sinner (fasiq) is suspect, and a disbeliever is not bound by Islamic rulings, so they cannot impose obligations on Muslims. The passage ends with an intra-Hanafi dispute: in the apparent narration (zahir al-riwaya), the word of a person whose state is unknown (mastur) is not accepted, equating him with a sinner. However, Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly accepts his word in his school, allowing judgment based on it. The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of the apparent narration, considering the majority opinion (akthar al-ra'y).

Core Idea
Conflicting oaths nullify; istihsan establishes diya as the least consequence of absolute killing.

Imagine two witnesses each claim a different man killed the victim, and the guardian says both are guilty. Strict qiyas would leave the family empty-handed, but Hanafi jurists use istihsan to ensure compensation. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and the key distinction between confession and testimony.

By strict analogy (qiyas), if the instrument of killing is unknown, the ruling is uncertain, so nothing is obligatory. This is because the legal consequence (qisas vs. diya) depends on the weapon.

The testifiers gave absolute testimony (mutlaq) – they said 'he killed him' without specifying the weapon. In Hanafi usul, mutlaq is not ambiguous (mujmal); it can be acted upon. Therefore, the least consequence – diya – is established.

This is a critical procedural difference in Hanafi law.

[2] His statement: 'the absolute is not ambiguous' – for the absolute is possible to act upon. Do you not see that Allah Almighty obligated kaffarah by freeing an absolute slave, and it was obligatory to act upon it? Had it been ambiguous, it would not have been obligatory to act upon it. Thus in some glosses of al-Hidayah.
[4] His statement: 'because in the second... etc.' – its clarification: Confession and testimony each encompass the existence of the entire killing and the obligation of qisas. There occurs denial in the confession from the one confessed to (the guardian), and in the testimony from the one testified for (also the guardian). However, the guardian's denial of the confessor in part of what he confessed does not invalidate his confession in the remainder, while the guardian's denial of the witness in part of what he testified invalidates the testimony entirely, because denial is a declaration of sin (tashqiq). The sin of a witness prevents acceptance, but the sin of a confessor does not prevent the validity of the confession. Thus in al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on conflicting confessions and contradictory oaths in homicide cases. The matn states that if two men both confess to killing Zayd, the guardian may kill both. However, if two separate oaths each name a different killer, and the guardian claims both killed him, the oaths are nullified. By qiyas, nothing is due because the killing is unknown without specifying the instrument. But by istihsan, since they testified to absolute killing (mutlaq), which is not ambiguous, the least consequence—diya—is established. The commentary elaborates: the possibility of differing instruments renders the killing unknown, but the mutlaq is workable, as in the Qur'anic case of freeing a slave. The commentary also distinguishes between confession (iqrar) and testimony (shahada): in confession, the guardian's denial of some part does not invalidate the rest, but in testimony, denial corrupts it entirely because testimony requires integrity. This is a key principle in Hanafi law.

المطلق (al-mutlaq): Absolute, unrestricted term; here, 'killing' without specifying the instrument.

Testimony of Executors: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf on Mutual Benefit
Intra-Hanafi dispute on executor testimony validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the testimony of executors for each other and explain the thamarah al-khilaf between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Testimony of executors is scrutinized for tuhma; the key dispute is whether mutual benefit alone invalidates.

In Hanafi fiqh, executors (wasis) are entrusted with managing the estate of a minor or an absent adult. Their testimony in court is subject to special scrutiny due to potential self-interest (tuhma). This lesson examines an intra-school disagreement: when two executors testify for each other regarding a debt against the deceased, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad accept the testimony, while Abu Yusuf rejects it due to mutual benefit. We will analyze the reasoning behind each position and its practical implication.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: the testimony of two executors for each other is valid (ja'iz).
This is the position of the majority of the early Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Abu Yusuf holds that such testimony is invalid (batil) because of mutual tuhma (suspicion of reciprocal benefit).
Abu Yusuf's reasoning extends tuhma to indirect benefit.
3
Analyse the opponent's proof
Abu Yusuf's basis: each executor benefits when the other's claim is accepted, creating a mutual interest that undermines credibility.
This is a rational (aqli) argument based on the nature of testimony.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad respond: the executors are not testifying for themselves; the benefit is indirect and does not reach the level of tuhma. The debt is against the deceased, not against the testifying executor.
The distinction relies on direct vs. indirect benefit.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: in a court following Abu Hanifa, the testimony is admissible; in a court following Abu Yusuf, it is rejected, potentially affecting the outcome of inheritance claims.
The choice of which view to adopt is a matter of tarjih for the mufti.

Mutual Testimony of Executors: Ruling and Four Categories
Ruling on mutual testimony with four-category classification and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mutual testimony of executors, distinguish the four categories, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding debt testimony.

Core Idea
Mutual testimony is valid when the right is in the dhimmah, invalid when it creates partnership suspicion.

Testimony can be suspect when the witness stands to benefit. For executors, mutual testimony raises the question of partnership in the estate. The Hanafi school classifies such testimonies into four categories, with a key disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad versus Abu Yusuf over debt testimony. Understanding the basis of this dispute—the suspicion of partnership (tuhmat al-shirkah)—is essential for applying the correct ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling for executors' testimony
Note that executors' testimony for the estate of the deceased (for a minor or adult) is invalid; but for others, it is valid. This baseline frames the exception of mutual testimony.
The phrase 'laghat shahādah' means the testimony is null/void.
2
Understand the four-category classification
The passage lists four types: (1) debt – disputed; (2) bequest of a share (e.g., one-third) – agreed invalid; (3) different items (e.g., female slave vs. male slave) – agreed valid; (4) mixed (slave vs. unspecified thousand or share) – agreed invalid.
The key variable is whether the testimony establishes a shared right (shirkah) triggering tuhmah.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute over debt
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad accept mutual testimony for debt; Abu Yusuf rejects it. The basis: Abu Hanifa argues debt is in the dhimmah (abstract liability), which can hold multiple claims without partnership; Abu Yusuf argues that after death, debt attaches to the estate, creating a common pool and thus suspicion.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different understandings of how debt operates post-death.
4
Extract the governing principle: tuhmat al-shirkah
When testimony creates a right that would be shared (shirkah), suspicion (tuhmah) arises and testimony is rejected. Where no partnership results, testimony is accepted.
This principle unifies all four categories: categories 2 and 4 create partnership; category 3 does not; category 1 is debated.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, accepting mutual testimony for debt. This is the view preferred by later authorities like al-Marghinani.
In practice, a judge following the Hanafi school would accept such testimony.

The basis of all of this is suspicion of partnership: whatever establishes suspicion, testimony is not accepted therein – that is the second and fourth; and whatever does not establish suspicion, it is accepted – like the third. As for the first type, disagreement occurred also based on that: the point of acceptance is that debt is obligatory on the dhimmah, which can accommodate multiple rights, so there is no partnership; therefore, if a stranger voluntarily pays off the debt of one of them, the other has no right of participation. The point of rejection is that debt after death attaches to the estate, the dhimmah becomes void thereby; therefore, if one of them collects his right from the partnership, the other shares in it; thus the testimony establishes a right of partnership, so suspicion arises, contrary to the debtor's life, because [the debt] is in the dhimmah due to its continuity, not in the property, so partnership does not occur.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Wiqayah discusses the validity of testimony when two executors or two parties testify for each other regarding the deceased's estate. The ruling is that such testimony is invalid (laghat) when it concerns the estate for which they are executors, but valid for others. The example given is two men testifying for two others that a deceased owes them a debt of one thousand, and the latter testifying for the former for a similar debt. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say it is valid, but Abu Yusuf rejects it. The reason is that after death, the debt attaches to the estate (tarka), creating a common right, thus suspicion of partnership. But during the debtor's life, the debt is in the dhimmah, which is not shared. The text then classifies such testimonies into four categories: 1) Debt (differed upon), 2) Bequest of a specific share like a thousand or one-third (agreed invalid), 3) Testimony for each other about different items like a female slave vs a bequest of a slave (agreed valid), 4) Testimony for each other about a slave vs a thousand or one-third (agreed invalid). The principle is suspicion of partnership (tuhmat al-shirkah): if the testimony would create a shared right, it is rejected; otherwise accepted.

Key Terms
شهادة (shahādah): Legal testimony given in court to establish a right or fact.
وصي (waṣīy): An executor appointed to manage the estate of a deceased or affairs of a minor.
تهمة (tuhmah): Suspicion of self-interest or bias that disqualifies a witness or invalidates testimony.
شركة (shirkah): Partnership or common ownership; here, shared right in the estate due to competing claims.

The Four Scenarios of Testimony for Bequests
Classification of testimony for bequests into four scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify inconsistent witness testimony for bequests into four scenarios and state the ruling based on the presence or absence of tuhmat al-shirkah.

Core Idea
Testimony for a bequest is accepted only when it does not create a suspicion of partnership between witnesses.

In this lesson, we examine a key principle in Hanafi testimony law: the suspicion of partnership (tuhmat al-shirkah). When witnesses give inconsistent statements about a bequest, their testimony may be accepted or rejected depending on whether it would create a shared interest between them. The commentary from al-Hidayah and al-‘Inayah classifies such cases into four distinct scenarios, each with a clear ruling. We will focus on scenarios 2, 3, and 4, where the ruling is agreed upon.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
List the four scenarios as enumerated in the text: (1) testimony for a debt, (2) testimony for a common share of the estate, (3) cross-testimony for specific items, (4) testimony for a specific item then for a common share.
Scenarios 2 and 4 involve a common share; scenario 3 involves specific items.
2
Determine the ruling for each agreed scenario
For scenario 2 (common share), testimony is rejected. For scenario 3 (cross-testimony for specific items), testimony is accepted. For scenario 4 (specific item then common share), testimony is rejected.
Scenarios 2 and 4 are rejected; scenario 3 is accepted.
3
Extract the underlying principle
The text states that all rulings are based on 'tuhmat al-shirkah' (suspicion of partnership). When the testimony would create a common interest in the same asset, the witnesses are suspected of colluding, so it is rejected.
The principle is that if the witnesses stand to benefit mutually, their testimony is unreliable.
4
Apply the principle to verify
In scenario 2, both witnesses claim a share of the same estate, creating a partnership. In scenario 4, the first pair claims a specific item, the second pair claims a share of the estate that includes that item, again partnership. In scenario 3, each pair testifies for a specific item that is separate, so no partnership.
The ruling matches the principle in each case.

The Debt Dispute: Does Testimony for a Debt Create Partnership?
Dispute on acceptance of testimony for a debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions on whether inconsistent testimony for a debt is accepted and explain the reasoning based on the nature of debt in the dhimmah versus the estate.

Core Idea
Testimony for a debt is disputed because debt can be in the dhimmah (no partnership) or attached to the estate (partnership) after death.

In this lesson, we delve into the first scenario from the previous classification: testimony for a debt. While the witnesses' statements are inconsistent, there is disagreement on whether the testimony is accepted. The two views are based on the legal nature of debt: is it a claim on the dhimmah, which can hold multiple rights without partnership, or does it attach to the estate after death, creating a common interest?

Guided Path
1
Recall the first scenario
The first scenario is testimony for a debt where the witnesses give inconsistent statements (e.g., different amounts). The text says 'they differed about it'.
This is the only scenario where there is disagreement.
2
State the two opposing views
One view accepts the testimony; the other rejects it.
The text does not attribute these views to specific scholars, but they are present in the Hanafi tradition.
3
Analyze the proof for acceptance
The accepting view argues that debt is obligated in the dhimmah, which is capable of bearing multiple rights simultaneously. Hence, no partnership arises. A stranger can pay off one debtor without the other having a share.
Key: debt in the dhimmah is not tied to a specific asset.
4
Analyze the proof for rejection
The rejecting view argues that upon death, the debt attaches to the estate, and the dhimmah becomes empty. So if one creditor collects from the estate, the other creditors share in that collected amount, creating a common interest. Thus, testimony establishes a partnership.
Key: after death, debt becomes a claim on the estate assets.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is whether the testimony is admitted or not. If admitted, both witnesses can enforce the debt; if rejected, neither can use this testimony.
The dispute affects how the estate is distributed.
