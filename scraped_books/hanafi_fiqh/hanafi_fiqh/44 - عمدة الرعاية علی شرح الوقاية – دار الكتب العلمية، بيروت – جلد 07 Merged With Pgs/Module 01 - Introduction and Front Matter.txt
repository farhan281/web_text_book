The Multi-Layered Structure of Hanafi Fiqh Texts: An Introduction to 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah
Describing the multi-layered structure of the book.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four layers of this edition (matn, sharh, super-commentary, footnotes) and explain the role of each in the Hanafi textual tradition.

Core Idea
A single page can contain matn, sharh, super-commentary, and footnotes — the Hanafi tradition is lived through its layered texts.

This passage introduces the edition of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a super-commentary on Sharh al-Wiqayah. Understanding this layered structure is essential for navigating Hanafi fiqh literature. The front matter shows how the publisher arranged the matn, its classical commentary, a later super-commentary, and modern footnotes on the same page.

Guided Path
1
Identify the top layer: Wiqayat al-Riwayah (matn)
The matn is the core text by Burhan al-Shari'ah, giving concise rulings. It is placed at the top of each page.
This is the most authoritative layer.
2
Identify the second layer: Sharh al-Wiqayah (commentary)
Below the matn is the classical commentary by Sadr al-Shari'ah, explaining the matn's wording and implications.
It directly depends on the matn.
3
Identify the third layer: 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah (super-commentary)
This is the super-commentary by Imam Abdul-Hayy al-Laknawi, which further analyzes the sharh and sometimes raises objections.
It is the main text of this edition.
4
Identify the bottom layer: Ghayat al-'Inayah (footnotes)
At the bottom of the page, the editor Dr. Salah Muhammad Abu al-Hajj adds modern footnotes providing references and clarifications.
These are contemporary and supplementary.
5
Understand the coverage of this volume
This part covers specific fiqh chapters (e.g., al-Mukatab, al-Ghasb, al-Jinayat). Knowing this helps locate topics within the multi-volume set.
Refer to the list in the passage.

Commentary
This passage is the front matter of the book 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah 'ala Sharh al-Wiqayah, a super-commentary on the Hanafi fiqh text Sharh al-Wiqayah. The author is Imam Abdul-Hayy al-Laknawi, a prominent Hanafi scholar. The work is structured in a multi-layered format: at the top of each page is the original matn (Wiqayat al-Riwayah) by Burhan al-Shari'ah Mahmud ibn Ahmad al-Mahbubi. Below it is the sharh (commentary) known as Sharh al-Wiqayah by Sadr al-Shari'ah Abdullah ibn Mas'ud. Then comes 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, the super-commentary by al-Laknawi. At the bottom of the pages, the editor Dr. Salah Muhammad Abu al-Hajj has added footnotes called Ghayat al-'Inayah. This volume (Part Seven) covers specific chapters of Hanafi fiqh, including those on mukatab (slave who contracts for freedom), wala' (patronage), ikrah (duress), hajr (legal interdiction), ma'dhun (permitted slave), ghasb (usurpation), shuf'ah (right of preemption), qismah (division), muzara'ah (sharecropping), musaqah (irrigation contract), udhiyah (sacrificial animal), dhaba'ih (slaughtering), karahiyah (disliked matters), ihya' al-mawat (reviving dead land), ashribah (beverages), sayd (hunting), ruhun (pledges), jinayat (homicide and bodily harm), diyat (blood money), ma'aqil (responsibility of blood money), wasaya (testaments), and khuntha (hermaphrodite). This layering allows the reader to access the primary text, its classical commentary, a later super-commentary, and modern footnotes all at once. The present edition is published by Dar al-Kutub al-'Ilmiyyah, Beirut, in 7 volumes, first edition 2009.

Key Terms
وقاية الرواية (Wiqayat al-Riwayah): The matn (core text) by Burhan al-Shari'ah Mahmud ibn Ahmad al-Mahbubi, a foundational Hanafi text on fiqh.
شرح الوقاية (Sharh al-Wiqayah): The commentary on Wiqayat al-Riwayah by Sadr al-Shari'ah Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, explaining the matn.
عمدة الرعاية ('Umdat al-Ri'ayah): The super-commentary by Imam Abdul-Hayy al-Laknawi on Sharh al-Wiqayah, providing further elaboration and analysis.
غاية العناية (Ghayat al-'Inayah): The footnotes and commentary on 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah by Dr. Salah Muhammad Abu al-Hajj, placed in the margins of the current edition.

The Contract of Kitabah: Definition and Legal Ruling
Definition and basic rulings of kitabah contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of kitabah, identify its pillar, cause, and conditions, and explain its legal effect (hukm).

Core Idea
Kitabah grants immediate freedom of the hand while the neck remains enslaved until payment.

The contract of kitabah is a unique arrangement in Islamic law that allows a slave to earn his freedom by paying an agreed sum over time. This lesson unpacks its definition, structural elements, and the resulting change in the slave's legal status. Understanding this contract clarifies the nuanced intermediate status of the mukatab slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Locate what is being defined (kitabah) and note that it is a contract ('aqd) with specific elements.
Contracts in Hanafi fiqh have rukn, sabab, shart, and hukm.
2
Analyze the definition
Break down the definition: contract between master and slave, using the word 'kitabah', resulting in immediate freedom of hand and eventual freedom of neck.
Notice the dual effect: yadan (hand) vs. raqabatan (neck).
3
Extract the structural elements
From the text, list the rukn (offer and acceptance), sabab (attachment to continuation and capability), and shurut (slavery of subject, known payment).
These are the conditions for validity.
4
State the ruling (hukm)
The hukm is that the slave becomes more entitled to his own person and earnings, and the master loses authority over him.
This is the immediate consequence of a valid kitabah.
5
Distinguish from ijarah
Note why kitabah is placed after ijarah: both involve consideration for something not property, but kitabah aims at eventual freedom.
This comparison aids memory and classification.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the Book of Kitabah (contract of manumission by payment) from the commentary 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah'. The commentator first explains why the author of the matn placed the contract of kitabah after the contract of ijarah (hire): both contracts involve obtaining wealth in exchange for something that is not property, requiring explicit mention of consideration via offer and acceptance. He cites 'al-Nihayah' for this point. Then he defines kitabah: it is a contract between a master and his slave using the word 'kitabah' or its equivalent, resulting in the slave being free in his hand (i.e., able to earn and trade) immediately, while his neck (i.e., full freedom) becomes free upon payment of the agreed sum. It is called kitabah because of the written document involved. The pillar (rukn) is offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul). The cause (sabab) is stated as 'attachment to continuation and capability' (possibly meaning the ongoing earning ability of the slave). The condition (shart) is that the subject must be a slave and the payment must be known in amount and type. The ruling (hukm) is that the slave becomes more entitled to his own person and his earnings, so the master no longer has any authority over him or his acquisitions. This is according to glosses on 'al-Hidayah'. The passage ends with a reference to 'al-Inayah' and 'al-Kifayah'. This text provides the foundational definitions for understanding the Hanafi ruling on the contract of kitabah.

Key Terms
الكتابة (al-kitabah): A contract between master and slave whereby the slave earns his freedom by paying an agreed sum over time, with immediate freedom of the hand (earning) and eventual freedom of the neck upon payment.
المكاتب (al-mukatab): The slave who has entered into a contract of kitabah with his master, thereby becoming entitled to his own earnings and eventual freedom upon payment.
عقد (aqd): A legal contract consisting of offer and acceptance, here specifically for kitabah.

The 'Aqila: Who Bears Blood Money and How Much
Explains who constitutes the 'aqila and what amounts they bear.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for 'aqila membership and liability, and explain the dispute with al-Shafi'i regarding the killer's contribution and the validity of mawla al-muwalat.

Core Idea
The killer is counted among the 'aqila and pays a share; the threshold is a wound exposing bone (mudihah).

This passage from Sharh al-Wiqayah clarifies who exactly is responsible for paying blood money (diyah) in cases of accidental killing or injury. It also sets the minimum amount that triggers 'aqila liability and addresses two key disputes with Imam al-Shafi'i: whether the killer contributes and whether clients by mutual alliance (mawla al-muwalat) are valid members of the 'aqila.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the 'aqila bears: accidental killings and injuries at or above the level of arsh al-mudihah. The hukm is wajib (obligatory) payment.
The threshold is set by the hadith below.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the proof: the hadith of Ibn Abbas (marfu') listing what the 'aqila does not bear, which implies what it does bear.
Hadith is ahad, transmitted by multiple compilers.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
From the hadith's negation (la ta'qil) of five categories, the 'aqila bears what is not excluded: accidental killing (not intentional), free persons (not slaves), and injuries at or above arsh al-mudihah.
The negation gives a clear implication (dalil al-khitab).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i holds the killer pays nothing (qiyas: part follows whole because the killer is excused) and rejects mawla al-muwalat. The Hanafi response is that imposing the full amount on the killer is oppressive but a partial share is not.
Another dispute: mawla al-muwalat is valid in Hanafi but invalid in Shafi'i because it bypasses the public treasury.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position—killer contributes, mawla al-muwalat valid—is mufta bihi, supported by sound reasoning and practice (istihsan). Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah endorses it.
This is the relied-upon view for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh al-Wiqayah explains the rules of the 'aqila (the group responsible for paying blood money in cases of accidental killing or injury). It opens with a ruling statement: if the immediate tribe (al-hayy) does not have enough members to pay the required diyah, they add the nearest relatives by paternal lineage, like in inheritance 'asabat. The killer himself, according to Abu Hanifa, is included as one of the payers, unlike the Shafi'i view that the killer pays nothing because he is excused; the Hanafi response is that requiring a full share from the killer would be oppressive, but a portion is not. Next, for a freed slave, his 'aqila is the tribe of his former master. For a client by mutual alliance (mawla al-muwalat), his 'aqila is his master's tribe, but Imam al-Shafi'i rejects this institution altogether as it undermines the public treasury. Finally, the 'aqila only bears the amount that is obligatory due to the killing itself (i.e., accidental or quasi-intentional) and injuries at or above the arsh of the mudihah (a head wound exposing bone, which is one-twentieth of full diyah). The proof is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: the 'aqila does not pay for intentional killing, slave, settlement, confession, or injuries less than mudihah. Thus the threshold is set at half a tenth of the full diyah.

Key Terms
العاقلة (al-‘āqilah): The group of agnatic relatives and allies who bear the blood money (diyah) for accidental killings and injuries.
الموضحة (al-mūḍiḥah): A head wound that exposes the bone; its arsh (compensation) is a fixed amount (half a tenth of full diyah).
مولى الموالاة (mawlā al-muwālāh): A client by mutual alliance, a non-Arab who enters into a pact of protection and inheritance with an Arab tribe.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A bibliography entry reveals the genre and hierarchy of a Hanafi source through its title and publication details.

This lesson focuses on the bibliography section of 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah, a Hanafi fiqh commentary. Bibliographies are not mere lists; they map the scholarly chain of transmission. By learning to read these entries, you will gain insight into which texts are primary (matn), which are commentaries (sharh), and which are super-commentaries (hasiyah). Guided Path
1
Identify the entry number and author
Each entry begins with a number (e.g., 197) and the title of the work followed by the author's name and date of death. Note the author's full name and death year in AH.

2
Classify the work by genre

Entries with 'Hashiyah' indicate a critical gloss on an earlier work.
3
Check the publication details
Note the publisher, edition number, and year. 'Matba'ah al-Khayriyyah' indicates a specific press. This helps assess the availability and reliability of the edition.
Older editions may have marginal texts that are themselves valuable.
4
Assess the scholarly chain
Some entries mention supervisors, editors, or thesis details (e.g., Master's thesis). This shows the contemporary academic engagement with the text.
Entries from manuscripts (e.g., 'from manuscripts of the Iraqi Ministry of Endowments') indicate rare sources.

Key Terms
حاشية (hāshiyah): A super-commentary or gloss on an earlier work, often a commentary (sharh).
شرح (sharḥ): A commentary that explains a matn (original text).
متن (matn): The original, concise text that forms the basis for commentary.

Reading the Classical Hanafi Bibliography (Fihris al-Maraji')
List of books and authors cited in the work

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key components of a classical Hanafi bibliography entry and explain its role in verifying the sources used in a fiqh work.

Core Idea
A Hanafi faqih's authority rests on the chain of books he cites — the bibliography is the root of that chain.

This passage presents the index of references ('fihris al-maraji') from 'Umda al-Ri'aya 'ala Sharh al-Wiqaya', a major Hanafi commentary. It lists 35 works, providing titles, authors, death dates, editors, and publication details. Understanding how to read such a bibliography is essential for tracing the origins of rulings and gauging the scholarly network behind a text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of each entry
Each entry includes: entry number, book title, author name with death date (e.g., 't 273 AH'), editor (if any), publisher, and publication details (place, year). Note the use of parentheses and abbreviations.
Example: Entry 318: 'Sunan Ibn Majah' by Muhammad bin Yazid (d. 273 AH), edited by Muhammad Fu'ad Abd al-Baqi, Dar al-Fikr, Beirut.
2
Recognize the types of works cited
Works range from hadith collections (Sunan, e.g., Sunan Abi Dawud), Hanafi fiqh commentaries (Sharh, e.g., Sharh al-Jami' al-Saghir), treatises on usul, and theological works. Classifying each helps understand the source's nature.
Entries 318-324 are all hadith collections. Entries 337-344 are commentaries on Hanafi texts.
3
Understand the significance of death dates
The death date (e.g., 't 273 AH') places the author in historical context, showing the depth of the scholarly chain. Earlier dates indicate primary sources; later dates indicate secondary commentaries.
For example, Sa'id bin Mansur (d. 227 AH) is earlier than al-Daraqutni (d. 385 AH), so his Sunan is an earlier collection.
4
Note geographical spread of publishers
Publishers span Damascus, Beirut, Cairo, Baghdad, Mecca, Riyadh, Lahore, reflecting the global nature of Hanafi scholarship. This helps identify the scholarly network and the work's circulation.
Entry 313 was printed in Lahore (Pakistan) in 1976, showing modern interest in lithographic reprints.
5
Appreciate the mix of early and later sources
The bibliography includes early works (e.g., Sunan Ibn Majah, 3rd century AH) and later commentaries (e.g., Sharh Ibn 'Aqil, 8th century AH). This shows the author's thoroughness and reliance on primary and secondary sources.
This mix is typical for a comprehensive commentary like 'Umda al-Ri'aya.

Key Terms
فهرس المراجع (fihris al-marāji‘): Index of references or bibliography listing sources cited in a work.
سنن (sunan): Hadith collections arranged by legal topic, as opposed to musnad or mu'jam. Often refers to works by Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, Nasa'i, etc.
شرح (sharḥ): Commentary or exposition on a base text (matn).
ت (t. (tūfiya)): Abbreviation for death date (توفي). Followed by the Hijri year.

Commentary
This passage is a bibliography listing the references used in the book 'Umda al-Ri'aya 'ala Sharh al-Wiqaya'. It includes a wide range of classical and later Hanafi sources as well as works from other madhhabs, indicating the scholarly reach of the commentary. The list includes primary matn texts (e.g., Matn al-Quduri), major commentaries (al-Mabsut, al-Muhit al-Burhani), and manuals (Mukhtar, Maraqi al-Falah). It also shows the use of modern editions and academic theses. Each entry typically gives the author, title, editor, publisher, and date. The presence of both Hanafi and non-Hanafi works demonstrates the comparative nature of the commentary.

Key Terms
متن (matn): A concise textbook of fiqh, typically memorized by students.
شَرْح (sharh): A commentary that explains and expands upon a matn.
نوازل (nawazil): Legal cases and responses to actual events, a genre of fiqh.
الفقه المقارن (al-fiqh al-muqaran): Comparative fiqh, the study of rulings across different madhhabs.
