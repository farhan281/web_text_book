Cutting a Finger Causing Paralysis: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qiṣāṣ for cut causing paralysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on qiṣāṣ when cutting one finger causes paralysis of another, and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view.

Core Idea
When cutting one finger paralyzes another, Abu Hanifa says no qiṣāṣ—only arsh—because exact equivalence is lost.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about retaliation (qiṣāṣ) when a single act of cutting one finger unexpectedly causes paralysis of an adjacent finger. The question is whether qiṣāṣ applies to the cut finger while the paralyzed finger receives only blood money (arsh), or whether both are compensated by arsh. Understanding the rationale behind each position deepens your grasp of the Hanafi principle that qiṣāṣ requires exact equivalence (mumathala).

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
A man cuts another's finger, and as a result an adjacent finger becomes paralyzed without being directly cut. What compensation is due?
2
State the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa rules: arsh for both fingers, no qiṣāṣ. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule: qiṣāṣ for the cut finger, arsh for the paralyzed finger.
The Sahibayn distinguish between the direct injury (cut) and the indirect injury (paralysis).
3
Trace the underlying rationale
Qiṣāṣ requires exact equivalence (mumathala) in the type and location of injury. Since paralysis is not a cut, equivalence is lost for the second finger; Abu Hanifa extends this loss to both fingers because the act is single. The Sahibayn see the cut as directly inflicted and thus eligible for qiṣāṣ despite the secondary paralysis.
The key difference lies in how far the condition of mumathala extends.
4
Identify the tarjih (preponderance)
Later authorities (al-Ispijabi, al-Burhani, al-Nasafi) adopt Abu Hanifa's view as correct and mufta bihi. This settles the operative ruling for fatwa.
The tarjih is based on the stronger adherence to the principle of exact equivalence.
5
Apply the mufta bihi to a new scenario
Suppose someone cuts a finger and causes paralysis of a non-adjacent finger (e.g., due to nerve damage). According to the relied-upon view, the ruling would likely be same: arsh for both, since the paralysis is still indirect.
This shows the generality of the principle: any indirect injury from a single act prevents qiṣāṣ for the entire incident.

Core Idea
Compensation falls when the loss is completely undone by natural regrowth.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the chapter of diyat (injuries): what happens when a pulled tooth grows back naturally? The Hanafi school holds that the victim's right to compensation (arsh) is dropped because the very purpose of compensation — restoring benefit and beauty — has been achieved. This principle underscores that liability is tied to actual, lasting loss.

2

3
Apply the ruling
When the condition is met, the arsh is dropped (sāqiṭ). No compensation is owed.
4

The reason given: the victim's right has been fully restored through the return of both benefit (function) and beauty (appearance). Compensation is only for loss; if loss is undone, liability ceases.
This is a case of qiyās: the ʿilla is the restoration of what was lost.
5
Consider the scope and limits
The ruling applies only when regrowth occurs. If the tooth does not regrow, or regrows but is defective, arsh remains due. Also, it applies to teeth only, not to other body parts.
Other body parts generally do not regrow; this ruling is specific to teeth.

If the wound heals completely, leaving no scar, no discoloration, and hair grows back normally, then the original state is fully restored. Since no permanent damage remains, the rationale for arsh – compensation for loss – disappears.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Master may either surrender the slave or pay immediate ransom for accidental injuries; the choice is binding and immediate.

This passage from al-Lubāb outlines the Hanafi ruling on a master's liability for accidental injuries caused by his slave. The master is given a choice: he can either hand over the slave to the victim, who then becomes the owner, or he can pay the blood money immediately. This option applies only to accidental injuries; intentional injuries follow different rules. Understanding this ruling clarifies the unique approach of Hanafi law in balancing the rights of the victim and the master.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a slave's accidental injury (jināyah khaṭa') upon a free person or another slave. The ruling gives the master a choice between two options.
2

The master may either hand over the slave (دفع) to the victim or ransom him (فداء) by paying the blood money (arsh) immediately.

In an accidental collision between two free persons, each 'aaqila pays full diyah because the death of each is attributed to the other's act; slaves are hadar, and intentional collision halves the liability.

Diya for Accidental Killing of a Slave: Capped Value and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on diya for accidental slave killing with qiyas reasoning and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on diya for accidental killing of a slave, explain the cap and symbolic deduction, and compare the views of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad with Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A slave's diya is his market value, but capped at the free person's diya minus a symbolic ten dirhams to show lower status.

When a slave is accidentally killed, the blood money is not a fixed amount like for a free person; it is based on the slave's value. However, the Hanafi school imposes a cap so that the slave's diya does not exceed that of a free person, and even deducts a small amount to symbolise the slave's lower rank. This ruling, however, is disputed within the school itself. Let's examine the reasoning and the difference of opinion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act? Accidental killing of a slave. What is the ruling? Liability for diya (blood money). Identify the basic rule: the killer must pay the slave's market value.
The base ruling is similar to property damage, but because the slave is human, there is a cap.
2
Locate the cap and its condition
Note the cap: the diya cannot exceed the diya of a free person. For a male slave, that is 10,000 dirhams; for a female, 5,000 dirhams. But there is a further deduction of ten dirhams. Understand why: to demonstrate degradation of status (inhitat al-rutbah).
The cap applies only if the slave's value equals or exceeds the free diya; otherwise, the lower value is paid.
3
Trace the reasoning (qiyas)
The text uses qiyas (analogy): the slave and free person share human qualities, so the slave's diya should not exceed the free person's. Since the free person has the added attribute of freedom, the slave's diya is even more deserving of being less. This is a jaliy (evident) analogy.
The deduction of ten is not part of the qiyas but is added to highlight the slave's lower status.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold the capped view. Abu Yusuf holds that the full value must be paid without cap. Identify the different basis: Abu Yusuf sees the slave as property, so full value is due.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) arises when the slave's value exceeds the free diya: Abu Yusuf's view would require more than 10,000 for a male, while the majority limits it to 9,990.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The majority view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is the relied-upon position for fatwa. The text gives it as the primary ruling.
Later Hanafi jurists have generally followed the majority view as more precautionary and consistent with the human dignity of the slave.

Commentary
This passage presents a key ruling: the diya for accidentally killing a slave is the slave's market value, but it is capped at the diya of a free person — for a male slave, at 10,000 dirhams (the full diya of a free man) minus a symbolic ten dirhams to indicate the slave's lower status.
