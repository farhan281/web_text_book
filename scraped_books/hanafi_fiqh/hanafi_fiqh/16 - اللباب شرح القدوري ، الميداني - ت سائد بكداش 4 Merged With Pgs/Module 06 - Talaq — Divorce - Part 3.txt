Raj'a: Valid Without Witnesses
States the ruling on raj'a validity and recommendation of witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on raj'a, explain why witnesses are not required, and apply the ruling to a dispute scenario.

Core Idea
Raj'a is valid without witnesses because it continues the existing marriage, not a new contract.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on raj'a (revocation of divorce). The passage from al-Hidayah clarifies that witnesses are not a condition for validity, but are recommended for precaution. We will explore the legal reasoning and the cross-madhhab difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage begins 'صحت الرجعة' – Raj'a is valid (sahih). Identify what is being ruled upon: the act of revoking a divorce during iddah.
Raj'a is not a new contract but a continuation of marriage.
2
Locate the dalil
The author says the proof is that raj'a is istidama (continuation) of the existing marriage, so witnesses are not required, analogous to fay' (return) in ila'.
This is a qiyas: the illa is the nature of continuation.
3
Understand the condition of denial
If the husband claims raj'a after iddah expires but the wife denies, the wife's word is taken (fariq: her say binds him).
This is because the burden of proof shifts to the one making the claim.
4
Learn the recommendation
Although witnesses are not a condition, they are mustahabb (recommended) for precaution to avoid later denial.
Also recommended: inform the wife so she does not commit sin.
5
Contrast with Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i requires two witnesses for raj'a; without them it is invalid. The Hanafi school disagrees.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that under Shafi'i, an unwitnessed raj'a would be void.

Dispute Over Raj'a Claims: Oath and ʿIddah Expiry
Intra-Hanafi dispute on raj'a: oath and iddah expiry.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the wife's oath when denying raj'a, apply the mufta bihi ruling, and explain the ikhtilaf regarding the validity of raj'a when the wife claims iddah has expired.

Core Idea
When a wife denies raj'a, the Hanafi fatwa requires her oath, per the Sahibayn, but Abu Hanifa exempts her; the ruling diverges when she claims iddah has expired.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn) concerning two interrelated issues: whether a wife must take an oath when denying a husband's claim of raj'a, and whether the raj'a is valid if the wife claims her iddah has already expired. Understanding the ikhtilaf and the mufta bihi is essential for application in marital disputes within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sub-issues
The passage deals with (1) whether the wife must take an oath when denying the husband's claim of raj'a, and (2) whether raj'a is valid if the wife says her iddah has expired and it is chronologically possible.
These are separate but related procedural and substantive questions.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position on each
Abu Hanifa holds that no oath is imposed on the wife (لا يمين عليها) because the husband claims a past event he cannot create now. In the scenario where the wife claims iddah expiry, Abu Hanifa rules the raj'a invalid (لم تصح الرجعة).
The rationale is that her word is taken as she is the one with current knowledge of her iddah.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the wife must take an oath (يمين) when denying. For the iddah-expiry scenario, they rule the raj'a valid (تصح).
The Sahibayn consider the husband's claim stronger and require the wife to swear to counter it.
4
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
On the oath issue, the fatwa is on the Sahibayn's view: the wife must take an oath. This is confirmed by al-Tashih and Qadikhan. The ruling on the iddah-expiry scenario is not explicitly given as mufta bihi in this text, so the ikhtilaf remains unresolved in this passage.
In practice, judges follow the Sahibayn's oath requirement.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
According to the fatwa, if the wife refuses to take an oath, she may be imprisoned until she swears or confesses. For the iddah-expiry scenario, if the judge adopts Abu Hanifa's view, the raj'a is invalid; if the Sahibayn's view, it is valid. The mufta bihi is not specified here for that scenario.
The dispute thus affects both procedure and substantive outcome.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The wife must take an oath in revocation disputes — the mufta bihi follows Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, not Abu Hanifa.

Revocation (رجعة) is a sensitive area of family law where claims and denials can easily lead to conflict. This lesson examines a specific procedural dispute within the Hanafi school: whether a woman must swear an oath when her husband claims he has revoked her and she denies it. The matn presents Abu Hanifa's view that no oath is required, but the later fatwa tradition gives precedence to the two companions, who require the oath. Understanding this tarjih is essential for any mufti dealing with revocation cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two Hanafi positions
Clearly state the question: when a husband claims revocation and the wife denies, must she take an oath? Then list Abu Hanifa's position (no oath) and the Sahibayn's position (oath required).
The text explicitly marks this as one of the six known istihlaf issues.
2
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)

This directly affects the outcome of the case.
3
Cite the tarjih authorities
Identify the later scholars who preferred the Sahibayn's view: al-Tashih, Imam Qadikhan, and al-Sadidi al-Zawzani. Note that al-Zawzani explicitly states he acted on this view in Rayy and Isfahan.
These are fatawa mutakhkhirin authorities, not the earlier Zahir al-Riwayah.
4

Core Idea
If the wife's claim of iddah expiry is plausible, Abu Hanifa invalidates the revocation; the Sahibayn uphold it.

Guided Path

Identify the scenario

The text says 'wal-iddatu tahtamil' – meaning that from the timing it is possible that the iddah ended. If it were impossible, the husband's word might prevail.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule the revocation valid. They consider the husband's declaration of revocation as an act he controls, and the uncertainty about iddah does not nullify it.
5
Determine the practical difference
If the wife is believed, the marriage is not reinstated and she is free to remarry. If the husband is believed, the revocation stands and the marriage continues.
The text does not give a tarjih; later Hanafi works may adopt one view depending on context.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri is an intra-Hanafi dispute about when the right to revoke (رجعة) ends upon completion of the 'iddah (waiting period). The text first states the general rule: if the woman's menstrual blood stops at exactly ten days (the maximum duration of menstruation according to the Hanafi school), the 'iddah ends and the right to revoke ceases immediately, even if she has not performed ghusl. However, if the blood stops before ten days, the cessation is not definitive because blood may return within the menstrual period. Therefore, the right to revoke continues until either she performs ghusl (purificatory bath) or a complete prayer time passes over her, which makes prayer obligatory upon her as a pure person. This establishes certainty that the menstruation has ended.

The text then presents a dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on one side, and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the other, concerning the status of tayammum (dry ablution) in this context. The issue: If a woman performs tayammum due to the unavailability of water for ghusl, does that suffice to end the 'iddah and terminate the right to revoke? Muhammad argues that tayammum is an absolute purification (طهارة مطلقة) when water is absent; it entitles one to certain acts like touching the mushaf, so it is analogous to ghusl. Therefore, performing tayammum alone should be sufficient, even without praying. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf respond that tayammum is not a true purification; it is only permitted out of necessity (ضرورة) to avoid missing obligations, and that necessity only arises at the actual time of prayer. Thus, tayammum must be followed by prayer (even supererogatory) for the 'iddah to end. They call this istihsan (preference), while Muhammad's view is based on qiyas (analogy).

The passage concludes that the correct (صحيح) position according to later Hanafi authorities (like al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, and Sadr al-Shari'ah) is the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. In ghusl, forgetting less than a limb cuts off raj'a by istihsan; forgetting a full limb preserves it.

A revocably divorced woman should beautify herself because the marriage tie remains and taking back is recommended.

This lesson examines the ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained by al-Maydani, regarding the conduct of a revocably divorced woman. Understanding this ruling clarifies the school's view on the ongoing relationship between the spouses.

Identify the act and its ruling

Locate the dalil (proof)
Identify the evidence that supports this recommendation. The text does not cite a verse or hadith but provides a talil (causation): 'because the marriage tie remains (al-zawjiyyah qa'imah) and taking back (raj'ah) is recommended, and beautification encourages it.' 3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof yields the ruling)
Understand the logical connection. Since the marriage is ongoing (even after revocable divorce), the wife's obligation to please her husband continues. The illa (cause) is the persistence of marriage.

Irrevocable divorce includes the third talaq or talaq before consummation.
5

The practical outcome: a revocably divorced woman living in the same household should present herself attractively to her husband. She should not avoid him or neglect her appearance. This facilitates reconciliation and avoids the extension of the iddah.

The Husband's Announcement Before Entry
Ruling on husband's announcement before entry

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi recommendation for a husband to announce his entry to a revocably divorced woman and explain the legal reasoning (hikmah) to avoid accidental revocation.

Core Idea
A husband should announce his entry to a revocably divorced woman to avoid accidentally seeing her unclothed and triggering an unwanted revocation.

This lesson continues from the previous one, focusing on the husband's conduct. While the wife is encouraged to beautify herself, the husband is simultaneously recommended to announce his presence before entering her room. The wisdom behind this is to prevent an unintended reconciliation if he sees her in a state of undress, which could lead to a renewed divorce and prolonged iddah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the husband is recommended to do. The act is: he should not enter upon her unannounced; instead, he should announce his entry by clearing his throat or making a sound. The hukm is mustahabb (recommended).
The text says 'yustahabb lahu an la yadkhula... hatta yu'dhinaha'.
2
Locate the wisdom (hikmah) behind the ruling
The commentary provides the reason: 'because she might be unclothed and his sight might fall on a place that would cause him to take her back.' In Hanafi fiqh, if the husband sees any part of the woman that arouses desire, it may constitute a raj'ah by action.
This is not a dalil from text but a practical consideration.
3
Understand the consequence of accidental revocation
If the husband sees her in a way that triggers raj'ah, the marriage is reinstated. If he then divorces her again, the iddah restarts, prolonging the situation. The announcement prevents this unwanted legal effect.
The iddah becomes longer, harming the woman.
4
Note the condition: if he intends to take her back
The ruling is especially important if he does not intend to take her back. If he does intend raj'ah, entering and seeing her might still trigger automatic revocation, so announcement is still prudent.
The text says 'in lam yakun qasdahu al-muraja'ah'.
5
Relate to the wife's beautification
Both rulings complement each other: the wife beautifies to encourage reconciliation; the husband announces to avoid accidental reconciliation. Together they foster a controlled environment for the iddah.
The rulings are not contradictory but balanced.

The Ruling on Tahlil and the Internal Hanafi Dispute
This passage presents Hanafi rulings on tahlil and the dispute among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on tahlil, identify the internal dispute among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad, and evaluate which view is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A marriage with the condition of tahlil is valid but makruh; yet the internal Hanafi dispute questions whether it achieves its purpose.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, with the sharh of al-Maydani, addresses a controversial topic in Hanafi fiqh: the validity and effect of a marriage entered with the explicit condition of tahlil (making a thrice-divorced woman permissible for her first husband). While the basic ruling states that the marriage is valid but prohibitively disliked, the Hanafi imams themselves disagreed over whether such a marriage actually achieves tahlil. This dispute has practical consequences for couples seeking to remarry after a permanent divorce.

Guided Path
1
State the basic Hanafi position on tahlil
Identify the ruling from the passage: a marriage with an explicit condition of tahlil is valid (sahih) but makruh tahrimi due to the hadith curse. If consummation occurs and divorce follows, the woman becomes halal for her first husband.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Zufar, which is the mufta bihi.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Note that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagreed. Abu Yusuf held the marriage is fasid (corrupt) and tahlil is not achieved. Muhammad held the marriage is valid but tahlil is also not achieved.
The ikhtilaf is only when the condition is verbal. If the condition is only in the heart, all agreed tahlil is achieved.
3
Examine the proofs of each position
Trace the dalil: the hadith curses the muhallil and the one for whom tahlil is done, but that does not nullify the marriage. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad each interpreted the effect of the condition differently.
The hadith is the primary dalil for all, but its implicature (dalala) is zanni.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Zufar argued that the curse does not void the contract; the condition is void but does not affect the marriage's validity. The marriage remains valid, so tahlil is achieved.
This is the tarjih supported by later authorities.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical consequence: after consummation and divorce, does the woman become halal for the first husband? According to Abu Hanifa and Zufar: yes. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: no. The relied-upon position is the former, as affirmed by al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, and Sadr al-Shari'ah.
The fatwa is that the marriage is valid, makruh, and tahlil is achieved.

The Husband's Intention of Lie or Divorce When Saying 'Haram'
Rulings on the husband's intention when saying 'haram' – part one: lie and divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband says 'haram' to his wife and claims he intended a lie or divorce, and distinguish between divine belief and judicial treatment.

Core Idea
If the husband claims a lie, he is believed before Allah but not in court; if he intends divorce, it is a single irrevocable divorce unless he intends three.

The word 'haram' is legally ambiguous. Its effect hinges on the husband's intention. This lesson covers two intentions: a lie (meaning no legal consequence) and divorce. The Hanafi school has a nuanced distinction between divine and judicial belief, and the ruling on divorce follows the principles of kinayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the uttered phrase and its category
The husband says 'haram' or similar. Recognize that this is a kinayah (indirect expression) that requires intention to produce a legal effect.
Kinayah is contrasted with sarih (explicit) words. For divorce, intention is necessary with kinayah.
2
Analyze the claim of lying
If the husband says 'I intended a lie,' the text says 'it is as he said.' Understand this as a divine belief. In court, however, the later authorities (including al-Sarakhsi) rule he is not believed and it is treated as an oath.
This is a shift from zahir al-riwaya to the operative fatwa.
3
Analyze the claim of divorce
If he says 'I intended divorce,' it becomes a single irrevocable divorce (talaq ba'in). If he intended three, it is three. This follows the general rule for kinayat.
The divorce is ba'in because the word is not an explicit divorce formula.
4
Distinguish between divine and judicial realms
The husband's claim of lying is accepted between him and Allah, but not in court. The court treats the utterance as an oath (yamin) unless the husband can prove a different intention through evidence.
This distinction is critical for fatwa and judicial practice.

The Husband's Intention of Zihar or Tahrim When Saying 'Haram'

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that 'haram' intended as zihar is zihar (per Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and that intending prohibition or nothing results in an oath (yamin), with an exception based on custom.

Core Idea
If the husband intends zihar, it is zihar despite Muhammad's objection; if he intends prohibition or nothing, it is an oath making him a muli, but custom may turn it into divorce.

The word 'haram' can also be intended as zihar (likening the wife to a prohibited relative) or as a general prohibition. The Hanafi school rules that intending prohibition or nothing leads to an oath (yamin) and thus ila. However, if he intends zihar, an intra-Hanafi dispute arises: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule it is zihar, while Muhammad disagrees. This lesson covers that dispute and also mentions the role of custom in turning tahrim into divorce.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario: husband says 'haram' intending zihar
The husband utters 'haram' and later says he intended zihar. Identify the ruling: according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, it is zihar. Muhammad disagrees.
Zihar requires expiation (kaffarah) before resuming marital relations.
2
Analyze the two arguments
The two (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) argue that 'haram' implies absolute prohibition, and zihar involves a prohibition, so the absolute may carry the restricted meaning. Muhammad argues that zihar requires explicit comparison (tashbih) with a prohibited woman, which is absent.
The dispute hinges on whether the word 'haram' includes the concept of zihar by implication.
3
Note the tarjih
Al-Isbijabi, al-Mahbubi, and al-Nasafi adopt the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. This is the correct and mufta bihi view.
Thus, if the husband intends zihar, it is zihar.

Analyze the scenario: intending prohibition (tahrim) or nothing
If the husband says 'I intended prohibition' or 'I intended nothing,' the default is that it is an oath (yamin). This is because the original ruling for making lawful things prohibited is an oath. He becomes a muli (one who makes ila).
If he says 'I intended nothing,' his claim is not believed in court; the apparent meaning is an oath.
5
Consider the effect of custom (urf)
Some later scholars (mentioned in al-Hidaya) turn the word 'tahrim' into divorce based on custom, without requirement of intention. Al-Mahbubi says fatwa is given by this. This is a separate development.
This shows the dynamic nature of Hanafi fiqh, where custom can influence ta'lil.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hanafi school on khul' (divorce initiated by the wife through compensation). The author begins with a linguistic and legal definition: khul' linguistically means removal, and in law it is the removal of the marital bond, conditional on the wife's acceptance using the word khul' or its equivalent. The core ruling is that khul' is permissible (la ba'sa) when the spouses fear they cannot uphold the limits of Allah, i.e., fulfill marital obligations. The proof is the Quranic verse from Surah al-Baqarah (2:229): 'There is no sin upon them in what she ransoms herself with.' The legal consequence is that a single irrevocable divorce (talaq ba'in) occurs upon acceptance, and the wife must pay the agreed compensation. The author then discusses the effect of nushuz (discord/disobedience): if the discord comes from the husband, it is disliked (makruh) for him to take any compensation; if it comes from the wife, he may take compensation but it is disliked to take more than the mahr he gave her. The footnote cited from Ibn 'Abidin strengthens this: if the husband is at fault, taking compensation is actually unlawful (haram). A narration from al-Jami' al-Saghir permits the husband to take even more than the mahr, but Ibn 'Abidin preponderates the view that it is better not to take more. The text thus presents the relied-upon Hanafi position: khul' is valid as a single irrevocable divorce, and the conditions around compensation are carefully regulated to prevent injustice.

النشوز (al-nushuz): Discord, disobedience, or aversion between spouses. The text defines it as 'النفرة والجفاء' (aversion and distance).
تطليقة بائنة (talaq ba'in): An irrevocable divorce that severs the marital bond immediately, requiring a new marriage contract (nikah) to reunite.

Core Idea
In khul', compensation is disliked if husband caused discord; taking more than dower is disliked if wife at fault.

This passage from al-Quduri explains the rules of compensation in khul' divorce, focusing on fault. The general rule is that khul' results in an irrevocable divorce and the wife pays the agreed amount. However, the husband's right to take compensation depends on who caused marital discord. The text presents a nuanced view with a distinction between the ruling in court and religious preference.

Divorce Upon Payment: Valid and Irrevocable
Explains the ruling of divorce upon payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of talaq 'ala mal and explain why the divorce is ba'in.

Core Idea
Divorce upon payment is valid and irrevocable (ba'in) because the wife pays to secure her freedom.

When a husband says 'you are divorced for one thousand dirhams' and the wife accepts, the divorce takes effect immediately. This lesson examines the legal reasoning behind this ruling and why the divorce is ba'in rather than raj'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize the scenario: the husband pronounces divorce conditional on payment, the wife accepts. The ruling is that the divorce occurs (valid) and is ba'in.
2
Understand the conditions
The husband must make the offer (e.g., 'you are divorced for X') and the wife must accept in the same session (al-majlis). Without acceptance, no divorce or payment.
Acceptance must be in the same session per Hanafi contract law.
3
Analyse why the divorce is ba'in
The divorce is ba'in because the wife pays to free herself; that freedom is only achieved by irrevocable divorce, not revocable (raj'i). The husband cannot take her back without a new marriage.
Had it been raj'i, the wife would not be fully free.

Khul' Compensation: The Plural 'Dirhams' Binds to Three
Ruling on khul' compensation when the wife uses a plural noun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a plural noun in khul' compensation obligates a minimum of three dirhams, even if the wife possessed less.

Core Idea
When a wife offers 'dirhams' in khul', the plural binds her to at least three dirhams.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in khul' where the wife uses the plural word 'dirhams' to describe the compensation. The Hanafi school, relying on linguistic principles, rules that the plural obligates a minimum of three dirhams, even if she had nothing or less than that amount. Understanding this rule clarifies how contractual language is interpreted in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a khul' divorce: the wife offers compensation for her release. The ruling in the text is that the khul' is valid (sahih).
The validity is not questioned; the focus is on the compensation amount.
2
Analyze the condition in the wife's offer
She says 'on what is in my hand of dirhams.' The key is that she used the plural 'dirhams' (دراهم) without specifying a number.
In Arabic, the broken plural form is used for three or more (non-human).
3
Apply the Hanafi linguistic rule on jam'
The Hanafi usul principle: the absolute indefinite plural (jamʿ munkar) indicates at least three unless there is a qualifying factor (qarīna). Here, no qualification is present.
This is a standard linguistic interpretation in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Determine the consequence
Since she used the plural but had less than three (or nothing), she must pay three dirhams—the minimum implied by her word.
Even if she had one or two dirhams, she still owes three because the offer was for a plural quantity.

Commentary
Here the wife specifies 'dirhams' (plural). In Arabic grammar, the plural form is used for three or more. Since she offered 'what is in my hand of dirhams' as compensation, and it turns out she has none (or less than three), the minimum number implied by the plural is three. Therefore, she must pay three dirhams to the husband. The commentator adds that even if she had one or two dirhams, she would still owe three because the offer was for a plural quantity. This ruling is based on linguistic principles that the plural denotes at least three.

Key Terms
جمع (jam'): Plural form in Arabic, which for non-human plurals indicates three or more.
خالعني (khāliʿnī): Imperative form of khul': 'Divorce me (by khul')'. The wife requests divorce in exchange for compensation.
دراهم (darāhim): Plural of dirham, a silver coin used as currency. In this context, used as a generic term for 'money'.
