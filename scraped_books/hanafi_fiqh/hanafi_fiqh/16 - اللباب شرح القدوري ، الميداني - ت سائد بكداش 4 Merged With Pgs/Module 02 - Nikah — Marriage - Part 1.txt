Hanafi Definition of Nikah and Its Contract Formation
Defines nikah linguistically and legally, states condition for its conclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of nikah and identify the required expressions for its valid formation.

Core Idea
Nikah is a contract granting ownership of pleasure; it requires past-tense offer and acceptance.

In this passage from the commentary on al-Quduri, the author begins the Book of Marriage by defining nikah both linguistically and legally, then explains the essential form for concluding the contract. Understanding this definition is foundational for all later rules governing marriage in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic definition
Find the phrase that defines nikah linguistically as 'joining and gathering' and note the authorities cited.
The author follows the choice of al-Muhit and al-Kafi.
2
Extract the legal definition
Locate the legal (shar'i) definition: a contract that benefits ownership of pleasure (mut'ah) with intent. Note that this focuses on the contractual aspect.
Other schools may include elements like procreation, but Hanafi emphasizes the contract of enjoyment.
3
Identify the formation requirement
The passage states that the contract is concluded with offer (ijab) from one party and acceptance (qabul) from the other.
Both parties can initiate the offer; it is not exclusive to the man.
4
Analyze the tense condition
The expressions must be in the past tense (like 'zawwajtuka' and 'tazawwajtu'). Understand why: although these words are originally declarative, they are given performative (insha') force by Shari'ah to meet the need for clarity.
This is a distinct Hanafi position; other schools may allow present or future tense.

Conditions of Witnesses for a Valid Nikah
States mandatory witness conditions for nikah validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six conditions required for witnesses in a valid Hanafi marriage and identify which combinations of witnesses render the marriage invalid.

Core Idea
A valid marriage requires two qualified witnesses: free, adult, sane, Muslim, hearing together.

The presence of witnesses is a fundamental condition for the validity of a Muslim marriage in the Hanafi school. This lesson breaks down the six specific qualifications required of those witnesses and explains why the absence of any one of them renders the marriage contract void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the marriage contract (nikah). The ruling is that it is not valid (la yan'aqid) without two witnesses meeting defined conditions.
The text begins 'ولا ينعقد نكاح المسلمين' — the marriage does not contract.
2
List the six conditions
From the text: witnesses must be (1) free, (2) adult, (3) sane, (4) Muslim, (5) two males or one male and two females, and (6) must hear and understand the offer and acceptance together.
The commentary adds that hearing together (sami'an ma'an) and understanding (fahiman) are required per the relied-upon position.
3
Classify each condition
Each condition is a shart (condition) for validity, not a rukn (pillar). The absence of any condition makes the marriage batil (void).
Contrast with conditions of enforceability (sihha vs nafadh).
4
Determine the consequence of omission
If any condition is missing, the marriage contract is void (batil) from the outset. It cannot be later validated by consummation or acknowledgement.
This is the Hanafi position; other schools may treat it as fasid or makruh.

Future Tense in Marriage Contracts
Ruling on future tense in marriage contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using future tense in the marriage contract and explain why it is valid based on the concept of tawkeel (agency).

Core Idea
A marriage contract is valid with future tense because it implies agency (tawkeel).

This passage from al-Quduri's matn addresses a specific issue in the language of the marriage contract. The Hanafi school permits the use of future tense verbs, such as the command 'zawwijni' (marry me), because it is interpreted as an implicit appointment of agency. This flexibility reflects the school's emphasis on substance over form in contractual validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The matn presents a scenario: a man says 'zawwijni' (marry me) to a wali, and the wali responds 'zawwajtuka' (I married you).
2
Recognize the linguistic issue
The verb 'zawwijni' is a command form, which indicates a future action. Some may think only past tense is valid for a contract.
3
Understand the reasoning
The sharh explains that this is considered tawkeel (agency). The husband implicitly appoints the wali as his agent to perform the marriage, making the contract valid.
4
Note the practical implication
One person can undertake both sides of the contract: as agent for the husband and as wali for the wife, meaning he can execute both offer and acceptance.
This is further elaborated in other sections of the sharh.

Essential Conditions for Marriage Witnesses in Hanafi Fiqh
Listing conditions for valid marriage witnesses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the eight conditions for marriage witnesses according to the Hanafi school and explain that these conditions are for validity, not just recommendation.

Core Idea
A valid marriage contract requires two witnesses with specific qualifications; none of them involve moral probity.

In this lesson, we examine the conditions for witnesses in the marriage contract as stated by al-Quduri. The Hanafi school holds that these conditions are indispensable; without them, the marriage is invalid. We'll break down each condition and understand why the school does not require witnesses to be just.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine the ruling on witnesses for marriage validity. The matn states that marriage is not concluded without certain conditions.
2
List the conditions
Extract the eight conditions from the passage: free, adult, sane, Muslim, two men or one man and two women, hearing together, understanding the contract.
The condition of being free is only for when both spouses are free; for a slave marrying a slave, witnesses can be slaves.
3
Distinguish conditions from pillars
Note that these are shurut (conditions) not arkan. Omission of any condition makes the contract invalid (batil).
4
Note the absence of justice
The passage explicitly omits justice ('adala). Understand that moral probity is not a condition for witnesses in Hanafi nikah.

The Hanafi Dispensation: Why Witnesses Need Not Be Just
Expanding on the permissibility of non-'udul witnesses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between tahammul and ada' and identify why this allows non-'udul witnesses.

Core Idea
Marriage witnesses bear testimony (tahammul), not deliver it (ada'); thus justice is irrelevant.

This lesson dives into the key expansion in the passage: witnesses for marriage need not be just ('udul). The commentary provides the reasoning: the requirement is for bearing witness (tahammul al-shahada), which is open to all sane adults, unlike delivering testimony in court (ada'), which requires justice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expansion
The matn states conditions, then the sharh adds 'عدولاً كانوا أو غير عدول' (whether they are just or not). This is the key expansion that relaxes the requirement of justice.
2
Understand the distinction
Learn the difference between tahammul (bearing testimony) and ada' (delivering testimony). Tahammul is the capacity to receive and retain testimony; ada' is the act of presenting it in court.
Hanafi usul holds that tahammul does not require probity; ada' does.
3
Apply the distinction
Apply this to the examples: blind, qadhf-punished, and relatives are capable of tahammul even if they cannot deliver testimony. Hence they are acceptable witnesses for marriage.
4
Recognize the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school requires witnesses to be 'udul based on Quranic verses on testimony. Hanafis limit those verses to ada' only. This is a valid ikhtilaf.

Marriage of Muslim to Zimmi Woman with Zimmi Witnesses
Intra-Hanafi dispute on marriage validity with Zimmi witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the validity of marriage with Zimmi witnesses and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) ruling.

Core Idea
A marriage with Zimmi witnesses is valid in the Hanafi school, but not established upon denial.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the validity of a Muslim man's marriage to a Zimmi woman when the witnesses are also Zimmi. Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf hold the marriage to be valid, while Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani requires Muslim witnesses. Later Hanafi authorities have affirmed the position of the majority, making it the operative ruling for fatwa. Understanding this dispute clarifies the school's methodology for resolving ikhtilaf and the practical consequences of witness qualifications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
Read the Arabic carefully: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say 'jaza' (permissible), Muhammad says 'la yaju' (not permissible) unless the witnesses are Muslim. Note that the marriage is 'valid' (sahih) according to the first two imams.
The term 'jaza' here indicates validity, not merely permissibility.
2
Understand the juhood (denial) exception
The text adds that the marriage is valid but 'la yuthbutu 'inda juhoodihi' – it is not established upon the husband's denial. This means if the husband denies the marriage, the Zimmi witnesses' testimony may not be accepted to prove it.
Juhood refers to the husband's denial of the marriage contract.
3
Identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon position)
Later authorities (al-Isbijaabi, al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shari'ah) affirmed the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. This is the operative ruling for fatwa.
The tarjih is based on the weight of later scholarship, not on the number of imams.
4
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The key practical difference is: under the majority view, the marriage exists but is difficult to prove if denied; under Muhammad's view, it is void ab initio. This affects court cases and enforcement of marital rights.
Thamarah al-khilaf shows the real-world impact of the legal dispute.
5
Apply the ruling to a modern scenario
Consider a Muslim in a non-Muslim country marrying a Christian with Christian witnesses. The marriage is valid per the Hanafi school, but if the husband later denies the marriage, the Christian witnesses' testimony may not be accepted in an Islamic court, leaving the wife without judicial recourse.
This demonstrates the ongoing relevance of the classical ruling.

Core Idea
Seven types of women are permanently forbidden: mother, grandmothers, daughter/granddaughters, sister/nieces, aunts, and wife's mother/grandmother.

Marriage in Islam is built on permissible relations, but certain women are eternally forbidden due to blood or marriage ties. Learning Objective

Core Idea
A free adult woman may marry without her wali's consent, but the wali may object if the husband is not suitable (kuf').

This lesson examines a classical intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a free, adult, sane woman needs a guardian's permission to contract her marriage. Abu Hanifa holds that she can do so independently, analogizing it to her right to manage her wealth. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) require a wali. The lesson clarifies the relied-upon position and the guardian's role in objecting to unsuitable matches.

Guided Path
1

The act is the marriage contract of a free, adult, sane woman. According to Abu Hanifa, the ruling is that it is valid (sahih) without a wali's permission.
This applies whether she is a virgin (bikr) or previously married (thayyib).
2

Abu Hanifa's proof is qiyas (analogy): since a woman can freely dispose of her property, she can likewise dispose of her marriage, which is her exclusive right. This is a clear analogy (qiyas jaliy).
The Quran and hadith are not cited here, but the qiyas is based on her capacity to manage her own affairs.
3

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (Sahibayn) require the wali's permission for the marriage to be valid. The text notes that Abu Yusuf later retracted to Abu Hanifa's view, and Muhammad reportedly retracted to the requirement of a wali.

5

Even though the wali's permission is not required for validity, the guardian still has the right to object if the husband is not a suitable match (kuf'). This allows family oversight without invalidating the contract.
The concept of kuf' (suitability in lineage, piety, profession, etc.) is a separate consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a woman who lost virginity through zina is treated as a virgin or thayyib for consent, and state the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: a woman who fornicated is still treated as a virgin; Sahibayn: she is a thayyib. The relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's unless zina is habitual.

A woman loses her virginity through adultery. Should she be treated as a virgin (silence suffices) or a thayyib (must speak)? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement: Abu Hanifa maintains she remains in the ruling of virgins, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) consider her a thayyib. State the dispute

People still regard her as a virgin; requiring speech would embarrass her and harm her interests. Customary perception (urf) prevails.

She is a thayyib in reality (hymen broken by intercourse), so she must speak like any thayyib. The physical fact overrides custom.

Wife's Denial of Marriage and the Oath in Nikah
Ruling on wife's denial and intra-Hanafi dispute on oath in marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting the wife's word when she denies a marriage and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether an oath is administered in marriage, identifying the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
In marriage disputes, the wife's word is taken without oath, but the fatwa requires an oath for practical necessity.

Marriage disputes sometimes involve one party denying the contract. The Hanafi school gives a distinct ruling for the wife: her word is accepted without requiring an oath. However, a deeper dispute exists among the founders regarding whether oaths are ever administered in marriage cases. This lesson unpacks both rulings, showing how the later fatwa adopted the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) over that of Abu Hanifa due to widespread need.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two distinct rulings in the passage
The passage addresses two separate but related issues: first, whose word is accepted when the wife denies marriage; second, whether an oath is administered in marriage disputes.
The first ruling is unanimous among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad (contra Zufar); the second is a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
State the majority Hanafi position on wife's denial
When the wife says 'I rejected the marriage,' her word is accepted without requiring an oath. This is because she denies the binding of the contract (inkar al-luzum).
The principle 'al-qawl qawluha' applies here. Zufar disagreed, but the school follows the majority.
3
Analyse the intra-Hanafi dispute on administering an oath
Abu Hanifa holds that no oath is administered in marriage disputes. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that an oath is required. Identify the basis of each view: Abu Hanifa likely considers marriage sacred and oaths unnecessary; the Sahibayn rely on general judicial practice ('umum al-balwa).
The key term 'istihlaf' means the judge administering an oath to a litigant.
4
Identify the reason for tarjih (preference)
The later authorities (al-Haqa'iq, al-Tatimma, Fatawa Qadikhan) gave fatwa according to the Sahibayn's position because of 'umum al-balwa' (widespread need). This practical necessity overrides the theoretical strength of Abu Hanifa's view.
This is a classic example of the Hanafi method adopting the more practical position for fatwa.
5
Note the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The mufta bihi today is that oaths are administered in marriage disputes, following the Sahibayn. However, the wife's word remains accepted without oath when she denies the contract.
The two rulings are independent: the wife's denial does not trigger an automatic oath; the oath is used to resolve disputed facts.

Sareeh and Kinayah Words for Nikah
Ruling on explicit and implicit marriage contract words

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sareeh and kinayah words for the marriage contract and state when intention or a contextual clue is required for validity.

Core Idea
Only 'nikah' and 'tazwij' are explicit; all other transfer words need intention or mention of mahr to form a valid marriage.

In Hanafi fiqh, the validity of a marriage contract depends heavily on the wording used. Not every expression of transfer constitutes a valid nikah. This lesson draws the critical line between explicit words that automatically conclude marriage and metaphorical words that require additional intention or context. Understanding this distinction prevents common errors in marital contracting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sareeh words
State the two words that are explicit (sareeh) for nikah: 'nikah' and 'tazwij'. These require no intention or contextual clue because their primary meaning is marriage.
In the Hanafi school, only these two words are sareeh.
2
Recognize the category of kinayah
Identify all words besides 'nikah' and 'tazwij' as kinayah. These include tamlik (transfer), hibah (gift), sadaqah (charity), bay' (sale), and shira' (purchase). They have primary meanings of property transfer.
Kinayah words are valid for nikah only if the parties intend marriage.
3
Determine the requirement for validity
For kinayah words, validity depends on either explicit intention (niyyah) or a contextual clue (qarinah) such as mentioning the mahr. Without either, the contract is not a valid marriage.
Mentioning mahr is a strong contextual clue that clarifies intent.
4
Apply al-Tatarkhani's nuance
According to al-Tatarkhani, if a kinayah word is used and the mahr is mentioned, no separate intention is needed because the mention of mahr itself signals marriage. Otherwise, intention alone suffices.
This nuance slightly relaxes the requirement when mahr is specified.

In marriage disputes, the woman's denial of acceptance is accepted, but the oath requirement divides Abu Hanifa from his students.

This passage from al-Lubāb addresses a procedural nuance: when a woman claims she rejected an offer of marriage, is her word accepted without further proof? The text presents Abu Hanifa's view that no oath is required, and the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad that an oath must be administered. Intriguingly, later Hanafi practice adopts the students' view due to widespread need (ʻumūm al-balwā). This lesson dissects the reasoning and the operative ruling.

The text covers two related points: (1) the woman's statement regarding rejection is accepted, and (2) whether an oath is required in marriage disputes. Note that the first point is less contested; the second is the main dispute.

The two disciples hold that an oath is required, analogizing to other disputes where the denier must swear. This would also apply to the woman denying acceptance.

5
Apply the muftā bihī ruling
In practice, a judge or mufti should require an oath from the woman when she claims rejection. If she refuses, her statement is not accepted and the marriage is considered valid.
This thamarah al-khilāf (practical consequence) directly affects dispute resolution.
