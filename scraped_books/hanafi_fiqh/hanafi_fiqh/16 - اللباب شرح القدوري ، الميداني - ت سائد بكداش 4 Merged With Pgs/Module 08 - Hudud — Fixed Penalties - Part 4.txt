The thief's driving of the donkey makes him the agent of removal, so the hadd applies.

In the jurisprudence of theft (sariqah), the act of removing property from a secure place must be directly attributed to the thief. This lesson examines a specific case from al-Quduri's matn: when a thief uses a donkey to carry stolen goods and drives it out. Group Theft: All Participants Liable for Hadd
Ruling on group theft cooperation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that all members of a group are subject to hadd if they cooperated in theft, and distinguish between a thief and a mere trespasser.

Core Idea
In group theft, all are cut because cooperation makes the act collective.

This lesson examines a ruling from al-Hidayah regarding group theft. The Hanafi school holds that if a group enters a secure place with the intention to steal and some members actually take the goods while others perform supporting roles, all are liable for the hadd punishment of cutting the hand. This ruling is based on the understanding that they are cooperating as a single unit, and it prevents loopholes that would allow thieves to escape punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a group entering a secure place with theft intent. The ruling is that all members are subject to hadd (cutting of the hand), even if only some physically take the goods.
The ruling applies only if the group cooperated; a mere trespasser who does not take is not a thief.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a rational argument (dalil aqli) based on custom and necessity. The text states: 'because the removal from all is considered cooperation' and that the common practice among thieves is that some carry while others defend. If cutting were prevented, it would close the door of hadd.
This is an istihsan (juristic preference) to uphold the hadd punishment.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The rationale is that the entire group's actions are considered a single act of theft due to cooperation (ta'awun). Each member's role is integral to the success of the theft, so the act of taking is attributed to all.
This is based on the legal maxim that cooperation makes all participants liable.
4
Note the distinction
The passage distinguishes between a thief (sariq) and a mudi' (waster/transgressor). If someone enters but does not take anything, he is not a thief and not subject to hadd.
This highlights that actual taking or participation in taking is required for hadd.
5
Evaluate the thamarah (practical consequence)
Without this ruling, thieves could easily assign roles and claim that only the actual taker is liable, making it nearly impossible to prove theft against all members. The ruling ensures that the hadd is applied effectively.
This reflects the Hanafi concern for the practical application of hudud.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses the hudud punishment for highway robbers (qutta' al-tariq). It distinguishes scenarios based on whether they kill and/or take property. In the first scenario, the punishment is a hadd (fixed penalty) from Allah, not qisas (retaliation). Therefore, the legal heirs' pardon is ineffective because the right of Allah cannot be waived by humans.

When robbers kill and take property, the Imam has a choice among three options according to Abu Hanifa: (a) amputate right hand and left foot, then kill and crucify; (b) kill only; (c) crucify only. Each option aims to destroy the criminals and deter others.

Abu Yusuf holds that the Imam cannot omit crucifixion—he must kill and crucify. Muhammad holds that amputation is dropped; the punishment is only killing and crucifixion. Both disagree with Abu Hanifa's full discretion.
The dispute is about whether the Imam has the authority to choose a lesser punishment.

The text states that the correct (al-sahih) position is Abu Hanifa's, and this is affirmed in al-Hidayah and al-Tajnis as Zahir al-Riwayah. Later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Mawsili chose Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage addresses the prohibition of intoxicating substances other than wine, specifically bang (hemp), hashish, and opium. The ruling is that consuming these is haram, because they corrupt the mind ('aql) and prevent one from remembering Allah and performing prayer. The reasoning is rational (dalil 'aqli): intoxicants are harmful to the mind and religious duties. However, the text notes that the degree of prohibition is less than that of wine (khamr). This distinction is reflected in the punishment: if someone consumes these substances and becomes intoxicated, they do not incur the hadd punishment (which for wine is 80 lashes according to Hanafi school), but rather a ta'zir (discretionary punishment) less than the hadd, as determined by the judge. This is because these substances are not explicitly mentioned in the Quran or hadith as intoxicants subject to hadd; the hadd is specifically for wine (khamr) based on the Prophetic tradition. The Hanafi school analogizes other intoxicants to wine in terms of prohibition, but not in terms of the fixed punishment. Thus, they fall under the category of makruh tahrimi (strongly discouraged) or haram, but the punishment is left to the discretion of the ruler.

Key Terms
البنج (al-banj): Hemp or any narcotic plant that intoxicates.
تعزير (ta'zir): Discretionary punishment set by the judge for offenses not subject to fixed hadd or qisas.
