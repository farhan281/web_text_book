Separation and Financial Consequences After Fosterage
Ruling on separation and financial consequences after fosterage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings on separation due to fosterage: requirement of Qadi's decree, the mahr rule before/after consummation, and the iddah maintenance rule.

Core Idea
Separation by fosterage requires a judge's decree; financial consequences depend on timing.

Fosterage creates a marriage prohibition. But the marriage is not automatically dissolved; a judge must decree the separation. The text then details the financial outcomes, which differ based on consummation, and clarifies that the wife gets no maintenance during her waiting period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first ruling: requirement of Qadi's decree
The text states separation does not occur except by the judge's separation (tafriq al-Qadi). Understand the reason: it involves nullifying the wife's rights, so judicial intervention is necessary.
This protects the wife's rights by ensuring due process.
2
Distinguish before vs after consummation for mahr
If separation happens before consummation, the wife gets no mahr. If after, she receives the lesser of the specified mahr (mahr musamma) and the mahr of her peers (mahr al-mithl).
The rule is based on the idea that the marriage existed but is rescinded, so only partial compensation may be due.
3
Apply the 'lesser of two' rule
Determine which amount is lower: the mahr specified in the contract or the mahr al-mithl. The wife receives that lesser amount.
If the specified mahr is extravagant, she does not get more than her peers.
4
Note the iddah maintenance rule
The wife must observe iddah (waiting period) but is not entitled to maintenance or housing from the husband during that time.
This differs from regular divorce where she may be entitled to maintenance.
5
Summarize the three rulings
Recap: (1) Qadi's decree required, (2) mahr: none before consummation, lesser amount after, (3) no maintenance/housing during iddah.
This passage is a concise statement of these rules, confirmed by al-Jawharah.

Conditions for Tahlil: Valid Marriage, Consummation, and Exceptions
Explains conditions for a triply divorced woman to become lawful to her first husband

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for tahlil and apply the Hanafi rulings to cases involving adolescents, conditional intentions, and invalid marriages.

Core Idea
Tahlil requires a valid marriage and consummation; ejaculation is not a condition.

The verse "hatta tankiha zawan ghayrah" (Quran 2:230) establishes that a triply divorced woman cannot remarry her first husband unless she marries and consummates with a second husband. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi interpretation of this verse, clarifying the exact conditions, how they apply to adolescents, and the validity of marriages conditioned on tahlil.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule for tahlil
Identify the Quranic basis and the three core conditions: valid marriage, consummation, and divorce/death of the second husband.
The verse uses the imperative 'hatta tankiha' indicating necessity.
2
Examine the condition of valid marriage
Distinguish between a valid (sahih) marriage and invalid (fasid) or suspended (mawquf) marriages. Only a sahih marriage suffices for tahlil.
Example: A slave's marriage without master's permission is mawquf and does not count until authorized.
3
Analyze the ruling for an adolescent boy
Understand that a murahiq (boy around ten with desire) is like an adult; consummation is sufficient even without ejaculation.
The Hanafi school does not require ejaculation; presence of desire and ability to penetrate are key.
4
Evaluate the effect of a condition or intention of tahlil
If the marriage is explicitly conditioned on tahlil, it is sahih but makruh tahrimi. If the intention is only in the heart, it is valid and permissible.
Al-Ispijabi notes unanimous agreement on the latter.
5
Identify what does NOT suffice for tahlil
Master's intercourse with his slave-girl does not make her lawful, as nikah (marriage) is required. Also, a fasid marriage is insufficient.
This reinforces that only a valid marriage contract fulfills the condition.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the ruling on tahlil (making a triply divorced woman lawful for her first husband). The author begins by stating the general principle: a woman divorced three times cannot remarry her first husband unless she marries another man in a valid marriage, consummates it (intercourse), and then either he divorces her or dies, and she completes her 'iddah. The marriage must be valid (sahih), not invalid (fasid) or suspended (mawquf, e.g., a slave's marriage without permission). If a slave marries without permission and has intercourse before authorization, the tahlil is incomplete until he repeats intercourse after authorization. The next section addresses the adolescent boy (murahiq) who has desires and is estimated at ten years by Shams al-A'imma. He is considered like an adult for tahlil because intercourse in a valid marriage exists, even without ejaculation; ejaculation is not a condition. The author compares him to a eunuch or a stallion that does not ejaculate. Then the text states that a master's intercourse with his slave-girl (mawla amatahu) does not make her lawful for the first husband because marriage (nikah) is specifically required by the Quranic verse. The next ruling concerns marriage with the express condition of tahlil (e.g., 'I marry you to make you lawful'): such a marriage is still valid but makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) due to the hadith cursing the muhallil and muhallal lahu. However, if consummation occurs, it fulfills the condition, and the woman becomes lawful for the first husband. Finally, al-Ispijabi notes that if the condition is only in the heart and not spoken, all scholars agree it is valid and the woman becomes lawful.

Definition and Ruling of Ila' (Swearing to Abstain from Marital Relations)
Defining ila', its conditions, and ruling when oath is kept or broken

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition, conditions, and rulings of ila' according to the Hanafi school, and apply the ruling when the oath is kept or broken.

Core Idea
An oath to abstain for four months leads to ba'in divorce if kept, or requires expiation or conditional consequence if broken.

Ila' is a specific type of oath that a husband may take regarding his wife. This lesson unpacks its definition, the conditions that make it valid, and the precise legal consequences of keeping or breaking the oath. We will also see how the Quranic verse (2:226) forms the basis for the four-month waiting period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Determine that ila' is a valid oath (yamin) made by a husband to abstain from sexual relations with his wife for a specified period (minimum four months).
It is not a divorce act itself but an oath with legal consequences.
2
State the conditions for valid ila'
Identify the conditions: the wife must be lawfully married at the time of the oath, and the husband must be legally capable of divorce (adult, sane, Muslim).
If these conditions are missing, the ila' is not valid.
3
Describe the two possible outcomes
Outcome 1: Husband keeps the oath (no intercourse for four months) → a ba'in divorce occurs at the end of the period. Outcome 2: Husband breaks the oath (intercourse within four months) → he must pay kaffara (if oath by Allah) or fulfill the suspended consequence (if conditional), and the ila' falls.
The correct Hanafi position holds that if the oath has both elements, both are required.
4
Identify the dalil and explain wajh al-dalalah
The Quranic verse (2:226) establishes the four-month waiting period for those who swear off their wives. It implies that after four months, a decision must be made (return or divorce).
The verse is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and the mufta bihi
There was a view that only one consequence applies upon hanth; the Imam's final position (mufta bihi) is that both kaffara (if by Allah) and the conditional consequence apply when the oath combines both.
As confirmed by al-Shurunbulali and Ibn 'Abidin.

The Ruling of Ila': Automatic Divorce After Four Months
States the core ruling of ila' and the effect of oath duration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling of ila' and explain how the duration of the oath affects its expiration.

Core Idea
Ila' after four months results in automatic irrevocable divorce; the oath expires only if limited to that period.

This passage from al-Quduri's matn establishes the core Hanafi ruling on ila' (an oath to abstain from marital relations). If the husband persists for four months, the marriage dissolves automatically in one irrevocable divorce. But the fate of his oath depends on its terms: a time-limited oath ends, while a perpetual one continues. Let's unpack the legal logic and the evidence from the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling of ila'
The matn states explicitly: if the husband does not approach his wife for four months, she becomes irrevocably divorced with one divorce. This is the Hanafi position.
'بانت' means ba'in (irrevocable) divorce.
2
Determine the effect on the oath
The commentary distinguishes two scenarios: if the oath was limited to four months, it expires; if it was perpetual, it remains.
This distinction affects future obligations: a remaining oath requires kaffarah if broken later.
3
Recognize the proof
The author cites the practice of five Companions: 'Uthman, 'Ali, the three 'Abdullahs, and Zayd ibn Thabit. This is a qawl sahabi (companion saying), considered a valid source in Hanafi usul.
The phrase 'وهو المأثور عن' indicates transmission from them.
4
Contrast with other schools
Unlike the Hanafi automatic divorce, Shafi'i fiqh holds that after four months the wife may seek judicial dissolution, but it does not happen automatically.
This is an example of thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).

Ruling on revival of ila' after remarriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the revival of ila' after remarriage and apply it to scenarios involving intercourse or continued abstention.

Core Idea
The ila' oath survives divorce and revives upon remarriage, triggering new consequences.

This passage from al-Lubab explains what happens when a husband who previously made an oath of ila' (swearing to abstain from intercourse) and divorced his wife remarries her. The central question is whether the original oath is cancelled or revived. The Hanafi position is clear: the oath is not nullified by divorce, and upon remarriage, the ila' becomes active again. From that point, the husband's options lead to either expiation (if he has intercourse) or another divorce after four months (if he continues abstaining). The commentary from al-Hidayah provides the reasoning behind this ruling, which is based on the principle that the oath remains binding as long as the possibility of marital rights exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
The passage begins with a conditional: if a man who divorced his wife due to ila' (after four months of abstention) remarries her, the ila' returns. Understand this as the starting point: the former oath is reactivated solely by the act of remarriage.

2

Once the ila' is revived, the passage gives two branches: (a) the husband has intercourse → he violates his oath (hanth) and must pay expiation (kaffarah); the ila' falls. (b) he does not have intercourse → after a new period of four months, another divorce occurs.

The commentary explains that the original oath is not nullified by the divorce because the removal of ownership (zawal al-milk) does not invalidate a suspended oath (yamin mu'allaq). The oath is attached to a condition that remains possible. Once ownership returns (via remarriage), the condition can be fulfilled, and the oath becomes operative again.
This is a key Hanafi usul principle: oaths are not automatically voided by intervening events that remove the subject matter temporarily.

The key distinction is whether the marriage is a 'new beginning' after a complete separation via another marriage.

Verbal Fay' When Unable to Have Intercourse: The Hanafi Position with Qiyas Proof
Ruling with proof via qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on verbal fay' when intercourse is impossible, explain the condition under which it becomes invalid, and apply the qiyas analogy to tayammum.

Core Idea
Verbal declaration suffices for fay' when physical return is impossible, but only as long as the inability persists.

In Hanafi law, a husband who takes an oath to abstain from intercourse (ila') must return to his wife (fay') within four months. But what if he cannot physically consummate? This lesson explains that a verbal declaration is sufficient—a substitute (badal) akin to tayammum. However, if the obstacle is removed within the period, the substitute falls away and actual intercourse becomes necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the husband's return (fay') to his wife after an oath of ila'. The ruling: when physical intercourse is impossible, verbal declaration alone suffices to nullify the ila'.
The text lists multiple obstacles: illness, distance, wife's condition, imprisonment, nushuz.
2
Locate the dalil
No explicit Quran or hadith is cited. Instead, the argument is based on qiyas (analogy) to tayammum, which is a substitute for wudu when water is unavailable.
The qiyas is jaliy (clear analogy) because the illa is evident: inability to perform the original act.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal link (illa) is the inability to perform the original act (ajz 'an al-asl) at the time the substitute is performed. If the ability returns before the goal is achieved (i.e., before the ila' period ends), the substitute becomes invalid.
Like tayammum: if water appears before prayer, tayammum is void.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may not accept verbal fay' and require actual intercourse in all cases. The Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih.
The practical consequence: in Hanafi, the verbal fay' prevents automatic divorce; in Shafi'i, divorce might still occur if intercourse is impossible.
5
Apply the ruling
Given a specific obstacle, check if it prevents intercourse. If yes, verbal fay' is initially valid. But if the obstacle is removed within the ila' period, the verbal fay' becomes invalid and physical fay' is required.
The text explicitly mentions: 'If he recovers within the period, that verbal fay' is invalidated.'

Core Idea
The phrase 'anti haram' is a kinayah: its effect depends on intention, but in court, lying is not accepted.

When a husband says to his wife, 'You are forbidden to me,' the words themselves do not clearly indicate divorce, zihar, or an oath. Therefore, the husband is asked about his intention, and the ruling varies accordingly. This passage from al-Lubab explains the Hanafi position, including an important intra-madhhab refinement: while the husband's claim of intending lying is accepted between him and Allah, it is not accepted in court, and the phrase is treated as a binding oath.

Classify the utterance

This distinction between religious and judicial realms is key.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi intra-school dispute regarding the nature of separation after li'an and explain the tarjih to the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
After li'an, the separation is a ba'in divorce—not a permanent prohibition—because retraction abrogates the li'an.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents an important intra-Hanafi dispute. The core question: when a judge separates a couple after li'an, is that separation a one-time irrevocable divorce (ba'in) or does it create a permanent bar to remarriage? We will examine the positions of the three major Hanafi imams and see how later scholarship resolved the ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
What is li'an? What triggers the separation? The husband accuses his wife of adultery without witnesses, leading to mutual oaths and curses before a judge, who then separates them.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold the separation is a ba'in divorce (one irrevocable divorce). Abu Yusuf holds it is a permanent prohibition (tahrim mu'abbad), meaning they can never remarry.
3
Examine the evidence for each
Abu Yusuf cites the hadith 'The two li'an participants never come together forever.' The majority respond that retracting the accusation (ikdhab) is a withdrawal that abrogates the li'an and its ruling, so the hadith applies only as long as li'an remains.
4
Understand the tarjih
Al-Isbijabi in his commentary affirms that the correct view is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school.
5
Apply the practical consequence
Under the mufta bihi, the couple can remarry after the wife completes her 'iddah (e.g., three menstrual cycles). Under Abu Yusuf's view, remarriage would be permanently forbidden.

Li'an about a child negates paternity automatically; the child is attached to the mother.

This lesson examines a specific application of li'an (mutual cursing). When a husband accuses his wife not of adultery but of bearing another man's child, the judge declares the child fatherless and attaches it to the mother's lineage. This ruling is a direct consequence of the li'an procedure and differs from cases where the accusation is of zina.

The child is attached to the mother (alhaqahu bi-ummihi). The child becomes like a walad al-zina in terms of paternal lineage.
4

The child does not inherit from the husband, and the husband does not inherit from the child. The child's maternal lineage is established.

Remarriage After Hadd: Why It Becomes Permissible
Ruling on permissibility of remarriage after hadd removes li'an eligibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that remarrying one's wife becomes permissible (mubah) after hadd punishment and explain the legal reasoning that the hadd removes li'an eligibility, thus lifting the prohibition.

Core Idea
Hadd punishment removes li'an eligibility; without li'an possibility, the marriage prohibition is lifted.

In the Hanafi school, when a husband accuses his wife of adultery without witnesses, the process of li'an (mutual oath-taking) is initiated. This process can lead to permanent separation. However, if the husband receives hadd punishment for slander (qadhf) or the wife for zina, the possibility of li'an is eliminated. This lesson examines the ruling that the marriage prohibition contingent on li'an is thereby removed, allowing the spouses to remarry.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the first sentence: 'And it becomes permissible for him to marry her.' This establishes that the ruling is permissibility (ibaha) after hadd.
The ruling applies to both spouses.
2
Recognize the Prior Prohibition
Understand that li'an had imposed a temporary prohibition on marital relations. The hadd punishment removes the legal capacity for li'an, thus eliminating the basis for the prohibition.
Prohibition was conditional on eligibility for li'an.
3
Examine the Reasoning (Ta'lil)
The text states: 'because when he was punished with hadd, he is no longer eligible for li'an.' This is the key legal cause (illat): loss of ahliyyat al-li'an.
Hadd for qadhf or zina disqualifies a person from being a competent witness (shahadat), which is required for li'an.
4
Apply to Different Scenarios
The passage covers three cases: (1) husband accused wife and was punished; (2) husband accused someone else and was punished; (3) wife committed zina or accused and was punished. In all, the hadd removes li'an eligibility from the punished party, permitting remarriage.
If both lose eligibility, ruling still applies.
5
Formulate the General Principle
Conclude: 'It is permissible for him to marry her when both or one of them exit from eligibility for li'an.' This is the summary from al-Durr.
The principle is that the prohibition is lifted as soon as either spouse can no longer participate in li'an.

The cause is the husband's explicit accusation of zina: 'You committed zina, and this pregnancy is from zina.' This is qadhf, which obligates either hadd punishment or, between spouses, li'an.

This difference affects the immediate legal status of the child.

Timing for Denying Paternity: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Ruling on validity of denying paternity immediately or within congratulations period

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the timing for denying paternity, explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
A husband's silence during congratulations is acknowledgment – denial must be immediate.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses the critical question: how long after birth can a husband validly deny paternity? The answer determines whether lineage is established. Abu Hanifa holds that the window is very short (the few days when congratulations are offered), while his two disciples extend it to the entire postpartum period (nifas). The later Hanafi authorities side with Abu Hanifa, making his view the operative one for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the overall ruling
According to the passage, denial of paternity must occur immediately after birth or within the period when congratulations are accepted (customarily 7 days). If done within that time, the denial is valid and li'an is performed. After that, lineage is established and denial has no effect.
The ruling is based on the husband's conduct – silence or acceptance of congratulations implies acknowledgment.
2
Identify the intra-madhhab difference
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) disagree with Abu Hanifa. They allow denial within the entire duration of nifas (postpartum bleeding, up to 40 days). Their reasoning is that denial is valid only in a short period, and nifas is the most appropriate measure because it is directly linked to childbirth.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn use qiyas: since denial is valid in a short period, and nifas is a short period connected to birth, it should serve as the limit. They argue that a fixed, measurable period (nifas) provides clarity.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) based on the illa of brevity.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa rejects a fixed duration because people's circumstances differ. Instead, he considers the husband's conduct: acceptance of congratulations, silence, or purchasing birthing supplies all indicate acknowledgment. The period is determined by custom (urf), not a strict numerical limit.
This is a form of istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas, taking into account the variability of human situations.
5

Later Hanafi authorities – Abu al-Ma'ali, al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, and Sadr al-Shari'a – all affirm Abu Hanifa's view as the correct one. Thus, the operative rule for fatwa is that denial must occur within the customary congratulations period (about 7 days).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a husband's right to prevent mahram relatives from entering his home, explain that he cannot prevent looking or speaking, and identify the mufta bihi position on visit frequency for parents versus other mahram.

Core Idea
The husband may prevent entry into his home but not prevent looking or speaking; parents have a right to visit every Friday, other mahram once a year.

A husband's authority over his home is clear, but it is limited by Shariah obligations to uphold family ties. This passage from al-Lubab, with commentary from al-Hidayah, lays out the precise boundaries: he can decide who enters his property, but he cannot sever the relationship between his wife and her family. Understanding this balance is crucial for marriage counsellors and muftis alike.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling

2
Understand the basis: property right vs. family ties
The husband’s right to prevent entry is based on his ownership. But preventing looking/speaking would sever family ties (qati'at al-rahim), which is prohibited. So the right is not absolute.
The text says 'for there is no harm to him' – meaning looking/speaking does not harm him, so he cannot forbid it.
3
Examine the two alternative opinions (qila)
Two other views are reported within the Hanafi school: (1) He may only prevent them from staying (qarar), not from entering or speaking. (2) He may not prevent her from going out to her parents, nor prevent them from entering every Friday. These show a range of interpretations.
These are not the mufta bihi (operative) positions, but they indicate the boundaries of ikhtilaf.
4

The author of al-Lubab states 'the estimation for other mahram is one year, and that is the correct view, as in al-Hidayah.' This means for relatives other than parents and children, the maximum visit frequency is once a year. Parents have more frequent access (every Friday), and spouses and children have normal household access.
'Other mahram' includes siblings, uncles, aunts, etc.

Consider: a wife’s adult brother wants to visit and stay for a month. The husband can prevent him from staying (qarar) but cannot forbid him from entering for a short visit and speaking. If the brother wants to visit regularly, the husband must allow a yearly visit. This balances the husband’s property rights with the wife’s family ties.
This is a common point of marital conflict; the fiqh provides a clear framework.

Duration of Custody for Male and Female Children: The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Defines the duration of custody rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi fatwa for the duration of custody for male (7 years) and female (9 years) children, and explain the intra-madhhab disagreement regarding the female child.

Core Idea
Custody of a male child ends at 7 years; custody of a female child ends at 9 years (fatwa).

This passage from Kitab al-Hadanah defines when the right of custody transfers from the mother/grandmother to the father. For a boy, the criterion is independence (estimated at 7 years). For a girl, there is a dispute: the apparent narration says until menarche, but the preferred fatwa says until the age of desire (9 years). Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying Hanafi family law.

Guided Path
1
State the rule for male child custody
The mother/grandmother have custody until the boy can independently eat, drink, dress, and perform istinja. Al-Khassaf estimated this age at 7 years based on common practice.
This estimation is the basis for the fatwa.
2
State the Zahir al-Riwayah rule for female child custody
According to the apparent narration, the mother/grandmother retain custody until menarche. The reason is that after independence, the girl needs feminine education; after puberty, she needs protection from a male guardian.
Menarche typically occurs at 12-14 years.
3
Identify the opposing view of Imam Muhammad
Imam Muhammad holds that custody over a female child ends at the age of desire (shahwah), estimated at 9 years, due to fear of fitna. This applies to all custodians, including the mother and grandmother.
This view is considered more cautious for later times.
4
Analyze the reasoning for the tarjih
Later Hanafi scholars (Abu al-Layth, authors of al-Tanwir, al-Durr) adopted Imam Muhammad's view because of increasing moral decay and the need for greater protection. The fatwa thus shifted to 9 years.
The tarjih is based on public interest (maslahah).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
Under Zahir al-Riwayah, a girl stays with her mother until ~12-14 years; under the fatwa, custody ends at 9 years. The mufta bihi is 9 years for all female children.
This is the operative ruling in most Hanafi fatwa collections.

Father's Claim of Child with His Son's Slave Woman
Ruling with rational proof on father's claim of child with son's slave woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a father has intercourse with his son's slave woman and claims the child, and identify the conditions, obligations, and the rational proof (qiyas) behind the ruling.

Core Idea
The father's claim is valid, but he must compensate by paying the slave woman's value at conception.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a unique scenario involving a father's right to his son's property for necessity. It explains the legal consequences when a father has intercourse with his son's slave woman, claims the child, and the conditions under which the claim is accepted. The ruling balances the father's need to preserve his lineage with the son's property rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the father's intercourse with his son's slave woman followed by claiming the child. The ruling: lineage is established, she becomes umm walad, and he must pay her value at conception. No uqr or child's value is due.
2
Identify the conditions for the ruling
The condition is that the son has not had intercourse with the slave woman. Additionally, she must not be an umm walad or mudabbara of the son. If either condition is violated, the claim is invalid and uqr is due.
These conditions are derived from the parentheses and the commentary in the text.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): the father may take ownership of his son's property for necessity. The need to preserve lineage (by having children) is analogous to the need for survival (food), but lesser in degree. Hence, he must pay the value.
This is a qiyas: asl = taking food (no compensation), far = taking slave woman (compensation required).
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof applies)
The text compares the necessity for food (survival) to the necessity for preserving lineage. For food, ownership transfers without compensation because survival is paramount. For lineage, the necessity is lesser, so ownership transfers with compensation (value at conception).
The father's ownership is considered to have occurred at conception, so he must pay the value at that time.
5
Note the exceptional cases
If the slave woman was previously an umm walad or mudabbara of the son, the father's claim is invalid, and he must pay uqr (mahr al-mithl). Similarly, if the grandfather (father's father) has intercourse while the father is alive, lineage is not established.
