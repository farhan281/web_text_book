Invalid Kitabah: The Slave Pays His Own Value
Explains ruling for invalid kitabah and obligation of value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for an invalid kitabah contract and apply the analogy (qiyas) to an invalid sale to explain why the slave must pay his own market value.

Core Idea
In an invalid kitabah, the slave pays his own market value, not the named item's.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with a specific problem: what happens when a kitabah contract is invalid (fasid)? For example, if a master writes a kitabah for his slave to pay a thousand pounds of wine (which is not valid property), the contract is void. The slave is emancipated upon paying, but what amount must he pay? The Hanafi school rules that the slave must pay his own market value, but with important adjustments compared to the named amount. The proof is derived by analogy to an invalid sale where the sold item perishes. Let's break down the ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an invalid kitabah contract. The ruling is that the slave must pay his value (qimah) upon emancipation. But what does 'value' mean? The text clarifies: it means the slave's own market value, not the value of the named item (like wine).
This is a wajib obligation — compensation for a failed contract.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The dalil is qiyas on an invalid sale (bay' fasid) when the sold item has perished. The text says: 'as in the invalid sale when the sold item perishes.'
The proof is analogical, not directly from Quran or Sunnah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof works)
In an invalid sale, the item is not lawfully transferred; if it perishes, the buyer must pay its value because returning the item is impossible. Similarly, in an invalid kitabah, the slave's person (raqabah) is to be returned (he was supposed to remain a slave until payment), but emancipation has made return impossible, so he must pay compensatory value.
The common illa is the impossibility of returning the subject matter.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and its resolution
Some early scholars interpreted 'his value' as the value of the named item (e.g., wine). But the author adopts the majority view that it is the slave's own value. The evidence is the analogy to sale: in sale, value is the item's price, not the substitute.
The tarjih (preference) is for the slave's own value, as in the majority opinion.
5
Apply the ruling to the example
Consider kitabah on 1000 pounds of wine. The slave pays his value. If his value is 1200, he pays 1200 (excess 200). If his value is 800, he pays 800 but does not reclaim the 200 he already paid (he does not get the difference back).
The named amount acts as a floor; the slave cannot pay less, but must pay more if his value exceeds it.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri deals with the ruling when a kitabah contract is invalid (fasid). The author begins by stating the matn: the slave must pay his value, but not less than the named amount, and must pay more if the value exceeds it. The commentary then explains that 'his value' means the slave's market value, because the invalid kitabah obliges him to return his person (i.e., his enslavement status), but since he was already emancipated (by paying) or the contract is rescinded, he must pay his value as compensation, analogized to an invalid sale where the sold item is lost. The text then distinguishes: if the kitabah was on the slave's own value (i.e., he agrees to pay his current market price), he becomes free upon payment because the price is clearly the substitute. But if on a garment (which is not specified in type or quality), he does not become free by paying any garment, because the parties' intent is unclear due to varying types; emancipation cannot be established without clear intention. Finally, the ruling is summarized: for any invalid kitabah where a specific property is named (like wine), the slave must pay his value, but if the value is less than the named amount, he does not get the difference back because the named amount was the agreed price and he cannot reclaim what he voluntarily paid. This is the Hanafi position.

Kitabah on an Unspecified Animal
Ruling on validity of kitabah when animal is unspecified

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kitabah when the consideration is an unspecified animal and apply the condition of specifying the genus to determine the contract's validity.

Core Idea
Kitabah on an unspecified animal is valid if its genus is specified; otherwise, the contract is void due to excessive ignorance.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a kitabah contract when the payment is to be made through an unspecified animal. The text from al-Lubab clarifies that such a contract is valid as long as the genus (jins) of the animal is specified, even if its type and description are not. However, if the genus is not mentioned—for example, merely saying 'an animal'—the contract becomes invalid due to excessive uncertainty. You will learn the distinction between tolerable and intolerable ignorance in kitabah contracts and how the Hanafi school facilitates manumission by tolerating minor ambiguities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, recognize that the act under discussion is a kitabah contract where the slave is required to pay an unspecified animal. According to the text, the ruling is that such a contract is valid (ja'iz) provided the genus is specified.
2
Understand the condition of validity
The key condition is that the genus (jins) of the animal must be specified, such as 'camel' or 'cow'. The type (naw') and description (sifah) need not be detailed. This is because specifying the genus reduces uncertainty to a minor level.
3
Analyze the consequence of not specifying the genus
If the genus is not specified—for example, saying 'dabbah' (beast)—the contract becomes invalid (la yajuz) due to excessive ignorance (jahl fahish), as it encompasses multiple genera like horse, donkey, mule.
4
Examine the analogy with marriage
The text references the chapter of marriage, indicating that this ruling follows the same principle: minor ignorance in the consideration (mahr or kitabah payment) is tolerated to facilitate the contract's objective (manumission or marriage).
5
Extract the principle of tolerable ignorance
The underlying principle is that in kitabah, minor uncertainty (jahl yasir) is overlooked to encourage manumission, whereas major uncertainty (jahl fahish) invalidates the contract. This mirrors the general Hanafi approach of easing conditions in philanthropic contracts.

Kitabah on Two Slaves with One Sum
Ruling on validity of single kitabah contract for two slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a single kitabah contract covering two slaves and explain why joint payment leads to freedom for both.

Core Idea
A single kitabah contract for two slaves is valid; joint payment frees both.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in the contract of kitabah (manumission by payment): Can a master write a single kitabah covering two slaves jointly? The passage gives a clear ruling that this is permissible, and when the slaves together pay the full sum, both become free. Let's examine the ruling and its underlying condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a master making a kitabah contract on his two slaves with a single contract and a single sum. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz).
2
Identify the condition for emancipation
The condition stated is that both slaves pay the full sum (the thousand dirhams). The text says 'فإن أديا: عتقا' – if they both pay, they both become free.
The condition is joint payment; the text does not address partial payment.
3
Trace the rationale
The rationale is given as 'لحصول الشرط' – because the condition is fulfilled. The condition is the payment of the agreed sum. Once paid, the purpose of the contract is achieved, leading to freedom for both.
This shows that the contract is effective for both slaves together.
4
Note the absence of other conditions
The text does not mention any requirement for separate contracts or individual shares. The validity of the single contract is affirmed, which is the Hanafi position.
Other schools may differ, but this passage focuses on the Hanafi view.

Wala' al-Itaqa as a Cause for 'Asabah
Defines wala' al-itaqa as a cause for 'asabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the priority rule for inheritance through wala' al-itaqa and apply it to determine the patron's share in a given estate.

Core Idea
The patron inherits as 'asabah only after all blood 'asabah relatives of the freed slave are exhausted.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri defines a key principle in Hanafi faraid: wala' al-itaqa (patronage from emancipation) creates a derivative right of inheritance. Understanding the hierarchy between blood 'asabah and the patron is essential for correctly distributing the estate of a freed slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal cause
Recognize that wala' al-itaqa is a cause (sabab) that establishes the patron as an 'asabah (residuary heir) of the freed slave.
This is a derivative 'asabah, not based on lineage.
2
Distinguish 'asabah types
Differentiate between 'asabah bil-nasab (by lineage) and 'asabah bil-sabab (by cause). The former is stronger.
Lineage 'asabah include sons, brothers, uncles, etc.
3
Apply the priority rule
If the freed slave has any blood 'asabah relative, that relative takes precedence over the patron. The patron inherits only in their absence.
The patron's right is subordinate.
4
Determine the patron's share
In the absence of blood 'asabah, the patron inherits the entire estate if there is no fixed-share heir (fard), or the remainder after fixed shares are given.
The patron is an 'asabah, so he takes what remains.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal nature of wala' al-itaqa (patronage from emancipation) as a cause for 'asabah (residuary inheritance). It explains that this wala' creates a derivative 'asabah relationship: the patron (mu'tiq) inherits from the freed slave (mu'taq) only when there are no blood 'asabah relatives. If there is a blood 'asabah, that relative takes precedence because his 'asabah is by lineage (nasab), which is stronger than the patron's 'asabah (sababiyya, i.e., by cause). If there is no blood 'asabah but there is a Quranic sharer (sahib fard), the patron takes the remainder after the fixed share. This is standard Hanafi inheritance law: the patron becomes 'asabah after the residuary heirs by lineage and after fixed shares are allocated.

Wala' of Emancipation as a Cause of Residuary Inheritance
Establishes wala' of emancipation as a cause of عصوبة and its order of inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of inheritance through wala' and determine when the mu'tiq inherits.

Core Idea
The mu'tiq inherits only after all blood agnates and Quranic share heirs have taken their shares.

This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri clarifies the legal status of wala' al-'itaqah as a cause of 'usubah. It establishes a hierarchy: blood agnates exclude the mu'tiq; Quranic share heirs take precedence; the mu'tiq inherits the residue. The commentary also explains the rationale regarding Arab lineages and points to the preferred position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of 'usubah
Distinguish between عصوبة نسبية (blood agnate) and عصوبة سببية (by cause, e.g., wala'). The passage states that wala' is a cause of عصوبة, but blood agnates take priority because their connection is stronger.
Blood agnates include sons, fathers, paternal uncles, etc.
2
Apply the priority rule
The mu'tiq only inherits when there is no blood agnate. Even then, if a Quranic share heir (e.g., daughter, mother) exists, the mu'tiq takes the residue after their fixed share. The passage says: 'إذا لم يكن هناك صاحب فرض في حال' meaning when there is no Quranic share heir at that time.
If the only heirs are a daughter (1/2) and the mu'tiq, the mu'tiq gets the remaining 1/2 as residue.
3
Handle the father's exception
The father has a dual status: he is a Quranic share heir (1/6 when there is a son) and also an 'asabah. Because of this, he excludes the mu'tiq entirely, even though he is a blood agnate. The passage contrasts this with a daughter who only has a fixed share, allowing the mu'tiq to take residue.
The father's case is 'بخلاف الأب، فإن له حال فرض، وحال تعصيب'.

Commentary

Inheritance When Mawla Dies Before Mu'tiq
Ruling on inheritance order when mawla predeceases mu'tiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for inheritance when a mawla dies before his mu'tiq and explain why the estate goes to the mawla's sons, not his daughters.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the wala' right passes only to male agnates; the mu'tiq's inheritance goes to the mawla's sons.

This lesson examines a specific inheritance scenario: a freed slave (mawla) dies before his emancipator (mu'tiq). The mu'tiq inherits from the mawla by wala'. But when the mu'tiq later dies, his estate does not pass to his own children; instead, it goes to the mawla's sons. This ruling highlights the agnatic nature of wala' in Hanafi inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The case involves four parties: the mawla (freed slave) who dies first, the mu'tiq (emancipator) who dies later, the mawla's sons, and the mawla's daughters.
2
Determine the order of death
The mawla dies first, then the mu'tiq dies. This triggers the ruling.
3
Apply the rule of wala'
Wala' is a cause of residuary inheritance (ta'sib). In Hanafi law, only male agnates can inherit through wala'. Thus, the mawla's sons are the residuary heirs.
4
Conclude the distribution
The mu'tiq's estate (including what he inherited from the mawla) goes entirely to the mawla's sons. The mawla's daughters are excluded because women cannot inherit through wala' as asabat.
5
Note cross-madhhab variation
Some other schools may allow daughters to inherit through wala' in certain circumstances, but the Hanafi position restricts it to male agnates.

However, the rights of wala' only pass to male heirs of the mu'tiq who are عصبة (residuary). The reasoning is that wala' creates a bond similar to male agnacy.

Core Idea
A woman's wala' is limited to her own emancipation and the chain from her freed slave.

This passage from Lubab al-Lubab defines the precise boundaries of women's entitlement to wala'. In Hanafi inheritance and clientage law, wala' is a bond of patronage that typically follows male agnates. However, women can acquire wala' in two specific ways, and this lesson unpacks them both.

This second exception is indirect: if woman frees A, then A frees B, woman gets wala' on B.

Core Idea
Women inherit wala' only from their own manumission, not from male relatives.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with two interrelated issues: the order of inheritance when the freed slave dies before or after the master, and more importantly, the extent of women's right to inherit by wala'. The author clarifies that wala' is a form of agnatic inheritance (ta'sib), and therefore women do not normally inherit it. However, there is an exception: women do acquire wala' if they themselves have performed the manumission, directly or indirectly. This ruling is based on a specific hadith from the Prophet (PBUH) mentioned in al-Hidayah.

The text states that women have no share of wala' except what they freed or the chain of their freedmen. This is the core ruling.

Understand the reason: wala' is ta'sib
The text explains that wala' is a form of agnatic inheritance, and women do not inherit by ta'sib except in the specified cases.

The hadith cited in al-Hidayah limits women's wala' to what they freed or the chain.

Women's Wala': The Hadith and Its Hanafi Proof
States the hadith on women's wala' and discusses its chain and acceptance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hadith on women's wala' and explain why the Hanafi school considers it a binding proof despite its weak chain.

Core Idea
Women's wala' is restricted to specific manumission relationships through a mashhur hadith accepted by Hanafis.

The right of inheritance through wala' (patronage) is an important part of Islamic law of inheritance. This lesson examines a hadith that defines exactly how women can acquire wala'. You will see that although the hadith's chain is debated, the Hanafi school accepts it as a strong proof due to its widespread acceptance among the Companions and its continuous practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal ruling in the hadith
Read the hadith text: 'Women have no wala' except from what they free...' This clearly restricts women's wala' to certain actions. Note the negation (ليس) and exception (إلا).
2
Analyze the hadith's chain of transmission
The passage lists criticisms from al-Zayla'i, Ibn Hajar, and al-Ayni who said the hadith is ghareeb or not established as a Prophetic saying. Then it mentions that al-Bayhaqi recorded it as a saying of the Companions (mawquf).
A hadith that is not proven as marfu' (raised to the Prophet) but is authentic as mawquf can still be used as proof in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the Hanafi concept of mashhur
Al-Haskafi considered the hadith strong because it is supported by numerous Companion sayings and continuous practice. For Hanafis, a mashhur hadith is close to mutawatir in strength, even if its individual chain is weak.
The Hanafi classification of hadith differs from the Shafi'i; mashhur here means it was well-known among the early scholars and Companions.

Determine the mufta bihi ruling
Based on the mashhur hadith, the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that women only acquire wala' from those they personally free, or through kitabah/tadbir by them or their freed slaves, etc. They do not inherit wala' from other freed slaves.
This is the operative rule for fatwa today.
5

Inheritance of Wala': Son Excludes Grandsons
Ruling with proof: son excludes grandsons in wala' inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for wala' inheritance to determine which agnatic heir inherits when the mawla leaves a son and grandsons.

Core Idea
In wala', the nearest agnate inherits — a son excludes grandsons.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a specific case in the inheritance of wala' (clientage). When a former master (mawla) frees a slave and then dies leaving both a son and grandsons from another son, who inherits the freedman's estate? The Hanafi ruling is that the son takes it all, to the exclusion of the grandsons. The reasoning involves the principle that wala' is attributed to the female owner and that it follows the nearest agnatic heirs, analogous to lineage but with key differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs
The mawla's surviving heirs are his son and children of another son (grandsons). Both are agnatic heirs ('asabah) of the mawla.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The son inherits the freedman's estate exclusively; the grandsons receive nothing.
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
Understand the basis: wala' follows the closest 'asabah
Wala' inheritance is not limited to the mawla's direct children but goes to his nearest agnatic heirs. Since the son is closer than the grandsons, he excludes them.
The maxim 'al-wala' li al-kubr' (clientage is for the eldest/closest) applies.
4
Contrast with lineage (nasab)
In lineage-based inheritance, grandsons may inherit alongside a son in some cases (e.g., under the doctrine of representation). In wala', the rule is simplified: the nearest agnate takes all.
The text explains that wala' is attributed to the woman owner, not the husband's lineage, which justifies the difference.
5
Apply the principle to new scenarios
Generalize: whoever is the closest agnatic heir among the mawla's 'asabah inherits the freedman's estate. More distant agnates are excluded.
This applies regardless of the number of heirs.

This passage from al-Lubab explains the inheritance of wala' (clientage) after the death of a freedman's master. The ruling states that if the master leaves a son and grandsons (sons of another son), the inheritance goes to the son alone, not the grandsons. The Ruling: Inherited Qisas Against the Father Drops
Ruling on inherited qisas against the father with example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a son inherits qisas against his father and explain why qisas drops and diyah becomes wajib.

Core Idea
A son cannot demand qisas against his father; the right drops and he must claim diyah.

This passage addresses a specific inheritance scenario where the right to qisas (retaliation for murder) passes to a descendant of the killer. The Hanafi school rules that the qisas cannot be enforced because the branch cannot punish its origin, but the diyah (blood money) becomes obligatory. Let us examine the ruling, its proof, and the practical example provided.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Understand the case: a father kills someone, his son inherits the right to qisas against him. The text gives the example: a father kills his wife's father, the wife dies, and her son (the killer's son) inherits the qisas.
The son is both heir of the victim and son of the killer.
2
Apply the principle
The key principle: 'the branch does not deserve punishment against its origin' (الفرع لا يستوجب العقوبة على أصله). This is a fixed legal maxim. Because of this, the son cannot execute his father.
This principle overrides the general right to qisas.
3
State the outcome
Since qisas drops, the son must pay diyah (blood money) instead. The ruling is that the inherited qisas is dropped and diyah is wajib.
The diyah is paid to the remaining heirs, or if none, to the state treasury.

Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf: Initial Establishment vs Inheritance of the Right
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the basis of the son's right to qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's view that the right to qisas is established initially for the son and the view that it is by inheritance, and explain why this difference does not affect the ruling.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa grounds the right in initial establishment; others in inheritance; same legal result.

The earlier passage mentions a subtle point: according to Sadr al-Shari'ah, the son's right to qisas is not inherited but established initially (ibtida'an) in Abu Hanifa's view. This lesson explores this intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and shows how differing legal reasoning can still lead to the same ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
One view: the son inherits the right from the deceased (the mother). Another view (Abu Hanifa): the right is established directly for the son because he is the next of kin, not through inheritance.
The text says 'according to Abu Hanifa, its establishment for the son is initially, not by inheritance.'
2
Analyze the basis
The inheritance view considers the right a property right that passes like any inheritance. Abu Hanifa views it as a personal right of retaliation that attaches to the closest relative, not transferred via inheritance.
This difference stems from different conceptions of qisas: as a claim of the victim's estate or as a personal right.
3
Determine the practical consequence
Despite the different bases, the ruling is identical: the son cannot execute his father; he must take diyah. The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is absent here.
This is an example of an ikhtilaf that does not change the fatwa (ikhtilaf fi al-ta'lil la fi al-hukm).
