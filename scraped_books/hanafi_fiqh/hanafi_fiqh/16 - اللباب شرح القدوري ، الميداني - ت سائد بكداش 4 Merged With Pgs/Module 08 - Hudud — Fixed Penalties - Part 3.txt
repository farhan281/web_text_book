Ta'zir for Insulting Nobles vs. Commoners: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ta'zir for insulting commoners.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on ta'zir for insulting commoners and evaluate the opposing view based on the principle of harm and distress.

Core Idea
Ta'zir for insult depends on the victim's distress, not social class.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about whether ta'zir is administered for insulting commoners. The base text from al-Hidayah holds that ta'zir is not given to commoners, but later Hanafi scholars like al-Hindawani and Ibn 'Abidin argue that the key factor is the victim's personal distress, not their social status. This dispute illustrates the Hanafi method of ta'lil (reasoning) and shows how later jurists expand the scope of a ruling by refining the operative cause.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling from al-Hidayah
The text says that ta'zir is given for insulting nobles (ashraf) because they feel distress, but for commoners ('amma) no ta'zir is administered, and this is considered 'better' (ahsanu).
2
Identify the 'illa (effective cause) for the distinction
The reason is that nobles feel distress (wahsha) from insults, while commoners allegedly do not due to their rough nature.
This distinction is based on the nature of the victim, not the act itself.
3
Examine the counter-argument by later Hanafis
Al-Hindawani, Abu al-Su'ud, and Ibn 'Abidin argue that the general principle 'any harm to a Muslim without right requires ta'zir' applies, and many commoners also experience distress.
They prioritize the general rule over the specific classification.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response that reconciles both views
The term 'ashraf' refers to those of noble character, regardless of social class. If a commoner has refined nature, he is treated as ashraf and ta'zir applies; if he is base, he deserves no ta'zir because he brought the insult upon himself.
This shows the flexibility of the term 'ashraf' and the emphasis on actual harm.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is whether a judge should order ta'zir for insulting a commoner. According to the first view, no; according to the second, yes if the person is distressed. The later view is more protective of dignity and is often adopted in modern fatwa.
The mufta bihi today leans toward the expansive view.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute. The base ruling states that ta'zir is administered for insulting nobles because they feel distressed, but for commoners it is not administered, and this is deemed better (الاحسن). The commentary from Ibn 'Abidin reports that al-Hindawani and Abu al-Su'ud consider ta'zir obligatory for insulting anyone, as per the principle that any harm to a Muslim requires ta'zir. Ibn 'Abidin further argues that many people of noble character experience distress even more than jurists, so the ruling should be extended. The response to the specification of 'ashraf' is that it refers to those of refined nature, and if a commoner has such nature, ta'zir applies; otherwise, if having bad character, the insult does not cause distress and thus no ta'zir. This shows the Hanafi principle that ta'zir depends on actual harm and distress.

Definition and Limits of Ta'zir
Defines ta'zir and limits its maximum by analogy with slave's hadd.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ta'zir and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on its maximum limit, including the proofs of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Ta'zir must be less than the minimum hadd: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad set its maximum at 39 lashes.

Ta'zir is a discretionary punishment for crimes that do not have a fixed hadd. This passage from al-Lubab defines ta'zir and then dives into an important internal Hanafi debate: what is the maximum number of lashes? The majority positions use analogy from the slave's hadd in qadhf, while Abu Yusuf allows more. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi fiqh correctly.

Guided Path
1
Define ta'zir linguistically and legally
Extract the linguistic meaning (ta'dib – discipline) and the legal meaning (punishment below the hadd) from the text.
The legal definition is key: it must not reach a fixed hadd.
2
Identify the stated maximum and minimum
The text states: maximum 39 lashes, minimum 3 lashes. Note that the minimum is not debated here.
The focus is on the maximum.
3
Trace the proof for 39 lashes
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad use qiyas: the minimum hadd is the slave's hadd in qadhf (40). Ta'zir must be less, so they reduce by one to 39.
This ensures ta'zir does not equal or exceed a fixed hadd.
4
Understand Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Abu Yusuf allows up to 75 lashes. His proof is the hadith 'Whoever reaches a hadd in a non-hadd is a transgressor', but he interprets that as forbidding reaching the freeperson's hadd (80), not the slave's.
Thus his maximum is 75 (80 minus 5 as a safety margin, or just below 80).
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: a judge imposing ta'zir must know which view to follow. The reliedupon position is 39 lashes (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad).
The text does not explicitly choose, but the majority is followed.

Maximum Ta'zir Lashes: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ta'zir maximum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on the maximum ta'zir lashes and explain the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
Ta'zir must always be less than the lowest hadd; the maximum depends on which baseline you choose.

In this passage, we explore an important intra-Hanafi dispute: what is the maximum number of lashes that can be inflicted as a ta'zir punishment? While all Hanafis agree that ta'zir must be less than any fixed hadd punishment, they differ on which hadd serves as the baseline. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani argue that the maximum is 39 lashes—one less than the slave's hadd for qadhf (40 lashes). Abu Yusuf, however, holds that the maximum can be as high as 75 lashes, based on the free person's hadd (80 lashes) minus five, citing a report from Ali ibn Abi Talib. Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school reasons by analogy (qiyas) and the role of hadith in setting limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What exactly is being disputed? The maximum number of lashes for ta'zir punishment.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Maximum 39 lashes. Why? The lowest hadd is 40 (slave's qadhf), so subtract one to avoid reaching that limit.
Muhammad agrees with Abu Hanifa.
3
State Abu Yusuf's position
Maximum 75 lashes (or 79 in one narration). Why? Default is freedom; the free person's qadhf hadd is 80, so subtract five following a report from Ali.
Zufar also holds this view.
4
Identify the common proof
Both sides rely on the hadith: 'Whoever reaches a hadd in a non-hadd matter is a transgressor.' They agree ta'zir must be less than a hadd, but differ on which hadd is the relevant minimum.
The hadith is ahad, zanni al-thubut.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (39 lashes), as it is more cautious and prevalent in Hanafi fatwa collections.
Al-Hidayah presents this as the stronger view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi minimum for ta'zir lashes and identify the four permissible forms of ta'zir mentioned in the text, including the dispute over slapping.

Core Idea
Ta'zir is discretionary, but the Hanafi school sets a minimum of three lashes to ensure deterrence.

This passage from al-Quduri and its commentaries establishes the minimum lashes for ta'zir as three, citing the need for effective deterrence. It then expands to list various corporal and non-corporal forms of ta'zir, while noting a disagreement among later scholars about whether the minimum is fixed or subject to judicial discretion. A key point of debate is whether slapping is permissible as ta'zir, with Imam al-Sarakhsi arguing against it due to the level of humiliation it entails.

2

This reflects the principle of ta'zir being flexible to suit the offender and offense.

Catalog the permissible forms of ta'zir
List all forms mentioned in al-Mujtaba: imprisonment, slapping on the neck, ear-rubbing, harsh speech, a frowning look, and verbal abuse (excluding qadhf).
These show that ta'zir is not limited to lashes.
4
Analyze al-Sarakhsi's objection to slapping
Understand his reasoning: slapping is an extreme form of humiliation and should be avoided for Muslims (ahl al-qibla).
This is a minority opinion within the Hanafi school.

The relied-upon position is the three-lash minimum, but with flexibility for the judge. Slapping is generally permissible but discouraged by some.

Ta'zir lashes must deter; minimum is three, but the imam adjusts based on the offender.

Permanent Rejection of a Muslim Qadhif's Testimony
Ruling with Quranic proof for permanent rejection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the testimony of a Muslim flogged for qadhf is permanently rejected even after repentance, and cite Quran 24:4 as the proof.

Core Idea
Quran 24:4's 'never accept their testimony' proves permanent rejection; the exception applies only to the fisq, not the testimony.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on the testimony of a Muslim who has been punished for false accusation of zina. The school holds that such a person's testimony is permanently inadmissible, even if he repents. The basis is the absolute wording of Quran 24:4, which the Hanafis interpret as unconditional.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal consequence
The act is being a Muslim who committed qadhf and received the hadd punishment. The consequence is that his testimony is rejected (saqatat shahadatuhu), and this rejection is permanent even if he repents (wa in taba).
The ruling applies only after the hadd has been carried out; mere accusation without punishment does not trigger permanent rejection.
2
Locate the Quranic proof
The author cites Quran 24:4: 'And never accept their testimony' (wa la taqbaloo lahum shahadatan abadan). The word 'abadan' (never) indicates perpetuity.
The full verse refers to those who accuse chaste women but cannot produce four witnesses; their testimony is rejected and they are declared sinners.
3
Understand the Hanafi interpretation of the exception
The next verse (24:5) says 'except those who repent...' The Hanafis interpret this exception as returning to the declaration of fisq (sinfulness) only, not to the rejection of testimony. Thus the rejection remains even after repentance.
This interpretation is a key point of ikhtilaf with the Shafi'i school, which holds that repentance restores testimony.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school argues that the exception applies to both the sin and the testimony, so repentance lifts the rejection. The Hanafi position relies on the grammatical structure of the verse.
The practical difference is whether a repentant qadhif can testify in court. Hanafi mufta bihi: no.
5
Apply to the non-Muslim case
The same verse does not apply to a non-Muslim who receives the hadd before Islam. His testimony after conversion is a new act, not covered by the permanent rejection.
This distinction will be examined in the next lesson.

Acceptance of a Non-Muslim Qadhif's Testimony After Conversion
Ruling with reasoning for accepting testimony after conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school accepts the testimony of a non-Muslim flogged for qadhf after he converts to Islam.

Core Idea
A non-Muslim's testimony given after Islam is a new legal act, not covered by the permanent rejection in Quran 24:4, which addresses believers.

This lesson addresses a subtle but important distinction: a non-Muslim who received the hadd for qadhf before embracing Islam can later testify in an Islamic court. The Hanafi reasoning is that the prior hadd does not create a permanent disqualification because the Quranic rejection (Quran 24:4) was directed at believers who committed qadhf. After conversion, any testimony is a fresh act with a new legal capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the ruling
The subject is a non-Muslim (kafir) who has been flogged for qadhf (hadd al-qadhf) and then later converts to Islam. The ruling is: his testimony is accepted (qabilat shahadatuhu).
This is in contrast to the Muslim qadhif, whose testimony is permanently rejected.
2
Understand the reasoning for acceptance
The reason given is: 'because he gained this testimony after Islam, so it is not included under the rejection' (li'anna hadhihi al-shahada istafadaha ba'da al-Islam, falam tadkhul tahta al-radd). The disqualification from Quran 24:4 applies to those who were Muslim at the time of the offense and punishment. A non-Muslim's testimony after Islam is a new entitlement.
The hadd punishment for qadhf is carried out regardless of faith, but the permanent rejection is a consequence attached to the Muslim who commits the crime. For a non-Muslim, the rejection does not carry over after conversion.
3
Distinguish from the Muslim qadhif
The Muslim qadhif's testimony is rejected permanently because the Quranic verse ('la taqbaloo lahum shahadatan abadan') addresses believers. The non-Muslim, at the time of the hadd, was not a believer; thus the verse's scope does not include him. His conversion creates a new legal personality for testimony.
This distinction is based on the addressee of the verse (the Muslim community) and the new status after Islam.
4
Apply the principle to understand consistency
The Hanafi school is consistent: the permanent rejection is a penalty attached to the Muslim who violates the law. A non-Muslim who converts is not subject to that penalty because his former state was not bound by the same Quranic address.
If a non-Muslim converts and then commits qadhf as a Muslim, he would then be subject to the permanent rejection.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies a key condition for the hadd of theft when multiple individuals collaborate. The matn by al-Quduri states that if a group steals and each share reaches ten dirhams, all are amputated; otherwise, none. Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling

The key condition is that amputation is wajib for each thief based on his own crime. Therefore, each thief's share must individually reach the nisab of ten dirhams. If any share falls short, that thief escapes the hadd.

Cases Where the Hand is Not Cut in Theft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of items exempt from the hadd of cutting in Hanafi law and explain the proof and reasoning for each.

Core Idea
The hand is not cut for stealing worthless, mubah, quickly spoiling items, or unharvested crops because ownership and protection are incomplete.

In the Book of Theft, al-Quduri presents the general rule that the hand is cut for stealing a nisab from a secure place. However, certain items are excluded because they are either of negligible value, permissible for all, or prone to quick spoilage. This lesson examines those exceptions and their proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the negative ruling and its scope
First, note that the text begins by stating 'wa la yuqṭaʿ' – the hand is not cut. This is a negative ruling (hukm salbi) that applies to specific categories of items. You must understand that this is an exception to the general rule of cutting for theft.
The general rule is that cutting applies when all conditions (nisab, hirz, ownership) are met.
2
Examine the first category: tāfih (worthless) and mubāḥ (permissible) items
The text lists items like wood, reeds, grass, fish, birds, game, and minerals (maghrah, nurah, zarnikh). These are considered tāfih because they are of low value or mubāḥ because they are originally permissible to all. The reasoning is that people do not guard such items strongly, so the severe deterrent is unnecessary.
The key principle: if an item is naturally abundant or not exclusive property, the need for a deterrent diminishes.
3

Items like fresh fruits, meat, milk, and watermelon are exempt. The proof is the hadith 'lā qaṭʿa fī al-ṭaʿām' (no cutting in food). Hanafi scholars interpret this as applying to foods that spoil quickly, because grains and sugar (which do not spoil) are unanimously subject to cutting.
The dalil is mursal but accepted; the interpretation is based on istithnā' (exception) by consensus.
4

The text exempts crops that have not been harvested and fruit still on trees. The reason given is that the ḥirz (secure place) is incomplete – fields and trees are not considered secure storage. Thus, the condition of proper hirz is not met.
This is a separate ground from value or perishability; it relates to the security of the item.
5

All three exemptions share a common rationale: the item either lacks significant value (tafih), lacks strong private ownership (mubah), or lacks proper security (hirz). The hadd is designed for serious infringements on clearly protected property, so where protection is weak, the punishment is lifted.
This shows the Hanafi school's careful calibration of punishments to the gravity of the violation.

In the Hanafi school, cutting applies when a thief drives a loaded animal out of the hirz because the animal's movement is legally attributed to the thief's act of driving.

Group Theft: All Participants Are Liable for Hadd
Ruling that all participants in a group theft are liable for hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on group theft and explain why all participants, not just the taker, are subject to hadd amputation.

Core Idea
In group theft, all participants are liable for hadd, not just the one who physically takes.

This ruling addresses accomplices in theft. You might think only the person who actually removes the property deserves punishment, but Hanafi law extends liability to all who assist, even if they don't touch the goods. Let's examine the reasoning and its practical effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? A group enters the hirz (secure storage) and only some physically take the property. The ruling: all are cut (amputated).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational: the act of removal (ikhraj) is attributed to each participant through assistance (mu'awanah). Customarily, some carry goods while others defend, so the removal is a collective act.
This is a form of istihsan based on custom and preventing circumvention of the hadd.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does this prove the ruling? Because if only the taker were cut, the hadd could easily be avoided by having one person take while others assist. The principle of 'sadd bab al-hadd' (closing the door to evasion) demands all participants be liable.
The key link is that assistance makes each participant a cause of the removal.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require that each person individually take property to be liable. For them, mere assistance might not suffice. This is a legitimate difference in ijtihad.
Hanafi position is based on broader purpose of implementing hadd.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that all are cut. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah and consistent with the principle of deterrence.
The ruling is strong because it prevents a loophole.

Commentary
This passage deals with group theft: if a group enters the hirz together and only some physically take the property, all members of the group are subject to cutting. The reasoning is that the act of removal (ikhraj) is attributed to each of them due to mutual assistance (mu'awanah). The typical modus operandi is that some carry goods while others guard or fight, so if only the taker were cut, the hadd penalty would be easily avoided. Thus, to uphold the purpose of the punishment (hadd), all participants are liable.

حرز (hirz): A secure place where property is stored, e.g., a locked house or shop
