Grandfather's Intercourse with Grandson's Slave Woman: The Role of Wilaya
Ruling on grandfather's intercourse with grandson's slave woman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a grandfather's claim to lineage from his grandson's slave woman is invalid when the father is alive, and apply the principle of wilaya hierarchy.

Core Idea
Grandfather has no guardianship over his son's property while the son lives, so lineage from grandson's slave is invalid.

This lesson examines a specific case from al-Quduri on the grandfather's intercourse with his grandson's slave woman. The Hanafi ruling turns on the absence of wilaya (guardianship) for the grandfather when his own son (the father of the grandson) is alive. We will explore why the grandfather cannot establish paternity in this scenario and how the hierarchy of guardianship operates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
The grandfather (jadd) is the father's father. The grandson is his son's son. The father (the grandfather's son) is still alive.
The presence of the living father is the key condition.
2
State the act and its consequence
The grandfather has intercourse with the grandson's slave woman. According to the text, lineage is not established from the grandfather.
This means the child is not attributed to the grandfather, and she does not become umm walad for him.
3
Identify the reason
The text gives the reason: 'because the grandfather has no guardianship (wilaya) while the father is present.'
Wilaya here refers to authority over the property of minors or slaves.
4
Explain the principle of wilaya hierarchy
In Hanafi law, the father has primary wilaya over his child's property. The grandfather only gains wilaya if the father is absent or incapacitated. Since the father (the grandfather's son) is alive, the grandfather lacks authority over the grandson's slave.
This principle also applies to other guardianship matters, e.g., marriage of minors.

Rulings on the Owner's Obligations in Istilad
States owner obligations and proof via qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three obligations of an owner when his slave woman bears his child and explain the legal reasoning (qiyas) behind them.

Core Idea
The owner pays the slave's value but not 'uqr nor child value because ownership retroactively validates the intercourse.

In this lesson, we examine the rules of istilad: when a man's slave woman gives birth to his child. The matn states three clear obligations: payment of the slave's value, no 'uqr (dower), and no child value. The commentary reveals the qiyas-based reasoning that the need to preserve lineage overrides the need to preserve wealth, drawing an analogy to acquiring food in dire necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three rulings
Read the matn: 'وعليه قيمتها، وليس عليه عُقْرُها، ولا قيمة ولدها'. List the obligations and exemptions.
The obligations are on the owner, not the slave.
2
Understand the basis of the value payment
The commentary explains the value is compensatory (ضمان تملك), not a penal or contractual payment. It is due on the day of conception because ownership transfers at that moment.
The owner's wealth status does not affect the amount.
3
Analyze why 'uqr is not owed
Ownership of the slave girl is established retroactively from before conception (لثبوت الملك مستنداً لما قبل العلوق). This makes the intercourse lawful, so no 'uqr (compensation for doubtful intercourse) is required.
'Uqr is only due when intercourse occurs with shubhah; here it is in valid ownership.
4
Analyze why child's value is not owed
The child is conceived from a free father (حُرَّ الأصل). Since the father is free, the child is born free and no compensation for the child is due.
The child is not owned, so no value is paid.
5
Apply the qiyas to necessity
The commentary analogizes: the need to protect one's fluid (preserve lineage) is greater than the need to preserve one's life. Just as one may take food without payment in dire need, one acquires the slave girl by paying her value.
The 'illa is the greater need, overriding normal property rules.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in the chapter of istilad (claims of paternity through slave girls). The text establishes that a paternal grandfather cannot claim paternity of a child born to his grandson's slave girl as long as the father (his son) is alive. The key legal principle is the absence of guardianship (wilayah) over the grandson's property when the father exists.

Wilayah is a prerequisite for establishing paternity in this context.

Paternity Claim by a Co-Owner of a Slave Girl
Ruling with proof on joint ownership and paternity claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a joint owner's claim establishes paternity over the entire child and distinguish the two Hanafi positions on the umm walad consequence.

Core Idea
Lineage is indivisible; a child cannot be partitioned between owners.

This passage addresses a scenario where a slave girl is co-owned and she gives birth. When one partner claims the child, does paternity follow only his share or the whole? Using qiyas and the principle of indivisibility of conception (ʿiluq), the Hanafi school resolves the issue, though a difference arises between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on the immediate status of the slave girl as umm walad. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning and the practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basic ruling
Read the matn: the child's lineage is established from the claiming partner, and the slave girl becomes his umm walad. Note that this is unanimous among the three Hanafi imams on the final result.
2
Identify the core proof
The commentary explains the reasoning via qiyas: because conception (ʿiluq) is indivisible (lā yatajazzā), the lineage cannot be split. Therefore, paternity established in the claimant's share necessarily extends to the partner's share.
The key phrase: 'العلوق لا يتجزأ' (conception is indivisible).
3
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds that only the claimant's share becomes umm walad immediately; he must then purchase the other share. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the entire slave girl becomes umm walad at once because istilād (begetting a child) is indivisible.
The difference is in the mechanism, not the final outcome.
4
Apply the muftā bihī position
For fatwa, the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad is often preferred because it aligns with the indivisibility principle without requiring a subsequent purchase. Understand the practical result: the slave girl becomes fully umm walad, and the partner is compensated.
Refer to Al-Hidayah's tarjih.

Master's Claim of Lineage from Mukātab's Slave-Girl: The Requirement of Confirmation

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The mukātab's confirmation is required for lineage because the master cannot own his acquisitions — a distinction from a father's claim over his son's property.

This passage from Lubāb al-Hidāya addresses a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: can a master claim lineage from a child born to his mukātab's slave-girl without the mukātab's consent? The ẓāhir al-riwāya requires the mukātab's confirmation, while Abu Yusuf disagrees. Understanding the reasoning behind each position reveals deeper principles about property rights and the limits of qiyas (analogy) in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
The master has intercourse with his mukātab's slave-girl. She gives birth. The master claims the child. What happens next depends on the mukātab's response.
The passage also clarifies that the pregnancy must have occurred during joint ownership (master + mukātab) for the claim to be about istīlād, not just emancipation.
2
State the ẓāhir al-riwāya ruling
If the mukātab confirms, lineage is established, and the master must pay 'uqr and the child's value, but the slave-girl does not become an umm walad. If the mukātab denies, lineage is not established.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Present Abu Yusuf's counter-argument
Abu Yusuf analogizes to a father claiming the child of his son's slave-girl: there, the father's claim is accepted without the son's confirmation. Similarly, the master's claim should suffice without the mukātab's confirmation.
The analogy (qiyas) is based on the shared feature of a superior claiming a child from a subordinate's property.

The commentarists distinguish the two cases: a father can own his son's property, so the son's ownership is not threatened. But a master cannot own the mukātab's acquisitions (kasb). Accepting the master's claim without confirmation would nullify the mukātab's ownership over his kasb, which is protected by the kitāba contract.
Therefore, the mukātab's confirmation is necessary to preserve his property rights.
5

Under ẓāhir al-riwāya, if the mukātab denies, the child remains with the mukātab as his property. Under Abu Yusuf, the child becomes free and belongs to the master, who must pay the value. The difference impacts the mukātab's ownership rights and the financial outcome.

Overview of the Book of Marriage (Kitab al-Nikah)
Table of contents for the marriage chapter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the main sections of the Hanafi Book of Marriage in their logical order and identify the key legal themes.

Core Idea
The Hanafi Book of Marriage systematically arranges rulings from contract formation to prohibited spouses to financial rights and special cases.

This lesson presents the table of contents for the Book of Marriage (Kitab al-Nikah) from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri. Although it lists only section headings, it provides a valuable roadmap for understanding the Hanafi rulings on marriage. By studying the sequence and grouping of topics, you will gain insight into the jurisprudential priorities and logical flow of Hanafi family law. Use this overview as a framework for the detailed lessons that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three broad phases of marriage
Group the headings into prerequisites (witnesses, guardianship), prohibitions (permanent and temporary), and contractual elements (offer/acceptance, compatibility, mahr).
This grouping helps see the overall structure.
2
Note the specific rulings that appear early
The first two headings are about witnesses, indicating that witness conditions are foundational for a valid marriage contract.
3
Recognize the inclusion of controversial topics
Headings like 'نكاح الشغار' and 'نكاح المتعة' show that Hanafi fiqh addresses both valid and invalid contracts for completeness.
Presence in the TOC does not imply endorsement.
4
Observe the progression from general to specific
The chapter moves from general conditions (witnesses, prohibitions) to specific cases (marriage of a slave, insane woman).
This mirrors the typical jurisprudential method.
