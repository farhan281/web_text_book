Li'an Not Required for a Mute's Accusation
States ruling and proof for mute's accusation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mute's accusation and explain why li'an does not apply, citing the principle that hudud are averted by doubts.

Core Idea
A mute's accusation is not explicit; doubt (shubha) prevents li'an and hadd.

The Hanafi school holds that li'an (mutual imprecation) is a procedure that requires explicit verbal accusation. A mute person, who communicates by gesture or writing, cannot provide such explicit expression. Moreover, his accusation carries inherent doubt, and the well-established principle is that hudud penalties are averted by doubts. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this ruling and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a mute husband accusing his wife of adultery. The ruling is that li'an does not apply.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The text gives two proofs: li'an requires explicit statement (tasrih), similar to the hadd of qadhf; and the accusation of a mute is not free from doubt, so hudud are averted.
The maxim 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubhat' is derived from a hadith.
3
Understand the analogy to qadhf
The text states that li'an is linked to tasrih, just like the hadd punishment for false accusation (qadhf). Since a mute's accusation carries ambiguity, the hadd of qadhf is also not imposed.
4
Recognize the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools, such as the Shafi'i, may consider a mute's gesture accompanied by an oath sufficient for li'an. This highlights a key point of ikhtilaf.

Commentary

Denial of Pregnancy: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Li'an
Intra-Hanafi dispute on li'an for denial of uncertain pregnancy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on li'an when a husband denies pregnancy, explain the reasoning of each, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Li'an not triggered by denial of uncertain pregnancy; accusation must be explicit at the moment.

This lesson examines a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute about when a husband's denial of paternity constitutes an accusation of adultery (qadhf) requiring li'an. Abu Hanifa and Zufar hold that without certainty of pregnancy at the time of denial, no li'an is required. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that if birth within six months proves the pregnancy existed, then denial becomes an accusation, triggering li'an.

Guided Path
1
State the baseline Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa and Zufar rule that no li'an is required when a husband says 'this pregnancy is not from me' even if she gives birth within six months.
The key is uncertainty of pregnancy at the time of statement.
2
Identify the opposing intra-Hanafi view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) argue that if birth occurs within six months, it proves pregnancy existed at the time, making the denial an accusation of adultery, so li'an becomes obligatory.
They focus on the retroactive certainty of pregnancy.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Sahibayn
The response: at the moment of speech, the husband is not a qadhib (accuser) because pregnancy is uncertain. Qadhf (hadd accusation) cannot be suspended on a future condition (ta'liq).
Ta'liq is invalid in hudud matters.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its basis
Later authorities (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shari'ah) preferred Abu Hanifa's view, making it the relied-upon position.
The tarjih is based on the principle that an accusation must be certain at the time it is made.

Conditions for Valid Disavowal of Paternity
Ruling with conditions and reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three windows for valid disavowal and explain why delay establishes lineage.

Core Idea
Silence on news of birth is tacit acknowledgment; disavowal must be immediate or within the customary congratulation period.

This lesson clarifies when a husband can legally deny paternity of a newborn. The Hanafi school sets strict time limits because the law presumes a father's acknowledgment through silence and celebration. Understanding these windows is essential for protecting lineage rights and avoiding false accusations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three valid disavowal windows
Locate the three scenarios in the text: immediately after birth, during the customary congratulations period, or when childbirth apparatus is purchased. These define the narrow timeframe.
The congratulations period is customarily seven days.
2
Understand the legal effect of valid disavowal
If the husband disavows within any of these windows, his disavowal is legally valid (sahih). However, he must then perform li'an because his accusation makes him a slanderer.
Li'an is the mutual oath-taking between spouses that severs the marriage and negates lineage.
3
Analyze the consequences of delay
If the husband remains silent beyond the window, his silence and acceptance of congratulations are treated as implicit acknowledgment of the child. Hence the lineage is established and cannot be undone by later disavowal.
Even if he later performs li'an, the lineage remains fixed according to Abu Hanifa.
4
Trace the reasoning (talil)
The ruling rests on two premises: (a) the husband needs to disavow a child that is not his, and (b) if he does not act promptly, his conduct (silence, accepting congratulations) implies consent. This is a form of istidlal (inference) from customary practice.
This is the view of Abu Hanifa, recorded in the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Iddah of a Slave Widow
Ruling with talil (reasoning) for slave widow's 'iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the 'iddah of a slave widow and explain the reasoning using the principle that slavery halves obligations.

Core Idea
Slavery halves the free widow's 'iddah: 4 months 10 days becomes 2 months 5 days.

In this brief passage, al-Quduri completes the 'iddah rulings for widows by addressing the case of the slave woman. The key principle applied here—that slavery halves legal obligations—has been used consistently in preceding sections. Understanding this ruling requires seeing how the free widow's period is halved exactly, not approximated, and recognizing that this is an application of qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).

Guided Path
1
Recall the free widow's 'iddah
Start with the established ruling: a free widow observes 'iddah for four months and ten days (Quran 2:234).
This is the basis for the analogy.
2
Identify the principle of tanseef
The text states 'slavery halves' (ar-raqq munassif). This principle is applied to various obligations like 'iddah and hudud, meaning that a slave's obligation is half of a free person's where the ruling is based on a textual command that does not explicitly mention slaves.
This principle was established earlier in the text.
3
Apply the principle to the free widow's 'iddah
Half of four months and ten days is two months and five days (4 months ÷ 2 = 2 months; 10 days ÷ 2 = 5 days). Conclude that the slave widow's 'iddah is exactly two months and five days.
Ensure the half is taken precisely, not rounded.
4
Note the evidentiary basis
Recognize that this ruling is not from a direct text but from qiyas jaliy on the free widow's verse, with the illa (cause) being slavery, which halves obligations. The Hanafi school considers this analogy strong enough to establish a wajib obligation.
Contrast with other schools that may require a direct text.

Iddah of a Widow: Free, Slave, and Pregnant
States the 'iddah periods for free, slave, and pregnant widows with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the 'iddah periods for free, slave, and pregnant widows, and explain the Hanafi proofs for each.

Core Idea
The Qur'an prescribes fixed 'iddah periods for widows; the Hanafi school applies halving for slave women based on the principle that slavery halves rulings.

This lesson covers the waiting period ('iddah) for a widow. The duration differs depending on whether she is free, a slave, or pregnant. The rulings are firmly grounded in the Qur'an, and the Hanafi school extends them using qiyas (analogy) to account for the status of slavery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
List the three types of widows mentioned: free, slave, and pregnant. Note their respective 'iddah periods: 4 months 10 days, 2 months 5 days, and until delivery.
For a slave who does not menstruate, the period is 1.5 months.
2
Locate the primary textual proof
For the free widow, the proof is Q. 2:234, which explicitly states 'four months and ten days'. This is a decisive (qat'i) text in both transmission and meaning.
For the pregnant woman, Q. 65:4 is the proof: 'their term is that they give birth'.
3
Trace the reasoning for the slave
The slave's 'iddah is half that of a free woman because 'slavery halves' (al-raqq munassif). This is a qiyas based on other half-rulings in fiqh (e.g., hadd punishments, divorce counts).
Menstruation cannot be halved, so for a non-menstruating slave, the month-based period is halved.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may hold that the slave widow's 'iddah is also 4 months 10 days, arguing the verse is general. The Hanafi school upholds the half due to the principle of slavery.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi ruling relies on the explicit text for free women and a well-established qiyas for slaves. The commentary confirms this as the mufta bihi position.
No tarjih is needed as the school is unanimous.

The 'Iddah of a Divorced Woman Who Inherits: The Longer of Two Terms
States the ruling for a divorced woman who inherits from her husband if divorced during illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the 'iddah of a divorced woman who inherits from her deceased husband due to illness divorce, explain why the longer term is taken, and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
When a divorced woman inherits due to a deathbed divorce, her 'iddah is the longer of the death 'iddah and divorce 'iddah.

In Hanafi fiqh, a unique situation arises when a man divorces his wife irrevocably during his final illness to deprive her of inheritance, but she ends up inheriting anyway if he dies within her 'iddah. How should she observe her 'iddah? Abu Hanifah rules that she takes the longer of the two possible 'iddahs as a precaution. This lesson unpacks that ruling, its rationale, and related details about menopause.

Guided Path
1
Identify the woman's status
Understand that she is an irrevocably divorced woman (mutallaqah ba'in) who was divorced during her husband's terminal illness.
The divorce is ba'in, not raj'i, so she cannot return without a new marriage.
2
Recognize the reason for inheritance
Recognize that the divorce was a flight (firar) from inheritance, but since he died within her 'iddah, she inherits under Hanafi law.
This is an exception to the general rule that a divorced woman does not inherit.
3
Recall the two possible 'iddahs
Recall that a widow observes 'iddah of death (4 months 10 days) and a divorced woman observes 'iddah of divorce (3 menstrual cycles or equivalent).
Both are wajib individually.
4
Apply the rule of precaution
Apply Abu Hanifah's ruling: she must take the longer of the two terms, starting with 4 months 10 days from death, then if no menstruation occurred during that period, add 3 more menstrual cycles.
This is precautionary (ihtiyat) to ensure full observance.
5
Consider the menopause extension
If her menstrual cycles are prolonged or she reaches menopause, her 'iddah continues until menopause or until she reaches the age agreed by ijtihad.
The age of menopause is disputed within the Hanafi school.

Abu Hanifa's ruling establishes that her iddah is not simply one fixed period but the longer of the two possible iddahs — the widow's iddah (four months and ten days) and the divorced woman's iddah (three menstrual cycles). Intra-Hanafi dispute on 'iddah of minor's pregnant wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the 'iddah of a minor's pregnant wife and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A confirmed pregnancy at the death of a minor husband ends the 'iddah with delivery, per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, despite the minor's inability to father.

This passage from al-Maydani's commentary on al-Quduri addresses a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: when a minor who cannot father children dies, and his wife is pregnant, what is her 'iddah? Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule that the Quranic verse on pregnant women applies generally, so her 'iddah ends with delivery. Abu Yusuf argues that because the child cannot be attributed to the minor, the 'iddah should be four months and ten days. This lesson unpacks their proofs and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its context
Restate the ruling from the matn: if a minor dies leaving a pregnant wife, her 'iddah ends with delivery. Note that this applies only when the pregnancy is confirmed (birth within six months of death).
The minor is defined as someone incapable of impregnation.
2
Locate the primary evidence
Find the Quranic proof cited in the passage: Surah At-Talaq 65:4, which states that the term for pregnant women is delivery. Explain how this verse is general and applies even to the minor's wife.
The verse is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
State Abu Yusuf's position: 'iddah is four months and ten days because the pregnancy cannot be legally attributed to the minor. Contrast with Abu Hanifa and Muhammad who maintain that the marriage itself creates the attribution.
Abu Yusuf's reasoning: the child's lineage is not established, so it is like a pregnancy after death.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Identify the authorities that side with Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: Jamal al-Islam, al-Burhani, an-Nasafi, and others. This is the relied-upon view.
The later tarjih is for the view of the two (qawlahuma) over Abu Yusuf's.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Work out the practical difference: if the wife delivers before four months and ten days, she completes 'iddah earlier per Abu Hanifa/Muhammad, while Abu Yusuf requires the full period.
This affects when she can remarry and her inheritance rights afterwards.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute from the commentary of al-Maydani on al-Quduri's matn. It addresses the 'iddah of a minor's wife who is pregnant at his death. The matn states that if a minor dies and his wife has an apparent pregnancy, her 'iddah ends with delivery. The commentator explains that this ruling is qualified: it applies only if the wife is an umm walad (a slave who bore her master's child) because a mere slave or mudabbara has no 'iddah upon the master's death due to lack of a marital relationship (they are not a 'marital bed'). The same applies if she is married to someone else or already in 'iddah, as she is not his bed. The Quranic proof is the general verse from Surah at-Talaq (65:4). Al-Hidayah reports that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that if the pregnancy is confirmed (birth within six months of death), the 'iddah ends with delivery. Abu Yusuf, however, argues that her 'iddah is four months and ten days because the pregnancy is not legally attributed to the minor (since he cannot father), so it is like a pregnancy occurring after death. The commentator notes that Jamal al-Islam, al-Burhani, an-Nasafi, and others deem Abu Hanifa's and Muhammad's view correct (al-mufta bihi). If the pregnancy is not confirmed (e.g., birth after six months), all agree on the default 'iddah of death. If pregnancy occurs after death, the 'iddah is four months and ten days regardless, and the child's lineage is not established from the minor because he has no semen; the marriage cannot produce conception.

Ruling on ʿIddah When Divorced During Menstruation
Ruling that partial menstruation at divorce does not count for ʿiddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ʿiddah when a divorce occurs during menstruation and explain why that menstrual period does not count.

Core Idea
A divorce during menstruation does not count that incomplete cycle toward ʿiddah.

This lesson examines a specific Hanafi ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri: if a woman is divorced while menstruating, that menstrual period is not counted in her ʿiddah. Understanding this helps you grasp the precise requirement of a 'complete menstrual cycle' for ʿiddah, avoiding a common misconception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The act is divorce (talaq) occurring while the woman is in a state of menstruation (hayḍ).
The ruling does not depend on the type of divorce; it applies to any revocable or irrevocable divorce.
2
State the ruling
The menstrual period in which the divorce occurs is not counted toward the ʿiddah.
3
Explain the reason
Because part of that menstrual period has already passed before the divorce, making it incomplete. The ʿiddah requires complete menstrual cycles (ḥayḍah kāmilah).
The classical text states: 'because part of it has passed, and ʿiddah is only counted with a complete [menstruation].'
4
Clarify the consequence
The woman must wait for the next complete menstrual period to begin the ʿiddah. So the ʿiddah effectively starts after the current period ends, with the first complete cycle beginning with the next menstruation.

Ruling on Overlapping ʿIddahs Due to Dubious Intercourse
Ruling on overlapping ʿiddahs when a woman has dubious intercourse during her ʿiddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on overlapping ʿiddahs when dubious intercourse occurs during an existing ʿiddah, and explain the reasoning based on the purpose of ʿiddah.

Core Idea
When dubious intercourse occurs during ʿiddah, the new ʿiddah overlaps and one menstruation counts for both.

The ʿiddah period serves to confirm the womb is empty of pregnancy. If a woman already in ʿiddah engages in intercourse under a mistaken belief of permissibility (shubhah), a new ʿiddah becomes necessary. But rather than requiring two consecutive periods, the two ʿiddahs run concurrently. This lesson explains the rule of overlapping (tadākhul) and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering act
Note that the woman is already in ʿiddah (from divorce or death) and then engages in intercourse with someone (including her ex-husband if the divorce was final) based on a mistaken belief that it is allowed.
The shubhah could be from either party.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is that she must observe a second ʿiddah due to the new cause (intercourse).
This is wajib.
3
Understand the overlap (tadākhul)
The two ʿiddahs run concurrently, so each menstrual period she experiences counts for both simultaneously.
The underlying purpose—ascertaining emptiness of the womb—is achieved.
4
Apply the condition for sequential completion
If the first ʿiddah ends (e.g., after three cycles) before the second is complete, she must continue until the second ʿiddah is finished.
The periods counted for both up to that point; subsequent periods count only for the second.
5
Work through the example
If the second intercourse occurs after she has already seen one period from the first ʿiddah, that first period belongs only to the first ʿiddah; the remaining periods count for both until the first finishes.
This clarifies the starting point of the overlap.

Merging of Iddahs When Intercourse Under Shubhah Occurs
Explains ruling for a woman in iddah who has intercourse under shubhah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a woman in iddah who has intercourse under shubhah, calculate the total number of menstrual periods required in each scenario, and explain the rationale based on the purpose of iddah.

Core Idea
When a woman in iddah has intercourse under shubhah, the two iddahs merge — the same menstrual periods count for both, achieving the purpose of ascertaining the womb's emptiness.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, expanded by al-Maydani in al-Lubab, explains a specific scenario: a woman already observing iddah (waiting period) after divorce who then has sexual intercourse under a mistaken belief that it is lawful (shubhah). The Hanafi school rules that this triggers a new iddah, but the two iddahs merge, meaning that the same menstrual cycles can count for both. However, the timing of the second intercourse affects how many total periods are needed. This lesson will walk you through the legal ruling and its two sub-cases, showing how the Hanafi school applies the purpose (illah) of iddah — ascertaining the emptiness of the womb — to derive the merging rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that upon intercourse under shubhah during iddah, a second iddah is obligatory, and the two merge.
The merging is the key Hanafi position.
2
Understand the rationale
The purpose (illah) is to know the womb is empty (bara'at al-rahim). Since that purpose is fulfilled by the menstrual periods, merging is allowed.
This is a rational (aqli) proof, not a textual one.
3
Distinguish timing sub-cases
If the second intercourse occurs after one period has been seen, the total periods required are four. If it occurs before any period, only three periods are needed.
The first period already counted for the first iddah affects the calculation.
4
Calculate the periods
For sub-case (a): the first period is from the first iddah, the next two are from both, and a fourth completes the second iddah. For (b): three periods serve both iddahs (six periods merged into three).
The calculation ensures each iddah is fulfilled according to its cause.
5
Note authority
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position in the Hanafi school, transmitted in Zahir al-Riwayah.
Other schools may differ on merging.

When Does the Iddah Begin? The Hanafi Ruling on Awareness
States the start of iddah and effect of unawareness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the iddah begins immediately upon divorce or death and explain why ignorance of the event does not affect its completion.

Core Idea
The iddah is defined as the passage of time, so it starts from the cause and ends when time passes, regardless of knowledge.

In this passage from al-Lubab, the Hanafi school clarifies a key point about the waiting period (iddah). Many assume that a woman must know about the divorce or death for her iddah to begin. But the Hanafi position is clear: the iddah is an objective period tied to the event itself. We explore the rationale: the iddah is essentially the passage of a specific time, so once that time elapses, the obligation is fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause of iddah
The iddah is triggered by either divorce or death. These events are the sabab (cause) for its obligation.
Divorce and death are the two causes mentioned in the text.
2
Determine the start of iddah
The iddah begins at the moment of divorce or death, not when the woman learns of it. This is based on the fact that the cause exists at that time.
3
Understand the definition of iddah
The Hanafi school defines iddah as the mere passage of a specific duration (madi al-zaman). It is not an act requiring intention or awareness.
This is the core proof from the text: 'because the iddah is the passage of time'.
4
Apply to the unawareness scenario
Since the iddah is defined as time passage, if the time period elapses while the woman is unaware, her iddah is complete. No additional period is required.
5
Contrast with other schools
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require knowledge for the iddah to begin, but Hanafi holds it starts from the event. This is a valid ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi.

The Beginning of 'Iddah: Immediate Start Upon Divorce or Death
Ruling on when 'iddah begins and ends if unknown

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when 'iddah begins and apply it to scenarios where the woman is unaware of the divorce or death.

Core Idea
The 'iddah begins immediately upon divorce or death, not upon knowledge.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational ruling of 'iddah: its starting point. The Hanafi school holds that the 'iddah begins right at the moment of divorce pronouncement or death, regardless of the woman's awareness. This is because the cause (sabab) of 'iddah is the marital dissolution itself. Consequently, if a woman remains ignorant until the period passes, her 'iddah is considered complete, since 'iddah is purely the passage of time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the 'iddah (waiting period) for a woman after divorce or death. The ruling is that it begins immediately after the divorce (talaq) or death (wafat).
This is the general principle for all types of 'iddah.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is that divorce and death are the cause (sabab) that triggers the obligation of 'iddah. The commentator says: 'because they are the cause for its obligation'. Therefore, the starting point is tied to the occurrence of the cause.
The reasoning is talil: the ruling follows the cause.
3
Apply to the case of ignorance
If the woman does not learn of the divorce or death until after the 'iddah period has passed, her 'iddah is considered elapsed. The text states: 'if she does not know … then her 'iddah has elapsed'.
This is because 'iddah is defined as the passage of time; once the time has passed, the obligation is fulfilled.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is a valid difference of opinion: some other schools hold that 'iddah begins from the time the woman gains knowledge, as the purpose includes awareness. The Hanafi school, however, adheres to the cause-based ruling.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate; each school bases its view on different usul principles.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence
The practical effect: a woman who is unaware for months can remarry immediately after the period from the divorce/death, without needing additional waiting time. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that the 'iddah ends with the passage of time, not knowledge.
This leniency is based on the principle that cause alone suffices.

Beginning of 'Iddah in Fasid Nikah and the Dispute on Iqrar
Ruling on start of 'iddah in fasid nikah and intra-Hanafi dispute on iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the start of 'iddah in fasid nikah, distinguish the two scholarly positions on iqrar, and apply the reconciled view of al-Sughdi.

Core Idea
In fasid nikah, 'iddah starts upon separation; but when a divorce is acknowledged later, the start depends on possibility of collusion.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri combines a general rule on 'iddah in invalid marriages with an important intra-Hanafi dispute about the timing when a divorce is acknowledged after the fact. Understanding this dispute shows how Hanafi scholars refine rulings based on the suspicion of collusion (muwada'ah). We will trace the positions of Imam Muhammad and later Mashayikh, and how Imam Abu al-Hasan al-Sughdi reconciles them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule for 'iddah in fasid nikah
Note that 'iddah begins immediately after separation (by judge) or upon the husband's declaration of abandoning intercourse. This applies only to consummated marriages; otherwise, mere separation suffices.
Seclusion (khilwa) in fasid nikah does not trigger 'iddah.
2
Recognize the exception for non-consummated marriages
If the marriage was not consummated, no 'iddah is required; separation of bodies is sufficient.
3
Introduce the dispute on iqrar
When a man claims a divorce occurred earlier, and the wife confirms, the question arises: does 'iddah start from the claimed date or the acknowledgment?
The concern is collusion (muwada'ah) to backdate the 'iddah.
4
Analyze the two positions
Muhammad (Zahir al-Riwayah) holds that 'iddah starts from the claimed date if the spouses were separated at that time. The later scholars (Mashayikh of Bukhara and Samarkand) hold that if the spouses were together, they are not believed and 'iddah starts from the time of acknowledgment.
The basis is the suspicion of collusion when they are together.
5
Apply al-Sughdi's harmonization
Al-Sughdi reconciles: Muhammad's view applies when the spouses were separated at the claimed time; the fatwa of the Mashayikh applies when they were together.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is the fatwa for the common scenario where collusion is possible.

The Obligation and Components of Ihdad
Defines the obligation and components of ihdad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of ihdad, identify its wajibat (required abstentions), and apply the exception for darurah (necessity).

Core Idea
Ihdad is a wajib mourning period requiring abstention from perfume, adornment, oil, and kohl, except when necessity permits.

Ihdad is a form of mourning observed by a woman during her 'iddah after an irrevocable divorce or the death of her husband. It is not merely a cultural practice but a legal obligation (wajib) rooted in the Shari'ah, intended to express grief over the loss of the marital blessing. In this lesson, we will examine who must observe ihdad, its essential components, and the one exception that allows a woman to break it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the women obligated to observe ihdad
Note the two categories: the irrevocably divorced woman (mabtutah) and the widow (mutawaffa 'anha). The obligation applies if she is an adult Muslim, even if a slave.
The divorce must be irrevocable (talq bain); a revocable divorce does not require ihdad.
2
Understand the nature of the obligation
Ihdad is wajib because it is a right of the Shari'ah (haqq al-shar'). The husband's command to abandon it does not lift the obligation.
The purpose is to show regret for the loss of the blessing of marriage.
3
List the wajibat (required abstentions) of ihdad
The text specifies four: leaving perfume, adornment (jewelry and silk), oil (even unscented), and kohl. These are the minimum required.
Other actions may also be prohibited by analogy, but these are explicitly mentioned.
4
Identify the exception for necessity
All prohibitions are lifted if there is a valid excuse (udhr), such as a medical need. The principle is: necessities permit prohibited things.
The excuse must be genuine; it does not apply to mere convenience.

The Ruling of Ihdad for Divorced and Widowed Women
Definition and ruling of ihdad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of ihdad, identify which women are obligated to observe it, and list the prohibited acts and exceptions.

Core Idea
Ihdad is wajib for the adult Muslim woman who is irrevocably divorced or widowed, requiring abstention from adornment and perfume.

This passage from al-Lubab sharh al-Quduri defines ihdad and its rulings. It clarifies that ihdad is not merely a custom but a legal obligation with specific restrictions, and it applies to both the irrevocably divorced woman (mubtattah) and the widow. The text also explains what ihdad entails and who is exempt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligated women
State which categories of women must observe ihdad according to the passage (adult Muslim mubtattah and widow).
The irrevocably divorced woman (mubtattah) is one who has received a third talaq (bainunah kubra).
2
List the core prohibitions of ihdad
What must a woman abstain from? Perfume, adornment (jewelry, silk), oil (even unscented), and kohl.
Adornment includes silk clothing; oil includes pure oil like olive oil.
3
Identify exceptions and additional prohibitions
When are the core prohibitions lifted? Only due to a legitimate excuse (e.g., medical need). Also note additional prohibited items: henna, safflower, saffron, and wars dyes.
Necessities permit prohibitions (al-darurat tubih al-mahzurat).
4
Recognize exempt categories
Which women are not obligated? Non-Muslim women and minor girls. Note that an enslaved woman must observe ihdad.
A non-Muslim is not addressed by this ruling; a minor is not legally responsible.

Explains absence of iḥdād in fasid nikah and umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two exceptions to the obligation of iḥdād during iddah and explain the causal reason (illah) that differentiates them.

Core Idea
Iḥdād is wajib only when a valid marriage is lost; an invalid marriage or a slave-master relationship lacks that loss.

This lesson addresses a key exception to the general Hanafi rule that iḥdād (avoiding adornment) is obligatory during iddah. The text clarifies that iḥdād is not required in two specific cases: the iddah following a fasid (invalid) marriage and the iddah of an umm walad (slave mother). The reason given is that iḥdād is meant to express grief over the loss of the blessing of marriage, and in these cases, that blessing was either non-existent or not of the same nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two exceptional cases
Read the opening sentence: 'There is no iḥdād in the iddah of an invalid marriage nor in the iddah of a mother of a child (umm walad).' Note these two cases.
These are exceptions to the general obligation of iḥdād for divorced women and widows.
2
Understand the underlying cause (illah)
The text explains that iḥdād is 'to show regret at the loss of the blessing of marriage.' For a fasid marriage, the marriage never validly existed, so no blessing was lost. For the umm walad, she loses her master, not a husband, so the blessing of marriage is not lost.
The illah is the loss of the blessing of marriage; when that blessing is absent, iḥdād is not required.
3
Contrast with the general rule
Recall that for a valid marriage, the divorced woman or widow must observe iḥdād (wajib). Here the text highlights that the obligation hinges on the validity and nature of the relationship.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school; other schools may require iḥdād in all iddahs.
4
Apply the principle to similar cases
Consider whether iḥdād would be required in other non-valid relationships, such as a void (batil) marriage. The same illah applies: no valid blessing, no obligation.
The ruling extends to any marriage that is not sahih at its inception.

Proposing (Khitbah) to a Woman in Iddah: Explicit vs. Implicit
Explains rules of proposing during iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between explicit and implicit proposals during iddah, cite the Quranic evidence, and apply the ruling to different scenarios.

Core Idea
Explicit marriage proposal during iddah is haram; only indirect hints are permitted by the Quran.

This lesson covers the second ruling in the same passage: the rules of proposing marriage (khitbah) to a woman who is in iddah. The text states that explicit proposal (tasrih) is haram, while implicit proposals (ta'rid) are allowed. The proof is the Quranic verse 2:235, which explicitly permits implicit offers and prohibits secret agreements, interpreted as explicit marriage contracts. Understanding this distinction is essential for anyone involved in arranging marriages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling on proposing during iddah
The text says: 'It is not befitting, rather it is forbidden, that a woman in iddah be proposed to explicitly.' Note that this applies to any mu'taddah, whether revocable or irrevocable divorce, or widow.
The ruling is haram (forbidden) for explicit proposal.
2
Distinguish between explicit (tasrih) and implicit (ta'rid)
Explicit proposal: words that clearly indicate a marriage offer, e.g., 'I marry you.' Implicit: words that hint at interest, e.g., 'I intend to marry' or 'I desire you.' The text provides definitions from Ibn Abbas and Sa'id ibn Jubayr.
The key is whether the words directly propose marriage or merely express interest.
3
Examine the Quranic proof (2:235)
The verse explicitly says there is no blame for implicit offers (ta'rid) but forbids 'secret contracts.' The Prophet explained 'secret' as the marriage contract itself. Thus, the verse is a dalil for permissibility of ta'rid and prohibition of explicit agreement.
The verse is the primary evidence; the hadith and Companion statements clarify its meaning.
4
Apply the ruling to common scenarios
Consider a man who says to a woman in iddah: 'When your iddah ends, I want to marry you.' This is implicit? Actually, if he mentions the condition ('when your iddah ends'), it might be considered ta'rid because it is not an immediate contract. The Hanafi school generally considers any conditional proposal as implicit.
The nuance lies in the wording; careful not to cross into explicit territory.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on leaving the house for a divorced woman and a widow during iddah, and identify the sounder view when nafaqah is waived in khul'.

Core Idea
A free divorced woman in iddah may not leave her house; a widow may leave by day or part of night due to need.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies the obligations of a woman in iddah regarding leaving her residence. The ruling distinguishes between divorced women (whether revocable or irrevocable) who are prohibited from leaving, and widows who are permitted to go out by day for livelihood. An important nuance arises when a woman takes khul' and forfeits her maintenance—then the sounder view maintains her prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of women in iddah
The text mentions two main categories: a free divorced woman (mutallaqah raj'iyyah or mubattatah) and a widow (mutawaffa 'anha zawjuha). Additionally, a slave woman is mentioned as an exception.
Both revocable and irrevocable divorces are treated the same for this ruling.
2
State the default rulings for each category
For a free divorced woman: leaving the house day or night is not permissible (haram). For a widow: leaving by day or part of the night is permissible (mubah), due to necessity.
The rationale given: divorced woman has maintenance (nafaqah) from the husband, so no need; widow has none.

Travel with a Revocably Divorced Woman: Prohibition and Implied Revocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on traveling with a revocably divorced woman, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between the majority and Zufar, and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.

Core Idea
Travel with a revocably divorced woman is prohibited unless revocation is witnessed, but later Hanafi authorities deem travel itself an implied revocation.

This passage addresses a practical scenario: can a husband take his revocably divorced wife on a journey during her 'iddah? The ruling turns on whether travel constitutes expulsion (prohibited by Quran) or implied revocation. We will examine the proof, the majority position, Zufar's counter-argument, and the eventual reliance on Zufar's view in later fatwa practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its original ruling
The act is the husband traveling with his revocably divorced wife during her 'iddah. The original Hanafi ruling (Zahir al-Riwayah) is that it is not permissible unless revocation (raj'ah) is formally witnessed.
Note that the prohibition is based on the Quranic command not to expel her from her house.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is Quran 65:1: 'Do not drive them out of their houses.' The prohibition is general and includes the husband. Traveling with her would be an expulsion unless there is an exception – and the exception here is witnessed revocation.
The Hanafi usul principle of 'amm (general) applies: the ruling covers all instances unless a specific takhsis (exception) is proven.
3
Understand the intra-Hanafi dispute
Imam Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) argues that travel itself indicates revocation: the husband would not travel with her unless he intends to keep her. Therefore, travel is not an expulsion but an act of retention, and no separate witnessing is needed.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school. Zufar's view is based on a different reading of the operative cause (illah) of the prohibition.
4
Trace the tarjih (preference) by later authorities
Later Hanafi authorities, including the author of al-Durr al-Mukhtar and Ibn 'Abidin, relied on Zufar's view. They considered traveling with a revocably divorced woman to be an implied revocation (dalalah al-raj'ah).
This shifts the operative ruling (mufta bihi) from prohibition to permissibility, as long as the travel itself indicates revocation.

The practical difference: under the majority view, if he travels without witnessing revocation, she remains divorced and he commits a sin. Under the relied-upon view, the travel itself constitutes revocation, so she returns to his marriage automatically.
This affects both the sinfulness and the marital status.

Travel of the revocably divorced woman with her husband: It is not permissible for the husband to travel with the revocably divorced woman unless he witnesses the revocation; due to the generality of His statement, the Exalted: 'Do not drive them out of their houses' (Quran 65:1), which includes the husband and others. Zufar said: He may do so, based on the fact that in his view travel itself is a revocation, because he only travels with her when he intends to keep her, so it is not an expulsion of the woman in 'iddah. The author of al-Durr al-Mukhtar relied on Zufar's statement, and Ibn 'Abidin transmitted his reliance from a number of Hanafi imams, and that traveling with a revocably divorced woman is an indication of revocation.

Commentary
This passage discusses the permissibility of travel with a revocably divorced woman (mطلقة رجعية). The established ruling is that it is not permissible for the husband to take her on a journey unless he formally witnesses the revocation (رجعة). The proof is the Quranic verse 'Do not drive them out of their houses' (65:1), which prohibits expelling her from her 'iddah residence. Travel would constitute such an expulsion. However, Imam Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) holds that travel itself can be considered a revocation because the husband only travels with her if he intends to retain her. Thus, no separate witnessing is needed. The text notes that later authorities like the author of al-Durr al-Mukhtar preferred Zufar's view, and Ibn 'Abidin confirms this reliance, indicating that traveling with a revocably divorced woman is considered an implied revocation.

Key Terms
المطلقة الرجعية (al-mutallaqah al-raj'iyyah): A woman divorced with a revocable divorce (first or second divorce where the husband may take her back without a new marriage contract).
رجعة (raj'ah): The act of taking back the wife during the 'iddah period, revoking the divorce.
