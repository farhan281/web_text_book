Joint Theft: Each Gets Nisab
Ruling on joint theft with individual nisab condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for joint theft and identify the condition that triggers the hadd punishment for each participant.

Core Idea
In joint theft, each thief is cut only if his personal share reaches the nisab of ten dirhams.

Theft (sariqa) is a hudud offense with strict conditions. One critical condition is the nisab, the minimum value of stolen goods. This lesson examines how the Hanafi school applies the nisab requirement when multiple thieves act together. The key point: each thief is treated individually based on his share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The passage addresses joint theft (ishtirak fi sariqa). The ruling is that all participants are cut (quti'u) – i.e., the hadd amputation applies.
The ruling is categorical: if the condition is met, hadd applies to all.
2
Locate the condition
The condition for the hadd is that each participant individually receives a share equal to or exceeding the nisab (ten dirhams or its value). This is indicated by 'fa-aṣāba kullu wāḥidin minhum ʿasharatu dirham' (each one gets ten dirhams).
The nisab is a prerequisite for hadd in theft, confirmed by hadith and consensus.
3
Apply the condition to a scenario
Test the ruling with a hypothetical: if three thieves steal 30 dirhams and divide equally, each gets 10 dirhams, so all are cut. If they steal 25 dirhams and divide equally, each gets ~8.33 dirhams (less than nisab), so none are cut; ta'zir may apply instead.
The share is calculated after division, not the total stolen.
4
Note the Hanafi specificity
The Hanafi position is that the nisab is measured per thief, not collectively. This differs from some other schools that may consider the total value.
This reflects the principle that hadd is individualised.

Commentary
This passage states that when multiple people jointly commit a theft, the hadd is applied to all of them as long as each individual's share of the stolen property reaches the nisab (minimum value, which is ten dirhams in Hanafi law). This applies whether they divide it equally or it is distributed such that each receives the nisab. The principle is that each participant is considered a thief of the full nisab from the joint act.

Key Terms
اشتراك (ishtirak): Participation or joint involvement in an act, here theft.
نصاب (nisab): The minimum value of stolen goods required for the hadd to apply; ten dirhams or equivalent.

Udhiyah Not Obligatory on the Poor and Traveler
States the exemption of poor and traveler from udhiyah with rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on udhiyah for the poor and traveler and explain the rationale of hardship (haraj) that lifts the obligation.

Core Idea
Udhiyah is not wajib on the poor and traveler due to hardship—the obligation is lifted to prevent difficulty.

This lesson examines a key ruling from al-Quduri's matn: udhiyah (sacrificial animal) is not obligatory upon two specific categories—the poor (faqir) and the traveler (musafir). While the Hanafi school holds udhiyah to be generally wajib for those who have the means, this exemption is based on the principle of removing hardship (raf' al-haraj). The commentator explains the rationale: for the poor, the lack of means is self-evident; for the traveler, the difficulties of performing the sacrifice while on a journey justify the concession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Recognize that udhiyah (sacrificial animal) is generally wajib in the Hanafi school for those who possess the nisab (minimum wealth) and are residents (muqim).
The default ruling is wajib, not sunnah as in other schools.
2
Identify the two exempted categories
From the matn: the poor (faqir) and the traveler (musafir). Understand the definitions: faqir is one who does not own nisab; musafir is one traveling a distance allowing qasr (approx. 48 miles).
These definitions are standard in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the reason for exemption
The commentary states the exemption is 'daf'an lil-haraj' (to repel hardship). For the poor, the hardship is obvious (lack of means). For the traveler, the hardship lies in the practical difficulties of slaughtering and distributing the meat while traveling.
The principle of haraj is a key basis for concessions (rukhsah) in Hanafi law.
4
Note the time-bound nature
The passage ends with 'wa tafutu bi-madi al-waqt' (it is missed with the passing of time). This means the obligation of udhiyah is tied to the specific days of Eid al-Adha; if the time passes, the obligation is lost even for those who were required to perform it.
This highlights that udhiyah is an act with a specific temporal window.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn of al-Quduri, followed by its commentary. The ruling is that udhiyah (sacrificial animal) is not obligatory upon two categories of people: the poor (faqir) and the traveler (musafir). The commentator explains that the obligation is lifted to avoid hardship (haraj). For the poor, the reason is self-evident: they lack the means. For the traveler, the reasons include the difficulty of carrying out the sacrifice while traveling, as it requires specific actions and conditions. The passage also notes that the obligation of udhiyah is time-bound; if the time passes, the opportunity is lost.

Key Terms
أضحية (udhiyah): Sacrificial animal slaughtered on the days of Eid al-Adha as an act of worship.
فقير (faqir): A person who does not possess the nisab (minimum wealth) required for udhiyah.
مسافر (musafir): A person traveling a distance that permits the shortening of prayer (approx. 48 miles or more).
حرج (haraj): Hardship or difficulty that justifies lifting an obligation.

Key Takeaway
