Conditions for Halal Hunting with Trained Animals
States the conditions for halal hunting with trained dogs and birds.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for halal hunting with a trained animal and identify which condition, if missing, makes the prey impermissible.

Core Idea
Hunting is halal only if the animal is trained, the hunter says Bismillah, and the prey dies from a wound.

This lesson covers the basic conditions for hunting game with trained dogs and birds of prey. The passage from Al-Quduri's matn, expanded by Al-Maydani, outlines the key requirements for the prey to be permissible for consumption. Understanding these conditions is essential for any hunter following Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Recognize that this passage governs the permissibility of eating game caught by a trained animal. It is a ruling on the conditions of hunting (ṣayd).
Hunting is a means to obtain food, not an 'ibadah but governed by fiqh rules.
2
List the three conditions
From the text, extract the three explicit conditions: (1) the animal must be trained (mu'allam), (2) the hunter must mention Allah's name at the time of sending it, (3) the prey must be wounded by the animal and die from that wound.
The commentary adds that mentioning Allah's name is fulfilled even if forgotten, as long as it is not intentionally omitted.
3
Understand the wound requirement
The passage restricts the ruling with 'wound' (jarḥ). If the prey dies without a wound (e.g., from shock or being crushed), it is not permissible. This condition is from the apparent narration (ẓāhir al-riwāyah).
The author signals that there is a differing view elsewhere, but the relied-upon position is that a wound is necessary.
4
Note the ruling on forgetting tasmiyah
The commentary clarifies that if the hunter forgets to mention Allah's name but does not intentionally omit it, the condition is still considered fulfilled. This is a concession based on the hadith 'My ummah is forgiven for mistakes and forgetfulness.'
Intentional omission invalidates the hunting.
5
Apply the conditions to a scenario
Given a scenario, check each condition. If any condition is missing, the prey is ḥarām. For example, if a trained dog catches a rabbit and kills it by biting (wounds it), and the hunter said 'Bismillāh' when sending it, the rabbit is ḥalāl. If the dog did not wound it, it is not ḥalāl.

Dog vs Bird: When Eating the Prey Invalidates the Hunt
Ruling on when a trained dog or bird eats from the prey after catching it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a trained dog or bird eats from the prey and identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the uneaten part.

Core Idea
If a trained dog eats from the prey, the prey is haram; if a trained bird eats, it remains halal.

This passage addresses a specific condition for the permissibility of hunted game: whether the hunting animal's consumption of the prey affects its lawfulness. The Hanafi school distinguishes between dogs and other beasts of prey versus birds, based on the nature of their training. Understanding this ruling is crucial for anyone engaged in hunting with trained animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Subject and Ruling for Dog
Determine whether the passage is about a dog or similar beast of prey. For a trained dog that eats from the prey, state the ruling: it is not to be eaten.
The ruling applies to any beast of prey (sabu') that is trained, not only dogs.
2
Understand the Rationale
The dog's eating is considered a 'sign of ignorance' ( alāmat al-jahl), indicating that the training is incomplete. A properly trained dog should not eat the prey.
This is the basis for the prohibition.
3
Differentiate for Birds
Contrast with the peregrine (bāzī) or other trained birds: even if the bird eats from the prey, the prey is lawful. Eating is not a condition for a bird's training.
Birds naturally eat; they are not trained to refrain from eating.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf on the Uneaten Part
For the bird's prey, Abu Hanifa holds that the uneaten part is prohibited, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold it is lawful. The text refers to al-Hidayah for details.
This ikhtilaf does not affect the ruling for dogs.

Ruling on Prey Found Alive: Slaughter Required or Carrion
Ruling on prey found alive: obligation to slaughter and consequence if omitted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a hunter reaches prey alive, explain the condition of 'life above that of a slaughtered animal' (hayāt fawqa hayāt al-madhbūḥ), and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding inability to slaughter.

Core Idea
Finding the prey alive requires slaughter; otherwise it becomes carrion even if the hunt was valid.

A successful hunt does not automatically make the prey halal if the hunter arrives while it is still alive. At that moment, the obligation shifts from hunting to proper slaughter. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling, the proof behind it, and an important internal disagreement about what happens when the hunter cannot perform the slaughter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon (finding the prey alive by the hunter) and the hukm level (wajib to slaughter).
The ruling is that slaughter becomes obligatory (wajib) upon the hunter.
2
Recognize the condition for the ruling
Note the condition: the prey must have 'life above that of a slaughtered animal' (hayāt fawqa hayāt al-madhbūḥ), meaning it could survive for at least a day or half a day.
This condition distinguishes a dying animal from one that could live.
3
Apply the ruling to a scenario of inability
Consider the case where the hunter cannot slaughter (no tool, no time). The text presents the apparent narration (zahir al-riwayah) that it is still carrion, and an opposing view from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf that it is permissible.
The key is whether the inability negates the obligation.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Study the dispute: the apparent narration says not eaten; Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (and a narration from all three imams) say it is eaten.
The dispute is about the effect of inability due to lack of tools or time.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
Determine which view is relied upon. The text indicates the apparent narration is the one used in zahir al-riwayah, but does not explicitly state the mufta bihi.
Later Hanafi authorities may clarify the tarjih.

Game Killed by Breaking a Limb: The Hanafi Intra-School Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on permissibility of game killed by a broken limb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the two Hanafi views on game killed by a broken limb and apply the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Breaking a limb does not cause blood flow, so it is like strangulation and the game is unlawful.

Hunting with a trained dog is permissible, but what if the dog kills the game by breaking a limb rather than cutting? The Hanafi school has an internal dispute. Abu Hanifa in one narration permits it, but the preferred view in al-Hidayah prohibits it. This lesson unpacks the qiyas behind both positions.

Guided Path
1
State the issue and the two positions
Identify the subject of the dispute: a trained hunting dog kills game by breaking a limb. State the two positions within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa permits it, and the relied-upon view in al-Hidayah prohibits it.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Examine Abu Hanifa's argument: breaking a limb causes an internal wound (jarahah batinah), which is analogous to an external wound. Therefore, it should be permissible.
This view considers any wounding sufficient for valid hunting.
3
Understand the relied-upon view's reasoning
Analyze the reasoning of al-Hidayah: the essential condition for lawful game is blood flow (inhar al-dam) caused by a cut. Breaking a limb does not achieve this, so it is analogous to strangulation (takhniq), which is prohibited.
This view prioritizes the observable effect of blood flow over the existence of internal injury.
4
Identify the effective cause ('illa)
Determine the 'illa (effective cause) in the qiyas: for the relied-upon view, the 'illa is the absence of blood flow. For Abu Hanifa, the 'illa is the presence of a wound. Note how different identifications of the 'illa lead to different rulings.
The qiyas hinges on which characteristic is considered legally meaningful.
5
Apply the mufta bihi position
Conclude with the operative ruling: the game is not lawful to eat. This is the mufta bihi as per al-Hidayah and the later Hanafi tradition. Remember the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference): a few may follow the minority view, but fatwa is on prohibition.
Always verify the fatwa hierarchy before applying a ruling.

Ruling When a Trained Dog Is Joined by an Untrained Dog in Hunting
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the prohibition of game when a trained dog is joined by an untrained dog

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi ruling when a trained dog is joined by an untrained dog, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute, and apply the precautionary principle to similar cases.

Core Idea
When permissible and prohibited combine, prohibition prevails as a precaution — the relied-upon Hanafi position.

This lesson examines a specific hunting scenario where a trained dog is joined by an untrained dog, a Magian's dog, or a dog released without tasmiyah. Al-Quduri's matn rules that such game is not eaten, but the passage reveals an intra-Hanafi dispute: some narrations from Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf permit it. We will navigate the tarjih mechanism and understand why the prohibition is the operative position.

Guided Path
1
State the base ruling from the matn
Al-Quduri rules that if a trained dog is joined by an untrained dog, a Magian's dog, or a dog released without tasmiyah, the game is not eaten.
This is the starting point of the discussion.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi variant
Al-Ispijabi reports that al-Hasan ibn Ziyad narrated from Abu Hanifa that it is permissible to eat, and a similar narration exists from Abu Yusuf.
These are weaker narrations (nawadir) compared to the zahir al-riwayah.
3
Determine the tarjih mechanism
The commentators state that the correct view is the apparent school position (zahir al-madhhab), which is prohibition, and the fatwa is upon this.
The tarjih is based on the authority of the six core books (zahir al-riwayah) and the precautionary principle.
4
Apply the legal reasoning (talil)
The prohibition is justified because the permissible (trained dog) and the prohibited (untrained dog) combine, so prohibition is given preponderance as a precaution.
This is a general principle in Hanafi fiqh: 'idha ijtimaa al-mubih wa al-muharram ghulliba al-muharram'.
5
Extract the practical consequence
For a hunter, if any untrained or prohibited dog joins the trained dog during the hunt, the game must not be consumed.
This ensures certainty that the conditions of lawful hunting are fulfilled.

Continuous Pursuit: The Condition for Eating Wounded Game
Conditions for eating game that is hit and escapes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on game that disappears after being shot and apply the condition that continuous pursuit is required for halal consumption.

Core Idea
If the hunter stops pursuing wounded game before finding it, the game becomes haram.

The Hanafi school addresses a common dilemma: when game is hit but escapes and disappears, what makes it lawful to eat? The key is whether the hunter maintains continuous pursuit. This passage from Al-Lubab establishes that stopping pursuit breaks the attribution of death to the hunter's shot, due to the principle that a mere possibility of another cause becomes decisive when the hunter is negligent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The text presents two cases: (1) the hunter pursues continuously until he finds the game dead with only his arrow's mark; (2) the hunter stops pursuing, then later finds the game dead.
The ruling differs based on this distinction.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For continuous pursuit: the game is eaten (halal). For stopping: the game is not eaten (haram).
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The principle is 'al-mawhum ka al-muhaqqaq' (what is conjectured is like what is certain). If the hunter stops, the possibility that the game died from another cause is treated as certainty, making it haram. However, this principle is suspended during pursuit due to necessity (hunting cannot be free of such possibilities).
This principle is specific to the chapter of hunting.
4

Even with continuous pursuit, if the game has another wound besides the hunter's arrow, it is haram because death could have been from that other wound.
This reinforces the principle of attributing death solely to the hunter's shot.

Example: A hunter shoots a deer, it runs out of sight for 10 minutes while he tracks it, and he finds it dead with only his arrow. Halal. If he stops to drink water and resumes after 5 minutes, then finds it dead, it is haram.
The pursuit must be continuous without abandonment.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
While the Sunnah recommends the throat and upper chest, any part of the throat suffices for valid slaughter.

This lesson examines a key detail in the fiqh of dhabh: exactly where the incision must be made. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the recommended spot (the throat and upper chest) and the minimally acceptable location (any part of the throat). We will explore the classical terminology and the key textual authority from al-Jami' al-Saghir as cited in al-Hidayah.

The author notes that 'halq' is used for the neck by part-for-whole (majaz). This means that cutting any part of the throat – top, middle, bottom – fulfills the requirement. This flexibility is a distinctive feature of the Hanafi school.

The commentary mentions two possible narrations from al-Jami' and al-Mabsut. The author favors the wider permission from al-Jami' al-Saghir, which is part of Zahir al-Riwayah.
The mufta bihi (operative position) is that any part of the throat is permissible.

Consider a chicken slaughtered with a cut made high on the neck near the head. According to the Hanafi school, this is valid because it is within the throat area. Conversely, if the cut is made below the chest, it would be invalid.

The Sufficient Cut in Zabihah: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on required cuts in zabihah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi position on the number of vessels that must be cut in zabihah and explain why the Sahibayn's view is not the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, cutting any three of the four throat vessels renders meat halal — not all four, nor a specific set.

When slaughtering an animal, the cut must sever certain vessels for the meat to become halal. The Hanafi school agrees that cutting all four vessels (throat, esophagus, and both jugulars) is sufficient. But what if only three are cut? This passage from al-Lubab details an important intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa says any three suffice, while his two main disciples require a specific combination. Understanding this dispute and the final preferred view is essential for correctly applying Hanafi slaughter rules.

Identify the anatomical vessels
First, learn the four vessels: al-halqum (throat/windpipe), al-mari' (esophagus), and al-wadajan (the two jugular veins). Their locations and functions are given in the source.
These terms are consistent across Islamic legal schools.

State the consensus on cutting all four
The passage states that if all four are cut, the meat is halal by consensus (ittifaq). This is the baseline.

Present Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa holds that cutting any three of the four vessels (most of them) suffices. The specific vessels do not matter; only the count matters.
This is the later-preferred view.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) require a specific set: the throat, the esophagus, and one jugular. A separate narration from Muhammad adds that for each individual vessel, its majority must be cut.
The text clarifies that the well-known position is actually only Abu Yusuf's.
5

Later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi sided with Abu Hanifa, making his view the operative one for fatwa. Thus, cutting any three vessels renders the meat halal.
Tarjih based on the preponderance of Abu Hanifa's evidence.

Core Idea
Donkey and mule meat is haram; horse meat is makruh tahrimi due to the animal's honor.

This lesson examines the rulings on three equine animals: domestic donkeys, mules, and horses. While donkeys and mules are clearly prohibited based on a sahih hadith, the status of horse meat involves an intra-madhhab dispute on the severity of dislike. We will explore the textual evidence, the qiyas extending the ruling to mules, and the reasoning behind the Hanafi preferred position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three animals and their rulings

The evidence is the explicit prohibition in hadith (نهى عن أكل لحم الحمر الأهلية) which is mutawatir and indicates haram in Hanafi usul.

This does not mean haram; avoiding is recommended but not sinful.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how slaughter (dhakah) purifies the hide and flesh of animals whose meat is not eaten, and identify the qiyas with tanning.

Core Idea
Slaughter purifies the hide and flesh of any animal, even if its meat is not halal to eat.

The passage also addresses a key principle in Hanafi fiqh: when an animal whose meat is not permissible (e.g., horse, donkey) is slaughtered according to Islamic rites, its hide and flesh become pure (tahir). This is based on the similarity to tanning: just as tanning removes impurities from a hide, slaughter removes the flowing blood and other moisture, thereby purifying the entire carcass. This lesson explains the legal reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling: slaughter purifies
The text states that if an animal whose meat is not eaten is slaughtered, its flesh and hide become pure. This is a ruling with a proof from qiyas.
The Arabic term used is 'طهر' (became pure) with both fatha and dumma on the ha, indicating the purification effect.
2
Understand the reasoning: removal of impurities
The purification occurs because slaughter removes the impure fluids (moisture and flowing blood). The hide and flesh themselves are not impure; what makes them impure is the blood. Once blood is drained, they become pure.
The text emphasizes that the impure element is the blood, not the skin or meat.
3
Compare with tanning (dibagh)
The text makes an explicit analogy (qiyas) to tanning: just as tanning purifies a hide that is otherwise impure, slaughter purifies the flesh and hide of a non-edible animal.
The word 'كما في الدباغ' (as in tanning) is the key indicator of the qiyas.
4
Note the scope: non-edible animals
This ruling applies specifically to animals that are not permissible for consumption (e.g., domesticated donkey, mule, horse). It does not apply to already halal animals because they are already pure before slaughter.
The text says 'ما لا يؤكل لحمه' (that whose flesh is not eaten).
5
Apply the ruling to practical cases
If a horse or donkey is slaughtered, its hide becomes usable for leather goods (e.g., mats, bags) after tanning, and even the meat, though not to be eaten, is considered pure (but not halal).
This has implications for using hides from non-halal animals in items like prayer mats.
