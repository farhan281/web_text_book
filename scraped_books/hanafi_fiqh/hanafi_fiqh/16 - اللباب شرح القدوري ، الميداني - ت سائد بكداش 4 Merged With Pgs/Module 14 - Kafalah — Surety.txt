Mukatab Cannot Become a Guarantor: The Prohibition Explained
States a prohibition with a reason (ta'lil).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling prohibiting a mukatab from becoming a guarantor and explain the legal reason (tabarru' mahd) that underlies it.

Core Idea
A mukatab cannot guarantee because it is a pure donation, not a necessity of earning freedom.

This lesson examines the short but decisive ruling that a mukatab – a slave under a contract of kitabah – may not act as a guarantor (kafil). The reason provided is that kafalah is a pure donation (tabarru' mahd), unrelated to the mukatab's need to earn money to pay his installments. Understanding this ruling sheds light on the Hanafi position that a mukatab's legal capacity is limited to acts that directly assist his acquisition of property for his freedom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is 'takafful' – to become a guarantor. The text states 'la yatakaffal' – he may not do so. This is a prohibition (haram).
The prohibition is absolute; no exceptions are mentioned in this passage.
2
Isolate the effective cause ('illah)
The text gives the reason: 'li-annahu tabarru' mahd' – because it is a pure donation. Kafalah involves taking on liability without receiving any compensation in return.
Tabarru' mahd contrasts with mu'awadah (exchange), which is permitted for trade.
3
Connect the 'illah to the mukatab's limited capacity
A mukatab is permitted to engage in trade and earning activities that directly help him pay his kitabah installments. Since kafalah is a pure donation, it does not serve that purpose. Therefore, it falls outside his permitted scope.
If the act were a necessity of trade (e.g., buying inventory), it would be allowed.

Commentary
This short passage from the sharh explains that a mukatab cannot act as a guarantor (kafil) for another person's debt or obligation, because kafalah is a form of pure donation (tabarru' mahd). It does not help him earn money to pay his kitabah installments; it is not a necessity of trade or earning. The matn statement 'ولا يتكفل' is thus expanded with the reason. The footnote clarifies that it means he does not guarantee anyone by any type of kafalah.

Key Terms
تكفل (takafful): To act as a guarantor or surety for another's obligation.
تبرع محض (tabarru' mahd): A pure donation; an act that is entirely voluntary and without compensation.

Mukatab Cannot Act as Guarantor
Ruling that a mukatab cannot be a guarantor with reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a mukatab acting as a guarantor and explain the Hanafi reasoning for its invalidity.

Core Idea
A mukatab cannot guarantee debts because it is a pure donation unrelated to his trade.

This lesson addresses a specific capacity issue in Hanafi fiqh: the mukatab (a slave with a manumission contract) is permitted to engage in trade to earn his freedom, but pure donations are outside his scope. Guaranteeing another's debt is considered a pure donation and therefore invalid. We will examine the ruling and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is a mukatab acting as a guarantor (kafil) for another person. The ruling is that it is invalid (batil).
The text begins with 'ولا يتكفل' meaning 'he does not give guarantee.'
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author provides the reason: 'لأنه تبرع محض، وليس من ضرورات التجارة والاكتساب' — because it is a pure donation and not from the necessities of trade and earning.
The dalil is a rational argument based on the nature of the mukatab's limited capacity.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does this reasoning prove the ruling? The mukatab's actions are restricted to those that benefit his kitabah (earning). Kafalah is a pure donation (tabarru' mahd) that does not serve that purpose, so it is invalid.
This shows that capacity for tabarru'at is distinct from capacity for mu'awadat (compensatory contracts).
4
Confirm the classification
The ruling is specific to the Hanafi school. Other schools may differ, but this passage presents the Hanafi position as a clear rule based on the objective of the kitabah contract.
Note: The mukatab does have legal personality, but it is limited.

He (the mukatab) does not give guarantee, because it is a pure donation and not from the necessities of trade and earning. (1) Meaning he does not guarantee anyone by any type of guarantee.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling that a mukatab (a slave who has contracted with his master for manumission upon payment) cannot act as a guarantor (kafil) for another person. The reason is that kafalah is a purely voluntary donation (tabarru' mahd), and the mukatab is permitted to engage only in activities that are necessary for his trade and earning (iktisab), since his primary concern is to earn money to pay his kitabah. Guaranteeing someone else's debt is not essential to his earning and does not benefit his kitabah. Therefore, it is not valid for him. The footnote clarifies that he does not guarantee anyone by any type of guarantee.

Key Terms
المكاتب (al-mukātab): A slave who has made a contract (kitabah) with his master to earn his freedom by paying a sum over time.
الكفالة (al-kafālah): Guarantee; a contract by which a person (kafil) assumes responsibility for a debt or obligation of another.
