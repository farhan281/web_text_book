Maintenance of Adult Daughter and Disabled Son: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on division of maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on maintenance of an adult daughter and disabled son, and identify the mufta bihi position along with its authority basis.

Core Idea
Maintenance of incapable adult children is wajib; the dispute is whether the mother bears a share.

When adult children are poor and unable to earn (such as a blind or chronically ill son or daughter), their maintenance is obligatory on the parents. But how should that obligation be divided? The early Hanafi jurist al-Khassaf and al-Hasan argued for a division proportional to inheritance shares, but the well-known transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah) places the entire burden on the father. Later authorities confirmed the latter as the operative position for fatwa. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the authority hierarchy that resolved this internal ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Conditions
First, note that maintenance (nafaqah) of these children is wajib when they are poor and unable to earn (e.g., adult daughter, disabled son, blind person). The obligation falls on the parents collectively, but the dispute is about the proportion.
The base text (Quduri) initially states a division of two-thirds on father, one-third on mother, following al-Khassaf.
2
State the First Opinion (Al-Khassaf & Al-Hasan)
This view divides maintenance in the ratio of inheritance shares: father 2/3, mother 1/3. The rationale is that maintenance follows the principle of 'al-ghurm bi al-ghunm' — burden according to benefit, which in inheritance terms reflects the parents' respective shares.
This is reported as a narration (riwayah) but not from the six core books of Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
State the Second Opinion (Zahir al-Riwayah)
The apparent (well-known) transmission from Imam Muhammad holds that all maintenance is the father's responsibility alone. This is the position that appears in the core books of the school.
This is referred to as 'Zahir al-Riwayah' — the primary authoritative source.
4
Identify the Tarjih Mechanism
Later Hanafi masters (al-Mahbubi, Sadr al-Shari'ah, al-Nasafi) explicitly state that fatwa is given according to Zahir al-Riwayah, overriding the narration of al-Khassaf. This is a classic example of preferring the widely transmitted ruling over individual ijtihad.
The commentary in al-Hidayah and its super-commentaries affirm this preference.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence: under al-Khassaf's view, the mother pays one-third of the maintenance; under the mufta bihi, she pays nothing. This affects how a mufti advises families in financial planning.
The khilaf is not about the obligation being wajib, but about who bears the financial burden.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The relied-upon Hanafi position: maintenance of poor adult children is solely on the father, not divided by inheritance shares.

This passage presents an internal Hanafi dispute regarding how maintenance (nafaqah) for an adult daughter, disabled son, and blind child is divided between parents when they are poor. One view apportions it according to inheritance shares (two-thirds father, one-third mother), but the authoritative Zahir al-Riwaya places the entire burden on the father. Understanding this dispute and its resolution is essential for applying the correct ruling.

Guided Path

This establishes the ruling for fatwa.

Thus, in practice, only the father is obligated to provide maintenance for these children; the mother bears no share.
This is the conclusive ruling in the Hanafi school.

The Comprehensive Principle for Maintenance of Relatives
Definition of a comprehensive principle for nafaqah allocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the comprehensive principle for nafaqah among ascendants and descendants, list the seven categories of relatives, and determine that maintenance among multiple descendants is shared equally based on closeness and part-whole relationship.

Core Idea
Maintenance among multiple relatives is allocated by closeness (qurb) and part-whole (juziyya), not inheritance shares.

The chapter on maintenance (nafaqah) can be perplexing due to its many scenarios. Our teacher Ibn Abidin devised a comprehensive principle (dabit kulli) that captures all cases in one systematic framework. This principle classifies all possible combinations of poor blood relatives and provides a clear rule for each, derived from earlier Hanafi works. In this lesson, we explore the first two categories – descendants only and descendants with collaterals – and learn that closeness to the provider, not inheritance shares, determines the obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of the principle
The principle aims to enumerate all rulings for maintenance of ascendants and descendants (usul and furu). Understand that it is a later systematization by Ibn Abidin, not found in earlier works.
This is a dabit kulli – a comprehensive maxim that covers every subsidiary case.
2
Distinguish between a single relative and multiple relatives
If only one poor relative exists, maintenance is obligatory. For multiple relatives, the principle breaks down into seven categories based on the combination of descendants (furu), ascendants (usul), and collaterals (hawashi).
The seventh category (collaterals only) is included for completeness though they are not blood ties.
3
Focus on categories 1 and 2: descendants only, and descendants with collaterals
For these, the criterion is 'closeness and being a part' (al-qurb wa al-juziyyah). This means the relationship is based on direct blood connection and the sense of being a part of the family, not on inheritance ratios.
Example: Two children must share maintenance equally even if one is wealthier or of a different religion.
4
Apply the criterion to a concrete case
Consider a poor Muslim father with two children – one Muslim, one Christian. According to the principle, both children are equally obligated to maintain him, regardless of faith or gender. The obligation arises from closeness, not from their potential inheritance shares.
This shows that the rule transcends differences in wealth, gender, and religion.

Explaining categories of blood relatives and maintenance case examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the seven categories of blood relatives relevant to maintenance and apply the Hanafi principle that obligation is based on closeness and direct lineage, not inheritance.

Core Idea
Maintenance for blood relatives depends on closeness and direct lineage, not inheritance shares.

In this passage, the commentator expands the rules of maintenance (nafaqah) among blood relatives. He first categorizes all possible combinations of relatives into seven logical groups. Then he establishes the key principle: the obligation to provide maintenance follows closeness (qurb) and direct lineage (juz'iyyah), not inheritance rights. This principle is applied to several concrete examples.

Guided Path
1
List the seven categories of blood relatives
Identify each of the seven logical combinations mentioned: single individual, offspring only, offspring + collaterals, offspring + ascendants, offspring + ascendants + collaterals, ascendants only, ascendants + collaterals, and collaterals only (added for completeness).
The seventh category (collaterals only) is not considered primary blood relatives but included for rational completion.
2
Identify the governing principle for offspring and collaterals
For the first two categories (offspring only; offspring + collaterals), the text states 'what is considered is closeness (qurb) and direct lineage (juz'iyyah), not inheritance.' That means obligation falls on the relative who is closest in terms of descent and has a direct line (e.g., child over grandchild).
Direct lineage means being a direct part of the lineage chain (e.g., son is direct; grandson is not).

Apply the principle to the examples
Trace each example provided: two children equally; son over son's son; daughter over son's son; daughter over full sister; Christian son over Muslim brother; daughter's child over full brother. Note how the closest and most direct relative bears the obligation alone.

Thus a son is preferred over a father based on the hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father,' implying the son's wealth is effectively the father's. The same applies to a mother. But between a grandfather and a son's son, they are equal in closeness, so maintenance is shared in proportion to their inheritance shares (i.e., one-sixth each).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A father may sell his absent son's movables for maintenance by istihsan, but not immovables.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the scope of a father's authority over his adult absent son's property. While the general principle is that guardianship ends at majority, Abu Hanifa makes an exception for movable goods when the sale is needed for the son's maintenance. Understanding this dispute provides valuable insight into the use of istihsan as a tool within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on father's sale of son's property
Identify the ruling given in the matn: sale of movables is permissible (jā'iz) according to Abu Hanifa; sale of immovables is not. Note that this is an exception based on istihsan.
The permission is restricted to movables and only for the son's maintenance (nafaqah).
2
Identify the opposing position of the Sahibayn
State that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that no sale is permissible at all—whether movable or immovable—because the father's guardianship (wilāyah) over the son's property ends at the son's majority.
This is the ruling by strict analogy (qiyās).
3

Abu Hanifa argues that movables require active preservation (ḥifẓ)—they may spoil, be stolen, or depreciate. Selling them is a means of preservation. The father's wilāyat al-ḥifẓ (guardianship of preservation) allows the sale of movables.

The One-Month Limit for Past Maintenance Becoming a Debt
Completing the ruling that past maintenance falls due after a month

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which past maintenance for relatives becomes a debt in the Hanafi school and explain the rationale for the one-month threshold.

Core Idea
Past maintenance for relatives becomes a debt only after a long period (≥1 month) or with the judge's permission.

In Hanafi law, maintenance for relatives who are not the wife differs from wife's maintenance. While wife's maintenance becomes a debt immediately upon a judge's decree, past maintenance for other relatives falls due if not claimed promptly. This lesson clarifies the critical threshold: the period must be 'long' – defined as one month or more – for the past maintenance to become a recoverable debt. We explore the reasoning behind this rule and the exception when the judge permits borrowing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the claim for past maintenance of relatives (not wife). The ruling: it is not a debt unless the period is long (≥1 month) or the judge permitted borrowing. Otherwise, it falls due (sāqit).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a rational argument (dalīl aqlī) from al-Fath and al-Zayla'i: if the past maintenance became a debt instantly, the claimants could never collect it because the period would always be short. Thus, they set a month as the minimum for a 'long' period.
This is a practical necessity reasoning (istiḥsān).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The reasoning establishes that a month is the dividing line. The phrase 'muddah tawīlah' is defined as one month or more. Anything less is 'short' and does not make the past maintenance a debt.
The threshold is based on customary practice ('urf).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The rule differs for wife's maintenance: it becomes a debt immediately upon the judge's decree, because it is obligatory regardless of her wealth. Also, the Shafi'i school holds that all past maintenance is a debt without time limit.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The later Hanafi masters (al-Maydani, following al-Fath and al-Zayla'i) adopt the month limit as the muftā bihī position. This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Hidayah explains the ruling on a master's obligation to maintain his slaves, both male and female. The author states that the master must provide maintenance regardless of the slave's status—be it qinn (full slave), mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death), umm walad (slave mother of the master's child), young, or old. If the master refuses to provide maintenance and the slaves have the ability to earn (e.g., through labor), they are permitted to earn and spend on themselves. This is a balanced solution preserving both the slave's life and the master's ownership. If the slaves cannot earn (e.g., disabled or not hireable), the master is compelled either to sell them (if they are legally saleable) or to provide maintenance. The sale transfers the obligation to the new owner. However, if the slaves are unsaleable (like mudabbar or umm walad), the master is only compelled to provide maintenance, not to sell. The passage also distinguishes this from a wife's maintenance, which becomes a debt in the husband's liability and is not extinguished by delay, whereas slave maintenance does not become a debt and would be nullified if not provided. Additionally, animals are not legally entitled to maintenance, so the owner is not compelled but is encouraged ethically.

أم ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne her master's child; she becomes free upon his death and cannot be sold.
كسب (kasb): Earning capacity; the ability of a slave to work and generate income.

Comparing Maintenance Obligations for Wife, Slave, and Animals
Contrasting rulings on maintenance debts for wives, slaves, and animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance debts for wives, slaves, and animals, and identify the rational basis (illa: legal capacity) for the distinction.

Core Idea
Wife's maintenance becomes a debt; slave's does not — because the wife has legal capacity to claim.

In this passage from Al-Hidayah (quoted in Al-Lubab), a key distinction in Hanafi maintenance law is drawn. While a wife's past maintenance becomes a debt she can recover, a slave's maintenance does not. Why? The slave lacks legal capacity (ahl al-istiḥqāq). For animals, there is no legal compulsion at all, only a moral duty. We will also see an exception for unsaleable slaves like mudabbar and umm walad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage discusses maintenance (nafaqah) for three groups: wife, slave, and animals. Note the ruling for each.
2
Determine the legal effect of delay
For the wife, delayed maintenance becomes a debt (dayn) that she can claim. For the slave, it does not become a debt; instead the owner can be forced to sell the slave to provide maintenance. For animals, there is no compulsion, only a moral duty.
3
Analyze the underlying reason (illa)
The key factor is legal capacity (ahl al-istiḥqāq). The wife has capacity to claim; the slave does not; animals are not entitled.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on capacity.
4
Apply the exception for unsaleable slaves
If the slave is not saleable (e.g., mudabbar, umm walad), the owner is obliged to spend but cannot be forced to sell. This is from al-Durr.
5
Practice: hypothetical scenario
Consider a husband who delays wife's maintenance for one month. State the debt owed. Contrast with a slave owner who delays slave's maintenance.
The debt is the cost of maintenance for that month.

Commentary
This passage is a continuation of rulings on maintenance (nafaqah). The author distinguishes three categories: wife, slave, and animals. For the wife, maintenance becomes a debt (dayn) upon delay, meaning the husband owes past maintenance. For a slave, maintenance does not become a debt; instead, the owner can be compelled to sell the slave to provide for him. This is because the slave lacks legal capacity to claim.
