The Cap on Compensation for Killing a Slave by Mistake
Ruling on compensation for killing a slave by mistake with maximum and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for accidentally killing a slave, distinguish the majority view from Abu Yusuf's view, and apply the capped amount with reduction.

Core Idea
Compensation for a slave killed by mistake is capped at the diyah of a free person minus ten dirhams to reflect the slave's inferior status.

This passage from al-Lubab deals with the compensation (diyah) for killing a slave by mistake. The key issue is whether the killer pays the full market value or a capped amount. The Hanafi school, led by Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, imposes a cap at the diyah of a free person with a token reduction, while Abu Yusuf argues for the full value. The commentary explains the reasoning based on qiyas and establishes the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the hukm level
What is the act being ruled upon? It is killing a slave by mistake (qatl khatā'). The hukm level is wajib (obligatory) — compensation must be paid.
The ruling is a financial obligation, not a ritual one.
2
State the ruling and the cap
The killer pays the slave's market value (qimah), but this is capped at the diyah of a free person: 10,000 dirhams for a male, 5,000 for a female. If the value reaches or exceeds the cap, the killer pays the cap minus ten dirhams.
The ten-dirham reduction symbolizes the slave's lower rank (riqq).
3
Understand the reasoning (qiyas)
The ruling is derived by qiyas: the slave shares human nature (ma'ani) with a free person, so compensation should not exceed that of a free person. The reduction shows the deficiency of slavery.
The dalil is qiyas jaliy — the analogy is clear and explicit.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf disagrees: he holds that the full market value is due without cap. He sees the slave primarily as property, not as a human subject for diyah analogy.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, as confirmed by later authorities (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili). The correct reduction for both male and female slaves is ten dirhams, not five.
Al-Tashih and al-Yanabi' affirm this as the well-known narration.

Compensation for Cutting a Slave's Hand with Maximum Limit
Ruling on compensation for cutting a slave's hand with a maximum limit and a reduction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the amount of compensation for cutting a slave's hand in Hanafi fiqh and explain the reasoning based on analogy with the free person's diyah.

Core Idea
The compensation for a slave's cut hand is half his value but capped at 4,995 dirhams, derived from the free person's diyah reduced to reflect the slave's lower status.

This lesson examines the precise ruling for compensation when a slave's hand is cut off. The Hanafi school sets the amount at half the slave's market value but imposes a maximum limit based on the free person's diyah for the same injury. The reasoning uses qiyas (analogy) while incorporating an adjustment to reflect the slave's degraded rank. Understanding this ruling requires tracing the analogical chain and the capping mechanism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is cutting off a slave's hand. The ruling is that compensation is wajib (obligatory). The amount is half the slave's market value at the time of injury, but with a maximum ceiling.
The obligation arises from the injury, not from a transaction.
2
Determine the base amount
The base compensation is half the slave's current market value (nisf al-qimah). For example, if the slave is worth 8,000 dirhams, the base is 4,000 dirhams.
Value is assessed at the time of injury.
3
Apply the maximum cap
The compensation cannot exceed 5,000 dirhams minus five dirhams, i.e., 4,995 dirhams. This cap is derived from the free person's hand compensation (half of full diyah = 5,000 dirhams), reduced by five dirhams to show the slave's lower rank.
If half the value is less than 4,995, the actual amount is half the value. If half the value exceeds 4,995, the cap applies.
4
Understand the Dalil (proof)
The proof is qiyas (analogy) on the free person. The hand is considered half of the human body, so by analogy, the compensation for a slave's hand should be half his value, but the amount is capped and reduced because the slave is not fully equivalent to a free person.
The qiyas is jaliy (clear) because the hand's proportional value is evident.
5
Note the reduction
The five-dirham reduction (from 5,000 to 4,995) is an additional element to emphasize the lower rank (inḥiṭāṭ martabah) of the slave. This reduction is not applied to the free person's diyah.
Some narrations question the exact cap, but the relied-upon position includes the reduction.

Diyah for Cutting a Slave's Hand and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on diyah for a slave's hand with intra-Hanafi dispute resolution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the diyah for a slave's hand, identify the intra-madhhab dispute, and apply the tarjih to determine the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
A slave's diyah is based on value, not a fixed amount; the correct view gives full value without cap.

This lesson tackles a specific issue: if a slave's hand is cut off, what compensation is due? The passage presents two positions within the Hanafi school: one caps the amount, the other gives full value. We will see which is relied upon and why the tarjih matters for practical fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is cutting a slave's hand. The ruling is that monetary compensation wajib is due. Determine the baseline: half the slave's value because the hand is half of a person.
The baseline is derived by analogy to a free person: half of the free diyah corresponds to half the slave's value.
2
Understand the reasoning behind the cap
The passage states that some narrations limit the compensation to 5,000 dirhams minus five, to reflect the lowliness of a slave's rank. This is based on a narration from Muhammad in al-Kitab.
The cap is a concession that reduces the liability compared to the pure proportional value.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Al-Tashih reports that the correct view (al-sahih) is that the full value is due without any cap. This overrules the narrated limitation.
The dispute is between the narrated view (Muhammad's narration) and the preferred doctrinal view (al-sahih).
4
Identify the tarjih and mufta bihi
Al-Tashih and the commentator al-Maydani prefer the uncapped view, making it the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Thus the mufta bihi is that full value is due, regardless of amount.
5
Apply the general rule to other injuries
The passage states that any proportion of diyah for a free person corresponds to the same proportion of the slave's value. This rule applies to all injuries below killing.
Also, note the secondary dispute: for killing a slave, liability is on the 'Aqilah according to Abu Hanifah and Muhammad, but on the injurer's wealth according to Abu Yusuf.

States the diyah for causing a free woman to miscarry a dead fetus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the amount of ghurrah for a stillborn fetus and identify who is liable to pay it.

Core Idea
Causing a free woman to miscarry a dead fetus obligates a ghurrah of 500 dirhams, paid by the 'āqilah.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on compensation for causing a stillborn miscarriage. The passage from Lubab al-Khidayah explains the amount of the ghurrah and clarifies that it is borne by the 'āqilah (the male relatives), not the striker alone. Understanding this ruling is essential for knowing how Islamic law values fetal life and assigns financial responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act triggers the obligation? Causing a free woman to miscarry a dead fetus. The ruling is that a ghurrah is wajib.
2
Determine the amount and its basis
The ghurrah is half of one-tenth of the full diyah. For a male fetus, half of one-tenth of a man's diyah (10,000 dirhams) = 500 dirhams. For a female, similarly 500 dirhams.
The calculation ensures the amount is fixed regardless of gender.
3
Identify who pays and how
The ghurrah is paid by the striker's 'āqilah (tribal group) over one year, not by the striker alone.
This is consistent with other diyat obligations.
4
Note the conditions for the ruling
The fetus must be free (not a slave) and dead when miscarried. If it is born alive and then dies, the full diyah applies.
The condition 'free' excludes slaves, for whom a different valuation applies.

The Proportional Equivalence of a Slave's Value to a Free Person's Diyat
States general rule linking diyat of free person to value of slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that the value of a slave is proportional to the diyat of a free person, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding liability for the slave's life.

Core Idea
A slave's value replaces diyat proportionally; but who pays for the slave's life? The 'aqila or the offender?

This passage from al-Lubab establishes a key principle in Hanafi diyat law: the value of a slave corresponds proportionally to the diyat of a free person. However, a disagreement arises among Hanafi jurists over who bears the full diyat when a slave is killed—the offender's clan ('aqila) or the offender himself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule linking slave value to free diyat
The text states that whatever is determined from the diyat of a free person (e.g., half, quarter) is applied proportionally to the slave's value. For example, if a free person's diyat is 1000, half is 500; for a slave worth 800, half is 400.
The proportional link is based on the value serving as compensation for blood, just like diyat.

Ruling with proof that feeding is invalid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on feeding in kaffarah qatl and explain the proof based on the principle of tawqif.

Core Idea
In expiation for killing, only prescribed alternatives are valid—feeding is not one of them.

This short but powerful passage from Al-Quduri establishes a cornerstone of Hanafi fiqh: expiatory measures are fixed by revelation, not human reasoning. It affirms that the only valid ways to expiate accidental killing are those explicitly mentioned in the Quran and Sunnah—freeing a slave or fasting—and that feeding the poor, however charitable, does not fulfill this obligation. The passage uses the principle of tawqif to close the door on analogical extensions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text states that feeding (الإطعام) is 'لا يجزئ' – it is not valid as a substitute in the expiation for killing. Determine the exact legal classification: the act of feeding for this purpose is invalid (bāṭil) and does not discharge the obligation.
The phrase 'لا يجزئ' means it is completely insufficient, not merely deficient.
2
Locate the dalil
The author provides the proof: 'لم يرد به نص' – no textual evidence (Quran or hadith) permits food as a substitute. Identify this as a negative proof: the absence of authorization indicates prohibition.
The principle is that all expiations (kaffārāt) are 'taʿabbudī' – based purely on revelation, not reason.
3
Understand the legal principle: tawqīf
The text states 'والمقادير تعرف بالتوقيف' – measures are known by divine ordinance. This means the alternatives are fixed and cannot be expanded by personal opinion (رأي). Recognize that this is a usul al-fiqh maxim applied to expiations.
Al-Quduri is implicitly refuting those who might analogize feeding from other expiations (e.g., zakat al-fitr or kaffarah yamin).

Rulings on What the 'Aqilah Does Not Bear
Lists four exceptions to 'Aqilah liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four categories of injury that the 'Aqilah does not bear and explain the rationale for each exception.

Core Idea
The 'Aqilah bears only accidental injury; it does not bear intentional, slave, unconfirmed confession, or settlement for intentional blood.

The 'Aqilah is the clan responsible for paying blood money (diyah) on behalf of a member who causes accidental harm. However, this responsibility is not unlimited. Al-Quduri and Al-Lubab delineate four specific situations where the 'Aqilah is exempt, shifting liability to the perpetrator alone. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly applying diyat rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first exception: intentional injury
Locate the phrase 'ولا تَعْقِلُ العاقلة جناية العمد'. The 'Aqilah does not bear intentional injury because the primary liability is qisas (retaliation), not diyah. Compensation for intentional harm is the perpetrator's personal debt.
Intentional killing (qatl al-'amd) is excluded entirely.
2
Identify the second exception: slave-related injury
Locate 'والعبد'. The 'Aqilah does not bear injury involving a slave, whether the slave is the victim or the perpetrator. The slave's liability is in his own person; his master may hand him over or pay ransom.
The author affirms this despite variant copies of Al-Quduri omitting it.
3
Identify the third exception: confessed injury without confirmation
Locate 'الجناية التي اعترف بها الجاني'. A perpetrator's confession only binds himself, not the 'Aqilah. However, if the 'Aqilah members affirm the confession, they become liable because it becomes their admission.
The exception is conditional: 'إلا أن يُصدقوه' (unless they affirm him).
4
Identify the fourth exception: settlement for intentional blood
Locate 'ما لزم بالصلح عن دم العمد'. If the victim's family forgives qisas in exchange for monetary compensation (sulh), this amount is a debt on the perpetrator's own wealth, not on the 'Aqilah.
The original obligation was qisas; the substitute payment follows the original liability.

The 'Aqilah's Liability for Accidental Injury to a Slave
States that 'Aqilah bears diyah for accidental killing of slave, but not for lesser injuries.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when the 'Aqilah bears diyah for accidental injury to a slave and explain the distinction between fatal and non-fatal injuries.

Core Idea
The 'Aqilah bears diyah for accidental killing of a slave, but not for non-fatal injuries.

In Hanafi law, the 'Aqilah (the perpetrator's clan) is responsible for paying blood money (diyah) for accidental killings. But does this apply when the victim is a slave? This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies that the 'Aqilah bears full diyah for accidental killing of a slave, because the slave's life is a human soul. However, for non-fatal injuries, the 'Aqilah is not liable; instead, the perpetrator pays from his own wealth, as those injuries are treated as property damages. This ruling highlights a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh between the value of a life and the value of a body part.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the ruling
What is the scenario? A free person accidentally kills a slave. The ruling: the diyah is upon the 'Aqilah of the free person.
The 'Aqilah is the clan or group responsible for paying blood money on behalf of the perpetrator.
2
Identify the rationale
Why does the 'Aqilah bear it? Because it is ransom for a soul (fidā' al-nafs). The slave's life is valued as a human soul.
This reasoning treats the slave's life as equivalent to a free person's life for diyah purposes.
3
Distinguish the boundary
Non-fatal injuries (e.g., wounds) are not on the 'Aqilah. The passage says such injuries are treated as property matters (amwāl).
Property damages are a personal liability, not a collective responsibility of the 'Aqilah.
4
Consider the cross-madhhab implication
Other schools may differentiate; the Hanafi school treats the slave's soul as a person for diyah, but other schools may treat all injuries to slaves as property.
This distinction is a valid ikhtilaf.

Diyah from Bayt al-Mal When No 'Aqilah Exists
State ruling with proof via analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah when the killer has no 'Aqilah and explain the qiyas (analogy) that supports it.

Core Idea
When a killer lacks a clan, the Muslim public treasury stands as guarantor for blood money.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi diyat law: communal responsibility for accidental homicide. When a killer has no 'Aqilah (clan) to bear the blood money, the obligation shifts to Bayt al-Mal, the public treasury. This ruling is derived through a powerful analogy to inheritance rules, highlighting the cohesive social ethic in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
Read the passage carefully. The ruling is: diyah is paid from Bayt al-Mal. The condition is: the killer has no 'Aqilah (clan). Ensure you understand that 'Aqilah refers to the paternal relatives who traditionally assist in paying diyah for accidental killing.
2
Isolate the proof text
The passage quotes from Al-Hidayah: 'Because the congregation of Muslims are his helpers... and if he died, his inheritance would be for Bayt al-Mal, similarly for debts.' This is the dalil. Identify the analogy: inheritance without heirs → Bayt al-Mal inherits; diyah debt without 'Aqilah → Bayt al-Mal pays.
The proof is a qiyas (analogy) based on a common effective cause: communal responsibility when the individual has no designated supporters.
3

Break down the analogy: Asl (original case) = a Muslim dies without heirs → his estate goes to Bayt al-Mal. Far' (new case) = a killer without 'Aqilah owes diyah → Bayt al-Mal pays. Illa (effective cause) = the Muslim community is the ultimate support system for every Muslim, stepping in when specific relatives are absent. Hukm al-Asl = Bayt al-Mal takes the estate. Hukm al-Far' = Bayt al-Mal pays the diyah.
This qiyas is based on the principle that 'the community is the helper of every Muslim' – a statement of collective responsibility.

The commentator Al-Maydani notes that this sentence from Al-Quduri is not present in all manuscripts. This shows the importance of riwayah (transmission) in fiqh texts, though the ruling itself is well-established in the madhhab.
