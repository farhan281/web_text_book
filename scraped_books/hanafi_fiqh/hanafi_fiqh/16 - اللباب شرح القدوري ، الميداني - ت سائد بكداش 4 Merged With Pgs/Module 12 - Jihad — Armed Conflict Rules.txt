Learning Objective

Core Idea
A slave who enters dar al-Islam as a Muslim becomes free by operation of law because a Muslim cannot be enslaved.

This passage addresses a specific scenario arising from the context of war: a slave flees enemy territory and enters Muslim lands as a believer. The Hanafi school holds that such a person gains freedom immediately, without needing a formal manumission. This ruling is based on the principle that Islam and slavery are incompatible for a free Muslim who has reached dar al-Islam.

Guided Path

2
State the ruling
The slave becomes free immediately upon entry.
3

The rational argument: because when he enters dar al-Islam, his hand appears (his freedom becomes manifest) while he is a Muslim, so he cannot be enslaved.
This is a direct application of the principle that a Muslim cannot be enslaved in dar al-Islam.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools may require a formal process for emancipation; the Hanafi position is mukhtalaf fih.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is immediate freedom versus a continued state of slavery until court action.

Definition and Punishment of Intentional Killing
Defines intentional killing and its obligatory punishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of intentional killing, identify the evidence used to prove intent, and explain the obligatory punishment (qisas) and the role of the guardians.

Core Idea
Intent in killing is proven by the use of a lethal tool; qisas is obligatory unless the guardians pardon.

This passage from al-Maydani's commentary on al-Quduri defines deliberate homicide and its legal consequences. The Hanafi school uses the instrument of killing as objective evidence of intent, resolving the difficulty of proving subjective intention. Understanding this evidentiary rule is essential for distinguishing intentional killing from lesser degrees of homicide.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being defined? Intentional killing ('amad). What is the ruling? The killer is liable to divine punishment (ma'tham) and legal retaliation (qisas).
The ruling is wajib 'ayn: personally obligatory on the killer, but conditional on the guardians' demand.
2
Locate the evidence for intent
The text states that intent is proven by the use of a weapon or something that separates body parts. This is a qiyas: use of a lethal tool substitutes for direct proof of intent, just as travel substitutes for hardship.
The analogy (qiyas) is from the principle that circumstantial evidence can stand in for direct evidence in legal proof.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
There are two narrations (riwayatan) regarding unsharpened iron: the more apparent (azharuhuma) view considers it 'amad, consistent with the general rule. The less apparent view may require sharpness.
The ikhtilaf is about the scope of 'what is treated as a weapon.' The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that unsharpened iron also constitutes 'amad.
4
Understand the punishment and its optionality
Qisas (retaliation) is wajib, but the victim's guardians (awliya') have the right to pardon or accept monetary compensation (diyah). The guardian cannot take diyah without the killer's consent.
This shows that qisas is a personal right of the heirs, not a fixed ḥadd punishment that cannot be waived.

Defining Shibh al-‘Amad: Quasi-Intentional Killing
Defines quasi-intentional killing according to Abu Hanifa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa’s definition of shibh al-‘amad and apply it to distinguish quasi-intentional killing from intentional and accidental killing.

Core Idea
Shibh al-‘amad: the blow is intentional, but the tool is not deadly — intent to strike, not to kill.

In Hanafi fiqh, homicide is categorized by the perpetrator's intent and the means used. This lesson defines the intermediate category of shibh al-‘amad, which lies between full intent and pure accident. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether qisas applies or a heavier diyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key components
Note that the definition has two parts: (1) intentional striking (ta'ammud fi al-darb) and (2) use of an instrument not ordinarily lethal.
The word 'shibh' means resembling; it resembles 'amad in intent to strike but not in outcome.
2
Define the phrase 'ma laysa min sha'nihi al-qatl ghaliban'
This phrase means 'something that is not usually (ghaliban) of the nature to cause death.' It is the crux of distinguishing shibh al-‘amad from 'amad.
If the instrument is ordinarily lethal, it is 'amad.
3
Distinguish from 'amad and khata'
Contrast with 'amad (intent to kill, lethal tool) and khata' (no intent to strike, accidental). Shibh al-‘amad has intent to strike but no intent to kill and non-lethal tool.
The name 'shibh al-khata' refers to this similarity to error.
4
Determine the legal consequence
The ruling is that the perpetrator must pay a heavy diyah (diyah mughallazah) but is not subject to qisas. This absence of intent to kill prevents retaliation.
This is the practical outcome.

Rulings for Intentional Killing: No Expiation and Disinheritance
States two supplementary rulings for intentional killing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two additional rulings for intentional killing in Hanafi fiqh and explain the evidence for disinheritance.

Core Idea
Intentional killing carries no expiation and bars inheritance from the victim.

After establishing that intentional killing is a major sin, the Hanafi school derives two important consequences: the killer is not required to perform expiation (kaffarah) and is deprived of inheriting from the victim. This lesson examines the textual and rational basis for these rulings, drawing directly from al-Lubab's commentary on al-Quduri.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the passage and list the two legal consequences mentioned: no expiation and no inheritance.
These are supplementary rulings, not the primary prohibition.
2
Analyze the rationale for no expiation
The passage states that intentional killing is a 'pure major sin' (kabirah mahda), and expiation has a 'meaning of worship.' Understand why expiation cannot be attached to a pure sin according to Hanafi usul.
Expiation is an act of worship that mixes with benefit; a pure sin cannot be mixed with worship.
3
Examine the evidence for disinheritance
The hadith 'La miratha li qatil' is cited. Identify its source (Prophetic hadith) and its classification (ahad, zanni al-thubut).
The hadith is general, applying to all types of unjust killing.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Recognize that some schools (e.g., Maliki) require expiation for intentional killing. The Hanafi position is based on the purity of the sin.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the broader tradition.
5
Apply to a scenario
Consider a case of intentional killing: the killer does not expiate and does not inherit from the victim. Confirm that the disinheritance is only from the victim's estate, not from other relatives.
This is a common nuance.

Shadhdh Narration: Diyah on the Killer When No 'Aqila
States a shadhdh narration and its reasoning, contrasting with the relied-upon position.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the relied-upon Hanafi position and a shadhdh narration regarding diyah liability when the killer has no 'aqila, and explain the legal reasoning behind the shadhdh view.

Core Idea
A shadhdh narration is a legal curiosity, not a basis for fatwa; the default rule (killer pays) returns only when the concession ('aqila) is impossible.

In Hanafi fiqh, the diyah for unintentional homicide is normally borne by the 'aqila, the killer's agnatic kin, as a facilitation. This lesson examines a weak (shadhdh) narration attributed to Abu Hanifa that if the killer has no 'aqila, the diyah falls on his own wealth. Though not operative for fatwa, this view illustrates the underlying legal reasoning: the default rule resurfaces when the concession's condition is absent.

Guided Path
1
State the relied-upon position
Identify the well-known (mashhur) Hanafi ruling: The 'aqila (agnatic relatives) bear the diyah for unintentional homicide. This is the mufta bihi position.
The passage begins with 'al-riwayah wa 'alayhi al-fatwa' indicating this is the operative view.
2
Identify the shadhdh narration
Locate the weak transmission attributed to Abu Hanifa: if the killer has no 'aqila, the diyah is on his own wealth. This contradicts the relied-upon view.
The term 'shadhdhah' (weak/isolated) signals it is not part of the zahir al-riwayah.
3
Analyse the reasoning (ta'lil)
The shadhdh view relies on a default principle: the killer is liable because he caused the loss. The 'aqila's liability is an exception (concession) for leniency. When the condition for the exception (existence of 'aqila) fails, the default rule should apply.
This is a rational (aqli) argument based on the original rule and the nature of concession.
4
Understand the authority weight
Recognize that this narration is classified as shadhdh (weak) and is not adopted for fatwa. The relied-upon view is from the main transmitted works (al-Durar, al-Bazzaziyyah, al-Hidayah).
In Hanafi usul, a shadhdh narration has minimal authority; fatwa is on the well-known position.
5
Trace the practical consequence
Note that the shadhdh view provides a clear answer for the scenario of missing 'aqila, while the relied-upon position does not explicitly address it in this passage, leaving it ambiguous whether the 'aqila liability remains or another entity pays.
The practical difference: shadhdh says killer pays; mufta bihi likely still holds 'aqila or state treasury responsible.
