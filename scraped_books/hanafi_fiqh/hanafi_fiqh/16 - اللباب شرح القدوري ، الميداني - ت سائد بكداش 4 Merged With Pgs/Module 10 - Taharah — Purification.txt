Ranking of Female Custodians in Hanafi Fiqh
Ranking of aunts and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of female custodians after the mother and grandmother, and identify the preferred position in the intra-Hanafi dispute between the daughter of the paternal sister and the maternal aunt.

Core Idea
The Hanafi order of custody prioritizes maternal aunt over the daughter of paternal sister.

This lesson examines the detailed ranking of female relatives entitled to child custody in the Hanafi school, as elaborated in al-Lubab. Beyond the mother and grandmother, aunts are included in a specific order. The passage also addresses an important intra-Hanafi dispute about who takes precedence between the daughter of the paternal sister and the maternal aunt, with the relied-upon position being the maternal aunt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic order of female custodians in Hanafi fiqh
Recall that custody is primarily given to the mother, then to the maternal grandmother, then to the paternal grandmother, then to full sisters, then to half-sisters. This lesson extends that order to aunts.
2
List the four types of aunts mentioned in the passage in their priority order
The passage specifies: (1) maternal aunt of the mother (khālah al-umm), (2) maternal aunt of the father (khālah al-ab), (3) paternal aunt of the mother (ammah al-umm), (4) paternal aunt of the father (ammah al-ab).
This order is derived by analogy.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
The text cites al-Khāniyya: some narrations prefer the daughter of the paternal sister (bint al-ukht li-ab) over the maternal aunt (khālah), but the correct view is that the maternal aunt is more entitled.
This is a dispute within the Hanafi school itself.
4
Apply the relied-upon position
When both a maternal aunt and a daughter of the paternal sister claim custody, the maternal aunt is given priority.
This is the muftā bihī position.
5
Note the authority for this ruling
The author of al-Lubāb cites al-Khāniyya as the source for the preferred view.
Al-Khāniyya is an authoritative Hanafi compilation.

Effect of Marriage on Custody Rights
Effect of marriage on custody rights and evidentiary rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain when marriage causes loss of custody rights in Hanafi fiqh and when it does not, and state the rule for return of custody and the evidentiary rules for proving divorce.

Core Idea
Marriage to a stranger voids custody; marriage to a mahram does not; custody returns upon separation.

This lesson focuses on the effect of marriage on the custody rights of female relatives. The Hanafi school detailed the conditions under which marriage extinguishes the right and the exceptions for mahram husbands. Additionally, the passage addresses the return of custody upon separation and the evidentiary weight given to the woman's statement regarding her marital status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule regarding marriage and custody
The passage states that any female custodian who marries a stranger (ajnabi) to the child loses her right to custody.
Stranger means a non-mahram.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason given is that a stranger husband may neglect the child or treat him harshly ('gives him little' and 'looks with aversion').
3
Recognize the exception for mahram husbands
If the husband is a mahram relative of the child (like the grandfather, uncle, etc.), the right is not lost, because close kinship ensures compassion.
The example given is the grandmother marrying the grandfather.
4
Note the effect of separation
If the marriage ends (divorce or death), the custody right returns because the obstacle is removed.
5
Apply the evidentiary rule for proving divorce
In case of dispute, the woman's word is accepted regarding denying the marriage or claiming divorce, provided she does not name a specific husband. If she names a specific man, her word is not accepted without proof.
This is a procedural rule from al-Durr.

Male Custody Order in the Absence of Female Custodians
Ruling on male custody when no female custodian exists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for male custody when no female custodian exists and identify the correct relative through the 'asabah order.

Core Idea
In absence of female custodians, the nearest male 'asabah relative is entitled to custody.

This lesson presents the Hanafi ruling on who among male relatives is entitled to custody of a child when no eligible female relative exists. The text ties custody rights to the order of 'asabah (residuary heirs) known from inheritance law, ensuring the child is placed with the closest male guardian.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The ruling applies when no female relative (mother, grandmother, aunt, etc.) is eligible for custody. Confirm that condition is met.
2
State the rule
If men dispute over custody, the most entitled is the one closest in 'asabah (nearest male relative through the male line).
The phrase 'أقربهم تعصيباً' is key.
3
Understand the basis
The ruling is based on the principle that guardianship (wilayah) belongs to the nearest relative, and the order of 'asabah is already established in inheritance law.
Refer to the known order: son, grandson, father, grandfather, brother, nephew, uncle, etc.
4
Recall the 'asabah order
The complete order of 'asabah for custody follows that of inheritance. The closer in degree and stronger in blood relation takes precedence.
E.g., father before grandfather, grandfather before brother, etc.

Kinayah for Emancipation: The Rule of Intention
Ruling on the kinayah expression and its conditions for emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on kinayah expressions for emancipation and explain why intention is necessary.

Core Idea
A kinayah expression for emancipation requires intention — without it, no legal effect.

This lesson examines a key principle in Hanafi law regarding emancipation (itq) when using allusive language (kinayah). The passage from al-Lubab and its commentary from al-Hidayah establish that phrases like 'I have no ownership over you' do not automatically free a slave; the speaker's intention (niyyah) is decisive. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Consider the statement 'la malika li alayka' (I have no ownership over you). Determine the ruling based on whether the speaker intended emancipation.
If intention, the slave is freed; if not, no emancipation.
2
Classify the expression
Recognize that this expression is a kinayah (allusion) because it could mean sale or emancipation. It is not explicit (sarih).
Ambiguity is the key characteristic of kinayah.
3
Understand the role of intention
Intention resolves the ambiguity. Without it, neither meaning is determined, so the statement has no legal effect.
Niyyah is the decisive factor.
4
Apply to other kinayah expressions
The same rule applies to all similar phrases like 'you have left my ownership', 'I have no claim over you', etc.
Any expression that could mean something other than emancipation is kinayah.
5
Analyze the legal reasoning (talil)
The requirement of intention is based on the ambiguity (ihtimal). Since the phrase bears multiple possibilities, it cannot be definitively applied without the speaker's intent.
This reasoning is provided in the commentary.

Expression 'La Sultan Li Alayka' is Not a Kinayah
Ruling on denial of authority as kinayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the expression 'la sultan li alayka' with intention of emancipation, and explain why it does not effect emancipation using the distinction between sultan (physical control) and milk (ownership).

Core Idea
Denying physical control does not deny ownership.

In Hanafi fiqh, not every statement that seems to relinquish control over a slave counts as a kinayah (implied consent) for emancipation. One such statement is 'I have no authority over you' (la sultan li alayka). This short passage from al-Hidayah clarifies that even if the master intends freedom, the slave is not freed because 'sultan' refers only to physical control (yad), which can be absent while ownership (milk) remains intact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key expression and intent
The master says 'la sultan li alayka' (I have no authority over you) and internally intends (nawa) to emancipate the slave. The question is whether this verbal act effects emancipation.
2
Recognize the ruling
The passage states unequivocally: 'He does not become free' (lam ya'tiq). This is the Hanafi ruling.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The commentary explains that 'sultan' means physical control (yad), not ownership (milk). Ownership can exist without physical control, as exemplified by the mukatab (a slave under a contract of manumission who has de facto control over himself). Therefore, denying sultan does not imply denying milk.
This is in contrast to the expression 'la sabil li alayka' (I have no claim over you), which is a valid kinayah because 'sabil' implies ownership.
4
Apply the distinction to the ruling
Since 'sultan' is limited to physical authority, its negation leaves the core of ownership untouched. Hence, the statement cannot serve as a kinayah for emancipation, even if the master intends it.

Core Idea

This lesson examines a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school about the conditions for valid slaughter (dhabh). By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on permissible slaughter tools, explain the exception of attached teeth and nails, and apply the rule to detached instruments.

Core Idea
Slaughter is valid with any sharp tool that cuts, but attached tooth and nail are prohibited because they kill by weight.

This lesson opens the Hanafi Book of Hunting and Slaughterings. It establishes the fundamental principle that any sharp tool capable of causing blood flow is acceptable for slaughter, with two explicit exceptions: attached teeth and nails. The Prophet's hadith provides the textual basis, and Hanafi jurists add nuance for detached instruments. Understanding these conditions ensures that your slaughter is valid according to the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule of valid tools
The passage states that slaughter is permissible with al-layṭah (reed peel), al-marwah (sharp stone), and anything that causes blood to flow by cutting the jugular veins.
2
Identify the exceptions and their textual proof
The exception is the attached tooth and attached nail. The prohibition is based on the Prophet's hadith: 'Whatever causes blood to flow... except the tooth and the nail.'
The prohibition is by consensus.
3
Understand the reasoning behind the exceptions
These instruments kill by weight (thiqal) rather than cutting, causing unnecessary suffering. The nail is also associated with the practice of Abyssinians (kuffar), which is prohibited to imitate.
4
Distinguish between attached and detached instruments
If a tooth or nail is detached and acts like a knife (i.e., cuts), the slaughter is valid according to Abu Hanifa, though disliked (makruh).
This is a Hanafi position; other schools may differ.
5
Note the recommended practices and disliked actions
It is recommended to sharpen the knife before laying the animal down. Reaching the spinal cord (nukha') or cutting off the head before the animal is still is disliked (makruh) due to unnecessary torture.

Commentary

The commentary draws from various sources like Hashiyat al-Hamawi, al-Jawhara, and Fath al-Bari to clarify terms and reasoning.

Sharpening the Knife and Reaching the Spinal Cord
Ruling on recommended sharpening and disliked reaching the spinal cord

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sharpening the knife before slaughter and explain why reaching the spinal cord is disliked but the meat remains lawful.

Core Idea
Sharpening the knife is recommended; cutting the spinal cord is disliked but does not render the meat unlawful.

This lesson from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri addresses two important etiquettes of dhabh: the recommendation to sharpen the knife and the ruling on cutting into the spinal cord or severing the head. Understanding these rulings ensures that the slaughter is performed with minimal suffering while maintaining the permissibility of the meat. The passage relies on a hadith forbidding the use of teeth and claws, extending the principle to unnecessary cruelty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acts and their rulings
First, locate the two distinct rulings: (1) sharpening the blade is mustahabb (recommended) and should be done before laying the animal down. (2) Reaching the spinal cord or cutting the head before the animal becomes still is makruh (disliked) but does not prohibit the meat.
2
Locate the underlying proof (dalil)
The commentary alludes to the hadith where the Prophet forbade slaughtering with teeth and nails because they cause torture. By analogy, any unnecessary cutting after the throat is cut increases suffering.
The hadith is narrated in Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof connects)
The prohibition of teeth and nails is based on the principle of avoiding extra pain. Reaching the spinal cord or cutting the head adds pain without any benefit, so the action is disliked. However, the meat is halal because the dislike is attached to the method, not the validity of the slaughter.
The dislike is tanzihi (slight), not tahrimi (prohibitive).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi agreement and cross-madhhab difference
The Hanafi imams agree on these rulings. Some other schools (e.g., Maliki) consider cutting the head as more severely disliked or even prohibitive. The Hanafi position relies on the principle that dislike of an action does not render the object unlawful.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi view.

Ruling on Slaughtering Non-Edible Animals
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the effects of slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the purification of meat and skin of non-edible animals after slaughter, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute on whether the meat becomes pure.

Core Idea
Slaughter purifies the skin of non‑edible animals, but whether its meat becomes pure is disputed within the Ḥanafī school.

This passage from al-Lubāb fī Sharḥ al-Qudūrī examines the effects of slaughter on animals whose meat is not ordinarily eaten. It presents the initial view of Abu Hanifa that both meat and skin become pure, and then discusses the differing views among later Hanafi scholars, particularly regarding the permissibility of eating such meat. Understanding this dispute illustrates how the school's positions evolve over time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What is the legal question? The effect of slaughter on animals whose meat is not normally eaten (e.g., mules, donkeys) regarding purity of meat and skin.
Issue: purification and permissibility of meat.
2
State the initial Hanafi view (Abu Hanifa)
According to the first view, slaughter purifies both meat and skin because it removes impure blood. This is analogous to tanning.
Tadhkiyah removes the impurity of blood.
3
Outline the opposing views within the school
Some later scholars (as cited in al-Kafi, al-Ghayah) hold that only the skin becomes pure, not the meat. Others say eating is makruh tanzih. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said no harm in eating.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the meat's purity.
4
Understand the tarjih (preferred position)
The relied-upon fatwa today is that eating the meat is makruh tanzih, though the skin is purified. This is the view of many mutakhkhirin.
Mufta bihi: makruh tanzih.
5
Note exceptions
Humans and pigs are not purified by slaughter due to the honor of humans and the inherent impurity of pigs.
Exception: adam and khinzir.

Commentary
This passage deals with the effect of slaughter (tadhkiyah) on animals whose meat is not normally eaten (e.g., mules, donkeys). The ruling: slaughter purifies both the meat and skin according to the first view (Abu Hanifa). The proof: slaughter removes flowing blood, which is impure, so the remaining parts become pure. However, there is a dispute about eating such meat. Some say it is makruh tanzih (slightly disliked), but the first view (permissible) is more correct. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad also say no harm in eating it. Later scholars differed: many shuyukh hold that only the skin becomes pure (like via tanning), not the meat, and this is considered more correct by some. The mufta bihi in later fatwa is that eating such meat is makruh tanzih. Two exceptions: humans and pigs—they are not purified by slaughter due to honor of humans and inherent impurity of pigs.

الدباغ (al-dabagh): The process of tanning animal skin to purify it.
