Definition and Causes of Nafaqah
Definition and causes of maintenance obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of nafaqah, list its three causes, and explain why a wife's maintenance is wajib regardless of her religion or wealth.

Core Idea
Maintenance is wajib for a wife upon her husband, unconditional on her religion or wealth.

This lesson introduces the Book of Maintenance (Kitab al-Nafaqat). You will learn the linguistic and legal definitions of nafaqah, the three causes that trigger its obligation, and the unconditional nature of a wife's right to maintenance. The passage emphasizes that once a valid marriage contract exists, the husband must provide food, clothing, and housing, regardless of the wife's faith or financial status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition
Read the definition given by Hisham from Imam Muhammad: nafaqah consists of food, clothing, and housing. Note that this is narrower than some other schools.
The Hanafi school limits nafaqah to these three; medical expenses are not included.
2
List the three causes of obligation
The text states three causes: marriage (zawjiyyah), kinship (qarabah), and ownership (milk). Remember that the author begins with marriage because it is the origin of lineage.
3
Analyze the unconditional nature of marital maintenance
The passage explicitly says maintenance is wajib even if the wife is non-Muslim, poor, not cohabited with, minor, insane, or old. Consider the legal reasoning: the obligation arises solely from the marriage contract.
This ruling is based on the Quran and Sunnah; the text does not cite them here but assumes their authority.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
Why does the author prioritize marriage over kinship and ownership? Because marriage is the root of lineage (nasab), and lineage is stronger than ownership. This ordering reflects a hierarchy of obligations.

Definition of Nafaqah and Timing of Wife's Maintenance
Opens the book on maintenance, defines nafaqah and its causes, and discusses the wife's maintenance obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of nafaqah, identify its three causes, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on when the wife's maintenance becomes obligatory, including the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Nafaqah is wajib upon marriage, but the Hanafi school debates whether actual transfer is required.

This lesson opens the chapter on maintenance (nafaqah) in al-Lubab. We begin with the definition of nafaqah, its causes, and the general ruling that a husband must provide for his wife. However, there is an important intra-Hanafi dispute on when this obligation begins, specifically whether the wife must actually move into the husband's house before she is entitled to maintenance. Understanding this dispute will sharpen your ability to distinguish between the authoritative transmission (zahir al-riwayah) and minority narrations.

Guided Path
1
Define nafaqah linguistically and legally
Identify the linguistic meaning (what one spends on dependents) and the legal definition given by Imam Muhammad: food, clothing, and lodging.
2
List the three causes of maintenance obligation
The text states that maintenance becomes obligatory due to marriage, kinship, and ownership. Note that marriage is the first mentioned because it is the origin of lineage.
3
State the general ruling for the wife
Maintenance is wajib for the wife upon her husband, regardless of her religion, wealth, age, or consummation, provided she does not refuse to live with him.
This is the zahir al-riwayah position.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
The text presents a narration from Abu Yusuf that adds a condition: the wife must submit herself in the husband's house (taslim nafsiha fi manzilihi). Contrast this with the zahir al-riwayah that does not require actual transfer.
The dispute is about the timing of the obligation.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position
The text indicates that the fatwa is on the zahir al-riwayah: maintenance is obligatory before consummation and transfer if the wife does not refuse to live with him.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, found in al-Mabsut and al-Muhit.

Commentary
This passage opens the book on maintenance (nafaqah) in al-Lubab. It first defines nafaqah linguistically and legally, then lists its three causes: marriage, kinship, and ownership. The author prioritizes marriage because it is the foundation of lineage, which is stronger than ownership. The central ruling is that a husband must provide maintenance for his wife, regardless of her religion, wealth, or age, and even if he is poor or young. However, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute about when this obligation begins. The maintenance includes customary food, drink, clothing, and lodging, interpreted as such because legal maintenance has a broader scope.

Whose Status Determines Spousal Maintenance? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Nafaqah
Explaining an intra-Hanafi dispute on nafaqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi opinions on whose financial status determines nafaqah, identify the mufta bihi, and apply the ruling to scenarios of differing financial status.

Core Idea
The mufta bihi considers both spouses' financial status, not just the husband's, for nafaqah.

The amount of spousal maintenance (nafaqah) depends on financial status, but whose status matters? The matn states it considers both spouses, but the commentary reveals an intra-Hanafi dispute. This lesson walks through the two positions, the tarjih, and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two opinions
Name each opinion within the Hanafi school: one considers only the husband's status (Zahir al-Riwayah), the other considers both spouses (the mufta bihi).
2
Name the scholars associated
The opinion of considering both is attributed to al-Khassaf (favored by al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah), while considering only the husband is the choice of al-Karkhi and the Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Determine authority hierarchy
Zahir al-Riwayah is the primary transmitted position, but later authorities (al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi) and the fatwa follow al-Khassaf. Understand that mufta bihi can override Zahir al-Riwayah.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
When spouses' statuses differ: under Zahir al-Riwayah, maintenance is solely based on the husband's capacity. Under the mufta bihi, both are considered; if the husband is poor, he pays his capacity and the rest remains a debt.
5
Apply to three scenarios
Practice: both wealthy → maintenance of wealthy; both poor → maintenance of poor; differing → apply the mufta bihi rule.

Maintenance When Wife Withholds for Prompt Dowry and When She Rebels
Rulings on maintenance with causal analysis and an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that maintenance is compensation for confinement (ihitbas) and distinguish between justified withholding (right to prompt dowry) and nushuz (unjustified leaving) to determine maintenance entitlement.

Core Idea
Maintenance is for confinement, not obedience — a wife who withholds with right keeps it; a wife who rebels loses it.

In the Hanafi school, maintenance (nafaqah) is not simply a consequence of marriage; it is compensation for the wife's availability (ihitbas) in the husband's home. This lesson examines two scenarios that affect that availability: the wife who withholds herself until she receives her prompt dowry, and the wife who rebels by leaving the house without right. The underlying principle — who is responsible for the loss of confinement — determines whether maintenance is owed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios and their rulings
Read the text and note the two cases: (1) wife withholds for prompt dowry → she has maintenance; (2) wife rebels (leaves without right) → no maintenance until she returns.
The ruling for the first scenario applies only to the prompt dowry (mahr mu'ajjal), not the deferred portion.
2
Understand the legal cause (illah): maintenance as compensation for confinement
Hanafis base maintenance on the wife's confinement (ihitbas) in the husband's house. If confinement is lost due to the husband's fault, maintenance continues; if lost due to the wife's fault, maintenance stops.
This is an example of ta'lil (causal reasoning) in fiqh.
3
Apply the illah to the first scenario
The wife withholds herself because the husband has not paid the due prompt dowry. Her prevention is 'with right' (bi-haqq), so the loss of confinement is attributed to the husband. Hence maintenance is wajib.
The text uses the principle: 'فكان فَوْتُ الاحتباس بمعنى من قبله'.
4
Apply the illah to the second scenario (nushuz)
The wife leaves the house without permission and without right. The loss of confinement is her own doing, so maintenance drops. When she returns, confinement is restored and maintenance resumes.
Note the distinction: nushuz here is defined as leaving the house, not merely refusing intercourse within the house.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute on deferred dowry
The text limits the first ruling to the prompt dowry. If the dowry is deferred and she has received the prompt portion, the majority (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say she cannot withhold. A minority view (attributed to 'the second') allows withholding for the entire dowry.
This difference affects whether maintenance continues when the wife withholds for a deferred dowry after receiving the prompt part.

Commentary

However, the commentator notes that this applies only to the prompt portion of the dowry; if the dowry is deferred (even partially) and she has already received the prompt part, the majority (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold she cannot withhold herself, with a contrary view attributed to 'the second' (likely Imam Muhammad or possibly Abu Hanifa — the text is ambiguous). The second scenario is nushuz (rebellion): if the wife leaves her husband's house without permission and without right, she forfeits maintenance until she returns because the loss of confinement is her own doing. The text distinguishes between leaving the house and merely refusing intercourse within the house—in the latter case, confinement is still present and the husband can exert his right, so maintenance is not forfeited.

Nafaqah for Minor Spouses: The Ihtibas Principle

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, maintenance is compensation for the wife's confinement; if no enjoyment is possible, no maintenance is owed.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a nuanced situation: what happens to maintenance (nafaqah) when a spouse is too young to consummate the marriage? The answer depends on which spouse is the minor, because Hanafi law treats maintenance as an exchange for the wife's availability (ihtibas). Let's walk through the three scenarios and their rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first scenario: minor wife
The passage states that if the wife is so young that she cannot be enjoyed even for service or companionship, she gets no maintenance even if she surrenders herself. Why? Because maintenance is in exchange for confinement (ihtibas), and confinement only has value if the husband can benefit from her.
The dowry (mahr) is still due because it arises from the contract itself, not from consummation.
2
Identify the second scenario: minor husband, adult wife
If the husband is a minor incapable of intercourse but the wife is an adult who can be enjoyed, she does receive maintenance from the husband's property. The surrender (taslim) is actualized from her side; the impediment is solely from the husband, so he is treated like an impotent man (majbub) or a eunuch ('unin).
This ruling is derived by qiyas (analogy) to cases of impotence and defects.
3
Identify the third scenario: both spouses minor
If both are minors, no maintenance is due. The prevention (man') arises from the wife's side (her minority), so the husband's disability is treated as non-existent. The wife's own inability to be enjoyed blocks her claim.
This is a refinement: the husband's impediment does not create obligation when the wife also cannot be enjoyed.
4

Maintenance is not a pure right of the marriage contract; it is compensation for the wife's confinement (ihtibas). Confinement means the wife is available for the husband's enjoyment. If she cannot be enjoyed (due to minority), the confinement has no value, so no maintenance is owed. If she can be enjoyed but the husband cannot, her confinement is still valid, so maintenance is owed.
This principle is unique to the Hanafi school and explains the difference from the Shafi'i view.
5
Apply the principle to each scenario
For each scenario, determine: (1) Is the wife capable of being enjoyed? (2) Is the husband capable of enjoying? (3) Who is the source of the impediment? The ruling follows from these answers.
The rule can be summarized: maintenance is wajib unless the wife's inherent inability prevents enjoyment.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Hanafi family law: the widow does not receive maintenance during her iddah, unlike the divorced woman. Contrast with the divorced woman
A divorced woman does receive maintenance during her iddah because her husband is alive and his property is still capable of bearing new obligations. The widow's case is different because the husband is dead.
This distinction is crucial in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Illness in the marital home does not cut off maintenance because the husband still benefits; the retention (ihtibas) remains.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a wife falls ill while living in her husband's house. Does she still receive maintenance? The Hanafi school says yes, based on istihsan, but Abu Yusuf adds a condition about whether she had submitted before the illness. Understanding this ruling clarifies the interaction between the wife's obligation of tamkin and the husband's duty of nafaqah.

Abu Yusuf distinguishes based on whether the wife submitted (tamkin) before or after falling ill. He argues that submission is a condition for maintenance.

Abu Yusuf holds that tamkin is a condition for nafaqah. If the wife falls ill before submitting, her later submission is invalid because she was not capable at the time.
His view prioritizes the literal condition of tamkin.

The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa: illness in the husband's house does not remove maintenance. The author's phrase 'in the husband's house' indicates this.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mother's compassion gives her priority—but only if she matches the alien's fee, not more.

This passage from al-Hidayah and al-Zaylaʿi clarifies the mother's right to nurse her child when the father brings an alien wet nurse. The key is that her compassion earns her priority, but she must accept the same fee as the alien; if she demands more, the father is protected from being forced to pay extra. We will unpack the ruling, its condition, and the Quranic proof that prevents harm to the father.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Condition
The passage states: if the father refuses to hire the mother and brings an alien wet nurse, but the mother agrees to nurse for the same fee as the alien, then the mother is more entitled to the child. Identify this as the primary ruling and note its condition: the mother must match the alien's fee.
The ruling is based on the mother's greater compassion and the child's interest.
2
Locate the Proof (Dalil)
The text cites Quran 2:233: 'No mother shall be harmed through her child, nor a father through his child.' The proof is used to support the second part of the ruling: if the mother demands more, the father is not forced to pay, to prevent harm to him.
The proof is a Quranic verse with certain transmission (qatiʿ al-thubut) but its application here is deemed zanni al-dalala.

The operative phrase is 'lā tuḍārra' (no harm). The Hanafi interpretation is that obligating the father to pay more than the alien's fee would harm him, so it is forbidden. Therefore, the father cannot be compelled to pay extra.
The prohibition in the verse is understood as preventing the imposition of extra cost beyond the customary fee.

The passage ends with a special case: if the father is insolvent and a paternal aunt volunteers for custody without pay, the mother is told to either keep the child without wages or give the child to the aunt. This shows that the mother's right to a fee is limited when the father cannot afford it and a volunteer is available.
This exception is from al-Badāʾiʿ and balances the child's interest with the father's financial capacity.
5

The practical outcome is that a mother cannot demand a fee higher than the alien's; if she does, the father may hire the alien instead. If she matches the fee, she gets priority due to her compassion.
This ruling protects the father from exploitation while still giving the mother preference when she accepts the market rate.

ثم لا بد من الحاجة والصغر والأنوثة، والزمانة، والعمى أمارة الحاجة؛ لتحقق العجز، فإن القادر على الكسب غني بكسبه، بخلاف الأبوين؛ لأنهما يلحقهما تعب الكسب، والولد مأمور بدفع الضرر عنهما، فتجب نفقتهما مع قدرتهما على الكسب.
«هداية».
قيد بالمحرم؛ لأن الرحم غير المحرم لا تجب نفقته، كابن العم وإن كان وارثاً.
ولا بد أن تكون المحرمية بجهة القرابة، ولذا قيدنا المحرمية بقولنا: منه: أي الرحم، فلو كان قريباً محرماً من غير جهة الرحم، كابن العم إذا كان أخاً من الرضاع فإنه لا نفقة له، كما في «البحر». عن «شرح الطحاوي».
(ويجب ذلك عليهم على قدر الميراث)؛ لأن التنصيص على الوارث تنبيه على اعتبار المقدار، ولأن الغرم بالغنم.
«هداية».
Continuation of rulings on maintenance. And that is obligatory according to the shares of inheritance. Then need, childhood, femininity, chronic illness, and blindness are indications of need; to confirm incapacity, for the one able to earn is rich by his earning, unlike the two parents; because they suffer the hardship of earning, and the child is commanded to repel harm from them, so their maintenance is obligatory even if they are able to earn. [Hidayah] It is restricted to mahram; because a non-mahram relative, like a cousin even if an heir, his maintenance is not obligatory. And mahramiyyah must be through lineage, so we restricted mahramiyyah by saying: minhu: i.e., the womb. So if a relative is mahram from other than lineage, like a cousin who is a foster brother, then there is no maintenance for him, as in al-Bahr from Sharh al-Tahawi. (And that is obligatory upon them according to the shares of inheritance) because specifying the heir is a hint to consider the amount, and because liability is based on benefit. [Hidayah]

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hidayah commentary on al-Quduri. It states the general rule that maintenance (nafaqah) of relatives is obligatory upon heirs in proportion to their inheritance shares. However, this obligation is conditional: the recipient must be in need (indicated by factors like childhood, chronic illness, blindness) and must be a mahram relative through lineage. A non-mahram relative, such as a paternal cousin, even if an heir, is not entitled to maintenance. The proof given is twofold: first, the mention of 'heir' in the texts implies that maintenance should be proportionate to inheritance; second, the legal maxim 'al-ghurm bi al-ghunm' (liability follows benefit) supports this division. The passage also notes an exception for parents: their maintenance is obligatory even if they are able to earn, because of the dignity of parents and the child's duty to prevent harm from them.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance: food, clothing, and shelter provided to those entitled.
محرم (mahram): A relative with whom marriage is permanently prohibited due to blood, fosterage, or marriage.
الغرم بالغنم (al-ghurm bi al-ghunm): A legal maxim meaning 'liability is based on benefit' or 'he who receives profit must bear loss.'

Key Takeaway
Maintenance of relatives is wajib upon heirs in proportion to their inheritance shares, but only for those relatives who are needy and mahram through lineage; parents are an exception.
