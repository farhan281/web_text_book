Ruling on Milk Mixing with Water in Fosterage
States ruling on milk mixed with water in fosterage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of predominance (ghalabah) to determine whether fosterage prohibition arises from a milk mixture.

Core Idea
In mixtures, the predominant substance alone determines the ruling; the overcome component is legally non-existent.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents a concrete application of a key Hanafi principle: when two substances mix, the legal effect follows the majority. Here, the mixture of breast milk with water is examined. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly identifying mahram relationships through fosterage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mixture
The passage deals with a mixture of breast milk and water. Note that the mixture is relevant only if it is consumed in a quantity that would normally be considered feeding.
2
Determine Predominance
Assess which component is greater in quantity or effect. The Arabic term 'al-ghalib' (predominant) is key. Here, predominance is based on volume. If milk is more than half, it is predominant.
Hanafi fiqh often uses volume for liquid mixtures, but other criteria may apply for different mixtures.
3
Apply the Ruling
If milk is predominant, prohibition of fosterage (tahrim) applies. If water is predominant, prohibition does not apply. The outcome determines whether the nursing child becomes mahram to the wet nurse and her relatives.
This ruling does not affect the permissibility of the mixture itself, only its effect on fosterage relationships.
4
Understand the Legal Principle
The reasoning given is 'because that which is overcome is not considered existent in legal effect'. This is a general Hanafi maxim (al-maghlub ghayr mawjud hukman) used in various chapters, such as in purity rules for water mixed with impurities.

Mixing Milk with Food: The Dispute Between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on fosterage with milk mixed in food

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on fosterage when milk is mixed with food, explain the qualification of the Sahibayn's view, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
When milk mixes with food, the fosterage prohibition falls — Abu Hanifa's view prevails.

In this lesson, we examine a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of mixing breast milk with solid food on the rules of fosterage (ridāʿ). Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on whether the prohibition of marriage due to fosterage applies when milk is the predominant ingredient in a food mixture. The commentary from Al-Hidayah provides a crucial qualification that resolves the apparent conflict.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
Recognize that the dispute is about fosterage (ridāʿ) and whether consuming breast milk mixed with food establishes a mahram relationship, making marriage prohibited.
The ruling in question is the prohibition of marriage (hurmah al-ridāʿ).
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that no prohibition applies, even if milk is the predominant component. His reasoning: the milk loses its independent identity when mixed with solid food.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and its qualification
The Sahibayn argue that prohibition applies if milk is predominant. However, Al-Hidayah qualifies this: it only applies when the milk has not been cooked with fire. If cooked, all agree no prohibition.
The qualification is key to reconciling the two views.
4
Apply the tarjih
In practice, the fatwa follows Abu Hanifa's position. The Sahibayn's view is valid but limited to uncooked milk and is not the operative ruling.
The author of Al-Lubab confirms Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon one.

The Hanafi school rules that as long as milk is the predominant component, the prohibition applies. Milk from a Deceased Woman: Prohibition of Fosterage Applies
States ruling on milk expressed after mother's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fosterage from a deceased woman and explain the qiyas that supports it.

Core Idea
Milk after death retains its legal effect; feeding it to an infant establishes the same fosterage prohibition as during life.

Some assume that after a woman's death, her milk becomes impure or loses its legal capacity to create fosterage relationships. The Hanafi school, however, holds that the milk remains effective because its substance is unchanged. This lesson explores the ruling and its qiyas-based reasoning from Al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the explicit ruling: if milk is expressed from a deceased woman and fed to an infant, the prohibition of fosterage (tahrim) applies.
The ruling is unconditional regarding the woman's life or death.
2
Identify the operative act
The act is 'awjara' – feeding the milk orally so it reaches the infant's stomach. This fulfills the essential meaning of rada' (fosterage).
Awjara requires the milk to enter the throat and reach the stomach; mere contact is insufficient.
3
Recognize the qiyas structure
The passage argues by qiyas (analogy): the milk after death is identical in substance to milk before death; therefore, its legal effect (prohibition) remains. The asl is milk from a living mother; the far' is milk from a deceased mother; the illa is the milk's unchanged nature.
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) based on the same effective cause.
4
Understand the implicit objection and response
An implicit objection: death may nullify the milk's effectiveness. The Hanafi response: death does not change the milk's composition; it remains nourishing and capable of sustaining life, so its legal property persists.
The school does not require the mother to be alive for the milk to be effective.

Core Idea
Dominance (ghalabah) determines the ruling when human milk mixes with animal milk.

When a woman's milk mixes with animal milk, whether the mixture establishes the prohibition of marriage (radāʿ) depends on which milk is predominant. The Hanafi school, by analogy with the case of mixed water, holds that the dominant substance governs the ruling. In this lesson, we examine the ruling, its proof, and the underlying legal principle.

Identify what the author says: prohibition attaches if human milk is dominant, not if animal milk is dominant.
2
Analyze the Proof: Analogy with Water
The ruling is based on qiyas with the case of pure and impure water mixed together: the dominant substance determines the ruling.
The asl (original case) is mixed water, the far' (new case) is mixed milk.
3
Identify the Illa (Effective Cause)
The common factor is dominance (ghalabah). The Hanafi principle: in mixtures of similar substances, the majority decides.
This illa is clear (jaliyyah).
4
Apply to Variations
If human milk dominates, the mixture is treated as human milk and prohibition applies. If animal milk dominates, it is like animal milk and no prohibition.
If proportions are equal, the text implies no clear ruling; precaution may require avoidance.
5

Note that some scholars hold that any amount of human milk establishes prohibition, but the Hanafi mufta bihi is the dominance rule.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Scope of Zihar: Wives Only and Collective Declaration
Stating the limits of zihar: only wives, and collective declaration effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that zihar is only valid from a wife (not a slave) and apply the ruling to a collective declaration to multiple wives.

Core Idea
Zihar is legally tied to marriage; a slave is not a wife, so no zihar. Collective zihar covers all wives.

This passage clarifies two essential limits of zihar: it can only be declared against a wife, not a female slave; and if a man says to his multiple wives collectively that they are like his mother's back, the ruling applies to all of them. The proof comes from the Quran and the analogy with divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its scope
The act is zihar. Determine against whom it is valid. The passage asserts it is only from a wife (zawjah).
The Arabic 'لا يكون إلا من زوجته' limits zihar to wives.
2
Locate the Quranic dalil
The author cites the verse from Surah al-Mujadilah (58:2) which uses 'min nisa'ihim' (from their wives). This restricts zihar to wives, excluding slaves.
The word 'min' indicates partitive/al-tab'id, but here it is used contextually to specify the category.
3
Understand why slaves are excluded
Zihar is analogized to divorce (talaq). Since divorce does not apply to a female slave (because she is not in a marriage contract that includes divorce), zihar does not either.
This is a qiyas: zihar is taken from talaq in effect and conditions.
4
Apply the ruling to collective declaration
If a man addresses his multiple wives with a zihar phrase (e.g., 'antunna 'alayya ka-zahri ummi'), he becomes a muzahir from all of them, just as if he divorced them collectively.
The analogy (qiyas) with divorce makes the ruling extend to all wives.
5
Summarize the practical result
Zihar is only valid from a wife, and if said to multiple wives collectively, it applies to all. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that zihar is invalid from a slave.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is reported here.

Valid and Invalid Defects in a Slave for Zihar Expiation
Listing permissible and impermissible physical defects in slaves for zihar kaffara.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which physical defects in a slave make zihar expiation invalid and apply the Hanafi principle of 'jins al-manfa‘ah' to determine validity.

Core Idea
Only defects eliminating the genus of benefit, not mere impairments, invalidate a slave for zihar kaffara.

When a person performs zihar (declaring his wife unlawful like his mother), one expiation option is freeing a slave. Not every slave is acceptable: the Hanafi school, as taught by al-Maydani, specifies which physical defects are tolerable and which disqualify. The key is not the number of defects but whether they destroy the very kind of usefulness (jins al-manfa‘ah) that a slave should provide. This lesson unpacks the rulings and the rational principle behind them.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the listed permissible defects
Read the text and note all defects the author says are permissible (يجوز). They include deafness (with conditions), opposite hand/foot amputation, ear/nose loss, one-eyedness, weak eyesight, castration, and impotence.
Each differs in severity, but all are acceptable.
2
2. Identify the impermissible defects
The text explicitly prohibits two: loss of both thumbs and insanity (lack of intellect). Note the reasons given.
These are the only absolute disqualifiers in this passage.
3
3. Extract the underlying principle
The author explains that permissible defects only 'impair' (ikhtilal) but do not 'eliminate' the genus of benefit. Impermissible defects eliminate it entirely. Understand this distinction.
The principle is rational (ʿaqlī), not purely textual.
4
4. Analyze the special case of deafness
Observe the footnote: deafness is acceptable only if acquired (can hear when shouted at). A person born deaf (and often mute) is not valid. This reconciles two narrations within the Hanafi school.
Show how the school resolves internal differences.
5
5. Apply the principle to new cases
Consider a slave with a missing thumb on one hand (not both) or one who is blind in both eyes (which would eliminate the genus of sight?). Hypothesize the ruling using jins al-manfa‘ah.
The principle guides rulings beyond the listed examples.

Freeing Half a Jointly-Owned Slave for Kaffarah: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of freeing half a jointly-owned slave with guarantee.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on freeing half a jointly-owned slave for kaffarah after guarantee, explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Freeing a share then guaranteeing the rest? Abu Hanifa says invalid due to ownership deficiency.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about using a jointly-owned slave for expiation (kaffarah). The central question: can a person free his half, then guarantee his partner's share and free it? Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on whether this satisfies the expiation, and the later authorities side with Abu Hanifa. Understanding this teaches us how ownership conditions affect the validity of manumission for expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Context
The act in question is freeing a jointly-owned slave for kaffarah after the freer guarantees (daman) the partner's share and then frees it. We need to determine if this fulfills the expiation obligation.
Note that the slave is jointly owned; the freer initially owns only half.
2
State the Hanafi Position and the Sahibayn's Opposition
Abu Hanifa holds that it is not valid. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that it is valid if the freer is wealthy (musir). State each position clearly.
The dispute only applies to the wealthy; for the poor there is consensus it is invalid.
3
Analyze the Dalil of Each Side
The Sahibayn argue that by guaranteeing the partner's share, the freer becomes full owner, so he is freeing the entire slave as his property. Abu Hanifa responds that the partner's share first decreased (while still in partner's ownership) and then transferred via guarantee; this deficiency (nuqsan) in ownership at the moment of freeing the first half prevents the expiation from being valid.
Note the role of timing: the deficiency exists at the initial freeing, before guarantee fully transfers ownership.
4
Identify the Tarjih (Preponderant View)
Later scholars such as al-Isbijabi, al-Mahbubi, and al-Nasafi declared Abu Hanifa's view as the correct one (al-sahih). Thus the mufta bihi is that it is not valid.
Even though the Sahibayn's view is also part of the madhhab, the operative ruling for fatwa follows Abu Hanifa.
5
Apply the Ruling to a Scenario
Consider a wealthy person who frees his half in a jointly-owned slave for dhihar kaffarah, then guarantees the partner's share and frees it. According to the mufta bihi, the kaffarah is not fulfilled and must be repeated. Compare with freeing his own exclusive slave in two statements, which is valid.
The analogy with the sheep for udhiyah shows that when the animal is wholly owned, a deficiency does not invalidate.

Core Idea
Any intercourse during the two-month kafarah fast breaks consecutiveness, requiring restart — even if the fast itself is valid.

This passage reveals an important intra-Hanafi dispute over the condition of consecutiveness (tataabu') in the kafarah fast of zihar. Guided Path

The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. It is considered more precautionary and based on the apparent meaning of the Qur'anic text. Later Hanafi authorities like Al-Marghinani prefer this view.

Custody of the Female Child: Age Limit Dispute in the Hanafi School
Intra-Hanafi dispute on custody age limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two competing Hanafi positions on the custody age limit for a female child and identify which is the mufta bihi (operative opinion) and why.

Core Idea
The mother/grandmother's custody until menstruation (zahir al-riwaya) was later overridden by the fatwa of nine years for all custodians.

Custody of a female child after separation is a critical area where the Hanafi school has an internal dispute. The apparent report (zahir al-riwaya) from Imam Muhammad grants the mother and grandmother custody until the girl menstruates. However, later scholars, considering social and protective needs, shifted the operative ruling to a uniform age of nine years for all custodians. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the hierarchy of authority that led to this change.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two groups of custodians
The passage distinguishes between 'the mother and grandmother' and 'others who have custody.' Note the different age limits stated for each group in the apparent report.
The base text (Quduri) originally set different limits: mother/grandmother until menstruation, others until the age of desire (nine).
2
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Read the commentary and later sources (al-Dur, al-Tanwir, al-Manh, al-Bahr, al-Tajnis, Tabyin al-Kanz) to see how the fatwa reversed the apparent report for mother/grandmother, making the limit uniformly nine years.
The fatwa is attributed to Abu Layth al-Samarqandi and is the operative opinion today.
3
Understand the reasoning behind each position
The Hidayah explains: before puberty, a girl needs to learn feminine etiquette (better taught by mother); after puberty, she needs protection (father is stronger). The later fatwa emphasizes early protection from desire starting at age nine.
The shift reflects a change in juristic concern: from education to modesty and protection.
4
Recognize the authority hierarchy
Note that the apparent report comes from the core books (zahir al-riwaya), which is normally the most authoritative layer. Yet the later fatwa overrides it, showing that practical necessity (tahqiq al-manat) can lead to tarjih of a weaker transmission.

Core Idea
Implicit words free only with intention; without it, the slave remains enslaved.

In Hanafi fiqh, words used for emancipation are divided into explicit (sarih) and implicit (kinayah). Explicit words like 'you are free' are effective regardless of intention. Implicit words, however, are ambiguous—they could refer to emancipation or other meanings like sale. Therefore, the master's intention (niyyah) is essential to determine the intended meaning. Note that intention is the decisive factor. Without intention, the words have no legal effect. The text provides the example: 'I have no ownership over you'—if intended, the slave is freed; if not, no emancipation occurs.

Options for Co-owner When Wealthy Partner Frees His Share
Lists three options for co-owner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three options available to a co-owner when a wealthy partner frees his share, and explain the distribution of wala' and compensation in each case.

Core Idea
A co-owner has three choices when his partner frees his share: free, claim compensation, or require istis‘ā’; wala’ varies accordingly.

When two people jointly own a slave and one of them, being wealthy, frees his share, the other co-owner does not lose his rights. Abu Hanifa lays out three distinct legal options for the remaining co-owner, each with different consequences for wala’ and compensation. Understanding these options clarifies the Hanafi approach to partial manumission and protection of co-ownership rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the situation: two co-owners, one wealthy (i.e., able to compensate) frees his share of a jointly owned slave. The other co-owner retains ownership of his share.
The manumitter must be wealthy (mūsir) to trigger these options; if poor, different rules apply.
2
List the three options
Memorize the three choices given to the co-owner: (1) free his own share, (2) claim compensation from the manumitter for the value of his share, or (3) require the slave to work to earn his freedom (istis‘ā’).
These are mutually exclusive; the co-owner chooses one.
3
Explain wala’ distribution for each option
For each option, determine who gets the right of wala’ (patronage). In option 1 (both free), wala’ is shared. In option 2 (compensation), the manumitter gets all wala’ after he pays and recovers from the slave. In option 3 (istis‘ā’), wala’ is shared between the co-owner (who initiates istis‘ā’) and the manumitter.
Wala’ follows the source of manumission; partial ownership leads to shared wala’ unless one party acquires full ownership via compensation.
4
Understand the rationale for compensation
The manumitter is liable because his action spoils the co-owner’s share by making it unsaleable or non-transferable. The co-owner can claim the value as compensation for this injury.
The manumitter then recovers the amount from the slave, making the slave effectively buy the share.

Co-owner's Options When the Manumitter Is Insolvent
Intra-Hanafi dispute on co-owner's options when the manumitting partner is insolvent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the co-owner's options when the manumitter is insolvent, and explain the two underlying principles that generate the ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa grants the co-owner a choice; the Sahibayn restrict to istis‘ā’ when the manumitter is insolvent.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on partial manumission. When one co-owner frees his share and is insolvent, what are the other co-owner's options? The answer turns on two key principles: the permissibility of freeing a slave in which one does not have full ownership (tajarru‘ al-‘itq), and whether the manumitter's wealth prevents the option of istis‘ā’. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of Hanafi manumission law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The manumitter is insolvent (mu‘sir). The co-owner wants to know his options.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa gives the co-owner a choice: free his share (thereby completing the emancipation) or demand istis‘ā’ (make the slave work for freedom). The co-owner cannot claim compensation.
Abu Hanifa relies on the principle that wealth does not prevent istis‘ā’.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad restrict the co-owner to only one option based on the manumitter's financial status: if wealthy, compensation only; if insolvent, istis‘ā’ only.
They hold that wealth prevents istis‘ā’ and that tajarru‘ al-‘itq is not permitted without full ownership.
4
Identify the two underlying principles
Al-Hidayah explains that the dispute rests on two 'letters': (1) tajarru‘ al-‘itq — is it permitted to free a slave in which you don't hold full ownership? (2) Does the manumitter's wealth prevent istis‘ā’?
Abu Hanifa permits tajarru‘ and says wealth does not prevent istis‘ā’; the Sahibayn hold the opposite.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
Practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the co-owner always has the choice to free or take istis‘ā’ when the manumitter is insolvent. Under the Sahibayn, only istis‘ā’. Jamāl al-Islām declared Abu Hanifa's view as the correct one (ṣaḥīḥ), and later authorities followed it.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's position.

Buying a Relative: Option to Emancipate or Seek Work
Intra-Hanafi dispute on buying a relative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's position on purchasing a relative as a slave, explain the Sahibayn's objection, and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Purchasing your own relative as a slave gives you a choice: free him or let him work for his freedom—unless you are wealthy, according to the Sahibayn.

Imagine a father discovers his son has been enslaved and legally purchases him. What must he do? Must he free him immediately? Can he demand his son work to pay off the value? The Hanafi school presents a nuanced ruling with an internal dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. This lesson dissects that dispute and identifies the operative position for practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Legal Problem
Recognize that the ruling applies when someone purchases a close relative (e.g., father, son) who is a slave. The key legal question is what obligations or options the buyer has over the enslaved relative.
The relative must be a direct ascendant or descendant; other relatives may have different rules.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the buyer has a choice: he may emancipate the slave immediately or demand that the slave work (istis'a') to earn his value. The buyer retains ownership and can exercise either option.
The option is not obligatory; the buyer may also choose to do nothing, but the slave remains enslaved.
3
State the Sahibayn's Objection
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani restrict the option: if the buyer is wealthy (yasir), he has no choice but must pay compensation (daman) to the seller (or to the slave's other owner). If poor, the slave must work for his value. They base this on the buyer's ability to pay.
Wealth is defined as having enough to pay the slave's value without hardship.
4
Analyze the Two Underlying Points from al-Hidayah
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah explains the dispute hinges on two matters: (1) whether the purchase itself counts as an act of emancipation (tajarru' al-i'taq) – Abu Hanifa sees ownership intact, while Sahibayn view the purchase as implicitly freeing the relative, hence requiring compensation; (2) whether the buyer's wealth prevents seeking work – Abu Hanifa says it does not, Sahibayn say it does.
These are usul principles that determine the outcome.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi Position
Later Hanafi authorities (Jamal al-Islam, al-Burhani, al-Nasafi) declare that the correct view is that of Abu Hanifa. Therefore, the relied-upon position for fatwa is that the buyer has the option to emancipate or seek work, regardless of wealth.
This illustrates how intra-madhhab tarjih works: later scholars weigh the proofs and choose one view as operative.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the status of a slave owned by two partners when each partner testifies that the other partner freed his share of the slave, and the other denies it. The text first states the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa: the entire slave is considered free, and the slave must perform sa'yah (working to earn his freedom and compensate the owners) for both partners, regardless of whether they are wealthy or poor. This ruling is based on the principle that the testimony of one who stands to benefit from his own testimony is not accepted. Since each partner claims the other freed his share, they have a right to compensation (tadmeen) or sa'yah, but because the other denies the claim, compensation is not possible, so sa'yah becomes the means. The text then notes that this same difference applies if the partners came to own the slave through gift, charity, or bequest, and affirms that the correct view is that of Imam Abu Hanifa. The commentary (sharh) then explains the details of the ruling, including that the testimony of a single individual is not accepted in this chapter, and even multiple witnesses are not accepted because they draw benefit. The testimony is not accepted because it establishes a right of compensation for themselves, and the Prophet (peace be upon him) said there is no testimony for one who draws benefit. The commentary also notes a textual variant in the phrase 'freed entirely'. The passage then presents the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani): they distinguish based on the financial status of the partners. If both partners are wealthy, the slave is freed without any sa'yah, because sa'yah is not established when the owners are wealthy; their wealth is considered a discharge of the slave's obligation. If both are poor, the slave performs sa'yah for both, because in their claim the obligation is sa'yah, not compensation. If one is wealthy and the other poor, the slave performs sa'yah only for the wealthy partner, because the wealthy partner is not discharged from his right. The ruling on wala' (right of inheritance and clientage) is also explained: the wala' belongs to both partners because each claims that his own share was freed by sa'yah (giving him wala') and the other's share was freed by the other's emancipation (giving the other wala'). The preferred position (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Hanifa, as stated in the matn.

تضمين (taḍmīn): The right to seek monetary compensation from the co-owner who allegedly freed his share; a form of financial recourse.
ولاء (walā’): The bond of clientage and the right to inherit from a freed slave, belonging to the person who effected the emancipation.

Emancipation for Idol or Devil and by Compulsion or Intoxication
Two rulings on emancipation validity under irregular conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on emancipation when the intention is directed to an idol or devil, and when the actor is under compulsion or intoxication, and explain the underlying principle that the act's validity depends on proper source and object.

Core Idea
Emancipation is valid regardless of improper intention or the actor's compromised state.

In this passage from Al-Lubab, we encounter two seemingly problematic scenarios of slave emancipation. The Hanafi school affirms that even if a person declares freedom for an idol or while under duress or drunk, the slave is legally freed. Understanding why requires grasping the Hanafi principle that the validity of a legal act hinges on proper subject and object, not on the actor's intent or mental state.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First scenario: freeing a slave with a stated intention for an idol or devil. Second: freeing under compulsion or intoxication.
2

In both cases, the emancipation is declared valid (sahih). The slave becomes free.
3
Understand the principle
The text gives the reason: 'because the emancipation originates from its proper source and is applied to its proper place.' The act is from a legally competent owner and directed at a valid slave.
The intention or state of the owner does not affect the legal validity.
4

If the intention was to honor the idol, the owner is sinful, and if it was an act of worship, he becomes an apostate. But the emancipation remains effective.
Sin and apostasy are separate from the contract's validity.

The text mentions that the ruling for compulsion and intoxication mirrors that of divorce (talaq) – both are valid under these conditions in Hanafi law.
This reinforces the principle that legal capacity suffices for validity.

Commentary
This passage provides two clear rulings. Ruling for the Poor Co-Owner: No Seeking Earnings (Sa'ya)
States the ruling that a poor co-owner does not seek earnings from the freed slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a poor co-owner who manumits his share of a jointly owned slave and explain the reasoning based on the guarantee principle.

Core Idea
A poor co-owner does not seek earnings from the slave; the wealthy co-owner bears the guarantee.

This passage presents a specific case of partial manumission: when a poor co-owner frees his share of a jointly owned slave. The matn states that he does not seek earnings (sa'ya) from the slave. The commentary explains the legal reasoning and confirms that this is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school. Understanding this ruling clarifies the distinction between the obligations of wealthy and poor co-owners.

Manumission for a False Deity
Ruling on manumission with improper intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on manumission when intended for a false deity and explain the legal reasoning that separates the act's validity from the intention.

Core Idea
Manumission is valid even if intended for the devil or an idol; the act's validity depends on the act itself, not the intention.

In this passage, we learn a fascinating principle: a person may utter words of manumission while intending to dedicate it to something other than Allah, yet the manumission remains legally effective. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the validity of the legal act and the sinfulness of the intention. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying rulings on manumission and other similar contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is manumission (i'taq). The ruling is that it is valid (sahih) and takes effect immediately. The slave becomes free regardless of the intention.
2
Locate the legal proof
The proof is that the act of manumission issued from a legally capable person (ahliyyah) and was applied to its proper object (the slave). This fulfills the requirements for validity, so the act stands.
3
Understand the role of intention
The intention (niyyah) is relevant only for sin or disbelief, not for the legal effect. The phrase 'for the devil' or 'for the idol' is legally disregarded. The manumitter is sinful (athim) unless his intention was veneration (ta'zim) of the false deity, in which case he becomes a disbeliever (kafir).
4
Apply the ruling
In practice, if someone says 'You are free for the devil', the slave is free. The manumitter must repent if sinful, and if his intention was worship, he must renew his faith.
