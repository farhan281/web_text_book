Sa'yah for a Slave Freed by Two Co-owners: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sa'yah with wealth condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding sa'yah when two co-owners both claim to have freed a jointly owned slave and identify the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
When co-owners both claim to have freed a slave, the slave's obligation to work for freedom is disputed: Imam Abu Hanifa requires it always; the Sahibayn waive it if the owners are wealthy.

This passage presents a nuanced internal disagreement within the Hanafi school. Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on whether a jointly owned slave must perform sa'yah (work for compensation) when each co-owner asserts that he has freed his share. The dispute hinges on the role of the owners' wealth and the principle that sa'yah is not established when compensation is possible.

Guided Path
1
Set the scenario
Two co-owners each claim to have freed the entire slave. Each believes the other is obligated to compensate, but the denial prevents that.
2
Identify the Sahibayn's principle
For the Sahibayn, sa'yah is not established when wealth is present (al-sa'ayah la tathbutu ma'a al-yasar). Wealth allows compensation, which discharges the slave.
This principle is the core of their differentiation.
3
Apply the principle to three wealth scenarios
If both wealthy: no sa'yah. If both poor: sa'yah for both. If one wealthy, one poor: sa'yah for the wealthy only.
4
State Imam Abu Hanifa's counter-position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that sa'yah is binding in all cases regardless of wealth. The impossibility of compensation (due to mutual denial) necessitates sa'yah absolutely.
The text does not provide his explicit dalil, but the tarjih implies his reasoning prevails.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi
Later authorities (Abu al-Ma'ali, al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shariah) affirm the Imam's view as the relied-upon position.
Thus the operative ruling is that sa'yah is always required.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), regarding the case of a slave jointly owned by two persons, each claiming to have freed his entire share. The Sahibayn hold that the slave's obligation to work for compensation (sa'yah) depends on the financial status of the owners. If both are wealthy, the slave owes no sa'yah because the owners could have claimed compensation instead but are prevented by the other's denial; wealth effectively discharges the slave. If both are poor, the slave must work for both, as compensation is impossible. If one is wealthy and the other poor, the slave works only for the wealthy one. The patronage (wala') belongs to both owners because each claims the other's share is freed by his freeing, and his own share is freed by the slave's work. The text then quotes Abu al-Ma'ali, who states that the correct position is that of Imam Abu Hanifa, meaning the slave must perform sa'yah regardless of the owners' wealth. Later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi affirm this, making it the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view. The reasoning: each owner's claim implies an obligation on the other to compensate, but since compensation is impossible due to denial, sa'yah becomes binding irrespective of wealth.

Key Terms
سعاية (sa'yah): The slave's working to earn money to compensate the owner(s) after partial emancipation, used here to resolve conflicting claims of co-owners.
تضمين (tadmin): Claiming monetary compensation from the co-owner who allegedly freed the slave, which is impossible here due to denial.
ولاء (wala'): Patronage rights that belong to the emancipator; here each co-owner claims patronage over the slave's entire person due to their respective roles.

Suspended Emancipation (Ta'liq al-‘Itq) in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on validity of suspended emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on suspended emancipation and explain why it is valid based on the nature of emancipation as isqat.

Core Idea
Emancipation is a removal of ownership, so it can be suspended on a future condition — unlike sales.

This passage from Al-Lubab clarifies a key Hanafi rule: a master can validly suspend a slave's emancipation on a future condition, even if he does not yet own the slave. The reasoning rests on the nature of emancipation as an isqat (removal of ownership). This ruling parallels suspended divorce and distinguishes emancipation from contracts like sale, where suspension is often invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is suspended emancipation (ta'liq al-'itq). The ruling is that it is valid (sahih).
2
Recognize the two types of conditions
The condition can be future ownership ('If I own you') or any other future event ('If you enter the house'). Both are valid.
The condition does not need to be ownership; any future condition works.
3
Understand the legal rationale (illah)
Emancipation is an isqat (removal) of the master's right, not a contract of exchange. Suspension (ta'liq) is valid in all acts of isqat.
This distinguishes it from sales (mu'awadah), where ta'liq is generally invalid.
4
Draw the analogy with divorce
The text explicitly compares it to suspended divorce (talaq mu'allaq), which is also valid. Both are isqat.
Divorce also removes a right (marriage bond), so it follows the same rule.

Emancipation of a Pregnant Slave Woman: Automatic Freedom of the Fetus
Ruling on emancipation of a pregnant slave woman and her fetus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on emancipating a pregnant slave woman, explain why excepting the fetus is invalid, and distinguish the wala implications based on the timing of birth.

Core Idea
Freeing the mother automatically frees the fetus; you cannot except it, just as you cannot except a limb.

This passage from al-Lubab explains a key Hanafi rule: when a slave woman is emancipated while pregnant, her unborn child becomes free as well. The author uses a simple analogy — the fetus is like a part of the mother — to justify the ruling. We will examine the reasoning, the invalidity of excepting the child, and the nuance regarding wala.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? (Freeing a pregnant slave woman). What is the hukm? (The mother and fetus become free, sahih).
The ruling is clear: both are freed automatically.
2
Understand the dalil (proof) by analogy
The author states the fetus is 'like a part' (bimanzilati juz' minha) of the mother. This is a qiyas: since a limb is included in emancipation, so is the fetus.
This analogy is the core of the Hanafi proof.
3
Analyze the exception rule
If the master tries to except the fetus, it is invalid, just as excepting a limb would be invalid. The reason is that the fetus is considered an integral part.
The exception does not affect the ruling; the child is still free.
4
Explore the timing nuance and wala
If the child is born within six months of emancipation, it is freed dependently. If born in less than six months (i.e., conception before emancipation), it is freed intentionally (maqsudan) and its wala does not go to the father's patrons.
This nuance affects patronage rights (wala).
5
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Shafi'is may allow excepting the fetus. Understand the practical thamarah: in Hanafi law, the exception is void; in Shafi'i, it might be valid.
This is a legitimate scholarly difference.

Emancipation of the Fetus Only
Ruling on emancipating the fetus without the mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on emancipating the fetus without the mother and apply the conditions that affect its validity (birth timing, 'iddah).

Core Idea
Emancipation of the fetus alone is valid if its existence at the time of statement is certain.

A master may wish to free an unborn child while leaving the mother enslaved. The Hanafi school addresses this scenario with specific rulings. The key is to determine whether the fetus existed at the moment of emancipation, which is inferred from the time of birth. This lesson explores the conditions and the legal reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The master says: 'The child in your womb is free.' The ruling: the fetus becomes free (sahih) but the mother does not. Confirm the hukm level from the passage.
2
Understand the talil (legal reasoning)
The text explains why the mother is not freed: she cannot be freed intentionally (no direct pronouncement) nor dependently (taba'iyyah) because that would reverse the subject (qalb al-mawdu'). The dependent (fetus) cannot make the principal (mother) dependent.
The principle of qalb al-mawdu' prevents the mother from being freed as a follower.
3
Determine the condition of existence (six-month rule)
If the child is born within six months of the emancipation statement, its existence at that time is certain, and the emancipation is valid. If born after six months, it may have been conceived later, so it is not freed due to doubt.
Six months is the minimum human pregnancy period in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Apply the exception for 'iddah
If the mother is in 'iddah from her husband and gives birth within two years (the maximum pregnancy period in Hanafi), the child is freed even if born after six months. The 'iddah indicates that the pregnancy predates the emancipation.
The 'iddah rule provides certainty of earlier conception.
5
Handle the case of twins
If the mother gives birth to two children, one before six months and one after, both are freed because they are considered one pregnancy (haml wahid). The early twin confirms the pregnancy existed at the time of statement.
This is an extension of the same principle: the existence is proven by the first twin.

Definition of Khamr and Badhaq: Two Prohibited Drinks
Defines the first two of four prohibited drinks

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of khamr and badhaq, and distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's conditions for khamr.

Core Idea
Khamr is defined by three signs; cooked juice is also haram, but the conditions differ between Abu Hanifa and his disciples.

In this lesson, we examine the opening of the Book of Drinks, where the author defines two of the four prohibited intoxicants. Understanding these definitions is crucial because the hadd punishment depends on precise classification. We will break down each term and note the difference between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and scope
The passage begins 'Kitab al-Ashriba' – the Book of Drinks. It states that prohibited drinks are four, but here only the first two are defined. Note that the legal term 'ashriba' is restricted to intoxicants.
2
Parse the definition of khamr
Khamr is defined as grape juice that (1) boils (ghala), (2) becomes strong (ishtadda), and (3) throws foam (qadhafa bi al-zabad). Identify each condition as a physical sign of complete fermentation.
Abu Hanifa requires all three; Sahibayn require only intoxication (ishtidad).
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi difference
The text explicitly contrasts Abu Hanifa's view with that of Sahibayn. Note the authority weight: Abu Hanifa's view is presented first and elaborated, though later fatwa authorities differ on which is mufta bihi.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
4
Define the second prohibited drink (badhaq)
The second type is grape juice cooked until less than two-thirds of it evaporates. This is called badhaq or tila'. It is also intoxicating and haram, but distinct from khamr.
The hadd punishment applies to drinking this as well.
5
Understand the legal significance
Why does this matter? The definition determines when the hadd punishment (80 lashes) is obligatory. If a drink meets Abu Hanifa's strict definition, it is khamr; otherwise, it may fall under cooked juice or other categories.
This is a thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of the intra-school difference.

Permissibility of Nabidh in Prohibited Containers
Fiqh ruling with proof from hadith and abrogation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on making nabidh in the four prohibited containers and explain the abrogation (naskh) that renders it permissible.

Core Idea
The container does not change the ruling; only intoxication matters.

This lesson examines a classic fiqh ruling on which vessels may be used to make nabidh (date wine). An earlier prohibition of four specific containers was abrogated by a clear hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him). Understanding this shows how abrogation works in Hanafi fiqh and the principle that the vessel itself has no legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four containers
The passage names four containers: al-dubbā' (gourd/pumpkin), al-ḥantam (green pottery jar), al-muzaffat (tarred vessel), and al-naqīr (hollowed wood). Learn their definitions and why they were originally prohibited.

The later hadith abrogates the earlier prohibition. The Hanafi school accepts this naskh because it is proven from the Sunnah and the context shows it was a later ruling.

Making nabidh in these containers is permissible (mubah) as long as the resulting drink is not intoxicating. The container itself has no legal effect.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Hunting and Slaughtering (Kitab al-Sayd wa al-Dhaba'ih). The author first notes the suitability of placing hunting after drinks: both can lead to heedlessness and diversion. He then provides a linguistic definition of sayd (hunting): it is the act of capturing, and the captured animal is called sayd, referring to any wild, untamed creature that can only be caught by a trick. The legal rulings will be added later. The initial ruling states that it is permissible (ja'iz) to hunt using trained (mu'allam) animals: the dog, the cheetah, the falcon, and all trained jawarih (predators). The jawarih are defined as every animal with a fang (nab) from the predators (siba') or with a talon (mikhlab) from birds of prey. The text then begins to list the conditions, but cuts off. The key point is that hunting with trained animals is permissible in the Hanafi school, provided the animal is trained (mu'allam) to respond to commands and refrain from eating the prey.

Key Terms
صيد (sayd): Hunting; the act of capturing wild animals, and the animal captured.
جوارح (jawarih): Predators; animals that hunt and catch prey, including dogs, cheetahs, falcons, etc.
معلم (mu'allam): Trained; an animal that has been taught to hunt and respond to commands.
