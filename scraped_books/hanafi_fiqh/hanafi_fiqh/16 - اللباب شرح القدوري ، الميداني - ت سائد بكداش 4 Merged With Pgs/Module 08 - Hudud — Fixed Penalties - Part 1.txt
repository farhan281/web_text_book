Definition and Conditions of Li'an (Mutual Cursing)
Linguistic and legal definition of li'an with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal definition of li'an and identify its conditions, including the requirement that the wife be muhsan.

Core Idea
Li'an substitutes for hadd when a husband accuses his wife of zina — but only if she is muhsan.

This lesson introduces the legal institution of li'an, a unique procedure that prevents the application of criminal hadd punishments in cases of marital accusations. Understanding its definition and conditions is essential to distinguish it from similar rulings.

Guided Path
1
Analyse the linguistic root
Identify that li'an is derived from la'n (curse), and that it involves mutual cursing starting with the husband cursing himself. This linguistic origin indicates the nature of the procedure.
The term is not derived from ghadab (anger) but from la'n, which implies banishment.
2
Identify the legal definition
Li'an is legally defined as emphatic oaths accompanied by cursing from the husband and wrath from the wife, standing in place of the hadd of qadhf for the husband and the hadd of zina for the wife.
The definition highlights that li'an substitutes for criminal punishments.
3
Determine the sabab (trigger)
The trigger is the husband's explicit accusation of zina against his wife or his denial of paternity of her child. Both actions amount to an implicit accusation of zina.
Explicit accusation is required; vague insinuations are not sufficient.
4
List the shurut (conditions)
The conditions for li'an are: (a) both spouses must be eligible to testify (Muslim, adult, sane, just), (b) the wife must be muhsan (free, Muslim, chaste), and (c) the accusation must be explicit.
The condition of muhsan is directly linked to the hadd of qadhf.
5
Connect the condition to the hikmah
The requirement that the wife be muhsan exists because li'an replaces the hadd of qadhf. Hadd is only due if the accused is muhsan; otherwise, the accuser is not liable for hadd.
If the wife is not muhsan, the husband cannot invoke li'an and may face a different ruling.

Legally, it is a set of sworn oaths accompanied by curses and imprecations that substitute for the hadd penalty of qadhf (false accusation of zina) for the husband and the hadd penalty of zina for the wife. Definition and Ruling of Intentional Killing
Defines intentional killing and its punishment in Hanafi law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define intentional killing (qatl al-‘amd) according to the Hanafi school and state its ruling including the dispute over non-sharp iron.

Core Idea
Intentional killing is defined by the instrument that separates body parts — not solely by sharpness — and demands qisas unless the heirs forgive.

This lesson opens the Hanafi Book of Criminal Offenses (Jinayat). We will examine how the school defines intentional killing (al-qatl al-‘amd) using the instrument as the indicator of intent. The ruling carries both spiritual and legal consequences: sin and retaliation. We will also explore an intra-Hanafi dispute about non-sharp iron.

Guided Path
1
Locate the five categories of killing
Read the first line: 'al-qatl ‘ala khamsati awjuh'. Identify the five types listed: ‘amd, shibh ‘amd, khata’, ma ujriya majra al-khata’, and al-qatl bi-sabab.
This classification is foundational for all homicide cases.
2
Extract the definition of intentional killing
Find the sentence beginning with 'fa al-‘amd'. Note the key phrase: 'ma tu‘ammada darbuhu bi-silah aw ma ujriya majra al-silah fi tafriq al-ajza’'.
The definition hinges on the instrument's capacity to separate body parts.
3
Identify the legal consequences
Read the next sentence: 'wa mujibu dhalika: al-ma’thamu, wa al-qawadu, illa an ya‘fuwa al-awliya’.' Recognize the dual consequence: sin (ma’tham) and qisas (qawad).
The exception of the heirs’ forgiveness is crucial.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Look at the last line: 'wa fi hadidin ghayri muhaddad: riwayatan, azharuhuma annahu ‘amd.' Understand that for non-sharp iron, there are two narrations. The more apparent (azhar) treats it as intentional.
This shows the school's objective approach: the instrument's effect, not just its shape.

Killing by a Group: Qisas and Heirs' Rights
Ruling on joint killing and effect of single heir appearing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on joint killing and explain the effect of a single heir appearing.

Core Idea
When a group kills one victim, all are executed; if only one heir appears, he executes and the others' rights lapse.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on joint homicide from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The passage clarifies that when multiple persons kill one victim, all are equally liable for qisas (retribution) because the soul's departure is indivisible. It also addresses a practical scenario: if only one heir of the victim is present, he may execute the killer(s), and the rights of absent heirs are extinguished. The reasoning hinges on the nature of death and the unity of the right to qisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the killing of one person by a group. The ruling is that all killers are executed in qisas, and nothing else is owed to the heirs.
This is a case of joint killing (qatl bi-jama'ah).
2
Locate the dalil
The reasoning given: the soul's exit (zuhuq al-ruh) is indivisible (la yatabba'du). Therefore, each killer is considered to have caused the entire death.
This is a qiyas based on the nature of death.
3
Apply the ruling to multiple killers
Since each killer is fully responsible, all must be executed. The heirs have no further claim (like diya) because qisas has been fulfilled.
4
Consider the scenario of a single heir appearing
If only one heir appears, he may execute the killer(s). The right of the absent heirs falls because the object of their right (the killer's life) is consumed by qisas.
Analogous to the killer dying before execution.
5
Understand the analogy
The loss of right is likened to the killer's death: if the killer dies, the right of qisas is lost for all heirs. Similarly, once qisas is carried out by one heir, it is lost for others.

The Definition of Hadd (Fixed Punishment)
Defines the term 'hadd' and explains its purpose

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term 'ḥadd' according to the Hanafi school, distinguish it from qisas and ta'zir, and explain its primary legislative purpose of deterrence.

Core Idea
A hadd is a fixed punishment as a right of Allah for deterrence, not purification.

This lesson opens the Book of Hudud by clarifying what a 'hadd' is. The author first connects hudud to other penal laws, then provides a precise technical definition that sets it apart from qisas (victim's right) and ta'zir (discretionary penalty). Understanding this definition is key to grasping the entire chapter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
Examine the word 'hadd' linguistically. The text states it means 'prevention', giving the example of 'haddad' (gatekeeper). Write down this meaning.
2
Extract the technical definition
The text defines 'hadd' in Sharia as 'the prescribed punishment as a right of Allah'. Note that it is a fixed, predetermined penalty owed to Allah, not to any individual.
3
Exclude qisas and ta'zir
The text explicitly excludes qisas (retributive justice) because it is a right of the slave (victim), and ta'zir (discretionary punishment) because it lacks prescription. Understand that only punishments meeting all three criteria—fixed, prescribed, right of Allah—are called hudud.
4
Identify the purpose of hudud
The text states the original purpose is deterrence (al-izdijar), not purification. Evidence for this is that hudud apply to disbelievers as well. This distinguishes them from other penalties.

Proof of Zina: Conditions of Testimony and Confession
Passage states the proof and conditions for the hadd of zina.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two means of proving zina in Hanafi law and identify the conditions that must be met for testimony and confession.

Core Idea
Zina is proven only by four just male witnesses in one session or by a voluntary confession.

This lesson examines the legal proof for the hadd of zina. The text establishes that zina is proven by either clear testimony (baiyina) or confession (iqrar). The focus is on the rigorous conditions for testimony: four male, free, just witnesses, testifying in one session. The imam must also investigate the precise nature of the act to avoid error.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two means of proof
The text states that zina is proven by baiyina and iqrar. Baiyina is testimony; iqrar is confession. These are the only two methods.
Confession is discussed later in the same chapter.
2
Define baiyina
Baiyina requires four male, free, just witnesses testifying in one session against a man or woman of zina.
The Arabic phrase 'فالبينة أن يشهد أربعةً من الشهود الرجال الأحرار العدول في مجلس واحد' specifies these conditions.
3
Explain the purpose of the imam's questioning
The imam asks 'what is zina?' and 'how is it?' to ensure testimony refers to actual unlawful intercourse, not other meanings (like fornication of the eyes) or coerced acts.
This prevents false accusations and ensures the hadd is applied correctly.

(وإن قال : علمتُ أنها على حرام)؛ لأن الشبهة حكمية، لأنها نشأت عن دليل، وهو قوله صلى الله عليه وسلم: «أنت ومالك لأبيك». والأبوة قائمة في حق الجد. (هداية).
Punishment for intercourse below the private parts: (Whoever has intercourse with an unrelated woman below the private parts), such as between thighs or between legs, he is given ta'zir (discretionary punishment); because it is a forbidden act without a prescribed measure. And his statement 'below the private parts' includes the anus, which is the statement of Imam [Abu Hanifah]; because it is not zina, as will come shortly. (And there is no hadd upon one who has intercourse with the slave-girl of his son, or the son's child) even if he is lower, and even if the son is alive. (Fath). (And if he said: I knew it was forbidden for me); because the shubhah is hukmiyyah, because it arose from an evidence, which is his statement ﷺ: 'You and your property belong to your father.' And fatherhood is established regarding the grandfather. (Hidayah).
Source pages p. 472

Commentary
This passage covers two matters. First, any sexual contact with a non-mahram woman below the private parts (e.g., touching, rubbing between thighs) does not constitute zina (adultery) subject to hadd, but rather a lesser offense deserving ta'zir (discretionary punishment). Imam Abu Hanifah also includes anal intercourse under this ruling, considering it not zina (which requires specific insertion in the private part). This view will be detailed later. Second, if a man has intercourse with his son's (or descendant's) slave-girl, there is no hadd punishment even if he knew she was forbidden to him. The reason is a shubhah (legal ambiguity) based on the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father.' This hadith creates a semblance of ownership that prevents the full hadd. This principle also applies to the grandfather (the father's father). Thus, the punishment is not fixed but subject to ta'zir or other consequences.

Key Terms
الشبهة الحكمية (al-shubhah al-hukmiyyah): A legal ambiguity arising from an evidence (dalil) that creates doubt, leading to the dropping of a fixed punishment.
التعزير (al-ta'zir): A discretionary punishment not fixed by shari'ah, imposed for offenses without prescribed hadd or kaffarah.

Ta'zir for Intercourse Below the Genitals
States punishment and reasoning for intercourse below the genitals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi punishment for intercourse below the genitals and explain why it is ta'zir not hadd, including the position on anal intercourse.

Core Idea
Intercourse below the genitals is ta'zir, not hadd, because it is not zina.

This passage from al-Quduri's al-Mukhtasar clarifies an important boundary in Hanafi hudud law. While zina (genital intercourse with a non-mahram) carries a fixed hadd punishment, acts below the genitals—including anal intercourse—fall into a different category. The text explains the ruling and its reasoning, highlighting the Hanafi distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is intercourse with a foreign (non-mahram) woman in any area below the genitals, such as thighs or abdomen. The ruling: ta'zir (discretionary flogging).
The text uses the verb 'uzzira (he is punished with ta'zir).
2
Analyze the Reasoning (Ta'lil)
The reason given: 'because it is an evil act (munkar) for which no specific punishment is prescribed (laysa fihi shay' muqaddar).' Since it is not a hadd offense, the judge has discretion.
Ta'zir punishments are not fixed by Shari'ah; they vary by situation.
3
Examine the Scope: Inclusion of Anus
The commentator notes that 'below the genitals' includes the anus (dubur) according to Imam Abu Hanifa. This is because anal intercourse is not considered zina in the Hanafi school.
The phrase 'as will come shortly' refers to later discussion on the definition of zina.
4
Note Intra-Madhhab and Cross-Madhhab Ikhtilaf
The text attributes the inclusion of anus to the Imam alone, implying a potential difference with the Two Companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Other schools may classify anal intercourse as zina with hadd.
Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for comparative fiqh.
5
Contrast with Hadd
Zina (hadd offense) requires genital penetration. Acts below the genitals lack this condition, hence no fixed punishment. The distinction prevents over-application of hadd.
This principle is foundational in Hanafi penal law.
