One Person Kills Multiple Victims: Retaliation by All Heirs
Ruling on qisas with multiple victims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one person kills multiple victims, distinguishing the case when all heirs appear versus when only one heir appears.

Core Idea
A single execution satisfies all heirs; if only one appears, he claims the right alone.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a specific scenario in qisas: a single killer who murders multiple people. The rule is straightforward but rests on the principle that the killer's life cannot be divided among victims. Understanding this ruling helps clarify how Hanafi law balances the rights of multiple heirs in retaliation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the parties
The act is intentional murder (qatl 'amdan) committed by one person against multiple victims. The parties are the killer and the heirs (awliya) of each victim.
2
Distinguish the two conditions
The ruling depends on whether all heirs appear to demand qisas or only one does. Note that 'appear' (hadara) means they come forward to claim execution.
3
State the ruling for each condition
If all heirs appear, the killer is executed once for them collectively and no additional compensation is due. If only one heir appears, the killer is executed for that heir alone, and the rights of the absent heirs lapse.
4
Identify the underlying principle
The principle is that the life of the killer cannot be divided or multiplied; one execution fulfills the right of all victims. If some heirs do not appear, their right to qisas is forfeited because the opportunity passes.
This is based on the idea that qisas is a single act.

If only one heir appears, the killer is executed for that particular heir, and the rights of the absent heirs are dropped because the opportunity for qisas has passed.

Death of the Person Liable for Retaliation: Qisas Lapses
States a clear ruling with a brief reason.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on qisas when the liable person dies and identify the reason for its lapse.

Core Idea
Qisas requires a living subject; upon death, retaliation lapses.

The Hanafi ruling on qisas is clear: if the killer dies before execution, the penalty of qisas is dropped. This is because retaliation is a physical punishment that cannot be applied to a dead body. However, the financial right of diyah (blood money) may still be owed from the killer's estate. This lesson explores the simple but important principle that qisas is a personal penalty that ends with the life of the liable person.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is qisas (retaliation) imposed on a convicted murderer. The ruling is that qisas lapses (sāqit) if that person dies before execution.
The ruling is unconditional: death of the liable person drops the qisas immediately.
2
Locate the underlying reason (ʿilla)
The reason given in the text is 'loss of the place of execution' (fawāt maḥall al-istīfā'). The physical body of the killer is the locus for retaliation. Once dead, no lawful execution can occur.
This is a rational reason (taʿlīl) based on impossibility.
3
Recognize the scope and limits
This rule applies whenever qisas has been decreed but not yet carried out, regardless of the cause of death (natural, accident, or even execution by another authority). The key point is that execution cannot be posthumous.
All schools agree on this point.
4
Note the adjacent right: diyah
Although qisas falls, the financial right to diyah (blood money) may still be claimed from the killer's estate. The text does not state this explicitly, but it is the corollary in Hanafi fiqh.
Diyah remains as a financial obligation on the estate if the killer had assets.

ومَنْ وَجَبَ عليه القصاص، فمات : سقط القصاص)؛ لفوات محل الاستيفاء.
And one upon whom qisas is obligatory, then dies: qisas lapses due to the loss of the place of execution.
Source pages p. 377

Commentary
This brief passage states a straightforward rule: if a person who is liable for qisas (e.g., a murderer) dies, the penalty of qisas is dropped. The reason given is that the 'place of execution' (مَحَلُّ الاسْتِيفَاء) is no longer present, meaning the body is not to be mutilated after death. However, the financial right (diyah) may still be owed to the heirs, depending on circumstances.

محل الاستيفاء (maḥall al-istīfā'): The physical body of the person who must undergo retaliation.

Cutting Off a Hand by Two People: No Retaliation
States the ruling and gives qiyas-based proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas when two people cut off one hand and explain the divisibility rationale using qiyas.

Core Idea
A divisible limb means no qisas; exact equivalence is required for retaliation.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi principle in the law of qisas for bodily harm. When two individuals together sever a single hand, neither can receive the same hand amputation as punishment. Why? Because the hand is divisible – each cutter is responsible only for a part, and qisas demands perfect correspondence. The same reasoning applies to legs, teeth, and any limb, but not to life itself, which is indivisible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State clearly: there is no qisas (retaliation) on either of the two cutters. The ruling is not execution or amputation of their hands.
2
Analyze the Rationale – Divisibility
The hand is divisible (mutajazzī'). Each cutter only cut a portion. Since qisas requires exact equivalence (mumāthala) between crime and punishment, cutting off a whole hand for a partial cut would be disproportionate.
Contrast with homicide: the soul is indivisible, so each killer is fully liable
3
Understand the Qiyas (Analogy)
The source text draws a qiyas jallī: multiple killers of one soul all face qisas (since death is indivisible), but multiple cutters of one limb do not (since limb is divisible). The illa (effective cause) is divisibility.
This is a clear example of tanqīḥ al-manāṭ
4
Contrast with a Stake Objection
Some may argue the hand is a single organ, so each cutter should be fully liable. The Hanafi response: the hand is composed of parts; each cut can be divided proportionally.
Thus the Shafi‘ī position may differ
5
Apply the Rule to Other Cases
The same ruling applies to cutting off a leg, pulling out a tooth, or any injury less than life. Joint actions on divisible organs never trigger qisas.
Instead, diyah (blood money) is shared proportionally

And if two men cut off the hand of one man, or his leg, or pull out his tooth, or similar things less than life: then there is no qisas on either of them; because each of them is a cutter of part of the hand; for the severance occurred by their combined action, and the location (body part) is divisible; so part is attributed to each, so there is no equivalence, contrary to the soul; because death is not divisible.

Commentary
This passage explains the Hanafi ruling on joint mutilation of a limb. If two people cut off a single hand, neither is liable for qisas because the hand is divisible; each person only cuts a part, and exact equivalence (mumathala) is required for qisas, which is impossible here. The text compares this to homicide: the soul is indivisible, so each co-killer is fully liable for the entire life. This is a clear analogy (qiyas jali) based on the attribute of divisibility.

Key Terms
مماثلة (mumāthala): Exact equivalence between the crime and the punishment.
متجزىء (mutajazzī'): Divisible; a limb can be split into parts.

Delay of Qisas for Wounds: The Principle of Sirayah
Ruling for delaying qisas for wounds and the principle of subsumption in combined injuries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for delaying qisas for wounds and explain the underlying rationale based on the possibility of sirayah (the wound spreading to cause death).

Core Idea
Qisas for a wound is delayed until full recovery to assess if the wound becomes fatal—sirayah subsumes the wound into killing.

In Hanafi fiqh, retaliation for a wound (jirahah) is not executed immediately after the act. The law requires waiting until the victim either recovers completely or dies from the wound. This lesson unpacks the first lines of the passage from al-Lubab, explaining the rationale of sirayah and the principle that when a wound and subsequent killing are of the same genre (both mistaken, no recovery), the greater compensation (diyah for death) subsumes the lesser (arsh for the wound).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a wound (jirahah). The ruling is that qisas is not executed until the victim recovers (yabra').
The delay is a condition for execution, not a condition for the act being a crime.
2
Understand the cause: sirayah
Sirayah means the wound's effect could spread and cause death. Until it's clear whether the victim recovers, we cannot treat the wound as a separate injury; it might be part of a fatal chain.
Sirayah is a key concept in qisas for bodily harm.
3
Analyze the case of mistaken cut then kill before recovery
If a person cuts off a hand by mistake, then kills by mistake before the wound heals, only one diyah (for killing) is due; arsh for the hand drops.
The greater diyah subsumes the lesser because both acts are of the same genre (mistaken), and no recovery occurred.
4
Extract the general principle
When two harms of the same type (e.g., both mistaken, or both intentional with no recovery) occur in succession without healing, the later greater punishment/compensation subsumes the earlier lesser one.
This principle applies only when the acts are of the same genre and no bar' (recovery) intervenes.

٤- وإن لم يبرأ بينهما : كَفَتْ دية القتل، وهي مسألة الكتاب.

٦- أو بالعكس (أي قطع خطأ، ثم قتل عمداً).

Book of Retaliation/Diyat.
Whoever wounds a man with a wound: retaliation is not executed against him until he recovers.
Whoever cuts off the hand of a man by mistake, then kills him by mistake before recovery: upon him is the blood money (diyah), and the payment for the hand (arsh al-yad) drops.
And these are eight scenarios: because the cutting is either intentional or mistaken, and similarly the killing, so they become four, then either there is recovery between them or not, so they become eight:
1. If both are intentional, and he recovers between them: retaliation is taken for the cut, then for the killing.
2. And if he does not recover: it is the same according to Imam (Abu Hanifa), contrary to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
3. And if both are mistaken, if he recovers between them: both are taken: so the diyah of the hand and the life are obligatory.
4. And if he does not recover between them: the diyah of the killing suffices, and that is the case of the book.
5. And if he cuts intentionally, then kills by mistake.
6. Or vice versa (i.e., cuts by mistake, then kills intentionally).
Whether he recovers between them or not: both are taken; due to the difference of the two offenses.
And every intentional crime in which retaliation drops due to a doubt (shubhah): such as the killer being a father, or the one entitled to retaliation being a child of the offender, or if among the killers there is a minor or the victim has forgiven.
Source pages p. 402

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri discusses the ruling on qisas (retaliatory punishment) for wounds and the interaction between wounding and subsequent killing. The author first states a general principle: retaliation for a wound is not executed immediately; rather, it is delayed until the victim recovers. The reason is that the wound is evaluated by its ultimate outcome due to the possibility of it spreading (sirayah) to the soul, thereby becoming a killing. The true nature becomes clear only upon full recovery.

Then the author presents a specific case: if a person mistakenly cuts off another's hand and then mistakenly kills him before the wound heals, the obligation is only the full diyah (blood money) for the life; the arsh (fixed compensation) for the hand is dropped. This is because both acts are of the same genre (jins) of offense (both mistaken injuries to life/limb), and the greater compensation (diyah of the soul) subsumes the lesser.

The author then outlines eight possible scenarios combining the cutting (hand) and killing, based on whether each act is intentional ('amd) or mistaken (khat') and whether there was full recovery (bar') between them. The eight scenarios arise from 2x2 combinations (intentional/mistaken for cut and kill) times two possibilities (recovery or not). The commentary then enumerates them:

1. Both intentional, recovery between acts: qisas is applied for the cut and then for the killing.
2. Both intentional, no recovery: Imam Abu Hanifa holds the same (qisas for both), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) differ. They likely consider that if there is no recovery, the wound is subsumed by the killing, so only one qisas applies. The term 'same' (kadhalik) refers to the previous ruling that both are taken.
3. Both mistaken, recovery: both compensations are taken—diyah of hand and diyah of life.
4. Both mistaken, no recovery: only the diyah of killing suffices; the hand's compensation drops. This is the original case mentioned in the book.
5. Cut intentional, killing mistaken: regardless of recovery, both compensations are taken because the acts differ in nature (one intentional, one mistaken).
6. Cut mistaken, killing intentional: same as above, both taken.

Finally, the author mentions a related ruling: any intentional act where qisas drops due to a shubhah (doubt)—such as a father killing his son, or the victim being a descendant of the killer, or the presence of a minor among the killers, or forgiveness by the victim—the diyah of the soul is taken, and the arsh of the limb is not separate because the doubt prevents qisas for the limb as well.

Key Terms
الجراحة (al-jirahah): A wound that does not reach the level of life-taking; a non-fatal injury.
السراية (al-siraayah): The spread of a wound's effect to cause death.
البرء (al-bar'): Complete healing of the wound such that no further risk of death remains.
أرس اليد (arsh al-yad): The fixed monetary compensation for loss of a hand.
شبهة (shubhah): A doubt that prevents the application of qisas, such as kinship or forgiveness.

Qisas for a wound is wajib but cannot be executed until the victim fully recovers, due to the risk of sirayah turning the wound into a killing.

Eight Scenarios of Combined Hand-Cutting and Homicide
Enumeration of eight scenarios of combined hand-cutting and killing, with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the eight scenarios of combined hand-cutting and homicide and explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement in scenario 2.

Core Idea
In combined injuries, the presence of recovery (bar') and the nature of each act determine whether qisas/compensation is separate or subsumed.

The passage from al-Lubab presents eight scenarios arising from two variables: whether the cutting and killing are intentional or mistaken, and whether the wound healed between them. This lesson walks through each combination and highlights the dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding scenario 2.

Identify the two variables
Cutting (hand) can be 'amd or khat'. Killing can be 'amd or khat'. So 2x2 = 4 basic types. Then for each, either recovery (bar') occurs or not, making 8.

Enumerate scenarios 1-4: both acts of same genre
Scenarios 1-2: both intentional; 3-4: both mistaken. Within each, sub-scenarios: recovery or not.
Recovery separates the acts; no recovery may cause subsumption.

Analyze scenario 2 dispute
Both intentional, no recovery: Imam says separate qisas for both; Sahibayn say only qisas for killing, wound qisas drops.
The practical difference: under Imam, two executions; under Sahibayn, one.

One intentional, one mistaken. Regardless of recovery, both compensations are taken because the acts are of different genres.
Different genres prevent subsumption.

Scenarios 7-8: shubhah cases

Accusing another does not free the accused from the oath; he must swear except regarding the accused.

In qasamah, the accused might try to shift blame by naming someone else. This lesson examines the ruling: his accusation is not accepted because it is a mere attempt to escape liability. In the Hanafi school, not all intoxicating grape juice is khamr; it must also throw foam to be fully prohibited.

Abu Hanifa requires the juice to ferment, become strong, and throw foam until clear, while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that intoxication alone suffices for prohibition. The fatwa of Qadikhan further clarifies that legal consequences such as hadd depend on actual intoxication.

Core Idea
A trained dog leaves its prey; three times is mufta bihi, but Abu Hanifa requires only certainty; some beasts are excepted by nature.

Hunting with trained dogs is permissible, but the meat is only halal if the dog is properly trained. The source text from al-Lubab Sharh al-Quduri presents the matn and its expansion, including internal Hanafi differences.

Training a Hunting Bird: The Criterion of Returning When Called

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the sign of training for a hunting bird and explain why it differs from the dog's sign based on the animal's natural disposition.

Core Idea
A trained falcon returns when called — its wild nature makes response the test, not leaving the prey.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi criterion for a trained hunting bird. The matn states a simple rule: the sign is that it returns when called. But why is it not the same as for a dog? The commentator explains using the concept of natural disposition (tabi'ah). Understanding this difference is crucial for correctly applying the hunting laws.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in the matn
Read the matn carefully: 'Training of the falcon: that it returns when you call it.' This is the explicit Hanafi ruling.
The ruling is directly stated without proof in the matn; the commentary provides the reasoning.
2
Distinguish the two animals and their natural habits
The commentary contrasts the falcon's wild, averse nature (mutawahhish) with the dog's tame, familiar nature (aluf). This distinction is key.

The commentator argues that the sign of training must be the opposite of the animal's natural habit. For the falcon, returning when called overcomes its wildness; for the dog, leaving the prey overcomes its tendency to devour.
This is an example of ta'lil (causal reasoning) in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Apply the rule to determine training
When assessing a hunting bird, check if it returns when called. If it does, it is trained and the prey is halal. If it does not, the prey is carrion.
The eating of the prey is irrelevant for birds; only the response to the call matters.

If the baz eats from the prey, it remains halal; but if the hunter finds it alive, he must slaughter or it becomes unlawful—unless he is unable, where there is disagreement.

Ruling on Two Hunters: Second Hunter Kills Wounded Game
Establishes ruling when second hunter kills game already wounded by first

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a second hunter kills game already severely wounded by the first, and explain the attribution of death reasoning from al-Hidayah.

Core Idea
If the first hunter severely wounds game, second's kill is not halal; second must compensate first for value minus wound.

Hunting involves precise rulings on attribution of death and liability. When two hunters target the same game, the outcome depends on whether the first wound was incapacitating. This passage from al-Quduri and al-Hidayah establishes that if the first hunter's shot puts the game beyond escape, the second hunter's kill does not count as proper slaughter, and he must compensate the first hunter for the value minus depreciation from the first wound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the second hunter did (kill game already severely wounded) and the hukm: meat not permissible to eat, and second hunter is liable.
Ruling has two components: prohibition of eating and financial compensation.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author cites al-Hidayah which provides a conditional reasoning: if the first shot was such that the game could escape, then death is attributed to the second shot; but if the first shot rendered it incapable of escape, then proper slaughter was possible and the second's kill is invalid.
The dalil is a rational argument (dalil aqli) based on the state of the game after the first wound.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal link)
The key legal link is that when the game is severely wounded (ithkhan) such that it cannot escape, the obligation of proper slaughter (dhabihah) becomes possible. Since the second hunter kills without slaughtering, the death is not due to a valid Islamic method, so the meat is not halal.
The condition of 'ability to slaughter' (al-qudra ala al-dhakah) is critical.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
This ruling is mukhtalaf fih: other schools may attribute death differently or have different liability rules. The Hanafi position relies on the Zahir al-Riwayah texts and al-Hidayah's reasoning.
The disagreement centers on whether the second hunter's act is the direct cause of death or merely an acceleration.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred position)
The mufta bihi position is as stated: not eaten, and second is liable. Al-Hidayah supports this without mention of internal Hanafi ikhtilaf, so it is strong within the school.
Tarjih authority: al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani) adopts this view.
