Qisas for Head Wound When Head Sizes Differ
Ruling on qisas for head wound when wound covers victim's full head width but not offender's

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas for a head wound when the wound covers the victim's full head width but not the offender's, and explain the reasoning behind the victim's choice.

Core Idea
When exact equivalence in qisas is impossible, the victim may choose to retaliate to the exact extent of his injury or take full blood money.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in qisas – retaliation for bodily harm. When a head wound spans the full width of the victim's head but the offender's head is larger, exact equivalence in retaliation becomes impossible. The Hanafi school provides a balanced solution: the victim is given a choice between limited retaliation or full compensation. We will explore the reasoning that supports this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
A head wound (shajjah) is inflicted that covers the area between the victim's temples (full width) but due to the offender's larger head, the same wound would cover less of the offender's head. Ruling: the victim has a choice.
The wound must be a shajjah that reaches the bone.
2
Understand the problem of exact equality
If qisas were applied exactly (same length), it would be disproportionate on the offender's head. If applied to cover full width of offender's head, it would exceed the original injury. Exact equality (musawat) is unattainable.
The difference in head size creates this imbalance.
3
Recognize the two options
Option 1: Qisas to the exact extent of the victim's wound, starting from whichever side he chooses. Option 2: Full blood money (arsh) for the injury.
The victim may choose either, not forced to one.
4
Analyze the underlying principle
The ruling is based on analogy (qiyas) with the case of the paralyzed hand: when exact equivalence is impossible, the victim's right is preserved by granting a choice between limited retaliation and compensation.
The common illa (effective cause) is the inability to achieve exact equality.
5
Apply to practice
Suppose A wounds B with a 5 cm cut across B's head. A's head is 10 cm wider, so 5 cm on A is a smaller proportion. B can demand a 5 cm cut on A's head (limited qisas) or take blood money.
The qisas must be carried out in a way that does not exceed the original injury.

Commentary
This passage continues the theme of qisas when exact equivalence is impossible. The case: A man inflicts a head wound (shajjah) on another person. The wound stretches across the full width of the victim's head (between his two temples), but the offender's head is larger, so if qisas were applied exactly, it would not cover the same proportion on the offender's head (either exceeding or falling short). The author explains that if the wounded person (victim) wants qisas, he cannot claim a wound that covers the whole width of the offender's head because that would be more than his own injury. But if he limits it to the exact length of his own wound, the offender's disfigurement might be less noticeable due to the larger head size. Thus the victim has the choice: either to retaliate to the extent of his own wound (starting from whichever side he prefers), or to take full blood money (arsh). The reasoning is analogous to the earlier case of the paralyzed hand: exact equality is unattainable, so the victim is given an option. The passage then transitions to a new topic: no qisas for the tongue and penis.

Key Terms
شجة (shajjah): A head wound that reaches the bone.
قرنان (qarnan): The two temples or the edges of the head from ear to ear.

Cutting Hair and Nails in the First Ten Days of Dhul Hijjah
A benefit on the permissibility of hair/nail cutting for the one sacrificing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on cutting hair/nails during the first ten days of Dhul Hijjah for one intending sacrifice, and identify the intra-madhhab nuance regarding khilaf al-awla.

Core Idea
Cutting hair or nails is permissible, not disliked — but some Hanafis said avoidance is better.

A common question arises for those intending to offer an udhiyah (sacrificial animal) during Eid al-Adha: can they cut their hair or nails in the first ten days of Dhul Hijjah? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position: it is permissible and not makruh. However, some later scholars considered it khilaf al-awla (contrary to what is better). Understanding this distinction helps avoid over-restriction while respecting scholarly preference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is cutting hair or nails for someone who intends to sacrifice during the first ten days of Dhul Hijjah. The ruling is mubah (permissible), explicitly stated as 'la ba'sa' and 'yubahu', and not makruh.
2
Note the supporting sources
The author cites classical Hanafi works like Sharh Ma'ani al-Athar by al-Tahawi and Nukhab al-Afkar by al-Ayni, which support permissibility.
These are from the earlier Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Recognize the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some later Hanafis (mutā'akhkhirūn) recommended not taking hair or nails, considering it khilaf al-awla (contrary to the preferred). This is not a prohibition but a recommendation of avoidance.
Khilaf al-awla is a category below makruh tanzihi.
4
Apply the weight of authority
The earlier position (Zahir al-Riwayah) is permissibility and is the muftā bihi. The later view is a minority opinion within the school.
The text presents permissibility as the main view.
5
Distinguish from other schools
The Shafi'i school considers it makruh. The Hanafi position is more lenient, relying on the absence of a clear prohibition.
This cross-madhhab difference is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a fā'ida (benefit) addressing a common question: Is it permissible for someone who intends to offer an udhiyah (sacrificial animal) to cut their hair or nails during the first ten days of Dhul Hijjah? The Hanafi position is clear: it is permissible (lā ba'sa) and not disliked (makrūh). The author cites several authoritative sources from the classical Hanafi tradition, including al-Tahawi and al-Ayni, to support this view. However, some later Hanafis (mutā'akhkhirūn) recommended against it, considering it khilāf al-awla (contrary to the preferred practice). The author also mentions that the writer (likely the commentator or editor) has a separate research paper on this issue comparing the four schools. This shows that while the earlier Hanafi position is permissibility, later scholars sometimes preferred avoidance, possibly influenced by other schools like the Shafi'is who consider it makruh. The relied-upon (muftā bihi) position in the Hanafi school remains permissibility, as indicated by the statement 'lā ba'sa'.

Key Terms

خلاف الأولى (khilāf al-awlā): An action that is permissible but contrary to what is preferred or better.
