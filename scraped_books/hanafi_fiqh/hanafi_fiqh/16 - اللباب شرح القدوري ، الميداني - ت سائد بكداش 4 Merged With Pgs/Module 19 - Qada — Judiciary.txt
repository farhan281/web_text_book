Administration of Qasamah Oaths: Refusal and Repetition
Explains the procedure of qasamah oaths and their legal effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a resident refuses the qasamah oath and explain the requirement to repeat oaths to complete fifty.

Core Idea
In qasamah, refusal leads to imprisonment, and oaths are repeated to complete fifty because the Sunnah demands it.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri details the procedure when a resident refuses to take the qasamah oath. It distinguishes qasamah from financial oaths and explains the repetition of oaths when the number of residents is less than fifty. Understanding these rules is crucial for a qadi dealing with suspicious deaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the qasamah oath. The ruling: refusing leads to imprisonment until the oath is taken. The oath itself is wajib.
Focus on the consequence of refusal, not the oath taking itself.
2
Locate the dalil
The Sunnah establishes that fifty oaths are required. The imperative to complete them indicates obligation.
The hadith on qasamah is from al-Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The obligation to complete fifty oaths implies that refusal is not permitted; enforcement via imprisonment is a means to uphold the Sunnah.
Consider why imprisonment is used: to preserve the sanctity of blood.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The passage contrasts qasamah with financial oaths: in financial claims, refusal (nukul) leads to dropping the claim, not imprisonment.
This distinction is key to understanding the special nature of qasamah.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) ruling, confirmed by al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah. Some schools reject qasamah in these cases.
The Hanafi school's view is authoritative in the Hanafi tradition.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri, a Hanafi fiqh manual, elaborates the rulings of qasamah (oath-taking in cases of homicide). The author first states the procedure: if one of the residents refuses to swear, he is imprisoned until he does so. This is because the oath in qasamah is an end in itself, not a substitute for a right, unlike oaths in financial claims. The diyah (blood money) is owed by the residents due to the apparent presence of the killed among them or their negligence. Qasamah aims to reveal the killer through avoidance of false oath, leading to qisas (retaliation). If they swear, they are cleared of intent and diyah becomes due. When the residents are fewer than fifty, oaths are repeated until fifty are completed, as the Sunnah requires fifty oaths. Repetition is allowed to fulfill the Sunnah. If the number is exactly fifty, the guardian cannot force repetition on one person because repetition is only for necessity.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasamah): A collective oath-taking in cases of suspicious death to establish liability among the residents of the locality.
النكول (al-nukul): Refusal to take the oath; in qasamah it leads to imprisonment until oath is taken.
الدية (al-diyah): Blood money paid by the residents when they swear, compensating for the killing.

Conditions for Qasamah: Participants and Evidence
States participant and evidence conditions for qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for qasamah participants and identify when the absence of evidence of violence invalidates the claim.

Core Idea
Qasamah requires free, adult, sane male participants and clear signs of violence; without these, no qasamah or diyah.

Qasamah is a unique Islamic procedure for unresolved homicide cases. However, it is not applied arbitrarily. The Hanafi school imposes strict conditions on who may participate and what evidence triggers it. This passage clarifies these prerequisites.

Guided Path
1
Identify the participants excluded from qasamah
List the four categories: minor, insane person, woman, slave. Understand that they are excluded because they cannot give a valid oath or are not considered people of support.
2
Understand the rationale for exclusion
Minors and the insane lack reliable speech; women and slaves lack the capacity to provide support (ahl al-nusrah) required for the oath.
The oath in qasamah is a serious collective assertion.
3
Identify the evidence condition
If a corpse is found without any signs of violence (wound, blow, strangulation), it is presumed natural death. Qasamah and diyah are not applicable.
The absence of visible trauma negates the presumption of homicide.
4
Apply to a scenario
Consider a body with no marks: no qasamah. Consider a body with a stab wound: conditions for qasamah may be met, provided participants are eligible.

This ruling ensures that qasamah is limited to cases where homicide is suspected.

حتف الأنف (hatf al-anf): Natural death, i.e., death from causes other than homicide, such as illness or old age.

The Accused's Claim of Another's Guilt in Qasama
Ruling on the accused's claim of another's guilt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when the accused in a qasama case names a specific killer and explain the reasoning behind the oath formulation.

Core Idea
Naming a specific killer does not exempt the accused from the oath; he must swear a compound oath.

In qasama, the accused is required to swear an oath denying guilt. But what if he claims someone else did it? Does that absolve him? This passage clarifies: he must still take an oath, but its wording is adjusted to account for his claim. Understanding this nuance prevents manipulation of the oath process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The accused in a qasama case states that a specific person (e.g., Zaid) killed the victim. Determine whether this statement is active or passive in construction? The passage covers both possibilities.
The text mentions both active and passive constructions, but the ruling is identical.
2
Understand the ruling
The judge does not accept the accused's claim as a full defense. Instead, he administers a compound oath: 'By Allah, I did not kill him, and I know no killer for him other than so-and-so.'
The oath has two parts: denial of own guilt and declaration of limited knowledge.
3
Analyze the talil (reasoning)
The reasoning: when the accused confessed the killing to one person, he becomes excluded from the oath regarding that person. Therefore, the oath must cover all others, including himself, affirming he is not the killer.
The principle: confession against a specific person removes that person from the oath's scope, but the obligation remains for the rest.
4
Apply the rule
If a similar scenario arises, you can now formulate the correct oath and explain why the accused cannot simply avoid swearing by naming another.

Commentary
This passage addresses the situation where the person accused in a qasama case claims that another specific person is the killer. Testimony of Relatives in Qasamah
Intra-Hanafi dispute about accepting testimony of accused's family in qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa rejects the testimony of the accused's close relatives in qasamah and evaluate the opposing view of the Sahibayn using qiyas.

Core Idea
Testimony of the accused's family is rejected due to tuhmah, despite a valid qiyas analogizing to a dismissed agent.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the admissibility of testimony from the accused's close relatives in qasamah proceedings. Abu Hanifa holds that such testimony is invalid due to inherent suspicion of partiality (tuhmah). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that once the guardian accuses someone else, the relatives' potential adversary status is removed, making their testimony acceptable, analogous to a dismissed litigation agent. We will analyze the reasoning behind each position and understand why Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon authority.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling: the testimony of the accused's close relatives is not accepted because of tuhmah (suspicion of partiality).
Tuhmah also prevents them from swearing the oath or paying blood money.
2
Identify the opposing view
State that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) accept the testimony under the condition that the guardian accused someone else.
They argue that the potential adversary status is removed.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the qiyas: the relatives are likened to a dismissed litigation agent (wakil) whose testimony is accepted after dismissal, because the adversary status ended.
The illa is 'removal of potential adversary status'.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Note that Abu Hanifa maintains that tuhmah persists regardless of later accusations; the tarjih (preference) by later authorities supports him.
Al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi relied on Abu Hanifa's view.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, the testimony is rejected; under Sahibayn, it is accepted, leading to different outcomes in qasamah cases.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

The Annual 'Aqilah Payment: Resolving the Manuscript Dispute on the 4-Dirham Cap
Intra-Hanafi dispute on manuscript variant of annual 'aqilah payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct Hanafi position on the annual payment from the 'aqilah for diyah and explain how a manuscript variant led to a misinterpretation.

Core Idea
Each 'aqilah member pays only 1⅓ dirhams per year, not 4; the total over three years is 4 dirhams.

This lesson examines a subtle but important intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the maximum annual payment per member of the 'aqilah when collecting blood money (diyah) over three years. The correct amount—derived from Imam Muhammad's explicit texts and confirmed by later authorities—is only about 1⅓ dirhams per year, totaling 4 dirhams. A manuscript variant of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar had led some Hanafis to mistakenly think the cap was 4 dirhams per year, but the authentic texts resolve this.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
The diyah is apportioned over three years, each year one-third. The passage states that no individual is charged more than four dirhams per year, but this is clarified by the subsequent commentary.
The initial statement in the matn could be ambiguous.
2
Locate the disputed phrase
The phrase 'la yuzadu al-wahid minhum 'ala arba'ati darahim' appears. In some manuscripts, it ends there, implying a cap of 4 dirhams per year. The correct text continues 'dirham wa daniqan', specifying the annual amount.
The missing phrase is critical.
3
Understand the manuscript variant
A minority view arose from incomplete copies of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar that omitted 'dirham wa daniqan'. This led to the interpretation that each pays 4 dirhams per year, totaling 12 over three years.
This variant is the source of the dispute.
4
Apply Imam Muhammad's explicit text
Imam Muhammad explicitly states that the total over three years is 3 or 4 dirhams per person, so per year it is 1 or 1⅓ dirhams. This aligns with the correct manuscript of al-Quduri.
Muhammad's text is the authoritative basis.
5
Affirm the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that each 'aqilah member pays 1 dirham and 2 daniq (1⅓ dirhams) per year, totaling 4 dirhams over three years. This is the asahh (most correct) view.
Confirmed by al-Hidayah and later commentators.

Commentary
This is a ruling from the Book of Blood Money ('Aql) regarding the method of collecting diyah (blood money) from the 'aqilah (the male agnates liable for payment). The passage states that the total diyah is apportioned over three years, each year one-third. The maximum taken from any individual in a single year is four dirhams. When the 'aqilah is small, each pays one dirham and two daniq per year; when large, the amount per person is reduced below that. The commentator then quotes al-Hidayah, which notes that this indicates the annual cap is four dirhams, but the total over three years would be twelve dirhams, which seems to contradict the explicit text of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. Imam Muhammad stated that the total over three years should not exceed three or four dirhams per person, meaning in each year only one dirham or one and one-third dirham is taken. The commentator clarifies that the correct and relied-upon position (al-ashah) is that of Imam Muhammad. The apparent discrepancy arose from a manuscript variant of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar that omitted the phrase 'dirham wa daniqan', leading some scholars to misinterpret the intended meaning. Once the correct text is restored, the positions align: the annual amount is one dirham and two daniq (which equals one and one-third dirhams), and over three years the total is exactly four dirhams, consistent with Muhammad's ruling. This passage exemplifies how careful textual transmission is crucial in Hanafi fiqh, and how later commentators resolved ambiguities by referring to the foundational texts of Imam Muhammad.

دانق (dāniq): One-sixth of a dirham. Two daniq equal one-third of a dirham.
دية (diyah): Blood money; financial compensation paid to the victim's heirs for unintentional killing or bodily harm.
