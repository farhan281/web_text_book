Conditional Divorce with 'Kullama': Repetition and Reliance on Subject
Explains rulings on 'kullama' conditional divorce, remarriage, and evidentiary rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that 'kullama' (whenever) causes divorce to repeat with each occurrence of the condition, and distinguish when the oath remains or is nullified based on the presence of the marital subject (mahall).

Core Idea
'Kullama' repeats divorce with each condition; the oath continues only if the wife remains a valid subject.

This passage from al-Lubab addresses a key Hanafi rule: when a husband says 'whenever' (kullama), the conditional divorce is not a one-time event but repeats. The reason is that 'kullama' requires generality and repetition. We'll explore how this rule interacts with remarriage, the effect of partial or total removal of marital authority (milk), and how disputes about the condition's occurrence are resolved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exception for 'kullama'
The passage starts by stating an exception: normally a conditional divorce occurs once when the condition is met, but with 'kullama' it repeats with each repetition of the condition until three divorces are exhausted.
Note: 'kullama' means 'whenever' – it implies generality.
2
Trace the effect of remarriage after three divorces
After three divorces, the wife becomes unlawful (mahall removed). If the husband later marries her after a valid intervening marriage, and the condition repeats, no divorce occurs because the consequence (jaza) has already been exhausted for that original marriage.
The oath is tied to the original marriage; remarriage does not revive it. Note Zufar's dissenting opinion.
3
Analyze the rule on removal of ownership (zawal al-milk)
If the husband swears a conditional divorce and then divorces once or twice (partial removal of ownership), the oath remains because the subject still partially exists. However, if ownership is removed entirely by three divorces, the oath is nullified because no subject remains.
Key: oath nullification depends on complete removal of the subject.
4
Examine occurrence of condition in vs. out of ownership
If the condition occurs while the wife is still in his marriage (ownership), the oath dissolves and divorce occurs. If it occurs when she is not in his ownership (e.g., after irrevocable divorce or death), the oath dissolves but no divorce occurs.
Divorce requires both the condition and the subject (mahall) to be present simultaneously.
5
Apply the rules for dispute between spouses
If they disagree about whether the condition occurred, the husband's word is taken (due to original non-occurrence), unless the wife brings evidence. But for conditions only she can know (like menstruation), her word is accepted regarding herself by istihsan.
This is a procedural evidentiary rule specific to divorce cases.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri discusses rulings on conditional divorce using the word 'kullama' (whenever). The author begins by stating an exception to the general rule: when the condition is phrased with 'kullama', the divorce repeats with each occurrence of the condition until three divorces are exhausted. The reasoning is that 'kullama' implies generality of actions, and generality necessitates repetition. Once three divorces occur, the wife becomes unlawful for the husband (the subject is removed). If he later marries her after a valid intervening marriage (the note references another husband), and the condition repeats, no divorce occurs because the three divorces already exhausted the consequence (jaza) within that original marriage. The oath does not remain with the condition after the marriage is renewed between them. There is a difference of opinion from Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) who held that the divorce would occur again. Next, regarding removal of ownership (zawal al-milk): if the husband divorces his wife once or twice after swearing an oath (e.g., conditional divorce), the oath is not nullified because the condition has not occurred; the subject (mahall) - i.e., the wife as a valid object of divorce - still partially remains. But if ownership is removed entirely by three divorces, the oath is nullified because the subject no longer exists. Then the text rules: if the condition occurs while the wife is still in his marriage (in his ownership), the oath dissolves and divorce takes effect. If the condition occurs when she is not in his ownership (e.g., after divorce or during 'iddah of a revocable divorce? Actually, the Hanafi position is that during 'iddah of a revocable divorce, she is still considered in his ownership? The text clarifies: if she is outside his ownership entirely, the oath dissolves but no divorce occurs due to lack of subject. Finally, the passage covers disagreement between spouses about whether the condition occurred. The default rule: the husband's word is taken because the original assumption is non-occurrence, unless the wife brings evidence. However, if the condition is something only the wife can know (like menstruation), her word is accepted regarding herself, and she is considered divorced if she claims the condition occurred, by istihsan (juristic preference) because she is trustworthy about her own state.

كلما (kullama): A word meaning 'whenever', used in conditional divorce to indicate repetition of the divorce with each occurrence of the condition.
الملك (al-milk): Ownership; here it means the marriage bond (nikah) that makes the wife a valid subject for divorce.
المحلية (al-mahalliyya): The quality of being a valid subject of divorce; the wife is a 'mahall' as long as she is in the husband's marriage (not irrevocably divorced).

Conditional Divorce: The Role of the Husband's Possession
States ruling on conditional divorce with possession condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional divorce when the condition occurs within or outside the husband's possession, and explain the concept of mahalliya.

Core Idea
Divorce occurs only if the condition is within the husband's domain, not merely upon its occurrence.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies a key distinction in conditional divorce. Many assume any fulfilled condition automatically triggers divorce, but Hanafi law requires the condition to occur within the husband's possession (milk). This lesson unpacks why the oath dissolves in both cases but divorce only happens when the condition is within his control, due to the requirement of mahalliya — the wife's legal eligibility to be divorced.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize that the ruling distinguishes between two outcomes based on where the condition occurs: within the husband's possession (milk) or outside it.
Possession here refers to matters under his legal authority or ownership.
2
Analyze the effect on the oath
Note that in both scenarios, the oath (yamin) dissolves because the condition is fulfilled. The condition's occurrence itself discharges the vow.
3
Analyze the effect on divorce
Contrast: divorce occurs only when the condition is within his possession. When outside, no divorce takes place despite the oath dissolving.
4
Understand the reason: mahalliya
The key concept is mahalliya — the wife's legal subjecthood for divorce. A condition outside the husband's possession does not create a valid context for divorce because the wife is not under his authority in that specific matter.
Mahalliya is a condition for the effectiveness of talaq.
5
Apply to a new example
Take a husband saying 'If you go to the market, you are divorced.' If the wife goes with his permission (within his authority), divorce occurs. If she goes against his will (outside his control), the oath dissolves but no divorce.
The key is whether the act is within his domain of command.

Dispute Between Spouses on Condition Existence
Sets rules for dispute resolution when spouses disagree on whether condition occurred

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for resolving disputes between spouses on the existence of a condition for divorce and identify the two exceptions where the wife's word is accepted.

Core Idea
In talaq disputes, husband's word is default; wife's word only when she provides evidence or has exclusive knowledge.

This passage from Al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents a critical rule in Hanafi family law: when a husband conditions divorce on a future event and the spouses later disagree on whether it occurred, who is believed? The answer rests on the principle of istishab (presumption of continuity) and the rules of evidence, with a special concession for matters only the wife can know.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
The spouses differ about the occurrence of a condition attached to talaq. The condition could be any future event (e.g., menstruation, entry into a house).
2
Apply the default rule
The husband's word is taken because the original state (istishab) is that the condition has not occurred. He is effectively the defendant, so his statement stands unless proven otherwise.
This aligns with the general principle: al-asl baqa' ma kana 'ala ma kana.
3
Note the evidence exception
If the wife brings bayyinah (evidence), her claim prevails. The wife is the claimant, so she bears the burden of proof.
4
Recognize the exclusive knowledge exception
If the condition is of a type that only the wife can know (e.g., menstruation), her word is accepted regarding herself, based on istihsan. This is because no one else can verify it, so she is trusted.
This exception applies only in matters affecting herself, not for external effects.
5
Understand the rationale
The wife is considered ameenah (trustworthy) in matters that cannot be externally verified. The default rule protects the husband's presumed continuity, but yields when knowledge is exclusive to the wife.

Conditional Divorce with Another Woman Included
Ruling on claim-limited conditional divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a wife's claim of fulfilling a condition only triggers her own divorce, not that of another woman included in the same condition.

Core Idea
A claimant's word only binds herself; her claim cannot divorce another.

In this lesson, we examine a specific scenario in conditional divorce (ta'liq al-talaq). The husband makes the wife's divorce conditional on her menstruation and includes another woman. When the wife alone claims she menstruated, the law separates the effect: she is divorced, but the other woman is not. The underlying principle is that a person's statement is accepted regarding their own rights but not against others without evidence. This ruling illustrates the Hanafi approach to claims (da'wa) and their limited scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the facts
The husband says: 'If you menstruate, you are divorced and so-and-so is with you.' The wife claims she has menstruated. The other woman makes no claim.
2
State the ruling
Only the wife is divorced; the other woman remains married. The wife's divorce is valid (sahih).
This is the explicit ruling from the text.
3
Extract the legal principle
The wife is considered a claimant (mudda'iyah) regarding the other woman. Her statement is not evidence against a third party.
Hanafi principle: a claimant's assertion only binds themselves.
4
Apply the analogy
The author compares this to an heir who acknowledges a debt against the deceased. The heir's admission is accepted only for his own share, not for the other heirs.
This is a qiyas (analogical reasoning) to clarify the scope of a claim.
5
Note the practical consequence
The other woman's divorce does not occur unless she herself confirms the condition or there is independent evidence. The wife's claim cannot prejudice the other woman's marital status.

Conditional Divorce: The Three-Day Rule for Menstruation
Ruling on conditional divorce tied to menstruation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a divorce conditioned on menstruation requires three days of continuous blood to take effect.

Core Idea
A divorce tied to hayd does not occur at first sight of blood; it requires three days of continuous bleeding.

In conditional divorce (talaq mu'allaq), a husband may say, 'If you menstruate, you are divorced.' Does the divorce take effect the moment she sees any blood? The Hanafi school answers with an emphatic no. Because not every bleeding is considered hayd—menstruation has a minimum duration of three days. Until the blood has persisted for three days, we cannot be sure it is true menstruation. Once three days pass, we retroactively know it was hayd from the start, and the divorce is effective from the first day of bleeding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Conditional Statement
The husband says: 'If you menstruate, you are divorced.' This ties the divorce to the occurrence of hayd.
The condition is the wife's menstruation.
2
Initial Trigger: Seeing Blood
The wife sees blood. At this moment, we do not yet know if it will be hayd.
Blood could be istihadah or other discharge.
3
Apply the Hanafi Requirement
The Hanafi school requires that the blood continues for a minimum of three days to be considered hayd. If it stops before three days, it is not hayd and the divorce does not occur.
This is based on the definition of hayd in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Retroactive Ruling
If the blood continues for three full days, we rule that it was hayd from the beginning, so the divorce takes effect from the first day she saw blood.
This retroactive application ensures consistency.

This lesson unpacks the critical condition of mentioning 'self' (nafs) and explains how different Arabic phrases affect the number and timing of divorces. If the husband says 'If you wish' (in shi'ti), the option is confined to the sitting (majlis) only.
It does not extend beyond the immediate gathering.

Witnesses for Raj'ah: Recommended, Not Required

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, witnesses for raj'ah are recommended, not required, unlike the Shafi'i school.

The husband who revokes a divorce (raj'ah) is encouraged to have two witnesses, but this is not a condition for validity. The Hanafi school holds that the absence of witnesses does not affect the legal effectiveness of the raj'ah. This lesson clarifies the recommendation and contrasts it with the Shafi'i position that requires witnesses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the recommended element
The act is raj'ah (revocation of divorce). The recommended element is ishhad (having two witnesses present).
2
Determine the hukm level and its basis
The ruling is mustahabb (sunnah) – recommended. It is not wajib (obligatory) nor fard, as there is no evidence making it a condition for validity.
Hanafi usul distinguishes between sunnah and wajib; the absence of a command in the imperative form indicates recommendation.
3
Recognize the inter-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school requires two just witnesses for the validity of raj'ah. In Hanafi, witnesses only prevent future denial.
Thamarah al-khilaf: If a husband revokes without witnesses, the raj'ah is valid in Hanafi but invalid in Shafi'i.
4
Apply the ruling in practice
A husband should ideally have witnesses, but if he does not, the raj'ah is still effective. However, without witnesses, proving the raj'ah later becomes difficult.
Witnesses serve as evidence, not a condition.
