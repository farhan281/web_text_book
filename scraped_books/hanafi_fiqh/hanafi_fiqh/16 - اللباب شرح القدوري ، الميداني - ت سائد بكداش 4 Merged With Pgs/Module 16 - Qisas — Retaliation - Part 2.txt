Arrow Passes Through Target and Kills Another
Ruling on liability when a single arrow kills two persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a single act can carry multiple legal rulings based on its multiple effects, and state the rulings for each victim when an intentional act kills an unintended person.

Core Idea
A single arrow can be two legal acts: intentional murder for the target, error for the unintended victim.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi case: a man shoots an arrow intentionally at one person, but the arrow passes through and kills another person behind him. How does the law treat this single physical act? The Hanafi school applies a powerful principle: the act is multiplied by its effects. This means the archer faces different liabilities for each death, combining retaliation for the intended target and blood money for the unintended victim. We will explore the reasoning behind this ruling, which is based on qiyas and the concept of multiplicity of effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two victims and their legal treatments
Read the text and identify the ruling for the first victim (the intended target) and the second victim (the unintended one). Note the different consequences: qisas vs. diyah on the aqila.
2
Understand the basis for qisas for the first victim
The first death is intentional (amd). Therefore, the standard penalty for intentional murder applies: qisas (retaliation). The archer's intention makes this a deliberate killing.
Qisas is wajib (obligatory) upon the archer, not his aqila.
3
Understand the basis for diyah for the second victim
The second death is unintended. Although the archer's act was intentional towards the first, towards the second it is considered a type of error (khatā'). Therefore, diyah (blood money) is payable, and it is upon the aqila (paternal relatives) of the archer, not upon him personally.
This is similar to killing by mistake, where diyah is on the aqila.
4
Trace the qiyas (analogy) used
The text compares this case to shooting at game (sayd) but accidentally hitting a human. In that case, the shooter pays diyah on his aqila. Here, the second death is analogous: the archer intended the first but not the second, so the ruling is the same for the second.
The underlying cause (illa) is the multiplicity of effects: one act produces two consequences, each with its own ruling.
5
Apply the principle 'the act multiplies with the multiplicity of effect'
The statement 'al-fi'lu yata'addadu bita'addudi al-athar' is the key principle. It allows a single physical action to be legally split into multiple acts for the purpose of assigning different rulings. This is a fundamental Hanafi tool in cases of unintended consequences.
This principle is cited from al-Hidayah and is widely used in fiqh of jinayat.

Arrow Killing Two: Qisas for First, Diyah for Second
Liability for an arrow that kills an intended and an unintended victim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the liability for an intended victim (qisas) from an unintended victim (diyah on the 'aqila) when a single act kills both.

Core Idea
A single act can produce multiple legal effects: qisas for the intended, diyah for the unintended.

What happens when one arrow kills two people—one targeted, one accidental? The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction: the shooter owes qisas (retaliation) for the person he meant to kill, but only diyah (blood money) on his 'aqila for the second. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this split ruling and the key principle that an act's legal consequences multiply with its effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two victims and the intent behind each killing
The first victim was shot intentionally ('amd). The second victim was killed unintentionally when the arrow passed through the first. In Hanafi law, 'amd triggers qisas; khata' (error) triggers diyah.
The text says 'because it is one of the two types of error' – this is khata' fi al-fi'l, not khata' fi al-qasd.
2
Determine the liability for each victim
For the first: qisas is wajib because it was intentional murder. For the second: diyah is wajib, to be paid by the 'aqila (agnatic relatives), because the killing was a form of error.
The 'aqila shoulders the financial liability for unintended homicides.
3
Understand the legal analogy (qiyas) that supports the ruling
The second killing is analogous to someone shooting at game and accidentally killing a human. In both cases, the act was directed at a permissible target but struck a person, so it counts as error.
The qiyas links the 'unintended victim' to the established case of shooting at game (khata').
4
Apply the principle of multiplicity of effects
The text states: 'the action is multiple according to the multiplicity of its effect.' A single shot that wounds an animal, kills a person, and breaks a pot produces three different legal consequences (injury, homicide, property damage). Here, the same arrow kills two people, so two legal consequences follow.
This principle is key to understanding why one act can give rise to both qisas and diyah.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Other schools may treat both killings as intentional (if the act was deliberately aimed at the first) or both as accidental, but the Hanafi school uniquely distinguishes based on the target's identity.
The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, only one qisas applies, and the additional killing is compensated by diyah.

Qisas for Head Wounds: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas for head wounds below mudihah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding qisas for head wounds below the mudihah and identify the mufta bihi (operative ruling).

Core Idea
Qisas applies to most non-bone-exposing head wounds, except simhaq, due to feasibility of exact equivalence.

This passage addresses a nuanced internal disagreement within the Hanafi school about whether retaliation (qisas) is prescribed for head wounds that do not expose the bone. While the base text (matn) states a blanket rule of no qisas, the commentator clarifies that this represents only one narration (from al-Hasan ibn Ziyad), and the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwaya) from Muhammad al-Shaybani is that qisas is generally applicable, with the sole exception of the simhaq wound. Understanding this dispute helps appreciate how later Hanafi jurists resolved early differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Extract the two conflicting views: Abu Hanifa (via al-Hasan) holds no qisas for any head wound below the mudihah; Muhammad al-Shaybani holds qisas for all except simhaq.
The matn's generality is restricted to Abu Hanifa's narration.
2
Understand the exception
Note that simhaq is excluded by consensus because exact equivalence is impossible—the thin membrane above the bone cannot be split precisely.
Consensus here means within the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the rationale for qisas
For other wounds (e.g., dhamia, bazi'a), equivalence is achievable by using a tool of exact dimensions, penetrating the flesh to the same depth.
The principle is feasibility of exact replication (mumathalah).
4
Identify the tarjih (preference)
The commentator declares Muhammad's view as 'zahir al-riwaya' (apparent transmission) and supports it with references from later works (al-Jawhara, al-Hidaya, Sharh al-Isbijabi).
This is the mufta bihi for the school.
5
Apply the ruling
For practical fatwa, qisas is wajib for all wound types below mudihah except simhaq; for simhaq, diyah is prescribed, and intentional/accidental are equal.
The same principle applies to the six types of shijaj below the mudihah.

Delay of Qisas for Wound Until Recovery
Ruling with proof for delaying qisas until recovery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for delaying qisas for a wound and explain the rationale based on the possibility of siraya.

Core Idea
Qisas for a wound is delayed until recovery because the wound may spread and become homicide.

In this passage, the Hanafi school explains a critical procedural safeguard in qisas: retribution for a wound cannot be carried out immediately. Instead, we must wait to see if the injury heals or proves fatal. This prevents a miscarriage of justice where a wound turns out to be killing. The ruling is based on qiyas (analogical reasoning) using the illa of siraya (spreading).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wounding (jirahah). The ruling is that qisas is not applied immediately; it is postponed until the victim recovers.
2
Locate the dalil
The author provides a rational proof (qiyas): the wound is evaluated by its outcome because it may spread (siraya) to the soul, turning it into homicide.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The 'illa is the possibility of siraya. Since the final legal category (wound vs. killing) is uncertain, the punishment must wait until certainty is achieved through recovery or death.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
This ruling is agreed upon (muttafaq alayh) among the schools; no dispute is recorded.
If there were a dispute, we would examine opposing views.
5
Evaluate the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is exactly as stated: delay until recovery. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.

Only after recovery is it confirmed that the injury is limited to a wound, and then qisas for the wound can be applied. This precaution prevents miscarriages of justice.

The Rulings of Qasamah in Homicide Cases
Explains rulings of qasamah in homicide cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on qasamah, including who takes the oath, the consequences of refusal, and the rules when fewer than 50 men are available.

Core Idea
Qasamah is upon the locality; refusal leads to imprisonment, not immediate liability.

When a slain person is found among a community with no known killer, Islamic law does not leave the matter unresolved. The Hanafi school prescribes qasamah—oaths sworn by the people of the locality—to either clear themselves from suspicion or establish liability. This lesson will walk you through the key rulings: who swears, what happens upon refusal, and how the oaths are administered when the locality is small.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties involved
The wali (heir of the victim) does not take an oath; the qasamah is upon the people of the locality (ahl al-mahallah). Understand why the wali is not required to swear—oaths are for defense, not for claiming.
2
Determine the consequence of an oath and refusal
If the locality swears, they are absolved from qisas (retaliation) but diyah (blood money) is still due. If one refuses, he is imprisoned until he swears; refusal in qasamah is not like refusal in financial cases where liability may be imposed immediately.
Contrast with financial nukul: in qasamah, the oath is primary and cannot be replaced by payment.
3
Apply the rule for insufficient numbers
If the locality has fewer than 50 men, the oaths are repeated until 50 are completed. The same person may swear multiple times.
This ensures the solemnity and evidentiary weight of 50 oaths.
4
Understand the basis for diyah
Diyah becomes obligatory due to the apparent killing or negligence in guarding (taqsir) by the locality. This is similar to accidental killing (khata) where the 'aqilah pays.
The 'aqilah pays over three years, including the value of a slave if applicable.

Commentary
This passage states the rulings of qasamah (oath of compurgation) in homicide cases. The wali (heir) is not made to take an oath nor is judgment given based on his oath because oaths are for defense, not for establishing claims. If the claim is for intentional killing (amad), the qasamah applies; if accidental (khata), the blood money is on the 'aqilah (male relatives). According to the Zahir al-Riwayah (the authoritative transmitted position), the qasamah is upon the people of the locality (ahl al-mahallah), and the diyah is upon their 'aqilah payable over three years. Similarly, the value of a slave found in such circumstances is also taken over three years. If one of the locality refuses to swear, he is imprisoned until he swears because the oath is required for the sanctity of blood. If the number of people in the locality is less than 50, the oaths are repeated until they reach 50 oaths. The diyah becomes obligatory due to the apparent killing or negligence in guarding. Qasamah is legislated to reveal the truth: if they swear, they absolve themselves from qisas (retaliation) but diyah remains; if they refuse, they are presumed guilty. The refusal to swear in qasamah differs from refusal in financial cases; here the oath is primary and cannot be replaced by payment.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasamah): Oaths taken by the people of the locality when a slain person is found among them, to deny responsibility or establish liability.
الولي (al-wali): The guardian or heir of the victim who seeks retribution or blood money.
العاقلة (al-'aqilah): The male agnatic relatives of the killer who are responsible for paying blood money in cases of accidental killing.
النكول (al-nukul): Refusal to take the oath after being required to do so.

Conditions for Qasamah: Participants and Signs of Homicide
Conditions for qasamah: participants and evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for qasamah and apply the Hanafi rules to determine whether qasamah applies in a given homicide scenario.

Core Idea
Qasamah requires both valid participants (free, adult, sane males) and clear signs of homicide.

Qasamah is a unique legal procedure in Hanafi fiqh for establishing liability in homicide cases when the perpetrator is unknown but strong circumstantial evidence exists. This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains two critical aspects: who is eligible to take the oaths, and what physical indicators on the victim's body trigger the procedure. These conditions ensure that qasamah is only used when there is credible evidence and credible participants.

Guided Path
1
Identify eligible participants
Note that minors, insane persons, women, and slaves are excluded. Understand the two reasons: lack of sound speech (for minors/insane) and lack of support capacity (for women/slaves).
The legal rationale is that oaths require credibility and social standing.
2
Determine the legal presumption from body condition
If the body shows no trace of violence, it is presumed natural death (ḥatf anfihi). No qasamah, no diyah.
The burden of proof shifts to the claimant to show homicide signs.
3
Distinguish blood flow types
Blood from natural openings (nose, mouth, anus, front) is normal and does not indicate homicide. Blood from eye or ear is abnormal and indicates homicide.
This distinction is based on customary experience (ʿurf).
4
Apply the rule for body found on a ridden animal
If the deceased is found on an animal driven by someone, qasamah is on that driver and diyah on his ʿāqilah, because the deceased was under his control.
This treats the driver as the custodian of the deceased.
5
Synthesize: conditions for qasamah
Qasamah requires both valid participants (free, sane, adult, male Muslims) and clear homicide indicators. Both must be present.
If either is lacking, the procedure does not apply.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri details the conditions for qasamah (a procedure of fifty oaths in cases of suspected homicide). The author begins by listing who cannot participate: a minor, an insane person, a woman, and a slave. The rationale is that minors and insane persons are not capable of sound testimony (their words lack legal weight), while women and slaves are not considered part of the 'support group' (ahl al-nusrah) that gives credibility to the oaths in qasamah. The passage then addresses the key issue of when qasamah applies based on the condition of the body. If a dead body is found with no external trace of violence (wound, beating, strangulation), there is no qasamah and no diyah—the assumption is natural death. This is because the legal presumption (al-‘urf) defines a homicide victim as one whose life was taken by the direct act of a living person. The author then distinguishes between different types of blood flow: blood from the nose, anus, or mouth (including private parts) is considered normal and does not indicate homicide. However, blood from the eye or ear indicates a violent act because such bleeding normally requires external force. Finally, the passage covers a scenario: if a dead body is found on a riding animal being driven by a man, the qasamah and diyah are imposed on that man and his clan (in the Hanafi system, the 'aqilah pays diyah). This is because the deceased was under his control (fi yadihi), analogous to being in his house. The chapter emphasizes that qasamah is not automatic—it requires clear indicators of foul play.

Key Terms
القسامة (al-qasāmah): A procedure where fifty oaths are taken to establish liability for a homicide when there is evidence but no clear perpetrator.
العاقلة (al-‘āqilah): The clan or group of male relatives who are responsible for paying diyah on behalf of the perpetrator.
حتف أنفه (ḥatf anfihi): Natural death, literally 'death of his nose', meaning death by natural causes without external intervention.
