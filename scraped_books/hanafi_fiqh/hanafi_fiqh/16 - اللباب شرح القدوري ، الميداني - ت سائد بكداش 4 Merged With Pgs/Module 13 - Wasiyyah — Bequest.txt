Tadbir: Definition, Effects, and Limitations
Defines tadbir and states its effects during master's life and after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of tadbir, identify the explicit phrases that create a mudabbar, and explain the restrictions on the master during his life and the slave's fate upon the master's death.

Core Idea
Tadbir is a will: the slave is freed from one-third of the estate, and the master cannot sell or give him away during his lifetime.

This lesson introduces the chapter on Tadbir—postponed emancipation. In the Hanafi school, tadbir is treated as a type of will (wasiyyah), meaning it takes effect after death and is limited to one-third of the estate. The passage defines tadbir, lists the explicit declarations that create a mudabbar, and lays out the master's rights and restrictions during his life and the consequences after his death.

Guided Path
1
Define tadbir linguistically and legally
Understand the etymology and the legal definition: suspending emancipation upon the master's death.
The linguistic root implies looking to the end of an affair.
2
List the explicit phrases that create tadbir
Identify the clear words such as 'If I die, you are free' or 'You are free after my death'.
Any wording clearly indicating deferment to death qualifies.
3
Determine the master's restrictions during his life
Once tadbir is valid, the master cannot sell, give away, or remove the slave from his ownership except to freedom.
The main restriction is alienation; the master retains use rights (labor, hiring, intercourse if female).
4
Apply the post-death rule: one-third limitation
The mudabbar is freed from one-third of the master's estate. If the estate has no other assets, the slave works for two-thirds of his value for the heirs.
Tadbir is a will; thus limited to one-third and can be adjusted proportionally if the slave exceeds the cap.
5
Handle debt priority
Debts take precedence over the will. If the master has debts that consume the full value, the slave must work for the full value to pay creditors.
If there is debt, the slave does not get freedom until the debt is satisfied by his labor.

Commentary
This passage is the chapter on Tadbir (postponed emancipation). It begins by defining tadbir linguistically and legally, then lists the explicit phrases that create a mudabbar. The key ruling is that once a slave becomes mudabbar, the master cannot sell or give him away, as his ownership is restricted. However, the master retains the right to use the slave's labor, hire him out, and if the slave is female, he may have intercourse and marry her off. Upon the master's death, the slave is freed from one-third of the estate, because tadbir is treated as a will (wasiyyah). If the estate has no other assets, the slave works for two-thirds of his value to compensate the heirs. If there is debt, the slave must work for the full value to pay creditors, as debt takes priority over a will. The commentary from al-Hidayah explains that the reasoning is analogous to kitabah (contract of emancipation) and that tadbir is a gratuitous act deferred to death, so it is limited to one-third.

Key Terms
التدبير (al-tadbir): Suspending emancipation upon the master's death.
المدبر (al-mudabbar): A slave whose emancipation is deferred to the master's death by explicit wording.
الوصية (al-wasiyyah): A gratuitous disposition taking effect after death, limited to one-third of the estate.
