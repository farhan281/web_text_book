Master Not Obliged to House Slave Wife
Ruling on master's non-obligation to house slave wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the master's obligation to house a female slave he marries off and identify who bears the responsibility.

Core Idea
When a master marries off his female slave, he is not obligated to provide a house for her; that duty lies with the husband.

This lesson covers a specific ruling from the chapter of Nikah in the Hanafi school. The matn states clearly that the master, upon marrying off his female slave, is not required to provide a separate marital home (bayt al-zawj) for her. This seemingly simple statement has important implications for the rights and responsibilities of the master, the husband, and the slave wife.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject
The ruling addresses the master (mawla) who marries off his female slave (amatihi). Understand who is being addressed.
2
Determine the legal consequence
The master is NOT obliged (laysa 'alayhi) to perform a certain action. This is a negation of obligation.
In Hanafi usul, the absence of obligation does not necessarily imply prohibition—it may be mubah.
3
Specify the action not required
The action is 'yubawwi'uha bayt al-zawj'—to provide her with a husband's home (a separate residence for the married couple).
This includes providing a house or apartment for the slave woman and her husband.
4
Infer the responsible party
By stating the master is not obliged, the ruling implicitly places the duty on the husband. In Hanafi fiqh, the husband is responsible for the wife's accommodation, including for a slave wife.
This is consistent with the general principle that the husband must provide housing, food, and clothing for his wife.

Guardianship of a Free Minor Woman: Sahibayn vs. Abu Hanifa
Intra-Hanafi dispute over guardian for free minor woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on guardianship of a free minor woman, explain the reasoning (talil) of each position, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
The paternal grandfather's compassion outweighs the brother's, making him the guardian – but Abu Hanifa gives it to the Sultan.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents a concise intra-Hanafi dispute: after the father and grandfather, who is the guardian (wali) of a free minor woman for marriage? Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the paternal grandfather (called 'her father' metaphorically) is the guardian because his compassion (shafaqah) is greater than that of the son (i.e., the brother). Imam Abu Hanifa, not quoted here but known from other sources, gives guardianship to the Sultan (ruler/judge). The commentator then notes that later Hanafi masters adopted the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace how Hanafi jurists prioritize guardians based on legal cause (illah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue is: who is the guardian of a free minor woman after her father and paternal grandfather are deceased? Muhammad says: her paternal grandfather (interpreted as 'her father' metaphorically). Abu Hanifa (not in the text) says: the Sultan. Note that the Sahibayn agree on the grandfather.
The text only gives Muhammad's view; Abu Hanifa's view is implied from the commentary.
2
Analyze the evidence (talil)
Muhammad's reasoning: the grandfather has greater compassion (shafaqah) than the son (brother). This is a teleological argument (talil) based on the purpose of guardianship – protecting the woman's interests. The more compassionate relative is preferred.
The Hanafi school uses shafaqah as a criterion to rank guardians.
3
Note the tarjih (preponderance) and its authorities
The commentator states that al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, and Sadr al-Shari'ah in al-Tashih relied upon the Sahibayn's position. This makes the grandfather the mufta bihi (operative ruling).
These are major authorities in the later Hanafi tradition.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: under the Sahibayn's view, the grandfather conducts the marriage. Under Abu Hanifa's view, the Sultan would do so. In practice, muftis follow the Sahibayn's view.
The Sultan is only resorted to when no qualifying male relative exists.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi and its weight
The mufta bihi is that the paternal grandfather is the guardian, after the father and father's father. This is the position of the Sahibayn, adopted by later scholars. The brother (son) is not preferred due to lesser compassion.
This ruling applies only when there is no father or grandfather.

Marriage of Slaves: Permission, Mahr, and Master's Duties
Rulings on slave marriage, mahr as debt, and master's duty to house

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid slave marriage in Hanafi law and explain the rulings on mahr and maintenance.

Core Idea
A slave's marriage requires the master's permission; the mahr is a debt on the slave's person, and the master need not provide housing.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rules governing the marriage of slaves. The rulings balance the slave's marital rights with the master's ownership. We will see why permission is essential, how the mahr becomes a debt, and what obligations (or lack thereof) the master has toward a married female slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core prohibition and its rationale
Locate the statement that a slave cannot marry without the master's permission. Understand why marriage is considered a defect for slaves: it affects their value and service.
The defect (عيب) rationale is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
2
Analyze the mahr as a debt on the raqaba
When a male slave marries with permission, the mahr becomes a debt on his person (رَقَبَة). This means he may be sold to pay it, but only once. If insufficient, the debt persists until emancipation.
The limitation to one sale protects the slave from repeated sales.
3
Understand the master's discretion regarding housing and maintenance
When a master marries off his female slave, he is not obliged to provide a separate house. The husband may visit her in the master's house. Maintenance (nafaqah) from the husband only becomes obligatory if the master provides a separate dwelling (تبوئة).
If the master houses her and then retracts, maintenance falls.

Stipulations in Marriage Contracts Affecting Mahr
Rulings on conditions in marriage: residency and polygamy stipulations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditions in marriage contracts that affect the mahr and apply the rule when a condition is breached.

Core Idea
A beneficial condition in marriage is valid; breach entitles the wife to mahr al-mithl, not the stipulated amount.

This lesson examines conditions (shurut) that can be included in a marriage contract concerning residency and polygamy. Unlike other schools, the Hanafi school upholds such conditions as valid and enforceable. Understanding the consequence of breaching a condition is essential for determining the wife's entitlement to mahr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions
Read the text and list the two specific conditions mentioned: not taking the wife out of town and not marrying another woman.
2
Ruling when condition is fulfilled
If the husband honors the condition, the wife receives the stipulated dower (al-musamma).
The stipulated amount is agreed upon initially.
3
Ruling when condition is breached
If the husband violates the condition (e.g., marries another or moves her), the wife gets mahr al-mithl (dower of her peers).
The stipulated amount is forfeited because the benefit was lost.
4
Understand the reasoning
The condition provides a benefit to the wife; when it is lost, she is compensated with the fair dower.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that conditions beneficial to the wife are enforceable.
5
Note the variable mahr variant
The text also discusses a scenario where the mahr is variable: 1000 if he stays, 2000 if he takes her out. In that case, breach leads to the higher amount or mahr al-mithl.
This shows flexibility in contractual stipulations.

Here, the conditions are considered beneficial and enforceable.

Conditions in the Marriage Contract and Their Effect on Mahr
Ruling on marriage conditions and mahr consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marriage conditions and determine the mahr due when a condition is breached.

Core Idea
Conditions in marriage are valid; breach shifts mahr from musamma to mahr al-mithl.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains how conditions attached to the marriage contract affect the wife's dower. The Hanafi school recognizes conditions that do not contradict the essence of marriage. Understanding this ruling is essential for differentiating between valid and invalid conditions and knowing the correct mahr obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Marriage Condition
The passage mentions conditions such as not taking the wife out of the city or not marrying another woman. Recognize that these are valid conditions under Hanafi law.
Conditions that benefit the wife and do not conflict with the contract's purpose are permissible.
2
Determine Fulfillment or Breach
Check if the husband fulfilled the condition (e.g., did not move her) or breached it (e.g., did move her or married another).
If condition is fulfilled, the named dower (mahr al-musamma) stands.
3
Apply the Mahr Ruling
If fulfilled → she gets the named dower. If breached → she gets the fair dower (mahr al-mithl), which is the dower of women of similar status.
The fair dower cannot be less than the named dower if the named dower was a minimum, nor exceed an agreed maximum (e.g., 2000 dirhams in the conditional dower case).
4
Consider Pre-Consummation Divorce
If the husband divorces before consummation, the named dower is halved regardless of condition, because the condition becomes void after divorce.
This is consistent with the general rule of half mahr upon divorce before consummation (Quran 2:237).

Mahr for an Unqualified Animal
Ruling on mahr when specified as an animal without description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mahr specified as an unqualified animal, identify the conditions for validity, and explain the husband's option to give the average or its value.

Core Idea
An unqualified animal as mahr is valid — the wife gets the average, and the husband may give the animal or its value.

In marriage contracts, the mahr must be specified to avoid uncertainty. But what if the mahr is simply 'a horse' or 'a donkey' without describing its quality? This passage from al-Quduri, with commentary from al-Hidayah, explains that such a specification is valid in the Hanafi school. The wife is entitled to the average quality of that species, and the husband may choose to give the average animal or its monetary value. However, if the genus itself is not specified, the marriage contract is still valid but the mahr becomes the mahr al-mithl.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A freed slave woman may annul her marriage to repel the husband's increased power over her.

This lesson examines a unique ruling in Hanafi fiqh: when a female slave is married and later freed, she gains the option (khiyar) to either remain with her husband or separate. This right exists whether the husband is free or a slave, and also applies to a mukatabah (a slave with a contract of manumission). The passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri provides both a rational justification—the increase in the husband's power due to the higher number of divorces needed after freedom—and mentions the hadith of Barirah as transmitted evidence. We will explore the legal reasoning, the textual proof, and a significant inter-madhhab difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Scope
State the core ruling: a freed slave woman has the choice (khiyar) to either stay in the marriage or annul it. Note that this applies regardless of whether the husband is free or a slave, and it also applies to a mukatabah.
The ruling is unconditional regarding the husband's status.
2
Understand the Rationale (Illah)
The text explains the reason: before freedom, the husband could divorce her with only two pronouncements (for a slave, the divorce count is two); after freedom, three pronouncements are required (as for free women). This is a rational argument (istidlal bi al-ma'qul) used by the commentator.

The hadith of Barirah (in Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim) is cited: when Barirah was freed, the Prophet ﷺ gave her the choice, and he did not distinguish between whether her husband was free or a slave. This supports the general application of the ruling.
The commentator al-Maydani usually only gives rational proofs but here also includes the hadith.
4

The Shafi'i school restricts the khiyar to cases where the husband is a slave, based on their interpretation of the same hadith. The Hanafi position (general application) is the mufta bihi.
Contrast the two schools' use of the hadith.
5
Determine the Legal Effect (Athar)
If the woman chooses to annul, the marriage is dissolved (faskh), not a divorce (talaq). If she chooses to stay, the marriage continues with full effect, and thereafter she is bound by the three-divorce rule.
The option must be exercised immediately upon knowledge of freedom according to some opinions.

Core Idea
The wife of an impotent husband must wait one year before seeking annulment; immediate option is not given.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on impotence ('innah) in marriage. The passage from al-Lubab records a disagreement within the school: while Imam Muhammad grants the wife an immediate option (khiyar), Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf require a one-year waiting period. The adopted position (mufta bihi) is the latter, as confirmed by later Hanafi authorities. We will walk through the arguments, the procedure, and the legal effects of the separation.

Guided Path
1
State the relied-upon Hanafi position
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the wife of an impotent husband does not have an immediate option to annul the marriage. She must bring the matter to a judge, who grants the husband one full year to consummate.
This is the mufta bihi position.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani held that the wife has an immediate option (khiyar) because impotence causes immediate harm (darar), analogous to the case of a eunuch. The passage quotes Muhammad's view.
Muhammad's argument is based on qiyas and harm prevention.
3

Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf respond that the harm from impotence is not necessarily immediate, as it may be treatable. A year is sufficient to determine incurability. The analogy to eunuchs is imperfect because impotence can be temporary.
The Hanafi response relies on the possibility of remedy and a cautious approach.
4

Under Muhammad's view, the wife can demand annulment immediately. Under the relied-upon view, she must wait a year. In practice, the judge gives a year, and only after failure does annulment occur.
This difference affects the timeline and certainty of the marriage.
5
Note additional subsidiary rules
The passage also covers: definition of impotence, effect of valid seclusion on dowry, illness during the year (months not counted), and loss of option if the wife knew or if she is retqa.
These details refine the application of the main ruling.

Separation for Impotence (al-‘Unna)
Explains the annulment, mahr, and conditions for the impotent husband.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on separation due to impotence, identify when the wife receives full mahr, and apply the conditions that affect her option to seek annulment.

Core Idea
Impotence gives the wife an option for annulment — one irrevocable divorce with full mahr if valid seclusion occurred.

Impotence (al-‘unna) is a marital defect that entitles the wife to seek separation. The Hanafi school treats this separation as a single irrevocable divorce (talaq ba’in), not an annulment. The husband’s impotence does not automatically dissolve the marriage; the wife must petition the qadi after a year. This passage from al-Quduri’s Mukhtasar, explained by al-Maydani, details the ruling and its conditions, including entitlement to full mahr, the effect of illness on the waiting period, and when the wife loses her option.

Guided Path
1
Identify the separation type
The text states the separation is 'taliqatan ba'inatan' (one irrevocable divorce). Recognize that this is a divorce originating from the husband's defect, not an annulment. It is irrevocable because the wife gains full control of herself.
Contrast with revocable divorce where the husband can take her back during idda.
2
Determine mahr entitlement
The wife gets the full mahr if valid seclusion (khalwa sahiha) occurred. Understand that valid seclusion with an impotent man is still considered valid and triggers full mahr and idda.
If no seclusion, only half mahr is due, as per general rules.
3
Understand the illness exception
Imam Muhammad holds that if either spouse is ill such that intercourse is impossible, the month of illness is not counted toward the one-year waiting period. Months with less than full illness are counted.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference; the author says Muhammad's view is the most correct.
4
Apply conditions for loss of option
The wife loses her right to seek annulment if: (a) she married the husband knowing his impotence (from a previous marriage or otherwise), (b) she remarries him after a previous annulment for impotence, or (c) she is ratqa' (has a physical obstruction).
The text cites al-Jawhara for the ratqa' exception.

Rulings on the Castrated (Majbub) and Eunuch (Khasi) in Marriage Annulment
Distinguishes between the castrated (majbub) and eunuch (khasi) regarding annulment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the majbub and khasi, and state the Hanafi ruling on annulment for each case.

Core Idea
For the castrated (majbub), annulment is immediate; for the eunuch (khasi), a one-year respite is granted.

This lesson addresses two specific cases of male reproductive incapacity: total castration (majbub) and testicular removal (khasi). The Hanafi school distinguishes them based on the possibility of cure and sexual function. The ruling determines whether the wife can obtain an immediate annulment or must wait a year, after which the judge separates them if no improvement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions
From the definitions in the text and footnotes, distinguish between al-majbub (penis and testicles removed) and al-khasi (only testicles removed, organ remains).
The key difference is that the majbub has no organ left, while the khasi retains the organ.
2
Extract the ruling for each
For the majbub: 'farrq al-qadi baynahuma fi al-hal' (judge separates immediately). For the khasi: 'yu'ajjal kama yu'ajjal al-'innin' (given a respite like the impotent).
The khasi is given a year (by analogy with the impotent) because of the possibility of recovery.
3

The majbub has no possibility of intercourse or cure, so no delay benefits anyone. The khasi retains the organ and may become capable, so a waiting period is allowed.
The text says 'li-ihitimal al-intishar wa al-wusul' (due to possibility of erection and penetration).
4
Note the conditions
The wife must request separation. For the khasi, the respite applies only if his organ does not become erect (i.e., he is currently impotent).
If the khasi's organ is already erectile, there is no need for annulment based on this defect.

When a Zoroastrian wife refuses Islam after her husband's conversion, the separation is considered an irrevocable divorce (talaq ba'in) in the relied-upon Hanafi view, and the wife receives full dower if consummation occurred.
