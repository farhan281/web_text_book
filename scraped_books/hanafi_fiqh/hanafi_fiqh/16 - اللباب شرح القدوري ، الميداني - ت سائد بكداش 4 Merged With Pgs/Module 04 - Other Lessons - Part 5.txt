Ruling on Omitting Tasmiyah: Intentional vs. Forgetful
Explains the effect of intentionally vs. forgetfully omitting tasmiyah on the validity of slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on omitting tasmiyah during slaughter and distinguish the consequences of intentional vs. forgetful omission.

Core Idea
Intentional omission of tasmiyah invalidates slaughter; forgetful omission does not, due to the principle of removing hardship.

This lesson addresses a key condition for valid slaughter: the recitation of tasmiyah (Bismillah). While the Quran explicitly prohibits eating meat over which Allah's name has not been mentioned, the Hanafi school introduces an exception for forgetfulness to avoid undue hardship. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining the permissibility of meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the slaughter of an animal with or without tasmiyah. The ruling is: intentional omission makes the meat carrion (haram); forgetful omission makes it halal.
Note that the act is the slaughter, not just the omission.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the Quranic verse (6:121) as proof. Identify the exact text and its source.
Surah Al-An'am, verse 121.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the verse prove the ruling? The verse is a prohibition of eating meat without mentioning Allah. In Hanafi usul, a prohibition indicates that the omission invalidates the act, making the meat carrion.
The exception for forgetfulness is not from the verse but from the principle of raf' al-haraj.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school considers tasmiyah a sunnah, not a condition. Their ruling differs: intentional omission does not invalidate the slaughter. Understand their proof and the Hanafi response.
This ikhtilaf is discussed further in the Al-Mukhtalaf Fih section.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi school relies on the absolute prohibition and the principle that forgetfulness is excused to avoid hardship. This is the mufta bihi position.
The ruling is based on the Quran and the maxim of lifting hardship.

Ruling on Leaving Tasmiya Intentionally and Forgetfully
Explains ruling of tasmiya during slaughter and exception for forgetfulness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on intentionally and forgetfully omitting the tasmiya during slaughter, and apply the principle that forgetfulness is excused, citing the Quranic proof and hadith.

Core Idea
Intentional omission of tasmiya makes meat carrion; forgetfulness is excused due to hardship removal.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling from the chapter of slaughter (dhabaih): the importance of saying 'Bismillah' (tasmiya) before slaughtering. The Hanafi school distinguishes sharply between intentional and forgetful omission, relying on both Quranic text and the prophetic principle of pardon for forgetfulness. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining the permissibility of meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the omission of tasmiya during slaughter. There are two scenarios: intentional and forgetful. State the ruling for each.
The intentional omission makes the animal carrion (maytah), forbidden. Forgetful omission makes it permissible.
2
Locate the dalil for intentional omission
The primary proof is Quran 6:121: 'And do not eat of that upon which the name of Allah has not been mentioned.' This general prohibition includes intentional omission.
The verse is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala, leaving room for specification.
3
Trace the exception for forgetfulness
The exception derives from the hadith: 'Allah has pardoned my ummah for mistakes, forgetfulness, and compulsion.' This specifies the general Quranic command.
Also the principle of removing hardship (raf' al-haraj) is invoked.
4
Note the Hanafi legal reasoning
The forgetful person is not addressed by the command at the moment of slaughter, so no sin or invalidation occurs. Intentionally omitting is a sin and invalidates the slaughter.
The ruling applies to both Muslims and dhimmis.
5
Recognize the ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) do not excuse forgetfulness, holding that the condition must be fulfilled consciously. The Hanafi position is based on ease and the explicit hadith.
The practical difference: Hanafis eat meat from a forgetful slaughterer; others may not.

It opens with the ruling statement: if the slaughterer intentionally leaves out the tasmiya, the animal becomes carrion (maytah) and is unlawful to eat. Then it states that if one forgets to say the tasmiya, the meat is permissible. The reasoning provided is that making it unlawful would cause significant hardship because forgetfulness is common, and the principle that hardship necessitates ease (al-haraj marfu') applies. Additionally, the forgetful person is not held accountable for what they forgot, as per the hadith: 'Indeed Allah has pardoned my ummah for mistakes, forgetfulness, and what they are forced to do.' Therefore, the forgetful person has not omitted an obligation that was incumbent upon them at the moment of slaughter, unlike the intentional omitting which is a sin and renders the meat invalid. This is because ritual slaughter must be a lawful act, and killing game in the Haram is prohibited, so it cannot effect lawful consumption. The commentator ends with 'End' (ah) before repeating the initial rulings with proofs.

Timing of Tasmiya in Slaughter and Hunting
Explains when the tasmiya must be said: at slaughter for domestic animals, at release/shooting for hunting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when the tasmiya must be said in slaughter and hunting, and apply the principle of al-maqdur to distinguish permissible and impermissible scenarios.

Core Idea
Tasmiya is required at the action within your control — slaughter for domestic animals, release/shoot for hunting.

This lesson examines a key ruling from al-Hidayah on the timing of the tasmiya (saying 'Bismillah') when slaughtering domestic animals and when hunting. The Hanafi school bases its position on the principle that the tasmiya must be attached to the action one has direct control over (al-maqdur), not the outcome which may be uncertain. Understanding this distinction is essential for determining the permissibility of meat in various scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts
The passage distinguishes between elective slaughter (dhabihah al-ikhtiyar) of domestic animals and hunting (sayd). Note that the ruling for tasmiya differs between them.
Elective slaughter is the normal killing of domestic animals; hunting includes using dogs, arrows, or falcons.
2
State the ruling for each
For slaughter, tasmiya is required at the moment of slaughter, and it is directed upon the animal. For hunting, tasmiya is required at the time of releasing the dog or shooting the arrow, and it is directed upon the tool.
3
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
The underlying principle is 'al-maqdur' — what is under your control. In slaughter, the act you control is the slaughter itself; in hunting, it is the release or shot, not the hitting of the game. Therefore, tasmiya is required at the act you can control.
4
Apply the examples
Work through the two examples: (1) If you lay down a sheep, say tasmiya, but then slaughter a different sheep, the meat is impermissible because the tasmiya was attached to the first sheep. (2) If you shoot at a deer with tasmiya but hit another animal, the meat is permissible because the tasmiya was attached to the shot, not the target.
The examples demonstrate that the principle works symmetrically for both contexts.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains the correct timing for the tasmiya (saying 'Bismillah') when slaughtering domestic animals and when hunting. The commentary quotes al-Hidayah, a major Hanafi work. It begins by stating the general rule: For 'elective' (ikhtiyari) slaughter of domestic animals, the tasmiya must be pronounced at the moment of the slaughter itself, and it is directed upon the animal being slaughtered. For hunting, the tasmiya is required at the time of releasing the hunting dog or shooting the arrow/weapon, and it is directed upon the tool (the arrow, gun, or the dog). The rationale (ta'lil) is that what the person has control over (maqdur) in the first case is the act of slaughter; in the second case, it is the act of releasing or shooting, but not the hitting (the actual injury to the game), which is beyond his control. Therefore, the tasmiya is required at the action he can control. Then it gives two practical examples: (1) If a person lays down a sheep, says 'Bismillah,' but then slaughters a different sheep with that same tasmiya (i.e., the intention was for the first but he slaughters another), the meat is not permissible— because the tasmiya was directed at the first sheep but the actual slaughter was of another. (2) If a person shoots at a specific game animal, says 'Bismillah,' but the arrow hits a different game, the meat is permissible— because the tasmiya was said at the time of shooting, which is the action he controls, and the hitting is incidental. The same rule applies to releasing a hunting dog: if he releases it with tasmiya but it catches a different animal than intended, it is permissible.

المقدور (al-maqdur): What is within the person's power and control.

Slaughter Area in the Throat vs. Upper Chest
Locating the valid area for legal slaughter with Hanafi proofs and internal differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the valid slaughter area and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Slaughter must be in the throat or labbah; cutting above the Adam's apple invalidates it.

The Hanafi school has two seemingly conflicting narrations on where to cut when slaughtering an animal. The earlier sources, al-Jami' al-Saghir and al-Mabsut, differ in their wording, leading to practical differences. In this lesson, we will examine these narrations and see how later authorities reconciled them to produce the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is dhabh (slaughter) of an animal. The dispute is about the valid location of the cut: must it be in the throat (halq) or can it be a wider area between the labbah (upper chest) and the jawbones?
2
Locate the two narrations
Find the two statements: one from al-Jami' al-Saghir (permissible in any part of the throat) and one from al-Mabsut (permissible between labbah and jawbones). Note the different implications.
Al-Jami' uses 'wa' (and) meaning 'or'; al-Mabsut cites a hadith.
3
Apply reconciliation by al-Nihayah
Understand that al-Nihayah treats al-Jami' as a restriction (taqyid) on the absolute (mutlaq) ruling of al-Mabsut. The wider area is limited to the throat only.
Al-Dhakhirah confirms that cutting above the windpipe (above the throat) invalidates.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
State the relied-upon position: slaughter must be in the throat area (from the Adam's apple to the beginning of the chest) or in the labbah (for camels). Cutting above the Adam's apple makes the animal not halal.
This is the view adopted by al-Nihayah and al-Dhakhirah.

The Permissible Days for Udhiyah: Ruling and Proof
Defines the permissible days for udhiyah with conditions and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three permissible days for udhiyah in the Hanafi school and explain the proof from the Companions' statements.

Core Idea
Udhiyah is valid only on three days: day of Nahr and the two following days.

The time for udhiyah is a matter of legal definition and has been established by the statements of the Companions. In this passage from al-Lubab, the author clarifies the number of days on which sacrifice is valid, along with a procedural condition regarding the Eid prayer on the first day. The Hanafi school's reliance on the Companions' words and the principle of taking the certain in conflicting reports form the basis of this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what the passage is ruling on (udhiyah) and what the ruling is: 'it is permissible in three days: the day of Nahr and two days after it.'
The ruling is about the validity condition, not the obligation level of the act.
2
Locate the proof
Find the evidence cited: the statements of Umar, Ali, and Ibn Abbas that 'The days of Nahr are three; the best of them is the first.'
This is a qawl sahabi, a source of law in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the proof's implication
The Companions explicitly limit the days to three. The Hanafi school takes this as the operative ruling, especially given the absence of a stronger opposing evidence.
The author mentions that there is contradiction in reports, so they took the certain (al-mutaqin), which is the least.
4
Understand the condition of Eid prayer
On the first day, the Eid prayer must precede slaughter if performed on time. If the Imam delays until noon, slaughter is permitted without prayer. On subsequent days, no prayer condition applies because the Imam's prayer is considered qada'.
The distinction between ada' (performance) and qada' (make-up) is key.
5
Apply the ruling
Practical consequence: sacrifice is valid on the 10th, 11th, and 12th of Dhul Hijjah. Slaughter at night is permissible but disliked.
After the 12th, udhiyah is not valid.

Compensating for destroying another's sacrificial meat requires paying its value and giving it in charity.

Navigating the Latter Chapters of the Hanafi Manual
Table of contents listing book chapters and page numbers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main chapters covered in the latter portion of the text and state the key sub-topics within each chapter.

Core Idea
The table of contents is your roadmap to Hanafi rulings on discretionary punishment, theft, highway robbery, intoxicants, and hunting.

Before diving into the detailed legal rulings on criminal and dietary matters, it is essential to understand how the author organizes these topics. This table of contents serves as a guide, listing chapter headings and their sub-topics. Knowing the structure helps you locate specific issues quickly and see the logical progression from punishment for crimes to rules on food and hunting.

Guided Path
1
Survey the major chapter titles
Identify the main 'كتاب' (book) headings. In this table, the chapters are: أحكام التعزير (Rulings of Discretionary Punishment), كتاب السرقة (Book of Theft), باب قطاع الطريق (Chapter of Highway Robbers), كتاب الأشربة (Book of Intoxicants), and كتاب الصيد والذبائح (Book of Hunting and Slaughtering).
Note that 'أحكام التعزير' is not explicitly labeled as a 'كتاب', but it is a major section preceding the others.
2
Note the sub-headings under each chapter
Each major chapter is followed by a list of sub-topics. For example, under 'كتاب السرقة', you see sub-topics like 'شروط القطع في السرقة', 'القدر الذي يُقْطَع فيه', 'اشتراط الحرز للقطع', etc. These indicate the specific rulings covered within that chapter.
Some sub-headings are indented or grouped; pay attention to the hierarchy.
3
Identify the flow of topics
The sequence moves from general criminal law (تعزير) to specific theft rules, then to highway robbery (an aggravated form), then to intoxicants, and finally to hunting and slaughtering. This logical order reflects a progression from crimes against individuals/property to dietary regulations.
Understanding this flow aids in contextualizing the rulings.
4
Recognize the mufta bihi note
Under 'كتاب الأشربة', the table includes a note: 'المفتى به تحريم النبيذ على قول الإمام محمد'. This indicates the relied-upon position for nabidh prohibition. Even in the index, substantive guidance is embedded.
Be alert for such authoritative statements within headings.
