Fasting Two Consecutive Months as Kafarah for Zihar
States the ruling and conditions for fasting kafarah when unable to free a slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid kafarah fast for zihar and identify the exceptions (Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, Tashriq) and the ruling if ability to free a slave arises during the fast.

Core Idea
The two-month fast for zihar must be consecutive and exclude Ramadan and prohibited fasting days; otherwise, it must be restarted.

In this lesson, we examine the rules of fasting two consecutive months as a substitute expiation when freeing a slave is not possible. The Hanafi school emphasizes strict consecutiveness and excludes days when fasting is obligatory (Ramadan) or prohibited (Eid and Tashriq). Understanding these conditions ensures the expiation is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is fasting two consecutive months as kafarah for zihar. Its ruling is wajib when the person is unable to free a slave.
This is the second tier of expiation (after freeing a slave).
2
Apply the condition of consecutiveness
The fast must be consecutive (mutatabi'ayn). Any break, even for a valid reason, invalidates the fast and requires restarting from the beginning.
The text states: 'due to the textual command.'
3
Identify excluded days
The two months cannot include Ramadan (because it is fard and cannot fulfill expiation), nor the days of Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, or the days of Tashriq (because fasting them is prohibited).
If such days occur during the two-month period, the fast is interrupted and must be restarted after those days.
4
Note special cases
If the person becomes able to free a slave during the fast (even on the last day), the obligation shifts to freeing the slave. The fast becomes supererogatory (nafl). The current day's fast should be completed as a recommended act.
This rule prevents combining the fast with later ability.
5
Determine the proper count
The two months are lunar months. If each month is 29 days, it counts as a month; otherwise, the total is 60 days. If one fasts by days and only achieves 59 days, the fast is invalid and must be restarted.
Lunar months vary; the minimum is 29 days per month.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement regarding the kafarah (expiation) for zihar when the husband cannot find a slave to free. The commentator expands on the matn of Al-Quduri. First, it clarifies that even if one needs a slave for service or to pay a debt, he is still considered 'able' and thus must free a slave, not fast. The fasting kafarah is two consecutive months, counted by lunar months; if each month is 29 days, it counts as a month, otherwise the total is 60 days. A special case: if one counts by days and fasts only 59 days, he must restart because consecutiveness is broken. However, if he fasts 29 days by the lunar month and then 30 days by days, it is valid. If during the fast he becomes able to free a slave (e.g., acquires wealth), even on the last day, freeing becomes obligatory and the fast is no longer sufficient; he should complete that day's fast as a recommended supererogatory act. The condition of consecutiveness is based on the Qur'anic text. Additionally, the month of Ramadan is excluded because fasting it is fard, and it cannot fulfill an expiatory vow. Similarly, days when fasting is prohibited (Eid and Tashriq) are excluded because they are invalid for expiation.

Key Terms
ظهار (zihar): A husband saying to his wife 'you are like my mother's back', making intercourse prohibited until expiation is performed.
كفارة (kafarah): Expiation for zihar: either freeing a slave, fasting two months, or feeding sixty poor people (in order of ability).
متتابعين (mutatabi'ayn): Consecutive days without interruption; if a break occurs, the fast must be restarted from the beginning.

Ruling on Breaking Fast During Two Months of Kafaara
States ruling and reason for restarting kafaara fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on breaking a kafaara fast and identify the exception for menstruation.

Core Idea
Even a single break invalidates the kafaara fast; only unavoidable menstruation is excused.

In this passage from al-Lubab, we learn a strict rule about the expiatory fast of two months: any break, even for a valid excuse, forces a restart. The exception is menstruation, which is beyond a woman's control. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the concept of tawatur (continuity) and its legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is fasting two consecutive months for kafaara. The ruling: if any day is broken, the entire series must be restarted.
2
Distinguish between excuses
Excuses like travel, illness, postpartum bleeding still require restart; menstruation does not.
Menstruation is treated differently because it is unavoidable.
3
Understand the reason
The loss of continuity (tawatur) is the cause for restart. Menstruation does not break continuity because it cannot be avoided.
Tawatur is a condition for validity.
4
Apply the exception
If menstruation occurs during the fast, it does not require restart; the woman continues after purification.
This is based on inability to avoid.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the matn with its commentary. It states that in the expiatory fast of two consecutive months (for zihar or other reasons), if one breaks the fast for even one day, whether with a valid excuse (like travel, illness, postpartum bleeding) or without an excuse, the entire series is invalidated and must be restarted. The exception is menstruation for women, because it is unavoidable and does not break continuity. The reason is the loss of required continuity (tawatur). The commentary also notes that this applies during the fast itself, and the ruling is confirmed in al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
تتابع (tawatur): Consecutive, uninterrupted sequence of fasting days; breaking it nullifies the validity of the expiation.
كفارة (kafaara): Expiation required for certain acts like zihar, consisting of freeing a slave, fasting two months, or feeding sixty poor.
