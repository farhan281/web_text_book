The Five Types of Killing in Hanafi Law
Introduction and classification of killing types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five types of killing recognized in Hanafi law and define intentional killing (al-ʿamd).

Core Idea
Hanafi law classifies killing into five types, each with distinct legal consequences.

In this lesson, we begin the study of Kitab al-Jinayat (Book of Crimes). The passage introduces the five-fold classification of killing that forms the basis for the laws of qisas, diyah, and kaffarah. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly applying the rulings that follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the book
The passage opens 'Kitab al-Jinayat.' Jinayat are transgressive acts against life or limb. Recognize that this classification covers both homicide and bodily harm.
The term includes intentional and unintentional harm.
2
List the five categories of killing
From the text, enumerate: ʿamd (intentional), shibh ʿamd (quasi-intentional), khaṭaʾ (mistaken), mā ujriya majrā al-khaṭaʾ (treated as mistake), and qatl bi-sabab (killing by cause). Memorize them in Arabic and English.
This is the standard Hanafi classification.
3
Define intentional killing (ʿamd)
According to the passage, ʿamd is defined as 'that in which one intentionally strikes another.' Focus on the element of intentional striking—not necessarily the intent to kill.
The full definition includes the means used; the passage introduces the core idea.
4
Analyze the practical significance of the classification
Each type triggers different legal consequences: qisas (retaliation) for ʿamd, diyah (blood money) for shibh ʿamd and some other categories, and sometimes kaffarah (expiation). Recognizing the type ensures correct rulings.
Later lessons will detail the rulings per category.
5
Note cross-madhhab variation
Other schools (e.g., Shafiʿi) may classify killing into three types (intentional, quasi-intentional, mistake) and treat killing by cause under one of these. The Hanafi five-fold distinction is unique.
Be aware of ikhtilaf but focus on Hanafi taxonomy.

Commentary
This passage introduces Kitab al-Jināyāt and enumerates the five types of killing recognized in Hanafi law. It begins with a linguistic note that 'jināyāt' is the plural of 'jināyah', meaning transgression, and in legal terminology it refers to transgressions against the person (life or limbs). The text then lists the five categories of killing that give rise to legal consequences: 1) عمد (ʿamad): intentional killing, where the perpetrator purposely strikes the victim with intent to kill or cause harm. 2) شبه عمد (shibh ʿamad): semi-intentional killing, where the perpetrator intentionally strikes but with no intent to kill, e.g., hitting with a light stick. 3) خطأ (khaṭaʾ): accidental killing, where there is no intent and the act itself is not intentional. 4) ما أُجري مجرى الخطأ (mā ujriya majrā al-khaṭaʾ): that which is treated as mistake, such as killing by mistake in the course of a permissible act. 5) القتل بسبب (al-qatl bi-sabab): killing by cause, e.g., digging a well that someone falls into. The text then begins to define ʿamad: it is where the striking is intentional. The full definitions continue in subsequent sections.

Key Terms
الجنايات (al-jināyāt): Plural of jināyah; in legal context, transgressive acts against life or limb.
العمد (al-ʿamd): Intentional killing: the perpetrator intentionally strikes with the intention to kill or do harm that typically causes death.
شبه العمد (shibh al-ʿamd): Semi-intentional killing: intentionally striking but without intent to kill, e.g., with a light object.
خطأ (khaṭaʾ): Accidental killing: no intent to strike or cause harm, e.g., shooting while hunting and hitting a person by mistake.
ما أُجري مجرى الخطأ (mā ujriya majrā al-khaṭaʾ): That which is treated as mistaken: an act where the perpetrator intentionally performs an act (e.g., shooting) but does not intend harm, and the death occurs unintentionally.
القتل بسبب (al-qatl bi-sabab): Killing by cause: an indirect killing where the perpetrator sets a cause (e.g., digging a well, placing a stone) that leads to someone's death.

Defining Quasi-Intentional Killing: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Defines shabah al-amd and presents intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's definitions of shabah al-amd and apply the Hanafi ruling on expiation and inheritance.

Core Idea
Quasi-intentional killing is defined by the tool, not the outcome — Abu Hanifa's view is the relied-upon position.

This passage from Al-Lubab defines quasi-intentional killing (shabah al-amd) and presents a crucial intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: does the definition hinge on the tool used or the likelihood of death? The answer determines whether the killing is subject to qisas or diyah, and whether expiation is required. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply Hanafi fiqh to homicide cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rulings for Shabah al-Amd
The text first states two rulings: no expiation (kaffarah) and deprivation of inheritance (hirman al-irth). Note the proof for inheritance deprivation: the hadith 'No inheritance for a killer.'
The kaffarah issue is based on the nature of shabah al-amd as a pure major sin, not requiring worship-like expiation.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Definition
Abu Hanifa defines shabah al-amd as intentionally striking with something that is not a weapon nor treated as a weapon. The focus is on the tool, not whether it usually kills.
This definition emphasizes the absence of lethal capacity in the tool, even if death occurs.
3
State the Sahibayn's Definition
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) give examples: striking with a large stone or a stick. Their implicit criterion is whether the tool is likely to cause death; if it is not likely, it is shabah.
The text is ambiguous: they only mention examples, but the commentary clarifies the underlying principle.
4
Analyse the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, a blow with a large stone that kills is shabah (diyah only, no qisas). Under Sahibayn, if the stone is considered deadly, it becomes amd (qisas possible).
This ikhtilaf affects blood money amounts and the possibility of retaliation.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's definition, as stated in al-Hidayah. The killer's intention and the tool's nature are key.
Students should understand that the school follows the tool-based definition.

Then it mentions that one of its rulings is that the killer is deprived of inheritance from the victim, based on the hadith: 'No inheritance for a killer.' The passage then defines shabah al-amd according to Abu Hanifa: it is when the killer intentionally strikes with something that is not a weapon nor treated as a weapon, regardless of whether the tool usually causes death. Then it presents the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn): they consider a blow with a large stone or a stick to be shabah al-amd, implying that they differentiate based on whether the tool is likely to kill—if it is not likely, it is shabah; if it is likely, it is intentional murder (amd). كفارة (kaffarah): Expiation: an act of worship to expiate sin, not prescribed for shabah al-amd.
حرمان الإرث (hirman al-irth): Deprivation of inheritance: the killer does not inherit from the victim.

Definition of Shubh al-Amd and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains the differing definitions of shubh al-amd between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shubh al-amd and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, identifying the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, shubh al-amd is a distinct category between intentional and mistaken homicide, but its definition is contested within the school.

This passage from al-Lubab defines shubh al-amd (semi-intentional homicide) and presents the differing views of Abu Hanifa and his two disciples. Understanding this dispute is crucial because it determines whether qisas applies or only diyah. The passage also clarifies the operative (mufta bihi) position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Shubh al-amd is a type of homicide; first, grasp the basic definition: striking with intent to hit but not to kill, using an instrument not designed to kill.
It is a distinct category between 'amd (intentional) and khata' (accidental).
2
State Abu Hanifa's definition
Abu Hanifa holds that shubh al-amd is any strike with an instrument that is not a weapon nor treated as a weapon, regardless of whether it is likely to kill or not.
The key is the instrument's nature, not lethality.
3
State the Sahibayn's definition
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that if the instrument is likely to kill (ghalabah al-qatl), the act is 'amd, not shubh. For them, shubh al-amd is limited to instruments that do not usually kill.
They focus on the consequence to infer intent.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the perpetrator is not subject to qisas but pays heavy diyah; under the Sahibayn, if the instrument is likely to kill, qisas applies as for 'amd.
This can be a matter of life or death.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's, as chosen by Imam al-Isbijabi, al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and others.
In practice, the definition of shubh al-amd follows Abu Hanifa's view.

Shibh al-'amd: Definition and Hanafi Rulings
defines shibh al-'amd and states its rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shibh al-'amd, distinguish it from 'amd based on the instrument, and state its rulings (no qisas, diyah mughallazah on 'aqilah, sin and kaffarah) while identifying the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Shibh al-'amd: intentional strike with non-lethal instrument; no qisas, heavy diyah on 'aqilah, and the killer owes sin and expiation.

This passage from Al-Labab Sharh al-Quduri sharpens the line between intentional murder ('amd) and quasi-intentional killing (shibh al-'amd). The test is the instrument: if it normally kills, it's 'amd; otherwise, it's shibh al-'amd. Getting this distinction right changes everything—qisas or not, who pays the diyah, and whether the killer must expiate. The passage also records a settled Hanafi position preferred by later authorities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Locate the exact wording defining shibh al-'amd: أن يتعمد ضربه بما لا يقتل به غالباً . Note that the key element is the instrument—something that does not normally cause death.
Compare with the definition of 'amd given earlier ('عظيمة مما يقتل به غالباً') to see the contrast.
2
Extract the legal consequences
The passage lists four consequences: (1) no qisas, (2) sin (mawathim) on the killer, (3) kaffarah (expiation) on the killer, and (4) diyah mughallazah (heavy blood-money) on the 'aqilah.
The passage says 'لا قود فيه' and 'فيه دية مغلظة على العاقلة'.
3
Understand the rationale for no qisas
Qisas is only for 'amd (intentional murder). Shibh al-'amd is not 'amd because the instrument is not normally lethal, so it does not meet the strict criteria for qisas.
The killer's intent to strike is present, but not the intent to kill (since the instrument is unlikely to kill).
4
Note the intra-madhhab tarjih
The commentary by al-Ispijabi states that the correct view is Imam Abu Hanifa's, and al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and others chose it. This is the mufta bihi (fatwa is upon it).
This is a typical Hanafi practice: the later scholars confirm the relied-upon position.
5
Apply the rule to a hypothetical
Imagine a person strikes another with a small pebble, causing death. Since the pebble does not normally kill, the case is shibh al-'amd. No qisas; the 'aqilah must pay diyah mughallazah; the killer must repent (sin) and perform kaffarah.
Contrast with a sword strike, which is 'amd because a sword normally kills.

Accidental Killing in Hanafi Fiqh: Two Types and Their Legal Consequences
Defines accidental killing, its two types, and states the principle of diyah on 'aqilah and inheritance deprivation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between error in intent and error in action in accidental killing, and state the Hanafi ruling on diyah liability and inheritance deprivation.

Core Idea
In accidental killing, diyah falls on the 'aqilah, and the killer forfeits inheritance — both derived by analogy to the basic case of khata'.

This passage from Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, commented by Al-Maydani, introduces the two forms of accidental killing (khata') recognized in Hanafi law. You will learn to distinguish error in intent from error in action, and understand the principle that governs blood-money liability and inheritance deprivation. The text also touches on the effect of doubt (shubhah) on qisas versus inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of khata'
Read the definitions: error in intent (khata' fi al-qasd) is when the killer mistakes the identity of the victim, thinking they are permissible target (e.g., game). Error in action (khata' fi al-fi'l) is when the killer intends a permissible act but inadvertently hits a human.
The key difference is what the killer mistakenly believes: the victim's identity vs. the act's consequence.
2
Extract the general principle
The text states that any diyah arising from the killing itself (not from a later settlement) is upon the 'aqilah (paternal male relatives). This is justified by analogy (qiyas) to the basic category of accidental killing.
The Arabic phrase 'اعتباراً بالخطأ' signals the qiyas reasoning.
3
Note the additional ruling on inheritance
The killer is deprived of inheritance from the victim. The text explains that doubt (shubhah) can remove qisas but does not remove inheritance deprivation.
This is a strict rule to deter killing, even accidental.
4
Recognize the exception regarding settlement
If the heirs accept diyah as a settlement (sulh) instead of qisas, the diyah is paid from the killer's own property, not the 'aqilah.
The footnote clarifies that this is a later transaction, not a diyah arising from the killing itself.

This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies a key Hanafi principle: qisas is not limited by superficial disparities between victim and perpetrator. Learning Objective

Core Idea
A father is not killed for his son because he is the cause of his son's existence, and the hadith explicitly exempts him.

This passage establishes a key exception in qisas law. It begins with the ruling that a man is not killed for his son, supported by both a hadith and a rational argument. The exemption is then extended to all ascendants, including grandfathers and grandmothers, and the passage also notes that the son is killed for the father, as no exemption applies in that direction.

The Hanafi school uses qiyas to analogy to two creditors in an estate, establishing equality in entitlement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is one man cutting the right hands of two men. The ruling: both victims (or their heirs) have a joint right to qisas and diya. State the hukm level: wajib? The text implies the right is established (thubut) but not that execution is wajib; it's a right.
The hukm level here is about entitlement, not obligation.
2
Understand the qiyas reasoning
The author analogizes to two creditors in an estate. The illa (effective cause) is equality in cause of entitlement. Identify the asl (creditors in estate) and its hukm (they share proportionally). Then apply to the far' (two victims of one hand cut).
The qiyas is khafi (concealed) because the illa is not textual but derived from similarity in cause.
3
Analyze the two scenarios
Scenario 1: Both victims appear. They may cut the offender's hand together and also take half diya, splitting it equally. Scenario 2: Only one appears. He may cut the hand, but then must pay half diya to the absent victim because the absent victim's right to qisas converts to diya.
The absent victim's right does not extinguish without compensation or pardon.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Other schools may not allow combining qisas and diya in this manner. For example, some might require the victims to choose either qisas or diya, not both. So the practical difference is the joint right to both.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is based on qiyas.

Commentary
This passage deals with multiple victims of the same hand cut by one offender. Since the offender cut the right hands of two men, he owes two hands in qisas but has only one hand to give. This is analogous to two creditors claiming against a single estate. If only one victim appears, he may cut the offender's hand, but then he must pay half diya to the absent victim because the absent victim's right to qisas is lost due to the hand being used, so it converts to diya. The absent victim's right is not automatically forfeited; it only extinguishes by compensation (diya) or pardon.

Shared Liability for One Perpetrator Cutting Two Right Hands
Ruling on shared liability with qiyas to creditors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one perpetrator cuts off the right hands of two victims and apply the qiyas to creditors in an estate to explain equal sharing.

Core Idea
Equal cause of entitlement leads to equal sharing, like creditors dividing an insufficient estate.

This passage addresses a scenario in jinayat (injuries) where a single perpetrator maims two victims in the same type of injury. The ruling illustrates a key principle: equal rights yield equal shares, whether in exacting retaliation or receiving blood money. The author draws an analogy to creditors in an insolvent estate to justify the equal division.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the two scenarios
Identify the basic facts: one person cuts off the right hands of two victims. There are two scenarios: (1) both victims are present, (2) only one is present.
2
Identify the ruling for both present
If both are present, they may cut off the perpetrator's hand (qisas) and take half the diyah, sharing it equally. Alternatively, they may take only the diyah.
The half diyah is for one hand, not two.
3
Trace the qiyas
The ruling is based on qiyas: the asl (original case) is creditors sharing an insufficient estate; the far (new case) is the two victims. The illa (effective cause) is equality in the cause of entitlement.
Asl: creditors, far: victims, illa: equal right to recovery.
4
Apply qiyas to the second scenario
If only one victim is present and cuts off the hand, the other victim is entitled to half the diyah from the present victim, because the present one's action eliminates the possibility of qisas for the absent one.
The right does not fall except with compensation (diyah) or pardon.

Slave's Confession to Intentional Murder: Validity and Rationale
Ruling on slave's confession to intentional murder with rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's confession to intentional murder and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) that distinguishes it from financial confessions.

Core Idea
A slave's confession to intentional murder is valid because he would not falsely admit to self-harm.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Hanafi fiqh regarding the legal capacity of slaves in criminal matters. The source passage from al-Lubab sharh al-Quduri states that when a slave confesses to intentional murder, qisas becomes binding. The commentator provides a rational proof: there is no suspicion (tuhma) that a person would confess to a punishment that harms only himself. This principle also explains why the same confession is not accepted for financial obligations, where coercion by the master is possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is a slave's confession to intentional murder. The ruling: qisas (retaliatory execution) becomes obligatory (wajib) on the slave.
The ruling is immediate and binding upon confession, without need for witnesses.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The text provides a rational proof (dalil aqli): 'because there is no suspicion in his confession to punishment upon himself'.
This is a self-evident principle: one would not knowingly subject oneself to harm unless truthful.
3
Understand the rationale (wajh al-dalalah)
The absence of tuhma (suspicion of falsehood) makes the confession trustworthy. The slave has no motive to falsely confess to a corporal penalty that he alone will suffer.
This applies only to corporal punishments, not to financial obligations where the master may coerce the slave.
4
Note the contrast with financial confessions
The text adds 'unlike money' — a slave's confession to a financial debt is not accepted because of potential coercion by the master (tuhma).
This distinction is unique to Hanafi fiqh and based on the concept of tuhma.
5
Recognize the Hanafi position and its authority
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is the mufta bihi position. It is mukhtalaf fih: other schools may accept financial confessions from slaves.
The Hanafi distinction relies on rational analysis of tuhma rather than blanket rejection.
