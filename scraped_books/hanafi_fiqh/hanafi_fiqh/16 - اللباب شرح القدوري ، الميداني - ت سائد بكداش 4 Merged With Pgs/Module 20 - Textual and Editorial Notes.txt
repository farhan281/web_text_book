Ruling on Blood-Money from Diwan Stipends in Three Years
Explains the ruling for when the killer is from the diwan, with proof from 'Umar's practice and consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the source of blood-money for a diwan member and explain the proof from 'Umar's practice and Companion consensus.

Core Idea
Blood-money follows the supporters, not the tribe; diwan replaces tribe.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling from the Book of 'Aqila: if the killer belongs to the diwan (military register), his blood-money is paid by the diwan members from their stipends over three years. The passage presents the Hanafi position, its proof from 'Umar's practice and Companion consensus, and a rebuttal of the Shafi'i objection that this abrogates earlier practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
Read the opening: 'If the killer is from the people of the diwan'. Identify the ruling: the blood-money is on the diwan.
The diwan is the military register established by 'Umar.
2
Locate the proof
The passage cites 'Umar's practice of placing blood-money on diwan members in the presence of Companions with no objection – this is ijma sukuti.
Ijma sukuti is a valid proof in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand the Hanafi response to abrogation claim
The author explicitly states that this is not naskh but taqrir ma'na – continuation of the underlying principle that blood-money falls on supporters.
Supporters were once tribe; now they are diwan.
4
Note the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi – diwan pays from stipends over 3 years; Shafi'i – 'ashirah pays immediately.
This difference arises from different interpretations of the dalil.
5
Apply the extension to crafts
The passage notes that if support is through a craft guild, their 'aqila is the people of that craft, following the same meaning.
This shows the principle is adaptable to changing social structures.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement and proof from the Book of 'Aqila. The author first repeats the matn: 'If the killer is from the people of the diwan' (the military register). Then he explains that the blood-money ('aql) is to be paid by the diwan. The proof is the practice of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), who, when he established the diwans, assigned the blood-money to the diwan members, and this was done in the presence of the Companions without anyone objecting, thus establishing consensus (ijma). The author then addresses the objection of Imam al-Shafi'i, who might claim that this practice abrogates the earlier practice of blood-money being on the 'ashirah (tribe). The Hanafi response is clear: this is not abrogation (naskh), but rather a continuation of the underlying meaning (taqrir ma'na). The original principle was that blood-money falls on those who provide support (ahl al-nusrah). That support was once through kinship, alliances, etc., but in 'Umar's time, the system of support had shifted to the diwan, so he followed the meaning and assigned it accordingly. The passage then states that the blood-money is taken from their stipends over three years. The author further explains that if a community today relies on a craft for mutual support, their 'aqila is the people of that craft, as stated in al-Hidayah. Finally, he defines 'ata' (annual or semi-annual stipend) and rizq (monthly or daily payment), and explains that the blood-money is taken from 'ata' rather than capital because it is lighter, which is the wisdom behind the 'aqila system.

Key Terms
العاقلة (al-‘āqila): The group responsible for paying blood-money on behalf of the killer, which in this passage is the diwan members.
العطاء (al-‘aṭā’): The annual or semi-annual stipend given to soldiers from the treasury.
الديوان (al-dīwān): The official register or department listing soldiers and their stipends, established by 'Umar.

Correcting the Annual Diyah Payment in Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar
Author corrects a manuscript error in Al-Quduri's text regarding annual diyah payment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct annual diyah payment amount according to Al-Quduri and explain how a manuscript omission caused a misinterpretation.

Core Idea
The annual diyah payment is one dirham and two daniqs — a manuscript omission once misled scholars.

This passage from al-Maydani's sharh corrects a common mistake. Many later scholars relied on an incomplete copy of Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, missing the phrase 'درهم ودانقان'. As a result, some mistakenly thought there was a discrepancy between Imam Muhammad's narration and Al-Quduri's text. The author shows that with the full text, both sources agree.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defective copy
The author of al-Hidayah used an incomplete copy of Mukhtasar al-Quduri that omitted 'درهم ودانقان'.
2
Understand the resulting error
Without that phrase, the annual amount appeared to be just 1 dirham, or 1 1/3 dirhams, causing confusion about the total.
3
Locate the correct text
Al-Quduri's full text says: 'لا يزاد الواحد على أربعة دراهم : في كل سنة ، درهم ودانقان' — one is not increased above four dirhams: in each year, a dirham and two daniqs.
4
Confirm the resolution
This matches Imam Muhammad's narration, so no contradiction exists.

The Sign of Training for a Hunting Bird vs. Dog
Defines the sign of training for hunting birds and dogs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a trained hunting bird and distinguish it from the sign of a trained dog.

Core Idea
Training for a bird is shown by returning when called; for a dog by refraining from eating.

The permissibility of hunting with animals depends on their training. This lesson explains how the Hanafi school defines training for birds of prey — a sign that differs from dogs due to their natural disposition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the animal type and its natural disposition
Recognize that a falcon (bāzī) is naturally wild and averse to humans, while a dog is accustomed to plundering. This difference determines the sign of training.
2
Identify the sign for a bird
For a bird, the sign of training is that it returns when called. Since its nature is to flee, obedience to the call indicates it has been trained.
3
Identify the sign for a dog
For a dog, the sign of training is that it refrains from eating the game. Since its nature is to eat what it catches, leaving the prey indicates training.
4
Apply the underlying principle
Training is defined as the animal abandoning what is customary for its species. Both signs reflect this principle adapted to each animal's nature.
5
Conclude the legal effect
Training is a condition (shart) for the permissibility of hunting. Without these signs, the hunt is not valid and the game is considered carrion (haram).

The Number of Testings: Abu Hanifa vs. the Two Companions
Records the intra-Hanafi dispute on the number of testings for training

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the number of testings required to confirm training, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa relied on the hunter's dominant opinion; the two companions required three testings for legal certainty.

While the definition of training is clear, Hanafi scholars differed on how many times an animal must demonstrate its training. This lesson explores the intra-madhhab dispute and its practical consequences for the hunter.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) require three testings to confirm training. Abu Hanifa holds that training is confirmed by the hunter's dominant opinion, without a fixed number.
2
Understand the rationale
The Sahibayn seek legal certainty and standardization; three is considered sufficient for certainty. Abu Hanifa relies on custom and probability, allowing flexibility based on the hunter's confidence.
3
Note the transmission from Abu Hanifa
The text mentions that the condition of three is also a narration from Abu Hanifa himself, indicating that there are two views attributed to him.
4
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is the view of the Sahibayn (three testings), as followed in later works like al-Kanz, al-Nuqaya, and al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.
5
Apply to a scenario
If a falcon returns only twice, according to the Sahibayn it is not yet considered trained; according to Abu Hanifa, if the hunter is confident it is trained, it is permissible. The mufta bihi requires a third test.

Grammatical Analysis of Homonomous Words in Fiqh Texts
Textual commentary on a homonymous phrase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between two homonymous words in a fiqh text and explain their different grammatical functions and meanings.

Core Idea
Two identical words can have different meanings based on grammatical context.

This brief passage from the Hidayah commentary highlights a rhetorical device known as tajnīs, where two words are homophonous but not synonymous. Understanding such linguistic nuances is crucial for correctly interpreting fiqh texts, especially when a single phrase carries multiple legal implications. In this lesson, we will dissect the two occurrences of 'kullihi' to see how one functions as an imperative verb and the other as an emphatic confirmation (tawkīd ma'nawī).

The second 'kullihi' is a noun construction used as a tawkīd ma'nawī (emphatic confirmation). It means 'all of it' and reinforces the word that follows.
In Arabic grammar, tawkīd ma'nawī uses words like kull, nafs, 'ayn to remove doubt.
4
Classify the rhetorical device
This use of two identical words with different meanings is called tajnīs (also known as paronomasia). It is considered a mark of eloquence in Arabic speech and writing.
Such devices are studied in balāghah (rhetoric) and are common in classical texts.

Commentary
This passage is a textual note from Hidayah, commenting on a response using two identical word forms (kullihi) with distinct grammatical functions: one is a verb (imperative 'eat it') and the other is a nominal construction (emphatic 'all of it'). Al-Binayah explains that the first is an imperative derived from 'akala, with the pronoun referring to locusts, while the second is a tawkid (emphatic confirmation) of the subsequent word. This rhetorical device (tajnīs) is considered a mark of eloquence.
