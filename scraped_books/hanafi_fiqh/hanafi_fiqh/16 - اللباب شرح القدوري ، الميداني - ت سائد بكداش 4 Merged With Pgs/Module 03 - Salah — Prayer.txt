Duration of Breastfeeding: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute with scriptural and analogical proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the maximum breastfeeding period, identify their proofs, and explain why the Sahibayn's view is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school's relied-upon position is 2 years, not 30 months.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute over the maximum breastfeeding period that establishes legal prohibition. Abu Hanifa held 30 months based on a Quranic verse, while the Sahibayn argued for 2 years based on the minimum pregnancy duration. Later authorities favoured the Sahibayn's view for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Identify the ruling from Abu Hanifa (30 months) and the Sahibayn (2 years).
Both views are transmitted in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
He uses verse 46:15, interpreting 'thirty months' as applying fully to both pregnancy and weaning. The debt analogy supports each getting the full term.
Reduction in pregnancy (actual duration) does not reduce weaning.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
Minimum pregnancy is 6 months, so the weaning period (al-fisal) is the remainder: 24 months.
This is based on the same verse but a different allocation.
4
Examine the tarjih (preference)
Later authorities (al-Fath, al-Tashih, al-Tahawi) declare the Sahibayn's view as the most correct (asahh) and mufta bihi.
The text in Sharh al-Hidayah answers objections to the 2-year limit.
5
Determine the operative ruling
For fatwa, the relied-upon position is 2 years. Breastfeeding beyond 2 years does not create mahram prohibition.
This is the case despite Abu Hanifa's original view.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the maximum duration of breastfeeding. The base text states that according to Abu Hanifa, the period is thirty months, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold it to be two years. The commentary, drawn from al-Hidayah and other sources, explains the reasoning: Abu Hanifa derives his view from the verse in Surah al-Ahqaf where Allah mentions pregnancy and weaning together with a total of thirty months. He argues that each (pregnancy and weaning) gets the full period, like two debts with a single term. However, since pregnancy is known to be at most nine months, the reduction applies to pregnancy, leaving weaning (breastfeeding) with the remainder up to thirty months. The Sahibayn argue that the minimum pregnancy is six months, so after deducting that, the weaning period is two years (24 months). Later Hanafi authorities such as al-Fath and al-Tashih consider the Sahibayn's view as the most correct (asahh) and the basis for fatwa, following al-Tahawi's choice. Thus, the relied-upon position in the school is that the breastfeeding period is two years, not thirty months.

الفصال (al-fisal): Weaning; the period of breastfeeding after infancy.
الحولان (al-hawlan): Two years; the period of weaning according to the Sahibayn.

Conditions for Amputation of the Thief
States the ruling of cutting for theft and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five conditions required for amputation of a thief's hand and explain the rationale for each condition.

Core Idea
Amputation is only wajib when the thief is sane, adult, speaking, sighted, and steals at least ten dirhams.

This lesson examines the ruling on amputation for theft from the Hanafi manual al-Quduri with the commentary of al-Maydani. The passage establishes the obligation of cutting and then lists the conditions based on the Quranic verse and the Prophetic practice. Understanding these conditions is crucial to apply the hadd correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage begins by stating that cutting becomes obligatory upon the thief. This is a hadd punishment.
2
Locate the primary proof
The Quranic verse (5:38) is cited as the basis. The imperative 'faqta'u' indicates obligation.
Hanafi usul: al-amr yufidu al-wujub.
3
List the conditions from the text
The passage explicitly requires sanity, puberty, speaking ability, sight, and nisab of ten dirhams.
These are external prerequisites for the penalty.
4
Analyze the rationale for each condition
Why are mute and blind excluded? Because of shubha (doubt) that could waive the hadd. Mute may have an excuse, blind may be confused.
Shubha is a key concept in Hanafi criminal law.
5
Trace the nisab proof from the Sunnah
The value of ten dirhams is derived from the shield (mijann) for which the Prophet cut the thief's hand.
This is an example of the Sunnah clarifying the ambiguous Quranic text.

The Requirement of Minted Dirhams for Amputation
Intra-Hanafi dispute: condition of stolen dirhams being minted for amputation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on minted dirhams for the hadd of amputation and distinguish the zahir al-riwayah from the rare narration.

Core Idea
For amputation, the Hanafi school requires minted dirhams; unminted silver does not trigger the hadd.

This passage addresses a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute about a condition for the hadd of amputation in theft. While the matn of al-Quduri generalizes to include unminted silver, the established transmission (zahir al-riwayah) insists on minted coins. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the severe punishment of amputation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Note that the matn says 'minted or unminted' (riwayat al-Hasan from Abu Hanifa), but the dominant transmission (zahir al-riwayah) requires minted, as held by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not a cross-madhhab one.
2
Trace the authority weight
Distinguish between 'riwayat al-Hasan' (nawadir, rare) and 'zahir al-riwayah' (established, more authoritative). The latter carries greater weight in fatwa.
Zahir al-riwayah is the relied-upon category in the Hanafi school.
3
Examine the dalil for the dominant position
Custom (urf) applies the term 'dirham' only to minted coins. The hadith 'no cutting except for a dinar or ten dirhams' is understood in that customary sense.
The hadith is cited to support the restriction.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if unminted silver of nisab value is stolen, the hand is not cut according to the relied-upon position, but would be cut according to the rare narration.
This shows how the choice of position affects the actual ruling.
5
Apply the tarjih
The position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (minted required) is deemed al-asahh (most correct) and is the mufta bihi. The reason includes 'kamal al-jinaya' (complete criminality).
Tarjih is based on stronger evidence and authority.

Conditions for Hunting with Trained Animals
States the conditions for permissible hunting and the prohibition if the animal eats from the prey.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for lawful hunting with trained animals and identify when the prey becomes haram due to the animal eating from it.

Core Idea
Trained animals must wound and not eat from prey; otherwise the game is haram.

Hunting with trained animals is a legitimate means of acquiring food, but it is governed by precise conditions that ensure the animal is truly trained and the hunt is conducted properly. This passage from al-Lubab clarifies the key requirements and explains why an animal eating from the kill invalidates the catch and indicates a lack of training.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is sending a trained animal (dog, falcon, etc.) to hunt. The ruling: the game is halal if conditions are met.
The default for hunting is permissibility, but it becomes conditional on fulfillment of shurut.
2
List the conditions (shurut)
Four conditions: (1) Animal must be trained (mu'allam). (2) Hunter must mention Allah's name at release (or forget, but not intentionally omit). (3) Animal must wound the prey (jarh). (4) Animal must not eat from the prey.
Wounding is essential: killing without wounding renders the prey haram in Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Identify what invalidates (mufsidat)
If the animal eats from the prey, that prey becomes haram. Additionally, subsequent catches by that animal are also haram until it is retrained.
Eating indicates the animal acted for itself, not for the hunter (a sign of incomplete training).
4
Note the exception for prior catches
If the animal had caught game before eating, those earlier catches are not retroactively made haram because the game has already been consumed — there is no subject (mahall) on which to apply the ruling.
This shows the Hanafi precision: rulings apply only to existing subjects.

Hunting with Mi‘rad and Bunduqah – Rulings and Principles
Explains rulings for mi‘rad, bunduqah, and the fiqhi principle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for hunting with a mi‘rad and bunduqah, distinguish between killing by wound and by weight, and apply the fiqhi principle for conflicting causes.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a hunted animal is only halal if killed by a wound that cuts; if killed by weight or crushing, it is haram.

This lesson explains the Hanafi rulings for two specific hunting tools: the mi‘rad (a featherless arrow or pointed stick) and the bunduqah (a clay pellet). The key distinction is whether the projectile wounds by cutting or kills by weight. We also derive a broader fiqhi principle used to resolve conflicts between causes of prohibition and permissibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the projectiles and their rulings
The scholar explains that a mi‘rad hitting with its side does not make game permissible, but hitting with its edge and wounding does. A bunduqah (pellet) and similar stones are never permissible because they crush without cutting.
2
Understand the reason for the distinction
The key is whether the projectile wounds (causes blood to flow) or kills by its weight. Only wounding constitutes proper slaughter (tadhkiyah).
3
Extract the fiqhi principle (dabit)
When causes of prohibition and permissibility conflict, if it is possible to avoid the prohibition, give it precedence; if not, treat the existence as non-existence.
From al-Hidayah
4
Apply the principle to the ruling on cutting a limb
If a shot cuts off a limb, the game is halal because the wound is certain, but the detached limb is not eaten because it died separately without proper slaughter.
5
Identify the proof (hadith)
The ruling on not eating the cut limb is based on a Prophetic statement (quoted incompletely in the passage).

Game belongs to the one who takes it by killing, not merely wounding.

Inkhan (weakening) is the threshold.

Second Hunter Kills Incapacitated Game
Ruling and reasoning on second hunter's kill after first incapacitates game.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a second hunter kills game already incapacitated by the first, explain the requirement for choice slaughter, and compute the compensation due.

Core Idea
An incapacitated game belongs to the first hunter and must be slaughtered properly; shooting it makes it carrion and requires compensation.

In the previous lesson we learned that if the first hunter's shot is non-incapacitating, the second hunter's kill is valid. Here we examine the opposite: when the first severely wounds the game so it can no longer escape, the second's shot is invalid. The Hanafi school combines rules of ownership and slaughter: the first hunter owns the game by effective injury, and the game must be slaughtered by choice. The second's shot destroys property without proper slaughter, rendering the meat unlawful and requiring compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Contrast the case where the first shot does not incapacitate (game can escape) versus where it does incapacitate (athkhanahu). In the first, the second's kill is valid; in the second, it is invalid.
The key distinction is whether the game was effectively captured.
2
Determine the ruling on the meat
The meat is not halal because the game was under control and required choice slaughter (cutting the throat). The second's shot killed it without proper slaughter, making it carrion.
This applies even if the first wound would not have been fatal.
3
Establish ownership
The first hunter becomes owner of the game by the incapacitating shot (ramy munkhin). The second destroyed property belonging to another, so he must compensate.
Ownership transfers upon effective wounding, not upon killing.
4
Calculate compensation
The second pays the value of the game at the time of destruction (i.e., after the first's wound) minus the devaluation caused by the first's wound. This prevents double recovery.
Value assessed on the day the second shot kills the animal.
5
Apply the principle of dhakāt al-ikhtiyār
When an animal is under control (captive), slaughter by cutting is obligatory. Shooting is not a valid substitute, even if it causes a swift death.
This distinguishes hunting (dharūra) from domestic slaughter (ikhtiyār).

Beginning of the time of udhiyah: The time of udhiyah for the people of major cities (amsar) and villages (qura) enters with the rising of dawn on the day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah), except that it is not permissible for the people of major cities to slaughter on the first day until the Imam prays the Eid prayer, or its time passes by noon, because in their case it is required to precede the Eid prayer over the udhiyah, or the exit of its time. So if neither exists, the udhiyah is not valid due to the absence of the condition. As for the people of as-sawad, i.e. villages, they slaughter after dawn due to the existence of time and the absence of the condition of prayer, because there is no prayer upon them. And what some have expressed that its earliest time is after the Eid prayer if one slaughters in a major city, and after dawn if in other than that: Al-Qahistani said: There is laxity in that, for udhiyah is an act of worship whose time does not differ between the major city and other than it. Rather its condition [differs]. So its earliest time in the right of the city dweller and the villager is dawn, except that it is required for the city dweller to precede the prayer upon it. So non-permissibility is due to the absence of the condition, not the absence of time, as in al-Mabsut. And to that reference is made in al-Hidayah and others. End. Then the consideration is the place of the udhiyah. So if it is in the village and the one sacrificing is in the city, it is permissible when dawn breaks; and in the opposite case, it is not permissible except after the prayer. (Hidayah).

Commentary
This passage addresses the timing of udhiyah. The base ruling states that the time for udhiyah begins at fajr on the day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah). However, for residents of major cities (amsar, such as Baghdad or Kufa), it is not permissible to slaughter until the Imam has performed the Eid prayer or until noon when the prayer time ends. This is because the prayer is a condition (shart) in their case. For villagers (ahl as-sawad), they may slaughter immediately after fajr since there is no Eid prayer obligation on them. The commentator then refines a misconception: some scholars said the earliest time differs by location (after prayer for city, after fajr for village). Al-Qahistani corrects this, citing al-Mabsut: the time is the same (fajr) for all; the difference is a condition (the prayer) for city dwellers. Therefore, non-permissibility in the city is due to missing the condition, not because the time hasn't entered. Finally, the passage notes that the determining factor is the place where the animal is sacrificed, not the place of the person. If the animal is in a village and the owner is in the city, it is permissible at fajr; conversely, if the animal is in the city and the owner is in the village, it is not permissible until after the Eid prayer.

Key Terms
أمصار (amsar): Major cities where communal Eid prayer is established
سواد (sawad): Villages or rural areas, originally meaning black land (agricultural)
شرط (shart): Condition: the Eid prayer is a shart for the validity of udhiyah for city dwellers
