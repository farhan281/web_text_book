Guardianship for a Woman Without a Lineage Guardian: Freed Slave and Absent Guardian
Rulings on a woman without a lineage guardian: freed slave guardian and absent guardian.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios where a woman without a lineage 'asabah guardian can be given in marriage validly, and identify who acts as her guardian in each case.

Core Idea
A woman with no 'asabah relative is given in marriage by her freed master or, if the nearest wali is unreachable, by the next relative.

In Hanafi marriage law, the guardian (wali) is essential for a valid contract. But what happens when a woman has no 'asabah relative from her lineage? This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses two specific cases: when her former master (who freed her) can act as guardian, and when the nearest guardian is absent with no possibility of communication. Understanding these rules ensures that no woman is left without a valid marriage option.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two distinct situations: (a) a woman with no lineage 'asabah but with a former master, and (b) a woman whose nearest guardian is absent with a disconnected absence (ghaybah munqati'ah).
Focus on the condition 'غيبة منقطعة'.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For (a): the freed master gives her in marriage, it is valid. For (b): the next closest relative gives her in marriage, not the sultan.
The freed master is an 'asabah through cause (emancipation).
3
Note the reasoning
The freed master is an 'asabah through cause, and he is the last of the 'asabah. For the absent guardian, it is in her best interest not to wait aimlessly, so the next relative takes precedence over the sultan.
This is a wilayah nazariyyah (guardianship based on consideration of interests).
4
Recognize the hadith evidence
The hadith 'The sultan is the guardian of the one who has no guardian' is cited, but in these scenarios the sultan is not the first option if a relative (through emancipation or distant blood) exists.
The hadith is used to justify the sultan's role only when no one else qualifies.

The reasoning is that this guardianship is based on considering the best interest of the woman (wilayah nazariyyah), and it would not be in her interest to wait idly for an unreachable guardian.

Guardianship of the Former Master for a Freed Woman with No Agnatic Wali
Ruling with proof: former master as wali for freed woman

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A former master becomes a guardian by causation, ranking last among agnates.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar establishes that a freed slave woman without agnatic relatives may be given in marriage by her former master. Although the master is not a blood relative, the act of emancipation creates a legal cause (sabab) that places him in the hierarchy of guardians. This ruling is supported by the general principle that a guardian is required for a valid marriage, as proven by the Prophet's hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Determine the specific situation described: a woman who has been freed and has no agnatic guardian (wali) from her lineage.
The phrase 'man lā wali la-hā' refers to absence of agnatic guardians by blood.
2
State the ruling (hukm)
What is the legal ruling for this scenario? The text says 'jāz' (permissible), which in context means the marriage is valid (sahih).
Jāz often implies validity in fiqh rulings.
3
Identify the proof (dalīl)
The ruling is supported by the hadith: 'Any woman who marries without a guardian, her marriage is invalid; if they dispute, the ruler is the guardian of the one who has no guardian.' This establishes the necessity of a guardian.
The hadith is hasan according to al-Tirmidhi.
4
Explain the reasoning (ta‘līl)
Why does the former master have authority? The text explains: 'because he is an agnate by reason of causation (sabab)'. The act of emancipation creates a legal tie that places him among the agnates (asabah).
He is the last of the agnates, meaning lineage agnates take precedence.
5
Apply the hierarchy of guardians
If there are no agnates by lineage, the former master becomes the wali. If even the master is absent, the ruler (imam) acts as guardian per the hadith.
The ruler is the ultimate guardian for those without any wali.

When the Nearest Guardian Is Out of Reach: The Role of the More Distant Wali
Ruling with proof (istihsan) on transfer of guardianship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for marriage by the more distant wali under ghaybat munqati'a and explain the istihsan-based reasoning.

Core Idea
Guardianship serves the woman's interest; when the nearest wali is unreachable, the next wali must act.

This lesson tackles a practical family law scenario: what happens when a woman's closest guardian is so far away that communication is impossible? The Hanafi school, prioritizing the woman's welfare, permits the next-eligible guardian to perform the marriage contract. This ruling is grounded in istihsan (juristic preference) and the nature of guardianship as a consideration-based authority (wilaya nazariyya).

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering condition
Recognize that the principle applies only when the nearest wali is absent in a 'disconnected absence' (ghaybat munqati'a) – a situation where he cannot be reached or consulted.
This is defined more precisely in later lessons; for now, understand it as a practical impossibility of communication.
2
State the ruling
The next wali in the hierarchy may legally marry the woman. This is explicitly permitted and valid (jāʾiz).
The text uses 'jāz' indicating permissibility and validity.
3
Grasp the reasoning: istihsan and wilaya nazariyya
The ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference) because strict analogy (qiyas) might require waiting indefinitely. The guardianship is for her benefit (wilaya nazariyya), so delegating to an unreachable guardian is against her interest.
The text argues: it is not beneficial to defer to someone whose opinion cannot be obtained.
4
Compare with related scenarios
If the nearest wali marries her from his remote location, that marriage also valid. If both the nearest and the next wali contract marriage, the first contract takes effect – they are treated as equal guardians.
This shows the school's effort to prevent confusion and prioritize the woman's stability.
5
Identify the authoritative support
Al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and Sadr al-Shari'a followed this istihsan position, indicating it is the relied-upon view in the school.
This anchors the ruling in the mainstream Hanafi tradition.

Definition and Duration of Disconnected Absence (Ghaybat Munqati'a)
Defines ghaybat munqati'a and notes a dispute on duration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ghaybat munqati'a and identify the intra-madhhab dispute on its duration, distinguishing the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Ghaybat munqati'a is absence too far for yearly contact, but its exact duration is disputed among Hanafi jurists.

In the previous lesson, we learned that a distant guardian may act when the closer guardian is absent. This lesson defines what qualifies as a 'disconnected absence' and reveals that Hanafi jurists disagreed on the precise time threshold. Understanding this dispute is crucial for determining when the more distant wali may validly perform the marriage contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
The text defines ghaybat munqati'a as the guardian being in a place that caravans reach only once a year. This is the default definition from Ibn Shuja, confirmed in al-Tashih.
This definition is the starting point for the discussion.
2
Note the attribution
The definition is attributed to Ibn Shuja and confirmed by the author of al-Tashih. This gives it authority as a transmitted position.
Ibn Shuja was a prominent Hanafi jurist.
3
Recognize the dispute
Al-Isbijabi reports that some Hanafi scholars estimate the duration by the duration of safar (travel), and that this is the basis for fatwa. This indicates a shift away from the yearly definition.
Safar in Hanafi fiqh is typically three days and three nights.
4
Consider further opinions
Al-Fadli in al-Sughra mentions that fatwa is given based on a month, but then states the correct view is three days. This shows a range of opinions.
The 'correct' is attributed to al-Fadli, possibly indicating a later preference.
5
Determine the thamarah
The practical effect (thamarah al-khilaf) is the time after which the distant wali may act: one year, the duration of safar, one month, or three days. Each has different consequences for the validity and timeliness of the marriage.
The mufta bihi is the duration of safar, but three days is also considered correct.

The Duration of Disconnected Absence in Marriage Guardianship: The Preferred View
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the duration defining disconnected absence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the preferred Hanafi view on disconnected absence and explain why later scholars shifted from a fixed duration to a pragmatic criterion.

Core Idea
Kafa'ah is a condition for marriage bindingness (or validity), and is assessed only from the husband's side.

In Hanafi marriage law, kafa'ah (suitability) ensures that the husband is not inferior to the wife in lineage, religion, wealth, freedom, and Islam. The school emphasizes lineage hierarchy: Quraysh > Arabs > non-Arabs. However, the legal effect of lacking kafa'ah is debated within the school: does it make the contract voidable or invalid? Core Idea
Kafa'ah ensures marital harmony through equality in religion, lineage, wealth, and profession — but its absence does not invalidate the marriage.

Kafa'ah (suitability) is a key consideration in Hanafi marriage law, protecting the family's honor by requiring certain equivalences between spouses. However, its precise conditions—especially wealth and profession—have been debated among Hanafi imams. Profession as a Criterion for Kafa'ah: Intra-Hanafi Differences
Intra-Hanafi dispute on profession in kafa'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the different Hanafi positions on considering profession in kafa'ah and identify which professions are considered vile according to some narrations.

Core Idea
Profession affects kafa'ah: vile professions like cupping or weaving may disqualify a match even if the man is otherwise equal.

This lesson explores the second factor of kafa'ah discussed in the passage: profession (sina'ah). The Hanafi school has internal differences on whether profession is considered and what constitutes a 'vile' profession that would affect kafa'ah. We will examine the views of Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and Abu Hanifa.

Identify the factor under dispute

According to Al-Hidayah, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad held that professions are considered generally. This is the view adopted in the base text of Al-Quduri (as indicated by the phrase 'at Abi Yusuf').

4

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A woman's marriage to a suitable man with deficient mahr is valid, but Abu Hanifa grants the wali the right to demand full mahr or divorce.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a wali can object when a woman marries a suitable man (kuf') but with less than her mahr al-mithl. Abu Hanifa holds that the wali can object, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say no. The passage also addresses the role of profession in determining kafa'ah, which is relied upon in fatwa. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to evaluate competing proofs within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The central question: Does the wali have the right to object to a marriage where the husband is suitable (kuf') but the dower (mahr) is less than the woman's mahr al-mithl?
Note that kafa'ah is established, but the mahr is deficient.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa says yes, the wali can object until the husband either completes the mahr al-mithl or divorces her. The wali's right is triggered by the deficiency in mahr despite suitability.
The wali's objection is a right, not an invalidation of the marriage.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that the wali cannot object. Their view is based on the man's suitability; the mahr deficiency is not grounds for objection.
This is a clear intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.

Note the tarjih (preference)
The commentator states that Abu Hanifa's evidence is preferred and relied upon by later authorities: al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, Sadr al-Shari'ah. Thus the mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
The tarjih does not invalidate the Sahibayn's view, but establishes the operative fatwa.

Understand the ruling on professions in kafa'ah
The passage adds that professions matter in kafa'ah: similar professions are suitable, but widely different ones (e.g., weaver vs. perfumer) are not. This is the relied-upon fatwa.
This is a separate ruling that complements the main dispute.

Marriage is valid without mahr because mahr is a legal obligation, not a pillar.

This lesson addresses a fundamental principle in Hanafi marriage law: the mahr (dower) is an obligatory consequence of the marriage contract, but it is not a condition for its validity. Guided Path

Minimum Mahr and Effects of Consummation/Divorce
Ruling with proof on minimum mahr and effects of consummation/divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compute the mahr due in various scenarios: when the named mahr is less than ten dirhams, when it is ten or more, and upon consummation, valid seclusion, or divorce before consummation.

Core Idea
The minimum mahr is ten dirhams; if named less, she gets ten; if named more, the named amount is due in full or half.

After establishing that marriage is valid without mahr, this lesson details the minimum mahr and how the amount changes with events. The Hanafi school sets the minimum at ten dirhams (about 30 grams of silver). If the husband names less than ten, the wife still receives ten upon consummation or death. If he names ten or more, the named amount is due. Divorce before consummation reduces the mahr to half, but only if the named mahr is at least ten dirhams. This lesson will guide you through these calculations.

Guided Path
1
State the Minimum Mahr
The minimum is ten dirhams (weight of seven mithqals). This applies whether coined or uncoined, or the value equivalent on the day of contract.
Other schools may have different minima (e.g., Shafi'i: three dirhams).
2
Apply the Rule When Named Less Than Ten
If the husband names less than ten dirhams (e.g., five dirhams), the wife is still entitled to the full ten upon consummation or death. If divorced before consummation, she gets five dirhams (half of ten).
The half applies to the default ten, not the named amount.
3
Apply the Rule When Named Ten or More
If the named mahr is ten dirhams or more, then upon consummation or death, the full named amount is due. Upon divorce before consummation, half of the named amount is due.
Example: named 100 dirhams → due 100 upon consummation, 50 upon divorce before consummation.
4
Identify the Events Triggering Full Mahr
Full mahr becomes confirmed (ta'akkud) by: actual intercourse (dukhul), valid seclusion (khalwah sahihah), or death of either spouse. Valid seclusion means being alone in a place where intercourse is possible without legal or natural obstacles.
Death ends the marriage, and all obligations become due.
5
Compute Half Mahr on Divorce Before Consummation
If divorce occurs before consummation or valid seclusion, the wife receives half the named mahr (if named ≥10). If named <10, she receives five dirhams (half of the minimum).
The half rule applies only when the divorce is before any act that confirms the full mahr.

Marriage Without Naming Mahr (Tafwid) and Mahr al-Mithl
Ruling on marriage without naming dower (tafwid) and determination of mahr al-mithl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on marriage without named dower and apply the rule for mahr al-mithl in cases of consummation, death, or divorce before consummation.

Core Idea
Marriage without naming a dower is valid; wife gets mahr al-mithl upon entry/death, half upon divorce before entry.

In Hanafi fiqh, a marriage contract is valid even if no dower (mahr) is named, or if it is stipulated that there is no dower. This is called tafwid (delegation). The wife is then entitled to mahr al-mithl (the dower of her peers) upon consummation, valid seclusion, or death of either spouse. If divorced before consummation, she receives half of that amount. This ruling is based on analogy with named mahr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Recognize that the marriage is one of tafwid: either no mahr was mentioned, or it was explicitly stated that there is no mahr. The marriage is still valid.
The woman is called al-mufawwidah.
2
Determine the triggering event
Three events trigger the obligation of full mahr al-mithl: actual consummation (dukhul), valid seclusion (khalwa sahiha), or death of either spouse. Divorce before any of these triggers only half.
Death includes both husband and wife.
3
Apply the mahr rule
Upon trigger, the wife is entitled to mahr al-mithl. This is determined by looking at the dower given to women similar to her in social status, age, beauty, wealth, and locality.
Half is given if divorce occurs before entry or seclusion.
4
Understand the proof
The proof is rational analogy (qiyas): just as a named mahr becomes due upon consummation or death, so does the mahr in tafwid because the dower is a contractual obligation confirmed by entry or death.
The passage uses the phrase 'because by entry the delivery of the substitute is realized, and thereby the substitute is confirmed.'

Mut'ah is wajib: three specific garments with a maximum of half her dowry and a minimum of five dirhams.

This lesson covers the essential ruling of mut'ah for a woman divorced before consummation when no mahr was fixed. The Hanafi school obligates a consolatory gift of three garments — a shift, headcover, and wrapper — with clear upper and lower monetary bounds derived from the sunnah.

Mahr al-mithl is determined by women's status in her paternal lineage.

The minimum value is five dirhams (approx. 15 grams of silver). This ensures a meaningful gift.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An invalid dower does not invalidate the marriage; the wife receives mahr al-mithl.

This passage from al-Quduri addresses a common scenario: what happens if a Muslim marries with a dower that is not valid property, such as wine or a pig? And what if no dower is specified at the time of marriage, but the couple later agrees? The marriage contract is separate from the dower specification. An invalid or absent dower does not harm the marriage; the wife's right to a proper dower is preserved through mahr al-mithl or later agreement.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on mahr for seclusion by a majbub

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the two Hanafi positions on mahr when a majbub secludes and divorces, and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
The wife of a majbub gets full mahr per Abu Hanifa; Sahibayn say half.

This passage addresses an uncommon but instructive scenario: a man whose penis and testicles have been completely removed (majbub) marries, secludes with his wife, and then divorces her. The question is whether such seclusion entitles her to the full mahr or only half. The Hanafi school is divided: Abu Hanifa rules for full mahr, while his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold for half. The dispute turns on the nature of the wife's submission and the possibility of consummation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
The husband is a majbub (complete removal of penis and testicles). The act is khalwah sahihah (valid seclusion) followed by divorce without any external obstacle.
Note the difference between majbub, khasi (testicles removed), and 'innin (impotent). Full mahr is agreed for khasi and 'innin.
2
State the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa: full mahr. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): half mahr.
The Ijma of the School is that there is mahr; the dispute is over the amount.
3
Compare the proofs (istidlal)
Abu Hanifa argues that the wife has submitted to the utmost possible; no greater submission can be expected. The Sahibayn argue that the majbub's excuse (complete incapacity) is greater than that of a sick person, so the seclusion does not justify full mahr.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on the legal purpose of seclusion.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's, as confirmed in al-Tashih and by later authorities like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi.
The tarjih is based on the strength of the reasoning and the chain of transmission (Zahir al-Riwayah).

Mut'ah for Divorced Women: General Rule and Exception
Ruling on mut'ah with textual commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mut'ah for divorced women and identify the specific case in which it becomes obligatory (wajib).

Core Idea
Mut'ah is recommended for all divorcées, but obligatory for the mufawwadah.

Mut'ah is a consolatory gift given to a divorced woman to ease the pain of separation. The Hanafi school holds that it is generally recommended (mustahabb) for every divorcée. However, there is one exception: a woman divorced before consummation without a specified mahr (the mufawwadah) must receive mut'ah. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its rationale, and a textual nuance in the classical sources.

1
State the general ruling on mut'ah
Identify the default ruling: mut'ah is mustahabb (recommended) for all divorced women, as a kindness to alleviate the sorrow of separation.
This is the base ruling from al-Quduri's matn.
2
Identify the exception and its conditions
Find the one case where mut'ah is not merely recommended but obligatory: a woman divorced before consummation whose mahr was not fixed in the marriage contract.
Such a woman is called al-mufawwadah.
3
Explain the legal basis for obligation
Understand why mut'ah becomes wajib for the mufawwadah: she is entitled to half of mahr al-mithl, but since no mahr was named, mut'ah substitutes for that half.
This reasoning is based on the principle that the wife's right to half the mahr is replaced by mut'ah.
4
Resolve the textual variant
Note that some manuscripts of al-Quduri read 'and he named a mahr for her' instead of 'and he did not name a mahr.' Follow the authorities (Najm al-A'imma, Rukn al-A'imma) affirming the correct wording is 'did not name.'
The correct wording ensures the exception applies only to the mufawwadah.

Slave Marriage Requires Master's Permission

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Marriage of a slave without master's permission is invalid because it is a 'ayb (defect) restricting the slave's usefulness.

This short passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, as explained by al-Maydani, establishes a foundational principle in slave jurisprudence: a slave cannot marry without the master's consent. The reasoning is not arbitrary but rooted in the legal concept that marriage imposes obligations that conflict with the master's absolute right to the slave's service. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the limits of a slave's legal capacity in Hanafi fiqh.

2

No specific Quran or hadith is cited; the proof is a legal reasoning based on the nature of slavery.
3

Why is marriage a defect? Because marriage imposes rights and duties (e.g., mahr, maintenance, companionship) that hinder the master's unrestricted use of the slave's labor and availability. This makes the slave less valuable.
This rationale is specific to slaves; for free persons, marriage is a commendable act.

Derive the condition for validity
The condition (shart) for a valid slave marriage is the master's permission (idhn). Without it, the contract is batil (void).
This condition overrides the general capacity of slaves to contract.

For free persons, permission is not required; they have full capacity. The defect reasoning only applies to slaves due to their legal status.
This helps highlight the exceptional nature of the ruling.
