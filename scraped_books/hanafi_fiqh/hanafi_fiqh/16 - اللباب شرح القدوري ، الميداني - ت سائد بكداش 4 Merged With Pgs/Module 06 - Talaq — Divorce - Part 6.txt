Travel with Revocably Divorced Wife During Iddah
Ruling on traveling with revocably divorced wife during iddah with proof and dissent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on traveling with a revocably divorced wife, identify the Quranic proof, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute with Zufar.

Core Idea
Traveling with a revocably divorced wife is prohibited unless the husband witnesses the revocation, based on the generality of Quran 65:1.

The iddah period after a revocable divorce is a time of waiting and potential reconciliation. The husband retains the right to take his wife back, but this must be done explicitly. This lesson examines whether a husband may travel with his wife during this period. The Hanafi school derives a clear prohibition from the Quranic command not to expel women from their homes, yet a notable dissenting opinion within the school views travel as an implicit act of revocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the husband's travel with his revocably divorced wife during her iddah. The matn states 'لا يجوز' (it is not permissible), indicating prohibition (haram) in the Hanafi school.
The prohibition is the default ruling; any exception must be justified.
2
Locate the primary proof (Quran)
The text cites Quran 65:1: 'Do not drive them out of their houses.' This command is general (amm), addressing all people, including the husband.
The generality is key: the prohibition applies to the husband just as it applies to others.
3
Apply the Hanafi usul on prohibition
In Hanafi usul, a prohibition (nahy) indicates haram unless there is evidence to the contrary. The generality of the verse covers all forms of leaving the home, including travel with the husband.
The dalal of the verse is zanni (likely), but it is sufficient for establishing prohibition.
4
Recognize the intra-madhhab dissent (Zufar)
Imam Zufar (a Hanafi) argued that travel itself constitutes an implied revocation (raj'a) because the husband's action shows his intention to retain her. Thus, travel is not expulsion but retention.
This is a valid ikhtilaf within the school; later authorities like al-Durr al-Mukhtar adopted Zufar's view.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the majority view is followed, travel without witness is a sin and the marriage remains revocable. If Zufar's view is followed, the travel itself restores the marriage, making further revocation unnecessary.
The mufta bihi (operative opinion) has shifted in later Hanafi practice towards Zufar's permissibility.

Remarriage of Bain Divorcee in Iddah and Subsequent Divorce
Ruling on a specific divorce scenario with expansion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for remarrying a bain divorcee during iddah and divorcing before consummation, and explain the reasoning based on qabd from prior intercourse.

Core Idea
Prior iddah substitutes for new consummation, making the divorce post-consumption.

This lesson examines a nuanced case in Hanafi divorce law. When a man divorces his wife with a bain (irrevocable) divorce and then remarries her during her iddah, the legal effect of the first intercourse remains. If he divorces again before consummation, the second divorce is treated as if after consummation due to the lingering 'possession' (qabd) from the first marriage. This triggers a full mahr and a fresh iddah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Read the passage and identify the sequence: first bain divorce, remarriage during iddah, second divorce before consummation.
This is a specific case; not all remarriages during iddah are valid.
2
State the Legal Consequences
According to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, the husband must pay a full mahr and the wife must observe a new iddah.
This is the key ruling to memorize.
3
Analyze the Reasoning
The reasoning is 'qabd al-awwal qa'im maqam al-qabd fi al-nikah al-thani' – the prior possession (due to first intercourse) stands in for the possession required in the new marriage.
The effect of the first intercourse (iddah) is the link.
4
Identify the Intra-Madhhab Disagreement
The text attributes this view to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, implying that Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani may hold a different opinion (likely that it is a divorce before consummation).
This disagreement affects whether the mahr is full or half.
5
Understand the Authority and Application
The mufta bihi position is the one stated; it is relied upon in later Hanafi fatwa.
The ruling is specific to bain divorce during iddah, not raj'i.

This passage presents a specific case in divorce law. A man divorces his wife with a bain (irrevocable) divorce. During her iddah, he remarries her, then divorces her again before consummation. Ruling on establishing paternity of a child born to a revocably divorced woman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the lineage of a child born to a revocably divorced woman and apply the two-year maximum pregnancy period to determine paternity.

Core Idea
A child born within two years of a revocable divorce is attributed to the husband unless the wife admits her iddah ended.

This passage from the Lubab explains a key Hanafi ruling regarding the paternity of a child born after a revocable divorce. The ruling hinges on the school's unique position that pregnancy can last up to two years. Understanding this rule is crucial for correctly establishing lineage and avoiding confusion about marital separation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage states that the lineage (nasab) of a child born to a revocably divorced woman is established if she gives birth within two years of the divorce.
The text mentions 'two years or more', but the commentary clarifies that the maximum is two years.
2
Identify the exception
The ruling has a key condition: the lineage is established only as long as the woman does not admit that her iddah (waiting period) has ended.
If she admits the iddah ended, the child cannot be attributed to the ex-husband.
3
Understand the proof (dalil)
The Hanafi school relies on the possibility of a long pregnancy, up to two years, based on a report from the time of Uthman (RA).
This is a zanni (probable) proof, but it is consistently applied in the school.
4
Note the opposite case
If the child is born before two years, the woman becomes separated from her husband (i.e., her iddah ends with the birth).
This indicates that the birth itself, if within a normal pregnancy period, ends the iddah and completes the separation.

Lineage of a Widow's Child: Majority View vs. Zufar
Intra-Hanafi dispute on lineage of a widow's child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the majority Hanafi view establishes lineage for a widow's child born within two years of death, despite Zufar's objection, and apply this rule to determine lineage in a given scenario.

Core Idea
A widow's child born within two years of death is attributed to the deceased husband, even after the iddah months end.

This lesson addresses a specific intra-Hanafi dispute: when a widow gives birth after her iddah of months (four months and ten days) has expired, can the child be attributed to her deceased husband? The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) holds that if the child is born within two years of death, lineage is established. Zufar disagrees, arguing that once the iddah expires by months, any later child cannot be from the marriage. We will examine the reasoning, the dispute's basis, and its practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
State the majority Hanafi position on the widow's child.
Identify the rule: lineage is established if the child is born within two years of the husband's death, provided the widow has not acknowledged the completion of her iddah.
This applies even if the marriage was not consummated.
2
Identify Zufar's opposing view and his dalil.
Zufar argues that once the iddah months (4 months 10 days) expire, the marriage is dissolved, so any child born six months after that (i.e., after a full pregnancy period from the end of iddah) cannot be attributed to the deceased. He compares it to a widow who admits iddah completion.
Zufar uses qiyas: just as an admission ends the firash, the law's declaration of iddah completion also ends it.
3

The majority response is that the iddah of death can end in two ways: by the passage of months or by delivery. The possibility of pregnancy keeps the firash (marital bed) intact even after the months have passed, so the child could still be from the husband.
Delivery is an independent method of ending iddah (Quran 65:4).
4

The text contrasts with the case of a minor widow (who has not reached puberty). In her case, the default is that she is not pregnant, so after the iddah months, the firash is definitely broken. For a mature woman, pregnancy is possible, so the default is that the firash remains until delivery or two years.
This distinction explains why Zufar's analogy to the minor fails.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf – practical difference.
If a widow gives birth, say, 13 months after death, the majority establishes lineage (child inherits, etc.), while Zufar rejects it. The mufta bihi view is the majority view.
The two-year limit is from death, not from the end of iddah.

Nasab and One Woman's Testimony: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on establishing nasab with one woman's testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the establishment of nasab through a single woman's testimony and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Paternity requires two witnesses unless pregnancy is apparent or husband admits; the Sahibayn dissent.

This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi disagreement on proving lineage. While the matn states that paternity is established without testimony only in two exceptional cases, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that a single woman's testimony suffices in all cases. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to trace how different usul principles—here, the strength of farash vs. general rules of testimony—yield different rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling from the matn
What does the matn say about when paternity is established without any testimony? The matn states it is established ''except if there is an apparent pregnancy, or an admission from the husband''. So paternity is automatically established without testimony only in those two cases.
This is the starting point of the dispute.
2
Understand the exception for apparent pregnancy
If the pregnancy is apparent but the husband denies birth, the matn says a midwife's testimony is needed. Why? Because it is possible the woman gave birth to a dead child and wants to foist another child on him. The testimony confirms the live birth. This shows that even in the exception, testimony may be required for a different issue (live birth).
3
State the Sahibayn's opposing position
The Sahibayn hold that in all cases (not just apparent pregnancy or admission), paternity is established by the testimony of one woman. Their reasoning is given in the Hidayah: the marital bed (farash) exists due to ongoing 'iddah, and it obligates paternity; the only need is to identify the child, and one woman's testimony suffices for identification, just as during marriage.
This is the core disagreement.
4
Analyze the underlying dalil (proof) for each side
Abu Hanifa's position is based on the general principle that testimony normally requires two witnesses. He allows exceptions only for necessity (apparent pregnancy or admission). The Sahibayn rely on the strength of the marital bed (farash) which is established by the marriage or 'iddah; they argue that since the obligation of paternity is already there, testimony merely identifies the particular child, which is akin to identification during marriage where one woman can testify.
Both sides use valid Hanafi usul principles.
5
Determine the tarjih (preferred view)
Later authorities like al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, and Sadr al-Shari'ah relied on Abu Hanifa's view. Therefore, the mufta bihi (operative ruling for fatwa) is that paternity is established without testimony only when pregnancy is apparent or the husband admits; otherwise, two witnesses are needed.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents (فهرس الموضوعات) from Al-Lubab fi Sharh Al-Quduri, covering sections on divorce (talaq), return (raj'ah), and oath of abstention (ila'). It lists the key topics within these chapters, providing an overview of the legal issues to be discussed, including conditions, delegation, the effect of coercion, and the rules of khul'. The page numbers accompanying each topic are for reference in the original text.

Key Terms
فهرس الموضوعات (fihris al-mawḍūʿāt): The table of contents listing the main topics covered in the book.
الطلاق (al-ṭalāq): Divorce; the dissolution of marriage by the husband's pronouncement.
الرجعة (al-rajʿah): The return to a revocably divorced wife during her waiting period without a new marriage contract.
الإيلاء (al-īlāʾ): A vow by a husband to abstain from sexual relations with his wife for a period of four months or more.
الخلع (al-khulʿ): Divorce initiated by the wife in exchange for compensation paid to the husband.
