Witness Requirements for Proving Breastfeeding
States the required witnesses for proving fosterage, rejecting women-only testimony.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the required witnesses for proving breastfeeding in Hanafi law and explain why women's testimony alone is not accepted.

Core Idea
Breastfeeding claims require the same proof as property: two men or one man and two women; women alone are not accepted.

This lesson addresses a specific evidentiary rule in Hanafi fiqh: the proof required to establish that breastfeeding has occurred, which can annul a marriage. The ruling may seem strict, but it rests on a clear legal analogy (qiyas) to property claims, emphasizing the gravity of breaking a marriage union.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is proving a case of fosterage (رضاع) that would invalidate a marriage. The ruling is that the testimony of women alone (without male witnesses) is not accepted. Instead, the proof must meet the same standard as property claims.
The ruling is obligatory (wajib) for the court to apply.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The text explains that women's testimony is only accepted in matters where men cannot have knowledge (e.g., childbirth, virginity). Breastfeeding is not such a matter—men can also witness it. Therefore, the exception does not apply.
This distinguishes breastfeeding from purely female-expertise issues.
3
Apply the analogy to property (qiyas)
Proven breastfeeding leads to annulment of marriage (إبطال الملك), which is a serious legal consequence. Just as property claims require strong proof (two men or a man and two women), so too does a claim of fosterage. This ensures the marriage is not broken on weak evidence.
The same witness requirement applies: two upright men, or one man and two women.

Commentary
This passage addresses the evidentiary requirements for proving that a fosterage (رضاع) has occurred, which can invalidate a marriage. The ruling states that the testimony of women alone (without a man) is not accepted to prove breastfeeding. The reason given is that women's testimony is allowed only in matters that men cannot ordinarily observe, but breastfeeding is not exclusively within women's knowledge (men can also witness it). Therefore, the standard for proving breastfeeding is the same as for property claims: two male witnesses, or one man and two women. The commentary explains that this is because proven breastfeeding leads to the annulment of the marriage (إبطال الملك), which is a serious matter requiring strong proof. Thus, the Islamic legal system requires a rigorous standard to avoid false claims that could break up families.

Rejection of Testimony from Locality Witnesses in Qasama
Ruling on the testimony of two witnesses from the locality against someone

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on testimony of locality witnesses in qasama and explain why it is rejected due to tuhma.

Core Idea
Witnesses from the locality of a murder are tainted by self-interest and their testimony is rejected.

In qasama cases, the entire locality where a victim is found may be liable for blood money if the killer is unknown. This creates a strong incentive for residents to point a finger elsewhere. The Hanafi school therefore blocks their testimony to preserve justice, even if they witnessed the act. This lesson unpacks the concept of tuhma (legal suspicion) as a disqualifying factor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Locate the exact ruling: testimony of two witnesses from the same locality where the slain was found is not accepted. Note that this applies whether the accused is from that locality or an outsider.
The ruling is not about the credibility of the witnesses as individuals, but about their relationship to the case.
2
Extract the legal cause (illa)
The text explicitly states 'due to the presence of tuhma'. Tuhma is the legal suspicion of partiality. Understand that tuhma here arises from the witnesses' potential liability in qasama: if no killer is identified, the locality collectively bears the blood money.
Tuhma is a recognized ground for rejecting testimony in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Analyze the reasoning (talil)
The ruling uses talil (reasoning by cause). The 'illa is tuhma, which makes the testimony unreliable. Recognize that this is a form of istihsan (juristic preference) based on blocking the means to falsehood.
The cause is not based on a textual source in this passage but on general legal principles.
4
Apply to similar scenarios
Consider extending this ruling: any witness with a direct personal stake in the outcome (e.g., familial or financial interest) may be disqualified. Test this by analogy: would a creditor's testimony against a debtor be accepted if it benefits the creditor?
Hanafi jurists use tuhma to exclude testimony in many situations, not just qasama.

If two people from the locality where the slain was found testify against a man from among them or from outside them that he killed him, their testimony is not accepted, due to the presence of suspicion (tuhma).

Commentary
This passage rules on the admissibility of testimony in qasama cases. If two witnesses from the same locality where the victim's body was found come forward to testify that a particular person (whether from that locality or an outsider) is the killer, their testimony is rejected. The reason is the existence of 'tuhma' (suspicion). Because the entire locality is potentially liable for qasama (oaths and blood money) if the killer is not identified, these witnesses have a personal interest in shifting blame to someone else. This self-interest renders their testimony unreliable. Therefore, the law precludes them from testifying, even if they are otherwise credible. This ruling applies regardless of whether the accused is from the locality or not, because the witnesses' motive to avoid liability remains.

Key Terms
التهمة (al-tuhmah): Legal suspicion of partiality or personal interest that disqualifies a witness from testifying.

Acceptance of Witness Testimony in Qadhf Despite Delay
Explanation of why testimony in qadhf is accepted despite delay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on delayed testimony in hudud and explain the exception for qadhf along with its legal rationale.

Core Idea
Delay in testimony invalidates in hudud except qadhf because it involves a personal right of the victim.

In Hanafi law, witness credibility is compromised if they delay reporting, as it may indicate a grudge. However, the hadd penalty for false accusation (qadhf) is unique: delayed testimony is accepted because the victim's personal right to clear his name takes precedence. This lesson unpacks that exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Recall that in Hanafi fiqh, if witnesses delay testifying about a hadd crime, their testimony is rejected due to suspicion (e.g., a grudge or enmity).
The text refers to 'تسقلم' (disqualification) because the delay makes them suspect.
2
Identify the exception
The exception is for hadd al-qadhf (false accusation of adultery). In this case, testimony is accepted even if delayed.
The phrase 'إلا في حد القذف خاصة' explicitly carves out the exception.
3
Analyze the rationale
Two reasons: (1) Qadhf involves a personal right (ḥaqq al-ʿabd) to remove disgrace, and delay does not prejudice such rights. (2) A lawsuit (daʿwā) is a prerequisite for qadhf, so the delay may simply be due to the absence of a lawsuit.
The text states: 'لأن فيه حق العبد... والتقادم غير مانع في حقوق العباد' and 'لأن الدعوى فيه شرط'.
4
Apply the exception
In practice, a witness who delays testifying about a qadhf can still be heard, as long as the victim's right is involved.
This exception only applies to qadhf, not to other hudud such as theft or drinking.

Commentary
This passage explains the exception for qadhf from the general rule that witnesses' testimony is rejected if they delay reporting. The general rule is based on suspicion that the delay indicates a grudge. However, in qadhf (false accusation of adultery), the testimony is accepted even if delayed because the right of the victim (the accused) is involved, and delay does not prejudice that. Moreover, since a lawsuit is required in qadhf, the delay may simply be due to the absence of a suit. Hence the witnesses are not deemed fasiq.

Key Terms
التقادم (al-taqaddum): Delay in reporting or testifying after witnessing an event; legally relevant for credibility of witnesses.
القذف (al-qadhf): False accusation of adultery or illicit intercourse; a hadd crime with specific punishment.
حق العبد (ḥaqq al-ʿabd): Right of a human being (as opposed to right of Allah); in qadhf, the victim's right to clear his name.
الدعوى (al-daʿwā): Lawsuit; in qadhf, a formal claim is required before punishment can be carried out.

The Time Limit for Tazkiah: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Taqaddum
Intra-Hanafi dispute on taqaddum limit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifah, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and the relied-upon view regarding the time limit for witness credibility assessment (tazkiah), and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
The time limit for tazkiah varies: Abu Hanifah left it to the judge; the relied-upon view sets it at one month.

When witnesses come forward long after an event, their credibility may be questioned. The Hanafi school debated how long is 'too long' before a background check (tazkiah) becomes necessary. This lesson explores the different opinions within the school and identifies which view is relied upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
List the opinions on the time limit
Identify each opinion mentioned in the passage: Abu Hanifah's delegation to the judge, the six-month indication from al-Jami' al-Saghir, and Muhammad's one-month limit.
Note that Abu Yusuf also narrated one month.
2
Examine the proofs behind each opinion
Muhammad argued that less than a month is 'immediate' (aajil), so the limit is a month. Abu Hanifah relied on istihsan and left it to judicial discretion.
No explicit text is cited; the arguments are based on reason and custom.
3
Compare the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the one-month rule, a witness testifying after two months must undergo tazkiah or his testimony is rejected. Under Abu Hanifah's view, the judge decides case by case.
This affects court procedure and the burden on witnesses.
4
Identify the tarjih and mufta bihi
Qadikhan states that 'a month and above is considered delayed' and that this is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).
The later tradition settled on one month for certainty.

Commentary

Muhammad al-Shaybani set it at one month, which is also narrated from Abu Hanifah and Abu Yusuf. The later authority Qadikhan affirms the one-month rule as the relied-upon position. The lesson shows a range of opinions within the Hanafi school, with the later tarjih favoring one month.
