Acts Treated Like Unintentional Killing: The Sleeping Person
Extending khata' rulings to excusable acts like a sleeping person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for a sleeping person who kills someone and explain the qiyas basis for treating it as khata'.

Core Idea
A sleeping person's killing is treated as unintentional — he is excused but still liable for kaffarah and diyah.

This short passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri addresses a specific case: a sleeping person who rolls over and kills someone. Although the act is involuntary, the Hanafi school holds the person liable for the same consequences as unintentional killing (khata'). Let's examine the ruling and the underlying analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the ruling
The case is a sleeping person who rolls over onto a man and kills him. The ruling is that it is treated like unintentional killing (khata') for all three consequences: kaffarah, diyah, and deprivation of inheritance.
2
Understand the reason (excuse)
The sleeper is 'ma'dhur' (excused) because his action is involuntary and he has no control. This excuse is the key factor that links it to khata'.
3
Recognize the analogy to khata'
The text explicitly states 'because he is excused like the mistaken' – this is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) where the 'illa (effective cause) is the absence of intent and presence of an excuse.
The 'illa is not the act itself but the state of the actor.
4
List the three consequences
1) Obligation of kaffarah (expiation: freeing a slave or two consecutive months of fasting), 2) Obligation of diyah (blood money) on the 'aqilah, 3) Deprivation of inheritance (if the killer was an heir).
These are exactly the same as for khata'.
5
Note the authority level
This ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is considered mutafaq alayh (agreed upon within the Hanafi school). It is the mufta bihi position.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf is recorded for this basic ruling.

This is established by qiyas (analogy) because the common effective cause ('illa) is the absence of intent and the presence of an excuse. The sleeping person is 'ma'dhur' (excused) just like the one who makes a mistake.

Ruling on obligations for unintentional killing with Quranic proof

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In unintentional killing, the killer must free a slave and pay blood money via his clan, and is deprived of inheritance due to negligence.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on unintentional killing (khata') as derived from the Qur'an and elaborated in classical texts. The school prescribes expiation (kaffarah) and blood money (diyah) as mandatory obligations, while clarifying the nuanced issue of sin and its effect on inheritance. Understanding these rules is essential for applying Islamic criminal law correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two obligations

The text mentions 'الكفارة، والدية على العاقلة'.
2
Examine the Quranic proof
Read the verse (Quran 4:92) cited as evidence. The imperative forms 'فَتَحْرِيرُ' and 'وَدِيَةٌ' indicate obligation according to Hanafi usul.
Dalil is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala.

Contrast with intentional killing where sin is major.
4
Apply the inheritance deprivation
The killer is deprived of inheritance from the victim because there is sin (إثم) in the act, making deprivation valid.
This applies to all killing, including khata'.

Killing by Cause (Sabab): The Hanafi Ruling on Indirect Homicide
Ruling for indirect causation homicide with examples and reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for indirect causation homicide and distinguish the legal consequences (diyah on 'aqilah) from those of direct killing (kaffarah, sin, inheritance deprivation).

Core Idea
Indirect causation (sabab) in homicide triggers only diyah, not kaffarah.

In this passage, we examine the concept of killing by cause (sabab) as opposed to direct killing (mubashir). The Hanafi school distinguishes them sharply: only direct killing carries full religious penalties. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act of indirect causation
Examine the examples given: digging a well on another's property without permission, placing a stone in someone else's property without permission. These are acts that create a dangerous condition that may cause death.
The act itself is not the direct cause of death; it is the condition that leads to death.
2
State the hukm level for each element
Note the legal consequences: diyah (blood money) is wajib on the 'aqilah (the killer's clan or insurance group). No kaffarah (expiation), no sin (ithm), and no deprivation of inheritance.
Diyah is a financial obligation; the other penalties are absent because the killing is not directly attributed.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil)
The text explains: the killing is 'non-existent from him in reality' (al-qatl ma'dum minhu haqiqatan). Liability for damages (daman) is attached by virtue of causation, but other religious penalties remain on the original principle (i.e., they require direct agency).
This is a key distinction in Hanafi usul: haqiqah vs. hukman.
4
Note the practical consequences
In practice, the 'aqilah pays diyah; the killer does not need to fast or free a slave (kaffarah) and is not considered sinful in the religious sense. The inheritance rights are unaffected.
This ruling is specific to indirect causation; direct killing has different rules.
5
Confirm the mufta bihi
The passage cites al-Hidayah as authority. This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school.
The authority weight is zahir al-riwayah.

Commentary
This passage discusses indirect causation (sabab) in homicide. Examples given: digging a well on another's property without permission, or placing a stone in a public or private place without authority. However, there is no kaffarah (expiation), no sin (ithm), and no deprivation of inheritance. The reasoning: the killing is not directly attributed to the person ('adam al-qatl haqiqatan). Liability is only for damages (daman), not for the full religious penalties. This distinction is based on the principle that guarantees (daman) follow causation even when direct agency is absent. The text cites al-Hidayah as authority.

Defining the two main categories of financial compensation in Hanafi criminal law.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Diyah is the substitute for a life taken; arsh compensates for injuries below the self.

This lesson opens the Book of Diyat in Sharh al-Quduri. The author first explains why this chapter follows the chapter on criminal offenses (jinayat), then defines two central terms: diyah (blood money) and arsh (compensation for wounds). Understanding this distinction is essential for studying Islamic criminal liability.

The phrase 'what is below the self' includes wounds, fractures, and loss of limbs.
3

The author stresses that diyah is a transmitted legal term (منقول شرعي), not a verbal noun; this means its meaning is fixed by Shari'ah, not by linguistic derivation.
This clarifies that the term is not open to analogical expansion beyond its legal definition.

Apply the Definitions
Restate each definition in your own words. For diyah: 'the wealth paid as compensation for taking a life.' For arsh: 'the wealth paid for injuries short of death.'
5

This reflects the structure of the text: first the crime, then the compensation.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Diyah Composition for Shibh Amd
Intra-Hanafi dispute on camel categories for heavy diyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the composition of diyah for shibh amd and explain the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Hanafi imams agree on heavy diyah for shibh amd but differ on the camel mix; the fatwa follows the simpler four-quarters view.

This passage records an important intra-Hanafi dispute between the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Imam Muhammad regarding the exact composition of the 100-camel heavy blood money (diyah mughallazah) for semi-intentional killing (shibh amd). While the common elements—liability on the 'aajilah and expiation on the killer—are agreed upon, the specific categories and the requirement of pregnancy vary. This lesson trains you to parse such ikhtilaf and understand the fatwa hierarchy within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common rulings
The passage first states that for shibh amd, heavy diyah is due on the 'aajilah and expiation on the killer. Both positions agree on this.
2
Extract the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
According to them, the 100 camels are divided equally into four quarters: 25 bint makhad, 25 bint labun, 25 hiqqah, 25 jadh'ah. Note the ages: second, third, fourth, fifth year respectively.
3
Extract the position of Muhammad
Muhammad prescribes 30 jadh'ah, 30 hiqqah, and 40 thaniyyah (sixth year), and all must be pregnant (khalifāt, with offspring in bellies).
4
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Muhammad's view requires higher age camels (thaniyyah) and pregnancy, raising the cost and difficulty for the 'aajilah. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf's view is more balanced and easier to fulfill.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The school's fatwa is on the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf because it is the majority view, easier, and more widely transmitted (zahir al-riwayah).

Commentary
This passage presents the intra-Hanafi dispute on the specific composition of the heavy blood money for shibh amd (semi-intentional killing). The first position, attributed to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, divides the 100 camels equally into four categories of 25 each, representing different ages. The second position, held by Muhammad al-Shaybani, prescribes 30 jadh'ah, 30 hiqqah, and 40 thaniyyah, all of which must be pregnant females (khalifat). The common elements: both require a heavy diyah (mughallazah) on the 'aajilah (the killer's male relatives) and kaffarah (expiation) on the killer personally. The ikhtilaf is only on the exact mix of camels. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, as it is the majority view and more practical.

Key Terms

عاقلة (‘āqilah): The male paternal relatives (asabah) of the killer who are liable to pay diyah collectively.
دية مغلظة (diyah mughallaẓah): Heavy blood money: a diyah of 100 camels (or equivalent) due in full, often with specific categories and without delay.
كفارة (kaffārah): Expiation: for shibh amd, it is to free a believing slave, or if unable, fast two consecutive months.
بنت مخاض (bint makhāḍ): A female camel that has entered its second year (and whose mother is pregnant again).
بنت لبون (bint labūn): A female camel that has entered its third year (and whose mother has given birth again).
حقة (ḥiqqah): A female camel that has entered its fourth year and is ready for breeding.
جذعة (jadha‘ah): A female camel that has entered its fifth year.
ثنية (thaniyyah): A female camel that has entered its sixth year (when its teeth have changed).
خلفات (khalifāt): Pregnant female camels (singular: khalifah).

Head Wounds (Shijaj) and Qisas: Classification, Consensus, and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Enumerating ten head wounds and discussing qisas for each category

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the ten head wounds, apply the rule of qisas for al-Muwaddihah, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas for lighter wounds, identifying the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.

Core Idea
Qisas applies only when exact equivalence is possible — impossibility shifts the penalty to monetary compensation.

This passage from al-Lubab classifies head wounds (shijaj) and lays down the rules for qisas (retaliation). It first clarifies that only al-Muwaddihah (wound exposing bone) is subject to qisas by consensus, while deeper wounds are not. Then it reveals an internal Hanafi disagreement: some say no qisas for any wound below al-Muwaddihah, while the authoritative Zahir al-Riwayah holds that qisas applies to most of them, except al-Simhaq. Understanding this dispute is crucial for correctly applying the principle of equivalence in retribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ten shijaj
List the ten head wounds in order from lightest to deepest: al-Harisah (scratch), al-Damiyah (bleeding), al-Badi'ah (cut), al-Mutalahimah (flesh tear), al-Simhaq (thin skin), al-Muwaddihah (bone exposed), al-Hashimah (fracture), al-Munaqqilah (displaced bone), al-Aammah (brain membrane), and al-Damighah (brain penetrated, counted as homicide).
The text mentions only al-Muwaddihah, al-Hashimah, al-Munaqqilah, and al-Aammah; al-Damighah is excluded from the ten due to being fatal.
2
Apply the rule for al-Muwaddihah
The text states unequivocally: 'fī al-muwaddiḥati al-qiṣāṣu in kānat 'amdan' — for al-Muwaddihah, qisas applies if intentional. This is because cutting to the bone is exactly replicable, satisfying the Quranic requirement of equivalence.
The proof is rational: imkān al-mumāthalah (possibility of exact likeness).
3
Identify the consensus zone (wounds above al-Muwaddihah)
For al-Hashimah, al-Munaqqalah, and al-Aammah, there is consensus (ijma') that no qisas applies, because equivalence is impossible — breaking bone or reaching the brain cannot be replicated exactly without risk of death.
The text says 'lā qiṣāṣa fīmā fawqahā bi-l-ijmā''.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on wounds below al-Muwaddihah
Two positions exist: (1) al-Hasan ibn Ziyād narrated from Abu Hanifa: no qisas in any of the six lighter wounds. (2) Muhammad al-Shaybānī in al-Aṣl (Zahir al-Riwayah): qisas applies to all five below al-Simhaq, but not to al-Simhaq itself (by consensus). The dispute hinges on whether exact equivalence is achievable in these wounds.
Muhammad's view is supported by practical possibility: a custom-made iron tool can cut to the exact depth.

Defining the equitable assessment for the six head wounds before mudihah and explaining its calculation method.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the method of calculating hukumat al-adl for wounds below mudihah and apply it to a hypothetical scenario.

Core Idea
For wounds without fixed diyah, compensation is determined by valuing a slave's depreciation and proportionally applying it to the free person's diyah.

This lesson introduces the concept of hukumat al-adl, an equitable assessment method used for six specific head wounds that are less severe than mudihah. Rather than a fixed amount, the Hanafi school prescribes a proportional calculation based on the depreciation in value. This method ensures fairness by linking compensation to actual harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wounds requiring hukumat

The method avoids arbitrary amounts by using a market-based reference.

A slave of similar build and age is valued without the wound and then with the wound. The difference in price represents the percentage depreciation.
The slave serves as a proxy for the free person's physical integrity; the same percentage is applied to the free person's diyah.
4
Apply the proportion to the full diyah
The percentage depreciation is applied to the full diyah of a free man (100 camels or 10,000 dirhams) to calculate the compensation.
For example, if depreciation is 5% (half of one-tenth), then compensation is 5% of the diyah.

Recognize the mufta bihi position

Diyat for Fingers and the Forearm Dispute
Diyat for fingers and an intra-Hanafi dispute on forearm inclusion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi diyat rules for fingers cut with the palm and forearm, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Fingers plus palm = fixed half diya; forearm extra requires a fair assessment.

This lesson covers the diyat (blood money) for cutting all five fingers of one hand. The text first establishes that each finger is one-tenth of the full diya, so five fingers total half diya. It then discusses the scenario where the cut includes the palm or part of the forearm, introducing an internal Hanafi disagreement on whether the forearm part should be compensated by a separate government assessment or subsumed under the hand's fixed diya. Understanding this dispute sharpens your skills in applying the Hanafi principle of tābi'iyyah (following) and the use of hukumat al-adl for unregulated injuries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling for fingers
From the matn: cutting all five fingers of the hand incurs half the full diya (since each finger is one-tenth). This is fixed.
This applies regardless of whether the cut is at the finger joints; the entire finger is considered.
2
Extend to cutting with the palm
When the fingers are cut together with the palm, the same half diya applies because the palm is a follower (tābi') of the fingers. No extra compensation for the palm itself.
The principle of tābi'iyyah: the subsidiary part follows the principal in diya.
3
Analyze the forearm variant
If the cut includes half the forearm (i.e., fingers + palm + part of forearm), the palm (including fingers) still gets half diya, but the extra forearm part (beyond the wrist) requires a government assessment (hukumat al-adl) because it is not covered by a fixed diya.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
4
Contrast Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf held that cutting the fingers with half the forearm is effectively cutting the entire hand (up to the wrist), so only the hand's fixed diya (half) is due. No separate assessment.
He considers the forearm portion as part of the hand, not an extra beyond the fixed limit.

Separate Arsh and Diyah for Loss of Senses Due to Head Wound
Ruling that loss of senses requires arsh of wound plus full diyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a head wound causing loss of a sense requires both arsh and diyah, and explain the distinction from loss of mind.

Core Idea
A head wound causing loss of a sense triggers both arsh and full diyah, as each sense is like a separate limb.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in diyat: when a mudihah (head wound exposing bone) leads to permanent loss of a sense such as hearing, sight, or speech. The Hanafi school holds that the victim is entitled to both the fixed compensation for the wound (arsh al-mudihah) and the full diyah for the lost faculty. The key distinction is with loss of mind, which does not attract separate diyah because its benefit is integrated. We will unpack the reasoning (ta'lil) that supports this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is causing a head wound (mudihah) that results in complete loss of hearing, sight, or speech. The ruling: wajib to pay both arsh al-mudihah and full diyah.
2
Locate the proof (ta'lil)
The proof is the logical reasoning: the senses are like separate limbs (a'da' mukhtalifah) with independent benefits. Hence, compensation for the wound and for the faculty are separate obligations.
No direct textual dalil is cited; the argument is by analogy and distinction.
3
Contrast with loss of mind
The text explicitly excepts the mind (bikhilaf al-‘aql), because the mind's benefit returns to the whole body. Thus, for loss of mind, only diyah is paid and the arsh is absorbed.
This contrast highlights the principle that independent benefits attract separate compensation.
4
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some schools may not require separate arsh, considering the loss as a direct consequence of the wound and thus merged into diyah. The Hanafi view relies on treating each sense as independently valuable.
The practical consequence: in Hanafi fiqh, the victim receives more compensation.
