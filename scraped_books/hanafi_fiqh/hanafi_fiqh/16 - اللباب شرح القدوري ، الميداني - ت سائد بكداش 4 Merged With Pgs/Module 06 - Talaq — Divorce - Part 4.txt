Core Idea

This lesson examines a specific scenario in khul' where the wife requests multiple divorces for a flat fee, but the husband executes only one. The Hanafi school apportions the compensation proportionally based on the number of divorces actually performed. Guided Path

If the wife had offered a thousand for two divorces and he divorced once, she would owe half. The rule is that the compensation is divided in proportion to the number of divorces requested.
This applies regardless of the amount or number, as long as the price is stated for multiple divorces.

This passage presents a key intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the interpretation of the word 'ala (على) in a khul' context. The wife offers a fixed sum for three divorces, but the husband divorces only once. Does she owe anything? The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, no payment, divorce is raj'i; under Sahibayn, payment of one-third, divorce is ba'in. This affects the husband's ability to revoke and the wife's financial liability.

Core Idea
A kinayah expression requires intention—the same phrase can be honor, Zihar, or divorce depending on what the husband meant.

In Hanafi fiqh, not all words are legally explicit. Expressions like 'You are to me like my mother' are considered kinayah (ambiguous)—their legal effect hinges on the speaker's inner intention. 2
List the possible intentions

Derive the ruling for each intention

The absence of the simile particle does not automatically make it explicit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal ruling for each possible intention when a husband says 'أنت علي مثل أمي' and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on dropping the ك.

Core Idea
Intentions determine if the phrase is zihar, divorce, honor, or nothing — and omitting the ك sparks a dispute.

This passage from Al-Lubab Sharh Al-Quduri explains how the husband's intention (niyyah) transforms a potentially ambiguous zihar phrase into different legal outcomes. The author also records an intra-Hanafi disagreement over the effect of omitting the letter ك (like) in the comparison. Understanding these nuances is essential for a mufti to evaluate such statements correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance and the role of intention
The husband says 'أنت علي مثل أمي'. The first step is to recognize that the phrase is ambiguous and can mean zihar, divorce, or honor. The ruling hinges on the husband's expressed intention.

If he intends zihar, it becomes zihar. If he intends divorce, it becomes an irrevocable divorce (bain). If he has no intention, it is nothing. These outcomes show that intention is decisive.

When the husband omits the ك (saying 'أنت علي أمي' instead of 'كأمي'), a dispute arises. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold the same rule: no intention means nothing. Muhammad al-Shaybani argues it becomes zihar without intention.

Analyze the tarjih (preference)
Later Hanafi authorities such as Jamal al-Islam, al-Burhani, and al-Nasafi prefer the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. Thus, the mufta bihi position is that the phrase without intention (or with dropped ك) has no legal effect.
Tarjih is based on the principle of ambiguity and avoiding automatic application of zihar.

The Expiation (Kaffarah) for Dhihar: Sequence and Conditions
Commentary on the ruling and conditions of dhihar expiation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sequential order of expiation for dhihar and identify which slaves are acceptable for expiation according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Expiation for dhihar follows a strict sequence: free a slave, then fast, then feed – all before any intimacy.

The prohibition created by dhihar is lifted only through a specific expiation (kaffarah) commanded by the Quran. This lesson explains the three types of expiation in their required order, the condition that expiation must precede intercourse, and the characteristics of an acceptable slave for expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that dhihar triggers an obligation (fard) to perform expiation before intercourse. The expiation is a composite obligation with three sequential options.
The ruling is derived from the imperative verbs in the Quranic verse.
2
Trace the sequential order from the Quran
Examine the verse (Al-Mujadilah 58:3-4): freeing a slave, then fasting two consecutive months, then feeding sixty poor persons. Note the conjunction 'fa' (then) indicating mandatory sequence.
The Hanafi school insists on this order; other schools allow choice.
3
Apply the condition of timing
The expiation must be performed before any physical intimacy (intercourse). This is commanded by 'min qabli an yatamassa' (before they touch).
Without prior expiation, intercourse remains unlawful.
4
Determine acceptable slaves for freeing
A valid slave must be fully owned and capable of utility. The text specifies that a believing or disbelieving slave, male or female, young or old are acceptable, but a blind slave or one missing both hands or both feet is not acceptable because such defects nullify the essential utility.
The term 'raqabah' (slave) encompasses all capable individuals; defects that remove all utility invalidate the expiation.
5
Note the multiplicity rule
If dhihar is pronounced against multiple wives, a separate expiation is required for each wife because the prohibition is established for each individually. This contrasts with ila (a vow of abstinence), where a single expiation suffices because the expiation is for the oath (God's name), which is not multiplied.
This distinction is mentioned in the commentary with reference to Al-Hidayah.

Conditions for the Slave in Zihar Expiation
Rulings on valid slaves for zihar expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid slave in zihar expiation and apply the Hanafi principle of fā'it jins al-manfa'ah to determine validity.

Core Idea
A slave whose defect destroys the entire genus of benefit is legally dead and invalid for expiation.

In zihar, a husband who declares his wife like his mother must perform expiation (kaffarah) before intercourse. One option is emancipation of a slave. But not any slave suffices. The Hanafi school sets clear conditions: the slave must be fully owned and retain the potential for all types of benefit. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proof, using the key concept of loss of an entire genus of benefit (fā'it jins al-manfa'ah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is emancipation of a slave as expiation for zihar. The ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) before intercourse, and the emancipation must be of a valid slave.
See the general rule: every expiation element must be sound.
2
Locate the general permissibility
The text states that any slave – Muslim or non-Muslim, male or female, young or old – is valid in principle. The proof is that the name 'raqabah' applies to them.
The focus is on the essence of being owned, not on religion, gender, or age.
3
Identify the invalidating defects
Blindness in both eyes, amputation of both hands or both feet, amputation of both thumbs, and insanity lacking intellect are invalid. These destroy the entire genus of benefit, making the slave legally dead.
The criterion is loss of an entire category of benefit, not mere impairment.
4
Distinguish permissible impairments
Partial defects – deafness (if one can hear when spoken to loudly), loss of one hand or foot, loss of ears or nose, one eye or weak vision, castration – are permissible because they only impair benefit, not eliminate the genus.
The deafness exception: a rare narration (nawadir) is reconciled by restricting it to congenital deafness (akhras).
5
Apply the principle to new cases
Given the illa (fā'it jins al-manfa'ah), assess any defect: does it remove an entire category of benefit (like sight, grasping, intellect)? If yes, the slave is invalid; otherwise, valid.
This is a talil (reasoning) approach.

Freeing a Slave in Installments for Dhihar Kafarah
Intra-Hanafi dispute on freeing half a slave before and after intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that emancipation is divisible and evaluate the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the validity of freeing a slave in installments for dhihar kafarah.

Core Idea
Emancipation is divisible: freeing half is not freeing the whole, so the kafarah must be completed before intercourse.

This lesson addresses a precise legal disagreement within the Hanafi school: does freeing half of a slave count as freeing the whole for the purposes of dhihar expiation? The answer determines whether a man who frees half his slave, has intercourse, and then frees the other half has fulfilled his obligation. The dispute hinges on the nature of emancipation — is it divisible or indivisible? Let us examine the proofs and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the condition
The man frees half his slave, then has intercourse, then frees the remaining half. The Quran requires emancipation to occur before returning to marital relations (qabla al-masis). The question: is the condition satisfied?
The condition is from the Quranic text: 'before you touch each other' (Surah al-Mujadilah 58:3-4).
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and his proof
Abu Hanifa holds it is invalid because emancipation is divisible; only half was freed before intercourse, but full emancipation is required before. His proof is the literal reading of the condition.
Abu Hanifa views the act of freeing a slave as divisible into parts; each part is an independent emancipation.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position and their proof
The Sahibayn hold it is valid because emancipation is not divisible; freeing half is legally freeing the whole, so the whole was freed before intercourse.
They argue that the act of freeing is a single, indivisible act; any amount of freeing triggers the full emancipation.
4
Analyse the later correction and tarjih
Al-Isbiji corrected the attribution of the divisibility view, confirming it is Abu Hanifa's. Al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi followed this, making the mufta bihi that emancipation is divisible and thus the expiation is invalid.
The 'Tashih' section in the text affirms this correction.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If one follows Abu Hanifa (mufta bihi), the expiation is invalid and must be repeated. If one follows the Sahibayn, it is valid. In practice, the relied-upon view is Abu Hanifa's, so the expiation must be redone properly.
The practical consequence is whether the man must free another full slave.

If he fasts by days and breaks (iftaar) after 59 days, he must restart (الاستقبال). This implies that he must meet the required number with consecutiveness.
Breaking before completing the required number invalidates the entire sequence.

If he fasts 29 days by lunar month and then 30 days by solar days, it is permissible (جاز). The total would be 59 days, which satisfies the two-month requirement when one month is lunar (29) and the other is counted as 30 days.
The permissibility of mixing demonstrates flexibility in meeting the two-month obligation.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement about the effect of marital relations during the two-month expiatory fast for zihar. State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the fast must be restarted in full. Abu Yusuf holds that it may continue without restart.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Examine each side's proof
Abu Yusuf argues that intercourse does not invalidate the fast itself, so consecutiveness is preserved. The majority argues that the Quranic condition requires the fast to be free from intercourse entirely.

Feeding expiation requires sixty feedings to persons who can consume food; freeing another's slave or feeding a non-adolescent does not count.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal concept of li'an (mutual imprecation) and its essential rulings. The author, al-Maydani, begins with a linguistic definition: li'an derives from la'n (curse), and it is called li'an because the husband curses himself first. If the judge separates them before the wife's li'an, the separation is valid because the objective (the end of the marriage) is achieved. A key point: the wife's right to demand li'an does not expire over time; silence does not nullify her claim. This is based on the principle that the rights of people are not invalidated by the passage of time.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The wife must li'an after the husband; her mere confirmation does not incur hadd or negate lineage.

After the husband performs li'an, the process requires the wife to also perform li'an. If she refuses, she is imprisoned until she li'ans or confirms the husband's accusation. Invalidity of Li'an Due to Lack of Capacity
Rulings on when li'an is invalid due to lack of capacity (minor/insane wife, mute husband).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three cases where li'an is invalid in Hanafi fiqh and apply the hadith 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubuhat' to justify the ruling.

Core Idea
Li'an fails when the wife cannot be punished for zina or the husband cannot speak clearly—doubt blocks hadd.

Li'an is a solemn marital procedure intended to resolve an accusation of zina between spouses. However, it is not applicable in every situation. This lesson examines three specific cases where the Hanafi school rules that li'an is invalid: when the wife is a minor or insane, and when the husband is mute. The underlying principle is the hadith commanding the avoidance of hadd punishments in cases of doubt. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly applying the law of li'an.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios of invalid li'an
Read the passage and list the three types of parties that block li'an: minor wife, insane wife, and mute husband.
These are found at the beginning and middle of the Arabic text.
2
Understand the rationale for minor/insane wife
The text states that a stranger accusing such a woman would not be punished with hadd for qadhf. The husband stands in the same position, so he does not undergo li'an either. This is because the wife is not legally responsible for zina.
Hadd punishment for zina requires the person to be adult and sane.
3
Analyze the mute husband's case
Li'an requires explicit verbal declaration (tasrih). A mute's accusation is ambiguous, and ambiguity creates doubt. Hadd punishments are averted by doubts.
The text explicitly states 'la yata'allaqu bi al-tasrih' (it is connected to explicit speech).
4
Apply the hadith proof
The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubuhat' (Ward off hadd punishments by doubts). This general principle is cited as the evidence that when there is doubt (such as with a mute's unclear accusation), the hadd of qadhf and consequently li'an are not applied.
The hadith is recorded in Musnad Abu Hanifa and is also reported as mawquf from Umar ibn al-Khattab.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may allow a mute to perform li'an through writing or signs. The Hanafi school insists on explicit speech, considering writing as insufficient due to doubt.
This difference stems from varying interpretations of the requirement of tasrih and the scope of the doubt principle.

Commentary
This passage presents two rulings from the Book of Li'an where the li'an process is invalid. First, if a husband accuses his wife who is a minor or insane, there is no li'an and no hadd punishment for the husband. The reasoning is that if a stranger accused such a woman, he would not be punished with hadd because the woman is not legally responsible for zina; similarly, the husband does not undergo li'an. Second, if the husband is mute, his accusation does not trigger li'an because li'an requires explicit verbal pronouncement (tasrih), like the hadd of qadhf. Moreover, a mute's accusation is ambiguous, and hadd punishments are averted based on doubts (shubuhat). The commentator cites the well-known hadith 'Ward off hadd punishments by doubts' (recorded by Imam Abu Hanifa in his Musnad as marfu', and also reported as mawquf from several Companions including Umar ibn al-Khattab). This hadith serves as the proof (dalil) for the principle that punishments are suspended when uncertainty exists. The commentary emphasizes the authenticity of this hadith, rebutting those like Ibn Hazm who weakened it.

Key Terms
الصغيرة (al-ṣaghīrah): A female minor who has not reached puberty; legally incapable of committing zina that incurs hadd.
المجنونة (al-majnūnah): An insane woman who lacks legal capacity; similar to a minor in that hadd punishment for zina is not applicable to her.
الأخرس (al-akhras): A mute person who cannot speak; li'an is not possible because it requires explicit verbal declaration (tasrih).
