Property connection creates doubt; without it, ignorance excuses no one.

In the previous lesson, we saw that a man who had intercourse with his father's or mother's slave-girl was not punished if he claimed ignorance because of a property connection (inbisat fi al-mal). The key lies in the Hanafi concept of 'inbisat fi al-mal'—a tangible financial link that can generate a plausible shubha (doubt). Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which denial of lineage constitutes qadhf in the Hanafi school and identify who has the right to demand hadd for a deceased victim.

Core Idea
Denying lineage is effectively accusing the mother of zina, triggering hadd if she was chaste and the claimant is directly affected.

This passage from al-Lubab clarifies a subtle form of qadhf: denying a person's lineage. While the words may not explicitly mention zina, the Hanafi school treats such denial as an indirect accusation against the mother, because legitimate lineage is established only through the father. The text specifies when this constitutes a hadd offense and who can demand punishment for a deceased victim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the offending statement
The qadhif says something like 'You are not the son of your father' or 'O son of the adulteress.' These statements deny the person's lineage from his father.
The denial must be explicit; mere doubt or hesitation is not qadhf.
2
Understand the implied accusation
By denying the lineage, the speaker implies that the mother committed zina, because if the child is not from the husband, it must be from an adulterous relationship. Thus, the mother is indirectly accused.
This is why the mother's status matters: if she is muhsana (chaste, free, Muslim, adult), the hadd applies.
3
Check the mother's condition
The hadd for qadhf by denial is only applicable if the mother was a muhsana at the time of the accusation and is deceased. If she is alive, she herself is the victim and must demand the hadd. If she is absent, the demand is suspended.
The text explicitly restricts the ruling to the mother's death, but notes that for a deceased man, his usul and furu' may also claim.
4
Identify the proper claimant for the deceased
Only those whose own lineage is affected by the qadhf – the parents (usul) and children (furu') – can demand the hadd for the deceased victim. The disgrace extends to them because of their familial connection.
This limits standing: not every heir can demand, only those directly affected in lineage.
5
Apply the ruling consistently
The same principle applies if the victim is a deceased man: his father and son can demand hadd on his behalf. The key is whether the qadhf affects the lineage of the claimant.
If the mother is alive, she demands. If dead, the affected relatives demand.

Retraction After Confession of Slander: Not Accepted
States the ruling on retraction after confession of slander

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on retraction after confession of slander and explain why it differs from retraction in purely divine rights.

Core Idea
Retraction after confessing slander is not accepted because the victim's established right outweighs the retraction.

When a person confesses to the crime of slander (qadhf), that confession creates a fixed right for the victim to demand the hadd punishment. The Hanafi school holds that once this right is established, the confessor cannot simply withdraw his statement. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this rule and its contrast with crimes that are purely divine rights, where retraction is accepted because there is no individual claimant to deny the retraction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the passage and identify the core ruling: retraction after confessing to slander is not accepted. Note that this is stated as a fixed rule, not subject to conditions.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason given is that the slandered person (al-mad'huf) has a right in the matter. The confession established that right, so the retraction is rejected because it would negate the victim's claim. The victim himself can deny the retraction – he is the 'mukadhdhib' (one who contradicts the liar).
The victim's right is a personal right (haqq al-'abd), which is more demanding than divine rights.
3
Contrast with divine rights
The passage mentions a contrasting category: crimes that are purely the right of Allah (haqq Allah), such as theft or zina. In those cases, retraction after confession is accepted because there is no specific victim with a personal right to deny the retraction. The state (or judge) does not have a personal stake like the victim does.
This distinction is key: the presence of a victim with an enforceable personal right blocks the retraction.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider: A person confesses to falsely accusing someone of zina (qadhf). Later, he retracts. According to the Hanafi ruling, the retraction is invalid and the hadd punishment for qadhf (80 lashes) will still be applied if the victim demands it. Now contrast with a confession of theft: if the thief retracts, the retraction is accepted because theft is considered a divine right (even though there is a victim, the hadd is classified as haqq Allah in this context).
Note: For theft, the victim's right to the stolen property is separate; the hadd punishment itself is considered a divine right. The Hanafi classification can be nuanced.

Ta'zir for Insults: The Refinement of Ibn Abidin
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ta'zir scope

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on ta'zir for insulting nobles versus commoners and apply Ibn Abidin's refined criterion.

Core Idea
Ta'zir for insult depends on the victim's sensitivity, not social class.

This passage from al-Hidayah and Ibn Abidin's Radd al-Muhtar examines whether insulting a commoner with animal names warrants ta'zir. The initial Hidayah ruling restricts ta'zir to nobles, but later Hanafis expand it based on actual harm. Ibn Abidin shifts the criterion from lineage to noble character, aligning with the general principle of preventing harm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling from al-Hidayah
Read the first part of the source: 'And it is said: If the one insulted is from the nobles... he is subjected to ta'zir... if from the common people, he is not.' Note the distinction based on social status.
The Hidayah calls this 'the best view'.
2
Understand the basis for distinguishing nobles
The reason given: nobles feel offended (وحشة), implying commoners are not similarly affected. This is based on a presumed lack of sensitivity among commoners.
The underlying principle: ta'zir is to remove harm, and harm is relative.
3
Recognize al-Hinduwani's challenge
Al-Hinduwani argues for absolute ta'zir, citing the general rule: 'Whoever commits a wrong or harms a Muslim without right... is subject to ta'zir.' He rejects the class distinction.
This aligns with a broader reading of the principle of harm.
4
Analyze Ibn Abidin's refinement
Ibn Abidin does not fully adopt al-Hinduwani's absolutism but refines the criterion: ta'zir is for those of noble character (كريم النفس) regardless of lineage. A commoner with a sensitive nature deserves protection.
He explains that the Hidayah's mention of jurists and 'Alawis is because they usually have noble character, not because of status per se.
5
Apply the refined criterion
Given a victim, assess their personal dignity and sensitivity. A person of noble character who is actually offended by the insult merits ta'zir; one who is not sensitive or has brought shame on themselves does not.
This prevents the ruling from being merely a class privilege.
