Purchasing a Mahram Relative Without Lineage: Does It Enter the Kitabah?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on purchase of mahram relative by mukatab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a non-lineage mahram relative enters the mukatab's kitabah, identify the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and explain why later scholars preferred Abu Hanifa's view as istihsan.

Core Idea
A mukatab's earning capacity suffices for lineage relatives but not for in-laws; Abu Hanifa's view prevails as istihsan.

This lesson examines a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute: when a mukatab (a slave who has contracted kitabah for freedom) purchases a mahram relative who is not connected by lineage (e.g., a mother-in-law or a foster relative), does that relative automatically enter the kitabah, effectively becoming a co-mukatab? Abu Hanifa and his two pupils disagreed. Later Hanafi authorities resolved the dispute using istihsan. Understanding this case sharpens your ability to distinguish between qiyas (strict analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference) in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is a mukatab purchasing a mahram relative who has no lineage relationship (e.g., a relative by marriage). The core question: does that relative enter the mukatab's kitabah, i.e., become a party to the contract? State Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions.
The ruling is not about validity of the sale but about the legal effect on the relative's status.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the mukatab has only earning capacity (kasb), not full ownership (milk). Earning capacity is sufficient only for the connection (silah) of lineage relatives (e.g., father, son). For non-lineage mahrams, the obligation of connection is weaker, so earning capacity does not extend to them. Thus, the purchased relative does not enter the kitabah.
The underlying principle: maintenance obligations for lineage relatives are broader than for other relatives.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue by analogy to lineage relatives: since the obligation of connection (silah) applies to both lineage and non-lineage mahrams, the purchased relative should also enter the kitabah. They rely on qiyas to extend the ruling from lineage to non-lineage mahrams.
In freedom, if a free man buys his brother, the brother becomes free; they analogize to the mukatab's purchase.
4
Evaluate the tarjih (preference)
Later Hanafi authorities (Al-Isbiji, Al-Mahbubi, Al-Nasafi) sided with Abu Hanifa, considering his view as istihsan (juristic preference). This means the ruling is based on equity and the specific nature of the mukatab's limited ownership, overriding the strict analogy.
The tarjih makes Abu Hanifa's view the mufta bihi (operative ruling) for fatwa.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
According to Abu Hanifa, the purchased relative remains a slave of the seller; according to the Sahibayn, he becomes a mukatab jointly with the purchaser. This difference affects the relative's obligations under the kitabah contract.
The practical difference is whether the relative must contribute to the kitabah payments or remain a slave.

Mukatab's Purchase of a Non-Lineal Mahram Relative: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on mukatab buying relative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a mukatab's purchase of a non-lineal mahram relative, explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and identify the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.

Core Idea
A mukatab's kasb suffices only for lineal relatives; non-lineal relatives require affluence—hence Abu Hanifa excludes them from the kitabah.

This passage from Lubab al-Mukatab addresses a nuanced intra-Hanafi disagreement concerning the effect of a mukatab purchasing a close relative who is not a lineal descendant. While the mukatab's earning capacity (kasb) is recognized, the school splits on whether the obligation of silat al-rahim extends to such relatives in the context of kitabah. Understanding this dispute sharpens the application of principles regarding ownership, earning, and familial obligations in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Legal Context
A mukatab purchases a dhu rahim mahram relative who is not a lineal descendant (la walad lahu baynahuma). The key question: does this purchase enter the mukatab's kitabah contract (i.e., does the relative become free upon the mukatab's freedom)?
The purchase itself is valid; the dispute is over its legal effect.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and Reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the purchase does NOT enter the kitabah. His reasoning: the mukatab has kasb (earning capacity), not milk (full ownership). The obligation to support non-lineal relatives (silat al-rahim) is only upon the affluent (musir), which the mukatab is not, since he is still a slave. Kasb suffices only for lineal relatives (walad).
Key distinction: lineal vs. non-lineal mahram relatives.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position and Reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the purchase DOES enter the kitabah. They argue that the obligation of silat al-rahim is established by lineage, not by wealth. Just as a free person would free such a relative, the mukatab's purchase should similarly be treated as an act of freeing through kitabah.
They draw an analogy to the free person's treatment of relatives.
4
Analyze the Later Tarjih
Later Hanafi authorities (al-Isbijabi, al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi) preferred the Sahibayn's view, considering Abu Hanifa's position as istihsan (juristic preference) that is not operative for fatwa. The mufta bihi is that the purchase enters the kitabah.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin.
5
Derive the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Practical consequence: If the purchase enters, the relative becomes free upon fulfillment of the kitabah. If not, the mukatab may own the relative as property, and the relative remains enslaved unless freed separately.
This difference directly affects the status of the relative.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Kitab al-Mukatab concerning a mukatab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom) who purchases a close relative (dhi rahim mahram) who is not a lineal descendant (la wilad lahu baynahuma). The matn states that at Abu Hanifa, such a purchase does not enter into the kitabah, meaning the relative does not become free as part of the kitabah contract. The sharh explains the reasoning: the mukatab has kasb (earning capacity) but not full milk (ownership). The obligation to support relatives (silat al-rahim) applies only to lineal descendants and ascendants when the person has earning capacity; for more distant relatives, the obligation is only upon the affluent (al-musir). Since the mukatab is not affluent (he is still a slave), he is not obligated to spend on non-lineal relatives, hence the purchase is not for the purpose of freeing them through kitabah. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) counter that the obligation of silat al-rahim is established through lineage and is not limited by ownership; they compare to a free person who would free such a relative. The author of al-Tashih reports that al-Isbijabi classified Abu Hanifa's view as istihsan, a preferred ruling due to juristic preference, and this was adopted by later Hanafi scholars like al-Mahbubi and al-Nasafi. Thus, the relied-upon position is that of the Sahibayn, making the purchase enter the kitabah, i.e., the relative becomes free upon the mukatab's freedom.

كسب (kasb): Earning capacity; the mukatab is allowed to earn and use his earnings, but he does not own property fully (milk) until freedom.

Ruler's Procedure When a Mukatab Cannot Pay
Ruling on the ruler's action when mukatab cannot pay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure when a mukatab fails to pay an installment (najm), and identify the conditions under which the ruler waits or cancels the kitabah.

Core Idea
The ruler investigates and grants up to three days grace if the mukatab has expected assets, otherwise cancels at the master's request.

When a mukatab misses a payment, the ruler does not immediately cancel the contract. Instead, he must investigate whether the mukatab has any expected funds—such as a debt due to him or property held by an absent person. If yes, a brief respite of two to three days is granted. If no, and the master demands cancellation, the ruler declares the mukatab unable (ta'jiz) and dissolves the kitabah. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this balanced procedure.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling context
Understand the scenario: a mukatab fails to pay an installment. The ruler must act. Determine the legal status of the act—it is a wajib (obligatory) procedural duty for the ruler.
The ruler's investigation is mandatory before any decision.
2
Distinguish two conditions
The ruler queries the mukatab about his sources. Two cases: (a) mukatab has expected assets (debt or absent property) → wait up to 3 days; (b) no assets and master requests ta'jiz → cancel immediately.
The waiting period is defined analogously to other legal deferments.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The reasoning considers both sides: the master's right to payment and the mukatab's chance to fulfill the contract. The three-day limit is the standard for presenting excuses, not to be exceeded.
This talil shows Hanafi concern for both parties' interests.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi attribution
The ruling is attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. This implies Abu Yusuf may have differed, though the precise view is not detailed here.
In later works, the mufta bihi position is as stated.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Mukatab addresses the procedure when a mukatab fails to pay an installment (najm) on time. The ruler (qadi) is instructed to investigate the mukatab's circumstances. If the mukatab has outstanding debts owed to him or property in the hands of an absent person that will arrive soon, the ruler should not immediately cancel the kitabah (ta'jiz), but grant him a short respite of two to three days. This is to consider the interests of both the mukatab and the master. The period of three days is defined as the maximum for presenting excuses, analogous to the time given to a litigant or debtor to fulfill their obligations; it may not be extended. However, if the mukatab has no means whatsoever (la wajh lahu), and the master requests his declaration of inability (ta'jiz), then the ruler must declare him unable and annul the kitabah contract. This latter ruling is attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (al-Shaybani). The passage also indicates that the mukatab may be eligible for zakat funds (a note in the original mentions giving from zakat to free him, citing Qur'an 9:60). The reasoning is based on the principle of avoiding harm to both parties: the master should not lose his right to payment unnecessarily, and the slave should be given a chance if he has a reasonable expectation of funds.

تعجيز (ta'jiz): Declaration by the ruler that the mukatab is unable to pay, leading to cancellation of kitabah.
أنذر (istinzār): Granting a period of waiting; the ruler waits up to three days for the mukatab to bring funds.

Defining the Mukatab's Incapacity: Abu Yusuf vs. the Correct View
Defines incapacity with Abu Yusuf's view and tarjih for Abu Hanifa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conflicting views on when a mukatab is considered incapable (عاجز) and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The relied-upon Hanafi view rejects Abu Yusuf's two-installment requirement; incapacity occurs sooner.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about the definition of legal incapacity (ʿajz) for a mukatab—a slave under a kitabah contract. The moment of incapacity determines when the master can dissolve the contract and reclaim full ownership. Abu Yusuf held that two consecutive missed installments are required, while the correct view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) disagrees, implying a shorter threshold. Understanding this ikhtilaf helps you appreciate how mujtahid imams weigh caution and practicality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The core question: what constitutes legal incapacity (ʿajz) that dissolves the kitabah contract? The source text addresses this definitional dispute.
A mukatab is a slave with a contract to buy freedom through installments (nujum).
2
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf explicitly holds: 'He is not considered incapable until two installments come one after the other upon him.' This means two consecutive missed payments are required.
The passage quotes his view directly.
3
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
The commentator Jamal al-Islam declares their view as 'the correct view' (al-sahih). While not detailing their exact threshold, it is the opposite of Abu Yusuf's, i.e., likely a single missed installment suffices.
Later authorities (al-Burhani, al-Nasafi) adopt this view.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Abu Yusuf's view, the slave enjoys a grace period of two installments; under the correct view, the master can dissolve the contract earlier, protecting his right to receive payment promptly.
This affects the timing of reversion to full slavery.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative position)
The commentator and later scholars affirm that the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is the relied-upon position for fatwa. A mufti should adopt this view.
Even though Abu Yusuf's view is also a valid ijtihad, the school settled on the correct view.

Consequences of the Mukatab's Incapacity
States the legal effects when a mukatab is declared incapable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal consequences when a mukatab becomes incapable and explain why his earnings belong to the master.

Core Idea
When a mukatab becomes incapable, the kitabah dissolves automatically and all his earnings revert to his master.

This lesson examines the scenario where a mukatab is unable to continue paying installments. The text explains the legal effects: the contract dissolves, the slave returns to full slavery, and any property he acquired during the kitabah period now belongs to the master. Understanding this ruling clarifies the Hanafi view on suspended ownership (mawqūf) and the nature of the mukatab's earnings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger event
The passage mentions two ways incapacity is established: by court decree (bil-qada) or by mutual consent (ar-rida). Incapacity here means failure to pay as per the agreement.
2
State the legal outcome for the slave
The mukatab returns to the rulings of slavery (ya'ud ila ahkam ar-riqq). He loses all privileges of kitabah and becomes a full slave.
3
Determine the fate of his earnings
Any earnings (aksab) in his possession belong to the master.
4
Explain the legal reasoning (illa)
The earnings go to the master because it becomes clear that they were always the slave's earnings, and since the slave is property, his acquisitions are the master's. The concept of mawqūf (suspension) is lifted.
The earnings were in a state of suspension: not definitively owned by anyone until the contract's outcome was known.
5
Apply the ruling to a variant case
Consider a mukatab who paid some installments. The text implies that even partial payment does not affect the outcome; all earnings still go to the master upon incapacity.
But note: if the kitabah is completed, the slave becomes free and keeps his earnings. That is outside this passage.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A mukatab's actions that create debt or are pure donations are invalid because they are not from his permitted earning.

A mukatab is a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom. While he has certain legal capacities, his transactions are restricted to protect his ability to pay his kitaba. This lesson examines two specific invalid actions: freeing his own slave for a price and giving a gift conditioned on compensation. The underlying principle is that these are not from his category of earning (kasb) and impose debt on an effectively insolvent person.

Guided Path

A mukatab is a slave who has a kitaba contract to earn and pay for his freedom. He is not fully free but has limited capacity to own property and engage in trade for his earning.

Freeing his slave removes ownership and creates a debt in his liability. Since he is already insolvent (muflis) due to the kitaba debt, this is not a valid earning. The contrasting case of marrying a female slave is permissible because it is earning (via mahr).

Any transaction by a mukatab that is not from his permitted earning (kasb) or its accessories, or that creates unnecessary debt, is invalid. This includes marrying his own slave (due to burden of mahr and nafaqah).

Qisas for Limb and Eye Injuries: The Principle of Exact Similarity
Rulings on qisas for cutting hands, feet, nose, ear, and eye injuries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for qisas in bodily injuries and determine when qisas applies versus when only diyah applies, using the principle of exact similarity (mumathalah).

Core Idea
Exact similarity between injury and retaliation is the bedrock of qisas in wounds; where it's impossible, diyah replaces it.

The Quran commands qisas for wounds, but how do we apply it to different body parts? The Hanafi school rigorously requires exact similarity (mumathalah) in type, location, and amount. This lesson explores specific scenarios: cutting a hand from the joint vs. forearm, foot, nose, ear, and eye injuries. Mastering these distinctions prevents miscarriages of justice.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
Recall the Quranic verse 'wa al-juruha qisas' (5:45). The Hanafi school interprets this to require exact similarity (mumathalah) in every aspect of the injury and retaliation. If such similarity is possible, qisas is obligatory; otherwise, it is not.
This is the foundational rule for all qisas cases below the soul.
2
Analyze the hand scenario
Consider two sub-cases: cutting from the joint (mifsal) vs. from the forearm. From the joint, similarity is possible because the joint defines the same boundary on any hand; therefore qisas applies. From the forearm, forearm lengths vary between people, making exact similarity impossible; thus no qisas. Hand size is irrelevant because the function of the hand is the same.
The joint is the anatomical landmark ensuring identical placement of the cut.
3
Apply to other limbs: foot, nose tip, ear
The same reasoning holds for the foot (cut at the ankle joint), the tip of the nose (marin al-anf, the soft part below the bridge), and the ear (cut at its base). In each case, a precise anatomical boundary allows exact similarity.
These body parts have defined joints or clear boundaries that permit identical retaliation.
4
Examine eye injuries
Two distinct scenarios: (1) The eye is completely gouged out from its socket – qisas is not possible because eyes differ in shape and size, making exact similarity unachievable. (2) The eye remains intact but vision is lost – qisas is possible by applying a heated iron to the eye in a controlled manner to destroy sight, as this achieves similarity in the loss of the function (sight) without removing the eye.
The principle focuses on similarity in the harm inflicted, not merely the body part.
5
Conclude the rule
Summarize: Qisas is wajib for intentional injury to a limb or organ whenever exact similarity can be maintained in type, location, and measure. When similarity is impossible, the punishment shifts to diyah (blood money) or other discretionary penalties.
This rule ensures that retaliation is just and not excessive.

No Shibh 'Amd for Injuries Below Life
Ruling with proof: shibh 'amd only in homicide

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that shibh 'amd applies only to homicide and explain the qiyas-based rationale distinguishing injuries below life from killing.

Core Idea
Shibh 'amd depends on the instrument's lethality; for wounds, the instrument does not change the injury.

In Hanafi criminal law, the category of shibh 'amd (semi-intentional) occupies a middle ground between intentional and accidental homicide. But does this category extend to non-lethal injuries? The Hanafi school answers with a clear 'no'. In this lesson, we examine the ruling and the reasoning (qiyas) that restricts shibh 'amd to homicide alone, based on the nature of the instrument and the uniformity of wounds.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal category shibh 'amd
Recall that shibh 'amd in homicide means intentional act with a non-lethal instrument. Now ask: does this definition apply to injuries that do not cause death?
Shibh 'amd is a fiqh category, not a Quranic term.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The text explicitly rules: 'There is no shibh 'amd for below life; it is only 'amd or khata'.' Memorise this as a distinctive Hanafi position.
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) extend shibh 'amd to some injuries.
3
Understand the qiyas (analogical reasoning)
The ruling is based on qiyas: the 'illah (effective cause) for shibh 'amd is the instrument's lethality. For homicide, different instruments produce different outcomes (death or not). For wounds, the outcome (the wound itself) does not vary with the instrument; a cut is a cut.
The dalil is 'aqli (rational), not a specific nass.
4
Contrast with homicide
In homicide, if you strike with a sword (lethal) = 'amd; with a small stick (non-lethal) = shibh 'amd if death occurs accidentally. For wounds, striking with either instrument produces a wound of similar nature; thus only intent ('amd) or accident (khata) matter.
This shows consistency in Hanafi usul: the 'illah must be effective in both the original and the branch.
5
Apply to a real scenario
Example: A person deliberately hits another with a light cane, causing a bruise. According to Hanafi fiqh, this is 'amd (intentional battery), not shibh 'amd, because the instrument does not create a new category.
The diyah (blood money) for such injuries is calculated by different rules, but the intent determines liability.
