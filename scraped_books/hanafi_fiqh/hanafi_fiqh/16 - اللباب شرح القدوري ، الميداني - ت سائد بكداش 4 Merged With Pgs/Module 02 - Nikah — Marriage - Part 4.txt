Ruling on Wife Embracing Islam in Dar al-Harb
Ruling on a wife who converts to Islam in enemy territory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a wife who converts to Islam in Dar al-Harb and explain why the separation is deferred until the expiration of the iddah.

Core Idea
A wife's conversion in enemy territory does not dissolve the marriage until her iddah expires, preserving hope for the husband's conversion.

In classical Hanafi fiqh, the status of a marriage when one spouse converts to Islam in non-Muslim territory (Dar al-Harb) is nuanced. This lesson examines the case where the wife converts while the husband remains non-Muslim. The key ruling, as explained by the commentator, is that the marriage does not end immediately. Instead, the wife must observe a waiting period (iddah), similar to a revocable divorce, giving the husband an opportunity to also embrace Islam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the act
The scenario is a married woman living in Dar al-Harb who converts to Islam. The act is her conversion.
Dar al-Harb is territory under non-Muslim rule, usually hostile.
2
State the immediate ruling
The marriage is not automatically dissolved; the wife must observe iddah.
This defers the separation, unlike cases where conversion in Dar al-Islam may have different effects.
3
Explain the reason for delay
The husband's conversion is hoped for, but presenting Islam to him is impossible in enemy territory. Therefore, the marriage is treated like a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i).
The analogy to talaq raj'i means the husband can still return to the marriage by converting during iddah.
4
Describe the iddah conditions
The iddah is three menstrual cycles for menstruating women, three months for non-menstruating, or until delivery if pregnant.
This applies regardless of whether the marriage was consummated.
5
Determine the outcome
Once the iddah expires without the husband converting, the marriage is dissolved (the woman becomes separated).
If the husband converts before expiry, the marriage continues validly.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary explains the ruling for a wife who converts to Islam while living in Dar al-Harb (non-Muslim territory, often an enemy land). The key point is that the separation does not occur immediately upon her conversion. Instead, she must wait for her iddah (waiting period) to expire. The iddah is defined as three menstrual cycles for a woman who menstruates, three months for one who does not (due to age or other reasons), or until delivery if pregnant. This deferral is because there is hope that her husband will also convert, but since he is in hostile territory, it is not possible to present Islam to him immediately. The situation is analogized to a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i), where the marriage persists during the iddah. Once the iddah ends without the husband converting, the marriage is dissolved. Importantly, this ruling applies whether or not the marriage had been consummated.

Key Terms
دار الحرب (Dār al-Ḥarb): Territory under non-Muslim rule, usually in a state of war or conflict with Islamic lands.
عدة (‘iddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after separation from her husband before she may remarry.
طلاق رجعي (ṭalāq raj‘ī): A revocable divorce during the iddah period, where the husband can take back his wife without a new marriage contract.

Marriage Continuity and Dissolution in Dar al-Harb
Rulings on marital separation due to conversion/captivity in Dar al-Harb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on marital continuity when a spouse converts or is captured in Dar al-Harb, and apply the principle of difference of abode (ikhtilaf al-dar) to determine separation.

Core Idea
Conversion does not instantly dissolve marriage; iddah runs first. Captivity dissolves only if spouses are separated by abode.

When a married couple resides in non-Muslim territory (Dar al-Harb), the Hanafi school has specific rules governing their marriage upon conversion or captivity. Unlike immediate dissolution, the law often suspends separation to allow for the possibility of the other spouse embracing Islam. This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri explains the interplay of iddah, difference of abode, and the effect of slavery on the marital bond.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Determine the specific case: wife converts alone, husband converts alone, one spouse leaves Dar al-Harb, or one/both are captured. State the ruling: marriage continues until iddah expires, continues indefinitely, or dissolves immediately.
The ruling differs based on whether the spouses remain in the same abode.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The passage provides rational proofs (qiyas). For the wife's conversion, it analogizes to revocable divorce: the husband's conversion is hoped for but difficult, so the marriage is treated like talaq raj'i. For captivity, the proof is the difference of abode (ikhtilaf al-dar).
Dalil is aqli (rational) rather than naqli (textual).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the analogy works: in revocable divorce, the wife observes iddah and the husband can take her back; here, iddah is observed while hoping for the husband's conversion. For captivity, separation due to abode difference is analogized to the separation caused by leaving Dar al-Harb.
The analogy is based on the shared attribute of potential reunion or abode change.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
After consummated marriage, if separation occurs (e.g., by conversion), Abu Hanifa holds that no additional iddah is required beyond the ongoing one. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad require a separate iddah. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf: the timing of when the woman can remarry.
The dispute is over whether the ongoing iddah suffices or a new one begins.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's view, as confirmed by al-Durr and Ibn Abidin. Thus, in practice, no separate iddah is needed if the iddah from the marriage was already running.
Authority weight: zahir al-riwayah, tarjih by later Hanafi authorities.

Commentary
This passage from al-Lubab fi Sharh al-Quduri addresses marital rulings when one or both spouses embrace Islam or are captured in Dar al-Harb (non-Muslim territory). The passage type is a ruling statement with rational proofs. The text begins by restating a prior rule: if a husband divorces his wife before consummation, she is not entitled to the full dower (mahr). The commentary adds that the dower is confirmed by consummation and does not fall due to separation. The main discussion then treats the case of a married woman in Dar al-Harb who becomes Muslim. Contrary to immediate dissolution, the marriage persists until her iddah (waiting period) expires—three menstrual cycles, three months, or delivery if pregnant. This is because the husband's conversion is hoped for, but presenting Islam to him is impractical, so it is analogized to a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i). The separation takes effect only upon completion of iddah, regardless of whether the marriage was consummated. Next, the passage covers the husband of a kitaabiyyah (Jewish or Christian woman) converting: the marriage remains valid since such a marriage is permissible initially. When one spouse leaves Dar al-Harb to Dar al-Islam as a Muslim, separation occurs due to the difference of abode (ikhtilaf al-dar). Similarly, if one spouse is captured (by Muslims) and becomes a captive, separation occurs for the same reason. However, if both are captured together, no separation occurs because the difference of abode does not apply; only slavery arises, which does not negate the marriage. Finally, a point of intra-Hanafi disagreement: if separation occurs after consummation (e.g., by conversion), Abu Hanifa holds that no additional iddah is required beyond the ongoing one, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) require a separate iddah. The editor notes that the relied-upon position (as per al-Durr and Ibn Abidin) is Abu Hanifa's view.

بينونة (baynunah): Complete separation that makes the woman unlawful to the husband without a new marriage contract.
دار الحرب (dar al-harb): Territory under non-Muslim rule where Islamic law is not enacted.
سبي (saby): Capture of a person in war, resulting in slavery.

Rulings on Spouses Converting or Emigrating from Dar al-Harb
Author states rulings on marriage continuity when one or both spouses convert or emigrate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on marriage continuity when one or both spouses convert or emigrate from Dar al-Harb, and identify the intra-madhhab dispute regarding 'iddah after separation.

Core Idea
Conversion of one spouse does not dissolve marriage; emigration or capture of one alone does.

This passage from Kitab al-Nikah addresses the critical question of what happens to a marriage when one or both spouses leave Dar al-Harb for Dar al-Islam, whether by conversion, emigration, or capture. The rulings depend on whether the spouses remain in the same abode (dar) and on the timing of the separation. A subtle intra-Hanafi difference emerges regarding the 'iddah for a woman after separation due to conversion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Determine whether the change is by conversion, emigration, or capture, and whether it involves one or both spouses.
2
Apply the rule of tabayun al-dar
If the spouses end up in different abodes (Dar al-Harb vs Dar al-Islam), separation occurs.
Tabayun al-dar means difference of abode.
3
Check same abode
If both convert and remain in the same dar, or both emigrate/captured together, marriage remains valid.
Slavery does not invalidate marriage.
4
Determine 'iddah after separation
If separation occurred before consummation, no 'iddah by consensus. If after, note the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
5
Resolve the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa says no 'iddah; the Sahibayn require a fresh 'iddah. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

Woman Emigrating from Dar al-Harb: Marriage and 'Iddah
Author rules on immediate remarriage for a female emigrant and the intra-Hanafi dispute on 'iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the mufta bihi Hanafi ruling on 'iddah for a female emigrant and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two disciples.

Core Idea
No 'iddah for a female emigrant — Abu Hanifa's view prevails over the Sahibayn.

Have you ever wondered what happens to a woman's marriage when she flees a non-Muslim land and arrives in a Muslim country? Does she need to wait before remarrying? The passage you are about to study tackles this very question. It presents a fascinating intra-Hanafi dispute, where Abu Hanifa takes a bold position based on a clear Quranic verse, while his two main disciples argue for a more cautious approach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the ruling
Read the first part of the passage: a woman emigrates as a muhajira from Dar al-Harb to Dar al-Islam. According to Abu Hanifa, she may marry immediately with no 'iddah. Identify the act (remarriage) and the ruling (mubah).
Note the exception for pregnancy.
2
Locate the evidence of Abu Hanifa
The passage cites Quran 60:10: 'And hold not to the marriage ties of disbelieving women.' Understand how this verse implies immediate dissolution of the marriage upon her emigration, thus no 'iddah is needed.
The verse is qat'i in transmission and meaning according to Hanafi usul.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's counterargument
The two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that the separation occurred after consummation in Dar al-Islam (since she is now in the Abode of Islam), so the normal rules of 'iddah apply, requiring three menstrual cycles.
This is a qiyas on regular divorce cases.
4
Analyze the response of Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa's response is that the Quranic verse is general and its clear meaning prevents any delay; requiring 'iddah would be clinging to the marriage tie, which the verse explicitly forbids.
The difference of dar itself severs the marriage; no need for formal divorce.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its authority
Later authorities like al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, and Sadr al-Shari'ah chose Abu Hanifa's view as correct. Thus the operative ruling for fatwa is no 'iddah.
The passage also notes the question of istibra' (one menstrual cycle) remains unresolved.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Marriage Without Witnesses or During Iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding upholding a marriage contracted without witnesses or during iddah upon conversion, and explain the reason for the tarjih (preferred view).

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa upholds a convert's marriage lacking Islamic conditions; Sahibayn don't; but the preferred view is Abu Hanifa's.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: when a non-Muslim couple marries without Islamic requirements like witnesses or proper iddah (according to their own custom) and then converts to Islam, is their marriage valid? The passage from Zad al-Fuqaha presents Abu Hanifa's view that the marriage is upheld, opposed by his students Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and Zufar, and then confirms the tarjih in favor of Abu Hanifa. Understanding this difference is crucial for applying Hanafi fiqh in contemporary conversion cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Disputed Case
The case: a disbelieving couple marries in a way that would be invalid in Islam (no witnesses or during what Muslims consider iddah), but it is valid in their own religion. They later both convert to Islam. Is their marriage upheld?
Focus on the condition: the marriage was valid in their prior faith, not merely a void union.
2
State the Positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa rules: the marriage is upheld upon conversion. Abu Yusuf, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Zufar rule: it is not upheld; they must remarry.
The Sahibayn demand that Islamic conditions (witnesses, correct iddah) be present at the time of the contract, even for converts.
3
Understand the Rationale Behind Each Position
Abu Hanifa: the marriage was valid in their religion; upon conversion, they are left as they are, barring an explicit Islamic prohibition that conflicts. The Sahibayn: Islamic conditions are universal; a contract lacking them cannot be recognized.
The key distinction: iddah for a disbeliever is not the same as for a Muslim. A disbeliever may not regard a waiting period, so it is not treated as a violation.
4
Identify the Tarjih (Preferred View) and Its Supporters
The text states: 'the correct is the statement of the Imam' (Abu Hanifa). This is relied upon by later Hanafi authorities: al-Mahbubi, al-Nasafi, al-Mawsili, and Sadr al-Shari'ah.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling for fatwa) is thus Abu Hanifa's position.
5
Derive the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
Under the preferred view, the couple remains married without a new contract. Under the Sahibayn's view, they must contract a new marriage with proper witnesses and correct iddah.
This has direct implications for couples converting today: they are not required to repeat their marriage if it was valid according to their prior faith.

A wife may give up her turn, but can revoke it because she dropped a right not yet due.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from al-Hidayah on the division of nights among co-wives. The core issue is whether a wife's voluntary waiver of her turn is final or revocable. Identify the act and its legal classification
