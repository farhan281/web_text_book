Manumission of the Coerced and Intoxicated
Ruling with proof via qiyas to divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on manumission under duress and intoxication, and distinguish between prohibited and permissible causes of intoxication.

Core Idea
Duress does not invalidate manumission; only intoxication from a permissible cause renders it void.

This lesson examines two common scenarios where a master utters a manumission: under coercion or while intoxicated. The Hanafi position is that duress does not remove legal capacity, so the act takes effect. However, intoxication is divided into prohibited (e.g., voluntary drinking) – where the act is valid – and permissible (e.g., necessity), which is akin to unconsciousness and invalidates the transaction. Let's explore the reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
What is being ruled upon? Manumission (freeing a slave) uttered by a coerced person or an intoxicated person. The ruling is that it is valid if the intoxication is from a prohibited cause.
The ruling is sahih (valid/effective).
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The text says it is 'as previously discussed in divorce,' indicating qiyas (analogy) to divorce. In Hanafi fiqh, divorce under duress is valid; similarly, manumission under duress is valid because both are unilateral dissolutions relying on utterance.
The proof is not explicit here but relies on an established principle.
3
Analyze the Rationale
Why is it valid? Because the utterance 'proceeded from its proper person (ahliyyah) in its proper place (mahall).' Duress does not negate legal capacity for such acts.
This is a qiyas jali (clear analogy).
4
Distinguish Between Prohibited and Permissible Intoxication
The Hanafis restrict the validity to intoxication from a prohibited cause (like voluntary drinking of alcohol). If intoxication is from a permissible cause (e.g., forced to drink by necessity), it is like unconsciousness and all transactions are invalid.
The key is the nature of the intoxication, not the intoxication itself.

Conditional Manumission: Valid by Qiyas to Divorce
States that attaching manumission to ownership or a condition is valid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why conditional manumission is valid in the Hanafi school and apply the reasoning to similar cases of release (isqat).

Core Idea
Manumission is a release (isqat), so it can be conditioned like divorce.

This lesson addresses a common question: can a master make a slave's freedom depend on a future event? The Hanafi school answers yes, because manumission is a release of rights, and releases can be suspended. We'll see the proof by analogy to divorce and apply it to examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is conditional manumission (ta'liq al-'itq). The ruling is that it is valid (sahih).
The text states 'صح' (valid).
2
Locate the dalil
The author uses qiyas: manumission is compared to divorce, which is also valid when conditional.
The proof is aqli (rational) based on the shared characteristic of being an isqat.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Both manumission and divorce are acts of releasing a right (isqat). Since suspension is permissible in divorce, it is permissible in manumission by analogy.
The common illa is 'being an isqat'.
4
Apply the ruling to examples
Two examples: (1) condition of future ownership: 'If I possess you, you are free'; (2) condition of an action: 'If you enter the house, you are free'. In both, the slave becomes free upon fulfillment.
The condition must be a future possibility.

Freedom of the Escaped Muslim Slave
Ruling with rational proof on slave freedom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the freedom of a slave who escapes from dar al-harb as a Muslim and explain the rational basis for this ruling.

Core Idea
A Muslim slave entering dar al-Islam becomes free, for a Muslim cannot be enslaved.

This brief but powerful rule from the Hanafi school addresses the status of a slave who escapes from non-Muslim territory (dar al-harb) and arrives in Muslim lands already a Muslim. The ruling is decisive: he becomes free. The underlying rationale is rooted in the inviolability of a Muslim's freedom. Let us examine the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
Read the text: a slave from dar al-harb comes to dar al-Islam as a Muslim. What is the legal consequence? The text says 'he becomes free' (عَتَق). This is the ruling.
2
Identify the proof (dalil)
The text gives a reason: 'because when he entered dar al-Islam, his hand became apparent while he is Muslim, so he cannot be enslaved.' Identify that this is a rational (aqli) proof, not a direct nass. The 'hand' refers to Islamic authority/protection.
3
Analyze the proof structure
Break down the proof: (1) The slave entered dar al-Islam. (2) He is a Muslim. (3) In dar al-Islam, a Muslim's person is inviolable; he cannot be enslaved. Therefore, the slave is free. This is a qiyas: the original case is a free Muslim in dar al-Islam; the new case is this slave; the illa (effective cause) is being a Muslim present in dar al-Islam with Islamic authority.
This is a 'qiyas jaliy' (clear analogy).
4
Consider any conditions or exceptions
The ruling applies when the slave arrives as a Muslim. What if he arrives as a non-Muslim? He would remain a slave. Also, the slave must have entered with the intention of staying? The text implies he 'comes out' to us, so he is fleeing, not just visiting.
These are implicit conditions from the wording.

Ruling with rational proof on freed slave's fetus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that freeing a pregnant slave frees her fetus and explain the reasoning by analogy to a limb.

Core Idea
Freeing a pregnant slave frees her fetus because the fetus is like a part of her body.

In this lesson, we examine a clear Hanafi ruling: when a master frees his pregnant slave, the unborn child is also freed. This is not a separate optional act; it is a necessary consequence. The source passage explains the rationale and confirms that the master cannot exclude the fetus from manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the master freeing his pregnant slave. The ruling is that both mother and fetus are freed (sahih).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author provides a rational proof: the fetus is like a member of the mother's body. Therefore, freeing the mother necessarily includes the fetus.
This proof is aqli (rational), not textual, but is accepted as a valid istihsan or analogy.
3
Analyze the analogy (qiyas)
The fetus is analogous to a limb: just as freeing a person frees all their body parts, freeing the mother frees the fetus. Excluding the fetus is like excluding a limb—invalid.
The illah (effective cause) is the inseparability or unity of the mother and fetus in legal consideration.
4
Note the practical consequence
If the master says 'I free her but not the fetus,' his exception is void. The fetus is freed regardless, and the master cannot retain ownership over it.

Freeing the Fetus Alone: Ruling and Conditions
Explains the Hanafi ruling on freeing a fetus separately

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on freeing a fetus separately from its slave mother, identify the condition for its validity (known existence), and explain why the mother does not become free.

Core Idea
Freeing the fetus alone frees the fetus, not the mother—but only if the fetus is known to exist (born within six months).

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi manumission law: a master frees only the fetus of his pregnant slave. The passage from Al-Hidayah explains the ruling and its rationale, focusing on the critical role of the fetus's existence at the time of the declaration. Understanding this ruling clarifies the limits of freedom by dependency (taba'iyyah) and the importance of certainty in legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The master declares freedom specifically for the fetus, not the mother. The ruling: the fetus becomes free, but the mother remains a slave.
The act is a direct manumission of the fetus.
2
Understand the rationale for the mother not being freed
The master did not intend to free the mother (no mention of her), and freeing her by dependency would invert the natural order (the follower becoming the followed).
This is based on logical reasoning (talil): the subject is reversed.
3
Identify the condition for the fetus's freedom
The fetus must be known to exist at the time of the declaration. This is established if the baby is born within six months of the statement (the minimum gestation period). If born after six months, its existence is uncertain, so it is not freed.
There is also a special rule for a woman in iddah: if born within two years, it confirms conception before the declaration.
4
Apply the rule to twin births
If twins are born, one before six months and one after, both are freed because they come from a single pregnancy. The existence of the first confirms the second.
The ruling is based on the unity of the pregnancy (haml wahid).
5
Note the effect on clientage
When the fetus is freed intentionally (not by dependency), its clientage (wala') does not transfer to the father's masters. It belongs to the manumitter.
This distinguishes from cases where the mother is freed and the fetus follows by dependency.

Freeing a Slave for Money: Conditions and Categories
Details the conditions and implications of freeing a slave for a financial consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid manumission for a consideration and distinguish between unconditional and conditional manumission.

Core Idea
Acceptance in the same session without intervening action makes a slave free immediately; the debt is binding.

This passage from Al-Lubab explains the Hanafi ruling on the ʿitq ʿala māl (manumission for property). It covers two scenarios: an unconditional offer (e.g., 'You are free for 1000 dirhams') and a conditional offer (e.g., 'If you pay me 1000 dirhams, you are free'). The slave's acceptance must occur in the same session without delay, and the property must be sufficiently known or else the slave pays his own value. We will examine these rulings and their practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two forms
Find the passages describing unconditional and conditional manumission. Note the key phrases: 'anta ḥurrun ʿalā alf dirham' and 'in adayta ilayya alfa dirham...'.
The unconditional form uses 'ʿalā' or 'bi-', while the conditional uses 'in adayta'.
2
Conditions for acceptance
For the unconditional form, acceptance must occur in the same session before the slave stands up, engages in another action, or speaks about another matter.
This condition ensures continuity of intent.
3
Effect of acceptance
Upon valid acceptance, the slave becomes free immediately (ʿataqa) and the stipulated property becomes a binding debt (lazimahu al-māl).
Freedom is immediate; payment is deferred.
4
Conditional form details
In the conditional form, the slave becomes a ma'dhun (permitted to trade) to earn the payment, but remains a slave until payment is made.
The debt is binding, but freedom is conditional on payment.
5
Property specification
The term 'mal' includes cash, goods, animals, and even food if the genus is known. Minor ignorance of description is tolerated. If the ignorance is excessive (e.g., 'a garment' without any specification), the slave becomes free and owes his own market value (qīmat nafsihi).
This is a concession to facilitate the transaction.

Conditional Tadbir: No Immediate Effect
Explains that conditional tadbir is not immediately effective

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on conditional tadbir and explain why the master retains full disposal rights over the slave.

Core Idea
A conditional tadbir is not effective immediately; the master can still sell the slave.

Tadbir is a promise of freedom upon the master's death. But when the master ties it to a condition that may not happen, such as dying from a particular illness, the tadbir has no immediate legal effect. This lesson explains the reasoning behind this ruling and its practical implication: the slave remains fully owned and can be sold, mortgaged, or gifted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Focus on the master's statement: he attaches tadbir to a condition like 'if I die from this illness' or 'on this journey'. Recognize that the condition is future and uncertain.
The condition is contingent on a specific event that may never occur.
2
Analyze the cause (sabab) for tadbir
Understand that tadbir normally becomes a cause for freedom upon death. Here, the cause is not established because the specified death is not certain to happen. The ruling says the tadbir does 'not constitute a cause at present'.
The uncertainty negates the sababiyyah (causality).
3
Compare with unconditional tadbir
Unconditional tadbir immediately gives the slave a right (the master cannot sell him). In conditional tadbir, the master retains full ownership because the tadbir is merely a suspended condition (ta'liq) like any other condition that does not bind the master.
The distinction turns on whether the tadbir is effective at the time of utterance.
4
State the ruling
Since the tadbir is not effective, the master may sell, mortgage, or gift the slave. The slave does not acquire any rights until the condition is fulfilled.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Freed from the Entire Estate: Umm al-Walad Upon the Master's Death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the umm al-walad's freedom upon the master's death and explain why she is not required to perform sa'yah for creditors.

Core Idea
Umm al-walad is freed from the entire estate because the child's need for its mother is essential, taking precedence over debts and inheritance.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh regarding the status of an umm al-walad (a female slave who bears a child to her master) when the master dies. The passage establishes that she is unconditionally freed from the entire estate, even if the master leaves behind debts, and that she cannot be compelled to work (sa'yah) to pay off those debts. The reasoning (ta'lil) hinges on the essential need of the child and the unique legal status of the umm al-walad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Act and Its Ruling
Determine what legal consequence is being stated. Here, the act is the freeing of the umm al-walad upon the master's death, and the ruling is that she is freed from the entire estate, meaning she becomes free immediately without any deduction or condition.
The Arabic verb 'عَتَقَت' (she became free) is in the past tense, indicating an immediate and automatic effect.
2
Locate the Proof (Ta'lil)
Identify the reason given for this ruling. The passage states: 'because the need for the child is essential' (لأن الحاجة إلى الولد أصلية). This is the core legal cause (illa) that justifies the priority.
'أصلية' means essential or primary, as opposed to supplementary needs.

The passage compares the priority of the umm al-walad's freedom to the priority of shrouding (تكفين) the deceased. Just as shrouding expenses are taken from the estate before debts and inheritance because it is an essential need, so too is the freeing of the umm al-walad.
Shrouding is a wajib expense that takes precedence over other claims, establishing a precedent for essential needs prevailing over debts.
4
Distinguish from Tadbir
Note that the ruling for tadbir (a declaration that a slave will be free after death) is different. Tadbir is treated as a bequest (وصية) related to supplementary needs, so it is limited to one-third of the estate. The umm al-walad is different because her connection to the child creates an essential, not supplementary, need.
Tadbir is analogous to a will, whereas the umm al-walad's freedom is a direct consequence of motherhood, not a testamentary act.
5

The passage adds that the umm al-walad is not considered fungible property (مال متقوم) according to Abu Hanifa. Therefore, creditors' rights do not attach to her, and she cannot be forced into servitude to pay debts.
'مال متقوم' refers to property that has commercial value and can be exchanged. The umm al-walad is not such, so she cannot be used as an asset to settle claims.

The Principle of Division (Tajzi'a) and Exceptions in Joint Ownership
Principle of tajzi'a and exceptions in joint ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of tajzi'a to rights arising from joint ownership and identify when exceptions apply (father or Muslim partner).

Core Idea
Rights divisible by nature are split; indivisible rights are fully enjoyed by each partner, except when one partner has a preponderance (father or Muslim).

In addition to the main ruling on dual paternity, the text introduces a general principle governing joint ownership: tajzi'a (division). This principle states that rights which can be divided are split among partners, while those that cannot are enjoyed in full by each. However, exceptions arise when one partner is the father of the other or when one is Muslim and the other a dhimmi. This lesson explains these exceptions and the condition that conception must occur in joint ownership.

Guided Path

Identify the Principle
The text presents tajzi'a: for divisible rights, each partner gets a share; for indivisible rights, each gets the full right.
Example: jointly-owned slave woman – each partner's right to intercourse? Actually, the 'uqr is divisible. The right to paternity is indivisible? The child is son to both fully.
2
Apply to the Case
In the umm walad case, the 'uqr is divisible (each owes half), while paternity and inheritance rights are indivisible (each has full son/father relationship).
This is why the child inherits fully from each.
3
Note the Exceptions
If one partner is the other's father, or one is Muslim and the other a dhimmi, the ruling changes because of preponderance.
The Hidayah mentions: the father has a superior right, and Islam gives preponderance.
4
Condition of Conception in Ownership
The ruling only applies if conception occurred during joint ownership. If the slave was pregnant when bought, it's a claim of emancipation, not istilad.
Then no 'uqr and mother is not umm walad.

Master's Intercourse with Mukatib's Slave Girl
Ruling when a master has intercourse with his mukatib's slave and claims paternity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for establishing lineage when a master claims a child from his mukatib's slave girl and explain why the master must pay 'uqr.

Core Idea
Lineage is established by the master's ownership cause, but 'uqr compensates for incomplete ownership.

This lesson examines a nuanced case where a master has intercourse with a slave girl owned by his own mukatib and then claims paternity. The Hanafi ruling balances two principles: the master's residual ownership over the mukatib's property (sufficient for establishing lineage out of caution) and the fact that the master does not have full sexual rights over that slave girl (hence requiring compensation, 'uqr). The condition for lineage is the mukatib's confirmation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal outcome
The act is the master having intercourse with his mukatib's slave girl and claiming the resulting child. The ruling: if the mukatib confirms, lineage is established, and the master owes 'uqr.
2
Understand the proof for lineage
Lineage is proven not by full ownership but by a 'cause of ownership' (sabab al-milk) — the mukatib's bondage means his property is ultimately tied to the master. Caution (ihtiyat) is applied in lineage matters, so this suffices.
3
Analyze the basis for 'uqr
The master must pay 'uqr because his intercourse was not preceded by full ownership of the slave girl. Though he owns the mukatib, the mukatib's right to earn and own is partially independent. Sexual use requires complete ownership, which is absent here.
4
Note the condition for both rulings
The condition is the mukatib's confirmation (tasdiq). Without it, lineage is not established and no 'uqr is due? The text does not explicitly state the consequence of denial, but the ruling hinges on confirmation.
Hanafis hold that if the mukatib denies, the master's claim fails.
5
Apply the ruling to a similar scenario
Consider a master who sells his mukatib a slave girl, then after the sale has intercourse with her. The same principle applies: the master has a residual connection but not full ownership, so if a child is born and claimed, the mukatib's confirmation establishes lineage, and 'uqr is owed.

Mukatab's Denial of Paternity: Lineage and the Umm Walad Ruling
Intra-Hanafi dispute on lineage establishment for mukatab's child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school requires the mukatab's confirmation for lineage and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A mukatab's denial of paternity blocks lineage because establishing it would invalidate his property rights.

This passage from the Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a mukatab (a slave working to purchase his freedom) who claims or denies paternity of a child born to his slave-girl. The core issue is whether the child's lineage can be established without the mukatab's confirmation. The apparent riwaya requires confirmation, but Abu Yusuf disagrees, drawing an analogy to a father claiming his son's slave-girl's child. Understanding this ruling requires analyzing the mukatab's unique property rights and the logical distinction that differentiates his case from the analogy.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (apparent riwaya)
Identify the ruling from the text: if the mukatab claims the child, it is born free upon paying its value, but the slave-girl does not become umm walad. If he denies, lineage is not established unless he confirms.
This is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
2
Identify Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Abu Yusuf argues that the mukatab's confirmation is not required; lineage can be established solely based on the claim. He analogizes to a father claiming a child from his son's slave-girl, where the son's confirmation is unnecessary.
The opposing qiyas is based on the father's ownership rights.
3
Follow the opposing proof (qiyas)
Explain the qiyas: asl = father claims child of son's slave-girl (lineage established without son's confirmation). far' = mukatab claims child of his slave-girl. The apparent illa is that both claim a child from a slave-girl under their control.
The objector (Abu Yusuf) uses this qiyas to argue for no need for confirmation.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response (distinction)
The apparent riwaya rebuts by distinguishing the illa: the father can own the child from his son's slave-girl because he has full ownership over his son's property. In contrast, the master of a mukatab cannot own the mukatab's acquisitions; the mukatab has a protected right to own his earnings. Therefore, establishing lineage would invalidate the mukatab's property right, requiring his explicit confirmation.
This distinction (farq) is the core of the tarjih.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under the apparent riwaya, if the mukatab denies paternity, the child does not inherit from him and remains a slave to the master. Under Abu Yusuf, the child would be free and inherit, potentially undermining the mukatab's financial obligations under the kitaba contract.
The mufta bihi follows the apparent riwaya.

Effects of a Valid Kitabah Contract
Explaining legal effects: exit from hand but not ownership, and consequences of manumission by master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two primary effects of a valid kitabah contract and explain the legal reasoning behind the master's retained ownership.

Core Idea
A mukatab leaves the master's hand but stays his property until payment—kitabah is an exchange.

This passage from the Lubab explains the legal consequences of a valid kitabah contract. Kitabah is a contract of exchange where the slave purchases his freedom by paying installments. The key distinction is that the mukatab gains physical and economic freedom (exits the master's hand) but legally remains the master's property until the full compensation is paid. This nuanced status is justified by comparing kitabah to a sale contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its validity
The passage begins with 'When the kitabah is valid' meaning the contract meets its pillar (offer and acceptance) and conditions (e.g., known compensation, the slave's consent).
2
State the first effect: exit from hand
The mukatab exits the master's yad (hand). This means the master loses physical control and cannot use the slave's services; the mukatab can earn and move freely.
This achieves the purpose of kitabah: enabling the slave to work and pay.
3
State the second effect: retained ownership
Despite exiting the hand, the mukatab does not exit the master's milk (ownership). The master still owns the slave's person.
Ownership serves as security for the unpaid debt.
4
Explain the legal reasoning
Kitabah is a contract of exchange (mu'awada), analogous to sale. In a sale, the buyer gets possession but the seller retains ownership until full payment? Actually in sale, ownership transfers immediately. The passage says 'it requires equality between the contracting parties; and that is negated by immediate emancipation and achieved by its delay.' So here the equality is: the master gives gradual freedom, the slave pays gradually. If freedom were immediate (like a gift), the master would get nothing. By delaying full freedom, the master retains ownership as a lien.
The qiyas (analogy) with exchange contracts explains the intermediate status.
5
Note the effect of master's emancipation
If the master unilaterally frees the mukatab before full payment, the slave becomes free (because the master owns the person) and the kitabah debt is dropped.
This shows the master's ownership is not a lien but full ownership; he can still free his property.

Mukatab Buys Father or Son: They Enter His Kitaba
Ruling that a mukatab's purchase of father/son enters them into his kitaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a mukatab purchases his ascendant or descendant, and explain the legal reasoning (silh and analogy to free person) that supports it.

Core Idea
When a mukatab buys a close relative, the relative enters the kitaba—not freedom—to maintain family ties.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling within the Hanafi law of kitaba: what happens when a mukatab (a slave who has contracted with his master to earn his freedom) purchases his own father or son? Intuitively, one might expect the purchased relative to become free, as would happen if a free person bought a relative. However, since a slave cannot free another slave, the law employs a different mechanism: the relative enters the mukatab's own kitaba. This ruling illustrates how the Sharīʿah pragmatically preserves family bonds even within the constraints of slavery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what action is being ruled upon and what the outcome is. Here, the act is a mukatab purchasing his own father (or ascendant) or son (or descendant). The ruling is that the purchased relative 'enters into his kitaba' – that is, the relative becomes a mukatab under the same kitaba contract.
The text explicitly says 'دخل في كتابته' (he enters into his kitaba).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
Identify the evidence or reasoning the author provides. The proof here is not a direct text from Quran or Sunnah, but a qiyas (analogical reasoning). The author cites the principle that the mukatab is 'from among those eligible to make a kitaba contract' even though he is not eligible to free a slave. Then he draws an analogy: 'Do you not see that the free person, when he has the power to free, the purchased relative becomes free?'
The phrase 'ألا يُرى' signals an analogy: just as a free person frees a purchased relative, a mukatab enters them into the kitaba.
3

The underlying reason (ʿillah) for this ruling is 'تحقيقاً للصلة بقدر الإمكان' – to achieve family connection (ṣilah) as much as possible. Since a slave cannot free, the law uses the next best available option: including the relative in the kitaba, which eventually leads to freedom.
The concept of ṣilah (family tie) is central to the Hanafi reasoning.
4

Consider the scope: this ruling applies only to ascendants (father, grandfather, etc.) and descendants (son, grandson, etc.). It does not apply to other relatives like siblings or cousins, because the bond of lineage (nasab) is strongest for direct ascendants/descendants.
The text mentions 'أباه وإن علا' (father even if above) and 'ابنه وإن سفل' (son even if below), indicating all degrees of ascendants/descendants.
