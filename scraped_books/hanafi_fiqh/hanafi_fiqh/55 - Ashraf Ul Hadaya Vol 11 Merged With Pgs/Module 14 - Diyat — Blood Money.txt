Settlement (Sulh) for Unintentional Killing (Khata')
Ruling on sulh for khata' and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh in unintentional killing and identify when an excess over the diyah is rejected.

Core Idea
Sulh for unintentional killing is like a sale of property; you cannot exceed the fixed diyah.

In this passage, we learn that unintentional killing (jinayat al-khata') creates a property obligation (diyah), making settlement (sulh) akin to a sale. However, because the diyah amount is fixed by Shariah, any settlement that exceeds it is void for the excess. This lesson explains the reasoning, the exception for qisas, and the important condition of spot acceptance (qabd) when different property types are exchanged.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Nature of the Right
Recognize that in unintentional killing, the required compensation is property (diyah), not a physical punishment like qisas. This makes sulh akin to a sale (bay').
Diyah is fixed by Shariah, so it is not subject to negotiation beyond the prescribed limits.
2
Compare with Sulh for Qisas
Contrast the rule for khata' with intentional killing: for qisas, an excess over diyah is permissible because qisas is not property and is valued only by the contract.
This highlights that the nature of the underlying right determines the permissibility of exceeding diyah.
3
Apply the Condition of Spot Acceptance
When the sulh involves a different type of property (e.g., goods instead of the same diyah type), immediate possession (qabd fi al-majlis) is required to avoid a debt-for-debt exchange (which is prohibited).
If the same type of diyah is agreed, no spot acceptance is needed because it is a simple exchange.
4
Understand the Effect of Judicial Ruling
If a judge has already determined a specific diyah amount, a subsequent sulh for a different type with an increase is valid because the right is specified. This differs from initial sulh where such an increase is not allowed.
The judge's ruling removes the ambiguity, making the exchange akin to a sale of a specified debt.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sulh discusses the ruling of sulh in unintentional killing (jinayat al-khata'). Unlike intentional killing where the underlying right is qisas (a non-property right), in unintentional killing the required compensation is diyah (property), making the sulh akin to a sale. Therefore, one cannot settle for an amount exceeding the prescribed diyah because the diyah is a fixed quantity ordained by Shariah. Any excess must be rejected. In contrast, in sulh for qisas, one can settle for more than the diyah because qisas itself is not property and is valued only by contract. However, if the settlement specifies one of the types of diyah, then no increase is allowed; but if a different type is agreed, it is permissible provided that the payment is taken in the same session to avoid a debt-for-debt exchange (which is prohibited). If the judge has already ruled for a specific diyah, then a later settlement for a different type with an increase is permissible because the right has been specified. The passage then states that sulh is invalid for claims of hadd (fixed punishments) because they are rights of Allah, not of the individual. Similarly, a mother cannot settle regarding the lineage of her child because that is the child's right. A person cannot settle for blocking a public path because it is a right of all Muslims. The hadd of slander (qadhf) is included in this ruling because the right of Shariah predominates. The final lines (from Kifayah) discuss that the invalidation of kafala upon sulh does not require consideration, and that when one agrees to drop a right for compensation, it falls as a sale.

Key Terms
جنایة الخطأ (jinayat al-khata'): Unintentional homicide or injury where the perpetrator had no intent to kill or cause harm.
قبض في المجلس (qabd fi al-majlis): Spot acceptance or taking possession in the same session of the contract, required to avoid a sale of debt for debt.

Invalidity of Sulh for Hudud, Lineage, and Public Rights
Ruling on invalidity of sulh for non-personal rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which types of claims cannot be settled by sulh according to the Hanafi school and explain the underlying rationale.

Core Idea
Sulh is invalid for hudud because they are rights of Allah, not personal claims.

The passage then shifts to the invalidity of sulh for claims involving fixed punishments (hudud), lineage of a child, and public paths. These are not personal rights but rights of Allah or of others, so they cannot be settled by the individual claimant. This lesson examines the rationale and scope of this prohibition, including the special case of hadd al-qadhf (slander).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Type of Right
Recognize that hudud are rights of Allah (haqq Allah), not personal rights. Therefore, the individual cannot waive them or settle them for compensation.
The same applies to rights of others (e.g., a child's lineage) and public rights (e.g., a public path).
2
Apply the Principle to Specific Cases
Apply the rule to (a) claims of hadd (any fixed punishment), (b) a mother claiming lineage of her child, (c) a person blocking a public path.
In each case, the individual lacks the authority to settle because the right belongs to Allah, the child, or the community.
3
Analyze the Special Case of Hadd al-Qadhf
Even though the victim of slander (qadhf) has a personal right to demand punishment, the view adopted is that the right of Shariah predominates, so sulh is invalid.
This is a strong position in the Hanafi school, emphasizing the public element.
