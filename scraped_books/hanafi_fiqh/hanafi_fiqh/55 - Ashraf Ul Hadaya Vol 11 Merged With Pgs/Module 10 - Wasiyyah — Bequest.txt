Priority of Debts from Health over Acknowledged Debts in Terminal Illness
Inter-madhhab dispute on priority of debts in terminal illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on priority of debts from health over debts acknowledged in terminal illness, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A debt acknowledgment in terminal illness cannot override existing creditors' rights.

When a terminally ill person acknowledges new debts, an immediate question arises: do these new debts compete with debts from before the illness? The Hanafi school argues that debts from health and known-cause debts take priority, protecting existing creditors. The Shafi'i school disagrees, insisting all debts are equal. This lesson dissects the arguments and the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of debts
Distinguish between (1) debts from health and debts from known causes during illness, and (2) debts acknowledged without cause during illness.
Health debts can be proven by witnesses or admission; known-cause debts include those from sale, hire, etc.
2
State the Hanafi position
The first category is paid first from the estate; the second only from the surplus after that.
3
Understand the Shafi'i objection
They argue the cause (admission) is the same in both cases, and analogize to mutual contracts valid in illness.
Imam Shafi'i, Malik, and others hold this view.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Admission cannot be a proof when it nullifies another's right; health creditors' right has already attached to the property.
This is analogous to the bar on gratuitous transfers beyond one-third.
5
Determine the practical difference
Hanafis pay health creditors first; Shafi'is distribute all debts proportionally.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and the Shafi'i school regarding the priority of debts when a person in terminal illness (marad al-mawt) acknowledges new debts. The Hanafi position, stated first in the matn, is that debts arising from health (dayn al-sihhah) and debts that became binding during illness due to known causes (e.g., purchase, hire) are given priority over debts that the sick person simply acknowledges during his illness without any known cause. The reasoning is that such acknowledgment is suspect because it may be a device to deprive existing creditors. The Shafi'i position, represented by al-Shafi'i, Malik, and others, holds that all debts are equal because the cause (sabab) – the admission itself – is the same: it proceeds from a sound mind and religion, and the dhimma is equally capable. They analogize to mutual contracts (sale, marriage) which are valid in illness as in health. The commentary (tashreeh) then elaborates: The Hanafi ruling applies to any debt from health (whether proven by witnesses or by acknowledgment, whether owed to an heir or a stranger) and any debt with known causes during illness. These are paid first from the estate, and only the remainder is used for the debts acknowledged without cause. Sufyan al-Thawri, Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, and even Imam Ahmad (by qiyas) agree. The text then presents a critical note (tanbeeh) on how al-Hidayah presented the Shafi'i proof, and the Hanafi response. Finally, the Hanafi proof (dalil) is given: an admission is not valid as a proof (dalil) when it entails invalidating another's right. In the sick person's admission, the right of health creditors has already attached to the property by way of istifa' (collection), so acknowledging a new debt would harm them. Therefore, just as the sick person is prohibited from gratuitous transfers (tabarru') and favoritism (muhabat) beyond one-third of the property, his acknowledgment of debts beyond that limit is also invalid. The text gives an analogy: if someone pledges a thing or gives it on hire, then acknowledges it belongs to another, that acknowledgment does not affect the pledgee's or lessee's right. Similarly, the health creditors' right is attached, so the acknowledgment of a new debt does not take priority.

Key Terms
مرض الموت (marad al-mawt): A terminal illness that most likely leads to death, as defined by legal criteria.
غرماء (ghuramā'): Creditors; those to whom money is owed.
تبرع (tabarru'): A gratuitous transfer of property without compensation.

Acknowledgment of Lineage for Non-Lineal Relatives: It Is Like a Will
Ruling on lineage acknowledgment for non-lineal relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledging lineage for siblings and uncles, explain why it is not accepted for lineage, and apply the rule that it is treated like a will in inheritance distribution.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a sibling or uncle as lineage is invalid for nasab but binding like a will on property.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a subtle distinction: when a person acknowledges another as a brother or uncle, such an acknowledgment does not establish legal lineage because it would attribute descent to a third party (the father or grandfather) over whom the acknowledger has no authority. However, the acknowledgment creates a property right analogous to a will (wasiyyah). Understanding this ruling requires careful analysis of the conditions under which the acknowledged person inherits and how it interacts with other testamentary dispositions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its dual ruling
Pinpoint the act: acknowledging lineage for a relative other than parent or child (e.g., brother, uncle). The ruling has two dimensions: lineage (nasab) is not established, but a property right similar to a will arises.
The text explicitly says 'his acknowledgment is not accepted' for lineage but then treats it like a will.
2
Determine the effect on inheritance
Contrast two scenarios: if there is a known heir (any relative with known lineage, near or distant), that heir takes precedence and the acknowledged person receives nothing. If there is no known heir, the acknowledged person is entitled to the entire estate.
The logic is that the acknowledger has full disposal over his property when there are no heirs, analogous to the authority to make a will.
3
Locate the analogy to a will
The author explicitly states the acknowledgment is 'like a will' (bi-manzilati al-wasiyyah) but not a real will. Identify the legal basis: the acknowledger's authority over his property in absence of heirs parallels the testator's power.
The analogy is tested with two hypotheticals to clarify its limits.
4
Examine the illustrative scenarios
First scenario: after acknowledging a brother, the person bequeaths all property to a stranger. The legatee gets one-third; the acknowledged person gets the rest (two-thirds). If the acknowledgment were a real will, they would share half each. Second scenario: during terminal illness, the acknowledger acknowledges a brother, the brother confirms, then the acknowledger denies the relationship and makes a bequest to another. The bequest is effective for one-third, and the retraction of acknowledgment is valid, so the estate goes to the legatee or to the public treasury if no bequest.
These contrasts show that the acknowledgment is inferior to a formal will but still carries weight.
5
Evaluate the madhhab difference
The Hanafi position is that the acknowledgment is effective only like a will. Imam Malik and Ibn Abi Layla differ on the share when there are other heirs (they give a portion). Imam Shafi'i holds that even the property right does not arise because lineage is not proven.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the Hanafi one as presented here.

He said: And whoever acknowledges lineage for other than parent and child, like brother and uncle, his acknowledgment is not accepted (in lineage). If he has a known heir, near or distant, that heir is more entitled to the inheritance than the acknowledged person. If he has no known heir, then the acknowledged person is more entitled to his inheritance because at that time he has the right to dispose of his property by will. And this is not a real will, but it is like a will. So that if he then bequeaths all his property to another, the property goes to the legatee to the extent of one-third. And if it were a real will, they would share half and half. But it is like a will such that if he acknowledges during his terminal illness, and the acknowledged person confirms, then the acknowledger denies the relationship, then bequeaths all his property to someone, the property goes to that legatee. And if he did not bequeath to anyone, it goes to the public treasury because his retraction is valid since the lineage was not proven, so the acknowledgment becomes void. Translation: Whoever acknowledges lineage other than parents and children, like brother and uncle, his acknowledgment is not accepted in lineage because it involves attributing lineage to another. If he has a known heir, near or distant, that heir is more entitled to the inheritance than the acknowledged person because when the lineage of the acknowledged is not proven from the acknowledger, he cannot compete with the known heir. Yes, if he has no heir, the acknowledged is entitled to his inheritance because the acknowledger has authority over his own property in the absence of heirs. Do you not see that the acknowledger has the right to bequeath all his property when there is no heir? So the acknowledged is entitled to all the property, even if his lineage from the acknowledger is not proven, because it involves attributing lineage to another and this is not a real will. So that if someone acknowledged someone as a brother and then bequeathed all his property to another, the legatee gets one-third of all the property. If it were a real will, they would share half and half. But it is like a will, such that if he acknowledged someone as a brother during terminal illness and the acknowledged confirmed, then the acknowledger denied the relationship and then bequeathed all his property to someone, all the property goes to that legatee. And if he did not bequeath to anyone, the property goes to the public treasury because his retraction is valid, as lineage was not proven, so the acknowledgment became void. Commentary on 'And whoever acknowledges lineage': If a person acknowledges lineage from other than parents and direct children, like brother, uncle, grandfather, grandson, etc., the acknowledgment is not accepted because it necessitates assigning lineage to another. For example, a brother is only such when he is the father's son, an uncle when he is the grandfather's son. So if the acknowledger has a known heir, whether close (like Quranic heirs and agnates) or distant (like uterine relatives), the inheritance of the acknowledger will not go to the acknowledged but to the known heir, because when the lineage of the acknowledged from the acknowledger is not proven, he does not compete with the known heir. He said: 'And this is not a real will' meaning acknowledgment of brotherhood etc. is not a real will but like a will. The clarification of not being a real will is from this: if someone acknowledges someone as a brother and then bequeaths all his property to another, the legatee gets one-third of all the property. If the first acknowledgment were a real will, they would share half and half. And the clarification of being like a will is from this: if someone during terminal illness acknowledges someone as a brother and the acknowledged confirms, then the acknowledger denies the relationship and then bequeaths all his property to another, all this property goes to that legatee. And if after his denial he did not bequeath to anyone, all the property goes to the public treasury because retracting from such a lineage acknowledgment is valid just as retracting from a will is valid. If the said acknowledgment were not like a will, retracting would not be valid.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on the effect of acknowledging lineage for relatives other than parents and children (such as brothers and uncles). The author, Al-Marghinani, explains that such an acknowledgment is not accepted to establish lineage because it involves attributing lineage to another (the father or grandfather) over whom the acknowledger has no authority. However, it is treated like a will (wasiyyah) with respect to inheritance. If the acknowledger has a known heir, that heir is more entitled to the inheritance; if no known heir, the acknowledged person takes all. The passage then illustrates that it is not a real will: if the acknowledger later makes a formal bequest, it takes precedence up to one-third, and the acknowledgment does not create a competing right. It is like a will because the acknowledger can retract it, just as a testator can revoke a will. The commentary in Urdu further clarifies that the ruling applies to all non-lineal relatives, and the acknowledged person does not compete with known heirs. This is based on the principle that the acknowledger has full authority over his property in the absence of heirs, akin to the authority to make a will.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrār): Acknowledgment or admission of a right or status by a person, which is binding on himself.
نسب (nasab): Lineage or descent, specifically paternal lineage.
بمنزلة الوصية (bi-manzilati al-wasiyyah): In the legal status of a will; having the same effect as a testamentary disposition in terms of property rights.
