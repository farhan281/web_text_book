Two Claimants with Equal Evidence: Option to Take Half or Leave
Ruling on conflicting claims of purchase with equal evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on two claimants with equal evidence and evaluate the alternative positions of the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools.

Core Idea
When two claimants present equal evidence without dates, each may choose half ownership or exit – preserving consent.

This passage addresses a recurring problem in legal testimony: two claimants each claim to have purchased the same item from the same seller, but neither specifies a date. The Hanafi school provides a unique solution by granting each claimant a choice (takhyīr). Understanding this ruling requires grasping how the school prioritizes the buyer's consent and how it differs from other madhāhib.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Scenario
Identify the key elements: two claimants, each says 'I bought this slave from the possessor', each produces evidence (bayyinah), no date mentioned.
Evidence is assumed equally strong; no priority by date.
2
State the Hanafi Ruling
Each claimant may either take half the slave for half the price, or leave the whole. This is an option (takhyīr), not an obligation.
The reasoning: each buyer's consent (riḍā) is affected by the possibility of wanting the whole.
3
Note the Other Schools' Narrations
Imam Mālik agrees with Ḥanafī. Imam al-Shāfiʿī and Imam Aḥmad have three narrations: (1) same as Ḥanafī, (2) casting lots, (3) invalidating both evidences and referring to seller.
The passage gives no proofs for these alternatives here.
4
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilāf
Practical outcome under Ḥanafī: co-ownership with choice. Under lots: one wins whole. Under invalidation: seller retains slave.
The Ḥanafī view is the muftā bihī in the Ḥidāyah.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute concerning two claimants who each claim to have purchased a slave from the same possessor and each present evidence of purchase without specifying a date. The Hanafi ruling, as stated in the commentary, is that each claimant has the right to choose: he may take half of the slave by paying half the price, or he may leave the entire slave. The reason given is that each buyer's willingness to proceed with the contract is affected because he may have desired the whole slave, but due to the conflicting claim, he is now only entitled to half. The passage then notes that Imam Malik, Imam Shafi'i, and Imam Ahmad have at least one narration that agrees with the Hanafi position. However, there are other narrations: from Shafi'i and Ahmad, a second narration requires casting lots between the claimants. A third narration from Shafi'i invalidates both evidences and refers the matter back to the seller. The Hanafi school relies on the first ruling, and the text implies this is the correct view.

Key Terms
بينة (bayyinah): Evidence, specifically the testimony of two witnesses (or one male and two females) establishing a claim.
تخيير (takhyīr): The right to choose between taking the half or leaving the whole.

Effect of Relinquishment After Qadi's Ruling in Conflicting Claims
Commentary on ruling and analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a claimant cannot take the whole slave after relinquishing his share post-ruling, and distinguish the rules for before-ruling and after-ruling scenarios.

Core Idea
Timing of relinquishment matters: after qadi's ruling, the sale is rescinded; before, the other claimant can take the whole.

When two claimants both prove ownership of the same item, the qadi may divide it between them. If one later relinquishes his share, the outcome depends critically on whether this happens before or after the judge's ruling. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains the reasoning, drawing an analogy to the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) and providing additional rules for when dates and possession are presented.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
State the main ruling: after the qadi rules the slave half for each, if one says he does not take his half, the other cannot take the whole.
The sale in that half is considered rescinded by the qadi's ruling.
2
Distinguish before vs. after ruling
Contrast the scenario with one where the relinquishment occurs before the qadi's ruling. In that case, the other claimant may take the whole.
Reason: no final judgment yet, so the other's claim to the whole remains.
3
Analyze the analogy to shuf'a
The commentary analogizes this to two pre-emptors: if one gives up his right before the qadi's ruling, the other takes the whole property; if after, he does not.
This shows the Hanafi method of qiyas (analogical reasoning).
4
Apply rules for date and possession
If witnesses provide dates, the earlier date prevails. If only one has a date, that claimant prevails. If neither has a date but one has possession, possession indicates prior purchase unless explicit testimony overrides.
These are supplementary rules for proving priority.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on a ruling from Al-Hidayah regarding conflicting claims over a purchased slave. The ruling states that if two claimants each prove purchase of the same slave, the qadi rules it half for each. If one then relinquishes his half after the ruling, the other cannot claim the whole slave. The reason given is that the qadi's ruling has effectively rescinded the sale in that half, and the contract cannot be revived without renewal. The claimant is considered a litigant (khasm) in that half because the other's entitlement has been proven by evidence. However, if the relinquishment occurs before the qadi's ruling, the other claimant may take the whole because no final judgment has been rendered. This is analogized to the case of shuf'a (pre-emption): if one pre-emptor yields before judgment, the other takes the entire property; if after, he does not. The passage also covers scenarios with dates: if one claimant proves an earlier date, he prevails; if only one has a date, he prevails; if neither has a date but one has possession, possession is preferred as it indicates prior purchase; but explicit testimony of earlier purchase overrides possession. This commentary follows the Hanafi method of analogical reasoning (qiyas) and preference (istihsan) in procedural matters.

Key Terms
خصم (khasm): A litigant or party against whom a claim is made; in this passage, the buyer who became a defendant after the qadi's ruling against him in the half.
شفعہ (shuf'a): The right of pre-emption (to purchase a co-owned property when it is sold to a third party); used here as an analogy.

Four Claimants, Different Causes of Ownership: Equal Division in Hanafi Law
Explains ruling when four claimants each present a different cause of ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when four claimants each present a different cause of ownership over the same property and explain the reasoning based on distinct transferors.

Core Idea
When four claimants each claim through a different transferor, each receives an equal quarter — the cause does not affect the division.

In property disputes, it is possible that multiple individuals each claim ownership of the same property through different legal transactions. The Hanafi school has a clear rule for such a scenario: if four claimants each present a distinct cause — purchase, gift with possession, inheritance, or charity with possession — and no one is in possession, the property is divided equally among them. This lesson unpacks the reasoning: each claim originates from a different transferor, making it as if the transferors themselves are asserting ownership. Understanding this principle helps you navigate complex multi-claimant disputes in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Recognize that the passage describes four claimants, each with a different cause of ownership: one claims purchase, another gift (hibah) with possession, another inheritance, another charity (sadaqah) with possession. The property is not in the hand of any claimant.
The causes are all valid modes of acquiring ownership in Hanafi law.
2
State the Ruling
The ruling is that each claimant receives a quarter (1/4) of the property. This is an equal division despite the different causes.
The ruling applies when no claimant is in possession (qabd) of the property.
3
Identify the Legal Principle
The principle is that each claimant is claiming ownership 'from his own transferor' (mumallik). The property is originally owned by the transferors, and each transferor's ownership is absolute (milk mutlaq). Since the claimants are not claiming from a common source, they are treated as independent claims.
The text says it is 'as if the four transferors themselves presented and established evidence on their absolute ownership.'
4
Apply the Principle
Because the claims are independent and equally strong in form (each is a valid legal claim), and no one has possession, the property is divided equally. The actual cause (purchase vs. gift etc.) does not affect the share because each cause is valid if proven, and the transferors are distinct.
If one claimant had possession, the ruling might differ, but this scenario assumes no possession.
5
Note the Textual Variant
The author discusses a textual variant in the phrase 'from those who sold them' (man ba'ahum). Some copies have 'man mulaqqibuhum' (from those who transferred to them). The author explains that the wording is inclusive (taghlib) because only one of the four transferors is a seller, but the phrase covers all. This shows attention to precision in legal texts.
This step is optional for understanding the ruling but shows the depth of Hanafi commentary.

The chapter of two men. He said: And if one of them claims purchase ... and if two of them, one claimed purchase from the seller, and the second claimed hibah with possession from some owner, and the third claimed inheritance from his father, and the fourth claimed sadaqah with possession from a person, then each of the four will be given a quarter. Because each claimant claims acquisition of ownership from his own transferor, so it is as if the four transferors themselves presented and established evidence on their absolute ownership. He said: 'from those who sold them' - in some copies it is 'from those who sold them' and both are by way of inclusion (taghlib) because among the four owners only one is a seller. And in some copies it is 'from those who transferred to them' (man mulaqqibuhum). They deduce by the word yatalaqqawna (they receive).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on a scenario where four claimants each present a different cause of ownership (purchase, gift, inheritance, charity) over the same property. The rule is that each receives a quarter of the property because each is claiming through a different transferor (mumallik). The reasoning is that it is as if the transferors themselves are present and each establishes their own ownership, so the property is divided equally among them. The author then notes a textual variant in the phrase 'from those who sold them' (man ba'ahum) explaining that it is inclusive of all transferors even though only one is a seller. This ruling is based on the principle that when multiple claimants have different legal origins of ownership, and the property is in the hand of none of them, they are treated as equal co-claimants.

Key Terms
شراء (shiraa): Purchase, a contract of sale transferring ownership in exchange for price
ہبہ مع القبض (hibah ma'a al-qabd): Gift with delivery of possession, a condition for validity of gift in Hanafi law
میراث (mirath): Inheritance, transfer of ownership from a deceased to his heirs
صدقہ مع القبض (sadaqah ma'a al-qabd): Charitable gift with delivery of possession
مملک (mumallik): Transferor, the person from whom ownership is obtained
ملک مطلق (milk mutlaq): Absolute ownership, ownership without reference to a specific cause or date

Preference of Evidence with Dates: The Daf' Principle in Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on preference of dated evidence in ownership claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Shaykhayn rule that the possessor with older dated evidence is preferred, explain the daf' reasoning, and identify the key variations in which Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad differ.

Core Idea
Evidence with a date implies daf'—later ownership must be acquired from a prior owner, so the possessor with older date repels the external claimant.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary examines a classic evidence dispute: when both an external claimant and the current possessor bring ownership documents with dates, and the possessor's date is older. The Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) rule that the possessor is preferred because his evidence inherently repels the claimant's case. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani initially disagreed but later recanted. We will unpack the legal reasoning and its applications in different possession scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Both the external claimant (kharj) and the possessor (dhu al-yad) produce evidence (bayyinah) of ownership with dates, and the possessor's date is older. Determine the initial ruling.
This is the basic case from which variations branch.
2
State the Shaykhayn's ruling
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that the possessor is preferred. This is also a narration from Muhammad, though he recanted from a contrary view.
The term 'Shaykhayn' here refers to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf together.
3
Analyze the daf' proof
The Shaykhayn argue that evidence with a date inherently contains the meaning of daf' (repelling the opponent's claim). The logic: if ownership is proven for a person at time T, any later ownership for another must be derived (talakki) from the first owner. Thus, the possessor's older date implies the claimant received ownership from the possessor, so the possessor's claim repels the claimant's.
This is a logical (aqli) proof, not based on a primary text.
4
Examine Muhammad's recantation
Muhammad had a narration that the possessor's evidence should not be accepted because both testimonies are on absolute ownership (milk mutlaq) without specifying the cause, making dates irrelevant. However, he recanted (raja'a) from this view, reinforcing the Shaykhayn's position.
The recantation lends weight to the Shaykhayn's view as the mufta bihi.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the possessor's evidence is accepted, he retains the property; if not, the external claimant wins. This difference directly affects the outcome of property disputes.
This shows why the tarjih matters in real cases.

Conflicting Purchase Claims: Shaykhayn vs Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tahaatur

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling when two parties present contradictory purchase claims without dates and distinguish the positions of the Shaykhayn and Imam Muhammad, including their proofs and the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
When purchase claims contradict without dates, the evidences cancel and possession prevails.

In judicial proceedings, when both parties claim to have purchased the same property from each other but neither provides a date, how does a Hanafi judge resolve the conflict? This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding their reasoning and the final relied-upon position is crucial for applying Hanafi evidentiary rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
Two parties each claim to have purchased a property from the other. Both present witnesses but no dates. Determine the core conflict: whose evidence prevails?
The property is currently in the possession of one party (dhu al-yad).
2
State the Shaykhayn's ruling and proof
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that the two evidences cancel each other (tahaatur) and the property remains with the possessor. Their proof: each purchase act implies an acknowledgment of the seller's ownership, so the two acknowledgments contradict and cancel by consensus. Additionally, the cause (purchase) does not yield its intended effect (ownership) here.
The key concept: tahaatur (cancellation) is applied to contradictory acknowledgments.
3
State Imam Muhammad's ruling and proof
Imam Muhammad accepts both evidences and awards the property to the non-possessor. His proof: a consistent sequence is possible — the possessor bought first, took possession, then sold back without delivery. Possession indicates precedence, and sale before possession is invalid even in real estate according to Muhammad.
Muhammad's view relies on the possibility of a consistent timeline.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical consequence: under the Shaykhayn, the possessor retains the property; under Muhammad, the non-possessor gets it. This affects property rights and judicial outcomes in similar cases.
The difference hinges on whether the contradictory acknowledgments are cancelable or a consistent sequence can be constructed.
5
Determine the mufta bihi position
The text states 'this is according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf', and the later Hanafi tradition relies on their view as mufta bihi. Thus, in fatwa, the evidences cancel and property stays with the possessor.
The tarjih authority: the text itself and the weight of the Sahibayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) in the Hanafi school.

Conditions for Mutual Set-Off (Qisas) of Price in Conflicting Sales
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditions for mutual set-off of prices

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Shaykhayn require proof of actual payment (qabd madmin) for mutual set-off of prices, and distinguish Imam Muhammad's position that the obligation alone suffices.

Core Idea
For qisas of price, the Shaykhayn require proof of actual payment; Imam Muhammad accepts mere proof of obligation.

In disputes where two parties each claim to have purchased from the other, witnesses may testify to the contracts but not to the payments. This raises the question: do the two price obligations automatically cancel (qisas)? The Hanafi school contains two views on this matter. The following passage from Al-Hidayah presents the positions of the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Imam Muhammad, with the relied-upon view being that of the Shaykhayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal question
Two parties each present witnesses that they bought from the other. The issue is whether the price obligations offset each other (qisas). Two sub-scenarios arise: witnesses testify to payment or not.

State the Shaykhayn's position and condition
If witnesses testify to actual payment, qisas occurs because each side has qabd madmin (guaranteed possession). Without such testimony, no qisas according to them.
Qabd madmin requires physical or constructive receipt of payment.
3
State Imam Muhammad's position and reasoning
Even without payment testimony, qisas occurs because the sale evidence establishes the wujub (obligation) to pay, and two mutual obligations cancel each other.
Imam Muhammad focuses on the contractual debt rather than actual receipt.
4
Analyze the basis of disagreement
The difference stems from what constitutes a sufficient basis for set-off: actual possession (qabd madmin) vs. mere personal obligation (dayn).
5
Determine the practical consequence and relied-upon view
Under the Shaykhayn, without proof of payment, each party must separately enforce the debt. Under Muhammad, the debts cancel automatically. The Shaykhayn view is mufta bihi.
This affects court rulings and party strategies.

Consensus on Cancellation: When Both Prove Sale and Delivery
Ruling on cancellation of evidence when both prove sale and delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school unanimously cancels conflicting testimonies of sale and delivery and apply this ruling to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
When both parties prove delivery, the physical impossibility of mutual transfer cancels both testimonies — a consensus even Muhammad joins.

In this passage, Al-Marghinani clarifies a critical boundary in evidence law: when both disputants bring witnesses proving both sale and delivery (qabd), the testimonies cancel each other. This ruling commands consensus among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad, even though in the earlier case (without delivery) Muhammad held a different view. Understanding why delivery triggers this consensus reveals the Hanafi emphasis on physical impossibility as a decisive factor in evidence evaluation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties' claims
Two parties each claim that the other sold and delivered the property to them. Both produce witnesses testifying to their own version.
This is a conflict of testimonies, not of rights.
2
Contrast with the previous case (without delivery)
Recall that when only sale was proven (no delivery), Muhammad allowed reconciliation by sequential execution. Here, delivery adds a physical act that cannot happen from both sides simultaneously.
Delivery (qabd) is a tangible, exclusive act.
3
Understand the rationale: impossibility of collective qabd
If both deliveries were true, the same property would have been physically transferred in opposite directions at the same time, which is impossible. Even Muhammad, who permits sequential sale, cannot accept simultaneous contradictory deliveries.
The reason is 'al-jam'u ghayru mumkin' — combination is impossible.
4
Recognize the consensus (ijma)
All three Imams — Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad — agree that the evidences cancel each other. This is a rare point of full agreement in a contested area.
Consensus strengthens the ruling and removes any practical dispute.
5
Apply the ruling
Since the evidences cancel, the property remains with the current possessor, as no valid evidence overrides the other.
The practical outcome is that the status quo prevails.

Abu Hanifa's Division When Claims Overlap: Whole vs Half
Ruling when one claims whole and another claims half

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Abu Hanifa's rule of munaza'ah to divide property when one claimant proves the whole and another proves half.

Core Idea
Disputed portion is divided equally; the rest goes uncontested.

When two claimants each present evidence on the same property, but their claims only partially overlap, how do we allocate shares? This lesson unpacks Abu Hanifa's method, which is based on the 'method of dispute' (tariqat al-munaza'ah). The key is to identify the portion that both are fighting over and award that portion equally, while the portion claimed by only one goes to that claimant unchallenged.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claims
One claimant says the whole house is his. The other says half is his. Note that the half claimant does not dispute the other half.
The claims overlap only over half the house.
2
Isolate the disputed portion
The disputed portion is the half claimed by both. The other half is undisputed.
3
Divide the disputed portion equally
According to Abu Hanifa, each claimant gets half of the disputed half: that is one-quarter of the total house each.
So the half claimant only gets his quarter from the disputed half.
4
Add the undisputed portion
The whole claimant gets the undisputed half (the half the other did not claim) plus his one-quarter from the disputed half, totaling three-quarters.

Resolving Claims on an Animal Using Age as Evidence
Ruling on animal dispute with birth evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of circumstantial evidence (hal) to resolve a property dispute when each claimant produces evidence with a date and the animal's age is known.

Core Idea
The animal's age acts as circumstantial evidence to prefer one claimant or override false claims.

When two people dispute ownership of an animal and each brings evidence that it was born in their possession with a date, how does a Hanafi judge decide? This lesson explores a ruling from al-Hidayah where the animal's age—an objective fact—resolves the conflict. By understanding this, you will see how the Hanafi school uses circumstantial evidence (hal) alongside formal testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
Two persons each claim an animal and produce evidence (bayyinah) that it was born in their possession. They also provide a date for the birth.
The evidence is assumed valid initially.
2
Check the animal's age against the dates
The judge examines the animal's age (سن) and compares it with the two dates. There are three possible outcomes: age matches one date, age is ambiguous (matches both or neither clearly), or age contradicts both dates.
Age is determined by experts or apparent signs.
3
Apply the ruling for each outcome
If the age matches one date, that claimant is preferred because the present condition (hal) testifies for him. If ambiguous, the animal is shared equally. If the age contradicts both dates, both evidences are invalid due to apparent falsehood, and the animal remains with the current possessor.
The ruling is based on rational preference (tarjih) via hal.
4
Understand the legal reasoning
The age is an external, objective fact that strengthens one claim. When it matches, the hal supports that claimant's evidence, making it preponderant. When it contradicts both, the falsehood of both is clear, so the status quo (possession) prevails.
This is a form of istihsan and reliance on apparent condition.

Equal Evidence: Gasp vs. Deposit Claim on a Slave
Ruling on slave claimed as gasp or deposit by two outsiders

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when two claimants present equal evidence on contradictory claims, the disputed property is divided equally.

Core Idea
Equal evidence on contradictory claims splits the property equally.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, we encounter a scenario where two outsiders bring evidence against a possessor: one claims the possessor usurped the slave, the other claims he received it as a deposit. Both proofs are equally credible. The Hanafi ruling responds by dividing the slave equally between the two claimants. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning (qiyas khafi) and the principle that equal strength of evidence leads to equal division.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and claims
Determine who the possessor is, and what each claimant asserts: one claims ghasb (usurpation), the other wadi'ah (deposit). Both produce evidence against the possessor.
The possessor is not a party to the dispute between the two claimants; he holds the slave.
2
State the ruling
The slave is divided equally between the two claimants. This is the explicit ruling of the matn.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The commentary adds that a deposit becomes ghasb if the depositary denies it, thus making both claims subject to liability. The key point is that the two pieces of evidence are equal in strength, so neither claim outweighs the other.
This is a form of qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) based on the principle of equality in evidence.
4
Note the cross-madhhab status
While the Hanafi school rules equal division, other schools may require additional evidence or prefer one claim over the other. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The ruling is mukhtalaf fih (disputed among schools).
5
Apply the principle to analogous cases
When any two claimants present equally credible but contradictory claims over a single item in third-party possession, the Hanafi school divides the item equally. This principle extends beyond slaves to other fungible goods.
The modern application: if two people claim ownership of a car held by a garage, each with a bill of sale, the car is shared equally.

Possession Indicators in Property Disputes
Rulings on property disputes decided by physical possession indicators

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of preference based on physical possession (tasarruf) to resolve property disputes in cases with multiple claimants.

Core Idea
In a dispute, the person with more apparent physical control over the property is preferred.

When two people claim ownership of the same movable property, the judge must decide based on outward indicators. The Hanafi school provides clear examples: a rider prevails over someone merely holding the reins; a wearer prevails over someone holding the sleeve. This lesson unpacks the principle of 'more apparent tasarruf' and its application to common disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed property and the claimants
Read each example in the passage and note the property (animal, cloth, carpet) and the two claimants.
Each case involves one item and two rival possession indicators.
2
Compare the degree of tasarruf
Analyze which claimant has more direct and exclusive physical control. Riding, wearing, and loading are strong; merely holding a rein or sleeve is weaker.
Tasarruf includes acts like riding, wearing, loading; sitting is not considered full possession.
3
Apply the rule: prefer stronger possession
Whichever claimant's tasarruf is more apparent (azhar) is given preference. If equal (e.g. both sitting), the property is shared.
The rule is based on the idea that tasarruf is specific to ownership (yakhtass bi al-milk).
4
Note exceptions where possession is equal
If both claimants have the same level of tasarruf (e.g. both riders on the same animal, or one sitting vs one pulling a carpet), the property is divided equally.
Sitting on a carpet is not considered a yad (hand), so it does not give preference.
5
Apply the torn cloth analogy
If a cloth is in one hand and a torn piece in another, both parts are from the same source, so the cloth is divided equally. The 'increase' does not increase entitlement.
This is an exception to the general rule: possession of a part does not give full ownership.

Section on dispute by hands. He said: And if two dispute over an animal, one being its rider and the other holding its reins, then the rider is preferred because his possession is more apparent; indeed, riding is specific to ownership. Similarly, if one is riding in the saddle and the other is his pillion rider, then the one in the saddle is preferred, unlike if both are riders where it is between them because they are equal in possession. Similarly, if they dispute over a camel with a load for one of them and a hanging jug for the other, then the owner of the load is preferred because he is the possessor. Similarly, if they dispute over a shirt, one wearing it and the other holding its sleeve, then the wearer is preferred because his possession is more apparent. If they dispute over a carpet, one sitting on it and the other holding it, then it is between them, meaning not by way of judicial decree because sitting is not considered a hand (possession), so they are equal. And if a cloth is in the hand of one man and a tear from it (a piece) is in the hand of another, then it is between them half and half because the increase (the tear) is of the same kind as the original, so it does not cause an increase in entitlement.

Commentary
This section (fis) provides rulings on disputes where possession indicators are physical. The key principle is that the person with more apparent control or use of the property is preferred. Examples: a rider over someone holding the reins; the saddle rider over a pillion rider; the owner of a load over someone holding a jug; the wearer of a shirt over someone holding its sleeve. However, sitting on a carpet is not considered strong possession, so if one sits and another pulls, it is shared. If a cloth has a torn piece in another's hand, both possess parts of the same cloth, so it is divided equally. The proof is based on the degree of disposition (tasarruf) being more apparent for the preferred claimant.

Key Terms
تصرف (tasarruf): Physical disposition or control over property, such as riding, wearing, or loading.
ید (yad): Hand or possession; physical control over property.

Ignorance in Confession: Subject vs. Recipient
Explains how ignorance affects validity of confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ignorance of the subject and recipient in confession, and distinguish between excessive and non-excessive ignorance of the recipient.

Core Idea
Ignorance of what is confessed is harmless; ignorance of who receives it may be fatal if excessive.

In Hanafi law, a confession (iqrar) is a powerful legal tool, but what happens when the confessor is unsure about the details? This lesson clarifies that ignorance of the subject matter does not invalidate the confession, while ignorance of the recipient does, but only when it is excessive. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of confession in courts and everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three forms of ignorance
First, recognize that ignorance in a confession can affect three elements: the confessor (muqarr), the subject (muqarr bihi), and the recipient (muqarr lahu). Each has a different ruling.
The text explicitly discusses all three, though the confessor case is briefly mentioned.
2
Note the basic ruling for subject ignorance
The matn states that ignorance of the subject (muqarr bihi) does not prevent validity. Understand why: a right can be established even if its exact value is unknown, as in cases of property destruction or injury.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) by analogy.
3
Understand the ruling for recipient ignorance
Ignorance of the recipient (muqarr lahu) prevents validity, but only when the ignorance is excessive (jahalat fahisha). Non-excessive ignorance does not invalidate.
The text gives an example: 'this slave belongs to one of the people' indicates excessive ignorance.
4
Follow the intra-Hanafi dispute
Shams al-A'imma al-Sarkhasi held that excessive ignorance invalidates confession entirely. Imam al-Natafi (in al-Kafi) held that it is still valid. The preferred view (mufta bihi) is that of al-Kafi.
The commentary uses 'اصبح' to indicate preference for al-Natafi's view.
5
Apply to the confessor ignorance case
When the confessor is unknown (e.g., 'I owe one of you a thousand dirhams'), the confession is invalid because the defendant cannot be identified.
This is a separate scenario from subject/recipient ignorance.

Clarifying Vague Confessions and Minimum Amounts
Rulings on compelling clarification of vague confessions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules for compelling a confessor to clarify vague terms and determine the minimum amounts for 'property', 'great property', and other terms in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
A vague confession is valid but the confessor must clarify, and the minimum amount depends on the description used.

When someone makes a vague confession like 'I owe something', the law does not leave it ambiguous. The qadi can compel clarification, and the confessor's word is accepted with oath if disputed. However, the minimum amount that must be admitted varies based on the term used — from one dirham for 'property' to two hundred dirhams for 'great property'. This lesson unpacks the rules and the reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the duty to clarify
First, grasp that a vague confession (majhul) creates a binding obligation to specify. The qadi can compel clarification, analogized to the case of manumitting one of two slaves without specifying which.
The confessor is the one who made it vague, so burden of clarification is on him.
2
Apply the principle of acceptance with oath
If the confessor clarifies a certain amount and the claimant disputes, the confessor's statement is accepted with his oath because he is the one who admitted and is now specifying.
This applies unless the clarification is manifestly contradictory.
3
Determine the minimum for each term
For 'thing' (shay), he must name something of value. For 'property' (mal), he is believed in any amount above one dirham. For 'great property' (mal azeem), the general view requires at least two hundred dirhams.
The nisab of zakat (200 dirhams) is used as the benchmark for greatness.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on 'great property'
Abu Hanifa has two narrations: one sets the minimum at ten dirhams (nisab of theft), the other agrees with the book's view of two hundred dirhams. Understand the reasoning behind each.
The theft nisab (10 dirhams) is considered great because cutting the hand is severe.
5
Extend to other currencies and non-zakat property
For dinars, the minimum is twenty; for camels, twenty-five; for non-zakat property, the value of the zakat nisab (e.g., 200 dirhams' worth) is used.
The principle is consistency with the lowest nisab of that type.

Ruling on validity of iqrar for a fetus with a valid cause

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An acknowledgment for a fetus is valid only if a valid cause is mentioned and the child is born alive within the gestational period.

This passage from a Hanafi text addresses the conditions under which a declaration (iqrar) of a debt or property in favor of an unborn child (fetus) is legally recognized. An acknowledgment for a fetus is void if the stated cause is legally impossible, but valid for a suckling infant because guardians can act on its behalf.

Ambiguous Acknowledgment for a Fetus: Abu Yusuf vs. Muhammad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ambiguous iqrar for a fetus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad on ambiguous acknowledgment for a fetus and explain the reasoning behind each view, including the analogy to the ma'dhun slave and mufawada partner.

Core Idea
An ambiguous acknowledgment for a fetus is valid according to Muhammad but invalid according to Abu Yusuf, based on whether a default trade interpretation applies.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding an acknowledgment (iqrar) that is left ambiguous—no specific cause is mentioned. The dispute centers on whether such an acknowledgment for a fetus can be given effect or must fail. Understanding this case requires analyzing the default presumption of absolute acknowledgments and the scope of legal proofs (hujaj).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt or obligation for a fetus, where the acknowledger does not specify the cause (sabab). The dispute: is it valid or not?
Focus on the term 'ibham' (ambiguity) and the two imams' rulings.
2
Understand Imam Muhammad's argument
Muhammad argues that iqrar is a legal proof (hujja) that must be given effect wherever possible. Since the ambiguity can be resolved by interpreting the cause as a valid one (e.g., bequest or inheritance), the acknowledgment is valid.
Note the principle: 'اعمال الحجة أولى' (giving effect to proof is prioritized).
3
Understand Abu Yusuf's argument
Abu Yusuf argues that absolute acknowledgments (without cause) are presumptively trade-related, based on the analogous cases of a ma'dhun slave or mufawada partner. Since a fetus cannot engage in trade, such an acknowledgment is impossible and thus invalid.
This is a qiyas khafi (subtle analogy) based on default interpretation.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
The practical consequence: under Abu Yusuf, no payment is due; under Muhammad, the acknowledger must pay, interpreting the cause as a valid one. The commentary suggests Muhammad's view is stronger, but the school does not definitively choose.
Note that Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly agrees with Abu Yusuf; and Imam al-Shafi'i has two views.

Acknowledgment with Condition of Khiyar: Condition Void, Acknowledgment Valid
Ruling on khiyar condition in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an acknowledgment with a condition of khiyar and explain the reasoning using the distinction between ikhbar (informative statement) and contracts.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment is an informative statement, not a contract; khiyar cannot affect its truth or binding force.

When someone acknowledges a debt or property but adds a condition that they have the option (khiyar) to cancel within a period, what is the effect? The Hanafi school teaches that the condition is void because khiyar is designed for contracts where rescission is possible, while an acknowledgment is a statement of fact that must be either true or false—it cannot be conditional. This lesson unpacks the reasoning (talil) behind this ruling and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) made with a condition of khiyar. The ruling splits into two: the condition is void (batil) and the acknowledgment itself is valid and binding (sahih).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is the reasoning (talil) given: 'khiyar is for cancellation (faskh), and an informative statement (ikhbar) does not admit it.' Identify this as the core argument.
This is a rational/language-based proof, not a textual nass.
3
Understand the nature of ikhbar
An acknowledgment is an ikhbar: a report that claims something to be true. If true, it must be acted upon; if false, it must be rejected. Neither situation allows a discretionary option.
Truth and falsehood are objective, not subject to choice or condition.
4
Distinguish khiyar in contracts vs iqrar
In contracts (uqud), khiyar works because contracts are based on mutual consent and can be rescinded or confirmed. In ikhbar, there is no such mutability—the statement stands on its own veracity.
This distinction is key to the Hanafi position.
5
Apply the ruling
Thus, the condition is invalid and ignored, but the acknowledgment itself remains binding. The person must fulfill what he acknowledged (e.g., pay the debt).
The void condition does not affect the substance of the iqrar.

Acknowledgment of Defective Dirhams: Clarification or Retraction?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on specification after acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on acknowledgment of defective dirhams and explain the legal effect of immediate versus delayed specification.

Core Idea
An immediate clarification after an acknowledgment is accepted; a delayed specification is a retraction.

In Hanafi contract law, a person who acknowledges a debt in dirhams may later claim the dirhams were defective. Does this claim clarify the original intent or constitute a retraction of the debt? This lesson explores the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on this question. Understanding this distinction is crucial for judges and muftis when evaluating iqrar (acknowledgment) cases.

Guided Path
1
State the case
A person acknowledges a debt of 1000 dirhams, then immediately or later states that the dirhams were defective (zuyuf) or of very low quality (satuqah).
The acknowledgment itself is valid; the dispute is about the effect of the subsequent specification.
2
Identify the two positions
Imam Abu Hanifa considers the specification a retraction (ruju') and rejects it. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) accept it as a clarifying statement (bayan mughayyir) if made immediately (muttasil).
The three other imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) agree with the Sahibayn.
3
Analyze the proofs
Abu Hanifa argues that the absolute contract requires sound goods; defective dirhams are not true dirhams (only metaphorically). The Sahibayn argue that the word 'dirham' includes both sound and defective; specification merely clarifies which type was intended.
The Sahibayn analogize to specifying weight ('except they weigh five') which changes the quantity but is accepted.
4
Apply the timing distinction
The Sahibayn accept the specification only if it is connected (muttasil) to the acknowledgment. If separate (munfasil), they agree with Abu Hanifa that it is a retraction.
Abu Hanifa rejects it in both cases because he considers any such claim a retraction.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Although the text does not explicitly state tarjih, later Hanafi fatwa works rely on the Sahibayn view as the operative position.
Fatwa is given on the view that immediate specification is accepted.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the validity of an acknowledgment (iqrar) of debt where the debtor later claims the dirhams were defective. The central issue is whether specifying the dirhams as 'zuyuf' (defective) or 'satuqah' (bad quality) after an absolute acknowledgment is a permissible clarification or an impermissible retraction. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that it is a retraction (ruju') and therefore unacceptable. His reasoning is that the absolute contract (here the sale or loan implied by the acknowledgment) requires the payment to be sound; claiming defect contradicts that requirement. Moreover, satuqah dirhams are not actually dirhams in the true sense (they are metaphorically called dirhams) and thus not part of the price (thaman). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) differ, holding that if the specification is made immediately (muttasil), it is accepted as a clarifying statement (bayan mughayyir). They argue that the word 'dirham' includes both sound and defective ones; specifying merely clarifies which type was intended. They analogize to saying 'except they weigh five', which changes the quantity but is valid. The passage also notes a textual correction: 'thumma qala' should be 'wa qala' to avoid implying Abu Hanifa's view is only for the separate case. The three other imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) agree with the Sahibayn. The underlying difference is whether defective dirhams are truly part of the genus of dirhams (Sahibayn) or not (Abu Hanifa, considering them a defect).

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrār): Acknowledgment of a right or debt; a unilateral declaration that creates a legal obligation.
زيوف (zuyūf): Defective or counterfeit dirhams (coins) that are not accepted as valid currency.
ستوقة (satuqah): A type of defective dirham that is of very low quality, often considered not true dirhams.
بيان مغير (bayān mughayyir): A clarifying statement that changes the apparent meaning of the earlier statement; it is accepted if made immediately (muttasil).

Credibility of Debtor's Claim of Zuyūf Dirhams
Dispute on debtor credibility for zuyūf dirhams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules to determine when a debtor's claim that dirhams are zuyūf is believed, based on the cause (loan, ghasb, wadi'a, absolute acknowledgment) and the connectedness of the statement.

Core Idea
The debtor's claim that dirhams are counterfeit is believed in usurpation but not in loan, because transaction customs presume good currency.

This passage from al-Hidayah examines a nuanced issue: when a debtor says the dirhams he owes are zuyūf (counterfeit), is he believed? The answer depends on the underlying cause of the debt, whether it is a loan, usurpation, deposit, or an absolute acknowledgment. The Hanafi masters differ on the role of connectedness (wasl) and the nature of the transaction. This lesson unpacks the dispute and the operative rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause of the debt
First, determine the legal basis for the obligation: is it a loan (qard), usurpation (ghasb), deposit (wadī‘a), or an absolute acknowledgment without mentioning the cause? Each carries a different default presumption about the quality of the dirhams.
e.g., loans are presumed to be with good dirhams; usurpation has no such presumption.
2
Apply the rule of connectedness
Determine whether the debtor's specification of zuyūf was connected (mawsūl) to the acknowledgment or separated (mafsūl). The ruling often depends on this factor, especially for loans and absolute acknowledgments.
Connectedness means the statement is made immediately without a pause.
3
Locate the Hanafi positions
Identify the views of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani for each scenario. Note the apparent position (zāhir al-riwāyah) and the narrations from non-usul sources.
Use the provided pronoun map: 'qāla' for base text, 'lahu' for Abu Hanifa, 'lahumā' for the Sahibayn.
4
Derive the muftā bihī
From the disagreement, deduce the relied-upon position for fatwa. The majority opinion holds that in ghasb and wadī‘a, the debtor is believed whether connected or separated; in loans, he is not believed except in a weak narration requiring connection.
The muftā bihī may vary by scenario.
5
Apply the exception for satuqa/rasaas
If the debtor claims the dirhams are made of iron or lead (satūqah/rasās), the ruling changes: connectedness is required because the statement alters the genus.
This contrasts with zuyūf, which is a description within the genus.

Commentary
This passage discusses several scenarios in acknowledgments of dirhams. First, in the case of a loan (qard), there is a narration from Abu Hanifa that if the debtor says the dirhams were zuyuf (counterfeit) and the statement is connected, he is believed. The reason is that loan requires returning the equivalent, and the received goods might have been counterfeit. However, the apparent position (zahir al-riwaya) is that transactions are with good dirhams, so the absolute reference is to good ones. Second, if the acknowledgment is absolute without mentioning the cause (bay' or qard), there is disagreement: some say he is believed by consensus because the word dirham includes all types; others, like al-Karkhi, say he is not believed because absolute acknowledgment refers to contracts, which involve good dirhams. The Sahibayn make a distinction: if the specification is connected, he is believed; if separated, not. Third, in cases of usurpation (ghasb) or deposit (wadi'a), the debtor is believed whether the statement is connected or separated, because ghasb and wadi'a do not imply goodness; a person usurps what he finds and deposits what he owns. However, Abu Yusuf holds that if separated, he is not believed, by analogy with loan. Fourth, if he says the dirhams were satuqa (iron) or rasaas (lead), he is believed only if connected, because these are not of the genus of dirhams but are metaphorically included, so the statement changes the nature. Fifth, if he says 'except that it is deficient by such amount' (istithna of quantity), he is believed if connected, because it is an exception of quantity. Finally, if the separation is forced by a natural pause (inqita' nafsihi), it is treated as connected. The passage ends with a ruling on acknowledging usurpation of a garment: if the returner brings a defective garment, he is believed because usurpation does not require a sound item.

Rulings on Connection and Separation in Acknowledgment of Defective Dirhams
Detailed rulings on when the debtor's claim about defective dirhams is accepted based on connection or separation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi majority view on when a debtor's qualifying statement about defective dirhams is accepted, distinguishing between ghasb/wadi'a and loan, and between genus-changing and genus-preserving defects.

Core Idea
In ghasb and wadi'a, the debtor is believed about defects even if separated; for iron/lead, connection is required.

When someone acknowledges a debt or deposit of dirhams and later claims they were defective, the timing of that claim matters. This lesson from Al-Hidayah explores the intricate rules of wasl (connection) and fasl (separation) in such acknowledgments, providing a clear framework for when the debtor's word is accepted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the underlying transaction
Determine whether the acknowledgment relates to ghasb (usurpation), wadi'a (deposit), or qard (loan). This affects the default presumption about the dirhams' quality.
2
Apply the default ruling for ghasb/wadi'a
In ghasb and wadi'a, the debtor is believed whether the defect claim is connected or separated. The reason is that a person usurps what they find and deposits what they own, with no expectation of good dirhams.
This is the majority view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Recognize the exception for non-genus materials
If the debtor claims the dirhams are iron (satūqah) or lead (raṣāṣ), which are not of the genus of silver dirhams, the statement must be connected to be accepted. Separation invalidates it.
Such materials require connection because they change the subject entirely.
4
Handle exceptions of quantity vs. description
If the debtor makes an exception of quantity (e.g., 'except that it is deficient by X'), it is accepted only if connected. But a description like 'zuyūf' (counterfeit) is not accepted even if connected, because exceptions of descriptions are invalid in the Hanafi school.
The word 'dirham' includes quantity but not description.
5
Account for forced interruptions
If the separation is due to a natural pause in speech (e.g., catching breath), it is treated as connected because the debtor could not avoid it.

If he said 'I usurped from him a thousand' or 'he deposited with me a thousand', then said 'they were zuyūf or banahrajah', he is believed whether connected or separated, because a person usurps what he finds and deposits what he owns, so there is no requirement for good ones, no custom. Thus it is a statement of type, valid even if separated. For this reason, if the returner of the usurped or deposited property brings a defective item, his word is accepted. From Abu Yusuf: he is not believed when separated, by analogy with loan, because the receipt in both is the cause of liability. If he said 'they are satūqah or raṣāṣ' after acknowledging usurpation or deposit, and he connected, he is believed; if separated, not, because satūqah is not of the genus of dirhams but the name includes it metaphorically, so it is a changing statement requiring connection. If he said in all this 'a thousand' then said 'except that it is deficient by such amount', he is not believed unless connected, because this is an exception of quantity, and exception is valid when connected, unlike zuyāfah because it is a description, and exception of descriptions is invalid. The word includes quantity but not description, and it is a verbal transaction as we explained. If the excess is due to a forced interruption of speech by his own breath, then it is considered connected because avoidance is impossible. And whoever acknowledges usurpation of a garment and then brings a defective garment, his word is accepted because usurpation is not specific to sound items.

Commentary
This passage provides detailed rules on when the debtor's qualifying statement about the dirhams is accepted based on whether it is connected (wasl) to the initial acknowledgment or separated (fasl). In cases of usurpation (ghasb) and deposit (wadi'a), the general rule is that the debtor is believed whether connected or separated. The reason is that usurpation and deposit do not presume good quality; a person usurps what he finds and deposits what he owns, which may be defective. Therefore, if the returner claims the dirhams were zuyūf or banahrajah (non-current), his word is accepted. This is unlike loan (qard), where the transaction is with good dirhams by custom. However, Abu Yusuf disagrees and analogizes ghasb and wadi'a to loan, requiring connection. The passage then discusses cases where the claimed material is not of the genus of dirhams, such as satūqah (iron) or raṣāṣ (lead). Since these are not genuine silver dirhams, the statement changes the subject, so it must be connected to be believed. If separated, it is not accepted. Next, the passage addresses exceptions of quantity: if the debtor says 'except that it is deficient by such amount' (istithnā' al-miqdār), he is believed only if connected. This is because exception of quantity is valid when connected, but zuyāfah (counterfeit) is considered a description, and exception of descriptions is invalid. The word 'dirham' includes quantity but not description. The passage also states that if the separation is forced by a natural pause (inqaṭā' nafsihi), it is treated as connected because the debtor could not avoid it. Finally, the passage gives an example of a garment: if one acknowledges usurpation of a garment and then brings a defective one, his word is accepted because usurpation does not require a sound item.

Key Terms
وَصْل (waṣl): Connecting the qualifying statement to the acknowledgment without interval.
فَصْل (faṣl): Separation of the qualifying statement from the acknowledgment by an interval.
سَتَوْقَة (satūqah): Iron; a material not of the genus of silver dirhams.
رَصَاص (raṣāṣ): Lead; similar to iron in being not of the genus.

Dispute over Wadi'ah or Ghasb: Who Bears the Burden of Proof
Ruling when one claims wadi'ah and the other claims ghasb, with two scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on burden of proof in disputes over the nature of possession (deposit vs misappropriation) to determine liability.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of taking triggers liability unless permission is proven; the denier of the cause of liability is believed with oath.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a nuanced ruling on disputes over whether property was taken as a trust (wadi'ah) or as a wrongful taking (ghasb). The ruling turns on who acknowledged what and who denies what. Understanding this distinction is critical for correctly assigning liability in similar financial disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two scenarios: in the first, the taker says he took as deposit, the owner says it was ghasb. In the second, the taker says the owner gave it as deposit, the owner says the taker misappropriated it. Each has a different outcome.
The first scenario leads to liability; the second does not.
2
Apply the principle of acknowledgment and denial
Acknowledgment of the act of taking creates a presumption of liability. The taker then claims an exemption (permission). The owner's denial of that exemption is accepted with oath because it is a denial of a claim of exemption. In the second scenario, the taker attributes the act to the owner, so the owner claims ghasb, and the taker denies it, so the taker's denial is accepted.
The difference hinges on who admits the act of taking.
3
Distinguish from the loan case
The passage compares to a case where the taker says 'deposit' and owner says 'loan'. Both agree the taking was with permission, so the one claiming a cause of liability (loan) must prove it. This shows that the rule hinges on whether permission is contested or agreed upon.
In the loan case, the taker is not automatically liable despite acknowledging taking.

وَمَنْ قَالَ لِأخَرَ أَخَذْتُ مِنْكَ اَلْفَ دِرْهَم وَدِيْعَةً فَهَلَكَتْ فَقَالَ لَا بَلْ اَخَذْتُهَا غَصْبًا فَهُوَ ضَامِنٌ وَإِنْ قَالَ أعْطَيْتَنِيْها وَدِيْعَةً فَقَالَ لَا بَلْ غَصَبْتَنِيهَا لَمْ يَضْمَنْ وَالْفَرْقُ اَنَّ فِى الْفَضل حوَّل أَقَرَّ بِسَبَبٍ الصَّمَانِ وَهُوَ الأخذُ ثُمَّ ادَّعَى مَا هُبْرِنُه وَهُوَ الْاِذْنُ وَالْآخَرُ يُنْكِرُهُ فَيَكُونُ الْقَوْلُ لَهُ مَعَ الْيَمِينِ وَفِي الثَّانِي أَضَافَ الْفِعَلَ إِلَى غَيْرِهِ وَذَالِكَ يَدَّعِي عَلَيْهِ سَيَبَ الصَّمَانِ وَهُوَ الْغَصَبُ فَكَانَ الْقَوْلُ لِمُنْكِرِهِ مَعَ الْيَمِيْنِ وَالْقَبْضُ فِي هَذَا كَالَا خُذِ وَالدَّفْعُ كَالْإِعْطَاءِ فَإِنْ قَالَ قَائِلُ الْأَعْطَاء وَالدَّفْعُ إِلَيْهِ لَا يَكُونُ إِلَّا بِقَبْضِهِ فَتَقُولُ قَدْ يَكُونُ بِالتَّخْلِيَةِ وَالْوَضْعِ بَيْنَ يَدَيْهِ وَلَوِ اقْتَضَى ذَالِكَ فَالْمُقْتَضِى ثَابِتٌ ضُرُورَةً فَلَا يَظْهَرُ فِي الْعِقَادِهِ سَبَبَ الضَّمَانِ وَهَلَمَا بِخِلَافِ مَا إِذَا قَالَ اَخَذْتَهَا مِنْكَ وَدِيْعَةً وَقَالَ الْآخَرُ لَا بَلْ قَرْضًا حَيْثُ يَكُونُ الْقَوْلُ لِلْمُقِرِ وَإِن أَقَرَّ بِالْأَحْدِ لأَنَّهُمَا تَوَافَقَا هُنَاكَ عَلَى أن الأخذَ كَانَ بِالْإِذَن إِلَّا أَنَّ الْمُقَرَّ لَهُ يَدَّعِى سَبَبَ الصَّمَانِ وَهُوَ الْقَرْضُ وَالْآخَرُ اَنَّ يُنْكِرُهُ فَافْتَرَقَا
And whoever said to another: 'I took from you a thousand dirhams as a deposit and it perished,' the other said: 'No, rather you took them as ghasb,' then he is liable. And if he said: 'You gave them to me as a deposit,' and the other said: 'No, rather you misappropriated them from me,' he is not liable. And the difference is that in the first, he acknowledged a cause of liability, which is taking, then claimed what would acquit him, which is permission, and the other denies it, so the statement is his (the latter) with oath. In the second, he attributed the action to someone else, and that one claims against him a cause of liability, which is ghasb, so the statement is of the denier with oath. And possession in this is like taking, and giving like handing over. If someone says: 'Giving and handing over to him cannot be except by his taking,' we say: 'It may be by leaving alone (takhliyah) and placing before him. And even if it necessitates that, the necessitated is established by necessity, so it does not appear as a cause of liability in the contract.' And this is unlike the case when he said: 'I took it from you as a deposit' and the other said: 'No, rather as a loan,' where the statement is of the acknowledger (the first), even though he acknowledged taking, because they both agreed that the taking was with permission, but the acknowledging party (the one in whose favor the acknowledgment is made) claims a cause of liability, which is loan, and the other denies it, so they differ.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with proof from the Hidayah on a dispute regarding whether property was taken as a deposit (wadi'ah) or misappropriated (ghasb). The matn states: In the first scenario, where A says 'I took from you a thousand dirhams as a deposit and it perished,' and B says 'No, you took them as ghasb,' then A is liable (dhamin). In the second scenario, where A says 'You gave me as a deposit,' and B says 'No, you misappropriated them,' then A is not liable. The reasoning (dalil aqli): In the first scenario, A has acknowledged the act of taking (which is a cause of liability) and then claims permission (idhn) to exempt himself. B denies permission. Since B is the plaintiff in effect, his statement is accepted with oath. In the second scenario, A attributes the act to B (saying 'you gave me'), and B claims ghasb (cause of liability). Here A is the defendant denying ghasb, so his statement is accepted with oath. The commentary then addresses an objection: since giving and handing over require possession by the recipient, acknowledging giving might imply possession which is a cause of liability. The answer: giving can be by takhliya (leaving alone) or placing before him, not necessarily possession. Even if possession is necessitated, it is by necessity and does not become a cause of liability. The passage distinguishes this from the case where one says 'I took as deposit' and the other says 'as loan.' In that case, both agree the taking was with permission, so the one claiming loan (cause of liability) must prove it, and the defendant's statement is accepted. The difference (ifiraq) is that here both agree on permission, so the burden shifts.

Key Terms
ضمان (daman): Liability to replace or compensate for the property taken.
اذن (idhn): Permission; here the permission to take the property, which exempts from liability.
تخلیہ (takhliyah): Leaving something alone; a form of delivery by giving access without physical handing.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, liability in property disputes turns on whether the taker acknowledged the act of taking and whether permission is contested; the rule is based on the maxim 'al-bayyinatu 'ala man idda'a wa al-yaminu 'ala man ankara'.

Acknowledgment of Lineage and Marriage: The General Rule
States general rule for acknowledgment of lineage and marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a valid acknowledgment of parentage, child, spouse, and master, and explain why a woman's acknowledgment of a child requires the husband's confirmation.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment binds only the acknowledger; a woman's claim of a child requires the husband's confirmation because lineage belongs to him.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes the foundational rule for acknowledging personal relationships. It distinguishes between a man's and a woman's acknowledgment and clarifies when confirmation from another party is required. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly assigning lineage and marital rights in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
The author states that a man's acknowledgment of parents, child, wife, and master is valid because it imposes obligations only on himself and does not attribute lineage to another. This is the key reasoning (ta'lil).
Note the phrase 'laysa fihi tahmil al-nasab 'ala al-ghayr' – it does not involve imposing lineage on another.
2
Examine the woman's acknowledgment
A woman's acknowledgment of parents, husband, and master is accepted for the same reason. However, her acknowledgment of a child is not accepted unless the husband confirms or a midwife testifies. The child's lineage is attributed to the husband.
Why? Because the child's lineage is from the husband (al-nasab minhu). The woman's acknowledgment alone would impose lineage on the husband without his consent.
3
Identify the authority needed for confirmation
The husband's confirmation (tasdiq) is required because the right belongs to him. Alternatively, a midwife's testimony about the birth is accepted because her word in such matters is reliable.
The midwife's testimony is mentioned as an independent basis; it does not replace the husband's confirmation but provides an alternative proof.

The ruling of acknowledgment for parents, child, wife, and master. He said: A man's acknowledgment of parents, child, wife, and master is permissible because he acknowledges what is binding upon him and it does not involve attributing lineage to another. A woman's acknowledgment of parents, husband, and master is accepted for the reason we mentioned, but it is not accepted for a child because it involves attributing lineage to another, namely the husband, since lineage is from him, unless the husband confirms her—since the right is his—or a midwife testifies to his birth, because the midwife's word in this matter is accepted, as has passed in the section on divorce. We have mentioned detail regarding a woman's acknowledgment in the Book of Claims. Confirmation of these individuals is necessary. Confirmation in lineage is valid after the death of the acknowledged because lineage persists after death. Similarly, the wife's confirmation is valid because the ruling of marriage remains. Likewise, the husband's confirmation after her death is valid because inheritance is among its rulings. According to Abu Hanifa, it is not valid because marriage is severed by death, and therefore he is not permitted to wash her according to us. Nor is confirmation valid on the basis of inheritance, because inheritance is non-existent at the time of acknowledgment; it is only established after death, and confirmation is attributed to the beginning of the acknowledgment.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn on acknowledgment of lineage and marriage relations. The author first states the general rule: a man's acknowledgment of parents, child, wife, and master is valid because it only imposes obligations on himself, not on others. The reasoning (ta'lil) is that acknowledging lineage or marital rights does not force anyone else to accept that relation; it only binds the acknowledger. For a woman, her acknowledgment of parents, husband, and master is also valid for the same reason. However, her acknowledgment of a child is not accepted unless the husband confirms it or a midwife testifies to the birth, because the child's lineage is attributed to the husband. The passage then discusses confirmation (tasdiq) after death: confirmation in lineage is valid after the acknowledged person's death because lineage outlives death. For marriage, the wife's confirmation after the husband's death is valid because the effects of marriage (like 'iddah) remain. But Abu Hanifa holds that the husband's confirmation after the wife's death is not valid because marriage is severed by death; thus, he cannot wash her, and inheritance is not a basis for confirmation since it arises only after death. The commentary explains conditions for validity from other sources (e.g., the woman not being married to another, not in 'iddah, etc.) and mentions a disagreement about acknowledging motherhood (some say it's invalid because lineage is through fathers, but al-Durr al-Mukhtar affirms it).

Key Terms
اقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment or confession, here of a personal relationship (e.g., parentage, marriage) that creates legal obligations.
تصدیق (tasdiq): Confirmation by the person whose rights are affected by the acknowledgment, e.g., the husband confirming the child.
قابلة (qabila): Midwife; her testimony about a child's birth is accepted in lieu of the husband's confirmation.

Confirmation After Death: The Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on confirmation after death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the husband's confirmation after the wife's death and state the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa invalidates the husband's confirmation after death because marriage ends; the Sahibayn uphold it for inheritance purposes.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a woman acknowledges a marriage, then dies, and her husband confirms the acknowledgment after her death. Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn disagree on the validity of this confirmation. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Hanafi school balances the cessation of marriage with the ongoing legal effects like inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed scenario
A woman acknowledges a man as her husband, then she dies. After her death, the husband confirms her acknowledgment. Is this confirmation valid?
The scenario is 'tasdiq al-zawj ba'da mawtiha' – the husband's confirmation after the wife's death.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa says the confirmation is invalid. His reasoning: marriage is severed by death (al-nikah ingata'a bi al-mawt). Therefore, the husband cannot confirm something that no longer exists. Supporting evidence: he is not permitted to wash her body.
The key term 'ingata'a' – completely cut off.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the confirmation is valid. Their reasoning: inheritance (irth) is one of the effects of marriage, and inheritance is established after death. Since confirmation is attributed to the beginning of the acknowledgment, and inheritance is a remaining effect, the confirmation is valid.
They also point out that if the husband could not confirm, he would be deprived of inheritance rights.
4
Determine the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon position is the Sahibayn's view because it is more expansive and facilitates inheritance. Later Hanafi scholars have adopted it.
The text says 'wa alayhi al-fatwa'? Not explicitly, but the commentary indicates preference for the Sahibayn.

The ruling of acknowledgment for parents, child, wife, and master. He said: A man's acknowledgment of parents, child, wife, and master is permissible because he acknowledges what is binding upon him and it does not involve attributing lineage to another. A woman's acknowledgment of parents, husband, and master is accepted for the reason we mentioned, but it is not accepted for a child because it involves attributing lineage to another, namely the husband, since lineage is from him, unless the husband confirms her—since the right is his—or a midwife testifies to his birth, because the midwife's word in this matter is accepted, as has passed in the section on divorce. We have mentioned detail regarding a woman's acknowledgment in the Book of Claims. Confirmation of these individuals is necessary. Confirmation in lineage is valid after the death of the acknowledged because lineage persists after death. Similarly, the wife's confirmation is valid because the ruling of marriage remains. Likewise, the husband's confirmation after her death is valid because inheritance is among its rulings. According to Abu Hanifa, it is not valid because marriage is severed by death, and therefore he is not permitted to wash her according to us. Nor is confirmation valid on the basis of inheritance, because inheritance is non-existent at the time of acknowledgment; it is only established after death, and confirmation is attributed to the beginning of the acknowledgment.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the matn on acknowledgment of lineage and marriage relations. The author first states the general rule: a man's acknowledgment of parents, child, wife, and master is valid because it only imposes obligations on himself, not on others. The reasoning (ta'lil) is that acknowledging lineage or marital rights does not force anyone else to accept that relation; it only binds the acknowledger. For a woman, her acknowledgment of parents, husband, and master is also valid for the same reason. However, her acknowledgment of a child is not accepted unless the husband confirms it or a midwife testifies to the birth, because the child's lineage is attributed to the husband. The passage then discusses confirmation (tasdiq) after death: confirmation in lineage is valid after the acknowledged person's death because lineage outlives death. For marriage, the wife's confirmation after the husband's death is valid because the effects of marriage (like 'iddah) remain. But Abu Hanifa holds that the husband's confirmation after the wife's death is not valid because marriage is severed by death; thus, he cannot wash her, and inheritance is not a basis for confirmation since it arises only after death. The commentary explains conditions for validity from other sources (e.g., the woman not being married to another, not in 'iddah, etc.) and mentions a disagreement about acknowledging motherhood (some say it's invalid because lineage is through fathers, but al-Durr al-Mukhtar affirms it).

Key Terms
اقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment or confession, here of a marriage relationship.
تصدیق (tasdiq): Confirmation by the husband after the wife's death, the validity of which is disputed.
میراث (mirath): Inheritance; the legal basis used by the Sahibayn to justify confirmation after death.
