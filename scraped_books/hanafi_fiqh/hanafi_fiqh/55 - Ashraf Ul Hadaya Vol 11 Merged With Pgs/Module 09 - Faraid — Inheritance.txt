Claiming Paternity of One Twin Establishes Both
Stating the ruling that claiming paternity of one twin establishes paternity of both

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that claiming paternity of one twin automatically establishes paternity of the other and explain the qiyasi reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
Claiming one twin's paternity automatically establishes both—because they share one conception.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from the Hanafi school: if a man claims lineage of one twin, both twins are recognised as his children. The reasoning is based on the biological reality that twins are conceived from the same act of intercourse and are born less than six months apart, making it impossible for one twin to be from a different father. Understanding this principle prevents attempts to acknowledge only one child for legal or financial advantage.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Read the Arabic and identify the exact legal consequence: claiming paternity of one twin establishes lineage for both.
The ruling is explicit: 'ثَبَتَ نَسْبُهُمَا مِنْهُ' – lineage of both is established from him.
2
Identify the dalil
The author gives a rational proof: 'because they are from one fluid.' This is a qiyas based on the nature of twin conception.
The proof does not rely on a textual scriptural source but on logical necessity (dalil aqli).
3
Analyse the wajh al-dalalah
Understand why shared conception necessitates shared lineage: twins are born within less than six months, so they cannot be from separate pregnancies.
The short interval between births is key: if it were more than six months, they could be from different conceptions.
4
Apply the principle
Consider the practical outcome: a man who acknowledges only one twin cannot later deny the other, as the established lineage of one compels the lineage of the other.
This prevents manipulation of lineage claims for inheritance or guardianship.

Commentary
This short ruling establishes a clear principle in Hanafi fiqh: when a man claims paternity of one twin, both twins are automatically recognized as his children. The reason is that twins are conceived from the same act of intercourse (same 'water' or semen) and are born within a short period (less than six months apart), so it is impossible that one twin was conceived from a separate act. Therefore, proving the lineage of one necessarily proves the lineage of the other. This ruling prevents disputes where someone might try to acknowledge only one child to avoid responsibilities for the other.

Key Terms
توأم (taw'am): A twin; one of two children born from the same pregnancy.
ماء واحد (ma' wahid): One fluid/semen; indicating that the twins were conceived from the same act of intercourse.

Acknowledgment of Brother or Uncle's Lineage
Ruling on acknowledging brother/uncle lineage with inheritance consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledging the lineage of a brother or uncle and identify the conditions under which the acknowledged person may inherit.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a brother or uncle does not prove lineage but may give inheritance if no known heir exists.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common scenario where someone claims a brother or uncle. The Hanafi school distinguishes between proving lineage (which requires strong evidence) and the practical inheritance consequences. The ruling hinges on whether a known heir exists, and if not, the owner's authority over property allows the acknowledged person to inherit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is acknowledging a relative other than parents or children, such as a brother or uncle. The ruling is that such acknowledgment is not accepted (ghayr maqbul) for proving lineage.
The Arabic phrase is 'لَا يُقْبَلُ إِقْرَارُهُ فِي النَّسَبِ'.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason is that acknowledging a brother or uncle inherently attributes lineage to a third party (the father), which is impermissible. The text states: 'لَانَّ فِيْهِ حَمْلَ النَّسَبِ عَلَى الْغَيْرِ'.
This is a key principle: lineage cannot be established via acknowledgment if it implicates someone else.
3
Determine inheritance effect with a known heir
If the acknowledger has any known heir (warith ma'ruf), even a distant one, that heir takes precedence and the acknowledged person receives nothing.
The term 'warith ma'ruf' includes any relative entitled to inherit, even if distant.
4
Determine inheritance effect without a known heir
If the acknowledger has no known heir, the acknowledged person is entitled to the entire estate. This is not because lineage is established, but because the owner has full authority over his property (wilayat al-tasarruf) in the absence of heirs.
The text analogizes to the power to bequeath all one's property to a stranger.
5
Distinguish from a will
The passage clarifies that this is not a true will; if the acknowledger later makes a will to another person, the legatee takes precedence over the acknowledged person.
This subtle point shows the hierarchy of claims.

If he has a known heir, close or distant, that heir is more entitled to the inheritance than the one acknowledged, because since his lineage from him is not established, he does not compete with the known heir. If he has no heir, the person acknowledged is entitled to his inheritance because he has the authority to dispose of his own property in the absence of an heir. Do you not see that he can bequeath all of it when there is no heir, so he deserves all of the property, even though his lineage from him is not established, due to the attribution of lineage to another. And this is not a true will? Indeed, whoever acknowledges something then bequeaths all of his property to another, the legatee takes all of the property, even if the first was a will.

Commentary
This passage tackles the ruling on acknowledging lineage of relatives other than parents and children, such as a brother or uncle. The author states that such an acknowledgment is not accepted for establishing lineage because it would attribute lineage to another person (e.g., the father). However, there is a practical effect regarding inheritance: if the acknowledger has a known heir (even a distant one), that known heir takes the inheritance, and the acknowledged person gets nothing because his lineage is not proven. But if there is no known heir, then the acknowledged person is entitled to the entire estate. This is not because lineage is established, but because the acknowledger has authority over his own property in the absence of heirs, akin to the power to bequeath all one's property. The passage ends with a distinction from a true will: if someone acknowledges a brother (implying inheritance) and then makes a will to another, the legatee takes precedence.

Key Terms
وارث معروف (warith ma'ruf): A known heir whose entitlement to inheritance is established, even if distant, who takes precedence over the acknowledged person.
ولایت التصرف (wilayat al-tasarruf): Authority to dispose of one's own property, which the acknowledger has fully in the absence of any heir.

Acknowledgment of Non-Parental Lineage: Ruling and Inheritance Effects
Ruling with proof on acknowledgment of non-parental lineage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of lineage for non-parental relatives and explain the financial consequences, including the distinction from a true will.

Core Idea
Acknowledging a brother does not create legal lineage but can transfer wealth like a will when no heir exists.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced area: when a person claims someone as a brother or uncle, does that make them an heir? The Hanafi school distinguishes between establishing nasab (which requires certainty) and the owner's authority to dispose of property. Here, the acknowledgment has no effect on lineage but can entitle the acknowledged person to inheritance as if it were a will.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is iqrar bi nasab for relatives other than parents and children. The ruling: such acknowledgment is not accepted to establish lineage because it would impose lineage on a third party (e.g., the father of the acknowledged).
The text explicitly says 'la yuqbal iqraruhu fi al-nasab'.
2
Distinguish the two scenarios by presence of a known heir
If the acknowledger has a known heir (any degree), that heir is more entitled to the inheritance than the acknowledged person. If there is no known heir, the acknowledged person inherits the entire estate.
The condition is whether a known heir exists at the time of death.
3
Trace the proof (dalil) via analogy
The passage draws an analogy to the owner's authority to bequeath all property when there is no heir. Just as one can make a will for all wealth, one can effectively give wealth through acknowledgment of lineage, even though lineage is not established.
The key phrase is 'a la tara an lahu an yusi jami'ahu' (do you not see that he can bequeath all of it).
4
Understand the distinction from a real will
The acknowledgment is not a true will (wasiyyah), but it is treated like one in certain aspects: if a subsequent will is made, the acknowledged person gets only one-third (like a legatee). If there is no will, the acknowledged person gets the whole estate.
The text illustrates: if both a will and acknowledgment exist, the legatee gets one-third, not half.
5
Apply the rule to retraction
If the acknowledger retracts the acknowledgment (e.g., denies it after confirmation in illness), the retraction is valid because lineage was never established. The estate goes to any legatee or to bayt al-mal.
This shows the acknowledgment is revocable like a will.

He said: And whoever acknowledges lineage other than parents and child, like brother and uncle, his acknowledgment in lineage is not accepted because in it there is imposing lineage upon another. So if he has a known heir, near or distant, that heir is more entitled to the inheritance than the one in whose favor the acknowledgment was made. Because when his lineage from him is not established, he does not compete with the known heir. And if he has no heir, then the one acknowledged inherits his estate because he has the authority to dispose of his own property in the absence of an heir. Do you not see that he can bequeath all of it when there is no heir? So he becomes entitled to all the wealth, even though his lineage from him is not established, because of the imposition of lineage upon another. And this is not a real will. Indeed, whoever acknowledges [someone as a brother] and then bequeaths all his wealth to another, for the legatee is one-third of all the wealth. And if the first were a will, they would share half and half. But it is in the position of a will, such that if he acknowledges in his illness [of death] as a brother and the acknowledged one confirms, then the acknowledger denies, then bequeaths all his wealth to a person, the wealth is for the legatee. And if he did not bequeath to anyone, it goes to the public treasury (bayt al-mal), because his retraction is valid since the lineage was not established, so the acknowledgment becomes void.

Commentary
This passage presents the core ruling: acknowledgment of lineage for relatives other than parents and children (e.g., brother, uncle) is not accepted to establish nasab because it would impose lineage on a third party (the common ancestor). However, it can have an effect on inheritance if the acknowledger has no known heir. In that case, the acknowledged person receives the entire estate, not as an heir, but as a de facto beneficiary through the implied authority of disposition. This is likened to a wasiyyah (will) but with the distinction that if the acknowledger makes a subsequent will, the acknowledged person gets only one-third (like a will), and if the acknowledger retracts or denies after confirmation, the retraction is valid. The ruling is based on the principle that the acknowledgment is not accepted for nasab but is effective for financial disposition under the authority of the owner, especially when there is no heir to prevent it.

Key Terms
اقرار نسب (iqrar bi nasab): An acknowledgment that another person is a relative (e.g., brother, uncle) for purposes of lineage and inheritance.
وصیت (wasiyyah): A will or bequest of property to another person.

Acknowledgment of a Brother After Father's Death: Split Effect on Lineage and Inheritance
Ruling on acknowledging a brother after the father's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledging a brother after the father's death and explain why lineage is not established but sharing in inheritance is valid.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of a brother splits: lineage fails, but financial sharing succeeds due to authority over one's share.

When a person acknowledges someone as his brother after his father's death, the acknowledgment touches both lineage (nasab) and inheritance. But lineage cannot be imposed on the deceased father without his consent. Hanafi law therefore splits the acknowledgment: the lineage part is invalid, but the financial part is accepted because the acknowledger has authority over his own share of the estate. This nuanced ruling is justified by a qiyas (analogy) with a buyer who acknowledges the seller's right to emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its two components
The acknowledgment aims to establish lineage (nasab) from the deceased father and to give the acknowledged person a share in the inheritance. Note that the acknowledger has authority over his own wealth but not over his father's lineage.
The act is an iqrar (acknowledgment) that has a dual object: nasab and mal.
2
Apply the Talil (ratio legis) from the text
The text states: 'because his acknowledgment included two things: imposing lineage upon another (over which he has no authority) and sharing in wealth (over which he has authority).' Understand that the ruling is based on the extent of the acknowledger's wilayah (authority).
Wilayah over wealth is present; wilayah over lineage of another is absent.
3
Examine the Qiyas analogy
The text analogizes to a buyer who acknowledges the seller's right to emancipation. The buyer's acknowledgment is not accepted for financial recourse (because he has no authority over the seller's status) but is accepted to affirm the right of emancipation. Similarly, here the acknowledgment is accepted for the financial sharing (the equivalent of the right) but not for lineage.
The illa (effective cause) is the split in authority: lack of authority over the third party's status but presence of authority over one's own property.
4
Derive the operative ruling
Conclude that the acknowledged person does not become a legal heir (lineage not established) but receives a share from the acknowledger's portion of the inheritance as a form of disposition (tasrif) from the acknowledger's own share.
This is not a true inheritance; it is an inter vivos gift-like transfer triggered by the acknowledgment.

Acknowledgment of Brotherhood After Father's Death
Inter-madhhab dispute on inheritance through acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of brotherhood after the father's death, explain the split-effect doctrine (tadammun shay'ayn), and apply it to distinguish between lineage and inheritance rights.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment can split: lineage fails, but property sharing binds because it is against oneself.

When a son acknowledges another as his brother after the father's death, a critical question arises: does the acknowledged person inherit? The Hanafi school answers with a nuanced 'yes'—but only in property, not lineage. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this split, the inter-madhhab dispute with Imam Shafi'i, and the intra-Hanafi variation on the share calculation. The key is understanding that the acknowledgment has two components, and the acknowledger has authority over his own rights but not over others'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components of the acknowledgment
The acknowledgment includes (1) imposing lineage on the deceased father and the acknowledged person, and (2) sharing in the father's property. The first involves another's right (the father's lineage), the second involves the acknowledger's own share.
This is the principle of tadammun shay'ayn.
2
Apply the Hanafi authority rule
The acknowledger has authority (wilayah) over his own rights but not over the lineage of others. Therefore, the lineage part fails, but the property part is valid as an admission against himself.
The analogy with the buyer acknowledging the seller's freeing of a slave illustrates this split.
3
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Imam Shafi'i and Ibn Sirin argue that inheritance is based on lineage; without proven lineage, no inheritance. The Hanafi response is that property sharing is independent.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: in Hanafi law, the acknowledged inherits; in Shafi'i, he does not.
4
Determine the share calculation (intra-Hanafi dispute)
The Hanafi view (approved by al-Marghinani) is that the acknowledged takes half of the acknowledger's share. Malik and Ibn Abi Layla say one-third of the acknowledger's share (one-sixth of the total).
The dispute hinges on whether the acknowledgment spreads over the entire estate or only the acknowledger's portion.
5
Conclude the mufta bihi position
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi half-share view, supported by the majority of later Hanafi scholars.
The principle of tadammun shay'ayn remains central.

Acknowledgment of Debt by One Son: Effect on Shares
Ruling on a son's acknowledgment that the deceased father received part of a debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one son acknowledges a debt receipt against the deceased, explain the legal reasoning based on circularity, and apply the rule to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
An heir's acknowledgment of a debt receipt is not binding on co-heirs; it leads to forfeiture to avoid circularity.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a nuanced ruling involving a deceased father's debt receivable and the conflicting claims of his two sons. One son's acknowledgment of a partial receipt, if denied by the other, results in the acknowledging son losing his entire share. The ruling is grounded in a logical argument that any other outcome would create an endless cycle of claims (dawr). Understanding this case reveals important Hanafi principles regarding heir acknowledgments and the structural constraints on inheritance claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the contested fact
A father dies leaving two sons and a debt of 100 dirhams from a third party. One son says the father already received 50; the other denies.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The acknowledging son gets nothing; the other son gets 50 dirhams (the remaining debt).
3
Locate the proof and its type
The proof is rational (dalil aqli) based on avoidance of circularity (dawr).
4
Analyze the reasoning
If the acknowledger were to take any part, the debtor would claim back from him, creating a cycle.
5
Note the consequence (thamarah)
The denying brother takes the acknowledger's share entirely (istighraq).

The Ruling of Takhāruj: Buying Out an Heir
Ruling on the validity of takhāruj as a sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on takhāruj and explain why it is valid as a sale regardless of the amount paid.

Core Idea
Takhāruj is valid because it is a sale — the price need not equal the share.

In inheritance, co-heirs sometimes wish to buy out one heir's share rather than divide the estate physically. The classical Hanafi texts address this transaction as 'takhāruj' (mutual release). This lesson examines the ruling from Al-Hidayah: it is valid irrespective of the amount given, because it can be structured as a sale. We will also consider the precedent set by Caliph Uthman.

Understand the legal basis
The transaction is validated as a sale (bayʿ). The heir sells his undivided share of the estate to the other heirs for a price agreed upon.

In practice, heirs can agree to give one heir a fixed sum or property in exchange for his exit from the estate, even if the value differs from his theoretical share.
The transaction is consensual; no coercion is permitted.

(Chapter on Takharuj) He said: When the estate is among heirs and they remove one of them from it with wealth they give him, and the estate is property or goods, it is permissible whether what they give him is little or much, because it is possible to validate it as a sale, and there is the athar of 'Uthman, for he made a sulh with Tamadir al-Ashja'iyya, wife of 'Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf, concerning a quarter of an eighth of her share for eighty thousand dinars. Translation: (Chapter on Takharuj) When the estate is shared among several heirs, then they separate one from it by giving him some wealth and the estate is property or goods, it is permissible whether what they gave him is little or much, because it is possible to validate this sulh as a sale, and there is the athar of 'Uthman: he made a sulh with Tamadir al-Ashja'iyya, wife of 'Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf, concerning a quarter of an eighth of her share for eighty thousand dinars. Commentary: He said: Chapter... This chapter contains the matters of takharuj. Takharuj is tafa'ul from khuruj; linguistically it means each partner extracting his share from the mutual division, or the extraction of maintenance according to each's share. In Shari'ah, takharuj means that all heirs remove one heir from the inheritance by giving him some wealth. Its occurrence is rare because no one is willing to exit without taking his share, or because it occurs after death, so it is placed at the end of the book. First benefit: The cause of takharuj is the request of the one being removed from the heirs when the other heirs are willing. Its conditions are: 1) The entire estate is not enveloped in debt. What is given to the exiting heir is more than his share from the estate in kind, when the estate consists of gold, silver, and their mixture. Some also stipulate that at the time of sulh, it should be known what kinds of assets are in the estate. Second benefit: An example of takharuj: A woman dies leaving her husband, one daughter, and one full sister. The shares: husband 1/4, daughter 1/2, full sister residue (1/4). The estate includes silver and clothes. The husband makes a sulh regarding the clothes. Then the distribution between the daughter and full sister: three shares: two for the daughter, one for the full sister. He said: And when the estate... If someone dies leaving goods or land in the estate, and the heirs remove one heir by giving him some wealth, the takharuj is valid, whether that wealth is little or much, because it can be validated as a sale, and a sale is valid in both cases, little or much, and since it is treated as a sale, it cannot be called bad, because release from non-guaranteed items is not a sale. He said: And in it is the athar of 'Uthman... The permissibility of takharuj is supported by the athar of 'Uthman, for he permitted the sulh of Tamadir bint Asbagh al-Ashja'iyya, wife of 'Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf, concerning a quarter of an eighth of her share for eighty thousand dinars. The Prophet allowed 'Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf to marry her, and she bore him only one son, Abu Salama. Ibn Sa'd narrated from Ibn 'Umar: The Prophet sent 'Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf with seven hundred men to Dumah al-Jandal in Sha'ban of the hijra year, calling them to Islam, but they refused. Commentary
This passage introduces the chapter (fask) on takharuj, which is the mutual exit of one heir from the inheritance in exchange for payment from other heirs. The core ruling is that takharuj is valid when the estate consists of land or movable goods ('iqar or 'urud), regardless of whether the amount given to the exiting heir is small or large. The justification is that this transaction can be validated as a sale (bay'), and sale is valid even if the price is low. The proof is the practice (athar) of Caliph 'Uthman ibn 'Affan, who arranged a sulh for Tamadir, the wife of 'Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf, for a quarter of an eighth of her share. The passage then explains the linguistic meaning of takharuj and its rarity in practice. The conditions are: 1) the estate is not completely debt-ridden; 2) what is given to the exiting heir is more than his share in kind from the estate; 3) some scholars require knowledge of the types of assets at the time of sulh. The commentary also provides an example: a woman dies leaving husband (1/4), daughter (1/2), full sister (residue: 1/4). The husband takes clothes from the estate and exits, leaving the remaining estate (including other assets) to be divided between the daughter and full sister in the ratio of their shares (daughter twice the sister). The passage concludes with a detailed biographical note on Tamadir and 'Abd al-Rahman ibn 'Awf, explaining the context of the athar of 'Uthman. The mufta bihi position is the validity of takharuj.

Definition and Conditions of Takharuj
Definition and conditions of takharuj with example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of takharuj and list its conditions according to the Hanafi school, and apply them to a faraid scenario.

Core Idea
Takharuj: heirs buy out a co-heir's share; condition: compensation exceeds share if ribawi assets are present.

This passage from Kitab al-Sulh explains takharuj, a rare but important inheritance mechanism. Understanding its definition and conditions helps ensure that the exit is valid and does not harm other heirs. We will parse the conditions and work through an example.

In Shariah terminology, takharuj means that all heirs give some wealth to an heir and remove him from inheritance. Its occurrence is very rare because no one is willing to exit without receiving their share, or because it occurs after death, so it is placed at the end of the book. Some hold the condition that at the time of the compromise, it must be known what types of assets are in the estate. The estate includes a dinar and clothes. The husband compromises on the clothes. Then the division between the daughter and sister will be in three shares: two shares to the daughter and one share to the sister.

Validity of Takharuj and the Athar of Uthman
Proves validity of takharuj via sale analogy and Uthman's precedent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on takharuj and explain how the athar of Uthman serves as evidence for its permissibility.

Core Idea
Takharuj is valid as a sale—the compensation need not equal the share's value.

In this lesson, we examine a key ruling in inheritance law: the permissibility of takharuj (buyout of an heir's share). The Hanafi school, unlike some others, treats takharuj as a sale, not a gift or discharge. This allows the compensation to be any amount, whether less or more than the share's actual value. The proof is an athar (precedent) from Caliph Uthman, who validated such a transaction. We'll analyze the ruling, the reasoning, and the evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on takharuj
Identify the act (takharuj) and its legal ruling (jā'iz/permissible) according to the source.
The ruling is unconditional regarding the amount of compensation.
2
Identify the legal reasoning
Understand why takharuj is treated as a sale (bay') and not as an ibra' (discharge). This allows any price, little or much.
Ibra' requires a liability; the share is not a liability until distributed.
3
Examine the athar of Uthman
Read the story of Tamadhir bint Asbagh and the compromise accepted by Caliph Uthman. What was the compensation and what was her share?
Her share was 1/4 of 1/8 = 1/32 of the estate; she received 80,000 dinars.
4
Extract the wajh al-dalalah
How does this athar prove the ruling? The disparity between share value and compensation shows disparity is permissible.
The exact numbers are not given, but the fact that Uthman permitted it is precedent.
5
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf
Some other schools require equivalent value to avoid gharar. The Hanafi response: sale allows any price.
Gharar is avoided because the share is defined, even if price differs.

This biography confirms the precedent and shows that takharuj was practiced by the Companions. The narration also clarifies the number of wives and shares. This athar serves as a strong dalil (proof) for the permissibility of takharuj.

Takharuj Rules for Gold, Silver, and Mixed Estates
Details takharuj conditions with gold/silver and mixed estates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid takharuj when the estate contains ribawi items and identify when immediate qabd is required.

Core Idea
Takharuj involving gold or silver is a sarf transaction requiring immediate hand-to-hand transfer to avoid riba.

When heirs agree to a compromise (takharuj) and the estate contains gold or silver, the transaction is treated as a sale of currency (sarf). This triggers special rules to prevent riba. This lesson breaks down the scenarios and conditions for validity, including the critical distinction between possession of guarantee and trust.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus of the compensation
First, determine if the compensation (badal al-sulh) is of a different ribawi genus than the exiting heir's share. If the estate is silver and they give gold, or vice versa, it is a sale of one genus for another.
Equality of value is not required for different genera, but immediate qabd is.
2
Check the status of the possessor of the remaining estate
If the person who holds the remaining estate is a denier (jāḥid) of the other's share, his existing possession (qabd damān) suffices for the required qabd. If he is an acknowledger (muqirr), his possession is trust (amāna) and a new qabd is necessary.
This distinction affects whether the transaction is valid without a new physical transfer.
3
Handle mixed estates with multiple assets
When the estate contains gold, silver, and other types of property, and the compensation is in gold or silver, the compensation must exceed the exiting heir's share from that particular genus. The excess corresponds to his share of the other assets, preventing riba.
The excess serves as the consideration for the non-ribawi portion.
4
Consider compensation in goods (urūḍ)
If the compensation is in goods (not gold or silver), no riba applies, so it is permissible unconditionally without the need for immediate qabd.
This scenario is the simplest.
5
Apply when both estate and compensation are dirhams and dinars
If the estate contains dirhams and dinars, and the compensation is also dirhams and dinars, the compromise is valid but requires immediate qabd in the session because it is a sarf transaction.
Even though the genus is changing, it is still sarf.

Commentary
This passage details the rulings of takharuj when the estate contains ribawi items (gold and silver). The key points are: (1) If the estate is only silver and the compromise is for gold (or vice versa), it is valid without equality of value, but immediate hand-to-hand transfer (qabd) in the session is required because it is a sarf (currency exchange) transaction. (2) If the person holding the remaining estate is a denier (i.e., denies the other's share), his existing possession suffices for the qabd because it is a possession of guarantee; if he is an acknowledger (muqirr), his possession is that of trust and does not count, so a new qabd is needed. (3) If the estate contains both gold, silver, and other assets, and the compensation is in gold or silver, the compensation must be greater than the exiting heir's share from that particular genus, with the excess corresponding to his share of the non-ribawi assets, to avoid riba. (4) If the compensation is in goods (urud), no riba applies, so it is permissible unconditionally. (5) If both the estate and compensation are in dirhams and dinars, it is valid as a sarf transaction with immediate qabd required. All these rules are derived from the general principle that takharuj is a sale, and when it involves ribawi items, the rules of sale (especially sarf) apply to prevent riba.

Key Terms
صرف (sarf): Exchange of gold for silver or vice versa, or of currency, requiring immediate hand-to-hand transfer (qabd) to avoid riba.
ربوا (ribā): Usury or interest, prohibited in transactions of certain commodities (gold, silver, food) when exchanged like for like with delay or inequality.
قبض (qabḍ): Physical or constructive possession, a condition for validity of certain sales like sarf.
تخارج (takharuj): A compromise among heirs where one or more exit the estate in exchange for a fixed compensation from the others.

Inferring the Number of Wives from a Share (Faraid Deduction)
Biographical anecdote with faraid deduction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to deduce the number of co-wives from the fractional share received by one wife in a Hanafi faraid calculation.

Core Idea
A wife receiving 1/32 of the estate indicates she is one of four wives sharing the 1/8.

In Hanafi faraid, wives collectively inherit one-eighth of the estate when the deceased has children. This passage from the biography of Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf shows how the share received by his wife Tamāḍur was used to deduce the total number of wives. You will learn to apply the same inverse calculation to uncover family structure from a given fraction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic share for wives
Recognize that wives collectively receive 1/8 of the estate when the deceased leaves children (Quran 4:12).
2
Determine the fraction received by one wife
In the passage, Tamāḍur received one-quarter of an eighth (ربع ثمن), which is 1/32 of the total estate.
1/4 × 1/8 = 1/32
3
Divide the collective share by the individual share
Number of wives = (Collective wives' share) / (Share of one wife) = (1/8) ÷ (1/32) = 4.
This works only if all wives share equally.
4
Reconcile conflicting reports
The passage notes another report that Abd al-Rahman left three wives. Reconciliation: Tamāḍur may have been excluded from the distribution (e.g., divorced or non-Muslim), so only three shared the 1/8.
Always consider possible exclusion when deducing.

Conditions for Valid Takharuj Involving Gold/Silver and Goods
Explains conditions for sulh involving gold/silver and goods to avoid riba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for valid takharuj when an heir receives gold/silver from an estate containing both gold/silver and goods, explain the requirement of qabd in the session for the sarf portion, analyze the objection regarding ibra', and identify the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah).

Core Idea
Takharuj with gold/silver must give more than the heir's share of that type; qabd in session is needed for the sarf part; ibra' is not valid for 'ayn.

This passage from Kitab al-Sulh addresses a common scenario in inheritance: when the estate contains both ribawi items (gold/silver) and non-ribawi goods, and the heirs wish to settle by giving one heir only gold/silver. The ruling details a condition to avoid riba, the rules of sarf, and responses to objections about alternative methods like ibra'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and condition
Recognize the scenario: estate has gold/silver and goods; one heir is given only gold/silver as settlement. The condition is that the given amount must exceed the heir's share of that type to avoid riba.
If equal or less, riba al-fadl occurs because the excess or entire amount lacks proper consideration.
2
Understand the sarf requirement and possession rules
The portion of the settlement equal to the heir's share of gold/silver is a sale of currency (sarf), requiring hand-to-hand possession (qabd) in the session. Also, if the remaining heir denies possession, his damani possession suffices; otherwise, amana does not substitute.
The general principle: substitute is possible only between homogenous possessions (both trust or both guarantee), or from guarantee to trust.
3
Analyze the ibra' objection and response
Question: Could the settlement be done by way of discharge (ibra')? Answer: Ibra' from a specific 'ayn (corporeal property) is invalid because isqat applies only to debts. However, some later scholars (sahib al-Nata'ij) consider ibra' from the claim of 'ayn valid.
The relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah) rejects ibra' in this context.
4
Resolve the house analogy objection
Objection: An earlier case involving sulh on part of a house was ruled invalid; this case seems analogous. Response: That ruling is from non-zahir al-riwayah; the zahir al-riwayah holds the sulh valid here.
The distinction hinges on authority weight within the Hanafi school.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi
The operative ruling: Takharuj is valid only if the gold/silver given exceeds the heir's share of that type, with immediate possession for the sarf portion. The ibra' approach is not accepted, and the house analogy does not apply.
This is based on zahir al-riwayah as preferred by Ashraf al-Hidayah.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Sulh (Compromise) covers multiple rulings. First, it discusses a case where the estate contains both gold/silver and other goods, and the heirs want to exclude one heir by giving him only gold or silver. The ruling is that this takharuj (mutual exclusion) is not valid unless the amount given exceeds the heir's share of that type. For example, if the heir is entitled to ten dirhams and some goods, the settlement must be for more than ten dirhams so that the ten dirhams are in exchange for his share of dirhams and the excess is in exchange for the goods. If the amount is equal or less, riba (usury) arises because the excess or the whole amount of that type is not properly matched with consideration. The same ruling applies whether the compensation is small or large when giving silver in exchange for gold or vice versa, because equality is not required between different currencies, but possession in the session is necessary as it is a sale of currency (sarf). The passage then explains a subtle point about possession: if the remaining heir who already has possession of the estate denies that it is in his possession, his prior possession suffices because his denial turns his possession into a guarantee (damani) possession, which can substitute for the required new possession. However, if his possession is merely a trust (amana), it cannot substitute. The general principle is that substitute is only possible between possessions of the same type (both trust or both guarantee), or from guarantee to trust, but not from trust to guarantee. The text then discusses an objection: could the arrangement be by way of discharge (ibra') instead of exchange? The response is that discharging from a specific 'ayn (corporeal property) is invalid because isqat applies only to debts. However, the author of al-Nata'ij notes that discharging from the claim of 'ayn is valid according to some jurists, so ibra' might be considered. Another objection based on an analogous case (sulh on part of a disputed house) is answered by stating that the analogous case is from non-zahir al-riwayah, while the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah) is that the sulh is valid. Finally, the requirement of hand-to-hand possession in the session is reaffirmed for the part involving currency exchange; if the consideration is goods, no such requirement exists as riba does not arise.

Sulh When Estate Includes Debts
Ruling on valid/invalid sulh with debts, with hiyal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between valid and invalid sulh conditions involving debts in an estate, and apply the preferred hila to avoid harm.

Core Idea
Sulh that transfers debt ownership without proper mechanism is invalid; ibra' or assignment makes it valid.

When heirs exclude one heir via sulh, and the estate includes debts owed by people, the handling of those debts determines the sulh's validity. This passage explains two conditions and presents legal devices for a fair outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The estate has debts (receivables) from people. Heirs want to make a sulh to exclude one heir by giving him something else. The debt is part of the estate.
2
Examine condition 1 (invalid)
If the heirs include the claimant in the sulh but exclude him from the debt, and keep the debt for themselves, the sulh is batil. Reason: they are transferring ownership of the debt to themselves without the debtor's consent.
Hanafi: tamlik of a debt to a third party requires hawala or debtor acceptance.
3
Examine condition 2 (valid)
If the claimant discharges the debtors from his share (ibra') and does not pursue them, the sulh is ja'iz. This is isqat (shedding) of his right, which is valid.
Ibra' does not require debtor acceptance.
4
Apply the preferred hila
To avoid harm to remaining heirs, the best hila: he remaining heirs lend the claimant his share in cash, then make sulh for the rest, and assign him to collect his share from debtors.
This preserves the estate balance.

Commentary
This passage deals with a specific scenario: when the estate includes debts (receivables) from people, and the heirs want to make a sulh (compromise) to exclude one heir by giving him something else. The ruling distinguishes two conditions. First, if the heirs include the claimant heir in the sulh on the condition that they exclude him from the debts and the debts remain solely for the other heirs, this sulh is invalid (batil). The reason is that this would transfer ownership of the debt from the claimant heir to the other heirs, which is a tamlik of a debt to someone other than the debtor (the one who owes it). In Hanafi law, a debt cannot be transferred to a third party without the debtor's consent or a valid assignment (hawala). Here, the claimant heir has a share in the debt as part of the estate, and giving it to the other heirs without proper transfer is invalid. Second, if the heirs stipulate that the claimant heir discharges (ibra') the debtors from his share of the debt and that the other heirs will not claim that share from the debtors, then the sulh is valid (ja'iz). The reason is that this is either an isqat (shedding or remission) of his right, which is valid for debts, or it is a tamlik of the debt from the debtor, which is permissible when the debtor agrees (here, the debtor is not explicitly involved, but the remission is valid). The text then presents two legal devices (hiyal) to achieve a valid outcome: (1) the remaining heirs voluntarily pay the claimant his share in cash, and (2) they lend the claimant his share and make sulh for the remaining property, with the claimant being assigned (ihala) to collect his share from the debtors. The second is preferred as it avoids harm to the other heirs. The commentary (tashrih) begins to explain the first condition, emphasizing that making someone other than the debtor owner of a debt is invalid.

Sulh and Division with Deceased's Debt: Mustaghriq vs Non-Mustaghriq
Ruling on sulh and division with deceased's debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the rulings on sulh and division when the deceased's debt is mustaghriq vs non-mustaghriq, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between qiyas and istihsan regarding division before debt payment.

Core Idea
A debt consuming the estate voids sulh and division; a partial debt permits sulh but division is disputed between qiyas and istihsan.

This passage addresses a critical intersection of inheritance and debt. When a deceased person leaves debts, heirs' ownership of the estate is contingent on debt payment. The ruling on sulh (compromise) and qisma (division) differs based on whether the debt covers the entire estate. Moreover, within the Hanafi school, there is a subtle difference of opinion regarding division before paying a non-consuming debt, between strict analogy (qiyas) and juristic preference (istihsan). Understanding this nuance is essential for correct fatwa in estate matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two debt scenarios
The passage distinguishes between mustaghriq debt (covering the entire estate) and non-mustaghriq debt (partial). The ruling for each depends on ownership status of the heirs.

2
Extract the ruling for each scenario
For mustaghriq: sulh and division are not permissible (la yajuz). For non-mustaghriq: sulh is not appropriate before debt payment, but if done, it is permissible; division before payment is disputed between istihsan and qiyas.
The text says 'la yanbaghi' (not appropriate) for sulh in non-mustaghriq, which indicates khilaf al-awla.
3

For mustaghriq, the heirs have not owned the estate (lam yatamallak al-waarith). For non-mustaghriq, the deceased's right (jiha) takes precedence, but ownership is partially established, allowing sulh if done.
The estate vests in the heirs at death but is encumbered by debt.

Al-Karkhi states division is not permissible by istihsan but permissible by qiyas. Qiyas considers the heirs' potential ownership; istihsan prioritizes the deceased's right and prevents complications. The preferred view (mufta bihi) follows istihsan.

Invalid and Valid Conditions for Sulh When Debt is in the Estate
Ruling on sulh with condition of excluding a heir from debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the invalid condition (making others owners of the debt) and the valid condition (ibrā') in sulh involving a heir's share of a debt, and explain the legal reasoning.

Core Idea
Sulh that transfers debt ownership to a non-debtor is batil; ibrā' that cancels the debt is valid.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a common scenario in inheritance: the deceased's estate includes debts owed by others, and the heirs wish to compromise (sulh) with one heir to simplify distribution. The matn presents two possible conditions: one that is invalid and one that is valid, based on the fundamental Hanafi principle that a debt cannot be owned by anyone other than the debtor. Understanding this distinction is essential to avoid invalid compromises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the invalid condition
The heirs include one heir in the compromise on condition that he is excluded from the debts, so all debts remain for the other heirs. This is presented as the invalid condition.
This condition makes the other heirs owners of the compromising heir's share of the debt, which is prohibited.
2
Understand why it is invalid
The text states the reason: 'fīhi tamlīk al-dayn min ghayr man ‘alayhi' – it involves making someone other than the debtor the owner of the debt. In Hanafi fiqh, a debt cannot be owned by a third party; it is a claim against a specific person.
This principle is derived from the nature of debt as a right that can only be transferred with the debtor's consent or via hawala.
3
Identify the valid condition
The valid alternative is that the compromising heir discharges (ibrā') the debtors from his share of the debt, so the debtors owe only the other heirs' shares. This is jā'iz because it is either an isqāt (dropping of claim) or tamlīk to the debtor himself.
In ibrā', the debt is effectively cancelled, so no transfer to a third party occurs.
4
Recognize the hilal approaches
The text mentions two legal stratagems (hīla) to achieve the valid outcome: (1) the other heirs pay the compromising heir's share voluntarily, or (2) they lend him his share and then he compromises, and they authorize him to collect his share from the debtors via hawala. The preferred method is the loan-hawala combination.
These stratagems avoid the invalidity while maintaining fairness among heirs.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the matn on sulh (compromise) in the context of inheritance when the estate includes debts owed to the deceased. The key point is that if the heirs include one of themselves in a compromise on the condition that the compromising heir is excluded from the debt (i.e., the debt remains entirely for the other heirs), the sulh is bāṭil (invalid). The reason given is that this would involve transferring ownership of a debt (the compromising heir's share) to someone who is not the debtor (i.e., the other heirs), which is not permissible in Islamic law. However, if the condition is that the compromising heir discharges the debtors from his share (ibrā'), and the other heirs will not recourse against the debtors for his share, then the sulh is jā'iz (valid) because it is a dropping of claim or a transfer of ownership of the debt to the debtor himself (by effectively canceling it), which is valid. The commentary further explains that in the invalid condition, the other heirs are made owners of the compromising heir's share of the debt, which is not allowed. In the valid condition, the compromising heir forgoes his share of the debt, thereby discharging the debtors, and the sulh is on the tangible assets only. This distinction is crucial in Hanafi fiqh for ensuring that sulh involving debts does not violate the principle that a debt can only be owned by the person liable for it.

Hiyal for Valid Sulh with Debt: The Preferred Method
Explanation of three hiyal and recommendation of the best one

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three legal stratagems (hiyal) for a valid sulh when the estate includes debts and state which is preferred and why.

Core Idea
Lend the share, compromise on the rest, then assign collection – the best hila minimizes harm.

When an estate includes debts, a sulh (compromise) among heirs must avoid involving the debt directly. The Hanafi school permits several hiyal (legal stratagems) to achieve a valid sulh. This passage from Al-Hidayah presents three options, two of which cause harm to the remaining heirs, and recommends the third as the best (awjah). Understanding these stratagems helps in structuring estate settlements within Shariah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem
The estate includes debts (duyūn). The compromising heir (muṣāliḥ) wants to settle his share with the other heirs, but the debt complicates direct sulh.
The debt is owed to the estate by third parties.
2
List the three hiyal
First: the compromising heir discharges the debtors from his share (ibrā'). Second: the other heirs pay him his share as a gift (tabarru'). Third: they lend him his share (qarḍ), make sulh on the rest, then he assigns them to collect from debtors (ḥawāla).
The first is mentioned earlier; the passage focuses on the second and third.
3
Understand the harm in the first two
In the first, remaining heirs lose the ability to collect that share from debtors. In the second, they give up liquid cash for a future claim, which is inferior (cash is better than credit).
Both cause loss (ḍarar) to the remaining heirs.
4
Detail the best hila
The heirs lend the compromising heir the exact amount of his share. Then they make sulh on the rest of the estate (beyond that share). Then the compromising heir assigns (yuḥīl) the remaining heirs to collect his share from the debtors.
This avoids immediate loss: the loan is a debt owed to the heirs, and they eventually collect via hawala.
5
Recognize the rationale
The loan does not transfer ownership permanently; the heirs will recoup the amount from the debtors. Thus, they neither forgive the debt nor give cash without return. This minimizes harm.
The text says: 'al-naqdu khayrun min al-dayn' (cash is better than credit).

Sulh on Unknown Estate Assets Without Debt
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sulh with unknown estate assets

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on sulh on unknown estate assets and identify the soundest view (al-aṣaḥḥ) as preferred in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Sulh on unknown estate assets is permissible if it avoids dispute, even if assets are unknown, provided riba risk is only a 'doubt of a doubt'.

When a deceased leaves no debts but the exact assets are unknown, can heirs settle their shares by sulh? This passage from Ashraf al-Hadaya presents an intra-Hanafi dispute. For measured/weighed items, some scholars prohibit due to potential riba, while others permit because the risk is too remote. For other unknown assets, the soundest view permits sulh because it prevents discord and the assets are in hand.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of assets
The passage distinguishes between (a) makīl (measured) or mawzūn (weighed) assets, and (b) other tangible assets (a'yān) that are not makīl/mawzūn.
This classification affects the riba consideration.
2
State the two opinions for makīl/mawzūn
One opinion (attributed to Zahir al-Din al-Marghinani) says sulh is not permissible due to the possibility of riba. Another (attributed to Abu Ja'far al-Hinduwani, declared sahīh in al-Khaniyyah) says it is permissible because the riba possibility is a 'doubt of a doubt' (shubhat al-shubha) and thus not legally credible.
Al-Marghinani's view is more cautious; al-Hinduwani's is more lenient.
3
State the two opinions for non-makīl/mawzūn
One opinion says it is not permissible because the sulh is considered a sale (bay') of an unknown item, which is invalid. The soundest view (al-aṣaḥḥ) says it is permissible because the ignorance does not lead to dispute, since the assets are in the possession of the remaining heirs.
Al-aṣaḥḥ is the relied-upon position.
4
Analyze the concept of shubhat al-shubha
The permissibility view for makīl/mawzūn argues that the risk of riba is based on two layers of doubt: first, whether the compensation is of the same genus as the estate asset; second, whether it is more or less. Such a 'doubt of a doubt' is too remote to affect the ruling.
This is a usul principle: remote possibilities are not legally considered.
5
Conclude with the mufta bihi ruling
For makīl/mawzūn, the preferred view is permissibility (al-Hinduwani's view, declared sahīh). For non-makīl/mawzūn, al-aṣaḥḥ is permissibility. Thus, in both scenarios, sulh is valid if it prevents discord.
The practical effect is that heirs can settle without knowing exact shares.

Zahir al-Din al-Marghinani says it is not permissible because of the possibility of riba: if the estate contains wheat (in which the sulh-making heir has a share) and the compensation for sulh is also wheat, then it is necessary that the wheat of the sulh be more than his share in the estate, because equality is a condition, but there is also the possibility it could be less, resulting in riba. ''And it is said that it is permissible'' - Faqih Abu Ja'far al-Hinduwani says it is permissible (al-Khaniyyah declares it sahih). The reason is that the possibility of riba is a doubt of a doubt (shubhat al-shubha), and a doubt is not a doubt of a doubt. Explanation: in this case, first there is a possibility that the compensation is of the same genus as a measured/weighed item in the estate, and second there is a possibility that the amount is more or less, leading to riba. Commentary
This passage deals with a scenario where the deceased left no debts, all assets are tangible (a'yān) but their exact identity and quantity are unknown (majhūl). The ruling depends on the type of asset. First, if the sulh is on makīl (measured by volume, e.g., grains) or mawzūn (weighed, e.g., metals), there are two opinions: one (attributed to Zahir al-Din al-Marghinani) says it is not permissible because of the risk of ribā (usury) since the compensation might be unequal without knowledge; the other (attributed to Abu Ja'far al-Hinduwānī, declared sahīh in al-Khāniyya) says it is permissible because the risk is based on a 'doubt of a doubt' (shubhat al-shubha), i.e., a very remote possibility that is not legally considered. Second, if the assets are not of the makīl/mawzūn type but are still unknown, again two opinions: one says it is not permissible because the sulh is considered a sale (bay') of an unknown item, which is invalid; the other (the soundest, al-aṣaḥḥ) says it is permissible because here ignorance does not lead to dispute, since the items are in the possession of the remaining heirs and can be seen. The commentary elaborates that the dispute stems from different views on the nature of sulh: whether it is a sale or a type of settlement that tolerates some ignorance to avoid dispute. The Hanafi position generally favors permissibility in such cases to facilitate amicable settlement.

Key Terms
مكيل (makīl): Items sold by volume measure, e.g., grains.
موزون (mawzūn): Items sold by weight, e.g., metals.
شبهة الشبهة (shubhat al-shubha): A remote probability; a doubt upon a doubt.
اصح (aṣaḥḥ): The soundest or most correct view among scholars.
بيع (bayʿ): Sale, exchange of property for compensation.

Sulh and Qisma: Engulfing vs Non-Engulfing Debt
Ruling on sulh and qisma when deceased's debt engulfs the estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on sulh and qisma when the deceased's debt engulfs the estate, and distinguish between the Hanafi internal dispute on qisma by qiyas and istihsan.

Core Idea
If debt engulfs the estate, heirs have no ownership, so sulh and qisma are invalid.

This passage from Al-Hidayah examines the impact of the deceased's debts on two key post-death transactions: sulh (compromise) and qisma (division) among heirs. When the debt covers the entire estate (mustaghriq), the estate belongs to creditors, not heirs, making any transaction invalid. When the debt is less than the estate, the heirs are advised to settle debts first, but if they proceed, later scholars validate sulh, while qisma remains debated between qiyas and istihsan, as highlighted by Imam al-Karkhi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two debt scenarios
First, classify the deceased's debt as engulfing (mustaghriq) or non-engulfing. This distinction determines the permissibility of sulh and qisma.
Engulfing means debt ≥ total estate; non-engulfing means debt < estate.
2
Determine the ruling for each scenario
For engulfing debt: sulh and qisma are invalid because heirs have no ownership. For non-engulfing debt: sulh is discouraged before payment but valid if done (according to later scholars).
The text uses 'لا ينبغى' (should not) not 'لا يجوز' (not permissible) for sulh in non-engulfing case.
3
Explore the internal Hanafi dispute on qisma
Imam al-Karkhi states that qisma in the non-engulfing scenario is invalid by istihsan (juristic preference) but valid by qiyas (analogy). This reveals a methodological tension between strict analogy and equitable consideration.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi between two principles, not between madhhabs.
4
Understand the role of qiyas and istihsan
Qiyas: Division is analogized to other property divisions - it does not harm creditors because each heir's share is determined. Istihsan: Division may cause harm to creditors (e.g., difficulty in collecting debt) or lead to disputes, so it is invalidated.
The text leaves both views without final tarjih, indicating a ongoing methodological debate.

Commentary
This passage addresses the impact of the deceased's debts on sulh and qisma (partition) among heirs. Two scenarios: (1) Debt engulfing the entire estate (mustaghriq): The estate is considered owned by creditors, not heirs. Therefore, the heirs have no right to transact (sulh or qisma) because they lack ownership. (2) Debt not engulfing: The heirs are cautioned not to make sulh until the debt is paid, because the deceased's right (i.e., the creditors' claim) takes precedence over the heirs' right. However, if they do make sulh, it is valid according to the later scholars (mutā'akhkhirīn). Regarding division (qisma) specifically, Imam al-Karkhi distinguished between qiyas (analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference): by qiyas, division is valid because each heir's share is determined, but by istihsan it is invalid to avoid potential harm to creditors. The commentary notes that Imam al-Shafi'i also agrees in the first scenario. This shows the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf between qiyas and istihsan.

Key Terms
دين مستغرق (dayn mustaghriq): A debt that covers the entire estate, leaving nothing for heirs.
قسمة (qisma): Division of inheritance among heirs.
استحسان (istiḥsān): Juristic preference; adopting a ruling that is more suitable for the situation.
قياس (qiyās): Analogical reasoning; applying a known ruling to a new case due to shared cause.
