Aariyah is a Trust, Not a Guarantee
Resolves the dispute on whether aariyah is a trust or a guarantee.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school classifies aariyah as a trust and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position on borrower liability.

Core Idea
A borrowed item is a trust; accidental loss does not incur liability unless negligence occurs.

This passage introduces a classic inter-madhhab dispute on the nature of aariyah (loan). The Hanafi position, rooted in the hadith 'al-ariyatu mu'adah', treats the borrowed item as a trust (amanah), meaning the borrower is not liable for accidental loss unless he transgresses. The Shafi'i school argues for guarantee (dhaman) because the borrower benefits without entitlement. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to analyze proofs and recognize the practical consequences of differing legal methodologies.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: aariyah is a trust (amanah) — the borrower is not liable for accidental loss unless he transgresses (ta'addi).
Note that 'amanah' in Hanafi fiqh covers deposit (wadi'ah), hire (ijarah), and loan (aariyah).
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
Recognize the opposing view: the Shafi'i school holds the borrower liable (dhaman) for any loss or damage, even accidental.
The Shafi'i argument is based on the borrower taking possession for his own benefit without a prior right.
3
Follow the Shafi'i proof
Analyze the Shafi'i dalil: the borrower took another's property for his own benefit without entitlement, therefore he is liable, similar to a buyer who is liable before delivery.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal: the permission (idhn) to use the item is established out of necessity for benefit and does not extend beyond that; the hadith only obligates return, not guarantee.
The Hanafi principle is that permission for a specific purpose does not imply liability beyond that purpose.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: under Hanafi, the borrower does not compensate for accidental loss; under Shafi'i, he must compensate. This affects daily lending and borrowing.
This difference applies to all items lent for free use.

Commentary
This passage introduces a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the liability of the borrower in aariyah. The Hanafi position, stated clearly in Al-Hidayah, is that aariyah is an amanah (trust). The borrower is not liable for accidental loss or damage unless he has been negligent or transgressed (ta'addi). The Shafi'i position is that the borrower is liable (dhaman) because he takes possession for his own benefit without any inherent right to the property (like a purchaser). The Hanafi response argues that the permission (idhn) to use the item is granted out of necessity for benefit, and this permission does not extend to imposing liability beyond the borrowed use. The hadith 'al-ariyatu mu'adah' (the loan is to be returned) indicates only the obligation to return the item, not to guarantee it.

Key Terms
امانت (amanah): A trust; the borrower holds the item as a trustee, not liable for loss without negligence.
مضمون (dhaman): Guarantee; liability to compensate for loss or damage.
تعدی (ta'addi): Transgression or negligence; exceeding the bounds of permission.

Liability of the Borrower in 'Ariyah: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i

Core Idea
The borrower is not liable for accidental loss because 'ariyah is a trust, not a sale — liability requires transgression.

In this passage, al-Marghinani presents the Hanafi refutation of the Shafi'i claim that the borrower of an item is liable for its loss. The dispute hinges on the nature of the contract: is it a trust (amanah) or a contract of exchange? The Hanafi school argues that the borrowed item is a trust in the hands of the borrower, and liability only arises from transgression (ta'addi) or negligence. This lesson will walk you through the arguments and proofs.

Guided Path
1
State the Shafi'i claim and its basis
Identify the Shafi'i argument: the borrower took possession of another's property for his own benefit, so he is liable, analogous to a buyer. Understand the qiyas (analogy) they use.
The Shafi'i analogize to a sale where possession implies liability.
2
Identify the Hanafi response to the claim
The Hanafi response: the word 'ariyah does not imply liability; it grants benefit without compensation. The borrower's possession is with permission, so no transgression (ta'addi) occurred, which is necessary for liability.
Focus on the legal nature of the contract as a trust.

The analogy to purchase is invalid because in sale, taking possession is part of the contract and carries its effect; in 'ariyah, possession is merely following permission. The obligation to return the item is a matter of expense (mu'unah), not a breach of possession.

These proofs collectively establish the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage is a presentation of the Hanafi scriptural proofs for the position that the borrower (musta'ir) is not liable for the borrowed item unless he transgresses (ta'addi). The author begins by listing five proofs. First, the Quranic verse (4:58) commanding the rendering of trusts; the Hanafis argue that since the specific item is destroyed, the obligation to return it falls away, implying no liability. Second, the hadith 'There is no liability on a trustee' (la daman 'ala mu'taman), though its chain is debated, the author weighs it as acceptable due to Bukhari's inclusion. Third, the hadith explicitly stating no liability on the non-wrongdoing borrower, which is strengthened by supporting evidence. Fourth, the athar of 'Umar comparing loan to trust with liability only upon transgression. Fifth, the athar of 'Ali stating loan is not a sale or guarantee. The author then addresses an objection from the hadith of Safwan, where the Prophet apparently instructed giving something as a guaranteed loan. The response is twofold: (1) the wording is disputed among narrators, with the most reliable narrator omitting 'guarantee'; (2) even if 'guarantee' is accepted, it means guarantee of return, not liability for damage. This objection and response reinforce the Hanafi position that the borrower is not liable without transgression.

عاریت (ariyah): A loan of a beneficial thing for use without payment, where the borrower is not liable for damage except in case of transgression.
ضمان (daman): Liability to compensate for loss or damage. Here, it means the borrower is not liable unless he transgresses.
مستعیر (musta'ir): The borrower who receives the loan (ariyah).

The Borrower's Right to Lend the Borrowed Item: Four Types of Loan and Effect of Personal Use
Explains four types of i'aarah and borrower's right to lend

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of 'ariyah (loan) in Hanafi fiqh and apply the ruling on the borrower's right to lend based on whether the loan is absolute or restricted, and whether the borrower has personally used the item.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the borrower's right to lend depends on the loan's terms; personal use may specify and restrict further lending.

Lending a borrowed item to someone else is not always straightforward. The Hanafi school divides loans into four categories based on whether time and benefit are specified. Understanding these categories is crucial for determining when a borrower may lawfully lend the item, and when doing so makes him liable. This lesson unpacks the Hidayah's ruling and the scholarly disagreement on whether the borrower's own use 'specifies' the use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four logical types of loan
The loan (i'aarah) can be absolute in time and benefit, restricted in both, restricted in time only, or restricted in benefit only. Recognize each type and its basic ruling.
These types are derived from the wording of the contract.
2
Determine if the loan is absolute or restricted
If the lender did not specify the time or type of benefit, the loan is absolute (i'aarah mutlaqah). If either was specified, it is restricted (muqayyadah). This classification governs the borrower's authority.
The presence of any restriction limits the borrower's discretion.
3
Apply the rule on lending to others
In an absolute loan, the borrower may lend the item to others even if the use varies (e.g., riding). In a restricted loan, he may only lend for uses that do not vary among users (e.g., loading) unless the alternative is similar or better.
The key is whether the benefit differs with different users.
4
Analyze the effect of personal use on specification
If the borrower personally uses the item in a certain way (e.g., rides the horse), the majority view (Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn) holds that this act specifies the use, preventing subsequent lending for that same use. A minority view (al-Sarakhsi, Khwaharzadah) disagrees, allowing further lending without liability. Understand both positions and the practical consequence (daman).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf with a clear mufta bihi position.

Translation: The author of Hidayah says: This is when the loan is absolute, and it has four types. First, the loan is absolute in time and benefit: in this case the borrower has the option to benefit in whatever manner and at whatever time he wishes, acting upon the absolute. Second, both time and benefit are restricted: he may not exceed what was stated, acting upon the restriction, unless the contrary is similar or better. Wheat is like wheat. Third, time is restricted but benefit is absolute. Fourth, its opposite: he may not exceed what he specified. So if he borrowed an animal and did not specify anything, he has the right to load his own goods or give it to another for loading, because loading does not differ, and he has the right to ride himself or let another ride, even though riding differs, because when he gave absolute permission, he has the right to specify. If he rode himself, he cannot let another ride, because his riding became specified. If he let another ride, he cannot ride himself. If he does, he will be liable, because the riding became specified. Commentary on 'He said: This and so forth': There is a very important objection to this statement of the author of Hidayah which we will mention at the end as Tanbeeh after explaining the text. First understand the text. The author of Hidayah says: This is when the loan is absolute, i.e., no time or particular use is specified. Then it should also be clear that rationally there are four types of loans. (1) Absolute: no time or benefit specified. Its ruling: the borrower may benefit in any way and as long as he wishes. (2) Restricted: both time and benefit specified. Its ruling: it is not permissible to exceed the specified time and benefit, so that the mu'ir's restriction is followed. However, if the violation is similar or better than the restriction, it is permissible. He said: 'And wheat is like wheat' etc. Wheat is like wheat. For example, someone borrowed a horse with the condition that today he will load ten maunds of wheat and travel three kos, and tomorrow he will return the horse. Then he cannot ride it, only load, and that only today. If he loads tomorrow, he will be liable, just as if he takes it beyond three kos. If he loads someone else's wheat of the same amount, that is also permissible, because wheat is equal. If he loads something lighter, that is even more permissible. Yes, if he loads iron instead of wheat, it is not permissible, because iron would hurt the horse's back. He said: 'And the third is that it is' etc.: That in which time is specified but benefit is not specified. Its opposite: benefit is restricted and time is absolute. Their ruling: he may not exceed the restriction. But if someone borrowed a horse and the mu'ir did not specify the kind of benefit or time, then he has the right to load his own goods or give it to another for loading, because loading is the same act from both. Also, he may ride himself or give it to another for riding, even though riding acts differ, but due to the absolute nature, he can specify whomever he wants. However, after specifying his own or another's riding, he cannot go against it. If he does, he will be liable, because it became specified by his specification. Fakhral-Islam's greatness... Some scholars said that riding does not become specified, such that after riding himself he can still let another ride, and after letting another ride he can still ride himself. In no case will he be liable. Shams al-A'immah al-Sarakhsi and Shaykh al-Islam Khwaharzadah chose this. Tanbeeh: What is the referent of 'this' in the author of Hidayah's statement 'And this is when the loan occurs'? Most commentators said: It refers to what was mentioned about the borrower's authority to lend. This is objected to because previously it is stated: 'He has the right to lend it if it is something that does not differ with different users.' So the meaning is that items that do not differ with different users, the borrower's authority to lend is only when the loan is absolute. However, in most fiqh books including the texts, it is explicitly established that for items that do not differ with different users, the authority to lend is when the loan is restricted to the borrower's own use. If there is no such restriction and the loan is absolute, the borrower has absolute authority to lend, whether the item differs with different users or not. The author of Hidayah himself clarified this at the end of the issue when he said: 'So if he borrowed an animal and did not specify anything, he has the right to load and give it to another for loading because loading does not differ, and he has the right to ride and let another ride even though riding differs.' Therefore, the referent that most commentators mentioned would only be correct if the previous statement 'if it is something that does not differ with different users' were not attached. For this reason, the fairest thing is that if the author of Hidayah had deleted his statement 'And this is when the loan is absolute' and said 'The loan is of four types', it would have been better. At this point, the author of Kafi's style is very good: He first said 'He has the right to lend' and mentioned Imam Shafi'i's difference and discussed the evidence from both sides, then said: 'Then this issue has two aspects. Either the loan occurs absolute with respect to the benefit, such as he gave a cloth for wearing without specifying the wearer, or an animal for riding without specifying the rider, or an animal for loading without specifying the loader. In this case, he has the right to lend, whether the borrowed item is one in which people differ in benefit like wearing and riding, or they do not differ like loading, acting upon the absolute wording. If the loan occurs restricted, such as if he borrowed to wear himself or to load himself, then he has the right to lend in what people do not differ in benefit, like loading, and he does not have the right to lend in what people differ in benefit, like wearing and riding.' Then he said: 'This is the discussion regarding the borrower's lending. As for the discussion of his own benefit from the borrowed item, there are four types.'

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on Al-Hidayah addressing the ruling of a borrower lending the borrowed item to a third party. The author of Hidayah first outlines the four logical types of loan ('iara) based on whether time and benefit are specified or not. He then applies this to the borrower's authority to lend. When the loan is absolute (no restriction on time or use), the borrower may lend the item to others regardless of whether the use varies among people. For example, if the item is an animal borrowed without specifying riding or loading, the borrower may load his goods, ride himself, or let another load or ride. However, once he personally uses the item in a particular way (e.g., rides it), that use becomes specified, and he cannot then let another use it in the same way; if he does, he becomes liable for any damage. Some later Hanafi scholars like Imam al-Sarakhsi and Khwaharzadah held that the use does not become specified, and the borrower can still lend after personal use. The passage also includes a Tanbeeh (warning) pointing out an apparent contradiction in the Hidayah's wording: earlier it suggests that the borrower can only lend items that do not vary with use when the loan is absolute, but later it gives an example of riding (which varies) being permissible when the loan is absolute. The commentator resolves this by noting that the correct position is that in an absolute loan, the borrower can lend any item, varying or not. The author of Al-Kafi is praised for his clear presentation of this issue, distinguishing between absolute and restricted loans and their effect on the borrower's right to lend.

مستعير (musta'ir): The borrower who takes the item on loan.

Verbal Formulas for Hibah in Hanafi Fiqh
Lists and proofs for verbal formulas of gift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the valid verbal expressions for constituting a hibah contract according to the Hanafi school and explain the ruling for each based on the provided proofs.

Core Idea
The validity of a gift contract depends on the specific wording; ambiguous expressions require careful Hanafi interpretation based on intent and context.

In Hanafi fiqh, a hibah (gift) contract is concluded by certain verbal formulas that indicate transfer of ownership. This lesson explores the explicit and implicit expressions recognized by the school, the proofs supporting them (including hadith and Quranic usage), and the critical distinction between hibah (ownership transfer) and 'ariyah (usufruct loan). Understanding these nuances ensures that one can correctly classify a transaction and know the legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit expressions for hibah
List the clear expressions: wahaba (gave), nahala (bestowed), a'ta (granted). Recognize that these directly imply transfer of ownership.
The hadith of Nu'man ibn Bashir uses 'nahaltu' and establishes it as valid for hibah.
2
Understand the ruling for 'feeding' (at'ama) expressions
When 'at'amtuka' is used with an object that is actually eaten (e.g., bread, dates), it implies hibah (ownership). If the object is not eaten (e.g., land), it is 'ariyah (loan for use).
The objection from Shaykh Anzari regarding the Quranic verse is addressed by the condition of mentioning a second object (maf'ūl thānī).
3
Analyze life-grant ('umra) and dwelling (suknā) expressions
Saying 'a'martuka' (I gave as a life-grant) results in hibah due to the hadith: the property belongs to the recipient and his heirs. 'Suknā' alone or in combination like 'hibah suknā' is interpreted as 'ariyah because usufruct is specified.
The principle: ambiguous expressions (muḥtamal) are interpreted according to the established meaning (muḥkam).
4
Examine 'clothing' (kasā) and 'granting' (manaḥa) expressions
'Kasawtuka' (I clothed you) is hibah because it implies ownership, supported by Quranic usage. 'Manahtuka' (I granted) is 'ariyah based on the hadith 'al-munḥatu mardūd' (the gift is to be returned).
If the same word may mean both, the established meaning (here 'ariyah) takes precedence.
5
Apply the general rule for ambiguous expressions
When wording can indicate either hibah or 'ariyah, the default is 'ariyah unless the giver's intention for hibah is clear from context or explicit additional words (e.g., 'hiya laka hibah taskunuhā' is hibah because 'taskunuhā' is advice, not a qualification).
This rule ensures legal certainty.

Regarding the third word 'a'martuka': By saying 'I have given you this thing as a life-grant ('umra)', hibah is concluded because of the hadith: 'Whoever gives a life-grant ('umra), it belongs to the recipient and his heirs.' Similarly, if one says 'I have made this house a life-grant for you.' Regarding the fourth word 'hamaltuka': The literal meaning is 'to mount', which would be 'ariyah, but it can also imply hibah. It is said: 'The amir mounted so-and-so on a horse' meaning he gave him ownership. So when intention is present, it is interpreted accordingly. If one says 'I clothed you with this garment' (kasawtuka), it is hibah because it implies ownership. Allah says 'or their clothing' (aw kiswatuhum). And it is said: 'The amir clothed so-and-so' meaning he made him owner. If one says 'I have given you this slave-girl as a gift (manahtuka)', it is 'ariyah due to the previous hadith. If one says 'My house is a hibah for you for dwelling' or 'dwelling is a hibah', it is 'ariyah because 'ariyah is established for transfer of usufruct, while hibah can be for either, so the ambiguous is carried on the established. Similarly for 'umra suknā', etc. If one says 'It is a hibah for you to dwell in it', it is hibah because 'to dwell' is advice, not an interpretation.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah is an expansion on the matn of al-Hidayah regarding the verbal formulas (alfāẓ) by which a gift (hibah) contract is concluded. The author lists several expressions and provides legal reasoning and proofs for each.

The primary explicit expressions are: wahaba (he gave), nahala (he bestowed), and a‘ṭā (he granted). The first is clear in the context of hibah. The second, nahala, is used in the Quran and hadith. The hadith of Nu‘man ibn Bashir is cited: the Prophet (ﷺ) used it to question a gift, and he ordered its return when inequality was found. This establishes that nahala is a valid expression for hibah.

For the expression aṭ‘amtu (I fed), the ruling depends on what is being fed. If the object is something actually eaten (e.g., grain, bread, dates), then saying 'I fed you this' means transfer of ownership (tamlik al-‘ayn), i.e., hibah. But if it is something not eaten (e.g., land), it means loan for use (‘āriyah) because the intent is to let the recipient benefit from its produce. The author addresses an objection from Shaykh Anzarī concerning the verse 'feeding ten poor people' (Sūrat al-Mā’idah): the response is that the verse does not mention a second object (maf‘ūl thānī), so the word iṭ‘ām retains its original meaning of mere permission (ibāḥah), not ownership.

For the expression a‘martu (I gave as a life-grant), the hadith of the Prophet (ﷺ) is cited: 'Whoever gives a life-grant (‘umrā), it belongs to the recipient and his heirs.' This transfers ownership, thus hibah.

For the expression ḥamaltu (I mounted), its literal meaning is 'to make ride', which would be ‘āriyah, but it is also used metaphorically for transferring ownership. If the giver intends hibah, it is honored.

Further expressions: kasawtu (I clothed) is hibah because it implies ownership, supported by the Quranic verse 'or their clothing' and common usage. The word manaḥtu (I granted) is specifically for ‘āriyah due to the hadith 'al-munḥatu mardūd' (the gift is to be returned). When someone says 'my house is hibah for you to dwell in' (hiya laka hibah taskunuhā), it is hibah because 'taskunuhā' is just advice, not a qualification. But if he says 'hibah suknā' (a gift of dwelling), it is ‘āriyah because it specifies usufruct. Similarly, combinations like 'umrā suknā' or 'suknā ṣadaqah' are interpreted as ‘āriyah because the usufruct element is established.

The key principle is that when a word can mean either hibah (ownership transfer) or ‘āriyah (usufruct transfer), the ambiguous is carried on the established (al-muḥtamal ‘alā al-muḥkam). Since ‘āriyah is more specific to usufruct, it takes precedence unless intention for hibah is clear.

Key Terms
ہبہ (hibah): A gift contract transferring ownership of a specific property without compensation.
عاریت (ʿāriyah): A contract granting temporary use of a property without ownership, usually free of charge.
عمری (ʿumrā): A lifetime grant: giving property for the recipient's life, after which it passes to his heirs.
سکنی (suknā): A gift of dwelling: giving the right to reside in a house, typically for the recipient's life.
نحلت (naḥaltu): Used in the hadith to mean 'I gave' or 'I bestowed' as a gift.

Hiba al-Musha': Hanafi Ruling and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Inter-madhhab dispute on gifting undivided shares

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifting an undivided share (hibah al-musha'), explain the condition of possession, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
In gift, possession is textually required; undivided shares cannot be completely possessed without separation.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents the Hanafi position on gifting an undivided share (hibah al-musha') and addresses the Shafi'i critique. The Hanafi school distinguishes between divisible and non-divisible items, requiring complete possession (qabd) after separation for ownership transfer in divisible items. Understanding this ruling requires grasping the role of textual evidence (mansus 'alayh) in gift contracts, which differs from sale or loan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi rule for undivided share gifts
First, understand that for divisible items, the gift contract is valid but ownership transfers only after the gifted portion is separated (mahuz) and completely possessed. For non-divisible items, the gift is valid even without separation.
This distinction is unique to Hanafi; Shafi'i does not require separation.
2
Understand the condition of possession (qabd)
The core evidence is the hadith requiring possession for gift validity. Complete possession of an undivided share is impossible without combining it with another item (not gifted). Hence, the Hanafi school insists on separation first.
Possession is a textual condition (mansus 'alayh) in gift, unlike sale.
3
Examine the Shafi'i objection and its basis
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that gift is a contract of ownership (tamlik) like sale, so common ownership (musha') should not invalidate it. He cites analogy with loan and bequest where common ownership is allowed.
Shafi'i sees the contract itself as sufficient for ownership transfer.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes between contracts requiring possession textually (gift) and those that do not (sale, loan). For gift, complete possession is mandatory; for loan, deficient possession suffices because it combines guarantee and gratuity. Validating gift of musha' would force the donor to bear division costs, which he did not undertake.
The Hanafi proof is based on textual imperative and avoiding imposition on the donor.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
In Hanafi, the donee owns the share only after division and full possession. In Shafi'i, ownership transfers immediately upon contract. This affects inheritance, sales, and other transactions before division.
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is the Hanafi view.

ترجمہ ہماری دلیل یہ ہے کہ قبضہ منصوص علیہ ہے ہیہ میں تو کامل قبضہ شرط ہو گا اور مشاع چیز کمال قبضہ کو قبول نہیں کرتی مگر غیر کو اس کے ساتھ ملانے سے حالانکہ وہ غیر موہوب نہیں ہے اور اس لئے کہ اس کی تجویز میں واہب کے ذمہ ایسی چیز لازم ہے جس کا اس نے التزام نہیں کیا اور وہ بیٹوارہ ہے اسی لئے قبضہ سے پہلے اس کا جواز ممتنع ہوا تا کہ اس کے ذمہ سپرد کرنا لازم نہ آئے بخلاف اس کے جو قابل قسمت نہیں۔ کیونکہ اس میں ناقص قبضہ ہی ممکن ہے تو اسی پر اکتفاء کیا جائے گا۔ اور اس لئے کہ واہب کے ذمہ بٹوارہ کا خرچہ لازم نہیں ہوگا۔ اور باری ایسی چیز میں لازم آتی ہے جس کے ساتھ اس نے تبرع نہیں کیا۔ اور وہ منفعت ہے اور ہبہ مال مین کے ساتھ ملاقی ہوا ہے اور وصیت کی شرط قبضہ کا ہونا نہیں ہے۔ اسی طرح بیع صحیح ، بیع فاسد، بیع صرف اور مسلم کسی میں بھی قبضہ منصوص علیہ نہیں ہے اور اس لئے کہ یہ ضمانتی عقود ہیں تو ہزارہ کا خرچہ لازم ہونے کے مناسب ہیں اور قرض ایک وجہ سے تبرع ہے اور ایک وجہ سے عقد ضمان ہے۔ پس ہم نے ناقص قبضہ شرط کیا نہ کہ بٹوارہ دونوں جہتوں پر عمل کرتے ہوئے علاوہ از میں قرض میں قبضہ منصوص علیہ نہیں ہے۔ اور اگر اپنے شریک کو ہبہ کیا تب بھی جائز نہیں۔ کیونکہ حکم کا مدار تو نفس شیوع پر ہے۔
The gift (hibah): Every item that is not divisible (ma la yuqsam) like dirhams and dinars and whatever else is divisible (ma yuqsam). Explanation: Items that after division become completely unusable, like a single slave or a single animal, or where the benefit before division is lost, like a small house, small bath, handmill etc. — such items are non-divisible, and others are divisible. A third reason for the difference: any item shared between two where one wants division and the other does not, and the qadi cannot force the unwilling one — such is non-divisible, and what remains is divisible. Secondly, in divisible items, the invalidity of a gift of an undivided share (musha') means that ownership is not established, not that the gift contract becomes void. So the gift contract for an undivided share in divisible items is valid, but ownership is established only when division occurs and it becomes separated (mahuz), i.e., the donor has no connection with it. Thirdly, consideration of shar'ah is at the time of possession, not at the time of contract. Thus, if one gives a share and separates it from others and hands it over, or gives half a house and before handing over gives the remaining half and hands over, the gift is valid. Fourthly, since possession is textually required in gift, complete possession is a condition, like facing the qiblah in prayer is textually required, so complete facing is necessary; facing only the hatim is insufficient. When these points are understood, look at the original text: "It is not permissible to gift a divisible item unless it is mahuz." Here mahuz means free from the donor's ownership and rights. This condition excludes gifting fruits on a tree without the tree, wool on a sheep, or standing crops without land, because in these cases the gifted item is not completely possessed, as it is still attached to the donor's property. The condition of being divided (maqsum) excludes an undivided share: if one takes possession of fruits while still on the tree and they remain common between the donee and others, the gift is not valid. So the meaning of the text is: For items capable of division, if the donor wishes to gift such an item, then if it is mahuz (free from donor's rights) and maqsum (divided), the gift is valid; if not mahuz and maqsum, it is not valid. For items not divisible (i.e., after division they become useless), a gift of an undivided share (musha') is valid.

Imam Shafi'i's viewpoint: Imam Shafi'i said: It is valid in both cases because it is a contract of transfer of ownership, so it is valid in both undivided and separate property, like sale in all its types. This is because an undivided share is capable of receiving the ruling of gift, which is ownership, so it is a valid subject. The fact that it is a gratuitous contract does not invalidate it due to common ownership, like loan (qard) and bequest (wasiyyah).

Translation: Imam Shafi'i says it is valid in both cases because it is a contract of transfer of ownership, so it is valid in both undivided and separate like sale in all its types. This is because an undivided share accepts the ruling of gift, which is ownership, so it is a locus for gift. That it is a benevolence does not nullify it due to common ownership, like loan and bequest.
Explanation of "And he said" etc.: Imam Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad say that whether the gifted item is divisible or not, the gift of an undivided share is valid in both cases. Thus in al-Rawdah: "The gift of an undivided share is valid whether divisible or not." In al-Malikiyyah: "It is not prevented by common ownership even if before division." In Hanbali fatawa: "The gift of an undivided share is valid even if its division is difficult, and it is valid from a partner or other, whether it can be transferred or not, and whether it is something whose division is impossible like a share in a slave, animal, or jewel."
The Hanafi proof: Our proof is that possession is textually required in gift, so complete possession is a condition. An undivided share does not accept complete possession except by combining it with another (which is not gifted). Also, validating it would impose on the donor something he did not undertake, i.e., division. Hence its validity before possession is prevented so that delivery is not obligatory. This is unlike what is not divisible, because deficient possession is possible there, so it suffices. Also, the donor is not liable for the expenses of division. The apportionment (muhaya'ah) is necessary regarding something he did not gift voluntarily, which is the usufruct. Gift is connected to the substance ('ayn), while bequest does not require possession. Similarly, a valid sale, invalid sale, currency exchange, and salam — possession is not textually required in them because they are contracts of guarantee (damān), so bearing the expense of division is appropriate. Loan (qard) is gratuitous from one aspect and a guarantee contract from another. So we condition deficient possession without division, acting on both aspects, and moreover, possession is not textually required in loan. Even if one gives a gift to his partner, it is not valid because the ruling revolves around the very nature of common ownership.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses the Hanafi ruling on gifting an undivided share (hibah al-musha') and presents the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school. The Hanafi position is that in items capable of division (qabil al-qismah), a gift of an undivided share is not valid unless the gifted portion is separated (mahuz) and actually possessed completely. In items not capable of division (ghayr qabil al-qismah), the gift of an undivided share is valid even without separation. The commentary first clarifies the distinction between divisible and non-divisible items: divisible items are those that can be divided without losing utility; non-divisible items are those where division destroys utility (e.g., a single slave or a small house). The author then explains that 'valid' means the gift contract itself is sound but ownership transfers only upon complete possession after division. The Shafi'i view, along with Maliki and Hanbali, holds that undivided share gifts are valid in all cases because the contract of ownership (tamlik) is similar to sale, and common ownership does not invalidate a gratuitous contract like loan or bequest. The Hanafi proof responds: possession is textually required (mansus 'alayh) in gift, and complete possession of an undivided share is impossible without mixing it with something not gifted. Validating it would force the donor to bear the cost of division, which he did not undertake. For non-divisible items, only deficient possession is possible, so it suffices. The author also distinguishes gift from loan, bequest, and sale, where possession is either not required or is a guarantee contract. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the Hanafi view.

Key Terms
هبة مشاع (hibah al-musha'): A gift of an undivided share in a jointly owned property.
محوز (mahuz): Separated and free from the donor's ownership and rights; completely possessed.
قبض منصوص عليه (qabd mansus 'alayh): Possession that is textually required as a condition for the validity of the gift.

Validity of Gifting an Undivided Share: The Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute on Hibah al-Musha'
Hanafi rebuttal to Shafi'i on hibah al-musha'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school invalidates a gift of an undivided share in divisible property, state the Shafi'i counter-argument, and evaluate the Hanafi response based on the requirement of possession (qabd).

Core Idea
A gift requires complete possession; an undivided share cannot be possessed without including what is not gifted.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents the inter-madhab dispute on hibah al-musha'. The Shafi'i school considers such gifts valid by analogy to sale, loan, and bequest. The Hanafi school invalidates them because the explicit condition of possession (qabd) in the hadith cannot be fulfilled for a musha' share without imposing the burden of division on the donor. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi proof and its responses to Shafi'i objections.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the gift of an undivided share (musha') in divisible property is invalid (batil) in the Hanafi school.
This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position and its proof
State the opposing view: Imam al-Shafi'i considers such gifts valid because a gift is a contract of ownership like sale, and musha' can accept ownership.
The Shafi'i also analogizes to loan and bequest where jointness does not invalidate.
3
Analyze the Hanafi dalil
Examine the Hanafi proof: the hadith 'لا تجوز الهبة حتى تقبض' requires possession; complete possession of musha' is impossible without adding a non-gifted item.
The dalil is a hadith ahad, zanni in transmission and meaning, but binding in practice.
4
Examine the Hanafi response to Shafi'i analogies
Understand why analogies to sale, bequest, and loan are rejected: sale and bequest do not require possession; loan has dual nature allowing deficient possession.
The key distinction: imposition of division without consent contradicts the gratuitous nature of hibah.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: in Hanafi fiqh, such a gift is invalid until division; in Shafi'i, valid immediately.
This affects estate planning, family transfers, and charitable gifts.

Commentary
This is an inter-madhhab dispute on the validity of gifting an undivided share (musha') of divisible property before physical division. The passage begins by stating the Shafi'i position that such a gift is valid, as reported in Fatawa Hanabila, because a gift is a contract of ownership like sale, and musha' can accept ownership. The Shafi'i also analogizes to loan and bequest where jointness does not prevent validity. The Hanafi response, from al-Hidayah, is then given with full proof. The Hanafi position is that the gift of musha' is invalid because possession (qabd) is a condition explicitly stated in the hadith 'A gift is not valid until it is taken possession of.' Complete possession of a musha' share is impossible without including something else that is not part of the gift. Moreover, allowing it would force the donor to bear the cost of division, which he did not undertake. The text then addresses potential objections: (1) if the donor voluntarily gives musha', he implicitly accepts the expense of division? No, because he may be content with co-ownership. (2) For indivisible items, even if division is not needed, rotation (muhayah) imposes a burden? The response: rotation relates to benefit (manfa'ah), not the gift itself. (3) Why distinguish from loan and bequest? Bequest does not require possession; loan is both a gratuity and a compensatory contract, so deficient possession suffices without division. Sales are compensatory, so incurring the expense of division is appropriate. The Hanafi proof relies on the explicit text (nass) requiring possession in gifts.

Revocation of a Gift (Hiba): The Hanafi Position and the Shafi'i Dispute
Ruling on donor's right to revoke a gift and inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on revocation of a gift, identify the Shafi'i proof, and explain the Hanafi response including the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A gift is revocable in Hanafi fiqh unless compensation or an impediment exists, contrary to the Shafi'i view of irrevocability.

This lesson examines one of the classical inter-madhhab disputes: whether a donor can revoke a gift after delivery. The Hanafi school, relying on specific hadith and customary practice, permits revocation to a non-relative (ajnabi) as long as no compensation has been received. In contrast, Imam al-Shafi'i holds that gifts are generally binding and irrevocable, with the sole exception of a father's gift to his child. We will analyze the evidence from both sides and see how the Hanafi school defends its position through textual interpretation and rational argument.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on revocation of a gift
Identify the ruling: the donor may revoke a gift given to a non-relative (ajnabi) after delivery, provided no compensation (iwad) has been received and no impediment (like marriage or mahram kinship) exists. Note that revocation is legally permissible but disliked (makruh tanzihi) based on the hadith comparing a revoker to a dog returning to its vomit.
2
Identify the opposing school's view and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i prohibits revocation except for a father from his child. His proof includes the hadith 'La yarji'u fi hibah illa al-walid min waladihi' and the rational argument that revocation contradicts the nature of a gift as a transfer of ownership.
The Shafi'i also cites the hadith comparing a revoker to a dog eating vomit as evidence of prohibition.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i hadith
The Hanafi school reinterprets the Shafi'i hadith: the negation is against absolute independence in revocation, but it does not deny revocation altogether; rather it allows it for the father by exception, and the Hanafi school extends this to all donors. The hadith about the dog indicates dislike (karahah), not prohibition.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi's own dalil and rational argument
The Hanafi school presents the hadith 'Al-rajulu ahaqqu bihibatihi ma lam yuthab minha' which explicitly states the donor's right to his gift until compensated. They also argue that by custom, the purpose of a gift is to seek reward or compensation; if that purpose is unfulfilled, the contract may be rescinded.
The Hanafi school also addresses the weakness claim against their hadith, defending its authenticity.
5
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
In a Hanafi context, a donor can take back a gift from a non-relative before compensation, while in a Shafi'i context, he cannot (except from his child). This affects property rights, family transactions, and contractual expectations.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the right of a donor to revoke a gift (hiba) in Islamic law, primarily between the Hanafi school and the Shafi'i school. The author, commenting on the Hidayah, begins by stating the Hanafi ruling: when a person gives a gift to a stranger (ajnabi), he has the right to revoke it and take back the gifted item. This is a general permission, though it is makruh (disliked) to do so without a valid reason. The author then introduces the Shafi'i position, which generally denies the right of revocation except in the case of a father's gift to his child. The Shafi'i evidence consists of two hadith: one narrated by Amr ibn Shu'ayb that 'No revoking of a gift except the father from his child,' and another from Ibn Umar and Ibn Abbas that prohibits a man from revoking a gift, with the exception of a father, and compares a revoker to a dog eating its vomit. The Hanafi response is twofold. First, they present their own proof: the hadith of Abu Hurairah and Ibn Abbas that 'the donor has more right to his gift as long as he has not been rewarded,' which explicitly allows revocation as long as no compensation (iwad) has been received. Second, they reinterpret the Shafi'i hadith: the prohibition is against absolute independence in revocation, but it allows revocation for the father, and the Hanafi school applies this exception generally to all donors. They also argue that the purpose of a gift contract is often to seek reward or compensation by custom, so if that purpose is not fulfilled, the contract accepts rescission (faskh). The commentary then addresses a technical point: the term 'ajnabi' is defined as one who is neither a spouse nor a blood relative who is a mahram. This excludes spouses and certain relatives. Two conditions for valid revocation are noted: (1) the gift must have been delivered to the donee, and (2) no impediment to revocation (such as kinship, marriage, or compensation) must be present at the time of the contract. The author corrects a misunderstanding from al-Inayah that the masculine wording excludes spouses; in fact, spouses are excluded by the second condition (marriage is an impediment). The passage concludes with a detailed discussion of the Shafi'i proof that revocation contradicts the nature of a gift as a transfer of ownership. The Hanafi response is that a gift is not an irrevocable transfer; it is a contract that can be rescinded, as evidenced by the Prophet's hadith. The father's case is special because according to the Shafi'i school, the father has a right of ownership in his child's wealth, so the gift is not a complete transfer. The Hanafi school does not accept this, but they still allow the father to revoke due to specific hadith. This nuanced discussion shows the depth of Hanafi legal reasoning, combining textual evidence, rational argument, and customary practice.

Key Terms
هبة (hibah): A contract of gift that transfers ownership of a specific property without compensation, but according to Hanafi fiqh, it does not create irrevocable ownership.
رجوع (ruju'): The act of the donor taking back the gifted item from the donee after delivery.
اجنبی (ajnabi): A person who is neither a spouse nor a mahram relative; effectively a stranger in terms of familial relation.
عوض (iwad): Compensation or something given in return for the gift, which, if given, prevents revocation.

Inseparable Increase Prevents Revocation of Gift
Ruling on revocation when donee adds an inseparable increase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an inseparable increase to gifted property prevents revocation and identify the condition under which revocation becomes permissible again.

Core Idea
An inseparable increase blocks revocation; removal of the increase restores the right to revoke.

This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies a key rule in gift revocation. When a donee adds a permanent structure or planting to gifted land, the donor loses the right to take it back. However, the ruling is nuanced by custom and the possibility of restoration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is revocation of a gift after an inseparable increase. The ruling is that the donor cannot revoke the gift as long as the increase remains.
Ruling is prohibition (haram) under these conditions.
2
Define ziyadah muttasilah
An increase that is physically attached and inseparable from the gifted property, such as a building or trees. The increase must be significant according to custom ('urf).
Custom determines what counts as a significant increase.
3
Recognize the effect of removal
If the donee removes the increase (e.g., demolishes the building), the land returns to its original state, and the donor regains the right to revoke.
The impediment is tied to the existence of the increase.
4
Apply the custom criterion
A trivial increase (e.g., a very small shop on vast land) may not be considered an increase that prevents revocation.
Custom ('urf) plays a role in determining significance.

Partial Claim on Gift Compensation: The Hanafi Dispute on Revocation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on donor's options when compensation is partially claimed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the majority Hanafi position on donor's options when half the compensation is claimed and explain why Imam Zufar differs using qiyas.

Core Idea
Partial claim on compensation does not give the donor partial revocation; only full revocation by returning the remaining compensation.

When a gift is given in exchange for compensation ('iwad), a later claim by a third party on part of either the gift or the compensation creates distinct legal consequences. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on the specific case where half of the compensation is claimed. We will see the majority position (Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn) and the dissenting view of Imam Zufar, who analogizes to barter sale. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish contract types and apply the school's principles of revocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
First, understand that we have a valid gift with compensation (hibah bi al-'iwad). After the donee has given the compensation, it is discovered that half of that compensation actually belongs to a third party (mustahaqq). The question: what options does the donor have?
There are two subcases: claim on the gifted property itself, and claim on the compensation. This lesson focuses on the latter.
2
State the Majority Hanafi Position
Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn hold that the donor cannot take back half the gift. Instead, he must either return the remaining half compensation to the donee and reclaim the entire gift, or be content with the remaining half compensation.
The donor's right to revoke the gift was extinguished by the compensation; partial claim does not revive it partially.
3
State Imam Zufar's Opposing View
Imam Zufar argues that just as the donee can recover half the compensation when half the gift is claimed, so too the donor can recover half the gift when half the compensation is claimed. He analogizes to a barter sale (bay' al-'ard bi al-'ard).
In barter, partial claim on one item gives the other party the option to reclaim the corresponding portion.
4
Analyze the Proof (Qiyas vs. Distinction)
The majority distinguishes gift from sale: in a gift, the compensation is given to remove the right of revocation (isqat al-haqq), not as a price. Therefore, partial invalidity of the compensation does not partially revive the revocation right. Zufar's qiyas treats the compensation as analogous to a price in sale.
The school prefers the majority view, as the nature of 'iwad in hibah differs.
5
Understand the Practical Difference
Under the majority, the donor risks losing half the compensation or having to return it to regain the gift; under Zufar, he can simply take back half the gift. The mufta bihi is the majority position.
This difference affects the donor's strategy when a partial claim arises.

Revocation of a Gift as Rescission vs. Return in Sale
Analyzes revocation by court or mutual consent as contract rescission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that revocation of a hibah by court or mutual consent is a rescission (faskh) and distinguish it from a return of a defective item in a sale after possession.

Core Idea
Revocation of a gift is a rescission; mutual return in a sale after possession is a new contract.

The passage clarifies a key distinction in Hanafi contract law: when a donor takes back a gift, it is not a new transfer but a rescission of the original contract. This difference affects whether possession is required and how the transaction is classified. The author contrasts this with the return of a defective item in a sale, where only a court-ordered return counts as rescission, while mutual agreement creates a new sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling on hibah revocation
The donor's revocation by court decree or mutual consent is a rescission (faskh) from the root—the original hibah contract is annulled. No new possession is needed for the donor to re-own the item.
This applies even if the gifted property has become jointly owned (shuyūʿ) or partially transferred by the donee.
2
Understand the implications
Because it's a rescission, the donor's ownership revives automatically. The contract is treated as if it never existed, not as a new transfer.
3
Contrast with return in a sale
In a sale, returning a defective item before possession is rescission. After possession, if by court order it is also rescission, but if by mutual consent it is considered a new sale.
The buyer only has the right to receive a sound item, not an unlimited right to rescind.
4
Derive the principle behind the difference
In hibah, the donor has an inherent right to rescind (unless restrictions apply). In sale, the buyer's right is limited to receiving a sound object; after possession, the buyer cannot unilaterally rescind.
This explains why mutual consent return in sale is a new contract—it goes beyond the right of the buyer.
5
Apply the ruling to a complex scenario
If the donee has sold half of the gifted land to a third party, the donor can still revoke the remaining half by court or mutual consent. The rescission operates absolutely on what remains.
The third party's ownership is unaffected because the rescission only cancels the original grant between donor and donee.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute regarding hibah bi shart al-iwad (gift with a condition of compensation). The Hanafi position, as stated in the matn, is that such a contract is initially a hibah but ultimately becomes a sale. Therefore, it requires mutual possession (taqabud) in the same session for both the gift and the compensation, and it is void if the object is undivided (shuyu'). The Hanafi response is that the contract encompasses both aspects (hibah and sale), and we combine where possible: the hibah delays ownership until possession, while sale is binding; hibah becomes binding with compensation. This combination is possible here, unlike the case of a slave selling himself, which is impossible because a slave cannot own himself.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed inter-madhhab dispute concerning hiba bi shart al-iwad (gift with a condition of compensation). The Ruling of 'Umra (Lifetime Gift) and Its Forms
Inter-madhhab dispute with expansion on three forms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'umra, identify its three forms, and explain the inter-madhhab dispute regarding ownership transfer.

Core Idea
An 'umra gift transfers full ownership; the condition of return is void.

The 'umra contract—a gift for the duration of the recipient's life—raises a critical question: does the property become the absolute property of the recipient or only a temporary usufruct? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position, its three distinct forms, and the evidence that settles the debate with other schools. You will learn how a corrupt condition does not invalidate a gift and why the recipient's heirs inherit the property.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position
Identify the core ruling: 'umra is valid and transfers full ownership; any condition that the property should return after death is void.
Note that the gift itself is sahih, and the corrupt condition is batil.
2
Identify the Three Forms of 'Umra
Distinguish the three forms: (1) Condition that after death it goes to recipient's heirs – valid; (2) Absolute statement without condition – valid; (3) Condition that it returns to donor – valid but condition void.
The third form is the most disputed; Hanafis uphold it as valid.
3
Identify Opposing Schools and Their Proof
Recognize that Imam Malik and the old Shafi'i view hold that 'umra transfers only usufruct. Their proof is the hadith: 'The 'umra that the Prophet permitted is when one says: It is for you and your descendants. If one says: It is for you as long as you live, then it returns to its owner.'
This hadith is also in Sahih Muslim.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
Understand the Hanafi response: the opposing hadith is interpreted as a special case where the condition of return is valid only if the recipient has no heirs; the general ruling is that 'umra belongs to the recipient and his descendants.
Hanafis rely on multiple authentic hadith, e.g., 'Whoever gives an 'umra, it belongs to the one given it, living or dead, and to his descendants.'
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Note the practical consequence: under Hanafi law, the recipient's heirs inherit the property; under Maliki/old Shafi'i, it returns to the donor upon the recipient's death.
This difference affects estate planning and inheritance.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling of 'umra (lifetime gift) and its forms. It begins by stating the Hanafi position: 'umra is valid; the recipient owns the property during his life and his heirs inherit it after him. The condition that the property returns to the giver upon the recipient's death is void. The reason is that the gift is valid, and a corrupt condition does not invalidate the gift. The commentator then explains three forms of 'umra: 1) Explicit condition that after death it goes to the recipient's heirs – this is valid and becomes absolute ownership (like a gift). 2) Absolute statement without any condition – there is a dispute: Imam al-Shafi'i has two opinions; the new view considers it valid and ownership passes to the recipient's heirs, supported by a hadith from Jabir. The old view of some Shafi'is considers it invalid or like a loan (ariyah). 3) Statement that after death it returns to the giver or his heirs – some declare it invalid, but the correct view (based on multiple hadith) is that it is valid and the condition is void. The passage also cites the opinions of Imam Ahmad, Malik, and others. It concludes with the Hanafi proof: the clear hadith in Sahih Muslim that 'umra given to someone and his heirs becomes their property irrevocably.

The passage presents the inter-madhhab dispute: Hanafis (along with Ibn Abbas, Jabir, and others in the new Shafi'i view) hold that 'umra transfers full ownership, while Malik and the old Shafi'i view hold that it only transfers usufruct (manfa'ah) and the property returns to the original owner after death. The Hanafi evidence is the explicit hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) ruled that 'umra becomes the property of the recipient and his heirs, and it does not return to the giver.
