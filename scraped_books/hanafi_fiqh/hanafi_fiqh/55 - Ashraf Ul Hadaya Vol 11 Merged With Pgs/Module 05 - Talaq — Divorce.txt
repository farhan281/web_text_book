Iqrar al-Marid: The Sick Person's Confession
Chapter opening and rulings on iqrar during death illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the priority of debts during marad al-mawt and identify when a confession is valid or invalid.

Core Idea
Debts with known causes take priority; confession for an heir is invalid as it mimics a bequest to an heir.

This passage from the chapter on the confession of a sick person lays down key principles governing financial transactions during death-illness. Understanding these rules is crucial for estate distribution and protecting the rights of heirs and creditors.

Guided Path
1
Identify debt categories
Note the types: debts of health, debts of death-illness, and debts with known causes (like documented loans).
The text stresses 'duyūn maʿlūmat al-asbāb' as having priority.
2
State the priority order
Health debts and known-cause debts take precedence over mere confessions during marad al-mawt. Only after these are paid are death-illness debts considered.
Reason: death-illness confessions are suspect as disguised bequests.
3
Recognize invalid confessions
A confession in favor of an heir is invalid, as it is a bequest to an heir, which is impermissible without other heirs' consent.
Exception: if the confession is for a stranger and later lineage is claimed, lineage takes precedence and the confession becomes void.
4
Understand exceptions involving marriage
If a sick person confesses a debt to a woman and later marries her, the confession remains valid because the debt was separate from the marital relationship.
This distinguishes from the lineage case where the confession is absorbed by the new relationship.
5
Apply lineage rules for children
If a boy with no known lineage confirms being the confessor's son, and it is biologically possible, lineage is established even if the confessor is ill. The original confession (if any) is void.
This protects the integrity of lineage over debt claims.

Commentary
This is the beginning of the chapter on the confession of a person in death illness (marad al-mawt) from the book on iqrar. The text summarizes key rules: (1) Debts incurred during health and those with known causes are paid before those incurred merely during death illness, because the latter are suspected of being fraudulent to deprive heirs. (2) After all priority debts are paid, the remaining estate goes to the death-illness debts. (3) A confession in favor of an heir is invalid because it is considered a disguised bequest to an heir, which is impermissible without the consent of other heirs. (4) If a patient confesses to a stranger and then claims he is his son, the lineage is established and the original confession (of debt) becomes void, because one cannot owe a debt to one's own son in a way that affects inheritance. (5) An exception: if the patient confesses to a woman lāhibiyyah (perhaps a female of a certain tribe) and later marries her, the confession remains valid because the debt was separate from the marriage. (6) For a boy who could be his son and has no known lineage, if the boy confirms, the lineage is established even if the confessor is ill. (7) Finally, summarized: ruling for parents, children, wife, and master (mawlā) is mentioned but details are not given here.

Key Terms
مرض الموت (marad al-mawt): Death illness; a illness from which one is likely to die, affecting legal capacity.
دیون معلومة الاسباب (duyūn maʿlūmat al-asbāb): Debts with known causes, e.g., loans with witnesses or documents.

Conditions for a Valid Confession: Freedom, Sanity, Puberty
Ruling on conditions for valid confession with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for a valid confession in Hanafi law and identify the exceptions for slaves and minors.

Core Idea
A valid confession requires freedom, sanity, and puberty; a slave's confession is valid for hudud/qisas but not financial matters.

Confession (iqrar) is a powerful legal instrument, but not everyone's confession is binding. The Hanafi school sets conditions on the confessor himself: he must be free, sane, and adult. However, there are important nuances for slaves and minors depending on their capacity. This lesson unpacks these conditions and their rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is iqrar (confession). The ruling is that it is binding (wajib) on the confessor as evidence of the fact confessed.
Confession is considered a proof (hujja) but limited to the confessor.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites the hadith of Ma'iz and the Ghamidiyya woman, where the Prophet (ﷺ) enforced stoning based on their confessions alone.
This hadith is in Sahihayn, showing the binding nature of confession.
3
Distinguish the conditions
The confessor must be free, sane, and adult. Freedom is required for absolute validity over all matters. Sanity and puberty are necessary because the insane and child lack the capacity (ahliyya) to undertake obligations.
These conditions ensure the confessor has legal capacity.
4
Note the exceptions
A ma'dhun slave (permitted to trade) is treated as free for confession. A mahjur slave's confession in financial matters is invalid, but valid for hudud and qisas. A minor who is permitted to trade is annexed to adults.
These exceptions are based on the principle of capacity and authority over property.

Commentary
This lesson covers the essential conditions for a valid confession (iqrar) in Hanafi law. The matn states that an insane person's confession is not binding because he lacks legal capacity (ahliyyat al-iltizam). However, a minor who is granted permission (idhn) to trade is treated as an adult due to this permission, so his confession is valid. The commentary explains that confession is binding because it serves as evidence of the occurrence of the confessed matter, citing the famous hadith of Ma'iz and the Ghamidiyya woman in Sahihayn. However, confession is a 'limited proof' (hujja qasira) that only binds the confessor, unlike testimony (bayyina) which is transitive. Three conditions are discussed: freedom, sanity, and puberty. For freedom, a free person's confession is valid in all matters. For slaves, there is a distinction between a ma'dhun (permitted to trade) and a mahjur (restricted). The ma'dhun's confession is valid like a free person's, but the mahjur's financial confession is invalid because his neck is the master's property, so his admission would impose a debt on the master's property. However, his confession regarding hadd (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation) is valid because in these matters he is considered to retain his original freedom. For sanity and puberty, a child or insane person cannot be bound by his confession except if the child is permitted to trade. The commentary concludes by emphasizing that capacity to undertake is a prerequisite.

Key Terms
اقرار (iqrar): A statement by a person acknowledging a claim against himself
حجت قاصرہ (hujja qasira): A proof that is limited to the confessor and does not extend to others
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave who is permitted by his master to engage in trade
