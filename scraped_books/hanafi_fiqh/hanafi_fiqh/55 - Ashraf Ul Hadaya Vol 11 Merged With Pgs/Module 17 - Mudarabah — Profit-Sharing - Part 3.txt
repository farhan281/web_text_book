Validity of Mudarabah When Mudarib Gives Capital as Bida'at
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mudarabah validity after return of capital as bida'at

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the validity of a mudarabah when the mudarib returns capital to the owner as a bida'at, and explain the role of takhliyah in preserving the contract.

Core Idea
Takhliyah transforms the owner into a stranger, allowing agency without voiding the mudarabah.

In this passage, we examine a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: what happens when the mudarib hands the capital back to the owner as a bida'at (voluntary trading assistance)? The majority of Hanafi imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) hold the contract remains intact, while Imam Zufar argues it dissolves. The key is the concept of takhliyah—the owner having fully vacated control—which enables the owner to act as the mudarib's agent without reclaiming the capital.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting positions
State the majority view (three imams: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) that the mudarabah remains valid, and Imam Zufar's view that it becomes void.
The source explicitly contrasts the two views.
2
Understand the basis of each position
Majority: after takhliyah, the owner becomes a stranger to the capital and can act as the mudarib's agent. Zufar: the owner transacts in his own property, so he cannot be an agent, effectively reclaiming the capital.
The analogy of initial condition (if owner must work at start) is used to support Zufar.
3
Analyze the objections and responses
Three objections are raised: (1) agency in own property not possible, (2) bida'at definition requires capital to belong to the giver, (3) helper analogy (tailor) suggests no profit share. For each, the majority provides a counter-argument.
Follow the logical flow of Q&A in the text.
4
Apply the tailor analogy and its rebuttal
The opponent analogizes to a hired tailor who seeks help from the customer; the customer's work does not entitle the tailor to wage. The majority responds that mudarabah is partnership (shirkah) where one can profit from another's labor.
This highlights the distinction between ijarah and shirkah.
5
Distinguish from the alternative case
The passage ends with a contrasting scenario: if the mudarib gives capital as a separate mudarabah (not bida'at), and that second mudarabah is invalid, the first mudarabah remains unaffected.
This shows the ruling is specific to bida'at, not all forms of delegation.

His saying: 'If he gives...' - If the mudarib gives all or part of the mudarabah capital to the owner as a bida'at (gift for trade), and the owner buys and sells, then according to our three imams this does not make the mudarabah contract void. This is the same reason that if at the beginning of mudarabah it is made a condition that the owner must work, then the mudarabah is not valid. Our proof: The owner had completely vacated (takhliyah) between the mudarib and the capital, and the mudarib had acquired the right to transact in it. Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the ruling when a mudarib returns the mudarabah capital (or part of it) to the owner (rabb al-maal) as a bida'at (a type of consignment where the worker does not share profit but is a volunteer), and the owner himself then trades with it. The three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) hold that the original mudarabah remains valid and does not become void. Imam Zufar argues it becomes void because the owner, by transacting in his own property, cannot be an agent; it is as if he took back his capital. The Hanafi response uses the concept of takhliyah (vacating): once the owner gives full control to the mudarib, the owner becomes a stranger to the capital and can act as an agent. The passage then addresses objections: (1) Agency in one's own property is not possible, but after takhliyah the owner is like a stranger. (2) The definition of bida'at requires the capital to belong to the giver, but here it is the owner's capital; the response redefines bida'at as taking help, not as a specific contract. (3) A stranger can help because he acts in another's property; the owner acts in his own; the analogy of tailor and customer is used to argue that the helper does not become entitled to wages, but the response says mudarabah is a partnership (not just hire) where profit from another's labor is valid. Finally, the passage distinguishes the case where the mudarib gives capital to the owner as a separate mudarabah (not bida'at): if that secondary mudarabah is invalid because the mudarib has no capital, the owner's trading is not by mudarabah but by the mudarib's command, so the first mudarabah remains intact.

Key Terms
تخلیہ (takhliyah): The owner vacating control over the capital, giving the mudarib full authority to transact.
بضاعت (bida'at): Here, giving capital to another for trade without sharing profit, where the worker is a volunteer (mutabarri').
قلب موضوع (qalb al-mawdu'): Inversion of the subject: making the mudarib both capital provider and worker, which is impossible.

Expenses of Mudarib in City vs. Travel
Ruling on mudarib's expenses with proof by analogy to ihtibas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mudarib expenses and explain the legal basis (ihtibas) that differentiates city from travel.

Core Idea
A mudarib's travel expenses come from the capital because he is detained (ihtibas); in the city, he is not, so expenses are his own.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a key distinction in mudarabah: the mudarib's personal expenses are treated differently depending on whether he works in his home city or travels for business. The ruling is justified by a powerful analogy to the maintenance of a judge and a wife, both of whom receive support because they are detained from their normal livelihoods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Pinpoint the two scenarios: mudarib working in the city (no expenses from capital) and mudarib traveling (all necessary expenses from capital).
The ruling is from the matn: 'When the mudarib works in the city, his maintenance is not from the capital. But if he travels, then his food, drink, clothing, and riding are from the capital.'
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy). The commentator states: 'Maintenance is due in exchange for detention, like the maintenance of a judge and the maintenance of a wife.'
This is a clear qiyas from the Hanafi school, not a direct textual proof from Quran or Sunnah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The effective cause (illah) is ihtibas (detention). In the city, the mudarib lives his normal life; in travel, he is detained from his ordinary residence. Therefore he deserves maintenance from the source that detains him (the mudarabah capital).
The judge is detained by public service (bait al-mal), the wife by marital confinement (husband). Mudarib traveling is detained by mudarabah.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is no intra-Hanafi dispute on this ruling. It is standard Zahir al-Riwayah.
This is a settled rule in the school.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The ruling is mufta bihi and authoritative. There is no alternative view mentioned.
No tarjih needed as there is no ikhtilaf.

The issue of mudarib's expenses. He said: When the mudarib works in the city, his maintenance is not from the capital. But if he travels, then his food, drink, clothing, and riding – meaning purchase and hire – are from the capital. The reason for the difference: Maintenance is due in exchange for detention, like the maintenance of a judge and the maintenance of a wife. The mudarib in the city resides with his original residence, but when he travels, he becomes detained by the mudarabah, so he deserves maintenance from it. This is contrary to a hired worker (ajir), because he will inevitably receive wages, so he does not suffer by spending from his own wealth. Mudarib's Travel Expenses: Is Medicine Included?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mudarib's travel expenses and medicine.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between certain and contingent expenses in mudarabah and state the mufta bihi position on medicine.

Core Idea
The need for medicine is uncertain; therefore it is not a recurring expense in mudarabah.

When a mudarib travels for business, the partnership covers his recurring living costs. But if illness strikes, who pays for medicine? This lesson unpacks the intra-Hanafi dispute: the majority (Zahir al-Riwayah) holds medicine as the mudarib's personal expense, while a rare report from Abu Hanifa includes it. The key lies in certainty versus contingency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the travel scenario
Determine whether the mudarib is considered a traveler (unable to return home at night). If he can return, no special expenses apply.
The ruling only applies when he cannot spend the night at home.
2
List the included nafaqah items
Recurring necessities: food, clothing, laundry, servant wages, fodder, oil – all limited to customary amounts (bil-ma'ruf).
Exceeding the customary amount makes the mudarib liable for the excess.
3
Locate the dispute
The specific point of ikhtilaf: is medicine included in nafaqah?
4
Analyze the majority reasoning
The need for nafaqah is certain (yaqini), while medicine is contingent on illness (arid). Certainty prevails, so medicine is from the mudarib's own wealth.
Analogy: a wife's expenses are on her husband, but her medicine is from her own property.
5
Contrast with the opposing view
Abu Hanifa's narration analogizes medicine to nafaqah because it is essential for body maintenance and trade. The majority counters with the certainty argument.
The narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad is considered nawadir, not relied upon.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the mudarib's expenses during travel, specifically whether medicine is included in the expenses chargeable to the mudarabah capital. The text first establishes the general ruling: the mudarib's necessary living expenses (nafaqah) while traveling for the mudarabah are taken from the mudarabah capital. This includes items like washing clothes, servant wages, animal fodder, and oil in regions where it is customary. All such expenses are limited to the customary standard (al-ma'ruf); if the mudarib exceeds this, he guarantees the excess. The key dispute is over medicine: in the Zahir al-Riwayah (the six core books of Imam Muhammad), medicine is not included in expenses and must be borne by the mudarib personally. However, a narration from Abu Hanifa (transmitted by Hasan ibn Ziyad) includes medicine in expenses, analogizing it to food and clothing because it is essential for maintaining the body and enabling trade. The Zahir al-Riwayah position distinguishes medicine from regular expenses on the basis of certainty: the need for food, drink, and clothing is certain and recurring, whereas the need for medicine is contingent on illness, which is not a certainty. This is supported by the analogy: the wife's expenses (food, clothing, shelter) are obligatory on the husband, but her medicine is from her own property. The text also clarifies that 'travel' here does not mean the legal travel distance (shar'i safar) but rather any distance that prevents the mudarib from returning home at night; if he can return, he is treated as if in the city market and expenses are not from the capital. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the one from Zahir al-Riwayah, as the text explicitly gives its reasoning and presents it as the default. Authority weight: Zahir al-Riwayah for the majority position; the narration from Abu Hanifa is from the Nawadir (rare transmissions) via Hasan ibn Ziyad. The text then provides an Urdu commentary summarizing and explaining these points.

Murabahah Cost and Profit Restoration in Mudarabah
Initial rulings on profit and murabahah cost inclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how profit restores capital in mudarabah and identify which expenses are included in the cost price for murabahah, applying the Hanafi principle of custom and increase in value.

Core Idea
Only goods-related expenses that increase value are included in murabahah cost; personal expenses are not.

This lesson focuses on two initial rulings from the passage: first, that when the mudarib earns profit, the rabb al-mal recovers any capital spent by the mudarib before profit is shared; second, that when selling goods on murabahah, only expenses that directly increase the value of the goods (like transportation) are added to the disclosed cost, not the mudarib's personal expenses. Understanding this distinction is crucial for the correct application of trust-based sales.

Examine the second ruling: cost inclusion in murabahah
When selling on murabahah (cost-plus), the mudarib includes only expenses incurred on the goods themselves, like transportation and tailoring, not his personal costs.
The seller must disclose all costs that truly increase the market price.

First, custom (urf) among merchants is to include direct goods expenses. Second, these expenses increase the value (qimah) of the goods, whereas personal expenses do not.
Both proofs together justify the distinction.

Commentary
This passage states several rulings regarding the mudarib's profit and expenses. Mudarib's Own Expenses: Loan vs. Partnership in Increase
Distinction between mudarib's own services and own commodities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mudarib's spending on services (calendering/transport) and spending on physical commodities (dye), determining the legal effect: loan vs. partnership in the increase.

Core Idea
Services without physical residue are a personal loan; dye that mixes creates a partnership in the increase.

The second part of the passage addresses a subtle distinction: when a mudarib uses his own money to pay for services like calendering (pressing) or transporting the goods, and he has been given general permission to act on his discretion, that expense is considered a voluntary loan (istidanah) that is not covered by the permission. However, if he uses his own dye to dye the cloth, he becomes a partner in the increase in value, and the dye is treated as a mixture. The underlying principle is whether the expenditure creates a physical asset that subsists (qaim) in the goods or is merely a service that vanishes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario: mudarib uses own money for services
The mudarib has 1000 dirhams capital, buys cloth, then spends his own 100 to have it calendered or transported. He was told 'act on your discretion' (i'mal bi-ra'yika).
His own money, not capital.
2
Determine the ruling for services
The mudarib is considered a volunteer (mutatawwi): the payment is a loan to the rabb al-mal that is not covered by the general permission. He cannot automatically claim reimbursement from capital or profit unless explicitly authorized.
The general permission does not include such loans.
3
Contrast with dye: mudarib becomes partner
If he dyes the cloth with his own dye, he becomes a partner in the increase caused by the dye. The dye is a specific commodity (ayn) that subsists in the cloth, unlike services.
The dye physically remains; services do not.
4
Analyze the proof using the usurper analogy
The text argues that if a usurper (ghasib) performs services on a usurped cloth, his labor is wasted (no claim). But if he dyes it, the increase belongs to him. This shows the distinction is intrinsic to the type of expenditure.
The same logic applies to the mudarib.
5
Apply the general permission to dye
When the mudarib becomes a partner through dye, the instruction 'act on your discretion' is deemed to include such mixing (khalat), so he does not guarantee (damn) the cloth.
The permission covers mixing that adds value.

Mudarib Dyeing Cloth: Partnership in Added Value
Ruling on mudarib dyeing cloth with own money

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a mudarib mixes his own physical property with mudarabah capital and distinguish between physical additions (dye) and services (cleaning).

Core Idea
Dye creates partnership in value; cleaning does not.

In a mudarabah, the mudarib is an agent trusted to invest the capital. Sometimes he may use his own funds to improve the capital, such as dyeing cloth. The Hanafi school rules that if the mudarib adds a physical substance that becomes part of the capital, he becomes a partner in that added value. Services like cleaning, which leave no permanent trace, do not give any ownership. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Context
Recognize that the mudarib uses his own money to dye cloth that belongs to the mudarabah. This is a mixing of personal property with partnership capital.
The dye is a tangible substance (ayn) that physically remains in the cloth.
2
State the Ruling
The mudarib becomes a partner in the increase in value caused by the dye. He is not liable for the cloth itself; the original value remains mudarabah property.
When sold, proceeds are split: value of white cloth to mudarabah, added value to mudarib.
3
Identify the Underlying Principle
The key distinction is between a property that inheres (ayn qa'imah) and a service that does not. Dye remains; cleaning (qasara) and carrying (haml) do not.
This explains why the same rule applies to a usurper (ghasib): his work is wasted for services but not for dye.
4
Connect to the Mudarib's Permission
The general permission 'work by your opinion' includes mixing his own property, as long as it does not harm the mudarabah.
Thus, the mudarib is not considered a guarantor.
5
Apply the Distinction to New Scenarios
Test other acts: tailoring, adding hardware, etc. Only those that leave a physical addition create partnership.
Modern example: renovating a mudarabah property with own paint creates partnership in added value.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the matn of al-Hidayah on mudarabah. It addresses the scenario where a mudarib (agent) uses his own funds to dye cloth that belongs to the mudarabah capital. The ruling is that the mudarib becomes a partner with the owner in the added value caused by the dye. He is not considered a guarantor for the cloth; rather, the ownership of the cloth remains partly for the mudarabah (the white cloth portion) and partly for the mudarib (the dye portion). When the cloth is sold, the proceeds are divided accordingly: the value of the white cloth goes to the mudarabah, and the increase due to dye goes to the mudarib. This is contrasted with services like cleaning (قصارة) and carrying (حمل), which do not create a specific property that inheres in the cloth. Therefore, if a usurper (ghasib) cleans or carries the cloth, his work is wasted (he gets no compensation), whereas if he dyes it, his work is not wasted because the dye becomes a part of the cloth and the usurper becomes a partner. The ruling also connects to the general permission given by the rabb al-mal to the mudarib to act according to his discretion (اعمل برأيك): such a permission includes the mudarib's mixing of his own property with the mudarabah property, and thus he is not liable for the cloth.

Profit and Loss Allocation in Mudarabah When Purchase Money is Lost
Calculates ownership and liability split after loss of purchase money

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the allocation of ownership and liability when the mudarib's purchase money is lost in a mudarabah, applying Hanafi principles of daman and agency.

Core Idea
When mudarabah capital is lost after purchase, the mudarib bears initial liability but recovers from the rabb al-mal, resulting in a fractional split of the purchased asset.

This lesson analyzes a classic mudarabah case from al-Hidayah where the mudarib makes a profit, uses the total to buy a slave, then loses the money before payment. The Hanafi jurists carefully allocate ownership of the slave and liability for the loss, distinguishing between the mudarib's personal guarantee and the mudarabah trust. Understanding this allocation is essential for proper profit distribution and capital preservation in mudarabah contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial state and profit realization
Start with the initial capital of 1000 dirhams. The mudarib buys linen and sells it for 2000, generating 1000 profit. Half (500) belongs to the mudarib, half to the rabb al-mal. The total liquid cash becomes 2000, composed of 1500 capital and rabb's profit, and 500 mudarib's profit.
Profit is only realized when the capital becomes cash (naḍḍ al-māl).
2
Determine the source of funds for the slave purchase
The mudarib uses the entire 2000 to buy a slave. The 2000 consists of 1500 from the mudarabah (including capital and rabb's profit) and 500 from mudarib's personal profit. Therefore, the ownership of the slave is split: mudarib owns 500/2000 = 1/4, mudarabah owns 1500/2000 = 3/4.
The mudarib is purchasing partly for himself and partly as agent for the mudarabah.
3
Apply the rule of liability (daman) for the price
The mudarib is the contracting party and is liable for the entire price (2000). However, since he is an agent for 3/4 of the slave, he can recover 1500 from the rabb al-mal. Net, the mudarib bears 500, the rabb al-mal bears 1500.
The mudarib's 1/4 is a guaranteed liability (mamdun) in his hands, while the mudarabah portion remains a trust (amanah).
4
Calculate the ownership fractions after loss
The slave is still owned as before: 1/4 by mudarib (personally), 3/4 by mudarabah. The mudarib's 1/4 is excluded from the mudarabah because it is guaranteed and contradicts the trust nature of mudarabah.
This separation prevents mixing guaranteed and trust property.
5
Compute the new capital base and future profit distribution
The mudarabah capital becomes 2500 (original 1000 + rabb's reimbursement 1500). When the slave is sold for 4000, the mudarabah's share (3/4) is 3000. Subtract capital 2500, leaving 500 profit to be split equally (250 each).
The slave cannot be sold on murabahah at a cost above 2000 because the actual purchase price was 2000.

Mudarib's Liability and Ownership When Purchase Money Is Lost Before Payment
Ruling on mudarib who buys slave with profit, loses money before paying

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on mudarib's liability when purchase money is lost before payment and determine the ownership fractions in the purchased asset based on profit crystallization.

Core Idea
Crystallized profit becomes the mudarib's personal property, so loss of purchase money is apportioned accordingly between mudarib and rabb al-mal.

This lesson examines a nuanced scenario from Al-Hidayah: a mudarib realizes profit, uses it to buy a slave, but loses the cash before paying the price. The ruling clarifies how liability and ownership are divided between the mudarib and the rabb al-mal, emphasizing the principle that realized profit privatizes a portion of the transaction. Understanding this ruling is crucial for correctly applying mudarabah rules in complex situations where profit is reinvested before full settlement.

Guided Path
1
Understand the initial facts and profit crystallization
Start with the basic setup: mudarib has 1000 dirhams capital on half profit. He buys cloth, sells for 2000, realizing a profit of 1000, of which his share is 500. This profit is now his personal property, though still in cash form.
Profit crystallization means the profit is separated from the capital and becomes owned by the parties according to the profit-sharing ratio.
2
Identify the purchase of the slave and the division of the price
When the mudarib buys a slave for 2000, he is using a mix: 1500 from the rabbit al-mal's capital/profit share and 500 from his own profit share. Therefore, 1/4 of the slave is purchased for himself and 3/4 for the mudarabah.
The division is proportional to the source of funds: 500/2000 = 1/4, 1500/2000 = 3/4.
3
Recognize the loss of funds and its effect on liability
The 2000 cash is lost before the slave is paid for. The full price (2000) is initially on the mudarib as the contracting party, but he has recourse to the rabb al-mal for 1500 (the share of the price that belonged to the mudarabah). Thus, net liability: mudarib bears 500, rabb al-mal bears 1500.
This is because the mudarib is an agent for the rabb al-mal regarding the 3/4 portion, so he can recover that from the principal.
4
Apply the rules of guarantee vs trust to determine ownership exit
The mudarib's 1/4 share in the slave exits the mudarabah because it is his guaranteed property (he personally bears the loss of that portion). The mudarabah property is a trust, and a guaranteed interest cannot coexist with a trust. Therefore, that 1/4 becomes his absolute ownership.
The remaining 3/4 continues in the mudarabah trust.
5
Compute the adjusted capital and profit calculation
After the loss, the rabb al-mal pays 1500, so the total capital becomes 2500 (original 1000 + 1500). For murabahah sale, the cost basis remains 2000 (the slave's purchase price), not 2500. When sold for 4000, the mudarabah's share (3/4) is 3000, subtract capital 2500, leaving 500 profit to be split per agreement.
This prevents the mudarib from claiming a higher cost basis that would reduce the apparent profit.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a ruling on a specific mudarabah scenario and its legal reasoning. The passage type is a ruling with proof (talil). The matn (base text) states the ruling: if a mudarib with 1000 dirhams on half profit buys cloth, sells for 2000, then buys a slave with 2000, but loses the 2000 before paying the slave's price, then the rabb al-mal (capital owner) guarantees 1500 and the mudarib guarantees 500. The entire price is initially on the mudarib because he is the contracting party, but he can recourse to the rabb al-mal for 1500 because the profit (500) had already been realized when the capital became liquid. Thus, when buying the slave, 1/4 of the price was from the mudarib's profit share (500) and 3/4 from the capital and rabb al-mal's share of profit. When the 2000 is lost, the mudarib must pay the full price but can get 1500 back from the rabb al-mal. However, the mudarib's 1/4 share in the slave exits from the mudarabah because it is his personal guaranteed property, while the mudarabah property is a trust; mixing them would create a contradiction. The remaining 3/4 remains in the mudarabah. The new capital becomes 2500 (1000 original + 1500 rabb al-mal's payment). For murabahah sale, the base cost is 2000 (the purchase price), not 2500, because the slave was bought for 2000. If sold for 4000, the mudarabah's share (3/4) is 3000, from which capital 2500 is removed, leaving 500 profit to be split between rabb al-mal and mudarib according to their profit-sharing ratio. This ruling emphasizes the separate nature of the mudarib's personal property and the mudarabah trust property, and how profit crystallization affects ownership shares.

Determining the Murabahah Price in Mudarabah Transactions
Ruling with proof on murabahah price calculation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the correct murabahah price when a sale occurs between the rabb al-mal and the mudarib and justify the ruling using the Hanafi principles of trust and shubha.

Core Idea
When a sale occurs between investor and agent, the lower effective cost is used for murabahah to avoid suspicion of betrayal.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a subtle question: if the rabb al-mal sells an asset to the mudarib, and then the mudarib wants to resell it on murabahah, what cost should he disclose? The Hanafi answer prioritises trust and transparency. Let us walk through the two sub-cases and the reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and transactions
Note who is the rabb al-mal (capital owner) and who is the mudarib (agent). In the first case, rabb al-mal buys a slave for 500 and sells it to the mudarib for 1000. In the second case, mudarib buys for 1000 and sells to rabb al-mal for 1200.
These are inter-party sales within the mudarabah contract.
2
Recognise the shubha (semblance of non-existence)
The sale between rabb al-mal and mudarib is effectively selling one's own property to oneself because the mudarib's profit share is from the same capital. This creates a shubha that such a sale might be non-existent. However, it is ruled permissible due to need and difference of objectives.
Shubha does not invalidate the sale but affects the murabahah price calculation.
3
Apply the murabahah principle: use the lesser price
Murabahah is built on trust and avoidance of any suspicion of betrayal. To avoid overstating the cost (which would betray the future buyer), the lower of the two prices is taken. In the first case, 500 is lower than 1000, so the mudarib sells on murabahah based on 500.
The original 1000 purchase price is not used because it includes an element of shubha.
4
Apply the same logic to the reverse case
In the second case, the mudarib buys for 1000 and sells to rabb al-mal for 1200. Here half the profit (100) is considered non-existent because it belongs to the rabb al-mal's own share. So the effective cost for murabahah is 1000 + half of 200 = 1100. The rabb al-mal sells on murabahah based on 1100.
This ensures that the murabahah price reflects the true cost without overstating.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a specific ruling regarding murabahah sales in the context of mudarabah. The passage type is a ruling with proof (talil). Two sub-cases are presented. First, if the rabb al-mal (capital owner) buys a slave for 500 and sells it to the mudarib for 1000, and then the mudarib wants to resell it on murabahah (cost-plus-profit), he must base the cost on 500, not 1000. This is because although the sale between rabb al-mal and mudarib is valid (due to the difference of objectives and necessity), it carries a shubha (semblance) of non-existence because it is effectively selling one's own property to oneself (since the mudarib's profit share is from the same capital). The foundation of murabahah is trust (amānah) and avoidance of any suspicion of betrayal. Therefore, the lower of the two prices (500) is taken to avoid overstating the cost and thus betraying the future buyer's trust. Second, if the mudarib buys a slave for 1000 and sells it to the rabb al-mal for 1200, and the rabb al-mal wishes to resell on murabahah, he should base it on 1100. This is because half the profit (100) is considered non-existent (ma'dūm) in respect of the rabb al-mal's share, as the sale between them effectively transfers the share of profit already belonging to the rabb al-mal. So the cost basis is 1100 (1000 + half of 200). This ruling highlights the importance of transparency and avoiding deception in murabahah transactions, even when the original sale involves parties with overlapping interests.

Fidyah Division When a Slave Purchased in Mudarabah Commits Accidental Murder
Ruling on compensation when a slave bought in mudarabah commits accidental murder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the fidyah is divided between the capital owner and the mudarib when a slave bought in mudarabah commits accidental murder, and why the mudarabah ends after payment.

Core Idea
Burdens of ownership are divided proportionally to shares, not equally to profit splits.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in mudarabah: when the jointly owned slave accidentally kills a free person. The fidyah (blood money) must be paid, and the key question is how to allocate this liability between the rabb al-mal and the mudarib. The Hanafi ruling is based on the principle that the burden follows ownership shares, which requires a careful calculation of each party's ownership percentage at the time of the incident.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Understand the given facts: mudarabah with capital of 1000 on half profit, slave bought for 2000, slave kills someone accidentally. The ruling is that three quarters of the fidyah is on the rabb al-mal and one quarter on the mudarib.
This is the direct answer from the text.
2
Understand the underlying principle
The reason (talil) is that the fidyah is a burden of ownership (mu'unat al-milk), so it is apportioned according to ownership shares.
This principle is derived from the maxim that burdens follow ownership.
3
Calculate the ownership shares
The slave is worth 2000. The capital was 1000, so the profit is 1000. The profit is split half to each: 500 to rabb al-mal, 500 to mudarib. Thus rabb al-mal's share = capital 1000 + profit 500 = 1500 (3/4), mudarib's share = 500 (1/4).
The shares are in quarters, not halves.
4
Consequences: mudarabah ends after fidyah payment
Paying the fidyah effectively divides the slave between them, and the mudarabah contract ends upon division. The slave is then owned individually in the same proportions.
This differs from the earlier case where the entire price was on the mudarib with recourse, avoiding division.
5
Compare with earlier case
In the earlier scenario, the entire price was on the mudarib (with right of recourse), so no division was needed and the mudarabah continued. Here, because the burden is shared proportionally, division is required, ending the mudarabah.
The service of the slave is also divided accordingly: mudarib works one day, rabb al-mal three days.

Murabahah Price Basis When Mudarib Buys from Rabb al-Mal
Rulings with proofs for Murabahah pricing in mudarabah transactions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that in a Murabahah sale involving a mudarib and rabb al-mal, the selling price must be based on the lower of the two actual costs to avoid shubha (suspicion) of dishonesty.

Core Idea
Murabahah is built on trust; any suspicion of dishonesty forces use of the lower price.

This passage from Kitab al-Mudarabah tackles a subtle scenario: when the mudarib and rabb al-mal transact directly with each other, and the same item is sold by Murabahah. The key principle is that Murabahah requires full transparency and avoidance of any hint of betrayal. The Hanafi school resolves the conflict by taking the lower price as the basis, even if it means ignoring the actual purchase price paid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
The passage gives two rulings: (1) mudarib buys from rabb al-mal, (2) rabb al-mal buys from mudarib. In each, determine who is selling by Murabahah and what the lower price is.
Case 1: mudarib sells Murabahah after buying from rabb al-mal. Case 2: rabb al-mal sells Murabahah after buying from mudarib.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The text states the sale is permissible due to difference of purposes (taghayur al-maqasid) to meet a need, but has a suspicion (shubha) of non-existence. Since Murabahah is based on trust, the lower price is taken to avoid shubhat al-khiyanah (suspicion of dishonesty).
The suspicion arises because the transaction is essentially selling one's own property to oneself indirectly.
3
Apply the rule to Case 2
For the second case, the profit is shared equally. Half of the profit is considered non-existent for the rabb al-mal's share, so his effective cost is 1100 (1000 purchase + half of 200 profit considered as if not earned).
This shows symmetry: the Murabahah price is the lower of the two effective costs.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter of mudarabah presents a scenario where the mudarib buys a slave on behalf of the mudarabah with 1000 dirhams on a half-profit sharing basis. The slave is worth 2000. The slave commits manslaughter. The diyah (blood money) must be paid. The ruling: the diyah is apportioned according to ownership shares. Since the slave's value is 2000, the original capital is 1000, so the profit is 1000. This profit is split equally: 500 for mudarib, 500 for rabb al-mal. Thus total ownership: rabb al-mal has his capital 1000 plus his profit 500 = 1500 (3/4), mudarib has his profit 500 (1/4). Hence diyah: 3/4 on rabb al-mal, 1/4 on mudarib. After paying, the slave exits the mudarabah contract: the mudarib's portion exits because he becomes a guarantor (he is liable for his share), and the rabb al-mal's portion exits because the judge's order to divide the diyah implies division of the slave, and the mudarabah ends with division. Unlike a previous case (referenced) where the entire price was on the mudarib and he had recourse, so no division was needed. Here, the slave is considered removed from their ownership due to the crime, and paying diyah is like a fresh purchase; thus the slave becomes jointly owned in quarters (mudarib 1/4, rabb al-mal 3/4) but not as part of mudarabah. Consequently, the mudarib serves the slave one day and the rabb al-mal three days. The key distinction: in the previous case, the liability was on the mudarib alone, so no division occurred; here, both are liable, leading to division and termination of mudarabah.

Blood Money for a Slave's Homicide in Mudaraba
Ruling with proof (talil) on fidyah distribution in mudaraba and its effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the distribution of blood money (fidyah) when a slave purchased with mudaraba capital commits accidental homicide, and explain why the mudaraba ends after payment.

Core Idea
Blood money is divided according to ownership shares; paying it ends the mudaraba because crime liability is not trade.

In a mudaraba, when the mudarib uses capital to buy a slave who then kills someone accidentally, the blood money (fidyah) must be paid. But how is it divided between the rabb al-mal and the mudarib? And what happens to the mudaraba contract? This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies the rule: the obligation follows ownership, and because liability for crime (daman al-jinaya) is fundamentally different from commercial liability (daman al-tijara), the mudaraba dissolves. Let's unpack the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Accidental homicide (jinaya khata) by a slave. The ruling: payment of fidyah (blood money) is wajib. The key principle is that fidyah is an expenditure of ownership—it is divided proportionally to each owner's share in the slave.
The Hanafi school treats fidyah as a financial obligation, not a penal one, hence proportional to ownership.
2
Calculate ownership shares
Given: mudaraba capital = 1000 dirhams; slave's current value = 2000 dirhams. Profit = 1000. Half profit (500) goes to mudarib, half (500) to rabb al-mal. Total ownership: rabb al-mal = capital 1000 + profit share 500 = 1500 (3/4); mudarib = profit share 500 (1/4).
This calculation assumes the slave is the only asset and no prior losses.
3
Apply proportionality to fidyah
Since fidyah follows ownership, the rabb al-mal pays 3/4 of the fidyah and the mudarib pays 1/4. This is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) according to the text.
The passage does not specify the amount of fidyah; it only determines the ratio.
4
Determine the effect on mudaraba
After paying fidyah, the mudaraba ends. The mudarib's share leaves trust (amanah) and becomes a guarantee (dhaman); the rabb al-mal's share leaves because the judge's order for division severs the partnership. The slave becomes jointly owned (3/4 rabb, 1/4 mudarib) but not as mudaraba—the slave serves each owner proportionally (3 days for rabb, 1 day for mudarib).
Contrast with a previous case where the entire price was a debt on the mudarib; no division occurred and mudaraba continued.
5
Understand the distinction (daman al-tijara vs daman al-jinaya)
In the previous case, liability was for trade (daman al-tijara), which is compatible with mudaraba. Here, liability for crime (daman al-jinaya) is outside the scope of trade, so the mudaraba cannot continue. This is the core legal reasoning (talil) for the dissimilar ruling.
This distinction is cited from Fawaid Zahiriyyah, reinforcing the Hanafi position.

Core Idea

This lesson examines a specific scenario in mudaraba: the mudarib receives 1000 dirhams from the rabb al-mal, buys a slave, but before paying the price, the capital is destroyed. The central legal question is whether the rabb al-mal must provide replacement capital once, repeatedly, or at all. The Hanafi ruling hinges on the classification of the mudarib's possession as a trust (amanah) and the requirement that fulfillment (istifa) requires a guaranteed (madhmun) possession. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining liability in Islamic partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and transaction
Recognize that the mudarib (agent) received capital from the rabb al-mal (principal) to trade. Here, the mudarib buys a slave with the capital but has not yet paid the price.
The capital is in the mudarib's possession as a trust (amanah).
2
Recognize the destruction of capital before payment
The capital (1000 dirhams) is destroyed after the purchase but before the price is paid. Thus, the mudarib still owes the price to the seller but has lost the original capital.
The timing of loss matters: after purchase but before payment.
3
Apply the amanah principle
The mudarib's possession of the capital is a trust (amanah), meaning he is not liable for loss without negligence. Because the possession is not guaranteed (daman), it cannot constitute fulfillment (istifa) of the debt. Therefore, the rabb al-mal must provide replacement capital each time it is lost, repeatedly.
Trust possession and guarantee are mutually exclusive; istifa requires guaranteed possession.
4
Contrast with the wakil for purchase
Contrast the mudarib with an agent (wakil) for purchase. If the wakil is given the price before purchase and it is lost after purchase, he can claim from the principal only once because agency can combine with guarantee. For example, a usurper (ghasib) appointed as agent retains liability. If the wakil receives the price after purchase, he cannot claim at all because the right to return was established by the purchase itself.
Agency allows coexistence of amanah and daman, limiting claims to once.
5
Derive the ruling
From the above, the Hanafi ruling is: In mudaraba, the rabb al-mal must replace the lost capital repeatedly (raj' marratan ba'da ukhra). The mudarib can demand replacement each time the capital is lost due to its trust nature. This differs from agency where claims are limited.
The ruling is based on the incompatibility of trust and guarantee in mudaraba.

Mudarib's Recourse When Capital Lost Before Paying Price
Ruling on mudarib's recourse when capital is lost before paying price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of a mudarib's recourse when capital is lost before paying the price, and explain the distinction from a wakil based on the nature of possession (amanah vs daman).

Core Idea
The mudarib's trust possession allows repeated recourse; the wakil's guaranteed possession limits recourse.

In a mudaraba, the mudarib holds the capital as a trust (amanah). This lesson examines what happens when the mudarib uses the capital to purchase an item but the capital is lost before he pays the price. The key difference from a wakil (agent for purchase) hinges on the nature of possession: trust versus guarantee. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rules of mudaraba and agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
Note that the mudarib's possession of capital is a trust (amanah). The loss falls on the rabb al-mal, and the mudarib can demand replacement repeatedly because istifa' requires a guaranteed possession (qabd madmun).
The text explicitly states: 'because the capital is a trust in his hand, and fulfillment only occurs by a guaranteed possession.'
2
Follow the dalil (legal reasoning)
The reasoning is talil: the ruling is derived from the principle that amanah and daman are mutually exclusive. Since the mudarib holds as amanah, he is not considered to have fulfilled the right until actual payment.
This is a qiyas based on the incompatibility of amanah and daman.
3
Contrast with the wakil's case
The wakil's possession can be compatible with daman (e.g., if the principal pays the price to him before purchase). Therefore, the wakil's recourse is limited to once, or none if payment occurs after purchase.
The text uses the example of a usurper (ghasib) who can also combine agency with daman.
4
Apply to the sub-cases
For the wakil: if the price is given before purchase, he returns once; if given after purchase, he does not return. For the mudarib, the money paid before purchase remains amanah even after purchase, so he can return repeatedly.
The key is whether the possession becomes madmun (guaranteed) or remains amanah.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on a mudarib who has been given capital (1000 dirhams) by the rabb al-mal to trade. The mudarib uses the capital to purchase a slave but before he can pay the price, the capital is lost. The mudarib is not held liable for this loss because his possession of the capital is a trust (amanah), not a guaranteed possession (daman). Since fulfillment (istifa') of a right (here, the vendor's right to the price) requires a guaranteed possession, and the mudarib's possession remains a trust, he does not fulfill the right by the loss. Therefore, the rabb al-mal must provide another 1000 dirhams for the mudarib to pay the price. Furthermore, whatever the rabb al-mal pays becomes part of the capital (ras al-mal). If that second payment is also lost before payment, the mudarib can again demand replacement from the rabb al-mal, and so on repeatedly. This is contrasted with the case of a wakil (agent) appointed for purchase. If the principal gives the price to the agent before purchase and it is lost after purchase, the agent can only have recourse once because agency can coexist with guarantee (daman), so the agent can be considered to have fulfilled his right by receiving the money after the purchase. In contrast, for the mudarib, the money paid before purchase remains a trust even after purchase, so he does not become a fulfiller, and can have recourse repeatedly until fulfillment actually occurs.

Whose Word is Accepted in a Mudaraba Capital Dispute?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on capital amount and profit in mudaraba.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whose word is accepted when mudarib and rabb al-mal dispute the amount of capital, and distinguish the initial and final Hanafi positions.

Core Idea
Mudarib's word on capital received, rabb al-mal's word on profit earned.

This passage from the chapter on disputes in mudaraba addresses a common disagreement: the mudarib claims he received less capital and made profit, while the rabb al-mal claims he gave more capital leaving no profit. The Hanafi school's position evolved, and this lesson traces that development and the reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
The mudarib has 2000 dirhams. He claims capital was 1000 and profit is 1000. The rabb al-mal claims capital was 2000 with no profit. Recognize the two distinct elements: capital received (maqbud) and profit (ribh).
The total amount in hand (2000) is not disputed; only its composition.
2
Recall the initial Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa initially and Imam Zufar held that the rabb al-mal's word is accepted. Their reason: the mudarib claims partnership in profit (he wants a share), and the rabb al-mal denies profit; the denier's word prevails.
This is the general rule: al-qawl li al-munkir.
3
Understand the final Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa later retracted. The final position is that the mudarib's word is accepted on the amount of capital. Reason: the dispute is essentially about what was received (maqbud), and the mudarib is the receiver, so he knows best.
The mudarib is an amin (trustee) regarding the capital, so his word is taken.
4
Apply the separate rule for profit
If the parties also dispute the amount of profit (separate from capital), the rabb al-mal's word is accepted. Reason: profit entitlement comes from a condition (shart) set by the rabb al-mal, so his word prevails.
Profit is not a receipt but a contractual condition.
5
Note the role of evidence
Whichever party brings bayyinah (witness testimony) for his claim of an excess, it is accepted, because evidence is for proof (ithbat).
Bayyinah overrides the default qawl.

Commentary
This passage, from the chapter on disputes (fisal fi al-ikhtilaf) in the book of mudaraba, addresses a disagreement between the mudarib and the rabb al-mal. The scenario: the mudarib has 2000 dirhams in hand. He claims that the capital given was 1000 dirhams and the remaining 1000 is profit. The rabb al-mal claims that he gave 2000 as capital, with no profit. The ruling in the text (the book, Al-Hidayah) is that the mudarib's word is accepted. Abu Hanifa initially held the opposite view, that the rabb al-mal's word is accepted, which is also the view of Imam Zufar. Their reasoning was that the mudarib is claiming partnership in profit (i.e., he wants a share of the profit), while the rabb al-mal denies that there is any profit. In disputes, the word of the denier (munkir) is generally accepted. However, Abu Hanifa later retracted this opinion and adopted the view in the book. The reason for the change is that the dispute is essentially about the amount of capital received (al-maqbud). The mudarib is the one who received the capital, and he knows best how much he received. Therefore, his word is accepted on the amount received, whether he is a trustee (amin) or a guarantor (damin). This principle is applied here. Finally, if either party brings evidence (bayyinah) to support their claim of an excess, it is accepted, because evidence is for proof (ithbat).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on competing claims in mudarabah to determine whose word is accepted and whose evidence is given precedence.

Core Idea
Owner’s word prevails, but mudarib’s evidence overrides—because the mudarib claims ownership against a denial.

In a mudarabah dispute, if the mudarib claims the capital was a loan while the owner says it was a deposit or mudarabah, a conflict arises. Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The mudarib claims the money was given as a loan (qard). The owner claims it was a bida'a, wadi'a, or mudarabah. Both agree that some contract existed but disagree on the type.
2
Determine the default ruling without evidence
Without evidence, the owner's word is taken. The owner is the defendant/denier (munkir), so his statement is accepted unless the plaintiff (mudarib) proves otherwise.
This is the general Hanafi principle: al-bayyinah 'ala man idda'a wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara.
3
Note the exception when the mudarib brings evidence
If the mudarib produces legal evidence (bayyinah: two male witnesses or one male and two females), that evidence is accepted over the owner's bare word. The case is then decided according to the mudarib's claim.
Why? Because the mudarib is claiming ownership transfer, which needs proof to overcome the owner's denial.
4

The text says: 'because the mudarib claims against him ownership, and he denies.' The mudarib is asserting that he now owns the capital (due to loan), while the owner denies that ownership passed. Thus, evidence is for the claimant.
This explains why the mudarib's evidence is stronger—he must prove his extraordinary claim.
5
Contrast with other madhahib (optional)
Imam al-Shafi'i reportedly holds that both parties take oaths, which can lead to a different outcome. Understanding this difference highlights the Hanafi emphasis on evidence hierarchy.
Not part of the core matn, but useful for comparative fiqh.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, the default is generality; the manager's word prevails unless the owner proves a restriction.

When a dispute arises between the owner and the mudarib over whether the trade was restricted to a specific type, whose statement is accepted? This lesson explores the Hanafi position, which rests on the default nature of the mudarabah contract. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying fiqh rules in financial partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Dispute
Recognize that the owner claims the mudarabah was limited to a specific type of trade (e.g., grain only), while the mudarib denies any restriction.
The focus is on the claim of a condition, not the contract's validity.
2
State the Ruling
The mudarib's statement is accepted (with his oath). The owner must prove the alleged restriction.
This is the majority Hanafi view (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad).
3
Explain the Default Principle
The default (asl) in mudarabah is generality ('umum) and absoluteness (itlaq). The mudarib is authorized to trade in any lawful goods unless a condition is explicitly stated.
Specification (takhsis) is an incidental condition that requires evidence.
4
Contrast with Wakala
In agency (wakala), the default is specificity (khusus); the agent's authority is limited to what is delegated. Thus, in a dispute, the principal's word would be taken.
This contrast highlights the unique nature of mudarabah.
5
Note the Ikhtilaf
Imam Zufar (within Hanafi) held that the owner's word should be taken. The mufta bihi follows the majority. Imām al-Shafi'ī reportedly holds that both parties take oaths.
These differences are legitimate scholarly variations.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive treatment of dispute resolution in mudarabah contracts from the Hanafi school. It opens with the general principle: when there is a conflict between the capital owner (rabb al-mal) and the agent (mudarib) regarding the nature of the contract, the default rule is that the statement of the capital owner is accepted because he is the one denying the claim of the mudarib who asserts a right (ownership of profit, partnership, etc.). However, this is nuanced by several scenarios. First, if the mudarib claims the money was a loan (qard) while the capital owner insists it was mudarabah, bida'ah, or wadi'ah, the capital owner's word is taken, but the mudarib may provide evidence. Second, if the capital owner alleges a specific type of trade was agreed upon but the mudarib denies any specification, the mudarib's word is accepted because the default in mudarabah is absolute generality, unlike agency where specificity is presumed. Third, if both agree there was a specification but disagree on the type, the capital owner's word prevails because authorization stems from him. Fourth, if both bring evidence, the mudarib's evidence is preferred because he needs to negate the restriction (forcing him to prove his lack of permission). Fifth, if both witnesses give conflicting dates, the later date voids the earlier. The passage then explains a specific phrase from the matn: 'And whoever has with him...' This illustrates a scenario where a third party claims the money is mudarabah while the holder claims it is bida'ah; the owner's statement is accepted because the mudarib claims either a wage, a condition, or partnership, and the owner is the denier. The commentary then addresses the phrase 'You gave me a loan' and the use of the term 'mudarib' in the matn even when both parties deny mudarabah. Various explanations are given, with the preferred one being that it is based on lexical conformity (mushakalah). The discussion on 'ownership' (tamalluk) clarifies whether the mudarib's claim to a loan implies ownership of profit or principal; the correct view is that it implies ownership of the principal, with profit being subordinate. Finally, the passage covers disputes where the capital owner specifies a particular trade but the mudarib denies it. Here the mudarib's statement is accepted because the contract is presumed general; the burden of proving the restrictive condition falls on the capital owner. Imam Zufar's contrary opinion is noted and rebutted using the analogy of the general contract. Later scenarios involve both parties agreeing on a specific trade but differing on the type, where the capital owner's word prevails, and the precedence of the mudarib's evidence when both produce proof. The passage concludes with a partially corrupted section on each party claiming different types.

Key Terms
مضارب (mudārib): The agent who manages the capital in a mudarabah contract; in this passage, he is the person who may claim ownership of profit or partnership.
رب المال (rabb al-māl): The capital owner who entrusts money to the mudarib; his word is generally preferred in disputes regarding the nature of the contract.
بضاعت (biḍāʿah): A commenda-like arrangement where the agent trades without profit-sharing; here it is a type of contract distinct from mudarabah.

Disputes over Specific Trade Types and Evidence in Mudarabah
Presents multiple dispute scenarios and whose word is accepted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule when the capital owner alleges a specific trade and the mudarib denies it, and identify the preponderant position in the dispute with Imam Zufar.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, the contract is presumed general; the party alleging a restriction must prove it, so the mudarib's statement is accepted when the capital owner claims a specific trade was agreed.

The previous lesson covered the denier principle. Here we delve into scenarios where both parties agree that a mudarabah existed but disagree on whether a specific trade was stipulated. The default in mudarabah is generality (itlaq), unlike agency (wakalah) where specificity is default. This lesson also explores the evidentiary rules when both parties bring witnesses, and the intra-Hanafi disagreement with Imam Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default of mudarabah: absolute generality
The text argues that an absolute mudarabah contract is valid for all trades, so if the capital owner claims a specific trade was agreed, he is alleging a restriction.
The evidence for this is: if the capital owner says 'Take this money as mudarabah at half,' the contract is valid and the mudarib can trade in everything.
2
Apply the principle that restrictions require proof
The mudarib's statement that no restriction was made is accepted because he is adhering to the default. The capital owner must prove the restriction.
This is analogous to the general rule that the original state (asal) is preserved until a change is proven.
3
Contrast with agency (wakalah)
In agency, the default is specificity, so if the agent denies a restriction, his word is not accepted; the principal's statement is accepted.
The text explicitly states: 'بخلاف وکالت کے کہ اس میں اصل خاص ہونا ہے'
4
Analyze the scenario where both agree on a specific trade but differ on the type
If both agree the mudarabah was specific (e.g., for 'trade') but disagree on the type (e.g., cloth vs. grain), the capital owner's word is accepted because authorization comes from him.
Here they are not disputing the restriction; they agree it exists. The dispute is over its scope, and the grantor's word prevails.
5
Evaluate the evidence rules when both produce evidence
If both parties provide witnesses, the mudarib's evidence is preferred because he needs to negate the restriction to avoid liability; the capital owner's evidence is unnecessary for his initial acceptance.
If witnesses give conflicting dates, the later date abrogates the earlier.
6
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Zufar holds that the capital owner's word is accepted in all cases of specific trade claims because authorization originates from him. The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) reject this, citing the contract's default generality.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive treatment of dispute resolution in mudarabah contracts from the Hanafi school. It opens with the general principle: when there is a conflict between the capital owner (rabb al-mal) and the agent (mudarib) regarding the nature of the contract, the default rule is that the statement of the capital owner is accepted because he is the one denying the claim of the mudarib who asserts a right (ownership of profit, partnership, etc.). However, this is nuanced by several scenarios. First, if the mudarib claims the money was a loan (qard) while the capital owner insists it was mudarabah, bida'ah, or wadi'ah, the capital owner's word is taken, but the mudarib may provide evidence. Second, if the capital owner alleges a specific type of trade was agreed upon but the mudarib denies any specification, the mudarib's word is accepted because the default in mudarabah is absolute generality, unlike agency where specificity is presumed. Third, if both agree there was a specification but disagree on the type, the capital owner's word prevails because authorization stems from him. Fourth, if both bring evidence, the mudarib's evidence is preferred because he needs to negate the restriction (forcing him to prove his lack of permission). Fifth, if both witnesses give conflicting dates, the later date voids the earlier. The passage then explains a specific phrase from the matn: 'And whoever has with him...' This illustrates a scenario where a third party claims the money is mudarabah while the holder claims it is bida'ah; the owner's statement is accepted because the mudarib claims either a wage, a condition, or partnership, and the owner is the denier. The commentary then addresses the phrase 'You gave me a loan' and the use of the term 'mudarib' in the matn even when both parties deny mudarabah. Various explanations are given, with the preferred one being that it is based on lexical conformity (mushakalah). The discussion on 'ownership' (tamalluk) clarifies whether the mudarib's claim to a loan implies ownership of profit or principal; the correct view is that it implies ownership of the principal, with profit being subordinate. Finally, the passage covers disputes where the capital owner specifies a particular trade but the mudarib denies it. Here the mudarib's statement is accepted because the contract is presumed general; the burden of proving the restrictive condition falls on the capital owner. Imam Zufar's contrary opinion is noted and rebutted using the analogy of the general contract. Later scenarios involve both parties agreeing on a specific trade but differing on the type, where the capital owner's word prevails, and the precedence of the mudarib's evidence when both produce proof. The passage concludes with a partially corrupted section on each party claiming different types.

مضارب (mudārib): The agent who manages the capital in a mudarabah contract; in this passage, he is the person who may claim ownership of profit or partnership.
رب المال (rabb al-māl): The capital owner who entrusts money to the mudarib; his word is generally preferred in disputes regarding the nature of the contract.
بضاعت (biḍāʿah): A commenda-like arrangement where the agent trades without profit-sharing; here it is a type of contract distinct from mudarabah.

Evidentiary Preference in Mudarabah Trade Disputes
Ruling on conflicting claims about trade type in mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi evidentiary rule when parties dispute the trade type in a mudarabah, distinguish between no-evidence and both-evidence scenarios, and apply the rule to new cases.

Core Idea
In mudarabah trade disputes, the rabb al-mal's word stands without proof; but if both have proof, the mudarib's proof prevails.

Mudarabah contracts often specify the type of trade. When a dispute arises, who should the qadi believe? This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a nuanced ruling based on whether evidence (bayyinah) is available. Understanding this rule is crucial for correctly resolving mudarabah conflicts in Islamic finance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and parties
Recognize that the mudarib and rabb al-mal disagree on the permitted type of trade. Both agree the mudarabah was specific (muqayyad) rather than general (mutlaq).
If the mudarabah were general, no dispute would arise.
2
Determine the default rule without evidence
When neither party produces evidence, the rabb al-mal's claim is accepted. Reason: authorization originates from him, and the contract is specific, so his statement is the default.
3
Determine the rule when both have evidence
If both submit proof (bayyinah), the mudarib's evidence is preferred over the rabb al-mal's.
This is because the mudarib must prove authorization to avoid liability.
4
Understand the rationale behind the preference
The mudarib needs to prove he was authorized for the claimed trade type to negate his liability for losses. The rabb al-mal does not need to prove anything because his authorization is presumed.
Liability (daman) is the key concern in mudarabah disputes.
5
Contrast with other madhahib
Note that the Shafi'i school differs: they would require the mudarib to prove authorization since he claims an exception to the rabb al-mal's default permission.
This ikhtilaf affects practical outcomes when both parties have evidence.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute in a mudarabah contract where both parties disagree on the type of trade permitted. The ruling is presented in two scenarios: First, if neither party has evidence, the rabb al-mal's claim is accepted because the contract is specific and authorization flows from him. Second, if both produce evidence (bayyinah), the mudarib's proof is preferred. The reason is that the mudarib must prove he was authorized to trade in that specific type to avoid liability for losses; the rabb al-mal, not being obligated to prove anything beyond his original permission, does not need to present proof. This is based on the principle that the one who needs to negate liability must provide evidence.

Key Terms
مضارب (mudārib): The person who manages the mudarabah investment; the working partner.
رب المال (rabb al-māl): The owner of the capital in a mudarabah contract; the silent partner.
بینہ (bayyinah): Legal evidence, typically witness testimony or documentary proof.
