Settlement in Intentional Murder: Ruling, Proof, and Distinctions
Ruling with proof and analogical distinctions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh in qisas, identify the Quranic proof, explain the analogy to nikah, and distinguish sulh in qisas from sulh in shuf'a and kafala.

Core Idea
Sulh in qisas is valid because the Quran permits remission; it is like marriage: any valid dower can be the consideration.

This passage from Kitab al-Sulh of Al-Hidayah establishes the Hanafi position that settlement (sulh) in cases of intentional murder (qisas) is permissible. The ruling is derived from a clear Quranic verse and supported by an analogy to marriage (nikah), comparing the exchange of property for a non-property right. The text also clarifies important distinctions, showing why sulh for qisas is valid while sulh for the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) and surety for the person (kafala) is not, helping you avoid common misunderstandings.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and the act
Identify that the passage discusses sulh (settlement) in intentional murder (qasas) — the act is the waiving of the right to qisas in exchange for compensation. The ruling is that such sulh is valid (sahih).
Hanafi: qisas is a right of the victim's heirs, not a ḥadd punishment (right of Allah).
2
Locate the primary proof (dalil)
The author cites Quran 2:178: 'Then whoever is granted some remission from his brother...' Ibn Abbas interprets this as referring to sulh. Identify the phrase 'min akhihi shay'' as indicating a partial waiver, which becomes the basis for accepting compensation.
The verse establishes that forgiveness (afw) can be partial and implies a valid settlement.
3
Analyse the analogy (tashbih) with nikah
The author states that sulh in qisas is like marriage (nikah) in that both involve an exchange of property for a non-property. Thus, any property that can serve as dower (mahr) in marriage can be the consideration in sulh. However, if the specified consideration is invalid (e.g., wine), in sulh nothing becomes obligatory (treat as absolute pardon), while in marriage mahr al-mithl becomes due.
This analogy clarifies the scope of valid consideration and the default when consideration fails.
4
Apply the rule on invalid consideration
When the sulh consideration is invalid (fasid), the Hanafi ruling is that the killer must pay the diyah (blood money) instead, because diyah is the original obligation (mu'jib al-dam) for the blood. This is different from marriage where the default is mahr al-mithl.
The diyah is the baseline; the sulh only modifies the mode of payment.
5
Contrast with sulh for shuf'a and kafala
The passage explains why sulh for the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) is invalid: shuf'a is a right to acquire property that is still owned by another, so there is no present ownership to trade. Similarly, surety for the person (kafala bi al-nafs) does not involve an existing property right. In contrast, qisas involves an actual ownership of the act (the right to kill), which can be exchanged.
If an invalid sulh for shuf'a is attempted, the shuf'a right is forfeited (as it is lost by waiver/silence). For kafala, there are two narrations on invalidity, with the mufta bihi being that it becomes invalid.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Sulh of Al-Hidayah establishes the ruling that settlement (sulh) in cases of intentional murder (qisas) is permissible. The proof is the Quranic verse: 'Then whoever is granted some remission from his brother, then follow-up with fairness and payment with kindness.' Ibn Abbas explains that this verse was revealed concerning sulh. The author draws an analogy between sulh and marriage (nikah): both involve an exchange of property for non-property. Thus, any property that can serve as valid dower (mahr) in marriage can be the consideration in sulh for qisas. However, if the specified consideration is invalid (e.g., wine), in sulh nothing becomes obligatory because it is treated as an absolute pardon (which yields no money), whereas in marriage the dower of the like (mahr al-mithl) becomes obligatory. The author then distinguishes sulh for qisas from sulh for the right of pre-emption (shuf'a): shuf'a is a right to acquire property that is still owned by another, so there is no legal capacity to sell it before acquisition; hence sulh for shuf'a is invalid by consensus of the four imams. In contrast, qisas is an ownership of the act (i.e., the right to exact retaliation) over the killer, so taking compensation in exchange for waiving that right is valid. If sulh for shuf'a is attempted, the shuf'a right is forfeited because it is lost by waiver or silence. As for surety for the person (kafala bi al-nafs), it resembles shuf'a in that sulh does not make property obligatory. However, there are two narrations on whether the kafala itself becomes invalid: the relied-upon view is that it does become invalid. The commentary further elaborates, citing the Quranic verse and explaining the interpretations of Ibn Abbas and the Salaf.

Key Terms
صلح (sulh): A contract of settlement or compromise, often involving waiver of a claim for compensation.
قصاص (qisas): Retaliatory punishment for murder or wounding, i.e., the right of the victim's heirs to exact retribution.
دیت (diyah): Blood money or financial compensation paid to the victim's heirs in lieu of qisas, especially in unintentional killing.
شفعہ (shuf'a): Pre-emption right: the right of a co-owner or neighbor to purchase a property sold to a third party by matching the price.
کفالت (kafala): Suretyship, specifically kafala bi al-nafs (surety for the person), where a guarantor ensures the presence of a person.
مہر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The dower of the like: the standard dower for a woman of similar social status, used as the default when no specific dower is mentioned or the specified one is invalid.
