Hibah of Undivided Property to Multiple Recipients: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on gifting undivided property to two recipients, with extension to sadaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifting an undivided property to two persons, identify the basis of the intra-Hanafi disagreement, and apply the distinction between hibah and rahn.

Core Idea
A single gift to two recipients creates shuyu' (co-ownership), which invalidates it under Abu Hanifa.

The Hanafi school contains a well-known internal disagreement on whether one can gift a single undivided property to two recipients. This lesson unpacks the matn from al-Hidayah, where Imam al-Marghinani records Imam Abu Hanifa's view that such a gift is invalid due to shuyu', while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold it valid, drawing an analogy to pawning a single item to multiple creditors. Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of the conditions for a valid gift and the role of possession (qabd).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The matn presents two cases: (a) two donors to one donee (valid, agreed), (b) one donor to two donees (disputed). Note that the first is uncontested; the second is the focus of ikhtilaf.
2
Extract Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules invalid because gifting to two effectively gives each an undivided half, creating shuyu'. Shuyu' prevents valid possession (qabd) of the whole, which is required for hibah.
3
Extract the Sahibayn's reasoning and analogy
The Sahibayn say valid because the transfer (tamlik) is a single act, so no shuyu'. They analogize to rahn: pawning one item to two creditors is valid because detention is full for each.
4
Analyze the distinction between hibah and rahn
Abu Hanifa counters: in rahn, detention is not ownership; each creditor holds the whole right of habs. In hibah, ownership is divided, so shuyu' occurs. Understand why the analogy fails for Abu Hanifa.
5
Connect to the sadaqah extension
The passage extends the principle: charity to two rich persons is also disputed, with similar reasoning about intention and shuyu'. Note the difference between hibah (aimed at donee) and sadaqah (aimed at Allah).

He said: And if two persons give a house as a gift to one person, it is permissible, because they handed it over as a whole and he took possession of it as a whole, so there is no co-ownership (shuyu'). And if one person gives it to two persons, it is not permissible according to Abu Hanifa, but the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it is valid, because this is a gift of the entirety from him to them, since the transfer of ownership (tamlik) is one, so co-ownership does not occur, just as if he pawned a house to two men. And his (Abu Hanifa's) argument is that this is a gift of half to each of them, and for this reason, if the gift was in something that cannot be divided, and one of them accepted, it would be valid. And because ownership is established for each of them in half, so the transfer of ownership is likewise, because it is its ruling. Based on this consideration, co-ownership occurs, unlike the case of pawn (rahn), because its ruling is detention (habs), and detention is established for each of them fully, so there is no co-ownership. And for this reason, if he paid off the debt of one of them, he cannot retrieve anything from the pawn. And in al-Jami' al-Saghir: If he gave charity to two needy persons with ten dirhams, or gifted it to them, it is permissible; and if he gave charity to two rich persons, or gifted it to them, it is not permissible. And the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say it is permissible for the rich as well. (The argument) made each one of them a proxy for the other, and capacity (salahiyyah) is established because each of them is a transfer of ownership without exchange. And he (Imam Muhammad) differentiated between gift (hibah) and charity (sadaqah) in ruling in al-Jami'; and in al-Asl (al-Mabsut), he equated them, saying: Likewise charity, because co-ownership is a preventer in both cases, since both depend on possession. The basis of the difference according to this narration is that charity is intended for the sake of Allah, and He is one, while gift is intended for the sake of the rich person, and they are two. And it is said that this is the correct view, and what is mentioned in al-Asl is intended as charity to two rich persons.

Commentary
This passage from the matn of al-Hidayah presents rulings on hibah involving multiple parties. First, two owners giving their shared house to one person is valid because they deliver it as a whole to one recipient, so no shuyu' (undivided co-ownership) arises. Conversely, one person gifting a single house to two recipients is invalid according to Abu Hanifa because each receives an undivided half, creating shuyu', which is a preventer. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it valid because the transfer is one act, so no shuyu' exists, drawing analogy with pawning a single item to two people. The text then addresses a related scenario from al-Jami' al-Saghir: giving 10 dirhams to two needy persons as sadaqah or hibah is valid, but to two rich persons is not. The Sahibayn allow it for rich as well. The text explains the reasoning: in sadaqah, the intention is for Allah, who is one, so it's as if giving to one; in hibah, the intention is to please the recipients, who are two, causing shuyu'. However, al-Mabsut equates the two, considering shuyu' a preventer in both. The preferred view is that the ruling in al-Asl refers specifically to sadaqah to two rich persons.

Key Terms
شیوع (shuyu'): Undivided co-ownership or commonality in a property that prevents valid possession and transfer of the whole.
تملیک (tamlik): Transfer of ownership; in hibah, it is a single act of granting ownership.
ہبہ (hibah): A gift: voluntary transfer of ownership without compensation.
