Rulings on Sadaqah: Comparison with Hibah
Ruling statement with qiyas proof: Sadaqah compared to Hibah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings of Sadaqah in the Hanafi school, including the requirement of possession, the prohibition of divisible musha', irrevocability, and the effect of a vow.

Core Idea
Sadaqah is like Hibah in requiring possession, but unlike Hibah it cannot be revoked because the reward is attained.

Sadaqah (charity) shares many rulings with Hibah (gift), but there is a critical difference: Sadaqah is irrevocable. In this lesson, we explore the rules for a valid Sadaqah, including the condition of possession, the prohibition of giving an undivided share, and the consequences of vowing to give all one's property. The Hanafi school also allows Sadaqah to a rich person by istihsan, as long as the intention is reward.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its hukm
Determine what Sadaqah is (a tabarru') and its default hukm (recommended) and when it becomes wajib (by vow).
2
List the conditions of validity
Identify that possession (qabd) is required and that the item must not be a divisible undivided share (musha').
The proof is qiyas to Hibah.
3
Understand the irrevocability
Recognize that Sadaqah cannot be revoked because the purpose (thawab) is considered attained at the moment of giving.
This is the key difference from Hibah.
4
Apply the vow rule
Understand that if someone vows to give charity of all their property, it becomes obligatory to give all of it.
There is a narration that it is the same as a vow of property subject to zakat.
5
Note the istihsan ruling
Accept that giving Sadaqah to a rich person is permissible if the intention is reward, and giving a gift (Hibah) to a poor person is also valid.
This is an exception based on istihsan.

Vow to Give Charity of One's Property: Obligation and Scope
Ruling with proof on charity vow scope and retention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on charity vows using 'mal' vs 'milk', apply the analogy to zakah, and evaluate the practical retention for expenses.

Core Idea
A vow of 'my property' obligates only zakah-eligible assets; 'my possession' obligates everything owned.

When a person vows to give charity of his property, is he required to give away all his wealth? The Hanafi school limits the obligation to the type of property subject to zakah, drawing an analogy that prevents excessive hardship. However, if the vow uses the word 'milk' (possession), even non-tangible rights are included. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, the internal and external disputes, and the practical concession that allows retaining necessary expenses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the vow wording
Determine whether the person said 'my property (mali)' or 'my possession (milki)'. The ruling changes based on the term used.
The source text explicitly distinguishes between 'mal' and 'milk'.
2
Apply the Hanafi default for 'mal'
For 'mal', the obligation is limited to property in which zakah is obligatory: cash, trade goods, grazing livestock, and tithe fruits. This is based on qiyas (analogy) to zakah.
The word 'sadaqah' in the vow is akin to zakah, so the same categories apply.
3
Trace the qiyas (analogy)
The original case (asl) is zakah on specific properties. The new case (far') is the charity vow. The effective cause (illah) is the custom of using 'mal' to mean zakah-eligible property in Islamic law.
This is a qiyas khafi because the illah is not explicitly stated but derived from customary usage.
4
Contrast with 'milk'
If the vow uses 'milki', the generality is enforced: all possessions (including non-property rights like marriage, qisas, and even khamr) are included, because 'milk' is broader than 'mal'.
A minority narration equates the two, but the preferred view distinguishes them.
5
Apply the retention concession
If fulfilling the vow would leave the person destitute, he may retain enough for himself and his family until he earns again. The amount depends on his profession: a merchant retains one month, a daily worker one day.
This is based on istihsan to prevent hardship.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling with proofs on the issue of a vow (nadhr) to give charity of one's property. It begins by presenting the Hanafi position: if someone says 'I have allegiance (alayya) to give charity of my property', it obligates charity only from the type of property subject to zakah – cash, trade goods, grazing livestock, and tithe fruits. This is based on qiyas (analogy) to zakah, as the vow is for sadaqah which resembles zakah in obligation. The text then presents inter-madhhab disputes: Imam Zufar and a narration of Imam Ahmad require all property due to the general word 'mal'; Imam Shafi'i (and a narration of Ahmad) require nothing, while another view from Shafi'i and Malik require only one-third (like a bequest). The Hanafi response is to specify the generality by analogy to zakah. Next, the passage distinguishes between vowing to give charity of 'mal' (property) vs. 'milk' (possession). For 'milk', all possessions are obligated, including non-mal rights like marriage and qisas, because 'milk' is broader. Finally, the text addresses the practical concern of survival: the person may retain necessary expenses for himself and family until he earns again. The mashayikh specify the amount: one month's provision for a merchant, one day's for a daily worker. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) to prevent hardship.

Key Terms
نذر (nadhr): A vow to perform an act of worship (like charity) if a condition is met or unconditionally.
مال (mal): Property that is typically subject to zakah: gold, silver, trade goods, grazing livestock, and agricultural produce.
ملک (milk): Possession or ownership, including non-tangible rights like marital rights (nikaah), retaliation (qisas), and rights to property like wine (khamr) which is not considered mal.

Key Takeaway
The scope of a charity vow depends on the wording: 'mali' limits to zakah-eligible property, while 'milki' includes all possessions, with a concession to retain necessary expenses.
