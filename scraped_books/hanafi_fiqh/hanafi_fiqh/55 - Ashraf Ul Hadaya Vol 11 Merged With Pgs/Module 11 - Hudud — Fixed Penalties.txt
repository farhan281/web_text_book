Bribery to Repel Injustice: Hanafi Dispensation
Passage discusses the permissibility of bribery to repel injustice, clarifying the scope of the hadith cursing the bribe-giver and taker.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on giving bribery to repel injustice, distinguishing between the legal status of the giver and taker, and justify it using the principle of necessity (darurah) and textual evidence.

Core Idea
Bribery to repel injustice is permissible for the giver; the taker is sinful — necessity overrides the general prohibition.

This lesson examines a nuanced Hanafi ruling on bribery (rishwah). While a well-known hadith curses both the bribe-giver and taker, the Hanafi school permits giving bribery when the sole intent is to repel an act of injustice or harm. This dispensation is grounded in the principle that necessity permits otherwise prohibited acts, supported by narrations from the early scholars. We will walk through the evidence, the restriction of the general hadith, and the practical fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Legal Question
The passage addresses the act of giving wealth (rishwah) to a person in authority to repel an injustice. The question is: does the general hadith cursing the bribe-giver and taker prohibit this absolutely?
The hadith is considered general (amm) in its wording.
2
Examine the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response restricts the hadith to cases where bribery causes pure harm to a rightful person (e.g., bribing a judge to exclude an heir). If the sole intention is to repel harm from oneself or one's ward, it is permissible.
This restriction is based on the legal maxim that necessity permits prohibitions (al-darurat tubih al-mahzurat).
3
Analyze the Proofs and Narrations
The passage cites a narration from Imam Abu Yusuf allowing the guardian of an orphan to give bribery from the orphan's wealth to repel harm, and a narration from Imam Muhammad in al-Siyar al-Kabir about Ibn Mas'ud giving bribery in Abyssinia. These serve as practical evidence.
The fatwa is said to be upon Abu Yusuf's narration.
4
Determine the Fiqh Ruling for Giver and Taker
For the giver, the act is permissible (mubah) when done to repel injustice. For the taker, it is makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked) because he is receiving wealth unjustly.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
5
Understand the Scope and Limitations
The dispensation applies only when the bribery is the only means to repel a genuine, severe harm. It does not extend to cases where the bribe causes harm to another or is used to gain an unfair advantage.
The textual basis includes the Quranic verse 'And He has not placed upon you in the religion any hardship' (22:78).

Sulh in Accidental Injury: Conditions and Limits
Ruling on sulh in accidental injury with conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid sulh in accidental injury and identify when increase beyond diyah is prohibited.

Core Idea
Sulh in accidental injury is like a sale but cannot exceed the fixed diyah amount.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on settlement (sulh) for accidental injury (jinayah khatā'). Since the only legal consequence is financial compensation (diyah), sulh resembles a sale. However, diyah is a fixed Sharia measure, so any increase in the prescribed amount is invalid unless it is for a different type of property. Immediate possession is also required to avoid the forbidden exchange of debt for debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal nature of accidental injury
The text states that accidental injury only imposes a financial obligation (diyah), not qisas. So the basis is property.
This distinguishes it from intentional injury, where qisas is possible.
2
Apply the sale analogy
Since it is a property claim, sulh functions like a sale. The consideration must be clearly defined and exchange is immediate.
Sale principles apply, including the need for immediate exchange.
3
Recognize the prohibition of increase over diyah
The diyah amount is fixed by Sharia (muqaddar shar‘an). Increasing it would nullify that fixed measure, so the excess must be returned.
This prohibition does not apply when sulh is made for qisas, because qisas is not property.
4
Understand the condition of immediate possession
When sulh is made for non-prescribed items, possession must be taken in the session to avoid the forbidden sale of debt for debt (bay‘ al-dayn bi al-dayn).
If a judge has already decreed a specific amount, this condition is relaxed because the debt becomes specified.

Commentary
This passage covers two main rulings. First, regarding sulh in accidental injury (jinayah khat'): since the legal consequence is solely financial (diyah), sulh is like a sale. As such, the consideration cannot exceed the fixed diyah amounts (1000 dinars, 10000 dirhams, 200 cows, etc.) because diyah is a fixed legal penalty. However, if the sulh is made for items other than these prescribed types, it is permissible, but immediate possession (qabd) in the session is required to avoid the forbidden sale of debt for debt (bay' al-dayn bi al-dayn). If a judge has already decreed a specific amount, sulh for a different amount with increase is permissible because the debt is now specified. Without a judge's decree, the parties' agreement on a specific amount functions like a judgment, so increasing it is not allowed. Second, the passage prohibits sulh on claims of fixed punishments (hudud) because they are rights of Allah; one cannot take compensation to drop them. This also applies to a woman claiming her child's lineage (nasab) because it is the child's right, not hers, and to encroachments on public property like roads. Hadd of qadhf is included because it is predominantly a right of Allah.

دیت (diyah): Blood money, a fixed financial compensation for homicide or injury, prescribed by Sharia as 1000 dinars, 10000 dirhams, or 200 cows etc.

Prohibition of Sulh on Hadd and Lineage Claims
Prohibition of sulh on hadd, lineage, and public rights.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why sulh is invalid in hadd claims and lineage disputes, and identify cases where the right belongs to Allah or a third party.

Core Idea
Sulh is prohibited in hadd and lineage claims because they involve rights of Allah or others, not personal property rights.

This lesson covers the second major ruling: absolute prohibition of sulh on claims of fixed punishments (hudud), lineage (nasab), and public rights. The reasoning is that these are rights of Allah (haqq Allah) or rights of a third party; one cannot take compensation to forgo something that is not one's own property. The hadd of false accusation (qadhf) is included because the Shari‘ah’s right predominates.

1
Identify the type of claim
The text lists claims of hadd, lineage (when a woman claims her child's lineage), and public road encroachment.
These are distinct from financial claims.
2
Classify the right involved
Hadd is a right of Allah (haqq Allah). Lineage is the right of the child, not the mother. Public road is the right of the public.
Rights of Allah and third parties cannot be sold or compromised.
3
Apply the rule: no compensation for another's right
Sulh involves giving compensation to drop a claim. If the right is not the claimant's own property, such compensation is invalid (bāṭil).
The claimant cannot take money to waive a right that does not belong to them.
4
List the examples provided
Examples: (1) sulh on a hadd claim (e.g., theft, false accusation), (2) a woman taking compensation to drop a child's lineage claim, (3) sulh on an encroachment on a public road, (4) the hadd of qadhf is included because the Shari‘ah’s right is dominant.
Each example illustrates the same principle.

Commentary
This passage covers two main rulings. First, regarding sulh in accidental injury (jinayah khat'): since the legal consequence is solely financial (diyah), sulh is like a sale. As such, the consideration cannot exceed the fixed diyah amounts (1000 dinars, 10000 dirhams, 200 cows, etc.) because diyah is a fixed legal penalty. However, if the sulh is made for items other than these prescribed types, it is permissible, but immediate possession (qabd) in the session is required to avoid the forbidden sale of debt for debt (bay' al-dayn bi al-dayn). If a judge has already decreed a specific amount, sulh for a different amount with increase is permissible because the debt is now specified. Without a judge's decree, the parties' agreement on a specific amount functions like a judgment, so increasing it is not allowed. Second, the passage prohibits sulh on claims of fixed punishments (hudud) because they are rights of Allah; one cannot take compensation to drop them. This also applies to a woman claiming her child's lineage (nasab) because it is the child's right, not hers, and to encroachments on public property like roads. Hadd of qadhf is included because it is predominantly a right of Allah.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): A fixed punishment prescribed by Sharia for certain crimes (e.g., theft, adultery, false accusation).
قذف (qadhf): False accusation of adultery; a hadd crime.
نسب (nasab): Lineage; the genealogical relationship of a child to parents.

Invalid Sulh for Hudud and Third-Party Rights
Ruling on invalid sulh for hudud, lineage, and public roads

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of rights for which sulh is invalid and apply the principle that sulh requires the right to belong to the claimant.

Core Idea
Sulh is invalid when the right belongs to Allah or a third party.

Sulh (compromise) is a valid way to settle financial disputes, but it has limits. The classical Hanafi texts teach us that sulh cannot be used to drop fixed punishments (hudud) because they are rights of Allah. Nor can it be used to give up rights that belong to others, like a child's lineage or public pathways. This lesson examines these boundaries through the lens of al-Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its exceptions
Read the passage to find the general rule: sulh is invalid for 'rights of Allah' and 'rights of others'. Note the three specific examples given.
The three examples are hudud, child's lineage, and public roads.
2
Understand the reasoning for hudud
Hudud are rights of Allah; no individual has the authority to drop them by accepting compensation. Even if a person apprehends a criminal, they cannot take money to release him.
This includes hadd for zina, theft, wine drinking, and qadhf.
3
Analyze the child's lineage case
When a mother claims lineage and makes a compromise with the husband, it is invalid because lineage is the child's right, not the mother's. She cannot drop it.
This is based on a narration from Imam Abu Yusuf.
4
Examine the public road case
A public road is a collective right; no individual can make sulh to allow encroachment or obstruction.
The right belongs to the entire community.
5
Explore the ikhtilaf on qadhf
The passage includes qadhf in the ruling. Imam Shafi'i holds the servant's right is dominant, but even then it is non-financial, so taking compensation is invalid.
This highlights the Hanafi principle that non-financial rights cannot be settled by sulh.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling that sulh (compromise) is invalid when the right in question belongs to someone other than the claimant. Three examples are given: (1) Fixed penalties (hudud) such as those for zina, theft, and drinking wine, because these are rights of Allah; no individual can take compensation to drop them. (2) A mother claiming the lineage of her child, because lineage is the child's right; she cannot make sulh regarding it, even with compensation, as it is not her right to drop. (3) Structures built on a public road, because the road is a public right; no single person can make sulh for it. The passage also includes the hadd of qadhf (false accusation of zina) in this ruling because, although it has a personal element, the right of the servant is dominant, but since it is non-financial, compensation cannot be taken. The commentary clarifies that if someone captures a criminal and accepts money to release him, the sulh is invalid and the money must be returned. If a woman claims lineage and the husband makes sulh to avoid the claim, it is invalid because the child's right is involved. This is based on a narration from Imam Abu Yusuf. The ruling is clear: sulh is only valid for financial rights that belong to the claimant.

صلح (sulh): Amicable settlement or compromise to end a dispute.
