Sulh in Intentional Killing: Valid Consideration and Analogy to Marriage
Ruling with proof by analogy on valid consideration in sulh for intentional killing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh in qatl amd, explain the analogy to nikah regarding valid consideration, and distinguish the consequence of invalid consideration between sulh and marriage.

Core Idea
Sulh in qatl amd is like marriage: valid property can be consideration; if invalid, sulh becomes forgiveness but diyah remains, unlike marriage where mahr mithl is due.

This passage uses an analogy between sulh (compromise) in intentional killing and marriage (nikah) to determine what constitutes valid consideration. In both contracts, any valuable property can serve as consideration. However, if the consideration is legally worthless (like wine), the two contracts diverge: in marriage, the wife automatically gets mahr al-mithl; in sulh, the victim's family gets nothing from the sulh but can still claim diyah as the original right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? What is its hukm? The act is sulh (compromise) in intentional killing (qatl amd). The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz).
The text states 'Sulh in intentional killing is like marriage' indicating permissibility.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof is cited? The proof is qiyas (analogy) to marriage. The shared characteristic is that both are 'exchange of property without property' (mubadala mal bi ghir mal).
The text says 'because both marriage and sulh are an exchange of property without property'.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does this analogy prove the ruling? In marriage, any valuable property can be mahr. By analogy, any valuable property can be badal al-sulh. The illa is the nature of the contract as a non-property exchange.
The text says 'whatever can be mahr in marriage can be the consideration in sulh here'.
4
Note the ikhtilaf on invalid consideration
Does the analogy hold for invalid consideration? The text shows a divergence: in marriage, invalid mahr triggers mahr mithl; in sulh, invalid consideration makes it pure forgiveness and diyah remains claimable. This limits the analogy to valid consideration.
The text contrasts: 'contrary to marriage, where if mahr is invalid or wine, mahr mithl is obligatory'.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Which position is mufta bihi? The Hanafi position is that sulh on invalid consideration (e.g., wine) results in forgiveness (no property due), but the family can still demand diyah. This is the relied-upon ruling.
The text states 'if one makes sulh on wine, then nothing is obligatory... pure forgiveness'.

His saying: It is like marriage. Sulh in intentional killing is like marriage, such that whatever can be mahr in marriage can be the consideration in sulh here, because both marriage and sulh are an exchange of property without property. But the point is that if the property agreed upon in sulh has some defect, then recourse will be made to diyah, because the actual compensation for killing was diyah. And if in intentional killing one makes sulh on wine, then nothing is obligatory, because in absolute forgiveness nothing is obligatory. That is, since wine is not a valuable property, its mention and non-mention are equal. So it becomes pure forgiveness, in which property is not obligatory. Contrary to marriage, where if the mahr is invalid or is wine, in both cases mahr misl is obligatory, because in marriage mahr misl is the original obligation, and even if there is complete silence, still mahr misl is obligatory by legal implication.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof by analogy. The author explains that sulh in intentional killing (qatl amd) is analogous to marriage (nikah) in terms of permissible consideration. Just as in marriage any valuable property can be mahr, in sulh any valuable property can be the consideration (badal al-sulh). However, if the consideration is invalid (e.g., wine), the sulh is void and the original right to qisas or diyah remains. In intentional killing, if the consideration is invalid, the victim's family reverts to claiming diyah (blood money). If sulh was made on wine, since wine is not legally valuable, it is as if no consideration was specified, thus it becomes pure forgiveness ('afw) which does not entitle them to any property. This differs from marriage: if the mahr is invalid or is wine, mahr al-mithl (equivalent mahr) becomes obligatory because marriage automatically entails a financial right for the wife. The ruling is based on the principle that marriage is a contract that inherently requires a dower, whereas sulh in qatl amd is a waiver of qisas in exchange for property; if the property is void, the waiver may still be valid but no property is due, reverting to the original right of the victim's family to demand qisas or diyah.

Key Terms
صلح (sulh): A compromise or settlement in lieu of a claim, here specifically in exchange for waiving qisas or diyah.
مہر مثل (mahr mithl): The equivalent dower determined by the dower of women of similar status.
بدل صلح (badal al-sulh): The consideration given in a sulh agreement.

Sulh by a Ma'dhun Slave for Murder
Ruling on sulh by a trade-permitted slave in murder cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a ma'dhun slave can make sulh in murder cases and explain the qiyas reasoning distinguishing between saving himself and saving his slave.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun slave may ransom his slave's life but not his own: the first is trade, the second is not.

This passage from Al-Hidayah examines the capacity of a slave permitted to trade (ma'dhun) when faced with murder liability. The key distinction turns on whether the act falls within the scope of his trade permission—ransoming another slave is like purchase, but ransoming himself is outside commerce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage deals with two distinct cases: (1) the ma'dhun slave himself commits murder, and (2) a slave owned by the ma'dhun slave commits murder. Note the different rulings for each.
2
State the ruling for each
For scenario 1: sulh to save his own life is not permissible (ghayr ja'iz). For scenario 2: sulh to save his slave's life is permissible (ja'iz). Commit these to memory.
The permissibility for the slave's case is subject to the condition that the murder was intentional.
3
Understand the underlying principle
The ma'dhun slave's permission is limited to trade (al-tijarah). Sulh for his own life is not a trade transaction, so it falls outside his authority. Sulh for his slave's life is analogous to purchasing the slave, which is within trade.
The text explicitly states: 'his master has permitted him only for trade, and this sulh is not from the chapter of trade.'
4
Follow the qiyas reasoning
The text uses qiyas: rescuing (istikhlaṣ) the killer slave by paying sulh is like purchasing him. Since the ma'dhun slave can buy slaves for trade, he can also make this sulh. The effective cause (illa) is that the killer slave becomes a commodity once the right to qisas passes to the heirs.
This is qiyas jali (clear analogy).
5
Note the exception for self-sulh
Even if the ma'dhun slave makes sulh for himself without permission, the sulh is effective: qisas is dropped, but the payment becomes a debt collectible only after his emancipation. This shows the contract is not void, but the slave is not held liable while still enslaved.
The text adds a parenthetical clarification: 'if he did make sulh, the qisas is dropped and the demand for the sulh payment will be after his emancipation.'

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the capacity of a ma'dhun slave (a slave permitted by his master to engage in trade) to make sulh in cases of murder. The text first states that if the ma'dhun slave himself commits murder, he cannot make sulh with the heirs to save his own life, because his permission is limited to commercial transactions (bāb al-tijārah), and sulh to save one's life is not a trade. However, if he does make such sulh, the qisas (retribution) is dropped, but the payment becomes a debt on him that will be claimed after his emancipation. In contrast, if a slave owned by the ma'dhun slave (i.e., his own slave) commits intentional murder, the ma'dhun slave is allowed to make sulh on that slave's behalf. The reasoning is that ransoming the killer slave is analogous to purchasing that slave: the killer slave's right to qisas passes to the heirs, so he effectively becomes like a commodity that can be bought. Since the ma'dhun slave is permitted to purchase slaves for trade, he may also make sulh to 'buy' his slave's life. This is qiyas jali: the effective cause is the ability to purchase. The distinction is that the ma'dhun slave cannot 'purchase' himself because that would be self-purchase, which is not valid for a slave.

Key Terms
عبد مأذون ('abd ma'dhūn): A slave given explicit permission by his master to engage in trade and commercial transactions.
استخلاص (istikhlaṣ): Rescuing or ransoming; in this context, paying sulh to free a slave from qisas.
