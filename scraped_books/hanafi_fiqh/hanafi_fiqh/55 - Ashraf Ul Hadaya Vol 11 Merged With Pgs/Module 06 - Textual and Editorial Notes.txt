The Binding Force of Iqrar: House and Courtyard
Ruling on iqrar of house and courtyard

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a person confesses a house building for himself and the courtyard for another, and explain why the iqrar is binding.

Core Idea
Iqrar of property ownership is binding: if you declare a part belongs to another, it becomes theirs.

This lesson examines a straightforward ruling from the chapter of iqrar (confession). The passage demonstrates how a person's own declaration against his interest can transfer ownership rights. Understanding this helps you grasp the binding force of iqrar in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the declaration
Read the passage: the declarant says that the house building is his and the courtyard is for another person. This is a clear iqrar of ownership for each part.
The declaration must be explicit and against the declarant's own interest (courtyard goes to another).
2
Understand the effect of iqrar
Iqrar is a binding proof against the declarant. The declarant's statement alone is sufficient to transfer the right to the other person, without requiring additional proof from the claimant.
The ruling directly follows from the principle that iqrar is binding.
3
Apply the ruling to similar cases
The principle is general: any iqrar of specific property (or part thereof) to another person is valid and effective, as long as the conditions of iqrar are met.
Conditions include legal capacity, free will, and clarity of the subject matter.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Dividing a Property Between Two Claimants
Intra-Hanafi dispute on judicial partition with equal evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of munaza'ah and contrast it with the Sahibayn's method of 'awl to resolve a property dispute between two claimants with equal evidence.

Core Idea
When evidence is equal, Abu Hanifa divides by awarding uncontested portions; the Sahibayn treat claimants as partners proportional to their claims.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about dividing a property when one claimant proves full ownership and another proves half ownership. Both produce valid evidence, so the court must decide how to divide. Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) propose different methods, leading to different shares. Understanding this dispute reveals deeper principles about proof strength and judicial partition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimants and their claims
Two claimants: one claims full ownership (the whole property), the other claims half ownership. Both present evidence (witnesses).
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's method: munaza'ah
The half claimant does not contest the full claimant's ownership of half the property. That half is awarded to the full claimant without contest. The remaining half is contested equally, so it is split equally between them.
Result: full claimant gets 3/4, half claimant gets 1/4.
3
Understand the Sahibayn's method: 'awl and mudarabah
They treat the claimants as partners proportional to their claims. The full claimant's share is 1 (the whole) and the half claimant's share is 1/2. Using 'awl (proportional reduction) as in inheritance, the total shares become 1 + 0.5 = 1.5, so the full claimant gets 1/1.5 = 2/3 and the half claimant gets 0.5/1.5 = 1/3.
Result: full claimant gets 2/3, half claimant gets 1/3.
4
Compute the resulting shares
Calculate the fractions under each method.
Abu Hanifa: 3/4 vs 1/4. Sahibayn: 2/3 vs 1/3.
5
Examine the variant when both are in possession
If the property is in the joint possession of both claimants, the full claimant gets half by judgment (the half in the other's possession) and half without judgment (the half already in his own possession). The half claimant has no claim on the portion in the full claimant's possession.
This variant shows that possession affects the application of the method.

Commentary
This passage from the Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding two claimants for a property: one claims full ownership, the other claims half. Both produce evidence. Abu Hanifa's method (tariq al-munaza'ah) gives the half-claimed portion as uncontested to the full claimant, leaving only the remaining half contested, which is divided equally—resulting in 3/4 for the full claimant and 1/4 for the half claimant. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) use the method of 'awl (proportional adjustment) and muḍārabah (as in inheritance), allocating shares proportional to their claims: the full claimant gets 2/3, the half claimant gets 1/3. The text then addresses a variant: if both claimants have possession of the property, the full claimant's evidence prevails for the half he does not possess (since he is an outsider), and his own possessed half remains with him without judgment. The Urdu commentary elaborates on the principles, including a discussion that greater number of witnesses does not give preference (unlike Shafi'i or Maliki views) because each pair of witnesses constitutes a complete proof. Preference is based on strength of the proof, not quantity. The commentary also lists four types of division scenarios: (1) by universal agreement using 'awl (8 cases), (2) by universal agreement using munaza'ah (case of two fudulis), (3) where Abu Hanifa uses munaza'ah and the Sahibayn use 'awl (this case plus two others), and (4) where Abu Hanifa uses 'awl and the Sahibayn use munaza'ah (five cases). The division methods are applied to various fiqh issues like inheritance, debts, wills, and similar matters.

Key Terms
مُنَازَعَة (munāza‘ah): Mutual contest or dispute; a method of dividing property by awarding uncontested portions first and splitting the contested portion equally.
عَوْل (‘awl): Proportional reduction of shares when the total of prescribed shares exceeds the fund; here applied analogously to divide property between claimants proportionally.
مُضَارَبَة (muḍārabah): Here used as a method of sharing or partnership, treating the claimants as partners with shares proportional to their claims.

Resolving Animal Possession Disputes with Conflicting Dates
Ruling on resolving animal possession when dates match age

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for preferring one claimant over another when the animal's age corroborates one birth date, and state the outcomes when the age is ambiguous or contradicts both dates.

Core Idea
The animal's actual age acts as circumstantial evidence to prefer one claimant's date over another.

When two claimants each present evidence that an animal was born in their possession, with different dates, the judge turns to the animal's observable condition (ḥāl) to break the tie. The Hanafi school uses this physical evidence as a form of qarīnah to weigh conflicting testimonies. This lesson walks through the three possible scenarios and their rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and the evidence
Two claimants each have a bayyinah (proof) that the animal was born in their possession, and they mention specific dates.
Bayyinah typically means two male witnesses in Hanafi law.
2
Observe the animal's actual age
The judge estimates the animal's age (e.g., by teeth, size) as a matter of ḥāl (current state).
The age is a physical fact that can corroborate or refute the dates.
3
Apply the ruling based on the age's relation to the dates
Scenario 1: Age matches one date → that claimant is preferred. Scenario 2: Age ambiguous (cannot clearly match either) → animal is shared equally. Scenario 3: Age contradicts both dates → both bayyinahs are rejected, animal stays with the current possessor.
In Scenario 3, the falsehood of both claims becomes apparent.
4
Understand the underlying legal reasoning
The ḥāl serves as circumstantial evidence (qarīnah) that strengthens one claim, analogous to preferring a witness whose testimony is externally corroborated.
This is a rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī) based on qiyās.

Two Claimants for a Slave: Usurpation vs Deposit
Ruling on conflicting claims of usurpation and deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two claimants present conflicting evidence of ghasb and wadi'ah for a slave and explain why the object is shared equally.

Core Idea
Equal evidence leads to equal division, regardless of the cause of claim.

This lesson examines a classic case where two claimants each present valid evidence but for different legal causes—one claims the slave was wrongfully taken (ghasb), the other claims it was entrusted as a deposit (wadi'ah). The Hanafi school treats the two claims as equal in probative weight and rules that the slave is shared equally. Understanding this ruling clarifies the principle of evidentiary balance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the possessor and the two claimants
The passage begins: 'If a slave is in the hand of a man'—this is a third party possessor. Two men then come forward, each claiming an interest in the slave.
Possessor is not a claimant; he is the object of the claims.
2
Understand the nature of each claim
One claimant says the slave was taken from him by ghasb (usurpation), meaning he owns it and it was wrongfully taken. The other says he gave the slave as wadi'ah (deposit) to the possessor, meaning he owns it and entrusted it.
Ghasb is a tort; wadi'ah is a trust.
3
Note that each claimant establishes evidence
The text says 'two men establish evidence against him (the possessor)'. Each claimant brings a valid bayyinah (proof).
Evidence is assumed to be equally strong.
4
Recognize the equality of the two pieces of evidence
Since both claims are supported by evidence, and there is no factor to prefer one over the other, the evidences are considered equal in weight.
No circumstantial advantage is mentioned.
5
Derive the ruling: equal division
Because the two pieces of evidence are equal, the slave is divided equally between the two claimants. The possessor's own claim (if any) is not considered.
The result is a 50/50 division.

Claiming Paternity of One Twin Establishes Paternity of Both
Ruling on twin slaves: claiming one child establishes lineage and proves both are free-born.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that claiming paternity of one twin automatically establishes paternity and freeborn status of both twins, and explain the legal reasoning based on shared conception.

Core Idea
Claiming one twin proves both are free-born because twins share a single conception (ʿulooq).

In Hanafi fiqh, a crucial principle regarding paternity claims on slave twins is that if a seller claims paternity of one twin still in his possession, both twins are deemed his free-born sons, invalidating any prior sale and manumission. This ruling stems from the fact that twins are conceived from a single act, so establishing lineage for one necessarily establishes it for the other. The passage from Al-Hidayah, citing Al-Jami' al-Saghir, explains this with careful conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the specific scenario? A man sells one of two twin slave boys, the buyer frees him, then the seller claims paternity of the other twin in his possession. The ruling: both are the seller's sons, and the buyer's manumission is void.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the necessary connection between twins due to common conception (ʿulooq). The text says 'because they are twins' (li-annahumā tawʾamān), indicating a logical necessity.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Establishing the lineage of the claimed child (through valid paternity claim) proves his original freedom (ḥurriyyat al-aṣl). Since twins share the same conception, the original freedom necessarily applies to the other twin as well. Thus the buyer's transaction on that twin was on a free person, rendering it void.
4
Note the exception
If the conception (ʿulooq) did not occur in the seller's ownership, then only the lineage of the child in his possession is established, but the sale of the other twin remains valid, because the claim lacks evidence of connection during ownership.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning the claim of lineage (nasab) for twin slaves. The base ruling: if a man sells one of two twin slaves and the buyer frees him, then the seller (original owner) claims the other twin (still in his possession) is his son, then both are his sons and the buyer's manumission is void. The reasoning: when the lineage of the child in the seller's possession is established because the cohabitation (that led to conception) occurred while he owned the mother and his claim is valid, then the original freedom (hurriyyat al-asl) of that child is proven. Since twins share the same conception, the lineage and original freedom of the other twin follow out of necessity. Therefore, the buyer's purchase and manumission were actually transactions on a free person by origin (hurr al-asl), so they are void. This differs from the case of a single child where the manumission is intentionally void due to the seller's claim. Here, the voidness of the buyer's action is secondary to the original freedom. Finally, if the cohabitation did not occur in his ownership, then only the child in his possession gets his lineage, but the sale is not invalidated because it is a claim of freedom without evidence of connection during his ownership; thus it is restricted to his authority. علوق ('ulooq): Conception; the moment the child is conceived (the joining of sperm and egg). The passage refers to it occurring in the owner's possession.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Prior Iqrar of Paternity Bars Later Self-Claim
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and two disciples on the effect of a claim of paternity after earlier naming the child as a slave's son.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the effect of a prior acknowledgment of paternity on a later claim of lineage, and distinguish Abu Hanifa's view from that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Lineage once established cannot be negated; the earlier iqrar binds, preventing a later self-claim.

This lesson examines a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school. A man first declares a child is the son of his absent slave, then later claims the child as his own. The slave denies paternity. Does the later claim override the earlier one? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, diverge. Understanding their arguments reveals core principles about the finality of lineage and the binding nature of acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling: the child is never the master's son, even if the slave denies. The earlier acknowledgment fixes lineage with the slave.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that if the slave denies, the master's later claim makes the child his son.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the Sahibayn's reasoning: an iqrar is revoked by the slave's denial, becoming void ab initio. They analogize to cases where duress/jest affect iqrar and to wala' transfers.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
See Abu Hanifa's counter: lineage, once established, cannot be nullified. The iqrar of lineage is not withdrawn by denial. The child's right is attached permanently.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the child remains attributed to the slave (slave status); under Sahibayn, if slave denies, child becomes the master's son and free.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), regarding the effect of a prior acknowledgment of paternity when followed by a self-claim. The case: A man has a child in his custody. He first says, 'This is the son of my slave so-and-so (who is absent).' Then he later says, 'This is my son.' Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the child is never his son, even if the absent slave denies paternity. The Sahibayn say: If the slave denies, then the child becomes the master's son. The same dispute applies if the man first says the child is the son of another man (born on his bed) and then claims the child for himself. The Sahibayn argue that an acknowledgment (iqrar) of paternity is revocable (murtadd) by the denial of the acknowledged person (the slave), making the acknowledgment as if it never existed. They support this by analogizing to cases where duress or jest affect iqrar, and to the case of wala' (clientage) where an acknowledgment can be negated by a stronger claim. Abu Hanifa argues that lineage (nasab), once established, cannot be negated (la yahtamil al-naqd). An iqrar of lineage is not revoked by denial; thus the child's lineage remains with the slave, and the master cannot later claim the child as his own. He compares to a witness who testifies about someone's lineage and is rejected for suspicion, then later claims the lineage for himself—he cannot. Additionally, the right of the acknowledged person (the slave) is attached subject to his affirmation, so if he later affirms, the lineage is established from him; but the child's right is also attached, so it persists. The passage concludes by noting that the issue of wala' follows this same difference, and explains that wala' can be invalidated by a stronger conflicting claim, unlike lineage.

رد (radd): Denial or rejection; the slave's denial of being the father of the child.
ولاء (wala'): Clientage; the bond of loyalty and inheritance between a freed slave and his former master.

Ruling on Disputed Term in Acknowledgment of Deferred Debt
Ruling on acknowledgment of deferred debt with dispute over term.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a debtor acknowledges a deferred debt but the creditor denies the term, and explain the qiyas used to derive it, including the oath procedure and the cross-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
In a disputed acknowledgment, the debtor's claim of a term is not accepted without proof, making the debt immediately due.

When a person acknowledges a debt that is supposedly deferred (mu'ajjal), but the creditor insists the debt was due immediately, a conflict arises. The Hanafi school resolves this by applying the principle that an acknowledgment against oneself is binding, and any claim of a right (like deferment) must be proven. This lesson examines the ruling, its analogy to a slave acknowledged as owned but claimed as rented, and the difference with other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
The debtor (muqirr) acknowledges a debt but claims it is deferred; the creditor (muqarr lahu) affirms the debt but denies the deferment. Identify the conflicting claims.
The creditor acknowledges the debt itself but disputes the term.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
According to the Hanafi school, the debt becomes immediately due (lazima halan). The debtor's claim of a term is not accepted without proof.
The ruling is based on the principle that one cannot claim a right against one's own admission.
3
Understand the qiyas (analogy)
The author analogizes to the case where a person acknowledges a slave in his possession but claims it is rented. In both cases, the claim of a right (deferment or rent) contradicts the apparent acknowledgment, so it is not accepted without proof.
The analogy highlights that the debtor is asserting a right for himself against his own admission, which shifts the burden of proof.
4
Note the exception for 'black dirhams'
The rule differs for describing black dirhams, because that relates to a description (sifa) of the debt, not a right. That case was covered in the chapter of surety.
This exception clarifies that only substantive claims (like deferment) trigger the rule, not mere descriptions.
5
Address the oath and cross-madhhab difference
The creditor is sworn that there was no deferment, because he is denying a right claimed against him. Imam Ahmad and some reports from al-Shafi'i hold that the debtor is sworn and the debt remains deferred.
The thamarah al-khilaf: who bears the oath and whether the debt is immediate or deferred.

Interpreting 'One Hundred and a Dirham': Istihsan vs Qiyas
Ruling on acknowledgment when debtor says 'Miah wa Dirham' or similar phrases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for acknowledging 'one hundred and a dirham' and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position by explaining the role of istihsan and custom.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, frequent custom overrides strict qiyas: 'one hundred and a dirham' means 101 dirhams, but 'one hundred and a cloth' requires explanation.

Imagine a debtor says, 'I owe you one hundred and a dirham.' Does he owe 101 dirhams, or is he saying he owes 100 dirhams plus a separate dirham? The ambiguity arises from the conjunctive 'waw' joining an ambiguous number with a specific unit. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi solution based on istihsan (juristic preference for custom) and contrasts it with the Shafi'i insistence on strict qiyas. By the end, you'll see how custom shapes legal interpretation in transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two acknowledged statements
First, note the two cases: (1) 'one hundred and a dirham' (مائة ودرهم) and (2) 'one hundred and a cloth' (مائة وثوب). The ruling for each differs in the Hanafi school. Understand the literal wording and the ambiguity: the number 'one hundred' is unspecified (مبهمة).
The governing principle is that the conjunctive waw does not by itself interpret the preceding number.
2
Understand the qiyas position (Shafi'i)
Al-Shafi'i and, in one narration, Imam Ahmad apply strict qiyas: in both cases, the hundred remains ambiguous, so the debtor must explain what the hundred refers to. Their reasoning: the waw is purely conjunctive, not explanatory, so it cannot remove the ambiguity.
This is the default rule in many areas of law when no custom intervenes.
3
Analyze the istihsan position (Hanafi)
The Hanafi school adopts istihsan, overriding qiyas based on custom. In frequent transactions (dirhams, dinars, measured/weighed items), repeating the unit with every number is cumbersome, so people drop the repetition. Thus, 'one hundred and a dirham' means 101 dirhams. For items not frequently transacted (e.g., cloth), no such custom exists, so the debtor must explain.
The key distinction is the frequency of the transaction (كثرة الاستعمال).
4
Apply the distinction to other phrases
The text extends the rule: if he says 'one hundred and three cloths', the plural 'cloths' (without waw) interprets both numbers, so all are cloths. But 'one hundred and two cloths' (with waw) follows the original rule: one cloth is due, and the debtor explains the hundred.
This shows the careful application of the custom principle to different wordings.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In a Hanafi court, for dirhams, the judge automatically rules 101 dirhams. Under Shafi'i, the judge would demand clarification from the debtor. This difference affects litigation and settlement.
The ikhtilaf is legitimate; both schools have sound reasoning.

Commentary
This section presents a dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the interpretation of ambiguous numeric statements in acknowledgments. The matn states: if a debtor says 'one hundred and a dirham,' the ruling is that all of it is dirhams, meaning 101 dirhams. If he says 'one hundred and a cloth,' only one cloth is due, and the debtor is asked to explain what the 'hundred' refers to (whether dirhams or cloths). According to qiyas, the first case should also require an explanation from the debtor, and al-Shafi'i holds this view. The reason is that 'one hundred' is ambiguous, and the conjunctive 'waw' does not serve as an interpretation. However, the Hanafi school adopts istihsan: in common usage, repeating the word 'dirham' with every number is cumbersome, so people suffice with mentioning it once after the numbers. This applies to items frequently subject to liability (dirhams, dinars, measured and weighed goods). For cloth and other items not measured or weighed, the original rule remains, and the debtor must explain. The text also discusses when two ambiguous numbers are mentioned (e.g., 'one hundred and three cloths'): since 'cloths' is mentioned without a conjunction, it serves as an interpretation for both numbers, so all is considered cloth. The Urdu commentary provides detailed explanation and notes that Imam Ahmad also follows the qiyas view in one narration.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): A juristic preference that overrides strict qiyas in favor of a more suitable ruling based on custom, necessity, or public interest.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning: applying the ruling of an original case to a new case due to a shared effective cause (illa).
مائة (mi'ah): The number one hundred; when used in acknowledgment without specification, it is ambiguous.
واو عاطفة (waw 'atifah): The conjunctive letter 'and', used to connect words; it does not have an interpretive function by itself.

Acknowledgment of Items in Containers: The Hanafi Principle
Rulings on acknowledgment of items in containers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on acknowledgment of items in containers and identify exceptions based on wording and the nature of the container.

Core Idea
An acknowledgment of a contained item includes the container, unless the container is not liable or the wording indicates removal.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a person acknowledges that they have wrongfully taken an item that is inside a container, the acknowledgment generally includes both the item and the container. However, this rule has important exceptions: if the container is not considered liable in ghasb (like a stable according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), or if the wording indicates removal (using 'min'), then only the item itself is due. This lesson walks through the various cases presented in Al-Hidayah to clarify the principle and its boundaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
For any acknowledgment of an item 'in' a container (dates in a basket), both are due because the container is a vessel for the item.
2
Recognize the exception for removal
If the wording uses 'min' (from), only the removed item is due; the container is not included.
3
Distinguish between liable and non-liable containers
Some containers like a stable are not considered part of the ghasb according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, so only the item inside is due.
4
Apply to composite items and custom
For items like a ring or sword, the name includes all components; for unusual containers like a dirham in a dirham, only one is due because it is not a container.

Commentary

Exception of Non-Thaman Items in Iqrār
Dispute on validity of excepting cloth from dirham debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on excepting a non-thaman item from a dirham debt and explain the reasoning (thaman requirement) vis-à-vis the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Istithnā' of a non-thaman item from a thaman debt is invalid in the Hanafi school because the excepted amount becomes unknown.

This passage presents a key dispute in the law of acknowledgment (iqrār) regarding the validity of an exception (istithnā') when the excepted item is of a different genus than the debt. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi emphasis on the quality of being 'thaman' (medium of exchange) for valid exceptions, a requirement not shared by the Shafi'i school. We will walk through the positions and their proofs.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad all rule the exception invalid, but with different reasoning: the Shaykhayn invalidate only non-thaman exceptions; Muhammad invalidates any different-genus exception.
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf exists on the scope.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position and proof
Al-Shafi'i holds the exception valid because both items are united in genus from the perspective of monetary value (māliyyah).
He considers monetary value as creating legal homogeneity.
3
Examine the Hanafi response
The Shaykhayn argue that homogeneity in being thaman is required; a garment is not thaman and cannot be measured against dirhams, so the excepted amount remains unknown (majhūl), rendering the istithnā' invalid.
Thaman items are those that serve as standard measures (gold, silver, measured/weighed goods).
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In a Hanafi court, the entire 100 dirhams are due; in a Shafi'i court, the debt is reduced by the value of the garment.
This shows the real-world impact of the ikhtilaf.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The position of the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.
This is presented as authoritative in the commentary.

Effect of 'In Shā' Allāh' on an Acknowledgment
Ruling on conditional exception via 'in shā' Allāh' in iqrār

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'in shā' Allāh' in iqrār and distinguish between ta'liq (suspension) and tājīl (deferment) in acknowledgments.

Core Idea
Saying 'in shā' Allāh' to an acknowledgment nullifies it; only deferment of time is valid.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common scenario: someone acknowledges a debt but adds 'if Allah wills' (in shā' Allāh). Is the acknowledgment binding? The Hanafi school rules that it is not, and the reasoning involves a critical distinction between suspending the obligation and merely deferring its due date. Understanding this distinction will help you apply the correct ruling when time references appear in acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is an acknowledgment (iqrār) of a right (ḥaqq) to which the phrase 'in shā' Allāh' is immediately attached. The ruling is that the acknowledgment does not bind the person (lā yalzamuhu al-iqrār).
The phrase must be connected (muttasil) to the acknowledgment; if separated, the ruling may differ.
2
Analyze the Two Possible Interpretations of 'In Shā' Allāh'
The author states that saying 'in shā' Allāh' is either an annulment (ibṭāl) or a suspension (ta‘līq). In both cases, the acknowledgment fails to bind: if it is an annulment, the acknowledgment is void; if it is a suspension, it is invalid because iqrār does not admit suspension by condition.
The analogy with divorce (talāq) is used: in divorce, conditional suspension is valid, but in iqrār it is not.
3
Distinguish Between Ta‘līq and Tājīl
The passage contrasts the invalid 'in shā' Allāh' with valid deferments like 'when I die' or 'at the beginning of the month'. These specify a future time for payment and are deferments (tājīl), not suspensions (ta‘līq). Deferment is valid in iqrār.
The key difference: ta‘līq makes the obligation contingent on an uncertain event; tājīl fixes a certain or knowable future date.
4
Apply the Consequence of Denial of the Term
If the claimant (muqarr lahu) denies the deferment, the debt becomes immediately due (ḥāl). This shows that the deferment is considered a statement about the timing, not a condition on the existence of the debt.
This reinforces that tājīl is a mere specification of the due date, not a true condition.

Acknowledgment of Wadi'ah vs Ghasb: Two Rulings
Two rulings on acknowledgment in property disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between two scenarios of acknowledgment (iqrar) in deposit vs usurpation disputes and state who bears liability based on who admits a cause of liability.

Core Idea
Whoever admits a cause of liability must prove exemption; the other's denial is believed.

This passage presents two contrasting procedural rulings when one person acknowledges receiving money but disputes the nature of the receipt as a deposit (wadi'ah) while the other claims it was usurpation (ghasb). The outcome hinges on whether the acknowledging person admitted an act that is itself a cause of liability. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the rules of evidence (bayyina) and burden of proof in property disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the Arabic text carefully. Note the first scenario: 'I took from you 1000 dirhams as a deposit.' The second: 'You gave me 1000 dirhams as a deposit.' The claims by the other party are identical: 'No, you usurped them.'
The difference is whether the acknowledging person admits to taking (which is an act of the taker) or receiving (which is an act of the giver).
2
Determine who admits a cause of liability
In the first scenario, admitting 'taking' (akhadha) is inherently an act of acquisition; in the absence of permission, it implies liability (damān). In the second, admitting 'giving' (dafa'a) is an act of the other party; the receiver's acknowledgment is of an act that is not inherently a cause of liability.
The cause of liability (sabab al-damān) in property law is unlawful taking (ghasb) or any acquisition without consent.
3
Apply the principle: who is the claimant and who is the denier
In the first scenario, the admitting person (A) acknowledges a cause of liability (taking) and then claims a defense (deposit/permission). The other (B) denies that defense. Since B is the denier of the defense, his statement is accepted (al-qaul qaul al-munkir). Thus A is liable. In the second, A acknowledges an act of B (giving) which is not a cause of liability. B claims a cause of liability (ghasb) which A denies. A is the denier of the liability, so his statement is accepted, and he is not liable.
The principle: The one who denies a claim is believed (with an oath). The one who asserts a claim must prove it.
4
Derive the rulings
First scenario: A is liable (dāmin). Second scenario: A is not liable. The reason is the same: the party who is the denier of the liability-causing fact is believed.
Memorize the rule: If A admits an act that normally creates liability, he must prove a legal excuse (e.g., deposit). If he admits an act that does not create liability, the claimant must prove liability.

The Legal Effect of Acknowledging 'Qabd' and 'Dafa'
Explanation that qabd is like akhdh and dafa like ita

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Acknowledging receipt does not imply liability; the cause matters.

In Hanafi fiqh, the words used in an acknowledgment can change the legal consequences. This passage clarifies that saying 'I received' (qabadtu) is equivalent to saying 'I took' (akhadhtu), and saying 'you gave' (dafa'ta) is equivalent to 'you gave' (a'tayta). The key is that acknowledging a giving does not automatically mean you have taken possession in a way that creates liability. We will explore the reasoning behind this.

Recognize that the passage equates 'qabd' with 'akhdh' and 'dafa' with 'ita'.

Acknowledgment of Possession: The Denier's Statement Prevails
Ruling of a deposit claim with acknowledgment of third-party possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a person acknowledges another's possession and then claims ownership against that person, and apply the principle of 'the statement of the denier is accepted' to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
Acknowledging another's possession shifts the burden of proof: the claimant must prove, otherwise the denier prevails.

The Hanafi school places great weight on acknowledgment of possession (yad). In this concise ruling, we see a practical application: Zayd says the money was a deposit with Bakr and he took it; Bakr says it is his. Because Zayd acknowledged Bakr's possession, Bakr becomes the denier and his statement is accepted. This lesson unpacks the logic and equips you to handle similar disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Zayd acknowledges that the money was in Bakr's possession as a deposit, and then claims it for himself. The ruling: Bakr takes the money.
The ruling is procedural, not substantive.
2
Locate the legal principle
The principle is 'he who acknowledges a fact favorable to another and then claims against it must prove his claim; otherwise the other's denial stands.' This is based on the Hanafi rule that the denier's statement is accepted (al-munkir qawluh).
The underlying maxim: al-umur bi-maqasidiha does not apply here; it's about burden of proof.
3

Zayd acknowledged Bakr's possession (yad) of the money, which is a fact favorable to Bakr. Then Zayd claims entitlement to the money. Bakr denies Zayd's claim. Since Bakr is the denier, his statement is accepted, and he keeps the money.
Zayd's current possession (having taken it from Bakr) does not override his prior acknowledgment.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: the ruling ensures that a person who admits another's prior possession cannot simply reclaim the item without proof, protecting the third party's rights.
This prevents abuse of the trust relationship.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Ijarah and Ariyah Acknowledgments

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Temporary possession in ijarah/ariyah does not indicate ownership; the owner's word stands.

This lesson examines a classic internal Hanafi dispute about conflicting claims between an owner and a user after a lease or loan. The core question: when each claims the item belongs to him, whose statement is legally accepted? The passage shows how Imam Abu Hanifa departs from strict analogy (qiyas) in favor of juristic preference (istihsan) based on the nature of temporary possession.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi positions
Identify the two positions: Abu Hanifa believes the owner's statement is accepted; Sahibayn believe the user's statement is accepted.
Both positions are within the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold the user's statement, arguing via qiyas on wadi'ah (custody).
Sahibayn use the established rule from wadi'ah.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Sahibayn analogize to wadi'ah where the person in possession (custodian) is believed. They claim the same should apply here.
The illa (effective cause) is possession (yad).

Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa counters with istihsan: possession in ijarah/ariyah is necessary (yad daruriyyah) — only to take usufruct. It does not indicate ownership, so the owner's statement is accepted.
The user's yad is limited to necessity; it is not proprietary.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If the owner's statement is accepted, he retains the item. If the user's, the user takes it. This is the practical difference.
Later fatwa prefers Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage is a continuation of the commentary on the Book of Settlement (Kitab al-Sulh) from Ashraf al-Hidayah. The author discusses various stratagems to make settlement valid when debts are involved. First, he presents a scenario where heirs stipulate that the settling heir (musalih) discharges the debtors from his share. This is valid because he becomes owner of the debt for the purpose of discharge. Then the text presents two stratagems mentioned by the author of Hidayah: either the other heirs pay out the settling heir's share as a gift, or they give him a loan equal to his share and settle on the rest, then appoint him to collect the loan. The preferred stratagem is to avoid loss to others. Then a major issue is discussed: when the estate consists only of tangible goods of the same genus (like wheat), and an heir settles for a measurable or weighable item. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Zahir al-Din Marghinani says it is invalid due to possibility of riba, because the substitute might be more or less than the actual share. Faqih Abu Ja'far al-Hindawani says it is valid because the doubt is remote (shubhat al-shabah). The author sides with the latter, citing al-Khaniyyah. Then another case: when the items are not even of the same genus but are unknown. Here there is an inter-madhhab dispute: the Shafi'i school says it is invalid due to ignorance, but the Hanafi position (and also Imam Ahmad) says it is valid because the ignorance does not lead to dispute, as the items are in possession. Finally, the ruling on settlement when the deceased's debt covers the estate: settlement is invalid because the estate hasn't transferred to heirs. If debt is less than estate, settlement should be postponed until debt is paid, but if done, later scholars deem it permissible. This passage illustrates the Hanafi school's nuanced approach to balancing contractual validity with the prohibition of riba and the rights of creditors.

حيلة (hila): A legal stratagem to achieve a permissible outcome while avoiding a prohibition, especially regarding riba.
شبتة الشبہہ (shubhat al-shabah): A remote doubt or a doubt about a doubt (doubt of resemblance), which is not considered legally relevant.

Wadi'ah is a Trust: No Liability Without Negligence
Ruling with proof: establishing wadi'ah as amanah and refuting objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a deposit (wadi'ah) is a trust (amanah), explain the proof from the hadith and necessity, and apply the condition that liability only arises with negligence or treachery.

Core Idea
A deposit is a trust, not a guarantee; the depository is not liable without negligence.

This passage from al-Hidayah establishes the foundational ruling on deposits (wadi'ah). The author declares that a deposit is a trust (amanah) in the hands of the depository; if the property is destroyed without his fault, he bears no liability. The proof is twofold: a direct hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the principle of necessity (hajah). The author also defends the authenticity of the hadith against criticism. In this lesson, we will dissect the ruling, its evidence, and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the author's claim: 'The deposit is a trust; if it perishes, the depository does not guarantee it.' This establishes the default rule of no liability.
The ruling is that wadi'ah is an amanah, not a daman.
2
Locate the Primary Proof
Find the hadith: 'There is no liability on the non-treacherous depository.' This is the textual basis (dalil naqli).
The hadith is from 'Amr ibn Shu'ayb's narration, recorded by al-Darqutni and al-Bayhaqi.

The author adds a rational argument: people need to make deposits; if depositories were always liable, they would refuse, harming social interests. This is an istihsan argument based on maslahah.

Address the Objection

This demonstrates how Hanafi jurists defend their proofs against hadith criticism.
5
Conclude the Position
The Hanafi position is firm: wadi'ah is an amanah; no liability without ta'addi (negligence/treachery). This is the mufta bihi (operative opinion) in the school.
The majority of fuqaha agree, making it a strong position.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It establishes the foundational principle of the law of deposit (wadi'ah) in the Hanafi school: a deposit is an amanah (trust), not a guarantee (daman). The depository (mūda') is not liable for loss or destruction of the deposited property unless he commits negligence or misconduct (ta'addi). The hadith is further supported by multiple corroborating narrations (shawāhid) from Ibn Majah, Darqutni's other narration, and reports from 'Ali and Ibn Mas'ud. Thus the ruling is firmly established.

Find the sentence in al-Hidayah that suggests 'Aariyah is derived from 'Ariyah, and understand why the author might have thought so.
The claim is implied: 'fainna al-‘ariyata min al-‘ariyyati' (indeed the 'Aariyah is from the 'Ariyah).

Examine Morphological Differences
Analyze the root letters and patterns. 'Aariyah is from the root ‘-w-r (hollow), while 'Ariyah is from the root ‘-r-w (deficient). Note how lexicographers classify them in different chapters.
Morphological pattern matters: ‘Aariyah is of the pattern fa‘ilah (active participle), while ‘Ariyah is fa‘īlah (adjective).

Check Correspondence in Letters and Order
Apply the conditions for small derivation (ishtiqaq saghir): full correspondence in letters, order, and meaning. Here letters differ (w vs r order) and order does not match.
Ishtiqaq saghir requires complete agreement in root consonants and sequence.

Consider Large Derivation Possibility
Even if we relax order requirement (ishtiqaq kabir), meaning correspondence is still necessary. The passage shows that the meanings are unrelated: 'Aariyah implies taking/giving, 'Ariyah implies coming.
Ishtiqaq kabir only requires same root letters (any order) and some meaning affinity. But here even that fails.

Apply the Distinction to Fiqh
Confirm that the two contracts have different legal effects: 'Aariyah is revocable, 'Ariyah is not. This practical difference reinforces the linguistic independence.

His saying: 'Indeed, al-'ariyah is from al-'ariyah...' — Upon this, Allamah Aini objected: If by that the author of al-Hidayah means it is derived, as Shaykh al-Ansari said, then that is not so because 'ariyah is hollow and wavy, therefore lexicographers mention it in the chapter of 'awr, while 'ariyah is deficient, its lām being a weak letter, therefore lexicographers mention it in the chapter of 'irw. Whereas derivation is that there is correspondence in letters, order, and meaning between the two words, like darb which is derived from daraba. If someone says this only applies in small derivation but in large derivation only verbal correspondence suffices and order correspondence is not necessary, like jund derived from jadhaba, the reply is that when derivation is mentioned absolutely, small derivation is meant. Moreover, here even large derivation is impossible due to lack of meaning correspondence, because the root of 'ariyah indicates taking and giving, while the root of 'ariyah indicates coming. It is said 'arawtu 'arwan meaning to intend to ask. And if the author of al-Hidayah means that the meaning of 'ariyah is taken from the meaning of 'ariyah, that is also false, because 'ariyah is a date-palm tree whose owner gave it to another for a year to eat the fruit. And if his aim is that the ruling of 'ariyah is like the ruling of 'ariyah, that also does not match, because the lender can take back the loaned item whenever he wishes, while the owner of the 'ariyah tree cannot do that (al-Binayah).

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of a statement in al-Hidayah suggesting that the word 'ariyah (loan) is derived from 'ariyah (a loaned date-palm). Allamah Aini refutes this on linguistic and semantic grounds. He explains that the two words belong to different morphological patterns and that the claimed derivation (ishtiqaq) is not valid because true derivation requires correspondence in letters, order, and meaning. Even if we consider large derivation (ishtiqaq kabir) which only requires letter correspondence, the meanings are unrelated: 'ariyah relates to taking/giving, while 'ariyah relates to coming. Therefore, the terms are independent. The passage also points out that if the intention was to analogize the rulings, that also fails because the lender in 'ariyah can reclaim the item anytime, unlike the owner of 'ariyah tree who cannot take back the tree during the year. This is a typical Hanafi focus on precise linguistic and legal distinction.

Key Terms
عاریت (ʿāriyah): A loan of a benefit (manfa'ah) for free, where the lender retains ownership of the item.
عریہ (ʿariyyah): A specific palm tree given to another to eat its fruit for one year.
اشتقاق صغیر (ishtiqāq ṣaghīr): Small derivation, where two words share all root letters and order, and also have a meaning correspondence.
اشتقاق کبیر (ishtiqāq kabīr): Large derivation, where two words share only the root letters (order may differ), and meaning correspondence is not required.

Linguistic Correction on 'Waha' in Hibah Context
Linguistic correction on the word 'waha'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the correct spelling and meaning of 'waha' as debated by Hanafi commentators and identify why this refinement matters for textual precision.

Core Idea
A single letter error can change the meaning of a word—Hanafi scholars debated 'waha' to preserve textual accuracy.

This passage, a tanbih from the commentary on Al-Hidayah, corrects a spelling error in the word 'waha' in the context of hibah. Several prominent Hanafi scholars, including Allama Sighnaqi, Sahib al-'Inaya, and Sahib al-Nata'ij, engage in a debate about the correct orthography and meaning. This lesson unpacks their arguments and shows why such linguistic precision is vital in the study of fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial claim
The passage begins with Allama Sighnaqi and others quoting Imam Matrazi that the word 'waha' with a 'dal' is an error; the correct is 'waha' with a 'waw' meaning weakness of a rope. Figure out what the base text likely had.
The base text probably read 'daha' (دھاء) which was corrected to 'waha' (وھاء).
2
Understand Sahib al-'Inaya's objection
He argues that a samaa'i masdar (a verbal noun heard from the Arabs) can be limited in its form, so the spelling with 'dal' might not be an error after all. Criticizing a non-error is itself an error.
Samaa'i masdar means it is based on usage, not strict analogy.
3
Follow Sahib al-Nata'ij's refutation
He claims Sahib al-'Inaya misunderstood. Evidence: Matrazi said 'waha al-jabal' meaning 'raha' (he saw the mountain), proving 'waha' is a verb like 'haya', not a masdar. If the intended meaning were the masdar, Matrazi would have said 'raha', not 'waha'.
Thus, Matrazi's usage indicates 'waha' as a verb, so the correction stands.
4
Conclude the correct reading
This debate shows that careful linguistic analysis can resolve apparent contradictions. The correct spelling is with 'waw' (وھاء) meaning weakness, not a verb form.
The correction ensures accurate transmission of the text.

Commentary
This passage is a textual note (tanbih) from the commentary, correcting the spelling and meaning of the word 'waha' in the context of hibah. Several Hanafi scholars, including Sighnaqi and others, reference Imam Matrazi's dictionary 'al-Mughrib' to argue that the correct spelling is with a 'waw' (وھاء) not a 'dal' (دھاء), as the meaning relates to weakness like a rope. This is a fine philological point, not a legal ruling, but it affects the precise reading of the base text.
