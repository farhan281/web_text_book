Rulings on Claim of Lineage (Da‘wā al-Nasab)
Fihrist entry on paternity claims and their effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on claims of lineage involving slave-girls, twins, absent slaves, and interfaith disputes, and identify the legal effects such as umm walad status, void sale, and freedom.

Core Idea
A claim of lineage can establish paternity, free a child, void a sale, and make a slave-girl an umm walad.

This passage from the Fihrist of Al-Hidayah summarizes key rulings from the chapter on claims of lineage (nasab). It covers scenarios involving seller and buyer of a slave-girl, twins, absent slaves, and disputes between a Muslim and a Christian. Understanding these rules is essential for grasping how paternity is established and its far-reaching effects in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the passage carefully and identify who is making the claim (e.g., seller, buyer, possessor) and the context (sale, birth, conflict).
The passage covers multiple distinct cases.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For each case, extract the Hanafi ruling: what happens to the child, the mother, the sale, or the conflicting claims.
Pay attention to the conditions like the six-month period.
3
Note differing opinions
Identify where Imam Shafi'i and Imam Zufar disagree (the case of a child dying before the claim). Understand the basis of their disagreement.
This is an example of legitimate ikhtilaf.
4
Recognize legal effects
For each ruling, note the legal effect: establishment of lineage, umm walad status, void sale, freedom of the child, or payment of value.
These effects are practical consequences.
5
Extract the underlying principle
Identify the general principle that governs these rulings, such as the importance of certainty in lineage and the protection of the child's status.
Hanafi fiqh prioritizes establishing lineage wherever possible.

Commentary
This passage summarizes rulings from the chapter on claims of lineage (nasab). Key points: If a seller claims the child of a slave-girl he sold, and she gives birth within six months, the child is his and she becomes umm walad (a mother of the master's child, entitled to freedom after his death). Imam Shafi'i and Imam Zufar differ if the child dies. If a sold slave is later claimed as the seller's son, the sale becomes void. Claiming one twin establishes lineage for both. Conflicting statements about a child being the son of an absent slave or the possessor's own son are resolved against the possessor. In a dispute between a Muslim and a Christian over a child, the Christian's claim of paternity prevails and the child is free. A woman's claim of maternity requires a female witness to birth. If a slave-girl is reclaimed from a buyer after giving birth, the father (original owner) must pay the child's value at the time of litigation. These illustrate the Hanafi rules on paternity and its legal effects.

Key Terms
ام ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne her master a child; she becomes free upon the master's death.
نسب (nasab): Lineage or paternity, established by birth or claim.

Lineage of Twins Established by Claim of One
Ruling that claiming lineage of one twin establishes lineage for both

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on establishing lineage for twins and apply it to a scenario where only one twin is claimed.

Core Idea
Claiming one twin's lineage establishes both twins' lineage because they share one pregnancy.

In family law, lineage (nasab) is fundamental for inheritance, guardianship, and marriage. A unique Hanafi rule concerns twins: if a father claims one twin as his child, the lineage of both twins is automatically established. This ruling is rooted in the principle that twins originate from the same pregnancy and thus share the same parentage. Understanding this rule helps clarify the legal status of twins in inheritance and other family matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Ruling
The act described is a father claiming lineage of one twin child. The ruling is that the lineage of both twins is established. Note that the claim is for one child only.
The ruling is unconditional—it applies regardless of the gender of the claimed twin.
2
Understand the Legal Basis
The basis for this ruling is the shared pregnancy. Since twins are born from the same conception and gestation, establishing lineage for one necessarily establishes it for the other. There is no need for a separate claim.
This principle is specific to the Hanafi school and may differ in other madhahib.
3
Identify Conditions for Application
The ruling applies only when the children are twins (born from the same pregnancy). Additionally, the claim must be made by the father (or the legal claimant of lineage).
If the children are not twins, each child's lineage must be claimed separately.
4
Apply to a New Scenario
Apply the ruling: if a man claims a twin daughter as his, what happens to her twin brother? Both become his children. Practice applying the rule to different scenarios to reinforce understanding.

Child in Joint Possession: Christian Claims Son, Muslim Claims Slave
Ruling on a child in joint possession claimed as son by Christian and slave by Muslim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a child is claimed as a son by a Christian and as a slave by a Muslim, and explain why lineage prevails over slavery.

Core Idea
Lineage and freedom prevail over claims of slavery in Hanafi law.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh where a child is under the joint physical control of a Muslim and a Christian. The Christian claims the child is his biological son, while the Muslim claims the child is his slave. The ruling establishes that the child is free and his lineage is attributed to the Christian, demonstrating the Hanafi principle that freedom is the default status and lineage takes precedence over property claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
Note that the child is in the possession (custody) of both a Muslim and a Christian. The Christian asserts paternity; the Muslim asserts ownership as a slave.
Possession here means physical control, not legal ownership.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The child is ruled to be the Christian's son and is free. He is not a slave to the Muslim.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
3
Understand the underlying principles
The ruling is based on two principles: (1) al-asl al-hurriyyah (freedom is the default status), and (2) lineage (nasab) is a stronger claim than proprietary ownership. The child's freedom is preserved, and his lineage is established with the claimant who asserts paternity.
Even though the Muslim has joint possession, his claim of slavery is not sufficient to overcome the Christian's claim of paternity.

Dispute over Marriage and Slave Purchase: Judicial Rulings
Judicial rulings for concurrent claims in marriage and sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi judicial rulings for concurrent claims in marriage and purchase, distinguishing between marriage (indivisible subject) and sale (divisible by option).

Core Idea
In marriage disputes, the woman's testimony decides; in purchase disputes, each claimant gets an option to share or rescind.

When two men each claim to have married the same woman, or each claim to have bought the same slave from the same seller, how does a judge resolve the conflict? This passage from al-Hidayah presents the Hanafi approach, balancing evidence, timing, and the unique nature of marriage as a non-shareable contract. The key principle is that the subject matter dictates the remedy: marriage cannot be shared, so the woman's word settles it; property can be shared, so each buyer gets a choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two distinct cases: (1) two men claiming marriage to the same woman, and (2) two men claiming to have bought the same slave from the same seller. Note the fundamental difference: marriage is indivisible, while ownership of a slave is divisible in value.
This difference drives the separate rulings.
2
Analyze the marriage scenario
When both men present evidence, neither is executed because the woman cannot be shared. The judge refers to the woman's confirmation (tasdiq) – she may choose one. If the evidence is timed, the earlier one prevails. If she confesses to one before evidence, confession stands but is overridden by later evidence from the other.
Evidence supersedes confession because evidence is a transmissible proof.
3
Examine the prior judgment scenario
If a judgment is already rendered for one, a later claimant cannot overturn it unless his evidence is earlier, showing error in the first.
This protects finality of judgments.
4
Analyze the slave purchase scenario
Both buyers present evidence; they are equal in claim, so the judge rules that each owns half. However, each buyer has an option (khiyar): take half for half the price, or rescind entirely.
This is analogized to two unauthorized agents whose sales are both ratified.
5
Understand the legal analogy (qiyas)
The slave case is compared to two fuduli agents selling to different buyers, with the owner ratifying both. The equality in the cause (same claim, same evidence strength) generates the same ruling: each buyer can choose to take half or void the contract.
The option compensates for the buyer's changed expectation (wanting the whole item).

Conflicting Marriage Claims: Evidence, Dates, and Affirmation
Rulings on conflicting marriage claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings when two men claim marriage to the same woman, distinguishing the roles of evidence, dates, acknowledgment, and prior judgment.

Core Idea
A single woman cannot be shared in marriage; when evidence ties, the woman's affirmation decides, and earlier evidence prevails.

In a dispute where two men both claim to be married to the same woman, the fundamental principle is that marriage is a union that cannot be shared. This passage from the Hidayah outlines the detailed procedural rules for a judge to resolve such claims, prioritizing mutual affirmation of spouses, dated evidence, and the strength of evidence over mere acknowledgment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
The passage begins with 'al-mahall la yaqbal al-ishtirak' — the place (the woman) does not accept sharing. This means a woman cannot be married to two men simultaneously. This principle governs all the following rules.
This is a qat'i principle in Hanafi fiqh; it limits possible resolutions.
2
Analyze the scenario without dates
When both claimants present evidence (bayyinah) without dates, the judge cannot prefer one over the other based on evidence alone. So he refers to the woman's acknowledgment (tasdiq). She affirms one of them, and that one becomes her husband.
Marriage is established by mutual affirmation (tasadiq) of the spouses; here the woman's affirmation creates the marriage.
3
Analyze with dates
If the evidences are dated, the one with the earlier date takes precedence. The earlier marriage is presumed valid and the later one is invalid, because the woman is already married.
Dates provide a clear way to resolve the conflict; the earlier date is decisive.
4
Consider acknowledgment before evidence
If the woman acknowledges one claimant before evidence is produced, that marriage is valid. But if the other claimant later produces evidence, that evidence overrides the acknowledgment because bayyinah (evidence) is stronger than iqrar (acknowledgment).
Evidence is a transitive proof (hujjah muta'addiyah) that affects others, while acknowledgment is only for oneself.
5
Apply rules for prior judgment and possession
If a judge has already ruled for one claimant, that ruling stands unless later evidence shows an earlier date, which would prove the first ruling wrong. Similarly, if the woman is in the husband's hand (living with him) and his marriage is apparent, an outsider's evidence is only accepted if it proves an earlier marriage.
Prior judgments are not overturned lightly; only proof of an earlier marriage justifies reversal.

Conflicting Purchase Claims: Options, Shares, and Precedence
Rulings on conflicting purchase claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules when two purchasers claim to have bought the same slave from the same seller, including the option to take half, the role of dates and possession, and the analogy to agents.

Core Idea
When two buyers prove purchase of the same item without dates, each has the option to take half for half price or walk away, because the item cannot be fully owned by both.

When two men each present evidence that they bought the same slave from the same seller, the Hanafi school provides a fair solution: the judge declares them co-owners of half each, but each has the option to accept his half or reject the entire transaction. This ruling is derived by analogy (qiyas) to a case where two unauthorized agents (fuduliyan) each sell the same item to different buyers with the owner's ratification. The passage also covers the effect of dates, possession, and the consequences of rejecting half.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the core problem
Two buyers each claim to have purchased the same slave (or item) from the same seller. Both present evidence, but no dates are provided. The item cannot be fully owned by both, yet both have valid contracts with the seller. The judge must resolve this conflict.
This scenario assumes the seller still possesses the slave; if the slave is in the hand of one claimant, that affects the ruling (see later steps).
2
The judge's ruling: half shares and option
The judge declares the slave to be half for each claimant (nisfayn). Then each claimant is given the option (khiyar): he may take his half for half of the price he agreed, or he may abandon the entire transaction. This is because the contract condition has changed—each buyer expected full ownership, but now gets half.
The option is a remedy to preserve the buyer's volition; he can walk away if half ownership is not what he intended.
3
Understand the analogy (qiyas) to fuduli agents
The passage analogizes this case to two unauthorized agents (fuduliyan) each selling the same item to two different buyers. If the owner ratifies both sales, each buyer has an option because his contract condition changed (from full ownership to half). The shared illa (effective cause) is the change in the contract's condition due to competition.
This is a qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) because the illa is not explicit but derived from the principle.
4
Analyze consequences of rejection
If after the judge's ruling, one claimant says 'I do not choose the half,' then the other claimant cannot take the whole. The sale is rescinded (munfasikh) for that half. However, if the rejection occurs before the judge gives the option, the other claimant may take the whole because the competition had not yet been formalized.
This distinction mirrors the case of pre-emption (shuf'ah): a pre-emptor's claim before judgment vs after judgment.
5
Determine precedence based on dates and possession
If both have dates, the earlier date wins. If only one has a date, that one wins. If neither has a date but one has possession (qabd), that one is preferred, because possession indicates earlier purchase. However, explicit testimony that the non-possessor's purchase was earlier overrides possession.
Possession is a strong indicator (dalalah) but can be overcome by explicit proof (sarih).

Seller's Claim of Paternity: Istihsan vs Qiyas
Dispute over validity of seller's claim of paternity when sold slave-girl gives birth within six months.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a seller's claim of paternity when a sold slave-girl gives birth, and evaluate the Shafi'i/Zufar counter-argument based on qiyas.

Core Idea
In lineage, istihsan overrides qiyas: the default of legitimate birth trumps apparent contradiction.

When a man sells a slave-girl and she later gives birth, who claims the child? The seller's claim seems to contradict his sale, but Hanafi law prioritizes lineage stability. This lesson unpacks a famous dispute where istihsan (juristic preference) sets aside qiyas (analogy) to protect parentage, highlighting a key methodological difference between madhabs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Scenario and Time Periods
The passage covers three possible birth windows after the sale: less than 6 months, between 6 months and 2 years, and more than 2 years. First, map out each scenario.
The time periods determine the strength of evidence for conception during seller's ownership.
2
State the Hanafi Ruling for Each Period
For births under 6 months: seller's claim accepted, sale void, mother becomes umm walad. For births between 6 months and 2 years: claim accepted only if buyer confirms. For over 2 years: claim invalid unless buyer confirms, but sale stands.
The middle period balances possibility of conception against the lack of certainty.
3
Identify the Opposing School's Position and Its Qiyas
Al-Zufar and al-Shafi'i hold that the seller's claim is always invalid because the sale itself acknowledges the child's belonging to the buyer – a clear contradiction.
Qiyas: the sale is analogous to an admission, so a later claim contradicts and is rejected.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response via Istihsan
Hanafi argues that the strong presumption of no zina (default) and the hidden nature of lineage outweigh the apparent contradiction. The 'connection of conception' is a stronger testimony.
Istihsan allows departure from qiyas for a more suitable ruling based on a hidden proof (dalil khafi).
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
Hanafi: child is free, mother becomes umm walad, sale void. Shafi'i: child remains property of buyer, mother is not umm walad. Knowing this difference affects legal outcomes.
This case illustrates how usul principles directly shape fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage presents a series of rulings from Al-Hidayah regarding the claim of lineage (nasab) and the establishment of umm walad status when a pregnant slave woman (jariyah) is sold and the buyer's possession. The text follows the matn's structure, expanding with proofs and intra-madhhab disagreements. The primary scenario: a slave woman is sold while pregnant. After the sale, she gives birth prematurely (less than six months from the sale) and the seller claims the child is his. The key principle is that the child is the 'asl (origin) for lineage, and the mother is tabi' (subsidiary). First ruling: If the child dies after the claim, and the birth was less than six months, the seller's claim does not establish nasab for the child (since he is dead) and consequently does not establish umm walad for the mother. The reasoning: the child no longer needs lineage (due to death), so establishing it is moot; the mother's status follows the child, so it also fails. Second ruling: If the mother dies first (and the child lives), and the seller claims the child within less than six months, the child's lineage is established, and the seller takes the child. The mother's death does not prevent this because the child is the origin; the subsidiary's loss is irrelevant. The proof is the hadith: 'اعتقها ولدها' (Her child freed her). For the mother, only the 'right to freedom' (haqq al-hurriyah) is established, meaning she becomes umm walad, but not fully free until the master's death. For the child, actual freedom is established because he is born free. Third: The dispute about returning the price. If the seller successfully claims the child (e.g., after mother's death), the sale of the slave woman is void ab initio since she was umm walad and cannot be sold. The price must be returned. Abu Hanifa says the entire price must be returned because the entire consideration fails (the mother was not a valid commodity). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) say only the share of the child's price is returned; the mother's share is not returned because according to them, umm walad's value is compensable in contract (mutaqawwim), meaning the buyer must guarantee it, so the buyer keeps the mother and pays her value. For Abu Hanifa, umm walad's value is non-compensable (ghayr mutaqawwim), so the buyer is not liable for her and must return the full price. Fourth: Cases from al-Jami' al-Saghir regarding when the buyer has freed the mother or the child. If the buyer freed the mother before the claim, the seller can still claim the child because the freedom does not prevent the claim; the seller takes the child and returns the child's share of the price. But if the buyer freed the child, the seller's claim is void because the child has been freed based on the buyer's ownership, and the seller cannot override that. The distinction: the manumission (itq) is a reality that cannot be undone, and it acts as a preventative (man') in the origin (the child) when done by the buyer, but only in the subsidiary (the mother) when done there. The child's freedom is a necessary consequence; the mother's freedom is only a right. Tadbir (declaration of freedom after death) is equivalent to itq in this ruling because it also cannot be reversed. The text concludes that the correct position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's: return of the entire price, as mentioned in the parallel case of the mother's death.

Once lineage (nasab) is established through acknowledgment, it cannot be rescinded even by denial.

Understand Abu Hanifa's argument
Abu Hanifa holds that lineage, once established, cannot be rescinded. He compares it to testimony: a witness who testifies to a child's lineage cannot later claim the child. The child's right to a lineage is attached from the moment of acknowledgment.
The distinction between wala' (patronage) and nasab is critical: wala' can be overridden by a stronger claim, but lineage cannot.
4
Analyze the analogy to wala'
The Sahibayn analogize to the emancipation case where the buyer's later claim transfers wala'. Abu Hanifa counters that wala' is weaker than lineage and can be superseded. The difference lies in the nature of the right: lineage is a fundamental, irrevocable status.
The text also discusses a variant where the slave neither confirms nor denies, distinguishing it from the present case.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: According to Abu Hanifa, the master's later claim is always invalid; the child remains the slave's son (or lineage uncertain). According to the Sahibayn, if the slave denies, the master can claim the child as his own.
Later Hanafi fatwa practice often leans toward Abu Hanifa's view due to the principle of irrevocability.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning a specific scenario: a man has custody of a child and first says, 'This is the son of my absent slave, Zayd,' then later claims the child as his own son. The ruling according to Imam Abu Hanifa is that the child will never be considered the man's son, even if the absent slave later denies paternity. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) hold that if the slave denies being the father, then the child becomes the master's son. The dispute extends to a similar case where the man first attributes the child to another free man claiming the child was born on his bed and later claims the child for himself.

The Sahibayn argue that the initial acknowledgment of paternity is rescinded by the slave's denial, making it as if no acknowledgment occurred. They support this by pointing out that acknowledgment of lineage can be affected by coercion or jest (as seen in other contexts), so it can be rescinded. They analogize to a case in sales: if a buyer acknowledges to the seller that he (the buyer) emancipated a purchased slave, but the seller denies this, and then the buyer says, 'I did emancipate him,' the patronage of the freed slave transfers to the buyer. Similarly, in the child case, the slave's denial erases the acknowledgment, and the master's subsequent claim is valid. If the slave confirms the acknowledgment, the master's later claim fails because the lineage is already established from the slave.

Abu Hanifa's argument is that lineage, once established, cannot be rescinded. The initial acknowledgment, whether true or false, is considered to have established the lineage of the child from the slave. This established fact remains even if the slave later denies it. Therefore, the master's subsequent claim to the child is contradictory to the established lineage and thus invalid. He compares to a case where a witness testifies to a child's lineage being from another man, and the testimony is rejected due to suspicion. The same witness cannot later claim the child for himself because his previous testimony binds him. Abu Hanifa also notes that the child's own right to a lineage is attached, and once the acknowledgment is made, the child's lineage from the slave is considered established and cannot be undone by the master's later claim or the slave's denial. The difference between lineage and patronage (wala') is mentioned: patronage can be overridden by a stronger claim, but lineage cannot.

Textually, this passage is a commentary expansion on the original matn of Al-Hidayah. The analysis shows the Hanafi school's method of legal reasoning using qiyas (analogy) and consideration of the nature of lineage (nasab) as a right that cannot be rescinded once established.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding the inviolability of lineage (nasab) after acknowledgment. The issue arises when a person first acknowledges a child as his son, then later claims the child as a slave, or when a seller acknowledges a slave as his son and then the buyer claims the child as his son. Abu Hanifa's position is that lineage, once established by acknowledgment or evidence, cannot be nullified. The acknowledger's earlier acknowledgment attaches the child's right to lineage, and the child may later confirm it; hence the acknowledger's later contrary claim is invalid. The Sahibayn analogize to the law of wala (clientage), which can be overridden by a stronger right. The commentary refutes this analogy on two grounds: first, the wala case itself is disputed by Abu Hanifa; second, wala and nasab are fundamentally different—wala is a weaker right that can be transferred (e.g., when a father draws the wala of his children from the mother's master), but nasab is permanent and unbreakable. The passage also explains 'suspended wala' (al-wala' al-mawquf), which attaches to the seller and is broken by the buyer's claim, further illustrating the difference. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is Abu Hanifa's view, affirming the stability of lineage.

Conflicting Claims Over a Child: Joint Possession vs. Lineage
Addresses the ruling when both spouses claim a child in their joint custody belongs to a third party.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a child in joint possession of spouses remains attributed to both despite conflicting claims, and apply the analogy of joint possession to other legal contexts.

Core Idea
When spouses each claim a child belongs to someone else, the child stays theirs—lineage cannot be shared like property.

If a child is living with a husband and wife, and each claims the child is from a different partner, the Hanafi school rules that the child remains theirs. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning, including the analogy of a cloth jointly held, and the key difference: lineage cannot be partitioned.

Guided Path
1
Identify the situation
The child is in the joint possession (physical custody) of both spouses, or the marital bed (firash) exists between them.
Joint possession implies both have equal apparent right.
2
Recognize each spouse's claim
The wife claims the child is from another man; the husband claims it is from another woman. Each is trying to exclude the other's lineage.
They are not claiming for themselves but against the other.
3
Apply the presumption
The apparent situation (zahir) is that the child is from them, due to possession or marital bed. So the child is considered theirs.
The burden of proof is on the one trying to negate the apparent.
4
Refute the conflicting claims
Each spouse's claim would nullify the other's right. The court does not accept such claims because they are mutually destructive without evidence.
The text says 'each one wants to nullify the right of the other, so his statement is not believed.'
5
Understand the analogy and its limit
The child is like a cloth in two men's hands, each claiming it belongs to him and a third party. The cloth remains shared. But lineage differs because it cannot be divided or shared, so no third party can be admitted.
The analogy helps to see the principle of joint possession, but the outcome differs due to the nature of lineage.

Ruling on a paternity dispute with joint custody

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on joint paternity claims when both spouses have custody and each claims the child is from a different partner.

Core Idea
Joint custody and valid marriage bed create a presumption of joint paternity until the child can speak.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a specific custody and paternity conflict: a child is in the joint physical possession of a husband and wife, but each claims the child is from a different spouse. The ruling provides a clear method for resolving the dispute, emphasizing the child's ability to speak and the legal presumptions of possession (qabdah) and matrimonial bed (firash). Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to the conditions under which the child's lineage is determined.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting claims
The husband says the child is his but from another wife; the wife says the child is hers but from another husband. Both have physical custody.
The dispute is not about the child's paternity between them, but about attributing the child to a third party.
2
Note the default presumption
Because both have possession and a valid marriage bed exists, the default is that the child belongs to both. Their claims are seen as attempts to cancel each other's right, so neither is accepted.
Firash is a strong basis in Hanafi law for presuming paternity.
3
Recognize the child's capacity condition
If the child cannot speak (too young), both are declared parents. If the child can speak, his statement is accepted, and he may choose which parent to affirm.
The child's testimony overrides the default presumption.
4
Understand the legal reasoning
Each spouse's claim is an attempt to nullify the other's right. Since the right to lineage is shared, neither can unilaterally exclude the other without evidence.
This is analogous to joint possession of property.
5
Appreciate the analogy with joint property
The text compares the case to two people jointly possessing a cloth, each claiming it belongs to him and a named third party. The cloth is shared between the possessors. However, lineage cannot be shared like property, so the child is not split.
The difference: property can be divided, lineage cannot.

Marriage and Fair Sale During Terminal Illness: The Hanafi Response to Shafi'i
Refutes Shafi'i's analogy by distinguishing essential needs from gratuitous acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on marriage with proper dower and sale at fair price during terminal illness and explain why these are allowed while gratuitous transfers are not.

Core Idea
Essential needs and value-preserving sales are permitted during illness; they do not nullify creditors' rights.

This passage addresses a dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding transactions during terminal illness (marad al-mawt). Imam al-Shafi'i analogized marriage and fair-priced sales to prohibited gratuitous transfers. The commentator defends the Hanafi view, arguing that marriage is an essential human need and that a sale at market value merely transfers creditors' rights from one asset to another without loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contested transactions
The two transactions under dispute are marriage with a proper dower (mahr al-mithl) and a sale at fair market price (mubaya'ah bi-mithl al-qimah). Note that both are done during terminal illness when creditors have rights over the debtor's estate.
2
State the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i analogized these to gratuitous transfers (e.g., gifts) which are restricted during terminal illness because they diminish the estate available to creditors.
3
Explain the Hanafi response for marriage
Marriage is an essential need (hajah asliyyah) necessary for procreation and preserving lineage. Spending on essential needs is not restricted even during illness. The proper dower ensures no gratuitous transfer beyond the norm.
4
Explain the Hanafi response for sale at fair price
A sale at fair price preserves the value of the estate; creditors' rights attach to the value (maliyah), not the specific form. The sale merely transfers the value from one asset to another, so creditors are not harmed.
5
Understand the health vs. illness distinction
In health, creditors' rights do not attach to the debtor's property because of earning potential. In illness, that potential is absent, so rights attach. Both initial and terminal stages of illness are treated the same in terms of restriction.

Sulh to Drop a Marriage Claim: Ruling and Reasoning
Ruling on sulh when a man claims marriage and the woman denies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh when a man claims marriage and the woman denies, and explain the legal reasoning that treats it as khul'.

Core Idea
A sulh to drop a marriage claim is valid as khul' but forbidden for a liar.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: a man claims a woman is his wife, but she denies it. To avoid litigation, she pays him to drop the claim. How does the madhhab treat this transaction? The passage from al-Hidayah provides a clear ruling, validating the sulh by analogy to khul' (divorce initiated by the wife with compensation), while also highlighting an important ethical caveat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and legal question
Consider the parties: a man claims a woman is his wife (she denies), and she offers money to drop the claim. The question: is the sulh valid, and what is its legal nature?
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The sulh is permissible (jā'iz) and is treated as being in the meaning of khul'. This means the transaction is valid and effects a divorce from the man's perspective based on his claim.
The ruling is from al-Hidayah, part of Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Analyze the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The reasoning is two-sided: from the man's side, based on his claim, the transaction can be validated as khul' (divorce for compensation). From the woman's side, she is giving wealth to ward off litigation (daf' al-khusumah), which is a legitimate objective in sulh.
This is a qiyas (analogy) to khul', using the possibility of interpretation.
4
Note the ethical caveat
The scholars add: if the man knows he is false in his claim, it is not lawful (halāl) for him to take the money in the sight of Allah. The legal validity remains, but the man bears a sin.
This shows the distinction between legal validity (sihhah) and moral permissibility (halāl/harām) in Hanafi fiqh.

Sulh in Marriage Claim: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Validity of Payment
Conflicting narrations on sulh in a marriage claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi narrations on sulh when a woman claims marriage and the man denies, and explain why the preferred view invalidates the sulh.

Core Idea
A man's payment to drop a marriage claim is invalid because it lacks valid legal countervalue.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah presents a dispute within the Hanafi school itself. Two different manuscripts of al-Mukhtasar report conflicting rulings on whether a man can pay a woman to drop her claim of marriage. By analyzing the reasoning behind each view, you will learn how Hanafi jurists scrutinize the mutuality of consideration in contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the two views
The case: a woman claims marriage, the man denies, but he pays her to drop the claim. Two narrations: one says it is permissible (jā'iz), another says it is not permissible (lā yajūz).
Both narrations are transmitted from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.
2
Understand the reasoning for validity
The first view treats the payment as an increase in mahr (dower), assuming the marriage is established. Since increasing mahr is permissible, the sulh is valid.
This reasoning hinges on the idea that the woman's claim is true, even if denied.
3
Understand the reasoning for invalidity
The second view argues that the payment is for dropping the claim. If dropping the claim is considered a separation (furqah), the husband does not pay for a separation (in khul', the wife pays). If it is not a separation, then the situation remains unchanged, so the payment has no corresponding benefit, making the sulh invalid.
This reasoning points to a lack of valid countervalue (muqabil).
4
Locate the tarjih (preferred view)
The text sides with invalidity by presenting its reasoning fully. The relied-upon fatwa in the Hanafi school is that the sulh is invalid, and the man can reclaim his money.

Agent's Liability in Sulh: Isqat vs Exchange
Ruling on agent's sulh: liability depends on type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's liability in sulh and distinguish between isqat (pure release) and exchange-based sulh using qiyas.

Core Idea
In sulh, the agent is a mere messenger for isqat, but becomes a party like a seller in exchange-based sulh.

Who bears the liability when an agent (wakil) makes a sulh (settlement) on behalf of his principal? The answer depends on the nature of the sulh. This lesson from al-Hidayah explains the distinction between sulh as a pure release (isqat mahd) and sulh as an exchange (mal bi mal), with a clear analogy to the agent in marriage. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly assigning liability in agency transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is sulh (settlement) performed by an agent (wakil). The ruling is that the agent is not liable for the compensation unless he specifically guarantees it. The principal is liable.
2
Classify the type of sulh
Determine whether the sulh is a pure release (isqat mahd) — like forgiving part of a debt or settling blood money — or an exchange of property (mal bi mal). The ruling changes accordingly.
Isqat mahd is a unilateral renunciation, not a contract.
3
Apply the analogy to the marriage agent
In isqat cases, the agent is like a wakil bil-nikah: he is a mere messenger (safir) who conveys the principal's will. He is not liable for the consideration (e.g., mahr). Similarly, here the agent does not owe the compensation.
The qiyas is jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Identify the exception: agent's guarantee
If the agent explicitly guarantees (yadman) the compensation, then he is held by the contract of guarantee, not by the sulh itself. Then he becomes liable.
This applies to both types of sulh.
5
Contrast with exchange-sulh (like sale)
If the sulh is an exchange of property for property, it is treated like a sale. Rights and obligations revert to the agent, so he is the one liable to pay the agreed property, not the principal.
This is another category beyond the initial ruling.

The ruling distinguishes between two types of sulh: (1) Sulh that is a pure release (isqat), such as forgiving part of a debt or settling for diyat in murder. In such cases, the agent is like a messenger; he is not liable for the compensation. The compensation is owed by the principal. (2) Sulh that is an exchange of property for property, which is analogous to a sale. Then the agent becomes the direct party, and he is liable to pay the property. The exception is if the agent specifically guarantees (yadman) the compensation; then he becomes liable as a guarantor. The proof is analogy: the agent in marriage (wakil bil-nikah) is not liable for the mahr, similarly here.

اسقاط محض (isqat mahd): A pure release or renunciation of a right, with no exchange of property.
سفير (safir): An intermediary or ambassador who conveys the will of the principal without personal liability.
وكيل بالصّلح (wakil bil-sulh): An agent appointed to make sulh (settlement) on behalf of the principal.
