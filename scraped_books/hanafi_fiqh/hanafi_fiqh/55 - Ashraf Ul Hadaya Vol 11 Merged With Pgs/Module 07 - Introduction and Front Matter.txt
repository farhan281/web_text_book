Introduction to Kitab al-Sulh and Kitab al-Mudarabah via the Table of Contents
Table of contents for two fiqh books.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main topics and rulings discussed in Kitab al-Sulh and Kitab al-Mudarabah as outlined in the table of contents, and explain the relationship between the two chapters.

Core Idea
This index is your roadmap to two complex fiqh topics: sulh (compromise) and mudarabah (profit-sharing partnership).

This lesson examines the table of contents for Volume 11 of Ashraf al-Hidayah. Rather than delving into detailed rulings, we survey the architecture of two essential fiqh books: Kitab al-Sulh (Book of Compromise) and Kitab al-Mudarabah (Book of Profit-Sharing). Understanding this structure will help you navigate the later detailed discussions and see how various rulings interconnect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main sections
The table is clearly divided into two major books: Kitab al-Sulh and Kitab al-Mudarabah. Notice the large headings marking the beginning of each.
These are the two core subjects of this volume.
2
Survey the subtopics under Kitab al-Sulh
Scroll through the list under Kitab al-Sulh. You'll see topics like types of sulh (with acknowledgment/denial), sulh over property, sulh over debts, and specific cases like marriage claims.
There is also a mention of Imam Shafi'i's viewpoint and Hanafi proof.
3
Survey the subtopics under Kitab al-Mudarabah
Under Kitab al-Mudarabah, look for definition, conditions, permissible/impermissible actions, delegation, and narrational differences.
Also note the inclusion of a legal stratagem (hila) for validity.
4
Note cross-references and additional features
The index occasionally points to differences between Jami' al-Saghir and Mabsut. Also, it includes specific rulings with conditions.
These indicate areas of scholarly discussion that will be detailed later.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents (fihrist) for Volume 11 of Ashraf al-Hidayah, listing the topics covered in two major books: Kitab al-Sulh (Book of Compromise) and Kitab al-Mudarabah (Book of Profit-Sharing/Quasi-Partnership). The author presents a sequence of rulings and subheadings, providing a map of the legal discussions that follow. For Kitab al-Sulh, it begins with issues of lineage acknowledgment (nasab) and then moves to the definition, types, and legal rulings of sulh (compromise) including its validity, conditions, and various scenarios such as compromise with acknowledgment (iqrar), with denial (inkar), and over debts. Hanafi positions are highlighted with occasional reference to Imam Shafi'i's viewpoint and the Hanafi proof. For Kitab al-Mudarabah, the table lists the definition, conditions, validity (including a legal stratagem or hila), permissible and impermissible actions for the mudarib, including issues of delegation and transfer of capital. The index also notes differences between narrations from Imam Muhammad's Jami' al-Saghir and al-Mabsut. This front matter serves as a guide for students to navigate the detailed rulings that follow.

Key Terms
صلح (sulh): A legal contract of compromise or settlement, resolving a dispute by mutual agreement, often involving payment of consideration to drop a claim.
مضاربة (mudarabah): A profit-sharing partnership where one party provides capital (rabb al-mal) and the other provides labor (mudarib), with profits shared as agreed.

Navigating the Chapters on Wadi'ah, Ariyah, and Hiba
Fihrist listing chapter headings and page numbers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to locate specific topics within the chapters on Wadi'ah, Ariyah, and Hiba by using the table of contents (fihrist) and distinguish between a fihrist entry and a legal ruling.

Core Idea
The fihrist is a map, not the territory: it guides you to rulings, but does not itself contain them.

Before diving into the detailed rulings on deposits, loans, and gifts, it is essential to understand how the material is organized. This lesson introduces you to the table of contents (fihrist) for these chapters. By learning to navigate the fihrist, you will efficiently locate specific issues and recognize that the fihrist is merely a reference tool, not a source of fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chapter headings
Look at the fihrist and note the three main chapters: كتاب الوديعة (Book of Deposit), كتاب العارية (Book of Loan), and كتاب الهبة (Book of Gift). Each chapter covers a distinct area of mu'amalat.
The chapter titles are in Arabic, but the subtopics are in Urdu.
2
Scan the subtopics within each chapter
Under each chapter heading, the fihrist lists specific issues and page numbers. For example, under 'كتاب العارية' you will find 'عاریت کی تعریف' (Definition of Ariyah) and 'عاریت کے اقسام اربعہ' (The Four Types of Ariyah).
Not all issues are exhaustive; the fihrist highlights key discussions.
3
Use the page numbers to locate full discussions
Each subtopic has a corresponding page number in the original volume. When studying, refer to these pages to read the complete legal analysis. The fihrist itself does not provide rulings.
Page numbers are omitted in this digital edition; focus on the logical order.
4
Distinguish between fiqh matters and fihrist entries
Recognize that entries like 'مودع ودیعت کی خود اور اپنے عیال سے حفاظت کراسکتا ہے' are summaries, not the actual ruling. The full ruling with evidence appears later in the text.
This fihrist is a memorization anchor; do not mistake it for the primary source.

Overview of Kitab al-Hibah (Book of Gifts) in Hanafi Fiqh
Table of contents listing key rulings of Kitab al-Hibah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the major topics covered in Kitab al-Hibah and state the Hanafi conditions for a valid gift (hibah), including the role of possession (qabd).

Core Idea
Hibah requires offer, acceptance, and possession; the Hanafi school emphasizes possession as a pillar.

This lesson offers an orientation to the chapter on gifts (Kitab al-Hibah) in a Hanafi fiqh text. The chapter covers the legal status of gifts, conditions of validity, rules of possession, revocation, and comparisons with charity. The following list serves as a table of contents, outlining key rulings and debates, particularly contrasting Hanafi views with those of Imam Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling on gift validity
From the list, notice that the Hanafi school requires offer, acceptance, and possession for a valid gift. The text mentions the necessity of possession (qabd) and discusses scenarios where the donee takes possession with or without the donor's permission.
Istihsan allows possession in the session without permission.
2
Understand the condition of possession
Possession can be in the donor's presence or after separation. The text distinguishes between permissible and impermissible possession scenarios, highlighting the Hanafi reliance on actual transfer of control.
Possession after separation requires the donor's permission.
3
Note the wording requirements
Specific phrasing can affect the legal nature of the transfer. For example, 'I have clothed you with this garment' constitutes a gift, while 'I have moved this slave girl' may be considered a loan ('ariyah).
The wording indicates intention, a crucial element in Hanafi law.
4
Examine gift of undivided share (musha')
The text states that gifting an undivided share is invalid (fasid) in the Hanafi school. This is a point of dispute with Imam Shafi'i, who deems it valid.
Fasad (invalidity) here means the contract is defective and cannot be perfected without partition.
5
Review revocation rules
Revocation of a gift is possible except in specific cases: gifts to mahram relatives, gifts where the donee has compensated the donor, or when the gifted item is destroyed. The text also says that revocation must be done with mutual consent or through a judge.
The right of revocation is a distinctive feature of Hanafi hibah law.

The Book of Iqrar: Definition, Authority, and First Ruling of Confession
Introduction to Kitab al-Iqrar with legal basis and first ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of iqrar as a report of the establishment of a right, identify the proofs (dalil) that make it binding, and apply the first ruling that it is binding even if the subject is unknown.

Core Idea
Confession (iqrar) is binding because a rational person does not lie against themselves — it is proof even when the matter is unknown.

This passage opens the Book of Confession in a commentary on Al-Hidayah. It explains the book's place in the sequence of fiqh chapters, establishes the legal authority of iqrar from the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma, defines iqrar, and states the first key ruling: a free, sane, adult's confession is binding regardless of whether the matter confessed is known or unknown.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition
Find and read the definition: iqrar is 'ikhbar an thubut al-haqq' (a report about the establishment of a right against oneself). Recognize that it is a form of testimony against the self.
The term 'iqrar' is derived from qarar (stability/establishment).
2
Trace the dalil (proof) for its authority
Locate the evidence cited: Quran 2:282 (command to dictate), hadith of Ma'iz (stoning based on confession), and consensus (ijma). Understand that each proves iqrar is a valid source of legal obligation.
The Quranic verse is used as dalil that the one who owes the right must dictate – a form of iqrar.
3
State the first ruling and its conditions
The text declares: when a free, sane, adult person confesses to a right, his confession is binding (lazim), whether the subject is unknown or known. Note the conditions of the confessor (muqirr): free, sane, adult, awake, and voluntary.
Even an unknown matter (majhool) is accepted; knowledge of specifics is not a condition.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a case: A sound-minded adult says 'I owe you something' without details. Under Hanafi law, this is a valid confession. The confessor cannot later evade liability by claiming the subject was unknown.
This shows the practical binding effect of iqrar.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Iqrar (Confession) in a commentary on Al-Hidayah. It first explains the order of books: after Da'wa (claims) comes Iqrar because the defendant either confesses or denies, and denial leads to dispute which requires Sulh (arbitration). Then after acquisition of wealth, one either profits (through trade or Mudaraba) or preserves it (through Wadi'a). Iqrar is placed before Sulh because it is closer to truth: a rational person does not confess falsely to his own detriment. The passage then establishes the authority of iqrar from the Quran (verse on dictation of debt), hadith (stoning of Ma'iz upon confession), and ijma. It then states the first ruling: if a free, sane, adult person confesses to a right, his confession is binding even if the matter is unknown. The definition: iqrar is a report of the establishment of a right. Commentary clarifies that the person must be free, sane, adult, and awake; even a permission-seeking slave or minor can make valid confession in certain contexts. The unknown nature of the confessed matter does not harm validity.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrar): A report (ikhbar) about the establishment of a right (thubut al-haqq) against oneself.
مقر (muqirr): The person who makes the confession (iqrar).

The Book of Iqrar: Its Placement and Legal Authority
Introduction to Kitab al-Iqrar with placement logic and proofs from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the sequential placement of Kitab al-Iqrar after Kitab al-Da'wa and state the primary evidences for the authority of iqrar in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Iqrar is a binding proof (hujja) for the one who acknowledges; its authority is established by Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma.

This passage introduces the Book of Iqrar (Acknowledgments) by explaining why it is placed after the Book of Claims (Da'wa) and before chapters on reconciliation, partnership, and deposits. It then provides the foundational evidences that make iqrar a legally effective institution. Understanding this front matter is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to iqrar as a source of obligation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the sequential logic
Why is iqrar placed after da'wa? Because after a claim, the defendant either acknowledges or denies. Denial leads to conflict and reconciliation, so iqrar is the immediate topic.
Note the binary nature of response: acknowledgment vs. denial.
2
Trace the chain of chapters
The passage shows that after iqrar comes sulh (reconciliation), then mudaraba (profit-sharing), then wadi'a (deposits). Follow the logical flow: acknowledgment → settlement → profit either by self or through others → safekeeping.
3
Recognize the reason for precedence
Iqrar is given priority because it is closer to the truth; a rational person does not harm themselves by falsely acknowledging.
This is a key maxim underlying the institution.
4
Identify the dalil
The passage cites Quran 2:282 (command to dictate debts), Quran 3:81 (the covenant), the hadith of Ma'iz (acknowledgment leading to stoning), and Ijma (consensus that iqrar establishes hudud, thus property rights a fortiori).
5
Understand the authority weight
These evidences establish that iqrar is a binding proof (hujja) for the one who acknowledges, and the Hanafi school treats it as authoritative.
Note that the hadith of Ma'iz is ahad, but its content is supported by other evidences.

The Book of Iqrar. ... Acceptance of the book... After the Book of Da'wa, the Book of Iqrar comes, and after it Sulh, Mudaraba, and Wadi'a, the reasons for their placement are very apparent. Because when the plaintiff's claim is directed towards the defendant, then there are only two possibilities: the defendant will either acknowledge it or deny it. Denial becomes a cause of dispute, and dispute requires reconciliation. Allah says: 'If two groups of believers fight, make peace between them...' Then after acquiring wealth through iqrar or sulh, the owner's situation is either he will benefit from it or not. If he benefits, it is either by himself or through another. By himself: the statement of profit has already come in Kitab al-Buyu' with its appropriate placement. Profit through another will come later, which is called Mudaraba. If he does not take profit, then either he will protect it himself or have another protect it. If he protects it himself, there is no need to mention it because no ruling is related to it in transactions. Thus remaining is protection by another, which is Wadi'a. Then the reason for giving precedence to iqrar is that iqrar is closer to the truth because the plaintiff in his claim and the acknowledgment of the one acknowledging are predominantly truthful. Since a rational person does not lie about himself to his own harm, the aspect of truthfulness predominates, and iqrar is closer to the truth. Therefore after da'wa, the rulings of iqrar are being explained. Benefit: The authority of iqrar is established by the Book of Allah, the Sunnah of the Messenger (peace be upon him), and the consensus of the Ummah. Allah's statement: 'Let the one on whom the right is due dictate' (i.e., acknowledge). If iqrar were not authoritative, this command would have no meaning. Also Allah's statement: 'He said: Have you acknowledged and taken My covenant on that?' Also it is established by authentic hadith that the Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered the stoning of Ma'iz based on his acknowledgment of zina. There is consensus of the Ummah that punishments and retaliation are established against the one who acknowledges. So property rights are established a fortiori.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Iqrar in the Hanafi tradition. The author begins by explaining the sequential placement of chapters after Kitab al-Da'wa (Book of Claims). The logic is that when a claim is made, the defendant either acknowledges (iqrar) or denies. Denial leads to conflict, necessitating settlement (sulh). After acquiring property, the owner may profit personally (buyu') or through others (mudaraba), or may need safekeeping (wadi'a). Iqrar is placed before these because it is a direct acknowledgment of a right, and the natural disposition of a sane person is to be truthful in self-incriminating matters. The author then cites evidences: Quranic verses (2:282 and 3:81), the hadith of Ma'iz (whose acknowledgment of adultery led to stoning), and the consensus of the Ummah that iqrar establishes hudud and qisas, thus property rights are even more clearly established. This front matter establishes the legal authority of iqrar as a proof (hujja) in Islamic law.
