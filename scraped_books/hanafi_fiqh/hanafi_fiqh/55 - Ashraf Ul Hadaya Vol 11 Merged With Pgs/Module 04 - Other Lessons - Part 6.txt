Istithna' of Pregnancy in Hibah: Void Exception but Valid Gift
Ruling on exception in gift of a slave-girl and analogy to other contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a gift excludes an unborn child and identify why the exception becomes a void condition, and name other contracts with the same rule.

Core Idea
An exception in a gift is void if the contract doesn't operate on the excepted item, but the gift remains valid.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a specific scenario: a person gifts a slave-girl but excludes her pregnancy. The ruling clarifies that such an exception is invalid and becomes a void condition, but—unlike in a sale—the gift itself remains valid. This rule applies to other gratuitous contracts like marriage and khul'.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Read the passage: the gift of the slave-girl is valid, and the exception of the pregnancy is void. Understand that the exception does not operate because hibah does not affect the pregnancy.
The text says 'the gift is valid and the exception is void'.
2
Identify the reason for the void exception
The exception only works in a place where the contract itself can operate. Hibah (gift) transfers ownership of tangible property; the pregnancy is a description (wasf) of the slave-girl, not an independent property. So the contract cannot affect it, making the exception void.
The text explains: 'because the exception only operates in a place where the contract operates'.
3
Explain the consequence: the exception becomes a void condition
Since the exception cannot take effect, it is treated as an invalid condition (shart fasid). However, hibah is a gratuitous contract that is not invalidated by void conditions.
Contrast with sale, lease, and pledge, which are invalidated by void conditions.
4
List other contracts with the same rule
The text notes that the same rule applies to marriage (nikah), khul', and settlement for intentional homicide (sulh 'an dam al-amd). These contracts also survive void conditions.
The principle: contracts that are not purely commutative are not annulled by void conditions.

Commentary
This passage covers two distinct rulings: first, the nature of a gift made with a condition of compensation (hibah bi shart al-iwad), and second, the effect of excluding an unborn child when gifting a slave-girl. The main text begins with the ruling of the Hanafi school: when a gift is made with a condition that the donee must give something in return, it is considered a gift at the inception, so both parties must take possession of their respective items in the same session; if the property is co-owned (musha'), the contract becomes void. However, if they take possession, the contract becomes valid as a form of sale, meaning it becomes binding, subject to options of defect and sight, and the right of pre-emption applies. This reflects the Hanafi compromise: initially it's a gift (so possession is required), but ultimately it's a sale (so it becomes binding). Zufar and al-Shafi'i argue that it is a sale from the start because the essence is exchange of property for compensation; they point to cases like selling a slave to himself being considered manumission, showing that meanings prevail over words. The Hanafis respond that the contract combines both aspects, and where possible, both should be given effect. Since hibah can have deferred ownership and can become binding with compensation, and sale is inherently binding, they combine: at the outset, it's a gift (ownership after possession), after possession, it's a sale (binding). This is unlike selling a slave to himself, which cannot be a genuine sale. The second ruling concerns a gift of a slave-girl with an exception of her pregnancy. Since the pregnancy is a description, not an independent subject of the contract, the exception is invalid and becomes a void condition. However, because hibah is a gratuitous contract, it is not invalidated by a void condition; the gift of the slave-girl itself is valid, and the condition is ignored. This ruling also applies to marriage, khul', and settlement for intentional homicide, as these contracts are also not invalidated by void conditions, unlike sale, lease, and pledge.

شرط فاسد (shart fasid): An invalid or void condition in a contract.

Hiba of a Slave Girl Except Her Pregnancy: Istithna Invalid, Gift Valid
Ruling that hiba of a slave girl with exception of the fetus is valid, but the exception is void and becomes a void condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a gift with an exception of a non-contractual part and explain why the exception is void.

Core Idea
An exception can only exclude what the contract covers; a gift does not cover the fetus, so the exception is void.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: someone gifts a slave girl but excludes her pregnancy. The Hanafi ruling, as established by the supporting reasoning, is that the gift is valid but the exclusion (istithna') is void. The reasoning is based on the nature of exception and the scope of the contract. Understanding this ruling illuminates a broader Hanafi principle: non-commutative contracts like gift, marriage, and certain reconciliations are not invalidated by void conditions, unlike sales and leases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hiba (gift) of a slave girl with an exception of her pregnancy. The ruling: the gift is valid (sahih) and the exception is void (batil).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The author states: 'because the exception only operates in a locus where the contract operates' (لان الاستثناء لا يعمل إلا في محل يعمل فيه العقد). This is a general principle.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof applies)
The contract of hiba transfers ownership of the slave girl. The fetus is considered a description (wasf) and subordinate to the mother, not an independent entity. Therefore, the contract does not operate on the fetus. Hence, the exception cannot be effective and becomes a void condition.
This was previously explained in the chapter of sales.
4
Determine the consequence: void condition does not invalidate the gift
Since the exception becomes a void condition (shart fasid), we must determine its effect. The text states that hiba is not invalidated by void conditions, unlike sale, lease, and pledge.
This is a key distinction in Hanafi law: non-commutative contracts tolerate void conditions; commutative contracts do not.
5
Apply the principle to other contracts
The same ruling applies to marriage (nikah), khul', and reconciliation (sulh) for premeditated homicide. These are not invalidated by void conditions. Conversely, sale, lease, and pledge are invalidated.
The fa'ida from Sharh al-Tahawi further categorizes istithna' into three ranks, confirming this ruling is the second rank.

Gifting a Slave Woman After Freeing Her Fetus vs. Tadbir
Ruling on gifting after fetus manumission vs tadbir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the effects of immediate manumission (itaq) and conditional manumission (tadbir) on the validity of a subsequent gift, and apply the Hanafi ruling on hiba al-musha'.

Core Idea
Immediate ownership removal validates the gift; tadbir leaves ownership intact, invalidating it.

This passage contrasts two scenarios from Hanafi fiqh: a master gifts a pregnant slave woman after either freeing the fetus or making it a mudabbar. The ruling hinges on whether the fetus remains in the master's ownership at the moment of gift. The reasoning uses analogy (qiyas) to the rules of valid exception and invalid gift of shared property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Case 1: The fetus is freed (itaq), then the mother is gifted. Case 2: The fetus is made mudabbar (tadbir), then the mother is gifted.
2
Determine the ruling for each
Case 1: Valid (jaza). Case 2: Invalid (lam yajuz).
3
Analyze the key distinction
In Case 1, the fetus no longer belongs to the donor at the time of gift because itaq transfers ownership immediately. In Case 2, tadbir does not transfer ownership; the fetus remains owned by the donor.
Tadbir is a suspended emancipation, not an immediate transfer.
4
Understand the analogies used
Case 1 resembles a valid exception (istithna) of the fetus from the gift, so the gift is valid. Case 2 resembles a gift of shared property (hiba al-musha') or a gift of something occupied by the owner's property, which is invalid.
Hanafis consider hiba al-musha' invalid unless possession distinguishes shares.
5
Apply to modern scenarios
The principle applies when a donor transfers a part of an asset that is still owned by them; the gift is invalid for the part.
For example, mixing gifted property with owned property before delivery.

Ruling with proof: gift valid, condition void

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a gift with a condition that contradicts its essence and explain why the gift remains valid while the condition is void.

Core Idea
A gift with a condition opposing its nature is valid; the condition is void.

Gifts are acts of pure generosity. Sometimes a donor attaches a condition that contradicts the very nature of a gift—such as requiring the donee to return it later. In Hanafi law, the gift remains valid while the condition is nullified. This lesson explains the ruling, the reasoning, and the proof from the Sunnah, distinguishing gifts from sales where invalid conditions may void the contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenarios
The passage lists several conditions placed on a gift: return of the gifted item, freeing a slave, making a slave an umm walad, or giving something in return. Recognize that these conditions all conflict with the essence of a gift (unconditional transfer).
2
State the ruling for each scenario
In every case, the Hanafi ruling is that the gift is valid (ja'iz) and the condition is void (batil). Note that the ruling is consistent across all examples.
3
Understand the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The reason is that the conditions contradict the muqtada al-'aqd (the inherent requirement of a gift contract). Since a gift is a pure transfer without counterpayment, any condition that imposes a return obligation is incompatible and thus invalid. However, the gift itself is not nullified by void conditions.

Examine the dalil (proof) from Sunnah
The author cites the Prophet's (peace be upon him) validation of 'umra (a lifelong gift) while voiding the condition of return. This shows that a void condition does not nullify a gift.

Why Conditional Release of Debt is Void
Ruling with proof explaining why conditional ibra' is void due to its hybrid nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that conditional ibra' is batil and distinguish pure isqatat from hybrid acts.

Core Idea
Conditional release of debt is void because ibra' is part tamlik, not a pure isqat.

Can a creditor say, 'If you pay half, I forgive the rest' and have it be legally effective? In the Hanafi school, such a conditional release is void. The reason lies in the nature of ibra' (release of debt): it is not a pure relinquishment but a mix of ownership transfer and relinquishment. Only pure relinquishments, like divorce and emancipation, can be made conditional. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The passage clearly states that any conditional ibra' (e.g., 'when tomorrow comes, it is yours' or 'if you pay half, the rest is forgiven') is batil (void). Recognize that this is a ruling of nullity, not prohibition.
2
Understand ibra''s dual nature
The author explains that ibra' is tamlik from one perspective (because debt is property) and isqat from another (because it cancels the obligation). This hybrid nature distinguishes it from pure isqatat.
Key evidence: ibra' is revocable upon return of the debt (yaruddu bi al-radd) and does not require acceptance – both signs of its tamlik aspect.
3
Apply the rule of ta'liq
Hanafi fiqh restricts conditional suspension (ta'liq bil-shart) to pure isqatat (isqatat mahda) such as talaq and itq. Since ibra' is not pure, it cannot be conditional.
Pure isqatat are those used in oaths and completely remove a right without any property transfer.
4
Distinguish the cross-madhhab view
Shafi'is treat ibra' as pure isqat, so they might allow conditional ibra'. Recognize that the Hanafi ruling rests on a specific conceptualization of ibra'.
The practical difference: under Shafi'i law, a conditional forgiveness could be valid.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. The author deals with cases where a creditor makes a conditional release of a debt: e.g., 'if tomorrow comes, the debt is yours,' or 'if you pay half, the rest is yours.' All such conditional releases are void (batil). The reasoning: ibra' (release of a debt) is a hybrid: it is a form of tamlik (transfer of ownership) because the debt is considered property, and also a form of isqat (relinquishment) because it cancels the obligation. Since it is not a pure isqat, it cannot be made conditional on a future event like pure isqatat (e.g., divorce and emancipation). Pure isqatat are those that can be used in oaths (e.g., 'if you do X, you are divorced/free'). Therefore, conditional suspension only applies to those pure relinquishments. The author also notes that ibra' is revocable by the creditor returning the debt (i.e., if the debtor returns the debt, the ibra' is nullified), and it does not require acceptance by the debtor, showing its mixed nature.

Hiba with Void Conditions: Valid Gift, Void Condition
Ruling with proof: hiba with void conditions valid, contrast with sale, 'umra example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a gift (hiba) with a void condition is valid, distinguish it from a sale (bay') with a void condition, and apply this ruling to the case of 'umra.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a void condition in a gift does not invalidate the gift—only the condition is void.

This passage from Al-Hidayah establishes a fundamental distinction between gifts and sales when a void condition is attached. The Prophet's (peace be upon him) permission for 'umra (life grant) serves as the primary textual proof. We will unpack the ruling, the proof, the rational reasoning, and the practical application to 'umra, ensuring you understand why the gift remains valid while only the condition is void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Read the passage and determine what act (hiba, sadaqa, 'umra) is being ruled upon and what the ruling is (valid/invalid). Note the key contrast with sale.
The passage lists several examples of void conditions (e.g., 'return the slave-girl', 'give back a part of the house'). The ruling is that the gift is valid, the condition void.
2
Locate the proof (dalil naqli)
Find the hadith evidence cited: the Prophet's permission for 'umra, which is a gift with a condition of return. The hadith shows the condition is void, the gift remains.
The main proof is the Hadith: 'Al-'umra is valid' (al-Shaykhayn). The commentary also includes the story of the woman's garden.
3

The passage explicitly contrasts hiba with sale: a void condition in sale invalidates the sale (makes it fasid). Identify the reason: gifts are benevolence (tabarru'), not exchange (mu'awada), so there is no risk of riba or imbalance.
The rational reason (ta'lil) given: gifts do not involve exchange, so void conditions do not render them void.
4
Apply to 'umra (life grant)
Apply the general principle to 'umra: a grant of property for life with condition of return is valid; the condition of return is void, so the property becomes an absolute gift to the recipient and his heirs.
The commentary discusses three forms of 'umra. Focus on the first form (explicit for heirs) which is valid by consensus.
5
Note the scope and ikhtilaf
Recognize that the ruling applies to all void conditions (shart fasid) in gifts. Note the Shafi'i difference on absolute 'umra (second form) but the Hanafi position is clear.
The relied-upon Hanafi position is that all forms of 'umra are valid, and the condition is void.

First, he says: 'I give you this house on 'umra; when you die, it belongs to your heirs or your descendants.' This 'umra is valid without dispute, and it is like a gift. In this case, after the death of the donee, the house becomes the property of his heirs. If there are no heirs, it goes to the public treasury; the donor does not get it back. This ruling is mentioned in the narration of Imam Muslim from Ibn Shihab from Abu Salama from Jabir, who said: I heard the Messenger of Allah saying: 'Whoever makes a 'umra for a man and his descendants, he has cut off his right to it; it belongs to the one who was given it and his descendants.' 

Second, he says absolutely: 'I have made it for you for your life; this is for you as 'umra,' without adding anything. There are two opinions of Imam al-Shafi'i on this. The more correct is that it is also valid and its ruling is the same as the first, as mentioned in the narration of Muslim from Ibn Jurayj from Abu al-Zubayr from Jabir: A woman in Medina gave her son a garden on 'umra. Then the son died, and after him the woman died, leaving a child and brothers (sons of the donatrix). The child of the donee said: 'The garden returns to us.' The sons of the donatrix said: 'It belonged to our brother during his life and after his death.' They brought the case to Tariq, the freed slave of Uthman. He called Jabir and he testified on the authority of the Messenger of Allah regarding 'umra that it belongs to the one to whom it is given. So Tariq judged accordingly.

Commentary
This passage covers two major rulings: (1) Hiba (gift) with void conditions is valid; only the conditions are void. This is contrasted with sale (bay'), where void conditions invalidate the contract. The rational reason given is that gifts are acts of benevolence and do not involve exchange (mu'awada), so there is no possibility of riba or imbalance. (2) 'Umra (life grant) is a specific application. The matn from Al-Hidayah states that 'umra is valid for the recipient and his heirs, and the condition of return upon the recipient's death is void. The commentary explains three forms of 'umra: the most common (explicit for heirs) is valid by consensus; the absolute form (without mention of heirs) is also valid according to the relied-upon view, supported by the hadith of the woman's garden. The passage also includes a detailed scenario from hadith to illustrate the ruling.

Key Terms
عمری (umra): A gift of property for the duration of the recipient's life, with the condition that it returns to the donor upon the recipient's death. This condition is void, so the gift is absolute.
شرط فاسد (shart fasid): A condition that contradicts the nature of the contract (mujtada al-aqd) but does not involve a forbidden thing.
مقتضائے عقد (muqtada al-aqd): The inherent requirements of the contract; i.e., what the contract necessarily implies.

Ruling on 'Umra (Lifetime Gift) and Validity of Conditional Revocation
Inter-madhhab dispute with hadith analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on 'Umra, explain why the condition of return is void, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using hadith proofs.

Core Idea
An 'Umra is a valid full gift; any condition of return is void because the gift is subject to inheritance.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a classical dispute: when someone gives property for the donee's lifetime, does the donee become full owner or merely a usufructuary? The Hanafi school insists on full ownership transfer, based on explicit hadiths that treat 'Umra as an absolute gift. The passage presents three forms of 'Umra, the positions of various schools, and the Hanafi proof from Sahih Muslim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three forms of 'Umra
Note the three variations in wording: (1) absolute gift for life, (2) gift for life and then to descendants, (3) gift for life with condition of return to giver or his heirs. Understand that the Hanafi ruling applies uniformly to all three.
The third form is also called ruqba.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that all three forms are valid gifts transferring ownership of the corpus; any condition of return is void. The property belongs to the donee and passes to his heirs.
3
Examine the key hadith proof
Read the hadith of Jabir in Sahih Muslim: 'Whoever gives a life-gift, it belongs to the one to whom it is given, alive or dead, and to his descendants.' Identify the operative phrase that indicates full ownership.
Also the hadith 'Hold on to your properties...'
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i old opinion
The Shafi'i old opinion (and Maliki) holds that 'Umra transfers only usufruct, and the corpus returns to the giver at death. Their proof is the hadith: 'The 'umra which the Messenger of Allah permitted is when he says: It is for you and your descendants. But if he says: It is for you as long as you live, then it returns to its owner.'
5
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response argues that the Shafi'i-proof hadith is not a general rule but refers to a specific case. Clearer hadiths from Jabir show that absolute 'Umra makes the donee owner. The condition of return is void because the gift becomes subject to inheritance, which cuts off the condition.
The story of the woman and her son illustrates this.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute concerning the validity and effect of 'umra (a lifetime gift). The author presents various opinions within the Shafi'i school and then provides the Hanafi ruling with extensive hadith evidence. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on Ruqba, explain the evidence for each view, and identify the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Ruqba — a gift conditional on who dies first — is invalid by Zahir al-Riwayah but Abu Yusuf deems it valid, and later fatwa may follow him.

The concept of Ruqba may seem like a simple estate planning tool, but it raised a significant intra-Hanafi dispute. This passage from Al-Hidayah examines the views of the school's founding imams, their proofs from hadith and linguistic analysis, and the practical consequences of each ruling. By understanding this dispute, you will appreciate how the school navigates conflicting textual evidence and internal disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Define Ruqba and identify the legal question
Understand the transaction: one person says to another, 'This property is ruqba for you,' meaning that whichever dies first, the property goes to the survivor. The core question is whether such a conditional gift (ta'liq al-tamlik bi al-khatar) is valid.
The word derives from muraqabah (watching), implying each awaits the other's death.

They rule that Ruqba is invalid (batil). Their evidence is an athar that the Prophet allowed 'Umra (life grant) but rejected Ruqba. They also argue that the condition suspends ownership on a contingency, which is invalid. The transaction thus becomes a loan ('ariyah) because unrestricted usufruct is implied.
This is the position of Zahir al-Riwayah.
3

Abu Yusuf rules that Ruqba is permissible (ja'iz). He argues that the statement 'My house is for you' is a valid transfer of ownership, and the mention of 'Ruqba' is merely a void condition, analogous to 'Umra. Several hadiths explicitly permit Ruqba (e.g., from Jabir, Ibn Abbas).
Other scholars like Shafi'i, Ahmad, and Sufyan al-Thawri share this view.
4
Analyze the response of the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) to Abu Yusuf's evidence
They contend that the hadiths permitting Ruqba are either weak or interpreted differently. The athar rejecting Ruqba is stronger. Moreover, the very meaning of 'Ruqba' involves contingency (khatar), which is invalid.
The later 'benefit' section reconciles the narrations: permissibility applies when ownership is immediate with a void condition, invalidity when ownership is suspended on the donor's prior death.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
If invalid, the property remains with the recipient as a loan (must return upon demand or death). If valid, the recipient becomes absolute owner, and the condition is void. The mufta bihi according to the text: the classical position is invalidity, but later scholars like the author of Ghayat al-Bayan preferred Abu Yusuf's view as more correct.
A mufti today may choose either view depending on the context and strength of evidence.

Is Ruqba invalid? Rulings of jurists. And Ruqba is invalid according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, and Abu Yusuf said it is permissible because his statement 'My house is for you' is a transfer of ownership, and his statement 'Ruqbi' is an invalid condition like 'Umra. And their (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad's) evidence is that the Prophet (peace be upon him) allowed 'Umra and rejected Ruqba, and because the meaning of Ruqbi according to them is 'If I die before you, it is for you', and the word is from 'muraqabah' (watching), as if he watches for his death, and this is suspending ownership on a contingency, so it is invalid. And when it is not valid, it becomes a loan ('ariyah) according to them because it implies unrestricted usufruct. Translation: And Ruqba is invalid according to the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), and Imam Abu Yusuf says it is permissible because his statement 'My house is for you' is a transfer of ownership, and his saying 'Ruqbi' is an invalid condition like 'Umra. The evidence of the two is that the Prophet (peace be upon him) allowed 'Umra and rejected Ruqba, and because the meaning of Ruqbi according to them is 'If I die before you, it is for you', and this word is from 'muraqabah', meaning he watches his death, and this is suspending ownership on a contingency, so it is invalid. And when Ruqba is not valid, it becomes 'ariyah (loan) according to them because it implies unrestricted usufruct. Explanation of his statement 'And Ruqba is invalid': Ruqba is that the owner says 'My house is Ruqba for you'. Its ruling: According to the two (Hasan ibn Ziyad and Imam Malik), it is invalid, and according to Imam Abu Yusuf, Sufyan al-Thawri, Ishaq, Imam Shafi'i, and Imam Ahmad, it is permissible. Because Imam Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, and Ibn Majah narrated from Jabir that the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'Umra is permissible for its people and Ruqba is permissible for its people.' Also Imam Nasa'i narrated from Ibn Abbas marfu'an: 'Whoever gives a life grant, it is permissible for the one to whom it is given, and whoever gives a ruqba, it is permissible for the one to whom it is given.' Similarly, Imam Nasa'i and Ibn Majah narrated from Ibn Umar marfu'an: 'No 'Umra and no Ruqba; whoever gives a life grant or a ruqba, it is his in life and death.' Also because when he says 'My house is for you', ownership is established, and stipulating Ruqba is an invalid condition like the condition in 'Umra, so the gift is valid and the condition is void. His statement 'and for them (the two) that...' The evidence of the two is that the Prophet (peace be upon him) allowed 'Umra and rejected Ruqba. In al-Mabsut, it is reported that this athar was narrated by Sha'bi from Qadi Shurayh. Also because according to the two, the meaning of Ruqba is: If I die before you, this house is yours, and if you die before me, it is mine. This word is derived from 'muraqabah' meaning waiting, as if each is waiting for the other's death. So this is suspending ownership on a contingency, which is not permissible. Therefore, the suspension is invalid, and when the suspension is invalid, Ruqba is not valid. Then the house that was given as Ruqba is 'ariyah (loan) with him because giving it absolutely implies obtaining usufruct. Based on this, the statement of Imam Abu Yusuf is strong, and the author of Ghayat al-Bayan considered it more correct. Benefit: The author of I'la al-Sunan wrote that the previous texts are explicit that 'Umra and Ruqba are both permissible, as Imam Abu Yusuf and the majority hold. As for Imam Abu Hanifa, Ibn Hazm did not mention any disagreement from him, rather he considered his statement to be like the majority's permissibility. But the correct narration from Imam Abu Hanifa is that Ruqba is invalid. Now if this means that the gift is absolutely valid and effective, and the condition of return to the donor or to his heirs after the donee's death is void, then there is no dispute. And if it means that Ruqba is invalid and the gift is not valid, then its effect is that the donee's ownership is suspended upon the donor's death. In this case, the ruling of invalidity from Imam Abu Hanifa and the ruling of permissibility mentioned in previous narrations are not contradictory, because the permissibility in the narrations is when the donee's ownership is immediate, but there is a condition of return to the donor after the donee's death. And the ruling of invalidity from Imam Abu Hanifa is when the donee's ownership is suspended upon the donor's prior death, so no contradiction. Moreover, there is also the meaning that 'Ruqba is when a person says: It is for the other, from me and from you.' Similarly, Hafiz Tabarani narrated in al-Mu'jam al-Awsat from Abdullah ibn Umar: 'Ruqba is when a person says: It is for the other, from me and from you.' (There is a narrator in its chain who is disputed, but Ibn Ma'in declared him trustworthy as per Majma' al-Zawa'id.) It is clear that in this meaning, Ruqba is invalid because it involves suspending ownership on a contingency.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of Ruqba, a conditional gift where ownership is contingent on who dies first. The author begins by stating the positions: Ruqba is invalid according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, while Abu Yusuf deems it permissible. Abu Yusuf's reasoning is that the donor's statement 'My house is for you' is a valid transfer of ownership, and the subsequent mention of 'Ruqba' is an invalid condition similar to that in 'Umra, so the gift stands with the condition void. In contrast, the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) rely on a reported athar that the Prophet (peace be upon him) allowed 'Umra but rejected Ruqba. They also analyze the linguistic meaning: 'Ruqba' is derived from 'muraqabah' (watching), implying that each party waits for the other's death, thus the ownership is suspended on a contingency (ta'liq al-tamlik bi al-khatar), which is invalid. When the condition is void, the transaction becomes a loan of usufruct ('ariyah) because the property is given for unrestricted benefit. The author then broadens the discussion to other schools: Hasan ibn Ziyad and Imam Malik hold invalidity, while Abu Yusuf, Sufyan al-Thawri, Ishaq, Shafi'i, and Ahmad hold permissibility, citing hadiths that explicitly declare Ruqba permissible (e.g., from Jabir, Ibn Abbas, Ibn Umar). To reconcile the conflicting narrations, a later 'benefit' (fa'idah) section clarifies that the dispute hinges on whether the ownership is immediate or contingent. The permissibility narrations apply when ownership is immediate but the donor stipulates return after the donee's death. The invalidity ruling from Abu Hanifa applies when ownership is suspended on the condition of the donor's prior death. Another interpretation of Ruqba (e.g., 'it is for the other from me and from you' as reported by Tabarani) also involves suspending ownership on a contingency, thus invalid. The text notes that Abu Yusuf's view is considered strong by the author of Ghayat al-Bayan.

Key Terms
الرقبى (al-ruqba): A conditional gift where each party says if the other dies first, the property goes to the survivor. The word is derived from 'muraqabah' (watching).
تعليق التمليك (ta'liq al-tamlik): Suspending the transfer of ownership upon a future uncertain event (risk/contingency).

Ruling on Ruqba: Conditional Gift Invalid in Hanafi School
Intra-Hanafi dispute over validity of Ruqba gift with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on Ruqba, identify the intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, and distinguish valid from invalid conditional gifts based on the principle of immediate ownership.

Core Idea
Ruqba is invalid if ownership is suspended on a risk; valid if immediate with a voidable condition.

The concept of Ruqba—a conditional gift where the giver says 'this is for you if I die before you'—sparks a notable intra-Hanafi dispute. While the Zahir al-Riwayah position declares it invalid, Imam Abu Yusuf argues for its validity based on hadith and analogy with 'Umra. This lesson unpacks the proofs, the underlying principle of suspending ownership on risk, and the practical resolution for modern gift transactions.

Guided Path
1
Define Ruqba and 'Umra
Understand the precise definitions: Ruqba involves mutual waiting (muraqabah) with ownership contingent on survival; 'Umra is a lifetime gift without reversion.
The linguistic root of Ruqba (raqaba = watch) indicates the uncertainty.
2
Identify the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: Ruqba is invalid (batil). Abu Yusuf: Ruqba is valid. Note that Abu Yusuf's view aligns with the majority of other schools.
The Zahir al-Riwayah is the ruling of the two, but later scholars weigh Abu Yusuf's view.
3
Analyze the proofs
Examine the hadith evidence: the prohibition hadith 'La 'umra wa la ruqba' vs. the permission hadith 'al-'umra ja'izah... wa al-ruqba ja'izah'. Discuss the reasoning about suspending ownership on risk (ta'liq 'alal-khatar).
The key is whether the condition prevents immediate ownership.
4
Apply the resolution
From al-Kafi's principle: if the condition prevents immediate ownership, the gift is invalid; otherwise, the gift is valid and condition void. Use this to assess real cases.
This reconciles the conflicting views.

Definition and Ruling of 'Umra and Ruqba
Defining 'Umra and Ruqba and stating their rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'Umra and Ruqba and state their respective rulings in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
In 'Umra, the condition of reversion is void but the gift stands; in Ruqba, the condition suspends ownership, so the gift is void.

This lesson distinguishes two types of conditional gifts: 'Umra and Ruqba. While both involve a condition about death, their rulings differ fundamentally based on whether the condition prevents immediate ownership. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the Hanafi position on such gifts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracts
The passage defines two types of conditional gifts: 'Umra (life-grant) and Ruqba (survivorship-grant). Note the Arabic phrasing for each.
'Umra uses 'for your life'; Ruqba uses 'for the last of us to die'.
2
Parse the wording of each
For 'Umra: 'I have made this house for you for your life; when you die it returns to me.' For Ruqba: 'This house is for the last of us to die.'
The wording shows the condition: in 'Umra it is a reversion after death; in Ruqba it is a contest of survival.
3
Apply the legal principle
The key principle: a condition that prevents immediate ownership invalidates the gift. In 'Umra, the condition does not prevent immediate ownership (the donee owns the house during his life), so the gift is valid. In Ruqba, the condition suspends ownership until an uncertain future event (who dies last), so the condition prevents immediate ownership, making the gift invalid.
The Hanafi school considers the condition in 'Umra void, so the donee becomes absolute owner; the property goes to his heirs after his death.
4
State the rulings
'Umra is valid (sahih); Ruqba is invalid (batil).
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the matn on charity (sadaqah) from al-Hidayah. The author begins by clarifying a principle: a condition in a gift (hibah) that prevents immediate transfer of ownership invalidates the gift, but a condition that does not prevent immediate transfer only invalidates the condition, not the gift. This is illustrated by 'umra (life tenancy) where the condition is valid because it does not prevent immediate ownership; the gift is valid. Then the main topic: sadaqah (charity) is analogous to hibah. It requires possession (qabd) to be valid because it is also a donation. The text then lists several rules: (1) Charity of undivided property (musha') that is divisible is not valid because it would force the beneficiary into a partnership they did not consent to. (2) Unlike hibah, charity cannot be revoked because the intended reward (thawab) has already been obtained in the sense that Allah has promised to reward the act. Some raise the objection that reward is in the Hereafter, but the text considers the promise of reward as certain. (3) Giving charity to a rich person is valid by istihsan (juristic preference) because the donor may still seek reward. Similarly, giving a gift to a poor person is also valid. Then the text transitions to a different topic: the vow (nadhr) to give charity. It is mentioned here but the detailed discussion follows on the next page. Finally, the definitions of 'umra and ruqba are given: 'umra is a gift for life that returns to the giver upon the recipient's death; it is valid because it does not prevent immediate ownership. Ruqba is a gift conditioned on the death of one of the parties being first; it is invalid because it prevents immediate ownership by making ownership contingent on an uncertain future event.

Vowing with 'Mali' vs 'Milki': Determining the Obligation
Expands the Hidayah matn on vows of charity, distinguishing between mal and milk.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between 'mal' and 'milk' to determine what is obligatory in a vow to give charity.

Core Idea
The scope of a charity vow depends on the word: 'mal' limits to zakatable assets; 'milk' includes everything owned.

When someone vows to give charity with their wealth, the extent of their obligation is not immediately obvious. The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes between the term 'mal' (zakatable wealth) and the broader 'milk' (all property, including non-wealth rights). This lesson unpacks that distinction and contrasts the Hanafi position with views of other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the vow's wording
Determine whether the person used 'mali' (my wealth) or 'milki' (my property). This single word changes the obligation.
2
Define 'mal' vs 'milk'
Understand that in Hanafi law, 'mal' is confined to zakatable assets (cash, trade goods, livestock, produce). 'Milk' is broader, including rights like marriage and retribution.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
If 'mali' is used, only zakatable property must be given. If 'milki' is used, all property including rights must be given.
This is based on the generality of 'milk' in Arabic.
4
Compare with other schools
Note that Imam Zufar and Ahmad (one narration) require all wealth for 'mali'; Shafi'i and Malik require one-third; Shami says nothing is obligatory. Identify the differences.
This shows the ikhtilaf.
5
Check the practical outcome
The mufta bihi Hanafi view is that the person keeps necessities if he becomes destitute. This is covered in the next lesson.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the matn's ruling on a vow (nadhr) to give charity. The text distinguishes between a vow regarding 'my wealth' (mali) and 'my property' (milki). 'Wealth' (mal) is specifically the type subject to zakat: cash, trade goods, grazing livestock, and 'ushri produce. If someone vows to give charity with his wealth, he must give from these categories only. Other schools hold different views: Zufar and Ahmad (in one narration) make all wealth obligatory; Shami says nothing is obligatory; Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad (in another narration) limit it to one-third like a will. If the vow uses the term 'my property' (milki), which is more general and includes non-wealth rights (like marriage, retribution, wine), then all property must be given in charity. There is also a narration that mal and milk are equivalent. The author notes that the difference between mal and milk is explained elsewhere. The practical solution: if the person becomes destitute, he is allowed to keep enough for his and his family's basic needs until he can earn, and then he must give charity with the amount he kept. The scholars specify that a merchant keeps one month's provisions; a laborer keeps one day's provisions because his income is daily.

ملک (milk): Property or ownership in a broader sense, including non-wealth rights like marriage and retribution.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi concession for a nadhir who becomes destitute, determining the amount of subsistence allowed and the obligation to compensate thereafter.

Core Idea
A destitute nadhir may keep enough for basic needs until he earns, then pay the kept amount.

When a vow to give all property leaves a person destitute, the Hanafi school provides a compassionate concession. The person may retain enough to support himself and his family until he can earn. This lesson details the ruling and how scholars determine the amount based on profession.

Guided Path
1
Identify when the concession applies
The person has vowed to give all his property (milki) and now has nothing left to survive.
2
Determine the amount of subsistence
The person may keep enough to cover basic needs until he can earn. According to the mashayikh, a merchant keeps one month's provisions; a laborer keeps one day's food.
The difference is based on how quickly income replenishes.
3
Understand the obligation to compensate
When the person later earns wealth, he must give charity with the exact amount he had kept for subsistence.
This is not a waiver but a deferral.
4
Note the authority weight
The concession is from the later scholars (mashayikh), not from the Zahir al-Riwayah texts by Imam Muhammad. It is mufta bihi.
Imam Muhammad did not specify amounts in his books.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the matn's ruling on a vow (nadhr) to give charity. The text distinguishes between a vow regarding 'my wealth' (mali) and 'my property' (milki). 'Wealth' (mal) is specifically the type subject to zakat: cash, trade goods, grazing livestock, and 'ushri produce. If someone vows to give charity with his wealth, he must give from these categories only. Other schools hold different views: Zufar and Ahmad (in one narration) make all wealth obligatory; Shami says nothing is obligatory; Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad (in another narration) limit it to one-third like a will. If the vow uses the term 'my property' (milki), which is more general and includes non-wealth rights (like marriage, retribution, wine), then all property must be given in charity. There is also a narration that mal and milk are equivalent. The author notes that the difference between mal and milk is explained elsewhere. The practical solution: if the person becomes destitute, he is allowed to keep enough for his and his family's basic needs until he can earn, and then he must give charity with the amount he kept. The scholars specify that a merchant keeps one month's provisions; a laborer keeps one day's provisions because his income is daily.

Ruling on Sadaqah to a Wealthy Person and Its Revocability
Explains ruling on sadaqah to wealthy and its revocation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the revocability of sadaqah given to a wealthy person and explain why the wording takes precedence over the recipient's state.

Core Idea
Sadaqah to a wealthy person is irrevocable because the wording indicates seeking divine reward, not worldly return.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, we examine a nuanced ruling: if you give charity (sadaqah) to a wealthy person, can you ever take it back? While common sense says 'why give to the rich?', the Hanafi school rules that the very word 'sadaqah' makes it irrevocable. This lesson unpacks the proof from the Quranic promise and the intra-madhhab dispute on this issue.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is giving sadaqah to a wealthy person. The ruling is that it cannot be revoked (no ruju').
2
Locate the proof
The passage cites the Quranic principle that Allah does not break His promise, implying that the reward for sadaqah is certain, which strengthens irrevocability.
The exact phrase: 'لان الله تعالى لا يخلف الميعاد'
3
Understand the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi scholars analogize sadaqah to a wealthy person to hibah, allowing revocation because the giver's aim is recompense. But the majority relies on the wording.
These are referred to as 'ba'd ashabina' – some of our companions.
4
Evaluate the tarjih
The tarjih favors the wording over the state of the recipient. The word 'sadaqah' itself implies no worldly iwad, and this is given precedence.
The passage states: 'مراعاة لفظه أولى من مراعاة حال التملك' – considering the wording is more important than considering the state of possession.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Book of Gift. It addresses the issue of giving sadaqah (charity) to a wealthy person (ghani). The commentator explains that the attainment mentioned refers to the promised reward, which is certain based on the Quranic principle that Allah does not break His promise. The matn states that just as sadaqah given to a poor person is irrevocable, so is sadaqah given to a wealthy person. This ruling is based on the principle of beneficence (bina bar ihsan), though analogy would suggest revocation is permissible since the wealthy person does not need it. Some Hanafi scholars indeed hold that sadaqah and hibah (gift) to a wealthy person are equal and both revocable, because the giver's aim is to obtain recompense (thawab). However, the relied-upon position (madhhab) is that it is irrevocable by consensus (bil-ittifaq). The reasoning: the very use of the word 'sadaqah' indicates the absence of intention for worldly recompense, and the wording takes precedence over the state of the recipient's possession. Thus, the Hanafi position is that such sadaqah is irrevocable.

Key Terms
صدقہ (sadaqah): Voluntary charity given with the intention of seeking reward from Allah.
رجوع (ruju'): Revocation or taking back of a gift or charity after it has been given.
عوض (iwad): Recompense or return; in this context, worldly compensation or reward.
