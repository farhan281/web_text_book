Sulh from Property for Usufruct: Rulings and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains sulh of property for usufruct, its ijarah analogy, and the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a sulh for usufructs is treated like ijarah, state the condition of duration, and contrast the positions of Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf regarding the effect of death on the contract.

Core Idea
A sulh exchanging property for usufructs is essentially an ijarah: it requires a fixed term and ends upon death — but Imam Abu Yusuf made an exception for dwelling and slave service.

This lesson examines a specific type of sulh (compromise) where a claim for property is settled by granting the plaintiff a usufruct. The Hanafi school treats this as a form of ijarah (lease) because the essential meaning is the same: transferring ownership of benefits in exchange for payment. Therefore, conditions like specifying a duration apply, and the contract is generally voided by the death of either party. However, within the Hanafi school, there is a notable difference between Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf on the scope of this analogy. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the principles of qiyas and recognize the flexibility within the school when the purpose of the contract (cutting off dispute) interacts with the rules of ijarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type and core ruling
The passage rules that sulh for usufructs is treated as ijarah. The legal basis is the existence of the meaning of ijarah: tamlik al-manafi' bi mal. Contracts are considered by their meanings.
2
State the conditions derived from ijarah
Since it's like ijarah, a fixed duration (taqwit) is required. Without specifying the term, the sulh is invalid (fasid).
Examples from the passage: dwelling for a year, riding for a specific journey, etc.
3
Determine the effect of death per general rule
According to the analogy to ijarah, death of either party voids the contract. This is the position of Imam Muhammad (zahir al-riwayah). The subject matter's destruction also voids it.
Partial enjoyment extinguishes part of the claim proportionally.
4
Examine Imam Abu Yusuf's exception
Imam Abu Yusuf distinguishes between types of usufructs. For dwelling in a house and service of a slave, death does not invalidate because the benefit is standard and can continue with heirs. For riding an animal and wearing clothes, death voids because personal usage varies.
His reasoning: sulh is meant to cut off dispute; voiding it upon death revives the dispute, which is contrary to the purpose.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The matn (Al-Hidayah) follows Imam Muhammad's position, making it the relied-upon view for fatwa. The stronger analogy to ijarah takes precedence over the purpose of the contract in this case.
Know the thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in outcome when a party dies.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on Sulh (compromise) discusses the ruling when a sulh agreement involves property being exchanged for usufructs (manāfiʿ). The author states that such a sulh is treated like an ijarah (lease) contract because it shares the same essential meaning: transferring ownership of usufructs in exchange for property. In Islamic law, contracts are judged by their underlying meanings and purposes, not just their names. Therefore, the conditions of ijarah apply: a fixed duration must be specified (taqwīt), and the contract becomes void (bāṭil) upon the death of either party during the term. The commentary elaborates with examples: if someone claims a house and settles for dwelling in it for a year, or riding an animal, or wearing clothes, or using a slave's services, or cultivating land, each is like an ijarah. The duration must be specified; if it is said 'forever' or 'until death', the sulh is invalid. A benefit (fāʾidah) is then mentioned: The view that such sulh is entirely like ijarah is that of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybānī. According to him, death of either party or destruction of the subject matter (the thing providing the usufruct) before the usufruct has been enjoyed makes the sulh invalid (fāsid). The plaintiff can revert to his original claim, but if he has already enjoyed some usufruct, that portion extinguishes a corresponding part of the claim. In contrast, Imam Abū Yūsuf holds that this sulh is not entirely analogous to ijarah. He argues that sulh is meant to end disputes; declaring it void upon death would revive the dispute. Therefore, in cases of dwelling in a house or service of a slave, if the defendant dies, the sulh remains valid and the plaintiff can still enjoy the usufruct (or his heir can take his place). However, in cases of riding an animal or wearing clothes, which are personal and usage varies among people, the sulh becomes void upon death because the heir cannot substitute. The passage concludes by citing Kifāyah in support.

Key Terms
صلح (ṣulḥ): A contract to end a dispute by mutual agreement, which can take various forms depending on the subject matter.
منافع (manāfiʿ): Usufructs; the benefits or services derived from a property or asset.
إجارة (ijārah): A lease contract where one party transfers ownership of usufructs in exchange for payment.
توقيت (taqwīt): Specification of a duration or time limit in a contract.

Borrower Cannot Sub-Rent the Borrowed Item
Ruling with proof: Borrower cannot sub-rent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a borrower renting out a borrowed item and explain the liability options for the lender.

Core Idea
The borrower cannot sub-rent because loan is inferior to rental; doing so makes him a usurper and the lender can choose whom to hold liable.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced ruling from the Hanafi school regarding the limits of a borrower's authority. While the borrower has permission to use the item, he does not have the right to transfer that right to another through a rental contract. The passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains the reasoning and the liability framework if the item is destroyed.

Commentary
This passage rules that the borrower (musta'ir) may not sub-rent the borrowed item. The reasoning is twofold. First, loan (ariyah) is legally inferior to rental (ijarah); a lesser contract cannot encompass a greater one. Second, if sub-rental were valid, it would have to be binding, which would prevent the lender from reclaiming the item until the lease expires, causing the lender undue harm. Therefore, the sub-rental is void. If the borrower nonetheless rents it out and the item is destroyed, he becomes liable for it because his possession without the lender's authorization becomes usurpation (ghasb). The lender has a choice: he can either hold the borrower liable directly, in which case the borrower cannot recover from the lessee (since the borrower is treated as having rented his own property), or he can hold the lessee liable, in which case the lessee can seek recourse from the borrower if the lessee was unaware that the item was borrowed, to prevent deception. This nuance protects the innocent lessee who was misled.

Key Terms
اجارہ (ijarah): A contract of lease or hire, which is binding and has a fixed term.
غصب (ghasb): Usurpation; wrongful possession of another's property without permission. Here, the borrower becomes a usurper if he rents out the item without authorization.
غرور (ghurur): Deception; the lessee is deceived into thinking the borrower had authority to lease.

Prohibition of Borrower Subleasing the Borrowed Item
Ruling with proof on borrower subleasing and resulting liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a borrower subleasing a borrowed item, explain the two proofs (inferiority of loan and harm principle), and identify the liability scenarios for the borrower, sub-lessee, and lender.

Core Idea
A borrower cannot sublease because loan is inferior to lease; a lesser right cannot grant a greater right.

Imagine you lend your car to a friend. Can that friend then rent it out to someone else for profit? The Hanafi school says no, and this passage from the Hidayah explains why, using a combination of contractual analysis and harm principle. Understanding this rule protects the lender's right to revoke the loan and prevents unauthorized exploitation of borrowed property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being prohibited? The act of the borrower (musta'ir) leasing the borrowed item to a third party. The ruling is that it is not permissible (haram).
2
Examine the first proof: loan inferior to lease
The author argues that i'arah (loan) is inferior (dun) to ijarah (lease). Since the borrower only has a gratuitous right to use, he cannot transfer a superior right (lease for consideration).
This is an analogy: a lesser right cannot encompass a greater one.
3
Examine the second proof: harm to the lender
If the lease were valid, it would be binding (lazim). This would close the door of revocation for the lender until the lease expires, causing increased harm (ziyadat al-darar).
The lender's right to revoke the loan at any time is fundamental.
4
Understand the liability outcome
If the borrower subleases and the item is destroyed, he becomes liable (daman) at the moment of delivery to the sub-lessee because the delivery without authority constitutes ghasb (usurpation).
The borrower is not merely a trespasser; he becomes a usurper.
5
Analyze the lender's options and the sub-lessee's recourse
The lender may claim compensation from either the borrower or the sub-lessee. If from the borrower, the borrower cannot recover from the sub-lessee. If from the sub-lessee, the sub-lessee can recover from the borrower if he was ignorant of the borrowing; otherwise not.
This is based on the principle of deception (ghurur).
6
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The three (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar) hold that the sub-lessee cannot recover from the borrower at all, because he is a second usurper and liable for his own act.
This is a minority view; the mufta bihi is the first view allowing recovery if ignorant.

A question may arise: When the borrower owns the benefit from the lender, it should be permissible for him to lease it, because his disposal would remain and the lender's right of revocation would remain, which would invalidate the lease. The explanation is that the lease would be either binding or non-binding. If non-binding, it contradicts the nature of lease (which is binding). Commentary
This passage states the ruling that a borrower (musta'ir) is not permitted to lease (sublease) the borrowed item to a third party. If he does so and the item is destroyed, he becomes liable for its value. The reasoning is based on two major proofs: First, a loan (i'arah) is a gratuitous transfer of benefit, while a lease (ijarah) is a remunerative contract involving consideration. Since the borrower only has permission to use the item for free, he cannot grant a higher right (lease) to another. In legal terms, a lesser right cannot encompass a greater one. Second, if the lease were considered valid, it would have to be a binding contract (lazim) by nature, which would prevent the lender (mu'ir) from revoking the loan until the lease expires. This causes increased harm to the lender. Therefore, the lease is invalid (batil). When a borrower subleases and delivers the item to the lessee, that delivery is considered an act of usurpation (ghasb) because it exceeds the borrower's authority. Consequently, the lender has the option to claim compensation (daman) from either the borrower (as the one who wrongfully delivered) or the sub-lessee (as the one who took possession without owner's permission). If the lender claims from the borrower, the borrower cannot recover from the sub-lessee because the sub-lessee is considered to have leased the borrower's own property (in effect). If the lender claims from the sub-lessee, the sub-lessee can in turn recover from the borrower (the 'lessor') provided the sub-lessee was unaware that the item was borrowed; this is to prevent deception. If the sub-lessee knew, he has no recourse because he was complicit. The three major Hanafi scholars (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar) hold that the sub-lessee, as a second usurper, cannot recover from the borrower at all and is solely liable for his own act.

Key Terms
مستعير (musta'ir): The person who borrows an item for free use.
معير (mu'ir): The lender who gives the item on loan.
غصب (ghasb): Usurpation; wrongful possession of someone else's property without permission.

Borrowing Land for Building or Planting: Ruling and Compensation
Ruling with proof: borrowing land for building/planting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on borrowing land for building or planting, explain the compensation rules when the lender reclaims the land, and compare the Hanafi view with that of Ibn Abi Layla.

Core Idea
In a loan of land without fixed time, the borrower bears removal costs because he is self-deceived, not deluded.

This lesson explores the Hanafi position on borrowing land for construction or cultivation. The key issue is what happens when the lender wants the land back: who pays for removing structures? The answer hinges on whether a time was fixed and the concept of self-deception (maghtarr) versus being deceived by another (maghrur). We will also examine an objection and the Hanafi response, as well as a later addition from the Mashayikh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its permissibility
The text states that borrowing land for building or planting is permissible (jaiz). Identify the core permissible act.
This is the starting point: 'ariyah (loan) of land for improvement.
2
Understand the default ruling on removal and compensation
If no time is fixed, the lender can force removal of structures without compensation. The borrower is maghtarr (self-deceived). Grasp why compensation is not owed.
The distinction between maghtarr and maghrur is critical.
3
Analyze the objection and the Hanafi response
An objection is raised: building and planting imply a long-term commitment, so an implicit term exists. The Hanafi rebuttal uses examples of temporary buildings and plants for sale. Understand the logical refutation.
This is a classical dialectical argument (dalil aqli).
4
Compare with the opposing view of Ibn Abi Layla
Ibn Abi Layla holds that the lender owns the building and must pay its value. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) between the schools.
This is a mukhtalaf fih issue.
5
Note the later addition by the Mashayikh
If removal harms the land, the lender may pay compensation and keep the structures. Understand the principle that the owner of the principal (asl) is preferred over the owner of the subordinate (tab').
This addition modifies the default rule to prevent land damage.

Explanation of his statement: 'And when he borrows land...' etc. If someone borrows land for building a house or planting a garden, and builds or plants, this is permissible. Hence there is no implicit time constraint from the lender, so he is not liable. This is our ruling. According to Ibn Abi Layla, the building belongs to the lender and he is liable for its value.

Wages for Returning Borrowed Items
Determines who bears the cost of returning a borrowed item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on who bears the wage of returning an item in contracts of 'ariyah, ijarah, and ghasp, and explain the reasoning based on the nature of possession and benefit.

Core Idea
The cost of return follows the party whose benefit is primary: borrower for 'ariyah, lessor for ijarah, usurper for ghasp.

When a contract ends, the item must be returned. But who pays for the return? In Hanafi fiqh, the answer depends on who primarily benefited from possession. The text distinguishes three cases: borrowed items ('ariyah), leased items (ijarah), and usurped items (ghasp). For ijarah, the key concept is takhliyah—the lessee's duty is to make the property available, not to physically return it—so the cost falls on the lessor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three contracts
List the three types of possession mentioned in the text: 'ariyah (loan), ijarah (lease), and ghasp (usurpation). For each, note who is responsible for the return wage.
2
State the ruling for each
For 'ariyah: borrower (musta'ir) pays. For ijarah: lessor (mu'jir) pays. For ghasp: usurper (ghasib) pays.
3
Analyze the reasoning for ijarah
Understand that the lessee's obligation is only takhliyah (making the property available). The lessor benefits by receiving rent, so the cost of return cannot be on the lessee.
4
Resolve the objection
An objection says both parties benefit. The Hanafi answer: lessor's benefit is stronger because he owns the asset and his benefit is actual wealth (mal haqiqi wa hukman), while the lessee's benefit is not fully considered wealth.

The reason the wage for returning a borrowed item is on the borrower is that the borrower took possession for his own benefit, and the cost of return is called wage, so this wage is due on him.
His statement: 'Because what is obligatory on the lessee...' etc. And the wage for returning a leased item is on the lessor because it is not obligatory on the lessee to return the specific leased item; rather, only to give the lessor access to it (takhliyah).
