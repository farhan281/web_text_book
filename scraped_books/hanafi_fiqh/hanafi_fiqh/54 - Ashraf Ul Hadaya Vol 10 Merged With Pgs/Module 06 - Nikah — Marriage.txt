Marriage of Free Minor by Mukatab or Slave Invalid
Ruling on validity of marriage contract by mukatab or slave for free minor daughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on marriage by a slave or mukatab for his free minor daughter and identify the reason for invalidity.

Core Idea
A slave or mukatab father cannot marry off his free minor child because he lacks full guardianship.

This ruling addresses the capacity of a father who is a slave or mukatab (a slave under contract of manumission) to contract the marriage of his free Muslim minor daughter. The text declares such a marriage invalid, highlighting the importance of the guardian's status for a valid nikah in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Identify the act being ruled upon: the marriage contract conducted by a mukatab or slave father for his free Muslim minor daughter. The ruling is that this marriage is not permissible (ja'iz nahi), i.e., invalid (batil).
The ruling is categorical; no exception is mentioned.
2
Determine the reason for invalidity
The invalidity stems from the father's unfree status. In Hanafi fiqh, full guardianship (wilayah) for marriage of a minor requires the guardian to be free, sane, and adult. A slave or mukatab lacks this exclusive capacity because he himself is owned property, so his authority over his free child is incomplete.
The mukatab's partial freedom does not restore full guardianship.
3
Consider the scope and exceptions
This ruling applies only when the father is a slave or mukatab and the daughter is free and Muslim. If the father is free and sane, he may contract the marriage. Note also that the text includes buying a boy for the daughter, which similarly requires valid guardianship.
The ambiguous word 'باڈمی' in the source may be a scribal error; the intended meaning is likely a slave father.

The reason is that a mukatab or a slave lacks full guardianship (wilayah) over his free children; his own status as owned property prevents him from having the necessary legal authority to contract marriage on their behalf. The same applies if he buys a boy (i.e., arranges a purchase for marriage) for her. In Hanafi fiqh, only a free father (or grandfather) has full guardianship for marriage of a minor. The exception is that if he is free and sane, he may act. Here, unfree status negates guardianship.

Disputes Between Spouses and Contracting Parties
Collection of four dispute-related rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rules for resolving disputes over mahr, household goods, and rent.

Core Idea
Hanafi law uses possession, gender suitability, and death to resolve common disputes.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents several procedural and substantive rules for resolving disputes between spouses (in mahr and household goods) and between lessor and lessee. These rulings rely on principles of evidence, gender association of property, and the fact of death. Mastering these rules equips you to handle common family and contractual conflicts in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Mahr dispute when one spouse dies
Identify the rule: if spouses dispute the amount of mahr and one dies, the evidence (from the deceased's heirs) is accepted over the living spouse's claim. This is because the deceased cannot testify, so their claim is given weight through their representatives.
The text says 'جو بھی مر کر دے اس کے بینہ قبول ہوں گے' meaning whichever dies, his/her evidence will be accepted.
2
Lessor-lessee dispute before possession
Understand that if the lessor (mu'jir) and lessee (musta'jir) dispute over the terms before the lessee takes possession, both parties take oaths (tahāluf), similar to a sale contract dispute. This is because the contract is not yet executed.
The phrase 'کرنے سے پہلے' likely refers to before taking possession (qabd).
3
Household goods dispute between spouses generally
Learn the baseline: items exclusively for men (e.g., a sword) are assigned to the husband; items exclusively for women (e.g., jewelry) to the wife. Items suitable for both are considered shared.
This ruling applies when both spouses are alive and disputing.
4
Household goods after one spouse dies
After the death of one spouse, the rule changes: items that are suitable for both men and women go to the surviving spouse. Items exclusively for one gender go to the survivor only if they are of that gender.
The text says 'جو مردوں اور عورتوں کے لائق ہے وہ زندہ کے لئے ہوگا'.

Commentary
Several rulings are given. First, in a dispute between spouses about the amount of mahr (dower), if one of them dies, the deceased's heirs' evidence is accepted regarding the claimed amount, because the claim of the deceased is given weight. Second, if a lessor (mu'jir) and lessee (musta'jir) dispute before the lessee takes possession, both take oaths (tahāluf) as in sale. Third, if spouses dispute about household goods (such as utensils, furniture), the items that are exclusively for men (e.g., a man's sword) are assigned to the husband, and items exclusively for women (e.g., a woman's jewelry) to the wife. Items suitable for both (e.g., a blanket) are for the surviving spouse after the other's death. Witness Disagreement on Mahr in Nikah: Intr-School

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on witness disagreement over mahr and apply the istihsan argument to derive the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Mahr is subsidiary; its dispute does not nullify the marriage.

When witnesses disagree on the mahr amount in a marriage, does that invalidate the contract? This lesson explores a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. You will learn how the subsidiary nature of mahr (tabi') leads to a ruling by istihsan that upholds the marriage with the lower amount.

Guided Path
1
State the case
The woman claims mahr of 1500, the husband says 1000. She brings two witnesses: one says 1000, the other says 1500. What happens?
2
Compare the rulings
Abu Hanifa: marriage valid with 1000 (istihsan). Sahibayn: testimony invalid, marriage not proven.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's analogy
They analogize to sale: if witnesses disagree on price, contract fails. Why? Because price is essential in sale.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's rebuttal
Mahr is subsidiary (tabi'); the contract can exist without specifying mahr. Disagreement on subsidiary doesn’t affect the foundation.
Also both witnesses agree marriage occurred, only differ on amount.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Abu Yusuf later agreed with Abu Hanifa, so the operative ruling is validity with the lower mahr.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of marriage when witnesses disagree on the mahr amount. The case: a woman claims mahr of 1500, husband says 1000, and her witnesses give differing amounts (1000 and 1500). Imam Abu Hanifa rules by istihsan that the marriage is valid with the lower mahr (1000). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule that the witnesses' disagreement invalidates the testimony and thus the marriage is not proven, likening it to sale where such disagreement nullifies the contract. However, al-Amali reports that Abu Yusuf later agreed with Abu Hanifa, indicating a shift in his position. Abu Hanifa's reasoning: mahr is a subsidiary element (tabi') in marriage; the core is the contract itself, which requires no mahr specification. Since both witnesses agree that a marriage occurred, albeit with different mahr amounts, the marriage is established and the lower amount is taken due to their consensus on it. The passage also includes a secondary discussion: whether the ruling differs when the husband is the plaintiff. Some say if the husband is plaintiff, testimony is rejected by consensus because his sole aim is the contract; others say the dispute applies in both cases, and this is the more correct view.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): A method of juristic preference where a ruling deviates from strict analogy (qiyas) in favor of a more equitable outcome based on a stronger evidence or public interest.
تابع (tabi'): Subsidiary element in a contract; in marriage, the mahr is subsidiary to the main contract.
مال (mal): Wealth or property; in this context, the mahr is considered mal.

Witness Disagreement on Mahr: Essence vs Subsidiary
Intra-Hanafi dispute on witness disagreement in nikah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa validates marriage when witnesses disagree on mahr, and distinguish the Sahibayn's opposing view based on qiyas to sale.

Core Idea
Mahr is subsidiary to marriage; disagreement on it does not void the contract.

In a nikah dispute, two witnesses agree that a marriage took place but give different amounts for the mahr. Does this disagreement invalidate the testimony and the marriage? The Hanafi imams differ: Abu Hanifa (and reportedly Abu Yusuf) uphold the marriage by istihsan, while the Sahibayn reject it by analogy to sale. This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind each position, focusing on the distinction between essence and subsidiary in marriage contracts.

Guided Path
1
State the Scenario
Identify the factual dispute: woman claims mahr 1500, husband says 1000. Witness A says 1000, witness B says 1500. Determine who is the claimant and what each party seeks.
The dispute is about the amount of mahr, not whether marriage occurred.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's Position and Proof
Abu Hanifa: marriage valid for 1000 by istihsan. His proof: mahr is subsidiary (tabi') to the marriage contract; witnesses agree on contract, so it is established. Disagreement in subsidiary leads to deciding the lesser amount.
Key principle: subsidiary elements do not break the essence.
3

Al-Hidayah notes two opinions on whether the dispute applies when the husband is claimant. The more correct view (asahh) is that it applies in both cases. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the validity of testimony in a marriage case where two witnesses agree that a marriage occurred but differ on the amount of the dower (mahr). The scenario: A woman claims her marriage was contracted for 1500 dirhams; the husband says it was for 1000. She brings two witnesses: one confirms 1000, the other 1500. The issue is whether the marriage is valid and whether the testimony is accepted.

Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the marriage is valid by istihsan (juristic preference) for the lesser amount (1000). His reasoning: In nikah, the dower (mal) is subsidiary (tabi') to the essence (asl) of the marriage, which is the legalization of marital intimacy (hullat al-bud' wa al-zawjiyya). Disagreement in a subsidiary does not affect the essence, which is unanimously established. The witnesses agree on the fact of marriage, so it is proven. Since they differ on the subsidiary amount, the court decides the lesser amount on which both witnesses implicitly agree (1000). Additionally, Abu Hanifa notes that mahr can be omitted entirely and the marriage would still be valid, further showing its subsidiary nature.

The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that the testimony is void and no marriage is established. They analogize this to a sale (bay'), where if witnesses disagree on the price, neither the sale nor the testimony is valid. In marriage, they argue, the witnesses have made the very contract uncertain: a marriage for 1000 is different from one for 1500, and each claim lacks the required two witnesses. Therefore, both testimonies are rejected.

A nuance: In al-Amali, Abu Yusuf's view is reported alongside Abu Hanifa's, indicating a possible retraction or that Abu Yusuf also accepts istihsan in this case. However, the established position of the Sahibayn against it remains.

The text also discusses a variation based on who is the claimant. Some scholars (mashayikh) restrict the dispute to when the woman is the claimant because her objective includes wealth (mahr), so the witnesses' disagreement is only over wealth, not the contract. When the husband is the claimant, his sole objective is the contract, and since witnesses disagree on an integral part (mahr), the contract itself becomes unproven. Other scholars hold the dispute applies in both scenarios, which is considered more correct (asahh).

Key points: This case illustrates the Hanafi principle of prioritizing the essence over subsidiary elements, and the use of istihsan to uphold transactions over mechanical application of witness rules."

Key Terms
مہر (mahr): The dower given by husband to wife, obligatory in marriage, but subsidiary in the sense that marriage can be valid without specification.
شہادت (shahada): Testimony given in court by witnesses; here specifically about a marriage contract and its terms.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference departing from strict analogy (qiyas) for a more equitable result; used by Abu Hanifa to validate the marriage despite witness disagreement.

Retraction of Testimony for Marriage: No Liability
States ruling and provides talil for no liability on retracting witnesses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on retraction of marriage testimony and explain the reasoning using the principle that liability requires equivalence.

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract marriage testimony owe no compensation because manafi' al-bud' are not property.

In this passage from al-Hidayah, we encounter a ruling that at first seems surprising: witnesses who retract their testimony about a marriage face no financial liability. The key lies in understanding what exactly is lost — the benefits of the vulva (manafi' al-bud'). Hanafi law treats these as non-compensable property, and this lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind that position. By tracing the principle 'al-tadmin yastad'i al-mumathala', you will see why no compensation is due.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Identify that the act is the retraction of two witnesses who previously testified to a marriage. State the ruling: no financial liability (daman) on them.
The ruling applies whether they testified for mahr mithl or less.
2
Locate the reasoning (talil)
The passage gives the reason: because the benefits of the vulva (manafi' al-bud') are not compensable (ghayr mutaqawwim) at the time of loss (inda al-itlaf).
The loss is the retraction that undermines the marriage.
3
Understand the governing principle
The principle is 'al-tadmin yastad'i al-mumathala' — liability requires an equivalent (mithl) to assess compensation. Since the benefit of the vulva has no market equivalent as property, no liability can be imposed.
This is a key Hanafi maxim limiting liability to compensable property.
4
Distinguish when liability arises
The passage notes that these benefits become compensable through ownership (tamalluk). After a valid marriage and consummation, the husband's rights vest and compensation might be assessed. But at the moment of retraction, no ownership has crystallized.
This prevents liability in the retraction scenario.
5
Apply to analogous cases
Contrast with retraction of testimony about property (e.g., a loan). There, the subject matter is mal mutaqawwim with a known equivalent, so liability arises. The difference hinges on the nature of the thing lost.
Always check whether the lost subject is mutaqawwim.

It states that if two witnesses testify that a woman has married with a proper dowry (mahr mithl) or less, and then retract, they are not liable for any financial compensation. The reasoning (talil) is that the benefit of the vulva (manafi' al-bud')—i.e., the right to sexual enjoyment—is not considered compensable property at the time when it would be lost (i.e., if the testimony is retracted after the marriage is deemed valid). In Hanafi law, liability (daman) requires the existence of a standard of equivalence (mumathala) to assess compensation. Since the benefit of the vulva is not a standard commodity, it cannot be evaluated for compensation at the moment of loss. However, if the marriage is actually consummated and the woman becomes entitled to maintenance or other rights, those benefits become compensable through the mechanism of ownership (tamalluk), i.e., through the marriage contract itself. But in this case, the retraction occurs before any such ownership is fully established? The text says 'at the time of injury' (inda al-itlaf), meaning the loss caused by retraction. Since no tangible property was lost, there is no basis for liability. This ruling underscores a key Hanafi principle: not every loss gives rise to financial liability; only losses involving compensable property (mal mutaqawwim) do. The benefits of the vulva are not considered mal mutaqawwim unless they are attached to an actual marriage that has been performed and rights have vested. Therefore, the witnesses bear no financial penalty even if their retraction undermines the marriage.

متقوم (mutaqawwim): A thing that has a recognized financial value in Islamic law, such that its destruction or loss requires compensation.
التضمين يستدعي المماثلة (al-tadmin yastad'i al-mumathala): The principle that liability (daman) requires an equivalent (mithl) to be assessed; if no equivalent exists, compensation cannot be imposed.

Core Idea

This passage from Al-Hidayah draws a crucial distinction between agents who become parties to a contract and those who merely convey the utterances of another. In certain contracts, the agent cannot separate the contract from attribution to the principal; therefore, the legal consequences attach to the principal, not the agent. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly assigning liability in marriage, divorce by compensation, and settlements for intentional killing.

1

Commentary
This passage explains the second category of agency contracts where the agent is merely a messenger (safir). The ruling is that the rights and obligations arising from the contract attach to the principal (muwakkil), not to the agent. Examples include marriage (nikah), khul', and sulh regarding intentional homicide (dam al-'amd). In marriage, the agent of the husband cannot be sued for the dower, and the agent of the wife is not obligated to hand her over. Similarly, in khul', the demand for compensation or the right to demand it goes to the principal, not the agent. The reason is that the agent in these contracts is a mere conduit; he must attribute the contract to the principal. If he attributed it to himself, it would be his own contract, not the principal's. Moreover, these contracts are of the nature of relinquishment (isqat), where the ruling is inseparable from the cause. Therefore, the agent is a messenger, and the legal consequences fall on the principal.

Key Terms
سفير (safīr): A mere messenger who only transmits the words of the principal without any personal authority or liability.
اسقاط (isqāt): Relinquishment or dropping of a right, such as a husband dropping his right over the wife's private parts in khul'.

The Agent as Messenger: Rational Proof from Isqat
Rational proof for agent as safir in second-type contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why in second-type agency contracts the agent is a mere messenger (safir) using the Hanafi rational proof based on the isqat principle.

Core Idea
Dropped rights cannot transfer: the agent cannot be a principal in isqat contracts.

We now explore the rational proof (dalil 'aqli) that establishes the agent's status in the second type of agency. The author of Hidayah presents a two-stage argument: first, an indicative proof (dalil inni) from the agent's need to attribute the contract to the principal; second, a causal proof (dalil limi) rooted in the nature of isqat. This lesson unpacks that reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling in question
State the ruling: In second-type contracts (marriage, khula', etc.), the agent is a mere messenger (safir), not a principal.
This is the starting point of the argument.
2
Analyze the indicative proof (dalil inni)
Observe that the agent must attribute the contract to the principal; if attributed to himself, the contract becomes his own. This indicates the agent does not bear contractual rights.
This is a sign, not a cause.
3
Analyze the causal proof (dalil limi)
Understand the underlying cause: the contract is an isqat (dropping of a right). Since a dropped right cannot be transferred or re-dropped, the cause and ruling cannot separate. Therefore, the contract and its effects must both belong to the principal.
The isqat nature prevents the agent from being a principal.
4
Confirm the conclusion
Therefore, the agent is merely a transmitter of the principal's words; rulings revert to the principal, not the agent.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Agency (Kitab al-Wakalah) in Ashraf al-Hidayah, a commentary on Al-Hidayah, addresses the second type of contracts in which an agent acts. The first type involves contracts like sale (bay'), where the agent can be a principal (asil) because the ruling (e.g., ownership) can separate from the cause (e.g., contract) due to options like khiyar al-shart. The second type includes marriage (nikah), khula', sulh 'an dam al-'amd, and similar contracts where the cause is of the category of isqat (dropping/relinquishment). The author begins by restating the established ruling: in these contracts, the agent must attribute the contract to the principal; if he attributes it to himself, it becomes his own contract and falls outside agency. This shows the agent is merely a messenger (safir) and a transmitter of the principal's words, not a principal himself. The author then explains the rational proof (dalil 'aqli) in two stages: first, a dalil inni (indicative proof) — the agent's inability to attribute the contract to himself is a sign that the contractual rights revert to the principal. Second, a dalil limi (causal proof) — the underlying 'illah (cause) is that in these contracts, the ruling cannot be separated from the cause because the cause itself involves isqat. Using the example of marriage, the cause (i.e., the act of marriage) is an isqat because through marriage, the woman drops her original freedom/prohibition, and the husband acquires ownership. Similarly, in khula', the husband drops his right, and in sulh 'an dam al-'amd, the heirs drop their right to qisas. Since a dropped thing cannot be transferred or re-dropped, it is impossible for the cause to issue from one person (the agent) and the ruling to be for another (the principal). Therefore, the contract and its ruling both belong to the principal, and the agent is merely a messenger. The passage then lists the examples of this second type: manumission for consideration ('itq 'ala mal), kitabah (contract of gradual manumission), sulh 'an al-inkar (compromise upon denial), as well as agency for gift, charity, 'ariyah, deposit, pawn, and loan. The common factor is that the ruling in these is established by possession (qabd), and possession involves taking hold of something owned by another, so the agent cannot be a principal. Exceptionally, agency for borrowing (istiqrad) is invalid because it would involve establishing ownership for the principal through borrowing, which is not permissible. However, sending a messenger to borrow is valid. The commentary clarifies that sulh 'an al-iqrar (compromise upon admission) belongs to the first type because it is analogous to sale.

Key Terms
سفیر (safir): A messenger or mediator who merely transmits the words of the principal without acting as a principal himself.
اسقاط (isqat): The act of dropping or relinquishing a right, such as ownership or a claim.
دلیل لمی (dalil limi): A proof that establishes the causal link, explaining why something exists by identifying its cause.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Agency (Kitab al-Wakalah) in Ashraf al-Hidayah, a commentary on Al-Hidayah, addresses the second type of contracts in which an agent acts. The first type involves contracts like sale (bay'), where the agent can be a principal (asil) because the ruling (e.g., ownership) can separate from the cause (e.g., contract) due to options like khiyar al-shart. The second type includes marriage (nikah), khula', sulh 'an dam al-'amd, and similar contracts where the cause is of the category of isqat (dropping/relinquishment). The author begins by restating the established ruling: in these contracts, the agent must attribute the contract to the principal; if he attributes it to himself, it becomes his own contract and falls outside agency. This shows the agent is merely a messenger (safir) and a transmitter of the principal's words, not a principal himself. The author then explains the rational proof (dalil 'aqli) in two stages: first, a dalil inni (indicative proof) — the agent's inability to attribute the contract to himself is a sign that the contractual rights revert to the principal. Second, a dalil limi (causal proof) — the underlying 'illah (cause) is that in these contracts, the ruling cannot be separated from the cause because the cause itself involves isqat. Using the example of marriage, the cause (i.e., the act of marriage) is an isqat because through marriage, the woman drops her original freedom/prohibition, and the husband acquires ownership. Similarly, in khula', the husband drops his right, and in sulh 'an dam al-'amd, the heirs drop their right to qisas. Since a dropped thing cannot be transferred or re-dropped, it is impossible for the cause to issue from one person (the agent) and the ruling to be for another (the principal). Therefore, the contract and its ruling both belong to the principal, and the agent is merely a messenger. The passage then lists the examples of this second type: manumission for consideration ('itq 'ala mal), kitabah (contract of gradual manumission), sulh 'an al-inkar (compromise upon denial), as well as agency for gift, charity, 'ariyah, deposit, pawn, and loan. The common factor is that the ruling in these is established by possession (qabd), and possession involves taking hold of something owned by another, so the agent cannot be a principal. Exceptionally, agency for borrowing (istiqrad) is invalid because it would involve establishing ownership for the principal through borrowing, which is not permissible. However, sending a messenger to borrow is valid. The commentary clarifies that sulh 'an al-iqrar (compromise upon admission) belongs to the first type because it is analogous to sale.

Key Terms
سفیر (safir): A messenger or mediator who merely transmits the words of the principal without acting as a principal himself.
اسقاط (isqat): The act of dropping or relinquishing a right, such as ownership or a claim.
القبض (al-qabd): Physical or constructive possession that transfers ownership or establishes legal effects.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Joint agents must act together in discretionary matters because the principal consented to both opinions, not just one.

When a principal appoints two agents together, a key question arises: can each agent act independently? The Hanafi school provides a clear answer based on the nature of the act. This lesson unpacks the ruling from al-Hidayah, explaining why joint agents are required to act collectively in matters that require personal judgment or discretion.

1
State the ruling
Identify the core legal rule: neither joint agent may act alone in the matter for which both were appointed. The text states this explicitly at the outset.

Notice that the restriction applies only to transactions requiring opinion (ra'y), such as sale and khul'. Acts that are purely ministerial (e.g., delivering a specific item) are not included.
This distinction is crucial to avoid overgeneralizing the rule.
3
Understand the proof
The reasoning (ta'lil) given is that the principal was satisfied with both opinions together, not with just one. Therefore, acting alone violates the principal's condition and intent.
This is a rational proof based on the principal's expressed consent.

Apply the distinction to the counterargument
Even if the consideration (price or dower) is specified, there is still room for opinion in choosing the buyer or deciding on terms. Hence, specification does not remove the discretionary nature.
This shows that 'requiring opinion' is broader than just setting the price.
5
Summarize the thamarah (practical consequence)
If one joint agent acts alone in a discretionary matter, the act is not valid. Both must participate or at least consent to the transaction.
This protects the principal's interest by ensuring collective judgment.

If a mukatab, a slave, or a dhimmi marries off his free Muslim minor daughter, or buys or sells for her, it is not valid. He said: And if a mukatab, a slave, or a dhimmi marries off his daughter while she is a free Muslim minor, or buys or sells for her, it is not permissible. Its meaning is that he disposes of her property, because slavery and disbelief sever guardianship. Do you not see that a slave does not have the right to marry himself, so how can he marry off another? Similarly, a disbeliever has no guardianship over a Muslim, to the extent that his testimony against a Muslim is not accepted. And because this guardianship is based on consideration, it must be entrusted to someone capable and compassionate so that the meaning of consideration is realized. Slavery removes capability, and disbelief cuts off compassion for a Muslim, so this guardianship is not entrusted to them. In the text, 'buy' and 'sell' refer to any disposition, meaning that these individuals have no right to any kind of disposal of the mentioned minor's property. The evidence is that slavery and disbelief sever guardianship, meaning that the mentioned transactions belong to the category of guardianship. Slavery in the slave and mukatab, and disbelief in the dhimmi, destroy their guardianship. Thus, when a slave has no guardianship over his own marriage, how can he have guardianship over another's marriage? Because having guardianship over another is a branch of having authority over oneself. So when a slave and a mukatab cannot have guardianship over themselves, how can they have it over others? Especially since a disbeliever has no guardianship over a Muslim. This is why a disbeliever's testimony against a Muslim is not accepted. 'And Allah will never give the disbelievers a way over the believers' (Quran 4:141). So how can he have guardianship over the marriage of a Muslim girl? Another evidence is that the guardianship established for boys and girls is based on consideration and compassion. Therefore, only one who is capable and compassionate can have guardianship over a minor boy or girl. Yet slavery removes capability, as Allah said: 'Allah sets forth an example: a slave owned by another, having no power over anything' (Quran 16:75). And disbelief removes compassion for a Muslim.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from a Hanafi commentary. It addresses the invalidity of a mukatab (a slave who has contracted to earn his freedom), a slave, or a dhimmi (a non-Muslim subject of the Islamic state) performing the marriage or any financial transaction for his free Muslim minor daughter. The ruling states that such acts are not valid. The proof is twofold: (1) These individuals lack guardianship even over themselves (a slave cannot marry himself; a dhimmi has no authority over Muslims), so they cannot act as guardians for a free Muslim minor, as evidenced by Quran 4:141. (2) Guardianship over minors requires both capability (qudra) and compassion (shafaqa), which are negated by slavery and disbelief respectively. A slave lacks power, as Quran 16:75 states; a disbeliever lacks compassion for a Muslim. Therefore, such guardianship cannot be entrusted to them. The ruling applies to all forms of financial disposal (tasarruf) on behalf of the minor. The explanation emphasizes that the minors in question are free Muslims, and the guardian must be a free, Muslim, capable, and compassionate person.

Key Terms
مکاتب (mukātab): A slave who has entered into a contract of kitaba with his master to pay a sum for his freedom; he remains a slave until full payment.
ولایت (wilāyah): Legal authority or guardianship over a person or property, especially in matters of marriage and finance for minors.
ذمی (dhimmī): A non-Muslim subject living under the protection of an Islamic state, with certain rights and limitations.

Agent for Debt Collection: Is He Also a Litigant?
Intra-Hanafi dispute with qiyas-based reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether an agent for debt collection is also an agent for litigation, and explain the analogical reasoning (qiyas) used by Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: debt collection is ownership acquisition of equivalents, making the agent a litigant; Sahibayn: collection and litigation are distinct functions.

This passage addresses a key procedural issue in Hanafi agency law: does appointing someone to collect a debt automatically empower them to litigate? Imam Abu Hanifa argues yes, based on the nature of debt as a liability satisfied by equivalents. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, emphasizing the difference between collection and litigation. Understanding this dispute clarifies the scope of agency authority in financial matters and the admissibility of evidence against the agent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the positions
Recognize that the core question is whether an agent for debt collection (wakil bi qabd al-dayn) is automatically an agent for litigation (wakil bi al-khusumah). Note Abu Hanifa's affirmative position and the Sahibayn's negative position.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's proof: tamlik via equivalents
Abu Hanifa argues that the agent is appointed to acquire ownership (tamlik) of the debt's equivalent, since debts are paid by equivalents. This makes the agent a principal in the rights of the debt, so he becomes a litigant.
The key is that collection of the 'ayn of the debt is impossible; the equivalent stands in for the right itself.
3
Trace the qiyas: analogy to agents for pre-emption, gift revocation, etc.
Abu Hanifa analogizes the agent for debt collection to agents for shuf'ah (pre-emption), revocation of gift, purchase, division, and return due to defect. These agents become litigants even before taking physical possession because they are principals in the rights arising from the transaction.
The agent for purchase is an exception: he is not a litigant before concluding the purchase.
4
Understand the Sahibayn's counter-proof: separation of functions
The Sahibayn argue that collection (qabd) and litigation (khusumah) are different acts. Consent to collection does not imply consent to litigation. Trustworthiness in property does not guarantee skill in legal proceedings.
Also narrated from Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Hanifa, and later supported by Shafi'i and Ahmad.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, the debtor can present evidence (bayyinah) of payment or discharge against the agent. Under the Sahibayn, such evidence would not be accepted against the agent; the debtor must sue the principal directly.
This affects procedural strategy in debt cases.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether an agent appointed to collect a debt (wakil bil qabd) is also automatically an agent for litigation (wakil bil khusumat) against the debtor. The ruling is crucial for procedural fiqh: if the agent is also a litigant, then evidence presented by the debtor against the agent (e.g., that the principal already received payment or discharged the debt) is admissible. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the agent is a litigant. His reasoning: the agent is effectively appointed to acquire ownership of the debt's equivalent, since a debt is a liability in the dhimmah and cannot be physically collected. The collected equivalent is considered, in a sense, the very right itself. Therefore, the agent is similar to an agent for pre-emption (shuf'ah), who becomes a litigant even before taking the property. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, arguing that debt collection and litigation are distinct functions; trustworthiness in property does not imply litigation competence. Consent to collection does not imply consent to litigation. This is also the view of Hasan ibn Ziyad from Abu Hanifa, and the later view of Imam Shafi'i, and apparently Imam Ahmad. The text then provides analogical arguments: Abu Hanifa likens the agent for debt collection to agents for pre-emption, revocation of gift, purchase, division, and return due to defect—all of whom become litigants because they are principals in the rights arising from the transaction. The agent for purchase is an exception: he is not a litigant before the actual purchase because the rights of exchange are not yet established. The commentary elaborates with examples: if Khalid loans 100 rupees to Shahid, the debt cannot be collected in specie; the debtor must return an equivalent. The agent for collection acquires ownership of that equivalent, thus becoming a litigant. The mufta bihi position is Abu Hanifa's, as indicated by the detailed defense and analogies.

Resolving a Mahr Dispute: Tahaluf and Mahr al-Mithl
Ruling on how to resolve a dispute between husband and wife regarding the amount of mahr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for resolving a mahr dispute when neither spouse has evidence, and apply the rules of tahaluf and mahr al-mithl determination.

Core Idea
Tahaluf nullifies the specified mahr but does not dissolve the marriage; the court awards mahr al-mithl based on customary standards.

In a marriage, the mahr is a right of the wife. If the spouses disagree on the amount and neither can prove their claim, the Hanafi school employs the procedure of tahaluf (mutual oath-taking) to resolve the dispute. This lesson explains how tahaluf works in this context, why the marriage remains valid, and how the court determines the mahr al-mithl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the evidence scenario
Determine who has evidence: if one party presents bayyina, it is accepted. If both present, the wife's evidence is preferred if her mahr al-mithl is less than her claim.
This is the first stage before tahaluf.
2
Apply tahaluf when evidence is absent
When neither has evidence, both parties swear oaths. This nullifies the specified mahr but does not invalidate the marriage because mahr is subsidiary.
Analogy to sale where absence of price invalidates the contract.
3
Determine the mahr al-mithl
After tahaluf, the court calculates the customary dower (mahr al-mithl) for a woman of similar status. Three sub-cases: (a) if mahr al-mithl ≤ husband's admission, husband's word stands; (b) if ≥ wife's claim, wife's word stands; (c) if between the two, mahr al-mithl is awarded.
The mahr al-mithl is determined by factors like family, beauty, education, etc.
4
Order of oaths and intra-Hanafi disagreement
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the husband swears first to expedite the consequence of his refusal (nukul). Abu Yusuf disagreed. The mufta bihi is the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
The analogy to the buyer in sale supports this order.
5
Apply the ruling to a variant: mahr in the form of a slave
If the mahr is a specific slave, and after tahaluf the value of the slave equals mahr al-mithl, the wife receives the value, not the slave herself, because ownership requires mutual consent.

Commentary
This passage addresses the procedure for resolving a dispute between spouses regarding the amount of mahr (dower). The text from al-Hidayah outlines the rules in stages. First, if either party produces evidence (bayyina), their evidence is accepted. If both produce evidence, the wife's evidence is preferred because it proves a higher amount—the wife is claiming more, and her evidence establishes the excess. However, this preference holds only if the wife's claimed amount is still within the range of her mahr al-mithl (customary dower for a woman of her status). If they have no evidence, then according to Abu Hanifa, the procedure of tahaluf (both parties swear oaths) is applied. But unlike sale, where tahaluf leads to dissolution of the contract (because the price is essential), in marriage the contract remains valid. The reason is that the mahr is subsidiary to the marriage; the marriage's validity does not depend on the specification of the mahr. Therefore, even if the specified mahr is dropped by tahaluf, the marriage continues. Instead, the court determines the mahr al-mithl. The passage provides three scenarios: (1) If the mahr al-mithl equals or is less than what the husband admitted, the husband's statement is accepted because the apparent circumstance (the wife's silence on the higher amount) supports him. (2) If the mahr al-mithl equals or exceeds what the wife claimed, her statement is accepted. (3) If the mahr al-mithl falls between the two claims (more than the husband's admission but less than the wife's claim), the wife receives the mahr al-mithl. This is because after tahaluf, neither party's unilateral claim is given full weight; the objective standard of the mahr al-mithl prevails. The text also notes that the husband begins the oath first according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, to expedite the consequence of refusal to swear (nukul), which is in favor of the wife. Abu Yusuf differed on this order. The passage ends with a variant case: if the husband claims marriage on a male slave (i.e., he claims she is married to him with a mahr in the form of a specific slave) and the wife claims marriage on a female slave (i.e., she says the mahr is that female slave). This is analogous to the previous case, except that if the female slave's value equals the mahr al-mithl, the wife gets the value, not the slave herself, because acquisition of a specific female slave requires mutual consent, which is absent. The value is substituted.

تحالف (taḥāluf): Mutual oath-taking in a dispute where both parties swear and their claims effectively cancel each other.

Resolving Mahr Disputes: Evidence, Oaths, and Mahr al-Mithl
Rulings on evidence and oaths in mahr disputes between spouses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi evidentiary and oath rules to resolve a mahr dispute between spouses and determine the appropriate mahr al-mithl in all subcases.

Core Idea
In a mahr dispute, if neither spouse has evidence, the marriage remains valid, both take oaths, and mahr al-mithl is due.

When a husband and wife disagree on the amount of mahr agreed at marriage, the court must resolve the conflict. The Hanafi school provides a detailed procedure based on who presents evidence, and if none, a mutual oath (tahāluf) that does not invalidate the marriage — a key distinction from sale contracts. This lesson walks through the entire process, including the intra-Hanafi difference on who begins the oath and a special case of mahr consisting of a slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the evidence scenario
Determine which spouse, if any, brings evidence (bayyinah). If only one has evidence, that evidence is accepted because it illuminates the claim. If both have evidence, proceed to step 2.
The husband is typically the defendant regarding the increase, but presenting evidence makes him a claimant in form.
2
Prefer wife's evidence when mahr al-mithl is lower
If both spouses present evidence, the wife's evidence is preferred because it claims an increase — but only if her claim exceeds the estimated mahr al-mithl. Otherwise, if mahr al-mithl equals or exceeds her claim, the husband's evidence prevails.
The rationale: her evidence is more credible when seeking an amount above the norm.
3
Apply tahaluf when neither has evidence
If both lack evidence, they take mutual oaths (tahāluf) according to Abu Hanifa. The specified mahr (tasmīyah) falls, but the marriage remains valid because mahr is subsidiary (tābi') to the marriage, unlike a sale where price specification is essential.
This distinguishes marriage from sale: in sale, lack of specification invalidates the contract.
4
Determine the payable mahr after tahaluf
After tahaluf, the court awards mahr al-mithl. There are three subcases: (a) if mahr al-mithl ≤ husband's acknowledgment, award husband's amount; (b) if mahr al-mithl ≥ wife's claim, award wife's claim; (c) if between, award mahr al-mithl.
The mahr al-mithl serves as a default when the specified amount is unenforceable.
5
Account for intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on oath order
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the husband's oath comes first to hasten the benefit if he refuses (nukūl). Abu Yusuf held that the wife's oath comes first. The relied-upon view is with the husband.
Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah adopts the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute lesson from Kitab al-Da'wa (Book of Claims) regarding a dispute between spouses over the amount of mahr (dower). The passage covers several scenarios: (1) When one spouse brings evidence, it is accepted because it supports their claim. (2) When both bring evidence, the wife's evidence is preferred because it claims an increase, but with a condition: the mahr al-mithl (proper dower) must be less than her claim. If her claim is within or below the mahr al-mithl, the husband's evidence prevails. (3) When neither has evidence, both take oaths (tahāluf) according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, but the marriage is not annulled. This is because the mahr is subsidiary (tābi') to marriage, unlike in a sale where failure to specify the price invalidates the contract. After tahaluf, the court awards mahr al-mithl. The passage details three subcases based on the relationship between the agreed amount (tasmīyah), the mahr al-mithl, and the conflicting claims. It also notes that the oath begins with the husband to expedite the potential benefit of his refusal (nukūl). Abu Yusuf differs on the order of oaths. A final subcase involves marriage on a slave or slave-girl as mahr: if the value equals the mahr al-mithl, the wife takes the value, not the specific item, because ownership requires mutual consent. The commentary concludes by reiterating the core principle: when both lack evidence, oaths are taken, the marriage remains valid, and mahr al-mithl is determined.

Key Terms
مهر (mahr): The dower or gift given by the husband to the wife in marriage; it can be specified or default to mahr al-mithl.
تحالف (tahāluf): Mutual oath-taking by both parties when they have no evidence and contradict each other; it nullifies the specified amount but not the contract.
مهر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The proper dower determined by the social status and family of the wife, used when the mahr is not specified or the specification falls.
بینه (bayyinah): Evidence or proof, typically two male witnesses or one male and two females in financial matters.

Mahr Dispute Between Spouses: Evidence, Oaths, and Mahr al-Mithl
Explains legal procedure when spouses dispute mahr, focusing on evidence, oaths, and mahr al-mithl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi procedure for resolving a mahr dispute between spouses and identify the different positions of Karkhi and Razi regarding tahlif.

Core Idea
When spouses disagree on mahr and have no evidence, both take oaths and mahr al-mithl is imposed; the marriage remains valid.

In a marriage, the mahr (dower) is a wajib right of the wife. But what happens when the husband and wife disagree about the amount and neither can prove their claim? This passage from al-Hidayah lays out the precise Hanafi procedure: from accepting evidence (bayyinah) to administering oaths (tahlif) and finally imposing a standard mahr (mahr al-mithl). The lesson also explores an important intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam al-Karkhi and Abu Bakr al-Razi over when tahlif is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the evidentiary scenario
Determine which spouse (or both) produces bayyinah (witness evidence). The ruling changes depending on who has evidence and how it relates to mahr al-mithl.
The woman's bayyinah is accepted to prove a higher mahr only if mahr al-mithl is less than her claim.
2
Apply the no-evidence rule
When neither spouse has bayyinah, both take oaths (tahlif). The marriage is not annulled; the specified mahr is dropped.
Contrast with a sale: disagreement on price annuls the contract.
3
Impose mahr al-mithl
After tahlif, mahr al-mithl is imposed. Its value compared to each claim determines the final amount: if it matches the husband's admission, that amount stands; if it matches the wife's claim, that stands; if it lies between, mahr al-mithl itself is given.
Mahr al-mithl is the dower of a woman similar in status to the bride.
4
Note the order of oaths
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold that the husband swears first, like the buyer in a price dispute. This speeds up the denial benefit.
Some later authorities say the husband swears first because delivering mahr is initially his duty.
5
Compare Karkhi and Razi
Karkhi holds tahlif in all cases before tahkim. Razi holds tahlif only when mahr al-mithl falls between the two claims. al-Hidayah follows Karkhi.
This difference affects the number of cases requiring oaths (five vs. one).

اشرف الہدایہ جلد - دہم نے کہا کہ تو نے مجھ سے دو ہزار پر نکاح کیا ہے پس جس نے مبینہ قائم کیا اسکا بینہ قبول ہوگا کیونکہ اس نے اپنے دعوی کو دلیل سے منور کر دیا ہے پھر اگر میاں بیوی دونوں نے بینہ قائم کیا تو عورت کا بینہ قبول ہو گا اس لئے کہ عورت کا بینہ زیادتی کو ثابت کرتا ہے اس کے معنی یہ ہیں کہ جب عورت کا مہر مثل عورت کے دعوئی سے کم ہو اور اگر دونوں کے پاس بینہ نہ ہوں تو ابو حنیفہ کے نزدیک دونوں سے قسم لی جائے گی اور نکاح فسخ نہیں کیا جائے گا اس لئے کہ تخالف کا اثر تسمیہ کے معدوم ہونے میں ہوگا اور نصیحت نکاح کے لئے محل نہیں ہے کیونکہ مہر نکاح میں تابع ہوتا ہے برخلاف بیچ کے اس لئے کہ عدم تسمیہ اس کو فاسد کر دیتا ہے جیسا کہ گذر چکا ہے پس بیع کو فسخ کر دیا جائے گا۔ لیکن مہر مثل کو حکم مظہر ایا جائے گا۔ پس اگر مہر مثل اس قدر ہو جس قدر کا شوہر نے اقرار کیا ہے یا اس سے کم ہے تو اس کا فیصلہ کیا جائے گا جو شوہر نے کہا ہے کیونکہ ظاہر اس کا شاہد ہے اور اگر اس سے کم ہو جس کا عورت نے دعویٰ کیا ہے تو عورت کے لئے مہر مثل کا فیصلہ کیا جائے گا۔ اس لئے کہ جب میاں بیوی نے قسم کھائی تو نہ مہرشل سے زائد ثابت ہوا اور نہ اس سے کم صاحب ہدایہ کہتے ہیں کہ مانتن نے اولاً تحالف کو ذکر کیا پھر تحکیم (حکم بنانا) ذکر کیا اور یہ امام کرخی کا قول ہے اس لئے کہ مہر سمی کے ہوتے ہوئے مہر مثل کا کوئی اعتبار نہیں ہے اور اس کے اعتبار کا ساقط ہونا تخالف سے ہوگا اس وجہ سے تمام صورتوں میں تحالف کو مقدم کیا جائے گا اور امام ابو حنیفہ اور امام محمد نزدیک شو ہر کی قسم سے ابتداء کی جائے گی تاکہ انکار کا فائدہ جلدی ظاہر ہو جیسا کہ مشتری میں ہے اور ابوبکر رازی کی تخریج اس کے خلاف ہے اور ہم نے باب النکاح میں پوری تفصیل کے ساتھ ذکر کیا ہے اور ہم نے ابو یوسف کا اختلاف بھی ذکر کیا ہم اس کا اعادہ نہیں کریں گے اور اگر شوہر نے دعوی کیا کہ اس غلام پر نکاح ہوا ہے اور عورت اس باندی پر دعویٰ کرتی ہے تو یہ مسئلہ پہلے مسئلہ کے مانند ہے مگر جبکہ باندی کی قیمت مہر مثل کے برابر ہو تو اس کے لئے اس کی قیمت ہوگی وہ باندی اس واسطے کہ باندی کا مالک ہونا بغیر باہمی رضا مندی کے نہیں ہوگا اور باہمی رضامندی پائی نہیں گئی تو قیمت واجب ہوگی۔

369. Book of Claims. Ashraf al-Hidayah Volume 10: He said, 'You married me for two thousand.' So whoever establishes bayyinah, his bayyinah is accepted because he has illuminated his claim with evidence. Then if both spouses establish bayyinah, the woman's bayyinah is accepted because her bayyinah proves the excess. Its meaning is that when mahr al-mithl is less than the woman's claim. And if both do not have bayyinah, then according to Abu Hanifa, an oath is taken from both, and the marriage is not annulled because the effect of the disagreement is the elimination of the specified mahr, and disposal (of the marriage) is not a place for annulment because mahr is subsidiary in marriage, unlike sale, because the lack of specification makes it invalid as has passed. So the sale would be annulled. But mahr al-mithl is made the basis. So if mahr al-mithl is equal to what the husband admitted or less, then it is decided according to what the husband said, because the outward circumstance is his witness. And if it is less than what the woman claimed, then mahr al-mithl is decided for the woman. Because when the spouses swore, neither more nor less than mahr al-mithl was proven. The author of Hidayah says that the author (al-Quduri) first mentioned tahlif, then mentioned tahkim, and this is the statement of Imam al-Karkhi, because when the specified mahr exists, mahr al-mithl has no consideration, and its consideration is dropped by the disagreement. Therefore, in all cases tahlif is given precedence. And according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, the oath begins with the husband so that the benefit of denial appears quickly, as in the case of the buyer. And Abu Bakr al-Razi's derivation is against this. We have mentioned it in full detail in the Book of Marriage, and we have also mentioned the disagreement of Abu Yusuf; we will not repeat it. And if the husband claims that the marriage was on this slave, and the woman claims on this slave-girl, then this case is similar to the first case, except that when the price of the slave-girl equals mahr al-mithl, then its price is due for her, because ownership of the slave-girl cannot be without mutual consent, and mutual consent is not found, so the price is obligatory. Explanation... The scenario is that if the spouses disagree on the amount of mahr, so the husband claims, 'I married this woman for one thousand,' and the woman claims, 'This man married me for two thousand.' Then whichever of them establishes bayyinah, his or her bayyinah is accepted because he has proven his claim with evidence. If the woman establishes bayyinah, its acceptance is clear because she is the claimant of excess, and the claimant's bayyinah is accepted. So her bayyinah is accepted. But there is some issue with accepting the husband's bayyinah because the husband is the denier of excess, so an oath should be required of him, not bayyinah. Yet despite that, if he produces bayyinah, it is accepted because he is a claimant in form, and that is sufficient for acceptance of bayyinah. And if both spouses establish their own bayyinah, the woman's bayyinah is accepted because it proves the excess mahr. But the woman's bayyinah is accepted only when mahr al-mithl is less than what the woman claims. Otherwise, if mahr al-mithl is equal to what she claims or more, then in both these cases the husband's bayyinah is accepted. And if both the man and woman are unable to produce bayyinah, then according to Imam Abu Hanifa, an oath is taken from both, but the marriage is not annulled, because the effect of the disagreement is that both claims regarding mahr are eliminated, and it becomes as if no mahr was mentioned. And not mentioning mahr is not a ground for the validity of marriage because mahr is subsidiary in marriage. So due to this disagreement, the specification of the mahr is eliminated, but the marriage is not annulled. Contrary to sale, because not mentioning the price in sale makes it invalid, as has passed in the Book of Sales. So the sale would inevitably be annulled after disagreement over the price. In any case, after disagreement over mahr, the mention of mahr is eliminated and the marriage is not annulled. And when the marriage is not annulled and the mention of mahr is eliminated, then to resolve the dispute, mahr al-mithl is made the basis. So if mahr al-mithl is equal to what the husband admitted or less, then the amount the husband admitted is ordered because the outward circumstance is a witness for the husband's statement, and the one whom the outward circumstance witnesses is the defendant, and in the absence of bayyinah, the defendant's statement is credible. So in this case also, the defendant's (i.e., the husband's) statement is credible, and the mahr he admitted becomes obligatory. And if mahr al-mithl is equal to what the woman claimed or more, then the amount she claimed is ordered because in this case the outward circumstance witnesses the woman. And if mahr al-mithl is in between, i.e., more than the husband's admission and less than the woman's claim, then mahr al-mithl is ordered for the woman. Because when both have sworn, neither more nor less than mahr al-mithl is proven: due to the husband's oath, more than mahr al-mithl is not proven; due to the woman's oath, less than mahr al-mithl is not proven. So when neither more nor less than mahr al-mithl is proven, then mahr al-mithl is ordered. The author of Hidayah said that Sheikh Abu al-Hasan al-Quduri first mentioned tahlif, so he said, 'And if they have no bayyinah, they do tahlif,' then he mentioned tahkim, so he said, 'But mahr al-mithl is decreed.' The author of Hidayah says that what al-Quduri mentioned is the statement of Imam al-Karkhi, because the consideration of the specified mahr is dropped by tahlif. So first the consideration of the specified mahr is dropped through tahlif, then mahr al-mithl is made the basis. Therefore, in all cases tahlif is given precedence and tahkim is delayed. The author of Hidayah said that according to the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), in the case of mahr disagreement, the oath is taken from the husband first so that the benefit of denial appears quickly, just as in the disagreement over the amount of price, the oath is taken from the buyer first. In al-Nihayah and Miraj al-Dirayah, quoting Fatawa Zahiriyyah, it is said that since the delivery of mahr is first obligatory upon the husband, so the oath is also first obligatory upon him. The author of Hidayah said that in the mentioned issue, Abu Bakr al-Razi's derivation is against his teacher Imam al-Karkhi's derivation. So Imam Abu Bakr al-Razi first makes mahr al-mithl the basis. Then, whichever one mahr al-mithl witnesses for, his statement is credible. And if mahr al-mithl is between both claims, then in that case there is tahlif and mahr al-mithl is obligatory. So Abu Bakr al-Razi does not hold tahlif except in one case: when mahr al-mithl does not witness for either spouse, i.e., mahr al-mithl is more than the husband's admission and less than the woman's claim. Only in that case does Abu Bakr al-Razi consider tahlif obligatory. Otherwise, if mahr al-mithl is equal to the husband's admission or less, then the husband's statement with an oath is credible. And if mahr al-mithl is equal to the woman's claim or more, then the woman's statement with an oath is credible. In any case, according to Imam al-Karkhi, in all five mentioned cases there is tahlif and then tahkim, and according to Abu Bakr al-Razi, there is tahlif only in one case.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah covers the ruling on a dispute between spouses regarding the amount of mahr. It is a detailed explanation of the legal procedure when husband and wife disagree on the mahr amount, addressing scenarios where one or both have witness evidence (bayyinah), or when neither has witnesses. The passage type is a ruling with proof, and it expands on the matn of al-Hidayah. The author first establishes the principle: when one spouse produces bayyinah (witnesses), it is accepted because evidence supports the claim. If both produce bayyinah, the woman's bayyinah is given priority because it supports the higher amount, but this applies only when mahr al-mithl (the standard mahr for her peers) is less than her claim. If mahr al-mithl equals or exceeds her claim, the husband's bayyinah is accepted. If neither has bayyinah, Imam Abu Hanifa rules that both take oaths (tahlif) and the marriage remains valid; it is not annulled because the disagreement only eliminates the specified mahr, not the marriage itself. This contrasts with a sale, where disagreement over the price voids the contract. After tahlif, mahr al-mithl is imposed as a basis for settlement. The passage then discusses the order of oaths: according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, the husband swears first because he is the one who would benefit from denial (similar to the buyer in a sale dispute). Abu Bakr al-Razi differs from his teacher Imam al-Karkhi: al-Karkhi applies tahlif in all cases before tahkim, while al-Razi applies tahlif only when mahr al-mithl does not corroborate either spouse's claim (i.e., it lies between both amounts). Otherwise, he lets the mahr al-mithl decide. The passage also covers a special case where the spouses dispute not the amount but the object of mahr (e.g., a specific slave or slave-girl). In that case, if the value of the object equals mahr al-mithl, that value is given, because ownership transfer requires mutual consent, which is absent. This passage is dense with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and demonstrates the subtlety of Hanafi fiqh in balancing evidence, oaths, and custom.

تحالف (tahlif): The process of both parties taking oaths to deny the other's claim when there is no evidence.
مہر مثل (mahr al-mithl): The dower of a woman similar to the bride in family, beauty, status, and location, used as a standard when no mahr is specified or agreed.

Tahlif in Kitabah Dispute: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute with qiyas and rebuttal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on tahlif in a kitabah dispute and explain the key difference in their understanding of the consideration (badal).

Core Idea
Tahlif depends on whether the contract is a pure exchange; Abu Hanifa says kitabah is not exchange until payment.

When the master and the mukatib disagree on the amount of kitabah, should they take mutual oaths? The Sahibayn say yes, analogizing to a sale dispute. Abu Hanifa says no, because the consideration is not for freedom but for immediate lifting of restrictions. Let us trace the legal reasoning behind this intra-madhhab disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed ruling
Determine whether tahlif is required when master and mukatib dispute the kitabah amount.
Tahlif means mutual oaths, leading to rescission if oaths are taken.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that they do not take oaths. Understand his reasoning: the consideration is not yet for freedom.
Fakk al-hajr (lifting of legal restrictions) is immediate, so at dispute time it's not a pure exchange.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say they do take oaths and the contract is rescinded. Understand their analogy to sale.
Kitabah is a commutative contract that accepts rescission, like sale.
4
Analyze the qiyas
Trace the analogy: asl = sale, far' = kitabah, illa = commutative contract subject to rescission. Abu Hanifa objects that the illa is not present in kitabah because the consideration is deferred.
The objection is that the exchange is not fully commutative at the time of dispute.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the master's statement is taken with his oath (defendant); under Sahibayn, both take oaths and the contract is rescinded.
Later fatwa often follows Sahibayn, making tahlif operative.

He said: If the master and the mukatib dispute regarding the kitabah amount, they do not take oaths according to Abu Hanifa, but the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) said they take oaths and the kitabah is rescinded, and this is the saying of al-Shafi‘i, because it is a commutative contract that accepts rescission, so it resembles sale. And for Abu Hanifa: the consideration is in exchange for lifting the restriction (fakk al-hajr) regarding possession and disposal, which is immediate and safe for the slave, and it only transforms to be in exchange for freedom upon payment. So before payment there is no exchange, so it remains a dispute over the amount of consideration only, not beyond that, so they do not take oaths.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding tahlif (mutual oaths) in a kitabah (contract of manumission by payment) dispute. The ruling: Abu Hanifa holds that no tahlif occurs between the master and the mukatib when they dispute the amount of kitabah. Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and Imam al-Shafi‘i hold that they do take oaths and the contract is rescinded. The proof of Sahibayn: kitabah is a commutative contract like sale; the master claims extra consideration, the slave denies, and the slave claims freedom upon paying what he admits, so tahlif is warranted. Abu Hanifa's proof: the consideration in kitabah is not for freedom itself but for lifting restrictions (fakk al-hajr) on the slave's possession and disposal, which is immediate and already secure for the slave. It only becomes a consideration for freedom at the time of payment. Therefore, before payment, it is merely a dispute over the amount of a future exchange, not a dispute over an existing obligation that would necessitate tahlif. Thus, no tahlif.

Key Terms
مكاتب (mukātib): A slave who has entered a kitabah contract with his master to purchase his freedom by paying installments.
كتابة (kitābah): A contract of manumission by payment, where the slave will be free upon paying the agreed amount.
فك الحجر (fakk al-ḥajr): Lifting of legal restrictions on a slave's ability to own property and engage in transactions.
تحالف (taḥāluf): Mutual oaths taken by two disputing parties in a contract, leading to rescission.
فسخ (faskh): Rescission or annulment of a contract.

Resolving Spousal Disputes over Household Goods
Ruling on dividing household goods without evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling for dividing household goods between disputing spouses without evidence by categorizing items based on customary suitability and possession.

Core Idea
Without evidence, household goods go to the spouse they customarily suit; common items go to the husband due to his overarching possession.

When a husband and wife disagree over who owns household items and neither can prove their claim, Islamic law provides a fair method based on apparent circumstances and possession. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling from al-Hidayah, which avoids deadlock by relying on what is outwardly known and the dynamics of household possession. You will learn how to categorize goods and apply the rule consistently, even after separation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Confirm that the spouses are disputing ownership of household goods with no evidence (bayyinah) from either side. This triggers the ruling.
The ruling applies whether the dispute occurs during marriage or after divorce.
2
Categorize the goods into three types
Classify each item as (a) customarily used by men, (b) customarily used by women, or (c) used by both. This classification is based on local custom (urf) and apparent suitability.
Examples: turban, bow → men; headscarf, jewelry → women; vessels, furniture → both.
3
Apply the primary rule
Men's items go to the husband; women's items go to the wife. For common items, they go to the husband because his possession (yad) encompasses the wife and her possessions.
The principle: 'The woman and what is in her hand are in the hand of the husband.'
4
Apply the exception for women-specific items
If an item is customarily specific to women despite being in the husband's general possession, the wife's claim prevails because her actual possession (wearing or exclusive use) presents a stronger apparent circumstance.
The commentary gives the analogy of two men disputing a garment: the one wearing it has stronger possession.
5
Understand the role of oath
The commentary adds that each spouse must take an oath before receiving the goods, affirming their ownership. This is part of the procedural resolution.
Oath-taking is a standard feature in claims when evidence is lacking.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa addresses how to resolve a dispute between spouses over household goods when neither party has evidence. It is a ruling_statement followed by extensive tashrih (explanation). The core principle is reliance on apparent circumstances (zāhir) and possession (yad). Goods are categorized: those typically used by men go to the husband (e.g., turban, bow), those typically used by women go to the wife (e.g., headscarf, jewelry), and goods usable by both (e.g., vessels) go to the husband because he is considered the primary possessor of the household. The wife has stronger possession over items specifically hers, overriding the husband's general possession. This is illustrated by the analogy of two men disputing a garment: the wearer's possession is stronger than the one merely holding the sleeve. The ruling is the same whether the dispute arises during marriage or after separation (divorce). The commentary clarifies that each party must take an oath before taking the goods.
