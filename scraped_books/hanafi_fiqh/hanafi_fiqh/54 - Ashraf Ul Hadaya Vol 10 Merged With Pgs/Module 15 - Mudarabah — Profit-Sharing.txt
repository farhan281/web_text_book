Dispute in Mudaraba: Absolute vs Restricted Terms
Ruling with proof on dispute resolution in mudaraba and agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for resolving disputes between the capital owner and mudarib regarding the scope of mudaraba and identify the mufta bihi position on credit terms in absolute agency.

Core Idea
Whose word is accepted depends on whether the default absoluteness of mudaraba is rebutted or not.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling on disputes in mudaraba contracts. The passage from the commentary on al-Hidayah presents two scenarios: one where the capital owner claims a cash-only restriction, and another where both parties agree the mudaraba was restricted but disagree on the type of goods. Understanding these scenarios and the underlying principles of default (asl) and agency (wakala) is essential for correctly resolving such disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and claims
In the first scenario, note that the capital owner (rabb al-mal) claims the mudaraba was restricted to cash sales, while the mudarib claims it was absolute. In the second, both agree it was restricted but differ on the specific commodity type (e.g., cotton vs wool).
2
Apply the default principle (asl) of mudaraba
Hanafi fiqh holds that the default in mudaraba is absoluteness (itlaq) and generality (umum). The mudarib's claim aligns with this default, making him the defendant (mudda' alyh) whose statement is accepted when no evidence exists.
3
Distinguish when absoluteness is negated
In the second scenario, both parties agree the mudaraba was not absolute (since each specifies a type), so the default is removed. The contract then falls under agency (wakala), where the principal's word prevails in a dispute.
This shift from mudaraba to agency is a key legal construct.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on credit terms
The passage extends to a general agency: if an agent is authorized absolutely to sell, he may sell for cash or credit. Imam Abu Hanifa allows any credit term, while the Sahibayn restrict it to customary terms only. Identify the mufta bihi position.
This ikhtilaf shows how usul principles (like 'urf) affect furu'.
5
Determine the operative ruling for fatwa
Later Hanafi jurists favour the Sahibayn's view (customary term only) due to practical considerations (avoiding harm). This is the mufta bihi for modern application.
Always check the tarjih authority when intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf exists.

Explanation of the issue: If there is a dispute between the capital owner and the agent (mudarib) regarding whether the mudaraba was absolute or restricted to cash sale: the capital owner claimed that he gave the wealth on condition that the mudarib sell it for cash, and the mudarib said that there was no condition of cash, rather he gave it for mudaraba, then in this case the mudarib's statement will be accepted. The proof is that the mudaraba command is derived from the capital owner, but there is evidence and indication for it being absolute against the capital owner's claim, and the indication is that the principle in mudaraba is that it is general and absolute. For this reason, if the capital owner said, 'I gave this wealth for mudaraba,' then just by saying that, mudaraba is established, and the mudarib gets authority to transact. So the mudarib who claims absoluteness, his statement aligns with the principle, and the one whose statement aligns with the principle is called 'mudda' alyh' (defendant) in fiqh terminology. Hence the mudarib is the defendant and the capital owner is the plaintiff; and this is when the plaintiff has no evidence. When there is no evidence, since the defendant's statement is accepted, here the mudarib's (the defendant's) statement will be accepted. In contrast, if the capital owner claimed mudaraba in one type of cloth and the mudarib claimed mudaraba in another type – for example, the capital owner said, 'I gave money for mudaraba in cotton cloth,' and the mudarib said, 'You said for woolen cloth' – then in this case the capital owner's statement will be accepted, because both the capital owner and the mudarib agreed that the mudaraba is not absolute, since each of them claimed mudaraba in one type. So when absoluteness is removed, the mudaraba contract is reduced to the level of pure agency (wakala). That is, the capital owner becomes the principal (muwakkil) and the mudarib becomes the agent (wakeel). It has already passed that in case of dispute between principal and agent, the principal's statement is accepted. Therefore here too, the capital owner being in the position of principal, his statement will be accepted. The author of Hidayah said that in the case of agency, an absolute command to sell includes both cash and credit sale. That is, if a principal appoints someone as agent to sell something and leaves it absolute – not mentioning cash nor credit – then in this case the agent is authorized to sell both for cash and on credit. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, in credit sale, the term can be anything, customary or non-customary; both are permissible. However, the two disciples (Sahibayn) restrict the term to be customary. That is, according to Sahibayn, the agent can sell on credit only with a customary term. So if the agent sold on credit with a non-customary term – e.g., fifty years – then according to the Great Imam it is permissible, but according to Sahibayn it is not permissible.

Commentary
This passage, from the commentary (sharh) on Al-Hidayah, addresses a dispute between the capital owner (rabb al-mal) and the mudarib over the scope of the mudaraba contract. It distinguishes two scenarios. First, if the capital owner claims the mudaraba was restricted to cash sales while the mudarib claims it was absolute, the mudarib's word is accepted. The reasoning is that the default (asl) in mudaraba is that it is absolute and general. Therefore, the mudarib's claim aligns with the default principle, making him the defendant (mudda' alyh) whose statement is taken when there is no evidence. The capital owner is the plaintiff (mudda'i). Second, if both agree the mudaraba was not absolute but differ only on the specific type of commodity (e.g., cotton vs. wool), then the capital owner's word is accepted. Here, because both sides agree it was restricted, the contract is treated as a pure agency (wakala). In agency, a principal's statement prevails in a dispute. The passage then notes an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding a general agency. If an agent is appointed absolutely to sell, he may sell for cash or credit. Imam Abu Hanifa allows any credit term, while the Sahibayn restrict it to customary terms only. This is a well-known ikhtilaf recorded in the texts. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position according to later Hanafi scholarship leans towards the Sahibayn's view for practical considerations (ihtiyat), but the text presents Imam Abu Hanifa's view as valid in principle.

Key Terms
مضاربت (mudarabah): A partnership where one party provides capital and the other provides labor, with profits shared according to agreement.
رب المال (rabb al-mal): The capital owner who provides the funds in a mudaraba agreement.
مضارب (mudarib): The working partner who manages the mudaraba capital and executes trades.
وکالت (wakalah): A contract of agency where one party (muwakkil) appoints another (wakeel) to act on his behalf.
متعارف (mut'āraf): Customary, commonly accepted practice in the community.

Key Takeaway
When disputes arise in mudaraba, the default of absoluteness determines the burden of proof; if the default is removed, the contract is treated as agency where the principal's word prevails.
