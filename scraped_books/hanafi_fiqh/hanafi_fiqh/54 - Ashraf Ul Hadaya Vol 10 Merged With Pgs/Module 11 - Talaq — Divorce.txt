Liability of Witnesses for Half Mahr Upon Retraction
Ruling with proof on witness retraction after testifying to pre-consummation divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness liability when they retract after testifying to a pre-consummation divorce, and explain the reasoning based on the concept of mu'akkad (confirmed liability).

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract after confirming a close-to-falling liability become liable for half the mahr.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: two witnesses testify that a husband divorced his wife before consummation, and after the judge rules, they retract. The key is understanding that before consummation, the mahr is not fully confirmed—it could fall entirely due to the wife's nushuz or apostasy. By their testimony, the witnesses make half the mahr binding, and upon retraction, they are liable for it. We will explore the reasoning and the foundational principle that a confirmer (mu'akkid) bears the same liability as an obligator (mujib).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the retraction of witnesses after their testimony was accepted and a judgment was made. The ruling is that they are liable for half the mahr (or the mut'ah gift if mahr was not specified).
The liability is wajib, not just recommended.
2
Understand the nature of pre-consummation mahr
Before consummation, the mahr is not mu'akkad (confirmed)—it is close to falling. Factors like the wife's nushuz or apostasy can cause the entire mahr to drop from the husband's liability.
This fragility is key to the reasoning.
3
Recognize the witnesses' role as mu'akkid
By testifying to the divorce, the witnesses change the status of the half mahr from 'close to falling' to 'binding' (mu'akkad). They become like the obligator (mujib) who creates the obligation.
The principle: 'ما على الموجب فهو على المؤكد'—what is on the obligator is on the confirmer.
4
Apply the principle of same liability
Since the witnesses made the half mahr binding, and then retracted, they must compensate. The obligation that fell on the husband now falls on them.
This is a qiyas: comparison with a party who causes an obligation.
5
Note cross-madhhab agreement
Imam Ahmad, Imam Malik, and one narration of al-Shafi'i also hold this view, showing it is widely accepted though not universally agreed upon.
This strengthens the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding witnesses who retract after testifying to a divorce before consummation. The text begins with the Arabic ruling from al-Hidayah: the witnesses are liable for half the mahr. The reasoning is that before consummation, the mahr is not firmly established; it could fall entirely if the woman becomes disobedient (e.g., by having relations with the husband's son from another wife) or apostatizes. By testifying to the divorce, the witnesses made the half mahr binding (mu'akkad) on the husband. Since they caused this obligation to become effective, and then retracted, they are liable for the amount they made binding. The text compares the role of the witnesses to that of a person who creates an obligation (mujib) — the one who confirms (mu'akkid) bears the same liability. The passage also notes that this is the position of Imam Ahmad, Imam Malik, and one narration of Imam al-Shafi'i, indicating broad agreement among major schools. The commentary then elaborates the scenario: the woman claims divorce, witnesses testify, the judge rules, then the witnesses retract — they are liable for half the specified mahr. In the case of mahr al-mithl, the witnesses are liable for the mut'ah gift. The proof further explains that the mahr before consummation is always susceptible to falling (due to nushuz or apostasy), so the witnesses by their testimony prevented that fall and created a binding obligation; upon retraction, they must compensate.

Key Terms
مؤكد (muʾakkad): Something that is confirmed or made binding; in this context, half the mahr becomes binding due to the witnesses' testimony.
موجب (mūjib): The one who makes something obligatory; here, the witnesses are like the originator of the obligation.
نشوز (nushūz): A wife's disobedience or defiance towards her husband, which can cause the mahr to fall before consummation.

Coerced Divorce Before Consummation: Recovery of Half Mahr
Ruling on coerced divorce before consummation with retraction and recovery of half mahr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on coerced divorce before consummation and explain the mechanism by which the husband recovers half the mahr from the coercer.

Core Idea
The coerced husband pays half mahr but recovers it from the coercer because the coercion confirmed the obligation.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi divorce law: a husband is coerced into divorcing his wife before consummation. According to the Hanafi school, the divorce is valid and half the mahr becomes wajib on the husband. However, the husband can reclaim that half mahr from the coercer. The passage also provides a deeper analogy-based proof (qiyas) comparing separation before consummation to annulment (faskh), and explains the role of witnesses.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and recovery mechanism
Identify the core ruling: when a husband is coerced into divorcing before consummation, half the mahr is wajib on him, but he recovers it from the coercer.
2
Identify the first proof (causation)
The coercer caused the divorce and thereby confirmed the obligation of half mahr; without coercion, the wife might have caused separation without any mahr.
This is a 'talil' (causal reasoning).
3
Understand the second proof: analogy to annulment
Separation before consummation is likened to annulment because the subject matter (bida') returns intact; in annulment, mahr drops entirely. Here, half mahr is initially wajib via testimony.
The passage clarifies it is 'in the sense of' annulment, not literal annulment, because a binding marriage cannot be annulled.
4
Connect to the witnesses' role
Half mahr becomes wajib on the husband initially due to the witnesses' testimony (they testified to the divorce). When they retract, they become liable for that half mahr.
The husband recovers from the coercer, not the witnesses, because the coercer caused the divorce.
5
Note the nuance: resemblance vs. actual annulment
The author of Hidayah explains that separation before consummation resembles annulment but is not actual annulment because marriage after binding does not accept annulment.
This is a critical distinction in Hanafi fiqh.

This is a ruling on coerced divorce before consummation. The ruling: if a person coerces a husband to divorce his wife before consummation, and the husband divorces under coercion, the half dower becomes wajib on the husband. When Both Agents Must Act: Tamlik and Ta'liq in Divorce
Explaining when both agents must act in divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between unconditional agency and tamlik/ta'liq, and state the Hanafi ruling that both agents must act in the latter two cases.

Core Idea
If the principal makes divorce conditional on both agents' consent or action, one agent alone cannot divorce.

In the previous sections, we learned that an agent can generally divorce the wife even if there are two agents, due to the principle that one agent's action suffices. However, this rule has exceptions. When the principal uses language that gives joint ownership of the divorce right (tamlik) or attaches a condition requiring both agents to act (ta'liq), the ruling changes. Both agents must now act together for the divorce to occur. In this lesson, we unpack these two exceptions and the qiyas (analogy) that supports them.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule and its exception
First, recall that in unconditional agency (tawkil), one agent can act alone even if there are two. Then note the two exceptions: (1) tamlik — when the principal says 'Divorce her if you both wish' or 'the matter is in your two hands,' and (2) ta'liq — when divorce is made conditional on both agents' actions, e.g., 'if you both enter the house.'
The general rule is from the default of agency; the exceptions arise from specific wording.
2
Analyze the first exception: tamlik
Examine the phrase 'Divorce her if you both wish' or 'her affair is in your hands.' According to Hanafi usul, this wording grants ownership (tamlik) of the divorce right to the agents. Ownership requires both agents to exercise the right because one cannot act in the property of another without permission. Additionally, tamlik must occur in the same session (majlis); both agents must be present and act together.
The dual form (shi'tuma) indicates joint agency.
3
Analyze the second exception: ta'liq (condition)
The second proof is that the principal makes divorce conditional upon the act of both agents. The text uses the analogy: if a person says 'if you both enter the house, my wife is divorced,' divorce only occurs when both enter. Similarly, if the condition is tied to both agents' actions, both must act. This is a clear qiyas (analogy).
The effective cause (illa) is that the ruling (divorce) is attached to a condition involving both parties.
4
Apply the qiyas to the case
Identify the asl (original case): a man says 'if both enter the house, wife is divorced' — divorce occurs only upon both entering. The far' (new case): principal says 'divorce if you both wish' — divorce occurs only when both pronounce divorce. The illa is that the act (divorce) is made dependent on both agents' actions. Thus, by analogy, both must act.
This qiyas is jali (clear) because the illa is directly observable.

Commentary
This passage discusses events from the agent's side that void the agency: death and continuous insanity absolutely void it. A special case is when the agent apostatizes and joins dar al-harb. Husband's Oath Refusal in Pre-Consummation Divorce Claim
Ruling with proof and extensions to analogous cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for half mahr when a husband refuses an oath in a wife's claim of divorce before consummation, and identify the scope of oath administration in related claims.

Core Idea
Refusing an oath in a financial claim triggers liability, not the claim itself.

This lesson examines a critical procedural ruling in Hanafi fiqh: when a wife claims divorce before consummation, the judge administers an oath to the husband. If he refuses, he becomes liable for half the mahr. The reasoning extends this principle to other claims involving property or rights, revealing a coherent legal logic grounded in the purpose (maqsad) of the claim. We will also see an important intra-Hanafi difference regarding pure lineage claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its condition
State the ruling: if a wife claims divorce before consummation, the husband is administered an oath; if he refuses, he must pay half the mahr. Note the key condition: the claim is before consummation, and the refusal triggers liability.
The ruling is explicit in the matn: 'استحلف الزوج فان نكل ضمن نصف المهر'.
2
Analyze the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
Understand why the oath is administered: because the claim has a financial purpose (al-maqsad huwa al-mal). In Hanafi law, oath administration is applicable in financial claims. The half mahr is at stake, so the oath is justified.
The commentary states: 'لأن الاستحلاف يجرى فى الطلاق عندهم لاسيما اذا كان المقصود هو المال'.
3
Trace the extensions to other claims
The passage extends the same principle to: (a) a wife claiming dower in marriage – oath administered, refusal establishes the dower but not the marriage; (b) claims of rights arising from lineage (inheritance, guardianship, maintenance, etc.) – oath administered because the purpose is those rights.
Each extension is linked by the common element: the claim involves a financial or proprietary right.
4
Examine the exception for pure lineage (nasab mujarrad)
For pure lineage claims (no associated rights), Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow oath only if the lineage could be established by confession (iqrar). This is an intra-Hanafi dispute, as the passage implies Abu Hanifa's position is different (probably no oath in pure lineage).
The phrase 'عندهما' indicates this is the view of the two companions, not Abu Hanifa.
5
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Given a similar case – e.g., a wife claims her husband divorced her after consummation but before payment of deferred dower – determine whether the oath is administered. Reflect on whether the claim is financial (dower) or purely personal.
This step consolidates understanding of the underlying illa (effective cause).

Oath-Taking in Family Claims: Unanimous Rulings and Dispute on Pure Lineage
Ruling and dispute on oath-taking in family claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when a defendant is required to swear in claims of divorce, marriage, and lineage, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding pure lineage.

Core Idea
Nukul (refusal to swear) establishes financial claims but not status; in pure lineage, oath is only taken if admission would prove the lineage.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies the Hanafi procedure for oath-taking in family law claims. It first presents a unanimous ruling: in divorce and marriage claims where the objective is monetary, the defendant must swear, and refusal (nukul) establishes the financial liability and, in divorce, the divorce itself. The same principle applies to lineage claims that include a financial right, such as inheritance. However, a dispute arises regarding pure lineage claims (nasab mujarrad), where the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) restrict oath-taking to cases where the lineage would be established by the defendant's own admission. This lesson walks you through the reasoning and the practical consequences of this distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the unanimous ruling on divorce and marriage claims
Note that if a woman claims divorce before consummation, the husband is made to swear. If he refuses, he pays half the dower (or full if after consummation) and the divorce is deemed to occur. Similarly, if she claims the dower in a marriage, nukul establishes the dower but not the marriage.
The ruling is unanimous (fi qawlihim jami'an).
2
Analyze the reasoning (qiyas) behind the ruling
The basis is qiyas on financial claims: since the objective in divorce and marriage claims is money, oath-taking (istihlaf) is valid. The illa (effective cause) is that the claim is primarily financial.
This is a qiyas khafi because the illa is inferred from the context.
3
Extend the ruling to lineage claims with associated rights
When lineage is claimed along with a right like inheritance, custody, or maintenance, the defendant is also made to swear by consensus. Nukul establishes the right but not the lineage itself.
The lineage is not established because it would impose on a third party.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on pure lineage (nasab mujarrad)
In pure lineage claims (no accompanying right), the two companions (Sahibayn) hold that oath is taken only if the lineage would be established by the defendant's own admission. Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly allows oath-taking generally.
The text sides with the Sahibayn, making it the mufta bihi.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Example: A woman claims a foundling as her brother to gain custody. Under the Sahibayn's view, she is not made to swear unless there is admission, because it would impose lineage on the foundling's father. Under Abu Hanifa's view, she would be made to swear.
The operative ruling follows the Sahibayn.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute. It first states a unanimous ruling: when a woman claims divorce before consummation, the husband is asked to swear. If he refuses his oath, he must pay half the dower (full dower if after consummation) and the divorce is deemed to have occurred. The same principle applies if a woman claims her dower in a marriage case; nukul establishes the dower but not the marriage itself. The reasoning is that the objective is financial, and oath-taking (istihlaf) is a well-established procedure in financial claims. The passage then extends this to lineage cases where a right such as inheritance, custody, or maintenance is claimed along with lineage. In such cases, by consensus, the defendant is made to swear, and nukul establishes the financial right but not the lineage itself. However, a dispute arises regarding pure lineage claims (without any associated financial right). According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad), in pure lineage claims, the defendant is only made to swear if the lineage would be proven by the defendant's own admission. For example, if a man claims another as his son, or a woman claims a child as her brother, the defendant is not made to swear because such a claim would impose lineage on a third party (e.g., the child's father). The commentary provides detailed scenarios: if a person claims inheritance as a brother, the defendant is made to swear because the claim includes a financial right; nukul establishes the inheritance but not the brotherhood. In the case of a foundling (laqit), a woman claiming to be the sister seeks custody; nukul establishes her right to custody. The passage is cut off before completing the discussion on the dispute, but it clearly shows the Hanafi school's methodology: oath-taking is a tool to resolve claims, especially financial ones, and the effect of nukul is limited to establishing the financial aspect, not the status aspect.

Key Terms
استحلاف (istihlaf): The process of asking the defendant to swear an oath in a legal claim.
نسل مجرد (nasab mujarrad): A claim of lineage without any associated financial or other right (e.g., inheritance, custody).
صاحبین (sahibayn): The two companions: Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.
