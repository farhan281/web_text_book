Classification of Agency Contracts: Agent as Principal or Messenger
Explaining why the agent is a mere messenger in isqat and possession contracts, but a principal in speech contracts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an agent acting as a principal (asil) in speech-based contracts and a mere messenger (safir) in isqat and possession-based contracts, and apply this classification to determine who holds contractual rights.

Core Idea
In Hanafi agency, the agent owns the contract in speech-based acts but is a mere messenger when forfeiture or possession is decisive.

The Hanafi school refines the role of an agent in contracts: is he a principal party (asil) or a mere conveyor (safir)? This distinction determines who bears rights and obligations. In this passage, the author of Ashraf al-Hidayah classifies nine agency contracts into three categories—forfeiture, possession-dependent, and speech-based—and explains the legal reasoning behind each classification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of contracts
From the passage, list the contracts: three isqat (forfeiture) contracts (emancipation for consideration, kitabah, sulh 'an al-inkar), six possession-based contracts (gift, charity, lending, deposit, pledge, loan), and by contrast, speech-based contracts like sale and sulh 'an al-iqrar.
The passage also mentions sulh 'an al-iqrar, which is treated like sale.
2
Understand the underlying rationale for each category
For isqat contracts, the principal forfeits his own right; thus the agent merely conveys that forfeiture. For possession-based contracts, the ruling (ownership, right to use, etc.) is established only upon possession over the principal's property; the agent facilitates but is not the originator. For speech-based contracts, the agent's own declaration (qawl) creates the contract, making him the originator.
The proof for isqat uses analogy (qiyas) to the second category (nikah, etc.). The proof for possession-based contracts uses the fact that possession occurs over the principal's property.
3
Apply the rule to determine agent role
For isqat and possession-based contracts, conclude that the agent is safir (messenger) and contractual rights attach to the principal. For speech-based contracts (like sale), conclude that the agent is asil (principal) and rights attach to the agent.
This has practical implications for who can sue, who is liable, and who owes performance.
4
Trace the consequences
If the agent is safir, the principal is the real party in interest; e.g., in a gift, the donee becomes owner of the principal's property directly. If the agent is asil, the agent holds the rights; e.g., in a sale, the agent can demand the price.
The passage explicitly states: 'rights of the contract are attached to the agent' in asil cases, and 'revert to the principal' in safir cases.

For charity: when the beneficiary takes possession, ownership and right of disposition are established. For lending: when the borrower takes possession of the borrowed item, the right to benefit is established. For deposit: when the trustee takes possession, the ruling of deposit is established – the trustee is obligated to safeguard the property, and if it is destroyed without negligence, no liability arises. For pledge: when the pledgee takes possession of the pledged item, the ruling of pledge is established – the pledgee must safeguard it, and if the debt is not recovered, he may recover it through the judge. For loan: when the borrower takes possession, he is entitled to dispose of the loaned property – that is the ruling of loan. Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah explains a key distinction in Hanafi agency law: whether the agent is an 'asil' (principal/original party) or a 'safir' (mere messenger). The commentator first clarifies that in contracts like sale and sulh 'an al-iqrar, the agent is considered an asil, meaning the rights of the contract (e.g., right to demand payment) belong to the agent, not the principal. Then nine types of agency are listed: three that involve forfeiture (isqat: emancipation, kitabah, and compromise on denial) and six that depend on possession (gift, charity, lending, deposit, pledge, loan). For the three isqat contracts, the agent is a safir because the essence of the contract is the forfeiture of a right by the principal, and the agent merely conveys that forfeiture. For the six possession-dependent contracts, the ruling is established only upon the occurrence of possession (qabd) over the object. Since possession takes place over the principal's property, the agent is considered a mere intermediary (safir) and the rights revert to the principal. In contrast, for contracts like sale that are concluded purely by speech without requiring possession, the agent's own declaration (qawl) is the operative factor, making him the asil, and thus rights attach to him. This classification determines who can sue, who is liable, and how ownership transfers.

Key Terms
سفیر (safir): A mere messenger who conveys the principal's words without being a party to the contract.
اصیل (asil): A principal party to the contract who possesses the contractual rights and obligations.
عقد اسقاط (aqd al-isqat): A contract of forfeiture where a party relinquishes a right or ownership.

Agency for Partnership and Mudarabah: Principal's Rights and Set-Off
Ruling on agency for shirkah/mudarabah and set-off rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency for shirkah/mudarabah as a mere messenger (safir) and apply the set-off rules when the principal demands payment, including the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, an agent for partnership or mudarabah is a mere messenger; the principal holds all contractual rights, including the right to demand payment and set-off under specific conditions.

This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies the limited role of an agent appointed for a partnership (shirkah) or mudarabah contract. The agent is not a party to the contract; he is merely a messenger (safir), so all rights and obligations belong to the principal. The passage then addresses a practical question: can the principal demand payment directly from the buyer? The answer is yes, but with important caveats about set-off. Understanding these rules is essential for correctly executing agency in commercial transactions and avoiding disputes over who bears the risk.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Nature of Agency for Shirkah/Mudarabah
Recognize that when someone appoints an agent for a partnership or mudarabah contract, the contract is attributed to the principal, not the agent. The agent is a mere messenger (safir) and executor (mujbir).
This is consistent with the general Hanafi principle that in contracts not requiring personal speech or possession, the agent's role is minimal.
2
Principal's Right to Demand Payment
The principal can demand the price from the buyer. However, the buyer may refuse because the principal is a stranger to the contract (ajnabi an al-aqd). If the buyer pays, it is valid and the agent cannot demand it again.
The buyer's refusal is a right, not a prohibition; if he pays, it settles the debt.
3
Understand the Set-Off (Muqasah) Scenarios
The passage presents three set-off cases: (1) buyer owes debt to principal → set-off occurs; (2) buyer owes debt to both principal and agent → set-off only with principal's debt; (3) buyer owes debt only to agent → set-off occurs according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
In case (2), the agent's debt is not offset because it is a separate obligation.
4
Examine the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Case (3)
For case (3), Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule set-off occurs because the agent has authority to release (ibra) the debt, but he guarantees it to the principal. Abu Yusuf may disagree, though not explicitly stated here.
The guarantee means the agent must compensate the principal if set-off occurs.
5

Given a scenario with a buyer, principal, and agent, determine whether set-off applies and who bears the loss.

The author of al-Hidayah said: If someone is appointed as an agent for a contract of partnership (shirkah) or mudarabah, then it is necessary to attribute the contract to the principal, and the rights of the contract revert to the principal, not to the agent. The agent is a mere messenger and executor.
Can the principal demand the price from the buyer? 
Yes, and if the principal demands the price from the buyer, the buyer has the right to refuse it to him because he is a stranger to the contract and its rights, since rights belong to the contracting party. If the buyer pays it to him, it is valid, and the agent cannot demand it from him a second time because the same price that was received is his right and has reached him, and there is no benefit in taking it and then giving it back. For this reason, if the buyer had a debt owed by the principal, set-off (muqasah) occurs. If the buyer had a debt owed by both the principal and the agent, set-off occurs only with the debt of the principal, not with the debt of the agent. And if the agent alone had a debt, set-off occurs according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, because in their view the agent has the authority to release (ibra) from it, but he guarantees it to the principal in both cases.

Core Idea
In mudarabah, generality is the default; the mudarib's word prevails when scope is disputed.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a common dispute between the mudarib and rabb al-mal: who decides the scope of the mudarabah? The Hanafi school presumes generality in the mudarib's authority, so his word is accepted unless both agree on restriction. The passage also reveals an internal Hanafi difference on whether an absolute command to sell includes any credit term or only customary ones. Understanding this ruling clarifies how Hanafi law protects the mudarib's operational flexibility while safeguarding the owner when the contract is explicitly restricted.

Dispute in Mudaraba and Scope of Absolute Sale Command
Rulings on credibility and scope of authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on whose statement is credible in mudaraba disputes and determine the scope of an absolute command to sell according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
In mudaraba disputes, the mudarib's word is taken unless both agree it's restricted; then it becomes pure agency where the principal's word prevails.

This lesson examines two interconnected rulings from Hanafi fiqh on mudaraba and agency. First, we learn whose statement is accepted when the mudarib and capital owner disagree about whether the mudaraba was absolute or restricted to cash. Then we explore an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the scope of an absolute command to sell: does it include credit for any term or only customary terms? These principles are essential for understanding the default rules in partnership and agency contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario and the default in mudaraba
Determine whether the mudaraba was absolute or restricted. The default principle (asl) in mudaraba is generality (umum) and absoluteness (itlaq). The mudarib gains authority by the mere mention of the word mudaraba.
This default shifts the burden of proof.
2
Determine who is the claimant and defendant
The party claiming against the default is the claimant (mudda'i). The mudarib who claims the absolute has his word consistent with the asl, so he is the defendant (mudda'a alayh). The rabb al-mal who claims restriction is the claimant.
In fiqh, the defendant's statement is taken if no evidence.
3
Apply the rule that the defendant's word is taken
Without evidence, the mudarib's word is credible because he is the defendant. This applies when the dispute is about cash vs. absolute.
If both agree it is restricted but differ on the type, the asl of absoluteness is abandoned; the contract becomes pure agency.
4
Understand the exception: when both agree it's restricted
If both parties agree the mudaraba was not absolute (e.g., each claims a specific type), the contract descends to pure agency (wakalah mahdah). Then the principal's (rabb al-mal's) word is taken.
This is because in agency, the principal's statement prevails in disputes.
5
Analyze the scope of an absolute command to sell
An absolute command to sell (al-amr al-mutlaq bi al-bay') includes both cash and credit sale. Abu Hanifa holds it includes any credit term, customary or not. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad restrict to customary terms.
The practical difference: a 50-year credit term is valid per Abu Hanifa, invalid per the Sahibayn.

Voidance of Agency by Principal's Loss of Capacity (Azl Hukmi)
Ruling on automatic voidance of agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three events that automatically void an agency and explain why the agent's knowledge is irrelevant in these cases.

Core Idea
Agency voids automatically when the principal loses capacity — the agent's knowledge is irrelevant.

In Hanafi law, agency is a derivative authority that depends on the principal's legal capacity. This passage from Al-Hidayah explains that when the principal loses the ability to command — due to financial incapacity, restriction, or dissolution of partnership — the agency ceases immediately. This is called 'azl hukmi (legal removal), which differs from intentional removal where the agent must be informed.

1
Identify the three causes of voidance

Note that these are cases where the principal's legal capacity ends.
2
Understand the principle: agency depends on authority
The passage states that the continuation of agency depends on the existence of the principal's authority (amr). When that authority ceases, the agency ceases.
This is the core legal reasoning.

Distinguish legal removal from intentional removal
Contrast 'azl hukmi (legal removal) with 'azl qasdi (intentional removal). Legal removal is automatic and does not require the agent's knowledge; intentional removal requires the agent's knowledge to take effect.

Translation: And when the mukatab appointed an agent then he became unable, or the ma'dhun lahu appointed an agent then he became restricted, or two partners appointed an agent then they separated, these circumstances void the agency upon the agent, whether he knows or does not know, because we have mentioned that the continuation of agency depends on the existence of the principal's authority, and the principal's authority has become void by restriction, inability, and separation. Explanation: The scenario is that if a mukatab appoints someone as agent and then the mukatab becomes unable to pay the kitabah (i.e., becomes insolvent), or a slave who is ma'dhun lahu appoints someone as agent and then the master prevents him from trading, or one of two partners appoints a third person as agent and then they separate (i.e., the partnership ends), then in all these cases the agency becomes void, whether the agent knows about these events or not, because we have already mentioned that the continuation of agency depends on the authority of the principal, and in these cases the principal's authority becomes void: the authority of the ma'dhun lahu due to restriction, the authority of the mukatab due to inability, and the authority of one of the partners due to separation. And in these cases there is no distinction between the agent's knowledge and ignorance because the agent's removal is a legal (hukmi) and involuntary matter, i.e., in these cases the agent is removed without any act of removal, and the agent's knowledge is a condition for intentional and voluntary removal, not for legal removal. If the principal intentionally and voluntarily removes his agent, then the agent's knowledge is necessary for removal; but if the agent is removed without the principal's will and intention, then the agent's knowledge is not required. In any case, in the mentioned cases the agent's removal is legal and involuntary, so this removal does not depend on the agent's knowledge; rather the agent becomes removed in any case, whether he knows it or not. For example, if someone is appointed as an agent to sell a specific item and then the principal himself sells that item, the agent becomes removed whether he knows it or not, because after the principal sells that item, there is no place left for the agent's action, so his agency does not remain.

Commentary
This passage discusses three scenarios where the principal's loss of legal capacity automatically voids the agency, regardless of the agent's knowledge. The three cases are: (1) a mukatab (slave on a contract of manumission) who becomes unable to pay, (2) a ma'dhun lahu (slave permitted to trade) who is later restricted by his master, and (3) two partners who separate (ending the partnership). In each case, the principal's authority (amr) is extinguished, and since the agency depends on that authority, it is voided. This is termed 'azl hukmi' (legal removal) as opposed to 'azl qasdi' (intentional removal) which requires the agent's knowledge. The reasoning is that the agent's actions derive their validity from the principal's command; when the command ceases, the agency ceases automatically. The example of the agent appointed to sell: if the principal himself sells the item, the agency ends because the subject matter is gone. The passage emphasizes that knowledge is irrelevant for legal removal; only intentional removal requires knowledge. This ruling is consistent with the Hanafi principle that agency is a derivative authority (wilayah tabi'ah) contingent on the principal's capacity.

Key Terms
عزل حکمی (azl hukmi): Legal removal of an agent that occurs automatically without the principal's explicit action, due to external events.
مکاتب (mukatab): A slave who has made a contract (kitabah) with his master to purchase his freedom in installments.
ماذون لہ (ma'dhun lahu): A slave who is permitted by his master to engage in trade.
