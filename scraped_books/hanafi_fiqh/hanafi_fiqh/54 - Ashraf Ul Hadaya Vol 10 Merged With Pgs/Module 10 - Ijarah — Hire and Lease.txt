Dispute of Witnesses over Rent Amount in Ijarah
Ruling on witness disagreement in ijarah contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two scenarios of witness disagreement in ijarah and apply the correct ruling based on whether the term has ended and who is the plaintiff.

Core Idea
In ijarah, witness disagreement on rent invalidates the contract at inception, but after the term, it becomes a debt claim where the lower admitted amount prevails.

Witness testimony is a key tool for proving contracts, but what happens when the witnesses contradict each other? In ijarah (hire), the ruling depends critically on timing. This passage expands the analogy: before the term, it is like sale; after the term, it becomes a debt claim. Understanding this distinction prevents errors in adjudicating rent disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main scenarios
The passage first splits the case into whether the dispute occurs at the beginning of the ijarah term or after the term has passed. State these two branches.
This classification is the key to the entire ruling.
2
Analyze the first scenario: during the term
If the contract is still in its initial period and witnesses disagree on rent, the testimony is rejected. Why? Because the contract is the subject matter, and disagreement makes it as if two different contracts exist—analogous to sale.
The analogy to sale is explicit: 'mithl al-bay'
3
Analyze the second scenario: after the term
Once the term has ended, only the rent amount is disputed. This becomes a debt claim. Now examine two subcases: who is the plaintiff?
The plaintiff is either the lessor (ajir) or the lessee (musta'jir).
4
Apply the debt analogy when the lessor is plaintiff
If the lessor claims a higher rent and witnesses disagree, the lower amount is accepted. This follows the rule for debt claims: when witnesses differ, the lower figure is taken as the agreed debt.
Analogous to a person claiming a debt of 1500 but witnesses say 1000 and 1500.
5
Apply the admission rule when the lessee is plaintiff
If the lessee sues and the lessor admits a specific amount, that amount becomes the rent regardless of witnesses. The lessor cannot claim more even with disagreeing witnesses.
The lessee's admission sets the maximum; witnesses cannot create a higher obligation.

The seventh case is that of ijarah (hire). It has two scenarios. Disagreement between the lessor (ajir) and lessee (musta'jir) over the amount of rent occurs either at the beginning of the ijarah term or after the term has passed. If the disagreement occurs at the beginning of the term, it is analogous to sale: just as in sale, when witnesses disagree, their testimony is not accepted, similarly in ijarah, due to the witnesses' disagreement, their testimony is not accepted. Because in this case, the aim is to prove the ijarah contract, and the contract has become different due to the rent amount disagreement: one witness testified that the rent is one thousand and another said it is one and a half thousand. Due to this difference, the ijarah contract is no longer one but two—one for one thousand, the other for one and a half thousand—and since the complete set of testimony for one matter does not exist, this testimony is not accepted and the ijarah is not proven. If the disagreement occurs after the ijarah term has passed, then there are two subcases: the plaintiff is the lessor or the lessee. If the plaintiff is the lessor and he claims the greater amount (one and a half thousand), and the witnesses disagree (one says one thousand, one says one and a half thousand), then testimony is accepted for the lesser amount (one thousand) and judgment is given in favor of the lessor for one thousand. Because when the ijarah term has passed, only the rent amount is disputed, and this is like a person claiming a debt of one and a half thousand against another, but one witness testifies for one thousand and the other for one and a half thousand—then testimony is accepted for one thousand. Similarly here, since the lessor's claim is merely a debt, testimony is accepted for one thousand. If the plaintiff is the lessee and the lessor denies, then whatever amount the lessor admits becomes the rent. The agreement or disagreement of witnesses does not affect this because if the lessor admits the greater amount (one and a half thousand), there is no dispute; if he admits one thousand, the lessor cannot take more than one thousand through evidence. Because the lessor claims one and a half thousand and brings proof, but if the witnesses disagree, it was already established that in such a case the lesser amount (one thousand) is accepted by the proof. Therefore, the amount admitted by the lessee becomes obligatory, whether little or much.

Tahaluf in Ijarah Disputes Before Possession
Ruling on tahaluf in ijarah before possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of tahaluf and evidence preference in an ijarah dispute before the lessee receives the benefit.

Core Idea
Before benefit is received, ijarah disputes are resolved by mutual oaths and rescission, mirroring sale rules.

This passage explains the Hanafi procedure when a lessor and lessee disagree about the rent or the duration of a lease before the lessee has actually used the rented property. The key principle is that the contract is not yet finalised, so the dispute is handled through mutual oaths (tahaluf) and the contract is cancelled. The rules for who swears first and whose evidence is preferred depend on what exactly is disputed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the precondition for tahaluf
Confirm that the dispute occurs before the lessee has taken possession of the benefit (e.g., before moving in or using the service). This is the essential condition.
2
Determine the subject of dispute
Is the disagreement about the rent amount (ujrah) or about the benefit itself (e.g., duration, description)? This dictates the order of oaths.
If rent is disputed, the lessee swears first; if benefit is disputed, the lessor swears first.
3
Apply the tahaluf procedure
If neither party has evidence, they take oaths. The party who refuses the oath loses and the other's claim is upheld. The contract is then rescinded.
4
Evaluate evidence if provided
If one party brings evidence, it is accepted. If both bring evidence, preference is given: for rent disputes, the lessor's evidence is preferred; for benefit disputes, the lessee's evidence is preferred.
When both rent and benefit are contested, each party's evidence is accepted for the part they claim, and the judge combines them.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete example
Work through the given example: lessor claims one month at 10 dirhams, lessee claims two months at 5 dirhams. The judgment is for two months at 10 dirhams – the lessee's claim on benefit and lessor's on rent are taken together.
This illustrates how evidence on each part is honoured separately.

Ruling on Dispute After Full Receipt in Ijarah
States ruling when dispute occurs after full benefit receipt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when lessor and lessee dispute after full receipt of benefit, explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and distinguish this from the ruling in sale.

Core Idea
After full benefit receipt, the lessee's word with oath is accepted; benefits have no independent value to base mutual oaths.

This lesson examines a procedural ruling from the chapter of ijarah: what happens if the lessor and lessee dispute over terms after the lessee has fully used the leased property? The Hanafi school holds that mutual oath-taking (tahaluf) is no longer possible because the subject matter (the benefit) is destroyed and has no substitute value. Instead, the lessee is treated as the defendant and his statement with oath is accepted. We will explore the reasoning of the three imams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
The text addresses a dispute between lessor and lessee after the lessee has fully received the benefit (istifa'). Determine what they might be disputing (e.g., rent amount, duration).
The dispute could be over any contractual term that affects payment.
2
State the ruling
The ruling is: no mutual oaths (tahaluf) occur, and the lessee's statement with his oath is accepted. This is the mufta bihi position.
Contrast with sale where mutual oaths are used when the subject matter is destroyed.
3
Analyze the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
They base their view on a general principle: destruction of the subject matter prevents mutual oaths. Since the benefit (subject matter) is fully consumed, tahaluf is blocked.
For them, the reasoning is straightforward: no subject matter, no oaths.
4
Analyze the reasoning of Muhammad al-Shaybani
Imam Muhammad normally allows mutual oaths in sale even after destruction because the sold item has a value (qima) that stands in its place. But in ijarah, benefits have no independent value; they exist only through the contract. Thus, no substitute value exists, so tahaluf is not applicable.
This highlights the unique nature of benefits in ijarah.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Since tahaluf is prevented, the lessee is the defendant (the one being claimed against), so his statement with oath is accepted. The lessor must provide proof if he wants to challenge.
This is a straightforward application of the rule 'al-bayyinah 'ala al-mudda'i wa al-yamin 'ala al-mudda'a 'alayhi'.

Even according to Imam Muhammad, who normally allows mutual oaths in sale by substituting the value of the destroyed item, here there is no value to substitute because benefits have no independent existence; they only exist through the contract. Rescinding the contract would leave nothing to base the oath on. Therefore, since mutual oaths are prevented, the lessee is considered the defendant (the one being claimed against), so his statement with an oath is given weight.

Ijarah is renewed moment by moment; partial receipt does not bar tahlif for remaining usufruct.

When a lease (ijarah) dispute arises after some of the usufruct has been used, the Hanafi school distinguishes between the past and future portions. This lesson examines the ruling: mutual oaths (tahlif) and rescission for the remaining period, and acceptance of the lessee's statement for the past period. The reasoning hinges on the unique time-based nature of usufruct, which contrasts with the indivisibility of a sale contract.

This distinction is key to the Hanafi position.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ijarah is gradual; past use is completed, so lessee's word holds; future use is pending, so mutual oaths lead to rescission.

When a lessor and lessee disagree about the rent after some benefit has been enjoyed, the Hanafi school provides a nuanced ruling based on the nature of ijarah.
