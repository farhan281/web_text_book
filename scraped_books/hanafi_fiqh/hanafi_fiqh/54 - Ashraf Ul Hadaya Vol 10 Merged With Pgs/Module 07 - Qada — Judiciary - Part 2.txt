Administering an Oath to a Zoroastrian: Hanafi Internal Dispute
Dispute within Hanafi school on oath formula for Zoroastrians

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on administering an oath to a Zoroastrian and explain the reasoning behind each.

Core Idea
A qadi must tailor the oath formula to avoid glorifying creation, causing an internal Hanafi dispute over Zoroastrians.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: should a Zoroastrian (Majusi) be sworn by 'Allah who created fire' or only by 'Allah' alone? Three views emerge from Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Muhammad, and al-Khassaf. Understanding this helps a qadi select the effective oath while respecting tawhid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
List the three positions: Abu Hanifa (only 'by Allah' for everyone), Muhammad ('by Allah who created fire' for Zoroastrians), and al-Khassaf (special formulas only for Jews and Christians, otherwise 'by Allah').
The matn (Quduri) states Muhammad's view as the default; the other two are mentioned as riwayat.
2
Understand the underlying reasoning
For each position, identify the rationale. Muhammad: Zoroastrians revere fire, so mentioning it deters perjury. Abu Hanifa: any mention of a created thing alongside Allah implies glorification of that thing, which is shirk. Al-Khassaf: scriptures are worthy of honor, but fire is not; thus only Jews/Christians get special formulas.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on the maqsad of the oath and the principle of avoiding shirk.
3
Analyze the tarjih
The text states that al-Khassaf's view is 'the choice of some of our scholars' (ba'd mashayikhina). This suggests a preference but not an absolute tarjih. Later Hanafi manuals may weigh the positions differently.
Tarjih may depend on context: if the Zoroastrian's reverence for fire is strong, Muhammad's view may be more effective; if concern for tawhid is paramount, Abu Hanifa's view prevails.
4
Note the practical application (thamarah al-khilaf)
The qadi must know which formula to use. The thamarah is the actual wording of the oath. If the wrong formula is used, the oath may not achieve its purpose (preventing false testimony) or may be considered improper.
This dispute directly affects court procedure.

Swearing on the Result vs. Cause: The Fundamental Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath wording based on talil of harm prevention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad al-Shaybani, and Abu Yusuf on oath wording in civil claims and explain the rationale for the majority view.

Core Idea
Swear on the result, not the cause, to protect the defendant from harm from later revocations.

When a defendant denies a claim, the judge must administer a specific oath. Should the oath be on the direct act (e.g., 'I did not sell') or on the resulting status (e.g., 'no valid sale exists')? The Hanafi school split among its early imams. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and preferred ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim types and recommended oath wording per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
List the four claims (sale, usurpation, marriage, divorce) and note the oath: on the result, not the cause.
For sale: 'No valid sale exists.' For usurpation: 'You have no right to its return.'
2
Understand the rationale for the result oath
The cause may be revoked later (iqalah, gift, khul, renewal), making the cause oath harmful and potentially false.
Example: sale canceled by iqalah; swearing 'I did not sell' would be false.
3
Contrast with Abu Yusuf's view
He holds the default is on the cause; only shift to result if the defendant alludes (ta'rid) to revocation.
This gives the defendant more initiative to protect himself.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under the majority view, a defendant who sold but canceled can truthfully swear the result oath. Under Abu Yusuf, he would perjure unless he mentions the cancellation.
The majority view is mufta bihi to avoid perjury and harm.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi ruling with proof regarding the proper wording of oaths (yamin) in various civil claims. The primary issue is whether the judge should administer the oath on the cause (sabab) or on the result (hasil) when the defendant denies the claim. The text presents the positions of the three major Hanafi imams: Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad (al-Shaybani) hold that in cases like purchase, usurpation, marriage, and divorce, the oath should be on the result—e.g., 'There is no valid sale between us' rather than 'I did not sell'—because the cause (e.g., the act of selling) may be subsequently overturned by iqalah (mutual rescission) or other revocations like gift or renewed marriage. Swearing on the cause would cause harm (darar) to the defendant if he later faces another claim based on a revived cause. Imam Abu Yusuf, on the other hand, holds that the oath should generally be on the cause, except when the defendant makes an allusion (ta'rid) to revocations; then it shifts to the result. A third view compares the defendant's denial: if he denies the cause itself, swear on the cause; if he denies the resulting liability, swear on the result. The preferred principle (al-asl) according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is that the oath is on the result whenever the cause can be removed by a later event (e.g., iqalah, khul', renewed marriage). However, there is an exception: when swearing on the result would lead to negligence of the plaintiff's right because the defendant could truthfully swear based on his own belief (e.g., the husband does not believe the divorced woman's claim for nafaqah, or the buyer does not believe the pre-emption claim). In such cases, the oath must be on the cause by consensus to protect the plaintiff's right. Further, if the cause cannot be removed by any later event (like a Muslim slave's claim of emancipation, where slavery cannot be reimposed after freedom due to apostasy or joining enemy territory), the oath is also on the cause by consensus. This contrasts with a female slave or non-Muslim slave, where slavery can be repeated (e.g., by apostasy or breach of covenant), so for them the oath remains on the result. The text provides rational proofs (talil) for each position, highlighting the Hanafi commitment to preventing harm (darar) to the defendant while balancing the plaintiff's right to a fair hearing.

Key Terms
التحليف (al-tahlīf): The act of administering an oath to a party by the judge in a legal claim.
الحاصل (al-ḥāṣil): The resulting legal status or outcome, e.g., existence of a valid sale, right to restitution, validity of marriage.
السبب (al-sabab): The causal act that gives rise to the legal claim, e.g., the act of selling, usurping, marrying, or divorcing.
التعريض (al-ta‘rīḍ): An allusion or indirect reference by the defendant to a fact that revokes the cause, such as mentioning iqalah or khulʻ.
الإقالة (al-iqālah): Mutual rescission of a contract; both parties agree to cancel the sale.

Exceptions and Consensus Cases in Oath Wording
Exceptions to the result oath rule with consensus cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify cases where the oath must be on the cause by consensus and explain the rationale for the exception when the plaintiff's consideration is at risk.

Core Idea
Oath shifts to cause when result oath would harm plaintiff's right or when cause cannot be revoked.

The general principle is to swear on the result. However, the text outlines two exceptions where the oath must be on the cause by consensus: (1) when swearing on the result would prejudice the plaintiff because the defendant could truthfully swear based on his own belief, and (2) when the cause itself cannot be removed by any later event, like a Muslim slave's emancipation. This lesson examines these exceptions and their underlying reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first exception: plaintiff's consideration at risk
When the plaintiff's right would be lost because the defendant can truthfully swear on the result according to his own belief (e.g., husband denies divorced wife's nafaqah, buyer denies pre-emption claim).
In these cases, result oath would harm the plaintiff, so cause oath is used by consensus.
2
Identify the second exception: cause cannot be removed
When the cause is irrevocable (e.g., Muslim slave's claim of emancipation), the oath is on the cause by consensus.
Contrast with female or non-Muslim slave where slavery can be repeated, so result oath remains.
3
Apply the rule to given scenarios
Given a scenario, determine whether the default result oath applies or one of the exceptions triggers a cause oath.
Use the criteria: Is the plaintiff at risk? Is the cause removable?

The text provides rational proofs (talil) for each position, highlighting the Hanafi commitment to preventing harm (darar) to the defendant while balancing the plaintiff's right to a fair hearing.

Key Terms
ترك النظر (tark al-nazar): Negligence of consideration; the situation where the defendant's oath on the result would harm the plaintiff because the defendant can honestly swear based on his own belief.
رفع الرافع (raf‘ al-rafi‘): Removal by a remover; the concept that a cause can be undone by a later legal event.

Swearing on the Result vs. the Cause: The Hanafi Principle in Claims
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath formula in claims.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for administering oaths in claims and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Oath on the result protects the defendant; swear on the cause only if the cause cannot be removed.

When a plaintiff has no evidence, the defendant is required to take an oath. But what should the oath be about? This lesson explores the Hanafi position that the oath should be on the resultant legal status (hasil ma'na) rather than the specific cause (sabab), and examines the disagreement with Imam Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the oath is taken on the resultant meaning (hasil ma'na) when the cause can be removed.
This is the principle (asl) according to the two.
2
Understand the rationale: darar prevention
Swearing on the cause could harm the defendant if the cause no longer exists; the oath on the result avoids this harm.
Harm removal (raf' al-darar) is a key consideration.
3
Examine the examples
The text provides four examples: sale (rescission), usurpation (gift/sale), marriage (divorce/khul'), and divorce (remarriage). Each cause can be nullified.
In each case, the oath wording is on the current legal status.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf holds that the oath is on the cause unless the defendant makes an allusion (ta'rid) that the cause may have been removed.
Abu Yusuf prioritizes the literal denial of the cause.
5
Recognize exceptions
If the cause cannot be removed (e.g., freeing a Muslim slave), or if swearing on the result would harm the plaintiff (e.g., different madhhab beliefs), then by consensus the oath is on the cause.
These exceptions protect the plaintiff's rights.

So according to the two parties, the principle is that the oath is taken on the resultant meaning provided that the cause can be removed by something that removes it. But when there is neglect of consideration towards the plaintiff, then by consensus the oath is taken on the cause. And this is like a woman divorced three times claiming nafaqah of the waiting period, and the husband is among those who do not believe in it; or a claim of pre-emption due to adjacency, and the purchaser does not believe in it. Because if he swears on the resultant meaning, he will be truthful in his oath according to his belief, so consideration for the plaintiff is lost. And if the cause is such that it cannot be removed, then by consensus the oath is taken on the cause, like a Muslim slave claiming emancipation from his master, contrary to a female slave and a disbelieving slave, because regarding the female slave, if she apostatizes and joins the abode of war, slavery recurs; and regarding the disbelieving slave, if he breaches the covenant and joins the abode of war, slavery is restored; but regarding the Muslim slave, it does not recur. Explanation of the case: If a person claims against another that he purchased his slave for one thousand, and the defendant denies, and the plaintiff has no evidence, then the oath shall be taken from the defendant (the seller) in these words: 'By Allah, there is no sale of this slave between me and him,' and the oath shall not be taken as 'By Allah, I did not sell this slave,' i.e., the oath is taken on the resultant meaning and not on the cause. The proof for this is that sometimes a specific property is sold and then rescinded (iqala), after which the sale does not remain. So if the oath is taken on the cause (the sale), the defendant would be harmed; therefore, to remove harm, the oath is taken on the resultant meaning. And if a person claims usurpation against another and the plaintiff has no evidence, the oath shall be taken from the defendant in these words: 'By Allah, this claimant does not deserve the return of the usurped item from me,' and the oath shall not be taken as 'By Allah, I did not usurp,' because sometimes a person usurps but the owner gives the usurped item to the usurper or sells it to him, or the usurper pays compensation and becomes owner of the usurped item. So if the oath is taken from the defendant as 'I did not usurp,' the defendant would be harmed; therefore, to remove harm, the oath is taken as 'The claimant has no right to the return of the usurped property from me,' because if usurpation existed, he would have the right to return. And if someone marries a woman and she denies it, or vice versa, and the claimant cannot produce evidence, then the oath shall be taken from the defendant in these words: 'By Allah, there is no marriage existing between us now,' and not 'By Allah, I did not marry her,' because after marriage, divorce can occur, and after khul', the marriage does not remain. So taking the oath as 'I did not marry' would harm the defendant; therefore, to remove harm, the oath is not taken in those words. And if a woman claims divorce against her husband and he denies it, and she has no evidence, then the oath shall be taken from the defendant (the husband) in these words: 'By Allah, this woman is not irrevocably separated from me for the reason she claims,' and not 'By Allah, I did not divorce her,' because after irrevocable separation, marriage can be renewed. So if the oath is taken as 'I did not divorce her,' the defendant would be harmed; therefore, to remove harm, the oath is not taken in those words but he should say: 'By Allah, this woman is not separated from me now.' The author of Al-Hidayah said: In all the mentioned cases, the oath is taken on the resultant meaning, and not on the cause (sale, usurpation, marriage, divorce), and the reason for this is that if the oath is taken on the cause, the defendant would be harmed; so to remove harm from the defendant, the oath is taken on the resultant meaning. The author of Al-Hidayah states: Taking the oath on the resultant meaning is the school of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad, and the school of Imam Abu Yusuf is that in all the mentioned cases, the oath is taken on the cause, except when the defendant makes an allusion (ta'rid) with the mentioned things, e.g., saying 'sometimes a sale occurs and then is rescinded' – in such a case, the oath is taken on the resultant meaning. Some scholars said: The denial of the defendant should be considered; if he denied the cause, then the oath is taken from him on the cause, and if he denied the effect (hukm), then the oath is taken on the resultant meaning. The author of Al-Hidayah said: According to the two parties (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), the principle is that the oath is taken on the resultant meaning provided that the cause is such that it can be removed by something that removes it – e.g., sale can be removed by iqala, usurpation can be nullified by gift or sale, marriage can be nullified by khul', and divorce can be removed by renewal of marriage. So, if the cause can be removed by something that removes it, then in that case according to the two parties, the oath is taken on the resultant meaning. Yes, if taking the oath on the resultant meaning does not consider the plaintiff, then at that time by consensus the oath is taken on the cause. For example, a woman who has been given three divorces claims nafaqah of the waiting period from her husband, and the husband, being of the Shafi'i school, does not believe in nafaqah for a triple-divorced woman; or a person claims pre-emption due to adjacency and the purchaser, being Shafi'i, does not believe in pre-emption due to adjacency. In such a case, the oath is taken on the cause, not on the resultant meaning, because if he swore on the resultant meaning and said 'By Allah, she has no right to nafaqah of the waiting period' or 'I have no pre-emption due to adjacency,' he would be truthful in his oath according to his belief, and consideration for the plaintiff (the woman and the pre-emption claimant) would be lost. And if the cause is such that it cannot be removed, then by consensus the oath is taken on the cause. For example, a Muslim slave claimed emancipation from his master; then the oath will be taken from the master by consensus: 'By Allah, I did not free him,' because emancipation is a cause that cannot be removed by anything. Yes, if a female slave claimed emancipation or a disbelieving slave claimed emancipation, then in both cases the oath is taken from the defendant on the resultant meaning, i.e., the defendant will say: 'By Allah, this female slave or this disbelieving slave is not free now.' The reason for this is that if a female slave becomes free and then apostatizes and goes to the abode of war, and if a disbelieving slave breaches the covenant and goes to the abode of war, slavery re-imposes upon them, and when slavery re-imposes, their freedom is terminated. So their emancipation is removed. And since the emancipation of a female slave and a disbelieving slave can be removed, the oath is taken from their defendant on the resultant meaning, and not on the cause. As for a Muslim slave, once he becomes free, his freedom is not removed, and slavery does not re-impose upon him; therefore, if a Muslim slave claims emancipation, the oath will be taken from the defendant on the cause.

This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa (Book of Claims) addresses a fundamental procedural issue: when a plaintiff has no evidence, how should the oath (yamin) be administered to the defendant? Thus, the passage provides a nuanced rule balancing protection of both parties.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa in Ashraf al-Hidaya deals with two interrelated rulings concerning oaths in property claims. Learning Objective

Core Idea
When both parties dispute and the goods exist, both take oaths — the hadith overrides contrary qiyas.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a classic fiqh scenario: the seller claims a higher price, the buyer denies it, and the goods still exist. The Hanafi school rules that both parties must take oaths, and if both swear or both refuse, the sale is rescinded. This ruling is grounded in a specific hadith, which overrides the qiyas that would apply after the buyer takes possession. Let's unpack the legal reasoning and the proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
Recognize that the dispute is about the price of existing goods. The ruling is that the judge administers oaths to both parties.
The ruling applies whether or not the buyer has taken possession.

The hadith states: 'When the two transacting parties differ and the sold item still exists, then both take oaths and return the goods.'
This is a textual proof (nas) that establishes mutuality.

Before possession, qiyas supports mutual oaths because both are deniers. After possession, qiyas would require only the buyer's oath. The hadith overrides this qiyas.

The Hanafi school relies on the hadith despite the qiyas objection. The ruling is mufta bihi: both swear, and if both swear or both refuse, the sale is annulled.

Whose Oath to Begin with in Mutual Oaths: Starting with the Buyer
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for administering oaths when buyer and seller differ, and explain the rationale for starting with the buyer.

Core Idea
Start with the buyer's oath because his refusal gives immediate judgment.

This passage addresses a procedural question in Islamic court: when both buyer and seller must take oaths, which party's oath should the judge administer first? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement, resolved in favor of the buyer. Understanding this ruling reveals how the school prioritizes practical benefit (istihsan) over a literal reading of hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the dispute
Recognize that both buyer and seller are required to take oaths. The question is: whose oath comes first?
2
State the preferred Hanafi position
The judge should start with the buyer's oath. This is attributed to Imam Muhammad, the later view of Abu Yusuf, and one narration from Abu Hanifa.
Mufta bihi (relied upon view).
3
Analyze the reasoning (talil)
The buyer's denial is stronger (ashadd inharan) because he denies both the obligation of the price and the duty to pay. Moreover, the benefit of nukul (refusal) is immediate: if the buyer refuses to swear, the price is imposed at once.
Contrast with seller: if seller refuses, delivery is delayed until price is paid.
4
Consider the opposing view and its proof
Abu Yusuf's earlier view was to start with the seller, citing the hadith 'the statement is that of the seller.' This hadith gives the seller precedence in statements, analogously in oaths.
The hadith is specific to when only one party's statement is taken.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (weighing of evidence)
The hadith is overridden by the stronger benefit (maslahah) of immediate judgment. Since the oath procedure is meant to resolve disputes swiftly, starting with the buyer achieves that goal.
Thus the mufta bihi view is adopted.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) about which party's oath should be administered first when both take oaths. The author presents the preferred view: start with the buyer's oath. This is attributed to Imam Muhammad, the later view of Imam Abu Yusuf, and one narration from Imam Abu Hanifa. The reasoning is that the buyer's denial is stronger (ashadd) because: (1) he is first demanded to pay the price, and he denies both the obligation of the price and the duty to pay; (2) the benefit of his refusal (nukul) is immediate—if he refuses to swear, the price is imposed on him at once. In contrast, if the seller refuses first, the demand for delivery of the goods is delayed until he receives the price, so no immediate resolution. The earlier view of Abu Yusuf was to start with the seller, citing the hadith 'the statement is that of the seller.' However, the author notes that this hadith is interpreted as giving the seller preference in the absence of mutual oaths, not when the hadith about mutual oaths applies. The practical benefit of starting with the buyer is decisive, so that view is mufta bihi.

Key Terms
نكول (nukul): Refusal to take an oath; the consequence is that the refuser's claim is rejected or the opponent's claim is established.
اشد انكارا (ashadd inharan): Stronger denial; the buyer's denial is considered stronger because he denies both the obligation and the payment, and his nukul has immediate effect.

Wording of the Oath in Price Disputes
Explains the precise wording of oaths for buyer and seller.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the correct wording of the oath for the seller and buyer in a price dispute and explain why the Hanafi school prefers negation alone.

Core Idea
The Hanafi oath in price disputes is negation-only; affirming the contrary is not required and less sound.

This lesson focuses on the precise wording (sifat al-yamin) prescribed by the Hanafi school for oaths when buyer and seller dispute the price. You will learn the two versions: the simple negation and the version that adds an affirmation, and understand why the school prefers the negation-only form based on the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him).

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
When the sale involves goods for goods or price for price, the judge may begin with the oath of either party because they are equal. The dispute is over the price: each claims a different amount.
2
State the seller's oath
The seller swears: 'By Allah, I did not sell it for the price claimed by the buyer (the lower price).' This is a pure negation.
Example: if buyer claims 500, seller swears 'I did not sell it for 500.'
3
State the buyer's oath
The buyer swears: 'By Allah, I did not buy it for the price claimed by the seller (the higher price).' Again, a negation.
Example: if seller claims 700, buyer swears 'I did not buy it for 700.'
4
Consider the alternative from Al-Ziyadat
Imam Muhammad in Al-Ziyadat adds an affirmation to the negation for emphasis: the seller adds 'but I sold it for the higher price,' and the buyer adds 'but I bought it for the lower price.'
This is a legitimate variant within the school.
5
Determine the preferred view
The correct view (al-ashah) is to limit to negation alone. This is because oaths are established for negation, as evidenced by the hadith of Qasama where the companions only negated without affirming.
The mufta bihi is negation-only.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement that provides the precise wording (sifat al-yamin) for oaths in sale disputes. It distinguishes between two cases: (1) when the dispute involves different types of consideration (e.g., goods for price), the judge has the discretion to start with either party because both are equal in this context. Thus, the concise negation is sufficient and more in line with the purpose of oaths.

The Proof from the Hadith of Qasama for Oath Wording
Explains the precise wording of oaths for buyer and seller.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hadith of Qasama supports the Hanafi position on oath wording in price disputes and analyze its derivation (wajh al-dalalah).

Core Idea
The Hadith of Qasama proves oaths are only negation; adding affirmation is an unnecessary expansion.

This lesson dives into the specific proof (dalil) used by the Hanafi school to prefer the negation-only wording for oaths. You will examine the Hadith of Qasama, its context, and how it is applied to the issue of oath wording in sale disputes.

Guided Path
1
Present the hadith text
The hadith states: 'By Allah, you did not kill, nor do you know his killer.' This is from the incident of Qasama where the Prophet instructed the defendants to swear.
The hadith is found in various collections; it is a well-known proof in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the operative phrase
The oath consists only of negation: 'you did not kill' and 'you do not know his killer'. There is no affirmative statement like 'but we are innocent' or 'but we know who did it'.
The hadith does not contain any affirmation.
3
Establish the wajh al-dalalah
The Hanafi jurists reason that since the Prophet (peace be upon him) only required the negation, any addition to the oath is not part of the Sunnah. This is the evidence for limiting oaths to negation.
The logical link: the Prophet's practice is the primary source.
4
Apply to price dispute oaths
By analogy (or direct application), the oath in a price dispute should also be limited to negation: the seller negates the buyer's claim, and the buyer negates the seller's claim.
No need to add 'but I sold it for X'.

In a price dispute, the judge swears the buyer first because his denial is severe and his refusal yields immediate judgement.

The preferred position (mufta bihi), held by Imam Muhammad, later Imam Abu Yusuf, and a narration from Abu Hanifa, is to start with the buyer. Note that this is the sahih (correct) view.

The Hanafi response is that the practical benefit of starting with the buyer (immediate effect) outweighs the general implication of the hadith. Thus, the buyer is sworn first.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof explaining the consequences of mutual oaths (tahaluf) in a sale price dispute. When Mutual Oaths Do Not Apply: Ajal, Khiyar, and Partial Receipt
Ruling on tahaluf for ancillary disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that disputes over ajal, khiyar, or partial receipt do not trigger tahaluf, and distinguish such disputes from those over the price's description or type.

Core Idea
Mutual oaths only apply to disputes about the price or object, not ancillary conditions.

In Islamic judicial procedure, tahaluf (mutual oaths) is a mechanism to resolve irreconcilable disputes in contracts. However, the Hanafi school restricts its application to disputes over the essential elements of a sale: the price and the object of sale. This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies that disagreements over the payment term (ajal), a condition of option (khiyar al-shart), or partial receipt of the price do not warrant mutual oaths. Understanding this distinction is crucial for judges and muftis when handling sale disputes.

The reason is that these matters are not part of the ma'qud alayhi (object) or ma'qud bihi (price). The text says 'هذا اختلاف في غير المعقود عليه والمعقود به'.
The analogy is drawn to disputes about reduction (hatt) or release (ibra'), which also do not trigger tahaluf.

Disputes About Ancillary Matters Do Not Require Mutual Oaths
Explaining why tahaluf is not required for disputes over term, option, and receipt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that tahaluf is only required for disputes about the subject matter and price, and identify which ancillary matters are excluded.

Core Idea
Only disputes about the essence of the sale (subject matter and price) trigger mutual oaths; ancillary terms do not.

In sale disputes, when should parties take oaths? The Hanafi school distinguishes between core elements and ancillary conditions. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this distinction, using the hadith and logical analysis to show why term, option, and receipt do not require mutual oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute categories
Distinguish between disputes about the subject matter (mabi') or price (thaman) and disputes about ancillary matters like term (mi'ad), option (khiyar al-shart), and receipt of price.
Ancillary matters do not affect the core sale contract.
2
Grasp the hadith basis
Recall the hadith: 'When the two transacting parties differ and the commodity is still in existence, they take mutual oaths and annul the transaction.' Understand that the Hanafi school interprets this as limited to disputes about the sale itself.
The hadith is general but Hanafi usul restricts its application.
3
Understand the Hanafi interpretation
The Hanafi position argues that tahaluf is attached to the sale, which is constituted by its essence (subject matter and price). Ancillary matters are not part of the essence, so disputes about them do not warrant oaths.
This is a qiyas-based reasoning from the hadith.
4
Distinguish price qualities from ancillary matters
Note that disputes about the quality or genus of the price (e.g., good vs. bad, dirhams vs. dinars) are treated as disputes about the price itself, because quality identifies the price. Term is not a quality, so it is different.
The passage clarifies this distinction with examples.
5
Apply the rule to determine whose statement is accepted
When tahaluf is not required, the denier of the ancillary matter (e.g., the one who denies the term or option) has his statement accepted with an oath, as per Al-Quduri.
This is based on the principle that the denier of a contingent condition prevails.

The hadith indicates that the obligation of tahaluf is attached to the dispute between the two transacting parties, and it is well known that the sale is established by the sold item and the price, not by the term, condition of option, or receipt of price. So the hadith means: when the contractors dispute about the sale and the price, then both take mutual oaths. Thus, it is understood from the hadith that tahaluf is obligatory due to dispute about the sold item or the price. The term, condition of option, and receipt of price are neither part of the sale nor part of the price, so they are not to be attached to the obligation of tahaluf like the sold item or price. Therefore, dispute about these matters is like dispute about discounting or forgiving the price, and from dispute about discounting or forgiving the price, by consensus tahaluf is not obligatory, so from dispute about these matters tahaluf will also not be obligatory. The author of Hidayah said that dispute about these matters is a dispute about other than the subject matter and price, because the absence of these matters does not cause any deficiency in that which constitutes the sale contract. That is, if the term, condition of option, and receipt of price are not present, the sale contract suffers no deficiency, whereas the absence of the subject matter and price causes deficiency in the sale contract. So this proves that these matters are not of the same rank as the subject matter. On the contrary, if there is a dispute about the quality of the price (e.g., good or bad) or about the genus of the price (dirhams vs. dinars), then this is like a dispute about the quantity of the price, and dispute about the quantity of the price obligates tahaluf. Therefore, dispute about the quality and genus of the price will also obligate tahaluf as if in the price itself. The reason is that dispute about quality and genus is in reality dispute about the original price, because the price is a pecuniary good, its identification is by quality, so when there is a dispute about the quality (identifier), there will also be a dispute about the identified (price). The term is not like that; dispute about the term is not like dispute about the quantity of the price, because the term is not a quality. Observe: after the term passes, the price remains. If the term were its quality, the passage of the term would cause deficiency in the price. Now as for whose statement is accepted when there is no tahaluf in these matters, Al-Quduri said that the contractor who denies the option and the term, his statement with oath will be accepted, because these two things exist due to a contingent condition. That is, when the term or option is stipulated, then these two things exist, otherwise not, and the statement of the denier of contingencies with oath is accepted. Therefore, the statement of the denier of these matters with oath will be accepted.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa addresses when mutual oaths (tahaluf) are required in a dispute between buyer and seller. The author first states the Hanafi position: if the dispute is about matters other than the subject matter (mabi') and price (thaman), such as the term (mi'ad), condition of option (khiyar al-shart), or receipt of price, then tahaluf is not required. This is in contrast to Malik and Shafi'i who require it. The Hanafi proof relies on the hadith: 'When the two transacting parties differ and the commodity is still in existence, they take mutual oaths and annul the transaction.' The hadith links tahaluf to the sale contract itself, which is constituted by the subject matter and price. Ancillary matters do not affect the essence of the contract, so disputes about them do not warrant mutual oaths. The author distinguishes between disputes about the qualities of the price (e.g., good or bad, dirhams or dinars), which affect the identity of the price and thus require tahaluf, and disputes about term or condition, which do not affect the price's identity. Finally, the author cites Al-Quduri: when tahaluf is not applicable, the denier of the ancillary term has his statement accepted with oath, since these terms are contingent and their existence depends on stipulation.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Mutual Oaths After Destruction of Sold Item
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tahaluf after destruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi positions on tahaluf after destruction and identify the practical difference between the views of Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
After destruction, the Hanafi majority holds that tahaluf is inapplicable; the buyer's statement with oath suffices.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding mutual oaths (tahaluf) when the sold item is destroyed after delivery. The issue arises when the parties dispute the price after the commodity is no longer extant. We will explore the views of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf versus Muhammad al-Shaybani, the proofs for each, and the exception when the price is a specific object.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The sold item is destroyed after the buyer takes possession. The seller and buyer then dispute the price. The question is whether mutual oaths (tahaluf) are administered.
This scenario differs from when the item is still extant.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: no mutual oaths; the buyer's statement with oath is accepted. Muhammad (and al-Shafi'i): mutual oaths are taken, leading to rescission and payment of the value.
Muhammad's view aligns with the Shafi'i school.
3
Examine Muhammad's proof
Muhammad argues that each party claims a different contract, and tahaluf prevents the buyer from overpaying, analogous to a dispute about the genus of the price after destruction.
The benefit (repelling excess price) justifies tahaluf.
4
Examine Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf's response
They argue that tahaluf after possession is an exception to qiyas, legislated only when the commodity is extant. After destruction, the contract is effectively terminated, and the benefit of repelling a price increase is not a contractual requirement.
The exception does not extend beyond its original scope.
5
Note the exception
If the price is a specific object (ayn) rather than a debt, both sides agree on tahaluf because the sold item is still extant on one side, so the benefit of rescission is achievable.
The item's existence on one side triggers the original rationale.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf on one side and Muhammad al-Shaybani on the other, regarding the application of mutual oaths (tahaluf) when the sold item is destroyed after delivery. Muhammad's view aligns with Imam al-Shafi'i. The core issue: after destruction, the original contract cannot be reversed by returning the item, so Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf argue that the rationale for tahaluf no longer applies. Tahaluf was legislated as an exception to analogy when the item is still extant, because it leads to rescission. After destruction, rescission is impossible, and the buyer has already received the item. The dispute is about the price, and the buyer's statement is accepted with oath. Muhammad argues that the dispute is about a different contract, and tahaluf serves to prevent the buyer from overpaying. However, this is limited to when the price is a debt; if the price is a specific object (ayn), both agree on tahaluf because the item is still present on one side. The passage also extends the same ruling to when the buyer has resold the item or is unable to return it due to a defect.

Ruling with proof: seller's evidence preferred in price dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the preference of the seller's bayyinah in a price dispute and explain the legal rationale distinguishing between oaths and evidence.

Core Idea
Seller's evidence preferred because the apparent makes him claimant; witnesses only see the apparent.

When a buyer and seller dispute the price of a deceased slave after a sale, the Hanafi school applies a key distinction between oaths and evidence. This lesson explores why the seller's bayyinah (witness testimony) is preponderant over the buyer's denial, and examines an intra-Hanafi disagreement about the exception. By understanding this ruling, you will grasp how Hanafi fiqh weighs apparent evidence over inner reality in court proceedings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Two slaves are sold together; one dies before payment. Buyer and seller dispute the price share of the deceased. Identify the act as a qada (court) issue involving evidence and oaths.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The seller's bayyinah (evidence) is preferred over the buyer's denial or oath. This is the mufta bihi position.
The seller's evidence proves a higher price, making him the claimant in the apparent.
3
Trace the legal reasoning (dalil)
The passage distinguishes between oaths (reality considered) and evidence (apparent considered). Witnesses do not know the inner truth, so the apparent is decisive. The seller, claiming a higher price, is the claimant in the apparent.
This is based on istihsan: the principle that evidence is evaluated on its apparent strength.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the exception
Some later mashayikh interpret the exception (istithna) as referring to tahalif (mutual oath); others as referring to the buyer's oath only. The core ruling on evidence preference remains unchanged.
This ikhtilaf affects the procedure when the seller does not use his evidence but accepts the buyer's admission.
5
Determine the operative ruling
The seller's bayyinah is preponderant. If the seller chooses to accept the buyer's admission, no oath is needed. The mufta bihi is that the seller's evidence is preferred.
This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school regarding a dispute between a buyer and seller over the price of a deceased slave sold together with a living slave. The author first states the ruling: the seller's evidence (bayyinah) that proves a higher price for the deceased slave is preferred. The reasoning (fiqh) is that in oaths (qasam), the actual reality (haqiqah hal) is considered because the oath is directed to one of the contracting parties who knows the reality; the seller is the denier of the reality (i.e., he denies the buyer's claim that the price was lower), so his statement is accepted. But in evidence (bayyinat), the apparent (zahir) is considered because witnesses do not know the reality; in the apparent, the seller is the claimant (he claims a higher price), so his evidence is accepted and preponderant. Then the passage discusses an intra-madhhab difference among later mashayikh regarding the interpretation of an exception (istithna) in the text. Some say the exception refers to mutual oath (tahalif), while others say it refers to the buyer's oath and that Abu Hanifa's view is that only the buyer's statement with an oath is accepted, unless the seller agrees to take only the buyer's admitted amount, in which case no oath is needed. This clarifies that the buyer becomes the denier only if he does not admit; if the seller accepts the buyer's admission, the buyer is no longer a denier and no oath is taken.

Key Terms
بینہ (bayyinah): In this passage, bayyinah refers to evidence or proof, specifically witness testimony that establishes a claim in court.
حقیقت حال (haqiqah hal): The actual reality or inner truth of a situation, known to the parties involved but not to witnesses.
ظاہر (zahir): The apparent or external aspect of a matter that is observable and can be testified to by witnesses.

Tahalif in a Two-Slave Sale: Intra-Hanafi Dispute and Procedure
Explains the procedure of mutual oath when both parties swear

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two Hanafi positions on the sequence of tahalif and apply the mufta bihi view to determine the outcome of a two-slave sale dispute.

Core Idea
In an intra-Hanafi dispute on tahalif, the mufta bihi view (Abu Yusuf) requires the buyer to swear first; if both swear, the sale is rescinded only for the living slave.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a critical procedural question: when a buyer and seller disagree on the price after one of two slaves dies, what is the correct order of mutual oaths? The author presents two views within the Hanafi school—Imam Muhammad’s and Abu Yusuf’s—and then explains why Abu Yusuf’s view is considered more correct, along with the fiqh principle that oaths are based on reality while evidence is based on appearances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The core issue is the sequence of oaths (tahalif) when the buyer and seller disagree on the price after one of two sold slaves dies. Both agree on the basic ruling that tahalif leads to rescission, but they differ on who swears first and the exact outcome.
The underlying principle is whether the buyer is the defendant (Abu Yusuf) or both are equal (Imam Muhammad).
2
Trace Imam Muhammad's position
Imam Muhammad holds that both parties swear simultaneously: the seller swears he did not sell for the buyer's claimed lower price, and the buyer swears he did not buy for the seller's claimed higher price. If both swear, the entire sale is rescinded, the living slave is returned, and the buyer pays the price of the deceased slave.
This view gives no priority to either party in swearing.
3
Trace Abu Yusuf's position (mufta bihi)
Abu Yusuf holds that the buyer swears first: 'I did not buy for the seller's claimed price.' If the buyer refuses, the seller's claim is enforced. If the buyer swears, then the seller swears: 'I did not sell for the buyer's claimed price.' If the seller refuses, the buyer's claim is enforced. If both swear, the sale is rescinded only for the living slave, and the buyer pays the price of the deceased slave.
This view is based on the buyer being the apparent defendant because the seller's claim is higher.
4
Analyze the tarjih and underlying principle
The author prefers Abu Yusuf's view, citing qiyas from a similar case in al-Mabsut. The fiqh principle is: in oaths, reality (haqiqah) is considered because the parties know the truth; in evidence (bayyinat), only the apparent (zahir) is considered because witnesses do not know reality. Thus, the seller, as the denier of reality (he denies the drop in price), his oath is accepted, and as the claimant of the apparent (higher value), his evidence is preferred.
This principle justifies why the buyer swears first: the buyer's oath is about reality (his own knowledge), and the seller's evidence is about appearance.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute explaining the procedure of mutual oath (tahalif) in a case where two slaves are sold together, one dies, and the buyer and seller disagree on the price. The author first presents the view of Imam Muhammad: both parties swear, the seller swearing he did not sell for the lower price claimed by the buyer, and the buyer swearing he did not buy for the higher price claimed by the seller. If both swear, the contract is rescinded, the living slave is returned, and the buyer pays the price of the deceased slave based on the value at the time of possession. Then the author presents the view of Abu Yusuf, which is considered more correct (asahh): the buyer swears first that he did not buy for the seller's claimed price; if he refuses, he must pay that price; if he swears, then the seller swears that he did not sell for the buyer's claimed price; if the seller refuses, he must accept the buyer's claimed price; if both swear, the sale is rescinded for the living slave, and the buyer pays the price of the deceased slave. The value is assessed on the day of possession. If there is a dispute over the value of the deceased slave, the seller's statement with an oath is accepted because he is the denier of the buyer's claim of a lower value. If one party has evidence, that evidence is accepted. If both have evidence, the seller's evidence is preferred because it proves a higher value. The author then relates this to a similar case in al-Mabsut (a foundational Hanafi text) to show consistency. Finally, he explains the underlying fiqh principle: in oaths, the reality is considered because the parties know the truth; in evidence (bayyinat), the apparent is considered because witnesses do not know the inner reality. Thus, the seller's oath and evidence are preferred because he is the denier of reality and the claimant of the apparent.

Key Terms
تحالف (tahalif): Mutual oath-taking by both parties in a dispute where each swears to the truth of their claim, leading to rescission of the contract if both swear.
قسموں میں حقیقت حال (fi al-qasam haqiqah hal): In oaths, the actual inner reality of the situation is considered because the person swearing knows the truth.
بینات میں ظاہر (fi al-bayyinat al-zahir): In evidence (witness testimony), only the apparent/external aspect is considered because witnesses do not know the inner reality.
اصح قول (asahh qawl): The more correct opinion, used to indicate the preferred view when there is ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

Dispute Between Master and Mukatab Over Kitabah Amount
Intra-Hanafi and cross-madhhab dispute on mutual oaths in kitabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the reasoning behind the Hanafi disagreement on mutual oaths in kitabah disputes and identify the practical consequence of each position.

Core Idea
In a kitabah dispute, Abu Hanifa sees only a claim of excess; the Sahibayn see a mutual claim — and the ruling differs.

When the master and the mukatab disagree on the amount of the kitabah payment, the Hanafi school itself splits. Abu Hanifa holds that no mutual oaths are taken — the mukatab's word with oath stands. The Sahibayn, joined by Imam al-Shafi'i, insist that mutual oaths are necessary and the contract is rescinded. Why the difference? It all turns on the precise nature of what the kitabah payment buys at each stage.

Guided Path
1
State the two Hanafi positions
Identify what Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn each rule regarding mutual oaths in a kitabah dispute. Note that the Sahibayn's view aligns with Imam al-Shafi'i.
2
Follow the Sahibayn's analogy
Understand why the Sahibayn analogize kitabah to a sale: both are commutative contracts that accept rescission. Identify the common cause (jami') they use: both parties make mutual claims.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's distinction
Examine Abu Hanifa's argument that the kitabah payment is not purely for emancipation; it primarily removes restrictions on the slave's disposal. Since that benefit is already realized, the dispute is one-sided.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical outcome: under Abu Hanifa the contract continues, under the Sahibayn it is rescinded after mutual oaths.
5
Apply rules of evidence
Note when evidence (bayyinah) is present: if one party brings evidence, it prevails; if both, the master's evidence is preferred.

Commentary

The three principal Hanafi jurists—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani—offer distinct solutions. They illustrate with examples of competing claims before a judge between a free person and a mukatab over jointly possessed property, where they split equally. When a Defendant Claims Property from an Absent Person
Expansion of procedural dispute on defendant's evidence of absentee ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on whether a defendant's evidence that property belongs to an absent person stops the plaintiff's litigation, and evaluate the objections of Ibn Shubruma and Ibn Abi Layla with their thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A defendant can escape litigation by proving the property belongs to an absent person, but only if the evidence is credible and the absent person is identifiable.

This passage from the chapter on those who cannot be litigants deals with a nuanced procedural issue: when a defendant in a property dispute claims that the property actually belongs to an absent third party, and provides witness evidence to that effect. The question is whether this evidence automatically dismisses the plaintiff's case or not. The Hanafi school has multiple layers of rulings, refined by Abu Yusuf and split between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, while early non-Hanafi scholars raised objections. Understanding this case illuminates the Hanafi methodology in balancing apparent possession with evidentiary proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and its reasoning
Read the passage from 'واذا قال المدعى عليه' to 'بيد خصومة'. The base ruling states that if the defendant produces evidence that the property is a deposit, pledge, or usurped from an absent person, the litigation stops. The reasoning is that the evidence proves his hand is not a hand of litigation ('yad khisumah') but a custodial hand.
This is the default Hanafi position from the Hidayah.
2
Understand the objections of Ibn Shubruma and Ibn Abi Layla
Ibn Shubruma argues the claim should not be dismissed because proving ownership for an absent person is impossible without a litigant to represent him. Ibn Abi Layla argues the defendant is already a litigant by his apparent possession, so his confession to hold for another is like transferring a debt; it is not accepted without independent proof.
These objections challenge the base Hanafi view.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's later refinement
Abu Yusuf distinguished based on the defendant's character: if the defendant is trustworthy, the base ruling stands; if he is known for tricks (muhtal), the litigation is not stopped, because he might collude to defraud the plaintiff.
This shows the Hanafi concern for justice and preventing abuse of court procedures.
4
Examine the identification dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad
When witnesses recognize the absent person by face but not by name, Muhammad holds the claim is not dismissed (like the second opinion), while Abu Hanifa rules it is dismissed because face recognition sufficiently proves the source. The wrording 'عرفه الشهود بوجهه' indicates that the item came from another, so the defendant's hand is not adversarial.
This highlights the different thresholds for certainty in evidence.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter 'On Those Who Cannot Be Litigants' addresses a procedural scenario in litigation. The plaintiff brings a claim; the defendant responds that the disputed property is actually held for an absent person (e.g., as a deposit, pledge, or usufruct), and he produces witnesses to that effect. The question is whether the defendant's evidence completely stops the plaintiff's case. The text records multiple scholarly opinions. First, the default ruling in the Hidayah: if the defendant proves his claim with evidence, the litigation is dismissed because he has established that his possession is not adversarial ('yad khisumah') but custodial. Ibn Shubruma (a non-Hanafi scholar) argues that the evidence should not dismiss the claim because it only proves the absent person's ownership, but since the absent person is not a litigant, the plaintiff's right to litigate remains. Ibn Abi Layla (another early scholar) also rejects dismissal without evidence, reasoning that the defendant, by his apparent possession, has become a litigant; his confession (i.e., stating it belongs to another) is an attempt to transfer a right from himself, so he must be believed only with proof. Abu Yusuf later refined his position: if the defendant is known to be righteous and trustworthy, the evidence dismisses the claim; but if he is known to be a trickster (muhtal), the judge should not accept it, because the defendant might have colluded with an unknown person to defraud the plaintiff. The text then discusses nuances in witness testimony: if witnesses merely say 'a man we do not know' deposited it, the claim is not dismissed because the absent person might be the plaintiff himself or because the plaintiff cannot trace the absent person. If witnesses recognize the absent person by face but not by name, then Muhammad holds that the claim still stands (like the second opinion), while Abu Hanifa rules that it is dismissed because the witnesses have sufficiently identified the source, so the defendant's hand is no longer adversarial. The passage concludes that this is a well-known issue with five divergent opinions.

Defendant's Evidence of Possession for an Absent Party Removes Litigation
Inter-madhhab dispute on effect of defendant's evidence for absent party

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a defendant's evidence of possession for an absent person removes him from litigation, and evaluate the counterarguments of Ibn Shubrumah and Ibn Abi Layla.

Core Idea
Evidence proving your possession is for an absent person proves non-hostile intent, freeing you from the lawsuit.

In this lesson, we examine a key procedural ruling from Kitab al-Da'wa: when a defendant claims that the disputed property belongs to an absent third party and brings evidence (bayyinah) to that effect, does he cease to be a litigant? The Hanafi school says yes, because the evidence shows his possession is not adversarial. However, early Kufan jurists Ibn Shubrumah and Ibn Abi Layla disagreed. This passage presents the Hanafi position, the counterarguments, and the Hanafi response, along with internal Hanafi nuances regarding the identity of the absent person and the defendant's character.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Summarize the ruling: if the defendant brings evidence that his possession is for an absent person (e.g., trust, pledge, lease), the judge dismisses the plaintiff's claim and the defendant ceases to be a litigant.
2
Identify the opposing views and their proofs
Ibn Shubrumah argues that ownership for the absent person cannot be established without a litigant, so the defendant remains a litigant. Ibn Abi Layla argues that the defendant's mere admission suffices without evidence.
Ibn Shubrumah's proof is aqli: no one can claim ownership for an absent party without authorization.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes two effects of the evidence: (1) establishing ownership for the absent person – impossible due to lack of litigant; (2) removing litigation from the defendant – possible because the defendant himself is the litigant defending his own capacity. The evidence succeeds in the second effect.
Analogy: a woman's agent for transfer is removed by her evidence of divorce, even if the divorce itself is not established.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Three opinions: Abu Hanifa accepts evidence if witnesses know the absent person by face (even without name). Muhammad requires full identity. Abu Yusuf distinguishes between a righteous possessor (accepted) and a known trickster (rejected).
These differences affect the outcome in specific cases.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Hanafi view, the plaintiff's case is dismissed upon evidence. Under Ibn Shubrumah, the case proceeds. Under Ibn Abi Layla, the case is dismissed on mere admission.
A judge must know which view to apply; the Hanafi view is the mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa discusses the legal effect of a defendant's evidence that his possession of a disputed property is for the benefit of an absent third party (e.g., as a trust, pledge, lease, etc.). The Hanafi position, as explained in the text, is that such evidence removes the defendant from being a litigant because it proves his possession is not hostile. The passage contrasts this with the views of Ibn Shubrumah, who argues that ownership of the absent person cannot be established without a litigant, and Ibn Abi Layla, who holds that the defendant's mere admission suffices. The Hanafi response is that the evidence serves two functions: it fails to establish ownership for the absent party (due to lack of litigant) but successfully removes litigation from the defendant since he is the one defending his own capacity. An analogy is given with an agent for transferring a woman who is removed by her evidence of divorce. The passage also addresses internal Hanafi differences: Imam Abu Hanifah holds that if witnesses know the absent person by face (even without name), the defendant is removed; Imam Muhammad disagrees. Abu Yusuf's final opinion distinguishes between a righteous possessor (whose evidence is accepted) and a known trickster (whose evidence is rejected). The issue is considered a major discussion in the chapter, with five opinions summarized.

The Muhimmah Case: Possessor as Litigant in Ownership vs. Tort Claims
Exposition of five opinions on possessor avoiding litigation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between claims of absolute ownership (da'wa mulk mutlaq) and claims of a specific tort (da'wa fi'l) in determining who is a proper litigant, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on passive theft claims.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a possessor is a litigant for ownership claims but not for tort claims unless the tort is specifically attributed to him.

In judicial procedure (qada), determining who is a proper litigant is crucial. The celebrated Muhimmah case from Kitab al-Da'wa illustrates how the nature of the plaintiff's claim decides whether a defendant who proves deposit from a third party can escape litigation. The passage presents five opinions, but the core principle revolves around the distinction between a claim of absolute ownership and a claim that the defendant committed a tort (usurpation or theft). We will dissect this principle and the intra-Hanafi dispute on passive theft claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of claims
Classify the plaintiff's claim as either 'da'wa mulk mutlaq' (absolute ownership) or 'da'wa fi'l' (a specific act like usurpation or theft). The ruling depends on this classification.
In ownership claims, the possessor is a litigant because his possession makes him the opponent. In tort claims, the litigant is the alleged perpetrator, not necessarily the possessor.
2
Apply the general rule
If the plaintiff sues the possessor for ownership, the possessor is always a litigant. If the plaintiff says 'you usurped' or 'you stole', the possessor is a litigant even if he proves deposit, because the claim is about his act, not his possession.
This is the base ruling from al-Hidayah, attributed to Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.
3
Examine the passive voice sub-case
When the plaintiff says 'it was stolen from me' (passive) without naming the thief, an intra-Hanafi dispute arises. Does the possessor remain a litigant if he proves deposit?
Here the plaintiff did not directly accuse the possessor, but the phrase implies a thief.
4
Compare the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that litigation is not repelled (istihsan), because mentioning theft requires a thief, and the possessor is the apparent one. Imam Muhammad rules that litigation is repelled (qiyas with usurpation without naming), because no act was claimed against the possessor.
The key difference: istihsan prioritizes the implication of the act (theft) over the literal wording.
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under the majority view (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), the defendant remains in court and must defend against the theft claim despite the deposit evidence. Under Muhammad's view, he would be dismissed. The mufta bihi is the majority view.
This affects strategy: a defendant cannot rely on a deposit defense if the plaintiff frames the claim as theft.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah, a commentary on al-Hidayah, focuses on a celebrated problematic case (Muhimmah) in the Book of Claims (Kitab al-Da'wa). The author presents five opinions from early scholars including Hanafi imams. The central issue: When a claimant (plaintiff) demands property in the possession of another (defendant), and the defendant claims the property came from a third party (e.g., deposited by an absent person), does the defendant become immune from litigation? The answer depends on whether the plaintiff's claim is one of absolute ownership (da'wa mulk mutlaq) or a specific tort (da'wa fi'l) like usurpation (ghasb) or theft (sariqa). If the defendant claims he bought the property from an absent person, he acknowledges his possession as ownership possession, so he becomes a proper litigant. If the plaintiff accuses the defendant of committing a tort (e.g., 'you usurped' or 'you stole'), the defendant remains a litigant even if he proves it was a deposit from a third party, because his liability arises from the alleged act, not his possession. A sub-issue arises when the plaintiff says 'it was stolen from me' without accusing the defendant directly (passive voice). Here, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the defendant remains a litigant because the act of theft implies a doer, and the possessor is apparent, though the plaintiff deliberately left him unnamed to avoid hadd. Imam Muhammad disagrees, arguing that since no act is claimed against the defendant, he should not be a litigant, analogous to the case of usurpation without naming a usurper. Finally, if both parties agree that the property originally belongs to a third party (plaintiff claims he bought from him, defendant claims deposit from him), litigation drops without need for evidence because there is no dispute over the current possessor's liability.

Key Terms
خصم (khasm): A litigant; the party against whom a legal claim is properly brought and who can defend in court.
ودیعت (wadi'at): Deposit; property entrusted by one person to another for safekeeping without transfer of ownership.
غصب (ghasb): Usurpation; wrongful taking of another's property without right, with or without force.
سرقہ (sariqah): Theft; taking of another's property secretly from a secure place, subject to hadd punishment.
دعویٰ ملک مطلق (da'wa mulk mutlaq): A claim of absolute ownership without reference to any specific act, made against the current possessor.
دعویٰ فعل (da'wa fi'l): A claim that the defendant personally committed a specific act (e.g., usurpation, theft) against the plaintiff's property.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference; departing from an apparent analogy in favor of a ruling that achieves a more just or appropriate result.
مھمہ (muhimmah): An important or problematic case in fiqh that is difficult to resolve, here meaning a landmark case in Kitab al-Da'wa.

Ruling on Claim of Purchase vs Deposit Without Evidence
Ruling on a claim scenario with mutual agreement on third-party ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a dispute where both parties agree the item belongs to a third party and identify when the defendant must produce evidence.

Core Idea
When both parties agree a third party owns the item, the dispute drops without evidence—unless the possessor claims agency.

This passage from the Book of Claims (كتاب الدعوى) addresses a specific scenario where the claimant says he bought an item from a third party, and the possessor says the same third party deposited it with him. Both agree the original owner is that third party. The Hanafi ruling is unexpected: the possessor does not need to prove the deposit, because the mutual agreement on third-party ownership shifts the basis of possession. This lesson will clarify when evidence becomes necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The claimant (مدعی) claims purchase from a third party; the defendant/possessor (مدعا علیہ/قابض) claims deposit from the same third party.
Both claims reference the same third-party owner.
2
Note the agreed fact
Both parties agree that the original owner is a third person, not the claimant or defendant.
This mutual agreement is the key condition.
3
State the default ruling
The dispute (خصومت) is dropped without the defendant needing to produce evidence of deposit.
No proof is required from either side initially.
4
Identify the exception
If the defendant claims the third party appointed him as an agent (وکیل) to take possession, he must produce evidence (بینہ).
Agency requires proof; a simple deposit does not.
5
Understand the legal rationale
Since both agree the item belongs to a third party, the defendant's possession is legally attributed to that third party as a custodian, not as a contested possessor. The claimant's purchase claim does not create a possessory conflict.
The ruling avoids unnecessary litigation over the nature of possession when ownership is undisputed.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Book of Claims (كتاب الدعوى). It addresses a scenario where a claimant (مدعی) claims he purchased an item from a third party, while the defendant (مدعا علیہ/قابض) claims the same third party deposited the item with him as a trust (ودیعت). Both parties agree that the original owner is a third party. The ruling is that the dispute (خصومت) is invalid (ساقط) without the defendant needing to produce evidence. The reason is that since both agree the item belongs to a third party, the defendant's possession is legally attributed to that third party (as a deposit), not as a contested possessor. However, if the defendant proves via evidence (بینہ) that the third party appointed him as an agent (وکیل) to take possession, then he can retain possession and the dispute becomes valid. This ruling follows the Hanafi position that mutual agreement on third-party ownership eliminates the need for proof of the nature of possession.

ودیعت (wadi'ah): A trust deposit where the depositor leaves an item with another for safekeeping without compensation.
خصومت (khusumat): A legal dispute or litigation between parties over a claim.
مدعی (mudda'i): The claimant who brings a legal claim against another.
مدعا علیہ (mudda'a 'alayhi): The defendant against whom a claim is brought.
قابض (qabid): The person who physically possesses the item in question.
