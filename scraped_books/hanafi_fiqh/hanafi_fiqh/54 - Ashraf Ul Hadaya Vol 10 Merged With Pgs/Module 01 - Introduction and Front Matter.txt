Ashraf al-Hidayah is a major Urdu commentary on the Hanafi fiqh text al-Hidayah; this volume covers Kitab al-Shahadah to Bab al-Tahluf.

Table of contents for Kitab al-Shahada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the main sections and key terms covered in Kitab al-Shahada from the table of contents.

Core Idea
The table of contents maps the full legal framework of testimony in Hanafi fiqh.

Before diving into the detailed rulings of testimony, it is helpful to have a bird's-eye view of the chapter. This lesson uses the table of contents of Kitab al-Shahada to orient you to the major topics: the required number of witnesses, the conditions for accepting testimony, who can and cannot testify, and the process of establishing witnesses' integrity. Familiarity with this structure will make your study of the subsequent rulings more efficient.

Guided Path
1
Scan the major headings
Identify the broad categories listed: legal status of testimony, number of witnesses, conditions of justice, process of tazkiya, and eligibility of witnesses.
These headings correspond to the main chapters within the larger book.
2
Identify the three major thematic blocks
Group the topics into (1) general rules and numbers, (2) conditions for witnesses (adala, tazkiya, wording), and (3) specific cases of who is accepted or rejected.
This grouping helps in recalling the structure later.
3
Locate key terms and rulings
From the list, pick out terms like 'shahada', 'tazkiya', 'adala', 'qadhf', 'hudud', and note the rulings mentioned (e.g., 'testimony of blind not accepted').
These terms will recur in detailed lessons.
4
Relate to your existing knowledge

This bridges theory to practice.

Commentary
This passage is a table of contents (fihrist) for the Book of Testimony (Kitab al-Shahada) from Ashraf Ul Hadaya Volume 10. It lists the main topics and sub-topics covered in the chapter, along with page numbers (likely original page numbers). The chapter begins with the legal status of testimony, then discusses the number of witnesses required for different cases: hudud (fixed punishments), qisas (retaliation), marriage, and other rights. It addresses conditions for accepting testimony, including the requirement of justice (adala) in witnesses, the process of tazkiya (investigating witnesses' character), and specific rulings such as when a single woman's testimony is acceptable. The chapter also covers who is eligible to testify and who is not: for example, a blind person, a leper, one punished for false accusation of adultery (qadhf), and close relatives are generally not accepted. The testimony of parents, spouses, and masters regarding slaves is not accepted, while siblings and uncles are accepted. The table also lists rulings for various categories: fornicators, singers, drunkards, gamblers, people of desires (ahl al-hawa), Muslims regarding dhimmis, harbis, uncircumcised, castrated, children of fornication, hermaphrodites, and workers. Finally, it includes two specific case scenarios involving testimony about an absent father's appointments. This front matter serves as a reference for the detailed discussions that follow in the main text.

Key Terms
شهادة (shahada): Legal testimony given in court to establish a claim or defense.
تزکیہ (tazkiya): The process of investigating and confirming the justice ('adala) of witnesses before accepting their testimony.
عدالت (adalat): Justice; the quality of being upright and avoiding major sins and persisting on minor sins.

Introduction to Wakalah: Definition, Cause, and Evidence
Introduction to the Book of Agency: definitions, cause, and proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of wakalah in Hanafi fiqh, identify its cause, and explain the Quranic evidence for its permissibility.

Core Idea
Wakalah is a non-binding contract of agency; its permissibility is proven by Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas.

This lesson establishes the foundational rules of agency (wakalah). Understanding the precise definition, the reason it was legislated, and the key proof from the Quran is essential before studying specific rulings. The placement of this chapter after testimony reveals the careful structural logic of the Hidayah.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and technical definitions
First, grasp the linguistic meaning of wakalah (entrusting/protection) and then the Hanafi juristic definition: appointing another as deputy in a specific, known disposition. Note that the task must be known to avoid ambiguity.
Technical definition: 'a person appoints another as his deputy in a specific and known disposition.'
2
Identify the cause (sabab) for legislation
Recognize that wakalah shares its cause with sale – the need for mutual transactions to sustain life. Since people cannot personally handle all affairs, delegation is necessary.
Sabab: 'the continuation of life is connected to mutual transactions.'
3
Analyze the condition (shart) and ruling (hukm)
Note the condition: the principal must be legally competent in the matter. The ruling: the agent obtains authority over that matter. The description: wakalah is a permissible (ja'iz) contract – both parties can dismiss without consent.
Shart: principal's competence; Hukm: agent's authority; Sifah: ja'iz (non-binding).
4
Examine the Quranic proof
Study the verse from Surah al-Kahf (18:19) where the Companions of the Cave sent one of them to buy food. The Quran mentions it without disapproval, applying the principle of shar'u man qablana to establish permissibility.
Principle: laws of previous prophets are binding if not abrogated.
5
Note the four sources of evidence
The text states that wakalah is proven by Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. Understand that each source confirms its legitimacy.
Evidence: Quran (Kahf 18:19), Sunnah (hadith), Ijma (consensus), Qiyas (analogy to sale).

The Book of Agency
Translation: This book is about the explanation of agency.
Commentary: After explaining the rulings of testimony, the rulings of agency are mentioned. The appropriateness between these two is that both testimony and agency are attributes of Allah the Exalted. Thus, Allah the Exalted said: 'Then Allah is a witness over what they do' and in another verse: 'Sufficient for us is Allah, and He is the best Disposer of affairs.' In the first verse, 'witness' is an attribute of Allah, and in the second verse, 'Disposer' (Wakil) is an attribute of Allah. The second appropriateness is that through both testimony and agency, another person is helped and his right is revived. The third appropriateness is that both testimony and agency are means of acquiring reward. Due to these correspondences, the Book of Agency is mentioned after the Book of Testimony.
Wakalah is read with both kasrah and fatha on the waw. Its meaning is entrusting, and it is also said that wakalah means protection. From this comes 'wakil': a name of Allah meaning Protector, and wakalah is also the noun of tawkil. Tawkil means expressing trust in someone other than oneself. A wakil is called a person who performs the task that has been entrusted to him. Wakil is used in the sense of maf'ul (passive participle) on the pattern of fasil. In the terminology of the jurists, wakalah is that a person appoints another as his deputy in a specific and known disposition. The cause of wakalah is the same as the cause of the legislation of sale, i.e., 'the continuation of life is connected to mutual transactions.' Since for mutual transactions, dispositions sometimes have to be entrusted to others, wakalah was legislated. And the operative word of wakalah is 'wakkaltu' (I appointed as agent) and its synonyms. The condition of wakalah is that the principal himself is authorized and competent in the matter for which he appoints the agent. The ruling of wakalah is that the agent obtains the disposition over the matter that the principal entrusted to him. The description of wakalah is that it is a permissible (ja'iz) contract. Each of the principal and agent has the right to dismiss the other without their consent, i.e., the principal can dismiss the agent without his consent. The validity of the contract of wakalah is established by four proofs: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. Commentary
This passage is the introductory section of the Book of Agency (Kitab al-Wakalah) from Ashraf al-Hidayah, a commentary on al-Hidayah by Burhan al-Din al-Marghinani. The author begins by explaining the placement of this book after the Book of Testimony (Kitab al-Shahadat). He provides three reasons for this sequence: (1) both testimony and agency (wakalah) are attributes of Allah, citing the verses where Allah is called 'Shahid' (Witness) and 'Wakil' (Disposer of affairs); (2) both involve helping another person and reviving their right; (3) both are means of obtaining reward. This shows the careful structural logic in the arrangement of fiqh chapters.

The passage then provides the linguistic and technical definitions of wakalah. Linguistically, wakalah means entrusting or protection. Technically, according to Hanafi jurists, it is 'a person appointing another as his deputy in a specific and known disposition.' This definition emphasizes that the agency must be for a particular task that is clearly known, which prevents ambiguity (gharar).

The cause (sabab) for the legislation of wakalah is the same as for sale: humans need mutual transactions to sustain life, and since they cannot personally handle all affairs, delegating to others becomes necessary. The condition (shart) is that the principal (muwakkil) must himself be legally competent (mukhtar wa majaz) in the matter for which he appoints an agent. For example, a minor cannot appoint an agent for a sale because he himself is not competent.

The ruling (hukm) is that the agent (wakil) gains the right to perform the specified disposition. The description (sifah) is that wakalah is a permissible (ja'iz) contract, meaning it is not binding; either party can terminate it unilaterally without consent. This distinguishes it from binding contracts like sale.

Finally, the author establishes the legitimacy of wakalah from four sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. The primary evidence cited is the Quranic verse from Surah al-Kahf (18:19) where the Companions of the Cave appointed one of them to buy food. The author applies the principle that when the Quran narrates an action of previous communities without criticism, that action is considered permissible for Muslims (shar'u man qablana). This is a key principle in Hanafi usul.

Key Terms
وکالت (wakālah): A contract whereby a person appoints another as his deputy in a specific, known legal disposition.
وکیل (wakīl): The person who is appointed to act on behalf of another; the agent.
مؤکل (muwakkil): The person who appoints an agent; the principal.
توکیل (tawkīl): The act of appointing an agent; expressing trust in another.

Definition and Proofs of Agency (Wakalah)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four proofs of wakalah and explain why the Quranic story serves as a legislative proof for us.

Core Idea
Wakalah is a revocable contract of delegation, justified by Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas.

This lesson introduces the Book of Agency in Hanafi fiqh. We will first understand why the book follows testimony, then define wakalah linguistically and technically. Finally, we examine the four proofs that establish its permissibility: Quran, Hadith, Ijma, and Qiyas. Understanding these proofs will ground your appreciation of this essential contract in personal and commercial affairs.

The Companions of the Cave after waking up said: 'Send one of you with this silver coin to the city, then let him see which food is purest, so that he may bring you provision from it.' Those persons entrusted one person with the responsibility of taking the silver coin and buying and bringing halal food, and that is wakalah. The Holy Quran mentioned this without disapproval, and the rule is that if the Holy Quran mentions a ruling pertaining to a previous nation without disapproval, then that ruling is also legislated for us. So it is proven that wakalah is legislated for us. And from Hadith, the permissibility of wakalah is established as follows: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) appointed Hakim ibn Hizam and Urwah al-Bariqi as agents to buy a sacrificial animal. (Tirmidhi) And he appointed Amr ibn Umayyah as agent for accepting the marriage of Umm Habibah bint Abi Sufyan (may Allah be pleased with them), and he appointed Rafi' as agent for accepting the marriage of Maymunah (may Allah be pleased with her). From Ijma, it is established that from the time of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) until today, there is consensus and continuous practice of the Ummah on the permissibility of wakalah. From Qiyas and rational evidence, it is established that sometimes a person is unable to protect his wealth due to long travel, sometimes he is unable to buy and sell due to lack of ability, sometimes he is unable due to many occupations, and sometimes he is unable due to weakness and old age. So all these impediments necessitate the permissibility of wakalah. Therefore, due to these reasons and impediments, wakalah was legislated.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof-type lesson. The author, Ashraf al-Hadaya, begins by explaining why the Book of Agency follows the Book of Testimony, citing three connections: both are attributes of Allah, both help another person, and both are means of reward. Then he gives the linguistic and terminological definition of wakalah. Linguistically, it means entrusting or protection. In Hanafi fiqh, wakalah is a contract where a person (principal) appoints another (agent) as his representative in a specific disposition. The cause (sabab) of its legislation is the same as that of sale: human need for mutual dealings. The condition is that the principal must himself be legally capable of performing the task. The ruling (hukm) is that the agent gains authority to act. The property (sifah) of wakalah is that it is a permissible (ja'iz) contract, revocable by either party without the other's consent. The author then provides the four proofs of its permissibility: (1) Quran: the story of the Companions of the Cave sending one to buy food, cited without disapproval, indicating it is permissible for us by the principle that stories of previous nations without condemnation are binding. (2) Hadith: the Prophet (ﷺ) appointed agents for buying a sacrificial animal and for marriage contracts. (3) Ijma: continuous practice of the Ummah. (4) Qiyas: analogical reasoning based on human incapacity in various situations (travel, lack of skill, multiple engagements, old age). All these proofs establish wakalah as a valid institution in Hanafi law. No inter-madhhab dispute is mentioned here, as the proofs are universally accepted, though the Hanafi school relies on them strongly. The lesson concludes that all these reasons necessitate the legislation of wakalah.

Key Terms
وکالت (wakālah): In Hanafi terminology, it is a contract where a person appoints another as his representative in a specific, known disposition.
وکیل (wakīl): The person appointed to perform a task on behalf of the principal.
مؤکل (muwakkil): The principal who appoints the agent.
