Agent's Authority to Take Security and Non-Liability for Loss
Ruling on agent's non-liability for loss of pledge or surety taken for sale price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's liability when a pledge or surety taken for the sale price is lost, and explain the distinction between a wakeel for sale and a wakeel for debt collection.

Core Idea
An agent for sale is a principal in contract rights; his possession of a pledge is a trust, not a guarantee.

This passage from Al-Hidayah addresses a practical scenario: an agent sells an item and takes a pledge or surety to secure payment. If that security is lost, is the agent liable? The answer depends on the nature of the agent's authority. The Hanafi school treats a wakeel for sale as having the status of a principal in the rights of the contract, which expands his authority and limits his liability for loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What exactly did the agent do? He sold the slave and took a pledge (rahn) or surety (kafil) for the price. The ruling is that if the pledge is lost in his possession or the surety becomes insolvent, the agent bears no liability (la daman).
This ruling applies whether the loss is due to destruction, death, or disappearance of the surety.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof (dalil) is the principle stated in the text: 'because the agent is a principal in the rights' (lianna al-wakeel aslun fi al-huquq). This is an istihsan reasoning that gives the agent the status of a principal in matters related to the sale contract.
The text does not cite a Quran or hadith directly; it relies on legal reasoning within the Hanafi school.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does being 'asl' lead to no liability? As a principal in the rights of the contract, taking possession of the price (qabd al-thaman) is the agent's own right, and his possession is a trust (amana). Loss of a trust without negligence does not impose liability. Since the pledge or surety is a substitute for the price, the same rule applies.
The key point: the agent's possession is qabd amana, not qabd daman (possessory guarantee).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The text contrasts this with an agent appointed solely to collect a debt (wakil bi qabd al-dayn). Such an agent acts purely by deputyship (niyaba), so his authority is limited to receiving the debt, not to taking security. If he takes a pledge or surety and it is lost, he may be liable.
This intra-Hanafi distinction is crucial for determining the scope of an agent's authority.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah, transmitted by Imam al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah without recorded ikhtilaf among the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad). It is the mufta bihi position.
The authority weight is zahir_riwayah, so it is the relied-upon ruling in the school.

So when taking the price is the agent's right, then taking surety and pledge that strengthen that right are also among the agent's rights. And the agent is their owner. Commentary
This passage from Al-Hidayah states a ruling: an agent appointed to sell a specific item (here a slave) has the authority to take a pledge or surety from the buyer for the price. If the pledge is lost in his possession or the surety turns bad (e.g., both die insolvent), the agent is not liable to the principal. The reasoning is that in the rights of sale, the agent acts as a principal (asl), not as a mere deputy. The right to receive the price belongs to him; taking a pledge or surety is a reinforcement of that right, so he owns those security measures. Therefore, if the pledged item is lost, he is not liable, just as he would not be liable if the cash price itself were lost from his possession (since it is an amana). This is contrasted with an agent appointed merely to collect a debt (wakil bi qabd dayn). Such an agent acts purely by deputyship (niyaba) and is only authorized to receive the debt, not to take a pledge or surety. If he does so, he may be liable. The text also provides three specific scenarios where the price becomes impossible to collect due to the loss of the pledge or the death/insolvency of the surety and buyer. In all cases, the agent bears no responsibility to the principal because his actions were within his authority and he did not cause the loss through negligence (ta'addi). The mention of the qadi following Imam Malik's school regarding discharge of the principal debtor upon kafala is an interesting jurisprudential note, but the Hanafi ruling remains that the agent is not liable. The principle here is that a wakeel for sale has broader discretionary powers compared to a wakeel for debt collection.

Key Terms
وکیل (wakeel): An agent appointed to perform a specific legal act on behalf of another (muwakkil).
اصیل (asl): Principal; one who acts on his own behalf. In this context, the agent is treated as a principal in the rights of the sale contract.
قبض امانت (qabd amanah): Possession of trust; possession where the holder is not responsible for loss unless due to negligence.
کفیل (kafil): Surety; one who guarantees the debt of another (makful 'anhu) to the creditor (makful lahu).

The Void Agency of a Surety: Conflict of Interest in Wakalah
Ruling that a kafil cannot be wakil with qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why appointing a kafil as wakil to collect the debt is invalid, and apply the qiyas principle to similar conflict-of-interest scenarios.

Core Idea
A kafil cannot be the creditor's agent because collecting the debt discharges his own liability—he works for himself, not the principal.

Agency (wakalah) is a contract built on trust: the agent acts for the principal, not himself. When a surety (kafil) is appointed to collect the very debt he guarantees, a conflict of interest arises. This lesson examines why the Hanafi school rules such an appointment invalid, using clear reasoning and a powerful analogy to the freed indebted slave case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The act is the creditor appointing the kafil as his agent to collect the guaranteed debt from the debtor. The ruling is that this agency is invalid (batil).
The ruling is clear and unanimous among Hanafi authorities.
2
Understand the Essential Pillar (Rukn) of Wakalah
The rukn of agency is that the agent works on behalf of another. If the agent would be acting primarily for his own benefit, the agency is void.
This is the core legal principle at play.
3
Analyse the Conflict of Interest
The kafil, by collecting the debt, discharges his own suretyship liability. Thus, he is acting for himself, not for the creditor. The rukn of agency is absent.
Additionally, the kafil's word would not be trusted as an amin (trustee) since he is benefiting himself.
4
Examine the Qiyas (Analogy) with the Freed Slave Case
The text compares this to a master who frees his indebted, permitted slave and becomes liable for the slave's value. If a creditor appoints that master as agent to collect from the slave, it is invalid for the same reason: the master's own interest conflicts.
This qiyas jaliy confirms the ruling through a parallel scenario.
5
Confirm the Ruling as Batil and its Consequences
Because the agency is void ab initio, any collection by the kafil does not discharge the debtor's obligation. The creditor can still claim directly from the debtor or the kafil.
The practical consequence: the kafil's collection is not a valid receipt for the creditor.

Commentary
This passage states a ruling: if a person (kafil) guarantees a debt on behalf of the debtor, and then the creditor appoints the kafil as his agent to collect the debt from the debtor, this agency is invalid. The reasoning is twofold. First, the essential pillar (rukn) of agency—working for another—is missing. The kafil, by collecting the debt, would be discharging his own liability (since he is a surety), so he is acting for himself, not for the creditor. Second, a wakil is an amin (trustee) whose word is accepted; but here, the kafil would be discharging himself, so his word would not be trusted. Therefore, the agency is void. The text provides an analogy: a master who freed his slave who was indebted with permission to trade. The master becomes liable for the slave's value to the creditors, and the slave is still pursued for the debt. If a creditor appoints the master as agent to collect from the slave, it is invalid for the same reason—the master's own interest conflicts.

Guarantor Cannot Be Agent for Debt Collection
Ruling with two rational proofs and a qiyas analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a guarantor's agency to collect the guaranteed debt is void, and explain the two rational proofs (dalil aqli) that invalidate it.

Core Idea
A guarantor acts for himself when collecting the debt, so he cannot be an agent for the creditor.

This lesson examines a key conflict of interest in agency contracts. When a person both guarantees a debt and is appointed to collect it, he serves his own interest, not the principal's. The Hanafi school provides two rational arguments proving the agency is void, and an analogy from slave emancipation. Understanding this ruling clarifies the foundational requirement that an agent must act solely for the principal's benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the specific scenario? A guarantor (kafil) is appointed by the creditor as agent (wakil) to collect the very debt he guaranteed. The ruling: this agency is absolutely void (batil) — the guarantor is never an agent.
2
Locate the first rational proof
The first proof is based on the definition of agency. The agent must work for another (al-‘amal li-ghayrihi). The guarantor, if agency were valid, would be acting for himself by discharging his own liability. Therefore, the essential pillar (rukn) of agency is missing, rendering the contract void.
This is a dalil aqli focusing on the nature of agency.
3
Locate the second rational proof
The second proof concerns the agent's trustworthiness. A necessary consequence of agency is that the agent's statement is accepted (e.g., claiming collection). The guarantor, being self-interested, would be exonerating himself, so his statement would not be accepted. Negation of this necessary condition negates the agency itself.
This shows that the agency fails on procedural trust grounds.
4
Follow the qiyas analogy
The author draws an analogy to a freed slave who was indebted. The master, who becomes liable for the slave's value, cannot be appointed agent to collect debts from the slave because he too acts for his own benefit. The same logic applies to the guarantor.
The 'illa (effective cause) is acting for one's own interest.
5
Understand the consequence
The agency is void ab initio (from the start). Even if the guarantor is later discharged from the guarantee, the agency does not become valid — a void contract cannot become valid later.
This is a general Hanafi principle: a void contract cannot transform into a valid one.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the Hanafi school concerning a conflict of interest in agency. The central issue is a person who has guaranteed a debt (kafil) and is then appointed by the creditor as an agent (wakil) to collect that same debt from the original debtor. The ruling is that this agency is absolutely invalid, both before and after the guarantor's discharge from the guarantee. The text provides two rational proofs. First, the essence of agency is working for another (al-‘amal li-ghayrihi), but here the guarantor would be acting for himself by discharging his own liability. Second, a necessary concomitant of agency is that the agent's statement is accepted as trustworthy; but the guarantor in this scenario is self-interested and his claim of collection would not be trusted. Both proofs destroy the foundational pillars of agency, rendering the contract void ab initio. The passage also includes a qiyas (analogy) with a freed slave who was indebted: the master, who becomes liable for the slave's value, cannot be appointed agent to collect debts from the slave for the same reason—he acts for himself. The commentary emphasizes that the invalidity remains even after discharge because a void contract cannot become valid later. This lesson teaches a key Hanafi principle: the agent must act solely for the principal's benefit, not his own. Students should note that the ruling is from the zahir al-riwayah and is relied upon for fatwa.

وكيل (wakil): A person appointed to act on behalf of another (agent).
براءة (bara'ah): Discharge from liability, specifically the guarantor's release from the guarantee.

Exception: Debtor Makes Agent Guarantor – Validity of Kafalah Attached to a Cause
Exception to the rule when debtor makes agent a guarantor upon payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that a guarantee (kafalah) attached to a future cause is valid, and apply the analogy to the case where the debtor makes the agent a guarantor.

Core Idea
A guarantee attached to a cause is valid by analogy to suretyship for future debts.

In this lesson, we examine an exception to the general rule that an agent is not liable for lost funds. If the debtor makes the agent a guarantor (damin) at the time of payment, the agent becomes liable even if the money is lost. The Hidayah clarifies this ruling by analogizing it to a valid kafalah for a future obligation. We will unpack the legal reasoning and the conditions that make such a guarantee effective.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that if the debtor makes the agent a guarantor at the time of payment, the debtor can reclaim from the agent even if the money is lost. This is an exception to the earlier rule that the debtor cannot reclaim.
This ruling applies only when the agent has explicitly or implicitly become a guarantor.
2
Locate the Proof
The proof is qiyas (analogy) to a valid suretyship for what will become due (kafalah bi ma dhaba lahu). The author says: 'This is a suretyship attached to the condition of possession, so it is valid, similar to the suretyship for what will become due to him from so-and-so.'
The asl is the validity of guaranteeing a future debt; the far' is the agent's guarantee.
3
Analyze the Illa
The common element (illa) is that both are guarantees for a future obligation that is contingent upon a cause (the creditor's possession). The cause here is the creditor taking the debt again.
Hanafi school accepts this type of kafalah because it is attached to a known cause.
4
Understand the Context
This is an exception to the rule that payment to an unauthorized agent is at the debtor's risk. The guarantee shifts the risk back to the agent.
The guarantee must be made at the time of payment.
5
Apply
Recognize that such a kafalah is valid in Hanafi law despite some schools requiring an immediate obligation. The ruling is based on custom and rational reasoning.
The mufta bihi is the validity of this guarantee.

Commentary
This passage presents exceptions and clarifications to the earlier ruling. It is divided into two main sub-issues. First, it discusses the exception where the debtor makes the agent a guarantor (damin) at the time of payment. In this case, even if the money is lost in the agent's possession, the debtor can reclaim it from the agent. The legal reasoning is based on the concept of kafalah (suretyship) attached to a cause. Here, the agent guarantees that if the creditor demands payment again, the agent will return the paid amount. This is valid because it is a guarantee for a future obligation, analogous to the valid suretyship for what will become due from someone. Thus, the debtor may recover from the agent. Second, the passage discusses the scenario where the debtor neither confirms nor denies the agency but pays on hope of ratification. If the creditor later demands the debt, the debtor's hope is cut off, and he can reclaim the payment from the agent. The same applies if the debtor disbelieved the agent but paid anyway. In all these cases, however, the debtor cannot unilaterally reclaim the paid money until the absent creditor appears, because the payment became a potential right of the creditor, similar to payment to a fuduli (unauthorized person) pending ratification. The debtor cannot undo his action until he despairs of the creditor's ratification. All these rulings are grounded in Hanafi legal reasoning, particularly using qiyas (analogy) to valid suretyship and the principle that one cannot revoke a transaction until its purpose is defeated.

کفالت (kafalah): Suretyship: a contract where one person guarantees the obligation of another.

Payment Without Confirmation – Debtor's Right to Reclaim and Its Limits
Exception to the rule when debtor makes agent a guarantor upon payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a debtor can reclaim payment from an alleged agent and identify when recovery is barred until the creditor appears.

Core Idea
A debtor who pays on hope of ratification can reclaim if hope fails, but cannot reclaim until the creditor appears because the payment becomes a potential right.

This lesson covers scenarios where the debtor pays a person claiming to be an agent without confirming the agency. The debtor may later reclaim the money if the creditor demands payment, but the debtor cannot unilaterally reclaim before the creditor's whereabouts are known. The Hidayah equates this to payment to a fuduli (unauthorized agent) and explains the limitation based on the possibility of ratification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
A person claims to be the creditor's agent. The debtor does not confirm the agency (either by silence or disbelief) but pays the debt anyway on the hope that the creditor will ratify.
The debtor's payment is not based on confirmation of agency, but on hope (رجاء) of ratification.
2
State the Ruling on Reclamation
If the creditor later demands the debt from the debtor, the debtor's hope is cut off, and the debtor can reclaim the payment from the agent.
This right to reclaim exists because the debtor only paid on a contingent hope, not on binding agency.
3
Understand the Limitation
The debtor cannot reclaim the paid money until the absent creditor appears. The payment became a potential right of the creditor, similar to payment made to a fuduli (unauthorized person) with hope of ratification.
The possibility of ratification prevents the debtor from undoing the payment unilaterally.
4
Follow the Analogy to Fuduli
The author analogizes to a debtor paying a fuduli (unauthorized collector) hoping for ratification. In that case, the debtor cannot reclaim until ratification is impossible, because the money may become the creditor's right if ratified.
This analogy shows consistency in Hanafi law regarding conditional payments.
5
Identify the Principle
The underlying principle: 'A person who undertakes an action for a purpose is not allowed to nullify it until he despairs of his purpose.' The debtor's purpose was to satisfy the debt via ratification; until that hope is definitively lost, he cannot undo the payment.
This principle governs many cases of contingent transactions.

Commentary
This passage presents exceptions and clarifications to the earlier ruling. It is divided into two main sub-issues. First, it discusses the exception where the debtor makes the agent a guarantor (damin) at the time of payment. In this case, even if the money is lost in the agent's possession, the debtor can reclaim it from the agent. The legal reasoning is based on the concept of kafalah (suretyship) attached to a cause. Here, the agent guarantees that if the creditor demands payment again, the agent will return the paid amount. This is valid because it is a guarantee for a future obligation, analogous to the valid suretyship for what will become due from someone. Thus, the debtor may recover from the agent. Second, the passage discusses the scenario where the debtor neither confirms nor denies the agency but pays on hope of ratification. If the creditor later demands the debt, the debtor's hope is cut off, and he can reclaim the payment from the agent. The same applies if the debtor disbelieved the agent but paid anyway. In all these cases, however, the debtor cannot unilaterally reclaim the paid money until the absent creditor appears, because the payment became a potential right of the creditor, similar to payment to a fuduli (unauthorized person) pending ratification. The debtor cannot undo his action until he despairs of the creditor's ratification. All these rulings are grounded in Hanafi legal reasoning, particularly using qiyas (analogy) to valid suretyship and the principle that one cannot revoke a transaction until its purpose is defeated.

Kafala bil-Nafs: Guarantor for the Defendant in Claims
Kafala bil-nafs procedure in claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on requiring a personal guarantor from the defendant when the plaintiff declares his witnesses are present, and contrast it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
A personal guarantor is required (by istihsan) only when the plaintiff has witnesses present in the city; the three-day limit from Abu Hanifa is the relied-upon view.

In Hanafi judicial procedure, protecting the plaintiff's right to a present defendant is paramount. When the plaintiff states in court that his witnesses are ready in the city, the judge may order the defendant to provide a personal guarantor for three days. This ruling, based on istihsan (juristic preference), balances the plaintiff's interest against minimal harm to the defendant. The Shafi'i school, however, rejects personal guarantees altogether, considering a human person not a valid subject of guarantee. This lesson unpacks the condition, the three-day limit, and the cross-madhhab dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State the ruling: when the plaintiff says 'I have witnesses present,' the judge orders the defendant to provide a personal guarantor (kafala bil-nafs) for three days. If no guarantor is given, the plaintiff may accompany the defendant (mulazama).
The condition 'witnesses present' is critical—absent witnesses yield no benefit.
2
Understand the evidential basis (dalil)
The Hanafi proof is istihsan: it considers the plaintiff's need (hak adami) and minimal harm to the defendant. Moreover, attendance is already obligatory on the defendant from the mere claim, so guaranteeing this attendance is not an extra burden.
Istihsan overrides strict qiyas that might oppose personal guarantees.
3
Recognize the inter-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i holds kafala bil-nafs impermissible because a human person cannot be the object of a guarantee contract. The Hanafi school responds by relying on istihsan as a valid source, establishing the rule despite Shafi'i objection.
The thamarah al-khilaf: in Hanafi courts, the judge can enforce this; in Shafi'i, he cannot.
4
Analyze the three-day estimation
The three-day limit is narrated from Abu Hanifa and declared 'al-sahih' (the correct view). It applies equally to all defendants regardless of status or wealth.
This estimation balances the plaintiff's need with the defendant's freedom.
5
Apply the exception for travelers
A traveler (gharib) is excused from providing a guarantor beyond the court session; mulazama is also limited to the session duration. This prevents undue hardship impeding travel.
The exception shows Hanafi concern for harm (darar) and practicality.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah discusses a procedural rule in Islamic legal claims (da'wa). The text is an expansion of a matn from the Hidayah, explaining the conditions under which a defendant may be required to provide a personal guarantor (kafala bil-nafs). The ruling is that if the plaintiff states in court, 'I have witnesses present in the city,' the judge orders the defendant to provide a guarantor for his own person for three days. This ensures the defendant does not flee, thereby preserving the plaintiff's right. The Hanafi school permits kafala bil-nafs by istihsan (juristic preference), considering the plaintiff's benefit and the minimal harm to the defendant. The three-day limit is a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa and is deemed the correct (sahih) position within the school. The text clarifies that this rule applies equally to all defendants regardless of social status or wealth. A critical condition is that the plaintiff must explicitly state that witnesses are present; if he says he has no evidence or his witnesses are absent, no guarantor is taken as there is no benefit. If the defendant fails to provide a guarantor, the judge orders the plaintiff to accompany the defendant (mulazama) for three days. An exception is made for a traveler (gharib): he is only required to stay for the duration of the court session, and no guarantor is taken beyond that. This exception prevents undue hardship that would prevent travel. The commentary also notes that Imam al-Shafi'i considers kafala bil-nafs impermissible, but the Hanafi school upholds its permissibility, as detailed previously in the Book of Kafala. The manner of mulazama will be explained later in the Book of Hajar. This passage illustrates the Hanafi reliance on istihsan to balance procedural fairness with the plaintiff's right to a present defendant.

Key Terms
كفالة بالنفس (kafala bil-nafs): A contract whereby a person guarantees the presence of another (the defendant) before the court.
بينة حاضرة (bayyina hadira): The plaintiff's statement that his witnesses are present in the city (ready to testify).
استحسان (istihsan): A method of juristic preference that permits departing from qiyas (analogy) in favor of a more suitable ruling.
ملازمة (mulazama): The plaintiff's companionship of the defendant to ensure his presence when no guarantor is available.
