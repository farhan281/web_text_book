Testimony on Inheritance: Establishing the Deceased's Possession
First two rulings on testimony for inheritance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which testimony regarding a deceased's possession is valid and explain why separate proof of death is unnecessary when the claim is based on inheritance.

Core Idea
Testimony that the deceased continuously possessed property until death is sufficient for inheritance; separate witnesses for death are not required.

This lesson examines the first two rulings from the passage. When an heir claims property through inheritance and alleges he gave it as a loan or trust, he does not need to produce separate witnesses for the death. The key is establishing the deceased's continuous possession until death—this is what the testimony must prove.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Plaintiff's Claim
The plaintiff claims the property belonged to his father and that the defendant had it as a loan (āriyah) or trust (wadīʿah). This claim already implies the father's death, as inheritance only arises after death.
The plaintiff does not need to separately prove the father's death because the inheritance claim incorporates it.
2
Determine Requirement for Proof of Death
The ruling states it is not necessary to produce witnesses for the father's death. Why? Because the claim of inheritance itself assumes death, and the focus is on possession.
This simplifies procedure: the heir only needs to prove the deceased's ownership and possession, not the fact of death through separate testimony.
3
Understand the Valid Testimony
Witnesses testify that the father had possession of the house until he died, and the house remained in his possession until death. This testimony is valid (jāʾiz) because it directly proves the deceased's ownership at the time of death.
Continuous possession until death is the key element—it establishes that the property was part of the deceased's estate.
4
Apply to a Scenario
Consider: A heir claims his father owned a shop and gave it as a loan to X. Witnesses say: 'We saw the father in possession of the shop until his last day.' This testimony is valid for inheritance, and no separate witnesses for death are needed.
The ruling applies to any property, not just houses.

Testimony on Current Possession and Admission in Inheritance Claims
Third and fourth rulings: testimony about current possession and effect of admission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why testimony about current possession of a living person is insufficient for inheritance and how the defendant's admission of the plaintiff's possession becomes decisive.

Core Idea
Testimony of current possession alone does not prove inheritance; but the defendant's admission of the plaintiff's possession is binding and conclusive.

The passage raises two further issues: what if witnesses only testify that the plaintiff currently possesses the property while the plaintiff is alive? And what if the defendant admits that the property was in the plaintiff's possession? This lesson explains the rulings based on Hanafi procedural law.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the Scenario of Current Possession Testimony
Witnesses say to someone alive: 'This house has been in possession of the plaintiff for a few months.' The text asks: will this testimony be accepted?
The scenario involves a living plaintiff, not a deceased. The testimony does not link the possession to a deceased owner.
2
Evaluate Why It May Be Insufficient
In Hanafi law, testimony about current possession of a living person does not prove inheritance because inheritance requires proof of the deceased's ownership. Without mention of the deceased, it only shows present control, not right of inheritance.
The ruling is that such testimony is not accepted for establishing inheritance; it might be relevant for other claims like ownership or lease.
3
Understand the Ruling on Admission
If the defendant admits that the house was in the plaintiff's possession at some point, the judge rules in favor of the plaintiff—the house is given to his possession. Admission is a stronger form of proof than testimony.
Admission (iqrār) is binding because it is against the defendant's own interest. It does not require further evidence.
4
Compare the Two Rulings
Testimony about current possession of a living person is insufficient for inheritance; admission of possession by the defendant is sufficient. The difference lies in the source: testimony may be incomplete, but admission is conclusive.
The practical consequence: an heir should seek admission from the possessor or produce testimony linking possession to the deceased.

Ruling on Heir's Oath When Claimant Lacks Proof
Ruling on oath for heir defending inherited property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a claimant lacks proof for a claimed inherited property and identify the condition under which the heir is required to swear.

Core Idea
When a claimant has no evidence, the heir swears based on his knowledge to affirm his inheritance.

In inheritance disputes, the burden of proof lies with the claimant. If the claimant cannot produce evidence, the defendant (the heir) is not automatically liable. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that the heir must take an oath to defend his ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the claim
Recognize that the heir is the defendant in possession of the inherited property, and the claimant asserts ownership without evidence.
2
Determine the evidentiary basis
The claimant has no proof (bayyinah). In Hanafi law, this triggers a shift in procedure: the defendant is asked to swear.
The absence of proof is the key condition.
3
State the ruling
The heir is required to take an oath (qasam) based on his knowledge that the property is rightfully his inheritance.
The oath affirms the heir's claim until contradictory evidence emerges.
4
Understand the legal effect
If the heir swears, the case is dismissed. If he refuses, the judge may ask the claimant to swear and then award the property.
The ruling upholds the principle that possession is evidence of ownership in the absence of proof.

Testimony on Inheritance: The Hanafi Dispute on Proving Transfer
Ruling on testimony for inheritance when property is proved to have belonged to deceased.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions regarding the required testimony for an heir to claim property from a third party and explain the reasoning behind the difference between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, an heir's ownership is considered new (muta'addid); proof of the deceased's ownership alone does not automatically transfer the property without showing the inheritance link.

When an heir claims property from a third party who held it as a loan from the deceased, a dispute arises among the Hanafi imams over what must be proven. This lesson examines the ruling in Al-Hidayah where Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require explicit testimony of the death and inheritance, while Abu Yusuf sees proof of the deceased's ownership as sufficient. Understanding this ikhtilaf helps clarify the Hanafi concept of ownership transfer upon death.

Guided Path
1
State the case
The claimant (heir) proves the deceased owned the property, which the defendant holds as a loan/deposit. What additional proof is needed?
2
Identify the Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa & Muhammad require proof of death + inheritance; Abu Yusuf says ownership proof suffices.
3
Analyze the reasoning
Abu Yusuf: heir's ownership is continuation of deceased's ownership. Abu Hanifa & Muhammad: ownership is new (muta'addid), requiring a transfer.
Evidence for newness: istibra' on inherited slave-girl; charity for poor deceased becomes permissible for wealthy heir.
4
Identify the practical concession
Testimony that deceased owned at time of death is sufficient due to necessity.
This eases the burden, but still requires proof of ownership at death.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is relied upon in fatwa.
It is stricter and followed by later scholars.

Commentary
This passage introduces a new chapter within the Book of Testimony: Testimony on Inheritance (al-shahada 'ala al-irth). It presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding what must be proved when an heir claims property from a possessor who held it as a loan or deposit from the deceased father.

Inheritance Claim on Deposit: Depositee Must Hand Over
Ruling on transfer of deposit to inheritance claimant when depositee confirms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions under which a depositee must hand over a deposit to an inheritance claimant and explain why the depositor's death is the decisive condition.

Core Idea
The depositor's death ends his ownership; the depositee must deliver to the sole heir if confirmed.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Al-Hidayah on inheritance claims involving deposits. When a depositor dies, the deposit becomes part of the estate. If the claimant asserts sole heirship and the depositee confirms, the law orders delivery. We break down the conditions and reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The scenario involves a deceased depositor, a claimant (son) asserting sole heirship, and a depositee who holds the deposit. Understand each party's position.
The claimant must be the only heir – this is critical.
2
Recognize the effect of the depositor's death
Upon death, the depositor's ownership over the deposit ceases. The deposit enters the estate. This is the key change from cases where the depositor is alive.
The depositee's confirmation does not harm a living owner because there is none.
3
Analyze the role of the depositee's confirmation
The depositee confirms the claimant's statement. This confirmation, combined with the absence of other heirs, establishes the claimant's right to take possession.
Confirmation alone is insufficient if other heirs exist – the depositee would be handing over property that belongs to others.
4
State the legal order and its basis
The court orders the depositee to hand over the deposit. The basis is that the property is no longer the depositor's and the mutual agreement (claimant assertion + depositee confirmation) identifies the lawful heir.
The ruling is unconditional once these conditions are met.

Conditions for a Valid Claim of Immovable Property
Conditions for a lawsuit over immovable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three necessary conditions for a valid claim on immovable property and explain why mentioning three boundaries is sufficient according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A valid claim requires four boundaries, but three suffice because the majority takes the ruling of the whole.

When you sue someone over a piece of land or a house, the court needs to know exactly what property you're claiming. The Hanafi jurists set three conditions that make a claim legally effective. Interestingly, they debated whether you must name all four boundaries or if three are enough. This lesson walks through those conditions and the reasoning behind the majority position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
Read the passage and list the three conditions a plaintiff must fulfill: (1) specify all four boundaries, (2) state that the property is in the defendant's possession, and (3) state that he has demanded it.
These are conditions for the claim itself, not for proving ownership.
2
Understand the boundary requirement
Boundaries must include names and lineages of adjacent owners. Abu Hanifa requires the grandfather's name for proper identification.
The lineage detail prevents confusion with similar-named neighbours.
3
Analyze the dispute with Imam Zufar
Imam Zufar holds that omitting a boundary is like an error: error invalidates the claim, so omission should too. The Hanafi majority responds that omission leaves the property undefined, while error changes it.
The key distinction: omission does not alter identity; error does.
4
Apply the majority principle
The Hanafi reasoning: mentioning three boundaries constitutes the majority (akthar), and by qiyas, the majority takes the ruling of the whole (al-akthar ka al-kull).
This principle is used in many areas of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Da'wa explains the three essential conditions for a valid lawsuit regarding immovable property (ghayr manqul). The author, commenting on Al-Hidayah, states that the plaintiff must: (1) specify all four boundaries of the property, including the names and lineages of the owners of adjacent properties, with the grandfather's name required according to Imam Abu Hanifa; (2) state that the property is in the possession of the defendant; and (3) state that he has demanded the property from the defendant. The passage then discusses a dispute with Imam Zufar regarding whether mentioning only three boundaries suffices. The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) is that three boundaries are sufficient because the majority (akthar) of the boundaries have been mentioned, and the majority takes the ruling of the whole. Imam Zufar analogizes omission (tark) to error (ghalat), arguing that just as an error in one boundary invalidates the claim, so does omission. The response distinguishes between omission and error: omission leaves the boundary undefined (its location remains), while error changes the property. The passage also clarifies that in immovable property, the defendant's possession must be proven by witnesses or the judge's personal knowledge to prevent collusion, unlike movable property where possession is apparent.

عقار (aqar): Immovable property such as land or houses.
منقول (manqul): Movable property; in this passage contrasted with 'aqar regarding proof of possession.

Household Goods Upon Death: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on division of household goods upon death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi views on dividing household goods when one spouse dies and identify the principle of possession (yad) underlying Abu Hanifa's position.

Core Idea
After death, possession belongs to the living — common goods go to the survivor.

When a spouse dies and the heirs dispute with the survivor over household goods, without evidence, the Hanafi school has three distinct views. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that all items suitable for both genders belong to the survivor because possession (yad) is with the living. Abu Yusuf gives the wife her trousseau based on custom, and Muhammad al-Shaybani divides items by gender suitability as during life. This lesson examines the reasoning behind each position and extends to cases where a spouse is a slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
One spouse dies, and the heirs of the deceased dispute with the surviving spouse over household goods. There is no evidence or clear ownership documentation.
The dispute is between the survivor and the heirs, not between the spouses themselves.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
All items that could be used by both men and women go to the surviving spouse. The reason: possession (yad) belongs to the living, and the dead have no possession, so the survivor's possession is unchallenged.
3
State Abu Yusuf's Position
The wife is given items that customarily make up her trousseau (jihāz), and the rest goes to the husband with his oath. Custom (zāhir) that a wife brings her dowry is a strong indicator overriding the husband's apparent possession.
Abu Yusuf applies the same rule in divorce and death because heirs stand in place of the deceased.
4
State Muhammad's Position
Items are divided as during life: men's items to the husband, women's items to the wife, and items suitable for both go to the husband or his heirs. Heirs succeed to the rights of the deceased.
Muhammad agrees with Abu Hanifa's reasoning that possession is for the living, but applies the gender-based division from the living case.
5
Extend to the Slave Scenario
If one spouse is a slave, Abu Hanifa says goods go to the free spouse because a free person's hand is stronger. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say a slave permitted to trade (ma'dhūn) and a mukatab are like a free person because they have a valid hand in lawsuits.
This shows how the concept of valid possession (yad mu'tabara) applies to different legal capacities.

Death of a Spouse: Dispute Between Heirs and Survivor Over Household Items
Intra-Hanafi dispute on household items after death of a spouse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) on allocating household items when one spouse dies and apply the principle of yad (possession) to resolve the dispute.

Core Idea
When one spouse dies, the living spouse's possession is default, but custom and suitability adjust the allocation.

This passage from Al-Hidayah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on a common inheritance scenario: a spouse dies, and the heirs of the deceased dispute with the surviving spouse over household items. Each of the three major Hanafi imams—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani—offers a different solution based on the principle of possession (yad) and the consideration of customary practice (urf). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply legal principles to fact patterns.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute trigger
One spouse dies, and the heirs of the deceased dispute with the surviving spouse over who owns the household items. The core question is: who gets what?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and its basis
Abu Hanifa holds that all items go to the surviving spouse because possession (yad) belongs to the living, not the dead. This is the default rule.
The living spouse has actual control over the items.
3
Explain Abu Yusuf's modification
Abu Yusuf says the wife is given her trousseau (jihaz) based on custom (urf), because it is known that women bring dowry. The rest goes to the husband after his oath. Custom is stronger than apparent possession.
Abu Yusuf prioritizes the customary practice over the husband's possession.
4
Explain Muhammad's refinement by suitability
Muhammad classifies items: items suitable for men go to the man (or his heirs), items for women go to the woman (or her heirs), and common items go to the man (or his heirs). This uses the same yad principle as Abu Hanifa but with gender distinction.
Muhammad's view is a middle ground between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
5
Note the special case of a slave spouse
If one spouse is a slave, the free spouse gets the goods because the free person's yad is stronger. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider a slave permitted to trade (ma'dhun) or a mukatib as like a free person in legal disputes.
This shows the Hanafi concern for the strength of possession.
