Witness Retraction After Qisas Execution: Hanafi vs Other Schools
Inter-madhhab dispute on witness retraction after qisas execution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness retraction after qisas execution, explain the key proofs, and evaluate the Shafi'i and Hanbali counter-arguments.

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract after qisas pay diyah, not qisas, because they are not the direct killers and forgiveness is recommended.

This lesson examines a dispute across four madhabs regarding witnesses who retract their testimony after a qisas execution has been carried out. The Hanafi school, alongside the Maliki, holds that the witnesses are liable for diyah (blood-money) on their 'aqila. Imam Shafi'i holds they are executed in qisas, while Imam Ahmad distinguishes between mistaken and intentional false testimony. We will analyze the proofs and the Hanafi response based on causation and the Shari'ah's emphasis on forgiveness.

Guided Path
1
Define the scenario
Understand the case: Hamid claims Khalid killed his father, two witnesses testify, judge decides, Hamid executes Khalid in qisas, then witnesses retract.
Keep the parties straight: wali = executor, witnesses = cause.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and proof
Hanafi: witnesses liable for diyah on 'aqila, not qisas. Proof: witnesses did not kill; wali did; wali not liable for qisas; forgiveness prevents inevitability.
Key principle: direct actor (mubashir) bears liability, not indirect cause (sabab) when causal link is uncertain.
3
Identify opposing positions and their proofs
Shafi'i: witnesses analogous to coercer (mukrih) who causes death, so they face qisas. Hanbali: if mistaken, diyah; if intentional, qisas.
Shafi'i's analogy relies on sabab being treated as perpetrator. Hanbali focuses on the witness's admission.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to each opponent
Hanafi responds: coercion leads to death necessarily, testimony does not because wali may forgive. Quran (2:237, 5:45) and hadith praise forgiveness, so causation is weak.
The Hanafi refutation uses dalil naqli to show that testimony is not mufdi (conducive) to death.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: under Hanafi, witnesses pay diyah; under Shafi'i, they are executed; under Hanbali, depends on admission.
This difference affects the lives of witnesses and their families.

Book of Retraction from Testimonies. Explanation of the case: A person, e.g., Hamid, claimed that Khalid intentionally and unjustly killed his father, and Khalid denies it. Then two witnesses testified that indeed Khalid intentionally and unjustly killed Hamid's father. The judge decided based on this testimony, and Hamid killed Khalid in retribution for his father. Afterwards, the witnesses retracted their testimony. Then these witnesses will be liable for the blood-money (diyah) to Khalid's heirs, and the diyah will be obligatory on the 'aqila of the witnesses, and qisas will not be taken from these witnesses. This is the school of Imam Malik. According to Imam Shafi'i, qisas becomes obligatory on the witnesses. Imam Ahmad's school is that if the witnesses said they testified by mistake, they are liable for diyah; if they said they intentionally gave false testimony, then qisas is obligatory on them. Imam Ahmad's proof: Khalid was killed in qisas due to the witnesses' testimony; it is as if the witnesses are the actual killers. If they retracted and claimed error, it is mistaken killing; if they retracted and claimed intentional false testimony, it is intentional killing. According to shari'ah, mistaken killing requires diyah, intentional killing requires qisas. Imam Shafi'i's proof: The witnesses are only a cause (sabab) in killing Khalid; their testimony caused him to be killed. If they had not testified, Khalid would not have been killed in qisas. The witnesses are not the perpetrators; the perpetrator is the wali (Hamid). Similarly, the one who coerces (mukrih) is a cause, not a perpetrator. For example, Arif told Wasif to kill Shahid, threatening to kill Wasif if he doesn't. So Wasif killed Shahid under coercion. Here Arif the coercer is a cause, not the perpetrator. The witnesses resemble the coercer in being a cause. It is established that in coercion, the coercer (who is the cause) is killed in qisas, not the coerced (who is the perpetrator). So similarly, in false testimony, since the witnesses are causes and not perpetrators, after retraction they should be killed in qisas. The author of Hidayah said: The witnesses are even stronger in causation than the coercer, because testimony tends toward the killing more than coercion. After their testimony, the killer's death becomes almost certain, and the wali is assisted in taking qisas, and no one prevents him. In contrast, in coercion, killing is not certain; rather, not killing is more likely. The coerced person is prevented by shari'ah and custom from killing. Shari'ah prevents because he knows it is prohibited, and custom prevents because people generally stop such acts. So coercion is not as effective in causing death. Thus, if the coercer is killed in qisas, then the witnesses who are more effective should also be killed. Our proof is that killing is an act, and that act was not committed by the witnesses; the perpetrator of the killer's death is the wali, and the wali is not subject to qisas for killing the killer. If the wali, who is the actual perpetrator, is not subject to qisas, how can the witnesses, who did not commit the act, be subject to qisas? The conclusion is that after retraction of testimony, qisas will not be exacted from the witnesses. Likewise, through the phrase 'وَكَذَا التَّسْبِيبَ' etc., the author of Hidayah refutes Imam Shafi'i's proof by saying that the witnesses are not even a cause (sabab) of the killing, because a cause is something that predominantly leads to the result. Here, the testimony does not inevitably lead to killing, because forgiveness is recommended and preferred in shari'ah. Indeed, Allah says: 'And to forgive is nearer to piety' (2:237) and 'And for wounds, legal retribution. But whoever gives it up as charity, it will be an expiation for him' (5:45). These verses show that forgiveness is recommended. Also hadith: 'Whoever suppresses rage while able to act, Allah will fill his heart with security and faith' and 'A caller will call on the Day of Resurrection: Where are those whose reward was upon Allah? None will stand except those who forgave people.'

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute from the Hidayah discussing the ruling when witnesses retract their testimony after a death sentence (qisas) has been carried out based on their testimony. The case: Hamid claims Khalid killed his father; two witnesses testify; judge decides; Hamid executes Khalid in qisas; then witnesses retract. The Hanafi position (also Maliki) is that the witnesses are liable for diyah (blood-money) on their 'aqila, not qisas. Imam Shafi'i holds they are liable for qisas. Imam Ahmad differentiates between mistaken and intentional false testimony: if they claim error, diyah; if they admit intentional falsehood, qisas. The text presents proofs for each. Imam Ahmad's reasoning: the witnesses are the de facto killers since their testimony caused the execution; the nature of their retraction determines whether it was intentional or mistaken killing. Imam Shafi'i's reasoning: witnesses are a cause (sabab) of death, analogous to a coercer (mukrih) who compels another to kill; since the coercer is executed in qisas, so should the witnesses. The author of Hidayah (representing the Hanafi school) refutes both. He argues that the witnesses are not even a proper cause because their testimony does not necessitate death; the wali (Hamid) could forgive, and forgiveness is highly recommended in the Quran and Sunnah. Thus, the testimony is not 'mufdi ila al-qatl' (conducive to killing) in a dominant sense. Moreover, the actual act of killing was committed by the wali, and the wali is not subject to qisas for killing the murderer. If the direct actor is not liable, how can the indirect cause (the witnesses) be liable? The Hanafi position relies on the principle that liability follows direct action, not mere causation, especially when the causal link is weak due to the possibility of forgiveness. The text then supports this with Quranic verses (2:237 and 5:45) and hadith emphasizing forgiveness. This shows that the Shari'ah encourages pardon, so the testimony does not inevitably lead to execution. Therefore, the witnesses are not considered the direct cause, and they only owe diyah as compensation for the wrongful death.

Key Terms
الرجوع عن الشهادة (al-ruju' 'an al-shahadah): Retraction of testimony after the judge has passed a verdict based on that testimony.
القصاص (al-qisas): Retributive justice: the offender is subjected to the same harm they inflicted (e.g., death for murder).
الدية (al-diyah): Financial compensation paid to the heirs of a victim in cases of homicide (especially mistaken killing) or bodily injury.
العاقلة (al-'aqila): The group of male paternal relatives (or tribe) who are collectively responsible for paying diyah in cases of accidental or mistaken killing.
السبب (al-sabab): A cause that typically leads to a result, but does not directly produce it. In legal causation, contrast with 'direct perpetration' (mubasharah).

Ruling on Agent's Confession in Qisas Cases
Explains Hanafi ruling on agent's confession in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's confession in qisas and explain why istihsan overrides qiyas in this case.

Core Idea
In qisas cases, an agent's confession is not accepted by istihsan due to doubt about authorization and the nature of qisas.

This lesson examines a specific case where agency law meets criminal law. While general principles of agency suggest that an agent's confession binds the principal, the Hanafi school applies istihsan to prevent injustice in qisas cases. You will learn the reasoning behind this exception.

2

Qiyas is the default method.

Understand the istihsan argument
Why does istihsan reject it? Two reasons: qisas not exacted from a substitute, and doubt about authorization.
Istihsan overrides qiyas here.

در قصاص کو دفع کرنے کیلئے وکیل کرنا بھی جائز ہوگا، ہاں اتنی بات ضروری ہے کہ اگر وکیل مجلس قاضی میں اپنے مؤکل پر موجب قد (قتل) کا اقرار کرلے یعنی یہ اقرار کر لے کہ واقعی میرا موکل قاتل ہے تو وکیل کا یہ اقرار استحسانا قبول نہ ہوگا ، اگر چہ قیا سا قبول کر لیا جائے گا۔ قیاس کی وجہ یہ ہے کہ توکیل درست ہونے کے بعد وکیل موکل کے قائم مقام ہوتا ہے، لہذا وکیل کا اقرار مؤکل کے اقرار کے مانند ہو گا ، اور مؤکل کا اقرار چونکہ خود اس پر درست ہے اس لئے وکیل کا اقرار اپنے مؤکل پر درست ہوگا۔ استحسان کی وجہ یہ ہے کہ وکیل کا اقرار مؤکل کے اقرار کے قائم مقام ہوتا ہے اور قصاص ایسی بہت سے وصول نہیں کیا جاتا جو دوسرے کے قائم مقام ہو۔ دوسری وجہ یہ ہے کہ اس میں یہ شبہ ہے کہ مؤکل نے وکیل کو اقرار کاحکم نہ دیا ہو اور بغیر مؤکل کے حکم کے وکیل اقرار کا مجاز نہیں ہے، اور جب وکیل اقرار کا مجاز نہیں ہے تو شبہ کی بنا پر موکل پر وکیل کا اقرار قبول نہ ہوگا۔
Regarding warding off qisas, appointing an agent is also permissible. Yes, it is necessary that if the agent in the court of the judge confesses to the basis of murder against his client, i.e., confesses that indeed my client is the murderer, then this confession of the agent will not be accepted by istihsan, even though by qiyas it would be accepted. The reason for qiyas is that once the agency is valid, the agent stands in the place of the client, so the agent's confession will be like the client's confession, and since the client's confession is valid against himself, the agent's confession will be valid against his client. The reason for istihsan is that the agent's confession stands in the place of the client's confession, and qisas is not exacted from someone who stands in the place of another. Another reason is that there is a doubt that the client may not have given the agent permission to confess, and without the client's permission, the agent is not authorized to confess. And when the agent is not authorized to confess, then due to this doubt, the agent's confession will not be accepted against the client.

Commentary
This passage addresses a specific ruling regarding wakalah in qisas cases. It states that while it is permissible to appoint an agent to defend against qisas, if the agent confesses in court that their client is guilty of murder, that confession is not accepted by istihsan (juristic preference), even though by qiyas (analogy) it would be accepted. The qiyas argument is based on the principle that the agent acts as the client's representative, so the agent's confession should have the same effect as the client's own confession. However, istihsan overrides this due to two considerations: first, qisas is not exacted from someone who is merely a substitute for another; second, there is a doubt as to whether the client actually authorized the agent to make such a confession, and since the agent is not empowered to confess without explicit authorization, the confession is not binding on the client. This shows the Hanafi use of istihsan to prevent potential injustice.

Key Terms
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that overrides qiyas in favor of a more equitable solution.
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning that applies a known ruling to a new case due to shared effective cause.
اقرار (iqrar): Confession or admission of a right or liability against oneself.
