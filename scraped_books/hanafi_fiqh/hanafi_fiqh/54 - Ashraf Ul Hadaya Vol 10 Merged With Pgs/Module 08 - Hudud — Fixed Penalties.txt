Testimony in Hudud (other than Zina) and Qisas
Stating the rule that two male witnesses are required in hudud and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness requirements in hudud (excluding zina) and qisas, identify the Quranic proof, and explain why women's testimony is excluded.

Core Idea
The requirement of two male witnesses in hudud and qisas is grounded in the dual and masculine forms of Quran 2:282.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies the category of testimony for hudud penalties (other than zina) and qisas. The ruling is straightforward: two male witnesses are required. However, the deeper legal reasoning involves precise extraction from the Quranic text and a supplementary argument excluding women, which distinguishes it from other areas of testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what legal act is being judged and the specific ruling: here, testimony in hudud (excluding zina) and qisas requires two male witnesses.
2
Locate the primary dalil
The author cites Quran 2:282: 'Wa istashhidu shahidayni min rijalikum' (And call to witness two witnesses from among your men).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'istashhidu' indicates obligation per Hanafi usul. The dual 'shahidayni' specifies exactly two, and 'min rijalikum' specifies males, thereby excluding women.
The grammatical form is key.
4
Understand the supplementary proof for excluding women
The author refers to the hadith of Zuhri and the doubt of substitution (shubhah badliyah), which is explained in earlier commentary: women's testimony is not accepted in hudud due to their potential substitution with men, creating doubt that prevents the strict hadd.
This reinforces the Quranic proof.
5
Apply to specific cases
The ruling applies to all hudud except zina (which requires four witnesses) and to qisas. Examples include hadd of qadhf, drinking alcohol, and theft.

Commentary
This passage presents the second category of testimony: hudud (other than zina) and qisas. Agency for Hudud and Qisas: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
States the permissibility of agency for rights except hudud and qisas, with an intra-Hanafi dispute on the extent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency for hudud and qisas when the principal is absent and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.

Core Idea
Agency for hudud and qisas is invalid when the principal is absent due to the doubt of pardon.

This passage addresses an important exception to the general permissibility of agency (wakalah) in Islamic law. While agency is broadly allowed for fulfilling and receiving rights, hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation) are treated differently because they are warded off by doubts (shubuhat). The text presents the ruling and then details an intra-Hanafi disagreement among the three founders of the school: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this dispute will sharpen your ability to identify how legal reasoning (ta'lil) shapes practical differences within a single madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule and Exception
The passage begins by stating that agency is valid for fulfilling and receiving all rights, except hudud and qisas. Note the wording: 'الا فى الحدود والقصاص'.
Hudud are fixed punishments; qisas is retaliatory punishment.
2
Understand the Reason for Invalidity
Agency is invalid for receiving hudud/qisas when the principal is absent from the session. The reason: hudud and qisas are repelled by doubts, and the doubt of pardon (shubhat al-'afw) exists when the principal is absent, since Shari'ah encourages pardon.
Compare with the absence of a witness (no doubt of retraction) and presence of the principal (no doubt).
3
Analyze the Practical Rationale
The text adds that not everyone is skilled in executing punishments, so if agency were completely forbidden, the door of execution would close. This is a maslahah-based argument.
This justifies allowing agency for receiving (execution) under certain conditions.
4
Map the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa: agency invalid only for receiving (execution) when principal absent. Abu Yusuf: invalid even for proving (establishing) hudud/qisas by bringing witnesses. Muhammad: two narrations, one with each.
The dispute is only when the principal is absent; when present, all agree agency is valid.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The text presents Abu Hanifa's view as the primary statement, and later Hanafi authorities have relied upon it. Thus, the operative ruling (mufta bihi) is that agency is invalid only for receiving, not for proving.
This is a key distinction: an agent can file a case but cannot execute the punishment without the principal present.

Commentary
This passage deals with the limits of agency in criminal and penal matters. The general rule is that agency is permissible for fulfilling and receiving rights. However, an exception is made for hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation). The ruling is that agency is not valid for receiving hudud and qisas when the principal is absent from the court session. The reason given is that hudud and qisas are warded off by doubts (shubuhat), and when the principal is absent, there is a strong possibility that he might have pardoned the offender, since the Shari'ah encourages pardon in such cases. This doubt is absent when the principal is present. The author contrasts this with the absence of a witness, where the assumption is that the witness has not retracted. The reasoning also includes practical necessity: not everyone is skilled in executing punishments, so if agency were completely prohibited, the implementation of punishments would be hindered. The passage then records an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa holds that agency is invalid only for receiving (executing) hudud and qisas when the principal is absent, but it is valid for proving them (establishing evidence). Abu Yusuf goes further, holding that agency is invalid even for proving hudud and qisas by bringing witnesses. Imam Muhammad's position is reported with two versions: one aligning with Abu Hanifa, the other with Abu Yusuf. The difference is said to apply only when the principal is absent; when present, agency is permissible because the agent's speech is considered the principal's own. The passage ends mid-sentence, comparing this to testimony.

Key Terms
حدود (hudud): Fixed punishments prescribed by the Shari'ah for major crimes (e.g., theft, fornication).
قصاص (qisas): Retaliatory punishment for murder or bodily harm, equivalent to the injury caused.
شبهة (shubhah): A legal doubt or ambiguity that can avert a fixed punishment.
عفو ('afw): Pardon or forgiveness, which is encouraged in hudud and qisas cases.
غيبة المؤكل (ghaybat al-muwakkil): The absence of the principal from the court session where hudud/qisas are to be executed.

Appointing a Wakeel for Hudood and Qisas: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on agency for hudood and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the appointment of a wakeel for hudood and qisas, and apply the mufta bihi to a given scenario involving the principal's absence.

Core Idea
Hudood and qisas are dropped by doubts; agency for taking them is impermissible in the principal's absence, but establishing them through witnesses is permissible.

In this lesson, we examine an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a wakeel can be appointed to handle hudood (fixed punishments) and qisas (retribution) cases when the principal is absent. The key issue lies in distinguishing between establishing the claim (thabat) and taking the punishment (istifa'). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi rulings on criminal agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its context
Determine that the act in question is the appointment of a wakeel (agent) for matters related to hudood and qisas, specifically when the principal is absent from court.
The general rule is that agency is permissible for all rights, but hudood and qisas have special rules.
2
State the general rule and its exception
Recall that agency for performing and obtaining rights is permissible, except for obtaining hudood and qisas when the principal is absent, due to the possibility of pardon (shubhah).
The exception is based on the principle that punishments are lifted by doubts.
3
Differentiate between the two phases: establishing and taking
Distinguish between 'establishing' the claim through witnesses (thabat) and 'taking' the punishment (istifa'). Abu Hanifa considers the doubt only affects the taking phase.
The doubt of pardon concerns the principal's intent to forgive, not the reliability of witnesses.
4
Contrast the positions of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
Abu Hanifa holds agency permissible for establishing but not taking; Abu Yusuf holds it impermissible for both. Note that Imam Muhammad's position is unsettled.
Both imams agree on the impermissibility of taking in the principal's absence.
5
Apply the mufta bihi to a concrete scenario
Given a scenario (e.g., victim's family member abroad appoints a lawyer), determine that the lawyer can present evidence but cannot demand execution until the principal is present.
The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view, as adopted in the Zahir al-Riwayah.

The chapter of agency. And as in the taking (of rights), and according to Abu Hanifa, litigation is a pure condition, because the obligation is attributed to the emergence of testimony, so agency applies in it as in all other rights. Translation: This same ruling applies to performing and obtaining all rights, except hudood and qisas. Indeed, appointing an agent to obtain hudood and qisas in the absence of the principal from the court is not permissible, because hudood and qisas are dropped due to doubts. And in the absence of the principal, there exists the possibility that the killer may be pardoned—indeed, it is recommended (mandub) by Sharia, so this is apparent—unlike the absence of a witness, because apparently he will not recant his testimony, and unlike the presence of the principal, because the doubt of pardoning is not present. And not everyone is able to take qisas, so if he is prevented from appointing an agent, the door of obtaining qisas would be completely closed. This is the saying of Abu Hanifa. Abu Yusuf said: Appointing an agent to establish hudood and qisas by presenting witnesses is also not permissible. Imam Muhammad's saying is with Abu Hanifa, and it is said (he is) with Abu Yusuf. Some scholars said that this difference is in the case where the principal is absent, not when he is present, because the agent's speech is attributed to the principal in his presence, so it is as if the principal himself is speaking. Abu Yusuf's proof is that appointing an agent is appointing a deputy, whereas in the chapter of hudood and qisas, even the doubt of absence is avoided, as in testimony upon testimony and as in obtaining qisas. Abu Hanifa's proof is that litigation is a pure condition, because the obligation of hudood and qisas is attributed to the crime, and the manifestation of the crime is attributed to testimony, so agency applies in it as in all other rights. Commentary: Sahib al-Quduri said: Appointing an agent to perform and obtain rights is permissible, except hudood and qisas, meaning appointing an agent to obtain hudood and qisas is not permissible. So if the principal is not present before the judge, the agent has no authority to take the hadd or qisas. Imam Shafi'i, Imam Malik, and Imam Ahmad said: In the absence of the principal, it is permissible for the agent to obtain hudood and qisas. And if the principal is present in court, there is consensus among the four imams that the agent can take the hadd and qisas. But in the absence of the principal, according to the Hanafis, it is not permissible for the agent to obtain hudood and qisas, while according to the three imams it is permissible. Our response: Hudood and qisas are punishments, and punishments are dropped due to doubts. In the absence of the principal from court, there is a doubt that the principal might acquit the killer and pardon him—indeed, this possibility is apparent and predominant because of the verses 'And to pardon is nearer to piety' and 'Whoever forgives, it is an expiation for him,' so pardoning the killer and criminal is recommended by Sharia. When there is a doubt of pardoning from the principal, and punishments are dropped due to doubt, then in the absence of the principal, the agent has no right to obtain hudood and qisas because of this doubt. Since hudood and qisas are dropped due to doubts and cannot be obtained through 'Kitab al-Qadi ila al-Qadi', testimony upon testimony, or testimony of women with a man. However, if after giving testimony for hadd or qisas the witness becomes absent from the judge's court, then hudood and qisas can be obtained even in the absence of the witness, even though there is a doubt that the witness might recant, because apparently the witness will not recant his testimony, since recantation is based on sin and falsehood, and for a Muslim, sin and falsehood are contrary to the apparent. So to avoid accusation of sin and falsehood, apparently the witness will not recant. And when it is apparent that the witness will not recant, obtaining hudood and qisas in his absence is permissible, because if the principal wanted to pardon the killer, he would have, but since he did not pardon while he was present in court, the doubt of pardoning remains no more, and qisas is not dropped, so the agent can obtain it. The phrase 'waisa kullu ahad' is an answer to a question: When the principal is present in the judge's court, appointing an agent to obtain qisas should not be valid, because in that case the principal can obtain qisas himself. The answer is that not everyone is able to obtain qisas or does not possess the ability to obtain it properly; if he is prevented from appointing an agent, the door of obtaining qisas would be closed. Sahib al-Hidayah said: This is what we mentioned—that it is permissible to appoint an agent for litigation in all rights and for performing and obtaining them. Then he made an exception for hudood and qisas, and said: If the principal is not present in the judge's court, appointing an agent to obtain hudood and qisas is not permissible. From this clarification it becomes known that appointing an agent to establish hudood and qisas by presenting witnesses is permissible—this detail is the madhhab of Imam Abu Hanifa. Summary: According to Imam Abu Hanifa, if the principal is not present in court, appointing an agent to obtain hudood and qisas is permissible. And Imam Abu Yusuf said: Just as appointing an agent to take hudood and qisas is not permissible, likewise appointing an agent to establish them is also not permissible. In short, in the absence of the principal, appointing an agent to obtain hudood and qisas is impermissible according to both Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, but there is disagreement about appointing an agent to establish hudood and qisas by presenting witnesses: according to Abu Hanifa, it is permissible; according to Abu Yusuf, it is impermissible. Imam Muhammad's saying is unsettled: some say he is with Abu Hanifa, some say with Abu Yusuf. Some scholars said that this disagreement is in the case where the principal is absent from court, but if the principal is present, there is consensus that it is permissible to appoint an agent to establish hudood and qisas, because in the presence of the principal, the agent's speech is attributed to the principal, so it is as if the principal himself is speaking. And when the principal is the speaker, there is no harm in establishing hudood and qisas.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Hidaya discusses the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of appointing a wakeel (agent) for matters related to hudood (fixed punishments) and qisas (retribution). The text begins by stating the general rule that agency is permissible for all rights, both for performing and obtaining them, with the exception of hudood and qisas. The exception is specifically for obtaining (istifa') hudood and qisas when the principal is absent from the court. The reason given is that hudood and qisas are dropped due to doubts (shubuhaat), and in the principal's absence, there is a possibility that the principal might have pardoned the criminal, especially since pardoning is recommended (mandub) in Shariah. The text then presents the positions of the three Hanafi imams: Abu Hanifa holds that agency is permissible for establishing (thabat) hudood and qisas by presenting witnesses, but not for taking them in the principal's absence. Abu Yusuf holds that it is not permissible for either purpose. Imam Muhammad's position is disputed between the two. The text also contrasts the Hanafi position with that of Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali schools, which allow agency for taking hudood and qisas even in the principal's absence. The commentary then elaborates on the reasoning, including an analogy with the absence of a witness (whose testimony is considered stable) and the presence of the principal (which removes the doubt). Finally, it explains that the disagreement is only when the principal is absent; if present, there is consensus on permissibility.

Key Terms

الخصومة (al-khusoomah): Litigation: the process of presenting a case in court, including pleading and dispute.
الحدود (al-hudood): Fixed penal punishments prescribed by Shariah for specific crimes (e.g., theft, zina).
القصاص (al-qisas): Retributive punishment for murder or injury, typically equivalent retaliation or blood money.
الشبهة (al-shubhah): Legal doubt that averts the application of prescribed punishments.

Wakalah for Establishing Hudud and Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on wakalah for hudud and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between wakalah for execution and establishment of hudud and qisas, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding the permissibility of appointing an agent to establish such cases.

Core Idea
In hudud and qisas, agency for execution is restricted; agency for establishment is permitted by Abu Hanifa but rejected by Abu Yusuf due to shubha.

The permissibility of appointing an agent (wakeel) in hudud and qisas cases is not uniform. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the act of executing the punishment and the act of establishing the case before a judge. This lesson explores the conditions under which each type of agency is valid and examines the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf on whether establishing hudud through an agent is permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of wakalah
Separate the concepts of wakalah for execution (istifa') of hudud/qisas and wakalah for establishment (ithbat) by presenting witnesses.
The source uses both terms: 'وصول' (execution) and 'ثابت' (establishment).
2
Determine the ruling for each when client is absent/present
Note that when the client is absent, both Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf agree on the prohibition of wakalah for execution. When present, it is allowed. The dispute is about wakalah for establishment.
The source clarifies that when the client is present, the agent's speech is attributed to the client, removing shubha.
3
Locate the point of disagreement
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that wakalah for establishing hudud/qisas is permissible even in the client's absence. Imam Abu Yusuf holds it is impermissible. Imam Muhammad's position is unsettled.
The dispute is specifically about ithbat, not istifa'.
4
Analyze Abu Yusuf's argument
Abu Yusuf argues that appointing an agent is a form of deputyship (niyabah), which introduces a shubha (doubt). Since hudud are averted by shubuhat, this wakalah is impermissible.
He analogizes to the non-acceptance of testimony upon testimony and the prohibition of wakalah for execution when client is absent.
5
Analyze Abu Hanifa's argument
Abu Hanifa argues that litigation (khusumat) is merely a condition (shart) for establishing hudud, not a substantive part. Conditions are delegable rights, and agency is permissible for all rights.
He distinguishes between the obligation of hadd (attached to the crime) and the procedural act of filing a case.
6
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: According to Abu Hanifa, a plaintiff can appoint a wakeel to bring a hudud/qisas case; according to Abu Yusuf, the plaintiff must appear personally.
This affects access to justice for victims unable to attend court.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of appointing a wakeel (agent) for establishing hudud and qisas (fixed punishments and retaliation). The discussion begins by explaining the ruling on executing hudud and qisas in the absence of a witness, then shifts to the core issue: whether one can appoint an agent to bring a case to court to prove such crimes. The text presents the position of Imam Abu Hanifa, who allows wakalah for establishing (ithbat) hudud and qisas, distinguishing it from wakalah for execution (istifa') which is not allowed when the client is absent. Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees, extending the prohibition to both establishment and execution. The commentary then provides the reasoning for each imam. Abu Yusuf argues that appointing an agent introduces a shubha (doubt) of deputyship, and hudud are averted by doubts. Abu Hanifa counters that litigation (khusumat) is merely a procedural condition, a right that can be delegated, and that the obligation of hadd attaches to the crime itself, not to the litigation process. Imam Muhammad's position is noted as unsettled. The text also clarifies that when the client is present in court, there is unanimity that establishment can be delegated. This passage is from Ashraf al-Hidayah, a commentary on al-Hidayah, which itself is a central text of the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
وکالت (wakālah): Agency; a contract where one person (muwakkil) appoints another (wakeel) to act on his behalf in a legal matter.
حدود (ḥudūd): Fixed punishments prescribed by Islamic law for specific crimes (e.g., theft, adultery).
قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliation in kind for intentional murder or injury, where the legal guardian of the victim has the right to exact retribution or forgive.
شبہ (shubhah): Doubt or ambiguity that can avert the application of hudud punishments.
خصومت (khuṣūmat): Litigation; the act of bringing a case before a judge, including filing a complaint and presenting evidence.

Wakalah for Defense in Hudud and Qisas
Expands on al-Hidayah's ruling on wakalah for defense

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wakalah for defense in hudud and qisas and explain why Abu Hanifa's view is preferred over Abu Yusuf's.

Core Idea
Defense against hudud is given more latitude: shubha does not block wakalah for daf', even if it blocks establishment.

This passage from Ashraf al-Hidayah addresses a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: is it permissible for an accused in a hudud or qisas case to appoint an agent to present his defense? While the same dispute exists here as in wakalah for establishing hadd, the commentator clarifies that Abu Hanifa's ruling is even more apparent (azhar) in this context. The key principle is that shubha (doubt) does not prevent daf' (repelling the punishment), unlike its role in establishing the punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue is whether the accused can appoint a wakeel to answer or defend (jawab/daf') in a hadd or qisas case. Abu Hanifa says ja'iz (permissible), Abu Yusuf says ghayr ja'iz (impermissible). Imam Muhammad's position is unsettled.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
2
Understand the key principle: shubha does not prevent daf'
The text argues that Abu Hanifa's view is more apparent because the doubt (shubha) that might invalidate agency for establishment does not block defense. Defense is given more latitude.
This is a core Hanafi usul principle in hudud: daf' al-hudud bi al-shubuhat is applied differently for establishing vs. repelling.
3
Analyze the dalil: testimony upon testimony
The text provides evidence that daf' can be achieved through weaker forms of proof. For example, in qisas, forgiveness can be proven through testimony upon testimony (shahadah ala al-shahadah) or the testimony of women (which normally would not be accepted in hudud). This shows that doubts do not prevent daf'.
The same logic applies to agency: if weaker evidence can establish forgiveness, then agency (which involves some doubt) should be allowed for defense.
4
Note the exception: agent's confession is not binding
The text specifies that the agent's confession (iqrar) is not binding on the client because there is doubt the client authorized it. This is a limitation on the agent's authority.
The agent can present defense but cannot confess on behalf of the accused.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Following Abu Hanifa, an accused can delegate defense to a wakeel. Following Abu Yusuf, the accused must appear personally. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
This affects court procedure in Hanafi jurisdictions.

Wakalah for Establishing Hudud and Qisas: The Core Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn on wakalah for establishing hudud and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn regarding appointing an agent to establish hudud and qisas, and state the proofs and practical consequence.

Core Idea
Agency in hudud is valid because litigation is a mere right; shubha does not bar it.

In this passage from the Sharh of Hidayah, we encounter a classical intra-Hanafi dispute: can a person appoint an agent to prove hudud and qisas in court? Imam Abu Hanifa says yes, while his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, say no. The difference turns on whether litigation (khusumah) is part of the substance of the claim or merely a condition. Understanding this dispute clarifies the role of shubha in penal law and the scope of agency in Islamic courts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The issue is whether it is permissible to appoint an agent (wakil) to establish (prove) hudud and qisas in court. Note that this concerns the plaintiff's side.
2
State Imam Abu Yusuf's position and proof
Abu Yusuf holds it is impermissible. His proof: appointing a deputy creates shubha, and hudud are warded off by shubha. He draws analogies: (1) shahada ala shahada is not accepted in hudud due to shubha of badal, and (2) appointing an agent to collect qisas in the principal's absence is invalid due to shubha of forgiveness.
The shubha here is shubhat al-niyabah (deputyship).
3
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa holds it is permissible. His proof: litigation is a mere condition (shart) for establishing the hadd, not part of the substance. A condition is a right, and agency is permissible in all rights. Therefore agency for litigation is valid.
He distinguishes the analogies: the shubha in those cases affects the substance of the proof, not the condition of litigation.
4
Compare the analogies and identification of shubha
Abu Yusuf's analogies rely on the idea that any form of shubha prevents hudud. Abu Hanifa responds that the shubha in the analogies (badal, forgiveness) pertains to the proof itself, whereas the shubha of deputyship here only pertains to the litigation process, which is a mere right.
This distinction is key to the Hanafi response.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
According to Abu Hanifa, the plaintiff can send a wakil to court to prove the hadd or qisas claim. According to Sahibayn, the plaintiff must appear personally. This affects the logistics of litigation.

5
Apply the distinction between qiyas and istihsan in Hanafi methodology
This is a classic example where istihsan overrides qiyas due to a stronger reason (shubha and the nature of qisas). Understand that Hanafi usul prioritizes istihsan over qiyas in certain contexts.

In this issue, the proof of Imam Abu Yusuf is that appointing an agent to establish hudud and qisas in the absence of the principal is actually appointing a deputy, and appointing a deputy is a kind of shubha (uncertainty). And it is accepted that hudud and qisas are warded off by shubha. The proof of Imam Abu Hanifa is that litigation is merely a condition for establishing hudud and qisas; without litigation, it is not possible to establish hudud and qisas. And in this, the statement of Imam Abu Hanifa is more apparent than for establishing the hadd. That is, when according to Imam Abu Hanifa it is permissible to appoint an agent to establish hudud and qisas, then to appoint an agent to repel hudud and qisas from the principal is even more permissible, because the shubha of badal, due to which Imam Abu Yusuf considers it impermissible, does not prevent the repelling of hudud and qisas. That is, despite shubhat, the repelling of hudud and qisas is established. For example, forgiveness from qisas is established through shahada ala shahada and through testimony of women with men. That is, if the branch witnesses testify on behalf of the original witnesses that the heirs of the victim have forgiven the qisas, or one man and two women testify that the heirs have forgiven the qisas, then due to this shahada ala shahada and testimony of women with men, forgiveness from qisas is established. However, it is necessary that if the agent in the judge's court confesses against his principal to the cause of qisas (i.e., he confesses that his principal is indeed the killer), then this confession of the agent will not be accepted by istihsan, although by qiyas it would be accepted. The reason for qiyas is that after valid appointment, the agent stands in place of the principal, so the agent's confession would be like the principal's confession, and since the principal's confession is valid against himself, the agent's confession would be valid against his principal. The reason for istihsan is that the agent's confession stands in place of the principal's confession, and qisas is not a matter that can be enforced through a substitute. Another reason is that there is a shubha that the principal may not have commanded the agent to confess, and without the principal's command, the agent is not authorized to confess. And when the agent is not authorized to confess, his confession is not accepted against the principal due to the shubha.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh of Hidayah deals with the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of appointing an agent (wakil) for the purpose of establishing hudud and qisas in court. The passage first presents the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf, then extends the discussion to agency on behalf of the defendant (man alayhi al-hadd). Finally, it addresses the ruling on the agent's confession. The passage type is an intra-Hanafi dispute with proofs from both sides, followed by a clarification of the relied-upon position for the defendant's agency and the istihsan regarding confession. The commentator (probably Allamah Abd al-Awwal Jaunpuri or similar) explains the reasoning of each imam. Imam Abu Yusuf argues that appointing an agent to establish hudud and qisas is essentially appointing a deputy (na'ib), and deputyship involves shubha which must be avoided in hudud. He draws an analogy (qiyas) with two other cases: (1) shahada ala shahada (testimony upon testimony) is not accepted in hudud and qisas due to the shubha of badal (replacement), and (2) appointing an agent to collect qisas in the principal's absence is invalid due to the shubha of forgiveness. Imam Abu Hanifa, on the other hand, considers litigation (khusumah) as a mere condition (shart) for establishing hudud and qisas, not part of the substance. Since litigation is a right (haqq) and agency is permissible in all rights, it is permissible to appoint an agent for litigation. The text then extends the dispute to agency for the defendant. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, it is permissible to appoint an agent to defend against hudud and qisas, and this is even more clearly permissible than for establishing them, because shubha does not prevent the repelling of hudud. Examples are given: forgiveness from qisas can be proven through shahada ala shahada and through the testimony of women with men, despite the shubha in these types of evidence. Thus, agency for defense is valid despite the shubha of deputyship. However, an important exception is made regarding the agent's confession: if the agent confesses that his principal is liable, that confession is not accepted by istihsan, even though qiyas would accept it. The commentator explains that this is because confession is a strictly personal act, and qisas cannot be enforced through a substitute. Additionally, there is shubha that the principal may not have authorized the agent to confess. The commentary follows the author's order: first the dispute on establishing hudud, then the extension to defense, and finally the ruling on confession.

Revival of Wakalah After Apostasy: Imam Muhammad vs. Imam Abu Yusuf
Intra-Hanafi dispute on revival of wakalah after apostasy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute regarding the revival of wakalah after apostasy and evaluate the practical consequence of each view.

Core Idea
Wakalah is either permission or authority; whether it revives after apostasy depends on that nature.

This passage from al-Hidayah examines a key intra-Hanafi disagreement. When a wakil (agent) apostatizes and joins Dar al-Harb, does his wakalah become void permanently or merely suspended? Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf offer opposing analyses, each grounded in a distinct conception of the contract's nature. Understanding this dispute reveals the depth of Hanafi contract theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The case involves an apostate wakil who later returns to Islam and to Dar al-Islam. The question is whether his previous wakalah remains valid.
The passage also treats the principal's apostasy separately.
2
State the two positions
Imam Muhammad holds that the wakalah revives. Imam Abu Yusuf holds that it does not, even if the wakil returns as a Muslim.
Both imams are within the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the basis of wakalah
Imam Muhammad views wakalah as permission (idhn) to act, which remains. Imam Abu Yusuf views it as authority (wilayah) based on ownership, which is destroyed.
This conceptual difference drives the entire dispute.
4
Examine Abu Yusuf's analogy
He analogizes to umm walad and mudabbar: joining Dar al-Harb is like death, voiding ownership permanently. The wakil’s authority is likewise destroyed.
Imam Muhammad rejects the analogy, noting that personal capacity (freedom, sanity, puberty) is not ownership-based.
5
Extend to the principal's case
For the principal (muwakkil), the Zahir al-Riwayah position holds that wakalah does not revive. But Imam Muhammad has a narration from Nawaadir that it does.
The distinction between wakil and principal is based on ownership vs. personal attributes.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the effect of apostasy and relocation to Dar al-Harb on the validity of wakalah. The core issue is whether wakalah revives when the apostate returns to Islam and to Dar al-Islam. Imam Muhammad holds that the wakil's wakalah revives because wakalah is fundamentally permission (idhn) from the principal, which remains intact; the impediment of being in Dar al-Harb is merely a temporary barrier (ha'il) that is removed upon return. Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees: for him, wakalah is authority (wilayah) to execute transactions; this authority is ownership-based and is destroyed when the wakil joins Dar al-Harb (treated as dead). A destroyed ownership cannot revive. He analogizes to umm walad and mudabbar which become free upon the master's death-equivalent. The passage then discusses the principal's own apostasy. According to Zahir al-Riwayah, the principal's wakalah does not revive upon his return, because the basis for the principal is ownership, which is totally lost. However, from Imam Muhammad there are two narrations: one from Nawaadir equating principal and wakil, and one from Zahir al-Riwayah distinguishing between them. The Zahir al-Riwayah position distinguishes on grounds that the wakil's eligibility is based on personal attributes that survive, while the principal's is based on ownership which is destroyed. This is a classical example of how Hanafi jurists analyze the underlying rationale (ta'lil) of contracts and legal capacity.

The Thief's Oath: Liability Without Hadd
Ruling with proof via qiyas on nukul's effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the thief's refusal of the oath and explain the legal reasoning (qiyas) distinguishing financial liability from the hadd penalty.

Core Idea
Nukul proves property claim but not hudud; the thief pays but his hand stays.

When a thief is caught and denies the theft, the victim may demand an oath. What happens if the thief refuses to swear? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling from al-Jami' al-Saghir: refusal leads to financial liability but not hand amputation. We will follow the commentator's reasoning through analogies to testimony and confession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the ruling: the thief is administered an oath; if he refuses (nukul), he becomes liable for the property (daman) but his hand is not cut (qat').
2
Understand the Dual Consequence
The thief's act (theft) triggers two potential outcomes: financial liability and hadd penalty. Nukul is effective only for the first.
3
Analyse the Analogical Proof (Qiyas)
The commentary compares nukul to testimony of one man and two women: in both, the evidence suffices for property but not for hudud. Identify the asl (testimony), far' (nukul), and illa (insufficient certainty for hadd).
4
Examine the Second Analogy (Confession Retraction)
A thief who confesses then retracts: hadd is dropped but liability remains. This reinforces that financial claims can survive weaker evidence.
5
Apply the Principle
Thus, in practice, if a thief refuses the oath, he must pay the value of the stolen property, but the hadd of amputation is not applied.
This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.

This passage presents a ruling from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani in al-Jami' al-Saghir regarding the thief's oath. While both sides agree that an oath is administered by consensus, they diverge on the consequence of refusal. Imam Abu Hanifa distinguishes between claims for limbs (where refusal leads to qisas) and claims for life (where refusal leads to imprisonment). The Sahibayn, on the other hand, hold that refusal always results in financial compensation (diyah). Understanding this dispute illuminates deeper principles about the nature of acknowledgment and the treatment of limbs versus life in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common ground
Both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree that in a qisas claim, the defendant is administered an oath by consensus. This is the starting point.
This consensus is mentioned in the Hidayah passage.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, if the defendant refuses the oath in a claim for qisas on limbs (below life), qisas is enforced. If the claim is for qisas of life, the defendant is imprisoned until he takes the oath or confesses.
Imprisonment is a coercive measure, not a penalty.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
The Sahibayn hold that in both cases, refusal leads to diyah (blood money) because they consider nukul an acknowledgment with ambiguity (shubha), which cannot establish qisas but does establish financial liability.
They treat limbs and life identically in this context.
4
Analyze the reasoning
Abu Hanifa's distinction is based on treating limbs like wealth (amwal), where one can voluntarily give up a limb (bazl). Life is different; one cannot voluntarily give up one's life. Therefore, for limbs, refusal is akin to allowing retaliation, but for life, imprisonment is used to compel the defendant to either swear or confess.
The example of cutting a hand for disease illustrates bazl.
5
Note the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: Under Abu Hanifa, a defendant who refuses the oath in a limb claim faces qisas (e.g., losing a limb), while under Sahibayn, he pays diyah instead. In a life claim, Abu Hanifa's ruling leads to imprisonment, while Sahibayn impose diyah immediately.
Later Hanafi practice often adopts the Sahibayn's view for life claims.

Someone claimed qisas against another, he denied it, then an oath will be administered. If he refuses the oath, then qisas is obligatory, and if he refuses the oath in qisas of life, then he will be imprisoned. He (the author of Hidayah) said: And whoever claims qisas against another and he denies it, the oath is administered by consensus. Then if he refuses the oath in what is less than life, qisas is incumbent upon him. And if he refuses in life, he will be imprisoned until he takes the oath or confesses. And this is according to Abu Hanifa. And they (the Sahibayn) said: in both cases, diyah is incumbent upon him, because refusal to take the oath is an acknowledgment with ambiguity according to them, so qisas is not established by it, and wealth (diyah) becomes obligatory. Especially when the prevention of qisas is due to a reason from the side of the one upon whom it is claimed, like if he confessed to killing by mistake while the guardian claims intentional killing. And according to Abu Hanifa, limbs are treated like wealth, so compensation (bazl) applies to them, unlike lives. For if someone said 'cut my hand' and he cut it, no guarantee (daman) is obligatory. This is an application of bazl, but it is not permissible due to lack of benefit. And this bazl is beneficial for ending the dispute, so it becomes like cutting a hand for a disease and pulling a tooth for pain. So when qisas is prevented in life, and the oath is a right established against him, he is imprisoned for it, as in qasamah. Translation: And whoever claimed qisas against another, then the defendant denied it, then by consensus the oath will be administered to the defendant. Then if the defendant refused the oath in less than life, qisas will be incumbent upon him. And if he refused the oath in qisas of life, the defendant will be imprisoned until he takes the oath or confesses. And this is according to Imam Abu Hanifa. And the Sahibayn said that in both cases, diyah will be incumbent upon the defendant. Because refusal of the oath is an acknowledgment with ambiguity according to the Sahibayn, so qisas will not be established by refusal of the oath.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on claims for qisas (retaliation) and the effect of the defendant's refusal to take the oath (nukul). The Hidayah states that by consensus, an oath is administered to the defendant in a qisas claim. Then the disagreement: According to Imam Abu Hanifa, if the defendant refuses the oath in a claim for qisas on limbs (below life), qisas is enforced. But if the claim is for qisas of life (murder), the defendant is imprisoned until he takes the oath or confesses. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that in both cases, the defendant must pay diyah (blood money) instead, because they consider refusal to take the oath as an acknowledgment (iqrar) but with ambiguity (shubha), so qisas (which requires certainty) is not established, but financial compensation (diyah) is due. The reasoning for Abu Hanifa's distinction: limbs are treated like wealth in that one can voluntarily give up a limb (bazl), so qisas is applied. For life, one cannot voluntarily give up life, so instead of enforcing qisas, the defendant is imprisoned to compel him to take the oath or confess. The passage also mentions an example where the defendant confesses to accidental killing while the claimant claims intentional, showing a situation where qisas is prevented and diyah might be due. The relied-upon position for fatwa is not stated here, but later Hanafi works often prefer the Sahibayn's view in such cases.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retributive punishment: retaliation for injury or killing, either like-for-like or blood money.
نکول (nukul): Refusal to take the oath after it is offered.
دیت (diyah): Blood money; financial compensation paid to the victim's heirs in cases of homicide or injury.
بذل (bazl): Voluntary surrender or offering; here, the idea that one can voluntarily consent to the loss of a limb.
