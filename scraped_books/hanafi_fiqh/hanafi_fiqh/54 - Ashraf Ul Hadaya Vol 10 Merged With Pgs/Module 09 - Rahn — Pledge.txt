Testimony Disagreement in Rahn (Pledge) Cases
Ruling on witness disagreement in rahn depending on claimant

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for witness disagreement in rahn cases based on who is the claimant and explain the underlying reasoning using the concept of 'useful claim' and the non-binding nature of rahn for the murtahin.

Core Idea
The pledgor's testimony rejected (no beneficial claim); pledgee's testimony accepted on lower amount (debt claim).

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling in rahn contracts when witnesses disagree on the debt amount. The outcome hinges on whether the pledgor (rahin) or pledgee (murtahin) is the claimant. We'll uncover why the pledgor's claim is considered 'useless' before debt repayment, while the pledgee's claim is treated as a pure debt claim, leading to acceptance on the lower amount. This demonstrates how legal capacity and the nature of the contract affect testimony.

Guided Path
1
Separate the two scenarios
Identify whether the rahin (pledgor) or the murtahin (pledgee) is the claimant. The ruling differs fundamentally.
The text explicitly treats each case.
2
Analyze the rahin's claim
Understand why the rahin's claim is considered non-beneficial: before debt payment, the rahin has no right to reclaim the pledged item, so his claim is useless. Testimony without a beneficial claim is rejected.
Key concept: da'wa mufida (beneficial claim).
3
Analyze the murtahin's claim
The murtahin can terminate the rahn contract anytime by returning the item, making the contract non-binding for him. Therefore, only the debt claim remains, and it is treated like a pure debt dispute.
The rahn contract is non-binding (ghayr lazim) for the murtahin.
4
Apply debt testimony rule
In debt claims, when witnesses disagree on the amount, the testimony is accepted on the lower amount (by earlier established Hanafi ruling).
This is a general rule for debt claims.
5
State the conclusion
For the rahin's claim: testimony rejected. For the murtahin's claim: testimony accepted on the lesser amount.
The underlying logic ensures consistency.

The sixth issue is about rahn (pledge). So if the rahin (pledgor) is the claimant, i.e., the rahin claimed that 'my such-and-such item is in pledge with so-and-so' and the murtahin (pledgee) denies it. Then the rahin presented witnesses, and one witness said that 'such item is pledged with so-and-so for a debt of one thousand' and another witness said that 'it is pledged for one thousand five hundred.' Then the rahin's testimony will definitely not be accepted. Because before the debt is paid, the rahin has no share in the pledged item. Hence, before payment of the debt, if the rahin wants to take back the pledged item, he has no right. So when before payment of the debt the rahin has no right to take back the pledged item, his claim is useless. And when his claim is useless, it is in the degree of non-existence, as if no claim was made. And when no claim was made, the testimony presented by the rahin remains without a claim. And it has passed earlier that without a claim, no testimony is accepted. Therefore, this testimony presented by the rahin will not be accepted. And if the murtahin is the claimant, i.e., the murtahin claimed that 'I have a right to such-and-such debt against so-and-so in exchange for the pledged item' and the rahin denies it. Then the murtahin presented witnesses, and the witnesses differed: one stated the amount of debt as one thousand, the other stated one thousand five hundred. Then on the lesser of the two amounts, i.e., one thousand, this testimony will be accepted. Its proof is that because the murtahin has at all times the option to break the rahn contract by returning the pledged item, so the rahn contract is non-binding for the murtahin. And when the rahn contract is non-binding for the murtahin, then the rahn contract is in the degree of non-existence. And from the murtahin's side, only the claim of a debt is valid. So the murtahin has claimed a debt against the rahin. And in the case of debt, with the witnesses' difference of one thousand and one thousand five hundred, the testimony is accepted on the lesser, i.e., one thousand, as has passed earlier.

Commentary
This passage discusses rahn (pledge) contracts. When the pledgor (rahin) claims the existence of the pledge but witnesses disagree on the debt amount, the testimony is rejected. The reasoning is that before debt repayment, the pledgor has no right to reclaim the item, so his claim is useless (ghayr mufid). Since there is no beneficial claim, the testimony is without a claim and thus rejected. However, when the pledgee (murtahin) claims the debt and demands payment, the rahn contract is non-binding for him (he can return the item anytime), so it is treated as a pure debt claim. Thus, the rules for debt testimony apply: if witnesses differ on the amount, the testimony is accepted on the lesser amount (by agreement of the scholars).

Key Terms
رہن (rahn): A contract where a debtor pledges an asset as security for a debt, which the creditor (murtahin) holds until repayment.
راہن (rahin): The pledgor/debtor who gives the asset as pledge.
مرتہن (murtahin): The pledgee/creditor who holds the asset until debt repayment.
