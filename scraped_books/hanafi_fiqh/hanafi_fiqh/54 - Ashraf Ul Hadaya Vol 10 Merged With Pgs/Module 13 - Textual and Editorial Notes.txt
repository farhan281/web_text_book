Textual Variant in Al-Hidayah: Majnun or Mahjur?
Clarifies the correct wording in the source text regarding the agent's condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct reading of the matn in al-Hidayah regarding the agent's condition and explain the legal distinction between 'majnun' and 'mahjur' in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The correct reading is 'mahjur' (restricted) not 'majnun' (insane); the latter is a scribal error.

This lesson examines a textual variant in the classic Hanafi work al-Hidayah. The original matn lists three categories: child, insane, and restricted. However, manuscript evidence and cross-references show that 'insane' is likely a mistake; the intended term is 'restricted'. Understanding this refinement is crucial for accurate reference and application of the school's foundational texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three terms in the matn
The matn mentions 'sabi' (child), 'majnun' (insane), and 'mahjur' (restricted). Note that the discussion in the surrounding text deals with restrictions on capacity.
2
Understand the legal context
The ruling applies to persons under legal interdiction (hajr): minors not permitted to trade, slaves with limited capacity, and those with intermittent lucidity. A continuously insane person has no legal capacity at all.
3
Examine the manuscript evidence
The margin of a manuscript of al-Hidayah gives 'mahjur' instead of 'majnun'. Allamah Nafi's al-Kafi reads 'sabi mahjur' (restricted child) or 'majbur'. This suggests a scribal error.
4
Conclude the correct reading
The correct term is 'mahjur'. If 'majnun' is retained, it must mean a person with lucid intervals (majnun yufiq) who understands the contract, aligning the ruling with that of a restricted person.

Taghleez in Oaths for Jews and Christians
Ruling with proof from Sunnah and Qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a judge must mention the scripture of a Jew or Christian when administering an oath, and explain the two proofs (hadith and qiyas) supporting this obligation.

Core Idea
A judge must mention the scripture of a Jew or Christian to make the oath binding and deter false swearing.

When a judge administers an oath to a non-Muslim litigant, the standard Muslim formula may not carry the same weight. The Hanafi school, drawing on the Prophet's practice and rational analogy, requires the judge to tailor the oath by mentioning the scripture revealed to that community's prophet. This lesson unpacks the evidence and application of this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? What is the hukm (legal classification)?
The act is 'administering an oath to a Jew or Christian'. The ruling is that the judge 'should mention their scripture' – this is wajib (obligatory).
2
Extract the First Proof: The Hadith
Locate the hadith of Abdullah bin Surya. What did the Prophet do and say? How does this prove the ruling?
The Prophet swore the Jew by the God who revealed the Torah, and later said he was reviving God's command. This indicates that such a formula is intended to be used.
3
Extract the Second Proof: The Qiyas
What rational basis is given? How does the respect Jews and Christians have for their prophets support the requirement?
The qiyas: because they revere their scriptures, mentioning them will deter false oaths more effectively than a generic formula.
4
Derive the Ruling from the Proofs
Combine the two proofs to state the complete Hanafi position: what must the judge mention, and for whom?
For a Jew, mention the Torah; for a Christian, mention the Gospel. The mention of the specific scripture is key.
5
Apply the Ruling in a Practical Scenario
Consider a judge who has to administer an oath to a Christian defendant. What exact words might the judge say, and why?
The judge might say: 'I swear you by the God who revealed the Gospel to Jesus' or similar. The exact wording is not fixed, but the scripture must be referenced.

This lesson examines a specific ruling: when an idol-worshiper (wathani) is involved, the oath must be taken only by Allah's name. A Muslim judge must not enter non-Muslim places of worship to administer an oath; the oath is given in court.

This ruling addresses a practical scenario in judicial procedure: where should a judge administer an oath when the litigant is a non-Muslim? The Hanafi school clearly prohibits the judge from going to the non-Muslim's place of worship for this purpose. In this lesson, we examine the ruling and its underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act is the judge administering an oath to a non-Muslim in the non-Muslim's place of worship. The ruling is that this is prohibited (haram).
The prohibition applies specifically to the judge entering their places of worship for this purpose.
2
Understand the reason (illah)
Why is it prohibited? Because the judge is generally prohibited from entering non-Muslim places of worship unless for necessity. Administering an oath there would imply honoring their rituals, which is not permissible.
The prohibition is based on avoiding anything that might be seen as endorsing falsehood.
3
Apply the ruling practically
In practice, the judge should administer the oath in the courtroom or another permissible location. The non-Muslim may be sworn in according to their customs, but not inside their temple.
The purpose of the oath is to assert truth, not to accommodate the witness's religious sensitivities.

No Obligation to Aggravate a Muslim's Oath by Time or Place
Clarifies that Muslim oaths require no mention of sacred time/place

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on taghleez al-yameen for Muslims and explain the rationale based on the purpose of oaths and the principle of removing hardship.

Core Idea
A Muslim's oath is valid and solemn without mentioning a sacred time or place—the glorification of Allah suffices.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies that a judge is not required to administer a Muslim's oath in a specific location (e.g., a mosque) or time (e.g., Friday). The text explains the reasoning: the oath's purpose is to glorify Allah, which is already achieved when a Muslim swears by His name. Requiring additional elements would impose hardship on the judge, which Islamic law seeks to remove.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Taghleez al-yameen is making an oath more solemn by adding conditions like time or place. The ruling is that it is NOT wajib (obligatory) for a Muslim's oath.
The ruling is a negative obligation (not required), not a prohibition.
2
Understand the purpose (maqsad) of the oath
The true purpose is ta'dhim (glorification) of Allah, the One sworn by. This is achieved simply by swearing by His name.
The text says 'المقصود تعظيم المقسم به'.
3
Apply the principle of removing hardship
Requiring taghleez would force the judge to arrange a blessed time/place, causing him difficulty. The Shari'ah removes such hardship (haraj).
The maxim 'الحرج مدفوع' (hardship is removed) is invoked.
4
Note the distinction with non-Muslims
The text implies that for non-Muslims, taghleez by scripture may be required, but for Muslims, time/place are not needed.
This distinction is made elsewhere in Hanafi fiqh.

Requiring additional elements would impose hardship on the judge, whereas the principle is that hardship is removed (رفع الحرج). The commentator cites Sahib al-Quduri to affirm that no such taghleez (aggravation) is wajib for Muslims.

Core Idea
A mukatab's possession is valid in litigation according to the Sahibayn, and this is the relied-upon view.

The possession (qabd) of a mukatab slave is a critical issue in Hanafi fiqh because it determines his standing in court. While Imam Abu Hanifa held that a mukatab's possession is not valid in litigation due to his incomplete freedom, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argued that his possession carries full evidentiary weight in legal disputes. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind the Sahibayn's position and its practical implications, which is the operative view in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and its parties
Recognize that this is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). The issue is whether the possession of a mukatab (a slave under a manumission contract) is valid in litigation.
The dispute is not between madhhabs but within the Hanafi school itself.
2
State each position and its basis
State Abu Hanifa's position: a mukatab's possession is invalid because he is still a slave and lacks full legal capacity. State the Sahibayn's position: his possession is valid because he is recognized as a legal person in financial matters, akin to a free person. Identify the underlying rationale—slaves permitted to trade (ma'dhun) are similarly treated.
The Sahibayn's view is based on practical necessity and analogy.
3

The Sahibayn provide a concrete example: if a free man and a mukatab both claim ownership of an item in their possession, and neither has witnesses, the judge divides the item equally. This shows that the court treats their possession equally. Similarly, if a third party holds the item and both produce witnesses, the item is still divided half each. This establishes that the mukatab's possession is given weight in litigation.
The ruling extends to all household goods and property.
4

The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the Sahibayn's view. This means that in practice, a mukatab can sue and be sued based on his possession, and his possession can defeat the claim of another person in court. Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah records this as the accepted view.
This position is from the zahir al-riwayah (the most authoritative transmission within the school).

The practical difference between the two views is that under Abu Hanifa, a mukatab's possession would be disregarded in court, making it difficult for him to defend his property. Under the Sahibayn, his possession is a valid basis for legal action, giving him procedural equality with free persons in financial disputes.
This aligns with the general Hanafi principle that possession is prima facie evidence of ownership unless rebutted.

Inter-madhhab dispute on defeating a lawsuit by proving property is held for an absent person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position that evidence of custodial possession repels litigation, and distinguish the conditions under which the lawsuit is or is not dismissed.

Core Idea
Proof of custodianship defeats the claim — possession is not adversarial.

In this passage, we examine a key rule in litigation procedure: if a defendant claims he holds disputed property for an absent person and provides evidence, does the plaintiff's lawsuit proceed? The Hanafi school says no, but early scholars like Ibn Shubruma and Ibn Abi Layla disagreed, and even within the Hanafi school there are nuances regarding the defendant's character and the specificity of witness identification.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what the Hanafi school holds: if the defendant produces evidence that the property is held for an absent person (as a trust, pawn, or usurpation), the lawsuit is dismissed because his hand is not a litigant hand.
The ruling applies to deposit, pawn, usurpation, and also rental.
2
Identify the opposing views
Ibn Shubruma argued the evidence cannot benefit the absent person due to his absence, so the lawsuit should not be dismissed. Ibn Abi Layla argued that the defendant, being in possession, is already a litigant and must prove his claim with evidence; the lawsuit is not dismissed without that evidence.
Both opponents are from the early Kufan tradition.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response splits the purpose of evidence: it proves custody (which repels the plaintiff's claim) while not proving ownership for the absent party (since he is not present). This is compared to a woman's agent who produces evidence of divorce.
The response uses a two-pronged analysis: one aim fails, the other succeeds.
4

Abu Yusuf added that if the defendant is known for tricks, the lawsuit is not repelled. Also, if the witnesses know the depositor only by face (not name), Abu Hanifa says the lawsuit is dismissed, but Muhammad disagrees.
These nuances show the Hanafi school's practical concern for preventing fraud.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical difference: under the Hanafi view, a plaintiff must sue the absent person instead; under the opposing views, the lawsuit against the defendant continues.
This affects who bears the burden of seeking the absent person.

Ruling on Possessor's Claim of Ownership by an Absent Person
Inter-madhhab dispute on litigation removal based on possessor's claim to absent owner.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on when a possessor's claim that property belongs to an absent person removes litigation, distinguish it from the views of Ibn Shubruma and Ibn Abi Layla, and apply the qiyas from agency to justify the Hanafi rule.

Core Idea
Evidence (bayyinah) removes litigation even if ownership for an absent person is not proven; mere acknowledgment is insufficient.

This lesson examines a key procedural issue in Hanafi litigation law: can a possessor who claims property belongs to an absent person avoid being sued? The Hanafi school, responding to rival scholars, develops a nuanced rule requiring evidence, not mere acknowledgment, and later adds a safeguard against trickery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Recognize that the disputed question is whether a defendant (possessor) who claims the disputed property belongs to an absent person can be removed from litigation by providing evidence or mere acknowledgment.
The plaintiff sues the possessor; the possessor claims he holds for an absent owner.
2
State the Hanafi position and its reasoning
The Hanafi school holds that if the possessor produces evidence (bayyinah) that his possession is non-adversarial (e.g., he is a trustee for an absent owner), then litigation is removed. The evidence is accepted to remove the possessor's own litigation status, even if it does not prove the absent person's ownership.
Key point: the possessor is a litigant due to his apparent possession; his evidence serves to negate his own litigation.
3
Analyze the opposing views
Ibn Shubruma argues that even with evidence, litigation is not removed because ownership for an absent person cannot be proven without a representative. Ibn Abi Layla argues that mere acknowledgment suffices, since acknowledgment is itself proof.
These views challenge different aspects: Ibn Shubruma attacks the effect of evidence; Ibn Abi Layla attacks the need for evidence at all.
4
Examine the Hanafi rebuttals
Against Ibn Shubruma: the evidence serves two purposes – it attempts to prove ownership (which fails) and removes the possessor's litigation (which succeeds). This is analogized to a wife's evidence that nullifies her husband's agent's authority without proving divorce. Against Ibn Abi Layla: the possessor's acknowledgment is self-serving (shifting liability) and therefore suspect; it is not accepted without proof, like a claim of hawalah (debt transfer).
The qiyas (analogy) is from the chapter of agency: the wife's evidence ends the agent's authority even without proving divorce.
5
Note the later view of Imam Abu Yusuf
Imam Abu Yusuf's later view adds a qualification: if the possessor is known for trickery (hila baazi), his evidence is not accepted and litigation continues. This prevents fraudulent schemes where a debtor colludes with a traveler to shield assets.
This intra-Hanafi nuance affects the mufta bihi position: evidence removes litigation only if the possessor is trustworthy.

Book of Claims: There will be no litigation between the claimant and the possessor. That is, the judge will not hear the claimant's lawsuit and will not demand evidence etc. from him. The reason for all these cases is that the defendant (possessor) has proven through evidence that his possession is not one of litigation. And a person who is like that, i.e., proves that his possession is not a possession of litigation, then he is not a litigant. Therefore, this possessor will also not be a litigant. Ibn Shubruma, i.e., Abdullah, the Qadi of Kufa, said: Even if the defendant (possessor) produces evidence, the litigation will not be removed, rather he will be the claimant's litigant and the judge will hear his case. His evidence is that the possessor has proven the ownership of the absent person through evidence, whereas proving ownership for an absent person is impossible. Because the possessor is not a litigant on behalf of the absent person since the absent person did not appoint him as an agent to prove ownership for himself. So since the absent person did not appoint the possessor as an agent to prove ownership for himself, this possessor will not be a litigant on behalf of the absent person. And when the possessor is not a litigant on behalf of the absent person, then proof of ownership for the absent person occurs without a litigant, and proof of ownership for an absent person without a litigant is impossible, because no one has the right to enter something into someone's ownership without his consent. In any case, it is established that in this place ownership is not proven for the absent person, and the defendant (possessor) based the removal of litigation on the proof of ownership for the absent person. So since things based on the impossible are also impossible, therefore the removal of litigation from the possessor will also be impossible. Our reply to this evidence is that the evidence presented by the possessor requires two things: 1) Ownership for the absent person is proven, but since there is no litigant in this, ownership for the absent person will not be proven. Second, that the litigation is removed from the defendant i.e., the possessor, and in this matter the defendant himself is a litigant. Therefore, the removal of litigation will be established in favor of the defendant (possessor). An analogy for this is that a person comes as an agent on behalf of a woman's husband to transfer the woman to her husband. Then the woman presents evidence that he divorced me. Even if this woman's evidence does not prove the divorce until the absent husband comes, it will be accepted to terminate the agent's right. Thus after this evidence, the agent will not be authorized to transfer the woman. The full details of this analogy have passed in the chapter of agency in litigation. Qadi Ibn Abi Layla said: The litigation will be removed by the mere acknowledgment of the defendant (possessor) that this slave belongs to such an absent person, even if he did not establish evidence. In summary, according to us, litigation is not removed without establishing evidence, but according to Ibn Abi Layla, litigation will be removed without establishing evidence. The evidence of Ibn Abi Layla is that the defendant (possessor) has acknowledged the ownership of the absent person, and acknowledgment itself proves. Therefore, by the possessor's acknowledgment, it is established that the owner of the slave is an absent person, but the possessor's possession is for safekeeping. And when the absent person's ownership is proven by mere acknowledgment, there is no need to present evidence. In any case, when the absent person's ownership is proven without evidence, then litigation is also removed from the possessor without evidence. The author of al-Hidayah replied to this saying: The possessor, due to his apparent possession, has become a litigant. So by his verbal acknowledgment, he wants to shift the right that is apparently incumbent upon him from his own person to the absent person. Therefore, his acknowledgment is suspect, and the word of a suspect person is not confirmed without proof. Thus, the word of this possessor will not be confirmed without proof, just as if a person wanted to transfer a debt from his liability to another's. For example, he said: I have transferred the claim of this debt to such a person. Then the word of this transferor is not confirmed without proof. Similarly here, the word of the possessor will not be confirmed without proof. The later view of Imam Abu Yusuf is that if the possessor is a righteous and good person, then his ruling is what we have stated, i.e., upon establishing evidence, litigation will be removed from him. And if this person is notorious for trickery, then litigation will not be removed from him even if he establishes evidence, as Ibn Shubruma said. Because a person who is a trickster sometimes secretly gives his property to a traveler, then the traveler openly deposits it with him and appoints witnesses to it. He does this trick so that the right of the other person (the claimant) is nullified and it becomes impossible for him to prove his right even through evidence. So when the judge considers the possessor suspect of trickery, he will not accept proof from him. The scenario is that, for example, a person is known for trickery and deceit, and others have rights and debts upon him. So the rightful creditors will claim against his property to recover their rights. Hence he devises this trick: he secretly gives his property to a traveler, and the traveler, after gathering witnesses, openly deposits it with him or pledges it to him. By this, the benefit is that the traveler leaves. But when creditors claim against him, this trickster will present those witnesses, saying, 'This is not my property; rather, such an absent person deposited it with me.' So he will escape the litigation of the creditors. Imam Abu Yusuf said: When this possessor is well-known for trickery, the judge will not remove litigation from him due to this suspicion, even if he establishes evidence. It should be clear that this ruling applies when the witnesses have identified the traveler by name and lineage. And if the witnesses said that a person whom they do not recognize by appearance or name has deposited with this possessor, then by consensus litigation will not be repelled from the possessor. The evidence is that it is very possible that the depositor is the same claimant but the witnesses did not recognize him. The second evidence is that the possessor has not referred the claimant to a specific person that the claimant could seek or pursue. So if litigation is removed from the possessor, the claimant will be harmed by this.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Claims (Kitab al-Da'wa) of Ashraf al-Hidaya discusses a specific scenario: when a defendant (possessor, qabid) claims that the property in his possession actually belongs to an absent person (gha'ib). The central question is whether this claim removes the defendant's status as a litigant (khasm), thereby barring the plaintiff's lawsuit. The author begins by stating the Hanafi position: if the possessor produces evidence (bayyinah) that his possession is not adversarial (i.e., he holds it for an absent owner), then litigation is removed. The judge will not hear the plaintiff's claim. The reasoning is that by proving his possession is non-adversarial, the possessor ceases to be a litigant. Then the author presents the opposing view of Ibn Shubruma (Abdullah, Qadi of Kufa). Ibn Shubruma argues that even if the possessor produces evidence, litigation is not removed; the possessor remains a litigant. His evidence is that the absent person's ownership cannot be proven because the possessor is not an agent for the absent person to establish ownership. Since proving ownership for an absent person is impossible without a representative, the possessed property cannot be attributed to the absent person. Thus, the possessor's claim of holding for an absent owner fails, and he remains a litigant. The author then provides the Hanafi response: the possessor's evidence serves two purposes. First, it attempts to prove ownership for the absent person, but since there is no litigant representing the absent person, that part fails. Second, it aims to remove the possessor himself from litigation, and for that purpose the possessor is a litigant on his own behalf. Therefore, the removal of litigation from the possessor is established. The author supports this with an analogy from agency: if a wife presents evidence that her absent husband divorced her, the divorce is not proven until the husband appears, but the evidence is accepted to nullify the agent's authority. Similarly, the possessor's evidence is accepted to remove his own litigation status. Next, the passage presents the view of Qadi Ibn Abi Layla: mere acknowledgment (iqrar) by the possessor that the property belongs to an absent person is sufficient to remove litigation, even without evidence. His reasoning is that acknowledgment itself is proof. The author of al-Hidayah (Sahib al-Hidayah) refutes this: the possessor, due to his apparent possession, is already a litigant. His acknowledgment is self-serving because it shifts liability from himself to an absent person. Therefore, his word is suspect and not accepted without proof. The author draws an analogy to someone who claims to have transferred a debt (hawalah) to another person; such a claim is not accepted without proof. Finally, the passage ends with the later view of Imam Abu Yusuf, which adds a nuance: if the possessor is a trustworthy, righteous person, then upon producing evidence, litigation is removed as per the Hanafi position. However, if he is known for trickery (hila baazi), then litigation is not removed even if he produces evidence. Imam Abu Yusuf explains the possible trick: the person might secretly transfer his own property to a traveler, who then openly deposits it back with him and appoints witnesses. This is done to defraud creditors. The judge, suspecting trickery, will not accept the evidence. Additionally, if the witnesses do not identify the depositor by name or appearance, litigation is not removed by consensus, because the depositor could be the plaintiff himself, and there is no specific person for the plaintiff to pursue.

Key Terms
خصم (khasm): A litigant or party to a lawsuit. In this passage, it refers to the person against whom a claim is made and who has standing to defend the case.
قابض (qabid): The possessor or holder of the property. In this context, the defendant who is physically holding the disputed item.
بينه (bayyinah): Legal evidence, typically the testimony of two male witnesses or one male and two females. Here it refers to any valid proof that establishes a fact in court.
غائب (gha'ib): An absent person who is not present in court. The possessor claims that the property belongs to this absent person.
حيله (hila): A legal stratagem or trick. In this passage, it refers to a deceptive practice where a debtor colludes with a traveler to shield property from creditors.
