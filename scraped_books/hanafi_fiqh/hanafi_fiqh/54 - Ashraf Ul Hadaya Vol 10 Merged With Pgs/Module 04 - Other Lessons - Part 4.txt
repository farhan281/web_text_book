Oath in Pure Lineage: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath in pure lineage claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding oath administration in pure lineage claims and apply the ruling to scenarios distinguishing when the oath is administered.

Core Idea
In pure lineage, Abu Hanifa denies the oath; the Sahibayn allow it only if acknowledgment would establish the lineage.

This passage explores an intra-Hanafi dispute on a procedural point: whether a qadi can administer an oath to a defendant in a claim of pure lineage (khali nasab). While Imam Abu Hanifa holds that pure lineage claims are not subject to the oath procedure, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) allow it under a specific condition: the lineage must be such that the defendant's simple acknowledgment would establish it. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying Hanafi law in lineage cases.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Clearly articulate the view of Abu Hanifa (no oath in pure lineage) and the Sahibayn (oath is administered if acknowledgment would establish lineage).
2
Identify the Sahibayn's key principle
Recognize that for the Sahibayn, refusal to take an oath (nukul) is treated as an acknowledgment (iqrar). This is the doctrinal basis for their position.
This principle is stated in the passage.
3
Apply the condition to examples
Work through the examples given: claims of father/son (oath allowed), brother (oath not allowed), woman's father (oath allowed), woman's son (oath not allowed). Identify why each falls on one side.
The key is whether acknowledging the lineage forces affiliation to a third party.
4
Contrast with claims of mawla/husband
Note that the Hidayah adds that claims of mawla (master) and husband/wife do not involve third-party lineage linking, so acknowledgment is valid and oath is administered.
This confirms the distinction.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
State the practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, a pure lineage claim fails without evidence; under Sahibayn, it may succeed if the defendant refuses the oath.
Later Hanafi practice often follows Sahibayn for judicial decisions.

According to Imam Abu Hanifa, istikhlaaf (administering the oath) does not operate in pure lineage (khali nasab), but rather in that lineage where along with lineage a claim of right from another is also made, as has passed in detail above. However, according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), istikhlaaf operates in pure lineage, i.e., in the case of a mere claim of lineage, the oath is administered to the defendant. But according to the Sahibayn, the oath will be administered to the defendant in pure lineage only when the lineage would be established by the mere acknowledgment of the defendant, because it has passed earlier that according to the Sahibayn, refusal of the oath is an acknowledgment. Therefore, every lineage that is established by the acknowledgment of the defendant will also be established by the defendant's refusal to take the oath. For example, in the case of a man, the acknowledgment of father and son, such as Khalid claimed against Shahid: 'He is my father' or 'He is my son', and did not claim any other thing like property. Then if the claimant Khalid could not produce evidence, the oath will be administered to the defendant Shahid. If the defendant takes the oath, lineage will not be established, and if the defendant refuses to take the oath, lineage will be established. This is because if the claimant Khalid claimed lineage and the defendant Shahid initially acknowledged the lineage, the lineage would be established. So since this lineage is established by acknowledgment, it will also be established by refusal of the oath. On the contrary, if Khalid claimed that Shahid is my brother, then the oath will not be administered to the defendant Shahid, because in this case, if Shahid explicitly acknowledged that Khalid is my brother, the lineage would not be established because it would necessitate attributing lineage to a third party, i.e., Khalid said that Shahid is my father's son, and this is not permissible. The summary is that where it is not necessary to attribute lineage to another, there the oath will be administered to the defendant in a pure lineage claim, and where it is necessary, it will not. Like the claim of a man being a father or son. And like the claim of a woman's father: if A'rif claimed against a woman that 'I am her father' and the woman denied this claim, and the claimant had no evidence, then the oath will be administered to the defendant (the woman). Then if the woman refuses to take the oath, lineage will be established, because if this woman acknowledged that A'rif is my father, the lineage would be established by that acknowledgment. Therefore, since she refused the oath, lineage will be established from that as well. And if A'rif claimed that I am the son of this woman, and the woman denied, and the claimant could not produce evidence, then the oath will not be administered to this woman. This is because if this woman acknowledged that A'rif is my son, the lineage would not be established by that acknowledgment, because in this acknowledgment it is necessary to attribute lineage to a third party, i.e., the husband. That is, the meaning of the woman saying 'A'rif is my son' is that A'rif is my husband's son, and his lineage is established from him. And it has passed earlier that where it is found that attributing lineage to a third party is necessary, since explicit acknowledgment does not establish lineage, therefore the oath will also not be administered to the defendant there. And the Hidayah said that the acknowledgment of mawla (master) and husband is valid for both man and woman because in it it is not necessary to attribute lineage to a third party. For example, a woman said about a man: 'He is my husband' or 'mawla', and the mawla or husband denied it, and the claimant woman had no evidence, then the oath will be administered to the mawla or husband. If he refuses to take the oath, then being a mawla or husband will be established, just as being a mawla or husband is established by the acknowledgment of the mawla or husband. Similarly, if a man said: 'This is my mawla' or 'This woman is my wife'. Then if the defendant acknowledges, then being a mawla or wife is established, and similarly if the defendant refuses to take the oath, then being a mawla or wife will also be established.

Commentary
This passage details the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding whether an oath can be administered in a claim of pure lineage (khali nasab) i.e., lineage with no accompanying financial claim. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, istikhlaaf (administering the oath) is not valid in pure lineage claims; it is only valid when lineage is claimed along with another right (like maintenance). The Sahibayn hold that istikhlaaf is valid in pure lineage claims, but only if the lineage would be established by the defendant's explicit acknowledgment. They consider refusal to take the oath equivalent to acknowledgment (nukul iqrar). The passage provides examples: a claim of fatherhood or sonship by a man (e.g., 'this is my father' or 'this is my son') – since the defendant's acknowledgment would establish the lineage without implicating a third party, the oath is administered and refusal establishes lineage. In contrast, a claim of brotherhood would implicate the father, so acknowledgment does not establish lineage, and therefore the oath is not administered. Similarly, a claim that a man is a woman's father: the woman's acknowledgment would establish lineage without third party, so oath is administered. But a claim that a man is a woman's son would implicate her husband, so acknowledgment does not establish lineage, and oath is not administered. The passage concludes with claims of mawla (master) and husband/wife: these relationships do not involve attributing lineage to a third party, so acknowledgment is valid and oath refusal establishes the relationship. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is not explicitly stated here, but typically in practice, the Sahibayn's view is given weight in judicial matters.

Key Terms
خالی نسب (khali nasab): A claim of lineage without any accompanying financial or other right.
نکول (nukul): Refusal to take an oath after it is offered to the defendant.

Swearing a Magian: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Presents two views on how to swear a Magian (Zoroastrian)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on swearing a Magian and explain the reasoning behind the preferred mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school differs: mention fire for Magians or not? The preferred view avoids glorifying fire.

When a Magian (fire-worshipper) appears in court, how should the judge administer an oath? The Hanafi school has multiple views, reflecting a tension between the practical goal of deterring perjury and the theological concern of glorifying created things. In this lesson, we examine the positions of Imam Muhammad, Abu Hanifa, and al-Khassaf, and see why later preferred practice chose to swear Magians only by Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
List the three views: (1) Imam Muhammad: swear by 'Allah who created the fire'; (2) Abu Hanifa: swear only by Allah; (3) al-Khassaf: for Jews/Christians, additional wording allowed; for all others (including Magians), only by Allah.
2
Understand the dalil for each
Imam Muhammad relies on Magian reverence for fire to deter false oaths (istihsan). Abu Hanifa insists on avoiding glorification of anything alongside Allah. Al-Khassaf distinguishes honored scriptures (Torah, Gospel) from fire, which should not be glorified.
This is a classic example of talil (causative reasoning) in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Trace the tarjih (preference)
The commentator states that al-Khassaf's view is the choice (ikhtiyar) of some later mashayikh, making it mufta bihi. The reasoning is that glorifying fire is inappropriate, while glorifying divine scriptures is acceptable.
The tarjih is based on a balance between purpose and principle.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Imam Muhammad, the judge says 'By Allah who created the fire'; under Abu Hanifa and the preferred view, only 'By Allah' for Magians. For Jews/Christians, al-Khassaf allows additional wording like 'By Allah who revealed the Torah'.
This affects the actual court procedure.
5
Apply the reasoning to a new scenario
Consider a Sabian (another non-Muslim group). Which position would apply? Extend the logic: if the group reveres a created entity, the same principle applies—swear only by Allah unless the entity is a revealed scripture.
This tests your ability to extrapolate the illa (effective cause).

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the formula for swearing a Magian (fire-worshipper). Imam Muhammad's position (recorded in al-Asl and al-Mabsut) is that the judge should say 'By Allah who created the fire', because the Magian reveres fire and this wording will deter him from perjury. Abu Hanifa's position is that no one should be sworn except by Allah alone, without adding any created entity, to avoid giving reverence to anything besides Allah. Al-Khassaf differentiates: for Jews and Christians, other attributes (like their scripture) may be mentioned due to the honor of those books, but for all others, including Magians, only the name of Allah is used. The commentator notes that some of our later scholars (mutakhkhirin) prefer al-Khassaf's view. The rationale is that glorifying fire is impermissible, whereas glorifying divine scriptures is acceptable. Thus the mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is that Magians are sworn only by Allah, not with mention of fire.

Dispute between Master and Mukatab over Kitabah Payment
Ruling on whether mutual oath is required when master and mukatab dispute the kitabah amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Imam Abu Hanifa rejects mutual oath (tahāluf) in a kitabah payment dispute and distinguish his reasoning from that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
In a kitabah payment dispute, the mukatab is a denier, not a claimant—so only his oath counts, not mutual oath.

The contract of kitabah (manumission by payment) is unique: it grants the slave the right to transact immediately, with freedom only upon final payment. When master and mukatab dispute the amount owed, the question arises: should they take mutual oaths as in a sale? The Hanafi school is divided, with Abu Hanifa offering a distinctive analysis based on who is the claimant and who is the denier. This lesson unpacks both proofs and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
State the issue and the two Hanafi positions
Identify the precise dispute: the amount of kitabah payment is contested. Abu Hanifa says no mutual oath; mukatab's statement with oath accepted. Sahibayn say mutual oath is required and contract rescinded.
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i agrees with the Sahibayn.
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's qiyas to sale
The Sahibayn analogize kitabah to a sale: both are commutative contracts that accept rescission. The common cause (illa) is that each party makes a claim the other denies. Therefore, mutual oath is required as in a sale.
This is a formal qiyas; identify the asl (sale), far' (kitabah), and illa (mutual claims/denials).
3
Examine Abu Hanifa's rebuttal
Abu Hanifa distinguishes kitabah: the payment is for the removal of the impediment to transactions, which is already obtained by the mukatab upon contract. Thus, the benefit is already received, and the dispute is only over the amount of payment. The slave does not claim freedom yet; he merely denies the master's claim of a higher amount.
Key point: the consideration is not freedom itself but the removal of the impediment, which is already realized.
4
Determine claimant and denier
Because the slave denies the excess, he is the denier (munkir) and the master is the claimant (mudda'i). In a claimant-denier scenario, mutual oath is not required; the denier's statement with oath is accepted. This is the foundation of Abu Hanifa's ruling.
Note the general principle: when one is claimant and the other denier, the denier's statement prevails with oath.
5
Apply the rules of evidence
If either party has evidence (bayyinah), that evidence is accepted. If both have evidence, the master's evidence is given priority because he is the claimant and his evidence supports his claim. If neither has evidence, the mukatab's oath is decisive.
The passage concludes with this practical rule, showing the interplay of evidence and oath.

Explanation of the issue: If the master and the mukatab dispute the amount of the kitabah payment, then according to Imam Abu Hanifa, mutual oath (tahāluf) is not obligatory upon both; rather, the statement of the mukatab with his oath is accepted. That is, just as a sale is a commutative contract that accepts annulment, similarly kitabah is a commutative contract that accepts rescission. The common effective cause between them is that just as a buyer and seller dispute the amount of the price—the seller claims a higher price and the buyer denies it, and the buyer claims entitlement to the sold item for the price he stated while the seller denies it—similarly, the master claims a higher kitabah payment and the mukatab denies it, and the mukatab claims entitlement to freedom from his master upon payment of the amount he claims while the master denies it. The proof of Imam Abu Hanifa is that the kitabah payment is in exchange for the removal of the impediment that the mukatab has regarding transactions etc. The removal of the impediment to transactions is obtained by the mukatab despite the dispute over the kitabah payment, because both the master and mukatab agree on the establishment of kitabah. In a case where there is one claimant and one denier, mutual oath is not obligatory; rather, the statement of the denier with his oath is accepted. Therefore, here mutual oath is not obligatory; rather, the statement of the mukatab, who is the denier, with his oath is accepted. This ruling is when both have no evidence. If one of them presents evidence, his evidence is accepted. If both present evidence, the master's evidence is given priority.

So he, through his verbal admission, wants to transfer the right that apparently lies upon him from himself to the missing person. Therefore, he is suspect in this admission, and the statement of a suspect person is not accepted without evidence. Thus, the statement of this possessor will also not be accepted without evidence, just as if a person wanted to transfer a debt from himself to another by hawala. For example, he said, 'I had transferred the claim of this debt to so-and-so.' Then the statement of this transferor is not accepted without evidence. Similarly here, the statement of the possessor will not be accepted without evidence. The later opinion of Imam Abu Yusuf is that if the possessor is a righteous and good person, then his ruling is as we stated: upon establishing bayyinah, the litigation is removed from him. But if this person is notorious for trickery, then the litigation will not be removed from him, even if he establishes bayyinah, as Ibn Shubruma said. Because a trickster sometimes secretly gives his property to a traveler so that the traveler openly deposits it with him and appoints witnesses, and he does this trick so that the right of the other (the claimant) becomes nullified, and it becomes impossible for him to prove his right through bayyinah. So when the judge considers the possessor suspect of trickery, he will not accept the proof from him. The case is that the possessor is known for trickery and deception and debts are due from him. So the creditors claim their rights from his property. He then devises this trick: he secretly gives his property to a traveler, and the traveler calls witnesses and openly deposits it with him or pawns it. The benefit is that the traveler leaves. When the creditors claim, this trickster produces those witnesses saying, 'This is not my property, but so-and-so (the missing traveler) deposited it with me.' Thus he escapes the litigation of the creditors. Imam Abu Yusuf stated that when this possessor is known and famous for trickery, the judge will not remove the litigation from him due to this suspicion, even if the possessor establishes bayyinah. Note that this ruling applies when the witnesses identified the traveler by name and lineage. If the witnesses said that a person deposited it with this possessor whom they do not recognize by face or name and lineage, then by consensus the litigation is not repelled from the possessor. The proof is that it is quite possible that the depositor is the claimant himself, but the witnesses did not recognize him. The second proof is that the possessor did not refer the claimant to a specific person that the claimant could search for. So if the litigation is removed from the possessor, the claimant would be harmed. And if the witnesses said that they recognize the depositor by face but not by name and lineage, then according to Imam Muhammad, due to the second proof, the same ruling applies: the litigation is not repelled. But according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the litigation will be removed. Because the possessor has proven through witnesses that this slave (the property) reached him from someone other than the claimant. Indeed, the witnesses recognize him by face, unlike the first case where it was not proven that it came from someone other than the claimant. So in the second case, the possession of the possessor (the defendant) is not a possession of litigation but a possession of custody, and that is the objective. As for harming the claimant, the answer is that the claimant harmed himself by forgetting his defendant, or others harmed him—in any case, the defendant did not cause harm. The author of Hidayah says that this issue is famous in Kitab al-Da'wa by the title 'Muhimmah' (difficult case), and five opinions have been mentioned under it. They are: (1) Ibn Shubruma's, (2) Ibn Abi Layla's, (3) Imam Abu Yusuf's, (4) Imam Muhammad's, (5) Imam Abu Hanifa's.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute. The author of Hidayah explains why a possessor who claims property is a trust from a missing person is not believed without evidence. He draws an analogy with hawala (transfer of debt): just as a person claiming to have transferred a debt is not believed without proof, similarly the possessor is suspect because he wants to shift liability. Then Imam Abu Yusuf's later opinion is given, distinguishing between a trustworthy and a trickster possessor. A vivid example of trickery is provided. Next, the ruling depends on whether witnesses identified the depositor. If they did not identify at all, by consensus the possessor remains liable. If they know the depositor by face but not name, Imam Muhammad holds the same, while Imam Abu Hanifa removes liability because possession is proven to be custodial. The author notes that this issue is known as 'Muhimmah' and lists the five famous opinions.

Defendant as Proper Party: Admission of Ownership vs. Alleged Wrongful Act
Distinguishing when a defendant becomes a proper party in property claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when a defendant in a property claim becomes a proper party (khasm) based on admission of ownership versus an alleged wrongful act, and apply the ruling to scenario-based questions.

Core Idea
A defendant admits being a proper party if he claims ownership; but if sued for a wrongful act, his trust claim does not remove him as a defendant.

This passage from al-Hidayah clarifies a critical distinction in Hanafi procedural law: who can be sued in a property dispute? The answer depends on whether the plaintiff's claim is about ownership or about a wrongful act. The defendant's own admission of ownership makes him the proper party, whereas if he is accused of theft or usurpation, his claim of being a trustee does not free him from the lawsuit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, the defendant says 'I bought it from a missing person' (admission of ownership). Second, the plaintiff says 'You usurped/stole it from me' (alleged wrongful act).
The defendant's response differs based on the nature of the plaintiff's claim.
2
Determine khasm status in each
In the first scenario, the defendant is a proper party (khasm) because he admitted ownership. In the second, the defendant remains a proper party even if he proves the item was a deposit, because he is sued for the act, not for possession.
The ruling does not depend on the truth of the defendant's claim but on the basis of the lawsuit.
3
Understand the reasoning
The defendant's claim of purchase admits his hand is a hand of ownership (yad milk), making him a khasm in ownership claims. For wrongful acts, he becomes a khasm due to the alleged act itself, irrespective of possession.
The proof is that a claim of absolute ownership is only valid against a possessor, whereas a claim of a wrongful act is valid against any alleged perpetrator.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It clarifies the status of a defendant in a property claim. The Arabic matn states: If the defendant says 'I bought it from a missing person,' he becomes a proper defendant because he admits ownership. But if the plaintiff says 'you usurped/stole it from me,' the defendant's claim of trust does not remove litigation, regardless of evidence. The reasoning: the defendant became a defendant due to the alleged act, not due to possession. For absolute ownership claims, the defendant is sued because of possession; for wrongful act claims, the defendant is sued because of the act, and possession is irrelevant. The commentary emphasizes that the defendant's admission of ownership makes him the rightful party, and the judge can hear the case. This ruling is foundational for understanding who can be sued in property disputes.

Key Terms
خصم (khasm): A party to a lawsuit; a defendant who is legally competent to be sued in the claim.
ید ملک (yad milk): A hand of ownership; possession that indicates ownership, as opposed to mere custody.
