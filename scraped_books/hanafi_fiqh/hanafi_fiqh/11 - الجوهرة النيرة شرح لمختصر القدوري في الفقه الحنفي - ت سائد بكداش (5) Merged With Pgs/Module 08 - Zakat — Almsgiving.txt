Maintenance of Minor Child Obligatory on Father Despite Religious Difference
Ruling with proof from Quran and analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a father must maintain his minor child regardless of religious difference, and cite the Quranic proof and the analogical reasoning supporting it.

Core Idea
Religious difference between parent and child does not cancel the father's duty to provide maintenance.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on Al-Quduri's statement about child maintenance. It clarifies a key Hanafi principle: the obligation of nafaqah is based on the relationship (paternity), not on shared religion. The author provides examples involving dhimmi parents and even an apostate child, and grounds the rule in a Quranic verse about kind treatment of parents.

Guided Path
1
Pinpoint the core ruling
Identify exactly what is obligated and on whom. The father (and grandfather) must financially support the minor child. This is wajib when the child has no wealth.
The grandfather's obligation is secondary.
2
Apply the condition
Note the condition: the child must be minor (saghir) and have no personal wealth. If the child is wealthy or self-sufficient, the father is not obliged.
The text explicitly says 'if he has no wealth'.
3
Test the religious difference scenario
Consider the example: a dhimmi couple, wife converts to Islam, child follows her in Islam. The father remains non-Muslim but still must pay maintenance. Similarly, if a minor apostatizes (valid per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), the father must still support him.
This shows that religious difference does not negate the duty.
4
Find the proof
The text cites Surah Luqman 31:15 for the obligation to support parents even if they are disbelievers. The same logic extends to children: the duty of maintenance is based on the relationship, not religion.
The analogy with wife maintenance also supports this.

Core Idea
The Prophet explicitly excluded consuming Magian slaughter — not all monotheists' meat is lawful.

This lesson examines a fundamental principle of halal slaughter: the slaughterer must be a monotheist (Muslim or Kitabi). The text from Al-Jawhara al-Nayira clarifies that three categories — Magian, apostate, and idolater — are excluded because they lack a valid monotheistic religion. The proof is a direct prophetic instruction that makes an exception for Magians in marriage and slaughter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The act of eating the slaughtered meat of a Magian, apostate, or idol worshipper. The ruling is that it is forbidden (haram).
The text states: 'ولا تؤكل ذبيحة المجوسي، والمرتد، والوثني' — the slaughter is not eaten.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The commentator cites a hadith for the Magian: 'Treat them as the People of the Book, except do not marry their women nor eat their slaughter.' This is a command of prohibition.
The hadith has various chains; the Hanafi school accepts it as valid proof.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (aspect of evidence)
The explicit phrase 'ولا آكلي ذبائحهم' (do not eat their slaughter) is a direct prohibition. In Hanafi usul, commands of prohibition indicate haram unless proven otherwise.
4
Note the cross-madhhab ikhtilaf (disagreement)
Though the Hanafi school relies on this hadith, other schools may consider it weak or interpret it differently. This is a legitimate scholarly difference.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in the madhhab.
5

The passage also outlines that a valid slaughterer must be a monotheist (Muslim or Kitabi), understand tasmiyah, and be able to control the slaughter. Those who do not meet these conditions (like a child who doesn't understand) produce carrion.
This expands the ruling: the prohibition extends to any slaughterer without valid milla.

Ruling on Intentionally or Forgetfully Omitting Tasmiyah
Legal effect of omitting the tasmiyah at the time of slaughter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on omitting tasmiyah during slaughter and distinguish between intentional and forgetful omission.

Core Idea
Intentional omission of tasmiyah invalidates slaughter; forgetfulness is excused.

In this lesson, we examine a passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra that clarifies the legal effect of leaving out the tasmiyah—saying 'Bismillah Allahu Akbar'—during slaughter. The Hanafi school treats tasmiyah as a condition (shart) for the meat to be permissible. However, the ruling distinguishes between a deliberate omission (which renders the animal carrion) and a forgetful one (which is excused, and the meat remains halal). Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying the rules of halal slaughter correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The act in question is slaughter (al-dhabh). It is a transaction that renders the animal permissible to eat. The tasmiyah is a condition (shart) attached to this act.
In fiqh, a condition is external to the act but must be present for the act to be valid.
2
State the hukm for intentional omission
According to the text, if the slaughterer intentionally omits the tasmiyah, the meat becomes carrion (maytah) and is forbidden to eat.
This applies to a sane adult who knows the requirement.
3
State the hukm for forgetful omission
If the omission is due to forgetfulness (nisyan), the slaughter is valid and the meat is permissible to eat.
Forgetfulness is excused in Islamic law, similar to errors in worship.
4
Identify the underlying principle
The differentiation is based on the principle that intentional violation of a condition nullifies the act, whereas forgetfulness does not, because the person did not intend to violate the condition.
This is why the Hanafi school distinguishes between intentional and unintentional omissions.

Horse Meat: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Respect vs. Permissibility
Commentary expands matn on rabbit meat with horse meat dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differ on horse meat and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Horse meat is halal per Sahibayn; Abu Hanifa deems it makruh tahrimi out of respect for jihad.

This passage expands on the permissibility of rabbit meat by introducing a key intra-madhhab dispute: the ruling on horse meat. Abu Hanifa's view stems from the Quranic emphasis on horses for riding, while the Sahibayn rely on a hadith from Khaybar. Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals how different evidence can lead to divergent rulings within the same school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and disputed rulings
The act is eating horse meat. Abu Hanifa rules it makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) rule it halal (permissible).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; other schools generally consider it halal.
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa's dalil
Abu Hanifa cites the Quranic verse (al-Nahl 16:8) where horses are mentioned only for riding, not eating. He argues since the verse is a favor (imtinan), mentioning eating would have been greater, so its omission implies prohibition. He also adds rational arguments: horses are instruments of jihad; eating them reduces respect and resources for war.
The Quranic evidence is qati al-thubut but zanni al-dalala according to Abu Hanifa.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's dalil
The Sahibayn rely on the hadith of Jabir: the Prophet forbade donkey meat but permitted horse meat at Khaybar. This is a specific, clear permission.
The hadith is ahad, but the Sahibayn consider it strong enough to override the Quranic inference.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response to the hadith
Abu Hanifa counters that the hadith is a specific incident (wāqi‘a) which does not override the general Quranic principle. Also, the hadith does not explicitly address the honor of horses.
This reflects a usul principle: 'amm in the Quran takes precedence over ahad hadith in Abu Hanifa's view.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Kasani, al-Haskafi) favor the Sahibayn's view due to the explicit hadith and because the rational argument of 'diminishing jihad' is less relevant today. The mufta bihi is permissibility.
This shows that even within the school, later tarjih may prefer a different view.

Valid Animals and Age Requirements for Udhiyah
States valid animals and age rules for udhiyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three valid species for udhiyah, identify the minimum age for each species, and apply the exception for sheep allowing jadha'.

Core Idea
Only domestic camels, cows, and sheep/goats are valid; sheep allow a younger age (jadha') if large enough.

This lesson from the Sharh of Mukhtasar al-Quduri outlines the conditions for the sacrificial animal (udhiyah). The Hanafi school specifies that only three types of domestic livestock are permitted, each with a minimum age requirement. Understanding these conditions ensures that the sacrifice meets the legal requirements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the valid species
The text states udhiyah is from camels, cows, and sheep/goats. Wild animals are not valid. Buffalo are included under cows.
2

For a hybrid of domestic and wild, the offspring follows the mother. If the mother is domestic, the offspring is valid for udhiyah; if the mother is wild, it is not valid.
Example: a wolf mounting a domestic sheep produces a valid offspring.
4
Note the condition of remaining parts
If most of the animal remains (i.e., not severely defective), it is acceptable. According to another narration, if enough remains to feed the buyer, it is acceptable.
This relates to defects or loss of parts; the main purpose is to eat from the meat.

Rulings on Eating, Storing, and Giving Udhiyah Meat
Explains permissibility and recommended charity of udhiyah meat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the permissibility of eating, storing, and giving udhiyah meat, and explain why charity is recommended but not obligatory.

Core Idea
Eating, storing, and giving udhiyah meat is permissible; charity of at least one-third is recommended but not required.

This lesson examines what the owner of an udhiyah (sacrificial animal) may do with the meat. The classical Hanafi position is one of flexibility: the meat may be eaten, stored, or given to others. The Quran and Hadith are cited as proofs. Many assume charity is mandatory, but Hanafi fiqh treats it as a recommended act (mustahabb), not an obligation. Understanding this distinction prevents misapplication of the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? The passage covers eating, feeding (rich and poor), and storing the meat of udhiyah. The hukm level is mubah (permissible) for all these actions.
Note that feeding includes both rich and poor; there is no restriction.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof does the author cite? Two Quranic verses: 22:28 (فَكُلُوا مِنْهَا وَأَطْعِمُوا الْبَائِسَ الْفَقِيرَ) and 22:36 (فَكُلُوا مِنْهَا وَأَطْعِمُوا الْقَانِعَ وَالْمُعْتَرَّ). Also a hadith: 'Eat from it and store.'
The hadith is referenced but not quoted fully.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the dalil prove the ruling? The imperatives 'كُلُوا' (eat) and 'أَطْعِمُوا' (feed) indicate permissibility in Hanafi usul. The context and additional evidence (the hadith permitting storage) show the command is for recommendation, not obligation.
Hanafi usul holds that the imperative indicates obligation only if there is no qarinah (context) shifting it to recommendation. Here the permission to store weakens the obligation.
4
Note the recommended practice
The passage mentions that it is recommended (yustahabb) not to reduce charity below one-third. Al-Khujandi recommends dividing into thirds: charity, hospitality, and personal consumption.
This is a practical guideline, not a legal requirement.
5
Distinguish obligatory vs. recommended
Some scholars held charity from udhiyah meat to be wajib. The Hanafi school disagrees, based on the permissibility to eat and store. The ruling is that eating, storing, and giving are permissible; charity is recommended but not obligatory.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate. The mufta bihi (operative position) is the Hanafi view.
