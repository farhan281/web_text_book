Death of Eunuch or Impotent: 'Iddah and Lineage Rulings
Rulings on 'iddah and lineage for eunuch and impotent man.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on 'iddah and lineage when a eunuch (khusi) or impotent man (majbub) dies, and distinguish the two Hanafi narrations for the majbub.

Core Idea
A eunuch establishes lineage normally; an impotent man has ikhtilaf—relied view treats him like a normal man.

This passage is a direct ruling statement from the matn addressing two specific cases: the death of a eunuch and the death of an impotent man. It clarifies how the wife's waiting period ('iddah) concludes and whether the child's lineage is attributed to the deceased husband. Understanding these rulings requires distinguishing between a khusi (castrated but capable of intercourse) and a majbub (penis removed). The Hanafi school has two narrations on the majbub that differ in legal consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of men
Recognize the physical distinction: al-khusi (eunuch, castrated but retains the penis and can engage in intercourse) and al-majbub (impotent, penis removed or non-functional). This distinction is the basis for the differing rulings.
The khusi is capable of intercourse despite sterility; the majbub cannot insert.
2
State the ruling for the khusi
If the khusi dies and his wife is pregnant (either at death or afterwards from pre-death intercourse), her 'iddah ends with delivery, and the child's lineage is established from him. The reason given is 'li-annahu yujāmi‘' (because he can engage in intercourse).
Lineage is based on the possibility of intercourse, not fertility.
3
Introduce the two narrations for the majbub
The text presents two riwāyāt (narrations) from the Hanafi school. In the first, the majbub is like a normal potent man (fahl), so the rulings are the same as for the khusi. In the second, he is like a prepubescent boy (ṣabī) — lineage is impossible because he cannot insert.
The second narration treats him as inherently incapable of fathering children.
4
Compare the consequences of each narration
Under the first narration: if pregnancy occurs after death (from prior relations), 'iddah ends with delivery and lineage is established. Under the second: if pregnancy occurred before death, 'iddah ends with delivery but lineage is still not established (since conception is impossible); if pregnancy occurs after death, 'iddah does not end with delivery but by months, and no lineage.
The key difference is whether pregnancy can be attributed to him after death.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The relied-upon view is the first narration (majbub like a normal man). This is supported by the principle of istiḥsān, giving effect to the possibility of pregnancy if he produces fluid (mā’). The presentation order also suggests preference.
The tarjīḥ is implicit; later Hanafi works confirm this as the stronger opinion.

This passage is a ruling statement from the matn concerning the death of a eunuch (khusi) and an impotent man (majbub) and its impact on the wife's 'iddah and the child's lineage. Order of Male Custodians in Hanafi Fiqh
Ruling on male custody order by asabah and preference rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi order of male custodians for children and apply the tiebreaking rules of piety and age.

Core Idea
Custody of a male agnate follows the asabah order of inheritance, with piety and age as tiebreakers.

When no female relative is available to care for a child, or when the child no longer requires female care, the right of custody (hadanah) transfers to male agnates. This lesson outlines the precise order of male custodians in the Hanafi school and the rules for resolving disputes among them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the trigger conditions
Determine whether male custody applies: either no female relative entitled to custody exists, or the boy can do without female care, or the girl has reached puberty.
If a female relative is present, she takes priority; this lesson assumes she is absent.
2
Order the asabah candidates
List the male agnates (asabah) in order of proximity: father, paternal grandfather, full brother, consanguine brother. More distant asabah follow the same inheritance order.
The father is always first, followed by the grandfather, then brothers. Distinguish between full and consanguine brothers.
3
Apply tiebreakers within the same degree
If two or more custodians are equally close (e.g., two full brothers), prefer the more pious (awra'), then the older in age.
Piety takes precedence over age; age only considered if piety is equal.
4
Apply the non-mahram restriction for girls
For a girl, only mahram males can take custody. Non-mahram relatives like the son of a paternal uncle or maternal uncle have no right to custody of a girl, but they do have a right to custody of a boy.
For boys, these relatives can be custodians because the risk of fitnah is lower.

Automatic Manumission of Mahram Relatives upon Ownership
Stating the ruling of automatic manumission when owning a mahram relative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that owning a mahram relative automatically frees that relative, and distinguish the different scenarios (owner age, religion, location, etc.) with the intra-Hanafi disagreements.

Core Idea
Owning a mahram relative automatically frees them—but the rule changes if the owner is a harbi in dar al-harb.

This passage from the Kitab al-Itq establishes a key principle: when a person comes into ownership of a close blood relative whom they cannot marry (mahram), that relative becomes free immediately. The commentator expands this by clarifying exceptions and disagreements, especially regarding non-Muslims and the abode of war.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic ruling
Read the first line: 'And if a man owns a mahram relative from him, it becomes free upon him.' Understand the core legal consequence: ownership of a mahram triggers automatic manumission.
The ruling is unconditional and operates by law, not by choice.
2
Note the owner's capacity
The text says the ruling applies whether the owner is young, old, or insane. Recognise that the cause is valid ownership, not legal capacity.
This overrides the common assumption that minors cannot free slaves.
3
Apply to dhimmis and harbis
The rule applies to dhimmis in dar al-Islam. For harbis in dar al-harb, note the disagreement: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say it does not apply; Abu Yusuf says it does.
This introduces the concept of dar al-harb affecting Islamic rulings.
4
Examine the Muslim in dar al-harb scenario
When a Muslim buys and frees a harbi slave in dar al-harb, Abu Hanifa requires takhlia sabil (allowing the slave to leave); Abu Yusuf accepts the verbal declaration. Muhammad's view is unsettled.
This shows another intra-madhhab ikhtilaf based on location.
5
Understand the slave's own purchases
A slave cannot own property, so buying his own child does not free the child. But if a slave buys a mahram relative of his master, the master's ownership triggers the freedom. Overwhelming debt prevents valid ownership.
These are edge cases testing the limits of the principle.

Furthermore, if a slave has overwhelming debt and buys the master's son, Abu Hanifa rules that the son does not become free because the slave effectively does not own anything due to the debt.

Priority in Claiming a Child from a Jointly-Owned Slave Woman and Inheritance Rules
Explains priority rules and inheritance distribution.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi priority rules when multiple claimants to a child differ in religion or legal status, and calculate the inheritance shares when the child and each father die.

Core Idea
Priority among claimants is determined by superiority in religion or legal status; inheritance is reciprocal as if each were the sole heir.

In Hanafi law, when a slave woman is jointly owned by two men and gives birth to a child, both may claim paternity. The law prioritizes certain claimants over others based on religion and legal capacity. Additionally, once paternity is established, an unusual inheritance scenario arises: the child inherits fully from each father, and the fathers collectively inherit from the child as a single father. This lesson examines these priority rules and the reciprocal inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimants and their categories
The text gives three scenarios: Muslim vs. dhimmi, Kitabi vs. Majusi, slave vs. mukatab. Recognize each party's status.
2
Apply the priority rule
The Muslim is more entitled than the dhimmi; the Kitabi is more entitled than the Majusi; the mukatab is more entitled than the slave. This hierarchy is based on superiority in religion or legal capacity.
Priority is absolute; the favored claimant takes sole custody and paternity (unless the other claims first).
3
Understand the inheritance when the child dies
When the child dies, the two fathers inherit from him as if they were a single father. They split the estate equally, because their paternity shares are recognized equally.
This is not the standard share of a single father (which might be 1/6 if the child had offspring), but here the child has no descendants, so the father(s) take all.
4
Understand the inheritance when one father dies
When one father dies, that father's son inherits from him as a complete son, taking the entire estate. The son is the sole heir from that father's side because the father has no other recognized sons.
The son's inheritance from each father is independent; he takes full share from each.
5
Note the effect of precedence in claim
If one claimant makes the claim before the other, that claimant has sole priority, regardless of the hierarchical categories. Timing can override religious/status preferences.
This rule is an exception: precedence by time takes precedence over inherent superiority.

Death of a Mukatab with Wealth: Freedom and Inheritance
Ruling and consequence of mukatab's death with assets

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the death of a mukatab with wealth and explain the legal rationale for deeming him free at the last moment of life.

Core Idea
The mukatab's death with wealth triggers freedom at the final instant, making surplus wealth inheritable by free relatives.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra addresses a specific scenario: a mukatab who has been paying off his kitabah dies while still owing some amount but also has assets. What happens to the contract? Is it void? What about his children? The Hanafi ruling clarifies these points with precise legal reasoning, showing that the kitabah is not abrogated, the debt is paid from the mukatab's earnings, and he is considered free in the last part of his life, thus his remaining wealth passes as inheritance to his free heirs and his children are born free.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the contract

Wala' After the Master's Death: Mukatab, Mudabbar, and Umm Walad
Rulings on wala' after master's death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules of wala' for a mukatab freed after the master's death, for a mudabbar and umm walad upon the master's death, and explain the effect of religion on wala' inheritance.

Core Idea
Wala' follows the cause of freedom; upon the master's death, his heirs inherit the wala' of those he would have freed.

This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira presents a set of rules regarding the legal guardianship (wala') that arises from emancipation. After the master's death, the fate of his slaves' wala' depends on their status: mukatab, mudabbar, umm walad. The text also clarifies that wala' is established irrespective of the religion of the parties, though inheritance may be affected. Understanding these rules is crucial for correctly determining inheritance shares and the continuity of wala' ties.

Guided Path
1

List the three types of slaves – mukatab, mudabbar, umm walad – and their conditions for freedom.
Mukatab: contract to pay for freedom; Mudabbar: declared free upon master's death; Umm walad: mother of master's child, freed at his death.
2
State the ruling for each
For mukatab freed after master's death: wala' to master's heirs. For mudabbar and umm walad: freed at death; wala' to master's estate (i.e., to his heirs).
The text says 'their wala' is for him' meaning the master, but since he is dead, it passes to his heirs.
3

Why does wala' pass to heirs? Because the master caused the freedom (or his death triggered it), so wala' attaches to his estate just as if he had freed them while alive.
This is supported by the hadith: 'al-wala' li man a'taqa' – wala' belongs to the one who emancipates.

Wala' is established regardless of religion of emancipator or slave. However, inheritance between Muslim and disbeliever is blocked unless the disbeliever converts before death.

Wala' Across Religions: Establishment Not Hindered by Disbelief
Ruling that wala' is established regardless of religion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that wala' is established irrespective of the religion of the emancipator or slave, and distinguish between the establishment of wala' and the right of inheritance.

Core Idea
Wala' is like lineage: disbelief blocks inheritance, not the bond itself.

In this passage, the author of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains that the bond of wala' (clientage) is established upon emancipation regardless of the religious affiliation of the parties. This ruling is derived by analogy with lineage (nasab), which is unaffected by religious difference. However, the practical consequence of wala'—inheritance—is subject to the general rule that a Muslim does not inherit from a disbeliever.

Guided Path
1

The passage begins with 'And the emancipation of a Muslim, dhimmi, and Magian in establishing wala' by emancipation are equal.'

The author compares wala' to lineage (nasab). Just as a child is related to his father regardless of religion, wala' is a fixed legal bond that does not depend on religious identity.
The text says: 'because wala' is like lineage, and disbelief does not prevent the establishment of lineage.'

Although wala' is established, inheritance through wala' is blocked if the inheritor is a disbeliever and the deceased is Muslim (and vice versa). The exception is when the disbelieving emancipator converts to Islam before death.
The text explicitly states: 'except that he does not inherit from him, because a Muslim does not inherit from a disbeliever, unless the emancipator becomes Muslim before death.'

The passage covers all combinations by stating it applies generally.

Wala al-Itaqah: Nature and Inheritance Priority
Introduces wala as ta'sib and its inheritance hierarchy, with intra-Hanafi disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that wala al-itaqah creates an agnatic relationship (ta'sib), state the priority order of inheritance among the mawali, and identify the intra-Hanafi disputes regarding inheritance between a mawla's son and father, and between a mawla's grandfather and brother.

Core Idea
Patronage of emancipation makes the patron the last agnate; only males inherit through it, and Hanafi imams differ on certain distributions.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rulings on wala al-itaqah (patronage of emancipation). The text from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira establishes that this form of patronage creates an agnatic bond, making the patron a residuary heir. However, the inheritance order among mawali (patrons) is nuanced, with clear disagreements among the founding Hanafi imams. By the end, you will confidently navigate the inheritance hierarchy and the key disputes.

Guided Path
1
Understand wala as ta'sib
Recognize that wala al-itaqah is defined as a form of agnatic relationship (ta'sib). This means it functions like lineage-based agnatic ties but is secondary to blood relations.
The patron inherits only if no blood agnates exist.
2
Identify the inheritance order
The text states that blood agnates (asaba min al-nasab) take precedence over the mawla. Only male mawali inherit; females do not. If there are multiple mawali, the closest in agnatic connection inherits.
Males-only rule is derived from the hadith quoted later.
3
Analyze the dispute: son vs. father
When a mawla leaves a son and a father, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the son inherits solely. Abu Yusuf gives the father a sixth and the son the remainder.
The majority view prioritizes the son as the closer agnate.
4
Analyze the dispute: grandfather vs. brother
When a mawla leaves a grandfather and a brother, Abu Hanifa gives the grandfather all; the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) split equally. Note that 'grandfather' here means paternal grandfather.
This dispute hinges on whether the brother is considered a closer agnate than the grandfather.

Inheritance of the Manumitter (Mu'tiq) in the Absence of Agnatic Heirs
Ruling on mu'tiq inheriting as 'asabah after qur'anic heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rules of wala' inheritance to determine when the mu'tiq inherits as 'asabah and when he is excluded.

Core Idea
The manumitter inherits as 'asabah only when no blood agnate or dual-status qur'anic heir exists.

This lesson clarifies the position of the manumitter (mu'tiq) in the inheritance of his freed slave (mawla). While the general rule is that the mu'tiq takes the entire estate when there are no blood relatives, the Hanafi school introduces a critical qualification: the presence of certain qur'anic heirs can either reduce his share to the residue or exclude him entirely. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correctly calculating fara'id cases involving wala'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of inheritance for the mu'tiq
Recognize that the mu'tiq inherits through wala' (patronage), which the Hanafi school treats as a form of 'asabah (agnatic heirship). This right is established by the Prophet's hadith: 'Wala' belongs to the one who manumits.'
Wala' is a legal relationship that cannot be transferred or sold; it passes to male agnates of the manumitter.
2
Distinguish between absence and presence of qur'anic heirs
When the freed slave dies with no qur'anic heir (sahib farḍ) and no blood agnate, the mu'tiq inherits the entire estate. If there is a qur'anic heir, the mu'tiq takes only the residue after the fixed share.
The base matn states: '...then his inheritance is for the manumitter.' The commentator clarifies this applies only when no farḍ heir exists.
3
Apply the 'remainder' rule for single-status qur'anic heirs
If the qur'anic heir has only a fixed share (e.g., daughter takes 1/2), the mu'tiq receives the remainder as 'asabah. The text exemplifies this with the daughter.
The mu'tiq is considered an 'asabah bi-al-wala', so he inherits after the farḍ property is distributed.
4
Identify the exclusion scenario: dual-status qur'anic heirs
When a qur'anic heir also serves as an 'asabah (e.g., father, who inherits as both farḍ and 'asabah), that heir takes the entire estate, excluding the mu'tiq. The commentator distinguishes this from the single-status case.
The father's dual capacity gives him priority over the mu'tiq, who is a weaker 'asabah.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Unlike the Hanafis, the Shafi'i school holds that the mu'tiq inherits everything if no 'asabah by lineage exists, even if there are qur'anic heirs. The Hanafi view prioritizes the qur'anic heir even if that heir is not an 'asabah.
This ikhtilaf reflects different interpretations of the hadith on wala' and the hierarchy of heirs.

Commentary
This passage addresses two major points: (1) the absence of sin (ithm) in unintentional killing, and (2) the rules of inheritance deprivation. On inheritance: the principle is that any killing that entails qisas (retaliation) or kaffarah prevents the killer from inheriting from the victim, because of suspicion that the killing was actually intentional. When Killing Does Not Bar Inheritance
Lists six exceptions to the inheritance barring rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six cases where a killer inherits from his victim in Hanafi law and state the underlying principle (absence of qisas and kaffarah).

Core Idea
A killer inherits when the killing does not obligate qisas or kaffarah.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah lists exceptions to the general rule that a killer does not inherit. The Hanafi school allows inheritance in cases where the killing is lawful, accidental, or justified. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correct application of inheritance law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general inheritance rule
Recall the well-known Hanafi rule: a killer is normally barred from inheriting from his victim. This bar applies when the killing is unlawful and intentional (requiring qisas) or even accidental (requiring kaffarah).
The rule is not in the text but is foundational context.
2
Classify each listed case

Determine for each whether it is intentional or accidental, lawful or unlawful.
Cases 1-2 are lawful executions; 3-4 are accidents; 5 is a special evidence-based liability; 6 is justified lethal force.
3
Extract the common element
Notice that in all six cases, the killing does not obligate qisas (because the act was lawful or accidental) and does not obligate kaffarah (because there is no sin or the killing is not classified as accidental killing requiring expiation). The absence of both qisas and kaffarah is the unifying factor.
Contrast with an unlawful intentional killing, which requires both qisas and kaffarah and thus bars inheritance.

Given a hypothetical scenario, decide if the killer inherits by checking whether the killing obligates qisas or kaffarah. For example, a person digs a pit on his own property and his father falls in and dies. This is similar to placing a stone; it's accidental, so no qisas or kaffarah, thus inheritance is allowed.

Shafi'i and Maliki Views on Killer's Inheritance
Presents opposing madhhab views on inheritance from a killer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi, Shafi'i, and Maliki positions on inheritance from a killer and blood money, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf in practice.

Core Idea
Only killing that requires qisas bars inheritance in the Hanafi school; Shafi'i bars any killing.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah presents two inter-madhhab disputes: the scope of the killer's inheritance and the inheritance of blood money (diyah). Understanding these differences is critical for applying inheritance rules across jurisdictions and for recognizing the Hanafi insistence on specific legal causation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position on killer's inheritance
State the Hanafi rule: only killing that necessitates qisas or kaffarah bars inheritance. All other killings (by minor, insane, muta'awwil) do not bar inheritance.
This is based on the principle that inheritance is not barred without a specific textual exception.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position and its apparent basis
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that any act of killing—by child, adult, sane, insane, or mistaken interpreter—bars inheritance. His basis is the general hadith 'The killer does not inherit' (lam yarith al-qatil).
The Hanafis do not reject the hadith but interpret it restrictively.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i dalil
The Hanafi school restricts the hadith to unlawful killing that requires qisas, arguing that the general wording is qualified by the principle that obligations are only established by definitive evidence. Other killings are not included.
This is a case of takhsis (specification) of a general text.
4
Distinguish the Maliki position on diyah inheritance
The Hanafi school holds that diyah is inherited like any other asset, including spouses. Imam Malik excludes spouses from inheriting diyah because he considers it due after death and not part of the estate.
The legatee (musa lahu) does not inherit diyah, meaning it is not subject to a bequest.

Inheritance of Qisas Against Father and Validity of Pardon Before Death
Discusses inheritance of qisas against a father and validity of pardon before death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a son who inherits qisas against his father cannot execute qisas (only diyah is due) and evaluate the Hanafi preference for istihsan over qiyas in the validity of a wounded person's pardon.

Core Idea
Qisas against one's own father falls, replaced by diyah; a wounded person's pardon before death is valid by Hanafi istihsan.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two interconnected issues: the consequence of inheriting the right of retributive execution (qisas) against one's own father, and whether a wounded person's pardon of qisas before death is valid. The Hanafi ruling balances textual principles with juristic preference (istihsan), rejecting a strict qiyas that would assign the right entirely to heirs. Understanding this passage deepens your grasp of how family sanctity and individual rights interact in Hanafi criminal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on inheriting qisas against a father
The text states that if someone inherits qisas against his father, the qisas automatically falls and diyah (blood money) becomes obligatory. The reason given is the sanctity of paternity (ḥurmat al-ubūwa).
This is a clear ruling: qisas is blocked by the father-child relationship.
2
Analyze the supporting examples
Two examples illustrate the rule: a father kills his son's mother, or kills his son's maternal brother. In both cases the son is the sole heir, so he 'inherits' qisas against his own father. The rule extends to any case where the father kills someone whose heir is his son.
Notice the structure: the victim is someone toward whom the son is an heir, so the son inherits the right of qisas from the victim.
3
Examine the conceptual objection and response
An objection is raised: How can qisas be 'inherited' when it is established directly for the heirs from the moment of death? The response offers two explanations: some say it truly is inheritance; others point to a specific scenario where inheritance is evident—a man kills his wife's father; the wife inherits qisas, then she dies leaving a child from the killer; that child inherits qisas from his mother against his own father.
The objection shows careful attention to the nature of rights in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Evaluate the pardon debate: qiyas vs istihsan
Al-Karkhi presents two views on whether a wounded person's pardon of qisas before death is valid. Qiyas says it is invalid because qisas would belong to the heirs after death; thus the wounded person would be pardoning someone else's right. Istihsan says the pardon is valid because the right is the wounded person's at the time of pardon; the heirs merely stand in his place for execution.
The Hanafi preferred view follows istihsan, as stated by al-Karkhi.
5
Determine the consequence of a valid pardon
If the pardon is valid (istihsan), the right of qisas is dropped. If the pardon was for a consideration or if diyah is due from the killer's wealth, that is separate—the text mentions that the forgiven amount (if any) is from the killer's wealth because the right is not property, akin to divorce.
This confirms that the rights involved are personal, not purely financial.
