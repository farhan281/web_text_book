Definition and Ruling of Homicide by Error in Action
Defines خطأ في الفعل and states its legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of khata' fi al-fi'l, list its legal consequences, and apply the ruling to both Muslim and dhimmi victims.

Core Idea
Intent to shoot a target, hitting a human: kaffarah and diyah on the 'aqilah are wajib.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira defines a specific category of unintentional homicide: error in action (khata' fi al-fi'l). Unlike error in intent, here the act itself was lawful but the unintended result is a human death. The author then lays out the exact consequences and supports them with the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The passage defines 'error in action' as shooting at a legitimate target but hitting a human. Contrast this with error in intent (e.g., intending to kill one person but killing another).
2
List the legal consequences (mu'jib)
The text states three consequences: (1) kaffarah (expiation), (2) diyah (blood-money) on the 'aqilah, (3) prohibition of inheritance. Diyah is paid over three years.
Kaffarah is freeing a believing slave or fasting two months; diyah is shared by male agnates.
3
Determine the scope of the ruling
The passage explicitly states that the same ruling applies whether the victim is Muslim or dhimmi (protected non-Muslim). This is justified by the general wording of Quran 4:92.
The Shafi'i school limits kaffarah to Muslim victims only.
4
Examine the Quranic proof
The author cites al-Nisa 4:92, which mentions diyah and freeing a slave for a victim from a covenanted people. The Hanafi interpretation holds that this includes dhimmis, as the covenant implies protected status.
The imperative form indicates obligation (wujub).

Commentary
This passage classifies homicide into two types of error: error in action (like shooting at a target but hitting a person) and error in intent. Error in action triggers two obligations: kaffarah (expiation: freeing a believing slave or equivalent) and diyah (blood-money) payable by the 'aqilah (male agnate relatives). Additionally, the killer is deprived of inheritance from the victim if he is an heir. The diyah is paid over three years. The ruling applies equally whether the victim is a Muslim or a dhimmi (non-Muslim under Muslim protection), as proven by the Quranic verse (al-Nisa 92) which addresses a victim from a covenanted community. The commentary clarifies that the verse is general and includes both Muslims and dhimmis.

Key Terms
خطأ في الفعل (khata' fi al-fi'l): Error in action: when a person intends a lawful act (like shooting at a target) but accidentally hits and kills a human.
العاقلة (al-'aqilah): The male agnate relatives of the killer who are liable to pay the diyah collectively.
الكفارة (al-kaffarah): Expiation for unintentional killing: freeing a believing slave, or if unable, fasting two consecutive months.

Key Takeaway

Killing a Converted War Captive Who Has Not Emigrated
Ruling on killing a Muslim war captive who has not emigrated

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a Muslim kills a convert in dar al-harb before emigration and explain why diyah is not due.

Core Idea
A Muslim's blood is protected only under Muslim authority, not by faith alone.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the law of jihad: a non-Muslim from dar al-harb embraces Islam but remains in enemy territory. If a Muslim kills him there, what are the consequences? The Hanafi school, based on Quran 4:92, distinguishes this case from that of a Muslim who has emigrated to dar al-Islam. Understanding the basis of blood inviolability (isma) is key.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Read the first part of the passage: a harbi becomes Muslim in dar al-harb and is killed by a Muslim before emigrating to Muslim land. Note the two key elements: conversion and remaining in enemy territory.
2
Extract the ruling
The passage states: 'nothing is due upon him except kaffarah.' Identify that only kaffarah (freeing a slave) is wajib; diyah (blood money) is not required.
This is the hukm: wajib for kaffarah, no diyah.
3
Analyze the dalil
The proof is Quran 4:92, which commands freeing a slave for killing a believer from an enemy people. Observe that only kaffarah is mentioned, not diyah. This omission (by implication) proves diyah is not due.
The verse is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala; the Hanafi interpretation relies on the absence of diyah.
4
Understand the 'illa
The passage explains the reason: 'his blood was not inviolable in dar al-Islam.' The lack of protection (isma) means no value (qima) for diyah. Protection requires actual emigration to Muslim territory.
The 'illa is that inviolability is tied to actual authority, not mere faith.
5
Distinguish the counterexample
Contrast with a Muslim who emigrated to dar al-Islam and then returned to enemy territory. If killed there, both kaffarah and diyah are due because his blood was already protected by Muslim land.
This distinction confirms the 'illa: once protected, always protected unless revoked.

Commentary
This passage discusses a special scenario: a person from dar al-harb embraces Islam but remains in dar al-harb without emigrating to Muslim territory. If a Muslim kills him there, the only obligation is kaffarah (freeing a slave), not diyah. This is based on the Quranic verse (al-Nisa 92) which mentions only kaffarah for a believer from an enemy people. The reason, according to the commentary, is that such a person's blood is not considered protected (ma'sum) in dar al-Islam because he never immigrated and thus his life has no 'value' (i.e., no diyah). However, if he had emigrated to Muslim lands (making his blood inviolable) and then returned to enemy territory, killing him would incur both kaffarah and diyah because his inviolability remains. The distinction hinges on actual protection by Muslim authority.

Key Terms
الحربي (al-harbi): A non-Muslim from dar al-harb (enemy territory) who does not have a covenant with Muslims.
دار الحرب (dar al-harb): Territory not under Islamic rule and not in treaty with Muslims.
أحرز دمه (ahraza damahu): His blood became inviolable/protected by entering Muslim territory or accepting Muslim authority.
الكفارة (kaffarah): Expiation; a compulsory act to atone for an offense, here freeing a slave (or equivalent fasting/feeding).

Retaliation for Teeth and Limbs
States ruling of qisas for teeth and limbs with Quranic proof and consensus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas for teeth and limbs and apply the condition that retaliation must be for the exact matching side and type.

Core Idea
Tooth for tooth, limb for limb — but only the exact match: right for right, left for left.

This lesson extracts the key rulings from a passage in Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah. The text covers the general principle of qisas for teeth and limbs, supported by a Quranic verse and consensus, and addresses a nuanced case involving tooth regrowth. By the end, you will be able to identify when qisas applies and the significance of matching sides.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the opening statement: 'And in the tooth: retaliation.' Note the organs mentioned (teeth, eyes, hands, fingers) and the rule that they are taken only for their exact counterpart (e.g., right for right, left for left).
The text explicitly states consensus (ijma) on this matching principle.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof
Locate verse Al-Ma'idah 5:45 quoted: 'And the tooth for the tooth.' Consider that the Hanafi school treats this as a primary textual proof (dalil naqli) for tooth qisas. Note that the verse is mutawatir in transmission (qat'i al-thubut) but its application to specific cases requires interpretation (zanni al-dalala).
The explicit mention of 'tooth' alongside other organs in the same verse establishes the general rule.
3
Understand the consensus
Recognize the consensus that the right eye is not taken for the left, and similarly for other paired limbs. This reinforces the principle of exact equivalence (mumathala).
Consensus (ijma) is a secondary proof; here it confirms the Quranic ruling.
4
Examine the special case of tooth regrowth
Read the scenario: a man extracts another's tooth, the victim retaliates, and the first man's tooth grows back. The ruling is that the first man must pay 500 dirhams because his retaliation is now invalidated. A waiting period of one year is prescribed to see if the tooth regrows; for an adult, no waiting is needed as regrowth is rare.
This case illustrates the practical application and the Hanafi reliance on predominant circumstances (ghalabah).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof on retaliation (qisas) for teeth and limbs. It opens with the matn: 'In the tooth: retaliation,' then the commentator expands by citing the consensus of Muslims that limbs are taken only for their matching counterpart (e.g., right eye for right eye, not left). The same applies to hands, feet, and fingers. The commentary then provides the Quranic proof from Surah al-Ma'idah: 'And the tooth for the tooth.' It clarifies that disparity in tooth size does not matter because their function (benefit) is the same. A special case is discussed: if a person extracts another's tooth and the victim retaliates by extracting the first's tooth, but the first's tooth grows back, then the original aggressor must pay five hundred dirhams because his act of retaliation is now proven unwarranted (the injury is nullified by regrowth). A waiting period of one year is prescribed to see if the tooth regrows; for an adult where regrowth is unlikely, no waiting is required. This is based on the Hanafi principle that rare occurrences are disregarded.

Key Terms
الْقِصَاص (al-qisas): Retaliation in kind for bodily harm, specifically here for teeth and limbs.
السِّنّ (al-sinn): A tooth, subject to qisas with its counterpart in terms of type (e.g., incisor for incisor) and side (right for right).
الْعَيْن (al-ayn): Eyes: right eye not taken for left, et cetera, by consensus.
