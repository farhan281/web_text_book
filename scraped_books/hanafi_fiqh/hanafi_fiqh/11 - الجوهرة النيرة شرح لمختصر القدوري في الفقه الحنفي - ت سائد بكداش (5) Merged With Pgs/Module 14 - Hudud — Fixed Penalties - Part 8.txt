Cutting and Sewing a Stolen Garment After Hadd
Ruling on alteration and compensation after hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ownership and compensation when a thief alters stolen property after receiving the hadd punishment, and explain the underlying principle that hadd and financial guarantee are mutually exclusive.

Core Idea
After the hand is cut, the thief owns the altered garment and owes nothing.

In the Hanafi school, the punishment for theft (hadd) and financial compensation (daman) generally do not combine for the same act. This lesson examines a case where a thief steals a garment, has his hand cut, then cuts and sews the garment into a new form. The passage rules that the altered garment becomes the thief's property and he owes no compensation to the original owner. This ruling rests on two principles: the substantial alteration extinguishes the victim's ownership, and the hadd precludes additional financial liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
First, note that the thief performed three actions: theft, hadd punishment (hand cut), and then alteration (cutting and sewing) of the garment after the punishment. The ruling is that the thief becomes the owner of the altered garment and is not liable for compensation.
The timeline matters: the alteration occurs after the hadd is applied.
2
Locate the two supporting reasons
The passage gives two reasons: (1) 'the property left the ownership of the victim' – meaning the alteration was substantial enough to sever the original owner's property rights. (2) 'imposing guarantee is difficult due to the cutting of his hand, since cutting and guarantee do not combine' – the hadd punishment excludes financial liability for the same theft.
The second reason is a broader principle: hadd and daman are mutually exclusive.
3
Apply the conditions for this ruling
For this ruling to apply, two conditions must be met: (a) the hadd punishment (hand cutting) must have already been executed for the same theft; (b) the alteration must be substantial enough that the stolen item is no longer recognizable as the original property.
If the hadd had not been applied, the ruling would differ (the thief might be liable for compensation or the item returned).
4
Test the principle with a hypothetical
Consider a thief who steals a plain cotton cloth, has his hand cut, then dyes it and sews it into a shirt. Applying the principle, the thief owns the shirt and owes nothing. If the thief had only cut the cloth without sewing (minor alteration), the ownership might not transfer because the alteration is not substantial.
The threshold of 'substantial alteration' is determined by custom and the degree of change.

Ownership of a Dyed Stolen Garment After Hadd
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ownership after hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Imam Muhammad on the ownership of a stolen garment dyed after the thief's hand is cut, and explain the special ruling for black dye.

Core Idea
After hadd, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf give the dyed garment to the thief; Imam Muhammad demands return with compensation—except black dye splits all three.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra presents an important intra-Hanafi dispute about the intersection of theft (hadd) and property transformation (dyeing). The core question: once the thief's hand has been cut, does the original owner retain any right to the property if it has been altered? The imams differ based on whether the hadd severs all ownership claims or whether the principle of ghasp (usurpation) still applies. Understanding this khilaf sharpens your ability to trace how Hanafi masters balance punishment, property rights, and compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A thief steals a garment, dyes it (red or yellow), and then the hand is cut. What happens to ownership? Note that the dyeing occurs after the hadd is executed.
The timing is crucial: the hadd has already been carried out.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf
According to 'at hima' (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf), the thief becomes owner of the dyed garment. The original owner's right is completely severed. This view treats the hadd as a transformative punishment that transfers ownership of the altered item.
They hold that combining hadd and financial guarantee would be contradictory.
3
State Imam Muhammad's opposing view
Muhammad (al-Shaybani) rules that the garment must be returned, and the thief receives compensation for the added value of the dye. He draws an analogy (qiyas) to ghasp (usurpation), where the usurper must return the property plus any increase.
Imam Muhammad does not see the hadd as extinguishing property rights; he prioritizes the owner's original ownership.
4
Examine the black dye exception
Black dye introduces further disagreement: Abu Hanifa says the garment is returned but with a deduction because black dye reduces the garment's value (deficiency). Abu Yusuf says it is not returned at all (like red/yellow). Muhammad maintains his general rule of return with compensation for the increase.
The difference hinges on whether black dye is considered an improvement (ziyada) or a defect (nuqsan).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: for red/yellow dye, two views (thief keeps vs. return with compensation). For black dye, three distinct rulings. A mufti must choose which view to follow based on tarjih principles.
Later Hanafi practice often follows Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf unless there is a strong reason to prefer Muhammad's view.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on theft after form change

Learning Objective

Core Idea
For Abu Hanifa, form change ≠ substance change; for the Sahibayn, form change is a legal transformation.

Imagine a thief who steals silver, loses his hand, returns the silver, and then the owner reshapes it into a utensil. The thief steals it again. Should his hand be cut? The answer depends on whether the change in form is considered a legal transformation. This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi disagreement on what constitutes a 'change' sufficient to treat the theft as a new act warranting the hadd penalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the passage carefully: a thief steals silver/gold, is punished (hand cut), returns the stolen property, the owner changes its form (e.g., lump into utensil or utensil into coin), then the thief steals the same item again. The question is whether the second theft incurs the hadd (hand cutting).
Focus on the fact that the same physical substance is involved, only its shape changes.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules that the hand is not cut for the second theft. His reasoning: the 'ain (property) did not change; only the form changed. In his view, silver and gold remain the same regardless of shape; they are fungible (mithliyyat). Therefore, the thief is not considered a new thief.
The key term is 'taghayyur' — for Abu Hanifa, change must be in substance, not in form.
3

The two say the hand is cut. They consider the change in form (e.g., from lump to utensil or utensil to coin) as a significant transformation that severs the original owner's connection, akin to weaving yarn into cloth. Hence, the second theft is a new act.
Note that they analogize to changes in other commodities where transformation creates a new legal object.
4
Examine the legal basis (dalil) for each view
Abu Hanifa looks at the essence: silver and gold remain essentially the same. The two look at the form: once changed, the item is treated as a new property. Both views are recorded in the zahir al-riwayah.
No explicit textual proof is cited in the passage; the dispute is based on talil (reasoning).
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If the second theft occurs, under Abu Hanifa the hadd is not applied (the thief may be subject to ta'zir instead); under the two, the hadd is applied. The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi.
Later Hanafi fatwa may prefer the view of the two as more stringent in protecting property.

Core Idea
A thief may be compensated for improvements to stolen property only if the improvement increases value—opinions differ on what counts as increase.

The key is how each imam categorizes the dye's impact on market value.

Black dye vs. safflower: black is seen as reducing value in Abu Hanifa's context, while safflower adds no permanent value. This distinction drives the different opinions.
Context matters: what is considered a deficiency may vary by time and place.

Trace the qiyas with ghasp
Muhammad's view uses istihsan to treat the thief like a usurper (ghāṣib) who improves property: the property is returned and the improver is compensated for the increase. The illa (effective cause) is the addition of value through one's action.
This qiyas is khafī (hidden) because it balances equity against the thief's wrongdoing.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on the chapter of highway robbers from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It begins by stating that if the guardians (awliya') of the murdered victims pardon the robbers, their pardon is not accepted because the hadd punishment for highway robbery (hirabah) is a right of Allah (haqq Allah), and such rights cannot be waived by individuals. Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Dropping Hadd When Incapable or Mahram Are Among Highway Robbers
Intra-Hanafi dispute on collective liability in highway robbery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the dropping of hadd due to the presence of a child, insane person, or mahram among highway robbers, and explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf in this intra-madhhab dispute.

Core Idea
If a child, insane person, or mahram is among highway robbers, the hadd drops from all because the crime is one and shubha affects the whole.

Highway robbery (hirabah) is a severe crime warranting hadd punishment. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira examines a nuanced scenario: what if one of the robbers is a child, an insane person, or a mahram relative of the victims? The ruling depends on how we view collective criminal liability and the role of legal doubt (shubha). Understanding this intra-Hanafi disagreement sharpens your ability to trace how different imams weigh the unity of the crime versus individual capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and the parties
State the exact issue: whether the hadd punishment (death or crucifixion) for highway robbery is dropped from all participants if one of them is a child, insane, or a mahram relative of the victims. Note: Abu Hanifa and Zufar say yes; Abu Yusuf says it depends on who actually took the property.
The scenario assumes a group of highway robbers; the condition is that one of them belongs to the mentioned categories.
2
Analyze the proof of Abu Hanifa and Zufar
Abu Hanifa argues that the crime is a single collective act (jinaayah waahidah). If part of the group cannot be liable (due to incapacity or shubha), then the entire act fails to establish the hadd. He uses qiyas: if a mistaken (khaati') and intentional (amid) person jointly commit murder, the hadd (qisas) is not established because the act of the mistaken is not a legal cause for qisas, and the intentional part is only part of the cause.
The key analogy is that the presence of an incapable participant makes the entire act legally ambiguous, so the strict hadd is avoided.
3
Analyze the proof of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf distinguishes based on who physically committed the taking (al-akhdh). If the child or insane person themselves grabbed the property, they are the principal (mutba') and the rest are followers (taba'); since the principal is not liable, the followers are also not. But if sane adults did the taking, they are the principals and are liable; the incapable are merely followers and are not liable, but they do not protect the capable from hadd.
Abu Yusuf's reasoning focuses on individual agency rather than collective unity.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The difference is in outcome: under Abu Hanifa, any presence of a child/insane/mahram drops hadd from all; under Abu Yusuf, only if the incapable or mahram actually performed the taking. In a typical gang, the capable adults usually do the taking, so Abu Yusuf would punish them while Abu Hanifa would not.
The mahram case has a separate shubha based on property rights between relatives, which drops hadd entirely according to all (including Abu Yusuf, as implied).
5
Identify the mufta bihi and its authority
The Hanafi school relies on the view of Abu Hanifa (and Zufar) that the hadd is dropped from all. This is the Zahir al-Riwayah position.
The tarjih authority here is the commentator (al-Haskafi or similar) who presents Abu Hanifa's qiyas as stronger.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from al-Jawhara al-Nayira on the Mukhtasar al-Quduri, addressing the chapter on highway robbers. Some early scholars considered these substances permissible if small amounts do not intoxicate, but the later Hanafi consensus (mufta bihi) is absolute prohibition.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is in punishment: wine drinker receives 80 lashes (hadd), while consumer of these substances receives discretionary ta'zir (less than 80 lashes).

And it is not permissible to eat banj, hashish, and opium, all of that is haram because it corrupts the intellect, so much so that a man becomes frivolous and corrupt, and it prevents him from the remembrance of God and from prayer, but its prohibition is less than the prohibition of wine. For if a person eats any of that, there is no fixed punishment (hadd) upon him even if he becomes intoxicated from it, just as if he drank urine and ate feces, it is haram but there is no hadd upon him for that; rather, he is given a discretionary punishment (ta'zir) less than the hadd. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with a proof (the illa of intellect corruption). The author, building on the earlier discussion of wine, now addresses other intoxicating substances: banj (henbane), hashish (cannabis), and opium. The ruling is that these are all haram (prohibited) because they corrupt the intellect, leading to immoral behavior and neglect of religious duties. However, the text notes that their prohibition is less severe than that of wine, and consequentially, consuming them does not carry the fixed hadd punishment (which is lashing for drinking wine). The penalty is a discretionary ta'zir, similar to the case of consuming impure substances like urine and feces. This distinguishes between types of prohibitions: some are inherently haram due to their impurity or harm, but the legislated hadd only applies to specific named intoxicants (like wine). The proof for the ruling is the damage to reason ('aql), which is the foundational principle for prohibition of all intoxicants.

Key Terms
البنج (al-banj): Henbane, a plant with intoxicating and narcotic properties.
الحشيشة (al-hashishah): Cannabis, an intoxicating substance derived from the hemp plant.
الأفيون (al-afyun): Opium, a narcotic substance obtained from poppy sap.
الحد (al-hadd): Fixed corporal punishment prescribed by Shariah for specific offenses (e.g., drinking wine).
التعزير (at-ta'zir): Discretionary punishment set by the judge for offenses not covered by hadd or qisas.
