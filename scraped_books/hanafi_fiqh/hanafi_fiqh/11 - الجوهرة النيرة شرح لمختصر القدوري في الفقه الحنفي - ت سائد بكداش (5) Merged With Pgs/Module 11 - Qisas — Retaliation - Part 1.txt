Jinaayah of Mudabbar Against Master: Qisas and Sa'yah
Ruling with proof (talil) on mudabbar's intentional killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mudabbar who intentionally kills his master, explain the reasoning for combining qisas with payment of value, and apply the ruling to a scenario.

Core Idea
The mudabbar's tadbir does not protect him from qisas; he pays his value because the bequest cannot benefit the killer.

This lesson examines a unique intersection of criminal and manumission law. When a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon his master's death) intentionally kills his master, two competing principles come into play: the master's blood demands qisas, yet the slave's impending freedom is a bequest that cannot reward the killer. We will see how the Hanafi school resolves this through a dual obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal actor and act
First, note that the actor is a mudabbar — a slave whose master has declared manumission upon his own death. The act is intentional killing (or intentional injury) against the master himself.
2
Determine the baseline ruling for a stranger
The passage says 'he is like a stranger' meaning qisas applies as if the killer were free and unrelated. Establish that for any intentional killing, qisas is obligatory for the killer if conditions are met.
This establishes the default rule: intentional killing of a master by any slave normally leads to qisas.
3
Apply the exception: tadbir as a bequest
The tadbir (promise of freedom) is treated as a bequest (wasiyyah). A bequest cannot be given to the killer of the testator. Therefore, the slave's freedom via tadbir is forfeited, but the tadbir itself cannot be revoked after taking effect (it's a suspended contract).
4
Deduce the financial obligation: sa'yah for full value
Since the tadbir remains valid but cannot benefit the killer, the slave must compensate the heirs for the loss of his own value. He pays his full market value through work (sa'yah). This is not blood money (diyah) but compensation for the loss of the slave's value to the estate.
5
Analyze the heirs' options
The heirs have two choices: (1) hasten qisas immediately, or (2) first collect the sa'yah payment (the slave's value) and then execute qisas. Choosing payment does not waive qisas because the payment compensates the loss of the slave, not the murder.

The Five Categories of Homicide (Qatl) in Hanafi Fiqh
Definition and classification of jinayah and qatl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define jinayah in Hanafi legal usage and list the five categories of unlawful homicide, distinguishing them from lawful killing.

Core Idea
Jinayah in Hanafi fiqh specifically means transgression against persons; unlawful homicide has five categories.

This lesson opens the Book of Crimes (Jinayat) with a precise definition of the term 'jinayah' and a classification of unlawful homicide. Understanding these foundational categories is essential for grasping the varying legal consequences—from qisas to diyah—that follow each type. We will also note how the Hanafi school expands the classic threefold division into five categories for greater clarity.

Guided Path
1
Define jinayah linguistically and legally
Read the passage's definition: al-jinayah linguistically means transgression; in Sacred Law it refers to acts upon lives and limbs. Note that in Hanafi usage, it is restricted to crimes against persons, while property crimes are termed ghasb or itlaf.
This narrowing is a technical convention of the jurists.
2
Identify the five categories of qatl
Locate the five types listed: 'amd (intentional), shibh 'amd (quasi-intentional), khata' (erroneous), ma ujriya majra al-khata' (treated as erroneous), and qatl bi-sabab (by cause). Understand the general nature of each.
The last two fall under the ruling of khata' but are named separately for clarity.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi variation
The passage mentions that Imam Muhammad (al-Shaybani) held only three types. The author (al-Quduri) added two more to explicitly cover cases that are treated like error but originate differently.
This shows how later Hanafi works expand classification while maintaining consistency with the earlier three.
4
Distinguish unlawful from lawful killing
The passage clarifies that these five categories apply only to qatl bi-ghayr haqq (without right). Lawful killings (e.g., capital punishment, self-defense, combat) are not included.
Understanding this boundary prevents over-application of the five types.

Intentional Killing: Definition, Consequences, and the Absence of Kaffarah
Defines intentional killing and explains its legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of intentional killing, identify its consequences (sin and qisas), explain why kaffarah is not required, and apply the ruling to cases involving poison and throwing from a height.

Core Idea
Intentional killing requires qisas and incurs sin, but kaffarah is not wajib — the Quran proves it by omission.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah defines intentional killing (qatl al-'amd) and its legal consequences in the Hanafi school. The author first states that the killer incurs sin (ithm) and is subject to qisas (retaliation) unless the heirs pardon. Notably, kaffarah (expiation) is not required. He then provides examples: poison cases and throwing from a height, illustrating the rules of liability. Finally, he proves the absence of kaffarah by citing Quran 4:93, which mentions only Hellfire for intentional killing, contrasting with the verse on accidental killing (4:92) where kaffarah is explicitly prescribed. This lesson unpacks these rulings and the underlying proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is intentional killing ('amd). State its three consequences: sin, qisas (unless pardoned), and no kaffarah. Note that qisas is wajib under Hanafi law, but can be waived by the heirs.
The matn says 'لا كفارة فيه' — no kaffarah in intentional killing.
2
Analyze the examples for liability
Poison scenario: If victim voluntarily consumes, giver has no diyah liability (only ta'zir); if forced, diyah is due. Throwing from height: Abu Hanifa says no qisas because death is not directly caused; Sahibayn say qisas if death is the usual outcome. Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
The distinction turns on direct causation (mubashara) vs. indirect causation (tasabbub).
3
Trace the dalil from Quran
Author cites Quran 4:93: 'And whoever kills a believer intentionally, his recompense is Hellfire.' No mention of kaffarah. Compare with 4:92 on accidental killing which prescribes kaffarah. Hanafi principle: absence in a comprehensive statement indicates non-obligation (al-muqtadi al-‘adam).
4
Evaluate the cross-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i holds kaffarah wajib in 'amd via qiyas on khata'. Hanafi response: Quran differentiates, so qiyas cannot override explicit textual distinction.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi killer does not perform kaffarah; Shafi'i does.
5
Determine the practical application
Mufta bihi: no kaffarah in 'amd; on the throwing case, the Sahibayn's view (qisas if death is usual) is preferred for caution.

Qisas for Tongue and Male Organ: The Mumathalah Principle
Explains absence of qisas for tongue and male organ with exception for glans

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas for the tongue and male organ, identify the exception for the glans, and explain the rationale based on the condition of exact equivalence (mumathalah).

Core Idea
Qisas requires exact equivalence; thus no qisas for organs that change size, unless the cut is at a known boundary like the glans.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi position on qisas for certain body parts. The key principle is al-mumathalah: retaliation must be exactly equal. For organs like the tongue and male organ, equality cannot be ensured due to their contractile nature. An exception is made for the glans because it has a fixed boundary like a joint, making exact retaliation possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The matn states that no qisas applies for cutting the tongue or the male organ. Understand that this is the default position.
This applies even if the entire organ is cut from the root, according to al-Asl.
2
Note the exception
Qisas becomes wajib if the glans (hashafah) of the male organ is cut. This is because the glans has a known, measurable location like a joint.
However, if only part of the glans is cut, qisas is not applicable due to uncertainty.
3
Understand the rationale
The rationale for the general rule is that the tongue and male organ contract and expand, making it impossible to ensure exact equivalence in retaliation. The glans is excepted because its cut location is fixed and known.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the condition of mumathalah.
4
Consider the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf holds that if the entire organ is cut from the root, qisas is wajib. This is a legitimate difference within the Hanafi school, but the majority (including al-Asl) takes the position of no qisas.
The mufta bihi is the majority view.
5
Apply to a similar case
The passage also mentions the lip: if the entire lip is cut, qisas applies because the cut is measurable; if only part of the lip is cut, no qisas for the same reason of uncertain equivalence.
This reinforces the consistent application of the mumathalah principle.

Commentary
This passage addresses the conditions for qisas (retaliation) for harming body parts. The base text (matn) states that no qisas applies for cutting the tongue or the male organ, except if the glans (hashafah) is cut. The commentary explains that this ruling applies when only a part of the organ is cut; if the entire organ is cut from the root, the same ruling holds according to al-Asl, but Abu Yusuf holds qisas in that case. The reason for no qisas is that these organs change size (contract and expand), making it impossible to ensure exact equality in retaliation. For the glans, it is like a joint, so the cut location is known and qisas applies. However, if only part of the glans is cut, qisas is not possible due to uncertainty. The commentary also mentions the lip: if the entire lip is cut, qisas applies, but not if only part, for same reason of equality.

Key Terms
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation in kind for intentional homicide or injury
الحشفة (al-hashafah): The glans (head) of the male organ
المماثلة (al-mumathalah): Exact similarity or equality required for qisas
