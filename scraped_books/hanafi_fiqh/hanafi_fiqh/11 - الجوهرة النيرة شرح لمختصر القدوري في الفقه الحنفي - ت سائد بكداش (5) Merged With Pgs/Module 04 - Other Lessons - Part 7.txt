Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Effect of Dog Eating Game on Previous Catches
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nullification of training and previous catches

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the permissibility of game caught by a dog before it ate from the catch, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A dog eating from its game nullifies future catches; but the past catches are disputed between Abu Hanifa and his two companions.

Hunting with trained dogs has specific rules in Hanafi fiqh. One key condition is that the dog must be trained to not eat the game. When a dog does eat, its training is nullified. But what about game it caught earlier while it appeared trained? This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute on this point, where a single act reveals a deeper methodological difference between the great imams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on training nullification
The passage begins by stating that if a trained dog eats from the game, its training is nullified (batala ta'limuhu). Any future catches until retraining are impermissible. This is agreed upon.
The nullification is conditional on the dog actually eating.
2
Identify the disputed point
The dispute is about the game caught earlier, while the dog still appeared trained. Does the nullification affect those catches retroactively? Note that the passage signals this with 'wa ma kana qad sadahu qabla dhalika min al-suyud'.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that those previous catches are also impermissible (la yahillu akuluha). His reasoning: the dog's eating shows that its training was defective from the start, so all catches made under that defective training are invalid.
This view is stricter.
4
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) hold that the previous catches remain halal (yahillu akuluha). Their reasoning: at the time of those catches the dog appeared trained, and the nullification only applies forward, not backwards.
This view is more lenient.
5
Analyse the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: a hunter following Abu Hanifa must discard all game caught before the dog ate; following the Sahibayn, only the game caught after the eating becomes haram. The mufta bihi in later Hanafi works often follows the Sahibayn due to leniency.
The text does not specify tarjih; later authorities like al-Haskafi and Ibn Abidin preferred the Sahibayn.

Conditions for Eating Game Caught by a Trained Animal

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Game is halal only if Bismillah is said at release, the animal wounds it, and it does not eat from it — else it is carrion.

This lesson covers the Hanafi rules for hunting with trained animals. The text from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains the three conditions for permissible consumption of game caught by dogs, falcons, or sparrowhawks. We will also examine a key dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the necessity of saying Bismillah at the time of release.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? Eating game caught by a trained animal. The baseline ruling is permissible (mubah) when conditions are met.
2
State the Hanafi conditions
List the three conditions from the text: (1) tasmiyah at release, (2) wounding, (3) the animal does not eat from the game.
3
Locate the dispute
The dispute is on intentional omission of tasmiyah. The Hanafi school holds it invalidates; Shafi'i holds it is still halal.
The text says 'khilafan lil-Shafa'i'.
4
Contrast the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i does not require tasmiyah as a condition; he considers it recommended. Thus, even intentional omission does not make the game haram for him.

The practical difference: a Hanafi must always say Bismillah at release; a Shafi'i may intentionally omit and still consider the game halal.

Commentary

The base text (matn) states that if a trained dog, falcon, or sparrowhawk is released upon game with the mention of Allah's name at the time of release, and the animal takes the game, wounds it, and it dies, then the game is halal. However, if it only drinks the blood but does not eat the flesh, it is still considered to have caught for the owner, so the game is halal.

The Condition of Wounding for Lawful Game
Clarifying the condition of wounding (jarh) for lawful hunting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of wounding (jarh) for lawful game in Hanafi fiqh and apply it to distinguish between halal and haram kills, including the intra-Hanafi difference on internal breakage.

Core Idea
Game is only halal if killed by a wound that causes blood flow; strangling or blunt force is insufficient.

This passage addresses a common question in hunting: what exactly makes game lawful when killed by a dog? The Hanafi school requires a visible wound that causes blood to flow. Understanding this condition is crucial for hunters to ensure their catch is permissible. The text also discusses an internal difference within the school regarding broken bones.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the default ruling
The act is the dog killing game (e.g., deer, rabbit). The default ruling before considering conditions? The matn states that if the dog strangles without wounding, the game is not eaten.
The ruling is that the game is haram unless a specific condition is met.
2
State the condition (shart) for lawfulness
The condition is wounding (jarh) — a physical injury that causes blood to flow. The commentator emphasizes that any kill without a claw or fang wound is invalid, whether by strangling or blunt force.
The condition is derived from the 'apparent narration' (zahir al-riwayah).
3
Understand the proof (dalil) for the condition
The proof is reasoning (ta'lil): breaking a bone without blood flow is like strangling because it does not cause blood to emit. The underlying principle is that blood flow is the sign of a proper wound.
The text uses an analogy: 'becomes like strangling' (qiyas jali).
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa holds that if a bone is broken internally, it is considered an internal wound and the game is halal. The majority (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) require external bleeding.
The mufta bihi is the majority view: external blood flow is necessary.
5
Apply the condition to arrows hitting non-fleshy parts
If an arrow hits the hoof or horn and does not reach flesh or cause bleeding, the game is not eaten. If it does reach flesh and bleed, it is halal.
This extends the condition from dogs to other hunting tools.

If he neglects to slaughter it and it dies, it is not permissible to eat because he had the ability to slaughter (the original method) before achieving the objective (consumption) through the substitute (the arrow), so the substitute's ruling becomes void. This is based on the principle that when one has access to the original means, the substitute's effect is cancelled. The commentary adds a condition: if the game falls into the hunter's hand but he cannot slaughter it (e.g., no knife), and the animal still has life equal to or more than a normally slaughtered animal, then it is not eaten. This latter point is from the authoritative transmission (zahir al-riwayah) in the Hanafi school.

تسمية (tasmiyah): Mentioning the name of Allah at the time of shooting, i.e., saying 'Bismillah'.
ظاهر الرواية (zāhir al-riwāyah): The authoritative and widely transmitted position in the Hanafi school.
بدل (badal): Substitute; here, the arrow (or hunting) is a substitute for slaughter.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hunting inedible animals and explain the legal basis (the purpose or benefit) that justifies it.

Core Idea
Hunting an animal is permissible even if its meat is not eaten, as long as there is a legitimate purpose such as using its skin or repelling harm.

This lesson examines the opening statement of the Book of Hunting and Slaughtering in Al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. The author gives a broad ruling that both edible and inedible animals may be hunted. The commentator explains the reasoning: the hunter may have a valid purpose beyond food. We will explore this principle and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being discussed (hunting animals) and what legal classification is assigned (permissibility/jawaz). The matn states 'yajuz' (it is permissible).
The ruling is permissibility (mubah), not obligation.
2
Identify the two categories of animals
The ruling applies to two categories: animals whose meat is eaten (ma'kul al-lahm) and those whose meat is not eaten (ghayr ma'kul). Both are equally permissible to hunt according to the Hanafi school.
This is a broader scope than some other schools.
3
Analyze the reasoning (talil)
The commentator provides the legal cause: the hunter has a purpose (gharad) in the non-edible animal, such as benefiting from its skin, hair, feathers, or horn, or to repel its harm. This purpose justifies the permissibility.
The talil is a key feature: it shows that permissibility is not limited to food.
4
Connect to broader Hanafi principles
The reasoning reflects the Hanafi principle that benefit (manfa'ah) can make an act permissible. This is consistent with the maqasid (objectives) of preserving life and utility.
This principle is applied in other chapters as well.
5
Note the scope and potential restrictions
The ruling applies generally, but may be restricted by other factors (e.g., hunting during Ihram, in the Haram, or using prohibited methods). The text does not discuss these here.
Further conditions from other sources may limit the application.

The Hanafi Position on Omission of Tasmiya in Slaughter and Hunting
Inter-madhhab dispute on tasmiya omission and timing rules.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Tasmiya omission: intentional makes meat haram, forgetful makes it halal – a balanced Hanafi principle.

The simple act of saying 'Bismillah' before slaughter or hunting carries significant legal weight. This passage presents a three-way dispute on whether the omission of tasmiya affects the permissibility of eating the meat. The Hanafi school takes a nuanced middle path, distinguishing between forgetful and intentional omission, and also specifies where the tasmiya must be said – at the throat for slaughter and at the release for hunting. In this lesson, we will map the three positions and understand the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
What does the Hanafi school rule regarding intentional vs forgetful omission of tasmiya? Identify the middle path between Shafi'i and Maliki.
2
Identify the opposing positions
What are the rulings of Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik? Shafi'i permits in both cases; Maliki forbids in both.
3
Follow the proofs
What evidence does each school rely on? Shafi'i on general permissibility; Maliki on strict requirement; Hanafi on hadith distinguishing forgetfulness.
Hanafi dalil: 'The slaughter of the Muslim is permissible even if he forgets the tasmiya.'

Analyse the Hanafi response
How does the Hanafi school reconcile the imperative of tasmiya with the hadith? By treating tasmiya as wajib that is waived by forgetfulness, and intentional omission as sinful making the meat haram.

Where is tasmiya required in slaughter (dhakāt al-ikhtiyār) and in hunting (sayd)? For slaughter, at the moment of cutting; for hunting, at the release of the dog/falcon or shooting.

Qibla Direction and Tasmiya in Slaughter
Rulings on facing qibla and proper tasmiya wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on facing qibla during slaughter and identify the preferred wording of tasmiya.

Core Idea
Facing qibla during slaughter is sunnah, not wajib, by consensus.

This lesson addresses two practical aspects of dhabh (slaughter): the direction of the animal and the invocation. Many assume that facing qibla is required for the slaughter to be valid, but the Hanafi school clarifies it is a recommended sunnah. The wording of tasmiya is also specified to ensure the invocation is performed in the best manner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage addresses two separate acts: facing the qibla and uttering the tasmiya. Each has a distinct ruling.
2
Distinguish between wajib and sunnah
Note the explicit claim that facing qibla is not wajib by consensus, but rather sunnah. This is a key distinction in Hanafi fiqh.
Sunnah means recommended; omission does not invalidate the act.
3
Examine the wording of tasmiya
The preferred form is 'Bismillah, Allahu Akbar'. Al-Halwani reports a variant without the conjunction. Adding 'al-Rahman al-Rahim' is considered good (hasan).
The form with 'wa' is more common; the variant without is also acceptable.
4
Understand the legal effect
Not facing qibla or using a different wording does not invalidate the slaughter, but following the sunnah is better and more rewardable.
The meat remains halal and valid.

Tasmiyah During Slaughter: Conditions and Invalidating Phrases
Statements of rulings on tasmiyah and related issues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish valid from invalid tasmiyah during slaughter according to Hanafi fiqh and state the ruling when additional phrases are added.

Core Idea
Tasmiyah must be pure dhikr; adding ‘wa’ before another name makes the meat haram.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, opens the Book of Hunting and Slaughtering. It clarifies that facing qibla is sunnah, not wajib, and focuses on the conditions of tasmiyah (saying 'Bismillah'). The tasmiyah must be pure remembrance of Allah, not a supplication. The passage then examines three scenarios when 'Muhammad Rasul Allah' is added to the tasmiyah, showing that the ruling depends on how it is phrased.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on facing qibla
Note that facing qibla during slaughter is not obligatory by agreement; it is only sunnah. This is a preliminary ruling before the main discussion on tasmiyah.
There is no ikhtilaf among Hanafis on this.
2
Understand the condition of tasmiyah
Recognise that tasmiyah is a condition (shart) for valid slaughter. It must be pure dhikr (ذكر خالص مجرد), not a request or supplication. For example, saying 'Allahumma ighfir li' invalidates the slaughter.
The phrase 'جردوا التسمية' from Ibn Mas'ud emphasises stripping tasmiyah of any extra meaning.
3
Analyse the three cases of adding 'Muhammad Rasul Allah'
Examine the three scenarios: (1) connected without 'wa' (e.g., 'Bismillah Muhammad Rasul Allah') – makruh but meat is lawful; (2) conjoined with 'wa' (e.g., 'Bismillah wa Muhammad Rasul Allah' with kasra) – haram because consecrated to other than Allah; (3) said separately before or after tasmiyah – no harm.
The key distinction is whether the addition is part of the tasmiyah or independent.
4
Consider rulings on other additions
Note that saying 'Bismillah wa salla Allahu 'ala Muhammad' is lawful (meat eaten) but disliked. Also, slaughter in sight of a guest to honour him is haram, but for hospitality in absence is fine.
This relates to the principle of 'ahilla li-ghayr Allah'.
5
Recognise language permissibility
Tasmiyah in Persian or Roman (non-Arabic) suffices, even if one knows Arabic.
This shows that the essence is the meaning, not the specific wording.

The Allowed Site for Slaughter (Dhabh)
Defining the permissible area for slaughtering an animal's neck.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the allowed area for dhabh according to the Hanafi school and explain the range from the throat to the labbah using the matn and commentary.

Core Idea
Slaughter is valid anywhere between the throat and the labbah — the neck, not the chest.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra explains the precise site for dhabh (slaughter) as defined by the Hanafi school. The matn of al-Quduri gives the core rule, and the commentary clarifies the anatomical boundaries using key terms like halq, labbah, and luhayyan. Understanding this range ensures that the act of slaughter is valid and the meat is halal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn statement
Find the exact words of al-Quduri's matn: 'Wa al-dhabh: bayna al-halq wa al-labbah.' This is the primary ruling being explained.
The matn is the foundation; commentary builds on it.
2
Define the anatomical terms
Understand that al-halq is the throat (windpipe and gullet area) and al-labbah is the hollow at the base of the neck above the chest (naqrat al-nahr). These terms define the upper and lower limits.
Al-luhayyan (jawbones) are also mentioned in commentary as the upper boundary in one narration.
3
Locate the commentary expansions
Note the two additional sources: al-Karkhi allows slaughter 'in the labbah and above it up to the jawbones', and al-Jami' al-Saghir permits 'the whole throat: its middle, upper, and lower part.' These show a wider permissible range.
These narrations provide flexibility but the matn's range is the relied-upon view.
4
Establish the allowed range
Synthesize: the core is between the throat (halq) and the labbah. The commentary confirms that the labbah itself is included (al-Karkhi) and the entire throat is acceptable (al-Jami' al-Saghir). Thus the cut can be anywhere from the throat down to the labbah.
Cutting below the labbah (into the chest) is not valid.
5
Determine the boundary
Recognize that the defining limit is the labbah. Any cut above the labbah, including the throat and up to the jawbones, is valid by some narrations, but the matn specifies 'between halq and labbah' as the standard.
The practical takeaway: ensure the cut is in the neck region, not the chest.

Slaughter of Domestic Animals That Become Wild: Voluntary vs. Compulsory Slaughter
Ruling on slaughter of domestic animals that become wild with conditions and examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between voluntary (ikhtiyari) and compulsory (idtirari) slaughter and apply the correct method when a domestic animal becomes wild, based on Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
When voluntary slaughter is impossible, compulsory slaughter through wounding makes the meat halal.

In the Hanafi school, the slaughter of domestic animals normally requires throat-cutting (dhabh) for sheep or chest-cutting (nahr) for camels. But what happens when a camel bolts into the desert or a cow falls into a well? This lesson explores the ruling on domesticated animals that revert to a wild state, based on the principle of necessity and the consensus of scholars.

Guided Path
1
Differentiate the Two Categories of Slaughter
Identify that slaughter is divided into voluntary (ikhtiyari) and compulsory (idtirari). Voluntary is performed at the throat between collarbone and jaws; compulsory is any wound that causes blood flow.
Voluntary is the default; compulsory is the exception.
2
Determine the Governing Principle
When one is able to perform voluntary slaughter, compulsory slaughter is invalid. When one is unable, compulsory slaughter becomes valid and renders the meat halal.
This principle applies universally to all domestic animals.
3
Apply to Domestic Animals That Become Wild
For camels and cows, they are strong and cannot be easily overpowered; hence compulsory slaughter is permitted regardless of location. For sheep, they are weak; compulsory slaughter is only permitted outside the city where they cannot be caught.
The distinction between desert and city for sheep is based on the ability to catch.
4
Recognize the Basis of the Ruling
The scholars are agreed (ijma) that compulsory slaughter is valid when voluntary slaughter is impossible. This consensus provides certainty for the ruling.
Ijma is a strong evidence in Hanafi usul.
