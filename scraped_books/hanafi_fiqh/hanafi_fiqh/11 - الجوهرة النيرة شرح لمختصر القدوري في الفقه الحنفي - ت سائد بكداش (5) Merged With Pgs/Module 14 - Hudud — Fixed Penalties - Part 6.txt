Effect of Partial Hadd of Qadhf on Testimony
Intra-Hanafi dispute on testimony after partial hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on the admissibility of testimony after partial hadd of qadhf, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa (via Al-Hidayah) and Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Partial hadd does not automatically bar testimony; only full completion after Islam renders it inadmissible by consensus.

This passage from Al-Hidayah examines a nuanced scenario: a person receives some lashes for qadhf before converting to Islam, then the remainder after conversion. Does the partial punishment affect his testimony? The Hanafi school has multiple riwayat. Through this lesson, you will navigate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and identify the mufta bihi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Focus on the first case: a disbeliever is struck one whip for qadhf, then converts to Islam, then receives the remaining lashes. The question is whether his testimony is accepted.
2
State Al-Hidayah's position
According to Al-Hidayah, his testimony is accepted. This is the view that is declared more correct (الأول أصح).
This is the mufta bihi.
3
Contrast Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf argues that testimony is rejected because the lesser part (one whip before Islam) is subservient to the greater (remaining after Islam), so the entire hadd is treated as post-Islam.
His principle: الأقل تابع للأكثر.
4
Understand the tarjih
The text states that the first opinion is more correct, without explicit dalil. This tarjih establishes the relied-upon position.
Tarjih is based on the weight of riwaya and acceptance by later scholars.
5
Apply to a Muslim who flees
If a Muslim receives part of the hadd and then flees, the apparent riwaya (zahir al-riwaya) accepts testimony unless the full hadd is completed. Other riwayat vary (one whip nullifies, or majority nullifies).
The mufta bihi follows the apparent riwaya.

The Hanafi school holds that the fixed punishment of amputation for theft is only incurred when the stolen property is taken from a place of secure storage (ḥirz).
