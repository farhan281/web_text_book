The Hanafi Position on Qasamah: Oaths Imposing Blood Money
Commentary on the ruling of qasamah and dispute with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah oaths, identify the Shafi'i objection, and explain the Hanafi proof from the hadith and the practice of 'Umar.

Core Idea
In qasamah, oaths can impose liability for blood money, contrary to their usual exonerating function.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute regarding the procedure of qasamah in homicide cases. The Hanafi school holds that when the people of the locality swear fifty oaths denying knowledge of the killer, they become obligated to pay the diya (blood money). Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, arguing that oaths in shari'ah are meant to clear the defendant, not to impose liability. We will analyze the Hanafi proofs and the practical consequence of this difference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the ruling: if the people of the locality swear the qasamah oath, they must pay the diya. Note that this is a wajib (obligatory) payment.
The ruling is clearly stated in the matn: 'So when they swear, it is decreed upon the people of the locality to pay the blood money.'
2
Identify the Shafi'i objection
State Imam al-Shafi'i's position: the oath does not create liability because its established function in shari'ah is to exonerate the defendant (mubarri'a), not to obligate payment.
This objection rests on the general purpose of oaths as a defense mechanism.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof from hadith
Analyze the hadith where the Prophet ordered fifty men to swear and then granted the heir one hundred camels. This shows that the oath can establish financial liability in qasamah.
The hadith is cited in the text: 'A man came to the Prophet... He said: Let fifty men from them swear... He said: Yes, you have one hundred camels.'
4
Consider the practice of 'Umar
The second Hanafi proof is the action of Caliph 'Umar, who made fifty men swear in a qasamah case and charged them the diya. This is a precedent from a Companion, supporting the Hanafi view.
The text mentions: 'Umar made fifty men swear in qasamah and charged them the blood money.'
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical difference: in the Hanafi school, if the accused swear, they pay diya; in the Shafi'i school, the oath does not result in diya, so the case would be dismissed or the diya would come from the state treasury (according to Shafi'i view on liability).
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position, as it is supported by explicit textual evidence.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah discusses the chapter of Qasamah, a judicial procedure in homicide cases. The author begins by stating the core ruling: if the people of the locality (mahalla) take the qasamah oaths, they are required to pay the blood money (diya). He then addresses an objection: what if the killer acted with others? The response is that anyone who swears 'I did not kill' while having participated in a killing is guilty of perjury, because in Islamic law each member of a group that kills one person is considered a killer. Thus, the oath is not taken lightly. Then a brief ruling is given: if someone refuses to swear, the judge imprisons him until he does. A note from 'Shahan' distinguishes between intentional killing ('amd) where the ruling is as stated, and accidental killing (khata') where if they refuse (nukulu), the diya is also imposed. The passage then proceeds to the commentary on the matn phrase 'So when they swear, it is decreed upon the people of the locality to pay the blood money.' At this point, the author engages in an inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i. Al-Shafi'i argues that oaths are intended by the Lawgiver to clear the defendant (mubarri'a), not to impose liability. The Hanafi school responds with two proofs: first, the hadith of a man who came to the Prophet complaining that his brother was killed between two villages; the Prophet ordered fifty men from the locality to swear and then gave the heir one hundred camels (the diya). Second, the practice of Caliph 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, who similarly made fifty men swear and charged them the diya. These show that in qasamah, oaths can create liability. The passage then addresses further details: the wali (heir) is not made to swear and is not granted the crime merely on his own claim, because the general rule is that the burden of proof is on the claimant. If one person among the locality refuses to swear, he is imprisoned. If there are fewer than fifty people, the oaths are repeated until fifty oaths are completed, as the number fifty is obligatory by the Sunnah. This passage clearly demonstrates the Hanafi position that qasamah oaths result in financial liability, supported by explicit narrations.

Key Terms
قَسَامَة (qasamah): A procedure in homicide cases where the people of the locality take oaths to deny knowledge of the killer, and if they swear, they must pay blood money.
مَحَلَّة (mahallah): A locality or neighborhood where the deceased was found, whose inhabitants are required to swear in qasamah.
دِيَة (diyah): Blood money paid to the heir of the victim as compensation for homicide.

Qasamah: Oaths on the Accused, Not the Heir
States the principle and procedural rules for oaths in qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the heir is not sworn in qasamah and explain the procedure for completing fifty oaths.

Core Idea
In qasamah, the fifty oaths are on the accused, not the heir—burden of proof controls.

In cases of found homicide, Hanafi law establishes a unique procedure called qasamah. This lesson clarifies a foundational rule: the victim's heir does not have to swear; rather, the accused residents of the locality must take fifty oaths. We will examine the textual proof and the procedural details that ensure these oaths are completed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the first sentence: 'ولا يستحلف الولي، ولا يقضى له بالجناية' – the heir is not made to swear, nor is judgment given for the homicide based on his mere claim. This is the principal ruling.
Note that 'لا يستحلف' is negative – the heir is not to be put on oath.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith: 'لو أعطي الناس بدعاويهم... لكن البينة على المدعي، واليمين على من أنكر'. This prophetic statement establishes the general rule of burden of proof.
The dalil is ahad (Sunnah) but considered strong by Hanafi scholars.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'البينة على المدعي' means the claimant must bring evidence; the heir is a claimant, so he cannot simply claim and swear. The phrase 'اليمين على من أنكر' means the oath is on the denier – i.e., the accused residents. Thus, the heir does not swear.
Hanafi usul: the imperative form implies obligation, but here it's informational.
4
Examine the procedure for completing fifty oaths
Two scenarios: (1) if any accused refuses, he is imprisoned until he swears; (2) if the residents are fewer than fifty, oaths are repeated on them until fifty are completed. The text explains that fifty oaths are wajib by Sunnah, so they must be completed.
Imprisonment is a coercive measure to enforce the religious obligation of swearing.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that the heir may be required to swear fifty oaths to establish the claim. The Hanafi position relies on the same hadith but interprets 'اليمين على من أنكر' as exclusive to the denier.
This ikhtilaf affects who bears the burden of the oath in qasamah cases.

Who May Participate in Qasamah
Lists those excluded and included from taking oaths in qasamah with rationales.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of persons excluded from qasamah and explain the two rationales (ahl al-qawl al-sahih and ahl al-nusrah).

Core Idea
Qasamah requires both valid speech and capacity for physical support, excluding minors, insane, women, and slaves.

Qasamah is a unique legal procedure where oaths are used to establish liability for murder when evidence is lacking. The Hanafi school specifies who may take these oaths, based on two criteria: the ability to speak a valid oath (ahl al-qawl al-sahih) and the capacity to provide support (ahl al-nusrah). This lesson examines the excluded and included categories as stated in the matn and commentary.

Guided Path
1
Identify excluded categories
The matn excludes minors, insane persons, women, and slaves. The commentary adds mudabbar and mukatib (categories of slaves).
All slaves are excluded regardless of their emancipation contract.
2
Understand the first rationale
Minors and the insane are not ahl al-qawl al-sahih (people of correct speech); their testimony and oaths are legally invalid.
The oath is a form of speech (al-yamin qawl).
3
Understand the second rationale
Women and slaves are not ahl al-nusrah (people of support); they are not expected to provide military aid, which is a key aspect of qasamah's historical context.
This is a social capacity criterion.
4
Identify included categories
The blind and those punished for qadhf (false accusation of adultery) are included because they are capable of valid oaths in other legal claims.
Blindness does not impair speech; qadhf punishment does not remove legal capacity.
5
Recognize the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school allows women to participate in qasamah, considering them eligible for oaths.
This difference affects who may swear and the number of oaths required.

Evidence of Homicide: When Qasamah Is Imposed
Defines when a found body triggers qasamah and diya based on physical evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios regarding physical evidence on a dead body and determine whether qasamah and diya become obligatory according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Only violent traces on the body trigger qasamah; natural bleeding does not.

Finding a dead body raises the question of homicide. The Hanafi school sets clear evidentiary conditions: only specific marks of violence indicate murder, while natural bleeding—like nosebleeds or vomiting—does not. This lesson unpacks the rulings on when the community must swear oaths and pay blood money.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
List the three conditions described: (1) no trace at all, (2) blood from natural orifices, (3) partial body with head.
Each scenario has a different ruling.
2
Define 'athar' (trace) of killing
From the commentary, compile what counts as a trace: wounds, beating marks, strangulation, and blood from eyes or ears.
Blood from nose, anus, or mouth is excluded.
3
Apply the body‑fraction rule
Note the threshold: more than half of the body, or half with the head, in the locality triggers liability. Less than half with the head does not.
The presence of the head is crucial.
4
Explain the rationale for each ruling
For no trace: the person is not a murder victim. For bleeding from orifices: it is natural (nosebleed, disease, vomit). For partial body: insufficient evidence the killing occurred in that locality.
The reasoning is based on probability and custom.

Commentary
This lesson details the evidentiary conditions that trigger or exempt from qasamah and diya (blood money). The matn states three scenarios: (1) If a body is found with no external signs of violence, there is no qasamah and no diya. الأثر (al-athar): Physical mark of violence on the body, such as a wound, beating mark, or strangulation, or blood from eyes/ears.
القتيل (al-qatil): A person killed by homicide, as opposed to natural death.
المحلة (al-mahallah): The locality or neighborhood where the body is found; its residents are subject to qasamah.

Eligibility for Qasamah Oath-Taking
States who may and may not perform qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the categories of persons eligible and ineligible for qasamah in Hanafi law and explain the rationale for each exclusion.

Core Idea
Qasamah requires sound speech and capacity for support; thus minors, insane, women, and slaves are excluded.

Qasamah is a unique proof in Islamic law used when a corpse is found in a locality. The Hanafi school restricts participation to those who can deliver a valid oath and are considered capable of bearing collective responsibility (nusrah). In this passage, the author lists the disqualified and explains the reasoning behind each exclusion.

Guided Path
1
List the ineligible persons
Read the passage and note all categories of people who cannot enter qasamah: minor, insane, woman, slave, mudabbar, mukatab.
Mudabbar and mukatab are specific types of slaves.
2
Understand the two rationales
Minors and insane are excluded because they lack sound speech (qawl sahih); an oath is a verbal act. Women and slaves are excluded because they lack nusrah (capacity to provide support and bear collective burden).
Nusrah is a key concept: the ability to defend and uphold the claim.
3
Identify the exceptions
The blind and the one flogged for qadhf are allowed because they can still give valid oaths in other legal matters.
Blindness or a past hadd punishment does not affect eligibility for qasamah.

Corpse Found on a Beast: Liability on Driver
Ruling with qiyas-based proof and exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on liability when a corpse is found on an animal driven, led, or ridden, and distinguish the effect of concealment versus open driving.

Core Idea
Liability for a corpse on a driven animal falls on the driver's 'aqila, unless driven openly by day.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: when a dead body is discovered on an animal that a person is driving, leading, or riding. The default ruling places diyah on the 'aqila (patrilineal relatives) of the person controlling the animal, by analogy to a house under one's possession. However, Imam Khwahar Zada introduces a critical refinement: if the animal was driven openly during the day, the driver bears no liability. Understanding this ruling clarifies how Hanafi jurists apply qiyas and consider circumstantial evidence (qarinah) in liability cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and act
The scenario involves a corpse (qatil) found on a beast (dabbah) that a man is driving (sā'iq), leading (qā'id), or riding (rākib). Identify the potential liable parties: the person controlling the animal, the people of the locality (ahl al-mahallah), or the 'āqilah.
2
State the default ruling
The default ruling is that diyah is upon the 'āqilah of the person controlling the animal, not upon the locality. This applies whether the person is driving, leading, or riding.
This is the position presented in the text before the exception.
3
Analyze the underlying proof (qiyas)
The ruling is derived by qiyas (analogy) to a house. Just as a corpse found in one's house is attributed to the owner, the animal is in the person's control (like his 'hand'), so attribution and liability follow. Recognize this as qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
The analogue (asl) is the house; the effective cause ('illah) is possession/control.
4
Apply the exception by Khwahar Zada
Imam Khwahar Zada qualifies the ruling: the default applies only if the animal was driven secretly (sirran mutahashshiman). If driven openly during the day (jaharan), the driver is not liable, as the lack of concealment negates attribution.
This exceptional is based on qarinah (circumstantial evidence) that the driver would not kill openly.
5
Consider practical implications
The distinction between open and secret driving is key. In practice, the mufti must assess whether the driving was concealed or public to determine liability.
This refinement is from later fatwa (fatawa mutakhkhirin).

Al-'Aqila and the Payment Schedule of Blood Money
Defining the 'aqila and diyah payment installments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of al-'āqilah, identify the payment installments for diyah, and apply the schedule to specific amounts.

Core Idea
The 'āqilah pays diyah in installments over three years from their stipends.

This passage from the Book of al-Mawaqil addresses a crucial question: who bears the blood money when a killing is not intentional? The Hanafi school assigns this responsibility to a group called al-'āqilah, defined for soldiers as the people of the military register (dīwān). The text also details how the payment is spread over three years, ensuring the burden is manageable. Understanding this ruling clarifies the communal support system in Islamic criminal law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Note that the ruling applies specifically to diyah in cases of shibh al-amd (quasi-intentional) and khata' (unintentional) killing. The diyah is wajib (obligatory) upon the 'āqilah.
The text says 'كل دية وجبت بنفس القتل' meaning every diyah that becomes obligatory by the killing itself.
2
Define al-'āqilah in the Hanafi school
The 'āqilah is defined as 'ahl al-dīwān' (people of the military register) if the killer is from them. This is a unique Hanafi position based on the organized system of state stipends.
Contrast with Shafi'i definition of 'ashīrah (tribe).
3
Understand the payment source and time frame
Payment is taken from the annual stipends ('atā') of the 'āqilah. The three-year period starts from the date of judgment, not the killing.
The text specifies 'تؤخذ من عطاياهم في ثلاث سنين'.
4
Apply the proportional schedule
If the diyah is one-third or less, it is paid in one year; if between one-third and two-thirds, in two years; if full diyah, in three years. The text gives the example of a hand (half diyah) paid in two years.
The rule applies proportionally: half diyah in two years, two-thirds in two years, etc.
5
Note the intra‐madhhab and inter‐madhhab discussions
The Hanafi definition of 'āqilah differs from al-Shafi'i's. Within the Hanafi school, for non-soldiers, the 'āqilah would be the tribe or those who would normally assist.
The text only mentions the Shafi'i view; no Hanafi ikhtilaf is recorded here.

This ruling ensures that the burden is distributed over time, allowing the 'aqila to pay from their regular income without undue hardship.

Distribution and Caps of Blood Money
Explains collection of diyah from stipends and from tribal 'aqila with caps and timeline.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for collecting diyah from the 'aqila, including the three-year schedule, the per-person cap of four dirhams total, and the rule that only future stipends are seized after judgment.

Core Idea
Diyah is collected over three years, capped per person at four dirhams total, and only future stipends are subject to seizure.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi framework for collecting blood money (diyah) from the 'aqila (tribal guarantors) in accidental killings. You'll learn how state stipends are handled, the three-year distribution timeline, the maximum contribution per person, and how to proceed if the tribe cannot cover the full amount.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of 'aqila
Determine whether the killer is registered in the state diwan (military register) or not. If registered, his 'aqila are the recipients of state stipends; if not, his 'aqila is his tribe (qabilah).
This distinction affects how the diyah is collected—from stipends or directly from tribemates.
2
Apply the timing rule for stipends
Only future stipends (paid after the qadi's judgment) can be taken; past stipends are not recoverable. If three stipends come in one future year, the entire diyah can be taken in that year.
The obligation arises with the judgment, not before.
3
Calculate the three-year distribution
The diyah is spread over three years. If the wajib is one-third of a full diyah or less, it is paid in one year. For multiple killers, each pays his share over three years by analogy to the whole.
This applies to both diwan and non-diwan 'aqila.
4
Determine the per-person cap
No single person pays more than four dirhams total over three years, which translates to one dirham and two daniqs per year. Imam Muhammad holds an alternative cap of three or four dirhams total, with annual payment of one dirham or one and a third dirham.
The text considers Muhammad's view as al-ashah (most correct).
5
Handle an insufficient tribe
If the tribe cannot cover the total diyah, add the nearest tribe by lineage following the order of 'asabat: brothers, then their sons, then uncles, then their sons. Fathers and sons are controversial—some include them, some exclude.
This ensures the victim's family is compensated.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Ma'aqil in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira continues the discussion of how blood money (diyah) for accidental killing (khat') is collected from the 'aqila (the paternal relatives who bear collective liability). The author begins by addressing the scenario where the 'aqila receive state stipends ('atiyah) in installments. If three stipends come in a single future year, the entire diyah can be taken from them. He then explains temporal distribution: if the diyah is spread over three years, each third is paid over the entire respective year; if the amount is a third or less of the full diyah, it is due in one year. An example: ten accomplices each pay one-tenth of the diyah over three years, by analogy to the full payment schedule.

The contribution per person is capped at four dirhams, paid over three years at one dirham and two daniqs per year. If the tribe cannot cover the total, the nearest tribe in lineage (nasab) is added, following the order of 'asabat: brothers, then their sons, uncles, then their sons. Lineal ascendants (fathers) and descendants (sons) are controversial: some include them due to proximity, others exclude them.

The Hanafi position includes the killer himself in the 'aqila, making him pay like any other member, reasoning that since he caused the death, excluding him would unjustly burden others. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, holding that the killer pays nothing. The Shafi'i school argues that the 'aqila obligation falls only on those capable of providing aid and protection (ahl al-nusrah), who failed to supervise the killer. Women and children are excluded from the 'aqila because they are not considered sources of support. Consequently, if the killer is a minor or a woman, they pay nothing under Shafi'i law.

Finally, the 'aqila of a freed slave is the tribe of his master, because the master's people are those who supported him and thus bear responsibility. This is supported by the hadith: 'The freed slave of a people is one of them.'

The Killer and the Freed Slave in the 'Aqila
Explains inclusion of killer in 'aqila and the 'aqila of a freed slave with a hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the killer is included in the 'aqila and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument, and state the rule for the 'aqila of a freed slave with its prophetic proof.

Core Idea
The killer pays his share alongside his tribe; the freed slave's 'aqila is his master's tribe, proven by hadith.

This lesson tackles two important questions: Should the killer himself contribute to the blood money? And who bears responsibility for a freed slave? The Hanafi school answers both with clear logic and prophetic authority. We'll examine the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i on the first issue and the hadith that settles the second.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on the killer
The killer is included in the 'aqila and pays like any other member. The reason: since he caused the death, excluding him would be unjust.
This is a rational proof (dalil 'aqli) rather than explicit nas.
2
Identify the Shafi'i opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the killer pays nothing. His school argues that the 'aqila obligation falls only on those capable of providing assistance (ahl al-nusrah) who failed to supervise the killer.
Women and children are also excluded in the Shafi'i view.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is based on equity: if the killer is excluded, others are penalized for his action. The passage does not provide a detailed rebuttal but affirms the inclusion.
This is a purely rational tarjih (preference).
4
Apply the ruling to the freed slave
The 'aqila of a freed slave is the tribe of his master. This is supported by the hadith: مولى القوم منهم (The freed slave of a people is one of them).
The hadith is hasan and establishes membership in the tribe for 'aqila purposes.
5
Understand the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Hanafi law, the killer pays a share. Under Shafi'i law, he pays nothing, and the 'aqila bears the full amount. This affects how the burden is distributed.
The same difference applies if the killer is a woman or child—Hanafi includes them, Shafi'i excludes.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayrah presents a series of rulings on the 'aqilah (the group liable for paying blood money) according to the Hanafi school. It begins with the case where the killer's own tribe is insufficient to bear the blood money: the closest other tribe by lineage is joined, following the order of 'asabat (male agnates). There is a difference of opinion on whether fathers and sons are included; some say yes due to closeness, others no. The killer himself is included in the 'aqilah and pays like one of them, because he is the cause. Al-Shafi'i disagrees, holding that the killer is not liable for any part of the blood money, and women and children are also excluded because they are not considered supporters. The 'aqilah of a freed slave is the tribe of his master, supported by the hadith 'The mawla of a people is from among them.' For a mawla al-muwalah (client by alliance), his master and the master's tribe are liable. The 'aqilah does not bear less than half a tenth of the blood money; above that they bear it, and for half a tenth, it is paid in one year. If the killer has no tribe and is not registered in the diwan (military register), his supporters by craft (e.g., fullers, coppersmiths) are his 'aqilah. According to al-Hidayah, if he has no 'aqilah at all, the blood money is from the public treasury (bayt al-mal). Finally, the son of li'an (a child born from a marriage annulled due to false accusation of adultery) has his mother's tribe as his 'aqilah; if they pay and later the father acknowledges him, the mother's 'aqilah can reclaim from the father's 'aqilah.

Key Terms
العاقلة (al-‘āqilah): The group of male agnates ( 'asabat ) who are liable to pay blood money (diyah) on behalf of the killer.
مولى الموالاة (mawlā al-muwālāh): A client by mutual alliance who does not have lineage ties; his master and the master's tribe are his 'aqilah.

Who Can Demand Hadd for a Deceased Slander Victim
Ruling on who may demand hadd for a deceased person.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state who may demand hadd for a deceased person according to the Hanafi school, explain the basis of the right, and distinguish the Hanafi view from the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
The right to demand hadd is based on lineage disgrace, not inheritance.

A deceased Muslim was slandered (qadhf). Who has the standing to demand the hadd punishment against the slanderer? The Hanafi school restricts this to the child and parent of the deceased, because the disgrace attaches to them. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and contrasts it with the Shafi'i view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the legal act? It is demanding hadd for a deceased slander victim. The ruling is that only certain relatives may demand.
The hadd itself is wajib, but the right to demand is a procedural condition.
2
Determine the Hanafi position
Only walad (child) and walid (parent) may demand. Explain why: because the disgrace (ʿār) affects them due to lineage.
This is supported by the condition 'except from one whose lineage is impugned'.
3
Analyze the basis
The right is not inherited; it is based on the harm of lineage impugning. Evidence: a murderer (who cannot inherit) still has the right, and a daughter's child has the right (according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf).
This shows the right is not property-like inheritance.
4
Compare with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i holds all heirs may demand because the right is inheritable. The Hanafi response: the right is personal disgrace, not inheritance.
The practical difference: only walad and walid can sue in Hanafi, while any heir can in Shafi'i.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute. The matn states that the hadd punishment for slandering a deceased person can only be demanded by someone whose lineage is impugned by that slander. The commentary explains that this refers to the child (walad) and the parent (walid) of the deceased, because the disgrace affects them, and it is linked to the honor of free birth. Al-Shafi'i holds that the right passes to every heir because in his school it is inheritable. The Hanafi school rejects this, stating the right is based on lineage disgrace, not inheritance. This explains why a murderer (who cannot inherit) still has the right to demand, and why a daughter's child has the right (according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) contrary to Muhammad. The commentary also specifies that siblings, uncles, maternal uncles, and their children have no right to sue.

قذف (qadhf): False accusation of adultery or fornication.
العار (al-ʿār): Disgrace or dishonor.
ولاية المطالبة (wilāyat al-muṭālabah): The authority to demand (a legal claim).
