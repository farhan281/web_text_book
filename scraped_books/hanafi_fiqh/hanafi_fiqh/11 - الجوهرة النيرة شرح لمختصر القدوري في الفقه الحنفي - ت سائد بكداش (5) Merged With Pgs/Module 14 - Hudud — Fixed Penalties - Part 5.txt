Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah, a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri, elaborates on various expressions that may or may not constitute qadhf (slander of unchastity) incurring the hadd punishment. The rulings are presented serially. The key principle is that for qadhf to be established, the statement must explicitly or necessarily imply that the accused committed zina. Thus, attributing zina to a body part like the thigh or back is not slander because zina is specifically vaginal intercourse (for women) or anal intercourse (for men). Attributing it to the private part (farj) is slander. A figurative expression like 'with his finger' negates slander. The nickname 'son of rainwater' is not slander because it is ambiguous and may be praise. These rulings illustrate the principle that qadhf requires a clear imputation of zina to a person capable of it, and any ambiguity or impossibility negates the hadd.

Agent, Messenger, and Ambiguous Constructions in Slander
Rulings on command, messenger, joint accusations, and ambiguous phrases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules on agent liability, messenger exceptions, and grammatical constructions to determine whether slander is established in cases involving commands, reports, joint accusations, and ambiguous nicknames.

Core Idea
The slanderer is the one who utters the accusation; commands and reports do not incur the hadd, and ambiguous nicknames do not count.

Who is liable for slander? The Hanafi school draws clear lines: the person who actually speaks the slanderous words is punished, not the one who commanded it. A messenger who simply relays the accusation is not a slanderer unless he adds his own affirmation. Grammatical constructions also matter: 'You and so-and-so committed zina' slanders both, while nicknames like 'son of rainwater' are too ambiguous to be slander. This lesson unpacks these nuances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actor
In each scenario, determine who is being held liable: the commander, the messenger, or the original speaker?
The ruling often turns on whether the person is the originator of the statement or merely a conduit.
2
Analyze the wording
Look at the grammatical structure: is the accusation direct? Does it include a conjunction that implies joint participation? Is it a report ('X said') or a command ('Go say')?
The Hanafi school pays close attention to the exact form of the utterance.
3
Apply the ruling
Based on the actor and wording, determine whether the hadd applies to each party. Use the passage's specific rulings as a guide.
Remember: the commander of slander is not a slanderer; the messenger is not a slanderer if he merely conveys; but if he asserts the slander independently, he is.
4
Interpret ambiguous cases
For ambiguous statements like 'I meant him as a witness,' the school does not accept such interpretations if the grammatical construction clearly implies joint participation. Similarly, nicknames that could be praise are not slander.
The principle is that the apparent meaning of the words controls, not the speaker's hidden intention.

When Accusation of Zina Does Not Trigger Hadd
Commentary on a matn ruling about qadhf and non-ownership intercourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between forbidden intercourse that triggers hadd for the accuser and that which does not, based on the nature of the prohibition (temporary vs perpetual) and apply the rule to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Accusation of zina triggers hadd only if the intercourse was within ownership—if it resembles zina itself, the accuser is spared.

In hudud law, falsely accusing someone of zina (qadhf) carries a prescribed hadd punishment of 80 lashes. However, the Hanafi school recognizes exceptions: if the intercourse itself was already akin to zina—i.e., forbidden and without any ownership or marriage right—then the accusation does not damage honor in the same way, and the hadd is waived. This lesson unpacks the precise boundary: when does the accuser escape the lash?

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Start with the matn: 'Whoever commits forbidden intercourse in non-ownership, his accuser is not flogged.' The key is the concept of milk (ownership/marriage right). If the sexual act is prohibited but still within a valid ownership relation (like a wife during menstruation), the prohibition is temporary and the underlying right remains true. Accusing such a person of zina is a false statement and earns hadd. Conversely, if the prohibition stems from a total absence of any right (non-ownership), the act strongly resembles zina, so the accusation loses its sting and the hadd is not applied.
The distinction hinges on the category of hurmah: mu'abbadah (perpetual) versus mu'aqqatah (temporary).
2
Classify the examples given
The commentator enumerates many scenarios. Sort each into one of two buckets: (1) acts that do NOT trigger hadd on the accuser (non-ownership intercourse) and (2) acts that DO trigger hadd (ownership-based temporary prohibitions). Examples for no hadd: intercourse with a woman in 'iddah from an irrevocable divorce, with a foster mother or sister (perpetual prohibition), with two sisters married in one contract, with a jointly owned slave, with a father's slave, etc. Examples for hadd: intercourse with a menstruating wife, with a Magian slave (temporary prohibition within ownership).
The list is exhaustive but follows a clear logic: if the prohibition is permanent and unconnected to ownership, the accuser is safe; if the prohibition is incidental to an otherwise valid bond, the hadd stands.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the mukatabah
The mukatabah (slave under contract of manumission) presents a borderline case. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider her still property, so the prohibition on intercourse is temporary (like a menstruating wife), hence the accuser is flogged. Abu Yusuf and Zufar argue that effective ownership has ceased (since mahr becomes due if he marries her), making the prohibition perpetual-like, so no hadd. Map this dispute onto the same temporary/perpetual axis.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear: under the dominant view (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), the accuser of a mukatabah is liable to hadd; under the minority view, he is not.
4
Apply the rule to novel scenarios
Given a new scenario, first determine whether the intercourse occurred within a valid ownership or marriage relationship. If yes, check if the prohibition is merely temporary (like state of ihram, fasting, etc.) or perpetual. Temporary prohibitions within ownership preserve the hadd for false accusation; perpetual prohibitions (even if coincidentally involving a spouse) turn the act into non-ownership and remove the hadd. Practice with cases like 'intercourse with a wife who is a maternal aunt by fosterage' or 'intercourse with a wife who is in a state of major ritual impurity'. The first is perpetual (fosterage) → no hadd; the second is temporary → hadd applies.
The core analytical skill is parsing the nature of the prohibition, not just the actor's identity.

Women's Testimony in Ta'zir: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the admissibility of women's testimony in ta'zir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding women's testimony in ta'zir cases, state the proofs on each side, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa rejects women's testimony in ta'zir because it is a punishment; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad accept it because it is a human right.

This passage presents an important internal debate within the Hanafi school: can women testify alongside men in ta'zir cases? The disagreement hinges on whether ta'zir is primarily a punishment (like hadd) or a private right (like debt). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to analyze legal analogies and their limits.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that women's testimony is not accepted with men in ta'zir. His reasoning: ta'zir is a punishment ('uquba), similar to hadd and qisas, which require male witnesses.
For Abu Hanifa, the punitive aspect overrides the private right aspect.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that women's testimony is accepted in ta'zir. Their reasoning: ta'zir is a right of the human (haqq adami) because the victim can pardon it, analogous to debt cases where women's testimony is accepted.
They emphasize the victim's control over the punishment.
3
Analyze the opposing proofs
Abu Hanifa argues from the nature of punishment (qiyas to hadd), while the Sahibayn argue from the nature of the right (qiyas to debts). Both use analogical reasoning but differ on the dominant attribute.
The dispute illustrates how different aspects of a case can lead to different rulings.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
In practical terms, if only male and female witnesses are available (e.g., three men and one woman), Abu Hanifa would not convict, while the Sahibayn would accept the testimony and impose ta'zir.
This affects the ease of proof in ta'zir cases.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The later Hanafi authorities (e.g., Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah) adopt the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad as the operative position for fatwa, due to the need for flexibility and ease in proving cases.
Thus, in contemporary Hanafi fatwa, women's testimony is accepted in ta'zir.

Order of Precedence Among the Four Hudud
States the order of four hadd punishments and the imam's discretion when they combine

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi order of the four hadd punishments, explain the rationale for each placement, and apply the rule that the imam begins with the hadd of qadhf when multiple hudud converge.

Core Idea
The order of hudud reflects the strength of their evidence; qadhf is last because its cause is uncertain and severity is already in rejecting testimony.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi sequence of the four fixed punishments (hudud) for major crimes: zina, drinking, qadhf. We will explore why each crime is placed where it is, and learn the procedure when a person is due multiple hudud simultaneously. The ruling is a 'statement of order'—a procedural wajib for the imam (ruler or judge) to follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four hudud crimes
Name the four fixed punishments: hadd of zina (unlawful sexual intercourse), hadd of shurb (drinking alcohol), hadd of qadhf (false accusation of zina), and hadd of sariqa (theft). The passage also mentions theft in the combined case, though it is not in the initial sequence.
The initial order (zina, shurb, qadhf) is the sequence presented in the matn (core text). The commentator notes that theft is also a hadd, but its placement is not discussed in this snippet.
2
Understand the rationale for each position
For zina: its evidence is strongest—Qur'anic text (Surah al-Nur, 24:2) with an emphatic command. For drinking: the cause (consuming wine) is certain to be prohibited. For qadhf: the cause is uncertain (the accuser may be truthful), and severity already exists through rejection of his testimony, so it is placed last.
The rationale is epistemological: the stronger the proof, the earlier in the order.
3
Learn the combined case rule
If all four hudud (qadhf, theft, zina, drinking) are due on one person, Abu Hanifa rules: begin with the hadd of qadhf (flog the accuser), then imprison him until he recovers, then apply the remaining hudud.
This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position. The reasoning may be that qadhf's hadd is 'lighter' (mere lashes) and can be executed first, or that rejecting testimony removes the accuser's reliability first.
4
Note the ta'zir intensity discussion
The passage also records a disagreement on ta'zir (discretionary punishment) severity: whether blows are concentrated in one spot or distributed over limbs. The resolution is that maximum ta'zir (near-hadd) is distributed; lesser ta'zir is concentrated.
This is a subsidiary point; it does not affect the order of hudud but shows the Hanafi method of reconciling contradictory narrations.

Learning Objective

When hudud combine, qadhf always comes first; the imam may choose between zina and theft, but stoning or qisas cancels the rest.

When multiple crimes are proven against one person, the Shari'ah prescribes a sequence for executing the punishments. This lesson presents the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa on the order of hudud when four specific hudud — slander, theft, zina, and drinking — are combined, and how the presence of stoning or qisas alters the order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default order
The text lists four hudud. Abu Hanifa begins with hadd of qadhf, then imprisonment, then the imam chooses between zina and theft, then the other, then drinking. Understand this base sequence.

Qisas for life takes precedence over qisas for less than life, both executed after qadhf.
5

The hadd of qadhf is always executed first because it is for accusing chastity. Capital punishments like rajm and qisas take precedence over other hudud, but they are delayed until after qadhf.
Civil liabilities (e.g., property restitution) are handled separately.

This passage addresses a critical intersection of penal law and liability. When the state punishes a transgressor, what happens if the punishment proves fatal? The imam (or his deputy) is the designated authority to carry out prescribed punishments. His action is sanctioned by the Shari'ah, so any death resulting is considered legally 'wasted'.

Commentary

The passage begins with an inter-madhhab dispute: the Shafi'i school holds that if the one who committed qadhf repents, his testimony is accepted, citing the Quranic verse 'except those who repent' (Quran 24:4-5). Testimony of a Former Kafir after Hadd al-Qadhf
Ruling on testimony after hadd for qadhf when the person converts to Islam.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A kafir's testimony after hadd is accepted upon embracing Islam because the hadd occurred while he had no valid testimony to invalidate.

The hadd punishment for false accusation of adultery (qadhf) has a permanent effect on the testimony of a Muslim: it renders his testimony inadmissible forever. But what about a non-Muslim who receives this punishment and then converts to Islam? This lesson examines the Hanafi position that such a person's testimony is accepted after conversion, based on the principle that the hadd only invalidates testimony that existed at the time of punishment. We will also explore the internal debates within the Hanafi school when the hadd is partially carried out in kufr and partially in Islam.

Guided Path

First, note that the passage begins with a clear ruling: if a disbeliever is punished with hadd for qadhf and then becomes Muslim, his testimony is accepted. Contrast this with a Muslim who, after receiving hadd, is forever disqualified from testifying. The key exception is the case of a kafir because at the time of hadd he lacked any valid testimony to invalidate.
2
Understand the 'illa (effective cause)
The underlying legal reasoning (ta'lil) is that the hadd invalidates testimony only if the person had valid testimony at the time of punishment. A kafir, according to the Hanafi school, has no adala (legal integrity), so his testimony is not considered valid even for his own community. Hence, the hadd does not meet any testimony to nullify. After embracing Islam, he acquires adala anew, and his testimony becomes valid.
This is the basis for distinguishing the case of a slave: a slave has valid testimony (in certain matters), so hadd invalidates it permanently even if he is later freed.
3
Analyze the partial hadd scenarios and transmissions
The passage presents multiple narratives when part of the hadd is carried out in kufr and part in Islam. The relied-upon apparent transmission (Zahir al-Riwaya) holds that testimony is not permanently invalidated; if the person repents, it is accepted. Alternative views consider the last whip or the majority of lashes as the determinant. Identify the arguments for each and note that the text declares 'the first is more correct'.
This step is crucial for understanding the tarjih (preference) mechanism within the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the principle to the case of a Muslim fleeing hadd
The passage also briefly addresses a Muslim who receives part of the hadd and then flees. The apparent transmission says his testimony is accepted as long as he is not struck all of it, mirroring the logic that invalidation requires completion. Other transmissions diverge. Observe how the same principle of 'completion' applies.
This shows consistency in the madhhab's approach.
5
Summarize the mufta bihi and practical consequence
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that if the majority of lashes occur in kufr, testimony is accepted after repentance. If the majority occur in Islam, it is rejected. This determines whether a convert can later act as a witness in court. The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is significant for judges and muftis.

He said in Al-Manzoomah [poem] from Abu Hanifa: The testimony of the slanderer is wasted by a whip, and from him it is narrated when the majority is carried out, and from him rejection when it is complete, and that is the statement of his two companions, so know. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri addresses the effect of the hadd punishment for slander (qadhf) on the admissibility of testimony (shahada) when the culprit is a non-Muslim (kafir) who later embraces Islam. It begins by stating the main ruling: if a kafir is lashed for qadhf and then becomes Muslim, his testimony is accepted. The reasoning is that at the time of the hadd, the kafir had no testimony that was legally considered (his testimony was not accepted even regarding dhimmis because he had limited standing), so the hadd did not invalidate any existing testimony. Upon embracing Islam, he acquires a new integrity (adala) based on his faith. This is contrasted with the case of a slave: if a slave is lashed for qadhf and then freed, his testimony is not accepted because as a slave his testimony was valid (in certain cases), so the hadd invalidates it permanently. The passage then considers a scenario where the qadhf was committed during kufr but the hadd is carried out after Islam. In such a case, the testimony is permanently invalidated because the hadd is applied when the person already has the capacity for testimony (as a Muslim), so it annuls it. This contrasts with the case where the hadd was applied during kufr — then no testimony existed to be invalidated. For the scenario where part of the hadd is executed in kufr and part in Islam, the commentary presents multiple narrations (riwayaat) from the Hanafi school. The apparent transmission (Zahir al-Riwaya) holds that testimony is not permanently invalidated; if the person repents, his testimony is accepted. This is because the invalidating factor is the completion of the hadd, and that completion did not occur during Islam. Another narration considers the last whip: if the last whip is in Islam, testimony is invalidated. Another considers the majority of lashes: if most are in Islam, testimony is invalidated; if most in kufr, it is not. The Hidayah provides a specific case: if a kafir receives one lash, then becomes Muslim, then receives the remaining lashes, his testimony is valid according to the preferred view. Abu Yusuf holds that it is rejected, but the first view is considered more correct. The passage emphasizes that if a person commits qadhf, then becomes Muslim, and then receives the complete hadd after Islam, his testimony is invalidated by consensus. It also discusses the case of a Muslim who partially receives hadd and then flees: the apparent transmission says his testimony is valid unless all lashes are given. Two other transmissions say it is invalidated by one lash or by the majority, respectively. Finally, a poem summarizing Abu Hanifa's positions is cited, noting that his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that testimony is invalidated only when the hadd is completed. The legal principle (talil) throughout is that the hadd invalidates testimony only if it is applied at a time when the person holds valid testimony that can be invalidated. This 'illa (effective cause) explains the difference between a kafir and a Muslim, and the partial hadd scenarios.

Key Terms
حد القذف (hadd al-qadhf): The fixed punishment of 80 lashes for false accusation of adultery or sodomy.
شهادة (shahada): Testimony given in a court, which requires the witness to be 'adl (integrity).
عدالة (adala): Legal integrity or trustworthiness of a witness, derived from being a Muslim and avoiding major sins and consistency.

Key Takeaway
A hadd for qadhf only invalidates testimony if it is applied when the person has legally valid testimony; hence a kafir's testimony is accepted after conversion because the hadd occurred without testimonial capacity.
