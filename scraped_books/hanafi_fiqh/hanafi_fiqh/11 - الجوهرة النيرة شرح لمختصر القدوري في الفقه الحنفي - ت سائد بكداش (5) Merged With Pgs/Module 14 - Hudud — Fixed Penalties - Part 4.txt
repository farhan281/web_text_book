Condition of Smell for Hadd al-Shurb and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on hadd for drinking wine and intra-Hanafi dispute on taqaddum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of smell for hadd al-shurb according to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on taqaddum for confession and testimony.

Core Idea
The presence of the smell of wine is a condition for establishing the hadd — after it fades, doubt invalidates the punishment.

This passage from al-Jawharat al-Nayyirah presents the Hanafi ruling on the hadd for drinking, focusing on the crucial condition of the smell of wine. An intra-madhhab dispute arises regarding taqaddum (lapse of time) affecting both testimony and confession. Understanding this condition and the ikhtilaf is essential for correctly applying the fixed punishment in a judicial setting, as it balances between rigorous proof and leniency in hudud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Ruling
Read the opening lines: if a person drinks wine and is seized with the smell present, and witnesses testify or he confesses while the smell is present, then the hadd is due. Note that the condition of smell is explicitly stated.
The text says: 'فشهد الشهود بذلك عليه، أو أقر وريحها موجودة : فعليه الحد'.
2
Recognize the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Confession after Smell Fades
The text states: 'فإن أقر بعد ذهاب ريحها : لم يُحَدَّ، هذا عندهما. وقال محمد : يُحد'. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that no hadd is applied if confession occurs after the smell is gone, while Imam Muhammad holds the hadd is applied.
The phrase 'عندهما' refers to the two companions.
3
Extend the Dispute to Testimony
The same dispute applies to testimony: if witnesses testify after the smell and drunkenness have disappeared, the two companions say no hadd, while Muhammad says hadd is applied.
The text: 'وكذا إذا شهدوا عليه بعد ذهاب ريحها والسكر: لم يُحَدَّ عندهما. وقال محمد : يُحد'.
4
Analyze the Basis of the Dispute: Taqaddum and Its Measure
Taqaddum (lapse of time) prevents acceptance of testimony by consensus. However, they differ on its measure: Muhammad measures it by a specific time (by analogy with zina), while the two companions measure it by the disappearance of the smell. For confession, Muhammad does not invalidate taqaddum, but the two companions still require the smell.
The text distinguishes between testimony and confession.
5
Understand the Dalil from Ibn Mas'ud and the Consensus Exception
The narration from Ibn Mas'ud instructs to 'shake him and if you find its smell, flog him' — showing the condition of smell. An exception: if the smell is present when witnesses seize him but disappears en route to the judge, hadd is applied by consensus because the testimony was valid at the time of observation.
This strengthens the two companions' position.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawharat al-Nayyirah, a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, deals with the conditions for applying the hadd punishment for drinking wine (khamr) and other intoxicants. The ruling is presented as a dispute between the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Imam Muhammad alone. The key issue is the effect of taqaddum (lapse of time) on testimony and confession. The author begins by stating the default ruling: if a person drinks wine and is seized while the smell of wine is still on him, and witnesses testify or he confesses while the smell is present, then the hadd (eighty lashes) is obligatory. However, if he confesses after the smell has disappeared, according to the two companions (عندهما) he is not flogged, but Imam Muhammad holds that he is flogged. Similarly, if witnesses testify after the smell and intoxication have vanished, the two companions say no hadd, while Muhammad says hadd is applied. The author explains that taqaddum prevents acceptance of testimony by consensus, but they differ on its measure: Muhammad measures it by a specific time period (by analogy with zina), while the two companions measure it by the disappearance of the smell. For confession, taqaddum does not affect it according to Muhammad, but the two companions require the smell to be present. An exception is noted: if witnesses seize the person while the smell is present or he is drunk, and then the smell disappears during transport to the Imam, the hadd is applied by consensus, because the witness testimony was valid at the time of observation. The passage then cites a narration from Ibn Mas'ud to support the condition of the smell. Finally, the author distinguishes between wine and nabidh (date wine): for wine, any amount triggers hadd; for nabidh, only if it causes intoxication (sukr). Drinking nabidh without intoxication does not incur the hadd because it is not intoxicating per se.

Key Terms
حد الشرب (hadd al-shurb): The fixed corporal punishment for drinking intoxicants, specifically eighty lashes for a free man and forty for a slave, according to the Hanafi school.
التقادم (al-taqaddum): Lapse of time between the commission of the offense and the testimony or confession.
الإقرار (al-iqrar): Confession; a voluntary admission of guilt by the accused. In this context, it is treated differently from testimony regarding taqaddum.

The Smell of Wine and Taqaadum in Hadd Evidence
Explains evidentiary conditions for hadd al-shurb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the role of the smell of wine as a condition for establishing the hadd for drinking, and distinguish the Hanafi imams' positions on the effect of passage of time (taqaadum) on testimony and confession.

Core Idea
The smell of wine is a condition for the hadd, but taqaadum's measure differs: time (Abu Hanifa) vs. disappearance of smell (Sahibayn).

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses evidentiary requirements in the hadd for drinking. It clarifies how the passage of time (taqaadum) affects the acceptance of testimony and confession, and highlights the key role of the smell of wine as a condition. Understanding these nuances is essential for applying hadd correctly in varying circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage deals with the hadd for drinking (shurb al-khamr). The general ruling is that intoxication from nabidh also entails hadd. Note that the focus is on evidentiary conditions.
The act itself is not debated; the dispute is about proof.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith of Ibn Mas'ud, where he instructs to shake the youth and have him exhale; if the smell is found, flog him. This hadith is the primary proof for the condition of smell.
Hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), but accepted in this context.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'ترتروه ومؤمزوه' followed by the conditional 'فإن وجدتم ريحها: فاجلدوه' directly links the flogging to the detection of smell, establishing smell as a necessary condition.
The conditional particle 'in' (if) indicates that the ruling (hadd) is contingent on the condition (smell).
4
Note the ikhtilaf among Hanafi imams
Abu Hanifa measures taqaadum for testimony by a specific time, analogizing to zina. The Sahibayn measure it by disappearance of smell. For confession, Muhammad holds that taqaadum does not invalidate, while the Sahibayn require smell at the time of confession.
This ikhtilaf affects the practical application when evidence is delayed.
5
Understand the practical consequence
If a person confesses after the smell has vanished, Muhammad would still flog him, while the Sahibayn would not. The consensus scenario (smell dissipates en route to the ruler) confirms that if the smell was present at the time of seizure, flogging is still carried out.
The thamarah al-khilaf is clear: whether hadd is applied when the smell is absent at the time of confession.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses evidentiary issues in the hadd for drinking. The author begins by stating the general ruling that intoxication from nabidh entails the hadd. He then discusses the effect of taqaadum (passage of time) on evidence. It is agreed that taqaadum prevents acceptance of testimony, but there is a difference of opinion on its measure: Abu Hanifa measures it by a specific time period, analogizing to the hadd for zina, while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) measure it by the disappearance of the smell of wine from the person. For confession, Muhammad holds that taqaadum does not invalidate the confession, so if a person confesses after the smell is gone, he is still flogged. The two companions hold that flogging is only when the smell is present at the time of confession. The passage then mentions a scenario: if witnesses apprehend a person while he still smells of wine or is intoxicated, and they take him to the ruler but the smell dissipates en route, he is flogged by consensus. The commentary explains 'tartara' as shaking and having him exhale, and 'mazmaza' as moving him back and forth. This clearly indicates that the smell is a necessary condition for establishing the hadd.

الرائحة (al-raaiha): The smell of wine emanating from a person, used as evidence of drinking and a condition for establishing the hadd.
الترترة (al-tartara): The act of shaking a person and having him exhale to detect the smell of wine.

Conditions for Hadd on Nabidh: Intoxication, Volition, and Definition of Sukr
Expands the hadd ruling for nabidh with conditions and intra-madhhab definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions for imposing the hadd for drinking nabidh and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the definition of sukr.

Core Idea
The hadd for nabidh requires actual intoxication from nabidh, voluntary drinking, and applies only after sobriety — and the definition of 'intoxication' itself is disputed among Hanafi imams.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expands on the conditions for applying the hadd punishment to someone who drinks nabidh (date or grape drink other than khamr). Unlike grape wine, which triggers hadd immediately regardless of quantity, nabidh only triggers hadd if the drinker becomes intoxicated. The passage also spells out critical procedural safeguards: the drinking must be voluntary, the intoxication must be proven to be from nabidh (not another substance), and the punishment is delayed until after sobriety. Finally, the Hanafi imams differed on the legal definition of intoxication itself — a difference with practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its key exception
Start with the central ruling: hadd for nabidh requires intoxication, unlike khamr. Understand that the condition of intoxication is specific to nabidh; khamr is stricter because even a small amount triggers the hadd.
The text says 'إنما شرط السكر ; لأن شربه من غير سكر: لا يوجب الحد' — this is the core distinction.
2
List the three procedural conditions
From the passage, extract: (1) intoxication must be from nabidh, (2) drinking must be voluntary (taw'an), not coerced or due to necessity, and (3) the hadd is applied only after the intoxication wears off. Note the rationale: doubt (shubha) prevents hadd, and an intoxicated person lacks the capacity to be deterred.
The text mentions that smell or vomiting alone are insufficient, and that coercion or thirst create doubt.
3
Analyze the definition of sukr (intoxication)
Examine the three definitions within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa (total loss of discrimination and comprehension), the two companions (delirious speech and confusion), and Abu Yusuf's specific test (inability to recite Surah al-Kafirun). Understand the reasoning behind each.
The text states 'وعند أبي حنيفة... وعندهما... وعند أبي يوسف...' — showing the variations.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
The passage notes that most mashayikh preferred the definition of the two companions. This means a person who is delirious but can still distinguish basic things would be flogged under the preferred view, whereas under Abu Hanifa's view they would not. Abu Yusuf's test provides an additional practical criterion.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under which definition a particular person would be subject to hadd.

The Hadd for Drinking: Rulings and Evidence
Commentary on the fixed punishment for drinking wine and nabidh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hadd punishment for drinking intoxicants in the Hanafi school and apply the rules for evidence and flogging.

Core Idea
The hadd for drinking is 80 lashes for the free person, proven by two male witnesses or one confession, but retracted by doubt.

This lesson unpacks the legal framework for the fixed punishment (hadd) for consuming intoxicants according to the Hanafi school. We will examine the textual basis for the penalty, the nuanced conditions for its application, and the intra-Hanafi disagreements on flogging procedure and proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is drinking khamr (grape wine) or any other intoxicating drink (nabidh) that causes intoxication. The ruling is that the free person incurs 80 lashes; the slave incurs 40 lashes.
For wine, the hadd applies merely by drinking; for other drinks, it requires actual intoxication.
2
Determine the evidence for the hadd
The hadd is established by the testimony of two male witnesses or by the accused's single confession. The text explicitly states this, with an alternative view from Abu Yusuf requiring two confessions.
Women's testimony is not accepted in any hadd.
3
Analyze the effect of retraction and doubt
If the accused confesses and then retracts, the punishment is dropped because hudud are pure rights of Allah, and doubts repel them. Similarly, a confession made while intoxicated is invalid due to possible falsehood.
This differs from the hadd for qadhf (false accusation), which includes a private right.
4
Examine the flogging procedure
The lashes are distributed over the body, avoiding the face and head. The famous opinion (mashhur) is that the person is stripped, while Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that they are not stripped.
The dispute affects whether the lashes are applied directly to skin or over clothing.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the chapter of the punishment for drinking from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar. It explains the hadd (fixed penalty) for consuming intoxicants, including wine (khamr) and nabidh (date drink). The commentator clarifies the matn's rulings and addresses related issues. 

The matn first states the penalty: a free person who drinks wine or becomes intoxicated receives eighty lashes. The commentator relates a story illustrating the ignorance of some officials and then addresses the case of a drunkard's confession: if a person confesses while drunk, the hadd is not imposed because his confession is suspect; it may be false. This is a pure right of Allah, so doubts repel the punishment, unlike the hadd for false accusation (qadhf) which involves a private right. Similarly, apostasy by a drunkard does not dissolve the marriage because belief is not established during intoxication. 

The commentator then explains the pronunciation of 'sukr' with different vowel patterns, linking it to whether the penalty applies merely for drinking wine or only after intoxication from other drinks. The preferred view (of the Shaykh) is that for wine, the hadd is incurred by drinking alone, while for other intoxicants, it requires actual intoxication. 

Regarding the execution of flogging, the lashings are distributed over the body, avoiding the face and head. The famous opinion (mashhur) is that the person is stripped, but Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani holds that he should not be stripped. For a slave, the penalty is reduced to forty lashes because slavery halves the punishment. 

The passage also covers evidence: the hadd for drinking is proven by two male witnesses or by the accused's confession once. Abu Yusuf requires two confessions, but the relied-upon view is that one suffices. Retraction of confession is accepted, and the hadd is dropped, because it is a pure right of Allah. Women's testimony is not accepted in any hadd punishment. 

The commentary emphasizes the principles of dar' (repelling punishments in cases of doubt) and the distinction between rights of Allah and rights of servants.

Hadd for Qadhf: Conditions and Quranic Proof
Commentary on the hadd for qadhf with Quranic evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for imposing the hadd of qadhf and identify the Quranic proof (Surah An-Nur 4).

Core Idea
The hadd for slander applies only to explicit accusations of zina against a muhsan person, and the victim must demand it.

In this lesson, we examine the fixed punishment (hadd) for slander (qadhf) as outlined in the Hanafi school. The passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira explains the foundational Quranic verse, the conditions for the punishment (explicit accusation, muhsan victim, and the victim's demand), and clarifies that implicit accusations or affirming another's slander do not incur the hadd. Understanding these nuances is essential for properly applying this hudud ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is slandering (qadhf) a muhsan person with explicit words of zina. The ruling is that the hadd (80 lashes for a free person) becomes wajib upon proof.
The hadd is a fixed punishment; it is not discretionary.
2
Locate the dalil
The foundation is the Quranic verse from Surah An-Nur, ayah 4, which prescribes punishment for those who accuse chaste women of zina without bringing four witnesses.
By consensus, the verse applies to both men and women who are muhsan.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The verse mentions 'accusing chaste women' and requiring four witnesses. This implies that the accusation must be of zina (as witnesses are needed for zina) and that the victim must be chaste (muhsan). Thus, the hadd is established for explicit accusations of zina against muhsan persons.
The requirement of four witnesses is specific to zina, confirming the subject.
4
Identify the conditions for the hadd
Three conditions: (1) The accusation must be explicit (sarih) using words like 'ya zani' or 'anta zanayta'. (2) The victim must be muhsan (free, adult, Muslim, chaste). (3) The victim must demand the punishment (it is his right).
Implicit accusations (kinnayah) do not incur the hadd.
5
Note the exceptions
If the accusation is implicit, e.g., 'you are the most adulterous of people' (meaning most capable), no hadd. Also, affirming another's slander by saying 'you are truthful' does not incur hadd because the affirmation is not explicit slander.
The hadd is a severe punishment; thus its application is restricted.

Commentary

The ruling applies by consensus specifically to accusations of zina, not other sins. Finally, the victim's demand is a condition because the hadd is considered a right of the individual in this case.

Core Idea
Ambiguous slander incurs ta'zir, not hadd; only clear, intentional denial of lineage triggers the fixed punishment.

Understand the linguistic reason
The preposition 'ala' (upon) when combined with the verb 'zanat' (which can mean 'to commit adultery' or 'to climb') clearly indicates the meaning of climbing the mountain, not a sexual act. This removes the element of slander.
In contrast, 'fi' (in) would not clarify the meaning and could imply a direct accusation.
3
Note the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
There is a differing opinion (Qila: yuḥadd) that some Hanafi scholars hold, but the relied-upon position is the first one.
The difference arises from whether the word 'zanat' alone is sufficient for hadd regardless of context.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The mufta bihi (operative ruling for fatwa) is that no hadd is imposed, as stated in Al-Hidayah and favoured by the majority.
This is based on the principle that clear linguistic meaning takes precedence over literal possible meanings.

Commentary
This ruling distinguishes between 'fi al-jabal' and 'ala al-jabal'. When the preposition 'ala' (upon) is used, the phrase clearly means climbing the mountain (the verb with hamzah is appropriate), so there is no hadd punishment because it is not understood as slander. However, a different opinion is mentioned (perhaps from some scholars) that he is still punished. This difference is recorded in Al-Hidayah.

Key Terms
على (ala): Preposition indicating 'upon', which when used with the verb 'zanat' clarifies the meaning of climbing.
