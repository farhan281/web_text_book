Conditional Tadbir: Suspended on a Specific Event
Ruling on conditional tadbir and its effect on sale and freedom

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between absolute tadbir and conditional tadbir and state the ruling on selling the slave in each case.

Core Idea
A conditional tadbir does not bind; the slave can be sold until the condition occurs.

This lesson examines a commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri regarding tadbir suspended on a condition. The text clarifies that such a conditional promise does not create an immediate mudabbar status, allowing the master to sell the slave. Only if the condition (e.g., death from a specific illness) is met does freedom take effect from one-third of the estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of tadbir
Determine whether the tadbir is absolute (unconditional) or conditional (suspended on a specific event).
In this passage, the master says 'If I die from this illness, you are mudabbar.'
2
Assess immediate effect
According to the commentary, such a conditional tadbir does not make the slave a mudabbar immediately. The slave is not considered mudabbar.
Contrast with absolute tadbir, where the slave becomes mudabbar instantly.
3
Determine permissibility of sale
Since the slave is not a mudabbar, the master retains full ownership and may sell the slave. This is a key practical difference.
An absolute mudabbar cannot be sold.
4
Recognize the condition's fulfillment
If the master dies according to the specified condition (e.g., dies from that illness), then the slave becomes free.
Freedom is granted from the one-third bequest portion of the estate, similar to an absolute mudabbar.
5
Note the estate limitation
The freedom is effective only from the one-third share allowed for bequests (wasiyyah), not from the entire estate.
This confirms that tadbir is treated as a type of wasiyyah in Hanafi law.

Mudabbar's Intentional Killing of Master: Qisas and Sa'ayah
Ruling on mudabbar's intentional killing of master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two obligations (qisas and sa'ayah) on a mudabbar who intentionally kills his master, and explain why the heirs' choice to collect sa'ayah does not drop qisas.

Core Idea
When a mudabbar kills his master intentionally, qisas and sa'ayah combine — payment is for the invalid bequest, not for the life.

The mudabbar is a slave promised freedom upon the master's death. If he kills the master, the tadbir becomes a bequest that cannot benefit the killer. This lesson unpacks the dual liability: qisas for the murder and payment of his value (sa'ayah) to compensate the heirs for the lost slave asset.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
The act is intentional killing of the master by the mudabbar. This is a jinayah 'amdan against the master's life.
Intentional killing of a free person by a non-owner normally mandates qisas.
2
Determine the primary ruling: qisas
Qisas is wajib because the mudabbar is like a stranger in this context; ownership does not block retaliation for intentional murder.
The master's ownership is suspended for criminal liability.
3
Determine the secondary ruling: sa'ayah
The mudabbar must work to pay his full value because the tadbir is a bequest (وصية) and a killer cannot receive a bequest. However, the emancipation already occurred (at tadbir), so it cannot be undone; instead, the mudabbar compensates by paying his value.
Sa'ayah is compensation for the invalid bequest, not diyah for the murder.
4
Analyze the heirs' options
The heirs may choose either to take qisas immediately or to collect the sa'ayah and then kill him. Choosing sa'ayah does not waive qisas because the payment is separate (for the slave's value), not blood money.
The two obligations are cumulative.

Unintentional Killing and Comparison with Umm Walad
Rulings on unintentional jinayah and comparison with umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an unintentional killing by a mudabbar requires sa'ayah but not diyah, and why an umm walad owes no sa'ayah for unintentional killing of her master.

Core Idea
Unintentional jinayah is hadar (no claim) but sa'ayah still binds the mudabbar; umm walad is different because her freedom is not a bequest.

Not all jinayat are treated equally. When the mudabbar kills his master unintentionally, the criminal liability is dropped, but the bequest issue remains. The umm walad, however, has a different status. This lesson clarifies these distinctions and explores why the master cannot be held liable for crimes against his own mudabbar.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish intentional from unintentional killing by the mudabbar
Intentional triggers qisas + sa'ayah; unintentional triggers only sa'ayah because the crime itself is hadar (no liability).
Hadar means no claim arises for the death.
2
Understand why sa'ayah remains even in unintentional cases
Sa'ayah is not a blood money but compensation for the invalid bequest. Since the tadbir is a bequest, and a killer cannot inherit, the mudabbar must still pay his value.
Also applies for injuries short of death: the injury is hadar but sa'ayah is due.
3
Examine the master's liability for crimes against his mudabbar
If the master injures his mudabbar, it is hadar because the mudabbar is his property; no debt can arise.
Same logic for mudabbar's unintentional crime against master's slaves: hadar.
4
Compare with umm walad
Umm walad becomes free upon master's death immediately (not a bequest). Thus if she kills him unintentionally, no sa'ayah is required; if intentionally, qisas applies.
Female mudabbar (mudabbarah) is like a male: she must pay sa'ayah because her freedom is a bequest.

Inclusion of Diyah in One-Third Bequest
Ruling that diyah is part of the estate and included in a bequest of one-third

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that diyah (blood money) is part of the deceased's estate and explain why it is included in a bequest of one-third.

Core Idea
Diyah is estate property, so it counts toward the one-third bequest limit.

This lesson clarifies that when a person bequeaths one-third of their estate, the diyah (blood money) they are entitled to is included in that third. You might wonder why diyah is treated as part of the estate rather than as a separate payment to heirs. The text gives two key reasons: the relationship between bequest and inheritance, and the principle that diyah is the property of the deceased, used to settle debts and fulfill bequests like any other asset.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the passage to find the direct ruling: if a person bequeaths one-third of his property, his diyah enters that bequest.
The ruling is unconditional; diyah is automatically included.
2
Extract the first reason
The passage states: 'the bequest is the sibling of inheritance.' This means bequests and inheritances share similar legal limits and treatment; thus the one-third limit applies to all estate assets including diyah.
This is a legal maxim (al-wasiyyah ukht al-mirath).
3
Extract the second reason
The passage states diyah is 'the property of the deceased,' implying it is part of the estate. Therefore, debts and bequests are settled from it just like other assets.
This establishes diyah as an asset of the deceased, not a separate compensation for heirs.
4
Apply the ruling
Consider a scenario: estate without diyah = 200,000, diyah = 50,000. Total estate = 250,000. Bequest of one-third = 83,333.33. If the bequest exceeds one-third after including diyah, heirs' consent is required for the excess.
The one-third limit is calculated on total estate including diyah.

Commentary
This passage states a clear rule: if a person makes a will leaving one-third of his property (the maximum allowed for bequests), his diyah (blood money) is included in that third. The reasoning is twofold. First, 'the bequest is the sibling of inheritance' meaning both transfer property after death and are subject to the same limits. Second, diyah is considered part of the deceased's estate (mal al-mayyit). Therefore, it must be used to pay debts, and bequests are fulfilled from it, just like any other asset. This reinforces that diyah is not a separate fund but an integral part of the estate, subject to the same rules as other property.
