Maintenance for the Wife: Definition and Ruling
Opening of the Book of Maintenance: definition and basic ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of nafaqah and identify which wives are entitled to maintenance according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Maintenance is wajib for a wife: food, condiment, and utensils, regardless of her religion or wealth.

This passage opens the Book of Maintenance (Kitab al-Nafaqat) in a Hanafi commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri. It lays out the foundational ruling that a husband must provide maintenance to his wife, and clarifies who qualifies, what is included, and how it should be delivered. Understanding this definition and scope is essential for applying the law of familial obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify eligible wives
Determine which wives are entitled to maintenance: free women, mukatabah, and Kitabiyyah. Note the exceptions (slave-girl, mudabbara, umm walad).
Maintenance is wajib only in a valid marriage and its 'iddah.
2
Define nafaqah materially
Nafaqah consists of food (the staple grain) and condiment (the customary seasoning of the land).
The definition is in-kind, not cash, due to price fluctuations.
3
Identify additional obligations
The husband must also provide cooking utensils and eating/drinking vessels (e.g., pot, ewer, jar, ladle).
If the wife is of high status and refuses to cook, he must provide prepared food.
4
Understand the mode of provision
Maintenance should be given in kind (food and utensils), not in dirhams, because prices fluctuate.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on actual support over monetary equivalence.
5
Apply the conditions
Check the marriage validity (sahih vs. fasid) and the wife's status (free/mukatabah vs. slave).
No maintenance for fasid marriage or its 'iddah.

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Maintenance (Kitab al-Nafaqat) in the commentary on Mukhtasar al-Quduri. The passage type is a ruling statement with definition. The author first states the general ruling: nafaqah (maintenance) is obligatory on the husband for his wife. This applies whether she is Muslim or a Kitabiyyah (Jewish or Christian), and whether she is free or a mukatabah (a slave with a contract of manumission). However, if the wife is an ordinary slave-girl (amah), a mudabbarah (whose manumission is deferred after the master's death), or an umm walad (a slave who bore her master's child), she has no maintenance unless there is a specific arrangement (al-tabwutah — possibly a contractual condition). The obligation arises only in a valid (sahih) marriage and during its 'iddah; for a fasid (irregular) marriage and its 'iddah, no maintenance is due. The commentator clarifies the matn's reference to a disbeliever as meaning a Kitabiyyah. The definition of nafaqah itself is given: it consists of food and drink: the staple grain (qut) and the customary condiment (idam) of the locality. In terms of preparation, if the wife is from a high-status family (dhawat al-hay'at) and refuses to grind and bake, the husband must provide ready-prepared food; otherwise she must do it herself. The passage warns against giving maintenance in dirhams (cash) because prices fluctuate, so in-kind provision is preferred. Finally, the husband must also provide cooking utensils and eating/drinking vessels like the ewer (kuz), jar (jarrah), pot (qidr), and ladle (magharfah). This reflects the Hanafi view that maintenance is an obligation of support, not merely a financial payment.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance consisting of food and condiment (and cooking utensils) owed to a wife, relatives, or others.
مكاتبة (mukatabah): A slave-woman who has a contract (kitabah) to earn her freedom; she is entitled to maintenance from her husband as if free.
أمة (amah): A female slave; not entitled to maintenance from a husband (since marriage of a slave requires master's permission, but maintenance is on the master).

Types of Maintenance Obligation in Hanafi Fiqh
Classifies types and conditions of nafaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three categories of maintenance obligation and state the conditions that trigger or waive each type.

Core Idea
Maintenance is obligatory by marriage unconditionally; by kinship it depends on wealth and need; by ownership it is not detailed here.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira classifies the obligations of nafaqah (maintenance) under Hanafi law. Three distinct legal relationships generate maintenance duties: marriage, blood kinship, and ownership. Each has its own conditions and exceptions, reflecting the school's nuanced approach to balancing need, contract, and religious status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three bases
The passage opens by naming the three types: zawjiyyah (marriage), nasab (kinship), and milk (ownership). Trace each as you read.
Milk refers to maintenance of slaves, but the passage only details marriage and kinship.
2
Analyze marriage-based maintenance
Note that it is obligatory whether the husband is rich or poor, and it does not fall due to the wife's wealth or her disbelief. Understand the rationale: it resembles a commutative contract because it is given in exchange for the wife's submission (taslim al-nafs).
This contrasts with charity-based maintenance.
3
Examine kinship maintenance for children
The passage lists three subcategories: children, parents, and other relatives (dhawi al-arham). For children: the father must provide regardless of his wealth, but the child must be free, poor, and the father free. If the child has wealth, maintenance is from that wealth.
Note the condition of freedom: slavery nullifies the obligation.
4
Examine kinship maintenance for parents
For parents: maintenance is obligatory on the child only if the child is wealthy (musir) and the parents are poor. Importantly, it is not invalidated by the parents' disbelief.
This exception for disbelief applies only to parents, not other relatives.
5
Examine maintenance for other relatives
For dhawi al-arham: the giver must be wealthy, the recipients poor, and they must be Muslims. Disbelief blocks the obligation entirely.
This stricter condition reflects the lesser degree of kinship compared to parents.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary by classifying maintenance obligations into three types based on the relationship: marriage (zawjiyyah), kinship (nasab), and ownership (milk). The commentator explains each type's conditions and scope. First: maintenance of the wife (and those in her status, such as a wife in 'iddah) is obligatory regardless of the husband's financial state (whether wealthy or poor) and does not fall due to the wife's own wealth or her disbelief (if she is Kitabiyyah). The rationale given is that it resembles a commutative contract ('iwaḍ) because it is owed in exchange for the wife's submission (taslim al-nafs). This means it is a reciprocal obligation, not a charitable one. Second: maintenance by kinship has three subcategories: (a) maintenance of children: it is obligatory on the father whether he is rich or poor, but only if the child is free, the father is free, and the child is poor. If the child has wealth, his maintenance is from his own wealth. (b) Maintenance of parents: it is obligatory on the (adult) child only if he is wealthy and they are poor, and it does not fall due to their disbelief (they may be non-Muslims). (c) Maintenance of other relatives (dhawi al-arham, blood relatives within the prohibited degrees of marriage): it is obligatory on the person if he is wealthy and they are poor, but it is not obligatory if they are disbelievers. This distinction shows that Hanafi fiqh differentiates kinship obligations based on closeness and religious status.

Key Terms
معاوضة (mu'awadah): A commutative contract where each party gives something in exchange; here wife's maintenance is like a counter-value for her submission.
ذوي الأرحام (dhawi al-arham): Blood relatives who are not entitled to fixed shares in inheritance but still have mutual maintenance obligations.
موسر (musir): A person who possesses wealth beyond basic needs, sufficient to be obligated to support others.

The Three Categories of Maintenance Obligation
Classifies maintenance into three categories with conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of maintenance in Hanafi law and identify the conditions that apply to each.

Core Idea
Maintenance obligations arise from marriage, kinship, or ownership, each with distinct conditions.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar lays out the foundational classification of maintenance (nafaqah) in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding these three categories — marital, kinship, and ownership — is essential for navigating the detailed rules that follow. We will examine each category's conditions and rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Read the first sentence: maintenance is obligatory by three types: marriage (zawjiyya), kinship (nasab), and ownership (milk). Memorize this classification.
These are the three causes of maintenance in Hanafi law.
2
Analyze marital maintenance
Note that marital maintenance is obligatory regardless of the husband's wealth or poverty, and it does not drop due to the wife's wealth or disbelief. Understand the rationale: it resembles a compensatory exchange (mu'awada) for the wife's submission.
This unconditional nature is unique to marital maintenance.
3
Dissect kinship maintenance
Kinship maintenance has three subcategories: children, parents, and dhawu al-arham (other blood relatives). For each, note the conditions: (a) children: father must provide even if poor, child must be free and poor; (b) parents: child must be wealthy, parents poor; disbelief does not affect; (c) dhawu al-arham: person must be wealthy, relatives poor, and disbelief drops the obligation.
Pay attention to the differences in conditions and the role of wealth and faith.
4
Understand ownership maintenance
Maintenance by ownership applies to male and female slaves. The passage defers details to later discussion, but the basic principle is that the master is responsible for the slave's maintenance.
This category is analogous to the maintenance of animals.

When Wife's Maintenance Becomes Obligatory: Al-Quduri's Tarjih
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on whether maintenance is due upon contract or after transfer to husband's house.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on when wife's maintenance becomes obligatory and apply al-Quduri's tarjih to determine the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Hanafi school splits: maintenance due upon contract or upon transfer? Al-Quduri chose Abu Yusuf's view.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute on the timing of a wife's maintenance. While the authoritative narration (zahir al-riwaya) holds that maintenance is due from the moment of a valid marriage contract, Abu Yusuf required actual transfer to the husband's house. Al-Quduri himself preferred Abu Yusuf's view, but added important exceptions when the husband waives his right to demand transfer or when the wife refuses to move to collect her dower. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply tarjih within the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the zahir al-riwaya position
Locate the statement: maintenance is obligatory upon valid contract even without transfer. This is the most authoritative view within the Hanafi school.
Zahir al-riwaya comes from the six core books of Imam Muhammad.
2
State Abu Yusuf's opposing view
Note that Abu Yusuf conditions maintenance on the wife's actual transfer (nuqla) to the husband's house. Without transfer, no maintenance is initially due.
Abu Yusuf's reasoning: maintenance is in exchange for being at the husband's disposal.
3
Analyze the second narration from Abu Yusuf
Read the nuance: if the wife requests maintenance before transfer and the husband does not demand transfer, she receives maintenance because the transfer is his right.
This shows that the husband can waive his right, restoring her entitlement.
4
Identify the exception for dower (mahr)
If the husband demands transfer but the wife refuses in order to collect her unpaid mahr, she still gets maintenance because mahr is also her right.
But if mahr is paid or deferred and she refuses, she becomes nashiza and loses maintenance.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Al-Quduri chose Abu Yusuf's view as the operative position. Thus, maintenance is not initially obligatory until transfer, unless the husband waives his right to demand transfer.
Tarjih authority: Al-Quduri in his Mukhtasar.

Core Idea
Clothing maintenance is wajib; the type and replacement depend on custom, financial status, and normal wear.

In this lesson, we explore the detailed rulings on what clothing a husband must provide for his wife as part of nafaqah. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah specifies different items for different seasons and financial abilities, and also clarifies when replacement is obligatory. Understanding these details helps ensure the obligation is fulfilled correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic obligatory items
The passage begins by stating the basic minimum: two shifts, two head-coverings, and a wrap. This is the default obligation unless otherwise specified by custom or financial status.

2
Distinguish between poor and wealthy husband
The text from al-Yanabee differentiates between a poor (mu'sir) and wealthy (mūsir) husband. For the poor, winter clothing includes a shift, wrap, head-covering, and cloak; summer includes shift, head-covering, and wrap. For the wealthy, higher quality items are specified, such as a Herawi shift and silk head-covering.
The descriptions (e.g., Herawi, Dinawari) indicate high-quality items based on regional repute.

Understand the replacement rules based on time and wear
If the clothing is prescribed for a six-month period, the wife cannot demand new clothing until the period ends. However, if the garment tears before the period due to normal wear, it must be replaced. If it tears due to abnormal use or neglect, no replacement is required.
Normal wear means the garment would not have torn if worn normally.
4
Analyze scenarios of remaining garments and savings
If the garment remains in good condition after the period because it was not worn, or because she wore another garment, or wore it only every other day, then new clothing must be provided. Similarly, if she saves her maintenance money and does not spend it, she still gets a new maintenance (but the existing clothing is taken into account).
The key is whether the wife actually used the clothing normally.
5

According to al-Khujandi, if the garment is stolen, no replacement is required. If the wife economizes on her maintenance and has extra at the end of the period, a new maintenance is due. If the maintenance is lost but the clothing remains, she has no claim for new clothing from the husband.
These rulings aim to prevent unjust enrichment or double claiming.

Ruling statement on replacement of clothing and maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a husband must replace clothing or provide additional maintenance, and identify exceptions such as theft and the wife's savings.

Core Idea
Normal wear does not obligate replacement until period end; abnormal tear does; theft does not obligate replacement.

Clothing (kiswah) is a wajib component of nafaqah, but its replacement is not automatic. This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah lays out precise conditions: when a garment tears abnormally, when it remains unworn, and what happens if the wife saves or loses her maintenance. Understanding these rules helps ensure the husband fulfills his obligation correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
Recognize that clothing is prescribed for a fixed period (e.g., six months). No replacement is due until the period ends, unless an exception applies.

2
Analyze the condition for abnormal tearing
If the garment tears before the period ends, determine whether the tear would have occurred under normal wear. If yes, replacement is wajib; if no (i.e., it would not have torn normally), it is not wajib.
Normal wear is judged by the custom of similar women.
3
Examine post-period scenarios
After the period, if the garment is still intact due to minimal use (not worn, worn sparingly, or alternating), a new set is wajib. Otherwise, if it was worn normally and lasted, no replacement.
The key is whether the wife economized on usage.
4
Distinguish clothing from maintenance
If the wife saves or loses maintenance money, she still receives the next maintenance. But if maintenance is lost while clothing is with her, nothing extra is due.
Maintenance (nafaqah) is a monetary obligation; clothing is in-kind.
5
Note the theft exception
If the garment is stolen, the husband is not obliged to replace it. This is a special exception reported from Al-Khujandi.
The wife's loss from theft is not transferred to the husband.

Core Idea
A nashiz wife loses maintenance until she returns; a minor incapable of enjoyment has no maintenance even if she surrenders.

Maintenance (nafaqah) is a fundamental right of the wife in a valid marriage. However, this right is not absolute: it depends on her fulfilling certain conditions, such as being available in her husband's home and being capable of marital enjoyment. Identify the two cases

Nushuz is defined in the commentary as the wife leaving her husband's home without his permission and without a valid right. If she does so, maintenance is suspended until she returns.

If the husband is living in the wife's house and she prevents him from entering, she is nashiz. However, if she asks him to move her to his own home or to rent a separate home and he refuses, she may prevent him without losing maintenance.
This nuance protects the wife's right to have her own marital home.

Maintenance is linked to the benefit (tamkin) that the wife provides; if she cannot provide the primary benefit (intimacy), maintenance falls away, but mahr is for the contract itself.

This is a clear analogy; the ruling of the asl (invalidity) applies to the far'.

This exception overrides the general rule due to the special status of umm walad.

The Shafi'i school holds that a pregnant widow does receive nafaqah; the Hanafi school restricts this to the umm walad exception.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf based on different approaches to the proof.

Ruling with subcases on disobedience

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A wife who causes separation through disobedience loses her nafaqah, unless the disobedience occurs after divorce or the separation is permissible.

This lesson covers the second major ruling from the passage: any separation caused by the wife's disobedience (ma'siyah) results in loss of nafaqah. Taslim, Illness, and the Ratqa' Wife: Maintenance Entitlement
Expansion of matn on taslim and special cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the timing of illness relative to taslim (delivery of herself) affects the wife's maintenance, and state the ruling for a ratqa' (obstructed) woman.

Core Idea
Valid taslim triggers maintenance; illness after taslim is irrelevant, but illness before taslim invalidates it.

This lesson addresses two special cases in spousal maintenance: the effect of illness on the validity of taslim (the wife's delivery of herself), and the maintenance of a woman with a vaginal obstruction (ratqa'). Abu Yusuf's rulings clarify the conditions that render the wife entitled to her maintenance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base principle
Maintenance is due upon valid taslim (the wife placing herself under the husband's authority). Understand what constitutes valid taslim.
Taslim is fulfilled when the wife is in the husband's home and accessible to him, even if not physically intimate.
2
Analyze the illness scenarios
Abu Yusuf distinguishes: If the wife performs taslim while healthy, then falls ill, she retains maintenance because taslim was valid. If she falls ill first, then attempts taslim, the taslim is invalid because she is unable to fulfill marital rights.
The illness here is assumed to prevent full conjugal life, though she may still be present in the home.
3
Apply to the ratqa' case
A ratqa' woman (with an obstruction preventing intercourse) is not entitled to maintenance until the husband transfers her to his home. Once transferred, he cannot return her because he can enjoy her without intercourse (like a menstruating woman).
The analogy to a menstruating woman shows that non-penetrative enjoyment is sufficient to fulfill the husband's right.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: If a ratqa' wife falls ill before being taken home, she gets no maintenance; if after, she gets it. Also, the husband cannot abandon her after taking her home.
This differs from other schools that might consider the obstruction a defect allowing annulment.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi position
The Hanafi view (via Abu Yusuf) balances the wife's right to maintenance with the husband's right to enjoyment, recognizing that intercourse is not the only form of marital intimacy.
This shows the school's nuanced approach to 'istimta' (enjoyment).

Husband's Inability to Pay Maintenance: No Separation, Borrowing Recourse
Ruling on husband's inability to pay maintenance and wife's borrowing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband cannot pay maintenance and apply the rules of borrowing with or without judicial permission.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, a husband's poverty does not dissolve the marriage; the wife must borrow to cover her needs.

When a husband cannot afford to pay his wife's maintenance (nafaqah), Islamic law balances the wife's right to support with the marriage's continuity. The Hanafi school takes a distinctive position: separation is not permitted due to poverty alone. Instead, the wife is directed to borrow on the husband's behalf. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the conditions under which the debt is enforceable, and the critical distinction between maintenance that has been judicially fixed and that which has not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage begins: 'ومن أعسر بنفقة امرأته لم يفرق بينهما ويقال لها استديني عليه'. Identify the two key elements: no separation, and instruction to borrow.
This is the settled Hanafi position (zahir al-riwayah).
2
Understand the borrowing mechanism
The commentary explains 'فائدة' (benefit): borrowing with permission (from husband or judge) allows the wife to assign the creditor to claim directly from the husband.
This is called 'إحالة' (assignment of debt).
3
Distinguish scenarios of permission
Three scenarios: (1) With permission – creditor can sue husband. (2) Without permission – wife alone is liable. (3) Before judge's order and maintenance not fixed – wife is a volunteer, no claim on husband.
The key variable is whether maintenance was previously fixed (by court or agreement).
4
Apply the condition of fixed maintenance
If maintenance was already prescribed (فرضت) before borrowing without permission, then it becomes a debt on the husband. If not, it is voluntary.
This protects the husband from unauthorized debts while ensuring the wife's basic needs are met.
5
Contrast with other schools
Note that the Hanafi school differs from others (e.g., Shafi'i) that allow separation or divorce in case of inability. The practical consequence is that the marriage remains intact and the wife's recourse is through borrowing.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf (difference of opinion) within the Ummah.

Maintenance When Husband Is Unable to Pay
Ruling on separation when husband cannot provide maintenance and borrowing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance when the husband is unable to pay, and explain the conditions under which the wife's borrowing becomes a debt on the husband.

Core Idea
No separation for non-payment; wife borrows with permission, otherwise volunteer.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a key fiqh question: what happens when a husband cannot pay maintenance (nafaqah)? The Hanafi position is clear – the marriage remains intact, and no judicial separation is granted due to poverty. Instead, the wife is instructed to borrow on the husband’s behalf, but the liability for repayment depends on whether she acted with permission or after a court order. Understanding these nuances is crucial for applying the ruling correctly in practice.

1

1) Borrowing with permission: husband liable. 2) Borrowing without permission: wife liable. 3) Borrowing before judge's order when no maintenance was decreed: wife is a volunteer, husband owes nothing.

4
Determine the husband's liability
The husband is only liable for repayment in two cases: when the wife borrowed with his permission or after a court order, or when maintenance had been decreed previously, regardless of the order of borrowing.
This protects the husband from unauthorized borrowing while ensuring the wife's needs are met under court supervision.

Maintenance from Absent Husband's Property: Eligible Relatives and Procedure
Judge's order for maintenance from absent husband's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of relatives eligible for maintenance from an absent husband's property and identify the conditions under which a judge may order such maintenance.

Core Idea
Only those whose maintenance is obligatory without a judge's order—wife, minor children, parents—can claim from the absent husband's property.

When a husband is absent and leaves property with a custodian, the wife, minor children, and parents may need financial support. The Hanafi school allows the judge to order maintenance from that property for these specific relatives only, and he takes a guarantor to protect the absent husband's rights. This lesson explains the procedure and the rationale behind the limitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the property relationship
Understand the scenario: an absent husband has property in the hand of a custodian (trustee, debtor, partner) who may or may not acknowledge the property and marriage.
The custodian could be holding the property as amanah, dayn, or mudarabah.
2
Examine the conditions for the judge's order
The custodian must acknowledge both the property and the marriage, or the judge must have independent knowledge. Denial of either blocks the order.
If the custodian denies both, no order; if he denies only one, still no order according to the commentary.
3
Understand the guarantor requirement
The judge takes a guarantor from the recipients (wife, etc.) to protect the absent husband. If the husband later proves the payments were not due, the guarantor is liable.
This is a precautionary measure for the absent owner.
4
Analyze the wife's oath
The judge also makes the wife swear that she has not received maintenance and that there is no cause (like nushuz) that drops her right.
This further protects the husband's property.
5
Conclude the restricted categories
Only those whose maintenance is obligatory before the judge's decree—wife, minor children, parents—are eligible. The decree merely assists them in collection.
Other relatives, even if needy, are not included because their maintenance is not automatically wajib.
