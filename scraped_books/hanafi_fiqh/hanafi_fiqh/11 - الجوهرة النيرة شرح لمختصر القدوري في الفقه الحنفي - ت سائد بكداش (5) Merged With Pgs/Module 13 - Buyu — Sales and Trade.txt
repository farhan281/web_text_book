Claiming a Child from a Shared Slave-Girl
Explains umm walad status and obligations when one co-owner claims the child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when one co-owner claims a child from a shared slave-girl and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on divisibility of istilad.

Core Idea
Istilad is indivisible: one co-owner's claim makes the entire slave-girl an umm walad.

When two people co-own a slave-girl and one of them has a child by her and claims it, what is the ruling? This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains that she becomes an umm walad for the claimant, with specific monetary obligations to the partner. It also highlights a disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students (the Sahibayn) on whether istilad (making her an umm walad) can be divided according to ownership shares. Understanding the relied-upon position is key for correctly applying the law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and basic ruling
Note the case: a shared slave-girl, one co-owner claims the child. The matn states she becomes an umm walad for him, and he owes half her 'uqr and half her value, but nothing for the child.
The claim is effective immediately – lineage is established.
2
Analyze the obligations
He pays half the value because he destroyed the partner's share by istilad (a transfer guarantee like sale). He pays half the 'uqr as compensation for intercourse under shubha (doubt) that prevented the hadd penalty. No payment for the child because lineage relates back to conception when the mother was still shared.
'Uqr is like dower; it is owed regardless of his wealth.
3
Understand the dispute on divisibility
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that istilad is indivisible – the whole slave-girl becomes umm walad. Abu Hanifa says only the claimant's share becomes umm walad, then he acquires the partner's share by a legal fiction and compensates.
The matn's phrasing 'wa sārat umm walad lahu' supports the Sahibayn's view.
4
Compare the proofs
The Sahibayn use an analogy: a single child cannot be from two waters, so the cause (conception) is indivisible, and so is the effect. Abu Hanifa argues that shares are capable of separate ownership by legal fiction, so divisibility is possible.
The tarjih (preference) is for the Sahibayn's view as it is the mufta bihi position.
5
Apply the ruling to a variant case
If both co-owners claim the child, lineage is established from both because the child's lineage must be whole – it is indivisible. No payment is due in that scenario.
The text briefly mentions this as an adjacent ruling.

Commentary
This passage addresses the situation where one co-owner of a slave-girl claims her child as his own. The ruling states that she becomes an umm walad (mother of his child) for him, meaning she is freed upon his death. He must pay half her 'uqr (a compensatory payment similar to dower for intercourse under a defective contract) and half her market value to his co-owner, but nothing for the child's value. The reason is that istilad (making her an umm walad) is indivisible: the whole slave-girl becomes an umm walad even though he only owns half. Imam Abu Hanifa differs slightly: he says only the claimant's share becomes umm walad, but then he automatically acquires the other share (by a legal fiction) and compensates the partner. The commentary explains the logic: the child's lineage traces back to the time of conception when the mother was shared, so the partner's share is unaffected; thus no compensation for the child. The half value is a transfer guarantee (daman naql) like a sale, so it applies equally to rich and poor. The half 'uqr compensates for the intercourse under ambiguity (shubha) that prevented the hadd penalty.

استيلاد (istilad): The act of a master impregnating his slave-girl, resulting in her becoming an umm walad (free upon his death).
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave-girl who has borne her master's child; she becomes free upon his death and cannot be sold.
عقر ('uqr): A compensatory payment due for intercourse under a defective contract or with a shared slave-girl, equal to the dower (mahr) of a free woman.

Joint Paternity Claims by Co-Owners: The Maximum Number Dispute
Explains joint paternity, 'uqr offset, and disagreement on maximum claimants

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on the maximum number of co-owners who can jointly claim paternity from a shared slave-girl and apply the 'uqr offset rule in such scenarios.

Core Idea
Lineage can be established from multiple co-owners, but the Sahibayn limit the number; Abu Hanifa allows any number.

This passage tackles a unique scenario in Hanafi fiqh: when two or more co-owners of a slave-girl both claim her newborn child. The core ruling is that lineage is established from all claimants, and the mother becomes an umm walad for each. A compulsory payment ('uqr) arises but offsets automatically because each co-owner owes the same amount. The main point of contention among the early Hanafi masters is whether there is a cap on the number of co-owners who can make such a joint claim. Let's examine the ruling, the offset logic, and the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base scenario and ruling
The passage begins with the case where both co-owners claim the child together. The ruling is that lineage is established from both, and the mother becomes umm walad for both. Each co-owner owes half the 'uqr (compensation for the diminished value of the slave-girl due to the child), but these debts cancel each other out.
This base ruling is agreed upon by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.
2
Understand the 'uqr offset reasoning
The text explains why the 'uqr offsets: each co-owner had intercourse with the share belonging to the other, so each owes the same amount. Since the obligations are equal and mutual, they cancel out (qisas in the sense of set-off).
The offset is automatic; no actual payment changes hands.
3
Distinguish the three opinions on maximum number
The passage then presents the ikhtilaf: Abu Hanifa allows any number of co-owners (two, three, four, five, or more) to jointly claim the child and establish lineage from all. Abu Yusuf limits it to two, Muhammad limits it to three. Note that the text does not give a specific proof for each; it merely states the positions.
This is a pure intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf al-madhhab).
4
Apply precedence rules for different-status claimants
The passage adds several scenarios where claimants have different legal statuses: Muslim vs. dhimmi, Kitabi vs. Majusi, slave vs. mukatib. In each case, the one with higher status takes precedence (the child is attributed to him).
This applies only if they do not jointly claim; if they do, the joint ruling applies with precedence? Actually the text says 'so the Muslim has precedence' – meaning the child is attributed to him alone if they dispute.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference between the opinions on maximum number: under Abu Hanifa, any number of co-owners can inherit from the child and have umm walad status; under Abu Yusuf, only up to two; under Muhammad, up to three. This also affects the distribution of the 'uqr and other rights.
Later Hanafi fatwa works may prefer the Sahibayn views for practical reasons, but the matn's implication supports Abu Hanifa.

Payment Terms in Kitabah: All Three Forms Permissible
Ruling on payment terms in kitabah with Shafi'i opposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on payment terms in kitabah and explain the Shafi'i objection and the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
In kitabah, the Hanafi school allows immediate, deferred, or installment payment; the Shafi'i school requires at least two installments.

A kitabah contract is an agreement where a slave earns freedom in exchange for fixed payments. This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses the permissible payment structures. The Hanafi school, valuing contractual freedom, allows flexible terms, while Imam al-Shafi'i insists on a minimum of two installments. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your appreciation of the Hanafi commitment to 'al-asl fi al-‘uqud al-ibahah' (original permissibility in contracts).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State the three permissible payment forms from the matn: immediate (hālan), deferred (mu’ajjalan), and installments (munajjaman).
The text uses 'halan', 'mu'ajjalan', and 'munajjaman'.
2
Locate the opposing school's view
Identify al-Shafi'i's objection: immediate payment is invalid, and at least two installments are required.
The source says 'la yajuzu halan, wa la budda min najmyn'.
3
Analyse the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school relies on the general command to fulfil contracts (Qur'an 5:1) and the principle of permissibility in the absence of prohibition.
No explicit prohibition for immediate payment exists in the primary texts.
4
Analyse the Shafi'i proof
Al-Shafi'i analogises (qiyas) to the Prophet's reported practice of using two installments, and considers immediate payment akin to a cash sale of a free person.
This qiyas is based on a specific sunnah, not a general principle.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If a contract is made with immediate payment, it is valid and binding for Hanafis but invalid for Shafi'is unless restructured into at least two installments.
Practical consequence affects enforceability in different jurisdictions.

Voluntary Acts (Tabarru') of the Mukatab: Loans, Guarantees, and Forgiveness
Rulings on qard, kafala with permission, and afw 'an al-qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the voluntary acts prohibited for a mukatab and explain the condition under which a guarantee becomes binding.

Core Idea
A mukatab cannot give loans or forgive retaliation; his capacity for donation is restricted to protect the master's right.

This lesson explores the legal capacity of a mukatab (a slave working for freedom) regarding acts of pure beneficence. The text addresses three specific acts: lending money, acting as a guarantor, and forgiving retaliation. Understanding these restrictions is crucial because they contrast with the mukatab's broader capacity in trade.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The text states that the mukatab does not give a loan (qard) because it is tatawwu' (voluntary). This is a prohibition based on lack of capacity.
Qard is a contract of lending, which is a gratuitous transfer.
2
Note the exception for kafala with permission
If the master permits guarantee, the mukatab is liable after freedom. This shows that with permission, he can undertake obligations.
Without permission, the guarantee would not be binding.
3
Identify the third prohibition
He cannot forgive retaliation (afw 'an al-qisas) for wounds or killing, as it is also tatawwu'.
Forgiveness of qisas is a right of the victim; the mukatab cannot waive it.
4
Understand the principle
The common thread is that these are donations (tabarru') which are restricted for the mukatab to protect the master's interest in the kitaba payments.
This principle derives from the mukatab's limited legal capacity.

Third, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that the mukatab may buy and sell in any manner (i.e., he is fully competent in trade), while his main students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) restrict him to customary transactions ('ala al-ma'ruf) – meaning he should not engage in unusual or speculative trades. The relied-upon position in the school is often the Sahibayn's view for caution. Fourth, the mukatab's acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt is valid, and he may also collect (istifa') debts owed to him. This shows that while his voluntary giving is restricted, his commercial dealings are allowed within limits.

المعروف (al-ma'rūf): Customary or recognized practice in business; the Sahibayn restrict the mukatab to such transactions.
الإقرار (al-iqrār): Acknowledgment of a debt; valid from the mukatab.

Khamr is intoxicating grape juice; Abu Hanifa requires foaming, the two companions do not.

The first two rulings establish what khamr is and when a drink earns that name. Understanding this foundational definition is crucial because all subsequent rulings—impurity, prohibition, penalty—depend on it. Impurity and Monetary Value of Khamr
Rulings on impurity, istihlal, and taqwim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the rulings on the impurity of khamr, the disbelief of those who deem it lawful, and the absence of monetary value for Muslims.

Core Idea
Khamr is severe impurity; considering it lawful is disbelief; it has no value for Muslims, so sale is invalid.

Rulings three through six establish the legal consequences of the definition. Because khamr is filth (rijs), it is inherently forbidden and severely impure. Its value is negated for Muslims, leading to practical rules about compensation, sale, and debt settlement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of prohibition
Note that khamr is haram li-‘aynihi (inherently forbidden) because it is rijs (filth), not because it intoxicates.
This is a key Hanafi principle: the prohibition is unconditional.
2
Understand the degree of impurity
Khamr is classified as severe impurity (najāsah mughallazah), like urine.
Severe impurity requires special purification (e.g., washing seven times with water and soil).
3
Recognize the consequence of istihlāl
Whoever considers khamr lawful (mustahill) becomes a disbeliever because the prohibition is known by necessity from religion.
This is a ruling on i‘tiqād (belief).
4
Grasp the negation of monetary value
For a Muslim, khamr has no taqwīm (value). Thus, destroying it does not require compensation, and its sale is invalid.
The passage explains that Allah degraded it, so it cannot be honored with value.
5
Apply the debt scenario
A Muslim cannot pay or receive a debt from wine sale; but if the debtor is a dhimmi, the Muslim creditor may accept it.
This is due to the permissibility of wine trade among non-Muslims.

Prohibition of Benefit, Penalty, Cooking, and Vinegar
Rulings on benefit, penalty, cooking, and vinegar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the prohibition of benefiting from khamr, the hadd penalty for drinking, the effect of cooking, and the permissibility of making vinegar.

Core Idea
Benefiting from khamr is haram; the drinker is flogged; cooking does not make it pure; but turning it into vinegar is allowed.

The final four rulings complete the legal picture: the prohibition of any benefit (including medicinal), the fixed penalty for drinking even without intoxication, the ineffectiveness of cooking after foaming, and the debated issue of making vinegar. These rulings show how the Hanafi school balances strictness with practical solutions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the prohibition of benefit
Because khamr is impure, any form of benefit (such as using it in food, medicine, or perfume) is forbidden.
This is based on the general rule that benefiting from impurities is haram.
2
Learn the hadd penalty
Drinking khamr incurs the fixed punishment of flogging, even if the drinker does not become intoxicated.
The hadd is 80 lashes in the Hanafi school, as recorded from the companions.
3
Analyze the effect of cooking
Cooking khamr after it has foamed does not remove its impurity or prohibition, but the drinker is not flogged unless intoxicated.
The penalty for a small amount applies only to raw, uncooked khamr.
4
Examine the permissibility of making vinegar
It is permissible to turn khamr into vinegar (takhallul), but al-Shafi'i disagrees.
This is a cross-madhhab ikhtilāf.

Rulings on Cooked Grape Juice (Bādiq and Munṣif)
Classification and prohibition of partially cooked grape juice

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on bādiq and munṣif and identify the conditions that trigger their prohibition.

Core Idea
Partially cooked grape juice is haram when it ferments and intoxicates.

We now examine the rulings on cooked grape juice, a common question in daily life. The Hanafi school distinguishes between types based on how much water evaporates during cooking. Even after cooking, these drinks remain prohibited if they become intoxicating. This lesson clarifies the conditions that make bādiq (minimally cooked) and munṣif (halved) forbidden.

1
Identify the two types
The passage describes bādiq (cooked until less than two-thirds gone) and munṣif (cooked until half gone).

Kitabi Slaughter: Conditional Permissibility
Ruling with conditional exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the slaughter of a Kitabi and identify the condition that makes it impermissible.

Core Idea
Kitabi slaughter is halal unless they believe Jesus is God, making them like polytheists.

The Hanafi school generally permits eating from the slaughter of a Muslim or a Kitabi (Jew or Christian). However, an important condition from al-Mustasfa clarifies that this assumes the Kitabi does not hold a belief that contradicts tawhid. In this lesson, we examine when a Christian's slaughter becomes impermissible based on their doctrinal beliefs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling
The passage states that the slaughter of a Muslim and a Kitabi is halal. Recognize this as the default Hanafi position.
2
Locate the condition
The text from al-Mustasfa adds a condition: the Kitabi must not believe the Messiah is God. If he does, the ruling changes.
This condition is a later elaboration, not part of the original text.
3
Apply the analogy
When the condition is violated, the Kitabi is likened to a Majusi (Zoroastrian), whose slaughter is not halal. Understand the analogical reasoning: belief in divinity of a creature renders the person a polytheist.
4
Determine the practical result
If a Christian explicitly holds the Trinitarian belief that Jesus is God, their slaughter is impermissible. If they do not, it remains halal.
This applies to Christians who adhere to mainstream Trinitarian theology.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the general Hanafi ruling that the slaughtered animal of a Muslim and a Kitabi (Jew or Christian) is halal. However, it adds a condition from al-Mustasfa: if the Kitabi holds the belief that Jesus (Messiah) is God (i.e., the Trinitarian Christian doctrine), then he is considered like a Majusi (Zoroastrian), and his slaughter is not permissible. This condition reflects a nuanced view that not all Kitabis are treated alike; their monotheism is relevant.

Key Terms
ذبيحة (dhabīḥah): The animal that has been ritually slaughtered according to Islamic law.
كتابي (kitābī): A person who follows a revealed scripture, specifically Jews and Christians in Islamic law.
مجوسي (majūsī): A Zoroastrian; their slaughter is not halal according to the jumhur.

Key Takeaway
A Kitabi's slaughter is halal only if he does not hold the belief that Jesus is God; otherwise, it is impermissible.
