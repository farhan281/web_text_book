Master's Liability for Injuring the Mukatab or Her Child
Ruling on master's liability for injury to mukatab and her child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the master's liability for injuring his mukatab or her child and distinguish the effect of intent on the ruling.

Core Idea
A master must pay compensation for unintentional injury; intentional injury drops qisas due to legal doubt.

This lesson addresses the master's tort liability towards his mukatab (a slave under a kitaba contract) and her child. The Hanafi school balances the master's ownership rights with protecting the mukatab from misuse of power.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The master injures the mukatab or her child. The ruling is that he must pay arsh (compensation for injury).
This is for unintentional injury, as clarified in the commentary.
2
Distinguish by intent
If the injury is intentional, the penalty of qisas (retaliation) is dropped due to legal doubt (shubha) arising from the master's partial ownership.
Shubha is a key Hanafi principle that prevents hudud.
3
Understand the basis
The commentary refers to the same reasoning used for intercourse (the master's shubha in ownership over the mukatab's person).

Commentary
This passage contains four distinct rulings with brief commentaries. First, if the master injures the female mukatab or her child, he must pay the arsh (compensation for injury). The commentator clarifies that this applies to unintentional injury; intentional injury may lead to qisas being dropped due to the legal doubt (shubha) regarding the master's right over the mukatab. Second, if the master destroys her property, he must compensate it because in matters of the mukatab's earnings, the master is treated as a stranger (i.e., he has no special privilege). Third, if a mukatab buys his father or son, they become part of his kitaba contract: they are freed when he is freed and enslaved if he is enslaved, and he cannot sell them. This extends to all blood relatives through birth (like grandparents and grandchildren). The mukatab also cannot return them due to a defect unless he becomes unable to pay the kitaba. Fourth, if a mukatab buys his umm walad (a female slave who has borne his child), her child from the mukatab enters the kitaba (the child is treated as owned by the mukatab and follows his kitaba), and he cannot sell the umm walad. This applies whether he buys her with her child or buys the child afterwards.

Key Terms
أرش الجناية (arsh al-jinaya): Compensation for bodily injury or killing, quantified according to the type of harm.
شبهة (shubha): Legal doubt that prevents the application of criminal penalties (hudud or qisas).

Master's Liability for Destroying Mukatab's Property
Ruling on master destroying mukatab's property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule when a master destroys the property of his mukatab and explain the reasoning.

Core Idea
In matters of the mukatab's earnings and property, the master is treated as a stranger and must compensate for any damage.

This lesson covers a crucial principle: the master cannot treat the mukatab's property as his own. If he damages it, he must pay compensation just like any outsider.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The master destroys property belonging to the mukatab. Ruling: he must compensate (gharam).
2
Understand the reasoning
The master is considered a stranger (ajnabi) with respect to the mukatab's earnings. He has no special right to dispose of or damage that property.
This affirms the mukatab's independent ownership of his earnings.

Mukatab's Purchase of Father or Son
Ruling on mukatab purchasing close blood relative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the legal effects when a mukatab buys his close blood relative and identify exceptions to the inability to return such a purchase.

Core Idea
A mukatab who buys his father, son, or other lineal relative brings that relative into his kitaba contract; they cannot be sold and their status follows his.

This lesson addresses the scenario where a mukatab (a slave with a contract of manumission) purchases a close relative such as a father or son. The Hanafi school integrates these relatives into the mukatab's kitaba, meaning they are freed when he is freed and enslaved if he fails, and they cannot be sold by the mukatab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the primary ruling
The mukatab buys his father or son. Ruling: the relative enters the mukatab's kitaba (contract).
This means the relative becomes part of the mukatab's ownership under the kitaba agreement.
2
Understand the consequences
The relative is freed when the mukatab is freed and is enslaved if the mukatab is enslaved. The mukatab cannot sell the relative.
This applies to all blood relatives through birth: grandparents, grandchildren, etc.
3
Note the exception on return
The mukatab cannot return the relative due to a defect or deficiency unless the mukatab becomes unable to pay (cajiz). In that case, he can return the relative.
This protects the other party's rights until the mukatab's inability is established.

Mukatab's Purchase of His Umm Walad
Ruling on mukatab buying his umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a mukatab buys his umm walad (female slave who bore his child) and explain the status of her child.

Core Idea
A mukatab who buys his umm walad brings her child into his kitaba and cannot sell her; the child's status follows the father's kitaba.

This lesson covers the special case where a mukatab purchases a female slave who has borne his child (umm walad). The Hanafi ruling integrates both the mother and child into the mukatab's kitaba with protections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The mukatab buys his umm walad (the mother of his child). Ruling: her child (from the mukatab) enters the mukatab's kitaba.
The child is considered owned by the mukatab and follows his status.
2
Understand the prohibition on selling the umm walad
The mukatab is not permitted to sell the umm walad.
This is akin to the rule for a master's umm walad; she cannot be sold.
3
Clarify the scope
The ruling applies whether the mukatab buys the umm walad together with her child or buys her first and then later buys the child.
The child's entry into kitaba is automatic when the mother is bought, if the child is also acquired.

Defining Jinaayah and Classifying Unlawful Killing
Defines jinaayah and classifies unlawful killing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of jinaayah and list the five types of unlawful killing with their basic rulings.

Core Idea
Unlawful killing is the second greatest sin after disbelief, and its classification determines the penalty.

This lesson establishes the foundational definition of jinaayah (crime against persons) and the Hanafi classification of unlawful killing. Understanding these categories is essential because each type carries a different legal consequence—qisas for intentional killing with a weapon, diyah or other penalties for lesser degrees. The passage also distinguishes between lawful and unlawful killing, and clarifies that repentance is accepted for this grave sin.

Guided Path
1
Define jinaayah linguistically and legally
Identify the linguistic meaning (transgression) and the legal restriction (crimes against souls and limbs, not property). Note that property crimes are termed ghasb or itlaaf.
This distinction is specific to Hanafi usage.
2
List the five types of unlawful killing
Memorize the five categories: 'amd (intentional), shibh 'amd (semi-intentional), khata' (mistake), ma ujriya majra al-khata' (treated as mistake), and qatl bi-sabab (by cause).
Imam Muhammad only mentioned three; al-Quduri added two for clarity.
3
Distinguish lawful from unlawful killing
The passage notes that lawful killings (stoning, combat, qisas, crucifying highway robbers) are more numerous; the five types only cover unlawful killing.
This prevents confusion with capital punishment.
4
Understand the theological ruling on unlawful killing
Unlawful killing is a major sin (kabirah), but repentance is accepted. The killer is subject to Allah's will, not eternally punished if a believer.
This reflects the Hanafi position on grave sins.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define intentional killing ('amd) according to the Hanafi school and explain why all iron items, even blunt ones, trigger qisas.

Core Idea
Any iron object qualifies as a weapon for qisas because iron is inherently created for might, as the Quran states.

This lesson delves into the precise Hanafi definition of 'amd (intentional killing), which requires both intent to kill and the use of a weapon. The passage expands on what qualifies as a weapon: not only sharp tools but also blunt iron items like hammers and lead pillars. The Quranic verse 'And We sent down iron, wherein is great might' (57:25) serves as the proof that iron itself is inherently a weapon, so no sharpness is required. This definition determines when qisas (retributive execution) becomes obligatory.

Guided Path

Identify the elements of 'amd

This differs from other schools that require the weapon to be sharp or usually fatal.

Definition of 'Amd and Its Implements
Defines intentional killing and lists implements requiring qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'amd (intentional killing) in Hanafi law, list the implements that constitute 'amd, and state the ruling of qisas for each.

Core Idea
Intentional killing includes any implement that separates body parts, whether sharp or heavy, because the defining factor is intent.

This lesson from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira examines the definition of intentional killing ('amd) and the instruments that make the killer liable for qisas. The Hanafi school takes an expansive view: any object that can cause death by separating body parts—whether metal, glass, stone, or fire—qualifies as a weapon for 'amd. Understanding this definition is crucial because it determines the punishment—qisas or diya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition of 'amd
Locate the sentence: 'Al-'Amd: what is struck intentionally with a weapon or with something that is treated as a weapon in separating parts.' Note the two key elements: intentional striking and the use of an implement that separates body parts.
The definition is broad: 'weapon' includes not only metal but also anything that can cut or separate.
2
List the categories of implements
Distinguish between 'silah' (iron and similar metals) and 'ma ujriya majra al-silah' (items like sharpened wood, stone, fire, glass, etc.). The passage provides examples: iron (sword, knife, etc.), brass, lead, gold, silver, glass, sharp stone, and anything used for dhabh.
Blunt iron objects like a blacksmith's hammer are included; sharpness is not required for iron.
3
Understand the ruling (qisas) and its basis
The text states that qisas is obligatory for 'amd. The primary proof is Quran 57:25 about iron having 'severe might', indicating iron is created for killing. The Hanafi school uses this to argue that any killing with iron is 'amd by default.
The dalil is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala, making qisas wajib, not fard.
4
Examine special cases: composite weapons and non-metal items
The passage discusses a 'marr' (a spear with iron head and wooden shaft): if the wooden part strikes, only diya; if the iron part strikes, qisas. Non-metal items like glass and sharp stone are treated as weapons because they separate parts.
This shows that the material matters: iron always gives qisas, while other materials require the capacity to separate parts.
5
Note the cross-madhhab nuance
The Hanafi school includes blunt iron objects, while other schools may require sharpness or that the implement normally kills. This difference can affect the qisas vs. diya ruling in borderline cases.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi and based on zahir al-riwayah.

This lesson presents a foundational rule from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar on liability in cases where multiple wounds cause death. The Hanafi school distinguishes between wounds that occur one after another and those that happen at the same time, as the timing directly determines who is legally considered the killer. Rulings on Unreliable Witnesses in Zina Cases and the Tazkiya Procedure

Core Idea
False witnesses who are legally incompetent incur hadd al-qadhf; tazkiya verifies integrity through open and secret methods.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a critical aspect of hudud procedure: what happens when the witnesses in a zina case are found to be unreliable. It distinguishes between witnesses who are fasiq (corrupt) but free and Muslim—against whom no punishment is imposed because they might still be truthful—and witnesses who are slaves, have been flogged for qadhf, or are blind, whose testimony is inherently impossible or legally invalid, thus incurring the hadd of qadhf. قال في «الأصل» : يحبسه الإمام حتى يسأل عن الشهود. فإن قيل: كيف يحبسه وقد قيل : ادرؤوا الحدود، وليس في حبسه قيل : إنما يحبسه تعزيراً ؛ لأنه صار متهماً؛ لارتكاب الفاحشة. فإن شهد أربعةُ، فوجدوا فُسَّاقاً، وهم أحرار، مسلمون: فلا حد على الرجل ؛ لأن شهادتهم لم تُقبل. ولا حد عليهم ؛ لجواز أن يكونوا صادقين. فإن بانوا عبيداً، أو محدودين في قذف، أو عُمياناً: فعليهم حد القذف؛ لأن العميان لا يرون ما شهدوا عليه، فتحققنا كَذِبَهم، فكانوا قَذَفَةٌ. .... وأما العبيد، والمحدودون: فليسوا من أهل الشهادة، فكانوا قذفةً، فوجب عليهم حد القذف. وقوله في السر والعلانية : التزكية نوعان: فالعلانية: أن يجمع القاضي بين المعدل والشاهد، فيقول المعدلُ: هذا الذي عدلته. والسر أن يبعث القاضي رسولاً إلى المزكي، ويكتب إليه كتاباً فيه أسماء الشهود، وأنسابهم ، حتى يَعرِفَهم المزكي، فمن عرفه بالعدالة: كتب تحت اسمه: عدل جائز الشهادة، ومن عرفه بالفسق: لم يكتب تحت اسمه شيئاً؛ احترازاً عن هتك الستر، أو يقول : الله أعلم، إلا إذا كان عدله غيره، وخاف إن لم يصرح بذلك : قضى القاضي بشهادته : فحينئذ يصرح بذلك. ومن لم يعرفه بعدالة ولا فسق : يكتب تحت اسمه: مستور. قال أبو حنيفة : أقبل في تزكية السرِّ ،المرأة، والعبد، والمحدود في القذف إذا كانوا عدولاً ولا أقبلُ في تزكية العلانية إلا من أقبل شهادته؛ لأن تزكية السر من باب الإخبار والمخبر به أمر ديني، وقولُ هؤلاء في الأمور الدينية مقبول إذا كانوا عدولاً. ألا ترى أنه يُقبل روايتهم عن رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم، ويجب الصوم بقولهم: رأينا الهلال وتزكية العلانية نظير الشهادة. وعلى هذا تزكية الوالد لولده في السر: جائزة؛ لأنها من باب الإخبار. ذكره في «النهاية»، وعزاه إلى «الذخيرة».
He said in al-Asl: The imam should imprison him until he asks about the witnesses. If it is said: How can he imprison him when it has been said: 'Avert the hudud', and there is no harm in imprisoning him? He said: He only imprisons him as a ta'zir because he has become accused due to committing an obscene act. If four testify, and they are found to be sinners, free, Muslims, then there is no hadd upon the man because their testimony is not accepted. And there is no hadd upon them because it is possible they are truthful. If they turn out to be slaves, or those who have been subjected to hadd for qadhf, or blind, then upon them is the hadd of qadhf because the blind do not see what they testified to, so we have confirmed their lying, and they are qadhifun. ... As for slaves and those who have been subjected to hadd, they are not among those qualified for testimony, so they are qadhifun, and the hadd of qadhf is obligatory upon them. His statement concerning secret and public: Tazkiyah is of two types: Public is that the qadi brings together the one who declares integrity and the witness, and the one who declares integrity says: This is the one I declared as upright. Secret is that the qadi sends a messenger to the one who declares integrity and writes to him a letter containing the names of the witnesses and their lineages so that the one who declares integrity may know them. Whoever he knows to be upright, he writes under his name: 'Upright, fit for testimony.' Whoever he knows to be sinful, he writes nothing under his name, as a precaution against exposing privacy, or he says: 'Allah knows best,' except if someone else declared his uprightness and he fears that if he does not explicitly state it, the qadi would rule on his testimony; then he explicitly states it. And whoever he does not know to be upright nor sinful, he writes under his name: 'Concealed.' Abu Hanifa said: I accept in secret tazkiyah the testimony of a woman, a slave, and one who has been subjected to hadd for qadhf if they are upright, but I do not accept in public tazkiyah except those whose testimony I accept. Because secret tazkiyah is of the category of reporting, and the matter reported is a religious matter, and the statement of such persons in religious matters is accepted if they are upright. Do you not see that their narration from the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, is accepted, and fasting becomes obligatory by their statement 'We saw the crescent'? And public tazkiyah is analogous to testimony. Accordingly, tazkiyah of a father for his son in secret is permissible because it is of the category of reporting. It is mentioned in al-Nihayah and attributed to al-Dhakhirah.

Commentary
This passage addresses the procedures when witnesses in a zina case are found to be unreliable. The ruling from al-Asl: the imam must imprison the defendant until the witnesses are investigated. A potential objection is raised: how can he be imprisoned when the principle 'avert the hudud' is known? The answer is that the imprisonment is for ta'zir (discretionary punishment) due to suspicion, not for the hadd itself. If four witnesses testify but are later found to be sinful (fasiq), free, and Muslims, no hadd is imposed on the accused because their testimony is rejected. The witnesses are also not punished for qadhf because they might be truthful. However, if the witnesses turn out to be slaves, or previously punished for qadhf, or blind, then they are subject to hadd qadhf because they lack qualification for testimony, confirming their lie. The passage then distinguishes two types of tazkiyah (witness verification): public (the qadi meets the modifier and witness together) and secret (the qadi sends a letter to the modifier). In secret tazkiyah, the modifier writes the assessment; if unknown, he writes 'concealed' (mastur). Abu Hanifa's position is that in secret tazkiyah, even a woman, slave, or one punished for qadhf can perform tazkiyah if they are upright, because it is akin to reporting religious news. In contrast, public tazkiyah is like testimony and requires full witness qualifications. This ruling is cited from al-Nihayah and al-Dhakhirah.

Key Terms
التعزير (al-ta'zir): Discretionary punishment determined by the qadi for offenses not covered by fixed hadd penalties.
الحد (al-hadd): Fixed punishment prescribed by Islamic law for specific crimes (e.g., zina, qadhf, theft).
قذف (qadhf): False accusation of unlawful sexual intercourse, carrying a fixed hadd punishment of 80 lashes.

Key Takeaway
Imprisonment for investigation is ta'zir, not hadd; false witnesses face hadd qadhf only when their testimony is certainly false due to incapacity or impossibility.
