Qisas for a Killed Mortgaged Slave: Joint Consent Required
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas for a killed mortgaged slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on qisas when a mortgaged slave is killed and explain why the relied-upon view requires joint consent of pawner and pawnee.

Core Idea
A pawner cannot demand qisas alone because the pawnee's security interest is harmed; joint consent removes the obstacle.

When a slave held as collateral (rahn) is killed, who has the right to demand qisas? The pawner still owns the slave, but the pawnee holds a security interest. This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether qisas is possible and whose consent is needed. The passage shows how the school balances the competing rights of both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the core issue
The act is the killing of a slave that is in pledge (rahn). The core legal issue is whether qisas (retaliation) can be applied, and if so, whose consent is required.
2
State the position of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (first narration)
According to these two imams, qisas is not obligatory until both the pawner (rāhin) and pawnee (murtahin) agree to demand it. If both consent, qisas can be carried out.
Their rationale: the pawner cannot act alone because it would prejudice the pawnee's right; the pawnee cannot act alone because he lacks full ownership.
3
State the dissenting position of Muhammad al-Shaybani (and a second Abu Yusuf narration)
Muhammad holds that even if both parties agree, there is no qisas. The value of the slave (its price) already serves as security; the debt can be recovered from other property, so retaliation is not available.
This view treats the security interest as fully compensable in money, blocking qisas.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the relied position, if both consent, the killer can face qisas. Under Muhammad's view, the killer only pays the slave's value, which then becomes the new collateral.
If the parties disagree (one wants qisas, the other wants compensation), they take the value and it becomes the pledge.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The reled-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: qisas requires joint consent. This is the basis for fatwa.
The text says 'wa hadha qawluhuma' (this is their statement), indicating it is given weight.

Commentary
This passage addresses the case where a slave who has been given as collateral (rahn) is killed. The commentators explain that qisas (retaliation) is not imposed unless both the pawner (rahin) and the pawnee (murtahin) agree to demand it. The reason given is that the pawnee does not own the slave fully (he has only a security interest), so he cannot initiate qisas alone, while the pawner, if he acted alone, would nullify the pawnee's right to recover the debt. Therefore, both must agree to waive the financial interest. However, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (in his well-known view), if both agree, qisas can be demanded; according to Muhammad al-Shaybani, even if both agree, there is no qisas because the slave's value is already a security, and the debt can be recovered from other property. Another narration from Abu Yusuf agrees with Muhammad. The text further states that if the pawner and pawnee disagree (one wants qisas, the other wants compensation), then they take the value of the slave (diyah or price) and it becomes a pledge in place of the slave. This shows the nuanced approach of the Hanafi school to the conflicting rights of pawner and pawnee.

Key Terms
رهن (rahn): A pledge or collateral given to secure a debt; the slave becomes a security (marhun).
راهن (rāhin): The pawner, who gives the collateral.
مرتهن (murtahin): The pawnee, who receives the collateral.
