Blood Money Inherited Like Other Wealth: Maliki Disagrees on Spouses
Ruling on inheritance of accidental blood money with Maliki dissent on spouses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that spouses inherit from accidental blood money and explain the Maliki objection, including the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Accidental blood money is inherited by all heirs including spouses in the Hanafi school; the Maliki school excludes spouses because the obligation arises after death.

This lesson addresses a key intra-madhhab difference regarding the inheritance of blood money (diyah) for accidental homicide. The Hanafi school treats diyah like any other property left by the deceased, so spouses receive their prescribed shares. The Maliki school disagrees, reasoning that since diyah becomes obligatory only after death, it is not part of the estate existing at the time of death and therefore spouses do not inherit from it. Understanding this dispute clarifies the practical application of inheritance rules in accidental death cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling and its basis
State clearly that the Hanafi position holds diyah is inherited like other wealth by all heirs, including spouses. The basis is that diyah is a compensation that becomes part of the deceased's estate.
The passage uses the phrase 'كسائر أمواله' (like his other wealth) to emphasize equal treatment.
2
Identify the Maliki ruling and its basis
State the Maliki position: spouses do not inherit from diyah. Their basis is that diyah is wajib after death, so it was not part of the estate at the time of death.
The opposing argument is summarized as 'لأن وجوبها بعد الموت' (because its obligation is after death).
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response counters that the time of obligation does not change the classification; diyah is a right that accrues to the deceased and passes to his heirs.
The Hanafi view treats diyah as an asset (mal) that enters the estate upon obligation.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi, spouses receive their fixed share from diyah; under Maliki, they receive nothing from diyah, though they still inherit from other property.
This difference affects estate distribution in accidental death cases.

The Hanafi view is founded on the principle that diyah is a compensation for the victim that devolves to his heirs, and the general rules of inheritance apply without exception.

Inheritance of Blood Money (Diyah) – The Spousal Inclusion
Inter-madhhab dispute on diyah inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school includes spouses in the inheritance of diyah and contrast this with the Maliki position.

Core Idea
Diyah is part of the estate; spouses inherit it under Hanafi law, unlike Malik.

This passage addresses a key inter-madhhab dispute: does blood money (diyah) belong to the same heirs as the deceased's other property, or are spouses excluded? The Hanafi text boldly states that spouses do inherit, directly contradicting the Maliki school. Understanding this difference is crucial for correctly distributing compensation in homicide cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling on diyah inheritance
Read the text carefully: ‘ويورث دم المقتول كسائر أمواله ويستحقه من يرث ماله ويدخل فيه الزوجان’. This explicitly states that diyah is inherited like any other asset and that spouses are included.
The phrase ‘كسائر أمواله’ anchors the ruling in general inheritance rules.
2
Recognize the opposing position (Malik)
The text says ‘خلافا لمالك’. Malik holds that spouses do not inherit diyah because the obligation arises after death, and marriage ends at death, so the spouse is not a legal heir for such posthumous obligations.
Malik's view is based on the nature of diyah as a debt incurred after death.
3
Understand the exclusion of the legatee (musa lahu)
The phrase ‘ولا يدخل فيه الموصى له’ clarifies that a beneficiary of a will is not a legal heir and therefore does not inherit diyah. Only the legal heirs (asabah and prescribed sharers) take it.
This reinforces that diyah follows the ordinary law of inheritance.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Hanafi fiqh, a widow receives her prescribed share (1/4 or 1/8) from the diyah. Under Maliki, she gets nothing from the diyah. This directly affects the compensation received by dependents.
The difference is significant in cases where the deceased leaves a spouse but no other property.
5
Locate the Hanafi proof (to be given later in the text)
This passage only states the ruling without proof. In the full text, the evidence is a hadith of al-Dahhak that the Prophet (peace be upon him) gave diyah to the heirs of a slain person, including the wife.
The hadith is the basis for the Hanafi insistence on spousal inclusion.

Acts Treated Like Error in Homicide: The Sleeping Person Case
Definition and legal consequences of acts treated like error

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for acts treated like error (ma ujriya majra al-khata) and explain the legal consequences (diyah, kaffarah, inheritance bar) with the reasoning from the text.

Core Idea
An act treated like error carries the same consequences but is not called error—intent or negligence is absent.

This passage introduces a unique category in Hanafi homicide law: acts that are not intentional nor negligent, yet still incur liability. The classic example is a sleeping person who rolls over and kills someone. Understanding this category is essential for distinguishing between true error (khata) and its legal equivalent, and for grasping the Hanafi principle that liability can arise from causation alone, even without fault.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Recognize that 'ma ujriya majra al-khata' refers to acts treated like error, exemplified by a sleeping person who kills by turning over.
2
List the legal consequences
Four consequences: no qisas, diyah wajib, kaffarah wajib, and deprivation of inheritance.
3
Analyze each consequence's rationale
No qisas due to lack of intent; diyah because death by action; kaffarah because death by weight; inheritance bar to prevent possible fraud.
4
Understand why it's not called khata
The act is not termed error because there is no negligence; it is a pure accident without fault.
5
Distinguish from true error
In true error, there is some negligence; here, none. The consequences are identical but the categories differ terminologically.

Commentary
This passage explains a category of homicide: those acts that are legally treated as error (khata) but are not actually termed error. Therefore, the term 'error' is not formally applied, but the rulings are attached to ensure justice.

Killing by Causation: Diyah on 'Aqilah, No Kaffarah
Commentary on matn of al-Quduri for killing by causation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three rulings for killing by causation (sabab) and apply them to scenarios involving indirect causation.

Core Idea
Indirect killing via transgression imposes diyah on the clan, not expiation.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on homicide caused indirectly (sabab), such as digging a well in a public path or placing a stone in non-owned land. The rulings differ from direct killing or killing by weight. We will unpack the three key consequences: diyah liability, absence of kaffarah, and preservation of inheritance rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act as qatl bi al-sabab
Distinguish causation from direct killing (mubasharah) or killing by weight (thiqal). The key is transgression (ta'addi) in a public thoroughfare.
The examples: digging a well or placing a stone in non-owned land.
2
Apply the three rulings
For each ruling (diyah on 'aqilah, no kaffarah, inheritance preserved), trace the reasoning given in the text.
Diyah is wajib; kaffarah is absent because the killer did not directly cause death.
3
Analyze the exceptions
If the well/stone is not in a public path, no liability. If the victim intentionally stepped on the stone, no liability. If a third party moves the stone, liability shifts.
These are conditions that affect the ruling.
4
Understand the istihsan ruling on disputes
When the guardian and digger differ on whether the victim jumped in, the digger's word is accepted by istihsan because he is not accused of intentional harm.
This overrides the default rule that the plaintiff's claim is taken if supported by oath.
5
Compare with other forms of homicide
Contrast with a rider whose mount tramples someone (kaffarah required) and with a driver/leader (no kaffarah). Understand the illa.
The presence or absence of the killer's weight (thiqal) is decisive.

Commentary
This is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of al-Quduri regarding homicide by causation (qatl bi al-sabab). The matn states three rulings: (1) the obligatory consequence is diyah on the 'aqilah, (2) there is no kaffarah (expiation), and (3) the killer is not deprived of inheritance. The commentator explains each: The killing is neither intentional ('amd) nor purely mistaken (khata'), but rather a cause (sabab) arising from transgression (ta'addi), such as digging a well in a public path or placing a stone in non-owned land. Since the death is not directly caused by the person's own act (mubasharah) nor by his weight (thiqal), kaffarah is not required. This differs from a rider whose mount tramples someone, because in that case the rider's weight contributes to the death. Similarly, a driver (sa'iq) or leader (qa'id) of an animal is not liable for kaffarah if the animal kills, as they neither directly cause the death nor does the victim die by their weight. The inheritance ruling: the causer is not barred from inheriting from the victim because there is no accusation of foul play. Additional details: liability only applies if the well/stone is placed in a public thoroughfare; if placed elsewhere, no liability. For the stone, liability only if the passerby did not intentionally step on it. If the stone is moved by another, the mover becomes liable. If the guardian of the victim and the digger disagree about whether the victim jumped in, the digger's statement is taken (by istihsan) because the digger is not accused of intentional killing. The Khujandi note attributes this view to Imam Muhammad.

Key Terms
عاقلة ('Āqilah): The male paternal relatives (asabah) of the killer who are responsible for paying diyah collectively.
سبب (Sabab): An indirect cause of death, as opposed to direct causation (mubāsharah) or by weight (thiqal). Requires transgression.
استحسان (Istihsān): Juristic preference; here used to prefer the digger's word over the guardian's due to lack of accusation.

Shibh 'Amd and the Definition of Diyah
Ruling on quasi-intentional killing and definitions of blood money.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for quasi-intentional killing (shibh 'amd) and distinguish between diyah, arsh, and qimah.

Core Idea
In shibh 'amd, aggravated diyah is on the aqila, not the killer—because the intention was harm, not death.

The Hanafi school recognizes a distinct category of homicide between intentional and accidental: quasi-intentional (shibh 'amd). This lesson unpacks its ruling and the essential terms of blood money—diyah, arsh, and qimah—as defined in the classical Hanafi text. Understanding these categories is crucial for correct legal classification and financial liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the initial ruling: shibh 'amd triggers an aggravated diyah on the aqila and kaffarah on the killer.
This is the core legal outcome for this lesson.
2
Distinguish key terms
Diyah (blood money) is for loss of life; arsh is for injuries less than death; qimah (value) is not used for human life due to lack of equivalence.
The text explicitly says diyah is not called value.
3
Recognize the categories of killing requiring diyah on aqila
Mistake, quasi-intentional, killing by cause, killing by child or insane person—all place diyah on the aqila.
This is the default rule.
4
Note the exception
If a father intentionally kills his son, the diyah is on his own wealth, not on the aqila.
This exception highlights the principle of paternal responsibility.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Diyat begins with a ruling: for quasi-intentional killing (shibh 'amd), the aqila must pay an aggravated diyah (diyah mughallazah) and the killer must perform kaffarah (expiation). The text then defines key terms. Diyah is blood money specifically for loss of life, whereas arsh is compensation for injuries less than death. The author distinguishes diyah from 'value' (qimah) because diyah compensates for a life, which has no true equivalent, so it is not called a value. Next, the passage lists cases where diyah is obligatory: killing by mistake, quasi-intentional killing, killing by cause (e.g., digging a well that someone falls into), and killing by a child or insane person (since their intentional act is treated as mistake). In all these cases, diyah is upon the aqila. However, an exception is made: if a father intentionally kills his son, the diyah is on the father's own wealth, not on the aqila, and it is payable in three years. The passage concludes by restating the initial ruling on shibh 'amd.

Hukumat 'Adl: Equitable Compensation for Unfixed Wounds

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hukumat 'adl is not fixed; it is determined by ijtihad, with three methods within the Hanafi school.

This passage defines hukumat 'adl for wounds without fixed arsh. You will learn the three ways Hanafi scholars derived the compensation amount, why al-Karkhi objected to al-Tahawi's method, and how the later preference for al-Karkhi's method is restricted to head and face injuries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wound category and ruling
The matn states: 'For wounds below the mudihat, there is hukumat 'adl.' Mudihat is a skull wound exposing bone, with fixed arsh of five camels. Wounds below this have no fixed amount, so equitable compensation (hukumat 'adl) is wajib.
Hukumat is wajib, not fard, because it is derived via ijtihad.
2
Understand al-Tahawi's method (slave value analogy)
Al-Tahawi compares the free person to a slave: value the slave without the wound, then with the wound. The percentage decrease in value is applied to the full diyah of a free person. Example: if decrease is half a tenth (5%), then 5% of full diyah (100 camels) = 5 camels.
This is qiyas on the valuation of slave defects.
3

Al-Karkhi objects that al-Tahawi's method can lead to a less severe wound (like simhaq) having higher compensation than the more severe mudihat. Instead, he compares the wound to the lowest wound with fixed arsh (mudihat). If this wound is half as severe, half the arsh of mudihat (2.5 camels) is obligated.
This method ensures consistency with the fixed arsh hierarchy.
4
Understand the third method (actual expenses)
Some Hanafi scholars define hukumat as the actual expenses incurred: doctor fees, medicines, etc., until recovery.
This is a minority view.
5

Shaykh al-Islam declares al-Karkhi's method the most correct ('wa huwa al-ashah'), but only for injuries to the face or head (where mudihat is located). For other body parts, the fatwa is on al-Tahawi's method.
The third method (expenses) is not adopted in the fatwa.

Diyat for Fingers, Palm, and Forearm
Rulings on diyat for fingers, palm, and forearm with an intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the diyat for combinations of finger, palm, and forearm injuries and state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the palm without fingers.

Core Idea
The palm is a follower of the fingers; a palm without fingers gets only a just assessment, not a fixed arsh.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra details the diyat for hand injuries, establishing that each finger carries one-tenth of the full diyat, making five fingers half the diyat. It then explores how the palm is treated as a follower of the fingers, and introduces an intra-Hanafi dispute on the compensation for a fingerless palm. By working through these cases, you will learn to apply the principle of taba'iyya (followership) and understand when a fixed arsh applies versus a discretionary assessment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fixed arsh for fingers
Start with the clear ruling: each finger of the hand has an arsh of one-tenth of the full diyat. Therefore, all five fingers together give half diyat.
This is the base ruling, agreed upon within the Hanafi school.
2
Incorporate the palm as a follower (tabi')
When the fingers are cut along with the palm (i.e., the hand at the wrist), the palm does not add extra compensation because it is a follower of the fingers. The diyat remains half.
If only one, two, or three fingers are cut with part of the hand, only the diyat for those fingers is due; nothing for the palm.
3
Analyze the edge case: palm without fingers
If a palm with no fingers is cut, there is no fixed arsh. Abu Yusuf holds that a just assessment (hukm 'adl) is given, less than the arsh of one finger, because the palm follows the fingers and cannot equal the followed.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute – the text does not mention Abu Hanifa's view, but the phrase 'indahuma' in the forearm case suggests the Sahibayn agree on the forearm ruling, possibly differing from Abu Hanifa on the palm.
4
Handle the forearm extension
When the cut extends to half the forearm, the hand (fingers and palm) gets half diyat, and the forearm portion gets a just assessment. This is the view of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
The commentary indicates that the Sahibayn agree; Abu Hanifa may have had a different view, possibly requiring a full arsh for the forearm.

And if the hand is cut while it has one finger: then upon him the diyah of the finger, and nothing upon him for the palm. Similarly, if it has two or three fingers: then in it the diyah of the fingers, nothing else. And if he cuts a palm that has no fingers: Abu Yusuf said: in it a just assessment that does not reach the arsh of a finger; because the finger is followed by the palm, and the follower does not equal the followed. His statement: (And if he cuts them with half of the forearm: then in the fingers and the palm: half of the diyah, and in the forearm: a just assessment), this is according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).
Source pages p. 250

Commentary
This section details the diyat for the fingers and the hand. The matn states that cutting off all five fingers of the hand entitles the victim to half the full diyah. The commentary explains that each finger carries one-tenth of the diyah, so five fingers make half. If the offender cuts the fingers together with the palm (i.e., the hand at the wrist), the diyah is still half, because the palm is considered a follower (tabi') of the fingers; the primary value is in the fingers. This is because the function of grasping resides in the fingers, not the palm. If only one, two, or three fingers are cut along with part of the hand, the diyah is only for the number of fingers cut, and nothing extra for the palm. But if a palm without any fingers is cut, there is a difference of opinion: Abu Yusuf holds that only a just assessment (hukm 'adl) is due, which must be less than the arsh of a finger, because the palm follows the fingers. This is an intra-Hanafi dispute; the text does not mention Abu Hanifa's view explicitly, but the phrase 'this is according to the two' (عندهما) for the forearm case implies that the Sahibayn agree, possibly differing from Abu Hanifa. Finally, if the cut is at the middle of the forearm, then for the hand (fingers and palm) half diyah is due, and for the forearm portion, a just assessment. The commentary notes that this is the view of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), implying that Abu Hanifa may have held a different opinion, perhaps requiring a full assessment for the forearm.

Key Terms
أصبع (isba'): Finger of the hand. Each finger carries one-tenth of the full diyat.
كف (kaff): The palm of the hand, considered a follower (tabi') of the fingers in diyat assessments.
ساعد (sa'id): The forearm. When cut along with the hand, the hand portion gets half diyat, and the forearm gets a just assessment.
حكومة عدل (hukm 'adl): A discretionary compensation assessed by a just arbiter for injuries that have no fixed arsh.
عندهما (indahuma): According to the two (i.e., Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), indicating the view of the Sahibayn.

Diyah for Two Penetrating Wounds
Ruling on diyah amount for two penetrating wounds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for diyah when two penetrating wounds are inflicted and identify the companion precedent that establishes it.

Core Idea
Two penetrating wounds together incur two-thirds diyah, not full diyah per wound.

How much blood money is due when a victim suffers two deep, cavity-penetrating wounds? The Hanafi school has a specific ruling based on the judgment of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA). This lesson unpacks that ruling and its proof, showing how a companion's precedent shapes the law of diyat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wound type
Determine that the wound is a jā'ifah, i.e., a wound that penetrates the body cavity (belly, chest, or skull). This is the subject of the ruling.
Only penetrating wounds qualify; superficial cuts do not.
2
State the number of wounds
The passage explicitly addresses two such wounds (jā'ifatān). The diyah amount is specified for a pair, not per individual wound.
For a single jā'ifah, the ruling is different (one-third diyah, according to some transmissions).
3
Recall the diyah amount
The diyah for two jā'ifatān is two-thirds of the full diyah (i.e., 666.66 dinars or equivalent). This is a specific fraction, not a multiple.
Full diyah is 1000 dinars or 10,000 dirhams.
4
Identify the proof
The ruling is based on the judgment of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA). In Hanafi usul, the qawl of a companion (qawl ṣaḥābī) when it is not known to be contradicted is a valid proof.
This is a zanni thubut but qati dalala proof.
5
Apply the ruling
Given the amount for two wounds, one can derive the amount for a single wound (one-third) and for more than two (e.g., three wounds would be full diyah, etc.) by analogy, though this source does not elaborate.
Other scenarios are covered elsewhere in the chapter.
