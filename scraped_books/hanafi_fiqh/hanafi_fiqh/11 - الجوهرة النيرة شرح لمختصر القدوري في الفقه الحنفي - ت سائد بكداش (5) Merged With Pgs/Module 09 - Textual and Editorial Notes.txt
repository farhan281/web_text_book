Effective and Ineffective Kinayah Expressions for Emancipation
Clarifying which allusive expressions effect emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given allusive expression as effective or ineffective for emancipation in Hanafi fiqh, based on whether it negates ownership or merely control.

Core Idea
Only kinayah that negates ownership, not mere control, effects emancipation with intention.

This lesson explores the Hanafi distinction between effective and ineffective allusive expressions (kinayah) for emancipation. While explicit words like 'you are free' require no intention, allusive expressions depend on both the specific wording and the owner's intention. Understanding which phrases negate ownership versus mere control is crucial for correct application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Expression
Determine the exact phrase used by the owner. Is it explicit (sarih) or allusive (kinayah)?
Explicit: 'you are free', 'your vulva is free'. Allusive: e.g., 'I have no ownership over you'.
2
Check for Intention
If the expression is allusive, ascertain whether the owner intended emancipation. Without intention, no emancipation occurs, even with effective kinayah.
Intention is not required for explicit words.
3
Analyze the Expression's Meaning
Does the expression necessarily negate ownership? If it negates something less (like control), it is ineffective. For example, 'لا سلطان لي عليك' (I have no authority) only negates control, not ownership, so it fails even with intention.
Key distinction: 'لا سبيل لي عليك' (I have no way over you) negates ownership because the master always has a 'sabil' over a slave; its absolute negation implies no ownership.
4
Apply the Ruling
Based on the analysis, state whether emancipation occurs. Effective kinayah with intention: emancipation. Ineffective kinayah: no emancipation regardless of intention.
Examples of effective kinayah: 'I have no ownership', 'you have left my ownership', 'I have released your way'.

Also, attributing freedom to a specific body part like 'your hand is free' does not emancipate because the part does not represent the whole. The commentary explains that expressions like 'I have set you free' (أطلقتك) are similar to 'I have released you' (خليت سبيلك) and work with intention, while 'I have divorced you' (طلقتك) does not work because divorce only prohibits intercourse without removing ownership. The passage follows the order of the matn, first discussing the explicit use of 'vulva', then the allusive expressions, then the distinction between description and simile (with nunation making it description, thus effective). This is a typical Hanafi treatment emphasizing the importance of linguistic precision and intention in manumission.

Key Terms
كنايات العتق (Kinayat al-Itq): Words that allude to emancipation but do not explicitly state it, e.g., 'I have no ownership over you.' They require intention (niyyah) to effect emancipation.
السبيل (al-Sabil): Literally 'way' or 'path'; in this context, it means authority or claim that arises from ownership; negating it absolutely implies negation of ownership.
السلطان (al-Sultan): Authority or control; it is not equivalent to ownership, so negating it does not imply emancipation.

Body Parts and the Simile-Description Distinction in Emancipation
Clarifying which body parts and grammatical forms effect emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that only body parts which can metonymically represent the whole person effect emancipation, and distinguish between descriptive (tanwin) and simulative (with 'like') constructions.

Core Idea
A body part stands for the whole only if it is indispensable for personhood; simile with 'like' invalidates emancipation.

This lesson focuses on the Hanafi treatment of body parts in emancipation declarations. Certain parts like the vulva can metonymically represent the entire person, while others like hand or foot cannot. Additionally, the grammatical construction matters: a descriptive phrase with nunation (e.g., 'your head is the head of a free') is effective, but a simile (e.g., 'like the head of a free') is not. These nuances highlight the school's precision in linguistic interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Declaration
Determine what the owner said: does it attribute freedom to a body part? Is it expressed as a simile (using 'like' or 'مثل') or as a description (with nunation)?
Key phrases: 'فرجك حر' (your vulva is free), 'رأسك رأس حرّ' (your head is the head of a free – with nunation), 'مثل رأس حر' (like the head of a free).
2
Check if the Body Part Represents the Whole
In Hanafi fiqh, only body parts that are conventionally used to refer to the entire person (e.g., 'vulva' for a female, 'head' for a person) can effect emancipation when attributed. Parts like hand, foot, thigh, hair do not represent the whole.
The vulva works for a female slave; the penis does not work for a male slave (sahih is no emancipation). Anus/rectum is disputed; the correct view is no emancipation.
3
Differentiate Description vs. Simile
If the expression uses 'مثل' (like), it is a simile and does not effect emancipation even if the part represents the whole. If it is a descriptive phrase (with nunation, e.g., 'رأس حر'), it is a description and effects emancipation with intention.
Example: 'رأسك مثل رأس حر' (your head is like the head of a free) – no emancipation. 'رأسك رأس حرّ' (your head is the head of a free) – emancipation.
4
Apply the Rule for Attributing Freedom to a Specific Part
If the part does not represent the whole (e.g., 'يدك حر'), no emancipation even with intention. If the part does represent the whole and the construction is descriptive, emancipation occurs with intention.
Know the list: representative parts: vulva, head; non-representative: hand, foot, shin, thigh, hair, penis (for male), anus (sahih).

Acknowledgment of Paternity: Emancipation of Slaves
Explains the ruling on emancipation by acknowledgment of paternity with conditions and intra-Hanafi differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a master's acknowledgment of paternity leads to emancipation and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the term 'brother'.

Core Idea
A master's acknowledgment of paternity frees the slave only if the slave could be his child and the master is the opponent; 'brother' does not emancipate in the apparent narration.

This lesson explores the legal consequences of a master's acknowledgment of kinship with his slave. When the master says 'this is my son', the slave may be emancipated and lineage established. But what about other relations like 'brother' or 'grandfather'? The Hanafi school distinguishes based on possibility and direct effect in ownership. We will unpack the reasoning and the internal disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his students.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the master's acknowledgment of paternity (saying 'this is my son'). The ruling: the slave is emancipated and lineage is established.
The emancipation is wajib as a consequence of the acknowledgment under specific conditions.
2
Locate the conditions for emancipation
The passage states conditions: (1) the slave could be born from the master (similar age, possibility), (2) the slave has no known lineage, and (3) the master is the opponent (i.e., the one affected by the acknowledgment).
The phrase 'وهو الخصم فيه' is crucial – the master is the opposing party, so his acknowledgment is accepted.
3

Misdirected Tasmiya and Interruption During Slaughter
Rulings on tasmiya misdirection and interruption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on misdirected tasmiya in slaughter versus hunting and distinguish between short and long interruptions in the validity of slaughter.

Core Idea
Tasmiya's validity depends on continuous intention; misdirection invalidates in slaughter but not in hunting.

In Hanafi fiqh, the recitation of Bismillah (tasmiya) is wajib during slaughter, but its application can be nuanced. This passage examines two key issues: what happens when tasmiya is intended for one animal but another is actually slaughtered, and how interruption between tasmiya and the cut affects the meat's permissibility. By understanding these distinctions, you can correctly apply the rulings in both domestic slaughter and hunting scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling for misdirection in slaughter
The passage first considers a sheep laid down, tasmiya said, but a different sheep is slaughtered. The ruling is 'la yajuz' (not permissible). Understand that the intention behind the tasmiya is tied to a specific animal; switching invalidates the slaughter.
This differs from hunting, as explained next.
2
Compare with hunting: misdirection in shooting or sending a dog
When hunting (shooting an arrow or releasing a dog), tasmiya is said at the time of releasing the tool. If the tool hits different game than intended, it is permissible (halla). The key is that tasmiya attaches to the tool, not the specific game.
The difference arises because slaughter involves direct contact with the animal, while hunting uses an intermediary.
3
Analyze interruption: short vs long
If after tasmiya there is a brief interruption (speaking, drinking, sharpening knife), the original tasmiya suffices. The ruling is 'ajza'ah' (it suffices). However, if the interruption is long (extended talk or engaging in another work), the original tasmiya no longer suffices and must be repeated.
The criterion is whether the interruption breaks the continuity of the act of slaughter.
4
Identify the underlying principle
The common thread is the continuity of intention and action. In short interruptions, the intention remains; in long ones, it is considered broken. In misdirection, intention was specific to one animal in slaughter but general to the tool in hunting.
This shows how fiqh considers the nature of the act (direct vs indirect) and the duration of breaks.

Core Idea
A dead fetus is carrion unless it dies by its mother's slaughter—Abu Hanifa forbids it, Sahibayn allow if fully formed.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: the ruling on a fetus found dead inside its slaughtered mother. Whoever slaughters a camel, or slaughters a cow, or a sheep, and finds in its belly a dead fetus: it is not eaten, whether its hair has grown or not. The specific [case] that is not permitted is what is cut from a living [animal], and this [fetus] is not called living absolutely. He said in the Tafsir (1) regarding the statement of Allah the Exalted: “Then when their flanks fall to the ground, eat of them” – meaning camels when they fall after slaughter, their flanks fall to the ground, and their soul has left: then eat of them, and it is not permissible to eat from the sacrificial animals except after the soul has left. * His statement: “Whoever slaughters a camel, or slaughters a cow or a sheep, and finds in its belly a dead fetus: it is not eaten, whether its hair has grown or not”, this is the statement of Abu Hanifa and Zufar. According to the two [Abu Yusuf and Muhammad]: if its creation is complete, it is eaten; otherwise, not. Due to his statement, peace and blessings be upon him: “The slaughter of the fetus is the slaughter of its mother.” (2) And because it is in the ruling of a part of her parts; evidenced by the fact that it enters into her sale and is freed by her freeing, so it becomes like the rest of her limbs. For Abu Hanifa: the statement of Allah the Exalted: “Forbidden to you is the dead [animal]” – and it is a name for what dies naturally, and this is present in the fetus; because it does not die by the death of its mother; for she may die and the fetus remains alive in her belly, and it dies while she is [alive], so its life is not connected to her life, so her slaughter is not a slaughter for it, so the two become like two sheep: the slaughter of one is not slaughter for the other. And because it is a source of life and blood; for its life is not conceivable after her death, and it has blood separate from her blood, and slaughter is legislated to drain the impure blood from the pure meat, and her slaughter is not a cause for the blood to exit from it. And regarding the hadith they narrated: it was narrated: “the slaughter of its mother” in the accusative case (1) by removing the preposition, i.e., like the slaughter of its mother. – As for when the fetus emerges alive and then dies, it is not eaten, by consensus; the disagreement is only when it emerges dead. – And they [Abu Yusuf and Muhammad] only stipulated that it be fully formed; because if it is not complete, then it is like a clot and blood, so its eating is not permissible. And the meaning of his statement: “whether its hair has grown or not” is: whether its creation is complete or not.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of eating a fetus found dead inside its slaughtered mother. The position of Abu Hanifa and Zufar is that the dead fetus is not eaten regardless of whether it is fully formed (having hair or not). The position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) is that if the fetus is fully formed (its creation complete), it is permissible to eat; otherwise not. The Sahibayn rely on the hadith: 'The slaughter of the fetus is the slaughter of its mother,' and on the analogy that the fetus is like a part of the mother, as it is included in her sale and manumission. Abu Hanifa counters with the Quranic prohibition of carrion (al-maytah), arguing that the fetus dies its own death independently of the mother; its life is separate, and the mother's slaughter does not cause the blood to flow from it. He interprets the hadith as meaning 'like the slaughter of its mother' (with an implied preposition), not equating the two. Additionally, if the fetus emerges alive and then dies, there is consensus that it is not eaten; the only disagreement is when it is found dead inside. The condition of 'full creation' (kamil al-khalq) is explained as completion of form versus being like a clot (mudghah) or blood. The term 'ash'ara' (having hair) is used to indicate complete creation.

Key Terms
جَنِين (janīn): The unborn offspring in the womb; a fetus.
ذَكَاة (dhakāt): The Islamic method of slaughtering animals that renders them halal.
أَشْعَر (ash‘ara): Literally 'had hair'; used as a marker of complete creation.

Ruling on Domestic Donkey, Mule, and Horse Meat
Matn statement on prohibited and disliked meats

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on domesticated donkey, mule, and horse meat, and distinguish between prohibition (haram) and disliked (makruh).

Core Idea
Domestic donkey and mule meat is haram; horse meat is makruh tahrimi according to Abu Hanifa.

This lesson presents a concise ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri on the permissibility of certain meats. You will learn that the Hanafi school categorically prohibits domesticated donkey and mule meat, while horse meat is subject to a well-known intra-madhhab difference: Abu Hanifa considers it makruh (prohibited dislike), whereas his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, permit it. Understanding these categories is essential for differentiating between absolute prohibition and lesser degrees of prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The matn addresses the act of eating meat from three animals: domesticated donkeys, mules, and horses. Determine the legal ruling for each: for donkeys and mules it is 'la yajuz' (not permissible, i.e., haram); for horses it is 'yukrah' (disliked) according to Abu Hanifa.
'La yajuz' in Hanafi legal terminology indicates haram, not merely disliked.
2
Distinguish between domesticated and wild
Note that the prohibition applies specifically to 'al-humur al-ahliyyah' (domesticated donkeys). Wild donkeys (al-himar al-wahshi) are halal. This is a key condition.
Mules are always included regardless of domestication, as they are the offspring of a donkey.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi difference
For horse meat, the matn attributes the disliked ruling to Abu Hanifa alone, implying that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold a different view—they consider horse meat halal (mubah).
This difference is well-documented; the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position often follows the Sahibayn, but caution is advised.
4
Memorize the ruling and its scope
Commit to memory: domestic donkey and mule meat = haram; horse meat = makruh tahrimi (Abu Hanifa) or halal (Sahibayn). Recognize that this ruling applies to the meat, but other parts (e.g., milk) may have different rulings.
The prohibition is on consumption; other uses (e.g., riding) are not affected.

Commentary
This is a ruling from the matn of Al-Quduri, stating three clear rulings. First, the meat of domesticated donkeys (not wild) and mules is absolutely forbidden (la yajuz). Second, horse meat is makruh (disliked) according to Imam Abu Hanifa, meaning it is prohibited in the sense of karahah tanzih? Actually the commentator later clarifies that Abu Hanifa considers it karahah tahrimi (prohibited dislike), not just tanzih. The other two imams, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, permit horse meat. This matn statement sets the stage for the detailed proof and dispute that follow in the commentary.

Key Terms

البغال (al-bighal): Mules, the offspring of a horse and a donkey.
الفرس (al-faras): Horse.
