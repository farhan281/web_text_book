Settlement (Sulh) in Qisas: Conditions and Effects
States validity of sulh, effect on qisas, and payment timing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on sulh in qisas, identify when qisas drops, and determine the default timing of payment.

Core Idea
A settlement with any money drops qisas and makes payment wajib; payment is immediate unless deferred by agreement.

Qisas (retaliation) is the default for intentional killing, but Islamic law allows the victim's heirs to forgo it in exchange for monetary compensation. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on such a settlement, known as sulh, and clarifies the conditions for its validity and the binding effects on both parties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a settlement (sulh) between the killer and the heirs. The ruling is that it is valid and binding: qisas drops and the agreed payment becomes wajib.
The text says 'qisas drops and the property becomes obligatory'.
2
Examine the condition on the amount
The amount can be little or much; there is no minimum tied to the standard diyah. Any mutually agreed sum is acceptable.
This emphasizes flexibility and the heirs' free consent.
3
Determine the default timing of payment
If the parties do not specify a term, the payment is due immediately (ḥāl). Only if they explicitly stipulate a deferred term does it become delayed.
The text states: 'if they do not mention immediate or deferred: it is immediate, unless a term is stipulated.'

One Heir's Pardon Drops Qisas for All
Ruling on one heir's pardon and its effect on qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one heir pardons qisas and explain why the remaining heirs receive diyah instead.

Core Idea
Qisas is indivisible; one heir's pardon drops it for all, converting to diyah.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra addresses a critical rule in qisas: when multiple heirs share the right to retaliation, that right is indivisible. If any one heir pardons or compromises, the right to qisas falls entirely, and the other heirs receive their share of diyah. The text also explores a nuanced scenario where a remaining heir kills the killer in ignorance of the pardon, and presents Zufar's dissenting view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
The passage considers a murder victim with multiple heirs. One heir either pardons (without compensation) or reaches a financial settlement. The immediate ruling: qisas is no longer available for any heir; each remaining heir gets their proportional share of diyah from the killer's wealth.
The pardoning heir forfeits any right to diyah.
2
Understand the ratio legis (illa)
The Hanafi rationale is that qisas cannot be divided (la yatabba'adu). Since the right is one and indivisible, dropping any part drops the whole. This is contrasted with a case where a man kills two men: there, each murder creates an independent qisas right, so pardon by one heir does not affect the second victim's qisas.
The distinction hinges on whether the obligation is singular or multiple.
3
Analyze the effect on the killer and the heirs
The killer must pay diyah from his own wealth over three years; the aqila do not bear responsibility for intentional killing. The pardoning heir receives nothing because he voluntarily relinquished his claim.
The conversion from qisas to diyah is automatic once qisas becomes impossible.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding ignorance
If one heir pardons and another heir kills the killer without knowing about the pardon or its legal effect, the majority Hanafi view is that no qisas is due; the killer pays half diyah. Zufar argues that qisas applies because the killer acted on a mistaken belief that the blood was still permissible.
The text does not provide a detailed rebuttal to Zufar, but the majority view is relied upon.
5
Determine the operative ruling (mufta bihi)
The relied-upon position is the majority view: no qawd on the ignorant remaining heir, only half diyah. The indivisibility of qisas and the automatic conversion to diyah are settled principles in the Hanafi school.
This positions the student to apply the rule in real fatwa scenarios.

Waiver by One Co-Plaintiff Extinguishes Qisas for All
Ruling and rational proof on co-plaintiff waiver in qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule that qisas is indivisible among co-plaintiffs, state the ruling when one co-plaintiff waives or settles, and distinguish it from the case of multiple victims.

Core Idea
Qisas is indivisible: one plaintiff's waiver extinguishes the right to qisas for all.

In Hanafi criminal law, the right to demand qisas belongs to the victim's heirs collectively. But what happens if one heir decides to waive or settle? This lesson examines the ruling from the matn and its rational proof, showing why qisas falls entirely for all, converting to diyah for the non-waiving heirs. The passage also contrasts this with the case of multiple victims, where each victim's qisas is independent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a waiver (afw) or settlement (sulh) by one of the co-plaintiffs (shuraka') in a blood crime. The ruling: the right of all co-plaintiffs to qisas falls entirely.
The ruling applies regardless of whether the waiver is gratuitous or for compensation.
2
Identify the legal consequence for the remaining co-plaintiffs
The remaining co-plaintiffs lose qisas but receive their share of the diyah (blood money) from the killer's estate.
The waiving co-plaintiff gets nothing from the diyah because he voluntarily dropped his right.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational: qisas is indivisible (la yatabba'adu). Therefore, if part of it falls (by waiver), the whole falls. This is stated explicitly in the text: 'li-anna al-qisas la yatabba'adu'.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof), not a textual one, but it is grounded in the nature of qisas.
4
Distinguish from the case of multiple victims
If a man kills two men and one guardian waives, the other guardian can still demand qisas because there are two separate obligations of qisas. Here, the obligation is one single qisas shared among co-plaintiffs.
This contrast strengthens the argument: indivisibility applies only when there is one victim.
5
Determine the payment details
The diyah is wajib upon the killer's estate in three years, because intentional killing (amd) is not borne by the aqila (tribal surety).
This is the standard Hanafi rule for intentional murder when qisas is not executed.

Killing After Waiver Without Knowledge: No Qisas, Zufar's Disagreement
Ruling on killer who unknowingly kills after co-plaintiff waived

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas when a co-plaintiff kills after a waiver without knowledge, and evaluate Zufar's opposing argument using qiyas.

Core Idea
Ignorance of waiver spares qisas; knowledge triggers it.

This passage explores a subtle intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: after one co-plaintiff waives qisas, the other kills the defendant unknowingly. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn) hold that ignorance of the waiver or its effect prevents qisas, requiring only half diyah. Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl, a notable Hanafi, argues for qisas by analogy. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish between intentional murder and mistaken killing in the context of joint rights.

1

The ruling applies whether the killer was unaware of the waiver or unaware of its legal effect.
2

Zufar argues that qisas is obligatory because the waiver had already made the defendant's blood inviolable; the killer's ignorance is irrelevant.
Zufar's position is stated explicitly in the text.

Zufar likens the case to someone who kills an innocent person mistakenly thought to be a murderer. In both, the victim's blood is protected.
This is a qiyas khafi: the illa is the protected status of the blood.

The practical difference: under Zufar's view, the killer would be executed; under the majority, he only pays half diyah.

The Hanafi school holds that qisas (retaliatory amputation) cannot be applied when multiple perpetrators jointly sever a single limb. The ruling extends: for any number of cutters of a single hand, or any number of victims from a single cutter, there is no qisas; only proportional arsh is due, divided equally among the liable parties.
The Shafi'i position is noted as a disagreement.

Ruling on qisas with absent heir

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An absent heir does not block qisas; the present heir may execute it immediately, and the absent heir later claims diya.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses a practical scenario in qisas: multiple heirs to a hand injury, one present and one absent. The ruling clarifies the interplay between the right to exact retaliation and the right to compensation, ensuring justice without delay.

You are given: a hand is cut, there are two heirs (claimants to qisas), one is present and one is absent.
2

The present heir may exact qisas on the entire hand immediately, without waiting for the absent heir. The reason: the absence removes the sharing (muzāḥamah) that would otherwise limit his right.
The text says 'fajaza lahu an yaqtass' – it is permissible.
3
Determine the absent heir's right
When the absent heir returns, he does not claim qisas again; instead, he receives diya (blood money) for his share of the hand.

Analyze the effect of pardon
If one heir pardons his right to qisas, that right is voided. The other heir may then proceed with qisas on the entire hand.
Pardon extinguishes the share, leaving the other heir's right unaffected.
5
Handle destruction by natural cause
If the victim's hand is destroyed by a heavenly affliction (e.g., disease) before qisas is carried out, no liability remains. Since qisas becomes impossible without the object, and the loss was not caused by the assailant, no diya is owed.
The text: 'la shay-a ‘alayh' – nothing is owed.

Ruling on validity of a slave's confession to intentional murder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a slave's confession to intentional murder and evaluate Zufar's counterargument using the principle that self-harming confessions are accepted.

Core Idea
A slave's confession to intentional murder is valid because it harms only his own life, not the master's property.

In Hanafi fiqh, confession (iqrār) is a strong form of evidence, but its validity can be challenged when it harms a third party. This lesson examines a specific dispute between the mainstream Hanafi school and the Hanafi scholar Zufar regarding a slave's confession to intentional murder (qatl al-‘amd). Understanding this case will sharpen your ability to distinguish between confessions that are self-harming and those that harm others, a key distinction in Hanafi evidence law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the ruling given by the matn: 'If the slave confesses to intentional murder, qisas becomes binding upon him.' This is the standard Hanafi position.
Qisas means retributive punishment, usually in the form of death.
2
Identify Zufar's objection
Zufar argues the confession is invalid because it leads to the annulment of the master's right (i.e., the slave's life is the master's property). He analogizes it to a slave's confession to a debt, which would harm the master and is thus invalid.
Zufar's analogy: confession to debt → master loses property; confession to murder → master loses slave's life.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds with the principle that the slave is harming himself (his own life), not the master's wealth. Therefore, the confession is trustworthy and accepted. The master's property right over the slave's life does not bar the confession because the slave's own life is at stake.
The key distinction: self-harm vs. harm to a third party.
4
Distinguish intentional from unintentional murder
The passage contrasts intentional murder (where qisas applies) with unintentional murder (where diya is due). For unintentional murder, the slave's confession does not bind the master; the diya remains a debt on the slave's neck (to be worked off).
This distinction reinforces the logic: in intentional murder, the slave's life is directly involved; in unintentional, it's a financial obligation that could harm the master.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence of the dispute: according to the Hanafi school, the victim's heirs can demand qisas from the slave; according to Zufar, the confession is invalid and no qisas is imposed, preserving the master's property.
This shows how a difference in legal reasoning can lead to opposite outcomes in practice.

Qisas in Head Wounds: The Exception of Simhaq
Explanation of qisas ruling for head wounds and consensus on no qisas for simhaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas for head wounds below mudihat and identify the exception for simhaq, explaining the rationale of impossibility of exact equality.

Core Idea
Qisas is possible for head wounds below mudihat except simhaq, because exact equality is impossible for that membrane depth.

This lesson examines the ruling on qisas (retaliation) for head wounds (shijaj) in Hanafi fiqh. The base text states no qisas for most head wounds, but an alternative narration from Imam Muhammad establishes qisas for wounds below mudihat, except for simhaq (the membrane covering the bone). We will explore the reasoning behind this exception and the intra-madhhab disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
Determine the act being ruled upon (head wound below mudihat) and its hukm (qisas wajib) according to Muhammad's narration (Zahir al-Riwayah). Also note the exception: simhaq has no qisas.
The matn's generality is interpreted restrictively.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational: equality (mumathala) is possible for wounds without bone fracture by measuring depth with a probe.
No direct text from Quran or Sunnah; it is a qiyas-based reasoning.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The possibility of exact retaliation (qisas) hinges on the ability to achieve equality. For simhaq, the membrane is too thin and deep, making precise cutting impossible without harming the bone, thus qisas falls.
This is a tahqiq al-manat (verification of the effective cause).
4
Note the ikhtilaf (intra-Hanafi)
Al-Hasan's narration from Abu Hanifa holds no qisas for any wound below mudihat. This contrasts with Muhammad's view that qisas applies except simhaq.
The matn's phrase 'no qisas in the rest of the shijaj' is general according to al-Hasan.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The text indicates Muhammad's view is Zahir al-Riwayah (the apparent narration) and thus the relied-upon position for fatwa.
The tarjih is based on the authority of 'Asl' and widespread transmission.

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Diyat discusses the ruling of qisas (retaliation) for head wounds (shijaj). The matn states: 'There is no qisas in the rest of the shijaj. Liability for Killing a Weapon-Wielding Aggressor
Ruling on killing a person who brandishes a weapon or stick

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which killing an aggressor brandishing a weapon or a stick incurs no liability in Hanafi fiqh, and explain the reasoning behind the distinction.

Core Idea
Killing a weapon-wielding aggressor is permissible without liability when help is unavailable, based on necessity.

In this passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, the Hanafi school addresses a critical question of self-defense: when does killing an aggressor become legally permissible without liability? The ruling distinguishes between a weapon and a stick, and varies conditions by time and place. The underlying logic is the absence of immediate help (ghawth). Let's explore the precise conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? The act is killing an aggressor who brandishes a weapon or a stick. The ruling is that there is no liability (dam hadar).
The ruling is mubah (permissible) in terms of legal classification.
2
Analyze the weapon condition
For a weapon (silāh), the condition is simple: if someone brandishes a weapon against another, at any time (night or day) and any place, the threatened person may kill the aggressor without liability.
The reason given is that a weapon causes immediate danger (la talbathu).
3
Analyze the stick condition
For a stick (ʿaṣā), the conditions are more specific: (1) at night inside a town (mīṣr), or (2) during the day on a road outside a town. In these situations, help is not readily available, so lethal force is justified.
The stick alone does not justify killing if help is available, e.g., during the day inside a town.
4
Understand the rational proof (dalīl ʿaqlī)
The proof is based on necessity: the victim cannot get help (lā yalḥaqu al-ghawth) and must defend himself. The ruling is derived from the general principle that self-defense is permissible when there is no alternative.
No specific Quran or hadith is cited; it is a rational deduction (qiyās).
5
Confirm the consensus
This ruling is muttafaq ʿalayh (agreed upon) within the Hanafi school and is the muftā bihi (operative position) from the Ẓāhir al-Riwāyah texts.
There is no intra-Hanafi or cross-madhhab dispute on this specific ruling.
