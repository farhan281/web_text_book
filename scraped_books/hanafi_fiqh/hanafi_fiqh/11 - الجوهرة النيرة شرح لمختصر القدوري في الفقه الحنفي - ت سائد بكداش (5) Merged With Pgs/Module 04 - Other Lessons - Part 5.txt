Wala, once established, is like lineage and does not transfer.

Partial Kitaba: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on partial kitaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi intra-school dispute on the validity of partial kitaba and identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.

Core Idea
Manumission (itq) is indivisible; therefore partial kitaba is invalid, and the slave becomes fully mukatab.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), on the permissibility of writing a kitaba contract for only half of a slave. The disagreement stems from a fundamental question: can manumission be partial? Abu Hanifa says yes, so kitaba can be partial; the Sahibayn say no, so kitaba must cover the entire slave. The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is that of the Sahibayn because manumission is treated as indivisible in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
State the Issue
The question: Can a master validly contract kitaba for half of his slave? The passage gives two opposing answers within the Hanafi school.
2
Present Abu Hanifa's View and Proof
Abu Hanifa argues that kitaba 'brings one towards freedom' and since manumission can be partial (a slave can be partially freed), kitaba can also be partial. Thus the half that is paid becomes free, and the other half becomes mustas'a (working to earn his freedom).
3
Present the Sahibayn's View and Proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that manumission (itq) is indivisible – you cannot be half-free. Therefore, kitaba, which leads to manumission, must also be indivisible. Hence the entire slave becomes a mukatab; if he pays the full sum, he becomes completely free.
4
Compare Practical Outcomes
Under Abu Hanifa: after paying half, the slave is half-free, half-mustas'a; earnings are split. Under the Sahibayn: after paying the full kitaba sum, he is fully free and keeps all earnings.
5
Identify the Mufta Bihi
Later Hanafi scholars prefer the Sahibayn's view because manumission is considered indivisible in the school. Therefore, a kitaba contract must cover the entire slave.

Two Types of Wala: Causes and Consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of wala, identify their causes, and explain the basic legal consequences according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Wala is of two types: emancipation-based (requires ownership at manumission) and contract-based (requires explicit agreement).

This passage from Kitab al-Wala in al-Jawhara al-Nayyira defines the two categories of wala: wala al-itaqa and wala al-muwalat. Understanding their causes is essential for determining inheritance and liability for blood money. The text also clarifies a common misconception and notes a minority view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
Read the first line: 'al-wala' naw'an' (wala is of two types). List them as they appear: wala al-itaqa (emancipation) and wala al-muwalat (alliance).
Wala al-itaqa is also called wala al-ni'mah (grace).
2
Extract the cause for each type
For wala al-itaqa, the cause is 'al-'itq 'ala milkihi' (manumission while owned). For wala al-muwalat, the cause is 'al-'aqd' (contract). Note the specific wording.
The author emphasizes 'fi al-sahih' for the first type, indicating the correct view.
3
Identify the legal consequences
For wala al-itaqa: inheritance and blood money ('aql) are established. For wala al-muwalat: the patron (higher) inherits from the client (lower) and pays blood money; the client does not inherit from the patron.
The passage explicitly states the client does not inherit from the patron.
4
Note the author's guard against another view
The phrase 'fi al-sahih' guards against those who say the cause of wala al-itaqa is merely the act of manumitting (i'taqa) without requiring ownership.
This is a cross-madhhab difference, likely with the Shafi'i school.
5
Recognize the contract requirement for muwalat
The passage states that these rulings are not established merely by conversion at someone's hands; a contract of alliance is required.
The contract must include the terms: inheritance for patron, blood money on patron's clan.

Joint Killing with Mixed Liability: No Qisas, Only Diyah
Ruling on joint killing with mixed qisas liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for joint homicide when one participant would face qisas alone and the other would not, and explain how diyah is allocated between them.

Core Idea
In joint killing, if one alone would escape qisas, both escape qisas — liability converts to diyah, each paying from his own or his 'aqila according to his individual case.

The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between individual and joint culpability in homicide. This lesson examines a scenario where two persons jointly kill, but only one of them would have been subject to qisas if acting alone. The school's ruling — no qisas for either, but diyah on each — reveals how the law balances deterrence with protection of life and family ties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Parties and Their Individual Liability
Determine the two participants. For each, ask: if he had acted alone, would qisas be wajib? Examples: stranger (yes), father (no), intentional killer (yes), mistaken killer (no), sword-wielder (yes), stick-wielder (no).
The passage lists pairs: stranger-father, intentional-errant, sword-stick.
2
Apply the Joint Killing Principle
The Hanafi rule: if two jointly kill and one of them would not have qisas if alone, then qisas is not inflicted on either. The presence of a non-qisas-liable participant blocks qisas for both.
This is based on the idea that qisas requires exact retribution, which cannot be apportioned when liability is not uniform.
3
Determine Diyah Source for Each Participant
For the one who would have qisas if alone, diyah is payable from his own wealth (fi malihi). For the one who would not have qisas if alone, diyah is on his 'aqila (male paternal relatives), as in accidental killing.
This matches the rule for individual homicides: the person who would have paid diyah from his own wealth if alone does so here; the one who would have had diyah on his 'aqila if alone keeps that arrangement.
4
Recognize the Exception: Father and Stranger
When the participants are a father and a stranger, diyah is wajib in both of their wealths, not on the father's 'aqila. This is because if the father had acted alone, he would pay diyah from his own wealth (since a father is never killed for his child, but he pays diyah from his wealth in such a case). The exception harmonises the joint case with the individual rule.
The text says: 'as for the father and a stranger if they participate, diyah is obligatory in both of their wealths, because if the father were alone, diyah would be obligatory in his wealth.'

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The actual killer is the one whose injury is conclusively fatal, not the first attacker.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a classic scenario to illustrate the principle of legal causation in homicide. When two people attack the same victim with different wounds, the Hanafi school does not automatically share liability; instead, it scrutinizes each injury's potential to cause death. Understanding this ruling refines your ability to apply qisas and diyah in complex cases.

Guided Path

State the scenario and the issue
Identify the two acts: belly-splitting (survivable or not) and neck-striking (always fatal). The question is who is the legal killer.

When the belly wound is survivable (life conceivable for a day or part of a day), the neck-striker is the killer because his wound is instantly fatal.

The ruling is based on causation via survival likelihood. The injury that definitively ends life is the legal cause of death.
This is a rational inference (qiyas) from the principle that causation follows conclusiveness.

One Offender, Two Victims: Sharing Qisas and Diyah
Ruling on qisas and diyah when one cuts both victims' hands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qisas and diyah when one person cuts the hands of two victims, and explain the distribution of rights in both scenarios (both present vs. one present).

Core Idea
Two victims sharing one hand: qisas is cut once, half diyah split.

This lesson presents a detailed case from Hanafi fiqh where a single offender cuts the right hands of two individuals. The ruling navigates the tension between qisas (retaliation) and diyah (compensation) when multiple victims share a single body part. Understanding this case sharpens the student's ability to apply the principles of injury compensation and joint claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and act
One offender cuts the right hands of two victims. Determine each victim's right to qisas (retaliation) on the offender's right hand.
Each victim initially has a right to cut the offender's right hand as qisas.
2
Recognize the conflict
There is only one right hand of the offender, but two claimants. They cannot both receive full qisas on the same hand.
This is a case of 'muzahama' (competition) among victims.
3
Apply the Hanafi resolution when both appear
The victims together cut the offender's one hand, and the offender pays half the diyah of a hand (which is half of full diyah of a person). The half diyah is split equally between the two victims.
The half diyah compensates for the part of the right that could not be fulfilled by qisas alone.
4
Consider the scenario with one victim present
If only one victim appears, he may cut the offender's hand immediately without waiting. The absent victim, when present, receives half of the full human diyah from the present victim.
The present victim's right to full qisas is restored due to the absence of competition, but he must compensate the absent victim for his share.
5
Summarize the legal reasoning
The right of each victim is established in the entire hand. Competition (muzahama) reduces the right to partial qisas and partial diyah. When competition is absent, full qisas revives, but compensation remains for the absent victim.
The commentary explains that the absent victim may either claim his right or forgive.

Forgiveness and Act of God in Hand Injury
Ruling on when one victim forgives or the offender's hand is lost

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings when one victim forgives and when the offender's hand is lost by an act of God, and apply these rulings to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
Forgiveness by one victim extinguishes his right; loss of the hand by act of God extinguishes all liability.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two scenarios in qisas for hand injuries: when one of two victims forgives, and when the offender's hand perishes by an act of God before execution. Understanding these rulings clarifies the boundaries of qisas and the principle that liability is tied to the specific limb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two distinct situations: (1) one victim forgives, (2) the offender's hand is lost by an act of God. Note that these are separate cases.
The text uses 'إذا عفا أحدهما' and 'وإن ذهبت يده بآفة سماوية'.
2
Determine the legal consequence for each scenario
For forgiveness: 'بطل حقه' – the forgiving victim's right becomes void. The other victim retains the right to cut the hand. For act of God: 'لا شيء عليه' – nothing is owed; the right of both victims is extinguished.
The consequence differs: one victim's forgiveness only affects his own right; act of God destroys the subject of qisas entirely.
3
Understand the rationale
The principle is that qisas is tied to the specific limb. When the limb perishes through no fault of the victim (act of God), the object of qisas no longer exists, so the claim is lost. There is no transfer to diyah or another limb.
This is consistent with the Hanafi rule that qisas is a right over the limb itself, not a monetary claim.
