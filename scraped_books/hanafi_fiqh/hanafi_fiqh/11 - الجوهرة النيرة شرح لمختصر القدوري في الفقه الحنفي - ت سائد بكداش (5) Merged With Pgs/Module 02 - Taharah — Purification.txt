Iddah of a Pregnant Woman Ends with Delivery
Explains iddah of pregnant woman and related details.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the iddah of a pregnant woman and identify the conditions under which delivery ends the iddah.

Core Idea
A pregnant woman's iddah ends with delivery, regardless of timing.

The Qur'an establishes that the iddah of a pregnant woman is until she delivers. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi commentary on this ruling, including cases of multiple births, miscarriage, and revocable divorce. Understanding these details ensures correct application of marriage law after divorce or death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Recognize that the iddah of a pregnant woman ends when she delivers her pregnancy, regardless of the time elapsed since divorce or death.
This is based on Qur'an 65:4 and consensus (ijma').
2
Explore the expanded details
Note that the ruling applies whether the pregnancy is from divorce or death, whether the woman is free or enslaved, and whether the child is stillborn or alive. Multiple births end with the last child.
Also, if more than half of the child emerges in a revocable divorce, the divorce becomes irrevocable (ba'in).
3
Understand exceptions and special cases
If she miscarries, iddah ends only if the fetus shows signs of creation (limbs formed). If menstruation ceases, iddah continues by waiting for menstruation until menopause, then by months.
A young girl who menstruates for the first time during iddah by months must restart iddah by menstruation.

Iddah of a Dhimmī Woman: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on Dhimmī iddah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the two companions regarding the iddah of a Dhimmī woman married to a Dhimmī, and evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa exempts a Dhimmī woman from iddah when married to a Dhimmī; the two companions require it.

The iddah of a non-Muslim woman living under Islamic protection raises questions of jurisdiction. In this lesson, we explore the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two main students on whether a Dhimmī widow or divorcee must observe iddah. The dispute hinges on whether iddah is a right of Allah only (binding on Muslims) or also a right of the husband (binding on all).

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The case: a Dhimmī woman married to a Dhimmī husband. Does she observe iddah upon his death or divorce?
There is consensus that if she is married to a Muslim, she must observe iddah.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa says: No iddah is required if their religion does not mandate it, because iddah is partly a right of Allah and she is not addressed for that right.
The husband is also Dhimmī, so his right is not enforceable in Islamic law.
3
State the companions' position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say: Iddah is obligatory upon her, regardless of her religion, because iddah protects lineage (a right of the husband).
They consider iddah a universal requirement for all women in Islamic jurisdiction.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, she may remarry immediately after her husband's death (according to her own religion). Under the companions, she must wait the prescribed iddah.
This affects her ability to remarry and the validity of the new marriage in Islamic law.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The text does not specify tarjih, but in the Hanafi school, Abu Hanifa's view is the primary position (mufta bihi).
Later Hanafi works often adopt Abu Hanifa's view.

Iddah of Enslaved and Amenorrheic Women
States iddah duration for enslaved and non-menstruating women

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the iddah rulings for enslaved women and non-menstruating women, cite the proof, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on mustas'ah.

Core Idea
An enslaved woman's iddah is two cycles; if she doesn't menstruate, one and a half months.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira clarifies the iddah duration for enslaved women and those who do not menstruate. The ruling is derived from a prophetic hadith and the principle that slavery reduces obligations. It also highlights a difference between Abu Hanifa and his two companions regarding the mustas'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories and rulings
Note two cases: an enslaved woman who menstruates has iddah of two cycles; a non-menstruating enslaved woman has iddah of one and a half months.
The half-month is 45 days.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith: 'The divorce of an enslaved woman is two pronouncements, and her iddah is two menstrual cycles.' This is the primary proof.
The hadith is weak (zanni al-thubut) but used in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the ta'lil (reasoning)
Slavery halves obligations (tanṣīf). Menstrual cycles cannot be subdivided, so halving is in number (2 cycles instead of 3). Months can be subdivided, so halving is by duration (1.5 months instead of 3).
Free woman's iddah: 3 cycles or 3 months if non-menstruating.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on mustas'ah
The mustas'ah (enslaved woman earning to buy freedom) is like a maktabah according to Abu Hanifa (2 cycles), but like a free woman according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (3 cycles).
The text does not give tarjih.

Commentary
This passage provides rulings on iddah for enslaved women and women who do not menstruate. The matn states that an enslaved woman's iddah is two menstrual cycles, and a non-menstruating woman's iddah is one and a half months. The commentary supports the first ruling with a hadith: the Prophet said, “The divorce of an enslaved woman is two pronouncements, and her iddah is two menstrual cycles.” The reason for the reduction compared to a free woman (whose iddah is three cycles) is that slavery reduces obligations analogously to halving the number of divorces, and since menstruation cannot be split, it is halved by number of cycles. The ruling applies to various categories of enslaved women: mudabbarah (promised freedom upon master's death), umm walad (mother of master's child), and maktabah (contractually purchasing her freedom). There is a dispute regarding the mustas'ah (an enslaved woman who is earning money to buy her freedom): Abu Hanifa considers her like a maktabah (so her iddah is two cycles), while the two companions consider her like a free woman (three cycles). For a woman who does not menstruate (e.g., due to young age or menopause), her iddah is one and a half months. This is because months can be subdivided, so free women's iddah of three months is halved to one and a half for enslaved women. The woman's husband's status (free or enslaved) does not affect her iddah; it is based on her own status. The passage ends with a question: if a divorced woman claims her iddah has ended after a period, after how many days is she believed? The answer is not provided in this window, but in Hanafi fiqh, the general rule is that a woman is believed regarding her own iddah due to necessity, unless her claim is impossible (e.g., she claims iddah ended within a few days).

The Ruling on Counting Menstruation During Divorce
Ruling with ta'lil from matn and sharh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on counting the menstruation that occurs at the time of divorce and explain the proof based on the requirement of three complete menstruations.

Core Idea
A menstruation is only counted if it is complete; divorce in progress renders it incomplete.

In Hanafi fiqh, the 'iddah of a divorced woman is calculated by three complete menstrual cycles. A key issue arises when a woman is divorced while already menstruating: does that current cycle count towards the three? The classical text clarifies that it does not, because the cycle must be entirely after the divorce to be considered 'complete.' This ruling is derived from the textual requirement of 'three complete menstruations' and is unanimously accepted across the schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The passage rules that when a man divorces his wife during her menstruation, that particular menstrual period is not counted in her 'iddah.
The act is divorce during menstruation; the ruling is that the current menstruation is excluded from the count.
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The sharh provides the proof: 'because the 'iddah is measured by three complete menstruations.' The key word is 'kawamil' (complete).
The proof is a causal explanation (ta'lil) based on the definition of 'iddah.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The word 'complete' implies that each menstruation counted must be entirely after the divorce. Since part of this menstruation occurred before the divorce, it is incomplete and thus not counted.
This is a straightforward linguistic reasoning: 'kawamil' excludes any cycle that has commenced prior to the moment of divorce.
4
Note the Ijma' (Consensus)
The passage does not mention any disagreement. The ruling is unanimous across madhahib (muttafaq alayh).
All schools agree that the current menstruation does not count, as it is not a complete cycle after divorce.

This passage states a ruling and its proof. The matn (original text) rules that if a man divorces his wife during her menstruation, that particular menstrual period does not count towards the three menstruations she must observe for her 'iddah. The sharh (commentary) provides the reason: the 'iddah is specified as three complete (kawamil) menstruations. Since the divorce occurred during the menstruation, part of that menstruation took place before the divorce, making it incomplete. Therefore, she must wait for three complete menstruations following the divorce, excluding the one in progress. This is a clear application of the principle that the count begins after the divorce and requires full cycles.

Two Hanafi narrations conflict: maternal aunt is like a mother, but the sister is closer by lineage.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about who has greater right to custody: the paternal half-sister (consanguine sister) or the maternal aunt. Custody among siblings' children follows a strict priority: full/uterine sister's children first, then consanguine sister's children, then nieces; non-mahram relatives have no right.

Even in enemy territory, a harbi's freeing of a Muslim or dhimmi slave is valid by consensus.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, later expanded by its commentators. The text presents a consensus (ijma) on the validity of emancipation when a harbi (non-Muslim enemy combatant) frees a slave who is either Muslim or a dhimmi (protected non-Muslim). The ruling underscores the universal protected status of such individuals, overriding general principles about harbi actions in dar al-harb.

The ruling is explicit: the emancipation takes effect.
2
Identify the proof
The text explicitly states 'إجماعاً' (by consensus). This is the proof cited. In Hanafi usul, consensus (ijma) is a primary source of law, considered qat'i (definitive) in both transmission and meaning.
No other proof is mentioned; consensus alone suffices.
3
Analyze the basis of the ruling
Why is this not simply covered by the general rule that a harbi's actions in dar al-harb are invalid? The slave's faith (Islam or dhimma) grants a protected status (hurmah) that is universally recognized, even in enemy territory.
The commentary explains that the harbi's authority over such a slave is acknowledged only for this beneficial act, not for harmful ones.

Does this ruling apply to any slave freed by a harbi? The text specifies 'Muslim or dhimmi' slaves, indicating that it does not apply to a harbi freeing a harbi slave. The consensus is specific to these categories.
The commentary notes prior disagreement about harbi slaves in general, but this consensus settled the matter for Muslim/dhimmi.

Ruling on a slave buying his own child

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A slave cannot own property; his purchase is void, so no emancipation follows.

This brief passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a specific scenario: a slave purchasing his own offspring. The ruling is straightforward but illustrates a fundamental principle of ownership in Hanafi fiqh. Let's examine the text and the underlying logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a slave purchasing his own child. The ruling is that the child does not become free (la yu'taq).
The text states the ruling directly: 'لا يعتق' (it does not emancipate).
2
Isolate the reason (illa)
The text provides the reason: 'لأنه لا ملك له' (because he has no ownership). This is the effective cause for the ruling.
The illa is the slave's lack of legal capacity to own property.
3
Understand the underlying principle
In Hanafi fiqh, a slave is himself owned property and cannot independently own other property. Therefore, any purchase he makes is invalid (batil).
This principle is consistent across the school (zahir al-riwayah).
4
Derive the consequence
Since the purchase is batil, the child does not become the slave's property and thus cannot be emancipated by this transaction.
The child remains a slave of the original owner.

Ruling When a Slave Buys His Master's Mahram Relative
Ruling on a slave buying a mahram relative of his master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a slave purchases a mahram relative of his master and explain the rationale that the slave's acquisition belongs to the master.

Core Idea
A slave's purchase of the master's mahram relative frees the relative because the master's ownership triggers emancipation.

In this lesson, we examine an exception to the general principle that a slave's actions are limited by his lack of ownership. The text presents a case where a slave buys a mahram relative of his master, and the ruling that the relative becomes free. The Hanafi school bases this on the rule that the slave's acquisitions belong to the master, and when the master thus owns his own mahram relative, emancipation is wajib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? A slave purchases a mahram relative of his master. The ruling is that the relative becomes free ('atiq). The text states: 'فإن اشترى ذا رحم محرم من مولاه عتق' — 'if he purchases a mahram relative of his master, it emancipates'.
The act is a purchase by a slave, and the ruling is the consequence of emancipation, not the validity of the purchase.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The text gives the reason: 'لأن المولى ملكه' — 'because the master owns him'. This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the principle that a slave's acquisitions belong to his master. The purchase is effectively attributed to the master, so the master now owns the purchased person.
The proof is not a textual source from Quran or Sunnah but a legal principle derived from the rules of slave ownership.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How exactly does the master's ownership lead to emancipation? The purchased person is a mahram of the master (e.g., his mother, sister, etc.). In Hanafi law, a person cannot own his mahram relatives; if ownership occurs, it necessitates emancipation. Therefore, since the master owns the mahram through the slave's purchase, the relative must be freed.
This is an application of the maxim: 'من ملك ذا رحم محرم منه عتق عليه' — whoever owns a mahram relative becomes obliged to free them.
4
Note the condition and scope
The ruling is conditional on the purchased person being a mahram relative of the master. If the slave buys a non-mahram slave, the purchase is still valid and the slave becomes the master's property, but no emancipation occurs (unless other rules apply).
This ruling is a specific exception within the broader rules of slave acquisitions.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical effect is that the mahram relative becomes free immediately upon the purchase. The master cannot retain ownership; he must let the relative go free. If the master wishes to keep the relative, he cannot; the emancipation is wajib (binding).
If the slave bought the mahram with the master's money, the master is not financially harmed because the slave's acquisition is for the master anyway.

Partial Emancipation: Divisibility and the Hanafi Intra-School Dispute
Ruling on partial emancipation with intra-Hanafi dispute and proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on partial emancipation, explain the qiyas of the Two, and apply the mufta bihi ruling.

Core Idea
Emancipation is either divisible (Abu Hanifa) or indivisible (Two); the relied-upon view rules complete freedom.

In this lesson, we examine the classical Hanafi dispute over freeing part of a slave. The matn presents both views: Abu Hanifa holds that the stated portion is freed and the slave must earn the rest, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule the entire slave becomes free without compensation. We will analyze the proofs, particularly the qiyas (analogical reasoning) that underlies the Two's position, and see how the school ultimately prefers their view for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two rulings
The act is the owner's statement freeing a part of his slave. Two rulings exist: per Abu Hanifa only that part is freed and the slave must pay the remaining value; per the Two the entire slave is freed.
The matn says 'عند أبي حنيفة... وعندهما'.
2
Examine the example cases
The commentator provides examples: half, third, quarter, unknown part, and 'sahm'. For each, apply both views.
Note that for unknown part and 'sahm', the Two still rule full freedom, while Abu Hanifa orders clarification or defaults to one-sixth for sahm.
3
Analyze the proof: the Two's qiyas
The Two argue that emancipation is indivisible because it removes a legal weakness (slavery), similar to divorce, qisas pardon, and istilad. They use analogy (qiyas shabahah).
Key concept: 'العتق إثبات العتق وهو قوة حكمية، وإثباتها بإزالة ضدها وهو الرق الذي هو ضعف حكمي'.
4
Consider Abu Hanifa's counterargument
Abu Hanifa holds that emancipation is divisible because it involves ownership which can be partial. The Two's analogy is rejected because emancipation differs from divorce etc. in being a transfer of right.
The source does not detail Abu Hanifa's full proof, but implies his view based on property divisibility.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and mufta bihi
The practical difference: if owner says 'half free', per Abu Hanifa the slave is half free and must earn half value; per the Two, fully free and owes nothing. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) view in the Hanafi school is that of the Two.
Later Hanafi fatwa authorities prefer the Two's view for its protection of the slave.

Therefore, if a mukatib buys the master's son, that purchase is his own, not the master's. Since the master does not acquire ownership through the mukatib's purchase, the condition of ownership of a mahram relative is not fulfilled, so emancipation does not occur. This is agreed upon by all scholars.

Legal status of mukatab after valid kitabah and his transactional capacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on the mukatab's status after valid kitabah and apply the rules governing his transactional capacity, including sale, purchase, travel, and marriage.

Core Idea
The mukatab leaves the master's hand but not his ownership, gaining capacity to earn through sale and purchase.

The Mukatab is a slave who has entered a kitabah contract to earn his freedom. A key fiqh question is what legal capacity he gains during the period of kitabah. The Hanafi school, through the majority of its mashayikh, distinguishes between leaving the master's hand (yad) and leaving his ownership (milk). This ruling has direct implications on what transactions the mukatab may perform, with whom, and under what conditions. In this lesson, we unpack the passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah to understand the precise legal position and its practical applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal status of the mukatab after valid kitabah
The text begins by stating the majority position: the mukatab leaves the master's hand (control) but remains in his ownership (milk). Note the distinction between yad and milk—a key Hanafi analytical tool.
A minority view holds that he leaves ownership entirely but does not own himself, like a buyer with an option condition.
2
Understand the immediate effect of fakk al-hajr
The kitabah immediately removes the legal interdiction (hajr), allowing the mukatab to earn. This permission is inherent in the contract, not requiring express consent.
Earning necessitates sale, purchase, and travel, so these become permissible.
3
Analyze the permissibility of sale, purchase, and travel
The text explains that these are allowed because kitabah implies permission to earn. The mukatab may even contract with his master as if with a stranger, except for specific prohibitions.
Prohibition on murabahah between them and on riba-like transactions (e.g., buying two dirhams for one) ensures fairness.
4
Examine the invalidity of conditions that contradict the contract
If the master stipulates that the mukatab cannot travel (e.g., leave Kufa), the condition is void because it contradicts the contract's purpose—control over one's hand for earning. The kitabah remains valid.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle that conditions contravening the contract's essence are null.
5
Recognize the restriction on marriage
Marriage requires the master's explicit permission. This is because marriage affects the master's interest (e.g., potential children) and is not directly required for earning.
Compare with travel, which is directly linked to earning; hence no permission needed.

The Mudabbirah Mukatabah's Choice When Master Dies Insolvent
Intra-Hanafi dispute over choice of payment for a mudabbirah mukatabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's rulings on the mudabbirah mukatabah's payment choice and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Partial freedom through tadbir: choice vs. compulsion hinges on whether freedom spreads to the whole slave.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute involving a female slave who is both a mukatabah (under kitabah contract) and a mudabbirah (declared free upon the master's death). When the master dies with no other assets, how much must she pay to secure her freedom? Abu Hanifa grants her a choice; his two disciples insist on the lesser amount without choice. Understanding their underlying principles reveals a deeper debate about the effect of partial freedom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the combined statuses
The slave is simultaneously a mukatabah (paying for freedom over time) and a mudabbirah (freedom deferred to master's death). The master dies leaving no assets besides her.
The tadbir is valid despite the existing kitabah because it adds an obligation of freedom that cannot be rescinded.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa: she has a choice to work for two-thirds of the kitabah amount or two-thirds of her market value. Sahibayn: she must pay the lesser of these two amounts, with no choice.
Both agree on the quantum if a choice is made; the dispute is solely about choice.
3
Trace the principle behind Abu Hanifa's view
Tadbir is a remission of kitabah, but remission during illness is limited to one-third of the estate. Thus only one-third of the kitabah is remitted; two-thirds remain. She faces two paths to full freedom: immediate (pay remaining kitabah) or deferred (tadbir). She may choose based on her preference.
Abu Hanifa sees the two-thirds as still slave, so she can opt for either compensation.
4
Understand the Sahibayn's opposing principle
They invoke the principle 'surat al-itaq': when part of a slave becomes free, all becomes free. Therefore, the tadbir instantly frees the entire slave. She is already free and must pay only the lesser amount immediately, as debt.
Hence no need for choice; the lesser is obligatory.
5
Identify the mufta bihi and practical consequence
Later authorities (e.g., al-Marghinani) prefer the Sahibayn's view. Under the mufta bihi, the slave must pay the lesser of two-thirds of kitabah or two-thirds of value immediately, without choice.
This protects the master's creditors by limiting the remission to the minimum.

Mukatab's Capacity: Tabarru' vs Mu'awadah with His Own Slave
Rulings on a mukatab's capacity to contract with his own slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on a mukatab's contracts with his own slave and explain the distinction between tabarru' (donation) and mu'awadah (exchange) that governs their validity.

Core Idea
A mukatab cannot donate but can trade: exchange contracts are valid, pure donations are not.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the limited legal capacity of a mukatab (a slave under a kitabah contract). It presents three scenarios involving his own slave: freeing for payment, gifting with compensation, and writing a kitabah contract. The first two are invalid because they are donations; the third is valid by istihsan because it is a mutual exchange. A final rule addresses the priority of emancipation when the mukatab's slave completes his kitabah first. Understanding this distinction between gratuitous and commutative acts is key to mastering Hanafi rulings on partial ownership.

Guided Path
1

Why can't a mukatab donate? Because he does not fully own himself or his property; gratuitous transfers would undermine his ability to complete his own kitabah payments.

Theft that involves debts or creditor relationships requires careful analysis. The ruling of amputation (hadd) is not automatically applied; it depends on whether the thief has a personal right to collect the debt, or whether the stolen item is considered a direct settlement of a debt. Slaughter purifies even non-edible animals, except humans and pigs, by analogy with tanning.

Islamic slaughter (dhabh) serves not only to make meat permissible but also as a means of purification. The ruling is established through a hadith and qiyas, with a key difference regarding the necessity of tasmiyah.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the matn followed by its commentary. The ruling states that when an animal whose meat is not eaten (such as a mule or donkey) is slaughtered according to Islamic law, its flesh and hide become pure (tahir). There are two exceptions: humans and pigs. Human flesh is impure by virtue of sanctity (hurma) and cannot be purified by slaughter, and the pig is excluded due to its inherent filth (najasa). The commentator draws an analogy with tanning (dabagh), citing the hadith 'Tanning of the hide is its slaughter' – just as tanning purifies the hide, slaughter purifies the entire animal. The text then contrasts this with an animal slaughtered by a Magian (Zoroastrian), whose slaughter is not considered valid dhabh in Islamic law. The commentator clarifies that the purity extends to the fat (shahm), so that if it falls into a small amount of water, it does not render the water impure. Purification of Non-Edible Animals and the Role of Tasmiyah
Ruling on purification of meat and fat of non-edible animals with ikhtilaf about tasmiyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the purification of non-edible animals and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) that tasmiyah is required.

Core Idea
Without tasmiyah, the slaughter of a non-edible animal does not purify it—otherwise the Magian's slaughter would be valid.

In this passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayra, we encounter a key ruling about the purification of animals that are not permissible to eat. The author explains that both the meat and fat become pure through proper slaughter, but only if tasmiyah is uttered. The passage also presents a rational proof that tasmiyah is indispensable, based on a comparison with the Magian's slaughter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being purified? The meat and fat of non-edible animals (ma la yu'kalu lahmuhu). The ruling is that they become pure through tazkiyah (ritual slaughter) with tasmiyah, making them permissible for use (though not for eating).
The purification is a condition for benefiting from the animal's parts.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is a rational argument (dalil aqli): if mere slaughter sufficed, then the slaughter of a Magian (who does not say tasmiyah) would also purify, but it does not. Therefore, tasmiyah is a necessary condition.
The proof is implicit: it uses a conditional hypothetical to demonstrate the necessity.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the proof establish the requirement? By showing that the alternative (requiring only slaughter) leads to an invalid conclusion (purification by Magian slaughter). Since that conclusion is false, the premise that tasmiyah is not required must be false.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is a difference of opinion: some say mere slaughter suffices for purification, while the preferred view (al-zahir) requires slaughter with tasmiyah. The text sides with the latter.
The ikhtilaf is within the Hanafi school; al-Hidayah prefers tasmiyah.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The apparent view (al-zahir) is taken as the mufta bihi position. The authority is al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah, who prefers the view requiring tasmiyah.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh of al-Jawhara al-Nayra discusses the rulings on the purification of animals whose meat is not eaten (e.g., carnivores). The author first states that just as the meat is purified by proper slaughter (tazkiyah), so is the fat, such that if it falls into a small volume of water, it does not render it impure. Then he presents a difference of opinion about using such purified fat for purposes other than eating, like oil mixed with carcass fat. The core issue: what is the cause of purification?
