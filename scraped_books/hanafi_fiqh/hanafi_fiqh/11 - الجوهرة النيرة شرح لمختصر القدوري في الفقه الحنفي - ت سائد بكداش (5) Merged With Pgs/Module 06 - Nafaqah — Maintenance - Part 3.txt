Jointly Owned Slave: One Owner Refuses to Spend
Ruling on joint ownership of slave when one owner withholds maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when one co-owner of a slave refuses maintenance and identify the right of recourse.

Core Idea
If one co-owner refuses maintenance, the other must spend and can recover the share.

This ruling addresses a practical problem in joint ownership of slaves: what happens when one owner fails to provide for the slave's basic needs. The Hanafi school provides a clear solution that balances the slave's welfare and the co-owner's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The case involves a mushtarak (jointly owned) slave and one owner refusing to pay maintenance.
2
State the obligation
The other owner must spend (nafaqah) on the slave immediately.
This is wajib.
3
Identify the remedy
After spending, the second owner has recourse (ruju') against the refusing owner to recover the share.
The recourse is a right, not a separate obligation.
4
Note the legal basis
The ruling ensures the slave is not neglected while protecting the spending owner's financial interest.
Based on the principle that joint maintenance is enforceable.

Commentary
This short ruling deals with a slave who is owned by two or more people. If one co-owner refuses to pay the slave's maintenance, the other co-owner is obligated to provide for the slave and can later recover the share from the refusing owner.

Key Terms
مشترك (mushtarak): A slave owned by more than one person jointly.

Spending on Beasts and Bees: Overburdening, Honey, and Joint Ownership
Rulings on beast overburdening, bee honey, and joint spending

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings on overburdening a beast, leaving honey for bees, and compelling a partner to spend on a jointly owned beast.

Core Idea
Kindness to animals is recommended; overburdening is disliked, leaving honey is recommended, and partners must share expenses.

This passage from Al-Jawhara Al-Nayra presents three distinct rulings related to spending on animals. First, it addresses the ethical treatment of beasts of burden, then the care of bees, and finally the obligations of joint owners. Each ruling reflects Hanafi principles of preventing harm and encouraging kindness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sub-rulings
Read the passage and list the three different scenarios: overburdening a beast, leaving honey for bees, and joint ownership spending.
Each scenario has a different hukm (legal ruling).
2
Determine the hukm for each scenario
For each, note whether the text says yukrah (disliked), yustahab (recommended), or wajib (obligatory).
The term 'yukrah' indicates makruh; 'yustahab' indicates mustahab; 'ujbira' indicates compulsion (wajib).
3
Analyze the conditions for the bee ruling
Note that leaving honey is only mustahab if no other food suffices. If alternative food exists, leaving honey is not required.
This shows that obligation depends on necessity.
4
Apply the joint ownership ruling
If one partner refuses to spend, the court compels him. This is based on the principle that each partner must contribute to necessary expenses.
This prevents harm to the animal.
5
Distinguish between makruh tanzihi and makruh tahrimi
The text does not specify which type of makruh, but given the context, it is likely makruh tanzihi (slightly disliked) as there is no explicit prohibition.
Hanafi scholars differ; here the absence of a strong deterrent suggests tanzihi.

Continuation of rulings on expenditures. It is disliked to burden a beast with what it cannot bear of heavy load and continuous travel. Likewise, if one has bees, it is recommended to leave something for them in their hives, and it is recommended that this be more in winter because it is difficult for them to come out. And if something else suffices as their food instead of honey, it is not obligatory upon him to leave honey. And if a beast is between two partners and one of them refrains from spending on it, he is compelled to do so.

Commentary
This passage presents several rulings concerning the care of animals. First, it is makruh (disliked) to overload a beast or make it travel continuously, as this causes undue hardship. Second, if one owns bees, it is mustahab (recommended) to leave some honey in the hives for them to eat, especially in winter when they cannot go out. If the bees have alternative food that suffices, it is not wajib to leave honey. Third, if a beast is jointly owned by partners and one partner refuses to spend on its feed, the court may compel him to contribute. These rulings are based on the principles of kindness to animals and preventing harm.

Key Terms
يكره (yukrah): In this context, it indicates that the action is legally disliked (makruh) and should be avoided, but not sinful.
يستحب (yustahab): It means the action is recommended (mustahab) and meritorious but not obligatory.

Spending on Divorced Woman During Iddah with Condition to Marry
Ruling on reclaiming spending with a fasid condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on reclaiming spending given to a divorced woman during iddah, and distinguish between explicit condition and implicit intention.

Core Idea
Explicit fasid condition allows reclaim; implicit intention does not — the key is the shart's presence.

This lesson examines a specific mas'ala from al-Waqi'at on spending during iddah. The ruling hinges on whether the man explicitly conditioned his spending on future marriage. Understanding this distinction clarifies when a transfer is a conditional exchange vs. a voluntary gift (mutabarri').

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The act is spending on a divorced woman during her iddah after a ba'in divorce. Note the type of divorce is irrevocable, so she is not entitled to nafaqah from her ex-husband.
2
Identify the condition or absence thereof
Determine whether the man explicitly stipulated that he would spend on condition of marriage after iddah, or whether he simply spent without condition, even if his intention was known by custom.
3
Apply the ruling for each scenario
In the first scenario (explicit condition), the man may reclaim his spending because the condition is fasid (invalid), so the spending was not a pure gift. In the second (no explicit condition), he cannot reclaim because he is considered a mutabarri' (voluntary donor).
4
Understand the legal rationale (ta'lil)
The rationale is that a fasid condition makes the transfer conditional, not a gift. Without an explicit condition, the spender is a mutabarri' who cannot reclaim.

Source Text

مسألة: قال في الواقعات: رجلٌ طلق امرأته طلاقاً بائناً، فجاء رجل إليها وهي في العدة، وقال لها: أنا أنفق عليك ما دمت في العدة بشرط أن أتزوجك إذا انقضت عدتك، فرضيت فأنفق عليها حتى مضت عدتها، ثم أبت أن تتزوج به فله أن يرجع عليها بما أنفق؛ لأنه أنفق بشرط فاسد. وهذا إذا أنفق عليها بهذا الشرط، أما إذا أنفق عليها ولم يشرط عليها التزويج، لكن علمت به عرفاً أنه أنفق لذلك: فالصحيح أنه لا يرجع عليها بشيء؛ لأنه متبرع، والله أعلم.
A mas'ala: It is stated in al-Waqi'at: A man divorced his wife with a ba'in divorce. Then a man came to her while she was in her 'iddah and said: 'I will spend on you as long as you are in the 'iddah on condition that I marry you when your 'iddah expires.' She agreed, and he spent on her until her 'iddah passed. Then she refused to marry him. He may reclaim from her what he spent because he spent with a fasid (invalid) condition. This applies if he spent with this condition. As for if he spent on her without stipulating marriage, but she knew by custom that he spent for that purpose, the correct view is that he cannot reclaim anything because he was a mutabarri' (voluntary donor). And Allah knows best.
Source pages p. 93

Commentary
This passage discusses a specific legal scenario from al-Waqi'at. A man supports a woman during her 'iddah after a ba'in divorce, with the explicit condition that she marries him after. When she refuses, he can recover the amounts spent because the condition is fasid (invalid) — a contract of support with an invalid condition is not a valid gift. The condition is void, but the spending occurred, so the man has a claim. However, if he never made the condition explicit, even if his intention was known by custom, he is considered a mutabarri' (voluntary donor) and has no right of reclaim. This distinguishes between conditional and unconditional transfers.

Key Terms
طلاق بائن (talaq ba'in): A type of divorce that is irrevocable; the husband cannot take his wife back without a new marriage and new mahr.
شرط فاسد (shart fasid): A condition that is invalid in Islamic law, e.g., a condition that contradicts the purpose of the contract.
متبرع (mutabarri'): One who gives voluntarily without expectation of return.
