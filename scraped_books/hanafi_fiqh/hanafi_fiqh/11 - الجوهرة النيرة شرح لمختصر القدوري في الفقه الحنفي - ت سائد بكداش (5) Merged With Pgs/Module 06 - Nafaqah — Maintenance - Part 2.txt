Learning Objective

Core Idea
The judge can allocate maintenance if the custodian acknowledges or if the judge knows the facts.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the classical Hanafi text Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah. The passage clarifies when a judge can intervene to provide maintenance for dependents of an absent husband whose property is held by a third party. Understanding these conditions is crucial for ensuring justice in family support cases.

Guided Path

Identify the parties and the asset

2
Examine the custodian's acknowledgment
Check if the custodian acknowledges both the property and the marriage. The judge can then order maintenance from that property.
3
Consider the judge's independent knowledge
Even without the custodian's acknowledgment, if the judge knows the facts (e.g., through evidence), he can still order maintenance. The type of property (trust, debt, mudarabah) does not matter.
4
Identify the barring scenario
If the custodian denies either the property or the marriage, the judge cannot issue an order, as the basis is contested.
5
Summarize the ruling
The judge's authority to allocate maintenance depends on reliable knowledge—either through the custodian's clear acknowledgment or through the judge's own verified knowledge.

Limitation of Maintenance from Absent Person's Property
Ruling limiting maintenance from absent person's property to specific relatives

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Only those whose maintenance is wajib before decree can claim from an absent person's property.

In Hanafi fiqh, the ability of a qadi to order maintenance from the property of an absent person (gha'ib) is not unlimited. This passage from the Mukhtasar al-Quduri, with its commentary, clarifies that only three categories of dependents—the wife, minor children, and parents—can claim maintenance from the absent person's wealth. The reason lies in the pre-existing obligation of their support, which exists independently of a court decree.

Guided Path
1

The passage states that a judge cannot order maintenance from the property of an absent person except for specific individuals. Recognize that this limits judicial authority.
2
List the Eligible Relatives
The commentator specifies three categories: wife, minor children, and parents (mother and father). Commit these to memory.
Minor children are those who have not reached puberty or are financially dependent.
3
Understand the Basis: Pre-existing Wajib
The reason for this limitation is that maintenance for these three is 'wajib before the judge's decree' (واجبة قبل قضاء القاضي). This means the obligation exists independently of any court order.
Other relatives, like adult children or siblings, do not have this inherent obligation; their maintenance becomes wajib only after a judge orders it.
4
Contrast with Other Relatives
For relatives not in the list, the judge cannot order maintenance from the absent person's property because their maintenance is not wajib before a decree, and a decree cannot be passed in the absence of the person (gha'ib).
The judge's decree for these three is merely an 'assistance' (إعانة) to enforce a pre-existing right.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a husband initially poor, later becomes wealthy. How does this affect the wife's maintenance? Past Maintenance Claims and the Nature of Nafaqah as Silah
Ruling that past maintenance is unenforceable without a decree or agreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on past maintenance claims and explain why maintenance is considered silah rather than iwad, and identify when it becomes enforceable.

Core Idea
Past maintenance is not a debt unless decreed by a judge or agreed upon, because nafaqah is a gift, not compensation.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah addresses a critical distinction in Hanafi fiqh: maintenance (nafaqah) is a gratuitous transfer (silah), not a compensation (iwad). Consequently, unpaid past maintenance does not automatically become a financial liability. Only a prior court order or a mutual agreement fixes the obligation. This rule illustrates how the Hanafi school's classification of a financial right determines its enforceability.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule and exceptions
Identify the default ruling: the wife has no claim for past maintenance. Then identify the two exceptions: if a judge had ordered maintenance, or if she had reconciled (agreed) with the husband on an amount.
2
Understand the legal reasoning (talil)
Analyze why past maintenance is not automatically owed: because nafaqah is silah (a gift) not iwad (compensation). In Hanafi law, a gratuity does not become a binding debt until it is fixed by a decree or agreement.
Contrast with debts; debts are binding from the moment of contract.
3
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Note the cross-madhhab dispute. The Shafi'i school treats nafaqah as a debt (dayn) from the time it falls due, so past maintenance is always owed regardless of a decree.
This distinction has practical consequences for legal strategy.
4
Apply the principle to new scenarios
Test whether a wife can claim arrears if she can prove the husband had the means but did not pay, without a prior order or agreement. Hanafi answer: no, because the obligation is not firmly established.
The ruling underscores the need for proactive legal action.

Effect of Death on Maintenance Debt and Related Rulings
States that maintenance debt ends with death, and rules on release, reimbursement, and advance payment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the effect of death on maintenance obligations and distinguish between recoverable and non-recoverable advance payments according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Maintenance is a gift (silah) — it ends with death and cannot be released in advance.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explains several subsidiary rulings (furu') arising from the principle that maintenance (nafaqah) is a gratuitous transfer rather than a pure debt. Understanding these details is essential for judges and muftis dealing with spousal maintenance disputes, especially when death or advance payment occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the effect of death on past maintenance
If either spouse dies after maintenance was ordered, any unpaid past maintenance drops. This is because maintenance is classified as a gift (silah), which is voided by death like a gift before possession.
This contrasts with a regular debt, which would survive and be claimable from the estate.
2
Understand the reasoning: maintenance as a gift
The text explicitly states, 'because maintenance is a gift (silah), and a gift is nullified by death'. This analogy to a gift (hibah) before delivery is key to all derived rulings.
The Hanafi school treats ongoing maintenance as a periodical obligation that is not yet a fixed debt until it accrues.
3
Distinguish valid vs invalid release (bara'ah)
A wife cannot validly release her husband from future maintenance obligations because bara'ah of an obligation not yet due is invalid. However, releasing past due maintenance is valid.
The principle: release of a future debt (dyn mustaqbal) is void; it must be an existing debt.
4
Track the reimbursement right and its termination
If the wife spent her own money after the judge fixed maintenance, she can claim reimbursement from the husband while both are alive. This right ends with either spouse's death, unless the judge specifically ordered repayment as a debt (dyn) — then it survives death.
The distinction hinges on whether the ordered maintenance was considered a debt (dyn) or a mere right to future support.
5
Analyze the advance payment ruling and intra-Hanafi difference
If the husband advanced a full year's maintenance and the wife dies before the year ends, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule he cannot recover any portion. Muhammad differs in some narrations, but the relied-upon position is no recovery.
This aligns with the gift analogy: once paid, it is considered a completed transfer; death does not create a right of return.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Advance maintenance is a gift (silah) per Abu Hanifa and a guaranteed debt per Muhammad.

This passage addresses a practical question: if a husband pays his wife several months or years of maintenance in advance, and she dies before that period ends, can the husband reclaim the unused portion? The Hanafi masters differ, with the majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) treating the advance as an irrevocable gift, while Imam Muhammad treats it as a conditional advance that must be refunded for future periods not yet lived. Understanding this disagreement reveals how legal characterization (silah vs. qabd madmun) drives the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the two positions
The matn presents: husband advanced yearly maintenance, wife dies mid-year. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf say no return; Muhammad says return the unexpired portion. Note the consensus that if goods are destroyed, no liability.
The dispute applies equally to clothing (kiswah) and to the husband's death.

They analogize to hibah (gift): once delivered, ownership vests absolutely. Since maintenance is a silah (gratuitous grant), it cannot be reclaimed after death. Hence the wife's heirs keep it.
Qiyas on hibah: gift delivery extinguishes right to retract.
3
Analyze Imam Muhammad's reasoning
He treats the advance as a qabd madmun (guaranteed receipt) for future entitlement. The wife is only entitled to maintenance for days actually lived; the unearned part must be returned, akin to a debt that is no longer owed.
Qiyas on debt: if a debt is paid early and the basis for it disappears, the payer may reclaim.
4

Under Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf, the wife's heirs keep the full advance; under Muhammad, they must return the unexpired portion. This affects inheritance distribution.
The dispute is purely about post-death reclamation, not during life.

Muhammad reportedly softened his position: if the advance covered one month or less, it is considered trivial and non-revertible. This exception shows attention to practicality.

Maintenance of a Slave's Child
Ruling on who is responsible for maintenance of a slave's child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on maintenance of a slave's child and identify the hierarchy of responsibility for maintenance.

Core Idea
A slave father is not obligated to maintain his child; the responsibility shifts to the mother or her relatives.

In this passage, we learn a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh regarding maintenance (nafaqah) of a child born to a slave father. Normally, the father is responsible, but slavery removes that obligation. The passage details who must provide for the child—first the mother if she has means, then her master if she is a slave, or the child's inheriting relatives.

Compare with a free father, for whom maintenance of his child is wajib.

The passage provides a hierarchy: (1) If the mother is free and has wealth, she must maintain the child. This hierarchy is based on the Hanafi principle that maintenance follows capacity and lineage.

The slave is exempt because he does not own property (unless permitted by his master to trade). In Hanafi law, maintenance obligations are tied to ability to pay. A slave generally lacks the financial capacity, so the obligation shifts to those who do have capacity.
Other madhhabs may differ because they consider lineage (the father is still the father) as the primary basis.

Father's Obligation for Infant Maintenance and Nursing
Ruling and proof concerning infant nursing and maintenance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the father's obligation for infant maintenance and nursing, identify the conditions for this obligation, and explain the exception when the mother must nurse.

Core Idea
The mother is not obliged to breastfeed; it is the father's responsibility to hire a wet nurse, unless no nurse exists, then the mother must nurse for free.

This passage from the Sharh of Al-Quduri addresses a common question in family law: who is financially responsible for the infant's nursing and maintenance? The Hanafi school provides a clear framework grounded in the Quran and analogy, balancing the rights of the child, mother, and father. Let's unpack the ruling, its proof, and the conditions that govern it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage states two key rulings: (1) the mother is not obliged to breastfeed the infant, and (2) the father is obliged to provide maintenance (including nursing expenses). Note the hukm level for each: the father's obligation is wajib; the mother's lack of obligation is a default, though it becomes wajib under necessity.
Maintenance includes food, clothing, shelter, and nursing expenses.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites Quran 2:233, specifically the phrase 'No mother shall be harmed on account of her child.' This is interpreted to prohibit compelling the mother to breastfeed against her will.
The verse also says 'Mothers shall breastfeed their children,' but the author explains this is a religious duty (diyanatan), not a legally enforceable obligation (hukman).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The prohibition of harm (laa tudaarra) is understood as preventing compulsion. Since compelling the mother to breastfeed would harm her, she is not obliged. The father's obligation is analogized to general maintenance (qiyas jaliyyah).
Analogy: as maintenance is wajib on the father, so is nursing, since both are necessary expenses for the child.
4
Note the conditions for the father's obligation
The passage lists conditions: the child must be free, the father must be free, and the child must be poor (i.e., no personal wealth). If the child has wealth, his own property pays for maintenance. Similarly, the obligation extends to daughters and sons who are chronically ill, blind, or insane.
These conditions ensure the obligation is placed on the correct party.
5
Note the exception for the mother's nursing
If no other wet nurse is available in the area, the mother is compelled to breastfeed to prevent the child's death, and she does so without wage. This overrides the default ruling due to necessity (darurah).
The father still bears the cost, but if the mother nurses under compulsion, she has no claim for wages.

Hiring the Mother to Nurse Her Child: The Hanafi Prohibition
Ruling on hiring the mother/wife with proof and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring a mother to nurse her own child, distinguish between revocable and irrevocable iddah, and explain the Quranic proof that makes the contract invalid during marriage.

Core Idea
A mother cannot be hired to nurse her own child because it is already a religious duty – taking wages would prove ability, making the duty enforceable.

This lesson explores a unique Hanafi ruling: the prohibition of hiring a mother to nurse her own child. The reasoning hinges on the distinction between a religious duty (diyānatan) and a legally enforceable obligation. We will dissect the Quranic verse, examine the exceptions, and navigate an intra-madhhab disagreement regarding women in irrevocable iddah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hiring a woman (wife or woman in iddah) to nurse her own child from the hirer. The ruling is that the contract is invalid (batil) unless no other wet nurse is available.
The ruling is found in the matn: 'فإن استأجرها وهي زوجته... لم يَجُز'.
2
Understand the exception
If no other wet nurse exists, she must nurse for free to prevent the child's harm. No wage is due.
This is a concession (rukhsa) to preserve life.
3
Locate the proof and its legal implication
The proof is Quran 2:233: 'وَالْوَالِدَاتُ يُرْضِعْنَ أَوْلَدَهُنَّ'. The imperative form indicates a religious duty (wajib diyānatan). Because she is excused only due to potential inability, accepting wages proves ability, making the duty concrete and thus barring compensation.
The proof is Qur'anic, qat'i in transmission (thubut) but zanni in meaning (dalala) because it is interpreted as a religious duty not legally enforceable.
4
Distinguish between types of iddah
For revocable divorce (raj‘ī), the marriage persists, so the ruling is the same as for a wife – invalid. For irrevocable divorce (bā'in), the marriage has ended, so she is like a stranger; thus hiring is permissible according to the sahih narration.
There is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the irrevocable case; the relied-upon view per this text is permissibility.
5
Note the intra-madhhab disagreement
Within the Hanafi school, there are two narrations on irrevocable iddah. The text (al-Jawhara al-Nayra) declares the permissibility narration as sahih (correct) and thus mufta bihi.
This illustrates the tarjih process within the school.

Commentary

The author begins by stating that a man must maintain his parents, grandparents, and grandmothers (both paternal and maternal) if they are poor, even if they differ in religion. Understanding this distinction is essential for determining when nafaqah becomes due.

Maintenance Distribution by Inheritance Shares
Illustrating maintenance distribution via inheritance shares

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that maintenance among relatives is distributed according to inheritance shares and apply it to given family compositions.

Core Idea
Maintenance among relatives is proportional to inheritance shares, not need or equality.

In this passage, the commentator elaborates on a fundamental Hanafi principle: when multiple relatives are obligated to support a needy person, their contributions are apportioned exactly as they would inherit from each other. This ties financial responsibility directly to the fixed shares of Islamic inheritance law, ensuring clarity and consistency. The text provides several concrete family scenarios to illustrate how this rule operates in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The passage opens with the statement that maintenance is obligatory 'according to the measure of inheritance' (على قدر الميراث). Note that this is the central principle: the ratio of contributions mirrors the inheritance shares of the wealthy relatives vis-à-vis the needy one.
2
Recognize the Inheritance Shares
Recall the basic Hanafi faraid shares: mother takes 1/6 or 1/3 depending on circumstances; grandfather takes 1/6; full brother takes the residue or may be blocked; maternal siblings take 1/6 each; etc. These shares directly determine the maintenance fractions.
3
Work Through the First Cases
Examine the scenarios involving a grandfather and grandson, mother and brother, or mother and paternal uncle. For each, identify the heirs and their respective shares, then observe that the maintenance is divided exactly according to those shares.
For example: needy person with mother and brother → mother's share = 1/3 (because no child), brother gets 2/3 as residue.
4
Analyze More Complex Scenarios
The passage includes cases with half-brothers, uncles, aunts, and cousins. Note how the commentator sometimes specifies exceptions (e.g., the maintenance of a child is on the full brother only, not the maternal brother) and why (because maternal brother is not an heir in that configuration).
5
Synthesize the Underlying Logic
The rule rests on the idea that those who would benefit from the needy person's estate (i.e., inherit) are also responsible for his support. This ensures justice and prevents hardship on non-inheriting relatives.
This principle is unique to the Hanafi school; other schools may rely solely on blood relation or need.

Apply the rule on difference of religion
The matn says maintenance is not obligatory with difference of religion, but the commentary clarifies that the pronoun 'their' refers to relatives other than the adult children. The bond of kinship (rahm mukkad) overrides religious difference.

The matn states maintenance is not obligatory upon a poor person. The reasoning: it is a charitable connection, and the poor person himself deserves maintenance. Contrast with maintenance of wife and minor child, which remain obligatory even if poor.
The poverty exception applies specifically to this category of maintenance.
5
Note the contrary cases
The commentary mentions that maintenance of a wife and a young child is obligatory even on a poor person, because those obligations are based on different legal causes (marriage and paternity).
This highlights the principle that each maintenance obligation has its own conditions.

A) Always, regardless of earning ability.
B) Only if the slave is a mukatab.
C) Only if the slave has no earning capacity.
D) Only if the slave is female.

Key Takeaway
A slave may take from the master's wealth for maintenance only if the master refuses and the slave cannot earn; otherwise, the slave must support himself.
