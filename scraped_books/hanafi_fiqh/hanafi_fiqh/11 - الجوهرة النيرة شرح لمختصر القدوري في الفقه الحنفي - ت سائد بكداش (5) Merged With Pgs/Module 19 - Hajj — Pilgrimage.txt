Head Wound (Shajjah) When Wound Size Differs
Commentary on victim's options for qisas or arsh when wound size differs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios of wound size disparity in shajjah and explain the victim's choice between qisas and arsh in each scenario.

Core Idea
When wound size differs, the victim chooses qisas to the measure of his wound or full arsh, but never exceeds the assailant's capacity.

In Hanafi penal law, head wounds (shajjah) have specific rulings for retribution (qisas) and compensation (arsh). This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains a nuanced situation where the wound on the victim and the assailant differ in size relative to their temple areas. Understanding these options is crucial for applying justice fairly without exceeding one's right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key variables
Note that the ruling depends on whether the wound covers the victim's entire temple area and whether it covers the assailant's temple area. These determine the relative sizes.
The temple area (ما بين القرنين) is the side of the head between the ear and the top.
2
Scenario 1: Wound covers victim's temples but not assailant's
When the victim's wound is as large as his own temple area, but the assailant's temple area is larger (wound does not cover it), the victim may choose qisas to the exact size of his wound or take full arsh.
Since the wound fits within the assailant's temple area, qisas is feasible without excess.
3
Scenario 2: Wound does not cover victim's temples but covers assailant's
When the victim's wound is smaller than his own temple area but as large as the assailant's temple area, the victim may choose qisas only to the size of the assailant's wound (i.e., the assailant's temple area) or take full arsh.
Exacting the full victim's wound would exceed the assailant's right because the assailant's temple area is smaller.
4
State the common rationale
In both cases, the victim has the choice between qisas (limited to the feasible measure) and arsh. The limitation prevents excessive retribution (ghuluw) beyond what is justly owed.
5
Apply to a concrete example
If the victim's wound is 5 cm long (covering his temple area) and the assailant's temple area is 7 cm, the victim may cut the assailant's head 5 cm (qisas) or take full arsh. If the victim's wound is 7 cm (but his temple area is 10 cm) and the assailant's temple area is 7 cm, the victim may cut only 7 cm (qisas) or take arsh.

قوله: (ومَن شَجّ رجلاً شَجَةً، فاستوعبت الشجَّةُ ما بين قرنيه، وهي لا تستوعب ما بين قرني الشاج: فالمشجوجُ بالخيار: إن شاء اقتص بمقدار شجته، يبتدىء من أي الجانبين شاء، وإن شاء أخذ الأرش كاملاً)، يعني يأخذ مقدارها طولاً وعرضاً. وكذا إذا كانت الشجَّةُ لا تأخذ ما بين قرني المشجوج، وهي ما بين قرني الشاج: فإنه يخيَّر المشجوج أيضاً : إن شاء أخذ الأرش، وإن شاء اقتص قدر ما بين قرني الشاح، لا يزيد عليه شيئاً؛ لأنه يتعذر الاستيفاء كاملاً؛ للتعدي إلى غير حقه.
He said: (Whoever wounds another in the head with a shajjah that covers the area between his two temples, and it does not cover the area between the temples of the assailant, then the wounded person has the choice: if he wishes, he may take exact retribution (qisas) to the measure of his wound, starting from whichever side he wishes, and if he wishes, he may take the full blood-money (arsh)). Meaning: he takes it in length and width. Likewise, if the wound does not cover the area between the temples of the wounded person, but it covers the area between the temples of the assailant, then the wounded person also has the choice: if he wishes, he takes the arsh, and if he wishes, he takes qisas to the measure of the area between the assailant's temples, not exceeding anything; because complete exacting (of the exact size) is impossible due to exceeding beyond his right.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling from the matn about head wounds (shajjah). It explains two scenarios where the size of the wound differs between the victim and the assailant. In the first scenario, the victim's wound covers his entire temple area, but the assailant's temple area is smaller. The victim may either take qisas by cutting the assailant's head to the exact length and width of his own wound, or he may take full arsh (compensation). In the second scenario, the wound covers the assailant's temple area but not the victim's; here the victim may take qisas only to the extent of the assailant's wound (which is smaller) or take full arsh. The reasoning is that exacting full qisas would exceed the assailant's right because the victim's wound is smaller. The commentary thus clarifies the discretion given to the victim to choose the option that is more beneficial to him, while ensuring justice.

Key Terms
شجة (shajjah): A head wound that reaches the bone.
قرن (qarn): The temple area: the side of the head between the ear and the top.
أرش (arsh): Compensation (blood money) for wounds less than lethal.
اقتصاص (iqtisas): Exacting retributive justice (qisas) for wounds.

Prohibition of Slaughtering Game by a Muhrim and Inside the Haram
Ruling on game slaughter by muhrim and in Haram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the prohibition rule for a muhrim slaughtering game and for slaughtering game inside the Haram, and identify which animals are excluded.

Core Idea
A muhrim must not slaughter game, and game slaughtered inside the Haram is invalid even by a non-muhrim.

This lesson focuses on two prohibitions related to game: slaughter by a person in ihram (muhrim) and slaughter within the sacred precincts of Makkah (Haram). The Hanafi school distinguishes between game and domestic animals, and the prohibition applies only to wild animals, not to livestock.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, a person in ihram (muhrim) slaughtering game; second, any person slaughtering game inside the Haram. Note that both scenarios involve game (wild animals), not domestic livestock.
2
Understand the scope of 'muhrim'
The term 'muhrim' is absolute; the prohibition applies whether the muhrim is inside or outside the Haram.
This includes both the Halal (outside) and the Haram (inside).
3
Understand the absolute prohibition in the Haram
Even if a non-muhrim ('halal') slaughters game inside the Haram, the meat is not permissible. The Haram's sanctity prohibits all game hunting and slaughter.
4
Identify the exception
Domestic animals (e.g., chickens, sheep) are not game. A muhrim may slaughter domestic animals, and domestic animals may be slaughtered in the Haram.
The prohibition is specific to 'sweid' (game).

And the muhrim. [page 453] He said: (And the muhrim), meaning specifically with regard to game. And the absolute term 'muhrim' includes the prohibition of his slaughter in the Halal and the Haram. Likewise, what is slaughtered in the Haram from game is not permissible, whether slaughtered by a non-muhrim or a muhrim. And the slaughter of one who understands slaughter and tasmiyah and controls it is permissible, even if a woman or a child. And the meaning of controlling slaughter is that he is able to sever the jugular veins. And the uncircumcised, the castrated, the eunuch, the hermaphrodite, and the effeminate: their slaughter is permissible, based on what we have mentioned.

Commentary
This section addresses a muhrim (someone in the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah). The commentator clarifies that the prohibition applies specifically to game (wild animals) not domestic livestock. A muhrim may not slaughter game even outside the Haram; the term 'muhrim' covers both locations. Moreover, if any game is slaughtered within the Haram boundaries, it is invalid regardless of whether the slaughterer is in ihram or not — thus the meat is forbidden. The passage then reiterates a general condition for a valid slaughterer: he must have the mental capacity to understand the act and the tasmiyah, and be able to physically perform the slaughter (cutting the jugular veins). This condition is satisfied even by a woman or a child who can understand and control. Finally, it lists several types of people whose slaughter is valid: the uncircumcised (aqlaq), the castrated, eunuch, hermaphrodite, and effeminate — all are capable of valid slaughter as long as they meet the conditions of sanity and ability.

Key Terms
محرم (Muhrim): A person in the state of ihram for Hajj or Umrah
حرم (Haram): The sacred precincts of Makkah

Conditions for a Valid Slaughterer in Hanafi Fiqh
Conditions for a valid slaughterer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions required for a person to be considered a valid slaughterer in Hanafi fiqh, and list examples of persons whose slaughter is valid despite physical or social characteristics.

Core Idea
Any sane person who understands slaughter and pronounces tasmiyah, and has physical control to cut the throat, may slaughter.

This lesson expands on the general condition for a valid slaughterer. The Hanafi school focuses on mental capacity and physical ability, not on gender, circumcision, or other physical attributes. The text explicitly validates the slaughter of women, children, and various individuals with different physical conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary condition
The person must understand the act of slaughter (dhakiy) and pronounce tasmiyah (Bismillah). This requires mental capacity.
A mentally incapacitated person cannot slaughter.
2
Identify the secondary condition
The person must have the physical ability to sever the jugular veins (fary al-awdaj). This is called 'controlling the slaughter' (dhabit al-dhabh).
If a person cannot physically control the knife, their slaughter is invalid.
3
List the persons explicitly mentioned
The text lists: woman, child, uncircumcised (aqlaf), castrated (majbub), eunuch (khasi), hermaphrodite (khuntha), and effeminate (mukhanath). All are valid provided they meet the two conditions.
4
Analyze the common factor
All these persons are capable of meeting the conditions of understanding and physical control. Their slaughter is valid; the school does not require additional qualities like masculinity or circumcision.
