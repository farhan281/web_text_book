Definition of 'Iddah and Its Three Types
Defines 'iddah and enumerates its three categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of 'iddah, identify its three categories, and determine which type applies to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The 'iddah is a wajib waiting period to ensure the womb is free — its type depends on the cause and the woman's condition.

In this lesson, we begin the Book of 'Iddah. The text defines the waiting period ('iddah) that becomes obligatory upon a woman when a marriage or its semblance ends. The purpose is to ascertain the purity of the womb. The author then introduces the three categories of 'iddah and explains when each applies.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read the definition and purpose of 'iddah. Note that it is a waiting period (tarabbus) that is incumbent (lazim).
2
Identify the three categories
Menstrual cycles (hayd), months (ashhur), and childbirth (wad' al-haml).
These cover all possible scenarios.
3
Distinguish the causes for menstrual 'iddah
List the five triggers: divorce, separation in invalid marriage, intercourse under semblance, emancipation of umm walad, death of her master.
4
Analyze the monthly 'iddah subtypes
Two types: (a) substitute for menstruation (minor/menopausal), (b) widow's 'iddah (4 months 10 days).
5
Note the childbirth rule
Childbirth ends any ongoing 'iddah according to the two imams (Abu Hanifa & Abu Yusuf).
This is the relied-upon position.

The book of 'Iddah. The 'Iddah is: the waiting period that is incumbent upon a woman upon the termination of marriage or its semblance. It is a period legislated to ascertain the purity of the womb. Commentary
This passage introduces Kitab al-'Iddah (Book of the Waiting Period). It begins by defining 'iddah as the mandatory waiting period a woman must observe after the termination of marriage or a marriage-like relationship. Its purpose is to ensure that the womb is free from pregnancy. The author then lists the three categories of 'iddah: (1) based on menstrual cycles (hayd), (2) based on months (ashhur), and (3) termination by childbirth (wad' al-haml). The ruling for each is explained: Menstrual 'iddah applies in cases of divorce, annulment of an invalid marriage, intercourse under a semblance of marriage, freeing of an umm walad, and death of her master. The monthly 'iddah has two subtypes: one as a substitute for menstruation (for minors and menopausal women) and another for widows who are not pregnant, which applies whether the marriage was consummated or not. For an invalid marriage, the 'iddah in case of separation is by menstruation, and in case of death, it is by four months and ten days (implied). Finally, childbirth ends any 'iddah according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf (the two imams).

الحيض (al-hayd): Menstrual cycles; the basis of 'iddah for women who menstruate in cases of divorce or separation.
الشهور (al-ashhur): Months; a type of 'iddah counted by months, applicable to minors, menopausal women, and widows.
وضع الحمل (wad' al-haml): Delivery of the child; terminating any ongoing 'iddah immediately.

Definition of 'Iddah and Its Three Types
Defines 'iddah and its three categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of 'iddah, list its three categories, and identify the circumstances that trigger each type.

Core Idea
Hanafi 'iddah safeguards lineage by mandating a waiting period—menstruation, months, or childbirth.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira establishes the foundational definition of 'iddah and its categories. Understanding this is essential before delving into the detailed rulings of each type. The author concisely lists the three types and their triggers, providing a clear framework for the entire book of 'iddah.

Guided Path
1
Define 'Iddah
Identify the legal definition: 'iddah is a mandatory waiting period for a woman after the termination of a marriage or its semblance, intended to ascertain the absence of pregnancy.
The term 'shubhah' (semblance) includes invalid marriages and doubtful intercourse.
2
Memorize the Three Types
Recall the three types: menstruation (quru'), months (ashhur), and childbirth (wad' al-haml). Each type applies under specific conditions.
These are the only recognized types in the Hanafi school.
3
Identify Triggers for Menstruation-Based 'Iddah
List the cases where 'iddah by menstruation is obligatory: divorce, separation in a fasid marriage, intercourse under shubhah, emancipation of an umm walad, and death of her master.
The umm walad's 'iddah is a unique Hanafi ruling.
4
Distinguish the Two Subtypes of Months-Based 'Iddah
Understand that months-based 'iddah has two varieties: (1) substitute for menstruation for minors and post-menopausal women, and (2) the widow's 'iddah when not pregnant. Note that for a valid marriage, consummation does not affect the widow's 'iddah.
In a fasid marriage, the widow's 'iddah is by menstruation, not months.
5
Apply the Rule of Childbirth
Childbirth terminates any ongoing 'iddah, regardless of its type. This is agreed upon by Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf.
The phrase 'at both of them' indicates a consensus between the two imams.

The Meaning of Aqra' and the 'Iddah Dispute: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i/Maliki
States 'iddah for free menstruating women is three aqra' and clarifies dispute with Malik/Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the meaning of 'aqra' in 'iddah and contrast it with the Shafi'i/Maliki position, including the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
The word 'aqra' in 'iddah verse means menstruations for Hanafis, pure periods for others — changing when the waiting period ends.

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah presents the Hanafi ruling on the 'iddah of a free woman who menstruates: it is three 'aqra'. The author then clarifies that 'aqra' means menstruations (hayd) in the Hanafi school, but the Shafi'i and Maliki schools interpret it as pure periods (tuhr). While both schools use the same Quranic text, their different linguistic and legal analyses lead to a practical difference in when the 'iddah ends. Let's explore the dispute and its consequences.

This is the core of the disagreement.

The Hanafi Definition of Aqra' in Iddah: Menstruation, Not Purity Periods
Ruling on iddah with disputed definition of aqra'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the meaning of aqra' in iddah, explain the prophetic evidence, and distinguish the practical consequence from the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Aqra' means menstruation — prophetic hadith settles the meaning over linguistic ambiguity.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayirah addresses a classic dispute: what does 'aqra' mean in the Quranic verse on iddah? The Hanafi school, supported by clear hadith, holds that it means menstruation. The Shafi'i and Maliki schools interpret it as purity intervals. Understanding this difference is crucial because it changes the date when the iddah ends, affecting remarriage rights.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: a free menstruating woman's iddah after consummation is three aqra', defined as menstruations (hayd).
This applies to both ba'in and raj'i divorce, and separations without divorce.
2
Identify the opposing view
Note that Imam Malik and Imam al-Shafi'i define aqra' as purity intervals (at-har) between menses.
They rely on linguistic flexibility and the Quranic verse itself.
3
Follow the Hanafi evidence
Examine the two hadith proofs: (1) the mustahadah leaves prayer during her aqra' (i.e., days of hayd), (2) the Prophet told Fatima to leave prayer when her qur' comes.
The phrase 'ayyam aqra'iha' directly links aqra' to menstruation.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Prophet's usage in the hadith is a decisive bayan (clarification) of the Quranic term; thus aqra' must be understood as menstruation consistently.
The opposing school's argument from linguistic ambiguity is overridden by this prophetic clarification.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If divorced in a purity without intercourse: Hanafi iddah ends upon purification from the third menstruation; Shafi'i iddah ends upon entering the third menstruation.
This affects the exact date of remarriage eligibility.

Iddah for Non-Menstruating Women: Three Months and Calculation
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on calculating 'iddah in months for non-menstruating women.

Pregnant woman's iddah ends at delivery, no matter how soon or how many children.

This lesson focuses on the clear ruling for a pregnant woman's iddah. The Quran and consensus establish that her waiting period concludes with childbirth. We will explore the details: whether it is divorce or death, free or slave, and what happens in cases of multiple births or miscarriage.

Consensus (ijma) is a strong proof in Hanafi usul.

If she gives birth immediately after the divorce or death, the iddah ends. If she gives birth to twins or triplets, the iddah ends with the last child. If more than half the child emerges from a revocably divorced woman, she becomes irrevocably divorced and the iddah may end.

If she miscarries, the iddah ends only if the fetus shows clear human features (mustabin al-khalq) or part of it. Otherwise, the miscarriage does not count.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on iyas age and post-iyas blood ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi age of menopause (iyas) and explain the prospective ruling on pure blood seen after iyas, distinguishing between intra-Hanafi opinions.

Core Idea
After menopause (iyas), only pure blood counts as menstruation (hayd) and only for the future.

The age of menopause (iyas) is a crucial determination for iddah calculations and rulings on menstrual blood. The Hanafi school has internal differences on the exact age and on the legal effect of blood seen after that age. In this lesson, we examine the relied-upon position and the reasoning behind it, as well as the practical consequences for a woman's iddah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of dispute
The primary dispute is the age at which a woman is considered menopausal (iyas). Within the Hanafi school, there are three opinions: 55, 60, and 70 years. The relied-upon view is 55 years, adopted by most later mashayikh as per 'al-Nihaya'.
2
Distinguish intra-Hanafi opinions on iyas age
Some early mashayikh said 60 years, others said 70. The later authorities settled on 55 years, which is the mufta bihi. The Shafi'i school holds 62 years.
The difference has practical implications for iddah and fasting obligations.
3
Understand the ruling on post-iyas blood
If a woman who has been ruled menopausal sees pure red blood, it is considered hayd and nullifies the iyas ruling prospectively only. If she sees turbidity or yellowness, it is not hayd but attributed to a corrupt vein (fasad al-munbat).
Prospectively means future actions are affected; past rulings (like prayers or fasts) remain valid.
4
Determine the requirement of a judge's ruling
Some mashayikh required a judge's ruling to preserve past acts from being invalidated. The preferred view is that no judge's ruling is needed; the iyas is effective by itself.
Thus, a woman can rely on her own iyas without court intervention.
5
Apply to a practical scenario
If a woman menstruates once or twice and then her menses stop permanently, she waits until age 55. At that point, she begins her iddah anew in months (three months for divorce, etc.).
This is the standard iddah for a menopausal woman who has no periods.

Iddah after fasid marriage or shubha is due to intercourse, not marriage; it is three hayd or three months for a free woman.

This lesson presents rulings on iddah for women whose intercourse was not based on a valid marriage. The key principle is whether iddah is due to the act of intercourse or due to the marriage bond. Guided Path

Identify the categories of women
The passage covers four categories: woman married with fasid marriage, woman penetrated by shubha, umm walad, and minor girl. Note the condition of dakhul (intercourse) for the first two.

Iddah of Umm Walad: Three Menstruations, No Maintenance
States the iddah ruling for umm walad with conditions and rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the iddah ruling for an umm walad upon her master's death or freedom, identify the conditions for its application, explain why she receives no maintenance, and distinguish her case from that of a regular slave woman or mudabbirah.

Core Idea
The umm walad's iddah is three menstruations due to intercourse, not marriage, so no maintenance and death/freedom are equal.

The iddah of a female slave who has borne a child to her master (umm walad) is a distinct ruling in Hanafi fiqh. Unlike a regular slave, the umm walad has a special status due to the child and is considered a 'firash' (bed) for her master. This lesson examines her waiting period when the master dies or frees her, the conditions that trigger it, and why she receives no financial support during this time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what legal act is being ruled upon. Here, the act is the iddah (waiting period) of an umm walad upon two events: the master's death or his freeing her. The ruling is wajib: she must observe an iddah.
The ruling is not fard but wajib because the proof is likely zanni.
2
List the conditions and exceptions
The text states two conditions: she must not already be in another iddah and she must not be married to another man. If either condition is absent, the iddah does not apply. Note also the exception for non-menstruating women: three months instead of three menstruations.
Conditions are external shurut for validity of the obligation.
3
Analyze the rationale for no maintenance
The passage explicitly says she has no nafaqah because this iddah is due to intercourse (iddah wat'), not due to a marriage contract. It compares to the iddah from a fasid marriage, where also no maintenance is due.
This is a key distinction from the iddah of a free divorced woman, who does receive maintenance.
4
Understand the equality of death and freedom
The text states that both events (death and freeing) produce the same iddah because the underlying cause is the same: the end of the relationship that made intercourse halal, and thus the iddah is solely due to the act of intercourse.
This highlights the principle that the cause (sabab) determines the ruling.
5
Distinguish the umm walad from other slave women
The passage concludes by noting that a regular slave woman or a mudabbirah with whom the master had intercourse does not observe an iddah upon his death or freeing. The reason is they are not his 'firash' (marriage-like bed), whereas the umm walad is considered a firash due to the child.
The concept of firash is crucial in Hanafi law for establishing paternity and related obligations.

The 'Iddah of a Slave When the Order of Deaths is Unknown
Intra-Hanafi ikhtilāf on 'iddah when order of death is unknown

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on 'iddah when the order of deaths is unknown and identify the legal reasoning behind each position.

Core Idea
When order of deaths is unknown, Abu Hanifa treats them as simultaneous; the Sahibayn require precaution combining months and menstruation.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two companions regarding the 'iddah of a female slave whose master and husband die with unknown order. The dispute turns on whether to apply the analogy of simultaneous death (as with drowning victims) or to exercise precaution (ihtiyāṭ) by covering both possible scenarios. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to trace legal reasoning in cases of temporal uncertainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
What is the 'iddah of a female slave when the order of death of her master and her husband is unknown?
The key variables are her status (slave or free) at the time of her husband's death and the type of 'iddah.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules that the 'iddah is four months and ten days without menstruation. He analogizes to drowning victims (al-gharqā): when two events occur with unknown chronology, they are judged simultaneous. Hence she is deemed free at the husband's death, so her 'iddah is that of a free woman (by months only), and menstruation is irrelevant.
The analogy to drowning victims is a qiyās based on the principle of simultaneous judgment (al-ḥukm bi al-muqārana).
3
State the Sahibayn's position and reasoning
Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad al-Shaybānī require four months and ten days with three menstruations. They argue that it is possible the husband died first (so a slave's 'iddah of three months with menstruation) or the master died first (so a free woman's 'iddah by months). To cover both possibilities, precaution demands combining months and menstruations.
Precaution (ihtiyāṭ) is a common Hanafi tool to ensure the legal obligation is fulfilled beyond doubt.
4
Compare the positions and identify the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilāf (practical consequence) is whether the woman must count menstruation during her 'iddah. Abu Hanifa drops it; the Sahibayn include it.
The difference affects when her 'iddah ends and when she can remarry.
5
Note the tarjīḥ (preferred view)
The text does not specify a muftā bihī, but later Hanafi works often adopt the Sahibayn's view out of precaution.
This reflects a general pattern: in cases of uncertainty, the school tends to choose the more cautious option.
