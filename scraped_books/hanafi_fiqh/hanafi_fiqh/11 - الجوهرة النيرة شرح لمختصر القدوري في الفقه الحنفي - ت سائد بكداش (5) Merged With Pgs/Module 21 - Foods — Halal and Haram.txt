Core Idea
Deliberate omission of tasmiyah at release renders game carrion; forgetfulness excused.

This lesson examines a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the requirement to mention Allah's name when hunting. The passage establishes that tasmiyah is necessary at the moment of releasing the trained animal, and deliberate omission makes the game unlawful. We will explore the ruling, its conditions, and the basis of the inter-madhhab dispute.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify exactly what the Hanafi school holds regarding tasmiyah: it is wajib at the moment of release, and deliberate omission makes the game carrion (haram to eat).
Also note the exception for forgetfulness.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i considers tasmiyah recommended (sunnah mu'akkadah) but not obligatory, so deliberate omission does not prohibit the game.
This is the core of the ikhtilaf.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand that Shafi'i bases his view on a different interpretation of the Quranic command and hadith, considering them non-obligatory.
No need to go deep into Shafi'i usul here.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Examine how the Hanafi school rebuts: the command is general and unrestricted, and the Prophet's consistent practice indicates obligation (wajib).
The source mentions this generally.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: a Hanafi cannot eat game intentionally shot without tasmiyah; a Shafi'i considers it permissible.
This shows the real-world impact of the dispute.

Ruling when the dog eats from the game after the hunter's possession.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Once the hunter possesses the game, any subsequent eating by the dog does not affect lawfulness.

In this lesson, we examine a specific ruling from the Jawhara al-Nayyira: what happens to the lawfulness of hunted game when the dog eats from it after the hunter has taken possession? The Hanafi position is clear and based on the concept of iḥrāz al-ṣayd (taking possession of the game). Understanding this principle is crucial for hunters and those who consume hunted meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
The act is hunting with a trained dog (ṣayd). The category is ibaḥah (permissibility) with conditions.
2

Three scenarios are given: (1) hunter cuts and gives a piece to the dog, (2) dog jumps and takes a bite while in the hunter's hand, (3) dog steals after handing over.
3
Identify the key condition (shart)
The condition for lawfulness is that the hunter must have taken possession (iḥrāz) of the game before the dog eats.
Possession (iḥrāz) is established by physically taking the game into the hunter's hand or control.
4
Apply the ruling to each scenario
In all three scenarios, the remaining game is lawful because possession (iḥrāz) had already occurred.
5
Distinguish the counter case
If the dog eats before the hunter takes possession, the game is not lawful (since it indicates lack of training).
The passage says 'bikhilāf mā idhā faʿala dhālika qabl an yuḥrizahu ṣāḥibuhu'.

Core Idea
An arrow's straight path determines permissibility; wind deflection invalidates the hunt.

In hunting, the archer's control is key. This lesson addresses a specific scenario: what if the arrow hits its target? The rule distinguishes between intentional aim and external interference, emphasizing that only hits along the arrow's intended straight path are attributed to the hunter.

Core Idea
Slaughter is only valid from a monotheist who is rational and recites tasmiyah; otherwise it is carrion.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses two crucial aspects of dhabihah: who may slaughter and under what conditions. Permissible Slaughter Instruments and the Required Cutting of Vessels
Explaining instruments for slaughter and condition of cutting vessels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for valid slaughter, explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on how many vessels must be cut, and distinguish the Hanafi position on detached tooth/nail from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Slaughter is valid if the instrument cuts by flow, but attached tooth/nail kill by weight — and the three Hanafi imams differ on how many vessels must be cut.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah opens the chapter on slaughter (dhabh). It establishes the foundational rule: any sharp instrument that makes blood flow is permissible, except the attached tooth and nail. But within this rule lie two layers of scholarly analysis: the disagreement among the Hanafi imams themselves on exactly how many of the four neck vessels must be severed, and the inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i over whether a detached tooth or nail can ever produce halal meat. In this lesson, we will unpack both layers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
The matn states: 'It is permissible to slaughter with a reed, with flint, and with anything that makes blood flow except the attached tooth and attached nail.' This establishes the norm that any instrument that causes blood to flow by cutting is valid, and the exception for attached tooth/nail is due to killing by weight.
The term 'anhar al-dam' (makes blood flow) is key – the instrument must cut, not crush or tear.
2
Define the four neck vessels
The four vessels (al-'uruq al-arba') are the windpipe (halqum), esophagus (mari'), and the two jugular veins (wadajayn). These are the targets of a valid slaughter cut.
These vessels are named in the commentary: halqum, mari', and the two wadajayn.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the required number
Abu Hanifa requires cutting any three of the four. Abu Yusuf requires cutting the windpipe, esophagus, and at least one jugular. Muhammad requires cutting the majority of each of the four vessels. The mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view as it is the easiest.
This ikhtilaf is practical: the minimum validation standard differs in everyday slaughter.
4
Examine the tooth/nail exception
The exception is restricted to attached tooth/nail. If detached, they are like any other sharp instrument and valid. The reason ('illa) is that attached ones kill by weight (bi-l-thiqal) rather than by cutting.
The consensus (ijma') mentioned in the passage refers to the prohibition of attached ones only.
5
Evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument
Al-Shafi'i holds the hadith 'do not slaughter with a tooth or nail' as absolute, prohibiting even detached ones. The Hanafi response is that the context refers to attached ones, and the 'illa of weight supports this restriction.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that Hanafi considers detached tooth/nail halal; Shafi'i considers it carrion.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Observable signs determine the legal nature of a hybrid: diet, sound, and excretion.

Classical Hanafi fiqh addresses rare cases like a hybrid between a dog and a goat. Since the animal has mixed features, the Sharia uses empirical tests to classify it as either a dog (haram) or a goat (halal). This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a series of progressive tests: diet, sound, water drinking, and excretion—each providing a decisive indicator of the animal's dominant nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hybrid's physical characteristics
Note that the hybrid has a dog-like head and a goat-like body. This mixed appearance triggers the need for behavioral tests.
The head is considered dog-like and the body goat-like, so the head is always removed if the animal is deemed goat-like.
2
Apply the diet test
Offer meat and grass. If it eats meat only, it is a dog (haram). If it eats grass only, it is a goat (halal) except the head. If it eats both, proceed to the next test.
If it eats only meat, the entire animal is not eaten.
3
Apply the sound test if diet is inconclusive
Strike the animal. If it barks, it is a dog (haram). If it snorts (like a goat), it is a goat (halal except the head). If it does both, proceed to the water test.
Striking is used to elicit the animal's natural sound.
4
Apply the water drinking test
Present water. If it laps (dog-like), it is a dog (haram). If it drinks (goat-like), it is a goat (halal except the head).
Lapping (walagh) is a dog trait; drinking (shurb) is a goat trait.
5
Apply the excretion test (alternative mentioned)
If it excretes cud (like a goat), the body is eaten (head removed). If it excretes intestines (like a dog), it is not eaten at all.
This test is mentioned with 'قيل' indicating it is a secondary opinion.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of Al-Quduri regarding the book of sacrifice (al-udhiyah). It opens by stating the time for sacrifice for the people of the countryside (ahl al-sawad), who are not residents of the city where the Eid prayer is held. They may slaughter after dawn (fajr) on the day of Nahr, and the sacrifice is permissible over three days: the day of Nahr and the two following days. The commentary explains that the reason is that the Eid prayer is not obligatory on them, and the time for slaughter begins with dawn. It then discusses a scenario where a villager enters the city for the Eid prayer and instructs others to sacrifice on his behalf: the permission is based on the place of the act, not the place of the beneficiary. The commentary also notes that if the imam prays the Eid prayer without delivering the sermon, the sacrifice is still valid because the sermon is not obligatory.

Next, the text addresses cases where the sacrificial animal is lost or detained. If a person's animal is lost until after the days of Nahr, or he finds it later, he should give it in charity rather than slaughter it. If he buys a sheep for sacrifice, loses it, buys another, and then finds the first, it is preferable to slaughter both. However, if he slaughters only the first, it suffices; if he slaughters only the second, it is permissible if it is equal or better, but if inferior, he must compensate the difference by charity. This ruling applies equally to rich and poor, as stated explicitly: 'whether in difficulty or in ease'. 

Then, an intra-Hanafi dispute is reported: some Hanafi scholars differentiate between the rich and poor in such cases. For the rich, the obligation is initially by revelation (shar'), so purchasing does not designate a specific animal; therefore, he may choose which to slaughter. For the poor, the obligation arises by purchase with intention, so the first animal becomes designated; if he buys another, he must slaughter both. Similarly, if a purchased animal becomes defective, the rich must replace it, while the poor may use it. If the animal dies, the rich replace it, the poor do not.

Finally, the passage discusses the offspring of a sacrificial animal. The general statement is that if the animal gives birth, the offspring is slaughtered with the mother because the obligation in the mother spreads to the offspring. However, some scholars qualify this: it applies only to the obligatory sacrifice (for the poor), not to the rich person's voluntary sacrifice. Another view is that slaughtering the offspring is not obligatory at all; rather, one may give it in charity, because the right (of sacrifice) is attached to the mother like her skin and halter. If one sells or eats the offspring, he must give charity of its value or price. If he keeps it until the days pass, he gives it in charity.

Substitution of the Sacrificial Animal: Loss and Replacement
Ruling on substitution and loss of sacrificial animal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ruling when a sacrificial animal is lost and another is bought, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on rich/poor distinction.

Core Idea
If you lose your sacrifice and buy another, it is best to slaughter both, but one suffices; the ruling is the same for rich and poor.

The passage deals with cases where a sacrificial animal is lost or detained after purchase. The preferred action is to slaughter both the original (if found) and the replacement, but only one is sufficient. An intra-Hanafi dispute arises: some scholars differentiate between the rich and the poor based on the nature of their obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A person buys a sheep for sacrifice, it gets lost, he buys another, then finds the first.
Also includes a detained animal found after the days of Nahr.
2
State the default ruling
It is better (afdal) to slaughter both. If he slaughters only the first, it suffices. If he slaughters only the second, it suffices if equal or better; if inferior, he must give charity for the difference.
This applies 'whether poor or rich' as per matn.
3
Analyze the proof
The text explicitly says 'سواء كان معسراً أو موسراً', indicating no distinction based on wealth.
The reasoning: the obligation for the rich is by revelation, not by purchase, so the first animal does not become designated; for the poor, the obligation arises by purchase, so the first becomes designated.
4
Present the ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi scholars (بعض أصحابنا) differentiate: for the rich, the ruling is the same (no obligation to slaughter both), but for the poor, the first animal is designated by purchase, so if he buys a second, he must slaughter both.
The opposing view is minority; the matn's ruling is the relied-upon.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
If a poor person experiences this, according to the minority he must slaughter both; according to the majority, he may choose one.
The practical difference is whether the poor person is forced to slaughter two animals or not.

* His saying: (And it is permissible on three days: the day of Nahr and two days after). If one detains his sacrificial animal until the days of Nahr have passed, or it gets lost, and he finds it after the days of Nahr: he is not required to slaughter it, but rather he gives it in charity, and does not leave any part of it. - If he bought a sheep for sacrifice and it got lost, so he bought another, then he finds the first: then the better is to slaughter both, and if he slaughters the first only, it suffices him, whether the value of the first is more than the value of the second or less.
And if he slaughters the second only: if it is similar to the first or better: it is permissible, and if it is inferior: he guarantees the increase (difference) and gives it in charity, and it is not necessary for him to slaughter both, whether he is in difficulty (poor) or in ease (rich).
And some of our companions said: If he is in ease (rich): then likewise, and if he is in difficulty (poor): he is required to slaughter both; because the obligation upon the rich is by revelation from the beginning, not by purchase, so it did not become designated by it (the purchase), and upon the poor: by his purchase with the intention of sacrifice, so it became designated upon him.
-
Similarly, if he bought a sound sheep, then it became defective with a preventing defect: if he is rich, upon him is another; and if he is poor, this one suffices him; because we mentioned that the obligation upon the rich is by revelation initially, not by purchase.
And upon this they said: If the purchased animal for sacrifice dies, then upon the rich (is) another in its place, and there is nothing upon the poor.
- If the sacrificial animal gives birth to a offspring, he slaughters it with her; because the obligation became designated in her, so it spreads to her offspring.
And some of our companions said: This is in the obligatory sacrifice; because the obligation became designated in it by purchase; but as for the sheep that a rich person bought to sacrifice with it, if it gives birth, its offspring does not follow it.
And our companions used to say that slaughtering the offspring is not obligatory, but if he gives it in charity, it is permissible; because the right did not transfer to it, but it is attached to her, so it is like its skin and its halter.
If he sells it or eats it, he gives in charity its value in the case of eating, and its price in the case of sale. If he keeps the offspring until the days of Nahr have passed, he gives it in charity.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of Al-Quduri regarding the book of sacrifice (al-udhiyah). It opens by stating the time for sacrifice for the people of the countryside (ahl al-sawad), who are not residents of the city where the Eid prayer is held. They may slaughter after dawn (fajr) on the day of Nahr, and the sacrifice is permissible over three days: the day of Nahr and the two following days. The commentary explains that the reason is that the Eid prayer is not obligatory on them, and the time for slaughter begins with dawn. It then discusses a scenario where a villager enters the city for the Eid prayer and instructs others to sacrifice on his behalf: the permission is based on the place of the act, not the place of the beneficiary. The commentary also notes that if the imam prays the Eid prayer without delivering the sermon, the sacrifice is still valid because the sermon is not obligatory.

Next, the text addresses cases where the sacrificial animal is lost or detained. If a person's animal is lost until after the days of Nahr, or he finds it later, he should give it in charity rather than slaughter it. If he buys a sheep for sacrifice, loses it, buys another, and then finds the first, it is preferable to slaughter both. However, if he slaughters only the first, it suffices; if he slaughters only the second, it is permissible if it is equal or better, but if inferior, he must compensate the difference by charity. This ruling applies equally to rich and poor, as stated explicitly: 'whether in difficulty or in ease'. 

Then, an intra-Hanafi dispute is reported: some Hanafi scholars differentiate between the rich and poor in such cases. For the rich, the obligation is initially by revelation (shar'), so purchasing does not designate a specific animal; therefore, he may choose which to slaughter. For the poor, the obligation arises by purchase with intention, so the first animal becomes designated; if he buys another, he must slaughter both. Similarly, if a purchased animal becomes defective, the rich must replace it, while the poor may use it. If the animal dies, the rich replace it, the poor do not.

Finally, the passage discusses the offspring of a sacrificial animal. The general statement is that if the animal gives birth, the offspring is slaughtered with the mother because the obligation in the mother spreads to the offspring. However, some scholars qualify this: it applies only to the obligatory sacrifice (for the poor), not to the rich person's voluntary sacrifice. Another view is that slaughtering the offspring is not obligatory at all; rather, one may give it in charity, because the right (of sacrifice) is attached to the mother like her skin and halter. If one sells or eats the offspring, he must give charity of its value or price. If he keeps it until the days pass, he gives it in charity.

الأضحية الواجبة (al-uḍḥiyyah al-wājibah): The sacrifice that is obligatory, either by revelation (for the rich) or by purchase with intention (for the poor).
