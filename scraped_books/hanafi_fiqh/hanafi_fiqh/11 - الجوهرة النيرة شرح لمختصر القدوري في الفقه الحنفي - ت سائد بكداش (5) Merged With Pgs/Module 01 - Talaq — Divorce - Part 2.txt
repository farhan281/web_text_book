Core Idea

This lesson examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the commencement of iddah in an invalid (fasid) marriage. While Imam Abu Hanifa holds that iddah starts immediately upon separation or the husband's verbal determination to abstain, Imam Zufar ties it to the last act of intercourse. Understanding this difference is crucial for accurately determining when a woman's waiting period ends.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Knowledge transforms marriage to a married woman from shubha (requiring iddah) to pure zina (no legal effect).

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents two contrasting rulings on a critical issue: what happens when a man marries a woman who is already married and consummates the marriage? The answer hinges entirely on his knowledge. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how Hanafi fiqh balances legal consequences with intention and awareness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two scenarios
The passage discusses a man who marries a married woman (mankūḥat al-ghayr) and has intercourse with her. Two possibilities: he did not know she was married, or he knew.
2
Determine consequences when ignorant
If he did not know, she must observe iddah after separation (either by divorce or annulment) and she becomes forbidden (ḥarām) to her first husband until that iddah ends.
Iddah here is to avoid confusion of lineage (istibrā’ al-raḥim).
3
Determine consequences when knowing
If he knew, the intercourse is considered pure zina (zinā maḥḍ). No iddah is required, and she does not become forbidden to the first husband; she remains his wife.
Zinā maḥḍ has no legal recognition; the marriage contract itself is void from the start.
4
Identify the key variable
Knowledge is the decisive factor. In Hanafi fiqh, ignorance (jahālah) creates a shubha (doubt) that can legally connect the act to a valid marriage, while knowing consent removes that doubt.
This reflects the principle that legal consequences depend on intent and awareness.
5
Apply to an extension
What if the man marries her but does not have intercourse? The passage does not address this, but by analogy, if no intercourse, no iddah is needed. The ruling hinges on intercourse.
But if marriage only without intercourse, the marriage is invalid and must be annulled.

Core Idea

This lesson from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira expounds a key Hanafi ruling regarding a widow's movement during her iddah of death. Core Idea

This lesson examines a specific conditional divorce scenario: a husband says 'Whenever you give birth to a child, you are divorced.' What happens if the wife gives birth to twins or triplets? The ruling hinges on the wife's marital status at each birth and the idda period. We will see how the Hanafi school applies linguistic analysis of 'kullama' and the rules of idda to determine the number of divorces.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional phrase and its linguistic implication
Recognize that 'kullama' (whenever) implies repetition of both condition and consequence in Hanafi usul.
This is a key linguistic point: kullama indicates that each time the condition occurs, the consequence is triggered, unless a legal impediment exists.
2
Determine the idda period for a pregnant woman
The idda of a pregnant woman ends only with delivery of all children in the womb. Until the last child is born, she remains in idda and is still a wife for purposes of further divorce.
Idda is a waiting period; during idda, the woman is still legally considered a wife for some purposes, including the capacity to receive additional divorces from the same husband.
3
Analyze each birth event in order
At each birth, check if the woman is still in idda. If she is a wife (in idda), the birth triggers a divorce. If she has become ajnabiyya (stranger) because idda ended, no divorce occurs.
The condition for divorce is being a wife at the moment of birth. After idda ends, she is no longer a wife.
4
Apply to the cases of twins and triplets
For twins: first birth triggers one divorce; she remains in idda until the second birth; at second birth, idda ends, so she is ajnabiyya and no second divorce. For triplets: first two births each trigger a divorce because she is still in idda (with remaining children); at third birth, idda ends, so no third divorce.
Thus the number of divorces equals the number of children born before the idda ends, which is (number of children - 1).

Ruling on reclaiming nafaqah under a corrupt condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on reclaiming nafaqah spent on a divorcee during ‘iddah when a corrupt condition (sharṭ fāsid) is attached, distinguishing between explicit stipulation and customary understanding.

Core Idea
A corrupt condition in spending creates a right of reclaim; mere customary intent does not.

This lesson examines a specific case from the fatwā collection al-Wāqi‘āt: a man spends on a woman during her ‘iddah of a bā’in divorce on condition that she marry him after the ‘iddah. When she refuses, can he reclaim what he spent? The answer turns on whether the condition was explicitly stated or merely understood by custom. We will unpack the legal reasoning and the practical distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario and parties
Understand that the woman is in bā’in divorce, so the husband has no right to return. Another man offers nafaqah during her ‘iddah with a condition of future marriage. She agrees, he spends, then she refuses. Determine the legal relationship: is it a contract with a condition?
The condition is not part of a marriage contract but a separate promise.
2
Classify the condition as fāsid (corrupt)
The condition ‘I will spend on condition that you marry me’ is a corrupt condition because it attaches a future obligation to a current act of spending. In Hanafi fiqh, such a condition does not invalidate the spending but gives the spender a right to reclaim if the condition is unfulfilled.
A corrupt condition (sharṭ fāsid) is one that is not valid in the original contract but does not render the act void.
3
Apply the ruling on explicit condition
When the condition is explicitly stated, the spender is not a donor; he spent under a conditional obligation. Since she reneged, he can reclaim the amount spent.
4
Distinguish the case of implicit (customary) condition
If no explicit condition is uttered, but the woman knows from custom that he expects marriage, then he is considered a donor (mutabarri‘). Custom alone does not create a legal condition. Therefore he cannot reclaim.
The text says 'al-ṣaḥīḥ' (the correct view) for this, indicating there is a weaker opposing opinion.

Commentary
This passage presents a legal case (mas’alah) from the book al-Wāqi‘āt. It discusses a scenario: a man divorces his wife with a bā’in (irrevocable) divorce. Then another man comes to her during her 'iddah and says he will support her financially on the condition that she marries him after the 'iddah. She agrees, he spends, but after 'iddah she refuses marriage. The ruling: he can reclaim the amount he spent because the condition is corrupt (fāsid). The reason: the spending was conditional, and the condition is invalid, so the obligation to support does not arise; thus the spender is not a donor but has a right to reclaim. However, if he spent without explicitly stating the condition, even if she knew by custom (ʿurfan) that he spent for that purpose, the correct view is that he cannot reclaim anything because he is considered a donor (mutabarri‘). This distinction hinges on whether the condition was explicit or merely understood by custom. The general principle: a condition in a contract of support that is futile (fāsid) renders the support reclaimable if the condition is broken.

Key Terms
بائن (bā’in): Irrevocable divorce, where the husband cannot take back his wife without a new marriage.
شرط فاسد (sharṭ fāsid): A corrupt condition that is invalid in the contract.
متبرع (mutabarri‘): A donor, one who gives something voluntarily without expectation of return.

The Hanafi Principle of Shubha: Why a Claim Drops the Hadd for Theft
Inter-madhhab dispute and subsidiary rulings on hadd theft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on how a claim of ownership affects the hadd penalty and apply the principle of shubha to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
Doubt wards off hadd: a mere claim of ownership creates sufficient doubt to prevent the fixed punishment.

This lesson explores a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the effect of a thief's claim of ownership on the hadd punishment for theft. The Hanafi school, relying on the principle that doubt (shubha) prevents fixed penalties, holds that the mere claim drops the hadd, while Imam al-Shafi'i argues that this would close the door of hadd entirely. We will analyze the reasoning and then examine related subsidiary rulings on oaths, retraction, unknown owners, and delayed testimony.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Determine the exact legal question being debated: Does a thief's mere claim of ownership over the stolen item suffice to drop the hadd punishment?
The claim is made before any confession or evidence.
2
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
Outline Imam al-Shafi'i's argument: accepting the claim would allow any thief to escape punishment, thus defeating the purpose of hadd.
His concern is practical: the door of hadd would be effectively closed.
3
State the Hanafi position and its proof
Explain the Hanafi reliance on the principle that 'fixed punishments are warded off by doubts' (الحدود تدرأ بالشبهات). The claim creates a possibility of truth, hence a valid doubt, so the hadd is dropped.
The doubt is based on the potential truthfulness of the claim, not on any external evidence.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Consider how a Hanafi judge vs. a Shafi'i judge would rule in a real case. The Hanafi judge would not cut the hand; the Shafi'i judge would investigate and may still apply hadd if the claim seems false.
The difference hinges on the role of doubt in their respective methodologies.
5
Analyze subsidiary rulings
Examine additional rules from the passage: the effect of an oath when the accused denies, retraction after confession, confession for an unknown owner, and delayed testimony. Each reinforces the distinction between rights of Allah (hadd) and rights of humans (property).
These rulings all stem from the same principle: shubha drops hadd but does not eliminate property liability.

Al-Shafi'i said: It is not dropped by mere claim, because no thief is incapable of it, so it would lead to closing the door of hadd. And for us (Hanafis): That the doubt (shubha) is a preventative, and it is realized by mere claim due to possibility (of truth), and because retraction is not valid after confession. And if one accuses a man of theft and he denies, he is made to swear; if he refuses to swear, he is not cut and he guarantees the property, because the property requires swearing, but the cutting does not require swearing. And if he confessed to that confession, then retracted his confession and denied, he is not cut and he guarantees the property, because retraction is accepted in hudud, and it is not accepted in the property which is the right of a human being. And if he said: I stole these dirhams and I do not know who they belong to, he is not cut, because confession for a non-specific person does not entail a ruling, so the dirhams remain under the ruling of his ownership. And if they testified against a man for theft after a time, he is not cut and he guarantees the property, and we have explained this.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on theft addresses several rulings regarding the fixed punishment (hadd) for theft. It begins with an inter-madhhab dispute: Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the hadd is not dropped simply because the thief claims ownership of the stolen property, since any thief could make such a claim, leading to the complete suspension of punishment. The Hanafi position responds by relying on the principle that doubt (shubha) is a preventative that wards off the hadd. Here, the mere claim creates a possibility of truth, thus doubt exists. Additionally, after a valid confession, retraction is not permitted in hadd cases according to Hanafis, but this claim is before any confession. The passage then provides a series of rulings: 1) If a person accuses another of theft and the accused denies, the accused is made to swear an oath; if he refuses, he is not cut (hadd applied) but must compensate the property, because oaths are taken for property rights but not for hadd. 2) If someone confesses to theft then retracts, he is not cut but must pay the property, because retraction is accepted in hudud (to avoid wrongful punishment) but not in property rights which belong to a specific human. 3) If someone says 'I stole these dirhams and I do not know who they belong to,' he is not cut because confession to an unknown owner does not establish the crime fully; the dirhams are considered his property until proven otherwise. 4) If witnesses testify after a delay (not contemporaneously), the hadd is not applied but the thief must pay the property. This is because the requirement for immediate testimony for hadd is not met. These rulings all stem from the Hanafi emphasis on shubha and the protection of property rights.

Key Terms
شبهة (shubha): Doubt; a valid uncertainty that prevents application of hadd.
حد (hadd): Fixed punishment prescribed by Allah for certain crimes like theft.
إقرار (iqrar): Confession; legal admission of guilt.
