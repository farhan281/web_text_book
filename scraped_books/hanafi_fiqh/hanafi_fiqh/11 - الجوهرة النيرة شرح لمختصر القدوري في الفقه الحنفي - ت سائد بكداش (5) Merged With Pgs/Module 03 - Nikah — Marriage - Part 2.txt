Core Idea
The paternal grandfather inherits the father's guardianship for paternity only when the father is dead, a kafir, or a slave.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi ruling on paternity claims by the paternal grandfather. The Mukatab's Inability to Manumit His Slave
Ruling on mukatab's power over his slave with qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mukatab freeing his slave and apply the qiyas from father/executor/mufawid to the mukatab.

Core Idea
A mukatab cannot free his slave—neither gratuitously nor for a price—because he lacks full ownership.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses the scope of a mukatab's authority over his own slaves. It prohibits direct freeing and conditional manumission but permits kitaba (a contract of exchange). The reasoning is drawn from an analogy (qiyas) with other figures who have limited ownership: the father, executor, and mufawid partner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Acts and Rulings
List three acts: freeing a slave (haram/batil), conditional freeing (haram/batil), and writing kitaba (halal/sahih). Note the distinction between gratuitous transfer and exchange.
The text says 'لا يجوز' for the first two, which indicates prohibition and invalidity.
2
Understand the Reason for Prohibition
The reason given is 'because he does not possess actual execution, so he does not possess conditional attachment.' This means the mukatab lacks full ownership, so he cannot effect freedom directly or conditionally.
Only kitaba is allowed because it is a contract of exchange (عقد مبادلة).
3
Trace the Qiyas
The text uses 'Do you not see that...' to analogize the mukatab to the father, executor, and mufawid partner. They cannot free for money but can contract kitaba. The effective cause (illa) is limited ownership/guardianship.
This is a qiyas jali (clear analogy) as indicated by the argument type.
4
Note the Exception for Marriage
The same class (mukatab, father, executor, mufawid) may marry off a female slave but not a male slave. This links to rulings on tazwīj.
This clarifies that the limitation is on freeing, not on all dispositions.

Child of Two Mukatab Parents Enters Mother's Kitaba
Ruling and commentary on child's kitaba following mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on which parent's kitaba a child born to two mukatab slaves enters, and explain the basis of maternal dependency.

Core Idea
The child follows the mother's kitaba because maternal legal dependence is stronger.

This lesson examines a specific scenario where a master marries his male and female slaves, then grants both a kitaba contract (a contract to purchase freedom). When a child is born after the kitaba, whose kitaba does the child enter? The Hanafi school rules that the child enters the mother's kitaba, based on the principle of maternal dependency (taba'iyya al-umm). This ruling has practical implications for earnings and maintenance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Read the passage carefully: a master marries his male slave to his female slave, then makes both mukatab (i.e., they enter kitaba contracts). The female slave gives birth to a child from that marriage.

2
State the ruling
The matn explicitly says: 'the child enters her kitaba and his earnings are for her.' So the child is included in the mother's kitaba contract, not the father's.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The commentator adds: 'because maternal dependency is stronger'. This is a rational proof (qiyas/dalil aqli) based on the legal maxim that the child follows the mother in lineage and status.
The proof is not a direct text but a general principle derived from multiple rulings.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The commentator elaborates: the child follows the mother in slavery and freedom (i.e., if she is freed, the child becomes free; if she remains a slave, the child is a slave). Therefore, her kitaba rightfully includes the child. Additionally, the child's maintenance is on the mother, and her maintenance is on the husband.
This shows the mother's legal bond is stronger in matters of dependency.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
While the Hanafi school holds this view, other schools might have different rulings. The cross-madhhab status is 'mukhtalaf fih' (disputed).
For example, some schools might argue the child follows the father for lineage purposes, but Hanafi emphasizes the mother's role in status and maintenance.

Drawing Wala' (al-Jarr) Through Marriage and Emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scenarios of jar (drawing) of wala' and identify who inherits from a child born to a slave father and free mother under the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The wala' of a child follows the free parent; manumission draws wala' to the manumitter.

Wala' is a legal bond created by manumission, carrying inheritance rights. The term 'jar' (drawing) describes how wala' can transfer through familial ties or subsequent manumission. This lesson examines two classic scenarios from the Hanafi text that clarify the rules of wala' distribution, helping you understand how status and patronage determine inheritance.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definition of wala'
Wala' is the patronage relationship between a manumitter and a freed slave, giving the manumitter inheritance rights (like 'asaba). In Hanafi law, wala' is treated akin to lineage.
2
Identify the first scenario: marriage of a slave to a free woman
The text states: a woman (a free patron) marries her male slave to a free woman. The child born from this marriage is free because the mother is free.
In Hanafi law, the child's status follows the better (free) parent.
3
Determine the child's wala' in the first scenario
The child's wala' goes to the mother's patrons (her 'asaba), not to the father's patrons (who are the woman owning the slave). This is because the father is a slave and cannot be a patron.
If the child dies, inheritance goes to the mother's patrons, not to the woman who owns the father.
4
Identify the second scenario: manumission draws wala'
If the woman later emancipates her slave (the father), that freed slave then 'draws' the wala' of his children to himself and his own patrons (his manumitter's agnates).
The woman retains wala' over the former slave, so she becomes the patron of the father.
5
Apply inheritance outcomes
After manumission, if the son dies, his inheritance goes to his father (the freed slave) as his 'asaba. If the father is also deceased, the inheritance goes to the woman who emancipated the father (his manumitter).
The woman's wala' over the father ultimately draws the child's inheritance to her.
