Ruling on liability when a stone is moved by a second person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a harmful object is moved by a second person and explain why the second person bears liability.

Core Idea
The later mover's act nullifies the earlier transgression — liability follows the direct cause.

Imagine someone places a stone on a path, then another person moves it elsewhere. Who is liable if someone gets hurt? This lesson examines a clear Hanafi ruling that assigns liability to the person whose act directly caused the harm, even if someone else started it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
First, recognize the act: one person places a stone in the road (first act), another moves it to a different spot (second act), and a person is injured. The ruling is that liability (daman) falls on the second person.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is rational (dalil aqli) stated in the text: 'because the first transgression has been nullified by the act of the second.' This is a causal argument.
The proof is explicit in the Arabic: لِأَنَّ التَّعَدِّيَ الْأَوَّلَ قَدْ زَالَ بِفِعْلِ الثَّانِي
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'قد زال بفعل الثاني' (has been nullified by the act of the second) indicates that the legal cause ('illah) of harm shifts to the second person's act. The first person's transgression is no longer the operative cause.
This is based on the principle that the direct, immediate cause takes precedence over an earlier indirect cause.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The text does not mention any intra-Hanafi or inter-madhhab disagreement. The ruling is considered muttafaq alayh within the Hanafi school from Zahir al-Riwayah.
No ikhtilaf is recorded on this specific scenario.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
Since there is no ikhtilaf, the ruling is straightforward and relied upon (mufta bihi). It reflects the general Hanafi approach to causation: liability follows the one whose act directly produces the harm.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Liability follows knowledge: a deceived digger is not liable; the commanding hirer bears the loss.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains a key principle in Hanafi tort law regarding hired workers. When a person hires a digger to dig a well on land that the hirer does not own, the question arises: who is responsible if the well causes harm? The ruling depends critically on the digger's knowledge of the land ownership. The reasoning reveals how Hanafi fiqh balances agency, deception, and attribution of acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the determining factor
The act is digging a well on non-owned land by a hired worker. The key factor is the digger's knowledge of the non-ownership.
2
State the two scenarios and their rulings
If the digger is unaware of non-ownership, liability is on the hirer. If the digger knows, liability is on the digger.
3
Trace the legal reasoning (talil)
For the unaware digger: he is excused (ma'dhur) because he acted under the hirer's command without fault. For the aware digger: the hirer's command is invalid over another's property, and there is no deception (ghurur), so the act is attributed to the digger.
The passage uses 'la yasihh amruhu fi mulki gharihi' and 'la ghurur'.
4
Identify the underlying principles
The principles of ghurur (deception) and ma'dhur (excused ignorance) govern liability. Knowledge negates the excuse and removes deception.
5
Apply to analogous cases
Consider cases where the hirer falsely claims ownership: if the digger believes him, there is ghurur and the digger is excused. If the digger knows the truth, he remains liable.
This extends the ruling to active deception.

Diyah Liability When a Worker Dies in Shared Digging
Ruling on death of a worker in a shared digging accident

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate diyah liability when multiple contributors cause a death, applying the Hanafi rule that the victim's own act cancels his share, and distinguish between permissible and transgressor acts.

Core Idea
When a worker's own act contributes to his death, his share of diyah is dropped; liability depends on whether the act was permissible.

In this lesson, we examine a case from Al-Jawhara al-Nayira where four hired diggers cause a well to collapse, killing one. The Hanafi school provides a precise method to apportion blood money (diyah) among the survivors, based on causal contributions. We also see how the legal classification of the act—whether it is a transgression or permissible—determines whether any liability arises.

Guided Path
1
Identify the facts
Four workers are hired to dig a well. Their digging causes a collapse that kills one of them. Determine the number of causal agents: each worker's act of digging is a cause (four causes total).
2
Apply the causation principle
The victim's own digging contributed to his death. The Hanafi rule states that no person can be liable for harm caused by his own act to himself. Therefore, the share of diyah corresponding to the victim's own cause is dropped.
3
Calculate the survivors' liability
Total diyah is divided equally among the four causes (each 1/4). The victim's 1/4 is dropped. The remaining 3/4 is borne by the three survivors, each paying 1/4 of the total diyah.
This calculation assumes equal causation. If the well were dug in a public road (transgression), liability holds.
4
Consider the location condition
If the well is dug on the hirer's own property, the digging is a permissible (mubah) act. Harm resulting from a permissible act is not guaranteed (ghayr madmun). Hence, no diyah is owed. This contrasts with the public road case where digging is a transgression.

Commentary

The text then adds a special condition: this liability only applies if the well is dug in the public road (tariq), where the digging is a transgression. Detailed Rules for Projection Causing Death: Two Aspects and the Role of Istihsan
Commentary on the two conditions for diyah from a projecting structure.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two scenarios of liability for a projecting structure in Hanafi fiqh and apply the rule of istihsan when the cause is unknown.

Core Idea
Liability for a projecting structure depends on which part hits; if unknown, istihsan imposes half diyah over qiyas.

In this expansion of the matn, the commentator clarifies that the general ruling of diyah for death caused by a projection is subject to two conditions (wajhayn). The distinction between the inner part (within the property) and the outer part (projecting into the road) determines liability. Furthermore, when the cause is unknown, the Hanafi school applies istihsan to impose half diyah, overriding the strict qiyas which would deny compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aspects
The passage states that there are two aspects (wajhayn) to consider: if the inner part (inside the wall) hits the victim, there is no liability; if the outer part (projecting into the road) hits, there is full liability.
The inner part is within the owner's property, so no transgression; the outer part is in the public way, so it is a transgression.
2
Understand the combined and unknown scenarios
If both parts hit the victim, liability is half. If it is unknown which part caused death, qiyas (analogy) would yield no liability due to doubt, but istihsan (juristic preference) imposes half liability.
Istihsan is a distinctive Hanafi principle that overrides strict analogy for a more equitable result.
3
Apply the ruling to real cases
In practice, determine the part of the structure that struck the victim. If it was clearly the outer part, full diyah is due. If unclear, apply istihsan and impose half diyah.
This avoids complete denial of compensation while acknowledging the owner's partial responsibility.

Liability for Placing Hot Embers in the Road
Ruling on liability for fire caused by embers in the road

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability for fire from embers placed in the road and apply the distinction between direct and indirect causation with intervening natural forces.

Core Idea
Liability for fire from embers depends on foreseeability: if the wind is foreseeable, you are liable as if you acted directly.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayrah addresses a classic tort scenario: placing hot embers in a public road. The ruling hinges on whether the resulting fire was a direct consequence of the act or was interrupted by an intervening cause. The Hanafi principle distinguishes between mubasharah (direct causation) and tasabbub (indirect causation), with foreseeability as the key factor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and initial ruling
The act is placing hot embers in a public road. The general ruling is that if they burn something while in their original position, the placer is liable (daman) because it is a direct result of his transgression.
2
Introduce the exception for wind
If the wind moves the embers to another place before the burning occurs, the placer is not liable because the wind's action nullifies (nasakha) his act. The wind is an intervening cause that breaks the causal chain.
3
Analyse the windy day exception
There is a second opinion (qila) that if he placed the embers on a windy day, he is liable even if the wind moves them. This is because he acted knowing the likely consequence; his act is treated as direct causation (mubasharah).
This opinion is considered the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
4
Extract the underlying principle
Intervening natural events break causation unless they were foreseeable. If the actor knew or should have known of the risk, the indirect act is treated as direct, and liability attaches.

If the slave dies before choice: claimant's right drops. If master kills the slave after injury: master is deemed to have chosen arsh (compensation). Slave's Two Injuries and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Insolvency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a slave's multiple injuries and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the master's insolvency after choosing ransom.

Core Idea
Two injuries on one slave mean shared liability: victims divide proportionally; insolvency after ransom splits the school.

When a slave commits two separate criminal injuries, both victims have claims that attach simultaneously to the slave's person. The master must either hand over the slave for division or pay full blood-money. A key dispute arises if the master chooses ransom but later cannot pay: Abu Hanifa holds his choice stands, while the Sahibayn require solvency at the time of choice. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its ratio, and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and master's two options
The slave commits two distinct criminal injuries (jinaayatani). The master is given a choice: hand over the slave to the victims' heirs to be divided according to their rights, or ransom the slave by paying the full arsh for each injury.
The master's obligation to choose is immediate; he cannot delay indefinitely.
2
Determine proportional division when multiple victims
If the injuries differ (e.g., killing one person and gouging another's eye), the victims share the slave as co-owners in proportion to their respective arsh values. For example, arsh al-'ayn is half of arsh al-nafs, so the victim of the eye gets one-third and the victim of death gets two-thirds.
The attachment of the first claim does not prevent the second; both claims exist concurrently on the slave's neck.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
The passage presents a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). If the master chooses ransom but later becomes insolvent (cannot pay), what happens to the victims' rights?
This dispute arises from a difference in understanding the conditions of a valid choice.
4
Compare Abu Hanifa's position with the Sahibayn's position
Abu Hanifa: the master's choice is valid even if he later becomes insolvent; the debt is on his dhimma and the victims have no claim on the slave. The Sahibayn: if the master did not have the amount in hand at the time of choice, the choice is void and the victims' right remains attached to the slave.
The Sahibayn view implies an implicit condition that the master must actually possess the funds to pay at the moment of choosing ransom.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, a bankrupt master's choice still binds the victims; they must wait for payment from his personal assets. Under the Sahibayn, the choice is void, so the victims can still seize and divide the slave.
Later Hanafi fatwa often prefers the Sahibayn view as more protective of victims' rights.

Master's Liability When Freeing a Slave Unaware of Injury
Ruling on master's liability when freeing a slave unaware of the crime

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that a master who frees a slave without knowledge of the slave's injury must pay the lesser of the slave's value or the blood-money (arsh), and explain the underlying reasoning based on the victim's right.

Core Idea
A master who frees a slave unaware of the injury pays the lesser of the slave's value or the blood-money.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: a master frees his slave without knowing that the slave had committed a criminal injury (jinaaya). Since the master was unaware, his act is not considered a choice to ransom the slave. However, because the freeing destroys an asset that was subject to the victim's right, the master incurs a financial liability. The key principle is that he pays the lesser of two amounts—the slave's market value or the blood-money of the injury. This ruling extends to similar situations where the master grants partial freedom, such as making a female slave an umm walad or mudabbara without knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the act
The master frees his slave. The slave had previously committed a criminal injury (jinaaya) giving the victim a right over the slave's person. The master is unaware of this crime.
2
Understand the basis of liability
Because the master did not know, he is not considered to have chosen to ransom the slave (which would require knowledge and intent). However, by freeing, he destroys the slave's person, which was an asset with a right attached (the victim could take the slave as compensation). Thus, he must pay.
Liability arises because he destroyed property subject to another's claim.
3
Determine the amount of liability
The amount is the lesser of: (a) the slave's market value at the time of freeing, or (b) the blood-money (arsh) of the injury. The reasoning: if the arsh is less, the victim's claim is only that much; if the slave's value is less, the master cannot be liable for more than he destroyed.
This is the principle of 'aqallu min qīmatihi wa min arshihā'.
4
Apply the same ruling to analogous cases
If the master makes a female slave an umm walad (by having a child with her) or mudabbara (promising freedom after his death) without knowledge, the same rule applies: he pays the lesser amount.
These acts also affect the slave's status and destroy the victim's right to take the slave.

Master's Liability for Arsh After Transacting the Slave
Ruling on master's obligation to pay blood money if he transacts the slave knowingly

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the actions that obligate the master to pay arsh and identify which actions do not constitute a choice of ransom.

Core Idea
Any ownership transfer after knowledge of the injury makes the master liable for arsh; mere use does not.

When a slave commits a criminal injury (jinaaya), the victim has a claim for arsh against the slave's person or the master's property. This lesson examines how the master's subsequent actions—selling, freeing, gifting, etc.—affect his liability, and clarifies that simply using the slave for work does not count as choosing ransom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering event
The master must have knowledge of the jinaaya before the transaction. If the transaction occurs before knowledge, different rules apply.
Knowledge is key; without it, the master is not making an informed choice.
2
Recognize the qualifying transactions
Selling, freeing, gifting, making mudabbara, or acknowledging (iqrar) the slave to another are actions that transfer ownership or free the slave, thereby obligating the arsh.
Each transaction indicates the master's choice to retain the slave by paying compensation.
3
Identify the exception: mere use
If the master simply employs the slave without transferring ownership, and the slave dies from the work, no guarantee arises. This is not considered a choice of ransom.
The master is still entitled to use his property; accidental death does not imply acceptance of liability.
4
Understand the concept of 'choosing ransom'
When the master sells to the victim or orders the victim to free the slave, he is deemed to have chosen ransom (mukhtār li al-fidā'), meaning he must pay the arsh.
The victim acts as the master's agent in freeing, so the master's liability is triggered.
