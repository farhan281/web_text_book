The 'Iddah of a Free Widow: Four Months and Ten Days
Ruling on 'iddah for a free widow

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the 'iddah period for a free widow and identify the condition (free woman) attached to the ruling.

Core Idea
A free widow must observe 'iddah for four months and ten days upon her husband's death.

The passage sets out a clear and unambiguous ruling: when a free woman's husband dies, she must observe a waiting period of four months and ten days. This is one of the foundational rules of family law in the Hanafi school, directly rooted in the Qur'an. Let's examine the precise ruling and its scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the 'iddah (waiting period) for a widow. The ruling is that it lasts four months and ten days. This is an obligation (wajib).
The Arabic uses 'fa-'iddatuhu' indicating a direct consequence.
2
Note the condition: free woman
The ruling explicitly applies to a 'free wife' (imra'atihi al-hurrah). This implies that the period may differ for a slave woman.
Other conditions, like pregnancy, are not mentioned here but are dealt with elsewhere.
3
Consider the legal category
The 'iddah is a wajib (obligatory) period. It must be observed before the widow can remarry. Failure to observe it makes a remarriage invalid.
The obligation is established by the Qur'an (2:234), though not quoted in this passage.
4
Recognise the absence of exceptions in this text
The passage does not mention pregnancy. From other sources, a pregnant widow's 'iddah ends upon delivery. This is a separate ruling.
This passage focuses solely on the standard case.

The commentary then reiterates the matn (base text) being explained. This ruling applies to non-pregnant free widows. If the widow is pregnant, her 'iddah ends upon delivery (Quran 65:4). The ruling here is straightforward and undisputed among the schools.

Iddah of Umm Walad upon Master's Death: General Rulings
Expands on the 'iddah of umm walad across various scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which an umm walad observes 'iddah upon her master's death and identify the ruling in scenarios involving marriage, emancipation, and prior divorce.

Core Idea
The umm walad's 'iddah upon master's death depends on whether she is his 'firash' at that time.

The 'iddah of an umm walad upon her master's death is a nuanced ruling that distinguishes between her relationship with the master and her marital status. The key concept is 'firash' (legal matrimonial bed): if she is not under the master's firash at his death—for instance, because she is married to another man or in 'iddah from that marriage—she does not observe 'iddah. However, if she later becomes available again (her marriage ends) and the master has not yet died, she must observe 'iddah. Additionally, emancipation modifies her 'iddah, aligning it with that of a free woman.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default 'iddah
Start by understanding the basic 'iddah of an umm walad upon her master's death: three menstrual cycles (or three months if non-menstruating), with no maintenance, because it is an 'iddah from intercourse.
This applies only if she is not currently married or in her husband's 'iddah.
2
Check her marital status at master's death
If she is married to another man or in 'iddah from that marriage, she is not under the master's 'firash' and therefore she does not observe 'iddah for the master at that time.
The master's death does not affect her existing marriage or 'iddah for her husband.
3
Consider the effect of emancipation
If the master emancipates her, her 'iddah changes. If the husband divorces her after emancipation, her 'iddah becomes that of a free woman. If emancipation occurs during her ongoing 'iddah from her husband, the effect depends on whether the divorce was revocable or irrevocable.
Revocable divorce: her 'iddah changes from emancipation; irrevocable: no change.
4
Analyze the scenario where her marriage ends before master's death
If her 'iddah from her husband expires and then the master dies, she must observe 'iddah for the master because she has returned to being his property and thus his 'firash'.
This demonstrates that the master's death triggers 'iddah only if she is his 'firash' at that moment.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the 'iddah (waiting period) of an umm walad (a slave woman who has borne a child to her master) upon the death of her master. The Sharh begins by stating that the previous rulings apply only if she is not already in 'iddah from another marriage and is not currently married. It explains that she receives no maintenance during this 'iddah because it arises from intercourse (istihsan), similar to the 'iddah from a fasid (void) marriage. For a woman who does not menstruate, the 'iddah is three months. The text emphasizes that death and emancipation are treated equally in this rule because both terminate the relationship and thus the 'iddah is for intercourse. Next, it states that if a master dies while having a slave woman he used to have intercourse with, or a mudabbara (a slave promised freedom after the master's death), or emancipates them, no 'iddah is required because they are not considered a 'firash' (marital bed/legal matrimony) for him; the 'iddah of the umm walad is a special concession due to her child. The core ruling: if a master marries off his umm walad and then dies while she is living with her husband or in 'iddah from her husband, she does not observe 'iddah for the master because she is no longer his 'firash'. However, if the master emancipates her and later her husband divorces her, her 'iddah becomes that of a free woman (four months and ten days or three menstrual cycles if pregnant). If emancipation occurs during her ongoing 'iddah: if the divorce was revocable (raj'i), her 'iddah changes (becomes the 'iddah of a free woman from the time of emancipation); if irrevocable (ba'in), it remains unchanged. Finally, if her 'iddah from the husband has already ended and then the master dies, she must observe three menstruations because she reverts to being his property and thus his 'firash'.

أم الولد (umm al-walad): A slave woman who has borne a child to her master, and the child is acknowledged by the master.
عدة ('iddah): The legally prescribed waiting period a woman must observe after the death of her husband or after divorce, during which she cannot remarry.
فراش (firash): A legal matrimonial bed or relationship; the basis for establishing lineage and 'iddah obligations.

Combined 'Iddah of Umm Walad upon Death of Master and Husband
Fiqh ruling with detailed scenarios and precautionary reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine the 'iddah of an umm walad when both master and husband die with uncertain timing, applying the Hanafi principles of ihtiyat and simultaneous death.

Core Idea
When uncertain whether master or husband died first, the longer 'iddah is imposed by precaution, and menstruations may be dropped.

This lesson addresses a complex scenario in Hanafi family law: a slave woman who is an umm walad (mother of her master's child) is married to a free man. Both her master and her husband die at unknown times. The Hanafi jurists determine her 'iddah (waiting period) by considering all possible sequences and applying the principle of precaution (ihtiyat) to ensure no obligation is missed. The passage covers three situations based on the known or unknown interval between the two deaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify possible death sequences
There are two possible orders: husband dies first, then master; or master dies first, then husband. Each leads to different 'iddah obligations due to her status changing from slave to free when the master dies.
Her status matters: as a slave, 'iddah for husband is 2 months 5 days; as a free woman, it is 4 months 10 days. As an umm walad, her 'iddah upon master's death is 3 menstruations.
2
Apply the rulings to each sequence
If husband dies first: she is a slave at that time, so she observes 2 months 5 days for him. Then the master dies after her 'iddah ends, she must observe 3 menstruations for the master – combined total: 4 months 10 days plus 3 menstruations (but since months include menstruations, the total becomes 4 months 10 days with 3 menstruations inside). If master dies first: she becomes free immediately upon his death, but no 'iddah for him because she is married. Then husband dies while she is free, so she observes 4 months 10 days without menstruations (if not pregnant).
The longer total is 4 months 10 days in both sequences, but presence of menstruations differs.
3
Apply ihtiyat when sequence is unknown
When the actual order is unknown but the interval between deaths is known, the longer obligation is imposed as a precaution. If the interval > 2 months 5 days, she must observe 4 months 10 days with 3 menstruations (the combined total). If the interval < 2 months 5 days, she observes 4 months 10 days without menstruations, because in the possible sequence where master dies first, no menstruations are required, and in the other, menstruations would have been completed before the interval ends.
The principle of ihtiyat ensures no obligation is missed, even if it means imposing a stricter requirement.
4
Handle completely unknown timing
If neither the interval nor the order is known, Abu Hanifa rules that the deaths are deemed simultaneous (like drowning victims). Therefore, she becomes free at the same moment the husband dies, so she observes the free woman's 'iddah of 4 months 10 days without menstruations.
This is based on the legal fiction of simultaneous death for unknown priorities.

Intercourse under Mistaken Belief: Multiple 'Iddahs Overlap
Ruling on two 'iddahs overlapping when a woman in 'iddah has intercourse under shubhah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on overlapping 'iddahs when a woman in 'iddah has intercourse under shubhah, and contrast it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
When multiple 'iddahs are due, the Hanafi school counts them concurrently; the Shafi'i school counts them sequentially.

This passage explores a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the 'iddah (waiting period). The matn establishes the Hanafi rule that when a woman in 'iddah has intercourse under a mistaken belief (shubhah), she must observe a second 'iddah, and the two 'iddahs overlap. The commentary then explains the underlying principle: whether the essential element of 'iddah is an action or refraining from action.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling from the matn: two 'iddahs overlap and the same menstrual cycles count for both.
The matn says 'عندنا' (according to us), indicating this is the relied-upon position.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that two 'iddahs cannot overlap; they must be observed sequentially.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Al-Shafi'i argues that the rukn (essential element) of 'iddah is an action (fi'l), i.e., the act of waiting (tarabbus), which includes refraining from marriage and leaving the home. Two actions cannot occur simultaneously, just as two fasts cannot be kept on one day.
Analogy to fasting: you cannot perform two acts of fasting on the same day.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school holds that the rukn of 'iddah is refraining from action (tark al-fi'l). Since refraining is not a positive act, multiple refraining can be combined. Therefore, two 'iddahs can overlap.
The response is implicit in the text: the Hanafi view is based on the nature of the rukn being tark.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, for a woman with multiple causes for 'iddah, the Hanafi school allows her to complete one 'iddah period covering all causes. The Shafi'i school requires separate sequential periods.
This affects the duration of her waiting period and when she becomes free to remarry.

Establishing Lineage after Divorce or Death: The Two-Year Rule
Rulings on lineage within two years of divorce or death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on establishing paternity for a child born after a final divorce or the husband's death, apply the two-year maximum gestation rule, and identify the intra-madhhab dispute regarding the exact two-year mark.

Core Idea
A child born within two years of divorce or death is attributed to the husband; at exactly two years, only if the husband claims paternity.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi principle: the maximum period of pregnancy is two years. Based on this, a final divorcee or widow who gives birth within two years of separation or death will have the child's lineage established to the former husband. However, if the child is born exactly at the two-year mark, the ruling depends on whether the husband claims the child. We will explore the textual basis, the practical application, and a significant intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for the final divorcee
The matn states that if the child is born within less than two years, lineage is established. If born at exactly two years, it is not established unless the husband claims. Note the condition: 'less than two years' vs. 'at completion of two years'.
Think about the rationale: possibility that pregnancy existed at divorce.
2
Examine the dalil (proof)
The proof is rational (dalil aqli): because it is possible that the pregnancy was present at the time of divorce. This relies on the two-year maximum gestation period.
The Hanafi school uses this rational inference to extend the possibility beyond the typical nine months.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Some Hanafi texts (as noted in the commentary) hold that lineage is proven at exactly two years without the husband's claim, because the womb is occupied for the entire two-year period. Also, there are two narrations on whether the husband needs the woman's confirmation when he claims.
This shows that even within the Hanafi school, there is some variation on the precise application.
4
Apply the rule to a widow
For a widow, the child's lineage is established if born within two years of death, regardless of whether the marriage was consummated. The same two-year window applies.
This is simpler because there is no possibility of remarriage affecting the iddah.
5
Consider the practical implication
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is that the two-year maximum is the school's position. The exact two-year mark requires the husband's claim. This matters in inheritance, guardianship, and social status.
Note the cross-madhhab difference: Shafi'i allows up to four years, Maliki five years.

Effect of Acknowledging End of Iddah on Lineage

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A woman's false claim of iddah expiry is exposed if she gives birth within six months, establishing lineage.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses a specific scenario: a woman in iddah (either divorced or widowed) acknowledges that her iddah has ended, but then later gives birth. How does this affect the lineage (nasab) of the child? The Hanafi school, following Abu Hanifa, makes a clear distinction based on the timing of the birth relative to her claim. The key is the Islamic legal principle that the minimum period of pregnancy is six months. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its logical proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Read the first sentence: a woman in iddah acknowledges the expiry of her iddah, then gives birth. The ruling: if birth occurs within less than six months from the claim, lineage is established; if six months or more, lineage is not established according to Abu Hanifa. Identify the two possible timings.
Note: the iddah period itself is already over when she makes the claim; the issue is the truth of her claim.
2
Understand the logical proof for less than six months
The commentary states: 'because her lie is clearly exposed' (fazahara kadhibuha bi yaqin). Here, the birth within six months proves that she was pregnant at the time of her claim. Since iddah ends only when pregnancy ends (if pregnant) or after the prescribed period, her claim that iddah ended was false; the child must have been conceived during the iddah, thus lineage to the ex-husband is established.
The proof is aqli (rational) based on the minimum pregnancy of six months.
3
Understand the logical proof for six months or more
Commentary: 'due to the possibility of its occurrence after the iddah' (li ihtimal al-huduth ba'd al-iddah). Six months is the minimum possible pregnancy; if she gives birth at six months or more from her claim, it is possible that conception occurred after her claim (i.e., after the iddah ended). Therefore, the child could be from a new marriage (if she remarried) or from a different man, so lineage to the ex-husband is not established.
Note: the possibility (ihtimal) is enough to prevent certainty in establishing lineage.
4
Apply the same logic to a widow
The passage extends the rule to a widow: if she acknowledges the expiry of her iddah (four months ten days), then gives birth within less than six months from the day of acknowledgment, lineage is established. If six months or more, it is not established. The same rational proof applies.
The widow's iddah is longer, but the six-month rule from the time of claim is independent.

Establishing Lineage: Six-Month Minimum and Husband's Response
Ruling on lineage when birth occurs at least six months after marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for establishing lineage (nasab) in Hanafi fiqh when a child is born at least six months after marriage, and explain the rulings on the husband's acknowledgment, silence, and denial, including the exception of pregnancy from zina.

Core Idea
Lineage is established by the marital bed (firash) if the child is born at least six months after marriage and the husband does not deny.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents the Hanafi rules for establishing paternal lineage, focusing on the role of the marital bed (firash) and the husband's response. We will examine the minimum pregnancy period, the implications of the husband's acknowledgment, silence, or denial, and the special case where pregnancy existed before marriage, including the intra-Hanafi dispute on marrying a woman pregnant from zina. Mastering these rules is essential for understanding family law and inheritance rights in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic condition for lineage
The passage states that lineage is established if the child is born at six months or more after marriage. Note that this is the minimum possible pregnancy duration in Hanafi fiqh.
If the child is born before six months, lineage is not established from the husband unless he acknowledges.
2
Understand the husband's three possible responses
The husband may: (1) acknowledge the child—lineage is established; (2) remain silent—lineage is established because silence implies acknowledgment; (3) deny the birth—lineage is established if one woman testifies to the birth, otherwise the husband must perform li'an.
Denial of birth (jahd al-wilada) is different from denial of paternity (nafy al-nasab). The latter requires li'an.
3
Analyze the exception: pregnancy before marriage
If the pregnancy existed before the marriage, the child is not attributed to the husband and the marriage is annulled—unless the pregnancy is from zina, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
Abu Yusuf differs on this point (see other sources).
4
Apply the rule to a dispute on marriage duration
If the husband says the marriage occurred four months ago and the wife says six months, the wife's word is taken because the apparent evidence (a child born in wedlock) supports her.
This is based on the principle that the child is presumed to be from the marriage unless proven otherwise.

Iddah for a Dhimmi Couple: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on iddah for dhimmi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on iddah for dhimmi women and explain the reasoning behind Abu Hanifa's view.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: no iddah for dhimmis because they aren't bound by Islamic law; Sahibayn disagree if in Dar al-Islam.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school regarding whether a dhimmi woman must observe iddah after divorce. The matn states the ruling, and the commentary expands the reasoning, revealing a key disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two major disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its context
The passage addresses a dhimmi divorcing his dhimmi wife. The matn states 'no iddah upon her' as a default ruling. Note that this is an intra-Hanafi dispute.
The context is within a commentary (sharh) on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that iddah is not required. His reasoning: iddah is obligatory for the right of Allah and the right of the husband. The dhimmi woman is not addressed by rights of Allah (like prayer), and the husband has waived his right because he does not believe in it.
The husband's non-belief is key – he does not consider iddah a right, so it is effectively waived.
3
State Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that iddah is required. Their reasoning: because the couple resides in Dar al-Islam, Islamic law applies to them in such matters.
They see iddah as a worldly legal requirement, not purely religious.
4
Analyze the basis of the dispute
The dispute hinges on two factors: (1) whether dhimmis are addressed by Islamic personal status rulings, and (2) the nature of iddah – whether it is purely religious (huquq Allah) or also a worldly legal matter.
Abu Hanifa emphasizes the religious dimension; Sahibayn emphasize the legal/jurisdictional dimension.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and practical consequence
The later school follows Abu Hanifa's view as mufta bihi. Practically, a dhimmi divorcee may remarry immediately without waiting, unless the case is brought before an Islamic judge who may apply the Sahibayn's view.
This is an example of tarjih in the later Hanafi tradition.

A marriage with a woman pregnant from zina is valid, but intercourse is forbidden until delivery because the fornicator's sperm has no sanctity.

Effect of Marriage on Women's Custody Rights
Ruling on loss of custody upon marriage with grandmother exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general rule that a woman's custody right falls upon marriage to a stranger, identify the grandmother exception, and explain the legal rationale (illah) based on harm to the child.

Core Idea
A woman's custody right ends when she marries a stranger to the child, but the grandmother is excepted if she marries the grandfather.

This lesson examines a key ruling from the Hanafi school on how remarriage affects a woman's right to custody (hadanah). The core principle is that the child's welfare is paramount: a stepfather who is a non-mahram is presumed to cause harm, so custody is transferred. However, a compassionate exception preserves a grandmother's custody when she marries the child's grandfather. Understanding this rule and its exception illuminates how Hanafi fiqh balances family ties and child protection.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Identify the ruling: any female custodian (mother, grandmother, etc.) who marries a man who is a stranger (i.e., not a mahram relative of the child) loses her right to custody. The text says 'سقط حقها' (her right falls).
The ruling applies to all female custodians listed in the chapter of custody.
2
Extract the Legal Rationale (Illah)
The text gives the reason: the child suffers harshness (jafaa) from a non-mahram stepfather, who may look angrily (shazr) and provide meagerly (nazr). Thus, the illah is harm to the child, and custody is removed to protect the child's welfare.
This is a qiyas-based reasoning: the marriage creates a conflict of interest, so the right is voided.
3
Analyze the Grandmother Exception
The exception: if the grandmother marries the child's own grandfather (the father of the child's father), she retains custody. The text gives an example and also extends the exception to the mother marrying the child's uncle or other mahram relatives who themselves have custody rights.
The exception preserves compassion (shafaqah) because the grandfather is a close relative and likely caring.
4
Understand the Return of Custody
If the marriage that caused the loss ends (by death or divorce of the new husband), the woman's custody right returns, because the obstacle is removed.
This shows the ruling is conditional on the existence of the marriage.

This overrides the default matn position.

This exception applies to both sons and daughters.

Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on child's choice in custody before puberty.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a child's choice between parents before puberty and explain how the Hanafi school rebuts the Shafi'i proof from the hadith.

Core Idea
A child before puberty cannot choose between parents—his interests are decided by guardians, not his whims.

This passage presents a well-known dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on whether a child can choose which parent to live with before reaching puberty. The Hanafi school argues that a minor lacks the judgment to determine his own best interests, similar to his inability to manage his property. The Shafi'i school relies on a hadith where the Prophet ﷺ gave a boy the choice. The Hanafi response provides two interpretations: either the choice was divinely guided, or the boy was actually an adult.

This is a talil (rational) argument based on maṣlaḥa.

Examine the Shafi'i hadith
Read the hadith: a woman complained that her husband wanted to take her son, and the Prophet gave the boy the choice. The Shafi'i school uses this as a proof.

5
Analyze the Hanafi response
Two rebuttals: (a) the Prophet's prayer 'O Allah, guide him' (اللهم اهده) indicates the choice was divinely inspired, not free; (b) the boy was an adult because he could draw water from a deep well (بئر أبي عنبة) requiring strength, so the hadith does not apply to young children.
The Hanafi school harmonizes the hadith with their position without rejecting it.

Core Idea
A slave becomes Umm Walad if she bears a child with formed limbs whom the master acknowledges; then she cannot be sold but remains in his service.

This lesson explains the foundational ruling on Umm Walad. When a female slave bears a child to her master, her status changes: she cannot be sold or transferred, yet the master retains rights of use. The ruling applies even if the child is stillborn or a miscarriage, as long as some part of its creation is apparent and the master acknowledges paternity. Core Idea
Paternity of the Umm Walad's child is not automatic; the master may deny if there is doubt, but caution is recommended.

Later Hanafi fatwa often leans towards caution (Muhammad's view).

The Father's Claim of Paternity (Istilad) over the Son's Slave-Girl
Conditions for father's istilad on son's slave-girl with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a father's valid istilad claim over his son's slave-girl and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa/Muhammad vs Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
A father's claim of paternity over his son's slave-girl is valid only if conditions of freedom, son's silence, and continuous ownership/wilaya are met.

This lesson examines the rules of istilad when a father claims a child born to a slave-girl owned by his son. The Hanafi school has an intra-school dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad allow the claim under strict conditions, while Abu Yusuf rejects it entirely. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah details the conditions and the differing views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is the father's claim (da‘wa) of paternity (istilad) over a child born to his son's slave-girl. According to the majority, the claim is valid (sahih) if conditions are met.
The text begins with the ruling that the father must pay value, indicating validity.
2
List the basic conditions for validity
The father must be a free Muslim, and the son must not himself claim the child. These are the first conditions mentioned.
Condition of son's silence is critical; if the son claims, the father's claim fails.
3
Note the dissenting view of Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf holds that istilad is never established from the father. If the son also claims, the child belongs to the son.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the text presents it as a minority opinion.
4
Understand the additional conditions of continuity
The father's claim requires continuous ownership of the slave-girl by the son from conception to claim, and continuous guardianship (wilaya) of the father over the son during that period.
If the father was a slave or non-Muslim at any point, the claim is invalid.
5
Apply conditions to edge cases
Consider scenarios: if the slave-girl is the son's umm walad or mudabbara, the claim is void; if ownership is interrupted, the claim fails.
The text also mentions that the grandfather cannot claim while the father is alive.

Commentary
This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah discusses the father's claim of paternity (istilad) regarding a slave-girl owned by his son. The passage type is an intra-Hanafi dispute explaining the conditions and validity of such a claim. The author states that the father's claim is accepted whether the son confirms or denies, and whether the father claims a legal doubt (shubha) or not, provided the father is a free Muslim and the son does not himself claim the child. However, if the father is a slave or a disbeliever while the son is Muslim, the claim is invalid according to both Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (عندهما). Abu Yusuf holds a stronger position: istilad is not established from the father at all; if the son also claims the child together with the father, the child belongs to the son and the slave-girl becomes the son's umm walad. The text then clarifies that the father's claim is conditional upon the slave-girl being owned by the son continuously from conception until the claim, and upon the father having guardianship (wilaya) over the son throughout that period. If the father was a disbeliever and later embraced Islam, or was a slave and later freed, the claim is invalid because the wilaya was not continuous. Similarly, the grandfather cannot claim while the father is alive. If the slave-girl leaves the son's ownership before the birth, the father's claim is void.

Key Terms
استيلاد (istilad): A father's claim of paternity over a child born to a slave-girl, making the child free and the mother an umm walad.
عندهما (indahuma): According to both Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the two companions).
علوق (‘uluq): Conception; the moment the slave-girl becomes pregnant.

Father's Liability for Son's Slave-Girl: Value, Not 'Uqr or Child's Value
Explains financial consequences of father's valid istilad claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a father who validly claims paternity of his son's slave-girl must pay only her full value, and distinguish between 'uqr and value liability.

Core Idea
When a father acquires a slave-girl through istilad, the full value subsumes the 'uqr, and no separate compensation for the child is due.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira tackles a specific scenario: a father has intercourse with his son's slave-girl and then validly claims paternity (istilad). What financial obligations arise? The Hanafi ruling is that the father must pay the full value of the slave-girl, but not the 'uqr (the payment for intercourse) nor the value of the child. Understanding the reasoning behind this distinction is essential for grasping the Hanafi approach to compensatory transfers (daman naql).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act: a father has intercourse with his son's slave-girl and validly claims paternity. The ruling: the father must pay the full value of the slave-girl, regardless of wealth, at the time of conception.
The value is a compensatory transfer (daman naql), not a penalty.
2
Locate the dalil
The basis is the principle that transfer of ownership requires compensation, analogous to sale. The text states: 'لأنا نقلناها إليه من ملك الابن، فلا تنتقل إلا بعوض.' (because we transfer her to him from the son's ownership, so she does not transfer except with compensation).
This is an analogy (qiyas) to sale, but also istihsan.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The full value is a guarantee of the whole slave-girl, while the 'uqr is a guarantee of a part (the right to intercourse). The whole includes the part, so the father is not additionally liable for 'uqr. The text compares to a man who cuts off another's hand and then the victim dies: the diyah for the hand is included in the full diyah.
The child's value is not due because the father owned the mother at conception, and the child had no value at that moment.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The text hints at Abu Yusuf's view: if istilad is not established, then 'uqr is due. But here, the father's claim is valid, so the majority view applies.
The intra-Hanafi difference does not affect this scenario.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The mainstream Hanafi position as stated is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi). The tarjih authority is unspecified, but the text clearly favors it.
This is the position in Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the financial liability of a father who has intercourse with his son's slave-girl and then validly claims paternity. The author explains the statement from the matn: the father must pay the full value of the slave-girl, regardless of his wealth, because she is transferred from the son's ownership to his ownership by the claim and intercourse. This is a compensatory transfer similar to a sale, so the value is due at the time of conception. Importantly, he is not liable for the 'uqr (a type of dower for intercourse) nor the value of the child. The reasoning is that by paying the full value, he has compensated for the entire property; the 'uqr would be a partial compensation for intercourse, and the greater payment subsumes the lesser. As for the child, since the father owned the mother at the moment of conception (because she transferred to him at that time), the child is born into his ownership, and thus he is not liable for any guarantee. The child becomes free (by origin, because the father is free) and there is no wala' (clientage) because he effectively becomes the owner-master. The passage ends with a definition of 'uqr: for free women it means the customary dower, for slave women it is a fixed fraction of her value (1/10 for virgin, 1/20 for non-virgin), as reported by al-Sarakhsi.

Key Terms
عقر (‘uqr): A financial payment due for intercourse; for free women it is mahr al-mithl, for slave women a fixed fraction of her value.
ضمان نقل (daman naql): A guarantee that accompanies the transfer of ownership; here it is like a sale where compensation is required.
ولاء (wala'): Clientage relationship that arises from freeing a slave; here the child has no wala' because the father owned the mother at birth.
