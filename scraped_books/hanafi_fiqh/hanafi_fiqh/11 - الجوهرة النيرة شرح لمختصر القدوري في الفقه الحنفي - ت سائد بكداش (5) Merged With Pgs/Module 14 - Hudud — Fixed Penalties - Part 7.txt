Exemptions from Hadd Theft and the Two Types of Hirz
States exemptions from cutting and defines two types of hirz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exemptions from hadd for theft according to the Hanafi school and explain the two types of hirz (secure place/guardian) with their conditions.

Core Idea
Hadd theft is not applied when there is a right in the property or when it is not properly secured – the key is hirz.

In this lesson, we examine the nuanced Hanafi rules on when the severe penalty of hand-cutting for theft is suspended. The source text from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, expanded by the commentator, lists specific exemptions and defines the critical concept of hirz (secure protection). Understanding these exemptions and the dual nature of hirz is essential to correctly apply the hudud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the exempted parties and the rationale for each
List the categories: slave from master, wife from husband during iddah, master from mukatib, thief from spoils. Note the common theme: a right or share in the property (shubhah milk).
The exemptions are based on the principle that hadd is dropped due to ambiguity.
2
Distinguish between the two types of hirz
Hirz bil-makan (by place): houses, shops, tents – secure by construction; cutting requires removal from the place. Hirz bil-hafiz (by guardian): a person protecting property; cutting is by mere taking.
For hirz bil-makan, the owner's hand is considered present until removal.
3
Apply the hadith proof for hirz bil-hafiz
The Prophet cut the thief of Safwan's cloak from under his head while he slept in the mosque. This shows that a sleeping guardian suffices as a hafiz.
The guardian must be close enough to see the property.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on hirz being type-specific vs universal
Al-Karkhi holds that any hirz secures all types of property; the mashayikh say each type has its appropriate hirz (e.g., stable for animals, not money). The text considers al-Karkhi's view correct.
This affects whether cutting applies for money stolen from a stable.

Commentary
This passage from a commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar explains exemptions from the hadd penalty for theft and defines the key concept of hirz (secure place). The author first lists individuals who are not subject to cutting despite taking property: a slave from his master (due to shared ownership rights), the freed master from his mukatib (slave buying freedom, as the master has a right in his earnings), and a thief from military spoils (due to his share). Similarly, spouses who are irrevocably divorced (bainuna kubra) do not have cutting applied if theft occurs during the iddah period because the marital right to mutual benefit persists.The passage then defines hirz as two types: (1) hirz by virtue of the place itself (e.g., houses, shops, tents) which secure property by their construction even without a guard, but cutting only applies upon removal from the place, not mere taking inside; (2) hirz by a guardian (hafiz), such as a person sitting with his belongings in a public space. For hirz by guardian, cutting is immediate upon taking because the owner's protective hand is removed. The author cites the hadith where the Prophet cut a thief who stole Safwan's cloak from under his head while he slept in the mosque, showing that a sleeping person can be a guardian customarily. The guardian must be close enough to see the property. Later scholars (mashayikh) add that the hirz standard depends on the type of property: e.g., stables secure animals but not money or jewelry. However, al-Karkhi holds that whatever is hirz for one type is hirz for all types, which the text considers correct. Finally, stealing camels from the road with their loads is not cutting because they are visible, unsecured wealth.

The Mosque as a Secure Place (Hirz) for Theft Punishment
Ruling on theft in mosque with owner as guard

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition of hirz for theft and explain why the owner's presence makes a mosque a hirz, applying the hadith of Safwan.

Core Idea
The mosque becomes a hirz when the owner is present as a guard, even if asleep—custom defines protection.

The hadd punishment for theft (qat') requires the property to be taken from a hirz (secure place). While mosques are generally public, the Hanafi school holds that the owner's presence as a guard transforms the mosque into a hirz by custom ('urf). This lesson examines the ruling, its proof from the hadith of Safwan, and the custom-based reasoning that a sleeping person near their property is considered a guard.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is theft from a mosque while the owner is present. The ruling is that the thief is subject to hand amputation (qat').
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Safwan: the Prophet cut off the hand of a thief who stole Safwan's cloak from under his head while he slept in the mosque.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but operative in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's action (cutting the hand) demonstrates that the mosque with the owner present qualifies as a hirz. The imperative implication (amr) of the act indicates the ruling is binding.
Hanafi usul: al-amr yufidu al-wujub.
4
Analyze the custom-based reasoning
The commentator explains that a sleeping person near their belongings is considered a guard by custom ('urf). Therefore, no difference exists between awake/asleep or whether the item is under or beside them.
This custom also explains why a depositary or borrower is not liable in similar circumstances—it is not negligence.

Hirz Varies by Property Type
Ruling that hirz depends on the type of property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that hirz is relative to the type of property and apply it to determine when the hadd for theft is applicable.

Core Idea
The same place may be a hirz for one property but not another; only customary protection counts.

The hadd punishment for theft (qat') requires that the stolen property was taken from a secure place (hirz). But what counts as a secure place depends on what is stored there. A stable is a proper hirz for a horse but not for dirhams. This lesson explains the principle of 'consideration by like' (i'tibar bi-mithlihi).

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The sheikhs said: 'Everything is considered according to its like in terms of hirz.' That is, what constitutes a proper hirz depends on the type of property.
2
Identify the positive examples
Stealing a beast from a stable or a sheep from a pen qualifies for amputation because these are customary hirz for those items.
Stable (istabl) for horses, pen (hazirah) for sheep.
3
Identify the negative example
If someone steals dirhams or jewelry from those same places (stable or pen), amputation is not applied because those places are not customary hirz for valuables.
The physical place is the same, but the legal assessment differs.
4
Apply the rule
In a new scenario, first determine the type of property, then ask: Is the place from which it was taken customarily used to secure that type of property? If yes, hirz is established and qat' may apply.

Generality of Hirz: Al-Karkhi's View
Karkhi's ruling on the generality of hirz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply al-Karkhi's principle that a hirz for one type of property is a hirz for all types in determining the application of hadd for theft.

Core Idea
A place that secures one type secures all types for hadd.

This lesson explains al-Karkhi's position that the legal protection of a hirz is not limited to the specific property it customarily holds. In Hanafi fiqh, the condition for applying the hadd punishment for theft requires that the stolen property be taken from a hirz. Al-Karkhi's ruling broadens this condition: if a container or location is considered a hirz for any property, it suffices for all types, even if the stolen item is different. This principle ensures that the thief cannot escape punishment merely because the stolen item was not the typical contents of the container.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Concept of Hirz
Hirz refers to a place or container that is customarily used to securely store property. For theft to incur the hadd, the property must have been in a hirz.
2
Understand the Dispute
Some earlier Hanafi scholars held that hirz is type-specific: a container meant for dates is only a hirz for dates. Al-Karkhi argues that hirz is general: if a place is a hirz for one type, it is a hirz for all types.
The author of al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah declares al-Karkhi's view as the correct one.
3
Apply the Principle to Examples
Apply al-Karkhi's ruling to the given examples: a grocer's jar (sharijah) or a date basket (qawasir al-tamr) becomes a hirz for dirhams, dinars, and pearls, even though they typically hold dates or groceries.
Thus, if someone steals gold from a date basket, the hadd applies.

No Amputation for Stealing Camels from the Road
Ruling on theft of camels from public road

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on stealing camels from a public road and explain why the condition of hirz is not met.

Core Idea
Stealing from a public road is not punishable by amputation because the road is not a secure place (hirz).

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the Hanafi school: the theft of camels from a public road does not trigger the hadd punishment of amputation. The reason is that the road is not considered a secure place (hirz) for property, even if the owner is present. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the crucial role of hirz in determining whether the hadd of theft applies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is stealing camels from a public road. The ruling is that the thief is not amputated (la yuqta').
The ruling is a negative one: the punishment does not apply.
2
Identify the missing condition
The text states the reason: because the property is 'mal zahir' (apparent property) that is not in a hirz. The condition for amputation is that the property must be in a secure place (hirz).
Hirz is a key condition in Hanafi fiqh for the hadd of theft.
3
Analyze the concept of mal zahir
Mal zahir is property that is openly visible and not in a secured enclosure. The road is a public space, so even camels with loads are considered unprotected.
The presence of the owner on the camel does not create a hirz because the road itself is not private.
4
Apply the ruling to similar cases
Consider other items left on a public road: if they are not in a secure place, the hadd does not apply. For example, stealing a bicycle left on a sidewalk without a lock would not incur amputation.
This illustrates the general principle: any property in a public, unprotected space is not subject to hadd.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
This ruling is specific to the Hanafi school. Other schools may have different definitions of hirz or consider the owner's presence as protective. This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi position is based on the idea that the road is inherently not a hirz.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on hadd for landlord-tenant and in-law theft, identify the shubha that drops the penalty, and apply the distinction to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
Ownership and relationship create shubha that drops hadd, but the tenant's exclusive hirz overrides the landlord's ownership for Abu Hanifa.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on two edge cases of theft: when a landlord steals from his tenant's separate dwelling, and when a person steals from the house of in-laws. Both Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree on the general principle that shubha (doubt) negates hadd, but they disagree on what constitutes a sufficient shubha. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the concept of shubha in hudud cases and appreciate the nuance within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two distinct fact patterns: (1) landlord and tenant in separate rooms, with theft either way; (2) theft from the house of in-laws (as-har/akhtan). Note the consensus points within each.
The dispute is only about certain parties; consensus exists for tenant stealing landlord and for theft from daughter's/wife's house.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his proof
Abu Hanifa holds that cutting applies in both scenarios (except the consensus exceptions). His proof: the tenant has a stronger right to the hirz (can exclude the owner), so the landlord's theft is from the tenant's hirz, not his own. For in-laws, the relationship alone does not create a shubha sufficient to drop hadd.
Abu Hanifa focuses on the exclusive right of possession (hirz) over ownership.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their proof
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differentiate: the landlord's ownership of the house creates a shubha, so no cutting when he steals from the tenant. But the tenant stealing from the landlord has no shubha (consensus). For in-laws, they say cutting applies when the house belongs to the son-in-law, because the relationship is not strong enough; consensus when it belongs to the daughter or wife.
The Sahibayn emphasize property ownership as a source of shubha, even when the owner has rented out the space.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, a landlord would be amputated for stealing a tenant's item; under Sahibayn, he would not. Similarly, a son-in-law stealing from his wife's parents' house would be amputated under Sahibayn but not under Abu Hanifa.
The difference hinges on whether ownership or possession creates the shubha.
5
Identify the consensus exceptions
Both sides agree that if the house belongs to a blood relative (daughter or wife), no cutting applies due to strong shubha of relationship. Also, tenant stealing from landlord (in separate room) is cutting by consensus.
Consensus narrows the scope of the dispute.

Core Idea
Hadd requires both breaching the hirz and removal; handing over is debated as equivalent removal.

In this passage, we examine a classic intra-Hanafi dispute regarding theft by collaboration. The scenario: one thief breaks into a house and hands the loot to an accomplice outside. The matn states that neither is subject to hadd, but the commentary reveals a three-way disagreement among the founding imams. Understanding this dispute sharpens our appreciation for the precise conditions (shurut) required for the hadd penalty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and initial ruling
The thief breaks in (naqaba), takes money, and hands it to another outside. The matn says 'no cut on either.' Recognize that this is the basic fact pattern.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa says no cut because the insider did not actually remove the goods from the house, and the outsider did not breach the enclosure. Each element (breach and removal) must be present for hadd.
3
Contrast with the Sahibayn's reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that handing over is equivalent to removal: the outsider's hand becomes a substitute for the insider's. Thus, the insider is considered to have removed the goods himself.
This is an example of istihsan or qiyas in Hanafi usul.
4
Note the variant from Abu Yusuf
A separate narration from Abu Yusuf states that if the outsider inserts his hand and takes the goods from the insider's hand, then both thieves are cut. This adds a third possible ruling.
5
Extract the general principle
The text concludes with a principle: whoever steals but does not remove the goods from the house is not cut, regardless of accomplice actions. This underscores the centrality of removal (ikhrāj) in the Hanafi definition of theft for hadd.

No hadd cut if the taker owns the item or has possessory rights, even if in another's custody.

This lesson examines a specific scenario from the law of theft (sariqa) involving a pawned item. The matn states two cases: the pawner stealing his own pawn from the pledgee or trustee, and the pledgee stealing the pawn from the trustee. In both cases, the hadd of cutting is not applied. Understanding the reasoning—ownership versus possessory right—is key to applying the rule correctly.

Identify the actors and their legal relationship

The pawner is not cut because the item is his property (milk). The pledgee is not cut because his hand (possession) stands in place of the owner's hand—i.e., he has a possessory right that prevents the application of the hadd.

This contrast highlights the importance of the talil.

Ruling on theft by throwing goods into the street and retrieving with qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a thief who throws stolen goods into the street and then retrieves them, distinguishing between visible and non-visible throws, and apply the qiyas to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
Throwing stolen goods out of sight is like destroying them — no hadd, but liability remains.

This lesson examines a nuanced scenario within the law of theft (hadd al-sariqa). The classical text from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah addresses whether a thief is subject to the amputation penalty if he casts the stolen item into the street and later retrieves it. We will analyze the key condition of visibility and the legal reasoning (qiyas) that determines when the act is considered removal versus destruction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling in the Matn
The matn states: if he throws it into the street and then comes out and takes it, he is cut.
2
Distinguish the Two Sub-cases
The commentary differentiates between throwing where he can see it vs. where he cannot. Both are ruled differently based on visibility.
Visibility determines whether the goods remain under his control.
3
Trace the Legal Reasoning (Ta'lil)
When thrown out of sight, the goods are considered consumed/destroyed (mustahlak) because liability for compensation arises inside the house. Therefore, no hadd applies.
Compensation (daman) is established before removal, negating the hadd.
4
Analyze the Qiyas (Analogy)
The commentary draws an analogy with slaughtering a sheep inside the house: destruction before removal negates the hadd but imposes compensation. Similarly, throwing out of sight is treated as destruction.
The qiyas is based on the common illa of consumption/destruction inside the hirz.
5
Note the Additional Condition of Retrieval
If he comes out but does not retrieve the goods, he is not cut because it shows intention to waste (tadyi') rather than theft.
Without retrieval, the act is not considered removal (ikhraj).
