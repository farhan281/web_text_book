Conditional Emancipation Upon Payment
Ruling on conditional emancipation and compulsion of master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conditional emancipation upon payment and immediate emancipation upon acceptance, and apply the Hanafi rule that the condition must be fully met for emancipation to occur.

Core Idea
A slave does not become free until the condition of payment is actually fulfilled; mere acceptance is insufficient.

In this passage from the book of emancipation, we examine a specific scenario where a master conditions his slave's freedom on payment. The ruling clarifies when emancipation actually takes effect and how the master is compelled to accept payment. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying conditional contracts correctly in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two formulations
The passage contrasts two statements: 'If you pay me one thousand, you are free' and 'You are free for one thousand.' In the first, emancipation occurs upon payment; in the second, it occurs upon acceptance even before payment.
The wording determines the timing of emancipation.
2
Apply the qiyas (analogy)
The conditional emancipation is analogized to 'If you enter the house, you are free' — the condition must occur before freedom. This establishes that a future condition delays the effect.
Qiyas jaliy: the illa is the conditionality of the event.
3
Understand the compulsion (ijbar)
When the slave brings the money, the judge compels the master to accept it. The master is considered to have taken possession through release (takhliyah), even without physical acceptance.
This ensures the condition can be fulfilled despite the master's reluctance.
4
Recognize the slave's status before fulfillment
Until the condition is met (acceptance in the second formulation, payment in the first), the slave remains a full slave and can be sold. If the master dies before the condition, the offer becomes void.
Contrast with mukatib (slave under contract of manumission) where forgiveness frees the slave.

Conditional Emancipation: Payment Source and Temporal Scope
Ruling on slave paying from prior earnings and temporal scope of 'in' vs 'idha'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's emancipation when payment is made from pre-existing wealth and apply the linguistic distinction between 'in' and 'idha' to determine the time frame for payment.

Core Idea
The condition is payment itself, not its source; 'in' restricts to the session, 'idha' allows any time.

Imagine a master tells his slave, 'If you pay me $1000, you are free.' The slave pays with money he had earned earlier. Does he become free? The Hanafi school says yes—but the master can reclaim the equivalent amount. Now consider the difference between 'if you pay' (إن أديت) and 'when you pay' (إذا أديت). The first limits payment to the current session; the second allows payment at any future time. This lesson unpacks both rulings, combining a substantive condition with a linguistic nuance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the legal issue
The act is conditional emancipation (ta'liq al-'itaq) where the condition is payment. The issue here is whether the slave can use money earned before the master's statement, and what temporal restrictions apply to the condition.
This is a classic Hanafi ruling on conditional contracts and ownership.
2
Analyze the payment-from-prior-earnings scenario
The slave pays from wealth earned before the statement. The Hanafi position: emancipation occurs because the condition (payment) is met. However, since that wealth belongs to the master (slave's earnings are master's property), the master has the right to reclaim the equivalent amount from the slave.
The ruling is based on the principle that the condition is the act of payment, not the ownership of the funds.
3
Understand the linguistic distinction between 'in' and 'idha'
When the master says 'in adayta' (if you pay), the particle 'in' is conditional and implies a choice for the slave. Therefore, payment must occur in the same session (al-majlis) as the statement. When he says 'idha adayta' (when you pay), 'idha' is temporal, like 'mata' (when), so payment is not restricted to the session.
This distinction is based on Arabic linguistics and affects the validity of late payment.
4
Apply the distinction to determine payment timing
If the master uses 'in', the slave must pay in that session; if he delays, the condition fails and emancipation does not occur. If 'idha' is used, the slave can pay at any future time, and emancipation occurs upon payment.
This has practical implications for how conditional statements are drafted.
5
Synthesize the complete ruling
Combine both parts: emancipation is valid upon payment regardless of the source of funds, but the master recoups the equivalent value. Timing depends on the particle used.
Both parts are necessary to fully understand the ruling.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on 'You are Free and You Owe 1000'
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two companions on emancipation with debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding emancipation with a debt condition and state the mufta bihi position according to Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Emancipation with an unaccepted debt: Abu Hanifa frees the slave; the Sahibayn require acceptance.

This passage from al-Yanabi' presents an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf concerning the master's statement 'You are free and you owe 1000.' While the first scenario—'Pay me 1000 and you are free'—is agreed upon, the second scenario sparks a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Hanafi school treats conditional emancipations and the role of acceptance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
Focus on the master's statement 'You are free and you owe 1000'. Distinguish it from the first scenario ('Pay me 1000 and you are free') which is agreed upon.
The first scenario results in immediate freedom regardless of payment.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the slave becomes free immediately and owes nothing, whether he accepts or not. The debt is not binding.
The text states: 'عتق في الحال، ولم يلزمه شيء، قبل، أو لم يقبل، عند أبي حنيفة'.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the slave must accept the condition. If he accepts, he becomes free and the debt is binding; if he does not accept, he remains a slave.
The text says: 'وقال أبو يوسف ومحمد: إن قبل عتق، ولزمه الألف، وإن لم يقبل: لا يعتق'.
4
Compare the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is stark: under Abu Hanifa, the slave is free with no financial obligation; under Sahibayn, the slave's status depends on acceptance.
This ikhtilaf has real-world consequences for the slave's freedom and liability.
5
Apply to the service condition
The third scenario ('You are free on condition that you serve me for four years') is analogous to the Sahibayn's conditional approach: freedom and obligation only upon acceptance.
The text gives the ruling: upon acceptance, the slave is free and must serve four years.

Slave's Payment from Earnings Prior to Emancipation
Ruling on slave's own earnings used for emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's payment from pre-earnings for emancipation and explain the master's right to reclaim the equivalent using qiyas.

Core Idea
A slave's earnings belong to his master, so using them to buy freedom is valid but the master can reclaim the amount.

This passage from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses a specific scenario: a slave pays a sum of money from his own earnings to fulfill a condition for emancipation, but the money was earned before the master's statement. The Hanafi ruling cleverly balances the validity of the emancipation with the master's property rights, using an analogy to usurped property. As we work through this, you will see how Hanafi jurists apply qiyas to resolve such tensions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a slave paying money from his pre-earnings as a condition for emancipation. The ruling: emancipation is valid (sahih), and the master can claim back the equivalent amount.
The validity hinges on the fulfillment of the condition (payment).
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is qiyas: the slave's payment is analogized to paying with usurped property (mal maghsud). The slave's earnings belong to the master, so paying with them is like paying with something he does not own.
The qiyas is based on the shared 'illa (effective cause) of using another's property without ownership.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (legal reasoning)
In the case of usurped property, the payment is valid but the owner can reclaim. Similarly, here the payment fulfills the condition, but the master can recover the amount because the money was his property.
The analogy demonstrates how Hanafi fiqh balances multiple legal interests.
4
Note the ikhtilaf (scholarly difference)
The Shafi'i and Maliki schools hold that a slave can own property, so his earnings are his own. Therefore, the master cannot reclaim the payment.
This difference stems from varying views on a slave's legal capacity to own.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is relied upon (mufta bihi) because it is based on the principle that a slave's acquisitions belong to the master, as established in the Zahir al-Riwayah.
This ruling protects the master's property rights while honoring the emancipation agreement.

Timing of Payment: 'In Adayta' vs 'Idha Adayta'
Explaining the effect of linguistic phrasing on the time limit for payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two Arabic conditional particles 'in' and 'idha' when used in emancipation statements and apply the Hanafi ruling on the time limit for payment.

Core Idea
The particle 'in' restricts payment to the session; 'idha' allows payment at any future time.

In Hanafi fiqh, the phrasing of a conditional emancipation matters. The difference between 'in adayta' (if you pay) and 'idha adayta' (when you pay) determines whether the slave must pay in the same session or can pay at any later time. This lesson from al-Hidayah explains the linguistic basis for this distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two phrases and their rulings
Find the two conditional expressions in the text: 'in adayta' (if you pay) and 'idha adayta' (when you pay). Note that each has a distinct ruling regarding the timing of payment.
The ruling for 'in' is that payment must be in the same session; for 'idha' it is not confined to the session.
2
Trace the reasoning for 'in adayta'
Understand why 'in' restricts payment: it is a choice (takhayyur) for the slave, like saying 'You are free if you wish'. A choice must be exercised immediately in the same session.
The analogy is to a direct offer of emancipation conditional on the slave's wish.
3
Trace the reasoning for 'idha adayta'
Understand why 'idha' does not restrict: it is used for a time condition, similar to 'mata' (whenever). Payment can be made at any future time as long as the condition is fulfilled.
The particle 'idha' indicates a temporal condition, not an immediate choice.
4
Analyze the linguistic analogy
Note that the reasoning relies on common Arabic usage. 'In' introduces a conditional that implies a choice, while 'idha' introduces a time-bound condition.
This distinction is specific to Hanafi fiqh; other schools may treat them equally.
5
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Given a master's statement, determine the deadline for payment. For example: if the master says 'You are free if you pay 100', the slave must pay in the same session. If he says 'You are free when you pay 100', the slave may pay later.
Ensure you identify the correct particle and apply the corresponding ruling.

The particle 'idha' indicates a time condition, similar to 'mata' (whenever). This is a linguistic distinction that affects the legal ruling.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayirah examines a specific type of emancipation: the master says 'You are free on condition you serve me for four years.' The key dispute arises when the master dies before the service is completed: does the slave pay the full slave value or only the value of the remaining service? The Hanafi school has an intra-madhhab disagreement between the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Muhammad's lone opinion.

Guided Path
1
Understand the conditional emancipation contract
Identify that the master's statement creates a valid emancipation immediately upon acceptance, with a binding obligation to serve. The service is a condition (shart) but the emancipation is not suspended; it takes effect at once.

The master dies before the full service term is completed. The service obligation is personal to the master and therefore becomes void. The question is: what must the slave pay to compensate for the benefit of freedom he received?

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the emancipation shares when a master makes multiple overlapping statements of emancipation. The scenario: a man has three slaves. Two enter, he says 'one of you two is free.' Then the one of those two who did not leave (the 'thabit') remains, and the third (the 'dakhil') enters. He then says 'one of you two is free' referring to the thabit and the dakhil. During his health (fi sihhatih), he is told to clarify who he meant. If he dies without clarifying, the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) rule: the thabit gets three-quarters freedom, and each of the other two (the one who left and the dakhil) gets half. Muhammad differs regarding the dakhil, giving him only a quarter. The commentary explains the reasoning. The two argue that the first statement entails a half share for the thabit and the one who left (the 'kharij', the other of the first two who exited). The second statement is between the thabit and the dakhil, so it also entails half for each in principle. However, the thabit's first half is already overlapped; the second statement's half is spread over his half, so only the quarter affecting his free portion remains, giving him three-quarters total. The dakhil gets half from the second statement because he had no prior claim. Muhammad counters that the second statement's validity depends on whether the first statement intended the kharij (valid) or the thabit (invalid). Since it alternates between valid and invalid, it yields only half of a full emancipation between the thabit and dakhil. From that half, the thabit already took a quarter (by consensus of the two? Actually the two also give him a quarter from the second statement as part of his three-quarters; Muhammad agrees the thabit gets a quarter from the second, so the dakhil's share from that half is the other quarter). Thus Muhammad gives the dakhil a quarter, which is half of the half from the second statement. The passage also briefly discusses the case if the statements were made during illness (marad) and he has no other property: the shares are calculated from one-third of the estate, with the emancipation portions (seven according to the two) being distributed.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Emancipation Shares in Marad al-Mawt
Intra-Hanafi dispute on shares of emancipation among three slaves in terminal illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between the positions of Muhammad and the Sahibayn regarding the division of emancipation shares among three slaves in terminal illness, and calculate the shares according to each view.

Core Idea
When a declaration wavers between validity and invalidity, its effect is halved – but the Sahibayn see no uncertainty in a simple circulation between two slaves.

This passage presents a refined intra-Hanafi dispute over how to allocate freedom shares when a terminally ill master makes two declarations regarding three slaves: the confirmed (thabit), the included (dakhil), and the excluded (kharj). The dispute turns on whether the second declaration is genuinely uncertain or merely circulates between two slaves, leading to different arithmetical outcomes when executed from the one-third bequest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three slaves and their roles in the two declarations
Al-thabit is confirmed half-free from the first declaration. The second declaration involves al-thabit and al-dakhil (and possibly al-kharj as well, but the dispute focuses on the second declaration's effect on thabit and dakhil). Understand that the first declaration freed half of thabit.
2
Apply the Sahibayn's method: equal division of the second declaration
The Sahibayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) treat the second declaration as circulating between thabit and dakhil, so each gets half of its value. However, because thabit already has half from the first, his additional share from the second is adjusted to avoid exceeding the total freedom allowed from the one-third estate.
The final shares under Sahibayn: thabit 3/7, dakhil 2/7, kharj 2/7 of a slave's freedom.
3
Apply Muhammad's method: uncertainty halves the declaration
Muhammad argues that the second declaration's validity depends on the first declaration's intent. If the first intended the excluded (kharj), the second is valid; if it intended the confirmed (thabit), the second is invalid. This uncertainty means the second declaration frees only half a slave, which is then divided equally between thabit and dakhil.
Under Muhammad: thabit gets 1/2, dakhil gets 1/6, kharj gets 1/3 (after reduction to one-third of estate).
4
Compute the final shares as a bequest from one-third
Both methods require reducing the freedom shares to the one-third limit of the estate. For the Sahibayn, each slave's value is represented as a fraction of the total 21 parts (3 for thabit, 2 for dakhil, 2 for kharj). For Muhammad, each slave's value is represented as a fraction of 18 parts (3 for thabit, 1 for dakhil, 2 for kharj). The slaves must work to pay the heirs for the unfreed portions.
The arithmetic differences are a direct consequence of the underlying legal reasoning.

Tadbir—declaring a slave free after the master's death—is a valid transaction in Hanafi fiqh. The jurists classify it into two types based on the presence or absence of an additional condition. Conditional tadbir adds a condition that may or may not occur, such as 'if I die from this illness' or 'if I drown'.

Effect of Master's Death on Mudabbar: Freedom, Debt, and Joint Tadbir
Explains three outcomes upon master's death: freedom from 1/3, earning if no assets, debt priority, plus joint tadbir dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three legal outcomes of tadbir upon the master's death and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the divisibility of joint tadbir.

Core Idea
Upon death, the mudabbar is freed from one-third; debt takes priority; joint tadbir is indivisible per the relied-upon view.

The tadbir contract is like a will: it is a donation effective at death. When the master dies, the mudabbar's freedom is limited to one-third of the estate. If the estate is insufficient, the slave must earn his freedom by paying the remaining value. If the master had debts, these take precedence, and the mudabbar must work to pay off the full value to creditors. Additionally, the Hanafi school debated whether tadbir over a jointly owned slave can be divided according to ownership shares. This lesson unpacks these rules and their practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that tadbir is a form of will (waṣiyya) and that upon the master's death, the mudabbar is freed from one-third of the estate, provided the estate is sufficient to cover that third.
The ruling is not a single hukm but a set of contingent outcomes.
2
Understand the debt priority rule
If the master has debts, debt payment takes precedence over the bequest of freedom. Since the tadbir cannot be revoked, the mudabbar must work (yas'ā) to earn his full value to pay off creditors.
This is because the mudabbar's freedom is conditional on the estate being able to cover both debt and the bequest share.
3
Trace the legal reasoning (dalīl)
The author states that tadbir is like a will because it is a donation annexed to death. Therefore, the one-third limit applies. The priority of debt is based on the principle that debt precedes bequests.
The proof is analogical (qiyās) to the rules of bequests.
4
Analyze the intra-madhhab dispute on joint tadbir
Abū Ḥanīfa analogized tadbir to manumission, allowing it to be divisible by ownership share. The two disciples (Ṣāḥibayn) rejected this analogy, holding that tadbir is a single contingent act that cannot be split. The relied-upon position (muftā bihī) is that of the Ṣāḥibayn.
The practical difference: if the tadbir is divisible, a partial owner can validly declare tadbir for his share; if not, the tadbir is invalid unless all owners agree.

Basic Ruling of Tadbir: Freedom from One-Third of Estate
States the basic ruling on tadbir upon master's death and when debts exist.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the basic ruling of tadbir upon the master's death, identify the condition regarding the one-third of the estate, and explain the slave's obligation to work (sa'y) when the value exceeds the bequest portion or when debts exist.

Core Idea
A mudabbar slave is freed from one-third of the master's estate upon death; if he exceeds that portion, he must work to earn the remainder.

This lesson covers the foundational ruling of tadbir — a master's declaration that his slave will be free upon his death. The passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains that the mudabbar becomes free from one-third of the master's wealth, which is the maximum allowed for bequests. If the slave's value exceeds one-third, or if there are debts, the slave must work to pay the balance. Understanding this ruling is essential for grasping how tadbir interacts with inheritance and debt obligations in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Legal Category
Recognize that tadbir is a unilateral declaration making a slave free upon the master's death; legally it is treated as a type of will (wasiyyah). Therefore, it is subject to the one-third rule for bequests.
Tadbir is not a sale or contract of kitabah.
2
State the Primary Ruling Upon Master's Death
The mudabbar becomes free from one-third of the master's wealth. This means freedom is granted from that portion of the estate, as if a bequest for the slave's value.
The master's estate is valued first; the slave's value is taken from one-third.
3
Analyze the Condition of the One-Third
If the slave's value is within the one-third (i.e., ≤ 1/3 of total estate), he is fully freed without any obligation. If it exceeds one-third, he must work (sa'a) to pay the excess over the one-third to the heirs.
The excess is calculated as the slave's full value minus the one-third portion.
4
Consider the Debt Priority
If the master has debts that exhaust the estate or exceed it, creditors have priority. The slave must work for his full value to pay off the debts, and he only becomes free after the debt is cleared.
Debts take precedence over bequests, including tadbir, in Hanafi law.
5
Understand the Legal Effect of Sa'y
Sa'y means the slave is ordered to work and earn money to pay the required amount. This is a judicial order, not a contract; the slave is effectively working for his own freedom.
The slave's status remains mudabbar until the payment is made; after payment, he becomes free.

Tadbir by One Co-Owner: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explaining co-owner options and Sahibayn's opposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's view on the options of the other co-owner when tadbir is made by one, and contrast it with the Sahibayn's view on indivisibility of tadbir, explaining the practical difference.

Core Idea
When one co-owner makes tadbir, Abu Hanifa gives the partner options; the Sahibayn hold tadbir is indivisible.

In this lesson, we examine a key intra-Hanafi dispute regarding tadbir (a slave's freedom upon the master's death) when the slave is co-owned. Abu Hanifa maintains that tadbir only affects the declarant's share, leaving the partner with several choices. His two distinguished disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, argue that tadbir cannot be divided, so the entire slave becomes mudabbar. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies how the Hanafi school deals with partial ownership in contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal issue
The issue is whether tadbir by one co-owner affects only his share or the entire slave. This determines the rights and obligations of the partner.
Tadbir is a will-like contract, and the dispute hinges on whether it is divisible.
2
Map Abu Hanifa's options
If the mudabbar is wealthy, the partner has five options: (1) free his share, (2) also make tadbir, (3) hold the mudabbar liable for half the value, (4) make the slave work (istis'a), or (5) leave as is. If insolvent, option (3) drops, leaving four options.
The options protect the partner's property rights.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's counterargument
The Sahibayn argue that tadbir is indivisible (لا يتبعض), so the entire slave becomes mudabbar. Consequently, the mudabbar is liable for the partner's share regardless of his wealth, because the transfer of ownership creates a liability that does not depend on financial capacity.
Their dalil is based on the nature of tadbir as a single contract.
4
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: In Abu Hanifa's view, the partner can choose to receive payment or not; in the Sahibayn's view, the mudabbar must compensate the partner. Also, the child of a mudabbarah is mudabbar, a point of agreement.
This dispute affects how the partner's share is handled upon the death of the mudabbar.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, later purchase of the mother creates umm walad status if lineage is established; Shafi'i disagrees because conception was during another's ownership.

This passage extends the umm walad ruling to a scenario where the father is not the owner at conception but later acquires the mother. This case sparks a key Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute. The author presents the Hanafi position and the Shafi'i counter, showing how different usul principles lead to different outcomes. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the Hanafi emphasis on lineage and current ownership.

Guided Path
1
State the factual scenario
A man has intercourse with a slave woman owned by another person, through marriage or ambiguous intercourse (shubha). The woman gives birth to his child, and the lineage is established. Later, the man purchases this slave woman.
The child's lineage is already established to the father before purchase.
2
Identify the Hanafi ruling and its basis
According to the Hanafi school, upon purchase, the woman becomes his umm walad from the moment he owns her. The basis is that the status is tied to lineage (nasab) and current ownership. Since the child's lineage is affirmed to the buyer, and he now owns the mother, the status is established.
The author says: 'thumma malakaha fahiya ummu walad lahu min hin malakaha.'
3
Present the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i holds that she does not become an umm walad. The reasoning: at the time of conception, the woman was not the man's property. The umm walad status is created at the moment of birth, and at that moment the mother was owned by another. Later purchase does not retroactively create the status.
The text quotes: 'wa inda al-Shafi'i: idha istawladaha fi milk ghayrihi, thumma malakaha, lam tasir umm walad lahu.'
4

The Hanafi response implicit in the text: the status is based on the current ownership and the established lineage. The timing of conception is irrelevant because the status is not created at birth but at the moment of ownership. The principle is: 'istilad is a branch of lineage' – once lineage is fixed and ownership is acquired, the branch is established.
This is derived from the earlier statement: 'al-istiladu talabu al-waladi, wa huwa far'un min al-nasabi.'
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
Under Hanafi, she is freed upon his death from the one-third (assuming he has no other heirs? Actually from his estate). Under Shafi'i, she remains a slave even after his death, because the status never attached. This difference affects her freedom and the child's status.
The child is free in both schools because the father acknowledges it, but the mother's status differs.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on paternity denial conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on denying paternity of an umm walad's child and apply the conditions that change the ruling.

Core Idea
Paternity of umm walad's child is presumptive unless 'azl or lack of seclusion creates doubt – then Abu Hanifa permits denial.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra expands on the matn's statement that paternity is established only by acknowledgment. It presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: when the master's actions introduce doubt, the imams differ on whether denial is permissible, recommended, or should lead to emancipation. Understanding these positions requires careful attention to the conditions of 'azl (coitus interruptus) and tahseen (seclusion).

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The passage first states the default: if the master had intercourse without 'azl and secluded the umm walad, he must acknowledge the child – it is forbidden to deny. This is wajib.
This is the baseline: certainty of paternity.
2
Recognize the doubt-creating conditions
If the master practiced 'azl or did not seclude her, doubt arises: the child could be from him or from another man. This doubt is the key that changes the ruling.
'Azl and lack of tahseen are separate conditions; either one alone is sufficient to create doubt.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that with doubt, it is permissible (ja'iz) for the master to deny paternity. He is not obligated to acknowledge based on uncertainty.
This is because the obligation to acknowledge is based on preponderance (ghalabat al-zann); doubt removes that.
4
State the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Abu Yusuf says it is recommended (mustahabb) to claim the child. Muhammad says it is recommended to free the child (and upon the master's death, also the mother) to avoid enslaving a potential free person.
Both Sahibayn lean towards preserving the child's freedom, albeit through different means.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa allows denial without proof; Abu Yusuf encourages acknowledgment; Muhammad encourages freeing the child (and umm walad). In fatwa, Abu Hanifa's view is generally relied upon.
This affects whether the child inherits, is enslaved, etc.

Core Idea
Paternity of a child from a co-owned slave woman is indivisible; the entire child belongs to both if both claim.

We will trace the arguments of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and determine the relied-upon position.

Note that the slave woman is co-owned, she gives birth, and one partner claims the child. The text distinguishes between one claimant and both claiming. Also, there is a preliminary discussion on the grandfather's claim if the father is dead or disbelieving.

When she bears a child and the master claims paternity, according to the standard Hanafi view (Imam Muhammad?), the claim requires the mukatib's confirmation to establish lineage. Abu Yusuf differs, holding that no confirmation is needed because the master's right over the mukatib's slave woman is stronger than over his son's slave woman (where lineage is established without confirmation in some cases). This shows a nuanced intra-Hanafi difference on the strength of rights.

Key Terms
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose master has declared that he will be free upon the master's death.
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave with a contract of manumission.
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who has borne a child to her master, thereby becoming entitled to freedom upon the master's death.

Inheritance between an Umm Walad, Her Master, and Their Child
Inheritance rule for child of umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the inheritance shares between the child of an umm walad and both parents according to Hanafi law.

Core Idea
The child of an umm walad inherits fully from both parents; each parent inherits from the child as a single parent.

The Hanafi school specifies unique inheritance rules for a child born to an umm walad (a slave woman who has borne her master's child). Unlike a regular child, this child inherits a full son's share from both the mother and the father (the master), and the parents each inherit from the child as one father. This rule ensures complete mutual inheritance despite the mother's slave status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties involved
The parties are the child (the son), the umm walad (mother), and the master (father). Note that the mother is a slave but has special status.
2
State the child's inheritance from each parent
The child inherits from each parent a full son's share. This means the child takes the same portion as a free-born son would from their free parent.
The commentator explains: 'because he acknowledges his entire inheritance' – the child has full patrimony rights.
3
State the parents' inheritance from the child
Each parent (the umm walad and the master) inherits from the child as a single father – meaning they take the share of one parent (e.g., one-sixth if other heirs exist, or the residue if none).
The commentator says: 'because they are equal in lineage' – both are the child's biological parents.
4
Understand the rationale
The child is free and acknowledged by the master, so full inheritance applies. The mother inherits because of her maternal bond, but her slave status does not prevent inheritance from her free child.
This contrasts with a regular slave mother who would not inherit from a free child.

Commentary
This passage presents two main rulings from the Hanafi school. First, regarding inheritance: when a child is born to an umm walad (a slave woman who has borne her master's child), the child inherits from both the mother (the umm walad) and the father (the master) as a full son, meaning they receive the full share of a son, not half. Conversely, the mother and father each inherit from the child as a single parent (i.e., one-sixth if there are other heirs, or the whole in some cases). The commentator explains that the child inherits fully because the master has acknowledged all of the child's inheritance rights, and the parents inherit equally because they are equal in lineage to the child. Second, the passage discusses the master's acknowledgment of a female slave as his umm walad. If the master acknowledges in good health that a slave woman is his umm walad, it is valid and she becomes an umm walad immediately, regardless of whether she has a child. This acknowledgment is binding. If the acknowledgment is made during a terminal illness (marad al-mawt), and she has a child, the same ruling applies. If she has no child, she still becomes an umm walad, but her freedom (which occurs upon the master's death) is limited to one-third of his estate, similar to a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death). This ensures that the acknowledgment does not harm the rights of legal heirs.

Key Terms
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who has borne a child to her master, and the master has acknowledged the child's paternity. She gains certain rights, including eventual freedom upon the master's death.
