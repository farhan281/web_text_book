Kaffarah and Diyat for the Fetus
Rulings on diyat for fetus and absence of kaffarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that kaffarah is not obligatory for killing a fetus, and differentiate the diyat owed for the fetus of a free woman versus that of a slave woman.

Core Idea
No expiation for a fetus because it is an incomplete soul; diyat varies by status of the mother.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira deals with two interconnected rulings: the absence of kaffarah for causing a miscarriage, and the amount of diyat due for the fetus depending on whether the mother is free or enslaved. The author uses qiyas to show that kaffarah is only for complete souls, and provides detailed scenarios from al-Hidayah and al-Karkhi to explain the valuation of the fetal diyat in cases of slavery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main ruling
The passage opens with 'ولا كفارة في الجنين' — there is no expiation for the fetus. Understand what kaffarah is and why it is omitted here.
Kaffarah is an expiatory act required for certain sins, like accidental killing of a believing person.
2
Analyze the proof for the ruling
The author provides two reasons: (1) kaffarah is established for complete souls (qiyas), and (2) the life of a fetus is uncertain, so the requirement of killing is not met.
The proof is based on qiyas: the fetus is like an incomplete soul, so the ruling is analogized.
3
Distinguish types of slave women
The passage differentiates between the fetus of an umm walad (who has borne a child to her master) and other slave women. For umm walad, the diyat is the same as for a free woman. For ordinary slaves, the diyat is the value of the fetus as property.
Umm walad has a special status closer to free status after her master's death.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider the scenario from al-Hidayah: a slave woman is struck, then the master frees the fetus, which is born alive and dies. The liable party pays the value alive, not full diyat, because the killing occurred during slavery.
The timing of the blow matters; the value is assessed at the time of the blow.
5
Note the absence of qisas and kaffarah
The passage ends by noting that for a slave's fetus, there is no retaliation (qisas) and no kaffarah, because the compensation is purely financial.
This reinforces the property nature of slaves in Hanafi law.

Kaffarah for Shibh al-Amd and Khata' and the Slave Conditions
States the ruling on kaffarah for semi-intentional and accidental killing and the Shafi'i disagreement on fetal kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the three options for kaffarah in shibh al-amd and khata', identify which slaves are acceptable, and explain why the Hanafi school does not require kaffarah for killing a fetus, contrasting with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for semi-intentional and accidental killing requires freeing a believing slave or fasting two consecutive months — feeding is not accepted.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on expiation (kaffarah) for non-intentional homicide. We will study the prescribed forms: freeing a believing slave, or if unable, fasting two consecutive months. The class also clarifies which categories of slaves are valid for manumission, and addresses the Shafi'i disagreement on kaffarah for fetal death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The passage concerns shibh al-amd (semi-intentional) and khata' (accidental) killing. The hukm is wajib kaffarah. The three forms are listed: freeing a believing slave, fasting two consecutive months, and note that feeding is explicitly excluded.
Kaffarah for killing is distinct from kaffarah for oaths or fasting.
2
Examine the conditions for the slave
Only a fully owned believing slave suffices. Mudabbar (promised freedom upon master's death) and umm walad (mother of master's child) are not valid because their enslavement is incomplete. A mukatab (slave with contract of manumission) is valid only if he has paid nothing; partial payment makes his freedom partially realized, thus invalid. The fetus in the womb is also invalid because it cannot see — akin to a blind person.
The rationale: the slave must be fully owned and a complete entity.
3
Analyze the dalil for excluding feeding
The text states that Allah only mentioned freeing and fasting in the verse on kaffarah for killing, and feeding is not mentioned. This is a direct dalil (nass) that restricts the options to those two; Qiyas cannot add to the nass.
This is a strict textualist position typical of Hanafi usul.
4
Address the ikhtilaf with al-Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that kaffarah is obligatory for killing a fetus. The Hanafi response: the fetus is not a complete soul; its life is uncertain, and its diyah is deficient (ghurrah). Kaffarah is only for complete souls.
The dispute is based on differing views on the status of the fetus.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The Hanafi position as stated in the text is the mufta bihi. The ruling on slave types and the exclusion of feeding are standard in the school.
This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.

The Hanafi Ruling on Kaffarah for Quasi-Intentional and Accidental Killing
Explains the required expiation for two types of homicide

Core Idea
The Quran mentions only freeing a slave or fasting—feeding is not an option in kaffarah for killing.

Expiation (kaffarah) is a divine remedy for unintended killing, but not all forms of expiation are equal. This passage clarifies that for quasi-intentional and accidental homicide, the Hanafi school adheres strictly to the Quranic text, permitting only the freeing of a believing slave or, if impossible, fasting two consecutive months. Feeding, common in other expiations, is explicitly excluded. We will dissect the ruling, the conditions for an acceptable slave, and the textual proof that guides the Hanafi position.

Qasamah: Oath-Taking in Unknown Homicide
Defines the procedure of qasamah and distinguishes Hanafi from Shafi'i views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for qasamah and explain the difference between Hanafi and Shafi'i positions.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the accused locality swears innocence; the wali does not swear.

When a dead body is found in a neighbourhood and no one knows who killed him, Islamic law provides a special procedure called qasamah to resolve liability. The Hanafi school places the oath on the community, not the plaintiff. This lesson walks through that procedure and contrasts it with Imam al-Shafi'i's approach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The passage begins with the condition: a corpse found in a locality (mahallah) and the killer unknown. This triggers the qasamah procedure.
No lawth (suspicion) is required in the Hanafi school.
2
Determine the Oath-Takers (Hanafi)
Fifty men from that locality are selected by the wali (guardian of the deceased). They swear by Allah that they did not kill the victim and do not know a killer.
The text says 'استحلف خمسون رجلاً منهم يتخيَّرهم الولي'.
3
Understand the Oath Wording
Although the text says 'we did not kill', each individual swears 'I did not kill' because it is possible he committed the murder personally.
Commentary clarifies: 'وإنما يحلف كل واحد منهم : بالله ما قتلت'.
4
Compare with Shafi'i View
Al-Shafi'i requires lawth (suspicion) before qasamah, and then the wali (not the locality) swears fifty oaths, resulting in the defendant paying diya.
Lawth includes signs, enmity, or testimony.
5
Note the Practical Consequence
Under Hanafi, the locality is exonerated by the oath; under Shafi'i, the defendant pays blood money if the wali swears.
This difference affects who bears the cost.

This distinction ensures each person takes responsibility only for himself.

Qasamah: The Oath that Imposes Liability – Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Explaining the ruling of qasamah and the Hanafi proof against Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on qasamah, explain the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the Hanafi proof based on the hadith.

Core Idea
In qasamah, the oath does not exonerate—it imposes collective blood money on the locality.

When a body is found with an unknown killer, Islamic law provides a unique procedure: fifty men from the locality swear they are innocent. But does this oath free them from liability, or confirm it? This lesson explores the Hanafi position and its proof against the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling from al-Quduri: When a slain person is found in a locality and the killer is unknown, fifty men chosen by the wali must swear that they did not kill nor know the killer. After the oath, judgment is given for blood money against the locality.
The oath is wajib on the fifty men, not on the wali.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Shafi'i requires lawth (suspicion) before qasamah can be initiated, and then the wali takes the oath, not the fifty men. After the oath, blood money is imposed on the accused (if there is a specific accused) or not at all if no accused is identified.
The key difference: Shafi'i sees the oath as exonerating, Hanafi sees it as imposing liability.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Shafi'i argues that oaths in Sharia are primarily exonerating for the defendant (e.g., in claims of debt, the defendant swears and is freed). Therefore, the oath in qasamah should also exonerate, and no blood money should be due after swearing.
This is a general usul principle: al-yamin tuqdi bi'l-bara'ah.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafi responds with a specific hadith: a man complained to the Prophet about his brother killed between two villages, and the Prophet ordered fifty men to swear. The established practice (understood by the Hanafi school) is that blood money was imposed after this oath. This specific evidence overrides the general principle.
The hadith is cited as a proof that qasamah is an exception where the oath confirms liability.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: In Hanafi fiqh, after the fifty men swear, the locality must pay blood money. In Shafi'i fiqh, if the oath is taken, no blood money is due and the case is closed.
This difference can have major financial implications for the community.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Liability for qasamah falls on those with permanent authority—owners, especially original settlers—not on temporary residents or new buyers.

In the previous lesson, we introduced qasamah as a collective liability when a corpse is found. Now we delve into a finer point: who exactly among the inhabitants bears that liability? For a stick, the justification is more limited: it is permitted only when the victim cannot obtain help—at night in the city (where people are asleep) or by day on a road outside the city (where no passersby can aid). In such cases, the killing is considered an act of lawful defense, and no blood money or punishment is incurred. The reasoning is that the victim is forced to kill to protect his own life.

Detailed Hanafi ruling on diyah installment system

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the payment of diyah by the 'aqilah in installments over three years and apply the rules for different fractions and special cases.

Core Idea
Diyah for accidental killing is paid by the 'aqilah in installments over one, two, or three years depending on the amount.

This passage from Kitab al-Ma'aqil explains the method of collecting diyah from the 'aqilah. The key is that the payment period is determined by the fraction of full diyah owed, and the three-year period begins from the judgment, not the killing. We will examine the rules for various amounts and the cap for those not in the diwan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation and liable party
The obligation is diyah (blood money) for accidental killing (qatl khata'). The liable party is the 'aqilah, the group of male agnates or tribe who pay on behalf of the killer.
This is a collective obligation on the 'aqilah, not the killer individually.
2
Determine the fraction of diyah owed
The full diyah is 100 camels or its equivalent. Determine what fraction is owed: full, half/two-thirds, one-third or less, or other fractions.
Half and two-thirds are treated similarly (two years).
3
Apply the corresponding installment period
If full diyah, payment is over three years; if half or two-thirds, over two years; if one-third or less, over one year. The three-year period starts from the date of judgment, not the killing.
The period is calculated from judgment to ensure stipends are future.
4
Handle fractional parts by analogy
For parts like a fingertip, the rule for the whole (half diyah of hand) applies by analogy: two years, even though the amount is small.
The principle: the part follows the ruling of the whole in terms of time.
5
Apply the special rules for non-diwan members
For those not registered in the military diwan, the 'aqilah is their tribe, and the payment is spread over three years with a per-person cap of 4 dirhams total, i.e., 1 dirham and 2 danqs per year.
The cap is on total diyah, not per year; it is apportioned over three years.

Core Idea
The aqilah is not liable for minor damages (below threshold), crimes of slaves, and confessions not confirmed by the tribe.

The Hanafi school exempts the aqilah from paying blood money in specific situations to prevent injustice. Small compensations (below half a tenth of full diyah) are left to the offender. A slave's limited legal capacity means his crimes do not burden his tribe. And a confession not confirmed by the aqilah is treated as a personal admission, not a basis for collective responsibility.

From the text: (1) smaller than half a tenth of diyah, (2) crime of a slave, (3) confession unless confirmed.

Understand the threshold for minor injuries
Half a tenth (1/20) of the full diyah is the boundary. Below that, the offender pays alone.
The text says 'half a tenth' (nisf 'ushr).

A slave is not a fully liable person; his crimes are on himself or his owner, not on the aqilah.
This applies regardless of whether the victim is free or slave.

An unconfirmed confession does not bind the aqilah because it might be false. The aqilah must confirm (taṣdīq) to accept liability.
This is a new benefit mentioned here compared to the earlier book.

Boundaries of 'Aqila Liability in Accidental Killing
Author enumerates exceptions to 'aqila liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for 'aqila liability and identify the exceptions that place the diyah on the perpetrator's own property.

Core Idea
The 'aqila only pays for large accidental harms – intentional killing, slave crimes, unconfirmed confessions, and settlements are excluded.

The 'aqila (paternal relatives collectively bearing blood-money) is a distinctive system that spreads responsibility for accidental killing. This lesson examines the precise boundaries of that liability as outlined in the Hanafi school, focusing on several key exceptions that shift the burden to the perpetrator individually.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule of 'aqila liability
The 'aqila is liable for accidental killing when the diyah amount reaches at least half a tenth of the full diyah. Below that, the perpetrator pays.
Half a tenth = one-twentieth of full diyah. Full diyah for a free male is 100 camels or equivalent.
2
List the specific exclusions
The 'aqila does NOT bear liability for: intentional killing, crimes committed by a slave, crimes confessed to without 'aqila confirmation, and amounts determined by settlement (sulh).
Intentional killing is excluded because it is a sin and diyah is not the primary remedy; the killer pays from his own property.
3
Analyze the confession and settlement rules
If the perpetrator confesses, the 'aqila is only bound if they confirm the confession. Settlements always fall on the perpetrator. This prevents the 'aqila from being forced to pay based solely on the perpetrator's admission.
The text adds the condition of confirmation here, which was not mentioned in the earlier Book of Diyat.
4
Examine special cases
For a delayed confession (years later), the diyah is paid by the perpetrator alone over three years. For killing a slave, the 'aqila pays full diyah, but for lesser injuries to a slave, the 'aqila does not pay (treated as property loss). If there is no 'aqila, the state treasury pays.
The deferment for a delayed confession starts from the date of judgment, not the date of the act.
5
Apply the rules to a scenario
Given a fact pattern, determine whether the 'aqila is liable or the perpetrator pays individually, citing the relevant exception or condition.
Example: If a free man accidentally kills another free man and the families settle on a reduced amount, the 'aqila is not liable; the killer pays the settlement.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of 'Aqila presents several rulings regarding the liability of the 'aqila (the group responsible for paying blood-money on behalf of a perpetrator of accidental killing). The author begins by stating a general rule: if the compensation (diyah) is less than half a tenth (i.e., one-twentieth) of the full diyah, then it is the personal responsibility of the perpetrator, not the 'aqila. This is because the 'aqila only bears liability for large amounts. Next, the 'aqila is exempt from paying for intentional killing (since it involves sin and is not eligible for diyah), and for crimes committed by a slave (because the slave's value is like property, and the master may be liable). The 'aqila also does not pay for a crime that the perpetrator confesses to, unless the 'aqila members confirm the confession; otherwise, they are not bound by his admission. Similarly, amounts determined by settlement (sulh) are on the perpetrator, not the 'aqila. The commentator clarifies that this is not a repetition from the Book of Diyat because here he adds the condition of confirmation. The passage then addresses a specific scenario: if a person confesses to accidental killing but the matter is not brought to a judge until years later, the judgment for diyah is against his own property, payable in three years from the date of judgment. Finally, if a free person accidentally kills a slave, the diyah is on the 'aqila of the free person. However, for injuries less than death to a slave, the 'aqila does not bear it; it is treated as property loss. If the killer has no 'aqila, the diyah is from the public treasury (as per an addition in some manuscripts).

Key Terms
العاقلة (al-'āqilah): The paternal relatives (asabah) of the perpetrator who collectively bear the blood-money (diyah) for accidental killing.
الدية (al-diyah): Monetary compensation paid to the victim's family for accidental killing or injury.
الصلح (al-ṣulḥ): A settlement agreement between the parties, which if reached, obliges the perpetrator to pay from his own property, not the 'aqila.

Liability of the 'Aqila: What They Bear and Do Not Bear
Rulings on what the 'aqila bear and do not bear in diyat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four rulings regarding 'aqila liability for diyat and settlements as per Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
The 'aqila bear diyat for accidental killing but not for settlements or injuries to slaves.

The 'aqila (the male paternal relatives of a killer) have a specific, limited liability in Hanafi law. They are required to pay diyat (blood money) for accidental killings, but not every financial obligation incurred by their member falls on them. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies exactly what the 'aqila bear and what they do not, including the special case of injuries to slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four rulings
Read the passage carefully and list the four distinct rulings about what the 'aqila bear and do not bear.
The passage mentions: (1) No liability for settlements, (2) Liability for accidental killing of a slave, (3) If no 'aqila, from treasury, (4) No liability for injuries below death to a slave.
2
Distinguish between diyat and other obligations
Diyat is a specific compensation for homicide. Settlements (sulh) are voluntary agreements. The 'aqila only bear the former when it results from an accidental killing.
3
Note the exceptional case of no 'aqila
If the killer has no male paternal relatives (asabah) to form an 'aqila, the diya is paid from the public treasury (bayt al-mal), not by the killer personally.
This ensures the victim's family is not left uncompensated.
4
Explain the treatment of slave injuries
Injuries to a slave that are less than death are treated as damage to property. Since the 'aqila are guarantors for blood money (diya), not property damage, they are not liable for such injuries.
The killer is personally liable for the slave's injury as a property owner.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of 'Aqila in Al-Jawhara Al-Nayyira states several rulings regarding the liability of the 'aqila (the tribal guarantors who pay diyat on behalf of a member). First: the 'aqila are not responsible for any financial obligation that arises from a settlement or reconciliation (sulh); that is a personal debt of the perpetrator. Second: if a free person accidentally kills a slave, the diya (full value of the slave) is upon the 'aqila of the free killer, not upon the killer himself. Third: if a killer has no 'aqila (e.g., he has no tribe or supporters), the diya is paid from the public treasury (bayt al-mal). Fourth: for injuries to a slave that are less than death (e.g., wounding), the 'aqila do not bear any liability because such injuries are treated as damage to property, which is a personal liability. These rulings are based on Hanafi jurisprudence. The commentator notes that in some editions of al-Quduri's Mukhtasar, the third ruling is explicitly added.

Key Terms
عاقلة ('āqila): The male paternal relatives (tribe) of the killer who collectively bear the diya for accidental killings.
صلح (ṣulḥ): A settlement or compromise that results in a financial obligation, as opposed to a liability imposed by law for homicide.
ديَة (diya): Blood money or compensation paid for homicide or injury, specified by law.
