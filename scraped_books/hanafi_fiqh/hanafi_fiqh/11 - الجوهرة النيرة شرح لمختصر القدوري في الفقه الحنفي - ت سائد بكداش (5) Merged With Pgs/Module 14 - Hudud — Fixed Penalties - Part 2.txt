Types of Tazkiyah: Secret vs Public and Who May Verify Witnesses
Expansion probe: definition and application of tazkiyah types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between secret and public tazkiyah in Hanafi law and identify who may perform each type.

Core Idea
Secret tazkiyah is like a report (khabar) so less strict; public tazkiyah is like testimony and requires full witness qualifications.

Tazkiyah (witness verification) is divided into two categories: secret and public. The Hanafi school treats them differently based on their legal nature. Secret tazkiyah is akin to reporting religious information, allowing even women, slaves, and those punished for qadhf to perform it if they are upright. Public tazkiyah, however, is analogous to testimony (shahadah) and requires the same qualifications as a court witness. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of tazkiyah
Identify the definitions from the passage: public tazkiyah is when the qadi brings the modifier and witness together face-to-face; secret tazkiyah is done via messenger and letter without public meeting.
The distinction hinges on the form of communication.
2
Understand the legal analogy
Note the passage's reasoning: secret tazkiyah is 'of the category of reporting' (khabar), so the rules of riwayah apply; public tazkiyah is 'analogous to testimony' (shahadah), so the rules of shahadah apply.
This analogy determines who may perform each type.
3
Identify who is accepted for each type
Abu Hanifa states: for secret tazkiyah, he accepts a woman, a slave, and one punished for qadhf if they are upright. For public tazkiyah, he only accepts those whose testimony is itself accepted (i.e., free, male? The text implies full shahadah qualifications).
The passage explicitly gives examples of secret tazkiyah: father for son is permitted.

Conditions for Valid Confession of Zina in Hanafi Fiqh
States rulings for iqrar (confession) of zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four conditions for a valid iqrar (confession) of zina in Hanafi fiqh and explain the rationale for requiring four separate sessions.

Core Idea
Confession of zina must be made four times in separate sessions, each time the judge rejects, to protect against false self-incrimination.

In Hanafi fiqh, a confession (iqrar) to zina is not automatically accepted. To prevent false confessions and uphold the principle of averting hudud through doubts, strict conditions are set. This lesson explains the ruling from Mukhtasar al-Quduri: the confession must be made four times in four different sessions, with the judge rejecting each time.

Guided Path
1

The passage is about iqrar (confession) for the crime of zina (adultery/fornication). Recognize that this is a hudud case with special procedural requirements.
2

The conditions: the confessor must be a sane adult; the confession must be made four times; each must be in a separate session (majlis); the judge must reject each time.
The judge's rejection is a required part of the procedure.
3
Understand the procedure
The judge should reprimand and express disapproval each time, hoping the confessor retracts. If he returns a second, third, fourth time, the judge repeats the rejection.
The judge's goal is to dissuade the confessor and create opportunities for retraction.
4
Apply the consequence of non-compliance
If the confession occurs four times in one sitting, it is treated as a single confession and is insufficient for applying hadd.
Four in one session = one confession; the hadd cannot be imposed.
5
Grasp the rationale
The system creates doubts (shubuhat) to avert the hadd punishment, aligning with the prophetic principle of averting hudud.
The rigorous requirement reduces the chance of false confessions.

In this passage, the commentator explains the stringent procedural requirements for a confession of zina in the Hanafi school. Unlike other hudud, zina requires four separate confessions, giving the confessor ample opportunity to retract. Note that the confession must be made four separate times in four different sessions (majalis). The judge should turn the confessor away each time, expressing disapproval. This gives the confessor multiple chances to retract.

Retraction of Confession in Hudud
Rulings on retracting confessions in hudud and effect on witness testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on retracting confessions for zina, theft, wine, qadhf, and qisas, and distinguish between rights of God and rights of servants.

Core Idea
Retraction of confession is valid in hudud except when a victim's right is involved.

This lesson examines the Hanafi stance on retracting confessions in hudud crimes. The key principle is that hudud are rights of God and can be dropped by retraction due to doubt, but when a private right is involved, retraction does not erase the victim's claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the crimes and rulings
List the crimes mentioned: zina, theft, wine, qadhf, qisas. Note the outcome of retraction for each.
Retraction is valid for zina, theft, and wine, but not for qadhf and qisas.
2
Examine the exception for theft
In theft, retraction is valid for the hadd (cutting) but not for the property (mal). Understand why: property is a right of the victim.
The property remains owed even after retraction.
3
Understand exceptions for qadhf and qisas
These are classified as rights of servants (huquq al-ibad). Retraction does not nullify the victim's claim.
Qadhf involves a false accusation, qisas involves bodily harm.
4
Apply the principle
If a crime involves both a hadd and a private right, retraction cancels only the hadd; the private right remains.
This is the key distinction in Hanafi fiqh.

The proof for the majority is a hadith: 'Whoever associates partners with Allah is not muhsan,' indicating that Islam is a condition. Nevertheless, the text notes that the old reading of Quduri (as in certain manuscripts) does not include Islam and the condition of both being in a state of ihsan; the author of the commentary (Ibn al-Shihna?) confirms the corrected version includes them. The passage then clarifies that intercourse in an invalid marriage does not confer ihsan—it is like zina. If a man marries a slave and consummates, then she is freed, he must consummate again after her freedom to become muhsan. Similarly, if a man consummates with a minor wife and she reaches puberty, he must consummate again after puberty.

If the wife was a slave, minor, insane, or a kitabiyya at the time of consummation, the husband does not become muhsan.

Postponing Hadd for Sick and Pregnant Offenders
Rulings with rational proofs for postponing hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on postponing hadd for sick and pregnant offenders and explain the rational basis (istihsan) for the distinction between stoning and flogging.

Core Idea
Flogging is postponed to avoid unintended harm; stoning is immediate because death is the intended punishment.

In Hudud, the timing of punishment may be adjusted based on the offender's condition. The Hanafi school distinguishes between stoning and flogging for the sick, and between pregnancy and postpartum for women. This lesson examines the rational proofs behind these postponements, rooted in the principle of preventing harm (darar).

Guided Path
1
Identify the offenders and their hadd types
The passage covers two categories: a sick person (with stoning or flogging as the hadd) and a pregnant woman (with flogging as the hadd). Note that stoning is not mentioned for the pregnant woman because stoning is not applicable due to pregnancy? Actually the text only mentions flogging for the pregnant woman, but the principle would apply similarly.
Stoning is only for married offenders (muhsan), and the text does not discuss the pregnant woman being muhsan, but the ruling is general.
2
Apply the rule for the sick: stoning immediate, flogging delayed
If the sick person's hadd is stoning, it is carried out immediately because death is the very punishment (إتلاف مستحق). If flogging, it is postponed until recovery to avoid unintended death (لا يُفضي إلى الهلاك).
Stoning is an exception because the intended effect (death) is already due; delay would negate the hadd.
3
Apply the rule for the pregnant woman: delay until delivery, and further for flogging until nifas ends
Punishment is postponed until after delivery to protect the unborn child (نفس محترمة). If the hadd is flogging, it is further delayed until the mother is purified from postpartum bleeding (نفاس) to avoid harm.
The same principle of avoiding harm applies: the child's life takes precedence.
4
Identify the proof: istihsan based on prevention of harm
The rulings are not directly from a primary text but are derived by istihsan (juristic preference) to achieve the maqasid of protecting life. The proof is aqli (rational): avoiding unnecessary harm is a fundamental objective of Shari'ah.
The text also equates the sick person's condition with extreme heat or cold, showing the general principle.
5
Note the broader principle and its limits
The maxim 'لا ضرر ولا ضرار' (no harm) underlies these postponements. However, stoning is not harmful beyond the intended punishment, so it is not postponed.
This shows that harm prevention must be balanced against the necessity of executing hadd.
