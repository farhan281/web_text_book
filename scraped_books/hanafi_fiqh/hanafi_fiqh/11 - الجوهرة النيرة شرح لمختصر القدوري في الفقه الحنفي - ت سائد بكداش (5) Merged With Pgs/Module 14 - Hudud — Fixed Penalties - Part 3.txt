Hadd for a Sick Person: Rajm vs. Jald
Ruling on applying hadd to a sick person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on applying hudud punishments to a sick person and distinguish between the immediate execution of rajm and the delay of jald.

Core Idea
Rajm is immediate for the sick; jald is delayed to prevent death.

This passage from Kitab al-Hudud addresses a specific scenario: when a sick person commits zina and is liable for hadd. The ruling draws a clear distinction between stoning (rajm) and flogging (jald), based on the nature of the punishment and the risk of additional harm. Understanding this distinction is crucial for proper judicial application of hudud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is zina committed by a sick person. The ruling depends on the hadd prescribed: if rajm, execute immediately; if jald, delay until recovery.
The sickness here is not specified; it applies to any illness that could be exacerbated by flogging.
2
Analyze the reasoning for rajm
Rajm is immediate because it entails death, which is already due as punishment. Delaying would not serve a purpose since the end result is the same.
The phrase 'la ma'na lil-imtina'' indicates no valid excuse to postpone.
3
Analyze the reasoning for jald
Jald is delayed to avoid causing death by flogging, which is not intended to kill. The principle is to prevent exceeding the prescribed harm.
This is analogous to delaying punishment in extreme heat or cold.
4
Apply the analogy to extreme weather
If the heat or cold is severe, flogging is also delayed to avoid additional harm. This shows the broader principle of avoiding unnecessary injury.
The text explicitly extends the reasoning to environmental conditions.
5
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
Some schools may delay rajm as well, but the Hanafi view is clear: rajm is immediate. Understand that this difference stems from how each school weighs the certainty of death versus potential harm.
The Hanafi position is based on the principle that the hadd is a right of Allah that must be executed unless there is a clear obstacle.

Hadd for a Pregnant Woman: Timing and Disputes
States hadd ruling for pregnant woman, delaying until delivery and post-partum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on when a pregnant woman's hadd is applied, explain the rationale for delays, and identify the inter-madhhab dispute regarding exile as part of the hadd.

Core Idea
A pregnant woman's hadd is delayed to protect the child; flogging waits until postpartum bleeding ends, but stoning is applied after delivery.

In this passage, the Hanafi school addresses a delicate situation: a pregnant woman who has committed zina. The rulings balance the obligation of hadd with the inviolability of the unborn child and the health of the mother. We will examine the timing of both flogging and stoning, as well as a key disagreement with the Shafi'i school over whether exile (taghrib) is part of the hadd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage covers three scenarios for a pregnant woman: (1) hadd of flogging, (2) hadd of stoning, and (3) the general rule of delaying until delivery. Note the different timing rules for each.
The general rule is delay until delivery for both, but further delays vary.
2
Understand the rationale for delays
Delay until delivery protects the child (a respected soul). Delay for flogging until postpartum bleeding ends is because nifas is considered an illness; stoning is not delayed for postpartum because the only reason for delay was the child, who is now born.
Compare with menstruation: a menstruating woman is flogged immediately because hayd is not an illness.
3
Analyze the proof for the general rule
The text cites Quran 24:2 as the complete statement of the hadd for zina. This is used to argue that exile is not part of the hadd, because the verse only mentions flogging.
The Hanafi argument hinges on the completeness of the Quranic verse.
4
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that exile is combined with flogging as the hadd for an unmarried person. The Hanafi response is that adding exile would make the 100 lashes only part of the hadd, and exile lacks a fixed measure, unlike hadd punishments.
This is an inter-madhhab dispute with practical consequences in sentencing.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi variation
Abu Hanifa narrates that for stoning, the punishment may be further delayed if the child needs the mother's care and no one else can raise the child. This is not the general position but shows flexibility.
The text also distinguishes between testimony and confession for detention purposes while pregnant.

Stoning in Nifas and Imprisoning the Pregnant Adulteress
Explains rulings on executing hadd penalties on women in nifas and pregnancy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the general Hanafi position and Abu Hanifa's narration regarding stoning a woman in nifas, and explain why imprisonment of a pregnant adulteress depends on the mode of proof (bayyina vs. iqrar).

Core Idea
Stoning in nifas is immediate unless the child needs the mother; imprisonment only if proven by evidence, not confession.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira discusses two related issues in hadd punishments for women: the timing of stoning for a woman in postpartum bleeding (nifas) and the imprisonment of a pregnant adulteress. The core principle is that penalties should not harm innocent third parties (the child). An intra-Hanafi dispute arises on whether to delay stoning until the child is weaned. Additionally, the mode of proof—evidence or confession—determines whether imprisonment is ordered.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage covers (a) stoning in nifas and (b) imprisonment of a pregnant adulteress. Note the distinct reasoning for each.
2
Trace the general position
The matn states immediate stoning because the child has separated; the delay was only for the child's protection during pregnancy.
3
Locate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
A narration from Abu Hanifa holds that stoning should be delayed until the child no longer needs the mother's care, if no one else can raise the child.
This is not the relied-upon position; the matn represents the general view.

Analyze the distinction in imprisonment
If adultery is proven by evidence (bayyina), the woman is imprisoned until delivery to prevent flight. If proven by confession (iqrar), she is not imprisoned because a confession can be retracted, making imprisonment useless.
This highlights the Hanafi principle that hadd penalties are averted by doubts (shubuhat).

For stoning, delay is only for the child's welfare; for imprisonment, the key is whether the proof is revocable.

Testimony for a Stale Hadd: General Rule and Exceptions
Rule for accepting stale testimony with exceptions and witness liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general Hanafi rule that testimony for a stale hadd is not accepted, identify the exceptions for qadhf, qisas, and confession, and explain the ruling on witness liability when testimony is rejected.

Core Idea
Stale testimony is rejected except for qadhf and qisas because they involve human rights.

This passage addresses a practical question: if witnesses delay reporting a hadd, is their testimony still valid? The Hanafi school generally rejects stale testimony, but makes crucial exceptions for qadhf (false accusation of adultery) and qisas (retaliation) because these penalties involve the rights of individuals, which are not forfeited by time. The passage also discusses confession and the liability of the witnesses themselves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The text states that testimony for a stale hadd is not accepted unless the witnesses were prevented from coming by distance from the Imam. The default is rejection except for qadhf.
Note the phrase 'لم تُقبل شهادتهم إلا في حد القذف خاصة'.
2
Recognize the exceptions
The exceptions are qadhf and qisas, based on the principle that human rights (huquq al-ibad) are not invalidated by staleness. Understand the reasoning: 'لأنهما من حقوق العباد'.
This talil connects to the broader Hanafi maxim on rights.
3
Understand the rule for confession (iqrar)
Confession is valid despite staleness, except for drinking wine where the condition of smell is required according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. Note the intra-Hanafi difference: Muhammad himself does not require the smell condition in either testimony or confession.
The text says 'إلا في شرب الخمر، فإن وجود الرائحة من شرطه عندهما'.
4
Address witness liability
If testimony is rejected due to staleness, the witnesses are not punished for qadhf because their testimony was complete in number; the hadd fell from the accused due to shubhah (doubt), not because of false testimony. Follow Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi's reasoning.
The key point: 'الشهادة كاملة العدد'.

Execution of Hadd After Judgement: Does Staleness Prevent It?
Dispute on whether staleness prevents execution of hadd after judgement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position that staleness prevents both initial acceptance of testimony and execution of the hadd after judgement, and explain the dissenting view of Zufr and why it is rejected.

Core Idea
Staleness blocks execution after judgement because implementation is part of judgement in hudud.

This passage addresses a subtle point: after a judge has ordered a hadd penalty, can it be executed if time has passed? The Hanafi majority holds that staleness prevents execution, just as it prevents initial acceptance of testimony. However, Zufr disagreed, and the author marks his view as 'munkar' (rejected).

1
Understand the principle
The text states that staleness prevents not only the acceptance of testimony but also the execution of the hadd after judgement. This is because implementation is considered part of the judgement (qaḍā') in the chapter of hudud.

2
Identify the dissenting view
Zufr (a Hanafi jurist) holds that staleness does not prevent execution after judgement. His view is mentioned and then dismissed as 'munkar' (reprehensible).
The word 'منكراً' appears in the text, indicating rejection.
3
Analyze the practical application
The text gives an example: If a person flees after receiving part of the hadd (e.g., some lashes), and is recaptured after a long time, according to the majority the hadd is not completed. Zufr would say it is completed.

Core Idea

This passage clarifies a critical distinction in Hanafi penal law: the effect of prescription (taqadum) on hudud punishments. While punishments for purely divine rights (like zina) are invalidated by time, punishments for human rights (like qadhf and qisas) are not. The passage also addresses nuances regarding confession, wine drinking, and a dispute on whether prescription prevents execution after a judgment has been passed.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Hudud in al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah explains the crucial Hanafi concept of shubha (legal ambiguity) as a bar to the application of hadd penalties. The commentator begins by citing the base text's ruling: a man who has intercourse with his son's or grandson's slave-girl is not subject to hadd, even if he explicitly knew it was forbidden. The reason given is that the shubha here is 'hukmiyyah' (legal/rule-based), originating from the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father' – this creates a semblance of ownership for the father over the son's property, including the slave-girl, thus generating a legal doubt. The passage then systematically classifies shubha into two categories. First, shubha fi al-mahal (shubha in the subject/place), also called shubha hukmiyyah, arises from a legal rule itself, not from factual ignorance. Six cases are listed, all involving either incomplete ownership or disputed property rights. In all these cases, the hadd is entirely dropped, even if the person was fully aware of the prohibition. However, other civil consequences remain: the mahr is obligatory (if it was a sexual relationship with a woman), lineage is established if claimed, and there is no hadd for false accusation (qadhf) regarding these individuals. Second, shubha fi al-fi'l (shubha in the act/behavior), also called shubha ishtibah, arises from factual uncertainty or mistaken belief about the permissibility of the act. Eight cases are listed. Here, the ruling bifurcates: if the person says 'I thought it was permissible' (zann), then hadd is not applied; but if he says 'I knew it was forbidden', then hadd is applied. This is because the shubha lies in the actor's mind, not in the legal status of the subject. A critical practical difference follows: in shubha fi al-mahal, lineage is established; in shubha fi al-fi'l, lineage is not established even if claimed. Finally, the passage discusses an important edge case: a man who divorces his wife three times (making her completely forbidden) and then has intercourse with her during her iddah. If he knew it was forbidden, hadd applies because the ownership is entirely severed with no remaining legal doubt. If he thought it was permissible, hadd is not applied because there remains some legal vestige (athar al-milk) in that the woman still has rights to lineage, maintenance, and housing during iddah, which could create a genuine doubt. The same applies to the umm walad after emancipation, the khul' divorcee, and the divorcee for compensation – they retain some effects of ownership (athar al-milk) that justify a plausible claim of ignorance.

Key Terms
شبهة (shubha): Legal ambiguity or doubt that prevents the application of hadd penalties. It is of two types: shubha in the subject (hukmiyyah) and shubha in the act (ishtibah).
شبهة حكمية (shubha hukmiyyah): Shubha arising from a legal rule or proof, not from factual ignorance. It is called shubha fi al-mahal. Example: a man having intercourse with his son's slave-girl due to the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father'.
شبهة اشتباه (shubha ishtibah): Shubha arising from factual uncertainty or mistaken belief about the permissibility of an act. Also called shubha fi al-fi'l. Example: having intercourse with a woman thought to be one's wife but who is actually a stranger.

Commentary on shubha in intercourse with relatives' slave-girls

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shubha in intercourse with a relative's slave-girl and explain why knowledge of prohibition removes that shubha.

Core Idea
Shubha in property disposal repels hadd only when the person was ignorant of the prohibition.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains a key Hanafi principle: shubha (plausible doubt) can prevent the application of hudud punishments, but it depends on the person's knowledge. The commentator distinguishes between two scenarios involving intercourse with a slave-girl who belongs to a close relative (father, mother, wife) or master. The ruling hinges on whether the person knew the act was haram or honestly believed it was permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenarios
The passage covers two scenarios: (1) a free man has intercourse with his father's, mother's, or wife's slave-girl; (2) a slave has intercourse with his master's slave-girl. The ruling for each depends on the person's state of knowledge.
The slave-girl is not his own property.
2
Distinguish knowledge from ignorance
If the person says 'I knew she was forbidden for me,' hadd (the prescribed punishment for zina) is applied. But if he says 'I thought she was lawful for me,' no hadd is applied.
Knowledge of prohibition removes any possible doubt.
3
Understand the basis of shubha
The commentary explains that the claim 'I thought it was lawful' is based on an apparent reason: the person has freedom of disposal (tasarruf) in the property of his parents and wife. This legal authority creates a plausible doubt (shubha) about the permissibility of intercourse with their slave-girls.
The shubha arises from the general permission to use their property, not from a specific permission for intercourse.
4

The ruling extends to the person who falsely accuses (qadzif) the one who had intercourse under shubha. Since the act is not conclusively zina, the accuser also escapes the hadd punishment for qadhf.
The hadd of qadhf (80 lashes) is applied only when the accused act is clearly zina.
5

The thamarah al-khilaf: in the Hanafi school, a person who claims ignorance avoids both the hadd for zina and the hadd for qadhf. Other schools may not recognize this shubha, making the person liable for hadd.
This shows how Hanafi fiqh prioritizes the principle of shubha in hudud.

Shubha and Hadd: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Unity of the Act
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hadd with shubha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hadd when either party claims shubha, and evaluate the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the one who admits knowledge.

Core Idea
When shubha exists for one, the hadd falls for both — the act's unity overrides individual knowledge.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira delves into the effects of shubha (legal doubt) on the hadd punishment for unlawful intercourse. It begins with clear cases where shubha is absent and hadd applies, then introduces a key intra-Hanafi dispute: if one party admits knowing the prohibition but the other claims ignorance, does the hadd apply to the knowledgeable party? The Sahibayn argue that the act's unity saves both, while Abu Hanifa holds otherwise. Understanding this debate sharpens the application of the principle that punishments are averted by doubts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and its condition
Read the opening lines: if a man has intercourse with his father's slave-girl and claims he thought it was permissible, he is not flogged. Note that the shubha arises from an apparent right to use the relative's property.
The shubha must be based on a plausible justification, not a mere claim.
2
Locate the consensus case
Find the case where both parties claim ignorance: the text says 'by consensus' the hadd is averted from both. This shows the principle that shubha removes punishment fully.
Consensus (ijma) among Hanafi jurists is cited from Al-Waqi'at.
3
Trace the dispute
When the man admits knowledge but the woman claims ignorance: Abu Hanifa says the man is flogged and the woman spared; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say both are spared. Identify the reasoning: the Sahibayn consider the act as one legal event — if shubha exists for either, it taints the whole act.
This is a valid ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The Sahibayn's position rests on the unity of the act (fiqh): an act cannot be halal for one and haram for the other. Therefore, if one party's action is excused by shubha, the other is also excused.
This aligns with the well-known Hanafi maxim: 'al-hudood tudra'u bi al-shubuhat' (punishments are warded off by doubts).
5
Apply the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that of the Sahibayn: whenever either party claims a valid shubha, the hadd is averted from both. This cautious approach prioritizes avoiding punishment in cases of doubt.
Later Hanafi fatwa collections generally prefer the Sahibayn's view in hudud matters.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar addresses the application of the hadd punishment for zina (illicit intercourse) in cases where the perpetrator claims ignorance (shubha) of the prohibition. The opening ruling distinguishes between explicit knowledge and claimed ignorance: if the person states they knew it was forbidden, hadd is applied; if they claim they thought it was permissible, hadd is dropped. This is because Islamic law suspends punishments when there is an element of legal doubt (shubha). The commentator then extends this principle to various scenarios: a woman in her waiting period after a revocable divorce, a borrowed or hired slave-girl, multiple acts, and the case where the woman also claims ignorance. A key intra-Hanafi dispute arises when the man admits knowledge but the woman claims ignorance: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the hadd is averted from both because the act is one, while Abu Hanifa holds that the man must be flogged and only the woman is spared. The passage concludes with additional rulings on paternity, ownership, and war booty, all revolving around the concept of shubha based on apparent ownership or a shared right to use.

Key Terms
شبهة (shubha): Legal uncertainty or doubt that prevents the application of hadd punishments. In this text, it arises from an apparent justification (e.g., access to property) that makes the perpetrator honestly believe the act was permissible.
حد (hadd): A fixed, divinely prescribed punishment for specific crimes (e.g., zina). In this passage, it refers to the flogging penalty for unlawful intercourse.
جارية (jariya): A female slave. In the context of this passage, the rulings concern intercourse with slaves who are owned by specific relatives or others.

Shubha as a Hadd-Averting Doubt in Zina
Ruling on shubha when one party claims ignorance in zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a plausible doubt (shubha) averts the hadd for zina when one party claims ignorance, and apply it to cases involving slave-girls.

Core Idea
If either party claims a plausible doubt of permissibility, the hadd for zina is averted from both.

The Hanafi school recognizes that certain doubts can avert the fixed punishment (hadd) for zina, even when the act is technically forbidden. This lesson examines a specific scenario: when one party—either the man or the woman—claims ignorance of the prohibition, the hadd is not applied. The key is that the doubt must be plausible, such as a mistaken belief about ownership or relationship. We will explore the principle through the text of al-Jawhara al-Nayyira and a concrete case from al-Waqi'at.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the default ruling
The act under discussion is zina (unlawful sexual intercourse). The default ruling is that it incurs hadd (lashes or stoning) if proven. Recognize that this passage discusses an exception.
2
Recognize the shubha scenario
The text presents a situation where either the man or the slave-girl says 'I thought it was permissible'. This claim creates a shubha—a plausible doubt based on a resemblance to a permissible act (like the slave eating from the master's wealth when needed).
The analogy with the slave's eating highlights that a legitimate permission in a similar context can create a reasonable doubt.
3
Apply the principle: shubha averts hadd
The text states that if either party claims ignorance, the hadd is averted from both, because the act is mutual. The default is that hadd is not applied when such a claim is made, unless both admit they knew the prohibition.
The ruling is based on the principle that hadd is averted by doubts (shubuhat).
4
Analyze the case from al-Waqi'at
The text cites a case: a man commits zina with his father's (or mother's, etc.) slave-girl. He claims he thought she was permissible; she says it is forbidden to him. The ruling is that hadd is averted from both by consensus. This shows that the shubha attaches even when only one party claims ignorance.
The consensus (ijma') reinforces the strength of this ruling in the Hanafi school.

When Ignorance Meets Knowledge: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Hadd
Intra-Hanafi dispute on opposite claim scenario for hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn in the opposite claim scenario for hadd and explain the role of subjective knowledge in shubha.

Core Idea
Subjective knowledge of prohibition can remove shubha for the knowledgeable party alone.

You have learned that shubha averts hadd. But what if only one party has a valid shubha? This passage reveals a disagreement among the Hanafi imams on whether the ignorance of the slave-girl can benefit the man who admits he knew the act was forbidden. The answer hinges on whether shubha is treated as a shared condition or individually assessed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposite scenario
The scenario reverses the typical case: the slave-girl claims she thought intercourse was permissible (halal), while the man admits he knew it was forbidden (haram).
This is the 'opposite' of the earlier case where both parties claimed ignorance.
2
State the Sahibayn position
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the hadd punishment is averted from both the man and the woman.
They consider the woman's claim of ignorance sufficient to create overall doubt benefiting both parties.
3
State the Abu Hanifa position
According to Abu Hanifa, the hadd is obligatory on the man (because he admitted knowledge) and is averted only from the woman (who was ignorant).
Abu Hanifa evaluates shubha individually for each party.
4
Understand the reasoning
For Abu Hanifa, the man's admission that he knew the prohibition negates any shubha for him; thus, he must be punished. For the Sahibayn, the act itself is doubtful due to the woman's claim, and that doubt extends to the man as well.
The ikhtilaf revolves around whether shubha is a personal condition or a characteristic of the act.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa's view, the man receives hadd; under Sahibayn's view, he does not.
This illustrates the importance of understanding the basis of ikhtilaf in hudud matters.

Shubha in Zina: No Hadd, No Lineage, and Special Cases
Rulings on the effects of claiming shubha by the man in zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five distinct rulings when a man claims ignorance in zina, and apply the underlying principle of shubha to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Shubha averts hadd but does not create lawful consequences like lineage or umm walad status.

This lesson examines a specific scenario where a man claims he thought the intercourse was permissible (shubha). The Hanafi school applies the principle that shubha blocks the hadd punishment, but it does not legitimize the act for other legal effects. The passage lays out five detailed rulings: no hadd, no lineage, the child's freedom if owned, the mother's non-umm walad status, and a special case involving spoils of war. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for properly applying the concept of shubha in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the man's claim and the first two rulings
The man says 'I thought she was permissible.' The first ruling: he is not subject to hadd. The second: even if he claims the child is his, lineage is not established. Identify that shubha blocks punishment but does not create lineage.
2
Analyze the child's status if owned by the man
If the man owns the child (the slave-girl's son), the child becomes free because a man cannot own his own progeny. This ruling reinforces that the child is legally free, though lineage is not established.
Child's freedom is a consequence of ownership, not of paternity.
3
Examine the mother's status (no umm walad)
If the man owns the mother (the slave-girl), she does not become umm walad (mother of his child). The intercourse was sinful, so she remains a saleable slave. Identify that shubha does not create the lawful relationship required for umm walad.
4
Understand the special case of spoils of war
If a warrior has intercourse with a slave-girl from the spoils before division, even if he admits knowing it was forbidden, he is not hadd. Reason: the spoils are communal property, giving him a right (shubha milk). However, lineage is still not established.
This shows that shubha can arise from a right of ownership, not just ignorance of prohibition.
5
Synthesize the principle
General principle: shubha averts hadd (punishment) but does not produce legal consequences that depend on a valid marriage or permissible relationship. Distinguish between the removal of punishment and the creation of legal effects.
Examples: no lineage, no umm walad, but child may be freed due to ownership.

Marrying a Mahram: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Hadd
Intra-Hanafi dispute on punishment for mahram marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding hadd for marrying a mahram, explain their respective dalils, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A marriage to a mahram is not automatically zina; Abu Hanifa invokes historical permissibility to block hadd, while the Sahibayn see it as pure illegality.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on whether a man who marries a permanently prohibited woman (like a mahram) and has intercourse is subject to hadd. The disagreement hinges on whether the act is inherently zina or merely a prohibited act (munkar) that was once lawful. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Hanafi school uses shubha (semblance of legality) to mitigate penalties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue and the two positions
Recognize that the dispute is about the punishment for intercourse after a marriage to a mahram. Abu Hanifa says no hadd (only ta'zir); the Sahibayn say hadd applies if the person knows the prohibition.
The act itself is agreed to be haram; the point of ikhtilaf is whether it is zina.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's dalil
Abu Hanifa argues that the act is not zina because Allah permitted marriage to mahrams in previous shariahs (e.g., Adam's children). Since zina was never permitted, an act once permissible cannot be intrinsically zina.
This is a dalil naqli (transmitted evidence) based on historical divine laws.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's dalil
The Sahibayn argue that the marriage contract is void (bāṭil) from the start, so there is no shubha (semblance) of legality. Therefore, intercourse is like zina, and hadd is due if the person knows the prohibition.
They emphasize that a void contract provides no legal protection.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical outcome: under Abu Hanifa, the offender receives ta'zir (discretionary punishment); under the Sahibayn, he receives hadd (fixed punishment) if he knew the marriage was prohibited.
This difference has serious implications for the severity of punishment.
5
Consider the mufta bihi within the Hanafi school
Many later Hanafi works (e.g., al-Hidayah) favour Abu Hanifa's view regarding mahram marriages, though the Sahibayn's view is also recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah. Tarjih often depends on the specific context.
The lesson shows that even within a single school, legitimate ikhtilaf exists.

Core Idea
Shubha exempts both; qusur may exempt only the deficient party depending on source.

This passage establishes a foundational qawida in Hanafi criminal law: when the hadd for zina falls from one participant, the effect on the other depends on the reason—shubha (ambiguity) or qusur (deficiency). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying hadd cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two exemptions
The text begins with a principle: if the hadd falls from one of the two due to shubha, it also falls from the other. Then it contrasts with qusur (deficiency). Shubha relates to a plausible excuse; qusur relates to incapacity or coercion.
Shubha: e.g., claiming marriage. Qusur: e.g., minor, insane, coerced.
2
Apply shubha rule
When one participant claims a valid marriage (shubha), the hadd falls from both because the crime is considered uncertain due to the shared ambiguity.
The denial of the other does not break the shubha.
3
Analyze qusur from woman's side
If the deficiency is from the woman (minor, insane, coerced, sleeping), only she is exempt. The man's hadd remains because his act is complete and without deficiency.
Coercion: if she was forced, she lacks consent.
4
Analyze qusur from man's side
If the deficiency is from the man (insane, child, coerced), the hadd falls from both because the act is not fully voluntary from the initiator.
Coercion here means he was forced.
5
Synthesize the principle
Shubha always exempts both; qusur only exempts both if originating from the man, otherwise only the woman.
The ruling is specific to zina; other hudud may differ.

Principle of Shared Status in Hadd for Zinā
Legal maxim on when hadd falls from one or both zānīs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of hadd falling from both zānīs when due to shubha, and distinguish when hadd falls only from one party due to qusur al-fi'l.

Core Idea
When hadd falls due to ambiguity (shubha), it drops from both; when due to deficiency (qusur), it drops only from the deficient party unless the deficiency is from the man.

This lesson introduces a fundamental maxim in Hanafi hudud law regarding zinā. The jurists distinguish two reasons for the hadd punishment to be lifted: shubha (ambiguity in the act) and qusur al-fi'l (deficiency in the act itself). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the hadd penalty in cases where one party has a valid excuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two reasons for hadd falling
The text states that hadd can fall due to shubha (ambiguity) or qusur al-fi'l (deficiency in the act). First, recognize which category a given scenario falls into.
Shubha relates to doubt about the act's permissibility; qusur relates to the actor's capacity or consent.
2
Apply the shubha rule
If the falling of hadd is due to shubha (e.g., one party claims a valid marriage), then the hadd falls from both partners because the ambiguity is shared.
The principle: 'as if one says, I thought it was marriage.'
3
Apply the qusur rule when deficiency is from the woman
If the deficiency is from her side (she is a minor, insane, coerced, or sleeping), the hadd falls from her but remains on the man. The man's full capacity and consent make him liable.
The examples: ṣaghīrah (minor), majnūnah (insane), mukrahah (coerced), nā'imah (sleeping).
4
Apply the qusur rule when deficiency is from the man
If the deficiency is from his side (he is insane, a minor, or coerced), the hadd falls from both. The act is not considered zinā on his part, and her participation is seen as defective as well.
This is symmetric to the previous case but with a different outcome for the woman.

The Smell Condition in Hadd for Drinking
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the evidentiary condition of smell for hadd al-shurb

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Hadd for drinking requires the smell of alcohol to be present at confession or testimony — doubt averts punishment.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a key evidentiary rule for proving the crime of drinking alcohol in the Hanafi school. It requires that the offender's mouth or body still smell of alcohol at the time of confession or witness testimony. The text also records an important intra-Hanafi disagreement: while Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf insist on this condition, Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that confession alone suffices. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply the principle that hadd punishments are averted by doubts.
