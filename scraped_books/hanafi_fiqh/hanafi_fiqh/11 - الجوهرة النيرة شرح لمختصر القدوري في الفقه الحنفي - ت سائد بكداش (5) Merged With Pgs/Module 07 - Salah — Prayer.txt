Hiring the Divorcée to Nurse After 'Iddah
Expansion of Quduri's ruling on hiring a divorcée after 'iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hiring a divorced woman after her 'iddah to nurse her child, distinguish between revocable and irrevocable divorce scenarios, and apply the rule that the mother is entitled to priority only if she accepts the wage of a stranger.

Core Idea
After 'iddah, the divorced mother is like a stranger: hiring her is permissible, and she is entitled to priority only if she accepts the stranger’s wage.

After a divorce, the mother may wish to nurse her child for payment. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the waiting period ('iddah) types: during a revocable divorce the marriage still exists, so hiring the wife for her own child is invalid; but after the 'iddah expires, or in an irrevocable divorce, she becomes an outsider. The law also protects the father from being forced to pay more than a stranger’s wage, while recognizing the mother’s compassion if she matches that wage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of 'iddah
First, recognize that for a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i), the marriage is still intact; for an irrevocable divorce (talaq ba'in), the marriage has ended. The ruling on hiring the woman to nurse her own child depends on this distinction.
'Iddah for a revocable divorce is a continuation of marriage; for an irrevocable, it is a waiting period before she becomes lawful to others.
2
Distinguish permissibility scenarios
For a woman in revocable 'iddah, hiring her to nurse her own child is not permissible because she is still a wife and nursing is not obligatory. But hiring her to nurse a child from another wife is permissible. For a woman after the expiry of 'iddah or in ba'in 'iddah, hiring is permissible (the more correct narration) because she is like a stranger.
There are two narrations for the ba'in case; the stronger one allows it.
3
Understand the mother's priority rule
When the father seeks a wet nurse, the mother is given priority if she agrees to the same wage as a stranger (ujrat al-mithl). If she demands more, the father is not obligated to hire her. This prevents harm to either party.
The priority is a right of the mother, not an obligation on the father.
4
Apply the Quranic proof
The ruling is supported by Quran 2:233, which prohibits harming the mother or father on account of the child. Forcing the father to pay more than the stranger's wage would harm him, while denying the mother the opportunity to nurse at the same wage would harm her.
The proof is a general prohibition of harm, not a specific command about nursing wages.

Commentary

He then distinguishes between a revocable divorce (talaq raj'i), where the marriage tie is still in effect and thus hiring one's own wife is impermissible because nursing is not obligatory upon her but she is entitled to it only as a non-hired favor; and an irrevocable divorce (talaq ba'in), where the marriage has ended and she becomes like a stranger, so hiring her is permissible. Duration of Maternal Custody of a Boy
Ruling on age limit for mother's custody of a boy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi criterion for the end of maternal custody of a boy and explain its connection to the age of prayer obligation.

Core Idea
Maternal custody ends when a boy can independently eat, drink, dress, and clean himself – estimated at seven years.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains the Hanafi ruling on when a mother's custody of her son terminates. The criterion is the child's ability to perform basic self-care tasks, which is linked to the obligation of prayer. Al-Khassaf estimated this age as seven years, but the actual rule depends on the child's independence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is maternal custody of a boy. The ruling is that the mother is entitled to custody until the boy can perform four tasks: eating alone, drinking alone, dressing alone, and cleaning himself (istinja).
2
Locate the dalil
The commentary ties the end of custody to the hadith: 'Command your children to pray at seven.' Since prayer requires cleanliness, a child who can perform istinja is ready for prayer and thus independent.
The proof is a hadith of zanni thubut.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The connection is that the child is commanded to pray at seven, so he must be able to purify himself. Hence, custody ends when he can do this, which is typically by seven.
Imperative 'command' indicates obligation, but the link is based on common practice.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (Shafi'i, Maliki) hold that custody continues until puberty. The Hanafi view is that after independence, the boy needs masculine discipline, which the father provides.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
Al-Khassaf's estimate of seven years is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi), but actual ability is the real criterion.
If a child is not independent by seven, custody continues.

Voluntary Acts Prohibited for the Mukatab
Ruling on the mukatab's inability to perform tatawwu'at except trivial charity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which voluntary acts a mukatab is prohibited from performing and identify the exception for trivial charity.

Core Idea
A mukatab cannot give gifts, pledge guarantees, or marry himself/his male slave; only small charity is allowed.

A mukatab has a special legal status: he owns his earnings but is restricted in disposing of wealth to protect the master's claim. This lesson examines the specific voluntary acts he may not perform and the rationale behind each prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mukatab's Status
Recognize that a mukatab is a slave under kitaba contract: he owns earnings but his disposal of property is limited.
2
List the Prohibited Acts
From the text, identify four prohibitions: giving gifts (hiba), charity (sadaqa) beyond trivial amount, acting as guarantor (kafala), and marrying himself or his male slave.
3
Note the Exception for Trivial Charity
The prohibition on charity is not absolute; small items like a loaf of bread, onion, or salt are permitted because they are insignificant.
The text specifies 'al-shay' al-yasir' (a small thing).
4
Understand the Rationale
Each prohibition prevents diminishing the master's property (through voluntary giving) or incurring debt without benefit (through marriage or guarantee).
For example, marrying a female slave is allowed because it brings earnings; marrying a male slave brings only debt.
5
Apply to New Scenarios
Using the principles, evaluate whether a given action by a mukatab is valid. Consider the amount, benefit, and whether it is a tatawwu'.

Ruling on Falcon Eating from Prey
Ruling on trained falcon eating from prey and its permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a trained falcon eating from the prey, identify the proof based on istihsan, and distinguish the Hanafi view from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Refraining from eating is not a condition of falcon training; the catch remains halal.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in hunting law: if a trained falcon eats from the prey it catches, is the prey still permissible to eat? The Hanafi school answers affirmatively, based on istihsan (juristic preference). We will unpack the rationale and compare it with the Shafi'i position, which requires the falcon to abstain from eating. Understanding this ruling clarifies how the conditions of animal training differ between species.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the act
The text states: 'If the falcon eats from it, it is eaten.' Determine the hukm (legal classification) and the act it governs. The act is hunting with a trained falcon; the ruling is that the prey is halal even if the falcon consumes part of it.
2
Locate the dalil and its type
The proof given is 'because refraining from eating is not a condition of its training.' This is an explicit use of istihsan (juristic preference), overriding any analogy (qiyas) that might compare the falcon to a dog. Note that the reasoning is based on the nature of falcon training (ta'lim) – the condition is responsiveness to the handler, not abstinence from eating.
3
Compare with the Shafi'i position
The Hanafi position differs from the Shafi'i school, which holds that a trained falcon must not eat from the prey for the catch to be valid. Understand the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf): in the Hanafi school, the catch is permissible; in the Shafi'i school, it is not.

Conditions for Continuity of a Hunting Animal's Command
Rulings on continuity of command for trained hunting animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for a hunting dog's continuity of command and apply them to distinguish permissible from impermissible capture.

Core Idea
A trained animal remains under the hunter's command only as long as its actions are means to the hunt, not independent.

This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah explores a subtle condition: the trained dog or falcon must remain within the original sent command. Prolonged sitting (juthum) breaks that link, while strategic ambush (kamun) and brief repositioning do not. We will work through the reasoning and the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What exactly is being ruled? The second prey caught after the dog sits on the first. The hukm: not halal. Then the second prey caught after ambush: halal. Also the falcon's second catch after brief landing: halal, provided the stay is not long.
Focus on the second prey, not the first. The first is halal by default.
2
Locate the underlying principle
The key principle is continuity of the original sending (irsal). The dog must remain acting under the hunter's command. Any action that indicates independence severs that link.
This principle is derived from the general permission to eat what trained animals catch, provided the hunter sent them and mentioned Allah's name.
3
Trace the talil (reasoning)
Why does prolonged sitting sever the command? Because the dog is no longer engaged in the hunt; it is resting. The pause is not a means to catch the next prey. In contrast, ambush (kamun) is a deliberate hunting tactic to position for the next catch, so it is a continuation. Similarly, a falcon landing briefly to reposition is like ambush; a long stay would break the sending.
The distinction is based on duration and intent: was the pause a means to hunt or a separate activity?
4
Apply the analogy to the falcon
The falcon case is analogous: if it lands on something (e.g., a tree) to get a better angle and immediately flies to catch, it is halal. If it stays long, it is like the dog's sitting.
The text explicitly adds the condition 'if it does not stay for a long time' for the falcon.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school holds that the dog's sitting does not break the sending; the second prey is halal. They focus on the dog's training, not the pause. The Hanafi school differentiates based on the duration indicating loss of command.
This ikhtilaf stems from different interpretations of what constitutes 'sending' and the animal's obedience.

Commentary
This passage addresses two scenarios. Ruling on Game Cut into Thirds
Ruling on game cut into thirds with anatomical reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on eating a game animal cut into thirds based on the location of the cut relative to the head.

Core Idea
If a cut severs the jugular veins, the game is halal; otherwise, the far part is carrion.

When a hunting weapon cuts the animal into two or three parts, the halal status depends on whether the cut passes through the major blood vessels near the head. The Hanafi school uses anatomical reasoning to determine which parts are edible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The animal is cut into three pieces. The ruling depends on where the larger portion of the cut lies: toward the tail or toward the head.
2
Apply the rule based on the cut's location
If the majority of the cut is from the side of the tail (meaning the cut is closer to the tail), then the whole animal is eaten. If the majority is from the side of the head, then only the larger portion (the part toward the head) is eaten, and the smaller portion (toward the tail) is not.
3
Understand the anatomical reasoning
The jugular veins (awdaj) run from the heart to the brain. If the cut is near the head, it severs these veins, like proper slaughter, making the entire animal halal. If the cut is near the tail, the head-end is halal but the tail-end is carrion because it was separated without severing the veins.
4
Relate to the general principle
The general principle 'mā ubīna min al-ḥayy' applies: any part cut from a living animal without proper severing of the blood vessels is carrion.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
If a hunter uses a shotgun that cuts a bird into two pieces, judge which piece contains the head and which the tail. The head-containing piece is halal; the tail piece is carrion if the cut did not pass through the neck.

Ruling on Udhiyah: Wajib or Sunnah?
Ruling on udhiyah with evidence, intra-Hanafi dispute, and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on udhiyah, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on its obligation level, and identify the five conditions that make it wajib.

Core Idea
Udhiyah is wajib per Abu Hanifa; its essence is shedding blood, and conditions include freedom, Islam, residency, wealth, and the day of Adha.

The opening line of this passage states plainly: 'الأضحية واجبة' — Udhiyah is wajib. But not all Hanafi imams agreed. In this lesson, you will dissect the ruling, the rational evidence provided, and the conditions that trigger the obligation. You will also learn why Abu Yusuf and Muhammad considered it a confirmed sunnah, and which position the school relies upon for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its evidence
Read the first part: the author declares udhiyah wajib and defines it as shedding blood from an'am. The evidence is aqli (rational): if you give the animal in charity alive, it does not suffice; therefore the worship lies in the blood, not in charity.
This is a causal argument: the act is defined by its essential element.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi dispute
Find where the text states 'وعن أبي يوسف: أنها سنة مؤكدة' and that Muhammad agreed via al-Tahawi. Note that Abu Hanifa's view is wajib, while the two companions hold it is sunnah mu'akkadah. This is an ikhtilaf within the madhhab.
The author mentions that wajib here means practical obligation, not creedal, so denier is not a disbeliever.
3
List the conditions for obligation
The text gives five conditions: free (not slave), Muslim, resident (muqim), wealthy (possessing yasar, like zakat nisab), and the day of Adha (10th–12th Dhul Hijjah). Each has a rationale explained in the commentary.
These are shurut al-wujub, not shurut al-sihhah.
4
Understand time and place restrictions
Udhiyah is obligatory on city dwellers, villagers, and desert residents. It is not obligatory on a traveler (musafir) even if he is wealthy, but it is obligatory on people of Mecca even during Hajj. The best time is the first day (yawm al-nahr) during daytime.
Slaughtering at night is valid but makruh.
5
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Consider a wealthy, free, Muslim resident on 10th Dhul Hijjah. According to the mufta bihi (wajib), he must sacrifice; if he does not, he is sinful. If he is a traveler, the obligation is lifted.
Contrast with the view of Abu Yusuf: no sin, but missed sunnah.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Quduri's Mukhtasar discusses the ruling on Udhiyah (sacrificial slaughter). The author begins by stating that Udhiyah is wajib (obligatory), based on the matn of al-Quduri. He defines udhiyah as the shedding of blood from an'am (cattle: camels, cows, sheep, goats), not other animals. The evidence (dalil) given is rational: if one were to give the animal in charity alive, it would not fulfill the obligation of udhiyah; therefore, the essence of the worship is the shedding of blood, not the charity. The commentator notes that charity with the meat after slaughter is mustahabb (recommended) but not wajib. A narration from al-Waqi'at emphasizes that purchasing an udhiyah is better than giving an equivalent amount in charity because the specific act of worship of shedding blood is not attained by charity. The commentator then clarifies that the statement 'udhiyah is wajib' is meant practically (amalan), not in terms of creed (i'tiqadan), so that denying its obligation does not lead to disbelief. Next, he presents the intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf considers it a sunnah mu'akkadah (confirmed sunnah), and al-Shafi'i agrees. Al-Tahawi also reports that Muhammad ibn al-Hasan held the same view as Abu Yusuf. Thus, the Hanafi school has two positions: wajib (Abu Hanifa) and sunnah (the two companions). The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is that it is wajib. The commentator then lists the conditions for obligation: the person must be free (not a slave), Muslim, resident (not traveling), wealthy (possessing the nisab), and it must be the day of Adha (10th, 11th, or 12th of Dhul Hijjah). The condition of freedom is because a slave does not own property; Islam because worship is not required from a disbeliever; residence because the obligation is dropped for travelers as they are excused from other duties; wealth because it is a financial worship like zakat; and the day because the act is linked to that time. Udhiyah is obligatory on city dwellers, villagers, and desert dwellers alike. It is not obligatory on a pilgrim who is traveling (i.e., in the state of ihram), but it is obligatory on residents of Mecca even if they perform Hajj. The recommended time for slaughter is during the day, though slaughtering at night is valid but makruh (disliked). The three days of Adha are all valid, with the first day being the best.

الأضحية (al-udhiyah): The sacrificial animal slaughtered on the days of Adha, specifically an'am (cattle).
إراقة الدم (iraqat al-dam): The act of causing blood to flow by slaughtering the animal; the essential act of udhiyah.
النعم (al-an'am): Cattle: specifically camels, cows, sheep, and goats. Other animals are not valid for udhiyah.
واجبة (wajibah): Legally obligatory (wajib) in the sense that it is required, but denial does not cause disbelief (not fard).
سنة مؤكدة (sunnah mu'akkadah): A confirmed sunnah; regularly performed by the Prophet but not obligatory; leaving it is disliked but not sinful.
يسار (yasar): Wealth sufficient to purchase an udhiyah (similar to the nisab threshold for zakat).
يوم الأضحى (yawm al-adha): The day of sacrifice, i.e., the 10th of Dhul Hijjah, and the two following days.
