The Legal Definition of Emancipation: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the definition of emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two Hanafi definitions of emancipation, trace their proofs, and apply the thamarah al-khilaf to the case of partial emancipation.

Core Idea
Emancipation removes ownership (Abu Hanifa) or establishes freedom (Sahibayn) — the difference determines whether half-free is free or a mukatib.

This passage from Kitāb al-ʿItq in Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah presents a foundational intra-Hanafi dispute: what is the legal nature of emancipation (iʿtāq)? Abu Hanifa views it as the removal of ownership (izālat al-milk), which is divisible; the Sahibayn view it as the establishment of freedom (ithbāt al-ʿitq) and removal of slavery (izālat al-riqq), both indivisible. This seemingly theoretical difference has a concrete legal consequence: when a master frees only part of his slave, the status of that slave changes dramatically depending on which definition is adopted.

Guided Path
1
Identify both definitions
From the text, extract the precise definitions: Abu Hanifa says emancipation is 'removal of ownership' (izālat al-milk); Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say it is 'establishment of freedom and removal of slavery' (ithbāt al-ʿitq wa izālat al-riqq). Note that both agree on the act itself but differ on its legal characterization.
The text explicitly states: 'ولأبي حنيفة: أن الإعتاق: إثباتُ العتق بإزالة الملك، أو هو إزالة الملك' and 'وقال أبو يوسف ومحمد الإعتاق: إثبات العتق، وإزالة الرق'.
2
Analyze the underlying premises
Abu Hanifa's view rests on the premise that ownership (milk) is a private right that is divisible, while slavery (riqq) is a divine penalty (haqq Allāh) that is indivisible and can only be removed incidentally. The Sahibayn view rests on the premise that slavery, as a penalty, cannot apply to half a person; therefore, if part is freed, the whole must be free. Also, note the analogy: emancipation is like informing (iʿlām), which establishes knowledge and removes ignorance simultaneously, both indivisible.
The text compares wuḍūʾ limbs (divisible) with prayer permissibility (indivisible) and divorce counts (indivisible).
3
Examine the proofs for each view
Abu Hanifa supports his view by stating that ownership can be partially removed, as in selling half a slave, but freedom cannot be partial. He gives examples: limbs of wuḍūʾ are divisible, but permissibility of prayer is indivisible; divorce counts are indivisible. The Sahibayn argue by analogy: just as informing establishes knowledge and removes ignorance (both indivisible), emancipation establishes freedom and removes slavery. They also argue that a penalty (slavery) cannot be conceived on half a body.
The co-ownership example: if one co-owner frees his share, it extends to the other's share — showing that emancipation cannot be partial in its effect.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The critical difference: if a master says 'I free half of my slave', according to Abu Hanifa, the slave does not become free but becomes a mukātab (a slave under contract to earn his freedom), obligated to pay half his value. According to the Sahibayn, the slave becomes fully free immediately. This is the practical outcome that matters in fatwa.
The text explicitly states: 'فبإعتاق البعض: لا يثبت شيء من العتق... وإنما هو مكاتب' (Abu Hanifa) and implies that for the Sahibayn, full freedom is established.
5
Synthesize the broader principle
This dispute illustrates how a definitional choice in fiqh generates different rulings. The definition of an act (here, iʿtāq) is not merely terminological; it determines which legal effects apply. Recognizing the divisibility or indivisibility of associated concepts is a key tool in Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh.
The text ends with: 'فأحكم هذا الأصل، واحفظه، ففيه فقه كثير.' (Master this principle, for there is much fiqh in it.)

Commentary
This passage from Kitab al-Itq in Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the legal definition of emancipation (i'tāq). The text first states Abu Hanifa's view: emancipation is the removal of ownership (izālat al-milk) from the slave, not the establishment of freedom per se. The reasoning is that ownership is the slave's right, and slavery is the right of Allah (a penalty), and a person can only remove his own right (ownership), not Allah's right (slavery) directly. Emancipation removes ownership intentionally, and slavery is removed incidentally. Furthermore, ownership is divisible (one can own half a slave), but freedom is indivisible (one cannot be half-free). Therefore, if someone frees only half of his slave, according to Abu Hanifa, no part of freedom is established; instead, the slave becomes a mukatib (a slave contracted to earn his freedom) who cannot be sold or gifted, but if he fails to pay, he is not returned to slavery. This differs from a deliberate kitabah contract. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree: they define emancipation as the establishment of freedom (ithbāt al-ʿitq) and removal of slavery, both of which are indivisible. Just as knowledge is established and ignorance removed simultaneously, and both are indivisible, so slavery as a penalty cannot apply to half a person. Hence, if part of the slave is freed, the whole becomes free, analogous to divorce. The practical consequence: for Abu Hanifa, freeing half results in a mukatib status; for the Sahibayn, it results in full freedom immediately. The text advises to master this principle as it has many applications.

Key Terms
الإعتاق (al-i'tāq): Legal emancipation of a slave; its definition is disputed: removal of ownership (Abu Hanifa) vs. establishment of freedom (Sahibayn).
إزالة الملك (izālat al-milk): Removal of ownership; Abu Hanifa's definition of i'tāq, which is divisible.
الرق (al-riqq): The legal state of slavery; considered a penalty from Allah, thus indivisible.
مكاتب (mukātab): A slave under a kitabah contract to earn his freedom; here, the status of a slave whose part was freed according to Abu Hanifa.

Nature of Manumission: Removal of Ownership or Establishment of Freedom?
Explaining intra-Hanafi dispute on manumission as removal of ownership vs establishment of freedom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the nature of manumission and explain the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) regarding the legal status of the mustas'i (the slave performing sa'y).

Core Idea
Manumission: removal of ownership (Abu Hanifa) or establishment of freedom (Sahibayn)? The answer changes the slave's legal capacity.

This passage from the Book of Manumission (Kitab al-Itq) delves into a foundational intra-Hanafi dispute: is the act of freeing a slave fundamentally about removing the master's ownership, or about establishing the slave's freedom? While this may seem theoretical, it directly impacts the rulings that follow—especially when one co-owner frees his share of a jointly owned slave. Understanding this dispute is key to mastering many derivative rulings (furu') in the chapter of 'itq.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify what Abu Hanifa (ra) holds: manumission is essentially the removal of ownership (izalat al-milk). The removal of slavery (izalat al-riqq) occurs incidentally, as a consequence.
The passage states: 'عنده: إزالة الملك قصداً، والرق ضمناً وتبعاً' (according to him: removal of ownership intentionally, and slavery incidentally and secondarily).
2
Identify the opposing view and its proof
State the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn): manumission is the establishment of freedom (ithbat al-'itq), which intrinsically removes slavery. Their proof: freedom is indivisible, like knowledge or divorce; once part is established, the whole is established.
The passage compares it to 'الإعلام: إثبات العلم، وإزالة الجهل' (informing: establishing knowledge and removing ignorance), both indivisible.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Detail the Sahibayn's rational argument: ownership is divisible by consensus (ijma'), but manumission is disputed. Slavery is a punishment (uquba), and a punishment cannot be half. Therefore, if any part of the slave becomes free, the whole must be free.
The passage says: 'الرق عقوبة، والعقوبة لا يتصور وجوبها على النصف' (slavery is a punishment, and a punishment cannot be obligatory on half).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response (implicit)
Abu Hanifa's position is that ownership is divisible, so a person can remove only his own share of ownership. The slave becomes fully free because the effect of freeing a part necessarily extends to the whole—but the slave owes the co-owner compensation through sa'y (earning). The mustas'i is like a mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) until he pays.
The passage does not give an explicit rebuttal, but the practical differences are spelled out.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is the legal status of the mustas'i. According to Abu Hanifa, he is like a mukatab (limited capacity: no inheritance, can't marry, etc.). According to Sahibayn, he is like a free debtor (full capacity, but owes a debt).
This difference touches inheritance, marriage, testimony, and return on failure of payment.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Manumission (Kitab al-Itq) is a commentary expanding on the matn of al-Quduri. It begins by stating the legal principle that slavery is a penalty imposed by the Law (shar‘) as a consequence of refusing Islam and submission. The penalty is a right of Allah, and a human cannot intentionally nullify another's right, but can do so incidentally. This is illustrated by the case of joint ownership: if one co-owner frees his own share, the effect extends to the partner's share; but if he directly frees the partner's share, it is invalid. The core of the passage is the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa (on one side) and Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) on the nature of manumission (i‘taq). Abu Hanifa holds that manumission is essentially the removal of ownership (izalat al-milk), and the termination of slavery (izalat al-riqq) happens incidentally. Since ownership is divisible, he can only remove his own share of ownership; but the removal of his share necessarily causes the slave to become partially free, and because freedom is indivisible, the slave becomes fully free, but he must perform sa‘y (earning) to compensate the partner for the value of the partner's share. The Sahibayn hold that manumission is the establishment of freedom (ithbat al-‘itq) and the removal of slavery intentionally, with removal of ownership as a secondary effect. Since freedom is indivisible, if any part of it is established, the whole is established, like divorce. Thus, according to them, when one co-owner frees his share, the entire slave becomes free immediately, and the freed slave owes the partner the value of his share as a debt, similar to a free debtor. The commentator stresses the importance of understanding this principle ('asl) and memorizing it, as it has many furu‘ (branches). The passage then discusses the status of the mustas‘i (the slave performing sa‘y). According to Abu Hanifa, the mustas‘i is like a mukatab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom) in that he works to free his neck from the remaining slavery. Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the options of a co-owner (sharik) when his partner (al-mu‘tiq) emancipates his share of a jointly owned slave. The author first states the base ruling from the matn, then expands on it, and finally contrasts the positions of Abu Hanifa with those of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn).

According to Abu Hanifa, if the emancipating partner is wealthy (mûsir), the other co-owner has three choices: (1) He may also free his own share, and the patronage (wala’) is shared between them, since Abu Hanifa holds that emancipation is divisible. If the emancipating partner is poor (mu‘sir), Abu Hanifa says the co-owner has only two choices: free his share or extract from the slave; he cannot demand guarantee because the poor partner cannot pay. In both cases, patronage is shared.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree fundamentally. They argue that when the emancipating partner is wealthy, the co-owner has no option but to demand guarantee (tadmin). He cannot free his share because they hold that emancipation is indivisible, so the whole act is from the first emancipator. He also cannot extract from the slave because that would be unjust when a wealthy person is liable. The entire patronage goes to the first emancipator. If the emancipator is poor, the co-owner can only extract from the slave; again, patronage goes entirely to the first emancipator. They deny the co-owner's right to free his share.

The text then notes that by consensus, the slave who pays through istis‘a’ cannot seek reimbursement from the poor emancipator, because he is buying his own freedom, not paying a debt. A contrasting scenario with a mortgaged slave (al-marhun) is provided: if a poor mortgagor frees a mortgaged slave, the slave can extract the lesser of his value or the debt and then recoup from the mortgagor, because he is either freeing himself or paying the mortgagor’s debt.

The legal reasoning here is based on qiyas and istihsan, with the key point of ikhtilaf being whether partial emancipation is possible (Abu Hanifa) or not (Sahibayn). The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that under Abu Hanifa, the co-owner has more flexibility, while under the Sahibayn, the first emancipator's act dominates completely.

Abu Hanifa's Ruling for Three Co-Owners: Options for the Third Owner
Explaining Abu Hanifa's ruling when second owner emancipates after first in a three-owner case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three options of the third co-owner according to Abu Hanifa and explain why the second freeer cannot be held liable.

Core Idea
The third owner can only claim against the first freeer because wala' attaches to him irrevocably.

This passage addresses a scenario where three people co-own a slave, and the first two free their shares consecutively. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the third owner retains options against the first freeer, while the second's emancipation is valid but does not create liability against him. This ruling contrasts with the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, who hold that the first emancipation frees the entire slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Three co-owners (A, B, C) own a slave. A frees his share, then B frees his share. Determine the legal options for C.
The slave is not fully free yet; only two-thirds are free.
2
List Abu Hanifa's three options for C
C can (1) hold A liable for the value of his share if A is wealthy, (2) free his own share to equal the others, or (3) make the slave work (sa‘yah) to earn his share's value.
These options are mutually exclusive; C chooses one.
3
Explain why C cannot hold B liable
The right to claim transfers to A because the first emancipation is the operative one, and wala' attaches to A. Once attached, wala' cannot be annulled, so B is immune from C's claim.
Wala' is a permanent bond between the freeer and the freed slave.
4
Describe what happens if C chooses ḍamān against A
If C holds A liable, A then has the option to either free the slave outright (since the remaining share now belongs to A) or make the slave work. A cannot hold B liable because C could not.
A stands in C's place regarding rights.
5
Compare with Sahibayn's view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that when A freed, the entire slave became free automatically due to the indivisibility of emancipation, so B's emancipation is void. Therefore, C only has a claim for his share's value from A, with no choice to free or make the slave work.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence of the dispute).

Subsequent changes in the slave's value do not affect liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when co-owners testify against each other's share according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf regarding sa'yah and the status of the slave.

Core Idea
When co-owners testify each other's share free, the slave is free but may owe sa'yah — but whether he owes it depends on the owners' wealth according to the Sahibayn.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute where two co-owners of a slave each claim the other's share is free. The central question: does the slave must compensate through labor (sa'yah)? Abu Hanifa says always; the Sahibayn differentiate based on wealth. Understanding this reveals deeper principles about daman (recourse) and sa'yah in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core case
Two co-owners each testify that the other's share in a slave is free. Neither confirms the other's claim. The slave becomes free, but what compensation is due?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Abu Hanifa: the slave must perform sa'yah for both shares regardless of the owners' wealth. Each owner claims recourse (daman) against the other, but since the other denies it, recourse fails; thus the slave must compensate through labor.
The patronage (wala') is shared between the two owners.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn): if both owners are wealthy, no sa'yah (they are presumed to have released the slave). If both are poor, sa'yah for both. If one wealthy and one poor, sa'yah only for the wealthy owner. Their principle: sa'yah is not established when the owner has the means to seek recourse but does not.
The poor owner cannot claim sa'yah because he believes he has recourse against the wealthy owner (which fails), so he is considered to have released the slave.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the slave remains in a suspended state (mustas'a, like a mukatab) until he pays. Under the Sahibayn, he is immediately free, with sa'yah only if owners are not wealthy. Regarding patronage (wala'), Abu Hanifa says it is shared; the Sahibayn say it is suspended until the owners agree.
5
Apply to a scenario
Take a case: both owners are wealthy. Apply both views: Abu Hanifa says sa'yah; Sahibayn say no sa'yah. If one wealthy, one poor: Abu Hanifa says sa'yah for both; Sahibayn say sa'yah only for the wealthy.
The practical difference is immediate freedom vs. continued obligation.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the legal consequence when two co-owners of a slave each testify that the other's share is free. The Imam's position (Abu Hanifa) is that the slave becomes entirely free but must work (sa'yah) to compensate each co-owner for the value of his share, regardless of whether the owners are wealthy or poor. This is because each co-owner claims that his partner's share was freed by the partner's own act (so he would be entitled to recourse against the partner) but the partner denies it, so recourse fails; thus the slave must compensate through labor. The patronage (wala') belongs to both co-owners equally. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differentiate based on the owners' financial status: if both are wealthy, no sa'yah is required (since they could have recourse against each other but choose not to, it is considered a release of the slave); if both are poor, the slave must work for both; if one is wealthy and the other poor, the slave works only for the wealthy one. Their reasoning is that a wealthy owner has no recourse against a poor partner, so he may claim from the slave; the poor owner, however, considers himself released because he believes he has recourse against the wealthy partner (though it fails), so he cannot claim from the slave. Regarding patronage (wala'), the Sahibayn hold it is suspended until the owners agree, whereas Abu Hanifa views the slave as akin to a mukatab—still a slave until he pays off the compensation. The passage also addresses a case where only one co-owner testifies: his testimony is binding on himself (he must free his share) but not on the other; no liability falls on the testifier because he did not cause emancipation of the other's share.

Key Terms
سعاية (sa'yah): The work a slave performs to compensate his owner for the value of his freedom when partial emancipation occurs without full compensation.
الضمان (ad-daman): Recourse that a co-owner seeks against his partner when the latter's act (or admission) causes loss of the former's share.
الولاء (al-wala'): The right of inheritance and loyalty that belongs to the emancipator over a freed slave.
المستسعى (al-mustas'a): A slave who is required to work (sa'yah) to compensate his owner for freedom; equivalent in status to a mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) according to Abu Hanifa.

Sa'ayah When Co-Owners Differ in Wealth: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Ruling and intra-Hanafi dispute on sa'ayah with wealth disparity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on sa'ayah when co-owners differ in wealth and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Only the wealthy partner demands sa'ayah; the poor partner's claim transforms into compensation against the wealthy, but the slave's freedom status is disputed.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi fiqh: two co-owners jointly manumit a slave, but one is wealthy (able to pay compensation) and the other is poor. The key question is whether the poor partner can demand sa'ayah (the slave's earnings to buy freedom) or whether his claim is shifted to the wealthy partner. The passage presents an intra-Hanafi disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two co-owners both manumit their shares of a slave. One is wealthy (mūsir), the other poor (mu‘sir). The slave's status and the rights of each partner are in question.
The passage assumes the manumission is valid; the dispute is about the consequences.
2
Understand the general rule for sa'ayah
In principle, when a co-owner manumits his share without the other's consent, the slave may be required to earn the value of the remaining share (sa'ayah). Here, both manumit, so the situation differs.
Sa'ayah is akin to a mukatabah contract – the slave works to pay off his freedom.
3
Analyze the wealthy partner's position
The wealthy partner says: I cannot get compensation from my poor partner, so I have a right to demand sa'ayah from the slave. This is agreed upon by all.
4
Analyze the poor partner's position
The poor partner says: My partner (the wealthy one) is liable to compensate me for his manumission, so I consider the slave released from sa'ayah. He effectively refers his claim to the wealthy partner.
The poor partner does not demand sa'ayah because he expects compensation from the wealthy partner.
5
Compare the two positions on the slave's status
Abu Hanifa holds that the slave remains enslaved until he pays the entire sa'ayah (for both shares). Sahibayn hold that the slave is free immediately upon joint manumission; sa'ayah is only due from the poor partner's share if the wealthy partner is unable to pay compensation.
This difference affects whether the slave can be forced to work to earn freedom or is immediately free.

Testimony of One Co-owner Against the Other: Sa'ayah and Wala'
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sa'ayah and wala'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding sa'ayah and wala' when one co-owner testifies against the other, and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Sa'ayah rights differ based on the denier's wealth; Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn split on the witness's claim.

In Hanafi law, when one co-owner of a slave testifies that his partner manumitted the slave, the testimony binds only the witness. The key dispute concerns whether the witness can demand sa'ayah (earnings toward freedom) from the slave. Abu Hanifa allows it regardless of the denier's wealth, while the Sahibayn restrict it to cases where the denier is poor. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two co-owners (A and B) own a slave. A testifies that B manumitted the slave. B denies it. Determine the legal position of each.
The testimony is only binding on A's own share (via iqrar), not on B.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position on sa'ayah
Abu Hanifa holds that both the witness (A) and the denier (B) can demand sa'ayah from the slave, irrespective of B's wealth. A claims either compensation or sa'ayah; since compensation is denied, sa'ayah remains. B maintains his share is his property and may claim sa'ayah.
Key principle: sa'ayah is a property right independent of wealth.
3
State the Sahibayn's position on sa'ayah
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say the witness (A) can demand sa'ayah only if the denier (B) is poor. If B is wealthy, A's only claim is compensation (which he cannot prove), so he has no sa'ayah. B always has sa'ayah regardless of his own wealth.
Distinction based on B's financial status: if wealthy, A loses sa'ayah.
4
Compare the ruling on wala' (patronage)
Abu Hanifa: wala' is shared between the two co-owners. Sahibayn: wala' is suspended because A claims wala' is for B, and B denies A's claim.
Suspension means wala' is undetermined until resolved.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, the slave must pay sa'ayah to both co-owners. Under Sahibayn, if B is wealthy, the slave pays only B; if B is poor, the slave pays both. Also, wala' is certain in Abu Hanifa's view but uncertain in the Sahibayn's.
Practical impact on the slave's obligations and the owners' rights.

A master tells his slave-girl, 'If you give birth to a child, it is free.' She delivers a stillborn first, then a live child. Is the live child free? Commentary

Explains ruling when master says 'you are free upon a sum'

Core Idea
In mu'awwad 'itq, acceptance in the session makes the slave free immediately and the amount a binding debt.

The key principle is that when a master uses a formula like 'you are free upon a sum' and the slave accepts in the same assembly, the slave becomes free instantly and owes that sum as a debt. Understanding this ruling involves distinguishing between immediate emancipation and conditional emancipation upon payment, as well as the scope of 'mal' and the effect of ignorance in specification.

Conditional Freedom upon Payment: Ruling and Conditions
Ruling on conditional freedom upon payment of money

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on conditional emancipation upon payment of money and explain the conditions under which the slave becomes free.

Core Idea
Freedom is conditional upon actual payment; mere promise or master's remission does not free the slave.

This lesson continues our study of mu'awwad 'itq. We now examine the case where the master suspends freedom upon the slave's payment of a sum. The key distinction: the slave does not become free upon the master's declaration alone; rather, freedom is tied to the actual delivery of money. The Hanafi school rules that the judge may compel the master to accept payment, but only full payment triggers emancipation. This passage also clarifies the slave's intermediate status as a ma'dhun and distinguishes this case from kitabah.

1
Identify the condition for freedom
The master says: 'If you pay me 1000, you are free.' Freedom is suspended on the condition of payment.
This is distinct from the first type where freedom is immediate upon acceptance.
2
Determine status before payment
Until the condition is fulfilled, the slave remains fully owned; the master may sell him.
The slave is not free by mere promise.
3
Analyze the effect of full payment
When the slave pays the full sum, the judge compels the master to accept; the slave becomes free.
The compulsion (ijbar) is a legal construct ensuring the slave's right.
4
Examine partial payment
The master is compelled to accept partial payment, but the slave is not freed until the full amount is paid.
The condition requires complete fulfillment.
5
Consider master's remission or death
If the master remits any part or all of the debt, the slave is neither released nor freed, unlike a mukatib. If the master dies before payment, the declaration becomes void.
Freedom is tied to actual payment, not mere forgiveness.

Commentary
