Testimony on Emancipation Without Claim: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on testimony admissibility without claim for emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on testimony for emancipation without a claim and explain why the Sahibayn's view is the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
Testimony on emancipation: Sahibayn accept for Allah's right; Abu Hanifa requires claim due to slave's benefit.

In Hanafi court procedure, a prior claim (da'wa) is typically required before testimony is heard. But what if two witnesses testify that a master freed his slave, and the slave denies it? This lesson examines a well-known disagreement within the Hanafi school between Abu Hanifa and his students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) over whether the testimony is accepted without a claim. The dispute hinges on whether emancipation primarily involves a right of Allah or a right of the slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the disagreement
State the scenario: two witnesses testify against a master that he freed his slave, but the slave denies it. Abu Hanifa says testimony is not accepted without a claim; the Sahibayn say it is accepted.
Note that if the slave were a female slave, there is consensus that testimony is accepted without claim.
2
Understand the dual nature of emancipation
Emancipation contains both a right of Allah (prohibition of re-enslavement) and rights of the slave (ownership, removal of oppression). The dispute is over which is predominant.
This is a key Hanafi analytical tool: distinguishing huquq Allah from huquq al-'ibad.
3
Trace the reasoning of the Sahibayn
The Sahibayn argue that the predominant element is the right of Allah because emancipation triggers obligations like zakat, sacrifice, and Friday prayer on the freed person. Therefore, testimony on a right of Allah is accepted without a claim.
They analogize to divorce and freeing a female slave, which are accepted by consensus without a claim.
4
Contrast with Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that the main purpose of emancipation is the benefit of the slave, similar to property claims. Since property claims require a claim, so should emancipation testimony.
He acknowledges exceptions for divorce and freeing a female slave because those involve sexual rights, which are purely divine.
5
Determine the practical consequence and mufta bihi
According to the Sahibayn, if testimony is accepted, the slave is freed. According to Abu Hanifa, the testimony is rejected and the slave remains enslaved. Later Hanafi practice follows the Sahibayn for facilitation.
This thamarah al-khilaf shows how a difference in usul classification leads to different outcomes in court.

Core Idea
The acceptance of testimony depends on whether the primary legal purpose is a right of Allah or a right of the individual.

In Hanafi law, the rules of testimony differ based on the nature of the right involved. This passage presents a classic intra-madhhab disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, on whether testimony to prove manumission is accepted without a claim from the slave. The crux lies in determining the predominant element in manumission: is it a right of Allah or a right of the slave?

If testimony is given without a claim: under Sahibayn the slave becomes free; under Abu Hanifa the slave remains enslaved until a claim is filed.
This practical difference affects the outcome in court.

The text does not specify a mufta bihi, but later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn view.
Students should note that this is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.

Claims, Oaths, and Testimony in Qasamah: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Rulings on specific claims, oaths, and testimony in qasamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on specific claims in qasamah, the oath of the accused, and the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding testimony from locality members.

Core Idea
A specific claim can exonerate the locality only if the accused is an outsider; testimony from insiders against outsiders is disputed.

This passage addresses procedural complexities in qasamah (collective oath) cases. When the guardian makes a specific accusation, the liability of the locality may or may not drop depending on who is accused. The accused's attempt to shift blame is handled by a special oath. The central legal dispute concerns whether testimony by two locality members against an outsider is accepted. Understanding these rules requires careful attention to the identity of the accused and the witnesses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the effect of a specific claim
Determine whether claiming against a person within or outside the locality removes qasamah from the locality. Note the ruling and the reasoning (ibrā').
The key distinction: insider vs. outsider.
2
Analyze the oath of the accused
When the accused (mustahlaf) names another as the killer, he is made to swear denying his own involvement and asserting only that other as known killer. Why is this oath structured this way?
The accused's self-serving statement cannot end the lawsuit; the oath protects his own liability.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on testimony
Contrast Abu Hanifa's position (testimony not accepted) with the Sahibayn's (testimony accepted). Identify the illah (reason) behind each view.
The disagreement hinges on whether the witnesses are considered biased when litigation is pending against all locality members.
4
Recognize the consensus scenario
If the accused is from the same locality and two locality members testify against him, all agree the testimony is rejected. Understand why unanimity exists here.
The bias is direct: the witnesses benefit by removing liability from themselves.

This passage covers the procedural rules when the legal guardian (wali) makes a specific claim in a qasamah case. This illustrates the Hanafi principle of preventing self-serving testimony in qasamah cases to ensure fairness.

Secret rectification is accepted from women, slaves, and those punished for qadhf because it is like reporting, not testimony.

In the Hanafi madhhab, a judge must verify the character of witnesses before accepting their testimony. This process, called tazkiyah, can be done secretly (sirr) or openly (‘alaniyah). Abu Hanifa draws a key distinction: secret rectification is treated like a simple report (ikhbar) of a religious matter, while open rectification is equivalent to full testimony (shahadah). This lesson unpacks who is qualified to provide each type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and general ruling
The passage states that when witnesses testify to an old hadd crime (e.g., theft, zina, drinking), the testimony is not accepted for the hadd punishment.
Old (متقادم) means the crime occurred some time ago, not immediately reported.
2
Note the exception
The only exception is the hadd of slander (قذف). Testimony for slander is accepted even if delayed.
Slander is a crime against personal reputation, considered a right of the individual.
3
Understand the rationale for exception
The hadd for slander protects the victim's honor; the right to clear one's name does not expire with time. The delay does not weaken the claim as much as in other hadd crimes.
Other hadd crimes like theft and zina are considered rights of God, and delay may raise doubts.
4
Apply the ruling to theft
Even though the hadd punishment for theft is not applied, the civil liability remains: the stolen property (or its value) must be returned.
This shows that civil rights are not extinguished by delay, only the punitive hadd.
5
Consider the practical effect
This rule encourages timely testimony and prevents malicious prosecution based on stale evidence.
The Hanafi school prioritizes certainty in hudud punishments.

That is, if they testify to theft, drinking wine, or zina after some time, it is not acted upon, and in theft, the property is guaranteed (i.e., the thief must return the stolen property or its value).

Commentary
This passage discusses the acceptance of testimony for hadd crimes that are old (i.e., reported after a delay). The rule is that such testimony is not accepted for the hadd punishment itself, with the exception of the hadd for slander (qadhf). For other hadd crimes like theft, drinking alcohol, or zina, if witnesses come forward after a period of time, the hadd is not implemented. However, in theft, the stolen property or its value is still recovered from the thief as a civil right. The reason for the exception for slander is that slander is a crime against individuals and the hadd is intended to protect reputation, so delay does not invalidate the testimony. The passage also mentions that the witnesses' distance from the Imam is not a barrier.

Key Terms
حد القذف (hadd al-qadhf): The fixed punishment for slander, i.e., falsely accusing someone of zina, which is 80 lashes.
حد متقادم (hadd mutaqadim): An old hadd crime; a crime that occurred some time ago.
