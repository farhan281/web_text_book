Combining Months and Menses in a Slave Woman's 'Iddah
Ruling and reasoning for combining months and menses in slave woman's 'iddah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the 'iddah of a slave woman when both husband and master die in unknown order, and identify the precautionary reasoning (ihtiyat) used by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
When the order of deaths is unknown, precaution requires observing the maximum 'iddah—combining both months and menses.

This passage presents a specific case in Hanafi fiqh: a slave woman whose husband and master both die, but the order is unknown. How long must she wait? The two leading disciples of Abu Hanifa—Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani—rule that she must combine the 'iddah of a widow (four months and ten days) with the 'iddah of a divorced slave woman (three menstrual cycles). This lesson unpacks their precautionary reasoning and contrasts it with the position of Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
A slave woman: her husband dies and her master also dies. The order of deaths is unknown.
The slave woman's 'iddah is affected by both marriage and slavery.
2
Identify the disputed ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) require her to observe both four months and ten days (widow's 'iddah) and three menstrual cycles (divorcee's 'iddah). Abu Hanifa likely requires only one of the two based on the most likely order.
The phrase 'عندهما' marks the Sahibayn's view.
3
Trace the reasoning
The reasoning is ihtiyat (precaution). If the husband died first, the widow's 'iddah (months) applies; if the master died first, the 'iddah upon emancipation (menses) applies. Since the order is unknown, both must be observed to be safe.
Ihtiyat is a key principle in Hanafi fiqh for cases of doubt.
4
Apply the reasoning
Consider each possibility: (a) husband dies first → she observes 4 months 10 days, then master dies → no further 'iddah? Actually, the 'iddah of death is already served; (b) master dies first → she begins 'iddah of menses, then husband dies → she must switch? The Sahibayn combine both to cover all possibilities.
The combination ensures the 'iddah is valid regardless of sequence.
5
Note the practical difference (thamarah)
Under Abu Hanifa's view, she might only need one 'iddah (whichever occurred first). Under the Sahibayn, she must complete both, which is longer and more restrictive.
The mufta bihi in this commentary is the Sahibayn's view.

Ruling on the 'Iddah of a Wife Purchased and Freed by Her Husband
Detailed ruling on 'iddah of a purchased and freed wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the 'iddah requirements when a husband purchases and frees his wife, and distinguish between the 'iddah from marriage (with iḥdād) and from emancipation (without iḥdād).

Core Idea
A purchased-and-freed wife observes two menses from marriage (with iḥdād) and one from emancipation (without iḥdād).

This lesson examines a complex scenario in Hanafi family law: a husband purchases his own wife, invalidating the marriage, then frees her, creating a dual 'iddah obligation. The passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira breaks down the waiting period into components from marriage and emancipation, each with distinct rulings on iḥdād.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering events
Determine which actions cause the 'iddah: purchase (invalidates marriage) and emancipation (frees the slave). These events create two separate 'iddah obligations.
2
Determine the number of menstrual cycles
The wife must observe three full menstrual cycles: two due to the dissolution of marriage, and one due to emancipation.
The first two menses are from the marriage; the third is from emancipation.
3
Distinguish the laws of iḥdād
During the first two menses, the wife must observe iḥdād (avoiding adornment) as is required for 'iddah from marriage. The third mense does not require iḥdād because it is from emancipation.
This is a key difference between the two types of 'iddah.
4
Consider the sub-case of prior ba'in divorce
If the husband had already divorced her irrevocably before purchasing her, the purchase makes her permissible for intercourse again (like a new marriage). Her 'iddah is then only from emancipation.
Ownership acts as a fresh cause for permissibility.
5
Handle timing of menses before emancipation
If she completes two menses (from marriage) before emancipation, the marriage 'iddah is fulfilled, and she must only observe three menses from emancipation (without iḥdād).
The two menses do not count toward emancipation 'iddah.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed ruling statement from the commentary explaining a scenario where a husband purchases his own wife (who has a child from him) and then emancipates her. The commentator elaborates on the 'iddah obligations. The ruling is that she must observe three menstrual cycles: two for the dissolution of the marriage (caused by the purchase, which invalidates the marriage contract) and one for the emancipation (because she was a slave). During the first two menses, she must observe the restrictions of an 'iddah from marriage (like avoiding adornment, i.e., ihdad), but during the third menses from emancipation, she does not observe ihdad because the 'iddah of a slave upon emancipation does not require mourning. The reasoning is that upon purchase, the marriage becomes fasid (invalid), so she enters 'iddah regarding others (she cannot marry anyone else) but not regarding the husband, because he still has the right of ownership which permits intercourse. Once he frees her, the right of ownership that prevented full 'iddah from him disappears, so she enters 'iddah regarding everyone, including him. The commentator also provides sub-rulings: if the husband had already divorced her with a ba'in (irrevocable) divorce before purchasing her, then purchase makes her permissible for him again, as ownership is analogous to a new marriage. Also, if she completes two menses before emancipation, then the 'iddah from marriage is fulfilled and she only needs to observe three more menses from emancipation. These rulings are cited from al-Karkhi, indicating transmission from earlier Hanafi sources.

Iddah of a Pregnant Widow of a Minor Husband: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on 'iddah of a widow of a minor husband, with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the 'iddah of a widow of a minor husband and apply the Qur'anic verse (al-Talaq 4) to determine her 'iddah based on the two positions.

Core Idea
Even though a minor cannot father children, the 'iddah of his pregnant widow is determined by delivery according to the majority Hanafi view.

This lesson examines a specific case within Hanafi fiqh: the 'iddah of a woman whose husband dies as a minor, and she is pregnant. The general Qur'anic rule for pregnant widows is that their 'iddah ends with delivery. However, because a minor cannot produce semen, some scholars question whether the pregnancy 'counts' for 'iddah purposes. The Hanafi school has an internal dispute, with Abu Yusuf differing from the majority position. You will learn to trace the legal reasoning and see how the Qur'anic text is applied.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling given in the matn (Mukhtasar al-Quduri) for a pregnant widow of a minor husband: her 'iddah is until delivery.
This is the position of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), but note that Abu Yusuf himself later dissented.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Abu Yusuf (in his separate opinion) holds that even if the woman has an apparent pregnancy at death, her 'iddah is four months and ten days. This is the intra-Hanafi disagreement.
Abu Yusuf's reasoning: since the child cannot be attributed to the minor husband, the pregnancy is treated like one occurring after death.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine Abu Yusuf's dalil: the child's lineage is not established from a minor (as he has no semen), so the pregnancy is legally equivalent to a post-death pregnancy. The ruling for post-death pregnancy is four months and ten days.
Know that a minor is assumed not to produce semen in Hanafi law.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Understand the response of the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) to Abu Yusuf's objection: they rely on the general Qur'anic verse (Surah al-Talaq: 4) which makes delivery the end of 'iddah for all pregnant women, without exception. The verse does not condition on paternity.
Their argument is that the Qur'anic text is general (amm) and applies regardless of whether the pregnancy is attributable to the deceased husband.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Calculate the practical difference: if she gives birth soon after death, under the view of the two, her 'iddah ends quickly; under Abu Yusuf's view, she must wait four months and ten days even if she delivers earlier.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is the position of the two, as per the matn.

Maximum and Minimum Duration of Pregnancy
Ruling with Quranic proof and inter-madhhab comparison

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the minimum pregnancy duration from combining two Quranic verses and state the Hanafi maximum of two years, comparing it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Pregnancy minimum six months derived from 30 months total minus 24 months weaning; Hanafi max two years.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayira establishes the Hanafi ruling on the minimum and maximum duration of pregnancy. The ruling is crucial for determining lineage and inheritance. We will analyze the Quranic proof for the six-month minimum and consider the inter-madhhab disagreement regarding the maximum duration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rulings
The passage states two rulings: the maximum pregnancy duration is two years, and the minimum is six months. Note the legal consequence: births within this range can establish lineage.
2
Examine the Proof for Minimum
Two Quranic verses are cited: Surah al-Ahqaf (46:15) stating pregnancy and weaning together are 30 months, and Surah Luqman (31:14) stating weaning is two years (24 months).
The proof is only for the minimum; the maximum is based on other evidence not in this passage.
3
Perform the Deduction
Subtract the weaning period (24 months) from the total (30 months) to get 6 months as the minimum pregnancy period.
This is a straightforward arithmetic deduction using the apparent meaning of the verses.
4
Understand the Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i held the maximum to be four years. The Hanafi school maintains two years. This difference affects lineage attribution for children born after two years but before four.
The Shafi'i proof is not given in this passage but is based on a hadith.
5
Apply the Legal Consequence
In Hanafi fiqh, a child born before six months or after two years cannot be attributed to the husband. This impacts inheritance, custody, and paternity.
The ruling is a condition (shart) for valid lineage.

Mother's Priority in Custody: The Legal Rule and Its Proof

Core Idea
The mother's right to custody is conditional on her not remarrying.

In this passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira, the author presents the fundamental Hanafi ruling on custody: after separation, the mother has priority over the father as long as she remains unmarried. The proof is a direct hadith from the Prophet ﷺ. We will analyze the ruling, its evidence, and the critical condition that changes the entitlement.

Guided Path

Identify the ruling
State the Hanafi position on custody after separation as given in the text: the mother is more entitled ('ahaqq') as long as she does not marry.

Locate the primary proof
Find the hadith cited: 'You are more entitled to him as long as you do not marry.' Note its source (Sunan Abi Dawud, Musnad Ahmad) and its chain status.
The hadith is ahad, so it provides zanni proof.
3
Analyze the condition
Understand the phrase 'ma lam tankihi' (as long as you do not marry). In Hanafi law, this condition is interpreted strictly: remarriage removes the mother's priority.
Other schools may interpret the condition differently.
4
Consider secondary reasoning
The author adds that the mother is more compassionate and capable, and cites the story of Abu Bakr and Umar to reinforce the ruling.
These additional proofs support the hadith but are not the primary dalil.
5
Determine the practical outcome
Know that if the mother remarries, custody passes to the maternal grandmother, then to the father, following the Hanafi sequence.
The sequence of custodians is detailed in later sections of the chapter.

Definition and Conditions of Emancipation ('Itq)

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Emancipation is only valid from a free, adult, sane owner over his own slave.

Emancipation ('itq) is a highly meritorious act in Islam, offering immense reward. However, Sharī‘ah imposes strict conditions to ensure it is valid and beneficial. This passage from al-Jawharah al-Nairah defines 'itq and spells out the four essential conditions that must be met for an emancipation to take effect.

The text states 'al-i‘tāq mandūb ilayh' – emancipation is recommended (mandūb). In Hanafi terms, this is sunnah muakkadah due to the strong hadith about reward.

Not every purchase of a relative frees them — only specific relationships create a right of emancipation.

The Hanafi school lays down precise rulings on when purchasing a slave who is a relative results in automatic emancipation. Why Divorce Words Cannot Free a Slave: Wording and Intent in Hanafi Law
Ruling with proof: divorce wording fails to free a slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why divorce wording cannot effect emancipation and apply the Hanafi principle that a word can only be used metaphorically for something weaker than its literal meaning.

Core Idea
Emancipation is stronger than divorce; a word can only be used metaphorically for what is weaker, not stronger.

In this passage, the Hanafi school addresses a subtle question: if a master says to his female slave 'You are divorced' intending to free her, does emancipation occur? The answer is no, and the rationale reveals a deep linguistic and legal principle about the hierarchy of ownership and the metaphorical capacity of words.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the ruling
Read the first part: the master says 'You are divorced' to his amah (slave-woman) with the intention of freeing her. The ruling is that she does not become free.
Note that the wording is explicit for divorce (talaq), not for emancipation (itq).
2
Understand the proof by analogy
The author compares this to zihar: if a man says to his wife 'You are to me like the back of my mother' intending divorce, it does not effect divorce. Similarly, divorce wording cannot effect emancipation.
The comparison is based on the idea that each wording is explicit for its own legal effect and cannot be transferred to a stronger effect.
3
Grasp the linguistic principle
The passage explains that linguistically, 'itq' means establishing strength, while 'talaq' means removing a restriction. A slave is like an inanimate object that gains capability through emancipation; a married woman is already capable but restricted by marriage. The removal of ownership (milk al-yamin) is stronger than removal of marriage (milk an-nikah). A word can be used metaphorically for something weaker, not stronger.
This principle: al-lafz yasluhu majazan lima huwa duna haqiqatihi, la lima huwa fawqaha.
4
Apply to other similar wordings
The passage lists other wordings like 'You are ba'in' or 'Cover yourself' that also do not effect emancipation because they are not suited for it.
The same logic applies: the wording must be capable of conveying the intended meaning.

He said in al-Mustasfa: Emancipation according to Abu Hanifa is the removal of ownership, and it is an expression of the ability to perform legal acts, and it is divisible in terms of existence and removal, as known in the sale of half and purchase of half, but there attaches to it a ruling that is indivisible, which is freedom, and it is indivisible because it is a legal strength by which the authority of ownership, the effectiveness of guardianship, and testimony appear, and strength is not divisible because it is inconceivable that a person be strong in some parts and weak in others. This is like the limbs of ablution: they are divisible, and connected to them is the permissibility of prayer, which is indivisible. Similarly, the number of divorces for prohibition. If this is the case: then by emancipating part, no freedom is established, and no slavery is removed, because the removal of slavery and the establishment of freedom is a ruling for the removal of all ownership; if some of it is removed, then half of the cause of emancipation has been found, so he is not free at all in his testimonies and all his rulings; rather, he is like a mukatib: he cannot be sold or gifted, except that if he becomes incapable, he is not returned to slavery, unlike intended kitaba. Commentary
This passage from Sharh al-Quduri discusses the intra-Hanafi dispute on partial emancipation. The matn states: When a master frees part of his slave, according to Abu Hanifa only that part becomes free and the slave must work to pay the remaining value. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), the entire slave becomes free. The commentator explains the scenarios: if the master specifies half, third, or fourth, Abu Hanifa frees only that portion; the Sahibayn free all. If the fraction is unknown (e.g., 'some of you'), Abu Hanifa requires clarification; the Sahibayn free all. If he says 'a sahm' (a specific share), Abu Hanifa says a sixth is freed (since 'sahm' means one-sixth in inheritance), the Sahibayn free all. The root of the dispute: Abu Hanifa views emancipation as divisible in its essence (it removes ownership, which is divisible), so only the part freed becomes free, and the rest remains in a state similar to kitaba (the slave works to pay his remaining value). The Sahibayn view emancipation as indivisible like divorce or qisas forgiveness: once any part is freed, the entire slave must be free. The commentator quotes al-Mustasfa explaining that emancipation is removal of ownership, which is divisible, but the legal effect (hurriyya) is indivisible. Abu Hanifa analogizes to the limbs of wudu: they are divisible, but the permissibility of prayer (the effect) is indivisible. The Sahibayn analogize to divorce: saying 'some of you is divorced' does not take effect partially. The passage also mentions a side ruling: if a mukatib (slave under a kitaba contract) buys his master's son, the son does not become free by consensus, because the master does not own the mukatib's earnings.

Key Terms
عتق (itq): Emancipation; the removal of ownership over a slave, making him free.
سعاية (si‘ayah): The slave working to earn money to pay his master for the remaining portion of his value after partial emancipation.
مكاتب (mukatib): A slave who has contracted with his master to earn his freedom by paying installments.

Key Takeaway
The intra-Hanafi dispute on partial emancipation hinges on whether emancipation is defined as removal of ownership (divisible) or affirmation of freedom (indivisible); the operative fatwa follows the Sahibayn that the whole slave becomes free.
