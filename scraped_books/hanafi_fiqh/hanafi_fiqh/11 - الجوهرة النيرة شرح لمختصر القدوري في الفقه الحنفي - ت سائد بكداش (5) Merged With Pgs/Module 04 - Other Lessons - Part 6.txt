Cutting Hand Then Killing: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on multiple qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state both positions of the Hanafi school on qisas when multiple intentional injuries precede death, and apply the principle of subsumption to analogous cases.

Core Idea
Two qisas for two independent acts: Abu Hanifa permits both; the Sahibayn say execution absorbs the hand claim.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about a scenario where an offender intentionally cuts off a victim's hand, then kills him before the wound heals. The core question is whether the guardian (wali) can demand qisas for the hand in addition to execution, or whether the death penalty subsumes the earlier injury. We will explore the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and his two companions, and see how this difference affects actual practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the two acts
Note that the offender committed two independent intentional acts: cutting the hand and killing. The victim died before the hand healed, so both are subject to qisas.
The acts are separate in time and nature.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his implied reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the guardian may choose: either have the hand cut off first (qisas for the hand) and then execute, or simply execute. His reasoning: each act gives an independent right of qisas, so both can be carried out.
The text says 'if he wishes' indicating an option.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their implied reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that only execution is carried out; the hand qisas is dropped. Their reasoning: the death penalty covers the entire body, so the claim for the hand is absorbed.
The hand is part of the body that is killed, so no separate punishment.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, the guardian can have the hand cut off before execution; under the Sahibayn, he cannot. This affects the guardian's ability to seek justice for each injury.
In practice, the imam or judge decides which option to follow.
5
Note the typical mufta bihi within the Hanafi school
Although the text does not give tarjih, the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa, giving the guardian the option. This is due to the principle that each independent wrong warrants its own remedy.
Some later authorities may qualify this with conditions.

Ruling on an Arrow Passing Through: Intent and Liability
Ruling with commentary on shooting through target

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between intentional and mistaken killing in a single act and apply the Hanafi ruling of qisas for the first and diyah on the aqila for the second.

Core Idea
One arrow, two killings: intention determines liability – qisas for the intended target, diyah for the unintended.

This lesson examines a specific scenario from Kitab al-Jinaayat: a person intentionally shoots an arrow at one man, but the arrow passes through him and kills a second. Both die. How does Hanafi law treat the two deaths? The answer hinges on the distinction between intentional ('amd) and mistaken (khata') killing. The ruling illustrates a key Hanafi principle: each victim is evaluated separately based on the intent directed toward them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its components
The act is one volitional shot, but it produces two deaths. Separate the deaths by their causal relationship to the shooter's intent.
The shooter intended to kill the first man; the second death was unintended.
2
Classify each death legally
The first death is intentional killing ('amd). The second death is mistaken killing (khata') because the shooter did not intend the second victim.
The classification determines the penalty.
3
Apply the Hanafi penalty for each classification
For intentional killing: qisas (retaliation) is wajib upon the killer. For mistaken killing: diyah (blood money) is wajib, payable by the killer's aqila (tribal guarantors).
The diyah is not on the killer's personal wealth but on the aqila.
4
Understand the reasoning behind the distinction
The author states: 'Because they are two offenses.' The first is 'amd, so its consequence is qisas; the second is khata', so its consequence is diyah, and diyah falls on the aqila.
This shows the Hanafi method of evaluating each victim independently.

Diya for Umm Walad and Compensation for Slave's Hand
Explains cap on diya for female slave and compensation for slave's hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the diya due for a female slave (umm walad) when her value exceeds the diya of a free woman, distinguishing between the two Hanafi narrations (zahir al-riwayah vs. narration of al-Hasan).

Core Idea
Diya for a female slave is capped at the diya of a free woman minus a fixed reduction based on sharia precedents.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira addresses a specific scenario in diyat (blood money) where the victim is a female slave or the hand of a male slave. When a slave's value exceeds the diya of an equivalent free person, Hanafi jurists apply a cap and a small reduction. The exact reduction amount is disputed within the school, between the widely-transmitted ten dirhams and the narration of al-Hasan ibn Ziyad of five dirhams. Understanding this ruling requires familiarity with the concept of 'cap' in slave compensation and the analogical reasoning behind the reduction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
State the act: the diya payable for killing a female slave (umm walad) or cutting off the hand of a male slave. The ruling: a fixed amount with a cap and reduction.
The act is an offense causing harm; the ruling is wajib (obligatory compensation).
2
Locate the dalil and reasoning
Find the evidence for the reduction. The author states it is based on sharia precedents: the nisab of theft (10 dirhams) and the minimum mahr. This gives a conventional basis for the ten-dirham reduction.
For the male slave's hand, the reasoning is similar: half the value capped at 5000 minus five (or ten in zahir al-riwayah).
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The matn (al-Quduri) reports the view of al-Hasan (five dirhams reduction), while al-Hidayah states the zahir al-riwayah (ten dirhams). Understand the reasoning for each: al-Hasan argues the reduction should be half because the diya of a free woman is half that of a man.
The majority (Sahibayn) follow the ten-dirham reduction, applying the same logic as for male slaves.
4
Evaluate the tarjih
Determine which view is mufta bihi. Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah prefers the ten-dirham reduction, making it the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Authority weight: zahir al-riwayah takes precedence over the nawadir (rare transmission of al-Hasan).

Commentary
This passage from Sharh al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explains two rulings from the matn of al-Quduri regarding compensation for slaves. First, for a female slave (umm walad) whose value exceeds the diya of a free woman (5000 dirhams), the obligatory diya is capped at 5000 dirhams minus a deduction. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the liability of a ghasib for a slave and a free child, and distinguish the cases where the ghasib's aqila pays diya.

Core Idea
A ghasib is liable for full value of a slave, but for a free child liability depends on cause of death and often falls on aqila.

This lesson clarifies the property-based liability for a usurped slave versus the tort-based liability for a free child in the custody of a ghasib. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correct application of Hanafi rules on ghasb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Separate the rulings for slave (property) and free child (person). The liability principles differ fundamentally.
2
Analyze the slave ruling
Note the consensus: ghasib pays full value if slave perishes. This is due to property-based liability.
Property liability requires transfer of ownership, possible for slaves.
3
Analyze the free child ruling
Identify the three scenarios: natural death (no liability), external acts (diya on aqila by istihsan), and suicide/fall (diya on aqila).
Istihsan departs from strict analogy here.
4
Examine third-party killing
Understand the options for guardians when a third party kills the child intentionally or accidentally.
Guardians may choose to pursue killer or ghasib; ghasib has recourse except if killer is pursued.
5
Synthesize the principles
Compare and contrast the two types of liability: property vs. person, consensus vs. istihsan.

Commentary
This passage discusses the liability of a ghasib (usurper) when the item usurped is a slave or a free child. For slaves, the ruling is straightforward: the ghasib pays the full value of the slave if it dies in his hands, by consensus. This is because ghasb is a property-based liability; the ghasib effectively guarantees the property's value. Free persons, however, cannot be subject to ghasb because they cannot be owned. For a free child, the ruling is more nuanced. If the child dies from natural causes like fever or sudden death, the ghasib is not liable because these are not caused by his act. If the death results from external forces like lightning, snakebite, or wild animal attack, the ghasib's aqila (tribal group) pays diya by istihsan, as the ghasib's possession exposed the child to risk. If the child dies by his own action (suicide, falling into a well, or a wall collapse), the ghasib is liable for diya on his aqila because the ghasib's illegal possession is the setting for the harm. If a third party kills the child intentionally, the heirs can choose: they may execute the killer or take diya from the ghasib's aqila, with the ghasib then able to seek reimbursement from the killer. If the killing is accidental, the heirs may claim diya from either the ghasib's aqila or the killer's aqila; if they claim from the ghasib, he can seek reimbursement from the killer, but if they claim from the killer, the killer cannot seek reimbursement from the ghasib because the ultimate liability is on the killer.

عاقلة (aqila): The tribal group or relatives who collectively pay diya in cases of accidental killing or injury.
استحسان (istihsan): Juridical preference or equitable discretion; a method of departing from strict analogy (qiyas) for a stronger reason or necessity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on diyah and qasamah for a body found in various locations, identifying when liability is imposed or waived based on proximity and habitation.

Core Idea
Liability for an unknown-death body is determined by whether the location connects to inhabitants; isolated places yield no liability.

This passage from the chapter on Qasamah presents a set of location-based rulings that determine when blood money (diyah) and the qasamah procedure apply to the discovery of a dead body without a known killer. The Hanafi school establishes a clear principle: liability rests on the nearest inhabited community that plausibly could have been involved, or is waived entirely if the body is found in an unowned, isolated place. The commentary expands on key factors like audibility of a shout and physical attachment to the bank of a river.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling context
Recognize that this is about a found body with unknown killer. The ruling determines whether diyah and qasamah are obligatory on the inhabitants of a locality or waived (hadar).
2

Understand that the decisive factor is whether the location is connected to habitation. The commentary uses audibility of a shout as a test: if no one would hear, it is isolated (hadar); otherwise, the nearest settlement is liable.
For two villages equally close, liability is shared.
4
Apply the guardian's claim exception
Note that if the legal guardian accuses a specific person from the locality, the qasamah does not drop from the locality—the procedure continues unless a confession or proof is established.
This shows qasamah is a communal obligation not voided by individual accusation.

List the three scenarios: (1) stealing a silver nugget and minting it into coins, (2) stealing a garment and cutting/sewing it, (3) stealing a garment and dyeing it.

2
State the ruling for each case
For case 1: the thief is amputated but the coins remain the owner's (Abu Hanifa). For case 2: the thief owns the new garment after amputation, no compensation. For case 3: according to the two disciples, the thief owns the dyed garment after amputation.
Case 3 has an intra-Hanafi difference; Abu Hanifa likely rules differently.
3
Identify the underlying principle
The key is whether the transformation constitutes istihlāk (complete re-creation, as in cutting/sewing and dyeing) or a mere form change (as in minting, which preserves the metal substance).
Istihlāk severs the original owner's property right.
4
Apply the 'hadd and compensation cannot combine' rule
Once the hand is amputated for the theft, compensation for the stolen item is not due, because the hadd (amputation) and the financial penalty cannot be combined. This is a fixed Hanafi principle.
This explains why no guarantee (damān) is owed after amputation in cases where the property is lost or transformed.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi difference in the dyeing case
The text attributes the ruling for dyeing to 'the two disciples' (Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad). Impliedly, Abū Ḥanīfa may differ, likely holding that dyeing does not amount to istihlāk and the cloth remains the owner's.
This difference arises from differing views on whether dyeing changes the substance.

The Four Prohibited Drinks in Hanafi Fiqh
Listing and defining the four types of prohibited drinks

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define the four prohibited drinks according to the Hanafi school and distinguish between khamr, tila', sukr, and naqi' of raisins.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school defines khamr exclusively as grape juice, but prohibits intoxicating infusions of dates and raisins equally.

The Hanafi school categorizes prohibited drinks into four specific types. While khamr is defined strictly as raw grape juice that has fermented, other intoxicating beverages like date wine (sukr) and raisin wine fall under a separate prohibition. Understanding these definitions is crucial for applying the rulings correctly and avoiding common misconceptions.

Guided Path
1
Learn the definition of khamr
Identify the first prohibited drink: khamr is the raw juice of grapes when it ferments (ghala), becomes strong (ishtadda), and throws foam (qadhafa bil-zabad). This is the only drink that is called khamr according to Hanafi fiqh.
Khamr is haram by Quranic injunction (5:90).
2
Understand tila'
The second drink is grape juice cooked until less than two-thirds of it is gone. This is not khamr but is called tila' and is permissible if it does not intoxicate.
If tila' becomes intoxicating after cooking, it becomes haram.
3
Identify naqi' of dates (sukr)
The third prohibited drink is the infusion (naqi') of dates when it becomes strong and ferments. This is called sukr and is haram.
Sukr is a type of nabidh that intoxicates.
4
Identify naqi' of raisins
The fourth prohibited drink is the infusion of raisins when it ferments and becomes strong. Like sukr, it is haram when intoxicating.
Raisin infusion is also called nabidh.

First Seven Rulings on Khamr in Hanafi Fiqh
Enumeration of legal rulings on khamr with internal Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the first seven rulings on khamr according to the Hanafi school and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on the definition of khamr.

Core Idea
Khamr is haram by essence, not just intoxication; its value is nil for Muslims but valid among dhimmis.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira enumerates ten legal rulings on khamr, of which we cover the first seven. These rulings define khamr, establish its inherent prohibition, classify its impurity, declare the grave consequence of deeming it permissible, negate its monetary value for Muslims, and forbid all forms of benefit. Understanding these rulings clarifies that khamr is intrinsically impermissible regardless of intoxication level.

Guided Path
1
Define khamr (first ruling)
Identify the first ruling: khamr is the raw grape juice when it becomes intoxicating.
2
Analyze condition for khamr (second ruling)
Note the internal dispute: Abu Hanifa requires qadhf bi al-zabad (foam throwing) in addition to fermentation; Sahibayn deem fermentation and strength sufficient.
This affects when the name 'khamr' applies.
3
Understand essence-based prohibition (third ruling)
Khamr is haram by virtue of being filth (rijis), not merely because it intoxicates. Intoxication is not the illa.
Quranic basis: 'al-rijis' is forbidden in essence.
4
Classify impurity (fourth ruling)
Khamr is najasa mughallaza (severe impurity), like urine, requiring special purification.
5
Consequence of mustahill (fifth ruling)
Anyone who considers khamr permissible becomes a kafir, emphasizing the severity of its prohibition.
6
Monetary value and transactions (sixth ruling)
For Muslims, khamr has no value: destroyer or usurper not liable; sale invalid. However, dhimmis may trade in it, and Muslims may accept debt payment from dhimmi's khamr proceeds.
Exception due to permissibility of khamr among dhimmis.
7
Prohibition of benefit (seventh ruling)
Any form of benefiting from khamr is haram because it is impure.
Passage cuts off, but the principle is clear.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Number of Refusals for a Trained Hunting Dog

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Hanafi school disagrees internally: is a dog trained after three refusals (Sahibayn) or based on the hunter's certainty (Abu Hanifa)?

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyirah presents a disagreement within the Hanafi school regarding the training of hunting dogs. While the matn of Al-Quduri states that training is established after three refusals, Imam Abu Hanifa held a different view—that it depends on the hunter's judgment. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the practical ruling on which catch is permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed element
The dispute is over the number of refusals that establish a dog as 'trained' (mu‘allam). The matn says three; Abu Hanifa says no fixed number.
Training here means the dog reliably refrains from eating the prey.
2
State the two positions
Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and one narration from Abu Hanifa: exactly three refusals. The sounder view of Abu Hanifa: training is based on the hunter's predominant opinion.

Sahibayn cite analogy with khiyar (three-day option), the story of Musa and Khidr (three chances), and a saying of Umar (three trials). Abu Hanifa relies on the general Quranic permission (5:4) and leaves it to custom.
No Quran or hadith specifies a number, so the dispute is over interpretation.

On Abu Hanifa's view, the third catch is halal if the hunter is confident; on Sahibayn's view, only the fourth catch and beyond are halal.
This directly affects what the hunter may eat.

Identify the relied-upon position (tarjih)
The text does not give explicit tarjih, but the matn states 'three times', and the commentator notes it is 'according to the two', implying the Sahibayn's view is the madhhab's position.
