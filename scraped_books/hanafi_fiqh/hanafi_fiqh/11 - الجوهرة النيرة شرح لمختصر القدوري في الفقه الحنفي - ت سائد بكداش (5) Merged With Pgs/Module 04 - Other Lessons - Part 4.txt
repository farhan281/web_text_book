Acknowledgment of Umm Walad Status: Conditions and Effects
Conditions for valid acknowledgment of umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the conditions under which a master's acknowledgment of a slave woman as umm walad is valid and its effect on her manumission.

Core Idea
A master's acknowledgment of umm walad is valid in health; in terminal illness, her freedom is limited to one-third if no child exists.

The status of umm walad can be established by the master's acknowledgment (iqrar). The Hanafi school details the validity of this acknowledgment based on the master's health and the existence of a child. The acknowledgment in health is always valid; in terminal illness, the presence of a child ensures full effect, but without a child, her subsequent freedom upon the master's death is capped at one-third of his estate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario: acknowledgment in health
If the master acknowledges in good health that a slave woman is his umm walad, the acknowledgment is valid and she becomes an umm walad immediately, regardless of whether she has a child.
This is unconditional.
2
Identify the scenario: acknowledgment in terminal illness (marad al-mawt)
If the master acknowledges in terminal illness, the ruling depends on whether a child exists. If there is a child, the acknowledgment is fully valid, and she becomes umm walad with full rights.
3
Analyze the case of terminal illness without a child
If the master acknowledges in terminal illness but there is no child, she still becomes an umm walad, but her freedom is limited to one-third of the master's estate, similar to a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon death).
This prevents the acknowledgment from overburdening the estate to the detriment of legal heirs.
4
Understand the rationale for the one-third limitation
In terminal illness, dispositions that favor some heirs (or non-heirs) are restricted to one-third of the estate to protect the rights of existing heirs. The acknowledgment of umm walad without a child is treated as a bequest of freedom, which is limited to one-third.
The text compares her to a mudabbar, who is freed from one-third.

Commentary
This passage presents two main rulings from the Hanafi school. First, regarding inheritance: when a child is born to an umm walad (a slave woman who has borne her master's child), the child inherits from both the mother (the umm walad) and the father (the master) as a full son, meaning they receive the full share of a son, not half. Conversely, the mother and father each inherit from the child as a single parent (i.e., one-sixth if there are other heirs, or the whole in some cases). The commentator explains that the child inherits fully because the master has acknowledged all of the child's inheritance rights, and the parents inherit equally because they are equal in lineage to the child. Second, the passage discusses the master's acknowledgment of a female slave as his umm walad. If the master acknowledges in good health that a slave woman is his umm walad, it is valid and she becomes an umm walad immediately, regardless of whether she has a child. This acknowledgment is binding. If the acknowledgment is made during a terminal illness (marad al-mawt), and she has a child, the same ruling applies. If she has no child, she still becomes an umm walad, but her freedom (which occurs upon the master's death) is limited to one-third of his estate, similar to a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death). This ensures that the acknowledgment does not harm the rights of legal heirs.

إقرار (iqrar): A legal acknowledgment or confession, here specifically the master's declaration that a slave woman is his umm walad.
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose master has declared that he or she will be free upon the master's death. The mudabbar is freed from one-third of the estate.

Master's Claim of Paternity to a Child Born to His Mukatab's Slave Woman
Intra-Hanafi dispute on paternity claim with conditions for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the master's claim of paternity regarding his mukatab's slave woman, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute with Abu Yusuf, and apply the ruling to scenarios involving confirmation, timing, and compensation.

Core Idea
The master's claim to paternity of a child from his mukatab's slave woman requires the mukatab's confirmation because the master does not own the mukatab's property.

This passage presents a delicate scenario where property rights and lineage intersect. The master has intercourse with a slave woman owned by his slave who is purchasing freedom (mukatab). The child's lineage hinges on the mukatab's acknowledgment, illustrating the Hanafi principle that the master cannot dispose of the mukatab's property without his consent. We will examine the majority view, Abu Yusuf's objection, the Hanafi response, and related rulings on compensation and timing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and act
Clearly identify the master (mawla), the mukatab (enslaved person under contract of manumission), and the slave woman owned by the mukatab. The act is the master's intercourse with that slave woman, resulting in a child.
The mukatab retains ownership of his property, including the slave woman, during the contract period.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its condition
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, the master's paternity claim is only valid if the mukatab confirms it. Without confirmation, lineage is not established.
This contrasts with the case of a son's slave woman, where the father's claim is accepted without the son's confirmation.
3
Examine Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf argues that the master's right over the mukatab's slave woman is stronger than over his son's slave woman because the son's property is still under father's disposal. Therefore, the claim should be accepted without confirmation.
He uses qiyas (analogy) with the son's case, arguing it is more deserving (awla).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response (istihsan)
The Hanafi response rejects the analogy because the master has no right to dispose of the mukatab's property, while a father does have disposal rights over his son's property. Hence the analogy fails, and the condition of confirmation remains.
This is a form of istihsan (juristic preference) based on a distinction in legal capacity.
5
Apply the ruling to related issues
Consider the timing of birth ('uqr liability), the status of the slave woman (umm walad), and the effect of the master later purchasing the slave woman. The passage also clarifies that if the child is from the mukatab herself (not her slave), lineage is established without her confirmation.
The child's value is assessed on the day of litigation.

Commentary
This passage addresses a complex scenario where a master has intercourse with a slave woman owned by his mukatab (a slave who has contracted to purchase his freedom). If the slave woman gives birth and the master claims the child, the ruling depends on the mukatab's confirmation. The text presents the Hanafi position (attributed to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) that the master's claim requires the mukatab's confirmation. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that the master's right over the mukatab's property is stronger than his right over his own son's property, and since paternity is established in the latter case without confirmation, it should be so here as well. The Hanafi response (lana) is that the master cannot dispose of the mukatab's property, whereas a father can dispose of his son's property, so the analogy fails. Consequently, without the mukatab's confirmation, the child's lineage is not established. The passage also clarifies that this ruling applies only to the mukatab's slave woman, not to the mukatab herself (if she is female). If the master claims a child from the mukatab herself, the lineage is established regardless of her confirmation or denial. Additionally, the master must pay 'uqr (compensation for unlawful intercourse) and the value of the child if the mukatab confirms, but there is no 'uqr if the child is born within six months of the kitaba contract (indicating conception after the contract). If the child is born after six months, the master must pay 'uqr if the mukatab chooses to continue the contract; she may also choose to rescind the contract (ta'jiz), becoming an umm walad, in which case no 'uqr is due. The value of the child is assessed on the day of litigation. The commentary also explains that the slave woman does not become an umm walad for the master because he does not have full ownership over her; the mukatab retains the right to sell her. The impediment to establishing lineage is the mukatab's right; if the master purchases the slave woman (even for a day), the mukatab's right is removed and lineage is established.

عقر ('uqr): Compensation paid for unlawful sexual intercourse with a slave woman, equivalent to the depreciation in her value due to the intercourse.
قيمة ولد (qimat walad): The monetary value of the child born as a result of the intercourse, to be paid by the father to the owner of the slave woman.

Master's Claim Over Mukatab's Jariya: No Umm Walad Status
Rulings on mukatab's jariya and mukataba's child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two rulings on the mukatab's jariya and apply the qiyas that the master lacks disposal rights over the mukatab's property.

Core Idea
Master's lack of ownership over mukatab's property blocks umm walad status and paternity.

This passage from the Book of Concubinage addresses a nuanced situation: a master claims paternity over a child born to a slave-girl (jariya) owned by his own slave under kitaba (mukatab). The Hanafi school rules that the jariya does not become umm walad for the master, and if the mukatab denies the lineage, it is not established. The underlying principle is that the master has no disposal rights over the mukatab's property, unlike a father over his son's property. This lesson unpacks the ruling, the proof, and the exceptions, including the contrasting case of the female mukataba herself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main rulings
From the matn, extract: (1) The jariya of a mukatab does not become umm walad for the master. (2) If the mukatab denies the child's lineage, it is not established.
Both rulings hinge on the master's limited authority over mukatab's property.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The commentary provides the Hanafi proof: 'the master has no right to dispose of the property of his mukatab, while the father has that right' – a qiyas (analogy) distinguishing the master from a father.
The opposite (father) case shows the master's lack of disposal right is the operative difference.
3
Understand the distinction with the mukataba herself
The passage notes that if the female slave is the mukataba herself (the female slave under kitaba), and the master claims paternity, lineage is established regardless of her confirmation or denial.
The mukataba is not owned by the mukatab; she is owned by the master, so the impediment vanishes.
4
Apply the uqr rules
For the mukataba, if she gives birth within six months of the kitaba, no compensation (uqr) is due. Beyond six months, uqr is due if she chooses to continue the kitaba; otherwise she can incapacitate herself and become umm walad.
The six-month mark is the minimum pregnancy duration for paternity attribution.
5
Note the exception for child with no known lineage
The uqr rule applies only if the child has no known father from another man. If the child has a known lineage, the master's claim is invalid.
This preserves the integrity of genealogies.

Definition and Legal Status of Kitaba
Definition of kitaba and its recommended status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of kitaba and explain why the Quranic command is interpreted as mustahabb (recommended) rather than wajib (obligatory).

Core Idea
Kitaba is recommended, not obligatory — the command is conditioned on the master's assessment of the slave's goodness.

This lesson opens Kitab al-Mukatab with the linguistic and technical definition of kitaba, the contract that grants a slave gradual freedom. The text clarifies the mukatab's intermediate legal status and establishes the core ruling: kitaba is mustahabb (recommended) when the slave requests it, not wajib (obligatory) on the master. The proof lies in the Quranic verse 'So contract kitaba with them if you know in them goodness' (al-Nur 24:33), where the condition 'if you know' indicates discretion, not compulsion.

Guided Path
1
Define kitaba linguistically and technically
Start with the literal meaning (al-damm, 'joining') and then the technical definition as a contract joining partial freedom (freedom of hand) to full emancipation (freedom of neck) through payment of a sum.
The term 'kitaba' also means 'writing', referring to the written contract.
2
Explain the legal status of the mukatab
The mukatab is in a mixed state: in some rulings like a free person (e.g., can own property), in others like a slave (e.g., needs permission for marriage). The ostrich simile illustrates this hybridity.
This intermediate status is a key feature of Hanafi fiqh.
3
State the hukm (ruling) of kitaba
Kitaba is mustahabb (recommended) when the slave requests it; it is not wajib (obligatory) on the master.
Contrast with opinions that consider it obligatory.
4
Analyze the Quranic evidence
The imperative 'fakatibuhum' is a command (amr). Hanafi usul holds that amr indicates wujub unless there is a qarina (indication) that limits it to nadb. The condition 'in 'alimtum fihim khayran' (if you know in them goodness) is such a qarina, making the ruling recommended.
The two interpretations of 'khayr' (goodness) are religious practice or not harming Muslims after freedom.
5
Identify the ikhtilaf (disagreement)
Some other schools (e.g., some Shafi'is) interpret the command as obligatory, making kitaba wajib when the slave requests it and has goodness. The Hanafi position is mustahabb.
Thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in whether the master is sinning if he refuses.

Kitabah: Definition, Conditions, Proof, and Intra-Madhhab Ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the essential conditions for a valid kitabah contract, explain the Hanafi ruling that the mukatab remains a slave until full payment based on the hadith, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on invalid subject matters.

Core Idea
The mukatab remains a full slave until the entire stipulated payment is made – a single dirham unpaid preserves the slave status.

This lesson opens the Book of Mukatab (Kitabah) by defining the contract of manumission by written agreement. We will examine the conditions that make the contract valid, including the requirement of lawful money, the slave's acceptance, and the permissibility of immediate or deferred payments. We then turn to the crucial proof from the Prophet's hadith that the mukatab does not gain any freedom until the last dirham is paid. Finally, we explore the intra-Hanafi difference on invalid subject matters like wine and pigs, and the diverse companion opinions on when freedom actually occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is kitabah (contract of manumission). Determine the hukm: it is mubah (permissible) but recommended if the slave is trustworthy. The text also discusses the conditions that validate it.
Kitabah is a mu'awadah (contract of exchange) and thus can include options.
2
Extract the conditions of validity
The text specifies: (1) the master must stipulate a known amount of lawful money (excluding carrion, blood, wine, pig); (2) the slave must accept (since it imposes a financial obligation); (3) payment can be immediate, deferred, or in installments. Also, the master may revoke before acceptance.

The mufta bihi is the view of the Sahibayn, as it is more accommodating and the default permissibility of stipulations in contracts supports it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the ownership of a mukatab's earnings and sadaqah after ta'jiz and explain the dalil from the hadith of Barirah.

Core Idea
Upon the mukatab's incapacity, his earnings and sadaqah revert to the master due to change in ownership (tabaddul al-milk).

This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah clarifies what happens to a mukatab's assets when he becomes unable to complete his kitabah. The ruling rests on a simple principle: the mukatab's earnings are essentially the master's, and even charity received by the mukatab becomes permissible for the master once ownership changes. The proof is taken from the well-known hadith of Barirah, which demonstrates that the same item can be sadaqah for one person and a gift for another when ownership shifts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the specific act? The mukatab's earnings (aksab) and sadaqah that he paid to the master before becoming incapable. The ruling: these belong to the master and are permissible (tayyib) for him.
The ruling applies to both earnings and charity assets.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is the hadith of Barirah, where the Prophet ﷺ said about meat given as sadaqah to Barirah and then presented to him: 'It is sadaqah for her, and a gift for us.' This establishes that ownership can change.
The hadith is found in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal link: the same item changed from being sadaqah (forbidden for the Prophet) to a gift (permissible) because ownership transferred. Similarly, the sadaqah given to the mukatab becomes permissible for the master when ownership changes upon ta'jiz.
The key is tabaddul al-milk (change of ownership).
4
Note the distinction from permission cases
The passage contrasts: if a third party gives something to the master with permission (e.g., as a gift), that is not sadaqah and does not rely on change of ownership. The sadaqah case is unique because the master takes it as a substitute for emancipation.
Understanding this distinction prevents confusion with ordinary gifts.

Freeing a Mukatab: Ruling and Consequences
Ruling on manumission of mukatab and its consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a master frees his mukatab and explain why the kitaba debt drops, and who owns the earnings and children.

Core Idea
Master's freeing of a mukatab grants immediate freedom and cancels the entire kitaba debt.

In the Hanafi school, the kitaba contract is a promise of freedom in exchange for payments. When the master voluntarily frees the mukatab before completion of payment, a question arises: does the slave still owe the remaining debt? This lesson clarifies that the freeing nullifies the debt automatically, based on the principle that the debt was consideration for freedom which has now been granted directly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the master freeing his mukatab. The ruling is that the slave becomes free (ʿatīq) immediately and the kitaba debt (mal al-kitāba) is dropped (yasquṭ).
This is a ruling of immediate effect; no further action is needed.
2
Understand the legal rationale
The kitaba debt was a consideration ('iwad) for the promise of freedom. Since the master granted freedom directly, the debt is no longer due. The master is considered to have discharged (ibra') the slave from the debt.
The text says: 'because he (the slave) did not commit to it except in exchange for freedom, and he obtained freedom without it, so it is not binding on him.'
3
Note the preservation of earnings
The phrase 'ma'a salamat al-aksāb' means the slave keeps all earnings he acquired during the kitaba period. He does not have to return them.
This confirms the mukatab's ownership of his earnings after freedom.
4
Determine ownership of children
Any children born to the mukatab during the kitaba period belong to him, not the master. Because the freeing discharges all obligations, including any claim over the slave's offspring.
Children are considered his property as part of his earnings.
5
Recognize cross-madhhab difference
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require the slave to pay a proportionate amount of the debt if freed before completion. The Hanafi position drops it entirely.
This is a point of ikhtilaf; the Hanafi reliance is on the principle of ibra'.

Kitabah with a Mudabbara: Three Hanafi Views
Ruling on kitabah with mudabbara and the ikhtilaf among Hanafi imams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi imams' positions on what a mudabbara-mukatab must pay upon the master's death and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Tadbir frees one-third; the kitabah applies to the remaining two-thirds — but the imams differ on the financial options.

This passage deals with a nuanced scenario: a female slave who is both mudabbara (to be freed upon the master's death) and mukatab (contract of kitabah). The initial ruling declares such a contract permissible, but the key dispute arises when the master dies without property. Here, the three eponymous Hanafi imams — Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani — offer distinct views on what the slave must pay to secure her freedom. Understanding this intra-madhhab difference sharpens your ability to trace how legal principles (tadbir as a bequest, the scope of kitabah) yield divergent outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine the core question: when a mudabbara is also made a mukatab, and the master dies with no property beyond the slave herself, what must she pay to become free? The controversy hinges on whether the kitabah covers the whole slave or only the portion not already freed by tadbir.
Tadbir is treated as a bequest, so it frees up to one-third of the slave's value from the estate.
2
Understand the initial ruling and two scenarios
The commentator first notes that kitabah with a mudabbara is permissible (jaza). Then two death scenarios: (1) if the master has property, the mudabbara is freed from the one-third (the tadbir bequest) and the kitabah is voided; (2) if no property, she faces an obligation – this is where the ikhtilaf occurs.
The scenario with property is straightforward; the dispute only concerns the no-property case.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that she has a choice: either pay the full kitabah amount (in installments) or pay two-thirds of her market value (immediately). His reasoning: the kitabah contract was concluded on what remained of her slavery (two-thirds), not on the portion already freed by tadbir. Thus she can fulfill the original contract or simply pay the value of the unfreed portion.
The idea: tadbir effectively freed one-third, so the kitabah only validly covers two-thirds.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's positions
Abu Yusuf says she must pay the lesser of the two amounts (full kitabah or two-thirds of value) with no choice, because freedom is achieved by paying the lesser. Muhammad says she has a choice between two-thirds of the kitabah or two-thirds of the value, since the kitabah was for the whole but one-third was already secured by tadbir, leaving only two-thirds to be paid via kitabah.
Both Sahibayn limit the payment compared to Abu Hanifa's full-kitabah option.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, she can opt for the full kitabah (payable in installments) or two-thirds value (immediate lump sum). Under Abu Yusuf, she must pay whichever is less, immediately. Under Muhammad, she may choose between two-thirds of the kitabah (installments) or two-thirds of value (immediate). This affects both the amount and the timeline of payment.
The commentator does not give a tarjih (preference), but the matn follows Abu Hanifa.

When a master gives tadbir (posthumous emancipation) to a female slave who is already under a kitabah contract, a new legal question arises: does this release her from the kitabah, or does she have to fulfill both obligations? Guided Path

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Payment Sequence and Wala
Presents Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn on whether the slave becomes fully free.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi intra-madhhab dispute regarding a slave owned by a mukatab who pays his kitabah before the mukatab's manumission, and explain the basis of Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions.

Core Idea
When a slave is partly free from two routes, Abu Hanifa gives him a real choice; Sahibayn say freedom is total and the lesser must be paid.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayyira presents a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi school. It involves a mukatab who owns a slave, and that slave pays his own kitabah before the mukatab completes his. The slave is simultaneously subject to a tadbir – a promise of freedom after the master's death. Two directions to freedom exist, but with different costs. Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ on whether the slave has a meaningful choice or whether he is automatically free with a lesser payment. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi jurists balance contractual obligations with the overarching aim of freeing slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal status
The scenario: a mukatab (first) owns a slave (second). The slave pays his own kitabah (installment) before the mukatab completes his payments and becomes free. The slave is also a mudabbar (promised freedom after the mukatab's death). Therefore, the slave is partially free from two directions: the kitabah (immediate, smaller payment) and the tadbir (deferred, larger payment).
The passage states that after payment, part of the slave is free (from the kitabah), but two-thirds remain slave due to the tadbir.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the slave has a genuine choice between the two routes. He can choose to be freed via the tadbir (paying the larger deferred amount) or via the kitabah (paying the smaller immediate amount). The choice is meaningful because people may prefer different benefits (e.g., deferring payment vs. immediate freedom).
The slave would then become fully free once he pays the chosen amount.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani argue that once part of the slave becomes free (from the kitabah payment), the entire slave becomes free immediately. Therefore, the slave is already fully free and must pay only one of the two amounts. Since he will inevitably choose the lesser amount, there is no real choice.
The difference hinges on whether freedom can be partial or is indivisible once triggered.
4
Compare the logical premises
Abu Hanifa sees the slave as still partly slave, hence capable of choosing between two distinct contracts. The Sahibayn apply the principle that freedom, once partially achieved, is indivisible (al-‘itq la yataba‘ad). Thus, partial freedom triggers full freedom automatically.
This principle appears in other Hanafi rulings (e.g., if a partner in a slave frees his share).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the slave may end up paying the larger amount if he chooses the tadbir; under the Sahibayn, he pays the lesser amount (the kitabah) and becomes free immediately. The wala belongs to the original master in both views.
The mufta bihi (operative position) is not specified here, but later Hanafi authorities often adopt Abu Hanifa's view in such disputes (tarjih by kathrat al-adilla or other principles).

Commentary
This passage deals with a specific scenario: a mukatab has a slave, and that slave (the second) pays towards his own kitabah before the mukatab (the first) completes his payment and gains full freedom. The mukatab also had a tadbir (a contract of manumission after his death) on this slave. The slave is part free, part slave. Two routes to freedom: one via the mukatab's ownership (the tadbir) and one via the slave's own kitabah (which is immediate). Abu Hanifa says the slave is in a position to choose between these two because each has different benefits (large deferred vs small immediate). The two (Sahibayn) argue that once part of the slave becomes free (due to the kitabah payment), the entire slave becomes free, and he is obligated to pay only one of the amounts, which will be the lesser, so there is no real choice. The commentary explains that the wala belongs to the original master because he still has a type of ownership.

Key Terms
تدبير (tadbir): A contract where a master declares his slave will be free after the master's death.
ولاء (wala'): The right of inheritance and clientage that transfers to the one who frees a slave.
