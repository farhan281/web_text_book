Preferred Slaughter Methods: Camels, Cows, and Sheep
Ruling on preferred slaughter method for camels, cows, and sheep

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on preferred slaughter methods for camels, cows, and sheep, and explain why the opposite method is permissible but disliked.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, the preferred slaughter method (nahr for camels, dhabh for cows/sheep) is mustahabb; using the opposite method is valid but makruh tanzihan.

This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra clarifies the recommended (mustahabb) method of slaughter for different animals according to the Hanafi school. While the Quran and Sunnah indicate a preference, the school emphasizes that any method that effectively drains blood is valid, ensuring ease and avoiding undue hardship. The key distinction is between recommended practice and legal validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
First, note the act being ruled upon: the method of slaughter. For camels, nahr (chest-cutting) is mustahabb; for cows and sheep, dhabh (throat-cutting) is mustahabb. The opposite method is permissible (ja'iz) but disliked (makruh tanzihan).
The ruling distinguishes between recommendation and permissibility.
2
Locate the dalil for the recommendation
The author cites Quranic verses: for camels, 'فصل لربك وانحر' (Kawthar:2) and the fact that the camel's chest has no meat, making nahr easier; for cows and sheep, 'إن الله يأمركم أن تذبحوا بقرة' (Baqarah:67) and 'وفديناه بذبح عظيم' (Saffat:107). These indicate the preferred method.
The Quran uses specific verbs (nahr for camel, dhabh for cow/sheep).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah for permissibility
The proof for permissibility of the opposite method is the hadith: 'أنهر الدم بما شئت' (Cause the blood to flow by whatever you wish). This general permission indicates that any method that effectively drains blood is valid, as long as the animal is alive.
The hadith supports a broad principle: the goal is blood flow, not a specific technique.
4
Note the ikhtilaf with Imam Malik
Imam Malik holds that using the non-preferred method invalidates the slaughter, rendering the meat haram. The Hanafi school rejects this, relying on the general hadith and the principle of ease.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi school's position is considered more practical.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred view)
The Hanafi position (mufta bihi) is that the meat is halal, though using the recommended method is better. This is based on the strength of the hadith and the absence of explicit prohibition of the opposite method.
The ruling aligns with the Hanafi maxim of 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' – the end (draining blood) validates the means.

Core Idea
Any method causing blood flow is permissible; the inherited Sunnah method is preferred but not required.

The Hanafi school holds that the method of slaughter is not a condition of validity—any technique that causes blood flow suffices. However, the Prophet (peace be upon him) demonstrated specific methods for different animals, and these are considered Sunnah muakkadah. This lesson unpacks the proof for both the permissibility and the dislike of abandoning the Sunnah, and clarifies a potential confusion from a companion's narration.

Identify the two rulings
The text begins by stating permissibility (jawaz) and dislike (karahah). Understand that permissibility is based on the hadith, and dislike is for opposing the inherited Sunnah.

The tool is not specified; any tool that causes blood flow is valid.

Understand the inherited Sunnah
The Sunnah for camel is nahr (standing with left leg tied). For sheep and cow, it is dhabh (lying on the side). Recognize that these are preferred but alternatives are permissible.

Apply to practice
From the passage, derive the ruling: facing qibla is Sunnah for all slaughters. For camels, nahr standing is best; for sheep/cows, dhabh lying down is best.

The Hanafi Dispute on Eating a Dead Fetus from a Slaughtered Animal
Intra-Hanafi dispute on fetus found dead in slaughtered animal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two Hanafi positions on eating a dead fetus found in a slaughtered animal and evaluate the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A dead fetus from a slaughtered animal: Abu Hanifa says haram (maytah); the Sahibayn say halal if fully formed.

When a slaughtered animal is found to have a dead fetus in its womb, a question arises: is the fetus permissible to eat? This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute on this issue. By understanding the proofs presented by Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, you will grasp how textual sources and analogical reasoning can lead to different conclusions within the same school.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's absolute prohibition
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Zufar: the dead fetus is not eaten whether it has hair (fully formed) or not. The reason is that it falls under the Quranic prohibition of carrion.
The matn presents this as the default position.
2
State the Sahibayn's conditional permission
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that if the fetus is fully formed (creation complete), it is halal to eat. If not fully formed, it is prohibited.
They rely on a hadith and analogy.
3
Examine the Sahibayn's proof
The hadith 'The slaughter of the fetus is the slaughter of its mother' and the analogy that the fetus is like a part of the mother (enters in sale, is freed with her) are their main arguments.
These proofs suggest the fetus is included in the mother's slaughter.
4
Examine Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa counters with the Quranic verse 'Forbidden to you are carrion' (5:3). Since the fetus dies naturally (not by the mother's slaughter), it is carrion. The hadith is interpreted as applying only if the fetus comes out alive and is then slaughtered.
The analogy is weak because the fetus is a separate life.
5
Determine the thamarah and mufta bihi
The practical difference: followers of Abu Hanifa avoid the fetus; followers of the Sahibayn eat it if fully formed. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's absolute prohibition.
Later authorities prefer Abu Hanifa's view.

Prohibition of Eating Fanged Beasts of Prey and Clawed Birds
Ruling statement on forbidden predatory animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on eating fanged beasts and clawed birds and identify the criteria that trigger the prohibition.

Core Idea
All predatory animals with fangs and all birds with claws are haram to eat in the Hanafi school.

This lesson presents a foundational ruling in Hanafi dietary law: the prohibition of consuming certain animals based on their physical characteristics. The passage from the matn of al-Quduri explicitly forbids eating any fanged beast of prey (sabu') and any clawed bird (tayr). Understanding this ruling requires parsing the terms 'dhi nab' and 'dhi mikhleb' and applying them to real animals.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the prohibited objects
The act is eating (akl). The objects are two: (1) kullu dhi nāb min as-sibā‘ (every fanged beast of prey); (2) kullu dhi mikhleb min at-ṭayr (every clawed bird).
The word 'kull' indicates generality — all such animals are included.
2
Define the key terms
What exactly is meant by 'dhi nāb' and 'dhi mikhleb'? Fangs (nāb) are canine teeth used for tearing; claws (mikhleb) are talons used for seizing prey. Only animals that naturally possess these traits for predation are included.
Domesticated animals like cats have claws but are not wild predators; however, cats are also considered beasts of prey by some scholars.
3
Determine the scope of prohibition
The prohibition covers all beasts of prey with fangs and all birds with claws. There is no exception for size or species; the trait governs.
Animals like dogs and wolves are included; cows and sheep are excluded because they lack fangs.
4
Apply the ruling to specific examples
Lion (fanged beast) and eagle (clawed bird) are haram. Chicken and cow are halal. Sparrow is halal because it has no claws.
Hyenas have fangs; Hanafis consider them haram, though other schools differ.
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Hanafi school is strict: all fanged beasts and clawed birds are haram. The Maliki school may allow some fanged animals (e.g., wolf) under certain conditions. This difference arises from different interpretations of the hadith evidence.
The Hanafi position is based on the general prohibition in hadith: 'Every fanged beast of prey is haram to eat.'

The Independent Life of the Fetus: Why Mother's Slaughter Does Not Extend
Ruling with proof on the legal status of a fetus found dead in a slaughtered mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the fetus found dead after maternal slaughter and explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) for the ruling.

Core Idea
The mother's slaughter does not slaughter the fetus; their lives and blood are separate.

When a pregnant animal is slaughtered, what happens to the fetus inside? Does the mother's slaughter automatically render the fetus permissible? This passage from Al-Jawharah al-Nayirah addresses exactly this question, presenting the Hanafi position with rational proof. The core issue is whether the life of the fetus is independent of the mother's life. Understanding this ruling clarifies not only the fiqh of slaughter but also the Hanafi approach to legal reasoning using analogy and independent evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the specific scenario? The mother is slaughtered, and the fetus is found dead inside her. The ruling: the fetus is not to be eaten (haram).
This is the clear position of the Hanafi school.
2
Locate the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The passage uses a rational argument: the fetus has its own life and blood independent of the mother. Therefore, her slaughter cannot suffice for him. They are like two separate sheep.
The proof is based on the nature of separate existence.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The legal link: slaughter is legislated to drain impure blood. The mother's slaughter does not cause the fetus's blood to exit. Hence, the fetus remains carrion.
The fetus's blood is separate and untouched by the mother's throat cut.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars (notably al-Shafi'i) rely on the hadith 'Dhakatu ummihi' (the slaughter of the mother is its slaughter). The Hanafi response interprets it metaphorically: 'like the slaughter of his mother', not literally.
The dispute is about whether the hadith overrides rational analogy.

Obligation of Udhiyah for Minor Children
Intra-Hanafi dispute on udhiyah for minors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two Hanafi narrations on udhiyah for minor children, identify the mufta bihi position, and explain the legal reasoning (talil) that supports it.

Core Idea
Udhiyah for minor children is not obligatory in the relied-upon Hanafi position because it is a pure act of worship not tied to maintenance.

The matn of al-Quduri states that a person sacrifices for himself and his minor children. However, the commentator (al-Jawharah al-Nayyirah) clarifies that there are two narrations from Imam Abu Hanifa. The first, via Hasan ibn Ziyad, makes it obligatory for the father by analogy with sadaqat al-fitr. The second, which is the Zahir al-Riwayah (the authoritative transmission), restricts the obligation to oneself. This lesson dissects the legal reasoning (talil) behind the mufta bihi position and its implications for fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn's explicit statement
Read the base text: 'He sacrifices for himself and his minor children.' Notice it appears to command sacrifice for both. This sets up the dispute.
The matn alone does not settle the issue; the commentary reveals the nuanced ikhtilaf.
2
Recognize the two narrations from Abu Hanifa
The commentator cites two positions: (1) the narration of Hasan ibn Ziyad, which makes it wajib for children by qiyas with fitrah; (2) the Zahir al-Riwayah, which makes it not wajib except for oneself.
The Zahir al-Riwayah is the mufta bihi (operative) position.
3
Examine the analogy with fitrah and its rebuttal
The Hasan narration argues: just as sadaqat al-fitr is wajib for dependants, udhiyah should be too. The rebuttal: the 'illah of fitrah is maintenance (nafaqah), while udhiyah is a pure act of worship (qurbah mahdah) where the principle is that worship is not obligatory from one person on behalf of another without evidence.
The principle 'al-asl fi al-qurb an la tajib 'ala al-ghayr bi sabab al-ghayr' is key.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its evidence
The Zahir al-Riwayah is relied upon because it is consistent with the principle of qurbah mahdah and is supported by consensus that one does not sacrifice for a slave. Hence, the father is not obligated to sacrifice for his minor children.
This does not prohibit doing so voluntarily; it only negates obligation.

The Effect of Purchase on Udhiyah Obligation: Wealthy vs. Poor
Intra-Hanafi dispute on ta'ayyun and offspring

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a wealthy person must replace a defective sacrificial animal while a poor person does not, and state the ruling on the offspring of the sacrificial animal according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Obligation of sacrifice arises from Shari'ah for the wealthy, but from purchase with intention for the poor — thus ta'ayyun differs.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira explores a crucial intra-Hanafi dispute: does purchasing an animal for sacrifice determine that specific animal as the obligatory one? The answer depends on whether the person was already obligated by Shari'ah. The wealthy, already obligated, are not bound to that particular animal; the poor, not originally obligated, become obligated by their purchase, making the animal specifically determined. This distinction has practical implications for defects, death, and the offspring of the sacrificial animal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Distinguish between the wealthy (mūsir) who is already obligated by Shari'ah, and the poor (mu‘sir) who is not originally obligated. The wealthy possesses nisab; the poor does not.
The definition of wealth for udhiyah is similar to zakat nisab.
2
Analyze ta'ayyun (determination)
For the wealthy, the obligation pre-exists, so purchasing an animal does not make it specifically obligatory (no ta'ayyun). For the poor, the obligation arises from the purchase with intention, so the animal becomes ta'ayyun.
Ta'ayyun means the specific animal is fixed as the sacrifice.
3
Apply to cases of defect or death
If the purchased animal becomes defective or dies, the wealthy must replace it because the obligation is not tied to that animal. The poor need not replace it because the obligation is fulfilled by that specific animal.
This is the relied-upon view in the text.
4
Examine the offspring dispute
Some companions hold that the obligation extends to the offspring, so it must be slaughtered. Others (the majority of our companions) say the offspring is not obligated; if sold or eaten, one must give charity.
Al-Khujandi adds that if slaughtered with the mother on Eid, it suffices.
5
Summarize the practical differences
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is whether a wealthy person must replace a defective animal and whether the offspring must be slaughtered.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Only domestic livestock are valid; the mother determines hybrid validity; minimum ages: thani for most, jadha' for sheep if large.

Sacrificing an animal during Eid al-Adha is a deeply rewarding act of worship, but it comes with specific legal conditions. For crossbred animals, the species of the mother determines validity. If the mother is domestic, the offspring is valid; if wild, it is not.

Sheep are the only species where an animal below thani (i.e., jadha') is accepted, but only if it meets the size requirement.

This lesson examines a ruling from al-Quduri's Mukhtasar: it is disliked for a Kitabi (Jew or Christian) to slaughter the udhiyah (sacrificial animal). The reason is that the udhiyah is a devotional act (qurbah), and a Kitabi is not qualified to perform acts of worship on behalf of Muslims. Identify the act and its ruling
The main act is a Kitabi slaughtering the udhiyah. The ruling is 'yukrah' (it is disliked). In Hanafi terminology, this is makruh tahrimi, meaning it is prohibited in a non-definitive way.

The dislike is because the udhiyah is a qurbah (act drawing near to Allah), and the Kitabi is not from its people – i.e., not qualified to perform Islamic devotions.

The first scenario (Kitabi slaughtering) involves makruh tahrimi – closer to haram. The second scenario (Muslim slaughtering by delegation) is makruh tanzihi – permissible but disliked. Understanding this hierarchy is key in Hanafi fiqh.
