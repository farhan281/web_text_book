Master's Liability When Acting After Knowledge
Further cases after knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that the master's full arsh is due when he sells, frees, or performs any transaction that prevents handing over the slave after knowledge, and identify which acts do not constitute choice (like renting, pawning, marriage).

Core Idea
Any deliberate act preventing handover after knowledge is ransom choice; full arsh due.

This lesson continues with the master's liability when he has knowledge of the slave's crime. The Hanafi school deems any transaction that prevents the victim from obtaining the slave as a choice of ransom, obligating full arsh. We cover sale, gift, tadbir, iqrar, renting, mukatib, and marriage, and the distinction between active choice and mere use.

Guided Path
1
State the rule after knowledge
If the master sells or frees the slave after knowing of the crime, the full arsh is wajib upon him.
Knowledge changes the ruling from lesser amount to full arsh.
2
List acts that constitute 'choice' (ikhtiyar)
Sale, gift, tadbir, iqrar, and freeing upon the victim's order are all acts that prevent the victim's access, thus the master is deemed to have chosen ransom.
Even if the sale is to the victim, it counts as choice.
3
Identify acts that do NOT constitute choice
Mere use (istikhdām) that destroys the slave accidentally, renting (ijārah), pawning (rahn), and marriage (tazwīj) are not considered choice. The master may still be forced to hand over or ransom.
The judge annuls the lease if the master rents after knowledge.
4
Apply to the mukatib case
If the master makes a contract of kitāba after knowledge, then the slave later becomes incapable (ʿajaza), according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf the master owes full arsh. If the master was unaware at the time of kitāba, he may choose to hand over or ransom.
There is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: Imam Muhammad may hold a different view.

Master's Liability for Crimes of Mudabbar and Umm Walad
Explains master's liability for jinaaya of mudabbar and umm walad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for master's liability when a mudabbar or umm walad commits a crime and determine the applicable value.

Core Idea
Master pays lesser of slave's value and arsh; for mudabbar two-thirds, for umm walad one-third.

This passage clarifies the master's financial liability when a mudabbar (a slave promised freedom after death) or an umm walad (a slave mother of her master's child) commits a crime. The ruling balances the rights of the victim and the master's limited ownership over these special categories of slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the crime
Recognize that the passage deals with two types of slaves: mudabbar and umm walad. The crime (jinaaya) can be homicide or injury, presumed to be unintentional (khata).
2
Determine the master's liability
The master is liable for the compensation from his own wealth, not from his 'a'ila (clan). The liability is immediate (halla).
This contrasts with free persons where 'a'ila pay diyah over three years.
3
Apply the limitation rule
The master pays the lesser of the slave's value and the arsh (prescribed compensation). This protects the victim from receiving less than arsh and the master from losing more than the slave's worth.
If the slave's value is less than arsh, master pays value; if arsh is less, he pays arsh.
4
Valuate the slave on the correct day
The slave's value is assessed on the day of the crime, not the day of tadbir (for mudabbar). For umm walad, the relevant portion is one-third of her full value; for mudabbar, two-thirds.
This is because the master's ownership is restricted: umm walad cannot be sold, and mudabbar's tadbir reduces his value.

Master's Liability for Mudabbar and Umm Walad's Jiniyah
Rulings on master's guarantee for slave injuries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for the master's liability when a mudabbar or umm walad commits an injury, and apply it to subsidiary cases such as hiring, marriage, and kitaba.

Core Idea
The master pays the lesser of the slave's current value or the blood money.

When a mudabbar (slave promised freedom upon master's death) or umm walad (slave mother of master's child) commits an injury, the master is financially liable. However, his liability is capped: he pays only the lesser of the slave's value (at the time of injury) or the full blood money (arsh). This lesson examines the main rule, its reasoning, and important sub-rulings involving hiring, marriage, and the mukatib slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the slaves covered
The ruling applies to mudabbar and umm walad — slaves with partial freedom after the master's death.
2
State the general rule
The master guarantees the lesser of the slave's value (at time of injury) or the arsh (blood money).
The value is assessed on the day of injury, not tadbir.
3
Note the valuation fractions
For umm walad, only one-third of her value is considered; for mudabbar, two-thirds. This reflects their partial emancipation.
4
Explain subsidiary rulings
If the master hired out the slave, the judge voids the contract. Marriage does not shield the master. If a mukatib becomes insolvent after knowing of the injury, the arsh is on him (Abu Hanifa & Abu Yusuf).
These rulings prevent the master from escaping liability.
5
Understand the rationale
The victim has no right to more than arsh; the master cannot lose more than the slave's value. Hence the lesser amount.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Master's Liability for Payment Without Court Order
Ruling on multiple injuries and master's payment without court order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the master's liability when he voluntarily pays for a mudabbar's injury, and explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Voluntary payment by the master creates liability to a second victim; compulsory payment by court order does not.

When a mudabbar commits multiple injuries, the master's payment to the first victim may affect the second victim's recourse. This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute: if the master pays voluntarily (without court order), does he become directly liable to the second victim? Abu Hanifa says yes; the Sahibayn say no. The distinction hinges on whether the master's payment was compelled or voluntary, and the 'mani' (preventer) status of the master.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the ruling when payment is by court order
Identify the agreed-upon rule: if the master pays the first victim by court order, he has no liability to the second; the second victim must pursue the first victim and share in what was taken.
This part is undisputed between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
Identify the dispute when payment is without court order
Note that the dispute arises only when the master pays voluntarily. The text explicitly presents Abu Hanifa's view (the second has a choice) and contrasts it with the Sahibayn's view (the second has no recourse against the master).
The dispute is intra-Hanafi, not inter-madhhab.
3
Analyze each position's 'illa (legal reasoning)
Abu Hanifa argues that the mudabbar's liability stems from the master's 'mani' status (preventer of sale). Voluntary payment transfers to the first what the second had a right to, so the master is liable. The Sahibayn argue that payment without court order is the same as with; the master paid when no right existed for the second, so he is not a transgressor.
The core difference is whether the master's voluntary act creates a new right for the second victim.
4
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, the second victim can sue the master directly. Under the Sahibayn, the second victim can only claim a share from the first victim. This affects who bears the ultimate loss.
The text does not specify which view is mufta bihi; both are recorded as valid within the school.
5
Note the valuation rule for the mudabbar
The passage concludes with a general rule: the value of the mudabbar is assessed at the time of injury, not at the time of tadbir or demand. This applies regardless of the dispute.
This rule is consistent with other Hanafi diyat rulings.

A valid demand transfers liability; attestation is precautionary but not mandatory.

Liability After Collapse of a Dangerous Wall
Rulings on liability for damages caused by a falling wall.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the liability (daman) for death, injury, and property damage when a dangerous wall collapses after a valid demand, and distinguish when the 'aqila is responsible versus the individual.

Core Idea
The 'aqila bears diyat for death and major injuries; property damage and minor injuries are personal; no kaffarah for the owner.

This lesson addresses the legal consequences once a dangerous wall collapses after a proper demand has been made. The Hanafi school distinguishes between loss of life, bodily injury, and property damage. The liability falls on the 'aqila (paternal relatives) for death and serious injuries, but only from the owner's personal wealth for lesser injuries and property damage. The passage also clarifies that the owner is not considered a direct killer and therefore no expiation (kaffarah) is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of damage
Determine whether the collapse caused death, injury, or property damage. The ruling differs based on the category.
The text specifically mentions 'نفوس' (lives), 'نفس' (life), 'ما دون النفس' (less than life), and 'أموال' (property).
2
Liability for death
If a person is killed, the diyat (blood money) is on the 'aqila of the owner. There is no kaffarah (expiation) because the owner is not a direct agent (ghayr mubashir).
The owner also does not lose inheritance rights.
3
Liability for injury
If the injury results in a compensation (arsh) reaching half a tenth of the full diyat for a man (i.e., 5 camels) or a tenth for a woman (also 5 camels), then it is on the 'aqila. If less, it is from the owner's personal wealth.
The threshold is based on the amount of the arsh.
4
Liability for property damage
Property damage (animals or goods) is always from the owner's own wealth, because the 'aqila does not bear responsibility for property (mal).
This includes both movable and immovable property.
5
Effect of denial by 'aqila
If the 'aqila denies that the collapsed wall belonged to the person claimed, then no diyat is due from them until witnesses prove ownership.
This prevents false claims.

Abu Hanifa: pays one-fifth (1/5) of the diya, based on ownership share. His proof: 'the victim died from the act of the wall, so liability is according to the share of ownership.'

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Liability for a jointly owned wall follows ownership share, unless the death is attributed to multiple causes.

This passage from al-Hidayah presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on diya liability when a wall owned by five persons kills someone after only one owner was warned. The dispute tests a key principle: is liability proportional to property share (Abu Hanifa) or split between valid and null causes (Sahibayn)? Understanding this disagreement sharpens your ability to handle cases of mixed liability in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the factual scenario
Establish the key facts: a wall owned by five persons falls and kills someone. Only one owner was formally warned (ashhad upon him). The warning does not affect the ownership shares but is relevant to the cause of death.

2
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Abu Hanifa holds that the warned owner pays only one-fifth of the diya on his 'a'qila, because liability is proportional to ownership share. The death is caused by the wall as a single act, so each owner's liability is according to his share.
The fact that only one was warned does not increase his share of liability.
3

The Sahibayn hold that the warned owner pays half the diya on his 'a'qila. They argue that the death resulted from two causes: the warned owner's share (a valid cause) and the unwarned owners' shares (a null cause). Thus, liability is split equally between valid and null.
They analogize to a case where a man wounds someone, and the victim also dies from a scorpion sting, snake bite, and lion attack; the man pays half.
4

Abu Hanifa's proof: the death is from the wall, so liability follows ownership. The Sahibayn's proof: the death has two distinct categories of causes – one valid, one null – so liability is half. The core difference is whether to treat the wall as one cause or as multiple causes based on ownership status.
No explicit rebuttal is given in the text; it remains a disagreement of causal attribution.
5
Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the warned owner pays 1/5 of the diya (less burden on his 'a'qila). Under the Sahibayn, he pays 1/2 (greater burden). The choice of opinion directly affects the financial liability of the 'a'qila.
The text does not specify which is mufta bihi; later Hanafi authorities often prefer Abu Hanifa's view in shared ownership cases.

A victim defending against a bite is not liable for the biter's lost teeth; the biter must pay for the arm injury.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi case involving a bite and the victim's defensive pull. Killing a Slave by Mistake: Value Cap and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on compensation for killing a slave by mistake with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for killing a slave by mistake, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), and calculate the payment according to the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The value of a slave killed by mistake is capped at 9,990 dirhams, because it is a crime against a human, not property.

This lesson examines the blood money (diya) for accidentally killing a slave. The matn of al-Quduri sets a cap of 10,000 dirhams, but with an unusual reduction to 9,990. Behind this lies a fundamental debate: is the killing an offense against property or against a human being? We will unpack the two Hanafi positions and understand why the majority view prevails.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the basic ruling
The act is killing a slave by mistake. The killer owes the slave's value, but not exceeding 10,000 dirhams. If the value equals or exceeds 10,000, the payment is 9,990 dirhams.
Note the odd reduction by ten dirhams — this is a deliberate symbolic difference from the free person's diya.
2
Examine the precedent from Ali (RA)
The commentator cites a case where ten men pulled a date palm that fell on one; Ali ruled each paid one-tenth of diya but dropped one-tenth because the victim contributed. This illustrates the principle of reduction when the victim contributes, though the reduction here is based on the slave not being equal to a free person.
This precedent supports the idea of deduction, but the specific ten dirham deduction is a rule of the school.
3
Understand the dispute: property vs. human analogy
Abu Yusuf argues it is jinaaya 'ala mal (aggression against property), so full value is due without cap, like damaging property. The two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) argue it is jinaaya 'ala nafs adami (aggression against a human person), so it should not exceed the diya of a free person (10,000), by analogy.
The two analogies lead to different outcomes: uncapped vs. capped at 9,990.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
For a slave worth 20,000, the majority position pays 9,990; Abu Yusuf would require 20,000. The difference is significant.
The thamarah also affects who pays — the 'aqila pay over three years, and kaffarah is obligatory in both views.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and its basis
The commentator states 'وهذا قولهما' (this is their saying), indicating the view of the two is relied upon. The majority position is preferred because it treats the slave as a human person, upholding dignity, and is recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah.
Tarjih authority: the text itself gives weight to the two, but Abu Yusuf's view is a legitimate minority within the school.

Diya for a Female Slave and Compensation for a Slave's Hand

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two variant rulings on the deduction from the diya of a female slave (ten vs five dirhams) and explain the principle of proportionality in the compensation for a slave's hand.

Core Idea
The diya for a female slave is capped at 5,000 dirhams with a deduction fixed at ten dirhams in the relied-upon narration.

This passage from al-Jawhara al-Nayyira clarifies the precise compensation amounts for injuries to slaves. The reasoning behind the deduction is tied to the nisab of theft (ten dirhams) and the proportionality of the female slave's diya to that of a free woman. The second part extends the same logic to the hand of a slave, where the compensation is half his value, with a cap and deduction mirroring the structure for the whole body.

The commentator affirms that the Hidayah's version (except ten) is the apparent (zahir) narration and thus the mufta bihi. For the hand, the matn's 'except five' aligns with the Hidayah's logic.

The Aqilah's Liability for Accidental Killing of a Slave
Intra-Hanafi dispute on aqilah liability for slave accidental killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on aqilah liability for accidental killing of a slave and evaluate Abu Yusuf's objection.

Core Idea
The aqilah bears the value of a slave killed accidentally, unless the slave was the perpetrator.

Umar ibn al-Khattab said: 'The aqilah does not bear liability for intentional killing nor for a slave.' This statement is the basis of a dispute within the Hanafi school. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad interpret it to refer to crimes committed by the slave, not crimes against the slave. Abu Yusuf interprets it literally, excluding slaves entirely from aqilah liability. This lesson unpacks the evidence and the preferred ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the contested ruling: does the aqilah (the killer's clan) bear the value of a slave killed accidentally?
This is a dispute among the three major Hanafi imams.
2
Present the Hanafi majority position
According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, the value is on the aqilah. They reason that aqilah liability for accidental death applies to all persons, including slaves.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
3
Present Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that the value is on the killer's own wealth, not the aqilah. He relies on Umar's statement.
His position is within the Hanafi school but not followed for fatwa.
4
Analyze the evidence
Examine Umar's statement: 'لا تعقل العاقلة عمداً ولا عبداً.' Abu Yusuf takes it literally. The majority responds by interpreting 'عبداً' as referring to the slave as perpetrator.
They argue that 'عبداً' is mahmul on jinaayat al-‘abd, not al-jinaayah ‘alayhi.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The difference affects who pays and over what period: with aqilah, payment is spread over three years; with personal wealth, it is immediate.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary

The 'aqilah only bears liability for accidental killing of a person. However, if a slave is killed accidentally, the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) hold that the slave's value is upon the 'aqilah. Ghurrah for Miscarriage: Hanafi Liability and Maliki Dispute
Ruling on ghurrah and Maliki dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ghurrah for causing a stillborn fetus, identify the condition for its applicability, and explain the Maliki disagreement on who pays and who inherits.

Core Idea
The ghurrah is on the 'aqilah, not the striker's own wealth.

When a person causes a woman to miscarry a stillborn fetus, Islamic law prescribes a specific compensation called ghurrah. But who pays it — the striker or his clan? And who inherits it — the mother or all heirs? The Hanafi school and Imam Malik give different answers. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position, its proof from hadith, and the practical consequences of the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is striking a pregnant woman causing her to miscarry a stillborn fetus. The ruling is the obligation of a ghurrah — a slave worth 500 dirhams.
The ghurrah is half a tenth of the full diyah of a man (10,000 dirhams), or one tenth of a woman's diyah.
2
Establish the condition for ghurrah
The ghurrah is only due if the fetus had formed distinct limbs (istabāna khalquhu). If it was mere fluid, no compensation is required.
3
Locate the dalil
The Hanafi proof is a hadith where the Prophet ﷺ placed a ghurrah on the 'aqilah of a woman who struck another causing miscarriage, without asking the gender of the fetus.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted as evidence.
4
Analyze the Maliki dispute
Imam Malik held that the ghurrah is on the striker's own wealth (not the 'aqilah) and belongs exclusively to the mother. The Hanafi school rebuts by likening ghurrah to diyah, which is on the 'aqilah, and applying general inheritance rules.
The thamarah al-khilaf affects who pays and who receives.
5
Note the live vs stillborn distinction
If the fetus is born alive and then dies from the blow, the full diyah (and kaffarah) become due, not just ghurrah.
This is a higher degree of liability.

Commentary
This passage addresses the legal consequences of causing a miscarriage. The base ruling is from the matn: If a man strikes a woman's belly and she miscarries a stillborn fetus, he must pay a ghurrah—a male or female slave valued at half a tenth of the diyah of a free man, which equals 500 dirhams (since a full diyah is 10,000 dirhams). This applies regardless of the fetus's gender, as proven by a hadith where the Prophet ﷺ ruled similarly without asking the sex. The ghurrah is due provided the fetus had formed limbs or at least some features (istibaan al-khalq). According to the Hanafi school, this ghurrah is upon the 'aqilah of the striker, payable over three years (as is typical for diyah). Imam Malik differed, placing the liability solely on the striker's personal wealth. The ruling applies to a free fetus: either the mother is free, or she is a slave impregnated by her master (or by a deceived man, maghrur, who believed her free), resulting in a free child. The ghurrah is inherited by the fetus's heirs (excluding the striker if he is an heir); it is not solely the mother's. Malik held it is for the mother. The passage concludes with a critical distinction: if the fetus is born alive and then dies from the blow, full diyah (and kaffarah) become due, and this is also on the 'aqilah. Thus, the lesser ghurrah is only for stillbirth; live birth followed by death elevates the obligation to full diyah.

The diyah is for killing the mother, and the ghurrah is for destroying the fetus. 

**Case 2: Mother dies first, then stillbirth.** If the mother dies first, then the fetus is delivered dead, only the mother's diyah is due; nothing is owed for the fetus because its life was dependent on the mother and could not be independently established. 

**Additional cases explained:** 
- If the fetus is born alive, then dies, and then the mother dies, two full diyahs are due (one for each). The mother's estate inherits from the fetus's diyah. 
- If the mother dies first, then the fetus is born alive and dies, two diyahs are also due. 

**Inheritance rules for the fetus's diyah:** The diyah obligated for the fetus is inherited by its heirs because it substitutes for the fetus itself. The text clarifies that if the fetus is born alive, it can inherit and be inherited from. If born dead, it does not inherit (since inheritance requires life at death) but can be inherited from (i.e., its diyah is distributed to its heirs). A note from Khizanat Abi al-Layth lists four categories of people who do not inherit but are inherited from: a mukatab (slave making a contract of manumission), an apostate, a fetus (if stillborn), and a killer (who cannot inherit from the victim). 

**Twin cases:** If twins are miscarried, two ghurrahs are due. If one is born alive then dies, and the other stillborn, then a ghurrah for the stillborn and a full diyah for the live-born are due, plus kaffarah (expiation) for the striker. If the mother dies and both are delivered dead, only the mother's diyah is owed. If both are born alive then die, three full diyahs are due (one for mother, two for each twin). 

**Etymology of 'ghurrah':** It is named 'ghurrah' because it is the first fixed compensation for an injury to a child; 'ghurrah' means the first of anything, like the 'ghurrat al-shahr' (beginning of the month).

Key Terms
غُرَّة (ghurrah): The fixed compensation for the loss of a fetus, set at one-twentieth of the mother's diyah (for a free woman) or a portion of the slave's value.
دِيَة (diyah): Blood money, the financial compensation paid to the heirs of a killed person.

Diyah for the Fetus of a Slave Woman
Explains the diyah for a slave's fetus and justifies the gender-based difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on diyah for a slave woman's fetus and explain the basis for the differential compensation between male and female fetuses.

Core Idea
The diyah for a female slave's fetus is twice that of a male because compensation is based on growth potential, not ownership status.

The Hanafi school provides a detailed ruling on the compensation (diyah) for the fetus of a slave woman, applying a unique ratio based on the gender of the fetus. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its justification via the 'illa of cutting off growth (qat' al-nushu), and the Hanafi response to an objection regarding gender preference.

Guided Path
1
State the plain ruling
Identify what compensation is due for a male vs female fetus of a slave woman. The matn states: for a male, half of one-tenth (1/20) of its value; for a female, one-tenth (1/10).
2
Apply the ruling to an example
Work through the numeric example: if the value is 10 dinars, male yields 0.5 diyah, female yields 1 diyah. Note the female's compensation is double.
3
Identify the potential objection
The objection states that preferring the female over the male in compensation is impermissible, since for free persons a woman's diyah is half a man's.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The response is two-fold: (1) equality is not automatically required; it is based on consensus for free persons but not for fetuses. (2) The 'illa is cutting off growth (qat' al-nushu), not ownership. Female fetuses may grow faster, so higher compensation is justified.
This is a talil argument – reasoning by the 'illa.
5
Connect to the underlying qiyas
The reasoning is analogical: after birth, females often mature faster, so the same potential for faster growth applies to the fetus. Thus the compensation reflects the loss of that growth.

Commentary
This passage addresses the diyat (blood money) for the fetus of a slave woman (janeen al-ama). The matn states that if the fetus is male, the compensation is half of one-tenth (i.e., 1/20) of its value if it were alive; if female, it is one-tenth (1/10) of its value. Thus, the female fetus's diyah is twice that of a male fetus of equal value. 

The commentator provides an example: if a male fetus would be worth 10 dinars if alive, the diyah is 0.5 dinars; for a female fetus worth 10 dinars, the diyah is 1 dinar. 

A potential objection is raised: this gives preference (tafdil) to the female over the male in compensation, which is generally impermissible (since in full diyah for free persons, the female is half of the male). The Hanafi response is twofold: 
1. Just as preference is not allowed, equality is also not allowed without basis; here, equality (in diyah for male and female free persons) is established by consensus, but that does not apply to fetuses. 
2. The basis of the obligation is 'cutting off growth' (qat' al-nushu), not the attribute of ownership (malikiyyah). Since the fetus is not yet a full owner of property, the compensation is based on potential growth. A female fetus may be faster in growth (nushu) than a male, just as after birth, females often mature faster. Therefore, a higher compensation for the female is justified.

قطع النشو (qat' al-nushu): The legal cause ('illa) for the obligation of compensation: the cutting off of the fetus's potential development and growth.

For a slave woman's fetus, compensation is based on development, not gender value, so male and female amounts differ.

The compensation (diya) for a fetus of a slave woman is not a fixed amount like that of a free woman but is tied to the fetus's value as property. This passage from Al-Jawhara al-Nayra details the precise fractions, addresses a key objection about gender preference, and expands on multiple scenarios including twins and the effect of emancipation. Understanding these rules requires grasping the principle that the obligation is based on 'cutting off development' (qat' al-nashw), not on ownership or gender worth.

Guided Path
1
State the basic rule for a single fetus
From the matn: for a male fetus, half of one-tenth of its hypothetical value if alive; for a female, one-tenth of its value. Note the example: value 10 dinars → 0.5 dinar for male, 1 dinar for female.

Commentary
This is a commentary (sharh) on the rulings for the indemnity of a fetus (janin) of a slave woman (amah), detailing various scenarios and addressing an objection regarding the differentiation between male and female fetuses. The passage begins by stating the basic rule from the matn: for a slave woman's fetus, the compensation is half of one-tenth of the fetus's hypothetical value if alive if the fetus is male, and one-tenth if female. The commentator explains this with an example: if the value would be ten dinars, then half a dinar for a male and one full dinar for a female. An objection is raised: this gives preference (tafdil) to the female over the male, which might be considered unjust. The Hanafi response is that equality (taswiyah) is also not permissible by consensus in other contexts, so preference is allowed here. The reasoning is that the obligation is based on cutting off development (qat' al-nashw), not on ownership, and the female may develop faster. The passage then covers several scenarios: if one twin is born alive and dies while the other is stillborn, a ghurra and a diya are required; if the mother dies and both are stillborn, only the mother's diya is due; if both are born alive then die, three diyas are due. The term 'ghurra' is explained as the first estimated amount for fetal injury, akin to the first of the month. Next, the issue of expiation (kaffarah) is addressed: no expiation is required for a fetus because expiation is for complete souls and the fetus's life is unknown, though voluntary expiation is allowed. The commentary also distinguishes between types of slave women: for a mudabbara (promised freedom after master's death) the same ruling applies, but for umm al-walad (mother of the master's child) the compensation is like that for a free woman. A specific case from Al-Hidayah is cited: if the master frees the fetus after a strike and it is born alive then dies, only the value (qimah) is due, not diya, because the death results from a prior act during slavery. Finally, quoting Al-Karkhi, it is noted that the compensation for a slave woman's fetus is taken from the striker's wealth immediately because the guarantee is like that of property, not subject to qisas or kaffarah.
