Core Idea
Each finger is worth one-tenth of the full diyah; five fingers total half.

This passage establishes the fixed compensation for the loss of fingers in Hanafi fiqh. Understanding the equal valuation of each finger is essential for calculating diyah in cases of accidental injury. The ruling is rooted in the sunnah and simplifies the determination of compensation.

Each finger is worth one-tenth of the full diyah. Therefore, five fingers together amount to five-tenths (5/10 = 1/2) of the full diyah.

Abu Yusuf considers everything above the palm and foot as dependent on the fingers. Thus, the diyah for the entire forearm or upper arm is based on the fingers' diyah (half diyah for all five fingers) and no separate government value.
This means the forearm is not independent in valuation.

Under the two's view, the victim gets more compensation when the cut includes forearm/upper arm because government value is added. Under Abu Yusuf, the compensation is limited to the fingers' diyah regardless of where the cut is made on the arm.
The difference becomes significant in cases of amputation above the wrist.
4
Analyze the principle behind each view
The two emphasize the functional primacy of the palm (grip) and treat limbs in sections. Abu Yusuf applies a holistic dependency principle: the forearm lacks independent benefit and is a follower of the fingers.
Both are valid within the Hanafi school; the mufta bihi is the view of the two.

It covers when full diyah is due, when hukuma (estimated compensation) applies, and an important difference between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding a child's regrown tooth. Understanding these rules clarifies how Hanafi law values different body parts and injuries.

Limb qisas that leads to death divides the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa imposes diyat al-nafs; the Sahibayn hold no liability.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about unintended consequences of qisas. When a person lawfully executes qisas on a limb but the wound spreads and kills the criminal, is the executor liable for the death? Abu Hanifa and his two disciples differ on the scope of the right to qisas and the legal effect of sirayah. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for accurate fatwa and illustrates the school's internal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the point of dispute
The scenario: a person has a right to qisas on a limb (e.g., cut off the hand of a murderer). He cuts it, and the wound spreads to the body, causing death. The dispute: is the executor liable for diyat al-nafs?
The right to limb qisas is established from intentional injury.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the executor must pay diyat al-nafs because he killed without full right. His right was only to the limb, not to take life. Qisas for life is dropped due to shubha (doubt), so financial compensation becomes wajib.
The shubha arises because the executor had a right to cut, but the death was unintended.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the executor is not liable because he fulfilled his legitimate right to cut. He cannot be held for an unforeseen consequence (sirayah) that he could not control. Restricting qisas with a condition of safety would block the door to limb qisas entirely.
The Sahibayn emphasize the practicality of qisas: if liability always attached, few would exercise their right.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The text includes a rebuttal: the executor proceeded to cut thinking his right was in the limb. After sirayah, it becomes clear that his right was actually in retaliation (qisas for life, i.e., the death penalty). Therefore, he was not absolving without knowledge of the full extent of the right.
This response questions the executor's awareness and the true scope of the right.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the practical consequence
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the executor must pay a large sum (diyat al-nafs); under Sahibayn, no payment. Later Hanafi fatwa works often prefer the Sahibayn's view as more practical, avoiding discouragement of qisas.
The mufta bihi (operative ruling) leans toward no liability.

Core Idea
If a hired digger unknowingly digs on another's land, the hirer is liable; if the digger knows, he is liable.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on liability when a person hires someone to dig a well on what the digger believes is the hirer's property, but it turns out to be another's land. The key distinction is the digger's knowledge. The rule balances the hirer's command and the digger's trespass, with ignorance excusing the digger through the principle of 'ghurur' (deception).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and actors
The act is digging a well. The actors are the hirer (musta'jir) and the digger (hafir). The land belongs to a third party.
2

The default is that the hirer is liable for damages, not the digger, because the digger acted under the hirer's command.
This shifts liability to the one who gave the order, not the physical actor.
3
Apply the condition of knowledge
If the digger did not know the land was not the hirer's, he is excused (ma'dhur) and the hirer remains liable. If the digger knew, then he becomes liable.

4

The hirer's command is not valid (nafidh) on land he does not own. Without deception (ghurur) from the hirer (if the digger knew), the act is attributed to the digger.
Ghurur (deception) only applies if the hirer misled the digger; here, if the digger knows, there is no deception.
5
State the final liability
Summarize: Hirer liable if digger ignorant; digger liable if digger knew.
This is a clear Hanafi ruling with no dispute among the school's founders.
