The Dispute on Hearing Oneself in Legal Speech Acts
Intra-Hanafi dispute on hearing oneself in speech acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Imam Karakhi and Hindwani on hearing oneself, identify the thamarah al-khilaf for talaq and itaq, and apply the same dispute to istithna, tasmiyah, and sajdah tilawah.

Core Idea
Hearing oneself in speech acts: Karakhi requires it, Hindwani does not — the same ikhtilaf echoes across divorce, manumission, exception, and prayer.

This passage from Al-Hidayah clarifies an important intra-Hanafi dispute. Imam Karakhi and Hindwani differ on whether the speaker must hear his own utterance for legal effects to occur. This is not just theoretical — it has practical consequences in divorce, manumission, oaths, and even in prayer and slaughter. Let's unpack the reasoning and its applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and its basis
The dispute originates from the prayer chapter where one may recite aloud (making oneself hear) or silently. The question: does this choice imply that hearing oneself is a condition for valid speech acts, or that it is not necessary?
The basis is a textual reference, not a direct hadith.
2
State the two Hanafi positions
Imam Karakhi holds that for talaq and itaq to occur, the speaker must hear himself. Hindwani holds that hearing oneself is not a condition; the utterance is effective even if not heard by the speaker.
Both are valid transmissions within the Hanafi school.
3
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf for talaq and itaq
If someone says 'You are divorced' but does not hear himself, Karakhi rules no divorce; Hindwani rules divorce occurs. Similarly for manumission.
The practical difference is immediate marital status.
4
Extend to istithna, tasmiyah, and sajdah
The same dispute applies when a person makes an exception (istithna) in a statement without hearing it, says tasmiyah over an animal without hearing it, or recites a verse of sajdah without hearing himself.
The principle is general to all speech-based acts.
5
Note the absence of tarjih
The passage does not specify which view is mufta bihi. Both positions are transmitted, and the later Hanafi scholars have not settled this clearly in this excerpt.
A mufti should be aware of both opinions and may need to exercise caution.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Karakhi and Hindwani regarding the requirement of hearing oneself when pronouncing legal utterances. The basis is from the prayer chapter where one can recite aloud (making oneself hear) or silently. Karakhi extends this to all speech acts: for talaq and itaq to be effective, the speaker must hear himself. Hindwani holds it is not required. The same ikhtilaf applies to istithna (exception) in oaths/divorces, to tasmiyah on zabiha (saying Bismillah), and to sajdah tilawah (prostration of recitation). The text does not resolve which view is mufta bihi.

Key Terms
نطق (nutq): Speech or utterance, the act of pronouncing words
استثناء (istithna'): Exception in an utterance, e.g., 'except for such and such' in divorce or oath
