Ruling on Closing the Mosque Door: Dislike and Exception
Ruling on closing the mosque door with proof and exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on closing the mosque door, identify the Quranic proof, and apply the exception for fear of property loss.

Core Idea
Closing the mosque door is makruh because it resembles preventing prayer, but it becomes permissible when property is at risk.

This lesson examines the ruling on closing the mosque door. While the default position is that it is disliked (makruh) because it resembles preventing people from prayer, the Hanafi school recognizes an exception when there is genuine fear for the safety of the mosque's belongings. This ruling demonstrates how fiqh considers both the primary prohibition and the exigencies of time and place.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and primary ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon (closing the mosque door) and identify the default ruling (makruh) and the reason given (it resembles preventing prayer).
The text explicitly states 'يكره' (it is disliked).
2
Locate the proof
Find the Quranic verse cited as evidence. The verse condemns preventing the mention of Allah in mosques, establishing that preventing prayer is a great injustice.
The verse is from Surah Al-Baqarah 2:114.
3
Identify the exception
Note the phrase 'قيل لاباس به اذا خيف على متاع المسجد' – some said there is no harm if there is fear for the mosque's property. This introduces a conditional exception.
The exception applies only when there is genuine fear, e.g., theft or loss.
4
Understand the rationale for change
The commentary draws an analogy to women attending mosques – rulings can change with circumstances (fitna, security). This justifies the exception based on 'taghayyur al-zaman' (change of times).
This is a principle that some rulings may adapt to prevent harm.
5
Formulate the final ruling
Combine the primary rule and exception: normally makruh, but permissible (and even recommended) when there is a valid need to protect property.
The mufta bihi in later Hanafi practice leans toward allowing closure when needed.

Repetition of Wajib Sajdah for Listener: Change of Assembly
Intra-Hanafi dispute on repetition of sajdah for listener

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on whether the listener's wajib sajdah is repeated when the reciter's assembly changes, and identify the mufta bihi with its proof.

Core Idea
The listener's cause is hearing, not recitation; unchanged assembly means no repetition.

This passage from al-Hidayah explores a subtle intra-Hanafi dispute: if the reciter moves to a new assembly and repeats a sajdah verse, does the listener who remains in place owe a new prostration? The answer hinges on identifying the correct sabab (cause) for the listener's obligation. We will unpack the conflicting views and see why the preferred position insists on a clear distinction between the reciter's and listener's causes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the legal question
Recognize the two key variables: the reciter's assembly and the listener's assembly. The question is whether a change in the reciter's assembly alone renews the wajib for the listener.
2
Understand the agreed case (listener's assembly changes)
If the listener's own assembly changes (even if the reciter stays), all agree the wajib is repeated because hearing is renewed.
Consensus case
3
Examine the disputed case and opposing views
When only the reciter's assembly changes, Fakhr al-Islam and Imam Muhammad hold the listener's obligation is repeated. Their proofs: Fakhr al-Islam uses qiyas (hearing based on recitation); Imam Muhammad says the sabab is recitation for both.
Two opposing positions within the school
4
Analyze the Hanafi response distinguishing the sabab
The author of al-Hidayah rejects this by specifying that the listener's sabab is hearing (sama'), not recitation. Since the listener's assembly did not change, hearing was not renewed, so no new wajib.
Key principle: sabab differs between reciter and listener
5
Derive the mufta bihi and practical consequence
The mufta bihi is that the listener does not repeat the sajdah. The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear: a person who hears a sajdah verse in one location and then hears it again in another location without moving himself does not perform a second sajdah.
Mufta bihi: no repetition

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the repetition of wajib sajdah tilawah when the assembly (majlis) changes. The discussion focuses on whether the obligation is repeated for the listener when only the reciter's assembly changes. The author first presents the view that if the listener's assembly changes, the obligation is repeated by consensus. Then he considers the case where the reciter's assembly changes but the listener's assembly does not. Fakhr al-Islam (Al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah) is quoted as saying the obligation is repeated for the listener because the cause (sabab) for the listener is recitation, and by qiyas, a change in recitation's assembly is treated as a change in hearing's assembly. Some scholars, including Imam Muhammad, hold that the cause is recitation for both, so both obligations are repeated. However, the author (Sahib al-Hidayah) declares the correct view to be that the listener's obligation is not repeated because the cause for the listener is hearing (sama'), not recitation, and his hearing assembly did not change. This is a nuanced application of the principle that the sabab for wajib differs between reciter and listener.

Key Terms

سبب (sabab): The cause or occasion that triggers the wajib; for sajdah tilawah, it is recitation (tilawah) for the reciter and hearing (sama') for the listener.
مجلس (majlis): Assembly or session; the context in which recitation occurs. Change of majlis can affect the repetition of wajib.

The Wisdom Behind the Two Eids
Explains the spiritual and historical reasons for the two Eids.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the spiritual and historical reasons for Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha as presented in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
The two Eids replace pre-Islamic festivals with worship-based celebrations rooted in gratitude and prophetic devotion.

Eid is more than a day of feasting. The hadith in this passage shows that the Prophet ﷺ replaced the pre-Islamic festivals with two better days: Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha. Understanding the wisdom behind these Eids deepens our appreciation of Islamic worship and connects us to the prophetic and Abrahamic traditions. This lesson explores the twofold joy of Eid al-Fitr and the sacrificial commemoration of Eid al-Adha.

Guided Path
1
Identify the replacement
Read the hadith: The Prophet ﷺ replaced the two days of play and amusement with Eid al-Adha and Eid al-Fitr. This establishes that Islamic celebrations are divinely ordained.
The term 'بدل' (replacement) indicates a complete substitution.
2
Analyze the joy of Eid al-Fitr
The text distinguishes two types of joy: physical (completion of fasting) and intellectual (ability to perform obligatory worship and charity). Identify both in the passage.
Physical joy is temporary, spiritual joy is lasting.
3
Analyze the wisdom of Eid al-Adha
Eid al-Adha commemorates the devotion of Ibrahim and Isma'il. The text mentions that sacrifice is prescribed to remind Muslims of their patience and obedience, and to connect with the pilgrims.
The story of Ibrahim is a lesson in submission.
4
Connect to Hanafi fiqh
The Hanafi school holds the Eid prayer as wajib (obligatory) based on the Prophet's continuous practice. Reflect on how the wisdom supports the legal ruling.
Muwāẓabah (continuous practice) is a key usul principle.

Permissibility of Two Cloths as Sufficient Shroud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum sufficient shroud (kafan al-kifayah) for a man and explain the evidence that two cloths are permissible.

Core Idea
Two cloths (izar and lifafah) suffice as a man's shroud; the sunnah of three cloths is recommended, not obligatory.

This passage from al-Hidayah addresses a practical question: when a man dies, how many cloths are legally required for his shroud? While the sunnah is to use three cloths, the school clarifies that only two are sufficient to fulfill the obligation—and they do so on the strength of a companion's directive and a rational analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is shrouding a deceased man using only two cloths. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz). This is called the shroud of sufficiency (kafan al-kifayah).
The two cloths must be an izar (lower wrap) and a lifafah (upper sheet).
2
Locate the primary dalil (Qawl al-Sahabi)
The author cites the instruction of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him) who said about his two garments: 'Wash them and shroud me in them.' This is recorded in the Musannaf of Ibn Abi Shaybah.
The Hanafi school accepts the saying of a companion as a source of law (qawl al-sahabi).
3
Assess the second proof (Hadith of Man in Ihram)
The Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered that a man who died in ihram be shrouded in two cloths. This reinforces that two cloths are sufficient.
This hadith is cited in the commentary as additional evidence.
4
Understand the rational proof (Qiyas)
The minimum clothing for a living person is two garments (izar and lifafah). By analogy, the same minimum suffices for the deceased.
This qiyas is based on the shared illa (cause) of covering the body.
5
Distinguish between kifayah and sunnah
Two cloths fulfill the fard kifayah (communal obligation). Using three cloths is the sunnah, but not obligatory. The family may choose two if needed.
The mufta bihi position is that two are permissible, but three are better.

Procedure for wrapping the shroud on the deceased, including order of layers, tying, and discussion on the turban.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the sunnah procedure for wrapping a man's shroud and explain why the turban is not included.

Core Idea
The shroud must be an odd number of pieces; wrap left side first so the right is outermost.

The sunnah of shrouding a deceased Muslim is a practical expression of respect and devotion to the Prophet's practice. This lesson walks through the precise order of laying the cloths and folding them, as transmitted in al-Hidayah, and addresses a common point of confusion: whether to include a turban.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of the shroud
The shroud for a man consists of three pieces: lifafah (upper sheet), izar (lower wrap), and qamis (shirt).

2
Understand the spreading order
Lay the lifafah first, then the izar on top, then dress the deceased in the qamis and place him on the izar.

Fold the izar from the left side over the body first, then from the right. Repeat with the lifafah. The left-then-right order ensures the right side is outermost, as in life.

4
Analyze the turban (imamah) issue

The hadith of A'ishah (three white cloths) and the odd-number sunnah are the strongest evidence.
5
Apply the preference for white cotton
The Prophet commanded wearing white and shrouding the dead in white. Hence, white cotton is preferred for the shroud.
This is a sunnah recommendation, not a condition of validity.

This lesson details the recommended shroud for a deceased woman according to the Hanafi school. The passage presents the sunnah shroud consisting of five specific garments, supported by a hadith of Umm 'Atiyyah. It also clarifies that using three cloths is permissible and sufficient, while less is disliked, and draws a contrast with the ruling for men, where one cloth is only permissible in necessity.

Distinguish between men and women
For men, the sunnah is three cloths (izar, qamis, lifafah); one cloth only in darurah. For women, the sunnah is five; three is sufficient.

The Sunnah Method of Wrapping a Man's Shroud
Describes method of wrapping man's shroud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the sunnah method of wrapping a man's shroud and identify the number and types of garments used.

Core Idea
The sunnah shroud for a man is three white garments: a shirt, a waist-wrapper, and a wrapper—wrapped in a specific order.

In this lesson, we will learn the practical sunnah method of shrouding a man as described in the Hanafi tradition. This involves three specific garments laid and wrapped in a precise sequence. Understanding this practice ensures we fulfill the sunnah with proper care and avoid common errors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sunnah garments
The passage explicitly names them: the wrapper (lifafah), the waist-wrapper (izar), and the shirt (qamis). Confirm these are the three required pieces.
Some may mistakenly count the turban; we learn it is not included.
2
Learn the sequence of laying the shroud
First lay the wrapper, then on it lay the waist-wrapper, then dress the deceased in the shirt and place him on the waist-wrapper.
Order matters: wrapper first, then waist-wrapper, then shirt on the body.
3
Master the folding method
Fold the waist-wrapper from the left side first, then the right side (right on top). Then fold the wrapper similarly. This ensures proper coverage and respect.
The right side is placed on top as a sign of honor.
4
Know when to secure the shroud
If there is fear that the shroud will loosen, tie it with a band to keep it closed. This is a practical precaution.
It is not part of the sunnah steps but a permissible addition.

The Turban Dispute in the Man's Shroud
Dispute about including turban in shroud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on excluding the turban from the shroud, state the evidence from Aisha and Ibn Umar, and identify the mufta bihi.

Core Idea
The turban is not part of the sunnah shroud because it makes the number even, contradicting the Prophetic practice of three garments.

In this lesson, we delve into the dispute over including a turban in a man's shroud. Some scholars consider it mustahsan, while the Hanafi relied-upon view considers it makruh because it would make the number of garments even. We will examine the evidence from Aisha's hadith and the practice of Ibn Umar, and see why the Hanafi school prefers the three-garment sunnah.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The author of Hidayah does not mention the turban among the shroud garments. This indicates the relied-upon view: the sunnah is three garments; a turban is not included. Some scholars consider including it makruh because it makes the number even (four).
Even numbers are disliked in shrouds according to this view.
2
Identify the opposing view
Some scholars (including some Hanafis) consider the turban mustahsan (recommended). Their evidence is the practice of Ibn Umar, who would put a turban on the deceased and place its tail over the face.
This practice is reported from a companion, not from the Prophet directly.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi scholars respond that Ibn Umar's practice contradicts the clear hadith of Aisha that the Prophet was shrouded in three garments. Since her hadith is directly from the Prophet, it takes precedence over a companion's action.
The principle: when a companion's action contradicts a Prophetic hadith, the hadith prevails.
4
Apply the principle of odd numbers
The sunnah is to have an odd number of garments (three). Including a turban makes it even (four), which is disliked. This supports the Hanafi position.
The preference for odd numbers is established in various acts of worship.

States the sunnah and sufficient shroud for a woman with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five garments of the sunnah shroud for a woman, explain the sufficient shroud (kifayah) of three garments, and apply the ruling on using fewer garments with the necessity exception.

Core Idea
The sunnah woman's shroud is five garments, but three are sufficient; less requires necessity.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi ruling on the shroud for a deceased woman. The text distinguishes between the preferred (sunnah) shroud of five specific garments and the sufficient (kifayah) shroud of three garments. We will also see how the school handles cases of scarcity, drawing from the practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the early Muslims.

Guided Path
1
State the sunnah shroud for a woman
List the five garments: dirʿ (shirt), izār (waist-wrapper), khimār (head-cover), lifāfah (wrapper), and khirqah (breast-band). Note that the khirqah is specific to women.
These are the recommended garments, but not obligatory.
2
Identify the proof for the sunnah shroud
The primary proof is the hadith of Umm ʿAṭiyyah: the Prophet ﷺ gave five garments to the women who washed his daughter. Additionally, a rational analogy (qiyās) is mentioned: since a woman wears five garments in life, she is given the same after death.
The hadith is aḥad but has clear meaning (qatʿī al-dalālah).
3
State the sufficient shroud (kifāyah) and its ruling
The sufficient shroud is three garments: izār, lifāfah, and khimār. Using these is permissible (jāʾiz) and avoids makrūh.
This is the minimum that is not disliked.
4
Apply the ruling on using fewer garments
Using less than three garments for a woman without necessity is makrūh. If necessary, it becomes permissible (shroud of necessity). Similarly, for a man, only one garment is makrūh unless necessary.
The precedent is the shroud of Muṣʿab ibn ʿUmayr in one garment due to scarcity.
5
Contrast with the man's shroud
The man's sunnah shroud is three garments, and restricting to one is makrūh except in necessity. This highlights the gender difference in recommended number of garments.
The evidence for the man's shroud is from other sources; here it is compared briefly.

Commentary
This passage gives the ruling on the woman's shroud. It begins by stating the sunnah (recommended) shroud: five garments: a shirt (dir'), a waist-wrapper (izar), a head-cover (khimar), a wrapper (lifafah), and a breast-band (khirqah) tied above the breasts. The proof is the hadith of Umm 'Atiyyah: the Prophet gave these five garments to the women who washed his daughter. Additionally, rational analogy (qiyas) is mentioned: since a woman normally wears five garments in life, she is given the same after death. Then the sufficient shroud (kafan al-kifayah) is three garments: izar, lifafah, and khimar. It is permissible to use these if one wishes. Using less than three without necessity is makruh (disliked). The passage also compares to the man's case: it is makruh for a man to use only one garment unless necessary, as in the case of Mus'ab bin Umair who was shrouded in one cloak due to scarcity. The hadith of Khabbab is cited to support this, describing the situation of Mus'ab's shroud. The Hanafi school holds that the sunnah for a woman is five garments, but three are sufficient and less is permissible only under necessity.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The woman's shroud is placed in a specific order: shirt, hair braids on chest, head-cover, waist-wrapper, then wrapper.

The shroud (kafan) is the final garment of the deceased. For a woman, the Hanafi school prescribes a specific sequence to ensure modesty and proper preparation. This lesson details each step as recorded in al-Hidayah.

This sequence is the sunnah method; following it is recommended but not obligatory.

Ruling on Perfuming the Shroud
Establishes the recommended practice of fumigating the shroud an odd number of times with Prophetic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on perfuming the shroud with odd fumigations and explain the evidence from the hadith.

Core Idea
Perfuming the shroud with an odd number of fumigations is sunnah muakkadah, based on the Prophet's command for his daughter.

The shroud may be perfumed before wrapping the deceased. The Hanafi school considers it a highly recommended sunnah to fumigate the shrouds an odd number of times, following the Prophet's practice with his own daughter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
Fumigating (taqmir) the shrouds before placing the deceased in them.
The term used is ijmar, meaning perfuming by smoke.

Determine the ruling

It is not wajib because the proof is ahad (single narrator hadith).
3
Cite the dalil
The Prophet commanded his daughter's shroud to be fumigated with an odd number.
Hadith: 'amara bi-ijmāri akfān ibnatihi witran'
4
Note the odd number
The fumigation should be done an odd number of times (e.g., 1, 3, 5).
5
Connect to funeral prayer
After shrouding, the funeral prayer is offered; it is fard (obligatory).
The passage ends by stating that prayer is an obligation.

Ruling on Muslim Handling of a Deceased Non-Muslim Relative
Ruling with proof on washing and burying a non-Muslim relative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muslim washing and burying a non-Muslim relative, identify the proof from the hadith of Abu Talib, and apply the condition of takhliyah.

Core Idea
A Muslim must wash and bury a non-Muslim relative if no non-Muslim relatives are present, but without sunnah rituals.

When a non-Muslim dies and only a Muslim relative is available, Islamic law imposes a duty upon that Muslim to handle the body. However, the procedure is simplified—washing like a najis cloth, wrapping in a simple cloth, and throwing into a pit—and does not include the rituals of a Muslim funeral. This ruling is derived from the prophetic command to Ali regarding his father Abu Talib. Understanding this obligation helps clarify the boundaries of Muslim–non-Muslim relations in matters of death.

Carrying the Bier: Four Carriers vs Two, and the Speed of the Procession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the number of carriers for a funeral bier and the proper walking speed, and explain the reasoning behind the Hanafi position in response to the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Four men carry the bier from its four corners — the sunnah; walking briskly without running — following the Prophet's instruction.

This chapter from Al-Hidayah tackles two practical aspects of funeral rites: how many should carry the bier and how fast should they walk. The Hanafi school asserts that four carriers are sunnah, based on explicit hadith and the wisdom of honoring the deceased and ensuring safety. The Shafi'i school argues for two carriers, citing an incident from the funeral of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh. The passage also clarifies that the procession should move briskly but not run.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position on carriers
The Hanafi sunnah: four men, each holding one of the four legs of the bier. Note the benefits: increase of congregation, honor for the deceased, and safety from falling.
2
Cite the Hanafi dalil
The evidence includes the hadith of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud: 'It is from the Sunnah to carry the bier from its four sides.' Also the hadith: 'Whoever carries the bier from its four sides, Allah will grant him a forgiveness that is binding.'
These hadith are ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for establishing a sunnah.
3
Present the Shafi'i counter-argument
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the sunnah is for two men to carry the bier, with the front man placing it at the base of his neck and the rear man on his chest. His evidence is the bier of Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, which was carried in that manner.
The Shafi'i position is based on this specific incident.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response uses tafsir of the incident: the two-man carry was exceptional due to the extreme crowding of angels at Sa'd's funeral. The road was narrow, so two men carried it, and even the Prophet walked on his toes. This is not general practice.
This is the tarjih mechanism: the specific incident does not override the general command.
5
Examine the ruling on walking speed
The carriers should walk briskly but not run (khabab). The Prophet said 'ma duna al-khabab' (less than trotting). The wisdom: if the deceased is righteous, hasten him to divine mercy; if evil, hasten to remove the burden. Running is prohibited because it disrespects the deceased.
Speed is sunnah, running is makruh.

The Sunnah Method of Grave Construction: Lahd vs. Shiqq
Inter-madhhab dispute on grave construction method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position on lahd from the Shafi'i position, cite the evidence for each, apply the ruling when the ground is soft, and respond to the Shafi'i proof.

Core Idea
Lahd is sunnah for firm soil; shiqq is only permissible when the ground is too soft — based on explicit hadith.

This passage presents a key inter-madhhab difference in burial practices. The Hanafi school, relying on a clear hadith, holds that lahd (a niche on the qibla side) is the sunnah method. Imam al-Shafi'i, citing the continuous practice of the people of Madina, argues that shiqq (a trench) is preferable. We will examine both proofs and the Hanafi response, which explains the Madinan practice as a local necessity due to soft soil.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position and its proof
Identify the sunnah method according to the Hanafi school and the direct textual evidence from the hadith: 'Lahd is for us, and shiqq is for others.'
The hadith is explicit and relies on the authority of the Prophet (PBUH).
2
Identify the Shafi'i position and its proof
State that Imam al-Shafi'i considers shiqq sunnah based on the tawaruth (inherited practice) of the people of Madina.
Tawaruth is a type of consensus or continuous practice.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
Explain that the Madinan practice was necessitated by the soft, sandy soil of Baqi‘ cemetery, not by a different sunnah. Thus, the hadith overrides the practice.
This shows the principle that local custom ('urf) does not override a clear text.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)

Core Idea
Women's graves are covered for modesty; men's graves are left uncovered because men's nature is openness.

This passage addresses a practical difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: whether to cover the grave of a deceased man during burial. The Hanafi school distinguishes based on the principle that women's states involve concealment (sitr) and men's states involve openness (inkishaf). Ruling on Grave Materials and Shape
Ruling with proof on grave materials and shape

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on using baked brick and wood in graves and explain why musannam (humped) is preferred over musattah (square).

Core Idea
Baked brick and wood are disliked in graves; raw brick and reed are recommended; the grave should be humped, not flat.

This passage from al-Hidayah and al-Jami' al-Saghir addresses two practical aspects of grave construction: permissible materials and the accepted shape. We will examine the proofs from the Prophet's practice and prohibition, and understand why the Hanafi school holds these rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
The passage covers two distinct issues: what materials are allowed or disliked for the grave, and what shape the grave should take (humped vs. square).
These rulings are found in the same passage but have different proofs.
2
Analyze the ruling on materials
Note that baked brick and wood are makruh tahrimi because they imply permanence (the grave is meant to decay) and baked brick carries the ill omen of fire. Reed and raw brick are mustahab (recommended) based on the Prophet's example.
The ill omen (tasha'um) from fire is a subtle reason.
3
Analyze the ruling on shape
The grave should be musannam (humped like a camel's hump) and not musattah (flat/square). The Prophet himself forbade squaring graves (tarbi') and his own grave was confirmed to be humped.
There is a cross-madhhab dispute: Shafi'is prefer square graves.
4
Connect the proofs to the rulings
The proofs are: (1) The Prophet's grave had a bundle of reed placed on it, indicating recommendation of natural materials. (2) The prohibition of squaring graves establishes the shape ruling. Both are ahad hadith, but accepted in Hanafi usul as zanni al-thubut.
The Hanafi school relies on these proofs despite their zanni status.
