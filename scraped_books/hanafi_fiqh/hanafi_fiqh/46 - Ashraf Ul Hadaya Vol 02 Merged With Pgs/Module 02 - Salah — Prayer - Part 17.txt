Praying Inside the Ka'bah: Proof of Permissibility
Establishes permissibility via hadith and rational proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on praying inside the Ka'bah and explain the proof from the Prophet's action and the analogical extension to fard.

Core Idea
The Prophet prayed inside the Ka'bah; his action makes it permissible for both nafl and fard.

This lesson examines the permissibility of praying inside the Ka'bah. The source presents a hadith of Bilal reporting the Prophet’s prayer during the Conquest of Makkah, and then argues that this ruling applies to both supererogatory and obligatory prayers. We will work through the evidence, the rebuttal of the objection that only nafl is allowed, and the second proof based on fulfillment of prayer conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Explicitly state what is being ruled upon (praying inside the Ka'bah) and the Hanafi ruling: jā'iz (permissible).
The ruling applies to both fard and nafl prayers.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary evidence is the hadith of Bilal, which reports that the Prophet placed pillars in a specific arrangement and prayed inside the Ka'bah.
This is a hadith ahad, transmitted through Ibn Umar.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's action (fi‘l) generally indicates permissibility (ibahah) unless restricted by other evidence. Since no such restriction is found, the act is permissible.
The Hanafi usul rule: al-asl fi'l-af‘al al-ibahah.
4
Analyze the objection and response
An opponent might argue that the Prophet's prayer was nafl only. The rebuttal: the Shari'ah proofs of permissibility are general; there is no evidence limiting it to nafl. Therefore, fard is included by the same reasoning.
This is an analogical extension (qiyas) of the ruling from nafl to fard.
5
Consider the second proof
All conditions of validity for prayer are met inside the Ka'bah, especially facing the qiblah. Complete covering of the qiblah is not required; facing the building suffices.
This rational proof reinforces the permissibility.

Commentary
This is a ruling with proof from the Sunnah and rational argument. The hadith of Ibn Umar, reported through Bilal, establishes that the Prophet prayed inside the Ka'bah during the Conquest of Makkah. The commentator then argues that this act indicates permissibility for any prayer, whether obligatory or supererogatory. The opponent's objection that it was only a nafl prayer is rebutted by stating that the legal reasoning for permissibility applies equally to fard. A second proof is provided: the conditions of prayer are fully met when praying inside the Ka'bah, including facing the qiblah, because the qiblah is the direction of the entire building, not a specific point, and complete facing of every part is impossible and not required.

Key Terms

فرض (fard): Obligatory prayer, such as the five daily prayers.

Congregational Prayer Inside the Ka'bah: Four Scenarios for Follower Orientation
Ruling on four follower orientations inside Ka'bah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for each of the four scenarios of congregational prayer inside the Ka'bah based on follower-imam orientation, and explain the reason for validity or invalidity.

Core Idea
Prayer inside the Ka'bah is valid unless the follower advances ahead of the imam or believes him to be in error.

This lesson examines a specific issue from al-Hidayah: how can a congregation pray inside the Ka'bah? The follower's orientation relative to the imam determines validity. Al-Marghinani outlines four scenarios, with rulings ranging from valid without dislike to absolutely invalid. Understanding these scenarios reinforces the fundamental Hanafi principle of mutaba'ah (following the imam) and the prohibition of preceding the imam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Four Scenarios
List the four possible orientations of the follower relative to the imam: (1) follower's face toward imam's back, (2) follower's face toward imam's face, (3) follower's back toward imam's back, (4) follower's back toward imam's face.
2
Understand the General Principle
Valid congregation requires that the follower does not advance ahead of the imam and does not believe the imam to be in error. Being ahead is defined spatially and orientationally.
Being 'ahead' here means the follower's body is positioned such that they are closer to the qiblah than the imam? Actually, inside the Ka'bah, orientation matters: if the follower's back faces the imam's face, the follower is effectively 'ahead'.
3
Apply to Each Scenario
Evaluate each scenario against the principle. Scenarios 1 and 3: follower not ahead and does not believe error → valid. Scenario 2: not ahead but faces the imam → valid but disliked due to resemblance to idol worship. Scenario 4: follower is ahead → invalid.
4
Note the Distinction from Taharri
Compare with the case of taharri (estimation of qiblah in darkness). If the follower believes the imam is mistaken about qiblah, even if not ahead, the prayer is invalid because of lack of trust in the imam.
In scenario 3, the follower does not assume the imam is wrong, so it is valid.
5
Consider the Practical Implication
In the second scenario (face-to-face), it is recommended to place a screen between imam and follower to avoid resemblance to worship facing a statue.

Congregational Prayer Around the Ka'bah in Masjid al-Haram
Ruling on congregation in Masjid al-Haram when followers surround the Ka'bah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for a follower's prayer when closer to the Ka'bah than the imam, and apply the condition of ittihad al-janib (same direction) to determine validity.

Core Idea
A follower may be closer to the Ka'bah than the imam only if they are not on the same side — being ahead only matters when direction is shared.

In the unique setting of Masjid al-Haram, followers often form circles around the Ka'bah, raising a question about their prayer's validity relative to the imam's position. This lesson clarifies that the concept of 'being ahead' only applies when both imam and follower share the same direction. Understanding this condition ensures the congregation remains valid despite the unusual spatial arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the scenario
Recognize that the act is a congregational prayer in Masjid al-Haram where the imam is at one location and followers surround the Ka'bah. Some followers may be physically closer to the Ka'bah than the imam.
The Ka'bah is the qibla, so all followers face it, but direction (side) matters relative to the imam.
2
Determine the condition for invalidity
A follower's prayer is invalid only if two conditions are met simultaneously: (a) the follower is in the same direction (side) as the imam relative to the Ka'bah, and (b) the follower is closer to the Ka'bah than the imam. If either condition is absent, the prayer is valid.
The term 'ittihad al-janib' (unity of direction) is the key principle here.
3
Apply the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
The underlying reason is that concepts of 'ahead' and 'behind' only make sense when both individuals are on the same side. When they face the same qibla but on opposite sides, one being closer does not constitute being ahead of the imam. Thus, the prayer remains valid except on the imam's own side.
This ruling is specific to the circular arrangement around the Ka'bah and does not apply to ordinary rows in a straight direction.

Commentary
This passage presents an inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the validity of praying on the roof of the Ka'bah building. The Hanafi position is that such prayer is valid (jā'iz) but disliked (makruh tanzīhī). The proof is based on the Hanafi definition of the Ka'bah: it is not merely the building but the entire plot (al-'arsa) and the airspace above it up to the heavens. This is supported by analogy (qiyās): if someone prays on top of Mount Abu Qubays (a mountain near Mecca), it is valid even though there is no building facing him, because the qiblah is the direction of the Ka'bah's location, not its walls. The building can theoretically be moved, but the sacred space remains. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that prayer on the roof is invalid unless a sutrah (barrier) is placed in front of the worshipper because, in his view, one must directly face the building of the Ka'bah. The Hanafi response is that the qiblah is the entire area of the Ka'bah, not its structure. The text then cites a hadith from Abu Hurairah listing seven places where the Prophet (ﷺ) forbade prayer, including 'on top of the House of Allah.' The Hanafi school interprets this prohibition as indicating karāha (dislike) rather than outright prohibition (tahrīm), because the primary evidence (the definition of the Ka'bah and the qiyās) establishes validity, and the hadith is understood to mean it is disliked due to lack of proper reverence (ta'ẓīm). Thus, the operative ruling (muftā bihi) is that the prayer is valid but makruh. The authority weight is from the Zahir al-Riwayah as transmitted through al-Hidayah.

الكراهة (al-karāha): Dislike (less than prohibition); the act is valid but discouraged.
العرصة (al-'arsa): Open ground or plot; the land area of the Ka'bah.

Prayer on the Roof of the Kaaba: Ruling and Proof
Explains the Hanafi ruling on praying atop the Kaaba with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on praying atop the Kaaba and explain the hadith proof and the definition of the Kaaba that justifies the ruling.

Core Idea
Praying on the Kaaba's roof is makruh tahrimi because it violates reverence and a Prophet's prohibition.

In this passage, the commentator defines the Kaaba as the entire vertical space from Earth to the heavens, not just the physical building. This definition has practical implications: prayer on nearby heights is valid, but directly on the roof of the Kaaba is disliked. The author grounds this ruling in both the need for reverence and a prophetic hadith prohibiting prayer on the roof. Let's explore the reasoning and proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? Praying while standing on the roof of the Kaaba. The ruling is makruh (disliked) and prohibited (manu').
The author uses both makruh and manu'; in Hanafi fiqh this is makruh tahrimi.
2
Understand the Hanafi definition of Kaaba
The commentator clarifies that 'Kaaba' refers to the entire space from the ground where the building stands up to the sky. This is why prayer on Mount Abu Qubays is valid even without the building in front.
This definition is essential: the roof is part of the Kaaba, so praying there is problematic.
3
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Abu Hurairah: the Prophet prohibited prayer in seven places, including 'fawqa dhahri baytillah' (on the roof of the House of Allah).
The hadith is ahad (single-narrator), hence the ruling is makruh tahrimi, not haram.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'nahā' (prohibited) in the hadith is a command to refrain. In Hanafi usul, such a command indicates prohibition, but because the transmission is zanni, the prohibition level is not absolute.
Thus, the ruling is makruh tahrimi—prohibited but not to the degree of haram.
5
Note the underlying reason (ta'lil)
The author gives a second reason: climbing onto the roof compromises the reverence (ta'zim) of the Kaaba. This is an independent basis for the dislike.
Combining the hadith and the ma'qul (rationale) strengthens the ruling.
