Agency in Partnership: Each Partner as Agent
Ruling that each partner is an agent for the other in purchases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that each partner is an agent for the other in partnership purchases and explain the ownership consequence.

Core Idea
In a valid partnership, each partner automatically becomes an agent for the other, making purchases jointly owned.

This lesson establishes one of the foundational rules of Islamic partnership (shirkah): the automatic agency between partners. Understanding this rule is crucial because it determines ownership of assets acquired during the partnership. The Hanafi school considers this agency inherent to the contract, unlike some other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled on? The act is a partner purchasing goods for the partnership. The ruling is that the purchase leads to joint ownership.
The ruling is clear: half for the purchasing partner, half for the other (in an equal partnership).
2
Note the basis: agency
Why does this joint ownership occur? Because each partner is an agent (wakil) for the other in partnership dealings.
Agency is implicit in the contract; no separate authorization is needed.
3
Distinguish from sole ownership
What if the purchase is not for the partnership? Then the purchasing partner owns it solely. The agency only applies to partnership transactions.
The intention behind the purchase matters.
4
Apply to a practical scenario
Take an equal partnership. Partner A buys inventory for the business for $100. How much does Partner B owe? $50, because half belongs to B due to agency.
The transaction is valid and the ownership is divided from the moment of purchase.

Stipulating Profit Shares According to Purchase Shares
Ruling that profit shares follow the stipulated shares in purchases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that profit shares must match the stipulated purchase shares in a partnership and explain the principle of 'ribh ma lam yadman'.

Core Idea
Profit entitlement is tied to liability; no profit without risk.

In a partnership (shirkah), partners are free to agree on profit shares, but this freedom is constrained by the principle that profit must correspond to the risk assumed. This lesson examines a classic ruling from the Hanafi school: if partners stipulate that the purchased goods are owned in half-shares, profit must be divided accordingly. The proof is the hadith prohibiting 'profit without liability' (ribh ma lam yadman).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states that if partners stipulate equal shares in the purchased goods, profit must be equal; if in thirds, profit in thirds. This is a direct ruling from the Hanafi school.
2
Understand the Basis of Profit
The commentary explains that profit can be based on three things: capital, work, or liability (daman). In shirkat al-wujuh (credit partnership), only liability applies because there is no capital or work.
The three bases are derived from different types of partnerships.
3
Apply the Hadith
The hadith 'لا يحل ربح مالم يضمن' (profit without liability is not permissible) is cited. This hadith directly supports the rule that profit must be proportional to the liability assumed.
The hadith is in Sunan Abi Dawud and Sunan al-Tirmidhi.
4
Derive the Principle
The underlying principle is that one cannot take profit without bearing corresponding risk. Any stipulation that deviates from this (e.g., profit share exceeding liability share) is invalid.
This principle is known as 'ribh ma lam yadman'.

Invalid Partnerships in Collecting Natural Resources
Ruling statement on invalid partnerships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partnership in collecting firewood, grass, and hunting, and explain why such partnerships are invalid.

Core Idea
Partnership in unowned things is invalid because ownership arises only upon taking possession.

This lesson addresses a specific type of partnership that the Hanafi school considers invalid: agreements to share the proceeds of gathering firewood, grass, or hunting game. The key issue is that these activities involve acquiring things that are not owned at the time of the contract. Understanding this ruling helps clarify a fundamental condition for valid partnerships.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what activity is being ruled upon and whether it is valid, invalid, or void. The passage states that partnership in ihtitab (firewood collection), ihtishash (grass gathering), and istiyad (hunting) is not allowed.
The ruling is batil (void).
2
Understand the reason for invalidity
Explain why these partnerships are invalid: the subject matter of the partnership is not yet owned. Ownership of firewood, grass, or game only arises when someone physically takes possession. Before that, the items are unowned and uncertain.
This is because partnership requires an existing, owned mal (property).
3
Note the legal consequence
What happens if two people agree to such a partnership? Each person's collection is exclusively his own; there is no sharing. The passage explicitly states: 'whatever each one hunts or gathers firewood or grass, it is his own.'
This prevents disputes over ownership.
4
Apply the principle to similar cases
Generalize the rule: any partnership aimed at acquiring unowned or future assets is invalid in Hanafi law. For example, a partnership to share any treasure found in a desert would also be invalid.
The key is whether the asset exists and is owned at contract time.

Profit Must Match Liability: The Hanafi Rule for Shirkat al-Mufawada
Ruling with proof on profit distribution in partnerships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule linking profit to capital or liability in partnerships and apply the hadith 'لا ربح مالم يضمن' to determine valid profit shares.

Core Idea
Profit must be proportional to capital or liability assumed — not arbitrary.

The passage you are about to read establishes a foundational rule in Hanafi partnership law: profit follows the ratio of capital or, in credit partnerships, the extent of liability (daman). This rule is derived from the prophetic prohibition of 'profit on what is not guaranteed' (ربح مالم يضمن). Understanding this link between risk and reward is essential to drafting valid partnership contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on profit distribution
The passage explicitly states that if the purchased item is shared equally (half each), profit must be equal; if in thirds, profit follows that proportion. Any inequality is not permissible (لا يجوز). This is the basic ruling.
The ruling applies to shirkat al-mufawada where capital is in the form of goods purchased jointly.
2
Understand the three bases of profit entitlement
The commentary explains that profit can be justified by three factors: wealth (capital), labor, or liability (daman). In shirkat al-wujuh (credit partnership), neither capital nor labor is contributed, so profit is based solely on the liability each partner assumes.
This threefold classification is a hallmark of Hanafi fiqh in partnership law.
3
Locate the hadith proof
The passage quotes the hadith from Ibn Umar: 'لا يحل سلف وبيع ولا شرطان في بيع ولا ربح مالم يضمن ولا بيع ماليس عندک'. The key phrase is 'ولا ربح مالم يضمن' — no profit on what is not guaranteed.
Although the hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut), it is accepted as a primary proof by Hanafis in these transactions.
4
Explain the wajh al-dalalah (how the hadith proves the ruling)
The prohibition 'profit without liability' implies that profit is only permissible when the person bears the risk of loss. In a partnership, the liability assumed by a partner is proportional to the capital contributed or the guarantee given. Therefore, profit must be in the same proportion.
This is an application of the general principle 'al-kharaj bi al-daman' (gain accompanies liability), which is also a hadith.
5
Apply to shirkat al-wujuh
In a credit partnership, partners have no capital and may not work. Their only contribution is their creditworthiness (liability). Hence, profit shares must be exactly proportional to the liability each guarantees. Any deviation would involve taking profit without liability, which is prohibited.
This distinguishes shirkat al-wujuh from mudarabah and shirkat al-mal.

Invalidity of Partnership in Mubahat Collection
States invalidity of partnership in collecting mubah items

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partnership in collecting mubah items and explain why it is invalid, citing the hadith proof.

Core Idea
Mubah property becomes owned by possession; partnership before possession is void.

This lesson examines why a partnership to collect firewood, grass, or hunt is invalid in Hanafi fiqh. The key principle is that ownerless property (mubah) is acquired solely by taking possession. Consequently, any agreement to share such items before collection is legally meaningless. The passage also provides a hadith from Sunan Ibn Majah as proof, comparing salt to free-flowing water. We will walk through the ruling, its basis, and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is forming a partnership (shirkah) to collect mubah items like firewood, grass, or hunting. The ruling is that it is batil (invalid). Each person's collection belongs solely to them, not to the partnership.
The invalidity is due to the nature of the subject matter, not a procedural flaw.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Sunan Ibn Majah where the Prophet (peace be upon him) compares salt to water that belongs to whoever takes it. This indicates that possession yields ownership of mubah items.
The hadith is ahad (single narration), so its thubut is zanni.
3

Abu Hanifa holds that the later payer is always liable – whether or not he knew of the first payment. Once the first payment is made, the second partner's agency to pay is automatically terminated. The later payment is invalid (considered nafl charity) and the partner must reimburse the first.
The key illa: the fact of the first payment, not knowledge, revokes the agency.
4
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani condition liability on knowledge. If the later payer did not know of the first payment, he acted within his valid permission and is not liable. If he knew, he is liable.
They argue that the agency remains until the agent is informed of its termination. Good faith without knowledge preserves authority.
5
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is straightforward: under Abu Hanifa, the later payer always compensates; under the Sahibayn, only if he had knowledge. The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) is Abu Hanifa's, but some later scholars adopted the Sahibayn's view for leniency.
This dispute is recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah and is a classic example of how agency rules interact with financial obligations.

Partner's Right to Share Collected Debt
Ruling on partner's right to share collected debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when one partner collects part of a joint debt and identify the other partner's right to share, as well as the subsequent joint action against the debtor.

Core Idea
A partner who collects part of a joint debt does not own it exclusively; the other partner has a right to share in the collected amount.

This passage from Kitab al-Sulh addresses a common situation in partnership: when one partner collects a portion of a joint debt, what are the rights of the other partner? The text establishes that the collected amount remains joint property, and the other partner can take his proportional share. Then both partners together pursue the debtor for the remainder. Understanding this ruling ensures equitable treatment of partners in debt recovery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Hukm
The act is: one partner collects half his share of a joint debt. The hukm (legal ruling) is that it is permissible (جايز) for the other partner to share in what was collected.
The permissibility is established by the phrase 'كان لشريكه ان يشاركه'.
2
Determine the Consequence
After the partner shares in the collected amount, both partners then jointly claim the remaining debt from the debtor. This is expressed as 'ثم يرجعان على الغريم بالباقي'.
The remainder is the part of the debt not yet collected by either partner.
3
Understand the Underlying Principle
The collected debt is joint property because both partners were co-owners of the debt. One partner's collection does not extinguish the other's right; the collected money is treated as jointly owned.
This is based on the principle of joint ownership in shirkat al-dayn.
4
Consider Scenario Variations
The commentary mentions a variation where the collecting partner guarantees the other's share. In that case, the non-collecting partner cannot share in the collected property but both still pursue the remainder.
This exception shows that the ruling can change based on the actions of the partners.
