Confession by Litigation Agent Before a Judge
Ruling on validity of confession by litigation agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling regarding a litigation agent's confession before a judge and cite the two hadith proofs that support it.

Core Idea
A litigation agent's confession against his principal is valid only before a judge; outside court, he loses his agency.

In Hanafi law, a litigation agent is appointed to represent the principal in court. Can that agent confess on behalf of his principal? This passage clarifies that such a confession is valid only before the judge, based on prophetic practice and the principle of avoiding assistance in oppression.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its condition
State the Hanafi ruling: the agent's confession is valid only before a judge. Note the consequence if he confesses elsewhere: he leaves the agency.
The ruling is from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad; Imam Zufar holds it is invalid entirely.
2
Examine the first proof: Hawazin delegation hadith
The Prophet accepted the chiefs' confession on behalf of their people while acting as judge. This shows an agent can confess before a judge.
The hadith is narrated by Bukhari; the chiefs were agents for their tribes.
3
Examine the second proof: prohibition of assisting oppression
If the agent knows the principal is guilty but refuses to confess, he aids injustice. Thus, he must confess the truth before the judge.
Hadith from Abu Dawud warns against supporting wrongful litigation.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The absolute wording of agency (وكالة) includes both confession and denial. Hence the agent has the authority to confess within the scope of litigation.
This principle is used to derive the ruling.

And if the litigation agent confesses against his principal before the judge, his confession is valid, and his confession against him before other than the judge is not valid according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on them both, except that he leaves the agency. Therefore, the confession is considered only in the judge's court so that litigation can occur; outside of it, the confession is not considered. This is the meaning according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad. (1) Imam A'zam looked at the absolute word as before. (2) A hadith also indicates that an agent can confess against his principal, and the confession is valid before a leader/iman. The summary of a long hadith: The tribe of Hawazin came as captives, then they repented and asked for their captives. The Prophet encouraged the Muslims to release them, and they agreed. The Prophet said: I do not know from this consent whether it is heartfelt. Go and confess before your chiefs, and let the chiefs come as your agents and confess before me that you are pleased to release them. Then all the chiefs came and confessed acting as agents for their people that they were sincerely willing to release the captives. This shows that agents can confess on behalf of their principals, and it is valid only before a judge/leader. The hadith fragment: 'Urwa reported that Marwan ibn al-Hakam and al-Miswar ibn Makhrama informed him that the Messenger of Allah stood when the delegation of Hawazin came...' (Bukhari, chapter on when the agent or intercessor of a people is allowed, p.309, no.2307). In this hadith, the chiefs confessed before the Prophet as agents, and the Prophet was a judge. So confession can be made before a judge. (3) If the agent knows that his principal is guilty but refuses to confess, this would be assisting in oppression, which is prohibited in hadith. 'Ibn 'Umar from the Prophet: Whoever assists in a wrongful litigation, he incurs the wrath of Allah' (Abu Dawud, chapter on a man who assists in litigation without knowing its rightness, vol.2, p.150, no.3598). Based on this hadith, confessing against the principal is permissible. However, if he confesses before other than a judge and witnesses prove it, he leaves the agency because he acted against the rule. He will not be given possession of the principal's debt. This is the meaning of 'except that he leaves the agency.' The question: The absolute word includes both, affirmation and denial. This ruling is based on that principle. Footnote: (a) When the delegation of Hawazin came as Muslims, the Prophet stood and said: I do not know who among you has given permission and who has not. Go back until your leaders bring your matter to us. Then the people returned and their leaders spoke to them, then they came back to the Prophet and informed him that they willingly give permission. (b) The Prophet said: Whoever assists in a wrongful litigation, he incurs the wrath of Allah.

Commentary
This passage provides a ruling with proof. The matn states that a litigation agent's confession against his principal is valid only before a judge (qadi), according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. If he confesses elsewhere, it is not valid, and he leaves the agency. The commentary explains that the absolute wording of agency (being an agent for litigation) includes both confession and denial. The authority for the validity before a judge is derived from the hadith of Hawazin delegation, where tribal chiefs acted as agents and confessed before the Prophet (who was the judge). Additionally, if the agent refuses to confess when the principal is guilty, he would be assisting in oppression, which is prohibited. Thus, the agent is allowed (and even obligated) to confess the truth before the judge. However, if the agent confesses outside the judge's presence, he oversteps his authority and is dismissed from the agency, meaning he can no longer act on behalf of the principal.

Key Terms
اقرار (iqrār): Confession or acknowledgment of a right or debt against oneself; here, by the agent against the principal.
خرج من الخصومة (kharaja min al-khuṣūmah): Leaves the agency of litigation; the agent is dismissed from his role as representative in the case.
اعانة على الظلم (iʿānah ʿalā al-ẓulm): Assistance in oppression, which is prohibited; here, not confessing the truth when the principal is guilty would be aiding him in injustice.

Validity of Confession by a Ma'dhun Slave
Ruling on a ma'dhun slave's confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a ma'dhun slave's confession of debts and usurpation, and identify the condition that makes it valid.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun slave's confession of debt or usurpation is valid because he has legal capacity in trade.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from the chapter on testimony (shahadat) regarding the validity of a confession (iqrar) made by a ma'dhun slave. The key point is that the slave's permission to trade grants him limited legal capacity, making his admissions binding. Understanding this helps clarify the scope of a slave's legal agency in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the actor and act
Recognize that the ruling concerns a ma'dhun slave (a slave given permission to trade) and his act of confessing (iqrar) to a debt or usurpation (ghasb).
2
Note the ruling
The text states that such confession is valid (ja'iz). This means it creates a binding obligation on the slave.
3
Understand the rationale
The validity stems from the slave's legal capacity in trade matters, granted by the master's permission. This capacity allows his admissions to be effective.
The confession does not affect the master's liability; the slave must fulfill the obligation after emancipation.

(5) And the confession of the ma'dhun regarding debts and usurped goods is valid. The confession of the ma'dhun regarding debts and usurped goods is valid. If the ma'dhun slave confesses that someone has a debt on him or that he has usurped someone's property which he is obligated to return, such confession is valid.

Commentary
This ruling addresses the legal effect of a confession (iqrār) made by a ma'dhun slave. Since the slave has been given permission to trade and thus has some level of legal capacity, his acknowledgment of debts or liability for usurped property (ghaṣb) is considered valid. The commentary explains that if he says he owes a debt or has taken someone's property wrongfully, his statement is accepted, and he will be responsible. However, as noted earlier, such obligations are enforced after his emancipation to protect the master's interests. This ruling aligns with the principle that the ma'dhun slave's transactions are effective within the scope of his permission.

Key Terms
اقرار (iqrār): A voluntary admission by a person against his own interest; here, the slave's acknowledgment of debt or usurpation.
غصب (ghaṣb): Unlawful taking of another's property; usurpation.
