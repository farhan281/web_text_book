Hawala: Permissibility and Conditions of Consent
States ruling of hawala and its conditions for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling of hawala (transfer of debt) and identify the conditions for its validity according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers the debt entirely, freeing the original debtor, and requires consent of all three parties.

Hawala is a contract of transfer of debt. In this passage, the author begins with the ruling that hawala is permissible for debts, supported by a hadith, and then clarifies a key condition: validity hinges on the consent of the original debtor, the creditor, and the new debtor. This lesson will walk you through the ruling, its proof, and the required consents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is hawala? The passage states it is permissible (ja'iz) for debts. This establishes the baseline ruling.
The ruling is mubah (permissible), not obligatory.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith: 'Procrastination by a wealthy person is injustice, and when you are referred to a wealthy person, follow him.' This is the primary textual evidence.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command 'fatba'hu' (follow him) indicates that accepting the transfer is permissible, implying the validity of hawala.
The command indicates permissibility, not obligation.
4
Identify the conditions
The passage explicitly states that hawala is valid with the consent of the muhil (original debtor), muhtal lahu (creditor), and muhtal 'alayh (new debtor). All three must consent.
Consent is a shart (condition) for validity.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Some narrations say the muhil's consent is not required, but the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) requires consent of all three.
Tarjih: The majority Hanafi position requires all consents.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof for the Book of Hawala (transfer of debt). It begins with the main ruling: 'Hawala is permissible for debts' and its validity requires the consent of the transferor and the transferee. The author then provides a definition: hawala shifts the debt from the original debtor to the guarantor, who becomes solely responsible. The proof is from a hadith: 'Procrastination by a wealthy person is injustice, and when you are referred to a wealthy person, follow him.' This indicates the permissibility of hawala. The passage also defines four key terms: muhtal 'alayh (the guarantor), muhil (the original debtor), muhtal lahu (the creditor), and mutamallal bih (the property). It further explains that hawala is valid only with the consent of all three parties: the original debtor, the creditor, and the guarantor. The reason for requiring the original debtor's consent is that he may not want to accept a favor from another. However, some narrations suggest that his consent may not be necessary.

Hawala Completion and Reversion upon Tawa
Ruling with evidence on hawala completion and reversion upon loss

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on reversion to the original debtor after a completed hawala and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the original debtor is not absolutely freed if the creditor's right is lost — no loss upon a Muslim's property.

This lesson examines the completion of a hawala (transfer of debt) and the exceptional right of the creditor to revert to the original debtor if his right is lost (tawa). The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ fundamentally on whether the original debtor is absolutely discharged. By analyzing the primary proofs (athar) and the underlying principle of protecting Muslim property, you will understand the practical implications of this dispute.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on hawala completion
Identify the general rule: after a valid hawala with consent of all three parties, the original debtor is freed from the debt. Note the exception: the creditor may revert if his right is lost (tawa).
The consent of all three parties (muhil, muhal lahu, muhal 'alayh) is required for the hawala to be completed.
2
Identify the opposing school and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the original debtor is absolutely discharged upon completion of hawala, regardless of whether the creditor can collect from the transferee.
This is a stricter interpretation of the finality of hawala.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Shafi'i rely on the athar of Shuraih: 'If a man makes a hawala to another and it is lost, he does not have recourse against the first.' This suggests complete discharge.
Shuraih was a famous judge in Kufa, and his opinion is considered authoritative by some.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school counters with the athar of Caliph Uthman: 'There is no loss upon the property of a Muslim.' This principle overrides the general rule of discharge, protecting the creditor from loss.
The Hanafi school prioritizes the prevention of loss (damn) over the strict finality of the transfer.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
In practice, a Hanafi creditor can reclaim from the original debtor if the transferee defaults or becomes insolvent, whereas a Shafi'i creditor has no such recourse. This affects the creditor's security and the original debtor's potential liability.
The difference is material in cases where the transferee is unreliable or becomes bankrupt.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof and includes an inter-madhhab dispute. It states the Hanafi position that upon completion of a valid hawala (transfer of debt) with the consent of all three parties (original debtor, creditor, and the person assuming the debt), the original debtor is freed from the debt. However, the creditor may revert to the original debtor if his right is 'lost' (tawa). The Hanafi school defines 'tawa' as situations where the creditor cannot recover from the transferee. The primary proof (dalil) cited is the statement of Caliph Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him): 'There is no loss upon the property of a Muslim,' meaning in hawala the creditor's property is not lost; he can reclaim from the original debtor if needed. This is the view of Abu Hanifa. In contrast, Imam Shafi'i holds that once hawala is completed, the original debtor is absolutely discharged, and the creditor cannot revert to him even if unable to collect from the transferee, citing an athar from Shuraih. The Hanafi response, as understood from the text, is that the principle of 'no loss' (la tawa) overrides the finality of hawala, protecting the creditor's right.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-hawalah): A transfer of debt from one person to another with the consent of all parties, whereby the original debtor is freed and the transferee becomes liable.
التوى (al-tawa): Loss or corruption of the creditor's right to collect from the transferee, allowing reversion to the original debtor.
المحيل (al-muhil): The original debtor who transfers his debt to another person.
المحتال عليه (al-muhal 'alayh): The person upon whom the debt is transferred, who becomes liable to pay the creditor.
المحتال له (al-muhal lahu): The creditor who transfers his right to collect from the original debtor to the transferee.

The Instances of Tawa: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on tawa in hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three cases of 'tawa' and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Tawa requires definitive loss: Abu Hanifa limits it; Sahibayn add judicial insolvency.

In hawala (transfer of debt), the concept of 'tawa' (loss of the creditor's right against the transferee) determines when the creditor can revert to the original debtor. This lesson explores a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa defines tawa strictly, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), expand it. Understanding this disagreement sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists balance textual evidence and practical flexibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal institution
Recognize that hawala is a transfer of debt: the creditor (muhal) agrees to seek payment from a third party (muhal 'alayh). Normally, the creditor cannot go back to the original debtor (muhil). However, if the creditor's right against the transferee is 'lost' (tawa), he may revert.
Tawa is the trigger for reversion.
2
State Abu Hanifa's two cases
According to Abu Hanifa, tawa occurs only in two situations: (a) the transferee denies the hawala, swears an oath, and there is no evidence against him; (b) the transferee dies insolvent (bankrupt).
His evidence is the athar from Hakam ibn Utbah.
3
State the Sahibayn's addition
The Sahibayn agree with Abu Hanifa's two cases but add a third: when a judge declares the transferee insolvent during his lifetime (hukm al-hakim bi iflasihi).
They cite the same athar interpreting 'even yufalis' to cover judicial insolvency, and another athar from Al-Hasan.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, temporary insolvency without death does not allow reversion; under the Sahibayn, a judicial declaration suffices. This affects the creditor's ability to pursue the original debtor.
The text notes Abu Hanifa's reasoning: wealth fluctuates, so insolvency is not permanent.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute defining the specific instances of 'tawa' (loss of the creditor's right) that allow the creditor to revert to the original debtor. Imam Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him) identifies two situations: (1) the transferee denies the hawala and swears an oath, and the creditor has no evidence; (2) the transferee dies insolvent. The evidence is an athar from Hakam ibn Utbah stating that the creditor cannot revert until the transferee becomes insolvent or dies insolvent. Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) accept these two but add a third: a judge's declaration of the transferee's insolvency during his lifetime. Their proof is the same athar, interpreting 'even yufalis' to include insolvency declared by a judge during life, plus an athar from Al-Hasan that if the transferee becomes insolvent after being wealthy, the creditor can revert. The practical difference (thamar al-khilaf) is that under Abu Hanifa's view, temporary insolvency does not trigger reversion unless death occurs, while under the Sahibayn, a judicial declaration of insolvency suffices. The text notes Abu Hanifa's reasoning: wealth fluctuates, so a judge's declaration is not conclusive.

Conditions for Reverting to Original Debtor in Hawala
Intra-Hanafi dispute on additional condition for return

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for the creditor to revert to the original debtor after hawala and identify the added condition by the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
After hawala, the creditor may revert only if the transferee dies insolvent or denies under oath — but the Sahibayn add judicial insolvency.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the conditions under which the creditor (muhtal lahu) may revert to the original debtor (muheel) after a hawala contract. Imam Abu Hanifa restricts reversion to two cases: the transferee's denial with oath or death in insolvency. His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, recognize a third: judicial declaration of insolvency. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for applying the school's mufta bihi position in modern financial transfers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two agreed cases
State the two conditions for reversion on which all Hanafi imams agree: the transferee denies the hawala and swears an oath with no evidence, or the transferee dies insolvent.
These are from the athar of Hakam ibn Utbah.
2
Identify the third case (Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani add a third condition: the judge declares the transferee insolvent during his lifetime.
This expands the interpretation of 'becomes insolvent' in the athar.
3
Compare the proofs
Both sides cite the same athar. Abu Hanifa interprets 'becomes insolvent' as actual death in that state; the Sahibayn include judicial insolvency as a definitive indicator.
The difference hinges on the certainty of insolvency.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is that of the Sahibayn, as judicial declaration provides sufficient certainty and practical necessity.
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah adopts this view.

The Transferor's Unproven Claim in Hawala
Ruling on transferor's offset claim when transferee seeks reimbursement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the transferor claims a setoff after the transferee pays, and apply the default principle (al-asl) to resolve similar disputes.

Core Idea
A transferor's unproven claim of a preexisting debt does not offset the transferee's right to reimbursement.

After the transferee pays the creditor, he seeks reimbursement from the transferor. The transferor may allege that the hawala was to repay a debt the transferee owed him. The Hanafi school rules that such a claim is not accepted without proof, and the transferor must pay the transferee the equivalent amount. This lesson examines the rationale and the principle of burden of proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The transferee (muhtal alayh) has paid the creditor and demands reimbursement from the transferor (muheel). The transferor claims the hawala was to settle a preexisting debt owed by the transferee.
The transferor's claim is a defense against the transferee's right.
2
Recognize the default principle (al-asl)
The transferee's payment gives him an apparent right to reimbursement. The transferor's claim contradicts this apparent right. The default is that the transferee's right stands until proven otherwise.
Al-asl: the existing state of affairs is presumed to continue.
3
Understand the burden of proof
The transferor bears the burden of proving his alleged debt because he is making a claim that opposes the apparent right of the transferee. Without evidence, his statement is not accepted.
The claimant must prove his claim; the one who denies merely has to take an oath.
4
Apply the ruling
Since the transferor has no proof, his claim is rejected. He must pay the transferee the like of the hawala debt.
The ruling protects the transferee from unjust loss.

Dispute Between Transferor and Creditor Over Hawala Purpose
Ruling on whose word is taken when transferor and creditor disagree

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when the transferor (muheel) and creditor (muhtal lahu) disagree on the purpose of a hawala, and identify whose statement is accepted and why.

Core Idea
When transferor and creditor dispute hawala's purpose, the transferor's word with oath is taken.

Hawala is a transfer of debt, but its purpose may be disputed. The transferor may claim it was for collection, while the creditor claims it was to repay a debt. This lesson presents the Hanafi ruling on whose statement is believed, along with the required oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The transferor (muheel) demands the collected money, claiming the hawala was a mere agency (wakalah) for collection. The creditor (muhtal lahu) claims it was to settle a debt owed by the transferor.
The transferor is the original creditor of the transferee, so he remains the owner of the debt.
2
Understand the default rule for conflicting claims
In Hanafi fiqh, when two parties disagree and neither has proof, the statement of the defendant (munkir) is accepted with oath. Here, the transferor is effectively the defendant against the creditor's claim of a debt.
The creditor's claim is a counterclaim requiring proof.
3
Apply the rule to the hawala dispute
Since the transferor denies owing the debt to the creditor, his statement is given precedence. He must swear an oath to support his claim.
The oath is a condition for accepting his statement.
4
Note the legal consequence
If the transferor swears, he can reclaim the money from the creditor. If he refuses the oath, the creditor's claim is upheld.
This aligns with the principle that the defendant's oath prevails.

Ruling on Muhal 'Alayh's Claim Against Muhil
Ruling on assignee's claim after performance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a muhal 'alayh demands payment from the muhil and the muhil claims a prior debt, and identify the evidentiary principle that governs this case.

Core Idea
A muhil's unproven counterclaim does not defeat the muhal 'alayh's apparent right based on performance.

This passage from the Book of Hawala addresses a common dispute in debt assignment. After the assignee (muhal 'alayh) pays the creditor, he seeks reimbursement from the assignor (muhil). The muhil may claim that the assignment was for a debt owed to him by the assignee. The Hanafi ruling clarifies that without evidence, the muhil's claim is rejected because the assignee's payment is strong apparent evidence of his right to collect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
The muhil (assignor) transfers his debt to the muhal 'alayh (assignee), who then pays the muhal lahu (creditor). After payment, the muhal 'alayh seeks reimbursement from the muhil.
Ensure you understand the three-party relationship in hawala.
2
Recognize the sequence of events
The muhal 'alayh has already paid the creditor. Now he demands from the muhil the amount he paid. This is the context of the dispute.
The muhal 'alayh is the plaintiff in this claim.
3
Understand the muhil's counterclaim
The muhil responds that the hawala was for a debt that the muhal 'alayh previously owed him. He does not provide proof.
This is a claim of a prior debt, not a denial of the hawala itself.
4
Apply the evidentiary rule
The muhal 'alayh's payment to the creditor is a visible act that supports his right to reimbursement. The muhil's claim is a counterclaim that requires evidence. Without proof, the muhil's statement is rejected.
The principle is that apparent evidence (payment) takes precedence over an unsubstantiated claim.
5
State the ruling
The muhil's statement is not accepted, and he must pay the amount to the muhal 'alayh.

Dispute Between Muhil and Muhal Lahu
Ruling on dispute regarding nature of hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when the muhil and muhal lahu dispute the reason for the hawala, and explain why the muhil's word is accepted with oath.

Core Idea
When the muhil claims the hawala was for collection, his word with oath prevails over the creditor's claim of debt.

In hawala transactions, disputes may arise about the nature of the assignment. The muhil may claim he assigned only as an agent to collect, while the muhal lahu claims it was a transfer of a debt. In such cases, the Hanafi school gives precedence to the muhil's statement with oath, based on the principle that the claimant must provide evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The muhil (assignor) demands from the muhal lahu (creditor) the debt he assigned. The muhil says he assigned only for collection; the muhal lahu says it was for a debt owed to him.
The muhal lahu has already collected from the muhal 'alayh (third-party debtor).
2
Understand the legal presumption
The muhal lahu is claiming a debt from the muhil, while the muhil denies it. By default, the denier's statement with oath is accepted under the principle 'al-bayyina 'ala man idda'a' (the burden of proof is on the claimant).
The muhil is effectively denying any debt to the muhal lahu.
3
Apply the principle of evidence
Since the muhal lahu claims a debt, he must provide evidence. If he cannot, the muhil's statement with oath is accepted.
The oath confirms the muhil's claim and places the burden on the muhal lahu to prove otherwise.
4
State the ruling
The ruling is: the statement of the muhil, with his oath, is accepted.
This is the operative rule for fatwa in such disputes.

Ruling on Suftaja (Finance Bill)
Ruling and proof on suftaja being makruh with condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on suftaja, explain its dalil, and apply the distinction between conditional and unconditional suftaja.

Core Idea
A loan with a stipulation of benefit (security) is riba; suftaja is makruh tahrimi when conditioned.

Suftaja is a financial practice where a loan is given in one location and repaid in another, often to avoid travel risks. The Hanafi school considers it disliked if the benefit of security is explicitly stipulated, because any benefit from a loan resembles riba. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and the critical condition that changes the ruling to permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Understand what suftaja is: a loan given in one city to be repaid in another, providing the lender security from travel risks. The ruling is makruh (disliked) under certain conditions.
The passage explicitly states 'ويكره السفاتج' (Suftaja is disliked).
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites two primary proofs: (1) the hadith of Fadala ibn Ubayd: 'Every loan that brings a benefit is a form of riba,' and (2) the practice of Umar forbidding a similar transaction. Identify these as the basis for the ruling.
The hadith is from Sunan al-Bayhaqi and Musannaf Ibn Abi Shaybah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Analyze how the hadith applies to suftaja. The phrase 'كل قرض جر منفعة' (every loan that brings a benefit) includes the benefit of security from travel risks. Therefore, suftaja falls under the prohibition.
The benefit is inherent in the structure, but the Hanafis differentiate based on explicit stipulation.
4
Note the condition that changes the ruling
If the repayment at a distant place is explicitly stipulated, it is makruh because the lender benefits. If no condition is made, it is permissible, as shown by the practice of Ibn al-Zubayr approved by Ibn Abbas.
The condition is the key factor: with condition = makruh; without condition = permissible.
5
Apply to a modern scenario
Consider a modern example: a loan is given in New York with the condition that the borrower's associate repays in London. The lender avoids wire transfer fees and exchange rate risk. This is makruh. If no such condition is mentioned, it is permissible.
Apply the same principle: any stipulated benefit triggers dislikedness.
