Ruling with proof on slave's statements.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A slave's statements concerning his own person are valid, but those affecting his master are invalid.

This lesson addresses a nuanced aspect of legal capacity in Hanafi fiqh: the slave, though lacking full ownership, retains personal legal capacity for acts concerning his own person. The text contrasts valid self-directed statements with invalid master-directed statements, and cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas as proof. Understanding this distinction clarifies the Hanafi position that slavery does not strip a person of all legal agency.

This indicates that the slave's statements regarding his own person (like marriage) are effective.

This difference stems from competing usul principles.
5

The master's right is limited to property matters, not personal status.

Slave's Legal Statements: Personal Validity vs. Financial Restriction
Ruling on slave's capacity: his statements affect himself but not his master

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's legal capacity and apply the distinction between personal and financial statements using hadith evidence.

Core Idea
A slave's personal acts like divorce are valid; his financial acts require the master's permission.

This passage tackles a fundamental question in Hanafi fiqh: what legal capacity does a slave possess? The answer is not a simple yes or no; it depends on whether the act concerns his own person or affects his master's property. We will explore the general principle, its application, and the prophetic proofs that establish it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Scope
Read the first sentence of the Arabic passage. The ruling distinguishes between statements that are 'nafidh' (effective) regarding the slave himself and those that are not effective regarding his master. Separate the two categories.
The slave is 'aql-mand' (intelligent) but capacity is limited due to master's right.
2
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The author cites two hadiths. Identify the first hadith (Ibn Abbas) and its key phrase 'إنما الطلاق لمن أخذ بالساق' (Divorce is only for the one who took hold of the shin). This is the primary proof.
This hadith is from Ibn Majah and Dar Qutni.
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah (Indication of Proof)
Explain how the hadith proves the slave's right to divorce. The phrase 'من أخذ بالساق' (one who took hold of the shin) refers to the person who performed the marriage (the husband). Since the slave is the husband, he has the right to divorce, not his master.
The Prophet forbade the master from divorcing the slave's wife.
4
Understand the Second Hadith as Corroboration
The second hadith (from Abu Dawood) shows a slave actually divorcing his wife, and Ibn Abbas confirms its validity. This supports the first hadith and shows the ruling is practiced.
The case involves a slave who divorced his wife and later both were freed.
5
Apply the Principle to Other Scenarios
The passage says 'his acknowledgment etc. is effective' regarding himself. Think: what other personal acts might a slave do? Manumission of his own slave? Testimony? Then contrast with financial acts like sale.
Financial transactions are not valid without master's permission because they affect his property.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi ruling on the legal capacity of a slave ('abd). The general principle is: a slave's statements (aqwal) are valid and effective (nafidha) when they pertain to his own person (haqq nafsihi), but they are not effective regarding his master's rights (haqq mawlahu). In other words, a slave has personal capacity regarding actions that affect his own life, lineage, and personal status, but his financial capacity is restricted because he is owned property. For example, a slave can pronounce divorce (talaq) because divorce is a personal act affecting his own marriage. He can also manumit his own slave (if he owns one) because that is a personal act of piety. However, a slave cannot engage in financial transactions like sale or purchase without his master's permission, as those affect the master's property. These establish that a slave has autonomy in personal matters like divorce despite being owned.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A fool under interdiction can marry, but any mahr beyond her mahr al-mithl is void.

This lesson examines a specific case in Hanafi fiqh: the marriage of a person placed under financial interdiction due to foolishness (safah). The passage clarifies that their marriage is valid, but the mahr is capped at the proper amount (mahr al-mithl) to prevent waste. This ruling balances the necessity of marriage with the need to protect the interdicted person's wealth.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the source: the marriage of an interdicted fool is ruled valid (jaza nikaahu). Note that interdiction does not prevent marriage.
Marriage is a basic need (hajat asliyya).
2
Identify the mahr ruling
When a mahr is specified, only the amount equal to mahr al-mithl (proper mahr) is valid; any excess is void (batil).
Excess is considered waste (safah).
3
Locate the reasoning (talil)
The ruling is based on the principle that interdiction prevents financial waste but not personal necessities like marriage.
This is a talil argument.

Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require guardian permission for marriage of an interdicted person; Hanafi allows it independently.
The dispute is about the scope of interdiction.
5

If an interdicted man marries and offers a mahr of 10,000 while her mahr al-mithl is 5,000, only 5,000 is binding.
The excess is not enforceable.

Iqrar for a Woman Who Later Becomes Wife Remains Valid
Ruling on iqrar for a stranger woman who later becomes a wife.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on iqrar made for a stranger woman who later becomes a wife, and explain why the timing of the iqrar is determinative.

Core Idea
Iqrar for a non-heir is valid; later becoming an heir does not invalidate it retroactively.

This passage clarifies a common scenario in inheritance and acknowledgment: what happens when a sick person acknowledges a debt to a woman who later becomes his wife? The Hanafi school provides a clear distinction between this case and that of acknowledging a child. Let's examine the reasoning behind the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What specific act is being ruled upon? The act is an iqrar (acknowledgment of debt) made for a woman who is a stranger (ajnabiyyah) at the time. The ruling is that the iqrar remains valid after she later marries the man and becomes an heir.
The ruling is 'sahih' (valid).
2
Determine the key factor
What is the decisive condition? At the moment of iqrar, the woman was not an heir. Marriage and heirship occurred later. The Hanafi school considers the status at the time of the iqrar to be decisive.
Time of iqrar is the point of evaluation.
3
Contrast with the previous case (ruling 47)
How does this differ from the iqrar for a child? In the child case, the child is considered an heir from birth (even if paternity is later acknowledged), so an iqrar made before the paternity claim is invalid because the child was always an heir. In the woman's case, she only became an heir after marriage.
The distinction hinges on whether the recipient was an heir at the time of iqrar.
4
State the underlying principle
What general rule can we derive? Iqrar is valid wherever there is no suspicion of causing harm to others (i.e., defrauding other heirs). Since the woman was a non-heir at the time of iqrar, the iqrar was made without prejudice to other heirs, and later changes do not retroactively invalidate it.
Principle: al-umur bi-maqasidiha? Or more specifically, validity based on time of act.

In Hanafi fiqh, establishing paternity through iqrar (acknowledgment) is a powerful tool to protect lineage and inheritance rights. This ruling addresses a common scenario where a man, even on his deathbed, acknowledges a boy of unknown parentage as his son. The passage explains the conditions under which such an acknowledgment is legally effective and the Islamic proof that supports it.

General Permissibility of Lineage Acknowledgment
Ruling on permissibility of man acknowledging relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of relatives a man may acknowledge as his own and identify the condition of confirmation (tasdiq) required for validity.

Core Idea
A man's acknowledgment of his own lineage is permissible if the acknowledged party confirms and it harms no one else.

This lesson presents a foundational ruling in Hanafi fiqh concerning the acknowledgment of lineage. It establishes that a man may voluntarily attribute certain close relatives to himself without external proof, provided the acknowledged person confirms the truth of the claim. Understanding this ruling clarifies the legal weight of personal statements in family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is acknowledging (iqrar) one's own lineage to certain categories of people. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz), not obligatory nor prohibited.
2
List the permissible categories
The passage explicitly mentions parents (father and mother as a pair), wife, son, and master (mawla). Memorize these categories.
The master (mawla) refers to a freed slave with whom one has a patronage relationship (wala').
3
Understand the condition of confirmation
The acknowledgment is valid only if the acknowledged party confirms (tasdiq) that the relationship is true. Without such confirmation, the acknowledgment has no legal effect.
This condition prevents one-sided claims that could harm others.
4
Grasp the underlying principle
The permissibility is based on the principle that the acknowledgment is a personal voluntary act that does not impose lineage on others or cause direct harm. If no one is harmed, it is allowed.
Even if it indirectly harms an heir (e.g., by reducing inheritance shares), it is still permissible because the harm is not direct.

Woman's Acknowledgment of Son: Husband's Confirmation Required
Ruling on woman's acknowledgment of son requiring husband's confirmation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a woman's acknowledgment of a son and explain why the husband's confirmation or a midwife's testimony is required for its acceptance.

Core Idea
A woman's claim to a son implicates the husband's lineage, so his confirmation is necessary.

In Hanafi fiqh, lineage (nasab) is established through the father. When a woman acknowledges a son, she is not only claiming the child for herself but also attributing paternity to her husband. Therefore, her mere statement is insufficient; the husband must confirm or a midwife must testify to the birth. This lesson explains the ruling, its rationale, and the supporting hadith evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify that the woman's acknowledgment (iqrar) of a son is not accepted legally unless the husband confirms (tasdiq) or a midwife testifies to the birth.
2
Understand the reason
A child's lineage is from the father. The woman's claim also imposes lineage on the husband, affecting his rights. Hence, his confirmation or external evidence is required.
If she claimed the child only for herself, it might be different, but here she is attributing paternity to her husband.
3
Examine the evidence
The hadith narrated by Abu Hurairah forbids a woman from bringing a non-relative into a family, which applies to falsely claiming a son. This supports the need for verification.
The hadith is from Sunan Abi Dawud and Nasa'i, classified as ahad (zanni al-thubut).
4
Apply the ruling
If the husband confirms or a midwife testifies, the child's lineage is established. Otherwise, the acknowledgment is invalid (batil).

Condition for Accepting a Woman's Acknowledgment of a Child
Conditional ruling on maternal acknowledgment of lineage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for accepting a woman's acknowledgment of a child in Hanafi fiqh and apply the proofs (husband's confirmation or midwife testimony) to determine the child's lineage.

Core Idea
A woman's claim of a child is not accepted without either husband's confirmation or a midwife's testimony of birth.

This lesson explains a crucial protection in lineage law: a mother cannot unilaterally attribute a child to her husband. The Hanafi school requires either the husband's confirmation or the testimony of a midwife. The ruling balances the rights of the mother, father, and child, supported by prophetic hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a woman's acknowledgment (iqrar) that a particular child is her son/daughter. The ruling: it is not accepted unless a condition is met.
Acknowledgment implies fixing lineage (nasab) from both mother and father.
2
Locate the primary proof
The passage cites a hadith from Abu Huraira: the Prophet forbade a woman from introducing someone not from the family into the lineage. This prohibits imposing lineage without basis.
The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear (qati' al-dalala) – imposing false lineage is a major sin.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The prohibition implies that a woman's unconfirmed claim is invalid because it would impose lineage on the husband without his consent. The condition of confirmation or midwife testimony is derived as an exception.
The midwife testimony is accepted because it is the only reliable way to prove birth when male witnesses cannot be present.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
If a midwife testifies to the birth, the child's lineage is established from the mother first, then from the husband by the principle 'al-walad li-l-firash' (the child belongs to the marriage bed).
This shows the interaction between testimony and the marital bed principle.
5
Note the legal effect
Without confirmation or midwife, the acknowledgment is rejected. This protects the husband from false paternity and maintains the integrity of lineage.
The ruling is mufta bihi and generally agreed upon across schools.

No Shuf'ah in a House Given as Mahr

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Shuf'ah only applies to property sold for wealth; mahr given in exchange for marital rights is not wealth.

This lesson examines a specific scenario where a house is transferred as dower (mahr) in a marriage contract. Although a property transfer occurs, the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) does not apply. The reason lies in the nature of mahr and the conditions required for shuf'ah to exist.

2

Shuf'ah is only available when property is sold in exchange for wealth (mal).
Wealth means something that can be measured and compensated.

Mahr is given in exchange for the right to marital intercourse (bud'), which is not wealth. The pre-emptor cannot offer the same consideration.
The text explicitly says mahr is not mal.

Conclude the ruling
Therefore, no shuf'ah exists in a house given as mahr.

Contracts Where Agent Acts as Mere Messenger (Safir)
Ruling on rights in safir contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify contracts where the agent acts as safir and state the Hanafi ruling that all rights and obligations belong to the principal, not the agent.

Core Idea
In contracts attributed to the principal, the agent is a safir (messenger) and bears no personal liability.

In Hanafi fiqh, not every agent bears the rights and duties of the contract. This lesson examines a category of contracts—such as marriage (nikah), divorce with compensation (khula), and settlement for intentional killing (sulh 'an dam al-'amd)—where the agent merely conveys the offer and acceptance. The ruling is clear: the agent is a safir (messenger) and all legal consequences attach to the principal. We will explore the reasoning and the proof from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contracts and the agent's role
List the contracts mentioned: nikah, khula, sulh 'an dam al-'amd. Note that in each, the agent explicitly attributes the contract to the principal (e.g., 'I marry you to so-and-so').
This attribution is key—it distinguishes the safir from a full wakil.
2
State the ruling
The matn rules: 'Its rights are attached to the principal, not the agent.' Therefore, the principal is liable for mahr, delivery of wife, payment of blood money, etc.
The agent simply conveys and is then free from any further obligation.
3
Understand the legal basis (safir concept)
The agent is a safir (mere messenger), not a party. The real contracting party is the principal. Hence, no liability falls on the safir.
This is a Hanafi distinction; other schools may differ.
4
Examine the proof (dalil naqli)
The hadith of Sahl ibn Sa'd: The Prophet (ﷺ) acted as agent for a Companion, married him to a woman, and made the mahr the man's responsibility, not his own. This proves the agent is not liable.
The hadith is from Sahih al-Bukhari and is ahad, but its meaning is clear.
5
Recognize the practical consequence
In a marriage contract, if the agent says 'I marry you to my principal,' the wife demands mahr from the husband, not the agent. Similarly, in khula, the wife must give compensation to the husband, not the agent.
The same applies to sulh for intentional killing: the blood money is due from the killer (principal), not the agent who negotiated.

The text states 'ولا يجوز تزويج الملتقط' — 'It is not permissible for the finder to marry (the foundling).' Note that this includes both marrying the foundling oneself and arranging marriage for the foundling to someone else.
The ruling is a clear prohibition (ḥaram).
2
Recognize the basis for prohibition
The commentary explains that the right to marry (wilāyah al-nikāḥ) belongs to three categories: relatives, owners, and the ruler. The finder is none of these.
This is a rule of capacity, not a general prohibition on foundling marriage.
3
List the three categories of persons with wilāyah
1) A relative (e.g., father, grandfather). 2) An owner (e.g., master of a slave). 3) The ruler or judge (sulṭān or ḥākim). These are the only ones who can contract marriage for a minor or incapable person.
The finder does not fall into any category, so he lacks authority.
4
Apply the rule to the finder
Since the finder has neither kinship, ownership, nor rulership over the foundling, any marriage he contracts (self or third party) is not permissible (lā yajūz). Additionally, there is no immediate benefit (maṣlaḥah) to justify granting such authority.
The ruling protects the foundling from exploitation.

The missing person's wife remains his wife until definite news — no separation, maintenance is wajib.

This passage from the Book of the Missing Person establishes a fundamental Hanafi rule: the wife of a missing person (mafqud) remains married to him and is entitled to maintenance from his wealth. No separation is permitted unless news of death or divorce arrives with certainty. The author provides three proofs: a prophetic hadith, an athar from Ali, and a report of consensus between Ali and Ibn Mas'ud. Understanding this ruling helps you appreciate how the Hanafi school prioritizes the preservation of the marriage bond even in uncertain circumstances.

The Missing Person's Wife: Hanafi vs. Maliki on Waiting Period and Contemporary Fatwa
Presents Imam Malik's view and the contemporary fatwa adopting it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between the Hanafi and Maliki rulings on the waiting period for a missing person's wife and state the contemporary fatwa adopted due to the corruption of the age.

Core Idea
Hanafi demands indefinite waiting; Maliki allows remarriage after 4 years. Today’s fatwa adopts Maliki to prevent hardship.

This passage presents a vital inter-madhhab dispute concerning a missing person's wife. Imam Malik, relying on a ruling of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab, holds that after four years the missing person is legally dead and the wife may remarry. The Hanafi school, however, requires her to wait indefinitely until definite news arrives. The text concludes that due to the corruption of the age, contemporary muftis issue fatwa according to the Maliki view to spare young women from indefinite hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue is the waiting period for the wife of a missing person (mafqud). The Hanafi position is indefinite waiting; the Maliki position is a four-year wait followed by iddah of four months and ten days.
The Hanafi view is based on the principle of istishab (presumption of continuity).
2
Examine the Maliki proof
The proof is an athar from 'Umar ibn al-Khattab. A woman whose husband was taken by jinn was commanded to wait four years, then her guardian was to divorce him, and then she observed iddah of four months and ten days.
This athar is reported by Darqutni and others.
3
Understand the tarjih (preference)
The text notes that although the Hanafi position is the original, due to the corruption of the age (zamanah kharab), fatwa is given on the Maliki view for young women.
The corruption of the age refers to increased fitnah and hardship.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Hanafi, the wife may never remarry; under Maliki, she can remarry after four years and iddah. The contemporary fatwa adopts the Maliki view to avoid hardship.
This is a clear example of applying a weaker ruling (marjuh) for maslahah.

The Ma'dhun Slave and General Trade Permission
Defining ma'dhun and ruling on general permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a slave with general trade permission, cite the hadith proof, and explain the Hanafi principles governing the ma'dhun's transactions.

Core Idea
General permission gives the slave full trading capacity, but the master must not suffer loss.

This passage introduces a foundational concept in Hanafi muamalat: the ma'dhun slave. A slave who is given general permission to trade by his master acquires the legal capacity to engage in all types of commerce. The ruling is grounded in a hadith and an athar, and is governed by two key principles: the master must not suffer loss, and the slave may engage in customary hospitality. Understanding this ruling clarifies the limits of a subordinate's authority and the role of custom in defining permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The passage states that if a master gives his slave general permission (idhn 'amm), the slave's disposal (tasarruf) in all types of trade is permissible (ja'iz). Identify the act (trade by a ma'dhun) and its hukm level (mubah).
General permission means no specific commodity or transaction is excluded.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith of Abu Taybah (Bukhari) is cited as proof. Abu Taybah was a slave who cupped the Prophet ﷺ and was compensated. The master's permission for this income-generating work is analogized to trade permission.
The hadith is ahaad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for a ruling of permissibility.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's approval and his intercession to reduce the slave's tax indicate that permission for wage work is valid. By extension, permission for trade is similarly valid because both involve income generation and the master's consent.
Custom ('urf) also supports this interpretation.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The passage notes that Imam al-Shafi'i holds that permission is limited to what is specifically stated, not general. However, the Hanafi school adopts the general view.
The difference leads to practical consequences in the scope of the slave's authority.
5
Apply the governing principles
The commentary lays down two principles: (1) the master must not suffer loss from the slave's trade, and (2) the slave may engage in customary hospitality. These principles limit the general permission and protect the master's interests.
An athar from Sufyan clarifies that a significant amount of capital sent with the slave indicates permission, while a trivial amount does not.

ضروری نوٹ ایسا غلام جس کو مولی نے تجارت کرنے کی اجازت نہیں دی تھی اب اسکو تجارت کرنے کی اجازت دے دی تو اسکو ماذون غلام کہتے ہیں۔ یا بچے کو ولی نے تجارت کرنے کی اجازت دے دی تو اس کو ماذون بچہ کہتے ہیں۔ اس کا ثبوت حدیث میں ہے۔ عن انس بن مالك قـال حـجـم ابـو طيبة النبي الا الله فامر له بصاع او صاعين من طعام وكلم مواليه فخفف عن غلته او ضريبته (الف) ( بخاری شریف، باب ضريبة العبد و تعاهد ضرائب الاماص... نمبر ۷ ۲۲۷) اس حدیث سے ثابت ہوا کہ حضرت ابوطیبہ غلام تھے اور مولی نے اس کو اجرت پر کام کرنے کی اجازت دی تھی۔ اور اسی ضمن میں تجارت کی اجازت کا معاملہ بھی آئے گا۔ اصول اس باب کے مسائل اس اصول پر طے ہوں گے کہ مولی کو غلام کی تجارت یا کاموں سے نقصان نہ ہو۔ البتہ چونکہ وہ عاقل بالغ ہے اس لئے تجارت کے درمیان کسی چیز کا اقرار کر لیا یا کوئی ایسی حرکت کی جس سے غلام پر جرمانہ لازم ہوتا ہو تو وہ اس کے آزاد ہونے کے بعد وصول کیا جائے گا تا کہ مولی کو نقصان نہ ہو (۲) یہ اصول بھی کار فرما ہوگا کہ تجارت کے درمیان معاون رواداری اور کھلانا پلا نا کر سکتا ہے جو تجارتی معاشرے میں رائج ہیں۔ (۱۹۷۳) (۱) اگر مولی نے غلام کو عام اجازت دی تو اس کا تصرف تمام تجارتوں میں جائز ہے۔ ف مولی نے غلام کو تجارت کی اجازت دیتے وقت کسی خاص چیز کے خریدنے یا بیچنے کی تخصیص نہیں کی تو اس سے عام اجازت ہوگی اور غلام تمام تجارتوں میں آزاد ہوگا ۔ یعنی تمام تجارتوں کی اہمیت حاصل ہو جائے گی۔ البتہ تجارت وہی کرے جو مولی کی مرضی ہو۔
اثر میں ہے۔ قال سفيان ونحن نقول اذا بعثه بمال كثير يبتاع به قلنا اذن له في التجارة وغر الناس منه وان كان انما بعث بالدرهم والدرهمین فليس بشيء (ب) (مصنف عبدالرزاق، باب العبد المأذون ما وقت اذنه ، ج عامن ، ص ۲۸۴، نمبر (۱۵۲۳۰)
Book of the Permitted (slave). [17] (1) When the master gives his slave a general permission, his disposal in all types of trade is permissible. Essential note: A slave whom the master had not given permission to trade, and now gives him permission to trade, is called a ma'dhun slave. Or a child to whom the guardian gives permission to trade is called a ma'dhun child. Its proof is in hadith. From Anas ibn Malik: Abu Taybah cupped the Prophet ﷺ, so he ordered a sa' or two sa' of food for him, and spoke to his masters, so they reduced his tax or tribute. (Bukhari, Book of Slave's Tax...) This hadith proves that Abu Taybah was a slave and his master had given him permission to work for wages. And in this context, the matter of permission for trade will also come. The principle of this chapter: the rulings will be based on the principle that the master should not suffer loss from the slave's trade or actions. However, since he is sane and adult, if he acknowledges something during trade or does an action that necessitates a fine, it will be collected after his emancipation to avoid loss to the master. (2) This principle will also apply: he may engage in customary hospitality and feeding during trade that are common in trading society. (1973) (1) If the master gave the slave general permission, his disposal in all trades is valid. When the master did not specify a particular item to buy or sell when giving permission, it will be a general permission and the slave will be free in all trades, i.e., he will have the capacity for all trades. However, he should only trade in what the master approves. There is an athar: Sufyan said, 'We say: If he sends him with a lot of wealth to purchase, we say he has permission in trade, and people may be deceived by him; and if he sends him with just one dirham or two, it is nothing.' (Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, Chapter of the Ma'dhun Slave: What Constitutes Permission, vol. 8, p. 284, no. 15230)

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of al-ma'dhun, a slave who receives permission from his master to engage in trade. The opening ruling from the matn states that a general permission allows the slave to trade in all types of commerce. The commentary then provides a definition and a hadith proof from Bukhari: the story of Abu Taybah shows that the Prophet’s ﷺ acceptance of his services and the reduction of his tax implies that such permission is valid. The commentary also establishes two key principles: (1) the master must not suffer loss from the slave’s dealings, and (2) the slave may engage in customary hospitality. The athar from Sufyan clarifies that sending a slave with a significant amount of money for trade constitutes permission, while sending with a trivial amount does not. Thus, the ruling is grounded in both textual evidence (hadith and athar) and rational consideration of custom and avoidance of harm.

Key Terms
ماذون (mādhūn): A slave or child given permission by his master/guardian to engage in trade.
اذن عام (idhn ʿāmm): General permission without specification of particular commodities or transactions.
ضروريته (ḍarībata): Tax or tribute paid by a slave to his master from earnings.

Key Takeaway
A general permission (idhn 'amm) from the master allows the slave to trade in all types of commerce, subject to the principle that the master must not suffer loss.
