Transgression and Cessation of Liability in Deposits
Deposit rulings: transgression and cessation of liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify acts of transgression (ta'addi) in deposits, and state the condition under which liability can cease even after transgression.

Core Idea
Transgression imposes liability, but if the depositary undoes the transgression and resumes proper custody, the liability is removed.

This final lesson in the series examines the effects of transgression beyond mixing and spending. The depositary may misuse the deposit by riding an animal, wearing a garment, employing a slave, or redepositing with another person. Such acts instantly make him liable. However, the Hanafi school provides a pathway to restore the trust status: if the depositary ceases the misuse and resumes proper custody before any loss occurs, the liability lifts. This is based on the principle that the cause of liability (ta'addi) has been removed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act of transgression
From the listed examples (riding, wearing, employing, re-depositing), recognize that any unauthorized use of the deposit is ta'addi.
These are all actions that change the nature of the trust.
2
Determine that liability attaches immediately
At the moment of transgression, the depositary becomes a guarantor (damin) and is liable for the value.
This is consistent with the statements of 'Umar and Hasan.
3
Consider the cessation of transgression
If the depositary stops the misuse (e.g., dismounts the animal, removes the garment, stops employing the slave, retrieves the re-deposited item) and keeps the deposit properly, the transgression is removed.
The key is that the deposit is restored to its original state without damage.
4
Apply the rule that liability ceases
According to the passage, once the transgression is removed and the depositary retains the deposit properly, the liability ends.
This is a leniency: the depositary gets a chance to revert to trustee status.
5
Contrast with permanent liability scenarios
If the deposit perishes while the transgression is ongoing, liability is fixed. If it perishes after cessation, there is no liability.
This encourages the depositary to correct his mistake promptly.

The Chapter of Deposit. (1) If the depositary mixes the deposit with his own property such that it becomes indistinguishable, he becomes liable for it. (2) If its owner asks for it and he withholds it while able to deliver it, he becomes liable for it. (3) If it becomes mixed with his property without his action, he becomes a partner with its owner. The Urdu commentary explains: The depositary who mixes the deposit with his own property so that it cannot be separated becomes a guarantor and must pay its value. Because of the mixing, he is no longer a trustee but a guarantor. The owner cannot access his right, so the only solution is to give him its value. There is evidence in the athar: Anas ibn Malik said: I was entrusted with wealth and placed it with my own wealth, and it perished among my wealth. I brought the matter to 'Umar, and he said: You are a trustee in yourself, but it perished among your wealth, so you must guarantee it. (Al-Bayhaqi, Sunan). So in this athar, because he mixed the deposit with his own wealth and it perished, 'Umar made Anas a guarantor. Khalt means mixing. (5) After that, if the owner asks for the deposit and the depositary withholds it from him while able to deliver it, then he becomes liable for it. That is, the owner demanded the deposit from the trustee, and the trustee was able to hand it over but did not, and the deposit perished, so the trustee becomes a guarantor. Not giving despite demand is transgression, and it has passed in the earlier athar that if he transgresses, the trustee becomes a guarantor. 'Umar ibn al-Khattab said: The borrowed item is like a deposit, and there is no liability in it except if one transgresses. (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq). Due to this athar, transgression makes the trustee liable. (6) And if the property becomes mixed without the trustee doing anything, then he becomes a partner with the owner. That is, if the deposit mixes with the trustee's property without the trustee's action, then the trustee becomes a partner with the owner; there is no liability because the trustee did not cause the mixing; it mixed on its own, so no transgression occurred on his part. For example, one thousand dirhams were deposited; the trustee spent six hundred dirhams, and the remaining four hundred perished. The four hundred perished as a deposit because no transgression occurred upon them. As for the six hundred he spent, that is transgression, so liability is necessary for it. This issue is based on the principle that whatever he transgresses upon imposes liability, and whatever he does not transgress upon remains a deposit. In the above example, one thousand were deposited; the trustee spent six hundred dirhams, then brought six hundred dirhams from his own side and mixed them with the remaining dirhams, and all the dirhams perished. Now the trustee becomes a guarantor for the entire one thousand. The six hundred he spent remain his own dirhams until he hands them over to the owner. And the earlier principle passed that if the trustee mixes the deposit with his own property, he becomes liable for the deposit. Here he mixed his own six hundred with the deposit's four hundred, so he becomes a guarantor for the entire one thousand. The saying of 'Umar has passed earlier. There is also a saying of Hasan: Al-Hasan said concerning a man who deposits a deposit, then moves it and takes some of it, he used to say: When he moves it, he becomes liable. (Al-Bayhaqi). In this athar, he mixed his own property with the deposit's property, so the trustee was made a guarantor. Similarly, he mixed his own property with the deposit's property, so he becomes a guarantor for all his property. Previously he was liable for six hundred, and now he also becomes liable for four hundred. The principle is that after the trustee spends, until he hands over that property to the owner, it is considered the trustee's own property. And the second principle is: if he mixes his own property with the deposit's property, he becomes liable for the deposit. (9) If the depositary commits excess (ta'addi) in the deposit, such as if it is an animal and he rides it, or a garment and he wears it, or a slave and he employs him, or he deposits it with someone else, then if he removes the transgression and retains it with himself, the liability is removed. This issue is based on the principle that the trustee committed excess in the deposit, but then removed the excess without destroying it and kept the deposit as a deposit. Since the excess ended, the liability also ends. Now if it perishes, liability is not necessary. The author gave four examples. Commentary
This passage is a set of rulings from the chapter on deposits (al-Wadi'ah) in Al-Sharh al-Thamiri, explaining the liability of the depositary (mudi') in various scenarios. The author first states three key rulings: (1) Intentional mixing: If the depositary mixes the deposit with his own property such that they become indistinguishable, he becomes liable (daman) and must pay the value. This is because he is no longer a trustee (amin) but a guarantor (damin). The proof is the hadith of Anas ibn Malik and the decision of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab. (2) Refusal to return: If the owner demands the deposit and the depositary withholds it despite being able to deliver, he becomes liable. This is an act of transgression (ta'addi), and the principle is that transgression removes the trustee's immunity and imposes liability, as per the statement of 'Umar about borrowed items being like deposits. (3) Involuntary mixing: If the deposit mixes with the depositary's property without his action (e.g., by accident), he does not become liable; instead, both become partners in proportion. Since there is no transgression, the trust remains intact. The commentary then discusses scenarios of partial consumption: if the depositary spends part of the deposit and the remainder perishes naturally, he is liable only for the portion he spent (the transgression), while the remainder remains a trust. However, if he spends part, then replaces it with an equivalent amount from his own property and mixes it with the remaining deposit, he becomes liable for the entire amount because the replacement is considered his own property and mixing it with the deposit triggers liability. This follows the principle that mixing one's own property with the deposit makes the depositary a guarantor for the whole. Finally, the passage addresses cessation of liability: if the depositary commits an act of misuse (e.g., riding an animal, wearing a garment, employing a slave, or re-depositing with another), but then undoes the misuse and resumes proper custody, the liability terminates. This is because transgression is the cause of liability, and once it is removed, the original trust status is restored. The author supports these rulings with narrations from 'Umar and Hasan al-Basri. Note that these rulings are from the Zahir al-Riwayah and are the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
التعدي (al-ta'addi): Transgression; any unauthorized use of the deposit.
زوال الضمان (zawal al-daman): Cessation of liability; the removal of the guarantor status.

Liability of the Depositary: Partial Spending, Replacement, Removal of Misappropriation, Denial, and Travel
Five rulings on depositary liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five rulings concerning depositary liability and apply the principle that liability attaches only to the misused portion unless mixing occurs.

Core Idea
Liability follows misuse; mixing replacement extends liability to all. Denial irreversibly triggers guarantee.

This passage from Kitab al-Wadi'ah presents five critical rulings that define when a depositary (amin) becomes liable for loss or damage to a deposit. The key principle: liability only attaches to the portion the depositary actually misuses, unless he mixes his own property with the remainder. The commentary expands on each scenario with examples, clarifying how trust status can be regained or permanently lost. Understanding these nuances is essential for correctly adjudicating deposit disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first scenario: partial spending
Read the first ruling: if the depositary spends part and the rest perishes without his fault, he is liable only for the spent portion. Note the principle that liability is limited to what was misused.
Example: 600 spent out of 1000; remaining 400 lost; liable for 600 only.
2
Analyze the second scenario: replacement and mixing
If the depositary spends part, then returns an equal amount and mixes it with the remainder, he becomes liable for the entire deposit. This is because the mixing makes the whole deposit like a loan, eliminating the trust distinction.
Mixing his own property with the trust renders all of it as a guarantee.
3
Understand removal of misappropriation
If the depositary misuses the deposit (e.g., rides an animal, wears a garment) but then stops and returns it to trust status, the liability ceases. Future losses are not his responsibility.
The liability is temporary and can be undone by restoring the trust condition.
4
Examine the effect of denial
If the owner demands the deposit and the depositary denies having it, he becomes liable even if he later confesses. Denial is a permanent breach of trust.
Confession does not remove liability; the depositary must return the deposit or its value.
5
Note the rule on traveling
The depositary may travel with the deposit if there is no risk of destruction, even if it is burdensome. Travel does not breach the trust unless forbidden or clearly risky.
The general principle: the depositary may act for preservation unless restricted.

Ruling on joint deposit when one depositor demands his share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on joint deposit when one depositor demands his share and explain the rationale behind Abu Hanifa's view, contrasting it with the Sahibayn's opinion.

Core Idea
A trustee cannot divide a joint deposit; giving to one partner risks liability (Abu Hanifa).

When two people jointly deposit an item with a trustee, and one comes alone asking for his share, a dispute arises: should the trustee give it? Imam Abu Hanifa prohibits it, while the Sahibayn allow it. This lesson explores their evidence, including a precedent from Caliph 'Umar, and explains the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario and Positions
State the factual situation: two depositors, one trustee, one depositor demands his share. Then articulate the two Hanafi positions: Abu Hanifa's prohibition and Sahibayn's permission.
Remember that the dispute is intra-Hanafi; both views are within the school.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's Reasoning
Understand why Abu Hanifa forbids giving: the trustee lacks authority to partition a joint property without both owners' consent, and there is risk of fraud or deception.
The trustee is an amin (trustee) with limited powers.
3
Analyze the Athar (Tradition) of 'Umar
Read the narrated story where a woman gave the deposit to one partner and was held liable by 'Umar. This serves as a proof (dalil) for Abu Hanifa's position.
The athar is a precedent from a Companion, used as evidence in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Understand the Sahibayn's Counterargument
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that the depositor is entitled to his share and the trustee may deliver it if there is no harm. They focus on the owner's right.
They do not see inherent risk or lack of authority as prohibitive.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf and Mufta Bihi
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa's view prevents partial delivery; Sahibayn allow it. The text sides with Abu Hanifa via the athar, making it the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position for fatwa.
In practice, cautious trustees follow Abu Hanifa to avoid liability.

Divisible Deposit with Two Trustees: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Ruling on divisible deposit with two trustees and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divisible deposits with two trustees and explain the intra-madhhab difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
For a divisible deposit with two trustees, Abu Hanifa requires equal division; Sahibayn allow one to hold all with permission.

When a depositor entrusts a divisible item to two trustees, a question arises: must they physically split the item, or can one hold it all with the other's consent? This passage from the matn presents the ruling and highlights a subtle disagreement within the Hanafi school between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The root of the dispute lies in how we interpret the depositor's trust: does trusting two people imply trusting each individually, or only jointly?

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
State the specific case: a man deposits a divisible item (e.g., 1000 dirhams) with two trustees. The ruling given is that they must divide it and each keep half; it is not permissible for one to give it to the other.
The matn explicitly says 'lam yajiz' (not permissible) for handing over to the other.
2
Understand the reasoning behind division
The underlying principle is that the depositor's trust extended to both trustees equally, not to one exclusively. Since the item is divisible and there is no necessity to keep it whole, the safest course is to split custody.
This is Abu Hanifa's position and the apparent meaning of the matn.
3
Contrast with the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn argue that trusting both implicitly means trusting each one individually. Therefore, with the other's permission, one trustee can hold the entire deposit. They view the trust as personal, not collective.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and basis
The matn favors division, and it is considered the more cautious (ahwat) position. The mufta bihi for fatwa is likely the division view, though the Sahibayn's view is also recognized.
Later Hanafi texts may give preference based on the specific circumstances.

Indivisible Deposit with Two Trustees
Ruling on indivisible deposit with two trustees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for an indivisible deposit with two trustees and identify the condition of mutual permission.

Core Idea
An indivisible trust may be held by one trustee with the other's permission – necessity overrides equal custody.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario: when an indivisible item (e.g., a cow) is deposited with two trustees, how should custody be managed? The Hanafi school permits one trustee to keep the entire item, provided the other trustee gives permission. This ruling is rooted in necessity and avoids the impossibility of dividing the item.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of deposit
Determine if the deposited item is divisible (e.g., grain, coins) or indivisible (e.g., a cow, a house). This classification governs the ruling.
Indivisible items cannot be split without destroying their utility or value.
2
Recognize the condition of permission
The ruling only applies if the other trustee explicitly permits one trustee to take sole custody. Without permission, the default is joint custody if possible.
Permission can be verbal or implied by circumstances.
3
Understand the underlying principle
Necessity (darurah) justifies departing from the general rule of shared custody. Since division is impossible, single custody is the practical solution.
This is similar to the maxim 'al-darurah tubih al-mahzurat' (necessity permits the prohibited).
4
Apply the ruling to a concrete example
Take the case of a cow deposited with two trustees. Since a cow cannot be divided, one trustee may keep it with the other's permission. If permission is not given, they must seek a third party or court intervention.
The example is explicitly mentioned in the commentary.

Depositor's Invalid Condition and Trustee Liability
Ruling on depositor's condition not to give deposit to wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a depositor's invalid condition and apply the distinction between necessary and risky deposits to determine trustee liability.

Core Idea
A depositor's impractical condition is invalid; the trustee is not liable unless the item is risky.

This passage examines a common scenario: the depositor tells the trustee not to give the deposit to his wife. Is such a condition binding? The Hanafi school holds that conditions which impose an impossible burden are invalid, allowing the trustee to delegate custody to his household. However, if the deposit itself is risky, the condition becomes valid, and the trustee must comply or bear liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is the trustee giving the deposit to his wife despite a prohibition. The ruling is that he is not liable (ḍāmin) in the general case.
The ruling changes when the item is risky.
2
Locate the dalil
The passage cites the Quranic verse commanding the return of trusts (4:58) as a general principle. This establishes the obligation to return the deposit, but does not directly address the condition.
The dalil supports the duty to return, but the condition is assessed separately.
3
Understand the reasoning for invalidity
The trustee cannot always keep the deposit on his person; he must sometimes rely on his family. A condition prohibiting this is impractical (مشق), hence invalid.
This is based on the principle that necessity overrides restrictions.
4
Distinguish the exceptional case
If the item is such that giving it to the wife creates a risk of loss (e.g., valuable or dangerous), the condition is valid, and the trustee becomes liable if he hands it over and it perishes.
The criterion is the nature of the deposit, not the wife's character.
5
Summarize the thamarah (practical outcome)
General rule: no liability. Exception: liability if the deposit is risky and the condition is valid.

Definition and Permissibility of 'Ariyah (Loan for Use)
Defines 'ariyah, states its ruling, and provides proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'ariyah, identify its hukm level (mubah), and cite the Quranic and hadith proofs that establish its permissibility.

Core Idea
'Ariyah is a transfer of usufruct without compensation, permissible by Quran, Sunnah, and Salaf practice.

This lesson introduces the contract of 'ariyah (loan for use), a common gratuitous transaction in daily life. You will learn its precise definition in Hanafi fiqh, its permissibility (mubah), and the textual evidence from the Quran and prophetic practice that form the foundation of this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition and ruling
Read the Arabic matn: 'al-ariyah ja'izah wa hiya tamlik al-manafi' bi-ghayr 'iwad'. Identify the two elements: permissibility (ja'iz) and the nature (tamlik al-manafi').
Note that Hanafis consider 'ariyah as a transfer of ownership (tamlik) of usufruct, not mere permission (ibahah).
2
Extract the Quranic evidence
Examine the verse 'wa yamna'oon al-ma'oon' (al-Ma'un 107:7). Understand how the word 'ma'oon' is interpreted by the Companions as items given on loan, establishing the legitimacy of 'ariyah.
The Quranic proof is indirect but strengthened by tafsir of the Salaf.
3
Analyze the hadith proof
Read the hadith of the Prophet borrowing a horse from Abu Talhah. Identify how his action demonstrates the permissibility of borrowing (musta'ir) and implicitly lending (mu'ir).
The hadith is from Bukhari, indicating its authenticity.
4
Recognize the valid contract wording
Review the seven expressions listed by the author (a'rtuka, at'amtu, etc.). Understand that any wording clearly indicating the transfer of usufruct without compensation suffices, provided the intention is not hibah (gift).
The contract is non-binding; the lender may revoke it at any time.

The Borrower Cannot Lease the Borrowed Item
Stating the prohibition and its liability consequence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a borrower cannot lease a borrowed item, explain the qiyas reasoning based on contractual bindingness (luzum), and apply the rule to determine liability.

Core Idea
A borrower cannot lease a borrowed item because the lower (revocable) right cannot grant a higher (binding) right.

In the previous lesson, we learned that 'ariyah (gratuitous loan) is a non-binding, revocable contract. Now we encounter a critical limitation: the borrower cannot sublease the item to a third party for compensation. This ruling is not arbitrary; it is derived from a comparison of the legal nature of the two contracts – 'ariyah and ijara. Understanding this prohibition reveals a deeper principle in Hanafi contract law: the weaker right cannot generate a stronger one.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its consequence
Locate the explicit statement in the text: 'The borrower may not lease what he borrowed. If he leases it and it perishes, he is liable.' This is the core ruling.

Understand the nature of 'ariyah (non-binding)
Recognize that 'ariyah is a contract of gratuitous loan that is inherently revocable. The lender can demand the item back at any time; there is no luzum (bindingness).
This is a key point: the borrower's right is weak and temporary.
3
Understand the nature of ijara (binding)
Ijara (lease) is a binding contract for a fixed term. Once contracted, neither party can unilaterally cancel it before the term ends. It creates a stronger, enforceable right.
The lessee has a secure right to use for the duration.
4
Apply the qiyas: the lower cannot include the higher
Use the qiyas reasoning from the commentary: since the borrower's right in 'ariyah is non-binding and weak, he cannot convey a binding, stronger right (ijara) to someone else. The lower cannot encompass the higher.
This is the 'illa (effective cause): the disparity in contractual strength.
5
Determine liability for violation
If the borrower nevertheless leases the item and it perishes (even without fault), he becomes liable for its value. The violation transforms his amana (trust) into daman (liability).
Liability arises from exceeding the permissible use, not from negligence per se.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A borrower cannot rent out a borrowed item because ijarah is binding while ariyah is revocable.

In this lesson, we examine a fundamental rule in the law of ariyah (gratuitous loan): the borrower may not sub-rent the item. The reasoning (talil) hinges on the difference in contractual binding force between ijarah (rental) and ariyah. Understanding this ruling clarifies the limits of the borrower's authority and the consequences of exceeding it.

Guided Path
1

2

If the borrower rents it out and the item perishes (even without negligence), he becomes liable (damin) for its value.
This is because he exceeded his authority as a borrower.

Ijarah is a binding contract (lazim) with a fixed term, while ariyah is non-binding and revocable at any time. The lower degree (ariyah) cannot encompass the higher (ijarah).
The borrower's authority is limited to personal use; he cannot transfer greater rights.

Thus, the borrower's act is invalid and makes him a guarantor.
5
State the principle
A borrower's authority is limited to personal use; he cannot transfer greater rights than he has. The lower contract cannot subsume the higher.
This rule ensures contractual consistency and protects the lender's rights.

Borrowing Fungibles: When 'Ariyah' Becomes 'Qard'
Ruling that borrowing fungibles constitutes a qard, not ariyah

Learning Objective

Borrowing consumable goods is legally a qard, not an ariyah, because the item itself is used up.

If you borrow a sword (non-fungible), you are expected to return the same sword (ariyah). If the item is naturally consumed by use, ariyah is impossible.

Returning a Deposit to the Owner's House: Liability and Proof
Ruling on deposit return without delivery to owner.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when a depository returns a deposit to the owner's house without personal delivery, and explain the Quranic proof (Surah an-Nisa 4:58) that supports this ruling.

Core Idea
Returning a deposit to the owner's house does not fulfill the trust; delivery must be to the owner personally.

This passage marks a critical distinction in the law of deposits (wadi'ah). Unlike borrowed items, a deposited item must be returned directly to the owner, not merely left at his home. The text presents the ruling—the depository remains liable—and supports it with the Quranic command to render trusts to their owners (4:58). Understanding this rule protects the owner's specific intent to keep the item away from his household.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Pinpoint the precise action described: the depository (amin) returns the deposited item to the owner's house but does not hand it over to the owner personally. The ruling is that the depository is liable (daman).
The liability arises immediately upon the incomplete delivery, regardless of whether the item is later lost or stolen.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites Quran 4:58: 'Indeed, Allah commands you to render trusts to their owners.' This verse is the primary evidence. Identify that it is a command (amr) and that in Hanafi usul, a command implies obligation (wujub).
The dalil is naqli (textual) and qat'i in transmission (thubut) but zanni in dalalah (meaning), as the verse can be interpreted to require delivery to the owner or his agent.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Analyze how the verse proves the ruling. The key phrase is 'ila ahliha' (to their owners). The preposition 'ila' indicates delivery, and 'ahl' refers to the rightful owner. The house or household is not the owner; thus, leaving the item at the house does not comply with the command.
The Hanafi school emphasizes the literal meaning of 'ahl' – the person who owns the trust, not a location or family members.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
The text provides a ratio (ta'lil): the owner entrusted the item to a third party specifically to keep it away from his own household. Therefore, delivering it to the household contradicts the owner's purpose and is invalid.
This rationale distinguishes wadi'ah from ariyah (borrowing), where custom may permit leaving the item at the owner's house.
5
Distinguish from similar contracts
Compare with borrowed items (ariyah). In Hanafi fiqh, returning a borrowed item to the owner's house may be acceptable by custom because the owner expects its return to his premises. But in wadi'ah, the owner's intention is to keep the item out of the household, so the stricter rule applies.
The key is the owner's manifested intention when entrusting the item.
