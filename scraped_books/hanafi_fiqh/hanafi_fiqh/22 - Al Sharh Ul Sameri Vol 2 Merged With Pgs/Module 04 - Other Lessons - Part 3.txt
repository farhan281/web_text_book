Core Idea

This lesson examines a specific exception to the general right of pre-emption (shuf'a). While pre-emption normally applies when land is sold, the Hanafi school holds that it does not apply when a house is given as dower (mahr). The reasoning turns on the nature of the consideration: dower is exchanged for marital rights (bud'a), not for property (mal), and the pre-emptor cannot offer the same. Understanding this distinction clarifies the boundary of pre-emption rights.

The text says dower is for the vulva (bud'a), not for property (mal).

Pre-emption only arises when land is sold in exchange for property.
4
Apply the condition to other transfers

If the transfer is not a sale for property, pre-emption is generally not triggered.

Shuf'ah for Consideration That Is Wealth
Condition for shuf'ah: consideration must be wealth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the consideration in a property acquisition qualifies as wealth (mal) for shuf'ah to apply, and distinguish transactions that do not create a pre-emption right.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah only applies when the buyer acquired property for wealth that the pre-emptor can also give.

This passage clarifies a critical condition for the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) in Hanafi fiqh. The right only arises when the buyer acquired the property in exchange for something that qualifies as wealth (mal) — something that can be given in return. The pre-emptor must be able to give the same type of consideration. This rules out cases like mahr in marriage, where the consideration is a benefit (the right to marital relations) that cannot be replicated by a third party.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling and its condition
Identify that shuf'ah is wajib (established) when real estate is owned (i.e., acquired) through a transaction with consideration ('iwad) that is wealth (mal). The condition is that the consideration must be mal.
The passage begins with the rule: 'And when one owns real estate with a consideration that is wealth, then pre-emption is established in it.'
2
Understand the rationale: the pre-emptor must be able to offer the same
The pre-emptor (shafi') can exercise the right by giving the same consideration he gives. Therefore, the consideration must be something the pre-emptor can provide — like dirhams, dinars, rice, or wheat. These are all forms of mal.
The Hanafi principle: shuf'ah is a right of substitution; the pre-emptor steps into the buyer's shoes, so he must give what the buyer gave.
3
Identify the counter-example and its rationale
If a husband gives land to his wife as mahr, the wife takes the land in exchange for the right to marital relations (buda'). The pre-emptor cannot give that benefit, so shuf'ah does not apply.
The passage explicitly says: 'the pre-emptor cannot give the vagina, so in such a case the pre-emptor has no right of pre-emption.'
4
Generalize the principle and apply to other cases
Any transaction where the consideration is not mal (e.g., khul', ibrā', or exchange for labor) would not create shuf'ah. The key is whether the pre-emptor could substitute the exact consideration.
This condition distinguishes shuf'ah from sales for monetary price or commodities.

Ruling: no pre-emption for compromise after denial or silence

Core Idea
A compromise after denial is coerced, not a voluntary sale – so shuf'ah does not arise.

In Hanafi law, the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) is triggered only by a genuine sale for wealth. But what happens when a property is transferred not in a voluntary sale, but as a compromise after a dispute? This lesson examines a specific scenario where the defendant denies a claim or remains silent, then later gives a house to settle. The ruling is clear: no shuf'ah applies because the transaction lacks the essential nature of a sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction

2
Recall the condition for shuf'ah
Shuf'ah attaches only to a sale (bay') that involves an exchange of mal (wealth) voluntarily. A coerced settlement or one not intended as a sale does not meet this condition.
Hanafi jurisprudence: 'La shuf'ah illa fi bay'' – no pre-emption except in a sale.
3
Analyse the nature of this sulh
The sulh after denial or silence is not a voluntary exchange for wealth; it is done under pressure from the claimant's persistent demand. The defendant gives property to end the dispute, not to sell it.
The text says: 'يجب ان لا يكون في مقابل المال' – it is not in exchange for wealth.
4
Derive the ruling
Since the condition for shuf'ah (a genuine sale for mal) is absent, no right of pre-emption arises. No neighbor or co-owner can claim shuf'ah on this property.
The ruling is definitive: 'لا حق شفعہ' – no right of pre-emption.

While a simple compromise may not trigger shuf'ah, the Hanafi school holds that when the debtor acknowledges the debt and then settles with property, the transaction is treated as a sale. Procedure for Pre-emptor in Court
Procedure when pre-emptor claims purchase and demands pre-emption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sequential steps a judge must follow when a pre-emptor claims shuf'ah, and apply the hadith 'al-bayyinah ala al-mudda'i' to determine when proof or oath is required.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor must prove his entitlement; the buyer's admission or oath decides the case.

When a pre-emptor comes to court demanding his right of shuf'ah, the judge must follow a specific procedure to ascertain the pre-emptor's ownership and the fact of sale. This procedure is rooted in the Prophetic hadith that places the burden of proof on the claimant and the oath on the denier. In this lesson, we will walk through the exact steps the judge takes, from questioning the defendant to administering oaths, and see how the hadith governs the entire process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two inquiries
The judge makes two separate inquiries: first, whether the pre-emptor owns the property giving him the right; second, whether the buyer actually purchased it. Each inquiry follows the same pattern of questioning, proof, and oath.
The order matters: ownership must be confirmed before the purchase is investigated.
2
First inquiry: ownership
The judge asks the defendant (buyer) about the pre-emptor's ownership. If the defendant admits it, the case proceeds. If he denies, the judge demands proof from the pre-emptor. If the pre-emptor fails to bring proof, the judge makes the buyer swear that he does not know of such ownership.
Refusal to swear is treated as an admission.
3
Second inquiry: the purchase
After ownership is established, the judge asks the buyer whether he actually bought the property. If he denies, the pre-emptor must prove the sale. If he cannot, the buyer swears that he did not buy.
The same proof/oath pattern applies again.
4
Connect to the hadith
The entire procedure implements the rule 'al-bayyinah ala man idda'a wal-yamin ala man ankara' – proof on the claimant, oath on the denier. Identify how each step aligns with this principle.
The exception for qasama is not relevant here; the general rule governs shuf'ah claims.
5
Summarize the flow

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When property is still with the seller, the judge must first rescind the sale in the buyer's presence before ruling on pre-emption.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) gives a co-owner or neighbor the right to buy property sold to a stranger. However, if the buyer has not yet taken possession, the pre-emptor cannot simply take the property from the seller because the buyer is the legal owner. This lesson explains the court procedure: the judge must bring the buyer, cancel the original sale, and then settle the pre-emption claim with the seller.

Guided Path
1

The pre-emptor brings the seller to court because the property is still physically with the seller (the buyer has not taken possession).
2

The judge must summon the buyer to court. He will not hear any evidence from the pre-emptor until the buyer is present. Then he must rescind the original sale between seller and buyer in the buyer's presence.

If the buyer has already taken possession, the case proceeds directly against the buyer, and no rescission is needed because the buyer is the rightful owner.
This distinction is crucial for correct procedure.

Settlement in Lieu of Pre-emption Voids the Right
Ruling statement on settlement voiding pre-emption

Core Idea
A pre-emptor cannot sell his right; settlement extinguishes the right and the compensation is returned.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri addresses a critical nuance: what happens when a pre-emptor accepts payment to waive his right of shuf'a? The ruling clarifies that the right is not a commodity—it is extinguished by agreement, and the consideration must be returned to the giver. 1
Identify the act and its ruling

The right is extinguished; it is not transferred or sold.
2
Understand the nature of the right
Why must the consideration be returned? Because the right of pre-emption is a legal entitlement (haqq), not a commodity (mal). It cannot be sold or bartered. The settlement is valid only as a means to waive the right, not to generate profit.
The Hanafi view: Shuf'a is a right established by law, not a property.
3
Identify the conditions for the ruling
What conditions must be met for the right to become void? (1) The pre-emptor must make a settlement (sulh) explicitly regarding his right. Pre-emptor's Options When Buyer Improves the Land

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The pre-emptor may take land with improvements at depreciated value or force removal; the buyer is treated as an unauthorized builder.

In this lesson, we examine what happens when a buyer of land improves it through building or planting before the pre-emptor's claim is settled. The Hanafi school grants the pre-emptor a choice, supported by a companion tradition. This ruling illustrates how pre-emption retroactively clarifies ownership rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that the buyer builds or plants after purchasing the land but before the pre-emptor's claim is adjudicated. The pre-emptor's right is then decreed by a court.
The improvements are considered increases to the land.
2
State the two options
The pre-emptor may either (1) take the land by paying the original price plus the value of the building/plants as if they were to be demolished/uprooted, or (2) force the buyer to demolish the building and uproot the plants, handing over the land empty.
Option 1 gives the buyer compensation for the depreciated value of improvements.
3
Understand the compensation valuation
The 'value of the building and plants' is not the standing market value but rather the value after removal (broken/uprooted materials). This is less than the cost or standing value.
The basis is that the buyer built without the pre-emptor's permission.
4
Review the athar from Ibn Umar
The ruling relies on the report that whoever builds on another's land without permission must demolish; with permission he gets compensation. Here, the pre-emptor's retroactive right makes the buyer an unauthorized builder.
The athar is from Abdullah ibn Umar (may Allah be pleased with him).
5

Imam Abu Yusuf and the Shafi'i school hold that the pre-emptor only has option 1 (take with compensation), not the right to force demolition. The majority Hanafi position (Imam Muhammad) includes both options.
The mufta bihi is the majority view giving both options.

A shafi' who voluntarily improves claimed land bears the loss of improvements; he only recovers the price paid.

No Pre-emption in Division of Property
Ruling that division among partners does not give pre-emption to neighbor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that division of property does not create a right of pre-emption for a neighbor and explain the distinction between sale and partition.

Core Idea
Division among co-owners does not create a right of pre-emption for neighbors — only a sale does.

This lesson clarifies a key distinction in Hanafi property law: the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) is triggered by a sale, not by a mere partition of jointly owned property. The source passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri states the rule concisely and provides the underlying rationale: pre-emption arises from an exchange of property for property (mubadalah al-mal bil-mal), which does not occur in division. Understanding this principle helps avoid confusion about when a neighbor can claim pre-emption.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and the Ruling
The act is the division of jointly owned property by partners. The ruling is that the neighbor does not acquire the right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) due to this division.
The text says 'fala shuf'ata' — there is no pre-emption.
2
Understand the Basis of Pre-emption
The right of pre-emption is established only through a sale (bay'), which involves an exchange of property for property (mubadalah al-mal bil-mal). The commentary explicitly states that pre-emption arises 'due to the sale of land and due to the exchange of property for property.'
This is the illah (effective cause) of shuf'ah.
3
Contrast Division with Sale
Division (qismah) is not a sale; it is merely separating the shares of the co-owners. No exchange of property for property takes place; each partner simply takes his own share. Therefore, the illah of pre-emption is absent.
In the commentary: 'there is no sale; rather, they have separated their shares.'
4
Confirm the Consequence
Since the necessary condition for pre-emption (a sale) is missing, the neighbor has no right to claim pre-emption. The ruling is definitive and not subject to disagreement in the Hanafi school.
The cross-madhhab status is muttafaq alayh (unanimous).

When Does a Pre-emptor Regain His Right After Waiving?
Ruling on pre-emption when buyer returns property by qadi's judgment or iqala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two scenarios of property return and state the Hanafi ruling for each.

Core Idea
A judge's judgment annuls the original sale; mutual return creates a new sale, reviving pre-emption.

Pre-emption (shuf'a) is a right that can be waived, but what happens if the buyer later returns the property? Does the waiver stand? The Hanafi school gives a nuanced answer based on how the return occurs. This lesson unpacks the distinction between a court-ordered return and a mutual rescission (iqala).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the Arabic text carefully. There are two conditions under which the buyer returns the property: (a) by a judge's judgment due to an option or defect, and (b) without a judge's judgment or by mutual rescission (iqala).
Note the Arabic phrase 'بقضاء' (by judgment) and 'بغير قضاء قاض' (without judge's judgment).
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For scenario (a): 'فلا شفعة للشفيع' – no pre-emption. For scenario (b): 'فللشفيع الشفعة' – pre-emption exists.
Remember the ruling is reversed for the two cases.
3
Understand the legal basis
The commentary explains: a judge's judgment annuls the original sale (فسخ), so no new sale occurs. Without a judge, the return is treated as a new sale from the pre-emptor's perspective because there is an exchange of property.
Key principle: a new sale triggers pre-emption; a rescission does not.
4
Apply the principle to examples
Think of a practical scenario: the buyer returns a house due to a hidden defect. If the case goes to court and the judge orders return, the pre-emptor loses his right. If the parties agree to cancel (iqala), the right revives.
This distinction is specific to Hanafi fiqh.
