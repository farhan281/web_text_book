Gift of Non-Existent Object Is Invalid
States ruling on gift of non-existent item.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifting a non-existent object and explain why subsequent creation does not validate the original gift.

Core Idea
A gift of a non-existent item is invalid even if it comes into existence later.

This lesson examines a key condition for a valid gift in Hanafi fiqh: the gifted object must exist at the time of the contract. Using the example of flour inside wheat or oil inside sesame seeds, we see that a gift of something not yet existent is fasid (invalid). Even if the donor later processes the raw material into the intended item, the original gift does not become valid; a new gift contract is required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Read the passage and determine what is being gifted (flour in wheat, oil in sesame) and the ruling (fāsid, invalid).
2
Identify the defect
Understand why the gift is invalid: the gifted object (flour or oil) does not yet exist at the time of the contract. It is ma'dum (non-existent).
The existence of the raw material (wheat, sesame) does not suffice.
3
Apply the principle
State the Hanafi principle: a gift requires the subject matter to be existent and deliverable at the time of the contract. Non-existence makes the contract fasid.
This is based on the requirement of tamlik (transfer of ownership) of an existing thing.
4
Contrast with similar cases
Compare with the gift of an undivided share (musha'), which is valid because the object exists even though it is not yet separated. Here the object is entirely non-existent.
The passage references a previous case (mas'alah no. 6) where musha' gift is valid.
5
Determine the practical outcome
If the donor later processes the wheat into flour and delivers it, the original gift remains invalid. A new gift contract must be made after the flour comes into existence.
This emphasizes that the defect in the original gift cannot be cured retroactively.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on gifting an object that is non-existent at the time of the gift. The example given is gifting flour while it is still inside wheat grains, or gifting oil while it is still inside sesame seeds. Since the flour or oil does not exist as a separate entity at the moment of the gift, the gift is void. Even if the donor later processes the wheat into flour or the sesame into oil and then delivers it, the original gift remains invalid because the condition of possession of an existing object was not met. A new gift contract would be required. The underlying principle is that a gift of a non-existent item is not valid in Hanafi fiqh, as opposed to a gift of an undivided share (musha') which is valid because the object exists albeit as part of a whole.

Key Terms
هبة فاسدة (hibah fasidah): An invalid gift that does not transfer ownership because of a defect in the object (e.g., non-existence).
معدوم (ma'dum): Non-existent; an object that has no physical existence at the time of the contract.

Gift with Condition of Compensation Treated as Sale
Ruling that gift with compensation becomes like a sale after possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position on hibah bi-shart al-'iwad from the Shafi'i/Zufar position and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A gift with a condition of compensation is valid only after mutual possession; thereafter it is treated as a sale with all its consequences.

In this passage, the commentator explains the hybrid nature of a gift given with a condition of compensation. The Hanafi school gives weight to both the wording (gift) and the meaning (sale), leading to distinct rules: possession is required as in a gift, but after possession, sale remedies apply. This differs from the Shafi'i and Zufar position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling: the contract requires possession of both the gift and the compensation for validity. Once possession occurs, it is treated as a sale, so return due to defects, option of inspection, and pre-emption apply.
The commentator emphasizes 'both wording and meaning are considered as far as possible'.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Zufar hold that the contract is a pure sale from start to finish; therefore, they do not require possession in the session for validity.
Their principle is to consider only the meaning (ma'na).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The opponent's reasoning: since the condition of compensation makes the transaction a sale in substance, the rules of sale apply exclusively, including the absence of a possession requirement for validity.
They rely on the overriding meaning over the literal wording.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school argues that ignoring the wording would disregard the parties' explicit expression. Both aspects must be observed: the gift aspect requires possession; the sale aspect applies after possession.
The commentator cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas requiring possession for gifts.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: in the Hanafi school, if possession does not occur, the contract is invalid. In the Shafi'i/Zufar view, the contract is binding immediately without possession in the session.
This affects the validity of the contract and the timing of when sale remedies apply.

Hiba bi Shart al-'Iwad: Gift with Condition of Compensation
Ruling on gift with condition of compensation and its dual nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on hiba bi shart al-'iwad and distinguish it from the Shafi'i/Zufar position by identifying the role of possession.

Core Idea
A gift conditioned on compensation requires possession on both sides; after possession, it is treated like a sale.

The gift with a condition of compensation (hiba bi shart al-'iwad) sits at the intersection of gift and sale law. This passage clarifies the Hanafi position: the contract is not valid until mutual possession occurs, after which it acquires all the effects of a sale, including options and pre-emption. Understanding this dual nature is key to avoiding confusion with a pure sale.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify that the Hanafi school requires mutual possession (taqabud) for validity, after which the contract takes on the rulings of a sale, including khiyar options and shuf'a.
2
Identify the opposing school and its position
Imam Shafi'i and Imam Zufar consider this transaction a pure sale from beginning to end, thus no possession is required for validity.
Their principle is to give precedence to meaning over wording.
3
Examine the Hanafi evidence
The Hanafi proof is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'A charity is not valid until it is taken into possession.' This is used to argue that possession is a condition for any gratuitous transfer, including hiba.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but used as evidence.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the opposing view
The Hanafi school argues that both wording (gift) and meaning (sale) must be considered as far as possible. Therefore, gift rulings (possession) are not completely discarded, but once possession occurs, sale rulings apply.
This is based on the principle of considering both aspects.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical consequence is that before mutual possession, the contract is not valid according to Hanafis; according to Shafi'i, it is valid and binding immediately. Also, the applicability of khiyar options and shuf'a may differ in timing.
For example, if possession never occurs, Hanafis treat the contract as invalid, while Shafi'is enforce it as a sale.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the ruling on hiba bi shart al-'iwad. It begins by stating the rule: when a gift is given with a condition of compensation, mutual possession (taqabud) of both compensations (the gifted object and its counterpart) is required. Only after possession does the contract become valid. Once validated, it takes on the legal effects of a sale: the recipient can return the item due to a defect (khiyar al-'ayb) or because of not having seen it (khiyar al-ru'ya), and if the gifted item is land, the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) applies. The commentator explains this duality: the transaction is a gift in wording but a sale in meaning. Hence both sets of rules apply: gift rules require possession for validity, while sale rules provide options and pre-emption. The evidence for the necessity of possession is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'A charity is not valid until it is taken into possession.' This is used to prove that possession is a condition in gifts. The text then mentions that Imam Shafi'i and Imam Zufar hold a different view: they give precedence to the meaning (sale) over the wording (gift), so according to them, possession need not occur in the session; the contract is immediately binding as a sale. The Hanafi position (taught by Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad) is to consider both aspects as far as possible, requiring possession for validity but then applying sale rules.

Key Terms
هبة (hibah): A gift; a contract of gratuitous transfer of ownership without compensation.

The passage uses a threshold of three days' journey. If the slave is returned from a distance of three days or more, the reward is a fixed 40 dirhams. For shorter distances, the reward is proportional to the distance.

If the slave's market value is less than 40 dirhams, the reward is reduced to the slave's value minus one dirham. This prevents the master from paying more than the slave's worth.

The passage cites a hadith from Ibn Umar (10 dirhams for a slave found in the Haram) and athar from Ibn Mas'ud (40 dirhams per slave) and Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz (daily rate). The Hanafi school adopts the 40-dirham amount from Ibn Mas'ud as the general rule.
The hadith on the Haram is specific to that location and does not override the general athar.
5

No intra-Hanafi dispute is mentioned. Other schools may have different fixed amounts; the Hanafi position relies on the strength of the athar.
The reward is mukhtalaf fih between schools.

Specific Instruction Does Not Constitute Trade Permission
Distinguishing between a specific errand and general trade permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a specific purchase instruction and general trade permission, and state the ruling regarding the slave's ma'dhun status.

Core Idea
A specific errand to buy a particular item does not grant a slave general trade permission.

This lesson examines a key distinction in the Hanafi law of ma'dhun (permitted) slaves. Not every instruction to buy something confers trade capacity; only explicit and general permission does. The passage clarifies that if a master tells his slave to fetch a specific cloth, that is a service errand, not an authorization to engage in commerce. Understanding this difference is essential to correctly applying rulings on slave trade permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The master gives a specific instruction to buy a particular item. The ruling is: the slave is not ma'dhun (not permitted to trade).
This is the core negative ruling of the passage.
2
Distinguish specific instruction from general permission
Contrast the specific errand (e.g., 'bring that cloth') with general permission (e.g., 'you are free to trade'). The former is a service, the latter is trade authorization.
This distinction is the legal basis for the ruling.
3
Locate the authority
The passage cites an athar from Shuraih: if a slave is assigned to one type of work and exceeds it, no liability falls on the master. This supports that permission is limited to what is explicitly allowed.
The athar is found in Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq.

Apply the logic
If buying a small item were considered permission, it would close the door of service. Thus, the permission is only for the specific errand, not for general trade.
The reasoning prevents conflating routine service with commercial activity.
5
State the practical consequence
The slave does not become ma'dhun via specific instructions. Any trade beyond the errand is without permission and the slave is liable.
This affects validity of transactions and liability.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The ma'dhun's debts bind his person, not his master — he can be sold to pay them.

In Hanafi fiqh, when a slave is authorized to trade (ma'dhun), he may incur debts in the course of business. This lesson clarifies that those debts are not the master's personal liability but attach to the slave's own person (raqabah). Consequently, the creditors have the right to have the slave sold to satisfy the debts, though the master may prevent the sale by paying the slave's value.

Selling the Ma'dhun Slave for Debt and Proportional Distribution
Ruling on sale of ma'dhun slave for debt with proportional distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling a ma'dhun slave for debt, explain the proportional distribution rule, and evaluate the Shafi'i/Zufar objection.

Core Idea
A ma'dhun slave can be sold for debt even if the debt exceeds his value; the price is divided proportionally among creditors.

The ma'dhun slave, permitted by his master to trade, incurs debts that attach to him. When his earnings are insufficient, the Hanafi school rules that he may be sold to satisfy creditors, with the proceeds distributed proportionally. This ensures fairness and prevents loss to any single creditor. The master may ransom the slave to prevent the sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the sale of a ma'dhun slave to pay off trade debts. The Hanafi ruling is that such a sale is wajib (obligatory) when the slave's own earnings are insufficient.
The ruling applies even if the debt exceeds the slave's current market value.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is an athar (mursal) from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i: 'The slave is sold for debt even if it is more than his value.' This indicates that the slave is to be sold regardless of the debt-to-value ratio.
The dalil is from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq; its thubut is zanni but its dalala is considered qati by the Hanafis.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit command 'yuba' al-'abd fi daynihi' is understood as a directive implying obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul. The phrase 'wa in kana akthara min qimatihi' removes any condition of debt being less than value.
This interpretation relies on the Hanafi principle that al-amr yufidu al-wujub.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Zufar hold that the slave should not be sold because it causes loss to the master without giving him a benefit.
They argue that the master's interest outweighs creditor rights when the sale results in net loss.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi response is that the master's potential loss is secondary to the creditors' rights; the master can always ransom the slave to prevent the sale. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is that in the Shafi'i view, creditors cannot force the sale and must wait until the slave is free.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a master sells to his ma'dhun slave at or below market value and explain why it is permissible based on the principle of no harm to creditors.

Core Idea
A master's sale to his ma'dhun slave at market value or less is permissible because it does not harm creditors.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in Hanafi sales law: a master sells an item to his ma'dhun slave, who is already burdened with debt. The question is whether such a sale is valid. The answer depends on the price: if it is at market value (mithl al-qimah) or less, the sale is valid because it either causes no loss or benefits the slave, and crucially, it does not harm the creditors. In this lesson, we will unpack the ruling and the underlying principle protecting creditors' rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and transaction
The parties are the master (mawlā) and the ma'dhun slave (a slave authorized to trade). The transaction is a sale of an item by the master to the slave.
2
Determine the price condition
The price must be at the equivalent of market value (mithl al-qimah) or less. Selling above market value is not covered here.
The text explicitly allows 'mithl al-qimah aw aqall'.
3
Analyze the effect on the slave
If sold at market value, no loss to the slave; if sold at a lower price, the slave benefits. Both scenarios are acceptable.
4
Consider the creditors
The sale must not harm the creditors. Since the price is at market value or less, the slave's estate is not diminished unfairly.
The text states: 'al-uṣl anna al-ghuramā' lā yaḍurrūna' (the principle is that creditors are not harmed).
5
Conclude the ruling
The sale is permissible (jā'iz). The ruling is consistent with protecting creditors' interests.
Implicitly, selling above market value would invalidate the sale.

Delivery before Payment Voids the Price (Sale to Ma'dhun Slave)
Ruling that delivery before payment voids the price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a master delivers goods to his ma'dhun slave before receiving payment, explain the legal reasoning, and identify the practical outcome.

Core Idea
Delivery before payment to a ma'dhun slave voids the price because master and slave are legally one entity.

In Hanafi fiqh, certain transactions between a master and his own slave are governed by unique principles. This passage addresses a specific scenario: a master sells an item to his ma'dhun (permitted to trade) slave, but delivers the item before receiving payment. The ruling may seem counterintuitive, but it follows from a fundamental maxim that a person cannot have a debt claim against his own property.

Identify the act and the parties
The act is a sale of goods by a master to his ma'dhun slave who is already in debt.
Ma'dhun slave has permission to trade but is wholly owned by the master.
2
Identify the condition triggering voidness
The master delivers the sold item before receiving the price (qabd al-thaman).
If payment were received first, the sale would be valid.
3
State the ruling
The price becomes void (batil) – the master cannot legally claim it.
The debt is considered non-existent in law.
4
Understand the reasoning
The master cannot have a debt upon his own slave because the slave is entirely his property; a claim of debt would be a claim against himself.
This is a core Hanafi principle.
5
Distinguish legal vs. ethical obligation
Legally, the master cannot recover the price, but ethically, the slave should pay it voluntarily.
The ruling does not forbid voluntary payment.

Core Idea
A master selling to his own slave is like a stranger: he may withhold the goods until payment.

This passage addresses a practical concern: if a master sells an item to his ma'dhun (permitted) slave, can he demand payment before delivery? The default rule might suggest that once delivery is made, the price cannot be claimed. However, the master can avoid this by retaining possession until payment. The ruling clarifies that the master is treated as any other seller in this transaction, granting him the right to withhold until the price is paid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling

The ruling is clear and unconditional.

The master is like a stranger (ajnabi) at the time of sale. This means he is not treated as the owner of the slave's property but as an independent seller.
This reasoning prevents the master from losing his right to payment.

Any seller, including a master, has the right to retain the sold item until the price is received, provided the sale is valid.
This is a general principle in sales (habs).
4

Without this ruling, the master would face a dilemma: deliver first and risk non-payment, or refuse delivery and potentially breach the contract. The solution is simply to withhold.
The ruling balances the master's right to payment with the validity of the sale.

Freeing a Ma'dhun Slave with Outstanding Debts
States ruling on master freeing a permission-slave with debts; master guarantees value to creditors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on freeing a ma'dhun slave with debts and explain the master's liability based on the athar from Musannaf Abdur Razzaq.

Core Idea
Freeing a ma'dhun slave is valid, but the master must compensate creditors for the slave's value.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in Hanafi commercial law: a master has a permission-slave (ma'dhun) engaged in trade who incurs debts. If the master then frees the slave, what happens to those debts? The passage establishes that the manumission itself is valid, but the master becomes a guarantor for the slave's market value to protect the creditors. We will examine the ruling, its proof from the early scholars (athar), and the underlying legal principle of liability based on harm caused.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
First, note the act being considered: the master frees (yu'tiqu) a ma'dhun slave who is indebted. The ruling states that this freeing is valid (ja'iz).
The validity of the manumission is established despite the debts.
2
Identify the Master's Liability
The master becomes a guarantor (dhamin) for the slave's market value (qimah) to the creditors (ghurma'), not for the full debt amount necessarily.
The liability is limited to the slave's value.
3
Locate the Dalil (Proof)
The proof is an athar (report) from Hammad and other early scholars, recorded in Musannaf Abdur Razzaq. The key phrase is 'qimat al-'abd 'ala al-sayyid' (the slave's value is upon the master).
This athar is a mursal hadith (zanni al-thubut) but accepted in Hanafi practice.
4
Understand the Underlying Principle
The commentary explains that the master is liable because his act of freeing caused a loss (darar) to the creditors, who could have sold the slave to recover debts. The principle is 'al-darar bi qadr al-ta'wīḍ' (liability is proportional to the harm caused).
This mirrors the broader Hanafi maxim that liability follows harm.

Post-Harvest Conditions in Muzara'ah: Invalidity and Custom Exception
Intra-Hanafi dispute on invalidity of muzara'ah with post-harvest conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why a condition for post-harvest work on the sharecropper invalidates a muzara'ah contract, and explain the Hanafi intra-school disagreement on custom as an exception.

Core Idea
A condition on the worker to do post-harvest tasks turns muzara'ah into a prohibited 'safqah fi safqah', unless custom allows it.

Muzara'ah (sharecropping) ends when the crop ripens. Any additional work after that is a separate hire. This lesson examines the ruling when the landowner stipulates that the worker must perform such tasks. The general rule is invalidity, but Imam Abu Yusuf raises an important exception: if local custom treats those tasks as part of the worker's duty, the contract remains valid. This dispute illustrates the interplay between original prohibition and customary practice in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling and its reasoning
The passage first states the general rule: any condition on the worker to do post-harvest work invalidates the muzara'ah. Understand that this is because the contract becomes a 'safqah fi safqah' (two transactions in one), mixing sharecropping with hire.
Safqah fi safqah is a prohibited form of combining contracts.
2
Locate the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees. He argues that if local custom ('urf) holds the worker responsible for those tasks, the condition does not invalidate. He draws an analogy to a miller being required to stitch a shoe if that is customary.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
3
Appreciate the role of custom
The key principle is that 'urf (custom) can modify what is considered an additional condition. Custom determines the scope of the worker's obligations. This is a recurring theme in Hanafi mu'amalat.
Custom does not change the original prohibition; it specifies what is part of the original contract.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the general rule, the contract is invalid and must be renegotiated. Under Abu Yusuf's view, if custom exists, the contract stands. Later Hanafi practice tends to follow the custom-based view.
The mufta bihi is not explicit here, but many later works prefer the custom-based position.

The meaning here is that fruit trees exist; one irrigates them, and whatever fruit appears in two or three months is divided between the tree owner and the irrigator and the worker according to the share. In the previous chapter were issues of partnership in cultivation and farming, and in this chapter are issues of partnership in fruit, concerning its permissibility and impermissibility. The same disagreement exists between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions as passed in the book of Muzara'ah, and the proofs of both are the same as passed in that chapter. [1719] (1) Imam Abu Hanifa said: Musaqat by giving some fruit is invalid. There is one difficulty: that the irrigator irrigates and gives him a dirham or dinar as wages or some broken fruit — that is permissible. And the second form: that due to irrigation, from the fruit that comes on the tree, he gives a third or a fourth — this form is invalid according to Imam Abu Hanifa, similar to muzara'ah (it passed that it is makruh). The hadith passed earlier: Thabit reported that the Messenger of Allah forbade muzara'ah and ordered hiring and said there is no harm in it (Muslim, chapter on muzara'ah and hiring, p. 10 no. 1549). From this hadith it is known that the Prophet forbade muzara'ah and musaqat. And in the hadith of Abu Dawud: From Jabir ibn Abdullah said: I heard the Messenger of Allah say: Whoever does not abandon mukhabarah, let him declare war from Allah and His Messenger (Abu Dawud no. 3406). And in the hadith after it: From Zaid ibn Thabit said: The Messenger of Allah forbade mukhabarah. I asked: What is mukhabarah? He said: That you take land for half, a third, or a fourth (Abu Dawud, chapter on mukhabarah, p. 27 no. 3407). From this hadith it is known that the Prophet severely forbade mukhabarah. And mukhabarah means taking land for cultivation for half, a third, or a fourth, whether for farming or for musaqat. [1720] (2) And the two companions say: It is valid when a known period is mentioned and both specify a common share of the fruit. The two companions say that musaqat is valid with two conditions: one is that the period of musaqat is specified — for how many months the tree [is to be irrigated]. (b) I heard the Prophet say: Whoever does not leave mukhabarah — i.e., sharecropping — then for them is a declaration of war from Allah and His Messenger. (c) The Prophet forbade mukhabarah. Commentary
This passage presents the classic dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Sahibayn), regarding the validity of musaqat (a contract where one cares for fruit trees in exchange for a share of the fruit). Imam Abu Hanifa rules that musaqat based on a share of the fruit is batil (invalid). He distinguishes between two scenarios: (1) if the irrigator is paid a fixed wage (dirham/dinar) or some broken fruit as hire, that is permissible; (2) if he is given a fractional share like one-third or one-fourth of the fruit that grows due to his work, that is invalid by analogy with muzara'ah, which is prohibited by hadith. The text recounts hadiths from Muslim and Abu Dawud forbidding muzara'ah and mukhabarah (a form of sharecropping). The two companions, however, hold musaqat to be ja'iz (valid) provided two conditions are met: (a) the duration of the contract is specified (e.g., number of months), and (b) the share of the fruit is designated as a common fraction (e.g., half, third, etc.). The commentary states that the same dispute and proofs apply as in the chapter on muzara'ah. The passage thus lays out the position of Abu Hanifa (invalid) and the Sahibayn (valid with conditions), along with the hadith evidence. The mufta bihi (relied-upon position) in the later Hanafi school is the view of the Sahibayn, making musaqat valid when conditions are met.

Key Terms
مساقاة (musaqat): A contract where a worker tends fruit trees (irrigation, etc.) in exchange for a share of the fruit.
جزء من الثمرة (juz' min al-thamara): A portion or share of the fruit produced, typically a fraction like half or third.
مدة معلومة (muddatun ma'luma): A known or specified duration for the musaqat contract, e.g., number of months.
