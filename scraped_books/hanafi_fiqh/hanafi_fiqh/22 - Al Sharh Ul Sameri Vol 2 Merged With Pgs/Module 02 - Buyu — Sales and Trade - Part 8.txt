Acknowledgment of Lineage from Non-Parents/Child Not Accepted
Ruling on acknowledgment of lineage from brother/uncle not accepted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on acknowledgment of lineage from non-parents/child and identify the principle that invalidates such acknowledgment.

Core Idea
You cannot establish another's lineage by your own acknowledgment—only the person whose lineage is at stake can affirm it.

In Hanafi law, acknowledging a blood relationship carries legal consequences. This lesson clarifies that an acknowledgment attributing someone to a third party (like claiming a brother) is invalid because it imposes lineage on another without consent. Understanding this principle is key to differentiating valid from invalid acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is acknowledging someone's lineage (e.g., claiming they are your brother or uncle). The ruling: such acknowledgment is not accepted (invalid) in Hanafi law.
Focus on the category: lineage acknowledgment from non-parents/child is outright invalid.
2
Understand the underlying principle
The reason for invalidity is 'tahmil al-nasab ala al-ghayr' – imposing lineage on another. Claiming a brother implies the person is your father's son, thereby imposing lineage on your father without his confirmation.
Similarly, claiming an uncle imposes lineage on your grandfather.
3
Apply the distinction
Contrast with a valid acknowledgment: if you claim someone as your own child (son/daughter), that is accepted because it does not impose lineage on a third party. Only lineage that directly relates to you is permissible.
The key is whether the acknowledgment establishes lineage from yourself or from another.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from the Book of Iqrār (Acknowledgment). The matn states that if a person acknowledges lineage from someone who is not his parent or child—for example, claiming someone is his brother or uncle—such an acknowledgment is not accepted in Islamic law. The Urdu commentary explains the reason: the brother claim implies that the acknowledged person is the son of the acknowledger's father, thus establishing lineage from the father, which is imposing lineage on another (tahmil al-nasab 'ala al-ghayr). Similarly, an uncle claim implies lineage from the grandfather. Since the general principle is that one cannot establish lineage from another, but only from oneself, these acknowledgments are invalid. The exception is if the individual acknowledges lineage for himself alone (e.g., 'this is my son'), which is accepted. The term 'tahmil al-nasab 'ala al-ghayr' is key: it refers to attributing lineage to another person by acknowledgment, which is prohibited.

Key Terms
تحمیل النسب علی الغیر (taḥmīl al-nasab ʿalā al-ghayr): Imposing lineage on another person by acknowledgment, e.g., claiming someone is one's brother which implies the father's lineage.
مقر (muqirr): The person making the acknowledgment (i.e., the one who says 'this is my brother').
مقر له (muqarr lahu): The person in whose favor the acknowledgment is made (i.e., the one acknowledged as brother/uncle).

The Hierarchy of Pre-emption Rights: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
States the three ranks of pre-emption right holders with proofs and rebuttal of Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three priorities of shuf'ah according to Hanafi law and explain the Hanafi rebuttal to the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Pre-emption rights follow a strict order: co-owner in property, then co-owner in rights, then neighbor — the neighbor's right is uniquely Hanafi.

This passage from al-Sharh al-Thamiri presents the Hanafi ruling on shuf'ah and its three-tiered hierarchy. It not only states the ruling but defends it against the Shafi'i view that limits pre-emption to co-owners only. We will walk through the categories, the proofs, and the inter-madhhab dispute, understanding why the Hanafi school includes the neighbor as a third priority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
List the three claimants in order: co-owner in the property (sharik fi nafs al-mabi'), co-owner in the rights (sharik fi haqq al-mabi'), and neighbor (al-jar). Understand that each subsequent rank only gets the right if the previous rank declines.
The rights include path (tariq) and water (shurb).
2
Understand the rationale
Why is the co-owner in property first? To prevent harm from an unwanted partner. Why the co-owner in rights? To protect shared easements. Why the neighbor? To prevent nuisance from an undesirable neighbor.
The Hanafi school emphasizes general harm prevention (darar) as the underlying illa.
3
Examine the Hanafi proofs
The text cites hadith: 'al-jar ahaqqu bishuf'atihi' (neighbor is more entitled) and the mursal hadith ranking partner over neighbor and neighbor over stranger. Note that these are ahad hadith, making the ruling wajib (not fard).
The epistemological weight is zanni al-thubut, hence the hukm level is wajib.
4
Understand the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that shuf'ah only applies when there is undivided common ownership, citing the hadith: 'when boundaries are set and roads are made, there is no shuf'ah.' He excludes the neighbor.
The Shafi'i proof is also a sound hadith, leading to ikhtilaf.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school interprets the Shafi'i hadith as referring to the termination of co-ownership in the property, not to the neighbor's right. They maintain that the neighbor's right is proven by other explicit hadith.
The thamarah al-khilaf is practical: a neighbor can compel purchase in Hanafi but not in Shafi'i law.

Second are those who are not partners in the land itself but share a right of the land, e.g., a path to the land or a water channel. They are called partners in the right of the sold property. They receive the right of shuf'ah in the second rank. Third are those who are neither partners in the sold property nor in its path or water, but their land adjoins it. They are called neighbors and receive the right of shuf'ah in the third rank. If these three types do not buy, then outsiders have the right. The proof of this order is the mursal hadith: 'I heard al-Sha'bi say: The Messenger of Allah ﷺ said: The shafi' (partner) has more right than the neighbor, and the neighbor has more right than the stranger.' (Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, bab al-Shuf'ah, vol. 8, p. 79, no. 14390; Musannaf Ibn Abi Shayba, from those who judged shuf'ah for neighbors, vol. 4, p. 521, no. 22717). In this hadith, shafi' means partner because in another narration: 'The partner has more right than the shafi', and the shafi' has more right than others.' (Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, vol. 8, p. 78, no. 14386). Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the hierarchy of pre-emption (shuf'ah) rights in Hanafi law. It begins with the matn stating the ruling: shuf'ah is wajib (obligatory) and establishes three categories of claimants in order of priority: first, the co-owner in the property itself; second, the co-owner in the appurtenant rights (like right of way or water share); third, the neighbor. The commentary explains and justifies this hierarchy with both rational reasoning and prophetic hadith. It notes that if the first priority claimant exercises their right, the subsequent claimants lose their right. If they decline, the right passes to the next. The text then engages in an inter-madhhab dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, who restricts shuf'ah to the first two categories and denies it to mere neighbors. Shafi'i bases his view on the hadith that shuf'ah only applies in undivided property; once boundaries and roads are separated, there is no shuf'ah. The Hanafi response is that this hadith refers to the termination of co-ownership in the property, not to the neighbor's right. The neighbor's right is proven by other authentic hadith, including 'the neighbor is more entitled' and the mursal hadith ranking the partner above the neighbor and the neighbor above a stranger. The analysis follows the text's own order: first the ruling, then the explanation of categories, then the proofs, then the Shafi'i objection and Hanafi rebuttal, emphasizing that the Hanafi position is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) doctrine.

Key Terms
الشفعة (al-shuf'ah): The right of pre-emption to purchase a property that has been sold to an outsider, granted to certain categories of persons (co-owners, co-owners in appurtenances, neighbors) to prevent harm.
الخليط (al-khalit): A co-owner or partner; in this context, it specifically refers to the co-owner in the sold property (شریک فی نفس المبیع).
حق المبيع (haqq al-mabi'): The rights attached to the sold property, such as right of way (طریق) and water rights (شرب). Those who share in these are considered co-owners in appurtenances.
الجار (al-jar): The neighbor whose property adjoins the sold property. In Hanafi, he has third priority pre-emption right.

Order of Pre-emptors and the Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Establishes order of pre-emptors and rebuts Shafi'i view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi order of pre-emptors and explain why the Hanafi school includes the neighbor as a pre-emptor, using the textual proofs and rebutting the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, pre-emption extends to partners, right-of-way partners, and neighbors — each in order.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'a) allows a person to step into a sale and buy the property under the same terms. But who qualifies? The Hanafi school sets a clear priority: first the co-owner in the property, then the partner in shared rights of way or water, and finally the adjoining neighbor. This lesson unpacks the evidence for this order and addresses the Shafi'i objection that mere neighbors have no right at all.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi order of pre-emptors
State the three categories in order: (1) partner in the sold property (sharik fi al-mabi'), (2) partner in the right of way or water (sharik fi al-tariq wa al-shurb), (3) neighbor (jar). Understand that earlier categories block later ones unless they waive.
2
Locate the primary evidence for the neighbor's right
Locate the hadith of Samurah: 'The neighbor of a house has more right to the house or land' and the mursal hadith of al-Sha'bi that explicitly orders the three classes.
These are the main Hanafi proofs for including the neighbor.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i objection
Examine the hadith of Jabir that pre-emption applies only before boundaries and roads are set. Understand how Imam al-Shafi'i uses this to deny the neighbor's right when there is no shared pathway.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school argues that the hadith of Jabir refers only to the end of partnership in the property itself, not to the neighbor's independent right. The neighbor's proof is stronger and earlier evidence.
Follow the dalil hierarchy: the Samurah hadith is specific to the neighbor and cannot be overridden by a general hadith.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In practice, under Hanafi law a neighbor can pre-empt even if he has no share in the property or rights; under Shafi'i he cannot. This affects real estate transactions in jurisdictions that follow either school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three sequential stages of pre-emption (establishment, confirmation, ownership) and apply the condition that immediate witness is required for confirmation.

Core Idea
Pre-emption right is like a rope: untie it fast or lose it.

Pre-emption (shuf'ah) protects a partner or neighbour from an unwanted third party buyer. But this right does not automatically grant ownership — it unfolds in three distinct legal stages, each with its own requirement. This lesson walks through the Hanafi position, supported by hadith, and highlights the critical role of immediate witness-bearing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three stages

For stage 1: a valid sale must occur. For stage 2: the pre-emptor must bear witnesses in the same gathering (majlis) upon learning of the sale. For stage 3: the buyer must hand over or a judge must decree.
Delay or reluctance after knowledge can invalidate the right (sāqiṭ).
4
Analyse the consequence of missing a stage
If the sale is absent → no right arises. If witnesses are not produced → the right is not confirmed and may be lost. If taking does not occur → ownership does not transfer.
The right is contingent; it does not automatically mature.
5

The Procedure for Claiming Shuf'ah (Pre-emption) in Hanafi Fiqh
Details the procedure for claiming shuf'ah with proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four steps a pre-emptor must take upon learning of a sale and apply them to determine if the right is lost.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is lost if not claimed immediately — wait and it vanishes.

Knowing you have a right to pre-empt a sale is not enough; the Hanafi madhhab demands immediate action. This lesson examines the precise sequence of actions the shafi' must perform to secure his right, from the first verbal claim to the judge's decree. The proofs from the Prophet and the Companion Shuraih underscore the urgency required.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The right of pre-emption requires an immediate claim upon learning of the sale. Any delay or reluctance nullifies the right.
This is the core principle derived from the hadith 'shuf'ah is like untying a rope' and the athar of Shuraih.
2
List the four steps
The pre-emptor must: (1) immediately verbalize his intention, (2) bear witnesses to the claim in the same council, (3) go to the seller, buyer, or property and declare before witnesses, and (4) proceed without delay to the judge to present the case.
These steps are sequential and must be performed in order.
3
Understand the rationale
The right of pre-emption is easily lost because it involves taking property from another. The steps ensure clarity and prevent disputes. Reluctance (i'rad) is signalled by any delay beyond the immediate council.
The term 'wathb' (leaping) in the athar emphasizes immediate, decisive action.
4
Analyze the proofs
The two main proofs are: (1) the hadith 'al-shuf'ah ka-hall al-'iqal' (shuf'ah is like untying a rope) indicating that once a rope is untied, it cannot be retied; likewise, delay destroys the right. (2) the athar of Shuraih: 'innama al-shuf'ah li-man wathabaha' (pre-emption is only for the one who leaps to it).
These proofs establish that immediate action is not just recommended but necessary.

If any step is omitted or delayed, the right of shuf'ah is lost permanently. The pre-emptor cannot reclaim it later, even if the sale has not yet been completed.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Shuf'ah only arises in sales of property; exchanges not considered 'mal' do not trigger pre-emption.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'ah) is not absolute. The Hanafi school has identified specific transactions where shuf'ah does not arise, even though property changes hands. This lesson presents those exceptions and explains the underlying principle: the transaction must be a sale of property (mal). Understanding these exceptions prevents a mistaken claim of shuf'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five transactions

2
Recognize the common principle
In each case, the exchange is not a sale of property (mal). The authors stress that khul', ijarah, dam 'amd, and 'itq are not considered mal for shuf'ah purposes.
The pre-emptor cannot offer an equivalent exchange, so the right does not arise.

Agent's Shuf'a Lost When Selling Without Claiming

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An agent who sells without claiming pre-emption abandons his right.

In Hanafi fiqh, pre-emption (shuf'a) is a right that must be asserted promptly. This lesson examines a specific scenario: when the seller's agent is himself a pre-emptor, his act of selling without expressing his desire to buy nullifies his shuf'a. The agent loses his right of shuf'a. He cannot later claim the property.

The phrase 'la yughayyiruha' (does not change it) means the pre-emptor did nothing to assert his right. Inaction equals forfeiture.
The Hanafi school uses this to require active demand, not mere presence.

Core Idea
Shuf'a arises the moment the seller's ownership ends, even if the buyer's option delays full transfer.

The answer depends on whose option is at play. This lesson explores a critical distinction: the buyer's option does not suspend the seller's transfer of ownership, so shuf'a is established immediately upon contract.

Guided Path

The key is whose ownership is transferred.

The condition for shuf'a is that the property leaves the seller's ownership. Since the seller has no option and the contract is binding on him, ownership exits his domain immediately, even if the buyer's ownership is not yet fully vested due to the option.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on the nature of ownership transfer.

Some schools or scholars may argue that shuf'a should wait until the option resolves. The Hanafi view is immediate because the seller's side is complete.

The option does not affect C's right.

A corrupt sale does not transfer ownership; thus no shuf'a.

In Hanafi fiqh, fasid is distinct from batil.

Shuf'a is a right attached to valid ownership. Since a fasid sale does not effect a valid transfer, the buyer does not acquire the ownership required to have shuf'a.

Rescission of a Corrupt Sale (Faskh al-Bay' al-Fasid)
Ruling on rescission rights in a corrupt sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that both parties in a corrupt sale have the right to rescind and apply it to a given scenario.

Core Idea
In a corrupt sale, both seller and buyer may rescind – the contract is not binding.

This brief passage from a Hanafi fiqh text establishes a key rule: when a sale is corrupt (fasid), it does not become binding. As a result, both the seller and the buyer have the equal right to annul (faskh) the contract. This mutual right of rescission ensures that neither party is forced into an invalid transaction. In this lesson, we will unpack how this ruling works and why it matters in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the ruling
The passage deals with a 'corrupt sale' (bay' fasid). Recognize that this is a sale that is legally defective but not void (batil). Examples include a sale with unknown price or delivery terms.
Fasid sales are those that have a defect in one of the conditions, but the contract still has some legal effect until rescinded.
2
State the legal consequence
The ruling says that each of the seller and buyer has the right to rescind (faskh). This means either party can unilaterally cancel the contract.
In Hanafi fiqh, faskh annuls the contract and restores property to its original owner.
3
Recognize the legal effect of faskh
When one party rescinds, the contract is undone. The buyer must return the item, and the seller must return the price. If the item has been lost or consumed, the buyer must pay the market value (often based on the price actually paid).
This differs from a valid sale, where rescission usually requires mutual agreement or a specific option (khiyar).
4
Contrast with a valid sale
In a valid (sahih) sale, the contract is binding and cannot be unilaterally rescinded. The ruling here highlights that a fasid sale is inherently unstable, giving both parties an escape.
This principle is rooted in the maxim: 'What is built on corruption is itself corrupt.' (ma buniya 'ala fasid fa huwa fasid).
5
Apply to a simple scenario
Imagine A sells his car to B but fails to specify the price (making it fasid). Both A and B have the right to cancel. If B wants to back out, he can do so without needing A's permission.
The right is mutual and independent; one party's rescission does not require the other's consent.

Division of Pre-emption Among Multiple Co-sharers: By Heads or by Shares?
Ruling on division of pre-emption shares among multiple co-sharers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on dividing pre-emption among multiple pre-emptors, contrast it with the Shafi'i position, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Pre-emption shares are divided by number of heads, not by property shares — equality prevents harm.

When multiple neighbors or co-owners have the right of pre-emption (shuf'a), the question arises: how should the right be divided? Should it go by the size of each person's property, or should they all get an equal share? The Hanafi school, based on athar from al-Sha'bi and Ibrahim al-Nakha'i, rules that pre-emption is divided equally by number of pre-emptors. Imam al-Shafi'i, relying on an athar from 'Ata, holds that it should be divided proportionally to shares. This lesson examines both positions and the practical consequence of the difference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that pre-emption among multiple eligible pre-emptors is divided equally by number of persons, regardless of the size of their ownership or adjacency.
This applies whether the pre-emptors are co-owners or neighbors by adjacency.
2
Identify the opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that pre-emption is divided according to the share each pre-emptor owns (i.e., the size of their property).
Opposing school based on athar from 'Ata.
3
Examine the proofs
The Hanafi proof: athar from al-Sha'bi and Ibrahim saying 'al-shuf'atu 'ala ru'usi al-rijal' and the hadith 'La darar wa la dirar.' The Shafi'i proof: athar from 'Ata saying 'al-shuf'atu bi al-hisas.'
Both proofs are from the Salaf, but the Hanafi choose the one preventing harm.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Hanafi, each of four pre-emptors gets 1/4 of the sold property; under Shafi'i, they get in proportion to their shares (e.g., 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/8).
This affects the distribution of both the land and the price.

Pre-emption with Measured or Weighed Items: Paying the Like (Mithl)
Ruling on pre-emption with makil/mawzun price.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for pre-emption when the sale price is a measured or weighed item and apply the distinction between mithli and qimi items.

Core Idea
For measured or weighed price items, the pre-emptor pays an identical amount — not the market value.

In pre-emption (shuf'a), when the buyer purchased the property using a measured or weighed item (e.g., wheat, dates), the pre-emptor does not pay the market value but rather the exact same quantity of that item. This lesson explains the rule and the underlying rationale: these items are mithliyat (fungibles) and have standard equivalents, so paying the like is just.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is pre-emption: taking the property from the buyer. The ruling is that the pre-emptor must pay the like (mithl) of the price items.
The matn says: 'اخذها بمثله' (he takes it for its like).
2
Locate the condition
The condition is that the sale price must be a measured (makil) or weighed (mawzun) item. Examples include wheat (makil) and dates (mawzun).
These are classified as mithliyat in Hanafi fiqh.
3
Understand the rationale (talil)
The reason is that makil and mawzun are mithliyat — they have standard equivalents. Therefore, paying the like is feasible and fair. In contrast, non-fungible goods (qimiyat) require payment of value.
This talil distinguishes the ruling from the previous case of goods ('arad).
4
Contrast with qimiyat
If the price were goods (e.g., one cow), the pre-emptor would pay the market value (qimah), not a similar cow, because cows are qimiyat and differ individually.
This contrast helps solidify the boundary.

Waiver of Pre-emption Based on Mistaken Price Information
Ruling with proof using talil (causation).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a pre-emption waiver when the pre-emptor acts on incorrect price information, and identify the condition that distinguishes a binding waiver from a non-binding one.

Core Idea
A pre-emption waiver based on mistaken price information is invalid; the right revives when the truth is known.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in the law of pre-emption (shuf'a). The pre-emptor is told a property was sold for a high price, so he waives his right. Later he discovers the actual price was lower or the payment was in a form easier for him. Does his waiver stand? The Hanafi answer is clear: the waiver is invalid because it was based on false information. The underlying principle is that a genuine abandonment (i'rad) requires full knowledge and free choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its initial ruling
The act is the pre-emptor's waiver of his right (taslim al-shuf'a). Initially, based on the reported price of one thousand, he waives. Note the reason: the high price.
The waiver is a verbal or physical indication of giving up the right.
2
Consider the new information
Later he learns the true price is less than one thousand, or the property was sold for wheat or barley whose value is equal or more. This new information contradicts the basis of his waiver.
3
Apply the legal reasoning
The Hanafi rule: a waiver based on a mistaken assumption is not a valid abandonment (i'rad). The pre-emptor's refusal was due to the high price, not a deliberate relinquishment of the right itself.
I'rad requires knowing the true facts and still abandoning the right.
4
Derive the ruling
Therefore the first waiver is batil (invalid) and the pre-emptor retains his right. He may claim pre-emption upon learning the actual situation.
This ruling applies to all similar cases of mistaken information.

Pre-emptor's Option When Buyer Demolishes Building
Ruling on pre-emptor's choice after demolition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the pre-emptor's choice when the buyer demolishes the building and apply it to a similar scenario.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor may take only the land at its current value, not the demolished building.

This passage from the chapter of pre-emption (shuf'ah) addresses a common complication: what happens if the buyer destroys the building on the purchased land before the pre-emptor exercises his right? The Hanafi ruling provides a clear choice, limiting the pre-emptor's right to the land alone. Understanding this ruling prevents the mistaken belief that the pre-emptor can claim the demolished structure or its materials.

Guided Path
1
Identify the triggering action
The buyer demolishes the building he purchased along with the land. This action changes the property's composition.
2
Recognize the legal effect
By demolishing the building, the buyer turns it from a dependent (tabi') into an independent asset (asl). The building's value is now separate from the land.
The pre-emptor's right attaches only to the land as originally sold.
3
Determine the pre-emptor's right
The pre-emptor may not demand the building materials or the land at the original price. His right is limited to the land alone.
4
State the two options
The pre-emptor may either take the land by paying its fair portion of the purchase price (the land's value after demolition) or decline entirely.
He cannot take only part of the land; it's all or nothing.
5
Understand the underlying principle
The buyer's intentional destruction of the building does not entitle the pre-emptor to a discount on the land. The pre-emptor's option is preserved but limited to what remains of the original sale.
This prevents injustice: the buyer does not gain by demolishing, and the pre-emptor is not forced to pay for a building that no longer exists.

Pre-emptor Cannot Take Demolished Materials
Ruling on pre-emptor's claim to rubble after demolition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that rubble from a demolished building is not included in shuf'ah and identify the condition (annexation) that excludes it.

Core Idea
Rubble is not part of the pre-empted property because it is no longer attached to the land.

When a buyer demolishes a building before the pre-emptor exercises his right, the resulting bricks and wood become independent chattels. This short passage from the matn makes clear that the pre-emptor has no claim to these materials under shuf'ah. Let's examine the rationale and the scope of the pre-emptor's right.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
The passage states 'it is not for him to take the rubble'. What is the act being ruled upon? It is the pre-emptor's claim to the detached materials after demolition.
2
Identify the Hukm Level
The claim is invalid (batil) because it is not part of the pre-empted property. The right of shuf'ah does not extend to items that are no longer annexed.
In Hanafi fiqh, batil means the claim has no legal effect.
3
Trace the Rationale
The key concept is 'annexation'. Shuf'ah right attaches to the land and everything permanently connected to it. Demolition severs that connection, so the rubble becomes an independent chattel. The pre-emptor cannot claim it under shuf'ah; he must negotiate separately.
This is derived from the broader Hanafi principle that shuf'ah applies only to real estate (aqar) and its fixtures.
4
Identify the Condition
A condition for an item to be included in shuf'ah is that it is attached to the land at the time the pre-emptor's right vests. Demolition before that time removes the item from the scope of shuf'ah.
If the buyer demolishes after the pre-emptor has taken possession, the materials would belong to the pre-emptor, but that scenario is not discussed here.

Pre-emptor Takes Fruit with Land When Included in Purchase
Ruling on inclusion of fruit in pre-emption

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that fruit on trees is included in pre-emption when sold with the land, and explain the principle of tabi' (dependency).

Core Idea
Fruit on trees is a dependent of the land; it passes to the pre-emptor automatically.

In Hanafi pre-emption law, the right of the pre-emptor (shafi') extends to everything that is considered a part of the land or a dependent (tabi') of it. This lesson examines a clear application: when a buyer purchases land with fruit on its trees, the pre-emptor takes the fruit along with the land at the same price. Understanding this ruling helps us see how the school treats attachments and dependencies in property transfers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Transaction
Consider the scenario: a person buys land that has palm trees bearing fruit. The fruit is present at the time of sale and is included in the purchase price.
The fruit must be sold together with the land; if sold separately, different rules apply.
2
Recognize the Pre-emptor's Entitlement
The pre-emptor has the right to step into the buyer's shoes and take the property. This right extends to all that is integral or dependent on the land.
The ruling does not require the pre-emptor to pay extra for the fruit.
3
Apply the Principle of Tabi' (Dependency)
In Hanafi fiqh, anything that is physically attached to the land and is treated as subordinate (tabi') is included in pre-emption. Fruit on trees is considered a dependent because it cannot be severed without affecting the land's value.
This principle is derived from the general rule that 'the follower follows the principal' (al-tabi' tabi').
4
Note the Practical Impact
The ruling ensures that the pre-emptor obtains the complete benefit of the property as it was sold, without being forced to deal with separate ownership of the fruit.
If the fruit were excluded, the pre-emptor might have to negotiate separately for it, undermining the purpose of pre-emption.
