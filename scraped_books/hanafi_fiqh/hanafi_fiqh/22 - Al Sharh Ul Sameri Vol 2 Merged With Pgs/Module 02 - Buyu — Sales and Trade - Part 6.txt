Core Idea
Salam in garments is valid only if length, width, and type are specified.

2

Type refers to the quality or weave, not just colour.
3
Examine the proof
The statement of 'Amir (a tabi'i) is cited: 'When one contracts salam in a garment whose yardage and type are known, there is no harm.' This indicates permissibility.
This is a qawl sahabi, which is a source of evidence in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Understand the legal basis
The specifications make the subject matter known, eliminating major uncertainty (gharar fahish), which is a condition for valid salam.
Without these, the contract would be fasid (voidable) due to ambiguity.

Prohibition of Disposal Before Possession in Salam
Ruling with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on disposing of the capital or subject matter in salam before possession, and explain the dalil from hadith and qiyas.

Core Idea
Both capital and subject matter in salam are prohibited from disposal before possession.

This lesson examines a key rule in salam (forward sale) contracts: neither the capital paid nor the goods to be delivered may be sold or gifted before physical possession is taken. The ruling is derived from the general prohibition on selling before possession, extended by analogy (qiyas). Understanding this prohibition prevents invalid transactions and ensures the integrity of salam contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is disposing (sale, gift, etc.) of the capital (ra's al-mal) or subject matter (muslam fih) before taking possession. The ruling is haram (prohibited).
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Ibn Abbas: the Prophet (PBUH) forbade selling food before taking possession, and Ibn Abbas extended this to all goods.
Hadith collected by Bukhari, Muslim, and Abu Dawood.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The prohibition on selling before possession is general. In salam, the capital (price) is analogous to the sold item (mabi') in a regular sale, so the same prohibition applies. The subject matter is itself the sold item, so it is directly covered.
The qiyas is that both are 'sold items' before possession.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is no significant disagreement among the madhabs on this principle; it is muttafaq alayh.
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view.

Prohibition of Partnership and Assignment Before Possession
Ruling on prohibiting partnership and assignment in muslam fih before possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on partnership and assignment in salam before possession and explain the qiyas evidence for the prohibition.

Core Idea
Disposal of muslam fih before possession is prohibited in all forms, including partnership and assignment.

This lesson extends the general prohibition of selling before possession to two specific derivative transactions: partnership (shirkah) and assignment (tawliyah). The salam contract involves prepayment for future delivery; until the buyer takes possession, the muslam fih (the commodity) cannot be disposed of in any manner. Understanding this rule prevents individuals from inadvertently entering invalid contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
The text explicitly prohibits two transactions: shirkah (selling a share) and tawliyah (selling at cost) in the muslam fih before possession. Note that this is not an exhaustive list; it includes any disposal.
The prohibition applies regardless of the sale price or the portion sold.
2
Recall the general principle
Recall the earlier principle: 'disposal of the sold item before possession is not permissible.' This is based on a hadith and forms the basis of the qiyas here.
The general principle is derived from the prohibition of selling food before taking possession.
3
Classify partnership and assignment as disposals
Both shirkah and tawliyah are forms of tasarruf (disposal) because they transfer ownership rights, similar to an outright sale. They are not distinct from sale; they are subtypes.
Tawliyah is a sale at cost; shirkah is a sale of a share. Both involve transfer of ownership.
4
Apply qiyas to extend the prohibition
Since the general rule prohibits all disposals before possession, and these are disposals, they are prohibited by qiyas. The illa (effective cause) is the risk of not being able to deliver the muslam fih.
The qiyas is from the original prohibition of sale; the illa is the disposal itself.
5
Note the cross-madhhab perspective
While the Hanafi school prohibits absolutely, some schools may permit such transactions if they are conditional on possession. However, the Hanafi position is mufta bihi for its followers.
This difference stems from varying interpretations of the hadith and the scope of qiyas.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Salam in jewels and gemstones is invalid because their attributes cannot be precisely standardized.

This lesson addresses a specific condition for the validity of salam (advance payment sale): the subject matter must be precisely describable. Jewels and gemstones vary greatly and are sold by the piece, making them unsuitable for salam. We will unpack the ruling and the underlying reasoning.

The commentary further explains that these items vary greatly and are sold by count, not weight.
2
Extract the Legal Reason (Illa)
The illa given is the inability to standardize attributes. Because jewels and gemstones differ in size, quality, and value per piece, they cannot be described with sufficient precision to avoid uncertainty (gharar).
In Hanafi fiqh, one condition for salam is that the subject matter must be precisely definable in type, quality, and quantity.

The commentary states a general rule: 'Things whose attributes cannot be standardized, salam in them is not permissible.' This principle applies to any good that cannot be exactly described.
Weight-based goods like wheat or gold are usually standardizable; piece-based goods like jewels are not.
4
Apply to Similar Items
Consider other items sold by piece, such as antique coins, custom jewelry, or handcrafted goods. Use the same illa to test whether salam would be valid according to the Hanafi school.
The key is whether the attributes can be fully specified to eliminate gharar.

Selling Silkworms and Bees Alone: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sale of silkworms and bees alone

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi positions on selling silkworms and bees alone, explain the reasoning of each imam, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Silkworms and bees are profitable but only a minority Hanafi view permits their sale alone.

This passage presents a dispute within the Hanafi school (and between Hanafis and Shafi'is) regarding the permissibility of selling silkworms and bees individually. The core issue is whether these insects are considered wealth (mal) in themselves. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Hanafi school determines what constitutes a valid object of sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What is being sold? Silkworms (dud al-qazz) and bees (nahal) alone, without their hives or trees.
The object of sale is the insect itself, not its produce.
2
State the conflicting positions
Imam Muhammad (a Hanafi) and Imam Shafi'i: sale is permissible. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: sale is not permissible (the mufta bihi).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, with the majority view opposing Imam Muhammad.
3
Analyze the reasoning
For permissibility: insects are profitable (mushaffa) and produce valuable goods (silk, honey), so they are considered wealth. Against: the insects themselves are not independently regarded as wealth; only their produce is.
The difference hinges on whether utility alone confers the status of mal.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
The Hanafi school relies on the view of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: selling silkworms or bees alone is invalid (batil).
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.
5
Note the practical consequence
Under the mufta bihi, silkworms and bees can only be sold as part of a larger transaction (e.g., with their hive or mulberry tree).
Selling the produce (silk, honey) alone is valid because it is clearly wealth.

Currency Exchange (Sarf): Definition and Conditions
Defines sarf and states ruling with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of sarf, identify its two conditions (equality and hand-to-hand), and explain why a weight difference constitutes riba.

Core Idea
Gold for gold must be equal in weight and immediate; any excess is riba.

Currency exchange (sarf) is a special sale requiring strict equality when trading same metals. The Hanafi school bases this on a prophetic hadith that warns against any excess, even if the quality differs. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Define sarf
Identify the legal definition of sarf as a sale where both countervalues are from the category of gold or silver (athman). Note that it applies to any form: coins, jewelry, or mixed alloys.
The term 'athman' is plural of thaman, meaning 'price' but refers to gold and silver as primary currencies.
2
State the ruling for same-species exchange
When selling gold for gold or silver for silver, the exchange must be equal in weight (mithlan bi mithl) and immediate (yadan bi yad). Any excess – whether in weight, number, or value – is riba and invalidates the transaction.
Quality differences (purity, craftsmanship) do not permit inequality in weight.
3
Identify the hadith proof
The hadith from Abu Sa'id al-Khudri lists six commodities (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) and commands like-for-like and hand-to-hand. The final phrase declares any increase as riba.
This hadith is the primary evidence for both conditions.
4
Analyze the ruling's rationale
The prohibition of excess prevents riba, which is exploitative gain. The hand-to-hand condition avoids credit-based trade that could lead to usury.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof defining the chapter of Bay' al-Sarf (currency exchange) from Kitab al-Buyu'. The author first defines Sarf: it is a sale where both exchange items are from the class of thaman (gold or silver). Then he states the ruling: gold for gold or silver for silver is only permissible if they are exactly equal in weight, even if one is superior in purity or craftsmanship. Any excess, whether in weight or value, constitutes riba (usury). The proof is the famous hadith from Abu Sa'id al-Khudri (radiyallahu 'anhu) where the Prophet (sallallahu 'alayhi wasallam) commands that gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, and salt be exchanged like for like and hand-to-hand. He warns that any increase is riba. Thus, for same-currency exchange, two conditions must be met: equality in weight and immediate possession (hand-to-hand). The commentary also clarifies that these rules apply to pure or mixed gold/silver, jewelry, coins, etc., but if impurity dominates, the precious metal's weight is calculated separately. The term 'athman (plural of thaman) refers to gold and silver as standard currencies.

Key Terms
الصرف (al-sarf): A sale where each of the two countervalues is from the category of gold or silver (athman).
الاثمان (al-athman): Plural of thaman; gold and silver used as currency.
مثلا بمثل (mithlan bi mithl): Equal for equal, referring to weight in sarf.
يدا بيد (yadan bi yad): Hand to hand, meaning immediate possession at the time of contract.

Condition of Hand-to-Hand Delivery in Sarf
States the requirement of qabd before separation and provides additional hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule requiring hand-to-hand possession (qabd) before separation in sarf contracts, and explain how each of the two hadith proofs supports this condition.

Core Idea
In sarf, exchanging both countervalues hand-to-hand before parting is a condition of validity; delay or excess invalidates.

This lesson focuses on a critical condition for the validity of a sarf (currency exchange) contract: hand-to-hand delivery of both countervalues before the parties separate. The author presents two hadith proofs that establish this rule, which distinguishes sarf from other sales and prevents riba. Understanding this condition is essential for anyone dealing with gold, silver, or other ribawi items.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the exact condition required by the text: 'لا بد من قبض العوضين قبل الافتراق' (it is necessary to take possession of the two countervalues before separation). Recognize that this applies to all sales of gold and silver within the same genus or across genera.
The ruling applies to both same-genus exchanges (gold for gold) and different-genus exchanges (gold for silver).
2
Understand the rationale
Gold and silver are ribawi items. Exchanging them on credit or with excess leads to riba al-nasi'a and riba al-fadl. The condition of immediate hand-to-hand delivery eliminates both forms of riba.
The rationale ties the ruling to the prohibition of riba.
3
Analyze the first hadith (dates)
Read the hadith of Abu Huraira about the Khaybar dates. It prohibits unequal exchange of dates (same genus) and provides the lawful alternative: sell for a different genus (dirhams) then buy. Apply this analogy to gold and silver.
This hadith addresses riba al-fadl and shows the permissibility of using a medium (dirhams) to achieve excess lawfully.
4
Analyze the second hadith (Bara' and Zayd)
This hadith explicitly prohibits selling gold for silver on credit (nasi'a). It directly supports the condition of hand-to-hand delivery, as credit implies parting before possession.
This hadith addresses riba al-nasi'a and reinforces the need for immediate exchange.
5
Apply the condition
Consider a scenario: if parties separate without both taking possession, the contract becomes fasid. If only one countervalue is handed over, separation invalidates. The only way to validly exchange with excess is to use a different genus as an intermediate step.
Practical consequence: sarf contracts must be concluded in the same sitting with immediate exchange.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the third ruling of the chapter of sales regarding the exchange of ribawi items, specifically sarf (exchange of gold and silver). It begins with the base text: 'And it is necessary to take possession of both countervalues before separation.' The commentator then explains the underlying principle: when selling a ribawi item for the same genus (e.g., silver for silver, gold for gold), equality in weight is required regardless of quality or craftsmanship. Any excess is forbidden. To lawfully obtain a better quality, one must sell the inferior item for cash (e.g., dirhams), then use that cash to purchase the superior item. He then cites the hadith of Abu Huraira regarding dates, which establishes that quality is irrelevant in same-genus ribawi transactions; the rule is equality of quantity. The same applies to gold and silver. The commentator then returns to the ruling on possession: since gold and silver serve as prices (thaman), they must be taken possession of before the parties separate. He cites a second hadith: Bara' and Zayd narrated that the Prophet forbade selling gold for silver on credit, meaning the transaction must be hand-to-hand. Thus, the condition of possession before separation is established by these prophetic traditions.

Key Terms
صرف (sarf): The exchange of gold for silver or silver for gold, or similar ribawi items of different genera.
قبض (qabd): Taking physical possession of the exchanged items in the same session.
تفاضل (tafadul): Excess in quantity or value when exchanging ribawi items of the same genus.

Selling Gold for Silver: Excess Permissible but Hand-to-Hand Required
Ruling on exchanging gold for silver with difference in quantity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on exchanging gold for silver with excess quantity and explain the condition of hand-to-hand possession based on the hadith of Abu Bakra.

Core Idea
Excess in gold-silver exchange is allowed, but spot possession is a must to avoid riba.

This passage presents a key rule in sarf (currency exchange). The matn declares that when selling gold for silver, excess in quantity is permissible, but mutual hand-to-hand possession (taqabud) is obligatory. The commentator elaborates why this is so, citing a famous hadith from Abu Bakra. Understanding this ruling helps distinguish between riba al-fadl (excess in same genus) and riba al-nasi'ah (credit), and their respective prohibitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the transaction and what is the legal classification? Note the two parts: excess allowed, possession required.
The act: a sale of gold for silver. Ruling: sahih if conditions met.
2
Locate the dalil
Find the hadith cited and note its source (Bukhari and Muslim). Recognize it as a textual proof (dalil naqli).
Hadith from Abu Bakra: prohibition of same-genus exchange except equal; permission for different genera.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the ruling? The phrase 'buy as we wish' indicates permissibility of excess; the prohibition for same genus implies the condition of equality is removed for different genera.
The command 'how we wish' (كيف شئنا) is key: it is general and unrestricted.
4
Analyse the condition of taqabud
Why is hand-to-hand possession obligatory? Because both gold and silver are weighable (mawzunat), and riba al-nasi'ah applies. Without immediate possession, it becomes a sale of debt for debt (kali bi kali), which is forbidden.
The commentator stresses that failure to possess before separation invalidates the contract.
5
Determine the consequence
What happens if parties separate without possession? The contract is batil (void) because it falls under the prohibition of al-kali bi al-kali.
Also, the price (thaman) is not considered specified until possession is taken.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth ruling on sarf: selling gold for silver. The matn states that excess (tafadul) is allowed, but hand-to-hand possession (taqabud) is obligatory. The commentator explains that since gold and silver are of different genera, the prohibition of riba al-fadl does not apply; therefore, one can give more gold for less silver, etc. However, since both are weighable substances (mawzunat), the prohibition of riba al-nasi'ah (credit) applies, so the exchange must be spot (hand-to-hand). He supports this with the hadith of Abu Bakra, which explicitly forbids same-genus exchanges except equal, but permits different-genus exchanges as one wishes. The commentator also notes that prices (thaman) are not considered specified until possession is taken, so failure to take possession before separation renders the contract invalid due to the prohibition of al-kali bi al-kali (debt for debt).

Invalidity of Sarf if Separation Before Possession
Ruling that splitting before possession voids the sarf contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on separation before possession in sarf and explain the legal consequence (batil vs. new contract via ta'ati).

Core Idea
Separation before possession in sarf voids the contract; later possession creates a new contract, not a cure.

The sarf transaction requires immediate possession of both countervalues before the parties separate. Failure to do so renders the contract void (batil) from the start. However, if they later take possession, it is not a correction but a new contract via ta'ati (mutual delivery). This distinction is crucial for understanding the strict requirements of sarf in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a sarf (exchange of gold/silver). The ruling: if the parties separate before taking possession of both countervalues (or one), the contract becomes void (batil).
Batil means a legal nullity; it cannot be later ratified.
2
Identify the condition
The condition for validity is possession (qabd) of both countervalues before separation. This is based on the prophetic command.
Possession can be actual or constructive (e.g., weighing), but must occur in the same sitting.
3
Analyze the exception
If possession occurs after separation, it does not cure the original contract. Instead, a new contract is considered to have taken place through mutual delivery (ta'ati).
Ta'ati is a valid mode of sale in the Hanafi school.
4
Identify the proof
The proof is the hadith narrated by Abu Bakra: the Prophet forbade silver for silver and gold for gold except equal, and permitted gold for silver as desired. The command to take possession before separation is derived from the requirement of hand-to-hand exchange.
The hadith is cited in the footnote of the text.

Selling Gold for Silver: Disparity Allowed, Possession Required
Ruling on sarf: disparity allowed, hand-to-hand possession required

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on selling gold for silver, explain the conditions of disparity and possession, and cite the hadith proof.

Core Idea
In selling gold for silver, disparity is permissible but hand-to-hand possession in the session is obligatory.

This lesson covers the Hanafi ruling on the exchange of gold and silver (ṣarf). You will learn why disparity in amount is allowed despite both being ribawi items, and why immediate possession is required. The ruling is supported by a hadith from Abu Bakra (ra). Understanding this principle is essential for modern currency exchange, where the same logic applies.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the act and its ruling
The source passage treats the sale of gold for silver. State the overall ruling: this is a valid contract (ṣaḥīḥ) provided two conditions are met.
The term for this exchange is 'ṣarf'.
2
2. Understand the two conditions: disparity allowed, possession required
Disparity (tafāḍul) in amount is permissible because gold and silver are different species (jins). However, both are weighable, so immediate hand-to-hand possession (taqābuḍ) in the session is wajib to avoid ribā al-nasī'ah (deferred ribā).
If possession is not taken, the contract becomes fasid.
3
3. Examine the hadith proof
The hadith from Abu Bakra: the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) forbade silver for silver and gold for gold except equal for equal, but commanded us to buy gold for silver as we wish ('kayfa shi'nā'). The command indicates permissibility of disparity, and the phrase 'yadan bi yad' in Muslim's version establishes the possession condition.
The imperative 'amaranā' (He commanded us) in Hanafi uṣūl indicates ibāḥah (permissibility) unless there is a qarīna (indication) of wujūb.
4
4. Apply the ruling: consequences of non-compliance
If the parties separate without possession, the contract becomes 'bay' al-kālī bi al-kālī' (credit against credit), which is prohibited. The commentary emphasizes that prices (athmān) are not specified by mere determination (ta'yīn) but require possession.
This is a key Hanafi principle: 'al-athmān lā tata'ayyanu bi al-ta'yīn'.
5
5. Note cross-madhhab status
Both Hanafis and Shāfi'īs require hand-to-hand possession and allow disparity. However, Shāfi'īs may not require specification of the price before possession, leading to a minor practical difference.
This ikhtilāf is legitimate; both schools rely on sound evidence.

Separation Before Possession Invalidates Sarf Contract
Ruling on effect of separation without possession in sarf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when parties separate before full possession in a sarf (currency exchange) contract and explain why the contract becomes void.

Core Idea
In sarf, if parties separate before both counter-values are possessed, the contract is void ab initio.

Exchange of gold, silver, or currency requires hand-to-hand possession in the same sitting. If the parties separate even partially before possessing both items, the contract is void. This ruling is based on a Prophetic prohibition of deferred exchange in ribawi items. The lesson explores the consequence and the exceptional case where a new contract can arise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a sarf contract (exchange of gold/silver/currency). The ruling given is that if the parties separate before both or even one of the counter-values is possessed, the contract becomes void (batil).

2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the hadith of Abu Bakra: the Prophet forbade selling silver for silver and gold for gold except equal for equal, and commanded exchange to be hand-to-hand. This implies the necessity of possession before separation.
The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command (amr) to exchange hand-to-hand indicates obligation (wujub) in Hanafi usul. Failure to possess before separation violates this command, rendering the contract void.
The hadith is cited as evidence that possession is a condition for the validity of the contract.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) consider the contract fasid (irregular) rather than batil, meaning it can be corrected by subsequent possession. This difference affects the legal effect of partial possession.
The Hanafi position is that the contract is void ab initio.
5

If the parties later take possession with mutual consent, it is treated as a new contract (bay' ta'ati), not a rectification of the original void contract.
This shows that the nullification is not irreversible, but a fresh transaction is needed.

Disposal of Price in Sarf Before Possession Not Allowed
Ruling on using the price in sarf before taking possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on disposing of the price in sarf before possession, identify the dalil from the hadith, and explain Imam Zufar's opposing view.

Core Idea
You cannot use the price in a currency exchange before taking possession—selling what you don't yet have is haram.

In a sarf (currency exchange) transaction, both items of exchange are considered prices (athman). This lesson examines a key prohibition: you may not dispose of the price you are supposed to receive before actually taking possession of it. The ruling is rooted in a Prophetic hadith and has an intra-Hanafi difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is disposing (tasarrut) of the price (thaman) in a sarf contract before physical possession. The ruling is that it is not permissible (haram). Understand that the price here is the gold or silver given in exchange.
Sarf: exchange of gold for silver or vice versa, or money changing.
2
Locate the dalil
The dalil is the hadith of Ibn Abbas: 'Whoever buys food, let him not sell it until he takes possession of it.' This is a hadith ahad, but accepted by the school.
Reference: Muslim, Bukhari, Abu Dawud.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith explicitly forbids selling food before possession. By qiyas, the effective cause ('illa) is the risk of loss and uncertainty, which applies to all property, including the price in sarf. Thus, the same prohibition applies to the price.
Both items in sarf are treated as sold goods (mabi') regarding possession.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam Zufar holds that prices are not specified by determination (ta'yin). Therefore, if one cannot take possession, they can substitute other coins from their own side, making the disposal permissible.
This is an intra-Hanafi disagreement.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is the majority view: disposal before possession is not allowed. The matn explicitly prohibits it, and the hadith's general applicability is upheld.
Imam Zufar's view is considered a weaker (marjuh) opinion.

The practical difference is whether the sale is unwound or completed with a revised price.

The commentary explains that according to Abu Hanifa, since the thaman became non-existent in its legal capacity before qabd, the sale is like a sale without a price, and the seller retains the commodity. The other two imams consider the contract valid with a due price.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If the price loses its currency status before possession, the contract collapses for lack of a valid price.

In this passage, we encounter a specific application of the principle that a sale's price must be valid and deliverable. Imam Abu Hanifa rules that if copper coins (fulus) that are current at the time of contract become demonetized before the buyer takes possession, the sale becomes void (bāṭil). This ruling extends the earlier discussion on debased dirhams and highlights the critical role of possession in solidifying the transaction when the price is a currency item.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a sale (bayʿ) transacted with current fulus. The ruling according to Abu Hanifa is that the sale becomes void (bāṭil) if the fulus are demonetized before possession.
The ruling is specific to fulus, not dirhams or dinars.
2
Isolate the condition of the price
At the time of the contract, the fulus must be current (nāfiqa) — they serve as legal currency by custom. This is an essential condition for the price to be valid thaman.
If the fulus were already non-current at contracting, the sale is void from the start.
3
Identify the event that triggers voidness
The fulus become non-current (kasadat) before the buyer takes possession (qabḍ) of the purchased goods. The timing is critical: before possession, not after.
If possession had already occurred, the ruling would differ.
4
Apply the consequence
Since the price loses its currency status and possession is not yet taken, the contract lacks a valid price. Hence, the sale becomes void (bāṭil).
This follows the principle that the price must be specified and deliverable at the time of possession.
5
Infer the underlying reasoning
Fulus derive their value as money from custom and legal circulation. Demonetization removes that status, reducing them to mere commodities. Without a valid price, the contract cannot stand.
This reasoning parallels the earlier rule for debased dirhams.

Selling with Current Fulus: Specification Not Required
Ruling on permissibility of sale with current fulus without specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on selling with current fulus and explain why specification is not required.

Core Idea
Current fulus are like gold and silver: they need not be specified in a sale.

This lesson covers a key principle in Hanafi sales: the treatment of fulus (base-metal coins) as currency. As long as fulus are in circulation, they function as thaman and do not require specification. Understanding this distinction prevents invalidity in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the ruling
The passage states that selling with current fulus is permissible even without specifying the exact coins. This is the basic ruling.
2
Compare to dirhams and dinars
Fulus in circulation are treated like gold and silver coins (dirhams and dinars) as thaman (price). Just as those coins do not need specification, current fulus also do not.
3
Identify the turning point
When fulus cease to circulate, their legal status changes. They become like commodities (متاع) and then require specification.
4
Apply the principle
In practice, if a sale is made with current fulus, any coins of equivalent value suffice for payment. No need to designate the exact coins at the time of contract.

Fulus Becoming Obsolete Before Possession: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on sale validity with obsolete price currency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the validity of a sale when fulus become obsolete before possession, explain their reasoning, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
When fulus lose currency before possession, the sale is void according to Abu Hanifa but valid according to Sahibayn—only the value must be paid.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about the effect of a change in the nature of the price after the contract but before delivery. The fulus (copper coins used as currency) become obsolete, raising the question: does the contract remain valid or does it become void? The answer differs between the Imams, leading to distinct practical outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
A buyer purchases goods for current fulus, but before taking possession (qabḍ), the fulus become obsolete (kasada). Determine the potential legal issues.
The key variable is the timing of possession relative to the obsolescence.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules the sale is void (bāṭil). Reason: once fulus become obsolete, they revert to being ordinary commodities, and the sold item is left without a valid price, leading to corruption (fasād) and voidness.
His view emphasizes the necessity of a valid price at the time of performance.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their sub-difference
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold the sale remains valid. The buyer must pay the value of the fulus at a specific date: Abu Yusuf says the value on the day of the contract; Muhammad says the value on the last day the fulus were current.
They consider the contract valid at inception; obsolescence merely converts the obligation from specific coins to value.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, both parties revert to pre-sale positions. Under the Sahibayn, the buyer pays the determined value and takes the goods. The difference also affects the quantum of payment if the value fluctuates.
This difference impacts legal strategy: a party facing obsolescence may prefer one ruling over the other.
