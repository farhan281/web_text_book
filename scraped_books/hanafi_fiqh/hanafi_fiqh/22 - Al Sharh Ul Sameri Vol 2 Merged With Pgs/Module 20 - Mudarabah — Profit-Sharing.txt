Definition of Mudarabah and Its Property Condition
Introduces mudarabah definition and property condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mudarabah and explain why the contract is only valid with tangible fungible property.

Core Idea
Mudarabah: profit partnership with capital from one and work from the other, requiring property valid for shirkah.

This lesson begins the chapter on Mudarabah (silent partnership). The author gives a concise definition and immediately links it to the conditions of a regular partnership. Understanding this definition and its property requirement is essential before exploring the detailed rules of mudarabah.

Guided Path
1
Dissect the definition
Identify the three pillars: contract (عقد), partnership in profit (الشركة في الربح), and the two contributions (مال from one, عمل from other).
The profit must be shared proportionally.
2
Identify the property condition
The text states mudarabah is only valid with property that makes shirkah valid. Recall that shirkah requires mal mutaqawwim (fungible, owned property).
Non-fungible assets like specific cars are not valid.
3
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith of Urwah shows the Prophet's approval of a profit-sharing arrangement: capital given, work done, profit shared. The prayer for blessing indicates its validity.
Though the hadith text has some corruption, the meaning is clear.
4
Examine the companion practice
The story of Umar's sons and Abu Musa shows early Muslim acceptance. This is an athar (practice) supporting permissibility.
Not a direct sunnah, but indicates ijma of the sahaba.

Definition and Evidence of Mudarabah
Defines mudarabah and provides proof from hadith and athar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of mudarabah and identify the hadith and athar that establish its permissibility in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Mudarabah: capital from one, labor from the other, profit shared—validated by the Prophet's approval.

This lesson opens the chapter on mudarabah, a foundational contract in Islamic finance. You will learn its precise definition and the primary textual evidence that the Hanafi school uses to justify it. The passage provides a clear definition followed by a hadith and an athar (Companion practice) that together establish the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contract and Its Ruling
Read the opening definition: mudarabah is a contract (عقد) of partnership in profit with capital from one partner and labor from the other. The ruling is permissibility (ibaha).
The term is derived from 'darb fi al-ard' (traveling), because the mudarib typically travels for trade.
2
Locate the Primary Dalil (Hadith)
The passage cites the hadith of Urwah ibn al-Ja'd: the Prophet gave him a dinar to buy a sacrificial animal, but he traded and made a profit, and the Prophet prayed for blessing. This shows prophetic approval of the arrangement.
Hadith from Sunan Abi Dawud; it is ahad (single narration) but its meaning (dalala) is clear.
3
Understand the Wajh al-Dalalah (How the Proof Works)
The Prophet did not prohibit the extra trade; rather, he endorsed it by praying for blessing. This indicates that such profit-sharing is permissible. The hadith demonstrates the essential elements: capital (dinar) from the Prophet, labor from Urwah, and shared profit (one sheep and the dinar).
The athar of the sons of Umar further confirms that the Companions practiced mudarabah with the approval of the caliph.
4
Identify Implied Conditions
From the definition and evidences, deduce the necessary conditions: capital must be known and delivered, profit share must be a common percentage (e.g., half, third), and the mudarib must perform the work. Loss is borne by the capital provider unless caused by negligence.
These conditions are not exhaustive but are derived from the foundational structure.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on Mudarabah (a type of partnership known in the Hanafi school as qirad). The author first defines mudarabah as a contract (عقد) of partnership in profit, where one party provides capital (رأس المال) and the other provides labor (عمل). The term mudarabah is derived from ضرب فی الارض (traveling in the land), because the working partner (مضارب) typically travels for trade. The author then provides two proofs: (1) a hadith of 'Urwah ibn al-Ja'd where the Prophet (PBUH) gave him a dinar to buy a sacrificial animal, but he traded and made profit, and the Prophet prayed for blessing; this indicates permissibility. Key Terms

الربح (al-rubh): Profit, the surplus over the capital after the mudarib returns the principal. It is shared between the parties according to a specified percentage (e.g., half, third).

Capital in Mudarabah Must Be Currency
States the capital condition for mudarabah and provides evidence from early authorities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi requirement for mudarabah capital, distinguish between currency and goods, and apply the hila of prior sale correctly.

Core Idea
Mudarabah capital must be actual currency; goods are invalid unless first sold for cash.

In this lesson, we examine a key condition for the validity of mudarabah (silent partnership). The Hanafi school requires that the capital be in the form of gold, silver, or current coins — not commodities like cloth or food. This rule is derived from the practice of the early Muslims and is supported by athar from Ibrahim al-Nakha'i and Hammad. We will also explore the permissible workaround (hila) that allows goods to be converted into currency before the mudarabah begins.

Guided Path
1
Identify the capital requirement
State that mudarabah capital must be in naqd (currency: gold, silver, or current coins), not 'urud (goods). This is analogous to shirkah.
The same condition applies to partnership (shirkah) as mentioned in earlier sections.
2
Examine the hila (workaround)
If goods are given, the mudarib must first sell them as an agent. Only after the sale do the proceeds become the mudarabah capital. This preserves the requirement of currency.
The hila transforms the transaction into a permissible sequence: sale first, then mudarabah.
3
Analyse the first athar (Ibrahim & Sufyan)
Ibrahim al-Nakha'i disliked giving cloth as mudarabah, restricting it to gold and silver. Sufyan added that if goods are given, the mudarib deserves only an equivalent wage, not a share in profit.
This athar shows the disapproval of mudarabah with goods and the alternative compensation.
4
Analyse the second athar (Hammad)
Hammad ruled that if goods are sold at a price less than valuation, the actual sale price becomes the capital. This confirms that the capital must be in currency, and the value is determined by the market sale, not by prior estimation.
This athar is cited to support the hila: mudarabah begins after the goods are sold.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the second condition of mudarabah: the capital must be in gold (دینار), silver (درہم), or current coins (رائج سکوں), not in goods (سامان). This is because mudarabah is a contract based on trust and profit-sharing, and goods have uncertain value. The second athar from Hammad shows that if goods are sold at a price less than valuation, the capital is the actual sale price, not the valuation.

الرائج (al-ra'ij): Current coins that are accepted as legal tender in the market, such as dirhams and dinars.
العروض (al-urud): Goods or commodities other than gold, silver, and currency, such as cloth, foodstuffs, etc.

Profit in Mudarabah Must Be Shared Proportionally
Condition that profit must be a common share, not a fixed sum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that profit in mudarabah must be proportional (musha'a) and apply the ruling to distinguish valid profit-sharing from invalid fixed-sum stipulations.

Core Idea
Profit in mudarabah must be a proportional share, not a fixed amount, to avoid gharar.

This passage establishes a fundamental condition of mudarabah: the profit share must be a common fractional portion (musha'a). It explains why stipulating a fixed sum is invalid and supports this rule with analogies from leasing and sales. Understanding this condition is critical for structuring valid Islamic partnerships.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on profit-sharing in mudarabah
Identify that profit must be a proportional share (e.g., half, one-third) of the actual profit, not a fixed number of dirhams. If a fixed sum is stipulated, the contract becomes fasid (corrupt).
2
Examine the textual proofs
The author cites the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij that the Prophet forbade land leasing with an exclusion of a specific amount, and the athar of Qatada disapproving of selling fruit with a fixed quantity excepted. These show that fixing a specific amount leads to uncertainty and potential injustice.
3
Analyze the analogy (qiyas)
The prohibition of excluding a fixed amount in leasing and sales is extended to mudarabah because the underlying cause (illa) is the same: uncertainty (gharar) and potential unfairness. In mudarabah, a fixed profit for one party could exhaust the entire profit or leave the other with nothing.
4
Apply the ruling to practical scenarios
Given a mudarabah contract with a fixed profit stipulation, determine that the contract is fasid and must be rectified by converting to a proportional share. For a contract with a proportional share (e.g., 60% to investor, 40% to worker), confirm it is valid.
The rectification does not void the entire contract; the invalid stipulation is removed, and the default rule of sharing (often by agreement or as per custom) applies.

Prohibition on mudarib sub-contracting mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a mudarib may sub-contract mudarabah capital and explain why permission is required.

Core Idea
A mudarib is a trustee, not an owner—sub-contracting capital requires the owner's explicit or implicit permission.

This brief ruling from a Hanafi fiqh text highlights a critical limitation on the mudarib's authority. While he has wide discretion to trade, he cannot transfer the capital to another mudarabah unless the capital owner (rabb al-mal) expressly permits it or gives a general authorization like 'act on your opinion'. This rule ensures the owner's trust is not violated and that he retains control over who manages his property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the prohibited act
Recognize that the mudarib is prohibited from giving the capital to another person as a mudarabah contract. This is not a simple hiring of an employee but a full transfer of the capital to another mudarabah.
Distinguish this from hiring workers or agents—those are generally allowed.
2
Note the default ruling
The default is that the mudarib 'may not' (laysa lahu) do this. It is impermissible (haram) without exception.
The ruling is stated negatively, indicating a prohibition.
3
Identify the exception
The prohibition is lifted if the rabb al-mal gives explicit permission or a general instruction like 'act according to your opinion', which implies authority to delegate.
The permission can be explicit or implicit through words that grant broad discretion.
4
Understand the rationale
The mudarib is a trustee (amin), not the owner. He cannot transfer the entire trust to another without the owner's consent because the owner's choice of mudarib is specific.
This protects the owner's right to choose who manages his capital.

Core Idea
In absolute mudarabah, the mudarib may buy, sell, travel, and hire; but cannot delegate to another without the owner's permission.

This passage from the Sharh al-Sameri establishes the fundamental rules for an absolute mudarabah (unrestricted profit-sharing partnership). It clarifies that the capital must be exclusively in the mudarib's control, and then details the scope of the mudarib's authority. A key internal Hanafi disagreement arises regarding travel to risky areas, and a critical prohibition prevents the mudarib from appointing a substitute mudarib without the owner's approval.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition of capital delivery
The passage states that the capital must be handed over to the mudarib and the owner must have no hand in it. This is a necessary condition (shart) for a valid mudarabah. Why? So the mudarib can trade freely and efficiently.

This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.

Core Idea
Liability in mudarabah hinges on when the mudarabah truly begins—profit-sharing or work commencement.

In this passage, we examine a scenario where a mudarib (worker) hands the capital to another person to work as a mudarib without the owner's permission. The key question is: at what point does the first mudarib become liable to the owner for the capital? The Hanafi school has two distinct views. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that liability only arises when the second mudarib actually earns profit, because only then does a true mudarabah come into existence. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that liability begins as soon as the second mudarib starts working, since that is when the mudarabah contract becomes effective. 3

Sahibayn: Liability begins when the second mudarib starts work. Commencement of work itself initiates the mudarabah contract.
4

Under Abu Hanifa, if the second mudarib works but fails to profit, the first mudarib is not liable. Under Sahibayn, he is liable immediately upon work.

If the second mudarib works and makes profit, both views agree on liability. The practical difference is only during the period before profit.

Loss in Mudarabah is First Deducted from Profit
Ruling with proof from athar about loss allocation in mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for loss allocation in mudarabah and explain the evidence from the athar.

Core Idea
Loss in mudarabah must be covered by profit before capital is touched.

In Hanafi fiqh, profit in mudarabah is considered subsidiary to capital. Therefore, when a loss occurs, it is first absorbed by profit, not the principal investment. This principle is grounded in an athar from the early tabi'un, which we will examine to understand the rule and its exceptions.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi rule of loss allocation
Identify the basic ruling: any loss in mudarabah property is deducted from profit, not from the capital. Recognize that this derives from the subsidiary nature of profit.
The rule is explicit in the matn: 'وما هلك من مال المضاربة فهو من الربح دون رأس المال'.
2
Understand the legal principle behind the rule
Profit is tabi' (subsidiary) to capital (asl). Just as branches are attached to roots, loss first affects the subsidiary before the principal. This is a foundational maxim in Hanafi muamalat.
The Urdu commentary emphasizes: 'نفع تابع ہے اور پونچھی اصل ہے'.
3
Examine the athar as evidence
Read the athar from Ibn Sirin and Abu Qilabah. Note that it presents two scenarios: when accounts are unsettled, the loss comes from profit and the mudarib gets nothing until capital is restored; when accounts are settled, new profit is not burdened with old loss.
The athar is from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq, #15099, and is mursal but accepted in Hanafi practice.
4
Analyze the two scenarios for practical application
First scenario: no settlement between contracts – loss from profit, capital must be made whole first. Second scenario: settlement (accounting or hiring) ends the first contract – subsequent profits belong to the new contract and are not used to cover past losses.
This prevents mixing of separate mudarabah contracts and protects the mudarib's entitlement in the second venture.
5
Apply the rule to a concrete case
Take a simple example: A invests 1000, mudarib B trades. Loss of 200 occurs before any profit. Then B trades again and earns 300. According to the rule, the 200 loss is deducted from the 300 profit, leaving 100 net profit, which is shared. Capital remains 1000 intact.
If the first contract had been closed (settled) and then a second contract begins with new capital, the 200 loss would not affect the second contract's profit.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Profit in mudarabah is not final until capital is fully recovered; distributed profit must be returned if capital perishes.

In a mudarabah, profit is shared as agreed, but it is only true profit after the capital owner's principal is intact. This lesson examines a scenario where profit has been distributed while the contract is ongoing, and then the capital is partially or fully lost. The Hanafi ruling requires that the profit be returned to cover the loss, based on the principle that loss is on capital and profit is only realized after capital is whole.

Guided Path
1

The act is returning previously distributed profit when capital is lost during an ongoing mudarabah. The ruling is that the profit must be returned until the capital owner recovers the principal.

2
Locate the dalil
The author cites athar from Ali (رضي الله عنه) and from Ibn Sirin and Abu Qilaba. These are transmitted reports (Sunnah ahad) that establish the principle.
The dalil is of zanni thubut and dalala.

Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Ali's statement 'loss is on the capital, profit according to their agreement' implies that profit is dependent on capital integrity. Ibn Sirin's report explicitly states that the mudarib gets no profit until the capital owner recovers his capital.
These reports directly support the ruling of returning profit.

The Hanafi ruling is clear, but some other schools may not require return if loss occurs after distribution. This is marked as 'mukhtalaf fih' in the source.
No opposing detail is given in this passage.

Evaluate the tarjih
The ruling is from Zahir al-Riwayah and is mufta bihi. The Hanafi position is relied upon based on the athar and the principle that profit is only after capital recovery.

Ruling on surplus or deficit after returning profit to cover capital loss

Learning Objective

In mudarabah, the mudarib is not liable for capital deficiency; any surplus after restoring capital is shared profit.

This lesson tackles a practical scenario in mudarabah: after returning previously distributed profit to cover a capital loss, what happens if there is still a shortfall or a surplus? The passage clarifies that the mudarib, being a trustee (amin), is not responsible for any remaining capital deficiency. Any surplus after full capital recovery becomes shared profit. If a surplus remains after restoring the capital, it is profit to be shared according to the agreed ratio. If a deficiency remains, the mudarib is not liable for it; the loss stays with the capital owner.

[1369] (31) And the mudarib should not marry off a male slave or a female slave from the mudarabah capital. If he has bought a male slave or female slave from the mudarabah capital and wants to get them married, he cannot do so without the owner's permission. Getting a female slave married brings a dowry, maintenance, and if a child is born, that child is also a slave. These are all benefits, but they are not from the matters of trade; therefore, the mudarib cannot marry the female slave or male slave without the owner's permission. The principle of this issue is that the mudarib can do trade-related matters, and he cannot do that which is not trade-related.

Commentary
This is a ruling from the Book of Mudarabah. The matn states that the mudarib is not permitted to arrange marriages for slaves (male or female) that were purchased with mudarabah capital. Although marriage of a female slave brings benefits like dowry (mahr), her maintenance being taken over by the husband, and offspring who become slaves of the owner, these are not essential acts of trade. The underlying principle (asl) is that the mudarib's authority is limited to acts of trade (mutalliqat al-tijarah). Marriage is not a trade-related activity; it is a personal matter. Therefore, the mudarib requires express permission from the capital owner (rabb al-mal) to conduct such a marriage. The Hanafi school strictly limits the mudarib's actions to those necessary or customary for commerce.

Key Terms
مضارب (mudarib): The working partner in a mudarabah who provides labor.
متعلقات تجارت (mutalliqat al-tijarah): Matters that are related to trade, including buying, selling, and customary activities.
