The General Principle of Oath in Iqrar and the Minimum of 'Mal'
Hadith proof for defendant's oath and ruling on ambiguous 'mal' iqrar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for accepting a confessor's (muqirr) statement regarding unspecified 'mal' and apply the hadith proof that the oath is on the denier.

Core Idea
A confession of unspecified 'mal' is accepted on oath, but only for at least one dirham because less is not 'mal'.

This passage from the Book of Iqrar explains the foundational principle that in the absence of evidence, the defendant's oath is decisive. It then applies this to a confessor who says 'I owe him property' without specifying an amount. The muqirr is treated as a denier of any greater claim, so his statement with oath is accepted, but the Hanafi school imposes a minimum of one dirham for the term 'mal'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Context
The plaintiff has no evidence (bayyinah). The defendant is the confessor (muqirr) who admits a claim but may dispute the amount. He is treated as a denier of any higher claim.
In iqrar, the confessor is like a defendant; he denies any greater claim.
2
Recall the Hadith Principle
The hadiths from Ibn Abbas and Abu Huraira establish that the oath is upon the defendant/denier. The judge must accept the defendant's oath as binding in the absence of plaintiff's evidence.
The principle 'al-yamin ala man ankara' is core to Hanafi procedure.
3
Apply to Iqrar
Since the muqirr is effectively the denier, his statement (specifying the amount) is accepted after he takes an oath. The judge does not require the claimant to provide evidence for a higher amount.
The acceptance is wajib (obligatory) once oath is taken.
4
Determine the Minimum for 'Mal'
The term 'mal' (property) in Hanafi law has a minimum value of one dirham. If the muqirr specifies less than one dirham, his confession is not accepted because that amount is not legally considered property.
Other minima apply for specific descriptions (e.g., 'great property' requires 200 dirhams).

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Iqrar with a ruling on the general principle that when a defendant (muqirr) confesses to an unspecified 'mal' (property), he is believed after taking an oath, as he is considered the denier of the claimant's potential greater claim. The hadith evidence from Ibn Abbas and Abu Huraira establishes that the oath is on the defendant. The commentary explains that the muqirr's specification is accepted, but not below one dirham because that is not considered 'mal' in legal usage. This is a ruling_with_proof.

Key Terms
مال (mal): In this context, property that is legally recognized, with a minimum value of one dirham.
اقرار (iqrar): A declaration or confession by a person against his own interest, affirming a right of another.
