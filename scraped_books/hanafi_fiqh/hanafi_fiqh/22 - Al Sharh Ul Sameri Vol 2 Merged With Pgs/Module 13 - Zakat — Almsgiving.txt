Zakat on the Wealth of a Safih
Ruling that zakat is obligatory on the wealth of a safih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule regarding zakat on the wealth of a safih and explain why the obligation remains despite interdiction.

Core Idea
Zakat is obligatory on a safih's wealth; interdiction does not lift the fard.

This brief passage from the matn states a clear ruling: zakat must be paid from the wealth of a safih (a legally interdicted person due to foolishness). While the safih is under guardianship, the obligation of zakat remains because he is a free adult Muslim. The guardian must ensure the zakat is discharged.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and subject
The act is 'taking out zakat' (ikhraj al-zakat), and the subject is the wealth of a safih.
Safih is an interdicted person with partial intellect.
2
Determine the hukm level
The ruling is that zakat is fard (obligatory) on this wealth. In Hanafi fiqh, zakat is fard due to qat'i evidence.
The passage uses 'takhruj' (imperative form) indicating obligation.
3
Note the guardian's role
Because the safih cannot manage his finances, the guardian (judge or trustee) is responsible for paying the zakat.
The guardian acts on behalf of the safih.
4
Distinguish from the insane person
A safih has some intellect, unlike a majnun. Therefore, zakat is wajib for the safih but not for the majnun.
The distinction is based on level of aql (intellect).

Commentary

Confession of 'Mal Adhim' Requires Minimum Nisab
Condition for a valid confession of 'great property'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum amount for a valid confession of 'mal adhim' and explain why it is linked to the zakat nisab.

Core Idea
A confession of 'great wealth' is only accepted if it reaches the nisab of zakat: 200 dirhams or 20 dinars.

When someone admits they owe 'great property' (mal adhim), the law must set a clear minimum to avoid vagueness. The Hanafi school uniquely defines this minimum by the zakat nisab. This lesson unpacks that ruling and its rational basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its condition
The act is a confession (iqrar) by a person that he owes 'great property' to another. The condition for this confession to be legally effective is that the amount must be at least two hundred dirhams or twenty dinars.
2
Understand the term 'mal adhim'
In Islamic law, 'adhim' (great) is not subjective but defined by the shari'ah. The minimum for 'great property' is the nisab of zakat: 200 silver dirhams or 20 gold dinars.
This is the Hanafi position; other schools may define 'adhim' differently.
3
Recognize the basis in qiyas
The ruling uses analogy: just as zakat is obligatory only on wealth reaching nisab (considered 'great' in shari'ah), likewise a confession of 'great property' is only accepted if the amount meets that threshold.
The illa (effective cause) is the shari'ah's recognition of nisab as the boundary of significance.
4
Apply the ruling
If the confessor admits to a specific amount less than 200 dirhams, the confession is not considered a confession of 'mal adhim' and is invalid as such. He must confess that amount or more.
Mufta bihi: minimum 200 dirhams for 'mal adhim'.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school may interpret 'great' according to custom rather than fixed nisab. This affects the validity of the confession in different contexts.
Legitimate ikhtilaf; both positions are valid in their respective schools.

No Shuf'ah in Buildings and Palms Without Land
Ruling statement with brief taʻlil: no shufʻah in buildings/palms without land

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on shuf'ah for buildings and palms sold without land and explain the legal reasoning behind it.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah is exclusive to land; buildings and trees without land are movable for preemption purposes.

In this lesson, we examine a specific limitation of the right of preemption (shuf'ah). The source passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri clarifies that shuf'ah does not extend to structures or palm trees when sold independently of the land. We will analyze the rationale and the distinction between immovable land (ʻaqar) and movable property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Subject
Read the opening sentence: 'No shuf'ah in buildings and palms when sold without the land.' Note that the ruling negates shuf'ah for two specific items when a condition is met (absence of land sale).
The subject is buildings (al-bina') and palm trees (al-nakhl) sold without the land (al-ʻarsah).
2
Understand the Condition
The condition is that the building or palm tree is sold without the underlying land. If the land were included, shuf'ah would apply. The Arabic phrase 'بدون العرصة' (without the land) is key.
ʻArsah means the land or ground upon which something is built or planted.
3
Trace the Reasoning (Taʻlil)
The passage explains that shuf'ah is a right attached specifically to land. When land is not sold, the building or tree becomes like movable property (manqul) in terms of legal effect. The hadith (referenced) restricts shuf'ah to land.
The hadith is likely 'al-shuf'ah fi al-dar wa al-ʻaqar' (preemption is in houses and land).

Paying Zakat for a Partner Without Permission
States a clear prohibition with an exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a partner paying zakat on another partner's wealth and identify the condition for its validity.

Core Idea
Zakat on a partner's property requires explicit permission — partnership does not grant authority.

This lesson examines a concise ruling from the chapter on partnership (Kitab al-Shirkah). It clarifies that partnership alone does not authorize one partner to fulfill the zakat obligation of the other. Zakat is an act of worship tied to individual ownership and intention, so specific agency (wakalah) is necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal subject and action
The ruling concerns one partner (أحد الشريكين) performing the act of paying zakat (أدى زكوة) on the wealth of the other partner (مال الآخر).
2
State the default ruling
The text states it is 'not for any one of them' (وليس لواحد منهما) — meaning it is impermissible (haram) to do so without permission.
The negation (ليس) indicates prohibition in this context.
3
Recognize the exception
The ruling is inverted by the exception: 'except with his permission' (إلا بإذنه). Permission transforms the act from prohibited to valid.
The exception is an explicit condition (shart) for permissibility.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
Zakat is an act of worship that requires the owner's intention (niyyah); partnership does not grant authority over another's religious obligations. Payment without permission is both invalid and an encroachment on property rights.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that agency (wakalah) is needed for financial disposals.

Liability of Second Payer in Mutual Zakat Authorization - Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on liability of second payer in mutual zakat authorization.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the liability of the second payer when both partners authorize each other to pay zakat, and explain the basis for each position.

Core Idea
When two partners authorize each other to pay zakat, the second payer's liability depends on knowledge according to the Sahibayn, but Abu Hanifa holds him liable regardless.

In partnerships, it is common for each partner to authorize the other to pay zakat on their behalf. A question arises when both pay: is the second payer liable? This passage presents the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) on this issue. Understanding their reasoning is key to applying the correct ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two partners mutually authorize each other to pay the other's zakat. Both end up paying. Determine who pays second and whether the second is liable to compensate the partner.
The scenario assumes both are authorized.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
Abu Hanifa rules that the second payer is liable (daminal) regardless of whether he knew of the first payment. For him, the authorization is automatically revoked upon the first payment; any subsequent payment is without authority.
Liability means he must compensate the partner's share.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
The Sahibayn differentiate: if the second payer was unaware of the first payment, he is not liable because the authorization remains valid until he knows it is revoked. If he knew, he is liable.
This gives weight to the agent's state of knowledge.
4
Analyze the reasoning
The disagreement hinges on whether revocation of authorization requires the agent's knowledge to take effect. Abu Hanifa views revocation as immediate; the Sahibayn consider knowledge necessary.
As in other areas of agency in Hanafi fiqh.
5
Note the practical consequence
If the second payer is unaware, according to Abu Hanifa he must compensate, while according to the Sahibayn his payment is valid as voluntary charity (sadaqah nafil) and he does not owe compensation. The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view.
Later Hanafi fatwa often relies on the Sahibayn.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the liability of a partner who pays zakat on behalf of the other after the other has already paid, when both had mutual authorization. Sadaqah Requires Possession and Cannot Be Given in Undivided Divisible Property
States two rulings: sadaqah is not valid without possession, and sadaqah in shared divisible property is not permissible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two rulings on sadaqah: that it is not valid without possession (qabd) and that it is not permissible in undivided divisible property (musha'), and identify the transmitted evidence (athar) supporting the possession requirement.

Core Idea
Sadaqah, like a gift, is not complete until the recipient takes possession; giving a share of undivided divisible property as sadaqah is invalid.

In this lesson, we examine a passage from a Hanafi fiqh text that clarifies two essential conditions for a valid sadaqah (charitable gift). Just as a gift (hibah) requires delivery, sadaqah requires the recipient to take possession (qabd). Furthermore, giving sadaqah of an undivided share in property that can be divided (musha') is not permissible unless the share is first separated. These rulings ensure that the transaction is complete and unambiguous.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings in the passage
Read the passage carefully and locate the two distinct legal statements: (a) sadaqah is not valid except after possession, (b) sadaqah is not permissible in divisible shared property.
The rulings are numbered (34) and (35) in the text.
2
Analyze the evidence for the possession requirement
The passage cites an athar (tradition) from Companions (Uthman, Ibn Umar, Ibn Abbas, Mu'adh, Shurayh) stating that sadaqah is not valid until possession is taken. Recognize that this is a dalil naqli (transmitted evidence) supporting the ruling.
Even though the full chain is not given, the reliance on Companion statements is a common Hanafi method.
3
Understand the rationale for the rule on musha'
The prohibition on sadaqah in undivided divisible property (musha') is to avoid uncertainty (gharar) and ensure the recipient can take possession of a specific, identifiable portion.
This aligns with the general Hanafi principle that gifts of undivided shares are invalid if the property can be divided.
4
Apply the rulings to a new scenario
Consider a scenario: A donor says, 'I give you one-third of this undivided field as sadaqah' but does not hand over anything. Determine validity based on the two conditions.
The sadaqah would be invalid for two reasons: lack of possession and giving in divisible musha'.

[1529] (40) And he is told: Withhold from it an amount that you spend on yourself and your family until you earn wealth. Then when you earn wealth, give charity equal to what you kept for yourself. Due to the vow to give all property, he would have to give all his wealth, which would destroy his children. Therefore, he may keep enough wealth to cover his own and his family's expenses. Then when he earns wealth, he should give charity equal to what he had kept. Because the vow obligated giving all wealth.
Source pages p. 362

Commentary
This passage provides a practical concession for a person who vowed to give all his property in charity. Since fulfilling the vow literally would leave him and his dependents destitute, the Shariah allows him to withhold a reasonable amount for basic necessities for himself and his family until he can earn more. Once he earns additional wealth, he must give charity equal to the amount he kept. This ensures that the vow is fulfilled without causing hardship, following the principle that vows should not lead to harm. The person is essentially allowed to take a loan from the vowed property to cover essential needs, and then repay it later.

Key Terms
امسك (imsak): To withhold or keep back a portion of the property for essential needs.

Key Takeaway
A vow to give all property does not require immediate destitution; the Hanafi school allows temporary retention of necessities with later compensation to fulfill the vow.
