Validity of a Safih's Will for Charitable Purposes
Ruling on a safih's will validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a safih's will during terminal illness and identify the one-third limit.

Core Idea
A safih's will for good causes is valid from one-third of his wealth.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: a person under financial interdiction (safih) who falls terminally ill and makes a will for charitable purposes. The ruling clarifies that such a will is valid, but restricted to one-third of his estate, balancing spiritual benefit with protection of heirs' rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and situation
The subject is a safih (foolish person under interdiction) who is terminally ill. Note that normally a safih's financial actions are restricted, but here a special exception is made.
2
Identify the act being ruled upon
The act is making a will (wasiyya) for 'amoor al-khair' (charitable matters). This includes sadaqah, freeing slaves, building mosques, etc.
The will must be for virtuous deeds, not wasteful or sinful purposes.
3
State the ruling
The ruling is 'jā'iz' (valid/permissible). The will is legally effective and must be executed after death.
This validity applies despite the general interdiction on the safih's financial transactions.
4
Identify the restriction
The will is valid only from one-third of the safih's wealth. The remaining two-thirds are reserved for legal heirs.
This one-third limit is a general rule in Islamic inheritance law, based on hadith.
5
Understand the rationale
Charitable wills are considered a spiritual need, not wasteful expenses. The one-third limit prevents excessive depletion of the estate, protecting heirs' rights.
The safih's interdiction is for financial prudence, but charitable intent is exempted within limits.

Core Idea
Bequests for pious deeds are valid only from one-third of the estate.

This lesson presents a fundamental rule in Hanafi inheritance law: the one-third limit on bequests. The testator may allocate up to one-third of his wealth for charitable or pious purposes, known as 'qurb' (nearness to Allah). This ruling ensures that the rights of legal heirs are protected while allowing for acts of charity after death.

The remaining two-thirds are reserved for legal heirs.
3
Understand the rationale
The one-third limit is based on the Sunnah and protects the rights of heirs. Without their consent, the bequest cannot exceed one-third.

Validity of Acknowledgment for a Fetus in Will and Inheritance
Explains the validity of iqrar for a fetus in wasiyyah and mirath using a temporal distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a legal disposition for a fetus is valid if its effects are deferred after birth, distinguishing it from invalid immediate dispositions like sale.

Core Idea
A fetus can inherit or receive a bequest because its effects occur after birth, when it is known and separate.

Can a legal acknowledgment be made on behalf of an unborn child? At first glance, uncertainty about the fetus's existence and identity might render any disposition invalid. However, Hanafi jurisprudence distinguishes based on when the legal effects take place. In this passage, the author explains that in will and inheritance, the contract is made during pregnancy but its effects are deferred until after live birth—at which point the child is known and separate from the mother. This key temporal distinction makes such dispositions valid, unlike the sale of a fetus which takes immediate effect and is thus invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
The passage addresses a case where someone during death illness acknowledges debts or makes a will on behalf of a fetus, and another case where an heir acknowledges after the testator's death that the child of a slave girl or goat belongs to someone else. The ruling is that such acknowledgment is sahih (valid).
Focus on the fact that the acknowledgment is for the fetus, not by the fetus.
2
Understand the governing principle
The author states a general principle: if a contract is concluded and its effects are executed at the time of the contract (i.e., immediately), then both must be valid simultaneously. But if the effects are deferred to a later time, the contract can be valid even if some uncertainty exists at the time of contract.
This principle leads to the distinction between sale (immediate effects) and inheritance/will (deferred effects).
3
Apply the principle to the contrasting case
Sale of a fetus (bai' al-haml) is invalid because the sale is executed at the time of contract; the fetus is part of its mother and unknown, causing jahalah (uncertainty). Since effects are immediate, the invalidity is clear.
Compare this with inheritance: the contract (i.e., the appointment of inheritance) occurs during pregnancy, but the transfer of ownership occurs after birth.
4
Examine the proof (dalil)
The author cites a hadith from Abu Hurairah: 'When the newborn cries, he inherits.' (Abu Dawud, Ibn Majah). This indicates that inheritance rights are established only after live birth, not during pregnancy. Thus, the effects are deferred.
The hadith's wording (istahal, meaning to cry or to be born alive) directly supports the deferral of inheritance rulings.
5
Derive the final ruling
Based on the principle and proof: for a fetus, any legal disposition whose effects are to be executed after birth (like inheritance or will) is valid. But dispositions whose effects are immediate (like sale) are invalid. Therefore, acknowledging on behalf of a fetus for a will or inheritance is valid.
The ruling is mufta bihi: valid and binding once the child is born alive.

Priority of Debts Acknowledged During Death Illness
Ruling on priority of debts acknowledged during death illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the priority of debts acknowledged during marad al-mawt and apply it to determine which creditors are paid first in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Acknowledged debts during death illness are valid but paid only after settling debts from health and known causes.

When a person in a fatal illness (marad al-mawt) acknowledges a debt, the validity and payment order depend on existing debts. If the person already owes debts from his period of health or from known causes during illness (such as a purchase or marriage mahr), those prior debts take precedence. The acknowledged debt is valid but subordinated. This lesson explains the rule, its rationale (preventing harm to prior creditors), and its application through a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of debts
From the passage, identify: 1) debts from health (dīn al-ṣiḥḥa), 2) debts arising during illness from known causes (asbāb ma‘lūma), and 3) debts acknowledged without a known cause during illness.
Known causes include purchases, marriages, etc. that are publicly known.
2
Understand the priority rule
The passage states that health debts and illness debts with known causes are given precedence (muqaddam) over the acknowledged debt without cause. If no such prior debts exist, the acknowledged debt is paid first.
3
Grasp the legal reasoning
The rationale is to prevent fraud (tadlīs). If the dying person could freely acknowledge debts without cause, he might harm existing creditors. The Quran (4:12) prohibits causing harm (ḍirār) in wills and debts.
The ḥadīth 'lā ḍarar wa lā ḍirār' also underpins this.
4
Apply the rule to the example in the text
A person in marad al-mawt has a health debt and a debt from a purchase (known cause). He then acknowledges a debt for 'Umar without cause. The health and purchase debts are paid first; 'Umar is paid only from any remaining property after selling assets.
If the estate is insufficient, the acknowledged creditor may receive nothing.
