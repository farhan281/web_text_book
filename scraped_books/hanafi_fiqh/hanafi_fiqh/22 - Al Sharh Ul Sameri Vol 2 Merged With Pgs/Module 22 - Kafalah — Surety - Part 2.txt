The Validity of Suretyship for a Deceased Debtor with No Estate: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on validity of suretyship when the principal is dead and leaves no estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the validity of suretyship when the principal debtor dies with no estate, and explain the reasoning of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Valid kafalah requires a binding debt and possibility of recourse; without these, the arrangement is mere tabarru'.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding a specific scenario: a man dies with debts but leaves no estate, and someone steps forward to guarantee those debts. The central question is whether this constitutes valid suretyship (kafalah) or merely a voluntary assumption (tabarru'). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of guarantee in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa)
Identify the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa: the suretyship is invalid (ghair sahih) because two conditions are missing — the debt must be binding on the principal, and the surety must be able to recover from the principal's property.
The principal is dead, so no binding debt; no estate, so no recovery.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) hold the suretyship is valid (sahih). Their reasoning: the surety voluntarily assumes liability, and the contract is based on willingness, not on the existence of property.
They focus on the surety's intent rather than the principal's capacity.
3
Analyse each side's dalil
Examine the proofs: Abu Hanifa uses qiyas on the general conditions of kafalah. The Sahibayn rely on the principle that voluntary assumption creates obligation. Also consider the hadith of Abu Qatadah, which both sides address.
Imam Bukhari classified the hadith under hawala, not kafalah.
4
Understand the Hanafi response to the hadith
Abu Hanifa interprets the hadith of Abu Qatadah as a case of tabarru' or hawala (transfer of debt), not kafalah. Therefore it does not set a precedent for valid kafalah without conditions.
This shows how textual evidence is contextualized.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: according to Abu Hanifa, the surety paying the debt cannot later claim reimbursement from the deceased's estate (since no kafalah). According to the Sahibayn, he can claim it as a debt owed by the estate.
This affects the surety's rights and the estate's liabilities.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) on the validity of suretyship when the principal debtor dies with debts and leaves no estate. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the suretyship is invalid (ghair sahih) because two essential conditions are missing: (1) the principal must have a binding debt (lazim), and (2) the surety must have the ability to recover from the principal's property after paying. Since the principal is dead, the debt is no longer binding, and since there is no estate (no property left), the surety cannot recover anything. Therefore, such an arrangement is not a valid kafalah but rather a voluntary assumption of the debt (tabarru'/ihsan). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad) argue that it is valid because the surety willingly assumes the liability; the absence of the principal's property does not invalidate the contract. The text also clarifies that the well-known hadith of Abu Qatadah, where he became surety for a deceased man, is not a case of kafalah but a separate category (ihala or tabarru'), as Imam Bukhari's classification indicates. The Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa's view) is that valid kafalah requires both the binding nature of the debt and the possibility of recourse.

Key Terms
مكفول عنه (makful 'anhu): The principal debtor on whose behalf the surety guarantees.
تبرع (tabarru'): A voluntary act of assuming a debt without the intention of suretyship; a gift or benevolence.

Kafalah for a Deceased Debtor: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Binding Debt
Ruling on kafalah when the principal's debt is not binding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Hanifa's ruling on kafalah for a deceased debtor with no estate, explain the Sahibayn's counter-argument using the hadith of Abu Qatada, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Kafalah requires a binding debt; if the debt is unenforceable, suretyship becomes gratuitous tabarru'.

In this lesson, we examine a key intra-Hanafi dispute: can one become a surety for a deceased debtor who left no estate? Abu Hanifa says no, the Sahibayn say yes. The dispute hinges on what makes a debt 'binding' and whether the hadith of Abu Qatada supports kafalah or tabarru'. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying kafalah correctly in financial transactions.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling on kafalah for a deceased debtor with no estate and the essential condition that makes it invalid.
The condition is that the debt must be binding on the principal (asil) and the surety must be able to recover from the principal's wealth.
2
Identify the opposing view and its proof
State the Sahibayn's ruling and the hadith they cite (Abu Qatada). Understand how they interpret the hadith as a valid kafalah.
The hadith appears to show the Prophet accepting Abu Qatada as surety for a deceased debtor with no estate.
3
Follow the Hanafi response
Explain how Abu Hanifa's school responds to the hadith: it is tabarru' (gratuitous), not binding kafalah. Note the evidence that Abu Qatada did not seek repayment.
Tabarru' is permissible in the Hanafi school but does not create a contractual suretyship.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical difference in rulings: according to Abu Hanifa, the surety cannot be compelled to pay; if he pays, it is a gift and he cannot recover. According to the Sahibayn, he can be compelled and can recover if the estate later appears.
This affects the rights and obligations of the surety.
5
Apply the ruling to a parallel case
Consider the first case in the passage: kafalah for a mukatab's kitabah debt. Why is it invalid? Relate it to the same principle of non-binding debt.
The mukatab's debt is conditional; if he cannot pay, the kitabah collapses and the debt falls.

Commentary
This passage addresses two cases where kafalah (suretyship) is invalid because the debt is not binding on the principal (asil). The first case: kafalah for the kitabah (contract of manumission) of a mukatab (slave under contract). Whether the surety is free or slave, it is invalid because the debt of kitabah is not binding on the mukatab; if he becomes unable to pay, he reverts to slavery and the debt falls. Since the debt is not binding on the principal, the kafalah is not valid. The second case: a person dies with debts and no estate. If someone becomes surety for him to the creditors, Imam Abu Hanifa rules it invalid, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule it valid. Abu Hanifa's reasoning: for valid kafalah, two conditions are necessary—the debt must be binding on the principal, and the surety must be able to recover from the principal's wealth. Here, after death with no estate, the debt is no longer binding (since the deceased has no obligation to pay from nothing) and there is no wealth to recover from. The Sahibayn argue that the debt was binding at the time of death and nothing has eliminated it, so kafalah is valid. They cite the hadith of Abu Qatada, who became surety for a deceased debtor with no estate, and the Prophet (peace be upon him) accepted it. The Hanafi response: Abu Qatada's act was tabarru' (gratuitous undertaking), not a binding kafalah. The difference is practical: in tabarru', the person does not intend to recover the amount from the principal's estate, whereas in kafalah the surety can reclaim. This is why Abu Qatada did not seek repayment. The text concludes that such tabarru' is permissible in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
كفالة (kafalah): A contract of suretyship where one person (kafil) guarantees a debt or obligation of another (asil) to a third party (makful lahu).
مكاتب (mukatab): A slave who has entered a contract of kitabah with his master to purchase his freedom by paying installments.
أصيل (asil): The principal debtor on whose behalf the kafalah is undertaken.
تبرع (tabarru'): A gratuitous act of kindness or gift, not a binding contract.

Kafala for a Deceased Person: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Dispute over validity of kafala for a deceased debtor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on kafala for a deceased person, explain the Sahibayn's counter-argument, and evaluate the Hanafi response using the hadith of Abu Qatada.

Core Idea
Kafala for a deceased person is not a binding guarantee; it is either hawala or tabarru'.

In this lesson, we examine a key dispute within the Hanafi school itself: can a person guarantee the debt of someone who has died? Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students – Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) – disagree. The Sahibayn cite a well-known hadith involving Abu Qatada, but Abu Hanifa's response reinterprets that hadith as a case of voluntary charity, not a binding contract. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish legal obligations from charitable acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
Clearly state the contested ruling: Is kafala (guarantee) for a deceased person valid? Abu Hanifa says no – it is considered hawala (transfer of debt) or tabarru' (donation). The Sahibayn say yes – it is valid as kafala because the debt remains established.
The text begins by stating Abu Hanifa's view, then presents the Sahibayn's view.
2
Examine the Sahibayn's dalil: the hadith of Abu Qatada
Read the hadith (from Bukhari and Tirmidhi) where Abu Qatada guaranteed the debt of a deceased person. The Sahibayn argue that this shows a binding guarantee for a deceased person is permissible.
The hadith is the primary textual evidence for the Sahibayn.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response
The Hanafi response is that Abu Qatada's action was voluntary (tabarru') and not a binding kafala. Evidence: he did not later demand repayment. This distinction protects the school's position that a binding guarantee requires the guarantee's consent and the existence of a living debtor.
The commentary explains that Abu Hanifa's view does not reject the hadith, but interprets it differently.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, a person who says 'I guarantee the debt of the deceased' is not legally bound as a guarantor; the obligation is either a transfer (hawala) or a donation. Under the Sahibayn, he is bound as a kafil. Know which view is relied upon for fatwa.
The text defends Abu Hanifa's view, so it is the mufta bihi.
