Definition and Conditions of Rahn (Pledge)
Definition and conditions for valid rahn contract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of rahn, identify the three conditions for valid possession, and explain why possession is essential for completion of the contract.

Core Idea
Rahn is a voluntary contract that becomes binding only upon valid possession; without qabd, the rahin may revoke.

In this passage from Sharh al-Thamiri, we learn that rahn is not merely a verbal agreement but requires physical transfer to become binding. The author presents essential conditions for valid possession, grounding them in Quranic and Sunnah evidence, and explains that since rahn is a voluntary act (tabarru'), the pledgor can withdraw before possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its components
Recognize that rahn is concluded by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) but is only completed (yatimmu) by possession (qabd).
The contract is valid (sahih) only after possession; before that it is non-binding.
2
Locate the primary evidence
Note the Quranic verse (al-Baqarah 2:283) that mentions 'a pledge taken in possession' and the hadith of the Prophet pledging his armor.
The word 'مقبوضة' indicates the necessity of possession.
3
Analyze the three conditions of valid possession
Understand the three conditions: mahraz (separate from partnership), mufarrag (empty of the pledgor's belongings), and mumayyaz (physically distinct from the pledgor's other property).
These ensure the mortgagee has exclusive control.
4
Understand the reason for non-binding before possession
Because rahn is a voluntary contract (tabarru'), it is not obligatory; the pledgor can revoke until possession takes place.
This is unique to Hanafi fiqh; other schools may differ.
5
Apply the conditions to a scenario
Given an example (e.g., pledging a house with the pledgor's furniture still inside), determine if possession is valid.
Check each condition: mahraz? mufarrag? mumayyaz?

Commentary
This passage introduces the chapter on Rahn (pledge/pawn) from the Sharh al-Thamiri. It provides a definition and essential conditions. Rahn is a contract that secures a debt. The author states that rahn is concluded through offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) but is only perfected (yatimmu) by possession (qabd) by the mortgagee (murtahin). This is because rahn is a voluntary contract (tabarru'), not obligatory, so the rahin can revoke before possession. The proof is from the Quran (al-Baqarah 2:283) referring to 'a pledge taken in possession' and the hadith of the Prophet pledging his armor to a Jew. For possession to be valid, three conditions must be met: (1) mahraz — the item must be separate from others' ownership, (2) mufarrag — the item must be empty of the rahin's own belongings (so the murtahin has exclusive control), and (3) mumayyaz — the item must be physically distinct from the rahin's other property, not naturally attached. These conditions ensure that the murtahin's possession is genuine and exclusive, fulfilling the purpose of security.

Key Terms
رهن (rahn): A contract of pledge or pawn where a debtor gives an asset as security for a debt; it is concluded by offer and acceptance and completed by possession.
محوز (mahraz): Separated from the ownership of others; not jointly owned.
مفرغ (mufarrag): Empty; free of the rahin's belongings so that the murtahin has exclusive control.
مميز (mumayyaz): Distinguished; not naturally attached to the rahin's property.

Validity of Possession Requires Isolation and Mortgagor's Option
Ruling on requirement of isolation for possession and pledgor's option to retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for valid possession in a pledge (rahn) according to the Hanafi school and explain the pledgor's option to retract before possession.

Core Idea
Possession of a pledge is valid only when the item is isolated; until then, the pledgor may retract.

In this lesson, we examine two interrelated rulings from the Hanafi school on the pledge contract (rahn). First, we learn that for possession (qabd) to be complete, the pledged item must be physically isolated from the pledgor's other property. Second, until possession occurs, the pledgor retains the right to rescind the contract. Both rules stem from the nature of rahn as a gratuitous contract (tabarru'), which is not binding until delivery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sub-rulings
The passage contains two distinct rulings: (a) valid possession requires isolation (mahwaz) of the pledged item, and (b) the pledgor has the option to retract before possession occurs.
These two rulings are interconnected; both rely on the principle that rahn is a gratuitous contract.
2
Locate the dalil for the first ruling
The author cites an athar of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz concerning honey: 'It is not permissible from honey except what is isolated and extracted.' This is used as proof for the requirement of isolation in possession.
The analogy (qiyas) with hibah (gift) is also employed: just as a gift requires isolation for valid possession, so does a pledge.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The athar shows that for a fungible item like honey, possession is not complete without physical isolation. By analogy, the same applies to pledged items: the item must be separated from the pledgor's property for the pledgee's possession to be valid.
This establishes a condition for valid qabd.
4
Understand the rationale for the second ruling
Since rahn is a gratuitous act (tabarru'), like hibah, it is not binding until delivery. Therefore, before possession, the pledgor may change his mind and retract.
This is a direct consequence of the first ruling.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the pledge contract is binding immediately upon offer and acceptance, even without possession. The Hanafi response is that rahn is analogous to other gratuitous contracts.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf with practical consequences.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Sharh. The matn states two key rules: (1) valid possession of a pledge requires it to be isolated and distinct (mahwaz, mufarigh, mumayyaz), and (2) until possession occurs, the pledgor (rahin) has the option to deliver or retract. Pledgee's Liability After Possession: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute on pledgee liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on pledgee liability, cite the two hadith proofs, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
Once the pledgee takes possession, the pledge enters his liability; destruction reduces the debt.

This passage presents the inter-madhhab dispute on whether the pledgee bears liability (daman) for the pledged item after taking possession. The Hanafi school holds that the pledge enters the pledgee's liability, meaning if it perishes, its value is deducted from the debt. The Shafi'i school treats the pledgee as a trustee (amin) who is not liable without negligence. We will examine the proofs from hadith and the debate.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the Hanafi position: the pledgee becomes liable (damin) upon possession; if the pledge perishes, the debt is reduced by its value.
2
Examine the Hanafi proofs
Two hadith are cited: a mursal hadith from Ata' (the pledgee's right is gone) and a marfu' hadith from Anas (the pledge is for what it contains). These show that the pledge serves as a guarantee.
Both are zanni in transmission and indication.
3
State the Shafi'i opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the pledgee is a trustee (amin); if the pledge perishes without negligence, the debt remains and the loss is on the pledgor.
4
Examine the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i proof is the hadith: 'لا يغلق الرهن له غنمه وعليه غرمه' (The pledge does not foreclose; its profit is for the pledgor and its loss upon him).
This hadith is also narrated.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response and thamarah al-khilaf
The Hanafi school interprets the Shafi'i hadith as referring to profits and maintenance expenses, not to the risk of destruction. The specific hadiths on liability take precedence. The practical consequence: under Hanafi, if the pledge perishes accidentally, the debt is reduced; under Shafi'i, the debt remains full.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute on the nature of the pledgee's liability after possession. The author first states the Hanafi position: once the pledgee takes possession, the pledged item enters his liability (daman). If it perishes, its value is deducted from his debt. This is proven by two mursal hadith: one from Ata ibn Abi Rabah about a horse, and another from Anas. These indicate that the pledge is considered as a guarantee for the debt. Then the author presents the Shafi'i position: the pledgee holds the pledge as a trust (amanah), and if it perishes without negligence, the debt remains unaffected and the loss is on the pledgor. Their proof is the hadith 'La yughlaqu al-rahn' (the pledge does not foreclose the right) which implies ownership remains with the pledgor, so risk remains with him. The Hanafi response is implied in the choice of proofs: the first hadith directly states that the pledgee's right is lost if the pledge perishes, which means he must bear the loss. The footnote clarifies that in the Hanafi view, the pledgor profits from the pledge and bears maintenance costs, but the pledgee bears the risk of loss. The dispute has practical consequences: under Hanafi, if the pledge perishes without fault, the debt is reduced; under Shafi'i, the debt remains full.

امانت (amanah): Trust; in Shafi'i view, the pledgee holds the item as a trust and is not liable for loss without negligence.

Two Conditions of a Valid Rahn: Binding Debt and Limited Liability
Conditions for a valid pledge in Hanafi fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions of a valid pledge (rahn) in the Hanafi school and apply them to distinguish binding from non-binding debts.

Core Idea
A pledge is only valid for a binding debt; liability is limited to the lesser of value or debt.

A pledge (rahn) is a contract of security, but not every debt can be secured. The Hanafi school sets two precise conditions: the debt must be legally binding (dayn madmun), and if the pledge perishes, the creditor's liability is capped at the lesser of the pledge's value or the debt. This lesson unpacks these rules using the example of a mukatib's kitabah money.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first condition
Locate the phrase 'لا يصح الرهن إلا بدين مضمون' (a pledge is valid only for a guaranteed debt). Recognize that 'dayn madmun' means a debt that is obligatory and must be repaid.
Binding debts include loans, sale prices, etc.
2
Understand why voluntary debts are excluded
The commentary explains that a mukatib's kitabah money is not binding because the mukatib can abandon it. Since the purpose of a pledge is to secure repayment, a non-binding obligation offers no need for security.
The pledge is invalid if the debt is not wajib al-ada.
3
Identify the second condition
Find the phrase 'وهو مضمون بالأقل من قيمته ومن الدين' (it is liable for the lesser of its value or the debt). Understand that if the pledge perishes, the pledgee's liability is limited to the amount of the debt or the pledge's value, whichever is lower.
Any excess value beyond the debt is held as trust (amanah).
4
Apply the rule to examples
Work through the examples: debt £100, pledge value £150 – liability £100; pledge value £100 – debt considered paid. Distinguish the case where the pledge value is less than the debt.
The pledgee cannot recover more than the value of the pledge from the pledgor.

This ruling is derived through legal reasoning (ra'y) and is consistent with the purpose of the contract.

Liability for the Pledged Item upon Destruction
Ruling with proof on liability for destroyed pledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the pledged item is guaranteed by the lesser of its value and the debt, and determine the effect on the debt in three scenarios.

Core Idea
The pledgee is liable only to the extent of the debt; excess is trust; deficiency reduces the debt.

A pledge (rahn) is a security; ownership remains with the pledgor. What happens if the pledged item is destroyed while in the pledgee's hands? The Hanafi school has a clear rule: the pledgee guarantees the item only up to the amount of the debt. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proofs, and its application when the value is equal, greater, or less than the debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is rahn (pledge) and its subsequent destruction. The ruling is that the pledged item is guaranteed (maddmun) by the lesser of its value and the debt.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position.
2
Examine the proof (dalil)
The passage cites a hadith that the pledgor retains benefit and bears loss, indicating the pledgee is not liable beyond the debt. Also the athar of Umar ibn al-Khattab directly states the rule for deficiency and excess.
Both proofs are zanni al-thubut and dalala.
3
Apply scenario 1: value equals debt
If the pledged item's value equals the debt and it perishes, the debt is considered fully paid. The pledgee cannot claim anything further from the pledgor.
Example: debt 100, item value 100 → debt discharged.
4

If the value exceeds the debt, the excess (fadl) is held as a trust (amana). Upon destruction without negligence, the pledgee is not liable for the excess; the pledgor loses it.
Example: debt 100, item value 150 → debt discharged, 50 lost as trust.
5

If the value is less than the debt, the debt is reduced by that value, and the pledgee claims the remainder from the pledgor.
Example: debt 100, item value 80 → debt reduced to 20.

This passage covers the central ruling on the liability for the pledged item (al-marhūn) in the Hanafi school. The marhūn is guaranteed (maddmūn) by the lesser of its value and the debt. This ruling is a distinctive feature of Hanafi fiqh and is based on the principle that the pledge is a security, not a transfer of ownership.

The Surplus of Pawn Value Over Debt is a Trust
Ruling on surplus of pawn value over debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the surplus of pawn value over debt, identify the proof from the athar of Umar ibn al-Khattab, and apply the ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The surplus of pawn value over debt is a trust; the mortgagee is only liable for the debt amount.

In Hanafi law, a pawn (rahn) secures a debt, but what happens when the pawn's value exceeds the debt? This passage clarifies that the surplus is not part of the security but a trust (amanah) in the mortgagee's hands. The ruling is based on a decisive tradition from Caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining liability if the pawn is lost or damaged.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
State the Hanafi position: when the pawn's value exceeds the debt, the surplus is a trust (amanah) in the mortgagee's hands. The mortgagee is responsible only for the debt amount.
This is a rule of the pawn contract, not a discretionary ruling.
2
Understand the consequences
If the pawn is destroyed without the mortgagee's negligence, the debt is waived, and the mortgagor cannot claim the surplus. The mortgagee is not liable for the surplus because it is a trust.
Trusts (amanat) are not compensated if lost without fault.
3
Examine the evidence
The proof is the athar of Umar: 'if it is less [than the debt], the full right is returned; if it is more, he is a trustee.' This explicitly declares the surplus a trust.
The athar is classified as qawl sahabi (saying of a Companion), which is a valid source in Hanafi usul.
4
Apply to concrete numbers
Example: debt = 100, pawn value = 150. The mortgagee is liable for 100; the 50 surplus is a trust. If the pawn is lost without negligence, the 100 debt is cancelled, and the 50 is not owed.
This illustrates the practical difference between liability and trust.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Some scholars may view the entire pawn as a guarantee, making the mortgagee liable for the full value. The Hanafi school, however, follows the explicit athar and distinguishes the surplus as a trust.
This ikhtilaf does not affect the operative fatwa (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school.

Pawning Attached Fruit: The Condition of Possession (Qabd)
Ruling with proof from Quran and intra-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on pawning attached fruit or crops and explain the Quranic condition of possession (qabd) that renders it invalid.

Core Idea
A pawned item must be fully possessed; attached fruit cannot be pawned without the tree.

A farmer wants to pawn only the fruit on his palm trees, keeping the trees themselves. Is this valid? The Hanafi school says no, because the pawn must be completely possessed by the creditor, and attached fruit is inseparable. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its Quranic proof, and the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
State what is being ruled upon: pawning fruit on trees without the trees, or crops in ground without the ground. The ruling is that such a contract is invalid (batil).
The matn explicitly forbids these two scenarios.
2
Locate the dalil
The Quranic verse 2:283 says 'فرهان مقبوضة' (pawns in hand). The word 'مقبوضة' indicates that the pawn must be taken into possession by the creditor.
The dalil is from the Quran, which is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala here.
3
Understand the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'مقبوضة' (in possession) implies that the pawned item must be fully separable and exclusively held by the creditor. Attached fruit or crops are not separable, so possession is incomplete.
This is why pawning fruit without the tree fails the condition.
4
Compare the opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Abu Yusuf permit pawning joint property (musha') by analogy to sale, where joint property can be sold. They also cite a hadith of Mu'adh ibn Jabal.
Their proof is qiyas on sale.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi response is that the condition of qabd is stricter for pawn than for sale. The hadith is interpreted as referring to a different context. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Abu Hanifa's, aligning with the explicit Quranic condition.
Thus, the Hanafi ruling stands: invalid.

Collateral Perishing in the Session Completes Sarf/Salam
Ruling: collateral perishing in session completes sarf/salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when collateral perishes in the contract session for sarf and salam, and explain why the contract is completed by constructive receipt.

Core Idea
Perishing of collateral in the session = receipt of the right, so the contract is complete.

In Hanafi fiqh, possession during the contract session is a key requirement for sarf (currency exchange) and salam (advance payment sale). But what if the collateral pledged for the price or commodity perishes in that very session? This passage reveals a remarkable rule: the destruction of the collateral in the mortgagee's hands substitutes for actual receipt, completing the contract. This reflects the Hanafi view that the collateral effectively stands in for the debt or commodity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
In both sarf and salam, one party pledges collateral (rahn) to secure the other's obligation. Determine who is the mortgagee (creditor) and who is the pledgor (debtor) in each case.
In sarf: seller is mortgagee, buyer pledges collateral for the price. In salam: buyer (rabb al-salam) is mortgagee, seller pledges collateral for the commodity.
2
Define the key legal rule
The passage states that if the collateral perishes in the contract session (majlis al-'aqd), the contract is completed and the mortgagee is considered to have received his right 'hukman' (de jure). This is a specific rule in Hanafi fiqh: perishing of the collateral in the mortgagee's possession equals receipt of the secured right.
This rule is not based on qiyas but on the particular nature of rahn as a trust (amanah) in the mortgagee's hands.
3
Apply to sarf (currency exchange)
In sarf, possession of both counter-values is required in the session. If the buyer pledges an item with the seller and that item perishes in the seller's hands, the seller is deemed to have received the price. Thus the sale is complete and cannot be rescinded.
Without this rule, the seller would have no price and the sale would fail. But the perishing substitutes for receipt.
4
Apply to salam (advance payment sale)
In salam, the buyer pays the price in advance, and the seller is obligated to deliver a specific commodity later. If the seller pledges an item with the buyer and it perishes in the buyer's hands, the buyer is deemed to have received the commodity. The salam contract is complete.
Again, the perishing of the collateral in the mortgagee's possession is treated as constructive receipt of the musallam fih.
5
Distinguish from normal rules
Normally, if the collateral perishes before the mortgagee takes possession, it is still the pledgor's risk. But here the mortgagee already holds the collateral (it was pledged in the session), so perishing in his hands is like his receiving the right. This is an exception for the contract session.
The ruling applies only if the perishing occurs during the majlis, not after.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Spending counterfeit money does not discharge the debt according to Abu Hanifa; the creditor remains liable.

Imagine you lend money to someone, and they pay you back with coins. You spend those coins, only to discover they were counterfeit. Is your debt now paid? According to Imam Abu Hanifa, it is not. This lesson examines this scenario and the dispute within the Hanafi school between Abu Hanifa and his two eminent students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this ruling helps clarify the principles of valid payment and risk allocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and legal question
The scenario is: a creditor takes payment from his debtor in the form of the same type of debt (e.g., dirhams), spends that money, and later discovers it was counterfeit. The legal question is whether the debt is discharged or remains due.
2
State Imam Abu Hanifa’s position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the creditor must return what he spent (i.e., the debt is not discharged). The money he spent was invalid, so he still has a claim against the debtor.
The text quotes: 'yarji'u bima antaqada' (he returns what he spent).
3
Identify the opposing position (Sahibayn)
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) likely hold that the debt is discharged because the creditor accepted and used the money, indicating acceptance of the payment despite its counterfeit nature.
Exact words from the Sahibayn are not in the source, but this is their typical stance in similar cases.
4
Analyze the basis of disagreement
Abu Hanifa focuses on the invalidity of counterfeit as consideration: the debt is a valid claim, and invalid currency cannot extinguish it. The Sahibayn emphasize the creditor’s action (spending) as implied acceptance, shifting the loss to the creditor.
The underlying usul principle: is the debt discharged by delivery of defective payment?
5
Determine the practical consequence
Under Abu Hanifa, the debtor must still pay a valid amount; under the Sahibayn, the debtor is free. The creditor bears the loss if he spent counterfeit money.
This difference affects who ultimately bears the risk of counterfeit currency.

Learning Objective

This passage addresses an important intra-Hanafi dispute regarding mortgage repayment when the dirhams received turn out to be counterfeit. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the debt is discharged if weight and count match, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) require value equivalence to prevent harm. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Hanafi school balances textual evidence and the principle of avoiding harm.

Guided Path

Sahibayn: the debtor must return the exact counterfeit dirhams and reclaim his good ones. They rely on the hadith 'La darar wa la dirar' (no harm) and argue that value must be equal to prevent loss.
4
Compare the illa (effective cause)
Abu Hanifa focuses on genus and measure (weight/count); Sahibayn focus on value and avoidance of harm.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under Abu Hanifa, the mortgagee cannot claim more; under Sahibayn, he can return the bad dirhams and demand his good ones back.

Core Idea
If the mortgagor destroys the pledge, he must replace the security — either by paying the debt immediately or pledging its value.

What happens if the very person who gave a pledge (mortgagor) destroys the pledged item? This lesson addresses that specific scenario. The mortgagee's security must not be lost due to the mortgagor's own action. The ruling provides a clear remedy: restore the mortgagee's position by either immediate payment (if the debt is due) or a substitute pledge (if the debt is deferred).

Guided Path
1

Distinguish the Two Scenarios
The ruling differs based on whether the debt is immediately due or deferred. If due now, the mortgagor must pay the debt immediately. If deferred, the mortgagor must pay the market value of the pledge, and that value becomes a new pledge until maturity.
The key is the nature of the debt – due or deferred.

The value substitutes the original pledge. The mortgagee's security is fully restored; the debt remains as is. The new pledge (the value) secures the same debt until its term.
The substitute is in place of the destroyed item, not a reduction of debt.

The principle is that the mortgagee's right to security cannot be nullified by the mortgagor's own wrongful act. The contract of rahn entitles the mortgagee to hold a pledge until repayment; destroying it breaks that entitlement, so the mortgagor must make it whole.
This flows from the general rule that one must not harm another's rights.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in the law of pledge (rahn): What happens if the pledgor (rahin) frees a slave that is currently pledged as security? This lesson unpacks the legal reasoning behind this obligation and the slave's subsequent recourse against the master.

This is because the slave effectively paid the master's debt.

This is based on the principle that damage to collateral must be compensated.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that the pledgee is the proper plaintiff for compensation when a third party destroys the pledged property, and explain why the substitute value remains as a pledge.

Core Idea
A pledge destroyed by a third party is compensated to the pledgee, who holds the substitute as pledge.

In a pledge (rahn) contract, the pledgee holds the pledged property as security. But what happens if a stranger destroys it? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling: the pledgee, not the pledgor, is the one to seek compensation. This lesson unpacks that rule and its underlying principle: the substitute takes the ruling of the original.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their roles
Recognize the three parties: pledgor (rahin), pledgee (murtahin), and the third party (ajnabī) who destroys the pledge. The pledgee has possession and a security interest.
2
State the legal question
When the third party destroys the pledged property, who has the right to claim compensation? The possible claimants are the pledgor (owner) or the pledgee (possessor).
3
Identify the Hanafi ruling
The text states: 'the pledgee is the litigant for its compensation.' This means the pledgee alone is the proper plaintiff to sue the third party, not the pledgor.
The rationale: the pledgee had responsibility (ḍamān) over the property.
4
Understand the consequence: substitute pledge
Once the pledgee receives compensation (the value), that value becomes a new pledge in his hands, replacing the original. The substitute stands in place of the original in all legal rulings.
This is based on the principle: al-nā'ib yanūbu manābat al-aṣl (the substitute takes the place of the original).
5
Apply the principle to other scenarios
Recognize that this maxim applies broadly: whenever a substituted property replaces an original in a legal relationship, the same rulings attach to the substitute.

Core Idea
Three hadith form the prophetic basis for rahn: permissible conditions, no harm, and consent.

In this lesson, we examine three authentic hadith that the Hanafi school uses to derive key principles governing rahn (pledge). These hadith provide the prophetic foundation for allowing conditions that protect against harm, forbidding any harmful act, and requiring mutual consent for any disposition of the pledged property. Understanding these proofs will help you see how detailed fiqh rulings are rooted in the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first hadith and its ruling implication
Read the hadith of Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib regarding mudarabah conditions. Note that the Prophet صلى الله عليه وسلم approved conditions aimed at preventing harm. This establishes the principle that protective conditions in contracts—including rahn—are permissible.
The hadith is reported by al-Daraqutni and is considered hasan.
2

Study the hadith 'La darar wa la dirar.' This is a general maxim prohibiting harm and reciprocating harm. In rahn, it means neither the pledgor nor the pledgee may cause harm to the other through misuse of the pledge or unfair terms.
This hadith is used across all fiqh chapters, but in rahn it specifically restricts actions like using the pledge without permission.
3

This hadith is the basis for the rule that any change in the pledge's status requires mutual consent.
4

Combine the three: protective conditions are allowed (hadith of Abbas), harm is prohibited (la darar), and consent is required (hadith of Amr). Together, they form the foundational principles for all detailed rulings of rahn in the Hanafi school.

Liability for Injury by the Pledged Property (Jīnāyat al-Rahn)
Ruling on injury by the pledge with a slave example and hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that injury by the pledged property is disregarded and apply it to scenarios of harm to the pledgor, mortgagee, and their properties.

Core Idea
Injury by the pledged property is legally disregarded — no compensation is due to either party.

Some may think that if a pledged item causes damage, someone must pay. Yet the Hanafi school rules that such injury is hādir — null and void — against both the pledgor and the mortgagee. This lesson unpacks that ruling using the example of a pledged slave and a prophetic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the injury
Understand that the pledged property (e.g., a slave) may cause injury (jīnāya) to the pledgor (rāhin), the mortgagee (murtahin), or their respective properties.
The injury can be physical harm to persons or damage to property.
2
Apply the ruling of hādir
In each case, the Hanafi position is that the injury is hādir — it carries no legal consequence and no compensation is due. The text explicitly states: 'wa jīnāyat al-rahn ʿalā al-rāhin wa ʿalā al-murtahin wa ʿalā mālihimā hadar.'
The term hādir means 'null and void', 'disregarded'.
3
Examine the rationale with the slave example
If the slave harms the pledgor, the loss is disregarded because the slave is the pledgor's own property; selling the slave to recover the loss would just be selling his own property. If the slave harms the mortgagee, the fault lies with the mortgagee for failing to protect the pledge; any decrease in the slave's value reduces his debt, giving him no benefit.
This illustrates why no external compensation flows.
4
Note the exception for murder
If the pledged slave kills the mortgagee, the general rule of hādir does not apply; instead, qisās (retaliation) is taken because it is a capital crime. This exception is explicitly stated in the text.
Qisās is a separate category of criminal liability, not civil compensation.
5
Consider the hadith proof
The footnote cites a hadith where a pledged horse died in the mortgagee's hands, and the Prophet ﷺ said to the mortgagee: 'Your right is gone.' This shows that the mortgagee loses his right in the pledge if injury occurs without his fault, supporting the rule that injury is disregarded.
