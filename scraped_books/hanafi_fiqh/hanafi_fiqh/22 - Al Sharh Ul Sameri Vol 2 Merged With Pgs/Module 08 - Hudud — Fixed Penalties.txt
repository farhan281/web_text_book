Slave's Acknowledgment: Debt vs. Hudud/Qisas
Distinction between slave's acknowledgment of debt vs. hudud/qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a slave's acknowledgment of debt (suspended until freedom) and acknowledgment of hudud/qisas (immediately binding), and explain the legal basis for the distinction.

Core Idea
A slave's financial acknowledgment is deferred to avoid harming the master; criminal acknowledgment is immediate because the slave's body is his own.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi ruling that distinguishes between a slave's acknowledgment of financial obligations and his acknowledgment of criminal acts. The distinction hinges on whether the acknowledgment would cause direct loss to the master (financial) or involves the slave's own person (hudud/qisas). Understanding this distinction clarifies the Hanafi approach to balancing rights: protecting the master's property while holding the slave personally accountable for crimes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of acknowledgment
The passage discusses two scenarios: a slave acknowledging a debt (mal) and a slave acknowledging a hadd or qisas crime.
Debt involves property; hudud/qisas involve bodily punishment.
2
Analyze the ruling for financial acknowledgment
The default ruling is that the debt is not binding immediately because it would harm the master; it becomes binding after freedom. However, if the master has given permission to trade, the acknowledgment in trade matters is immediately valid.
This balances the master's property rights with the slave's capacity.
3
Analyze the ruling for criminal acknowledgment
If the slave acknowledges a hadd or qisas, the punishment is executed immediately without waiting for freedom, even if it causes loss to the master.
The slave has autonomy over his own person.
4
Understand the underlying rationale
Financial acknowledgment affects the master's property (mal) and is therefore restricted; criminal acknowledgment affects the slave's own body (nafs) and is therefore his own responsibility.
The key distinction: property vs. person.
5
Recognize the authority of the ruling
This ruling is from the zahir al-riwayah and is the mufta bihi position. It is a compromise between invalidating the slave's acknowledgment entirely and allowing immediate enforcement that harms the master.
Some other schools may differ; the Hanafi position is considered balanced.

Commentary
This passage addresses the slave's acknowledgment (iqrar) and distinguishes between two types: financial acknowledgment and criminal acknowledgment. The ruling is: a slave's acknowledgment of a debt (mal) is not binding upon him immediately because it would necessitate payment from his master's property, causing harm (dharar) to the master. However, since the slave is a sane adult ('aqil baligh), his acknowledgment is not completely invalid; rather, it is suspended and becomes binding after he is freed. After freedom, he must pay the debt from his own property. This is the default ruling when the master has not given the slave permission to engage in trade (idhn bil-tijara). If the master has authorized the slave to trade, then the slave's acknowledgment in trade-related matters is valid immediately because it is part of his authorized business. On the other hand, if the slave acknowledges a crime that entails a hadd punishment (e.g., drinking wine, theft, zina) or qisas (retaliation for intentional murder or injury), that acknowledgment is binding immediately, even if it results in loss to the master. The reason is that these punishments are bodily penalties (hudud al-abdan) affecting the slave's own person. In matters of his own body and life, the slave has autonomy (istiklal); he can incriminate himself because the punishment is personal. Therefore, hudud and qisas are executed immediately upon his acknowledgment, without waiting for his freedom. This distinction balances the master's right to his property with the slave's personal responsibility for crimes.

Key Terms
الحد (al-hadd): A fixed punishment prescribed by Islamic law for certain crimes (e.g., wine drinking, theft, zina).
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation punishment for intentional murder or bodily harm, equal to the harm inflicted.

Slave's Acknowledgment of Hadd or Qisas: Immediate Enforcement
Ruling on immediate enforcement of slave's penal acknowledgment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a slave's acknowledgment of hadd or qisas and explain why it is immediately enforceable, distinguishing it from acknowledgment of debt.

Core Idea
A slave's acknowledgment of hadd or qisas is binding immediately because it concerns his own person, not his master's property.

In Hanafi law, a slave's legal capacity is limited in property matters, but his person is his own. When a slave confesses to a crime that incurs a fixed punishment (hadd) or retaliation (qisas), his confession is accepted and the penalty is carried out without delay, even if it causes loss to his master. This lesson examines the rationale and scope of this rule.

1

What is the act? A slave's acknowledgment of hadd or qisas. The ruling: it is binding immediately (lazima fil-hal).
2
Contrast with other acknowledgments
In Hanafi fiqh, a slave's acknowledgment of debt is not immediately binding; it waits until manumission. Why the difference?
3
Locate the rationale
The commentary explains: hadd and qisas affect the slave's own person (life/limb), not the master's property. Therefore the slave is autonomous in these matters.
4
Apply the ruling
If the slave acknowledges drinking wine (hadd) or intentional murder (qisas), the penalty is inflicted immediately. The master cannot delay it.
5
Note the consequence
Even if the master suffers financial loss (e.g., the slave's death by qisas), the penalty is still carried out.

Wakalah for Hudud and Qisas Invalid When Principal Absent
Ruling on exception for hudud and qisas in wakalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on wakalah for hudud and qisas when the principal is absent, explain the reason based on shubha, and cite the supporting hadith.

Core Idea
Wakalah for hudud and qisas is invalid if the principal is absent, because absence creates doubt of possible pardon.

This lesson examines a specific exception in the law of agency (wakalah). While wakalah is generally permissible for most legal acts, you will see that hudud and qisas require the principal's presence. We will explore the rationale based on the principle of averting punishments through doubt (shubha) and the supporting hadith from the Prophet ﷺ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is wakalah (agency) to execute hudud or qisas when the principal (muwakkil) is absent from the execution assembly. The ruling: such wakalah is invalid (ghayr sahih).
The text explicitly says 'la tasihh'.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Aisha: the Prophet ﷺ commanded to avert hudud as much as possible. This is the primary proof.
Hadith from Tirmidhi and Ibn Majah.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The command to avert hudud implies that any doubt (shubha) should lead to dropping the punishment. The principal's absence creates a doubt that he may have pardoned, so executing through a wakil is invalid.
The principle of 'dar' al-hudud bil-shubuhat' (avert punishments by doubts) is applied here.
4
Note the ikhtilaf and its scope
The text notes that this is a matter of ikhtilaf (mukhtalaf fih) with other schools, but does not elaborate. The Hanafi position is that the condition of presence is necessary.
The cross-madhhab status is mukhtalaf fih.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The text presents the ruling as the established position (mufta bihi). The authority is from the Zahir al-Riwayah, and the reasoning is consistent with the principle of averting punishments.
No tarjih authority is explicitly stated; the text simply states the ruling.

Sulh: Permissible for Property, Usufruct, and Injury; Impermissible for Hadd
Ruling on permissible and impermissible claims for sulh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh for various claims and identify the Quranic and prophetic proofs for each category.

Core Idea
Sulh is valid for private rights but not for Allah's fixed punishments.

Sulh (settlement) is a key tool for resolving disputes amicably in Islamic law. However, not all claims can be settled; some involve rights that belong solely to Allah. This lesson delineates which claims are eligible for sulh and which are not, drawing directly from the Quran and Sunnah. Understanding this distinction prevents legal errors and ensures that hudud punishments are not compromised.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories
List the four types of claims mentioned: property (amwal), usufruct (manafi'), intentional injury (jinayat 'amd), and unintentional injury (jinayat khata'). Also note the excluded category: hadd.
2
State the ruling for each category
For property, usufruct, and both types of injury, sulh is permissible (ja'iz). For hadd, sulh is impermissible (la yajuz).
3
Determine the legal nature of permissible sulh
For property and usufruct, sulh takes the status of a sale or ijarah respectively. For intentional injury, sulh equates to forgiving qisas in exchange for compensation. For unintentional injury, sulh is analogized to the intentional case.
This classification affects the rights and obligations of the parties.
4
Locate the proofs for injury claims
Sulh for intentional injury is based on Quran 2:178, which permits forgiving qisas in exchange for 'something'. The hadith of Rubayyi' bint al-Nadr confirms this practice. Sulh for unintentional injury is derived through qiyas (analogy).
The hadith shows the Prophet allowing settlement for a broken tooth.
5
Understand the prohibition for hadd
Hadd is a right of Allah (haqq Allah) that cannot be waived or compromised by individuals. The hadith of the Makhzumiyyah woman demonstrates that even intercession is rejected; the hadd must be executed once proven.
This principle ensures the inviolability of hudud punishments.

Commentary
This passage categorically states the types of claims for which sulh (settlement) is valid in Hanafi fiqh. The author lists three categories: claims involving property (amwal), usufruct (manafi'), and both intentional (amd) and unintentional (khata') bodily injury. For property and usufruct, sulh takes the legal status of a sale or lease respectively. For intentional injury, the settlement equates to forgiving qisas (retaliation) in exchange for monetary compensation, which is explicitly permitted by the Quranic verse on qisas (2:178). For unintentional injury, analogical reasoning (qiyas) extends the same permission to settle for a lesser amount than the full diyah. The prohibition of sulh for hadd (fixed punishments) is absolute: hadd is a right of Allah, and once proven, it cannot be remitted or compromised through settlement, as evidenced by the hadith wherein the Prophet severely discouraged intercession in hadd cases. The commentary also includes a footnote explaining the Quranic verse and the hadith narrations in full.

Key Terms
صلح (sulh): A settlement or compromise to end a dispute by mutual agreement, often involving a consideration.
حد (hadd): A fixed punishment prescribed by Islamic law for certain crimes (e.g., theft, adultery, false accusation).
جناية عمد (jinayat 'amd): Intentional bodily injury that triggers a right of qisas (retaliation) in the victim.
جناية خطأ (jinayat khata'): Unintentional bodily injury that only incurs a monetary penalty (diyah) and no qisas.

Sulh Permissible in Jinayat but Not in Hudud
Ruling with proof on sulh in jinayat vs hudud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh in jinayat and hudud, cite the Quranic and hadith proofs, and apply the distinction between huquq al-ibad and huquq Allah.

Core Idea
Sulh settles disputes over injuries but cannot compromise Allah's fixed punishments.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri clarifies a key distinction in Islamic criminal law. While compromise is encouraged in cases of personal injury and blood money, fixed punishments (hudud) are Allah's rights and cannot be waived by human agreement. This lesson examines the evidence from Quran, hadith, and legal analogy.

Commentary
This passage is from the book of Sulh, covering the general ruling on compromise in cases of injuries and fixed punishments. The author states that a person who commits an injury (like cutting a limb) may spend his own property to save his limb (i.e., to avoid retaliation). The victim has the right to forgive the qisas and accept monetary compensation. The Quranic verse 2:178 is cited as proof, which permits forgiveness and accepting payment in intentional killing. By analogy (qiyas), the same applies to unintentional injuries. The hadith of Rubayyi' bint al-Nadr further supports this: when she broke a girl's tooth, the Prophet initially ordered retaliation, but the parties agreed on compensation (arsh) instead, demonstrating the validity of sulh on diyah. Then the passage contrasts this with hudud (fixed punishments). Hudud are the rights of Allah; once proven, they cannot be forgiven or compromised because no human has authority to forgive a divine right. The hadith about the Makhzumiyyah woman who stole and the Prophet's refusal to intercede is cited to show that even intercession is discouraged in hudud. Thus, the core principle is: sulh is valid on huquq al-ibad (rights of people) but invalid on huquq Allah (rights of Allah).
