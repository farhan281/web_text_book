Buyer Cutting Fruit Reduces Pre-emptor's Share
Ruling on buyer cutting fruit after purchase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the buyer cuts fruit after purchase and explain the consequence for the pre-emptor's share.

Core Idea
If the buyer cuts the fruit, the pre-emptor loses his claim to it and the price is reduced accordingly.

This lesson examines the effect of a buyer's action after purchasing land with fruit. The Hanafi school holds that the pre-emptor's right attaches to the land and its fruit as a unit. If the buyer separates the fruit by cutting it, the pre-emptor's right to that fruit is lost, and only the land is subject to pre-emption at a reduced price. This ruling is grounded in the principle that the pre-emptor's claim is on the property as it existed at the time of sale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The case involves land sold with fruit (e.g., a date palm with ripe dates). The buyer cuts the fruit after the sale but before the pre-emptor exercises his right.
The act of cutting is deliberate by the buyer; accidental damage is not covered in this passage.
2
Determine the legal effect on the pre-emptor's right
The pre-emptor's share in the fruit falls away (saqata). He can only claim the land, not the fruit, because the fruit has been separated and is no longer part of the sold unit.
The right of shuf'a is over the property as it was sold; the buyer's act changes the property composition.
3
Identify the consequence on the price
The pre-emptor pays a reduced price: the original price minus the value of the fruit. This ensures the pre-emptor pays only for what he receives.
The reduction compensates for the loss of the fruit that was part of the sale.
4
Understand the rationale
The buyer's cutting separates the fruit from the land, making it an independent asset. The pre-emptor's right only covers what was sold as a unit; since the buyer has taken possession of the fruit by cutting, the pre-emptor cannot claim it.
This is consistent with the principle that shuf'a is a right to take the property as it stands at the time of sale.
5
Consider cross-madhhab differences
Other schools might treat malicious cutting differently. The Hanafi position is based on the physical separation regardless of intent.
This passage does not discuss malice, so it remains the standard ruling.

Pre-emptor's Options of Sight and Defect After Award
Pre-emptor retains khiyar al-ru'yah and khiyar al-'ayb after being awarded property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two khiyar options available to a pre-emptor after award and apply them to a scenario.

Core Idea
A pre-emptor becomes a new buyer with full khiyar rights, including sight and defect options.

When a pre-emptor is awarded property through shuf'ah, a new sale is formed between the pre-emptor and the buyer. This grants the pre-emptor the same rights as any buyer, including the option to cancel if the property was unseen (khiyar al-ru'yah) or if a defect is discovered (khiyar al-'ayb). Importantly, these rights are independent of any waiver the buyer may have made with the original seller.

Guided Path
1
Identify the event
Recognize that when a pre-emptor is awarded the property, a new sale contract is created between the pre-emptor and the buyer.
This new sale is distinct from the original sale between seller and buyer.
2
Apply the buyer's rights
As a new buyer, the pre-emptor obtains all contractual options (khiyar) that any buyer enjoys.
3
Distinguish the two options
Khiyar al-ru'yah applies if the property was not seen before the award; khiyar al-'ayb applies if a defect is found after award.
The two options are independent and can be exercised separately or together.
4
Note the independence
The pre-emptor is not bound by any prior defect waiver agreed between the buyer and seller.
5
Conclude the ruling
The pre-emptor may return the property for a defect even if the buyer had accepted it, and may also cancel if he had not seen the property.
This ensures the pre-emptor is not unfairly deprived of inspection rights.

Fruit Picking Reduces Pre-emption Price
Ruling on price reduction when buyer picks fruit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on pre-emption price when the buyer picks fruit, and explain the rationale based on the fruit's change in legal status.

Core Idea
If the buyer picks the fruit, the pre-emptor's price is reduced by its value because the fruit is no longer part of the sale.

Pre-emption (shuf'ah) allows a co-owner to step into a sale and take the property at the same price. But what happens if the buyer alters the property before the pre-emptor exercises his right? This passage from al-Sharh al-Sameri addresses a common scenario: the buyer picks fruit from a garden he bought with the fruit. The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that protects the pre-emptor from paying for something he cannot receive.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The buyer picks fruit from a garden he purchased with the fruit. The ruling: the pre-emptor's price is reduced by the value of the fruit.
2
Understand the legal status of the fruit
Initially, the fruit is part of the sale (tabi'). When the buyer picks it himself, it ceases to be a follow-up and becomes an independent principal (asl) because the buyer intentionally severed it.
This is unlike natural destruction, where the fruit remains tabi' and the price is not reduced.
3
Apply the reduction
The pre-emptor pays the original contract price minus the market value of the fruit picked by the buyer.
The reduction is exactly the value of the fruit, not an arbitrary amount.
4
Distinguish from force majeure
If the fruit had fallen due to an act of God (e.g., wind), the price would not be reduced because the loss is not the buyer's fault.
The buyer's action changes the legal status.

Pre-emptor’s Options: Inspection and Defect
Ruling on pre-emptor’s options with hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the options available to a pre-emptor who acquires an unseen or defective property, and explain the legal basis for these options.

Core Idea
The pre-emptor’s acquisition is a new sale, so he gets all sale options independently of the buyer.

When a pre-emptor is awarded a property he has never seen, does he have the right to inspect it and reject it if defective? The Hanafi school answers yes, and this lesson explains why. The key principle is that the pre-emptor's taking is a fresh purchase, entitling him to all the options that any buyer has. We will examine the rulings and the hadith evidence that support them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Pre-emptor’s Situation
The pre-emptor is awarded a property he has not seen. He may later see it and find a defect. The text rules on his rights.
The pre-emptor here is the one who successfully claims shuf'ah (pre-emption).
2
Recognize the Legal Nature of the Acquisition
The pre-emptor's taking is considered a new sale (bay' jadid) between him and the original buyer. This is crucial because it means all sale options transfer.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position.
3
Apply the General Sale Options
Any buyer has khiyar al-ru'ya (option of inspection) if he buys an unseen item, and khiyar al-'ayb (option of defect) if the item has a defect. Therefore, the pre-emptor also has these options.
The hadith proofs are cited to support these options.
4
Note the Independence from Buyer’s Waiver
Even if the original buyer had waived defects from the seller, that waiver does not bind the pre-emptor, because his sale is separate.
This is the practical consequence of the new sale principle.
5
Review the Hadith Evidence
Two hadiths are provided: one from Abu Hurairah on khiyar al-ru'ya, and one from Aisha on khiyar al-'ayb. The pre-emptor is analogized to these buyers.
The proofs are ahad but their meaning is clear (dalala qati').

Deferred Payment and Pre-emption
Ruling on pre-emptor's options when buyer purchases with deferred payment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the pre-emptor's options when the original buyer purchased with deferred payment and explain why the deferred term does not transfer to the pre-emptor.

Core Idea
The deferred payment is personal to the buyer; the pre-emptor must pay spot or wait and then pay spot.

In this lesson, we examine a specific scenario in pre-emption (shuf'a): when the buyer has purchased the property on credit (thaman mu'ajjal). The Hanafi school rules that the pre-emptor does not inherit the same credit terms but has an option to pay cash now or wait. This distinction arises because credit is a personal trust arrangement, not a condition of the property itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the act
The subject is the pre-emptor (shafi'), the act is exercising pre-emption after a deferred sale.
2
State the primary ruling
The pre-emptor has an option: take the property with spot payment now, or wait until the term ends and then pay spot.
The text says 'fa al-shafi'u bil khiyar'.
3
Identify the rationale
The deferred payment was a personal trust between original buyer and seller, not a condition of the property. Pre-emption creates a new sale where spot payment is the default rule.
The text states: 'this is not related to the sale itself, but to the seller's trust'.
4
Note the possibility of voluntary concession
The buyer may voluntarily grant deferred terms to the pre-emptor, but is not obliged.
The text: 'the buyer may, if he wishes, give credit...'
5
Conclude the operative rule
The pre-emptor must pay spot price; waiting is allowed but payment is always spot.
The text: 'it is wajib upon the pre-emptor to pay the cash price'.

Agency is valid for sarf and salam despite strict possession conditions, as proven by Umar's appointment.

The Hanafi school permits appointing an agent for special contracts like currency exchange (sarf) and advance payment sales (salam). This may seem surprising because these contracts require immediate possession or full prepayment. However, the practice of the Sahaba, specifically Umar ibn al-Khattab appointing his son Ibn Umar as agent in a sarf transaction, establishes the ruling. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's recovery when he pays more than the market price, and distinguish between the positions of Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
An agent who pays above market price recovers only market value from principal under Muhammad, but full price under Abu Yusuf via pawn analogy.

This lesson examines a nuanced rule in agency (wakalah) concerning the guarantee of the sold item (daman al-mabi'). When an agent pays more than the market price for the principal's purchase, can he recover the full amount from the principal? The answer differs between Imams Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, rooted in their analogies to trust and guarantee. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the boundary between trust (amanah) and guarantee (daman) in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
The agent pays for the purchased item; what is the legal consequence regarding his right to reimbursement?
Focus on the agent-principal relationship, not the buyer.
2
State Imam Muhammad's Position
The agent can only recover the market price from the principal; any excess is dropped and cannot be recovered from the buyer or the principal.
The item is a trust before restraining; after restraining it becomes guaranteed to market value.
3
Understand the Reasoning
Before restraining (qabd), the item is an amanah (trust); after restraining, it becomes madhmun (guaranteed). The agent's liability is limited to market price.
Restraining is key to transforming liability.
4
Compare Imam Abu Yusuf's Analogy to Pawn
Abu Yusuf analogizes to pawn (rahn): if a pawned item is destroyed, the debt is reduced by the item's value, and any surplus is returned. Similarly, the agent recovers the full price paid from the principal, with the sold item serving as security.
The analogy hinges on the concept of 'restraining' making the item guaranteed.
5
Apply to an Example
If market price is 1000 dirhams and the agent paid 1200, according to Muhammad the agent recovers 1000 from the principal; according to Abu Yusuf the agent recovers 1200 from the principal (collecting the excess 200 as reimbursement).

Agent's Authority to Sell at Low or High Price
Ruling and dispute on agent's authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the agent's authority to sell at a below-market price and distinguish the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Absolute agency is not unlimited — custom (urf) restricts the agent's discretion to avoid excessive loss.

When a principal appoints an agent to sell goods without specifying a price, how much discretion does the agent have? This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two leading disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), over whether an agent may sell at an excessively low price. The dispute hinges on whether absolute (mutlaq) wording is interpreted literally or by custom (urf).

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Identify that Abu Hanifa holds the sale valid at any price, low or high, because the absolute wording of the agency gives full discretion.
He relies on the literal meaning of 'sell'.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn hold that selling at a price so low that people would not customarily accept such a loss (ghubn fahish) is invalid.
They restrict absolute agency by custom (urf).
3
Identify the dalil for each side
Abu Hanifa's dalil is the literal interpretation of the word 'bay' (sale). The Sahibayn rely on the Quranic principle of 'ma'ruf' (custom/kindness) and the maxim that absolute terms are interpreted by custom.
The Sahibayn analogize from Quran 2:236.
4
Analyze the basis: literal vs. custom
Abu Hanifa looks at the mutlaq wording; the Sahibayn look at the mutlaq wording as understood by urf. This is a fundamental usul difference.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, the sale is valid even at a huge loss; under the Sahibayn, the agent is liable and the principal may rescind if the loss is excessive.
Later Hanafi fatwa follows Sahibayn to protect the principal.

Agent's Purchase: Customary Price and Increase
Ruling on agent's purchase price limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's purchase price and identify when a higher price is permissible.

Core Idea
An agent may buy at fair price or with a customary slight increase; excessive overpayment invalidates the purchase.

When you appoint someone to buy an item, the agent must not overpay beyond what people normally tolerate as a small loss. This lesson explains the boundary between a valid slight increase and an invalid excessive overpayment, and discusses the exception when the principal orders a specific unique item.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is the agent concluding a purchase. The default ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) when done at the fair price or with a customary small increase.
The Arabic uses 'يجوز عقده' meaning 'his contract is permissible'.
2
Understand the two-part condition on price
Price must be either mithl al-qīma (fair price) or with a ziyādah yataghāban al-nās (increase customarily tolerated). Exceeding this – ghubn fāḥish – makes the contract invalid (lā yajūz).
Ghunb (loss) fahish (excessive) is determined by custom.
3
Analyze the rationale: suspicion of self-dealing
The prohibition against excessive overpayment is to prevent suspicion that the agent bought the item for himself at a high price and then passes it to the principal. The agent's good faith is presumed only up to the customary increase.
The Urdu commentary mentions 'شبہ' (suspicion) and 'تہمت' (accusation).
4
Recognize the exception for specific items
If the principal orders a specific unique item (e.g., 'buy that particular horse'), the agent cannot buy it for himself, so no suspicion exists. Thus, the agent may pay even more than the customary increase.
The key: the agent's inability to buy that specific item for himself removes the self-dealing concern.

Defining Minor and Excessive Price Disparity (Ghubn) in Hanafi Sales Law
Defining two types of price disparity in sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'ma yataghāban al-nās' and 'la yataghāban al-nās' and explain their legal significance in Hanafi sales law.

Core Idea
A sale is invalid if the price exceeds what experts consider acceptable—excessive disparity (ghubn fāhish) is not overlooked.

In Hanafi sales law, not every price difference invalidates a contract. The law distinguishes between minor disparity that people routinely overlook and excessive disparity that destroys the fairness of the exchange. This passage defines the boundary between the two.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage defines two categories: 'ma yataghāban al-nās' (what people mutually overlook) and 'la yataghāban al-nās' (what they do not overlook). They are defined in relation to the valuation of expert appraisers.
2
Understand the role of experts
The benchmark is the range of price that expert appraisers would assign to the item. The experts' valuation sets the acceptable range.
3
Define 'ma yataghāban al-nās'
This is the range within which experts differ and which people generally accept as a normal variance. It is the tolerable disparity.
4
Define 'la yataghāban al-nās'
Any price that falls outside the experts' valuation is considered excessive disparity. People do not normally accept such a large discrepancy.
This is ghubn fāhish and may invalidate the sale.
5
Note the legal consequence
Although the passage itself does not explicitly state the ruling, the definition serves as the basis for the Hanafi rule that ghubn fāhish renders a sale invalid (fasid) if the buyer was deceived.
Further chapters detail the conditions and remedies.

Ruling on agent guaranteeing the price from his own side

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that an agent's guarantee of the sale price is void and explain the two reasons for its invalidity.

Core Idea
An agent cannot both receive and give the price, nor hold goods as trust and guarantee.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi sales law: when an agent authorized to sell a property voluntarily guarantees the price from the buyer, that guarantee is void. The passage gives two legal reasons: the agent's role as receiver conflicts with becoming the giver, and the goods held in trust become a liability. Understanding this ruling clarifies the limits of an agent's authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an agent for sale (wakīl bi al-bayʿ) guaranteeing the price from the buyer (ḍamān al-thaman). The ruling is that this guarantee is bāṭil (void).
2
Understand the first reason
The agent's job is to receive the price from the buyer. By guaranteeing, he becomes the one who gives the price. One person cannot be both the receiver and giver of the same price; this is a contradiction (tanāquḍ) that invalidates the guarantee.
In Hanafi legal reasoning, a single contract cannot have conflicting obligations.
3
Understand the second reason
The sold item was in the agent's possession as a trust (amānah). By guaranteeing the price, the agent imposes liability (ḍamān) on himself for the item's value. This conflicts with the trust status, as a trustee is not liable for loss without negligence. The contradiction renders the guarantee void.
Trust and liability are mutually exclusive under Hanafi law.
4
Synthesize the ruling
Both reasons independently make the guarantee void. The agent cannot validly undertake a guarantee that contradicts his primary roles of receiving the price and holding the goods in trust.
The ruling is clear: any such guarantee is legally ineffective.

Selling Half a Slave: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Dispute on whether an agent can sell half of a slave.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on an agent selling half of a slave and explain how custom (ma'rūf) restricts absolute commands according to the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
An absolute command to sell is not absolute — custom may restrict it to selling the whole.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of an agent's authority. When a principal says 'sell my slave,' does that authorization include selling only half? Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the word 'sell' is absolute and includes both whole and half. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) disagree, arguing that custom (ma'rūf) dictates selling the entire slave to avoid the harm of partnership. Their view is supported by an athar from Ibrahim and al-Hasan that an agent acts according to custom.

Guided Path
1
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that the absolute command 'sell' includes selling half because the word 'sell' applies to both whole and part. Therefore, the half-sale is valid.
Hanafi principle: al-muṭlaq yujrā ʿalā iṭlāqih.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn hold that the absolute command is restricted by custom. Since custom is to sell the whole to avoid partnership, selling half alone is invalid unless the other half is also sold before dispute.
Custom (maʿrūf) qualifies the absolute.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's proof
They cite the athar of Ibrahim and al-Hasan that an agent eats and dresses 'with maʿrūf,' establishing that absolute agency commands are interpreted by custom. By analogy, an agent authorized to sell must sell according to custom.
The athar is from the practice of the salaf.
4

If an agent sells half only, Abu Hanifa deems the sale valid and binding; the Sahibayn deem it invalid unless the other half is subsequently sold before any objection. If the other half is sold later, the first sale becomes valid retroactively as a means.
The difference affects the validity of agency contracts.
5
Note the unresolved tarjih
This passage presents both views without choosing which is mufta bihi. In later Hanafi works, the view of the Sahibayn is often preferred in practice due to custom, but Abu Hanifa's position remains part of the school.
The student should be aware of both as valid opinions.

Sulh 'an Dar: No Pre-emption
States that pre-emption does not arise in sulh 'an dar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on pre-emption in sulh 'an dar and distinguish it from sulh 'ala dar.

Core Idea
Sulh 'an dar is not a sale, so no pre-emption arises.

This lesson examines a key distinction in Hanafi transactional law: when a defendant settles a claim by giving a house (sulh 'an dar), the transfer is not a sale but a fidya (ransom) to end the dispute. Consequently, the right of pre-emption (shuf'a) does not attach. By contrast, if the sulh is structured as a sale (sulh 'ala dar), pre-emption applies. Understanding this difference is essential for correctly applying shuf'a rules in property settlements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two forms of sulh

The preposition (عن vs على) changes the legal nature.
2
Recognize the basis of pre-emption
Shuf'a is a right that arises only when property is sold (bay') or a contract equivalent to a sale. It does not apply to mere transfers.
Hanafis restrict shuf'a to purchase contracts.
3
Apply the rule to sulh 'an dar
Since sulh 'an dar is not a sale (the defendant is ransoming himself), no pre-emption arises.
The house is given as compensation, not sold.
4
Contrast with sulh 'ala dar
In sulh 'ala dar, the defendant sells the house to the plaintiff, so a sale occurs and pre-emption applies.
This is treated as a regular purchase.

Sulh on a Debt: Partial Receipt and Remission, Not Exchange
Explains the principle that sulh on a debt is partial receipt and remission, with a slave example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi interpretation of sulh on a debt and apply it to distinguish sulh from mu'awadah (exchange).

Core Idea
Sulh on a debt is partial collection and remission, avoiding riba by not treating it as an exchange.

This lesson explores the Hanafi ruling on sulh (compromise) when it involves a debt. The key insight is that such a sulh is not considered an exchange (mu'awadah) because that would require equality and prompt possession, leading to riba. Instead, it is interpreted as the creditor recovering part of his due and waiving the rest. This principle is derived from the hadith of Ka'b ibn Malik and has practical implications for debt settlement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and its ruling
The act is sulh (compromise) on something owed by a debt contract. The Hanafi ruling is that it is valid (sahih).
The ruling is sahih because the interpretation avoids riba.
2
Understand the legal interpretation
The sulh is not treated as an exchange (mu'awadah) but as the creditor taking part of his right and remitting the remainder. This is a legal construction to prevent riba.
If treated as exchange, it would require equality of value and immediate possession, which is impossible when accepting less.
3
Analyze the rationale
Treating it as exchange would require the two amounts to be equal. Since they are not, riba would occur. Therefore, Hanafi jurists interpret it as partial receipt and remission.
This rationale is based on the prohibition of riba in the Quran and Sunnah.
4
Examine the evidence
The hadith of Ka'b ibn Malik: the Prophet (peace be upon him) gestured to take half the debt, and Ka'b took half and left half. This shows that accepting part of a debt and remitting the rest is permissible.
The phrase 'ترك نصفا' (left half) directly supports the concept of remission.
5
Apply to a scenario
Example: A creditor is owed 1000 good dirhams. He accepts 500 bad dirhams as sulh. This is interpreted as him taking 500 dirhams and remitting the remaining 500 dirhams plus the quality of being good.
The same principle applies to any debt settlement where the amount or quality differs.

Ruling on changing an immediate debt to a deferred one via sulh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sulh for deferment of a debt and explain the legal reasoning (ta'wil) that prevents riba.

Core Idea
Sulh to defer a debt is valid because the creditor waives the right to immediate payment, not because a new exchange occurs.

This lesson examines a key ruling from the Hanafi school regarding the permissibility of changing the payment term of a debt through sulh. You will learn how the scholars interpret such an agreement to avoid riba, using the hadith of Jabir as evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify that the sulh for deferment is valid (sahih) and the reasoning: it is treated as the creditor waiving his right to demand immediate payment.
The matn states: 'ولو صالحه على الف مؤجلة جاز وكانه اجل نفس الحق' – it is permissible and as if he postponed the same right.
2
Identify the riba concern
If the agreement were classified as a sale of debt for debt (exchange), it would require immediate possession to avoid riba. Why does this create a problem?
The sharh explains that treating it as an exchange would require qabd in the session for dirhams, otherwise riba occurs.
3
Analyze the ta'wil
The scholars interpret the sulh as a waiver (isqat) of the original term, not a new exchange. This avoids riba.
The creditor waives his right to demand immediate payment, so no exchange of debt for debt occurs.
4
Cite the dalil
The hadith of Jabir shows that requesting postponement is permissible, and the Prophet's involvement confirms the principle.
The hadith from Bukhari: Jabir asked the Jew for a delay; though the Jew refused, the act of seeking deferment is recognized.
5
Apply the ruling
Conclude that the sulh is valid and the debt becomes deferred. If the agreement involves changing the type of currency (e.g., dinars to be paid later), the ruling differs.
The matn also states: 'ولو صالحه على دنانير الى شهر لم يجز' – sulh for dinars to be paid after a month is not permissible.

Sulh of a deferred debt for less cash is riba: an exchange, not a waiver.

This passage addresses a common financial scenario: a creditor agrees to accept less than the full amount of a deferred debt in exchange for immediate payment. If the creditor simply forgives part of the debt without taking anything in return, that is valid ibra'. But here the creditor receives cash earlier, which is a benefit, so it becomes a sale.

Core Idea
Unequal exchange of same-genus items in settlement is haram riba, even if one party reduces for early payment.

This lesson examines two scenarios from the chapter on Sulh (settlement): exchanging inferior dirhams for superior ones in lesser quantity, and early payment in exchange for a reduction of debt. The Hanafi school treats both as riba al-fadl and declares the settlement invalid. Settlement by a Third Party (Fuduli): Binding and Suspended Contracts
Rulings on fuduli settlement with and without delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between binding and suspended settlement contracts made by a fuduli and identify the conditions that make them effective.

Core Idea
A fuduli's settlement binds him if he assumes liability; otherwise it hangs on the defendant's approval.

In Hanafi fiqh, a settlement (sulh) can be conducted by a third party (fuduli) who is not the original disputant. However, the validity of such a settlement depends on whether the fuduli takes personal responsibility or whether the defendant ratifies the contract. This lesson examines the key scenarios from the Sharh al-Sameri, clarifying when the settlement is immediately binding and when it remains suspended.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two binding scenarios
When the fuduli says 'I settle with you for this 1000' (pointing to his own property) or 'for this slave', or when he says 'I settle with you for 1000' and actually delivers the amount, the settlement is complete and binding on the fuduli. He must deliver the property.
In these cases, the fuduli has assumed liability by specifying or handing over his own property.
2
Identify the suspended scenario
When the fuduli says 'I settle with you for 1000' but does not deliver, the contract is suspended (mawquf). It does not become binding until the defendant (the original debtor) ratifies it.
The fuduli did not assume personal liability, so the contract awaits the principal’s consent.
3
Understand the role of ratification
If the defendant ratifies the suspended contract, it becomes valid and the defendant is bound to pay the 1000. If he refuses, the contract is void.
Ratification retroactively validates the contract as if the fuduli had authority.
4
Recognize the fourth form (from commentary)
If the fuduli never specified his own property and never delivered, and the defendant did not command or ratify, no liability arises on anyone, and the settlement is void from the outset.
This is the default when the fuduli acts completely without connection to the dispute.

Partner's Options When One Settles His Share for a Garment
Ruling on partner's options after settlement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options available to a non-settling partner when one partner settles his share of a joint debt for a garment, and identify the condition under which one option is removed.

Core Idea
A non-settling partner may either pursue the debtor or take half the garment, unless the settling partner guarantees a quarter.

When two partners jointly lend money to a third person, one partner may accept a settlement from the debtor for his share—for example, taking a garment instead of cash. The Hanafi school grants the other partner flexibility: he can still demand his share directly from the debtor, or he can become co-owner of the garment taken by his partner. However, if the settling partner guarantees a quarter of the total debt, the option to take the garment is blocked. This ruling ensures fairness and prevents one partner from being forced into an unwanted arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Two partners share a single debt owed by a third party. One partner settles his share by accepting a specific item (here, a garment).
2
State the first default option
The non-settling partner may choose to pursue the debtor directly for his half of the debt, as his claim remains on the debtor.
3
State the second default option
Alternatively, he may take half of the garment received by his partner, becoming a co-owner, and then both partners together pursue the debtor for the remaining half.
4

If the settling partner guarantees to pay the non-settling partner a quarter of the total debt (i.e., half of the non-settling partner's share), the second option is removed. The non-settling partner can only pursue the debtor for the remaining half, now with his partner's assistance.

On the other hand, Imam Abu Yusuf permits it, considering that each partner's share is his own property (dhati mal). He argues that just as one can dispose of his own property freely, he can compromise his share. His principle is that one can dispose of his own property even without the other's consent, as the focus is on the individual's own loss or benefit.

Key Terms
سلم (salam): An advance payment sale where the price is paid upfront for goods to be delivered later.
صلح (sulh): A compromise or settlement where parties agree to terminate or modify a contract or claim.
شريكان (sharikan): Two partners who jointly enter into a contract.
رأس المال (ra's al-mal): The principal amount paid in a salam contract.
تفریق صفقة (tafriq al-safqah): Splitting a single transaction into parts, which is generally prohibited in Hanafi law.

Key Takeaway
In a joint salam contract, a partner cannot unilaterally compromise to recover his capital because it splits the single transaction and causes loss to the other partner.
