Dispute Between Craftsman and Owner on Work Specifications
Rule for resolving disputes between craftsman and owner over work specifications in hire contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on resolving disputes over work specifications in an ijara contract and state the legal consequence of the craftsman violating the owner's instructions.

Core Idea
In a dispute over work specifications, the owner's word with oath prevails; the craftsman is liable if he violates the order.

When a craftsman (tailor or dyer) disagrees with the cloth owner about what was ordered – for example, the owner claims he ordered a cloak but the tailor says a shirt – how is the dispute resolved? This lesson presents the Hanafi ruling that the owner's statement, supported by his oath, is taken as true. If he swears, the craftsman becomes liable for the cloth. This ruling protects the trustor's rights in hire contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the dispute
The dispute involves the cloth owner and a hired craftsman (tailor or dyer). The owner claims one specification (e.g., cloak), the craftsman claims another (e.g., shirt).
2
Determine whose statement is given precedence
The Hanafi rule is 'al-qawl qawl sahib al-thawb' – the owner's statement is accepted. This is because the owner is the trustor (mu'min) and his property is at stake; the craftsman is the trustee (amin).
3
Recognize the evidentiary requirement: the oath
The owner's statement must be supported by his oath (yamin). If he swears, his claim is upheld; if he refuses, the craftsman may be believed.
4
Apply the legal consequence: liability of the craftsman
If the owner swears, the craftsman is liable (damn) for the cloth or the difference in value. He also forfeits his wage because he violated the order, as supported by the athar of Umar (RA).

Subcontracting in Ijarah: When the Worker Must Work Personally
Ruling on subcontracting in ijarah with proof from athar and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between cases where a hired worker may or may not subcontract the work and apply the Hanafi ruling with its proofs.

Core Idea
A condition in the contract binds the worker; violation makes him liable.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in ijarah (hiring contracts): when a craftsman is hired to do a job, can he get someone else to do it? The answer depends on the terms of the contract. If the worker was specifically designated, he must perform personally; otherwise, he may delegate. Let's examine the ruling and its evidence from the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Scenarios
The text presents two opposite rulings based on whether the contract specifies personal work or leaves it general. First, identify the condition that triggers each ruling.
2
State the Ruling for Each Scenario
For the first scenario (worker specified): subcontracting is not allowed, and the worker is liable if he does so. For the second (no specification): subcontracting is permissible.
3
Locate the Proof
The proof for the first ruling includes an athar from al-Sha'bi that the worker is liable (dhamin) for violating the condition, and the hadith 'Muslims are bound by their conditions.' Understand how these proofs support the ruling.
4
Analyze the Dalalah
The hadith 'المسلمون عند شروطهم' indicates that conditions in contracts are binding (wajib al-wafa'). Thus, specifying personal work creates an obligation that the worker must fulfill himself.
5
Apply the Ruling to Practical Cases
Consider real-life examples: hiring a tailor to sew a dress with the condition that he does it himself vs. hiring a construction company without naming specific workers. Apply the ruling accordingly.

Dispute Between Worker and Customer Over Work Type
Ruling on disputes about the type of work ordered

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi evidentiary rule that the customer's word with oath is accepted in disputes over the type of work ordered, and explain the proof from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
The defendant's word with oath prevails when the claimant lacks evidence.

This lesson addresses a common dispute in ijarah contracts: the customer claims he ordered one type of work (e.g., a qaba), but the worker claims another (e.g., a qamis). Who is believed? The Hanafi school rules that the customer's word is taken with his oath. This procedural rule is rooted in the general principle that the oath is on the defendant, supported by explicit proofs from the Sunnah and the practice of early scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
The customer (sahib al-thawb) claims he ordered a specific job (e.g., qaba or red dye). The worker (tailor/dyer) claims a different job was ordered (e.g., qamis or yellow dye).
2
Determine who is claimant and who is defendant
The worker is the claimant (mudda'i) because he demands wages for the work performed. The customer is the defendant (mudda'a alayh) because he denies that the work was as ordered.
Per Hanafi usul, the claimant must bring evidence; the defendant takes an oath.
3
Apply the evidentiary principle
Since the worker (claimant) cannot produce evidence, the customer's statement is accepted with his oath. If the customer swears, the worker becomes liable for the loss (the cloth).
This follows the general rule: 'al-bayyina 'ala al-mudda'i wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara.'
4
Identify the proofs
The text cites an athar from al-Thawri in a mudaraba dispute where the owner's word is taken, and the hadith from Bukhari: the Prophet judged that the oath is upon the defendant.
These proofs establish the principle for ijarah by qiyas.
5
State the ruling and its consequence
The ruling: al-qawl qawl sahib al-thawb (the customer's word is accepted). Consequence: if the customer swears, the worker is liable for the cloth (damages) and loses his wages.

Burden of Proof in Wage Disputes: The Customer's Word with Oath
Ruling on dispute over gratuitous work vs. paid work

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of burden of proof in disputes over wages vs. gratuitous work.

Core Idea
In a wage dispute, the worker's claim is not automatically accepted; the customer's denial with oath suffices when proof is absent.

This passage addresses a common dispute in hire contracts: the customer claims the work was done for free, while the worker demands wages. Imam Abu Hanifa rules that the customer's word with oath is accepted, based on the general principle that the burden of proof lies on the claimant. We will examine the ruling, its evidence, and the procedural logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and their claims
Recognise the customer (صاحب الثوب) who claims the work was without wages (بغير اجرة) and the craftsman (الصانع) who claims it was for wages (باجرة).
The craftsman is also called the 'worker' or 'ajir'.
2
Classify who is claimant (mudda'i) and who is denier (munkir)
The craftsman claims wages, so he is the mudda'i seeking to establish a right. The customer denies, so he is the munkir.
In Hanafi law, the claimant must bring proof; the denier's denial with oath is sufficient.
3
Apply the evidentiary rule
The hadith states: 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier.' Since the craftsman has no witnesses, the customer's oath is accepted.
This rule applies except in qasamah (oath for blood money).
4
Derive the final ruling
The customer's word with oath prevails; the craftsman receives no wages.
The contract is thus considered gratuitous (gift or tabarru' work).

Burden of Proof in Ijarah: The Owner's Oath
Ruling on burden of proof in ijarah disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that in disputes between a cloth owner and an artisan, the owner's denial under oath prevails, and identify the claimant (artisan) and defendant (owner) based on the hadith 'al-bayyinah 'ala man idda'a'.

Core Idea
Without evidence, the owner's denial under oath defeats the artisan's wage claim.

When a cloth owner and an artisan disagree about what work was ordered or whether a wage was agreed, who should the judge believe? This lesson examines two scenarios from the Book of Ijarah: a dyer who dyes the wrong color, and a dispute over whether the work was for free or for a fee. Abu Hanifa rules that the owner's statement is taken with his oath. The key is identifying who is the claimant (mudda'i) and who is the defendant (munkir), then applying the prophetic hadith: 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the defendant.'

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenarios
Read the two cases: (1) artisan ordered to dye red but dyes yellow, owner refuses wage; (2) owner says work was without wage, artisan says with wage. Note that in both, the artisan claims entitlement to a wage and the owner denies.
The first case involves a defect, the second a pure wage dispute.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each
Abu Hanifa holds that in both cases, the owner's statement is accepted when accompanied by his oath. If the owner swears, the artisan is liable for the cloth's value (case 1) or loses the wage claim (case 2).
The ruling is the same: 'al-qawl qawl sahib al-thawb ma'a yaminihi'.
3
Analyze the mudda'i / munkir dynamic
The artisan claims a right (wage or that his work was correct) – he is the mudda'i (claimant). The owner denies – he is the munkir (defendant). In Hanafi law, the defendant's statement is accepted with oath if the claimant has no evidence.
The artisan's claim contradicts the apparent state (zahir) that work was done without additional agreement.
4
Locate and apply the dalil
The hadith 'al-bayyinah 'ala man idda'a wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara' (Bukhari, Darqutni) is the primary evidence. It places the burden of proof on the claimant (artisan) and the oath on the defendant (owner). The commentary also cites an athar from Sufyan al-Thawri on mudaraba for analogy.
This hadith is a 'amma' (general) rule accepted across schools, though its application may vary.

Abu Yusuf's Exception: The Hareef's Presumption of Wage
Abu Yusuf's ruling on wage dispute when the artisan is a 'hareef' (regular customer).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Yusuf distinguishes between a 'hareef' and a non-professional artisan in a wage dispute, state the thamarah al-khilaf, and evaluate which position is mufta bihi.

Core Idea
An artisan's profession or prior dealing creates a presumption of wage—Abu Yusuf's exception to the default rule.

In this passage, we encounter an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Yusuf and his teacher Abu Hanifa. While Abu Hanifa held that in a wage dispute the owner's denial with oath always prevails, Abu Yusuf introduced a nuance: if the artisan is a 'hareef'—meaning he either practices that trade or has previously worked for wages for this same owner—then his claim to a wage is accepted. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions

Look at the text: 'hareef' is explained as either a professional in that trade or someone who has previously done similar work for wages for the same owner.

Analyze Abu Yusuf's dalil
Abu Yusuf uses 'zahir al-hal' (apparent circumstance) as evidence. The fact that the artisan is a professional or has a prior wage relationship indicates that the work was done for pay.
This is a form of qiyas based on prevalent custom ('urf).

Abu Hanifa relies on the principle that the defendant's oath is decisive (al-bayyinah 'ala man idda'a) and the default is that work is without wage unless proven.

In practice, if the artisan is a known professional, Abu Yusuf rules wage is due; Abu Hanifa rules it is not due without proof. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is generally Abu Hanifa's, but Abu Yusuf's view is applied in certain contexts.
Later Hanafi masters like Al-Marghinani follow Abu Hanifa.

When an ijarah contract is invalid due to a defect (fasid), the worker's labor is not wasted. The law ensures fair compensation through the equivalent wage (ajr al-mithl). However, a key limitation protects the employer: if the agreed wage is lower, the worker cannot demand more.

Ujrat al-Mithl in Fasid Ijarah and Possession Liability
Explains ujrat al-mithl in fasid ijarah and effect of possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ujrat al-mithl in fasid ijarah and explain how possession triggers rent liability.

Core Idea
In fasid ijarah, ujrat al-mithl is due but capped at the named wage; possession alone makes rent obligatory.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Sameri clarifies two key issues in ijarah: the wage due in an invalid lease (fasid) and when rent becomes obligatory. Understanding these rules is essential for both contracting parties and for judges resolving disputes about rental obligations. The author presents evidence from Quran and the practice of early authorities (athar).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Distinguish between the rule on ujrat al-mithl in fasid ijarah (capped at the named wage) and the rule on rent being due upon possession even without use.
These are two separate but related principles.
2
Locate the dalil for each ruling
For the first ruling, the author cites the Quranic verse 'bil ma'ruf' to support custom-based wage. For the second, he cites the athar from Ibn Tawus and al-Thawri showing that rent becomes due upon possession and is recalculated if possession is lost.
The Quranic dalil is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalala; the athar strengthen the ruling.
3
Note the inter-madhhab dispute
The Shafi'i school holds that ujrat al-mithl is due without any cap, even if exceeding the named wage. The Hanafi school caps it at the named wage. Understand the basis of each view.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear in court judgments.
4
Apply the possession rule to the usurpation scenario
If the lessee takes possession, rent is due. If a usurper takes it from the lessee's hand, the rent drops for the period of usurpation because the lessee no longer has possession.
This follows the principle that possession (qabd) is the trigger for liability.
5
Synthesize the legal principles
Combine both: In fasid ijarah, the lessee must pay ujrat al-mithl (capped by named wage) and this obligation arises upon possession, not use. Loss of possession due to usurpation suspends the obligation.
This synthesis helps in complex cases where both issues arise together.

Defects and Destruction in Ijarah: Option to Rescind vs Automatic Termination
Explains that a defect harming habitation gives option to cancel; destruction terminates lease automatically.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a defect that gives the lessee the option to rescind and destruction that automatically terminates the ijarah, and apply the correct ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
A defect giving option to rescind differs from destruction that ends the lease automatically.

This lesson addresses two common problems in ijarah: when the property develops a defect, and when it is destroyed. The Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether the subject-matter still exists. If it does but is defective, the lessee has a right to rescind. If it ceases to exist, the contract dissolves automatically. The hadith of the slave provides the proof for the first ruling, and legal reasoning (istiḥsān) supports the second.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition of the leased property
Determine whether the property still exists but has a defect (e.g., a wall collapses) or whether it is destroyed or its benefit permanently lost (e.g., the house collapses completely, water supply cut off).
If the property exists but the defect is minor and not harmful to habitation, no right to rescind.
2
Apply the ruling to each scenario
For a harmful defect: the lessee has the right to rescind (faskh), but if he consents to the defect after it appears, he loses that right. For destruction/permanent loss: the ijarah terminates automatically (insikhā'), and no consent is needed.
Some earlier Hanafi authorities hold the second scenario also gives only a right to rescind, but the relied-upon view is automatic termination.
3
Understand the proof (dalīl)
The hadīth of the slave (Abu Dawud 3510) shows that a defect allows rescission in sales, and by analogy (qiyās) it applies to ijarah. The principle 'al-kharaj bi'd-daman' is derived from it, meaning the lessee owes rent for the period of beneficial use.
This hadīth is aḥad (zannī al-thubūt) but accepted by the Hanafīs.
4
Note the practical consequences
If the lessee rescinds due to defect, he owes rent for the time he used the property. If the contract terminates automatically, future rent is no longer due, but past rent for beneficial use is owed.
The lessee cannot rescind if he knew of the defect at the time of contracting or later consented to it.

Commentary
This passage covers two distinct scenarios regarding defects in leased property. Core Idea
A defect harmful to the benefit allows rescission, but consent blocks it; benefit used must be paid for.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Khiyar al-shart is valid in ijarah, just as in sale, based on general hadith and analogy.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh: the validity of stipulating an option to rescind (khiyar al-shart) in an ijarah (lease) contract. The author declares that such an option is valid, just as it is in sale, and provides proof from the Sunnah. We will explore the legal reasoning and the evidence that supports this position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage states: 'ويصح شرط الخيار في الاجارة كما في البيع' – the stipulation of an option is valid in ijarah just as in sale. Identify that this is a ruling of permissibility (sahih), not obligation.
The ruling is about the validity of the stipulation, not the contract itself.
2
Recognize the qiyas argument
The text says ijarah is also a contract ('عقد') like sale. Ijarah is a commutative contract (mu'awadah), so the same rule applies. Identify the analogy between sale and ijarah.
Qiyas is the method used to extend the ruling from sale to ijarah.
3
Locate the dalil for sale
The primary proof for khiyar al-shart in sale is the hadith: 'الخيار ثلاثة ايام' from al-Daraqutni, and also the hadith of khiyar al-majlis from Bukhari and Muslim. Understand that these are used as the basis for the analogy.
The dalil is a hadith ahad, which is zanni in both transmission and meaning.
4
Apply the dalil to ijarah
Since ijarah is an exchange contract similar to sale, the same hadith applies. The option period is three days, as per the hadith. The Hanafi school thus allows khiyar al-shart in ijarah up to three days.
The general phrase 'al-khiyar thalathatu ayyam' is not limited to sale.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school does not allow khiyar al-shart in ijarah because it leads to gharar (uncertainty) regarding the period of use. Identify this as a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that a Shafi'i would not permit such a stipulation.

Borrowed Item as a Trust: The Hanafi Ruling
Declares that the borrowed item is held as a trust by the borrower.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a borrowed item is a trust, identify the condition that triggers borrower liability, and cite the primary proofs from hadith and athar.

Core Idea
A borrowed item is a trust; the borrower is not liable unless he transgresses the terms of use.

This lesson clarifies the legal status of a borrowed item in Hanafi fiqh. Many assume that any loss makes the borrower responsible, but the school holds that the default is amanah (trust) — no liability for accidental loss. The key is whether the borrower committed ta'addi (transgression). We will examine the proofs from the Sunnah that establish this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Category
State that the borrowed item is an amanah (trust) in the borrower's hand, not a guarantee (daman). The borrower is not liable for accidental loss.
This is the default status; liability arises only with misuse.
2
Locate the Primary Proofs
The text cites two proofs: the hadith of Safwan ibn Ya'la (Abu Dawud) and the athar from 'Ali ibn Abi Talib (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq). Identify each and note that they are both ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Analyze the Hadith
The Prophet distinguished between 'ariyah madmūnah (guaranteed) and 'ariyah mu'addāh (returned), affirming that the borrowed item falls under the latter. This proves it is not guaranteed.
The phrase 'bal mu'addāh' directly indicates that the item is merely to be returned, not compensated if lost.
4
Analyze the Athar of 'Ali
The athar explicitly states: 'The 'ariyah is not guaranteed; it is only a favor unless he violates (terms), then he becomes liable.' This confirms the amanah status and introduces the condition of ta'addi.
The athar became the basis for the Hanafi rule: ta'addi transforms the trust into a guarantee.
5
Extract the Condition for Liability
From the athar, the condition that triggers liability is yukhalif (violation) or ta'addi. Without it, the borrower remains a trustee. This is the practical application of the rule.
Ta'addi includes using the item beyond permission or negligence.
