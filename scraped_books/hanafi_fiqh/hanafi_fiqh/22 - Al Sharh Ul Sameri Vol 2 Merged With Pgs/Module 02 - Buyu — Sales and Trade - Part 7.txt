Sale with Customarily Determined Fulus Price
Ruling on sale with price expressed as fulus equivalent to half a dirham

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling that a sale with a price expressed as fulus equivalent to a known dirham amount is valid despite minor uncertainty, and distinguish it from invalid uncertainty based on the principle of jahāla yasīra.

Core Idea
A slight uncertainty about the price is excusable if custom resolves it — validity depends on whether the ignorance is minor and removable.

What happens when a buyer says, 'I'll pay you the number of fulus that equals half a dirham' without naming the exact count? This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling that such a sale is valid because the uncertainty is minor and resolved by local custom. We'll also explore a difference of opinion within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The buyer purchases a specific item (e.g., cloth) for a price expressed as 'fulus equivalent to half a dirham'. The exact number of fulus is not stated at the time of contract. This creates an apparent uncertainty (jahāla) in the price.
Fulus are copper coins whose market value fluctuates, but the reference to half a dirham provides a known benchmark.
2
Classify the Uncertainty
Determine whether the jahāla is minor (yasīr) or major (fāḥish). Here, the uncertainty is minor because the customary number of fulus for half a dirham is well-known among merchants in that locality.
Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between jahāla yasīra (excusable) and jahāla fāḥisha (invalidates the contract).
3
Apply the Custom Principle
Custom (ʿurf) resolves the ambiguity. The buyer must pay the amount of fulus that is customary for half a dirham in that market. Since custom makes the price determinable, the price is not considered unknown (majhūl).
The ruling relies on the presumption that both parties consent to the customary value.
4
State the Ruling
The sale is valid (jāʾiz / ṣaḥīḥ). The buyer must pay the customary number of fulus for half a dirham in that locality.
The author of the text (Al-Quduri / Al-Marghinani) affirms validity as the muftā bihī position.
5
Note the Intra-Hanafi Disagreement
Imam Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) considers even this slight uncertainty sufficient to render the sale corrupt (fāsid). However, the relied-upon position (muftā bihī) is that of the majority of the Ḥanafī imams: validity.
This difference illustrates that not all Ḥanafī authorities agree on the threshold of jahāla yasīra.

Mixed Currency Exchange: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn on Riba Spread
Intra-Hanafi dispute on spread of corruption in a mixed currency transaction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on a combined currency exchange with deficiency and explain why the thamarah al-khilaf matters.

Core Idea
A single bargain with one riba component is entirely void (Abu Hanifa); the Sahibayn split the contract into two, preserving the valid part.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute over a transaction with a moneychanger. The buyer asks for both fulus (copper coins) and a dirham that is deficient in weight, all as a single bargain. Abu Hanifa rules the whole sale void because the riba corrupts the entire contract, while the Sahibayn treat the fulus exchange as separate and valid. Understanding this dispute sharpens the Hanafi principle of safqa wahida versus internal separability.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Abu Hanifa holds that the entire sale is corrupt (fasid) because the transaction is a single bargain (safqa wahida) and the riba from the outset taints the whole.
This applies even though the fulus part would be valid in isolation.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani distinguish two separate transactions: the fulus exchange is valid, and only the deficient dirham part is corrupt.
They consider the two components internally separable.
3
Identify the core issue
The dispute hinges on whether corruption (fasād) spreads (yasrī) from a defective part to the whole when the contract is concluded as one bargain.
Abu Hanifa emphasizes unity; the Sahibayn emphasize separability.
4
Examine the evidence
Both sides rely on the nature of the offer and acceptance. The phrase 'give me for half of it fulus and for half of it a dirham except a grain' is read as either a single proposal or two simultaneous proposals.
No explicit textual dalil; the reasoning is based on the structure of the contract.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, the buyer gets nothing and must return both fulus and the deficient dirham; under the Sahibayn, the buyer keeps the fulus and returns only the deficient dirham.
This affects the parties' obligations and potential legal claims.

Riba Spreading in a Single Contract: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on riba when mixing fulus and silver

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether riba in one part of a single sale contract invalidates the entire contract, and distinguish Abu Hanifa's position from that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A single sale with riba in part is entirely void for Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn split the contract to save the valid part.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute (ikhtilaf) between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The case involves a moneychanger who receives one dirham and is asked to give fulus (copper coins) worth half a dirham and a silver coin weighing half a dirham minus one qirat. The silver-for-silver exchange involves a deficiency, constituting riba al-fadl. The central question is whether this riba contaminates the entire single transaction or only the part directly affected, revealing a fundamental disagreement about the indivisibility of a single contractual deal (safqah wahidah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Transaction
Understand the scenario: a customer gives one dirham to a moneychanger and requests two items in return: fulus worth half a dirham and a silver coin weighing half a dirham minus one qirat. The total consideration is one dirham, but the silver part involves a deficiency.
The fulus are a different genus (copper) than silver, so no riba in that part.
2
State the Two Positions
Abu Hanifa: the entire sale is fasid (corrupt) because the contract is a single safqah and the corruption spreads. Sahibayn: the sale is valid for the fulus part and invalid only for the silver part; the two parts are treated as separate transactions.
This is a classic example of the 'spread of corruption' principle.
3
Analyze the Reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that the customer's request is a single offer ('give me...') making it one deal; therefore, invalidity in any part invalidates the whole. The Sahibayn consider the contract divisible: the fulus exchange is independent and valid, while the silver exchange is separate and invalid due to riba.
The key concept is safqah wahidah vs. ta'addud al-'uqud (multiple contracts).
4
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa, the moneychanger must return the dirham and take back both the fulus and the silver coin. Under the Sahibayn, the fulus part stands; the silver coin transaction is void, so the customer keeps the fulus and the moneychanger keeps the dirham proportionate to that part? Actually, the dirham is the price for both; the resolution would be to return the deficient silver coin and adjust the price accordingly.
Later Hanafi fatwa often prefers the Sahibayn's view to preserve the validity of transactions.
5
Reflect on the Principle
This dispute illustrates a broader Hanafi principle: when a contract combines valid and invalid elements, does the invalidity spread? The answer depends on whether the contract is considered unitary or composite. The Sahibayn favor preserving validity where possible.
Compare with the rule in other schools: e.g., the Shafi'i approach to partial invalidity.

Commentary
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute (mas'alah ikhtilafiyyah) between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). The case: a person gives a moneychanger one silver dirham and asks for in return: (a) fulus worth half a dirham, and (b) a silver coin weighing half a dirham less one qirat. The total given by the customer is one dirham; he receives fulus and a smaller silver coin. The issue is that in the part involving silver-for-silver (the half dirham given for the deficient coin), there is a deficiency of one qirat, which constitutes riba al-fadl (usury of excess). The question is whether this riba invalidates the entire single sale or only the part involving silver. Abu Hanifa holds that the entire sale is invalid (fasid) because the contract is a single 'safqah' (deal) and corruption spreads through the entire contract. The Sahibayn reason that the transaction can be split into two independent exchanges: (i) half dirham for fulus (different genus, no riba) and (ii) half dirham for a deficient silver coin (same genus, riba). They rule that the fulus part is valid, and only the silver-for-silver part is invalid, and the corruption does not spread. The commentary explains the reasoning behind each position.

Key Terms
صفقة واحدة (safqah wahidah): A single contractual transaction or deal. In Hanafi law, if a single contract contains both valid and invalid elements, its unity means that the invalid part can spread and render the whole contract invalid, according to Abu Hanifa.
ربا (riba): Usury or interest, specifically in this context, riba al-fadl: inequality in exchange of like items (silver for silver).
صيرفي (sairfi/sayrafi): Moneychanger, a person who exchanges coins, especially different currencies or precious metals.

Offsetting Deficiency with a Different Genus in Exchange
Ruling on composite exchange with deficiency offset by different genus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a deficiency in a part of a composite exchange is offset by the inclusion of a different genus, preventing riba, and apply this principle to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
A deficiency in silver is neutralized when paired with a different genus (fulus) in a single exchange, avoiding riba.

This lesson examines a specific sale scenario where a silver coin is deficient. The ruling demonstrates a key Hanafi principle: riba only occurs if the same genus is exchanged with inequality; a different genus absorbs the imbalance. Understanding this helps avoid invalid transactions in composite exchanges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of the exchange
The buyer gives one dirham (silver). He receives: (a) fulus (copper coins) worth half a dirham, and (b) a silver coin that is half a dirham less one qirat. Note that fulus are a different genus from silver.
2
Recognize the deficiency
The silver coin received is deficient by one qirat compared to half a dirham. If this were a pure silver-for-silver exchange, the inequality would cause riba al-fadl.
Qirat is a small unit of weight; 1 qirat ≈ 0.2 grams.
3
Apply the different genus principle
The fulfillment is a single transaction. The deficient silver coin is paired conceptually with the half dirham of the price, and the remaining one qirat of value (the surplus) is covered by the fulus. Since fulus are a different genus, no riba occurs.
The commentary states: 'the deficient half silver will be matched against the deficient half dirham, and the remaining surplus one qirat will be matched against the fulus for half a dirham.'
4
Confirm the validity
All scholars agree the sale is valid (sahih). The deficiency is neutralized by the presence of a different genus in the composite exchange.
This is a unanimous Hanafi ruling.

A mixed exchange is valid if same-genus portion is equal and different-genus portion allows excess.

Validity of a Mixed Exchange with Matched Silver Deficits
Ruling on a single exchange with matched silver deficit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a mixed exchange of dirham for fulus and a deficit dirham, and explain why the matched deficit prevents riba.

Core Idea
A deficit in silver is harmless if exactly matched by an equal deficit from the other silver, with the surplus going to a different commodity.

Imagine offering one dirham and requesting half a dirham worth of fulus and half a dirham minus one rati in dirhams. At first glance, this appears to involve an unequal exchange of silver, potentially usurious. However, the Hanafi school validates this transaction by analyzing it as two offsetting exchanges: the silver deficit is matched, and the extra portion goes to fulus. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
List the items being exchanged
The buyer gives one full dirham (silver coin). The seller gives (a) fulus (copper coins) worth half a dirham, and (b) a silver piece weighing half a dirham minus one rati.
2
Separate the silver-for-silver component
The seller's silver (half dirham minus one rati) is matched against an equal weight of silver from the buyer's dirham. This leaves a remnant of silver from the buyer's dirham.
The deficit in silver on the seller's side is exactly the weight difference between half a dirham and half minus one rati.
3
Match the remnant with the fulus
The remaining silver from the buyer's dirham (one rati) is exchanged for the fulus. Since fulus are a different genus (copper vs silver), no riba occurs in that part.
4
Apply the principle of riba al-fadl
Riba al-fadl forbids any surplus in the exchange of the same genus. Here, the silver exchanged is exactly matched in weight; the surplus is against a different commodity.
5
Conclude the ruling
Since there is no uncompensated surplus of silver, the entire sale is valid (sahih).
This conclusion is according to all Hanafi authorities.

Ruling on exchange of a small dirham for its weight and fulus

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Equality in same-species exchange permits surplus to a different genus without riba.

This third case in the series tests the boundary of riba in currency exchange. The customer gives a full dirham and asks for a small dirham (weight half minus one rati) and the remainder in fulus (copper coins). The Hanafi ruling is that the sale is valid because the silver portion is exchanged equally and the surplus is against a different commodity. This builds on the principle established by the hadith and generalised across similar cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a sale (bay') of a full dirham for a small dirham (half dirham minus one rati) and fulus for the remaining weight. The ruling: the sale is valid (sahih) because the silver exchange is equal and the surplus (half dirham plus one rati) is compensated by fulus, a different genus.
The small dirham weighs exactly half minus one rati, so the silver exchanged is equal weight for equal weight.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites the hadith 'مثلا بمثل يدا بيد' (like for like, hand to hand) as the principle. This hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim prohibits any inequality or delay in exchanging same-species items (e.g., gold for gold, silver for silver).
The hadith is qat'i al-thubut (certain transmission) but zanni al-dalalah (interpretation-dependent) regarding riba al-fadl.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'like for like' requires equality in weight and immediate delivery. The Hanafis infer that if equality is maintained for the same species, any surplus exchanged for a different species is not riba because the surplus is not of the same genus. Here, the silver part is equal; the extra silver (half dirham plus one rati) is exchanged for fulus, a different currency genus.
This distinction between silver and copper (fulus) is crucial: fulus are not considered a ribawi item in the same category as silver.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require that all components of a single sale be exactly balanced if the same species appears, or they may treat the entire dirham as a single unit. The Hanafi school allows splitting the sale into two concurrent exchanges: silver for silver (equal) and surplus for fulus.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate; both schools strive to uphold the prohibition of riba.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position as stated by the author of Al-Sharh al-Thamiri is that the sale is valid. This is the mufta bihi (operative fatwa) in the Hanafi school, relying on the explicit division of the sale into two distinct exchanges based on the parties' intent.

When property is given as a pawn (rahn), a natural question arises: who is responsible for its upkeep, and who benefits from any increase it produces? Perishment of Principal Pawn: Redemption of Increase
Ruling on redemption when principal perishes and increase remains

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the pawner's redemption payment when the principal pawn perishes and the increase remains, applying the Hanafi proportional division of debt.

Core Idea
When principal pawn perishes, debt is proportionally canceled; increase is redeemed by paying its share.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in pawn (rahn) transactions: the principal pawned item is destroyed, but its increase (like offspring) survives. The Hanafi school provides a precise method to determine how much of the debt is canceled and how much the pawner must pay to reclaim the increase. Understanding this ruling prevents common errors in proportional allocation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its context
The passage rules that when the principal (asl) perishes but the increase (nama') remains, the pawner redeems the increase by paying its share of the debt. The debt is divided proportionally based on the values of the principal at seizure and the increase at redemption.
This is the standard Hanafi position; no intra-school dispute is recorded.
2
Understand the valuation dates
The principal's value is taken on the day the pledgee (murtahin) took possession (qabd). The increase's value is taken on the day of redemption (fakak). This is because increase grows over time, and its value at redemption matters.
This ensures fairness: the increase is valued when it is actually being redeemed.
3
Apply the proportional allocation rule
The debt is divided between principal and increase in proportion to their values at the specified dates. The principal's share is canceled because it perished; the increase's share must be paid to redeem the increase.
The rule derives from the principle that the pawn secures the debt proportionally.
4
Work through the example step-by-step
Debt: 9. Goat pawned at value 10, later destroyed. Kid born, value at redemption: 5. Total value at redemption: 15 (but principal value at seizure for division). Division: goat's share of debt = 10/15 × 9 = 6 (canceled). Kid's share = 5/15 × 9 = 3 (payable).
The pawner pays 3 to redeem the kid; the remaining debt (6) is canceled.
5
Reflect on the underlying rationale (talil)
The ruling is based on the idea that each part of the pawn secures its portion of the debt. The principal's destruction extinguishes its security portion; the increase remains secured by its portion, so it must be redeemed.
This talil shows that the pawn is not a single security but a proportional one.

Core Idea
Increasing the pledge is valid; the new collateral joins the original to secure the same debt.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Increasing the Debt (Ziyada fi al-Dayn)
Ruling on prohibition of increasing debt with intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the majority of Hanafi scholars forbid increasing the debt secured by an existing pledge and evaluate Abu Yusuf's opposing view.

Core Idea
Increasing the debt against the same pledge is invalid—the new debt is not secured by the old collateral.

This lesson examines the second ruling from the passage: the prohibition of increasing the debt (ziyada fi al-dayn). There is a notable intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold it invalid, while Abu Yusuf permits it by qiyas with sale. We will analyze their proofs and the practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
The issue is whether the same pledge can secure an additional debt without a new contract or renewal of possession.
This is separate from adding collateral.
2
State the Majority Hanafi Position
Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad rule that it is not permissible (la yajuz) and the pledge does not become a pledge for the second debt.
3
Present Abu Yusuf's Argument
Abu Yusuf permits it by qiyas (analogy) with a sale: just as the price in a sale can be increased and remain secured by the sold item, so can the debt remain secured by the pledge.
This is an analogy of extending a contract.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response is that the analogy is weak because in a sale, the seller retains ownership until full payment; in a pledge, the creditor already possesses the item for the first debt. Adding a second debt would imply the creditor possesses it for both, which is not supported by the original contract.
The possession (qabd) is specific to the first debt.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
If the pledge is destroyed, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, only the first debt is deducted from its value; the second debt is unsecured. According to Abu Yusuf, both debts are deducted.
This practical consequence matters in financial transactions.

The two goats together become collateral for the original nine pounds. If they are destroyed, the debt is deducted proportionally from both. The second ruling (41) addresses increasing the debt itself (ziyada fi al-dayn). Here, if the pledger wants to borrow more money against the same collateral, Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad rule that this is invalid: the additional debt will not be secured by the original pledge. The underlying reasoning is that the pledge is already fully possessed (maqbud) by the creditor for the first debt, so a second debt cannot be included. The verse (Quran 2:283) mentions a pledge that is possessed, so the goat is fully possessed for the first debt. Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees and permits adding to the debt, analogizing the price (thaman) in a sale — just as the price can be increased later and included under the sold item, so can the debt and pledge. The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

الزيادة في الدين (al-ziyada fi al-dayn): Increasing the amount of the debt secured by the same collateral.
مرهون مقبوض (marhun maqbud): A pledge that is physically possessed by the creditor.

Ruling on proportional liability for creditors in joint pledge

Core Idea
When a joint pledge is destroyed, each creditor bears loss proportional to their debt share – not the full debt.

This passage builds on the concept of joint pledge (رهن مشترك). It answers a critical practical question: if the pledged item is destroyed, what happens to the debts? The Hanafi ruling is clear – the loss is shared proportionally. This prevents injustice to any creditor and ensures that the debtor remains liable for the unpaid balance.

The debtor remains obligated to pay the balance.

Sale Conditioned on Pledge: Validity and Seller's Option
Ruling on sale with pledge condition and istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on a sale conditioned with a pledge, distinguishing between the validity of the sale and the enforceability of the condition.

Core Idea
The condition to pledge does not invalidate the sale, but the buyer cannot be forced to fulfill it.

This lesson examines a specific sale scenario where the seller requires the buyer to pledge a specific item as collateral for the price. The Hanafi school, using istihsan, upholds the sale's validity despite the extra condition, but the buyer's refusal to pledge does not void the contract—instead, the seller gains an option to either waive the condition or annul the sale, unless the buyer pays immediately or provides substitute collateral.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Condition and Its Legal Nature
The seller conditions that the buyer pledges a specific item for the price. Recognize that this is an additional condition beyond the essential terms of sale (price and object).
Pledging (rahn) is a voluntary act (tabarru') in Hanafi law.
2
Apply Qiyas: Why the Condition Would Invalidate the Sale
By analogy (qiyas), the condition resembles a prohibited 'two sales in one' due to the hadith (Sunan Abi Dawud). The extra condition introduces uncertainty and potential usury, making the sale fasid (invalid).
The hadith text is quoted in the source.
3
Apply Istihsan: Why the Hanafi School Upholds the Sale
The Hanafi school employs istihsan (juristic preference) because the condition supports the sale by providing security. Istihsan overrides the harsh result of qiyas, making the sale sahih (valid).
Istihsan is a recognized source; the condition is beneficial and does not conflict with the essence of the sale.
4
Determine the Consequences of Buyer's Refusal to Pledge
Since pledging is voluntary, the buyer cannot be forced. The seller then has an option: either accept the sale without the pledge or rescind the sale entirely.
The option is a khiyar (choice) for the seller due to the unfulfilled condition.
5
Identify Exceptions That Remove Seller's Option
The seller's option to annul is removed if the buyer pays the price immediately or pays the value of the pledged item as substitute collateral. This binds the sale.
The buyer can 'cure' the defect by providing immediate payment or alternative security.

Interdiction of Minor, Slave, and Insane from Transactions
States rulings on interdiction of minor, slave, and insane from transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the interdiction of minors, slaves, and the insane, identify the proof from hadith, and explain the suspended (mawquf) status of their transactions.

Core Idea
Transactions of minors, slaves, and the insane are suspended or invalid without permission, due to lack of capacity or ownership.

This lesson introduces the foundational rulings of interdiction (hajr) in Hanafi fiqh. We will examine why minors, slaves, and the insane are restricted from transacting, and how their transactions can become valid through guardian or master permission. The hadith proofs reinforce the principle that valid transactions require both ownership and legal capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories and their rulings
List the three types of persons whose transactions are restricted: minor, slave, and insane. For each, state the basic ruling (not permissible without permission) and the specific nuance (e.g., slave has intelligence but lacks ownership).
The minor lacks complete intellect; the slave lacks property ownership; the insane lacks intellect entirely or intermittently.
2
Locate the proofs for each interdiction
For the minor and insane, the proof is the hadith 'the pen is lifted' (رفع القلم). For the slave, the proof is the hadith 'no sale except in what you own' (لا بيع إلا فيما تملك). Identify the source and the legal reasoning (wajh al-dalalah).
The slave's hadith is from Abu Dawud and is ahad, making the ruling zanni in both transmission and meaning.
3
Distinguish between the two types of insane persons
Differentiate between the insane person who occasionally recovers (majnun yafiqu) and the one whose mind is permanently overwhelmed. Note that transactions during lucidity are valid; otherwise they are invalid.
This distinction is crucial for determining the validity of past transactions.
4
Understand the suspended (mawquf) status
Recognize that the minor's and slave's transactions are not absolutely void but suspended (mawquf) upon guardian/master permission. If permission is given later, the transaction becomes valid retroactively.
This is a key Hanafi principle: lack of capacity does not make the contract batil, but rather inoperative until ratification.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
The Shafi'i school holds that a discerning minor's transaction is valid if beneficial. Compare this to the Hanafi requirement of guardian permission. Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).
This ikhtilaf is based on different interpretations of the hadith and the definition of capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a guardian may ratify or annul a transaction made by a minor, slave, or insane person, and apply the Hanafi ruling to a given scenario.

Core Idea
The guardian's khiyar ensures a transaction serves the ward's benefit, but only if the ward understood and intended it.

When a minor, slave, or insane person enters a transaction with understanding and serious intent, the transaction is not automatically void—it is suspended (mawquf) pending the guardian's approval. The guardian has the option (khiyar) to ratify or annul based on the ward's best interest (maslaha). This lesson unpacks the condition of understanding and intent, the guardian's discretionary power, and the proof from the Sunnah that supports this Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties subject to interdiction
The passage mentions three categories: the minor (sabi), the slave (mamluk, though interdiction here is limited), and the insane (majnun). Not all their actions are invalid; some can be valid with guardian's permission.
Slave interdiction is specific to financial transactions without master's consent.
2
Extract the condition for guardian's khiyar
The text says 'while he understands the sale and intends it' (wa huwa ya'qilu al-bay'a wa yaqsiduhu). The transaction must be comprehended and seriously intended—not in jest or play.
The term 'yaqsiduhu' excludes mere playful utterances.
3
Understand the guardian's option
The guardian may either ratify (ijaza) if there is benefit (maslaha) or annul (faskh) if not. This is a discretionary right, not an obligation.
Ratification makes the transaction binding from the start; annulment nullifies it.
4
Analyze the dalil (proof)
The hadith of Umm Salama appointing her young son 'Umar as her agent for her marriage to the Prophet shows that a discerning minor's action can be valid when authorized. It supports the principle that a child's transaction is mawquf on guardian's permission.
The hadith is from Sunan al-Nasa'i and is zanni al-thubut.
5

The passage also states that contracts of a minor and insane person are not valid (no. 1058). This applies when they lack understanding or if the guardian does not ratify. The earlier ruling (1056) applies specifically to those who understand and intend.
Thus, a discerning minor's transaction is not automatically void; it is suspended.

Core Idea
The Sahibayn protect the bankrupt from harassment unless new wealth is proven.

This lesson examines a nuanced disagreement within the Hanafi school on the consequences of a bankruptcy declaration. Imam Abu Hanifa held that bankruptcy is not a permanent status, allowing creditors to pursue the debtor at any time. However, his two foremost students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), argued that the judge should interpose to protect the bankrupt unless creditors present evidence of new wealth. Guided Path

Identify the point of disagreement
The dispute centers on whether the judge should prevent creditors from pursuing a bankrupt debtor after the declaration of bankruptcy.
The difference is about judicial intervention, not the validity of the debt.

Imam Abu Hanifa held that wealth comes and goes; therefore, bankruptcy is not a permanent state. Creditors may always pursue the debtor to recover their rights.
His reasoning is based on the impermanent nature of wealth.

Analyze the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argued that once the judge declares bankruptcy, the debtor is considered continuously bankrupt. The judge should interpose to protect the debtor from harassment, unless creditors prove new wealth through legal evidence (bayyinah).
This view prioritizes the dignity and protection of the debtor.

The relied-upon position for fatwa is that of the Sahibayn, as it is more protective of the bankrupt's circumstances and adopted by later Hanafi jurists.
This is the standard Hanafi ruling today.

Validity of Istithna in Iqrar: Whole Exception and Dissimilar Kind
Two rulings on exception in iqrar: whole exception and dissimilar kind.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on istithna in iqrar to determine whether the exception is valid and what amount is binding.

Core Idea
Excepting the whole is revocation; excepting a different kind is valid only if both are close in genus as a price.

Confession (iqrar) is a strong legal act—once made, it cannot be undone by a mere exception that leaves nothing. The Hanafi school also restricts the validity of exception to items that are of a close genus to the confessed debt, measured by their suitability as a price (thaman). This lesson unpacks these two crucial rules through concrete examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is making an exception (istithna) within a confession (iqrar). Determine whether the exception is valid or invalid based on two scenarios.
The exception is a statement attached to the confession.
2
Rule 1: Exception of the whole
If the confessor excludes the entire amount confessed, the exception is void and the full confession is binding. This is because exception must leave a remainder; otherwise it is a revocation.
Example: 'I owe you 100 except 100' → 100 is binding.
3

If the excepted item is of a different genus (e.g., a dinar from dirhams), check if both are close in kind as a price (thaman). Dinar and dirham are close; similarly, wheat is close because it can serve as price. Cloth is not close.
If close: exception valid, deduct value. If not close: exception invalid, full amount binding.
4
Understand the criterion: closeness as thaman
The Hanafi criterion is whether the excepted item can be used as a price in transactions. Items measured by weight/volume that are commonly used as money or commodity money are close to dirhams.
This is a fiqh ruling based on customary usage in the market.
