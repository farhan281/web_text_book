Gift of a Share in Undivided Property
Ruling on gifting a fractional share with condition for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for gifting an undivided share and explain the condition that validates it.

Core Idea
A gift of an undivided share is defective until the property is divided and delivered.

This passage introduces a key distinction in Hanafi law: the gift of an undivided share (shiqs mushāʿ) is considered fasid (defective), not batil (void). This means it can be rectified through subsequent division and delivery. The defect arises because possession of an undivided share creates uncertainty (gharar). The text cites an athar from the prominent tabi'i Ibn Shubruma to support this ruling, distinguishing between a specified sum and an unspecified fraction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its initial ruling
Determine what act is being discussed and what the immediate legal classification is. The act is gifting an undivided share. The initial ruling is fasid (invalid/defective).
The matn states 'fāl-hibatu fāsidah' – the gift is defective.
2
Identify the condition that makes it valid
Find the phrase that indicates how the defect is removed. The text says 'fa-in qasamahu wa sallamahu jāz' – if he divides and delivers, it becomes valid.
Division and delivery after the gift cure the defect; they are not required at the time of the gift itself.
3
Locate the dalil
Identify the proof cited. The author cites an athar from Ibn Shubruma: 'I asked Ibn Shubruma about it, he said: if he specifies... if he specifies a third or a fourth, it is not valid until he divides.'
The athar is from Musannaf Abdur Razzaq, a hadith/athar collection.
4
Examine the reasoning (wajh al-dalalah)
Understand how the athar supports the ruling. It distinguishes between a specified sum (mu'ayyan) that can be taken from the property without division, and a fraction (musha') that requires division. This analogically applies to gifting a shiqs musha'.
The Hanafi position uses this athar to show that division validates the gift, contrary to schools like the Shafi'i that consider it batil and irreparable.

Commentary
This passage presents a specific ruling within the chapter of gifts: the gift of an undivided share (shiqs musha') in divisible property is invalid (fasid) if given without division. The matn states that if the donor subsequently divides the property and hands over the specific portion to the donee, the gift becomes valid. The Urdu commentary elaborates that the condition for validity is that at the time of possession, the gifted item must be physically separated. This is based on the principle that possession of a musha' share without division leads to uncertainty (gharar). The commentator cites an effect (athar) from Ibn Shubruma, a prominent tabi'i and judge, who distinguishes between gifting a specified sum (e.g., 100 dinars) and an unspecified fraction (e.g., one-third). The specified sum is valid because it can be taken from the donor's property without division of the whole; the fraction requires division because it is a share of the whole. This supports the ruling that division validates the gift. The lesson thus clarifies that the defect (fasād) in such a gift is due to the lack of division at the time of possession, and it can be remedied by subsequent partition and delivery.

Gift of Flour in Wheat or Oil in Sesame Seeds
Ruling on gifting an extracted substance while still in its raw material

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifting a nonexistent substance and explain why subsequent existence does not validate the gift.

Core Idea
A gift of a nonexistent item is invalid even if the item later comes into existence.

Imagine gifting flour that hasn't been ground yet—it's still inside the wheat grain. Hanafi law considers this gift invalid from the outset because the subject matter (flour) does not exist at the time of the contract. This lesson analyses the ruling, the principle behind it, and distinguishes it from the valid gift of undivided (musha') property. Understanding this distinction is critical to applying the condition of existence in gift contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Recognize that the act is gifting flour while it is still in wheat, or oil while in sesame seeds. The matn states the ruling: the gift is invalid (fasid).
The ruling is clear from the source: 'فالهبة فاسدة'.
2
Understand the Reason for Invalidity
The gift is invalid because the subject matter (flour or oil) does not exist at the time of the contract. Hanafi law requires the gifted item to exist (mawjud) at the time of the gift.
The commentary uses the term 'معدوم' (ma'dum) to describe the nonexistent item.
3
Analyse Whether Later Existence Cures the Defect
The source considers whether grinding the wheat or extracting the oil after the gift makes it valid. It concludes it does not—the initial contract remains invalid.
This contrasts with some other contracts where a later act can perfect the contract.
4
Distinguish from the Gift of Musha' (Undivided Property)
In an earlier ruling (issue 6), a gift of undivided property became valid after division because the item existed at the time of the gift—it was merely undivided. Here, the item did not exist at all, so the defect is incurable.
The key difference: existence vs. division.
5
Apply the Principle to a New Scenario
Consider a case like gifting milk that is still in a goat. Apply the same principle: the milk is nonexistent at the time of the gift, so the gift is invalid even if the milk is later milked.
This reinforces the general rule that gifts of future or potential items are invalid.

Commentary
This lesson presents a ruling on a specific scenario: gifting flour that is still inside the wheat grain, or oil that is still inside the sesame seeds. The matn declares such a gift invalid (fasidah). Even if the donor later grinds the wheat or extracts the oil and then gifts the resulting flour or oil, the original gift remains invalid—it does not become valid by subsequent processing. The Urdu commentary explains the underlying principle: at the time of the gift contract, the subject matter (flour or oil) does not exist; it is ma'dum (nonexistent). A gift of a nonexistent thing is invalid in Hanafi law. The commentary distinguishes this from the earlier ruling on musha' (shared property). In the musha' case, the item exists at the time of the gift; it is merely undivided. Here, the flour and oil do not exist at all until processing. Therefore, the defect is not curable by later coming into existence. However, if the donor makes a completely new gift after the flour or oil has been produced, that new gift is valid. The lesson thus reinforces the principle that the subject matter of a gift must exist at the time of the contract.

Gift of an Item Already in Donee's Possession
Ruling on gift when the object is already held by the donee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on hibah when the donee already possesses the item, identify the hadith evidence, and explain why pre-existing qabd suffices.

Core Idea
If the donee already holds the gift, ownership transfers immediately – no new possession needed.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in the law of gifts (hibah): what happens when the item being gifted is already physically held by the recipient? The Hanafi ruling is clear: the gift is valid and ownership transfers upon the contract without requiring a new act of possession. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the Prophetic hadith that supports this exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a gift (hibah) where the gifted object is already in the donee's hand. The ruling: the donee becomes owner by the gift even without renewing possession.
This is an exception to the general condition that qabd is required for a gift to become binding.
2
Locate the dalil
The evidence is a hadith from Sahih al-Bukhari: the Prophet (peace be upon him) purchased a camel from Umar, but did not take possession; instead, he immediately gifted it to Ibn Umar, who was already riding it.
The hadith is ahad (single narration), but accepted in fiqh.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet did not require Ibn Umar to dismount and retake possession. This shows that pre-existing possession fulfills the condition of qabd.
The legal link: the condition (qabd) is already satisfied.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school generally requires a new act of possession even if the donee already holds the item, as they consider qabd an independent pillar of the contract.
The difference stems from different interpretations of the general rule.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi based on this explicit hadith. The hadith provides a specific exception that overrides the general requirement.
The ruling is firmly established in Zahir al-Riwayah.

Father's Gift to His Minor Son
Ruling on a father's gift to his minor son without possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a father's gift to his minor son and explain why possession is not required under Hanafi law.

Core Idea
A father's gift to his minor son is complete by contract alone; possession is not required.

This lesson presents a clear exception to the general rule that gifts require possession for completion. The Hanafi school grants this dispensation based on the father's natural guardianship over his minor child. Understanding this ruling clarifies the unique position of parental gifts in Islamic law and the role of guardianship in property transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a father gifting something to his minor (still a child) son. The ruling is that the son becomes the owner immediately upon the contract (offer and acceptance), even if he never takes physical possession.
The key phrase is 'ملكها الابن بالعقد وان لم يقبض' – owns by the contract even without possession.
2
Recall the general rule for gifts
Normally, a gift (hiba) in Hanafi fiqh requires possession (qabd) by the donee for the gift to be complete and binding. Without possession, the donor can revoke.
This general rule prevents uncertainty and protects the gift's finality.
3
Understand the Hanafi exception
The father is the natural guardian (wali) of his minor child. His possession is considered equivalent to the child's possession due to the unity of interest. This creates a special ruling (rukhsah) that dispenses with formal possession.
This exception applies to the father, grandfather, and sometimes the appointed guardian.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani reportedly requires possession even from the father. However, the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that of Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf: no possession needed.
Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah prefers Abu Hanifa's view.

This lesson presents the ruling that a father's gift to his minor child is valid and complete (tam) by the mere contract alone, without requiring the child to take possession. The reason is that the father is the natural guardian (wali) of his minor child, and the child's hand is considered the same as the father's hand in matters of benefit. The Urdu commentary reiterates that the child becomes owner by the contract even if possession did not occur. This principle applies specifically to the father (and also to the grandfather and appointed guardian) gifting to their minor child. The lesson thus establishes a special dispensation for parental gifts to minors, recognizing the unity of interest between parent and child.

Father's Gift to Minor Child: Completion by Contract Alone
Rules that father's gift to his minor child is complete by contract alone.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a father's gift to his minor child is complete by the contract alone and identify the proof from the practice of Uthman ibn Affan.

Core Idea
A father's gift to his minor child is valid immediately; the father's existing possession as guardian fulfills the requirement of possession.

In Hanafi fiqh, a gift (hiba) normally requires offer, acceptance, and possession (qabd). However, when a father gives a gift to his minor child, a unique rule applies: the gift is completed by the contract alone, without any new act of possession. This lesson examines the ruling, its basis in the father's guardianship, and the evidence from the practice of the Caliph Uthman ibn Affan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the exception
The act is the father giving a gift to his minor child. Note the general rule that gifts require possession, and how this case is an exception.
The general rule of hiba requires qabd (taking possession) for completion.
2
Understand the reason
The father is the natural guardian of the child's property, already possessing it. Thus, the condition of possession is already satisfied.
The father's possession as guardian is legally equivalent to the child's possession.
3
Examine the proof
Review the athar from Uthman ibn Affan (r.a.), which states that a father may take possession on behalf of his minor child, validating the gift.
The athar is recorded in Sunan al-Bayhaqi and Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq.
4
Apply the principle
Confirm that the ruling applies to any guardian (wali) or executor (wasi) making a gift to a minor under their care.
The ruling is not limited to fathers; it extends to all legal guardians of the minor's property.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Contrast with the Shafi'i view that may require separate delivery to the minor or a different guardian.
The difference stems from varying interpretations of the possession requirement for minors.

A gift to a minor is valid only when the guardian takes possession.

This passage addresses a common situation: a stranger gives a gift to a minor child. Since the minor cannot legally take possession independently, the father, as the child's natural guardian, acts on the child's behalf. Understanding this confirms the Hanafi position that possession by the guardian completes the gift.

3
Apply the Hanafi rule that the father's possession suffices
The father's taking possession of the gifted item on behalf of the child makes the child the owner. No separate possession by the child is needed.
4
State the consequence
Once the father takes possession, the gift is completed (tammat) and the child becomes the owner.

Gift to an Orphan Completed by Guardian's Possession
States that a gift to an orphan is valid by guardian possession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifts to orphans and apply the condition of guardian possession to determine validity.

Core Idea
An orphan cannot take possession, so the guardian's possession perfects the gift.

A gift (hiba) requires possession (qabd) for completion. For an orphan who lacks legal capacity, the law provides that a guardian (wali) or executor (wasi) takes possession on his behalf. This lesson clarifies the ruling and the conditions for validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and legal category
The act is a gift (hiba) given to an orphan (a minor who has lost his father). Gifts are contracts that require offer, acceptance, and possession.
2
State the ruling
The gift is valid (sahih) when the orphan's guardian takes possession of the gifted item on the orphan's behalf.
The orphan does not need to take possession himself; the guardian's act suffices.
3
Identify the legal basis
The orphan lacks legal capacity to accept and take possession. The guardian acts as a substitute, and his possession establishes the orphan's ownership by operation of law.
This is based on a previous athar (tradition) from Caliph Uthman, implying that a guardian's possession is effective for a minor.
4
Determine the conditions of the guardian
The guardian must be a valid wali: the father (if alive), then grandfather, then executor, then judge. If the guardian is not legally authorized, the gift may not be valid.
Hanafi law ranks guardians: father → grandfather → executor → judge.
5
Apply to practical scenarios
If a donor gives a gift to an orphan, and the guardian takes possession, the orphan becomes owner immediately. If no guardian takes possession, the gift is incomplete.
The orphan cannot ratify the gift later upon maturity without a new offer and possession.

Gift to a Minor: Father's Possession Completes the Gift
Ruling on gift receipt for minor by father

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gifts to minors when the donor is the father or a stranger, and explain the rationale based on the father's existing possession as guardian.

Core Idea
For a gift to a minor, the father's existing possession as guardian fulfills qabd, making the gift valid upon contract.

This lesson explores a unique Hanafi ruling: when a father gives a gift to his young son, the child becomes the owner immediately without a new physical transfer. This is because the father, as the child's guardian, already holds the item. The passage also covers gifts from strangers and to orphans, and provides an athar from Caliph Uthman as proof. Understanding this ruling highlights the Hanafi emphasis on existing guardianship in property transfers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? A gift (hibah) from a father to his minor son. The ruling: the gift is valid (sahih) immediately upon the contract, without the need for a new taking of possession (qabd) by the father.
The minor cannot take possession himself, so the father acts on his behalf.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites an athar from Caliph Uthman ibn Affan (radiyallahu anhu) as proof. The athar states that if someone gives a gift to a young child who cannot take possession, and the gift is announced and witnessed, it is valid, and the father is the guardian for possession.
This is a marfu' or mawquf report, considered reliable in Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The phrase 'wa in waliyaha abuhu' (and its guardian is his father) indicates that the father's role as guardian includes taking possession for the child. Since the item is already in the father's custody (e.g., in his house), no new physical transfer is needed.
The existing de facto possession suffices for legal possession (qabd hukmi).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require a new act of possession even when the father is the donor. The Hanafi school relies on the athar and the principle that the father's possession is the child's possession.
This difference affects whether the gift is revocable before new qabd.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi, the child becomes owner immediately; under the opposing view, the gift remains incomplete until a new transfer occurs. This matters for issues like sale or inheritance of the gifted item in the interim.
Also, the father cannot simply revoke the gift after contract because ownership has transferred.

Guardian's possession completes the gift for an orphan, just as a father would.

This lesson extends the earlier ruling on gifts to minors by showing that any legal guardian (wali or wasi) can take possession on the orphan's behalf, making the gift valid and transferring ownership. The proof is the same athar from Uthman ibn Affan, applied by analogy (qiyas) to all guardians.

The proof is the athar from Uthman ibn Affan, which states that the father takes possession for the child. This establishes the principle for a guardian.
The athar was cited in a previous lesson.
4
Apply the analogy (qiyas)
The underlying reason (illa) is guardianship (wilayah) for the minor's benefit. Since both father and other guardians share this illa, any legal guardian can take possession.
This is a qiyas jali (clear analogy) because the illa is explicit.

Some schools require the orphan to take possession himself if he is discerning (mumayyiz). The Hanafi school, however, relies on the guardian's possession in all cases.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether the orphan's own act is needed.

Revocation of a Gift to a Non-Relative and Its Exceptions
Ruling on revoking a gift to a non-relative and conditions preventing revocation

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A gift to a non-relative can be revoked unless compensation or an attached increase prevents it; revocation is permissible but disliked.

In Hanafi fiqh, a gift (hibah) to a non-relative is not automatically final. The donor retains the right to take back the gift, though this act is considered disliked (makruh). However, two circumstances permanently bar revocation: if the donee gives compensation (iwad) or if an increase attached to the gifted item occurs. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and the disagreement with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on revoking a gift to a non-relative
Identify the basic rule from the matn: 'If one gives a gift to a non-relative, it is permissible for him to take it back.' Note that this is conditional on the gift still existing and the donee not being a relative.
The ruling applies only to non-relatives; gifts to relatives are final per Hanafi.
2
Identify the two exceptions that prevent revocation
From the text, list the two exceptions: (1) Compensation (iwad) – if the donee gives something in return, the gift becomes like a sale. (2) Attached increase (ziyada muttasila) – if an inseparable increase occurs (e.g., a tree planted on gifted land), the donor cannot revoke.
Attached increase belongs to the donee; separating it harms the gift.
3
Recognize that revocation is disliked (makruh tanzihan)
The hadith comparing the revoker to one who returns to his vomit shows the act is disliked, not forbidden. The permisiveness remains until compensation or increase.
The karahah is tanzihiyah, not tahrimi.

The hadith 'A man has more right to his gift as long as he has not been compensated for it' directly grants the donor a right to revoke before compensation. This is the primary dalil.
The phrase 'ma lam yuthab minha' is the key condition.
5
Contrast the Shafi'i position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that no gift can be revoked after delivery, except a father's gift to his child. His proof is the hadith of Ibn Umar and Ibn Abbas: 'It is not permissible for a man to give a gift and then take it back, except a father...'.
The Shafi'i hadith appears to prohibit all revocation.
6
Understand the Hanafi response and the practical consequence
Hanafi scholars interpret the Shafi'i hadith as referring to revocation after compensation (i.e., once compensated, it is like a sale and cannot be revoked). Alternatively, it condemns the act but does not invalidate it. Thus, the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear: in Hanafi, donors can reclaim gifts before compensation; in Shafi'i, they cannot.
The Hanafi position is considered mufta bihi in the school.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi ruling on revocation of a gift to a non-relative. The matn states: 'If one gives a gift to a non-relative, he may take it back.' The commentary explains that this right exists as long as the gift still exists, the donee has not given compensation, and the donee is not a relative. Revocation is permissible but makruh (disliked), as shown by the hadith comparing the revoker to one who returns to his vomit. Two exceptions prevent revocation: (1) Compensation (iwad) – if the donee gives something in return, the gift becomes like a sale and cannot be revoked. (2) Attached increase (ziyada muttasila) – if an increase occurs that is attached to the gifted item (e.g., a tree grown on gifted land), the donor cannot revoke because the increase belongs to the donee and cannot be separated. The passage then presents an inter-madhhab dispute: Imam al-Shafi'i holds that no gift can be revoked after delivery, based on a hadith that prohibits revocation except for a father. The Hanafi response is that the Shafi'i hadith indicates prohibition (tahrim) but is interpreted as referring to revocation after compensation, or that the Hanafi permissibility is limited to cases before compensation and is disliked. The Hanafi position is that the donor has a right to revoke until compensated, and the hadith of Abu Huraira supports this.

Key Terms
رجوع في الهبة (Ruju' fi al-Hibah): The act of the donor taking back the gifted item from the donee.
عوض (Iwad): Compensation given by the donee to the donor in exchange for the gift, which transforms the gift into a sale-like transaction.
زیادة متصلة (Ziyada Muttasila): An increase that is physically attached to the gifted item, such as a tree planted on the land or a child born to a gifted animal.

Right to Revoke a Gift: Rule and Exceptions (Hanafi)
States ruling on gift revocation and its limitations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on gift revocation, identify the exceptions (compensation and attached increase) that prevent revocation, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
A donor can revoke a gift before compensation, but it is legally valid yet disliked.

Gifts (hibah) are acts of generosity, but what if the donor wants the gift back? The Hanafi school recognizes a right to revoke under certain conditions, while strictly discouraging its exercise. This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamari explains the general rule, the exceptions that make revocation impossible, and the evidence from hadith. We will also examine the Shafi'i position that gifts are generally irrevocable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the revocation of a gift by the donor. The Hanafi ruling is that revocation is legally permissible before compensation but is makruh (disliked).
Do not confuse legal capacity with moral recommendation.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites the hadith: 'A man has more right to his gift as long as he has not been rewarded for it.' This is the primary proof for the permissibility of revocation before compensation.
The dalil is from sunnah ahad with zanni thubut and dalala.
3
Identify the exceptions
Revocation is prevented by: (1) the donee giving compensation (iwad), (2) an attached increase (ziyadah muttasilah) occurring on the gifted item, and (3) the donee being a relative (unlike a stranger).
Attached increase includes cultivation, building, dyeing, birth of offspring, etc.
4
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that no revocation is allowed except for a father regarding his child. His proof is a hadith prohibiting return of gifts. The Hanafi response interprets Shafi'i's hadith as an ethical prohibition, not a nullification of the legal right.
The Hanafi reconciliation: the first hadith allows revocation; the second discourages it.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: A Hanafi donor can legally revoke a gift before compensation, though it is sinful. A Shafi'i donor cannot revoke at all (except father), so the gift remains with the donee.
The mufta bihi in Hanafi is the permissibility of revocation before compensation.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thamari discusses the Hanafi ruling on revocation of gifts (hibah). The author states the general rule: a donor (wahib) who gives a gift to a non-relative (ajnabi) has the right to take it back unless one of three conditions prevents revocation. The first condition is if the donee (mawhub lahu) gives compensation (iwad) for the gift. The second is if an attached increase (ziyadah muttasilah) occurs. The third is if the donee is a relative (dhul rahm). However, the text clarifies that even when revocation is allowed, it is makruh (disliked) based on the hadith equating it to returning to one's vomit. The proof for the right to revoke is the hadith: 'A man has more right to his gift as long as he has not been rewarded for it.' This shows that before compensation, revocation is permitted. The author then addresses a key inter-madhhab dispute. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that no revocation is allowed at all except for a father regarding his child. His proof is another hadith that prohibits taking back gifts. The Hanafi response is that this hadith is interpreted as indicating prohibition (hurmat) but not invalidity; revocation is still valid but sinful. The Hanafis reconcile the proofs by saying the first hadith (about being more entitled before compensation) allows revocation, while the second hadith discourages it. The passage also explains the exception of attached increase: any increase that becomes part of the gifted item (like cultivating land, dyeing cloth, birth of a child from a slave girl) prevents revocation because it mixes the donee's property with the gift. Additionally, the concept of 'consumption' (istihlak) is introduced through an athar (report from the Companions), stating that if the gift is consumed or changed, revocation is no longer possible. This covers cases like selling, re-gifting, or physically consuming the item.

Key Terms
هبة (hibah): A gift; transfer of ownership without compensation, revocable under certain conditions in Hanafi law.
عوض (iwad): Compensation given by the donee to the donor in return for the gift, which makes the gift irrevocable.
زیادة متصلة (ziyadah muttasilah): An increase physically attached to the gifted item, e.g., cultivation, building, or birth of offspring, which prevents revocation.
استهلاك (istihlak): Consumption or disposal of the gifted item by the donee (selling, re-gifting, eating, or altering) that makes revocation impossible.

Core Idea
Once the gifted item leaves the donee's ownership or a party dies, revocation is impossible — ownership changes extinguish the right.

We have learned that a gift cannot be revoked after the donee takes possession or after a counter-gift. This lesson presents two more bars: the death of either contracting party and the transfer of the gifted item out of the donee's ownership. The Hanafi school supports these rulings with athar (companion traditions) from Umar (radi Allahu anhu) and Sufyan.

State the ruling for each exception

Trace the dalil

Death of a Contracting Party Prevents Gift Revocation
Ruling that death of either party prevents gift revocation, with proof from an athar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that death of either donor or donee permanently bars revocation of a gift, and identify the proof from the athar of Sayyiduna Umar (رضي الله عنه).

Core Idea
Death of either contracting party permanently ends the right to revoke a gift.

In Hanafi fiqh, gifts (hibah) are generally revocable by the donor. However, certain events permanently bar revocation. This lesson focuses on one such event: the death of either the donor or the donee. Once death occurs, ownership transfers to heirs, and the original contract is effectively closed. The ruling is supported by a narration from Sayyiduna Umar (رضي الله عنه) as recorded by Imam al-Tahawi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is gift (hibah). The ruling is that if either the donor (waahib) or the donee (mawhub lah) dies, the donor cannot revoke the gift.
This is an exception to the general permissibility of revocation.
2
Understand the reasoning for donee's death
When the donee dies, the gifted item becomes the property of the donee's heirs. Ownership has transferred to a third party, so revocation is impossible.
The same applies if the donee consumes or sells the item – ownership changes.
3
Understand the reasoning for donor's death

Core Idea
Ruqba — a gift conditional on death — is invalid but still transfers ownership absolutely.

In this lesson, we examine the ruqba contract, a conditional gift where ownership hinges on who dies first. The Hanafi school holds an internal dispute: Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad deem it invalid due to a prophetic prohibition, while Abu Yusuf permits it based on another hadith. The key nuance is that even if the contract is invalid, its execution results in absolute transfer of ownership — a critical practical difference.

Abu Yusuf disagrees, citing a hadith from Jabir that permits both 'umra and ruqba.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.

According to the majority view, if ruqba is performed, the condition is voided and the property becomes absolute for the recipient. According to Abu Yusuf, the condition would be enforceable.
Thus, the ruling on the contract's validity differs, but the outcome after execution is similar in transfer.

Waqf of an Undivided Share: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute with dalil naqli

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the proofs of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding waqf of musha', identify the mufta bihi position, and apply the exception for indivisible properties.

Core Idea
Waqf of an undivided share is permissible; possession is not required for validity.

The permissibility of endowing an undivided share (musha') is a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad. The crux of their disagreement lies in whether delivery of possession (qabd) is a condition for the completion of waqf. This lesson walks through each imam's evidence and arrives at the relied-upon position in the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
State clearly: Abu Yusuf permits waqf of musha' even if divisible; Muhammad does not permit unless the property is first physically divided. Note the operative ruling (mufta bihi).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; both views are within the school.
2
Analyze Abu Yusuf's Proof
His view rests on the principle that qabd is not a condition for waqf completion – the declaration alone suffices. He cites the hadith of Banu Najjar, who jointly endowed land for the Prophet's mosque without prior division.
This hadith appears in Sahih al-Bukhari under the chapter on joint waqf.
3
Analyze Muhammad's Proof
Muhammad holds that qabd is essential; without physical delivery, the waqf is incomplete. He relies on the hadith of Abu Talhah, who divided his garden among relatives before making it a charitable endowment. He analogizes waqf to gift (hibah) where possession is required.
Abu Talhah's action shows division preceding the transfer of ownership.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi and Exception
Later Hanafi jurists preferred Abu Yusuf's view: waqf of musha' is permissible. However, a waqf that mixes the owner's share with a religious institution (e.g., half for mosque, half for self) is invalid in both views. Also, properties that are inherently indivisible (bathhouse, mill) may be endowed without division by consensus.
The exception for indivisible property is based on necessity (darurah).

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the permissibility of endowing an undivided share (musha'). Imam Abu Yusuf considers it permissible, while Imam Muhammad requires that the property be divided before the waqf can take effect. The Hanafi school's mufta bihi position is that of Abu Yusuf, allowing waqf of musha'. This is the preferred ruling in later fatwa works.

Condition for Completion of Waqf: Perpetuity Required
States condition of permanence for a valid waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition of perpetuity for the validity of waqf according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, and distinguish the positions of Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i on this issue.

Core Idea
A waqf is not complete unless its benefit ultimately reaches perpetual charity.

In this lesson, we examine a key condition for the validity of a waqf according to Abu Hanifa and his student Muhammad al-Shaybani. They require that the waqf's benefit must not be interrupted; it must ultimately end in perpetual sadaqah. This condition distinguishes their view from that of Abu Yusuf and the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its scope
Read the statement: 'waqf is not complete according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad until it is such that its end is not interrupted.' Recognize that this is a condition for valid completion, not a pillar.
The condition is called 'adam al-inqita' (non-interruption).
2
Understand the condition of perpetuity
The waqf property must ultimately be directed to a permanent charitable purpose (e.g., poor and needy) so that it continues forever. If the waqf terminates or reverts to individuals without a perpetual tail, it is invalid.
E.g., endowing for children and then for the poor is valid; endowing only for children without a perpetual tail is invalid.
3
Apply the condition to examples
Examine two cases: a temporary waqf (e.g., for 10 years) vs. a waqf that ends in perpetual charity. Determine validity under Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
Temporary waqf is invalid; waqf with ultimate perpetual charity is valid.
4
Compare with Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf does not require perpetuity; a waqf that benefits specific individuals without a perpetual tail is valid in his view. Note that this is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
Later Hanafi practice follows Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (mufta bihi).
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Imam al-Shafi'i also does not require perpetuity; temporary waqf is valid. This highlights the unique Hanafi position.
The practical consequence: a waqf that terminates is void in the majority Hanafi view.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling regarding the completion of waqf according to Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad. They hold that a waqf is not valid and complete unless its ultimate destination is perpetual, i.e., it should not be temporary or have an interrupted end. This means that the benefactors should be such that the property ultimately goes to perpetual charity (sadaqah jariyah) like the poor and needy, so that it continues forever. The waqf should not be conditional upon an event that would cause it to terminate. For example, if a person endows a property for his children and after them for the poor, that is valid because it ends in perpetual charity. But if he endows only for his children without specifying a permanent destination after them, the waqf may be incomplete according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad because it would eventually become ownerless (if children die out) or revert to the waqif's heirs. This condition is known as 'adam al-inqita' (non-interruption). The key point is the condition of perpetuity for the validity of waqf according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.

Compensation for Usurped Property: Mithli vs Qimi
Ruling on compensation for usurped property with proofs and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for usurped property, distinguish between mithli and qimi property, and apply the relevant proofs.

Core Idea
If a usurped item perishes, the usurper must return its like (mithl) if mithli, or its value if qimi.

This lesson covers the ruling on ghasp (usurpation). The Hanafi school holds that if someone forcefully takes another's property and it is destroyed, he must compensate. The form of compensation depends on whether the property is mithli—having standardized equivalents—or qimi—unique items valued individually. We will examine the proofs from Quran and Sunnah that establish this distinction.

Guided Path
1
State the prohibition of ghasp
Identify that usurping property by force is haram, based on Quran 2:188 and the hadith prohibiting taking a brother's item even in jest.

2
List the two compensation scenarios
If the usurped item perishes, the ruling depends on its type: mithli (standardized) → return its like; qimi (unique) → pay its value.
The examples of wheat (mithli) and cow (qimi) clarify the distinction.
3
Identify the primary proofs
The hadith 'the hand is responsible until it returns' (for returning the item if still existent) and Quran 2:194 (retaliation in kind) used by qiyas to justify mithli compensation.
Note that the verse 'bimithli' is key for the like-for-like principle.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Given a case – e.g., Aisha broke a bowl – the Prophet ordered giving a like bowl. That is consistent with mithli compensation.
This illustrates practical application.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school generally does not distinguish mithli/qimi and holds that only value (qima) is due for all destroyed property.
Understanding this ikhtilaf reinforces the unique Hanafi position.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between fungible goods (mithli) and non-fungible goods (qimi), each with a distinct measure of liability. Commentary

The main ruling is that if a usurped item perishes, the guarantor must pay either its like (if it is a fungible, mithli item such as wheat) or its value (if it is a non-fungible, qimi item such as a cow). The author distinguishes between mithli and qimi items with examples: wheat is a fungible (standard measure), so a like amount is given; a cow is not fungible, so its monetary value is given. For value, the hadith of freeing a share of a slave shows the principle of fair valuation. The passage also includes the hadith 'The hand is responsible for what it has taken until it returns it,' indicating that the actual item must be returned if it still exists. Overall, the passage establishes the basic framework for guarantees in cases of usurpation.

The Obligation to Return the Actual Usurped Item

Core Idea
The usurper must return the exact item, not its value, as long as it remains.

When someone wrongfully takes another's property (ghasb), the first and foremost duty is to undo the wrong by restoring the very same item. This lesson examines the clear ruling from the hadith that the usurper is obligated (wajib) to return the 'ayn (the actual property) if it still exists. Understanding this priority prevents the mistaken notion that paying compensation is always an acceptable substitute.

This lesson examines the Hanafi definition of ghasb (usurpation). The text clarifies that ghasb is restricted to property that can be physically moved and transformed. This distinction has important legal consequences: while the usurper of movable property incurs specific obligations, the wrongful occupation of land is treated under different legal rules.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Liability for Usurped Real Estate
Intra-Hanafi dispute on liability for usurped real estate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on liability for usurped real estate, explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement, and evaluate the evidence for each view.

Core Idea
Ghasb applies only to movables; land cannot be 'usurped' in the strict sense.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute: is the usurper of land (ghasib) liable if the land perishes in his hands? The disagreement turns on the definition of ghasb: does it require physical taking of a movable object, or does dispossession alone suffice? Understanding this dispute sharpens our grasp of how Hanafi scholars derive rulings from the same sources and why the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) favors no liability for real estate.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: the usurper of real estate is not liable (ghayr damin) if the property is destroyed.
This is the majority view within the early Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Recognize that Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani disagrees: he holds the usurper is liable (damin) because dispossession itself constitutes ghasb.
Imam Muhammad is the third great Hanafi figure; his view is known but not mufta bihi.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine Imam Muhammad's hadith: 'Whoever takes anything from land unjustly will be sunk into seven earths' – this indicates that taking land is a grave sin and, by implication, creates liability.
Opposing proof stresses the moral dimension and permanent dispossession.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Understand how Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf interpret the same hadith: the threat is a warning against oppression, not a ruling of civil liability. They cite their own proof: 'Whoever revives dead land, it is his, and there is no right for a wrongful root.' This shows that land cannot be 'taken' in the sense of ghasb.
The response distinguishes between sin and liability.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: under the majority view, the usurper pays only for damage he caused (e.g., destroying a building), not for the land's value. Under Muhammad's view, he would owe the full value of the land.
This difference is significant in property disputes.

(٤) والغصب فيما ينقل ويحول
(4) And usurpation is in that which is movable and transformable.
Source pages p. 379

Commentary
This is a ruling statement defining the scope of ghasb (usurpation). According to the Hanafi school, the act of ghasb can only occur in items that are movable and transformable (manqul wa muhawwal). Immovable property like land and buildings cannot be physically seized and taken away; therefore, the strict rules of ghasb (including liability for value if destroyed) do not apply. Instead, the act of wrongfully occupying land is considered a different form of trespass with different legal consequences.

Key Terms
غصب (ghasb): Wrongful taking and control of another's property without right, limited to movable property in Hanafi fiqh.
ينقل ويحول (yanqulu wa yahuwalu): That which can be moved and transformed from one place to another, i.e., movable property.

Checkpoint
According to the Hanafi school, what type of property can be the subject of ghasb (usurpation)?
A) Any property, movable or immovable.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, ghasb (usurpation) is defined exclusively over movable property; land falls under separate rules.
