Introduction to Partnership: Types and Legal Evidences
Defines shirkah, its two types, and establishes legitimacy via Quran and Hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of partnership in Hanafi fiqh and identify the Quranic and Hadith evidences for its permissibility.

Core Idea
Partnership in Hanafi law is of two fundamental types: ownership-based and contract-based, both grounded in Quran and Sunnah.

This passage opens Kitab al-Shirkah by defining partnership and its two categories. Understanding these types is essential because they determine the rights and obligations of partners. The author also provides textual proofs, showing that partnership is not a worldly innovation but rooted in revelation.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of shirkah
Identify the two broad categories: shirkat al-amlak (ownership partnership) and shirkat al-uqud (contractual partnership). Note the key difference: one arises without a contract, the other requires mutual agreement.
Memorize the Arabic terms: شركة املاک and شركة عقود.
2
Analyze shirkat al-amlak
Understand that this type is involuntary, arising from inheritance or co-purchase. No offer and acceptance is needed; joint ownership is a given fact.
Example: two brothers inherit a house.
3
Examine the three proofs
The author cites (1) Quran 4:12 mentioning 'partners in the third', (2) a hadith qudsi where Allah is the third partner, and (3) the practice of a Companion including others as partners.
Each proof establishes the legitimacy of partnership at different levels.
4
Draw the legal implication
From the proofs, the Hanafi school rules that partnership is permissible (mubah) and recommended when it aids cooperation in righteousness.
There is consensus among schools on its basic permissibility.

Commentary
This passage introduces the book of Partnership (Kitab al-Shirkah) from a Hanafi fiqh work. It begins by stating that partnership is of two fundamental types: (1) Shirkat al-Amwal (Partnership of Ownership) — this occurs when two or more individuals come to own an asset jointly, either through inheritance (e.g., two sons inherit a house) or through co-purchase (they buy an item together). This type does not require a contract of partnership; it arises from the events of inheritance or purchase. (2) Shirkat al-Uqud (Partnership of Contracts) — this is a contractual partnership formed by offer and acceptance to engage in business or other endeavors. The author then provides evidence for the permissibility and validity of partnership. The first proof is from the Quran: 'But if they are more than that, they are partners in the third' (Surah an-Nisa, 4:12). This verse, concerning inheritance of the one-third bequest, implicitly acknowledges that multiple heirs become partners in property. The second proof is a hadith qudsi: Allah declares that He is the third partner of two partners as long as there is no betrayal. This encourages honesty in partnerships. The third proof is a practice of the Companions: Abdullah ibn Hisham, a Companion blessed by the Prophet's supplication, would include Ibn Umar and Ibn al-Zubayr as partners in his food purchases, indicating the permissibility of partnership by mutual agreement. The Urdu commentary expands that partnership is of two types: shirkat al-amlak is where ownership is shared without a formal contract, while shirkat al-uqud requires a contract. It also notes that the Quranic verse and hadith establish the legitimacy of partnership.

Key Terms
شركة املاک (shirkat al-amlāk): Partnership of ownership: joint ownership arising from inheritance or co-purchase without a formal partnership contract.
شركة عقود (shirkat al-ʿuqūd): Contractual partnership: a partnership formed by mutual agreement (offer and acceptance) for business or profit.
شركة (shirkah): Partnership; joint participation in ownership or a contract.

Partnership (shirkah) in Islamic law is a broad concept. This lesson begins the Book of Partnership by defining its two fundamental types: Shirkat al-Amwal (ownership partnership) and Shirkat al-Uqud (contractual partnership). Partner Cannot Dispose Without Permission
Ruling on partner's authority over other's share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a partner's disposal of another's share and apply the hadith proof to determine permissibility.

Core Idea
A partner is a stranger to his co-owner's share; any use without permission is haram.

This lesson examines a fundamental principle in Hanafi partnership law: despite physical co-ownership, each partner's share remains legally distinct. The text establishes that a partner cannot use or dispose of the other's share without explicit permission, supported by a hadith from the Farewell Pilgrimage.

Guided Path

The text states: 'It is not permissible for either of them to dispose of the portion of the other except with his permission.'
2
Locate the dalil
Identify the proof cited by the author. Here, it is a hadith narrated by Umar during the Farewell Pilgrimage.
Hadith: 'It is not lawful for any man to take anything from his brother's wealth except what he willingly gives.'
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Analyze how the hadith proves the ruling. The phrase 'la yahillu' indicates prohibition, and the exception clause requires willing consent.
The ruling is derived directly from the text: permission is necessary for any disposal.
4
Note the practical solution
The commentary provides a solution for shared use: partners should set up a rotation schedule rather than using without permission.
This shows that the prohibition does not prevent use altogether, but requires mutual agreement.

The Four Types of Contractual Partnership in Hanafi Fiqh
Enumeration of four types of contractual partnerships

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four types of contractual partnership (Shirkat al-Uqud) and distinguish each based on the nature of contribution (capital, labor, or credit).

Core Idea
Contractual partnership in Hanafi fiqh has four distinct forms: Mufawada, Inan, Sana'i, and Wujuh—each defined by how partners contribute.

This lesson introduces the foundational categorization of contractual partnerships (Shirkat al-Uqud). The matn enumerates four types: Mufawada (full partnership), Inan (limited partnership), Sana'i (partnership of craftsmen), and Wujuh (credit partnership). Understanding these categories is essential before studying the detailed rulings of each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus
Recognize that contractual partnerships are one of the two main divisions of Shirkah (alongside joint ownership). The matn states 'the second type is partnership of contracts.'
2
Note the four species
Memorize the four names: Mufawada, Inan, Sana'i, and Wujuh. These are the only recognized subtypes in the Hanafi school.
3
Distinguish each by capital source
For each type, identify what is contributed: Mufawada requires equal capital and authority; Inan allows unequal contributions; Sana'i is skills/labor; Wujuh is creditworthiness without capital.
4
Understand the cross-madhhab difference
Note that Shirkat al-Wujuh is accepted by Hanafis but rejected by Shafi'is and Malikis due to the absence of capital. This ikhtilaf is a legitimate difference of opinion.

Mufawada is a distinctive form of partnership in the Hanafi school, characterized by absolute equality between partners. This lesson defines it and lays out the strict conditions that must be met, supported by hadith and athar. Define mufawada and its three equalities

This reasoning distinguishes mufawada from other partnerships where a slave may participate.

Mufawada is that two men become partners and are equal in their capital, authority, and liability. [1307] (5) It is valid between two free, Muslim, adult, sane men, and is not valid between a free man and a slave.
In brief, mufawada partnership is that two individuals become partners, so both are equal in capital, authority, and debt. The meaning of mufawada is equality. So both partners contribute equal capital, both have equal authority in buying and selling, and any debt arising from trade falls equally on both, and both are equally responsible for its repayment. This is called mufawada partnership.
The proof for the permissibility of mufawada partnership is this hadith: From Salih ibn Suhayb from his father who said: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) said: 'There are three things that contain blessing: selling on credit, mufawada, and mixing wheat with barley for household use, not for sale.' (Ibn Majah, Bab al-Shirkah wa al-Mudarabah, p. 326, no. 2289) In this hadith, most narrations have 'muqaradah' from qard, and another has 'mufawada' from fawdah. The common narration is 'muqaradah'. This narration does not serve the purpose, but the narration with mufawada shows the virtue of mufawada partnership. (2) There is an athar: From Ibn Sirin: 'Mufawada is in all capital.' (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, Bab al-Mufawadin, vol. 8, p. 259, no. 15138) From this athar, the permissibility of mufawada partnership is established. And for the condition that both partners' capital must be equal, there is this athar: Sufyan informed us: 'Mufawada does not exist unless equality in capital and they mix their wealth, and mufawada and partnership do not exist with goods.' From this athar it is understood that in mufawada partnership, both partners' capital must be equal. The next part of the same athar indicates that they are also equal in debt: 'And if one of the mufawada partners borrows and says I borrowed such and such, he is believed against his partner; and if one of them dies, the other may take; and if the creditor wishes, he may take from either; and if one sells his merchandise, the buyer may take from either of them.' (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, Bab al-Mufawadin, vol. 8, p. 259, no. 15140) This athar states that the selling, buying, and cancellation of one will be binding on the other. From this, equality in authority is established. [1307] (5) So mufawada partnership is valid between two free Muslims, adult, sane, and is not valid between a free man and a slave. If both are free, both Muslim, both sane and adult, then mufawada between them is valid. But if one is free and the other is a slave, then mufawada between them is not valid.
The reason is that in mufawada, both partners are each other's agents (wakil) and guarantors (kafil). That is, when one buys, half is for himself and half as agent for his partner. And if a debt arises, half is on him and half on his partner as guarantor. They can only be each other's agent and guarantor when both are free. If one is a slave, he cannot be an agent or guarantor. Therefore, he cannot enter into mufawada. Hence, mufawada is not valid between a free man and a slave. (4) The earlier athar stated: 'When there is a mufawada partnership, the order of each is valid against his partner in selling, buying, and cancellation.' (Musannaf 'Abd al-Razzaq, Bab al-Mufawadin, p. 359, no. 15137) That in mufawada partnership, each is a partner to the other in selling, buying, and cancellation.
Footnote: (a) Ibn Sirin said: Mufawada can only be in all capital. (b) If one borrows in mufawada and says he borrowed such and such, his partner is also believed. If one dies, the other may take. The creditor may take from whichever he wishes. The buyer may take from whichever he wishes. (c) Ibn Sirin reported: If there is mufawada, then each one's transaction is also for his partner in selling, buying, and cancellation.

Commentary
This passage defines and provides proofs for mufawada partnership (shirkat al-mufawada). The definition requires equality in three respects: capital (mal), authority (tasarruf), and liability (dayn). The word 'mufawada' itself means equality. The proof for its permissibility is a hadith from Ibn Majah, though the author notes the more common narration is 'muqaradah' (mudarabah), but the mufawada version indicates blessing. Additional athar from Ibn Sirin and Sufyan establish that mufawada requires equal capital (not goods) and that each partner's transactions are binding on the other. The passage then states the validity conditions: both partners must be free, Muslim, adult, and sane. It is invalid between a free person and a slave because in mufawada, partners act as mutual agents (wakil) and guarantors (kafil), and a slave lacks legal capacity for these roles. The footnote adds that mufawada covers all capital and that partners are believed regarding debts and can be pursued by creditors interchangeably.

وکیل (wakil): An agent authorized to act on behalf of another in transactions.
کفیل (kafil): A guarantor who assumes responsibility for a debt or obligation.

Mufawadah: Definition and Conditions of Validity
Defines mufawadah and states its validity conditions with proof from athar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mufawadah partnership and list the conditions required for its validity.

Core Idea
Mufawadah is only valid between two free, Muslim, adult, sane men because each must be agent and guarantor for the other.

This lesson explains the Hanafi concept of mufawadah (full partnership) and the essential conditions for its validity. The text defines the partnership and then provides the ruling along with the underlying rationale, supported by narrations from Ibn Sirin and Sufyan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its ruling
The text defines mufawadah as a partnership where two men are equal in wealth, transactions, and liability. The ruling is that it is valid (sahih) only when all conditions are met; otherwise invalid (batil).
2
List the conditions from the text
The conditions are: both partners must be free, Muslim, adult (baligh), sane (aqil), and equal in wealth, transactions, and liability.
These are shurut for validity.
3
Understand the rationale
Each partner acts as agent (wakil) and guarantor (kafil) for the other. A slave lacks legal capacity for wakalah and kafalah, so mufawadah with a slave is invalid.
This rationale explains why freedom is essential.
4
Analyze the proof (athar)
The text cites narrations from Muhammad ibn Sirin and Sufyan establishing equality in wealth and liability as necessary conditions. These are mursal hadiths, considered zanni in thubut but qati in dalalah for Hanafis.
The principle 'la takunu al-mufawadah hatta yastawiya fi al-mal' is used as evidence.
5
Apply to a counterexample
If one partner is a slave or non-Muslim, the partnership is invalid. The text specifically says it is not permissible between free and slave.
Other schools may differ; Hanafi is strict on equality and capacity.

The Two Pillars of Mufawadah: Agency and Guarantee
States the two essential bases of mufawadah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two essential elements of the mufawadah contract (wakalah and kafalah) and explain how each operates in the distribution of purchases and debts.

Core Idea
Mufawadah partnership is built on mutual agency for purchases and mutual guarantee for debts.

This brief ruling from the Hanafi text establishes the legal foundation of the mufawadah partnership. Unlike simple partnerships based on joint ownership, mufawadah requires two specific contractual relationships: agency (wakalah) and guarantee (kafalah). Understanding these elements is crucial to distinguishing mufawadah from other forms of shirkah and to applying the rules correctly in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its basis
The passage states that the mufawadah contract is 'contracted upon agency and guarantee'. Recognize that these two elements are not optional but constitute the foundation of the contract.
Mufawadah is the highest level of partnership in Hanafi fiqh, requiring equal capital, equal shares in profit and loss, and full trust.
2
Understand wakalah (agency) in this context
Each partner is an agent for the other in all transactions. This means that when one partner buys something, half of the purchase belongs to the other partner automatically, and the buying partner acts as an agent for the non-purchasing partner.
This mutual agency is unrestricted; each partner can transact on behalf of the other without specific permission.
3
Understand kafalah (guarantee) in this context
Each partner is a guarantor for the debts incurred by the other. If one partner takes a debt, half of that debt becomes the responsibility of the other partner, as he is a guarantor.
This guarantee is also mutual and without limit, making each partner liable for half of any debt the other incurs in the course of business.
4
Trace the practical effect
The commentary explains the arithmetic: any purchase is split half to the purchasing partner and half to the other under agency; any debt is split half to the incurring partner and half to the other under guarantee. This dual distribution is the practical consequence of the two elements.
The 'effect' (athar) refers to earlier narrations from Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq that support this ruling.
5
Distinguish mufawadah from other partnerships
Contrast mufawadah with inan (limited partnership) where agency and guarantee are not automatically present. In inan, each partner must specifically authorize the other for transactions, and each is not automatically liable for the other's debts.
This distinction is essential for fatwa and transaction structuring.

Effect of Inheritance or Gift on Shirkat al-Mufawadah
Ruling on inheritance/gift in mufawadah with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a mufawadah partner inherits or receives a gift of partnership capital and explain why it converts to inan.

Core Idea
Inheritance or gift that disrupts capital equality immediately voids mufawadah, converting it to inan.

Shirkat al-Mufawadah requires absolute equality in capital and liability. What happens if one partner receives additional wealth through inheritance or gift? The Hanafi school rules that the mufawadah becomes invalid (batil) and automatically transforms into a Shirkat al-Inan, which tolerates unequal contributions. This lesson examines the conditions, proof, and exceptions to this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the requirement of capital equality
Recall that mufawadah demands both partners contribute equal capital. Any event that breaks this equality threatens the contract's validity.
The athar states: 'لا تكون المفاوضة حتى تكون سواء في المال' (Mufawadah is not valid until they are equal in wealth).
2
Determine the effect of inheritance or gift
If one partner inherits or receives a gift of property that can be part of the mufawadah capital (dirhams, dinars, current coins), and takes possession, the capital is no longer equal.
Possession (qabd) is required for gifts because ownership transfers only upon takeover.
3
Recognize the automatic conversion
The mufawadah becomes batil (invalid) but does not dissolve completely; it converts into a Shirkat al-Inan, which permits unequal capital.
Inan partnership is the default form; mufawadah is a special contract with stricter conditions.
4
Distinguish type of property
Only property that is eligible for mufawadah (cash) triggers the invalidation. Inheriting goods or real estate does not affect equality because those are not part of the mufawadah capital.
The commentator clarifies: 'If he inherits property in which mufawadah is not valid, like goods, then the mufawadah does not become invalid.'
5

Ruling on liability for purchase price and recourse between partners

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule of liability for purchases in Shirkat al-'Inan and distinguish it from Shirkat al-Mufawada by applying the agency-suretyship distinction.

Core Idea
In 'Inan partnership, the seller can only claim from the partner who made the purchase—each is an agent, not a surety.

When a partner buys goods for a partnership, who is the seller supposed to ask for payment? The answer depends on the type of partnership. This lesson focuses on Shirkat al-'Inan, where each partner is only an agent (wakil) for the other. The ruling is clear: the seller demands the price solely from the purchasing partner. The purchasing partner then recovers the other partner's share internally.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of partnership
The passage discusses Shirkat al-'Inan, where partners are agents (wakil) but not sureties (kafil). This determines external liability.
In Mufawada, partners are both wakil and kafil, which changes the ruling.
2
State the ruling on external liability
The seller can only demand full payment from the partner who made the purchase. The non-purchasing partner has no direct liability to the seller.
This is the key point: the seller's claim is limited to the contracting partner.
3
Explain the reason: agency vs suretyship
In 'Inan, each partner is an agent for the other in purchases, but not a surety for the other's debts. Therefore, the seller cannot claim from the non-purchasing partner.
Suretyship would allow the seller to claim from either partner, as in Mufawada.
4
Describe the internal recourse
The purchasing partner can recover the other partner's share of the price from him, since the goods are owned jointly.
This is an internal matter between the partners, not involving the seller.
5
Contrast with Mufawada
In Mufawada, partners are sureties for each other, so the seller could claim from either partner. This difference stems from the scope of authority in each partnership type.
The practical consequence: in 'Inan, the seller bears the risk of the purchasing partner's insolvency; in Mufawada, the risk is shared.

Partnership capital must exist at commencement; its destruction beforehand voids the contract.

This is a ruling (hukm) that a partner's possession of the partnership property is a possession of trust (yad amana). This means that if the property is lost or destroyed without the partner's negligence or transgression (ta'addi), he is not liable (daman). The reasoning is that the partner's possession is with the consent of the other partner and is gratuitous (ghayr mubaddal), similar to a deposit (wadi'a). The text provides two athar (traditions from the Companions or Successors) as evidence: First, that 'Ali (RA) did not hold a common hired worker (ajir mushtarak) liable for loss without transgression, because he had permission to possess. By analogy (qiyas), the partner is similar. Second, that the mudarib (one who receives capital in mudaraba) is a trustee, as stated by al-Hasan, so by extension, a partner is also a trustee. This ruling establishes that partners are not guarantors of the capital; they are trustees. However, if a partner transgresses (e.g., uses the property without permission), he becomes liable. This is the Hanafi position based on the principle that permission negates liability (al-izn yunafi al-daman).

Definition and ruling with proof from early practice

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A partnership based on reputation and creditworthiness is valid in Hanafi law, even without any capital.

This lesson introduces Shirkat al‑Wujuh (partnership by creditworthiness), a unique Hanafi institution where partners contribute nothing but their good name and ability to secure credit. The source passage defines the arrangement, declares it permissible, and supports it with a historical precedent from the early Muslim community. Understanding this ruling clarifies that Islamic commercial law recognizes trust and social standing as legitimate capital.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Shirkat al‑Wujuh is a contract where two or more persons with no financial capital form a partnership based solely on their creditworthiness (wajh). They buy goods on credit using their reputation and sell for profit.
The term 'wujuh' literally means 'faces' — referring to the partners' ability to obtain credit through their social standing.
2
Identify the conditions for validity
For the partnership to be valid (sahih), both partners must be legally competent, agree to share profit according to their liability (dhaman), and the partnership must involve buying goods on credit and selling them. There is no requirement to contribute any capital.
Profit is divided based on the liability assumed by each partner, not on capital, since there is no capital.
3
Examine the evidence (dalil)
The commentator cites an athar from the Muwatta' of Imam Muhammad: Ya‘qub partnered with ‘Uthman ibn ‘Affan without capital, using only his creditworthiness. ‘Uthman agreed, establishing a precedent from the Sahaba.
This athar demonstrates that the practical application was known and accepted in the early Muslim community.
4
Note the profit division principle
In Shirkat al‑Wujuh, profit is shared according to each partner's share of the liability (dhaman). If one partner assumes more liability (e.g., guarantees more of the debt), he is entitled to a higher share of profit.
This is based on the Hanafi maxim: 'Profit is proportional to liability.'
5
Recognize the cross‑madhhab dispute
While Hanafis consider this partnership valid, some Shafi‘i scholars reject it because capital is not contributed. The Hanafi position is based on istihsan (juristic preference) and the practice of the Sahaba.
The dispute centers on whether capital is essential (Shafi‘i) or whether creditworthiness can substitute (Hanafi).

[1330] (28) As for partnership of creditworthiness, two men enter into partnership while they have no wealth, on condition that they buy with their creditworthiness and sell, so the partnership is valid on this basis. The form of shirkat al-wujuh is that both partners have no capital, but they buy others' goods on credit through their own connections and sell and earn profit. Thus two men making such a partnership is permissible. It is called wujuh because one buys and sells through his face and authority. Evidence for its permissibility is in this long athar: He said: My father informed me, he said: I used to sell cloth in the time of Umar ibn al-Khattab, and Umar said: No non-Arab should sell in our market, for they do not understand the religion and do not uphold proper weights and measures. Ya'qub said: So I went to Uthman ibn Affan and said to him: Do you have a cold gain? He said: What is it? I said: Cloth, whose place I know, with its owner selling it cheaply, unable to sell it. I will buy it for you, then sell it for you. He said: Yes, etc. (from Muwatta' Imam Muhammad, chapter of partnership). In this athar, Ya'qub had no wealth; he partnered with Uthman for cloth, proving shirkat al-wujuh is permissible. Footnote: My father informed me that he said: I used to trade cloth in the time of Umar ibn al-Khattab. Umar said: No non-Arab should trade in my market, because they do not understand the religion and do not give correct weights and measures. Ya'qub said: I went to Uthman ibn Affan and asked: Do you have a cold gain? He asked: What is it? I said: Cloth, I know its place, it is being sold cheap, its owner cannot sell it. I will sell it for you, then buy it for you. Uthman said: Yes.

Commentary
This passage establishes the validity of shirkat al-wujuh (partnership by creditworthiness) in the Hanafi school. The matn defines it as a partnership of two persons without capital, relying on their reputation to buy on credit and sell. The commentary elaborates that this is permissible and traces the derivation of the term wujuh from 'face' or 'authority'. To support its permissibility, the commentator cites a historical report (athar) from the time of Umar and Uthman, where Ya'qub partnered with Uthman without possessing capital, using only his creditworthiness. The footnote provides a full translation and explanation of the athar.

Key Terms
شركة الوجوه (shirkat al-wujuh): Partnership in which partners contribute their creditworthiness (literally 'faces') instead of capital, buying on credit and selling.
وجه (wajh): Creditworthiness, reputation, influence derived from one's social standing.
