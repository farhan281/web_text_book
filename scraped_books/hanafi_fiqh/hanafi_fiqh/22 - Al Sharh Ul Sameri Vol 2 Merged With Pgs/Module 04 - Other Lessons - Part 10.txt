First Valid Form of Muzara'ah According to Sahibayn
Explains the first valid form of muzara'ah according to Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the first valid form of muzara'ah according to the Sahibayn and explain why the produce belongs entirely to the seed owner.

Core Idea
When land and seed are from one party and labor and ox from another, the seed owner gets all produce.

This passage introduces the first of four forms of muzara'ah (sharecropping) that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) consider valid. In this form, the land and seed belong to one person, while the labor and ox belong to another. The produce goes entirely to the seed owner, and the other party is treated as a hired worker. This ruling is supported by a hadith where the Prophet ordered the produce to be given to the seed owner and expenses to be returned to the landowner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and contributions
The first form involves two parties: one provides land and seed, the other provides labor and ox. Note that the ox is considered part of the worker's contribution, similar to labor.
The ox is not owned by the landowner; it is provided by the worker.
2
Determine the ruling
According to the Sahibayn, this form is permissible (ja'iz). The entire produce belongs to the owner of the seed.
The seed owner effectively hires the worker and the ox, so the arrangement is like an employment contract.
3
Analyze the evidence
The passage cites a hadith from Abu Dawud: the Prophet ordered that the crop be given to the seed owner and expenses be returned to the landowner. This shows that the seed owner is entitled to all produce.
The hadith illustrates the principle that the one who provides the seed owns the crop.
4
Note the ikhtilaf with Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa may consider this form invalid. The Sahibayn's view is considered mufta bihi in this case.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the Sahibayn's position is relied upon.

Sharecropping with Mixed Ownership: Validity and Crop Ownership
Rulings on sharecropping with mixed asset ownership and evidence for crop ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of each sharecropping scenario when assets are owned by different parties and explain who owns the crop based on the seed-ownership principle.

Core Idea
In Hanafi sharecropping, the crop belongs to the seed owner; other contributors receive wages.

Sharecropping (muzara'ah) commonly involves multiple parties contributing land, labor, oxen, and seeds. The Hanafi school provides detailed rulings on the validity of such contracts depending on who owns which asset, and a fundamental principle: the owner of the seed owns the crop. This lesson unpacks five scenarios from our text, the evidence for the seed-ownership rule, and an intra-Hanafi disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage discusses the validity of sharecropping contracts when land, labor, oxen, and seeds are owned by different combinations of parties. First, locate each scenario (3, 4, 5 and the extra scenario) and state whether it is valid (ja'iz) or invalid (batil) according to the Hanafi school.
Scenario numbers (3), (4), (5) refer to the sequence in the matn.
2
Classify the ownership combinations
For each scenario, list which party owns which asset. For example, scenario (3): land from one person, labor+oxen+seeds from another. This helps you see the pattern of validity.
The key factor is whether the seed owner is the landowner or not.
3
Identify the crop ownership rule
The commentary presents two opposing proofs: the athar of Mujahid (seed owner gets all crop) and the hadith of Rafi' (landowner gets all crop). Determine which proof the Hanafi school relies on and why.
The Hanafi response distinguishes the Rafi' hadith as applicable only to unauthorized sowing.
4
Apply the rule to each scenario
Using the seed-ownership rule, determine who gets the crop in each valid scenario. In invalid scenario (5), the crop still goes to the seed owner, but the landowner receives rent. Check the commentary for explanation.
In valid contracts, the crop is divided per agreement; here the rule applies to void contracts.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Abu Yusuf narrates that scenario (5) is valid, unlike the zahir al-riwayah position. Understand the reason: the ox is not subordinate to the land, so hiring it for future produce is not customary.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that scenario (5) is invalid.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh al-Samari presents multiple rulings on sharecropping (muzara'ah) when the assets (land, oxen, seeds, labor) are owned by different parties. The author first states three scenarios in the matn: (3) If only the oxen are from one party and the rest from another, it is valid. The commentary presents two views: one from the athar of Mujahid that the crop belongs to the seed owner, and another from a hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij that the crop belongs to the landowner. Three Essential Conditions for Valid Muzara'ah (Sharecropping)
States three conditions for valid muzara'ah with proofs from hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three essential conditions for a valid muzara'ah contract in Hanafi fiqh and identify which hadith supports each condition.

Core Idea
Muzara'ah requires a known duration, a common share of produce, and no fixed quantity for either party — violating any one makes the contract invalid.

The sharecropping contract (muzara'ah) is a partnership in cultivation where the landowner and worker share the crop. The Hanafi school has laid down precise conditions to prevent dispute and unfairness (gharar). In this passage from Sharh al-Thamiri, three non-negotiable conditions are explained with proofs from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three conditions
Read the passage carefully and extract the three conditions that the author states are required for a valid muzara'ah: a fixed, known term; the crop must be shared in common (musha'); and no fixed quantity (like a specific number of qafiz) may be reserved for either party.
These conditions are found in the numbered points [1704] (6), [1705] (7), and [1706] (8).
2
Analyze the proof for condition 1: fixed term
The author cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas on salam: 'Whoever pays in advance for dates, let him pay for a known measure, known weight, and a known term.' Though this hadith concerns salam, the analogy (qiyas) is that a known term is essential to avoid dispute. Write down the hadith reference and explain how it proves the condition.
The hadith is from Muslim; the author uses it as a dalil by way of analogy.
3
Analyze the proof for condition 2: common share
The author cites the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij, where the Prophet forbade renting land in exchange for a specific portion of land (e.g., a corner reserved for the owner). The rationale is that if one party gets a fixed portion, they might end up with all or nothing if that portion is affected. Identify the hadith and explain how it leads to the requirement that the entire crop be shared proportionally.
The hadith is in Bukhari and Muslim.
4
Connect condition 3 to condition 2
The third condition (no fixed quantity) is a logical extension of the second. If a fixed measure (qafiz) is set aside for one party, it creates the same problem as a fixed portion of land: one party may receive nothing. Understand that this is a straightforward application of the same principle.
The author says this condition invalidates the contract directly (bātil).
5
Apply: test a hypothetical contract
Consider a contract where the landowner and sharecropper agree on a 5-year term, a 50-50 share of the crop, but the landowner also stipulates that the first 200 kg of wheat are his. Identify which condition is violated and state the ruling. Then explain why the hadith of Rafi' applies.
This involves specifying a fixed quantity for one party, making the contract invalid.

Commentary
This passage from the Sharh al-Thamiri presents three essential conditions for the validity of a muzara'ah (sharecropping) contract, each supported by hadith proofs. The conditions are: (1) a fixed, known duration (muddah ma'lumah), (2) the produce must be shared in common (mushtarak) between the parties, and (3) no fixed quantity (e.g., a specific number of qafiz) may be allocated to one party exclusively. The commentary explains the reasoning: without a fixed term, disputes arise because each party has conflicting interests regarding when to end the contract. The hadith of Ibn Abbas regarding salam (advance payment) is cited analogically to emphasize the need for a known term. For the second condition, the hadith of Rafi' ibn Khadij is cited, showing that the Prophet forbade a practice where a specific portion of the land was designated for the landowner, because it could lead to one party getting nothing if that portion or the rest failed. This demonstrates that the share must be a proportion of the entire crop, not a fixed quantity. The third condition follows from the same principle: stipulating a fixed amount for one party vitiates the contract, as it could result in unfairness. The overall ruling is that if any of these conditions is violated, the muzara'ah is invalid (batil).

Key Terms
مدة معلومة (muddah ma'lumah): A fixed, known duration for the sharecropping contract, such as a specific number of months or years.
مشاع (musha'): Shared in common; the produce must be owned jointly by the parties, not allocated specific portions beforehand.
قفزان (qafzan): A specific dry measure (plural of qafiz); stipulating a fixed quantity for one party invalidates the contract.

Invalid Conditions in Muzara'ah: Earmarking Produce from Canals
Ruling on invalid conditional allocation of produce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on stipulating produce from specific irrigation canals in a sharecropping contract, explain why it is invalid, and cite the hadith proof from Sahih Muslim.

Core Idea
Earmarking produce by location in muzara'ah creates gharar and invalidates the contract.

This passage addresses a common pitfall in sharecropping (muzāra‘ah) contracts: parties trying to allocate uncertain yields based on location. The author uses an explicit hadith from Sahih Muslim to demonstrate that such conditions are forbidden because they introduce uncertainty (gharar). We will examine the ruling and its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a sharecropping contract where the landowner and worker agree that the produce from the main canal banks or small ditches belongs exclusively to the landowner, and the rest is shared. The ruling: this condition renders the contract invalid (fasid).
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites a hadith from Sahih Muslim where Rāfi‘ ibn Khadīj reports that the Prophet forbade such conditional allocations because they lead to gharar.
The hadith is a sunnah ahad, ẓannī al-thubūt and ẓannī al-dalāla.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalālah
The hadith explicitly states 'for that he forbade it' (zajara). The phrase 'known and guaranteed' (ma‘lūm maḍmūn) indicates the condition for validity. The earmarking is uncertain because the yield on banks is variable.
The prohibition indicates the condition is invalid.
4
Note the ikhtilāf
Some other schools might allow such conditions if both parties agree, but the Hanafi school strictly prohibits it due to gharar.
The muftā bihī is the Hanafi position.
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
If a contract stipulates 'all wheat from the north side of the field belongs to the worker', it is invalid because it is uncertain.
The contract must share all produce proportionally.

Output Distribution in Valid Sharecropping: Honoring Conditions
Ruling on output distribution in valid sharecropping

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid contract's terms are binding; the produce is divided exactly as agreed.

This lesson addresses a key principle in Hanafi mu'amalat: once a sharecropping contract is valid and all conditions are met, the output must be distributed according to what the parties stipulated. The author uses the well-known hadith, 'Muslims are bound by their conditions,' to reinforce this. We will examine the ruling, its proof, and the underlying principle.

The hadith is a general principle for all contracts.

The hadith is a strong textual evidence (dalil naqli).

Worker Gets Nothing When No Crop in Muzara'ah
Ruling on worker's compensation when land yields nothing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the worker's entitlement in muzara'ah when no produce is obtained and explain the principle that the worker's share is contingent on actual harvest.

Core Idea
In muzara'ah, no produce means no share for the worker—risk is shared, not guaranteed.

Muzara'ah is a partnership in cultivation where the worker's reward is a percentage of the harvest. What happens when the crop fails entirely? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position that the worker receives nothing, because the contract does not guarantee a fixed wage. We will explore the reasoning and contrast it with other views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contract Type
Recognize that the ruling applies specifically to muzara'ah (sharecropping), where the worker's compensation is a share of the produce, not a fixed wage.
This distinguishes it from contracts like ijara (hiring) where wages are owed regardless of output.
2
State the Ruling
The text clearly says: if the land produces nothing, the worker gets nothing. There is no obligation on the landowner to pay from his own pocket.
The ruling is a direct consequence of the contingent nature of the share.
3
Understand the Rationale
The worker's entitlement is tied to the existence of produce. Since no produce exists, no share is due. The landowner also receives nothing, so both parties share the risk.
This avoids making the landowner bear all the risk.
4
Contrast with Other Views
Some schools may argue that the worker deserves ujrat al-mithl if the landowner caused the failure, but the Hanafi position requires that such compensation be stipulated in the contract; otherwise, the worker bears the loss.
The Hanafi view prioritizes the terms of the contract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on wage cap in invalid Muzari'ah and apply the mufta bihi view.

Core Idea
In invalid Muzari'ah, the worker's wage is capped by the agreed share, not the full market rate.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about compensation when a Muzari'ah (sharecropping) contract is invalid. The central question: Is the worker entitled to the full market wage, or is his wage limited to the share he originally agreed to? Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of contractual consent and default compensation in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract and its invalidity
Recognise that the Muzari'ah contract is invalid (فاسد) due to a missing or defective condition. In this case, the seed comes from the landowner, which typically renders the contract invalid under Hanafi rules.
The invalidity triggers the default rule of ajr al-mithl (equivalent wage).
2
Note the default wage principle
For invalid contracts where work has been performed, the worker is entitled to ajr al-mithl — the market-equivalent wage. The dispute is about whether this wage can exceed the share originally stipulated.
Ajr al-mithl is a compensatory measure, not a punitive one.
3
Understand the dispute: cap vs. no cap
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that ajr al-mithl is capped at the stipulated share. Imam Muhammad holds that it is uncapped and equals the full market wage.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, meaning both views are from the same school.
4
Analyse the rationale for each position
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf argue that the worker consented to the stipulated share, so he cannot claim more. Imam Muhammad argues that the invalid contract nullifies the agreement, so the market wage is the only fair compensation.
The difference lies in the weight given to the worker's initial consent.
5
Identify the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position
The preferred view for fatwa is that of Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf: the wage is capped. This preserves the contractual consent and avoids burdening the landowner with an unexpected higher wage.
Tarjih is based on the principle of maintaining consent and avoiding hardship.

The Validity of Musaqah: Abu Hanifa vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on contract validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rulings of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on musaqah, cite their respective proofs, and explain the practical difference between the two positions.

Core Idea
Musaqah is invalid per Abu Hanifa but valid per Sahibayn; the mufta bihi opinion allows it with two conditions.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute over the validity of musaqah – a contract where a worker irrigates and cares for fruit trees in exchange for a share of the harvest. Imam Abu Hanifa considers it invalid, while his two disciples, the Sahibayn, allow it under specific conditions. Understanding this dispute reveals how the Hanafi school balances textual evidence with practical necessity (istihsan).

Guided Path
1
Identify the positions
Start by clearly stating the ruling of each imam. Abu Hanifa says musaqah is batil (invalid) for a portion of the fruit. The Sahibayn say it is jayiz (valid) when two conditions are met: a known duration and a specified common share.
Note the contrast: Abu Hanifa's ruling is absolute nullity; the Sahibayn make validity conditional.
2
Examine the proofs (dalil)
Identify the primary evidence for each side. Abu Hanifa relies on hadith forbidding muzara'ah and mukhabarah, which he extends to musaqah by analogy. The Sahibayn rely on the hadith of Khaibar, where the Prophet contracted for half the produce, indicating permissibility.
Both sides use hadith; the dispute is over which hadith takes precedence (general prohibition vs. specific practice).
3
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical consequence of the disagreement. If musaqah is invalid per Abu Hanifa, the contract is void and the worker receives ujrat al-mithl (fair wage). If valid per Sahibayn, the contract is enforceable and the worker gets the agreed share.
This difference affects millions of agricultural transactions; the mufta bihi opinion facilitates such contracts.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
Know which position is relied upon in the school. The later Hanafi authorities, including al-Marghinani, prefer the Sahibayn's view due to widespread need and the strength of the Khaibar hadith. Thus, for fatwa, musaqah is valid with the two conditions.
This shows how the school resolves internal ikhtilaf through tarjih based on need and textual harmony.
