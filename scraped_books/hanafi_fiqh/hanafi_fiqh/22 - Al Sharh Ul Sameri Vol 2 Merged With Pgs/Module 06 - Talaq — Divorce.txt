Minor's and Insane's Contracts and Declarations Invalid
Ruling on minor's and insane's verbal acts with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of contracts, declarations, divorce, and emancipation by a minor or insane person, and identify the exception for contracts that may have benefit.

Core Idea
A minor and insane person have no legal capacity for binding verbal acts; their divorce and emancipation are void, but contracts may be suspended pending guardian approval.

This lesson addresses a fundamental principle of legal capacity (ahliyyah) in Hanafi fiqh. The passage establishes that the statements of a minor (sabi) and an insane person (majnun) are generally of no legal effect, except for contracts that may involve both benefit and harm, which are suspended until the guardian gives permission. The proofs are drawn from hadith evidence, including the famous hadith of the lifting of the pen.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their general ruling
The passage states that contracts, declarations, divorce, and emancipation by a minor or insane person are not valid. Note that 'not valid' for contracts means suspended, not automatically void.
Talaq and itaq are completely invalid; aqd is mawquf.
2
Distinguish between types of acts
Some acts involve pure harm (iqrar, talaq, itaq) and others may involve benefit or harm (aqd like sale). The ruling differs: pure harm acts are void, while beneficial contracts are suspended.
This distinction is based on the nature of the act.
3
Examine the hadith evidence
The author cites three hadith: 1) the hadith that every divorce is valid except that of the mentally deficient; 2) the hadith of Ali that a boy's divorce is invalid until puberty; 3) the hadith of lifting the pen from three: child, insane, sleeping.
These hadith establish the principle of lack of accountability.
4
Apply the principle of capacity
The underlying reason is that these individuals lack complete intellect (aql), which is a prerequisite for legal capacity (ahliyyah al-ada'). Their verbal acts have no legal effect unless a guardian intervenes for contracts.
This is a consistent Hanafi principle.
5
Understand the exception for contracts
Contracts like sale are not automatically invalid because they can be beneficial; they are suspended (mawquf) on the guardian's permission. The guardian can ratify them, making them valid retrospectively.
This exception does not apply to divorce or emancipation.

Commentary
This passage continues the rulings on legal restriction for minors and insane persons. It explicitly states that their contracts ('uqud), declarations (iqrar), divorce (talaq), and emancipation (itaq) are invalid. The commentary explains why: these acts are verbal (aqwal) and they lack intellect (aql). However, contracts that can be beneficial, like sale, are an exception — but even these are not valid outright; they are suspended pending guardian's approval. Declarations, divorce, and emancipation are purely harmful (divorce loses a wife, emancipation loses a slave), so they are entirely prohibited. The proof comes from multiple hadith: the hadith of the Prophet that divorce by the mentally deficient is invalid, the hadith of Ali that a boy's divorce is invalid until puberty, and the well-known hadith that the pen is lifted from three persons: the child, the insane, and the sleeping. Thus, the Hanafi school firmly establishes that these individuals have no capacity for such binding verbal acts.

Slave's Divorce is Effective
Ruling statement: a slave's divorce is effective.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that a slave's divorce is valid and effective in Hanafi law and explain the underlying principle of personal autonomy.

Core Idea
A slave's divorce is valid because it is a personal act, not a property matter.

In Hanafi fiqh, the capacity to divorce is not tied to status as a free man. Even a slave husband can pronounce talaq, and his divorce takes effect immediately. This lesson establishes that ruling and the rationale behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
Read the first line: 'وينفذ طلاقه' — 'his divorce is effective.' The subject is 'العبد' (the slave). The ruling is that his talaq takes effect.
The ruling is stated positively; no conditions are mentioned here.
2
Locate the evidence
The commentary references a hadith previously mentioned. Recall that the Prophet (peace be upon him) affirmed a slave's right to divorce. This serves as the dalil.
Evidence is not quoted directly but is implied to support the ruling.
3
Understand the rationale
The passage states: 'This is his personal loss, so he will have the authority to bear it.' Divorce is a personal matter affecting the slave himself, hence he has full authority to initiate it.
Contrast with financial matters where a slave lacks capacity.
4
Apply the principle
Generalize: in Hanafi law, personal actions (marriage, divorce, acceptance of Islam) are within a slave's capacity. Master's permission is not required for talaq.
Exceptions: if the slave is coerced by the master, the talaq is not effective.

Master's Divorce on Slave's Wife is Invalid
Ruling that master cannot divorce slave's wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a master's divorce of his slave's wife is invalid and identify the proof from the hadith.

Core Idea
Divorce is exclusively the husband's right; the master has no authority over the slave's marriage.

This lesson addresses a specific issue in slave marriage: the master has control over many aspects of the slave's life, but not over divorce. The text clearly states that only the husband (the slave) can divorce his wife, and the master's attempt is null. Let's explore the reasoning and the prophetic evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The master pronouncing divorce on his slave's wife. What is the hukm? The divorce does not occur (invalid).
2
Locate the dalil
What proof is cited? The hadith from Ibn Abbas where the Prophet forbids a master from separating his slave and his wife, saying 'innama al-talaq li man akhadha al-saq'.
The hadith is from Ibn Majah and al-Daraqutni.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the hadith prove the ruling? The explicit phrase 'divorce is for the one who takes the shin' limits divorce to the husband (who consummates the marriage), excluding the master.
4
Understand the legal principle
The underlying principle is that divorce is a personal right of the husband. The master, not being the husband, cannot exercise this right.
This is a clear application of the general rule in Islamic law.

Slave's Divorce is Effective; Master's Divorce is Not
Rulings on divorce by and over a slave's wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a slave's divorce and explain why the master's divorce of the slave's wife is ineffective, citing the evidence and reasoning.

Core Idea
A slave can divorce his wife because it is his personal loss; his master cannot divorce her.

This passage presents two complementary rulings: first, a slave's divorce of his wife is legally effective; second, the master's divorce of the slave's wife is not effective. The author explains that the slave has authority over his own marital rights, as divorce entails personal consequences. The ruling is supported by a hadith and a rational principle of personal loss. Let us unpack these rulings and their proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the Arabic: 'وينفذ طلاقه' means his (the slave's) divorce is effective. 'ولا يقع طلاق مولاه على امرأته' means the master's divorce of the slave's wife does not take effect.
These two statements together clarify who has the right to divorce the slave's wife.
2
Analyze the evidence
The author references a hadith (mentioned earlier in the text) that permits a slave to divorce. For the master, the reasoning is that the master does not personally suffer the consequences of the divorce; it is the slave's loss.
The hadith is a proof (dalil) from the Sunnah. The rational argument (istidlal) is based on personal harm.
3
Understand the principle
The underlying principle is that the right of divorce belongs to the husband because he bears the financial and social consequences. For a slave, he is the husband, so he has the right. The master has no marital connection to the slave's wife.
This principle aligns with the Hanafi view that talaq is a personal right of the husband.
4
Note the cross-madhhab element
Some other schools may differ on whether a slave can divorce without his master's permission. The Hanafi position, as stated here, affirms the slave's independent right.
The passage does not detail the ikhtilaf, but it is implied by the ruling's inclusion.
5
Apply the ruling
State the mufta bihi: a slave's divorce of his wife is valid and effective immediately. The master's divorce of the slave's wife is void and has no legal effect.
This is the operative position for fatwa in the Hanafi school.

Iqrar for a Divorced Wife During Terminal Illness: Lesser of Debt or Inheritance
Ruling on iqrar for divorced wife in terminal illness with example and proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rule that a divorced wife in terminal illness receives the lesser of her acknowledged debt and inheritance share, and explain the reasoning based on preventing harm.

Core Idea
To prevent fraud, a divorced wife in terminal illness receives only the lesser of the iqrar debt and her inheritance.

This passage addresses a situation where a husband divorces his wife three times during his terminal illness, then acknowledges a debt to her, and dies. The wife, still in her waiting period, would normally inherit as a wife. But the Hanafi school restricts the iqrar to prevent the husband from favoring her over other heirs by divorcing her and then acknowledging a large debt. The ruling: she takes the lesser amount.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
What are the key facts: triple divorce in terminal illness, iqrar of debt, death during iddah.
2
State the ruling
The wife receives the lesser of the debt amount and her inheritance share.
3
Understand the rationale
The ruling is based on suspicion of fraud: the husband may have divorced her to circumvent inheritance limits and then used iqrar to give her more.
4
Connect to the proof
The hadith prohibiting causing harm through a will (al-idrar fi al-wasiyyah) is applied by analogy to iqrar, restricting it to prevent harm to other heirs.

No Pre-emption for Non-Wealth Transactions: Khul', Rent, Sulh for Murder, and Emancipation
Rulings: no pre-emption for specified non-wealth transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition for shuf'ah and identify which transactions involving property do not trigger shuf'ah.

Core Idea
Shuf'ah requires the consideration to be wealth; non-wealth transactions like khul', ijara, sulh, and 'itq do not create pre-emption.

Shuf'ah (right of pre-emption) attaches to certain property transfers, but not all. The key condition is that the property must be acquired in exchange for mal (wealth). In this lesson, we examine four specific transactions from the matn where the consideration is not mal: khul' divorce, renting, settlement for murder, and freeing a slave. Understanding these exceptions sharpens your grasp of shuf'ah's legal basis.

Guided Path
1
Recall the condition for shuf'ah
Shuf'ah requires a property transfer for a consideration that is mal (wealth). If the consideration is not mal, shuf'ah does not attach.
This is a core Hanafi principle.
2
List the four exceptions
The text mentions: khul' (divorce compensation), ijara (rent), sulh for intentional murder (blood money), and 'itq (emancipation of a slave).
These are all cases where the property is given for a non-wealth purpose.
3
Analyze why each consideration is not mal
For khul', the compensation is for dissolution of marriage; for ijara, rent is for usufruct not ownership; for sulh, blood is not marketable; for 'itq, freedom is a religious act not a commodity.
The nature of the transaction determines whether the consideration is mal.
4
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Given a new transaction, check if the consideration is mal. If not, shuf'ah does not apply.
Practice with examples to internalize the principle.
