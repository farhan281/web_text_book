Confession of Unspecified 'Darahim' Means Three Dirhams
Ruling stating that unqualified 'darahim' defaults to three dirhams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the minimum amount implied by an unqualified confession of 'darahim' and explain the linguistic basis.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, unqualified 'darahim' defaults to three dirhams due to Arabic plural minimum.

This lesson addresses a simple but important linguistic default in Islamic law. When someone confesses to having 'darahim' without specifying a number, the Hanafi school uses the Arabic language's plural rule to determine the minimum. Understanding this default helps avoid ambiguity in financial claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
Recognize that the passage deals with an iqrar (confession) of a debt using the word 'darahim' without any additional qualification.
The act is a verbal confession in a legal context.
2
Recognize the Linguistic Rule
In Arabic, the plural form (jam') for non-human nouns indicates at least three. This is the 'urf al-lughawi (linguistic custom) applied in Hanafi fiqh.
The plural 'darahim' is considered a jam' qillah (plural of paucity) or general plural; both imply a minimum of three.
3
Apply the Rule
Since the confession is unqualified, the default understanding is that the debtor owes three dirhams. This is binding unless the confessor clarifies a different number.
The passage states 'they are three' (فهى ثلثة) as the default.
4
Note the Exception
If the confessor specifies a number greater than three, that specification is accepted because the plural can include higher numbers. The default only sets the minimum.
The text says 'unless he clarifies more than that' (الا ان يبين اكثر منها).
5
Consider Madhhab Differences
The Hanafi position is based on linguistic custom. Other schools, like the Shafi'i, may treat the confession as ambiguous and require proof or clarification without a default minimum.
This difference affects evidentiary burden in disputes.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
'Kadha kadha' without conjunction is a compound number like eleven; less is not accepted.

In Hanafi fiqh, the precise wording of a confession matters. When a person says 'such-and-such such-and-such dirhams' without the word 'and', the Arabic numeral structure dictates that it means a compound number between 11 and 19. This lesson unpacks that linguistic rule and its legal consequence: the minimum accepted amount is eleven dirhams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the expression
Recognize that the phrase 'kadha kadha' appears twice without any connecting word.
2
Recognize the absence of waw
Notice that there is no conjunction 'waw' between the two occurrences. In Arabic numbers, the numbers 11–19 are written as two words without 'waw'.
3
Analyze the numeral construction
Understand that 'kadha' stands for an unspecified number. Repeating it without a connector follows the pattern of 'ahad ashara' (11), where the second word completes the compound.
4
Apply the ruling
Therefore, the confession cannot be accepted for any amount less than eleven dirhams; it must be at least that amount.

Core Idea
The conjunction 'wa' in 'kadha wa kadha' turns the pair into a compound number starting at twenty-one.

This lesson clarifies a specific rule in Hanafi law regarding confessions (iqrar). When someone says 'such-and-such and such-and-such dirhams,' the minimum amount he can be held to is twenty-one, not a lower number. This is based on the Arabic linguistic convention for compound numbers from 21 to 99, where the two parts are joined by 'waw'. Understanding this helps in interpreting ambiguous financial confessions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase
Recognise 'kadha wa kadha' as two indefinite numbers joined by 'waw'. In Arabic, this construction is used for compound numbers like 21-99.
2

Numbers 21-99 are expressed as: second number (e.g., one) + 'wa' + first number (e.g., twenty). For example, 21 is 'ahad wa ishrun'. The phrase 'kadha wa kadha' mirrors this structure.
3
Apply the minimum
Since the smallest compound number is 21, the confession cannot be interpreted as less than 21 dirhams. However, a larger amount is permissible because it still falls within the compound range.
4
Contrast with 'kadha kadha' (without waw)
The previous lesson likely dealt with a different construction. This distinction is crucial for accurate interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal effect of 'ala' and 'ila' in confessions and apply the rule that they indicate a debt obligation.

Core Idea
In Hanafi fiqh, 'ala' and 'ila' are legal terms creating an acknowledgement of debt.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on confessions using the words 'ala' (upon) and 'ila' (to). In Islamic law, the wording of a confession determines its legal implications. The Hanafi school treats 'ala' and 'ila' as clear expressions of debt, as supported by an athar from Sarq. Understanding this classification is essential for muftis and judges when evaluating claims.

Guided Path

Identify the words under analysis
Recognize that 'ala' (على) and 'ila' (الى) are the key terms in the confession.
Both are prepositions that take on a legal meaning in this context.
2
Understand the linguistic and legal meaning
In Arabic, 'ala' is used to impose an obligation upon oneself. Thus, saying 'ala' means the speaker acknowledges a debt.
The same applies to 'ila', which also imposes an obligation.
3
Cite the supporting evidence
The text cites an athar from Sarq in Darqutni where a man said 'ala' to indicate a debt owed to him.
This athar confirms the legal usage of 'ala' for debt.
4
Derive the ruling
Both 'ala' and 'ila' constitute a valid confession of debt. The amount must be specified later, but the words themselves create the acknowledgement.
The confession is binding once the amount is declared.
5
Note the condition
For the confession to be effective, the amount of debt must be specified. The words alone create a general acknowledgement.
This condition is implied from the broader fiqh of iqrar.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In Hanafi confession, 'indi' and 'ma'i' declare a trust, not a debt.

The words we use in legal declarations carry precise meanings. In the Hanafi school, the phrases 'indi' (عندی) and 'ma'i' (معی) are interpreted as indicating that the speaker holds the item as a trust (amanah), not as a debt. This distinction has a significant practical consequence: if the item is destroyed without negligence, the holder is not liable to compensate. According to the Hanafi school, these words create a confession of a trust (amanah), not a debt. The confessor is merely acknowledging that he holds the item on behalf of the claimant.
The ruling is based on the literal meaning and customary usage.
3
Understand the Linguistic Distinction
Contrast with words like 'علی' (upon me) which indicate debt or obligation. 'Indi' and 'ma'i' describe physical possession, not financial liability.
This is a key principle in the Hanafi approach to confession (iqrar).
4
Apply to a Scenario
Imagine someone says, 'I have Zaid's book with me (indi).' The Hanafi ruling says this is a trust. If the book is lost without negligence, the holder is not liable. If the holder had said 'I owe Zaid a book' it would be a debt.

Ruling on four responses that imply confession of debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four responses that constitute a confession of debt under Hanafi law and explain why each implies acknowledgment.

Core Idea
A debtor's response that acknowledges the debt's existence is a confession—even if he claims payment or asks for time.

In Hanafi law, a confession (iqrar) is not always explicit. Sometimes a debtor's words, while seemingly defensive, actually confirm the claim. This lesson examines four such responses from the matn of Al-Sharh al-Sameri, showing how each amounts to an admission of the debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four responses
List the four statements from the matn: 'Weigh it', 'Check it', 'Give me time', and 'I have paid it'. These are the responses that, according to the Hanafi school, imply confession.
2
Understand the legal principle
The common element is that none of these responses deny the claim. Instead, they address a secondary matter (verification, time, discharge), thereby affirming the existence of the debt.
If the debtor had said 'I deny it', that would not be a confession.
3
Apply the principle to each response
For each response, articulate how it acknowledges the debt: 'Weigh it' implies the money exists; 'Check it' implies the debt is acknowledged but quality needs examination; 'Give me time' implies the debt is due; 'I have paid it' implies the debt existed but was discharged.
The last response is often misunderstood as a denial; Hanafis treat it as a confession of the original debt.
4
Contrast with a clear denial
A plain denial like 'I owe you nothing' would not be a confession. The distinguishing factor is whether the statement, on its face, admits the claim's basis.
This ruling applies only when the debtor's words are not an unambiguous rejection.

Commentary
This ruling deals with responses that implicitly acknowledge a debt. Confession of Deferred Debt with Denial of Deferral
Ruling when the confessor claims deferral and claimant denies it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a confessor claims a debt is deferred but the claimant denies deferral, and identify the oath requirement.

Core Idea
If the claimant confirms the debt but denies deferral, the debt is due immediately and the claimant takes the oath.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in debt confession: the confessor admits owing a sum but claims it is deferred, while the claimant agrees the debt exists but insists it is payable immediately. The Hanafi ruling balances admitting the debt (which is established) and disputing the deferral (which must be proven by the one denying it).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two elements of the confession
The confessor makes two statements: acknowledgment of a debt and a claim of deferral (ta’jīl). Recognize these as separate legal components.
The debt itself is admitted; the deferral is an additional condition.
2
Determine what is confirmed and what is denied
In this scenario, the claimant confirms the debt but denies the deferral. This means the debt is undisputed, but the deferral is contested.
If the claimant also denies the debt, a different rule applies.
3
Apply the rule: debt becomes immediately due
Because the deferral is not admitted, the default rule (immediate payment) applies. The debt must be paid at once.
The default in debts is immediate payment unless deferral is proven.
4
Understand the oath requirement
The claimant is required to swear an oath that the debt was not deferred. This is because the claimant is the one denying an additional claim (the deferral).
The burden of proof for the deferral lies with the confessor, but the claimant denies it and thus must take an oath.

Confession with Immediate Exception (Istithna) in Hanafi Law
Ruling on immediate exception in confession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on immediate exception (istithna) in a confession and explain the condition of being immediately connected (muttasil).

Core Idea
An immediate exception in a confession is valid; the remaining amount is binding.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in Islamic law of confession (iqrar): when a person confesses a debt but immediately excludes a portion. The Hanafi school validates such an exception provided it is made in the same breath. We will examine the ruling, its evidentiary basis from the hadith of the 99 names, and the key condition of immediacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the exception
The act is a confession of a debt (iqrar bi-dayn). The confessor excludes a specific amount or item immediately after the confession.
The exception must be part of the same utterance.
2
State the ruling
The exception is valid (sahih), and only the remaining amount is binding upon the confessor.
Both little and large exceptions are treated equally.
3
Identify the condition (shart)
The exception must be muttasil (immediately connected) to the confession, without any break in speech or intervening action.
If separated, it is considered a retraction, not an exception.
4
Locate the dalil
The hadith of Abu Hurairah: 'Allah has ninety-nine names, one hundred minus one.' The Prophet used an immediate exception ('illa waahidan') after saying 'one hundred'.
This demonstrates the validity of immediate exception in speech.
5
Explain the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's usage shows that an exception immediately following a general statement is recognized and the intended meaning is the remainder.
The hadith is ahad but accepted as evidence in this context.

This ruling deals with the validity of making an exception (istithna) immediately within a confession. The Arabic states that if a person confesses a debt but immediately excludes a portion, the exception is valid and only the remaining amount is binding. Defining the Khuntha Mushkil: The Sign of Urination
Defining the ambiguous hermaphrodite based on urination sign

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sign that identifies a khuntha as female and define the khuntha mushkil when no sign appears.

Core Idea
If urine comes from the female passage, she is a woman; if no sign, she is a khuntha mushkil.

In Hanafi fiqh, the khuntha (hermaphrodite) is identified by physical signs. The most primary sign is the place of urination. This lesson explains that rule and defines the khuntha mushkil as one for whom no such sign appears, leaving the person in an intermediate legal status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is determining the gender of a khuntha. The ruling: if urine exits from the female passage, the person is ruled as female.
2
Locate the sign and its implication
The sign is the place of urination. It is decisive. If it comes from the male organ, then male; if from the female passage, then female.
3
Define the khuntha mushkil
When no such sign appears, the person is khuntha mushkil, meaning ambiguous with no preference given to either gender.
The Hanafi school gives the khuntha mushkil specific rulings, e.g., in prayer, inheritance, treating them as a distinct legal category.
