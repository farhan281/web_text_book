Formation and Completion of Rahn (Pledge)
States the ruling and conditions for valid rahn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the conclusion and completion of a rahn contract and identify the proofs from Quran, Sunnah, and the practice of the Companions.

Core Idea
Rahn is concluded by offer and acceptance but completed only by possession with specific conditions.

In the Hanafi school, a pledge (rahn) is not a binding contract upon mere offer and acceptance. It requires actual possession (qabd) by the pledgee to become complete. This lesson examines the ruling, the evidence from the Quran, the example of the Prophet ﷺ, and the practice of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, along with the three conditions for valid possession: isolation, emptiness, and distinguishability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is rahn (pledge). The passage states that rahn is concluded (yan'aqid) by offer and acceptance, and completed (yatimmu) by possession. Until possession, the contract is valid but non-binding; the rahin can revoke.
The ruling level is sahih (valid) but not binding (lazim) until qabd.
2
Locate the primary dalil from the Quran
The Quranic verse (Al-Baqarah 2:283) is cited: 'farihanun maqbudah' (pledges taken in hand). The adjective 'maqbudah' indicates that possession is integral to the pledge's validity.
The dalil is qat'i in thubut and dalalah according to Hanafi usul.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The word 'maqbudah' is a passive participle meaning 'taken in hand'. It implies that a pledge not in possession is not fully a pledge. The Hanafi school uses this to require actual physical possession for the contract to be complete.
This reasoning is unique to Hanafi; other schools may interpret differently.
4
Identify additional proofs from Sunnah and Athar
The Hadith of Aisha shows the Prophet ﷺ bought food from a Jew on credit and pledged his armor, confirming the permissibility of rahn. The letter of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, while about gifts, supports the principle that property must be isolated (muhraz) and set apart for effective possession.
These strengthen the Hanafi stance but are not the primary legal basis.
5
List the conditions for valid possession
Possession is valid only if the pledged item is: (1) muhraz (isolated from shared ownership), (2) mufarrah (free from the rahin's other belongings), and (3) mumayyaz (physically distinguishable from the rahin's property). The passage draws these from the practice of Umar.
If these conditions are not met, possession is not effective, and the rahn remains incomplete.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof. It states the basic law of rahn (pledge) in the Hanafi school: rahn is concluded by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul) but is only completed (yatimmu) by possession (qabd). The condition of possession is emphasized by the Quranic verse 'farihanun maqbudah' (pledges taken in hand). The Hadith of Aisha confirms that the Prophet himself engaged in a rahn transaction, making it legitimate. The Hanafi school further stipulates three conditions for valid possession: (1) muhraz – the item must be isolated from shared ownership, e.g., after partition; (2) mufarrah – the item must be free from the rahin's belongings, e.g., a house empty of his goods; (3) mumayyaz – the item must be physically distinguishable from the rahin's property, e.g., fruit must be separated from the tree. This is supported by the practice of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz on gifts, showing that isolation is required for effective possession. Until these conditions are met, the rahn is not considered completed and the rahin retains the right to revoke.

رهن (rahn): A contract where property is given as security for a debt.
قبض (qabd): Possession that must be muhraz, mufarrah, and mumayyaz.

Rahin's Option to Withdraw Before Possession
Ruling statement on khiyar prior to qabd in rahn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a rahn (pledge) contract is not binding until possession is taken, and explain the rahin's right to withdraw before delivery.

Core Idea
Pledge is not binding until possession; the pledgor may cancel anytime before delivery.

This lesson clarifies a key concept in Hanafi muamalat: the non-binding nature of a pledge (rahn) before the pledgee (murtahin) takes possession. Unlike compensatory contracts, rahn is a gratuitous contract (tabarru') that requires full execution to be binding. Therefore, the pledgor (rahin) retains the option to withdraw until the property is physically handed over.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its stage
The act is a rahn (pledge) contract. The specific stage considered here is before the murtahin takes possession (qabd) of the pledged item.
This is a pre-execution phase.
2
State the ruling
The rahin has the option (khiyar) to either deliver the item and complete the contract, or revoke the pledge entirely.
The contract is not binding (lazim) at this stage.
3
Understand the legal basis
Rahn is a tabarru' (gratuitous) contract, and in Hanafi fiqh, such contracts are not binding until fully executed by delivery. The ownership of the pledged item remains with the rahin until possession transfers.
This differentiates rahn from sales (mu'awadah) which are binding upon offer and acceptance.
4
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Unlike the Hanafi school, some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) consider rahn binding immediately upon offer and acceptance, regardless of possession.
This affects the practical outcome: in Hanafi, the rahin can cancel before delivery; in Shafi'i, he cannot.

This is a distinctive Hanafi position arising from the non-binding nature of gratuitous contracts before delivery.

Liability of the Pledged Property After Possession
Ruling with proof and beginning of dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the liability of a pledged item after possession and explain the two hadith proofs that establish daman.

Core Idea
After possession, the pledged item is in the murtahin's daman; its loss reduces the debt.

In Hanafi fiqh, once the creditor (murtahin) takes possession of the pledged property (marhun), the property enters his guarantee (daman). This means if the item is destroyed, even without negligence, the debt is reduced by its value. The school supports this with two hadiths: one through 'Ata' and one through Anas. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proofs, and notes the Shafi'i disagreement where the property is considered a trust.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the murtahin taking possession of the marhun. The ruling: the marhun enters the murtahin's daman (liability).
This is not about the act of pledging, but about the legal consequence after possession.
2
Locate the first dalil
The text cites a hadith from 'Ata' (mursal) where the Prophet said 'dhahaba haqq al-murtahin' (the right of the murtahin is lost) when a pledged horse died.
Mursal hadith is accepted in Hanafi usul as binding.
3
Locate the second dalil
The hadith of Anas: 'ar-rahnu bima fihi' (the pledge is for what it secures). This indicates the pledge covers the debt, implying the murtahin bears risk.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
In the first hadith, 'loss of right' means the murtahin cannot claim the debt if the pledge perishes, so he bears the loss. The second hadith directly states the pledge guarantees the debt, so its destruction reduces the debt.
Both proofs are zanni al-thubut and zanni al-dalalah.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Imam al-Shafi'i holds the marhun is an amanah (trust), citing 'la yaghluq ar-rahn'. The practical difference: if destroyed without negligence, Hanafi reduces debt; Shafi'i keeps debt intact.
The full Hanafi response is not present in this passage.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with proof and the beginning of an inter-madhhab dispute. The Hanafi position: once the murtahin takes possession of the marhun, it enters his daman (liability). The text provides two proofs: first, the mursal hadith of 'Ata' where the Prophet said 'the right of the murtahin is lost' meaning he does not get the debt back if the pledged horse dies; second, the hadith 'ar-rahnu bima fihi' meaning the pledge covers what it secures. His proof is the hadith 'la yaghluq ar-rahn' – the pledge does not become permanently closed (i.e., the murtahin cannot keep it upon default). The Hanafi school argues that the Prophet's statement 'dhahaba haqq al-murtahin' indicates that the loss of the pledged item causes loss of the right to the debt, implying liability. Single Pledge for Multiple Debts: No Partial Release
Ruling and commentary on a single item securing multiple debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a single pledge securing multiple debts and apply the rule that partial payment does not release any portion of the collateral.

Core Idea
A single pledge secures all debts; paying one does not free the collateral.

When one item is pledged for multiple debts, a common question is whether paying off one debt releases part of the item. This passage from a Hanafi fiqh text clarifies that the entire collateral remains pledged until all debts are fully paid. Understanding this principle is vital for transactions involving shared collateral.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction
What is the case described? A single item (e.g., a goat) is given as collateral for two separate debts from two creditors.
The item is indivisible for the purpose of the pledge.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The contract is valid (sahih). One pledged item can secure multiple debts simultaneously.
This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
3
Analyze the effect of partial payment
When one debt is paid in full, the entire item remains pledged for the other debt until it is also fully paid.
Partial payment does not release any portion of the collateral.
4
Understand the underlying principle
The pledge is indivisible; it secures the totality of debts until complete satisfaction. The item remains tied to the debt until every penny is paid.
Based on the principle that a pledged item is not released piecemeal.
5
Note practical implications
If the pledged item is destroyed, each creditor's share of the value is deducted proportionally from the debt. The debtor remains liable for the balance.
See the example in the commentary: goat worth 8, debts of 5 each, loss split equally.

Who May Safeguard the Pledged Item
Ruling on permissible custodians for a pledged item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state who may safeguard a pledged item per the Hanafi school and identify the condition that distinguishes a permissible custodian from an impermissible one.

Core Idea
A creditor may safeguard a pledge through his household and dependents; hiring a non-dependent worker is not allowed without liability.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on delegation of custody for a pledged item (rahn). The creditor (murtahin) is not required to personally guard the pledge at all times; he may entrust it to specific individuals. The source clarifies who qualifies and why, drawing on customary practice to avoid hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling
The creditor may safeguard the pledge himself or through his wife, children, and a dependent servant. This is the core permissibility.
2
Examine the rationale
The commentary explains the custom: a person leaves home for various needs, so delegation is necessary and does not constitute transgression (ta'di).
3
Distinguish between servant types
A 'special servant' is one dependent on the creditor for sustenance (fi 'iyalihi); a hired worker who leaves after work is not included. Only the former qualifies for delegation without liability.
The Urdu text explicitly differentiates 'khādim khāṣṣ' from a hired worker.
4
Derive the condition for non-liability
Delegation to these individuals does not constitute ta'di (misuse). If the item is lost or destroyed while in their custody, the creditor is not held negligent unless he was otherwise at fault.

The Three Causes of Legal Interdiction (Hajr)
States three causes for hajr with textual proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three causes of hajr and explain the Hanafi proofs from Quran and Hadith.

Core Idea
Hajr protects property: minority, slavery, and insanity strip legal capacity.

Legal interdiction (hajr) is a protective measure in Islamic law that restricts a person's ability to engage in financial transactions. This lesson introduces the three universally accepted causes that necessitate hajr in the Hanafi school: minority (sighar), slavery (riqq), and insanity (junun). We will examine the textual proofs from the Quran and Sunnah that establish these grounds, and understand the underlying rationale of protecting property from mismanagement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three causes
Read the opening statement: 'The causes that necessitate hajr are three: minority, slavery, and insanity.' Recognize that these are the only causes in the Hanafi school for absolute interdiction.
Other schools may include safah (foolishness) as a separate cause, but Hanafis subsume it under lack of intellect.
2
Analyze the Quranic proof (al-Nisa 4:6)
Examine the verse: 'And test the orphans until they reach the age of marriage; then if you perceive in them sound judgment, hand over to them their property.' The imperative to test and the condition of sound judgment imply that before that, property is withheld – i.e., hajr.
The term 'rushd' (sound judgment) is key; its absence justifies interdiction.
3
Analyze the Hadith proof (raf' al-qalam)
The hadith 'The pen is lifted from three: from the insane until he recovers, from the sleeper until he awakes, and from the child until he reaches puberty' shows that the actions of these individuals are not legally binding, which includes financial transactions.
This hadith directly supports hajr for the minor and the insane; slavery is covered by specific rulings.
4
Understand the rationale for each cause
For minority: lack of experience and intellect, but temporary; guardian permission can validate transactions. For slavery: risk of harm to the master, so interdiction protects the master's interests. For insanity: complete lack of intellect, leading to absolute interdiction.
The degree of interdiction varies: absolute for insanity, conditional for minority and slavery.

Core Idea
Imprisonment of an insolvent debtor is only for investigation, not punishment; once insolvency is proven, release is wajib.

This lesson presents the Hanafi ruling on how a ruler (hakim) should treat a person who claims insolvency (muflis). The school distinguishes between investigative detention and punitive imprisonment. The source passage states the procedure: the ruler holds the muflis for two to three months to investigate his financial state, and if no property is found or if the debtor provides evidence of insolvency, he must be released immediately. The ruling is supported by a hadith from Abu Sa'id al-Khudri that indicates the debtor's freedom once his assets are exhausted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and its Ruling
The act is the imprisonment of a muflis by the ruler for investigation. According to the Hanafi school, this imprisonment is wajib (obligatory) on the ruler as a means to protect creditors' rights and verify the debtor's claim.
The imprisonment is not a penalty for debt; it is solely investigative.
2
Understand the Conditions and Duration
The imprisonment lasts two to three months, during which the ruler investigates the debtor's condition. If no hidden property is discovered, the ruler must release him. Additionally, if the debtor provides evidence (bayyinah) that he has no property, he is released even before the period ends.
The two-to-three month limit is a guideline; actual duration may vary but must be limited to investigation.
3
Locate the Dalil
The author cites the hadith of the man who suffered a blight on his fruits. The Prophet ﷺ told the creditors to take what they found and then have no further claim. This implies that after the debtor's assets are exhausted, he is free from detention and further pursuit.
The hadith is from Sahih Muslim; its transmission is zanni, but its meaning is clear.
4
Analyse the Wajh al-Dalalah
The Prophet's instruction 'خذوا ما وجدتم وليس لكم إلا ذلك' (Take what you find, and you have nothing else) indicates that once the debtor's assets are given to creditors, the debtor has no remaining obligation. This directly supports the rule that a muflis cannot be held beyond the exhaustion of his assets.
The ruling is derived from the implied conclusion, not an explicit command.
5

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an ambiguous confession and apply the principle that the judge can compel clarification, citing the hadith of Ma'iz and Ghamidiyya.

Core Idea
An ambiguous confession is valid but requires clarification; the judge compels specification before acting.

This passage from the Book of Confession addresses a practical situation: someone confesses vaguely, saying 'purify me' without specifying the sin. The Hanafi ruling is that the judge must ask for clarification and can compel it if the confessor refuses. This protects the rights of both the claimant and the accused while fulfilling the legal process.

State the core rule
Read the first part of the passage: 'And it is said to him: clarify the unknown. If he does not clarify, the judge compels him to clarify.' Identify that the confessor is commanded to specify the ambiguous element, and the judge has authority to enforce this.
The ruling applies to any ambiguous confession in legal matters.
2
Identify the judge's authority
The passage explicitly states that if the confessor refuses, the judge compels him. This is a judicial power to ensure that the confession is meaningful before any legal consequence is applied.
The compulsion may include imprisonment or other coercive measures, as understood from Hanafi jurisprudence.
3
Locate the prophetic precedent
The author cites the hadith of Ma'iz and Ghamidiyya. Both said 'purify me' without specifying the sin. The Prophet asked 'From what should I purify you?' until they clarified it was zina.
This hadith is found in Sahih Muslim (1995).
4
Analyze the legal implication
The Prophet's question establishes that ambiguity in a confession requires clarification. The confession is not rejected outright; rather, it is treated as valid but needing specification. This is the basis for the judge's duty to seek clarification.
The hadith uses an ambiguous request (tahhirni) to demonstrate the need for specification.
5
Consider the epistemological weight
This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah (the authoritative transmission) and is agreed upon across schools (muttafaq alayh). The proof is a hadith ahad with qati thubut but zanni dalala, yet it is sufficient for establishing a rule.
The ruling is considered wajib for the confessor to clarify, and the judge's compulsion is also wajib.

Rulings on Ambiguous Acknowledgment (al-Iqrār al-Mujmal)
Explains rulings when acknowledgment is ambiguous and judge's role

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rules for ambiguous acknowledgment, including the judge's duty to compel clarification, and apply the minimum thresholds for the terms 'māl', 'māl ʿaẓīm', and 'darāhim kathīra'.

Core Idea
An ambiguous acknowledgment is valid but the judge must compel clarification; the acknowledger's word with oath determines the amount.

This lesson explains the Hanafi treatment of ambiguous acknowledgments (al-iqrār al-mujmal) in property and debt cases. When a person says 'I owe so-and-so something' without specifying, the acknowledgment is binding but the judge must demand clarification. If the person refuses, the judge compels him. The Prophet's handling of Mā'iz and Ghāmidiyya provides the proof for this procedure. Additionally, the Hanafi school sets fixed minimum amounts based on Shariah definitions and Arabic linguistic norms for terms like 'property', 'great property', and 'many dirhams'.

Recognize the ambiguity
Identify that the acknowledgment is mujmal (ambiguous) because it does not specify the amount or nature of the thing owed. This triggers the judge's role.
2
Understand the judge's duty
The judge must ask the acknowledger (muqirr) to clarify. If he refuses, the judge compels him, based on the hadith of Mā'iz where the Prophet asked 'From what should I purify you?'
3
Apply the linguistic thresholds
Depending on the term used (māl, māl ʿaẓīm, darāhim kathīra), different minimum amounts apply: 1 dirham for māl, 200 dirhams for māl ʿaẓīm, 10 dirhams for darāhim kathīra. These are fixed by Shariah and Arabic usage.
4
Accept the acknowledger's statement with oath
If the claimant disputes the amount, the acknowledger's statement is accepted with his oath, based on the principle that evidence is on the plaintiff and oath on the denier (hadith: 'The oath is on the defendant').

If he said: 'He has property on me', reference in its explanation is to him, and his statement is accepted in little and much. The acknowledger says: 'So-and-so has property on me' — regarding how much property, he will be asked, and whatever he says, little or much, his statement will be accepted. However, his statement will not be accepted for less than one dirham, because less than one dirham is not called property. Since the acknowledged (the one for whom acknowledgment is made) has no evidence against it, the acknowledger's statement with oath must be accepted as he says. So if he said: 'He has great property on me', he is not believed for less than two hundred dirhams. In Shariah, two hundred dirhams or twenty dinars are called 'great property'. Therefore, zakat is obligatory on two hundred dirhams or twenty dinars. Hence, when the attribute 'great' is added to property, acknowledgment for less than two hundred dirhams is not accepted; he must acknowledge that amount or more. Commentary
This passage from Al-Sharh al-Samarqandi (or similar) explains the rules for ambiguous acknowledgment (al-iqrār al-mujmal). The author begins by stating that if someone acknowledges a loss or debt without specifying the amount, the judge must demand clarification. If the person refuses, the judge compels him to clarify, because the acknowledgment creates a right for another. The proof is the Prophet's handling of Mā'iz and the Ghāmidiyya woman; they asked for purification without specifying from what, so the Prophet asked for clarification. This establishes that ambiguity in acknowledgment triggers a duty of clarification. The next point: if someone says 'something is owed to so-and-so on me', he must specify something of value. If he specifies something valueless, that is considered a retraction, which is not allowed after acknowledgment. The proof is the athar of Shurayh, who prevented a man from denying his acknowledgment. Then the rule that the acknowledger's statement is accepted with oath if the claimant disputes the amount. This is based on the principle that the burden of proof is on the plaintiff and the oath on the denier, supported by hadiths from Ibn 'Abbās and Abu Hurayra. Then specific cases: if the acknowledgment uses the word 'property' (māl), the acknowledger is believed for any amount down to one dirham (because less is not considered property). If he says 'great property' (māl ʿaẓīm), he is not believed for less than 200 dirhams, because the Shariah defines wealth subject to zakat at 200 dirhams as 'great'. If he says 'many dirhams' (darāhim kathīra), he is not believed for less than ten dirhams, due to Arabic usage: the plural 'darāhim' is used up to ten. The passage concludes that subsequent issues are derived from linguistic conventions and do not require independent hadith proofs.

Key Terms
إقرار (iqrār): Acknowledgment of a right or liability owed to another; a unilateral declaration that creates a legal obligation.
مقر (muqirr): The person who makes the acknowledgment (the declarant).
مقر له (muqarr lahu): The person in whose favor the acknowledgment is made (the claimant).
مجمل (mujmal): Ambiguous, not specifying the amount or nature of the thing acknowledged.
مال عظيم (māl ʿaẓīm): Great property; defined in Shariah as 200 dirhams or 20 dinars, the minimum for zakat.

Minimum for Confession of 'Many Dirhams': Ten Dirhams
Intra-Hanafi dispute on defining 'darahim kathirah'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum amount for a confession of 'darahim kathirah' according to Abu Hanifa and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute with Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A confession of 'many dirhams' requires at least ten dirhams due to Arabic plural usage, not the zakat nisab.

When someone confesses owing 'many dirhams', how many dirhams must they specify for the confession to be valid? This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute where Abu Hanifa sets the minimum at ten dirhams based on linguistic convention, while Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue for two hundred dirhams by analogy to the nisab of wealth. Understanding this dispute sharpens your appreciation of how language and shari'ah standards interact in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify Abu Hanifa's ruling: a confession of 'darahim kathirah' is not accepted for less than ten dirhams.
This is the mufta bihi position in the school.
2
Identify the opponent's view
Recognize that Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ: they require two hundred dirhams, equating 'kathirah' with the nisab of zakat.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute, not between madhhabs.
3
Follow Abu Hanifa's proof
Examine the linguistic reasoning: the plural 'darahim' in Arabic is used only up to ten; after eleven, the singular is used. Adding 'kathirah' does not override this convention.
The key example: 'ashara darahim' (ten dirhams) vs 'ahada ashara dirhaman' (eleven dirhams).
4
Analyze Sahibayn's proof
Understand their argument: 'kathirah' means abundance, and the shari'ah standard for abundance is the nisab (200 dirhams), so the confession must be at least that amount.
They prioritize the established shari'ah meaning over the linguistic norm.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Note the practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, a confession of 10 dirhams is valid; under Sahibayn, it would be rejected until it reaches 200 dirhams.
The mufta bihi follows Abu Hanifa, so the minimum is ten dirhams.

Exception of a Different Genus: Validity Based on Closeness
States Hanafi ruling on exception from a different genus

Core Idea
Exception from a debt is valid only if the excepted item is of a closely related genus, such as both being thaman.

When someone acknowledges a debt in dirhams but excepts an item of a different genus, the validity of the exception depends on whether the two items are closely related. The Hanafi school defines closeness by the capacity of both items to serve as thaman (price/currency). Core Idea
When someone says 'a hundred and dirham,' it means one hundred dirhams, not a hundred plus an extra dirham.

In Islamic legal practice, acknowledgments (iqrār) often involve numbers and units. The phrase 'a hundred and dirham' might seem ambiguous: does it mean a hundred of something plus a dirham, or does 'dirham' explain what the hundred refers to? The Hanafi school, relying on customary usage, interprets the word 'dirham' as an explanation (tafsīr) of the hundred, so the obligation is one hundred dirhams. Based on the above, the acknowledgment is for one hundred dirhams only. The word 'dirham' does not add an extra dirham; it explains the unit of the hundred.

Ruling on Confession with 'In Sha Allah'
Connecting a confession to God's will makes it non-binding.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a confession immediately followed by 'in sha Allah' and apply the condition that distinguishes binding from non-binding.

Core Idea
A confession immediately connected to 'in sha Allah' is invalid and non-binding in Hanafi fiqh.

This lesson addresses a unique nuance in Hanafi fiqh: when someone confesses a right but adds 'in sha Allah' immediately, the confession loses its binding effect. The reasoning is based on uncertainty (ta'liq) and analogy to oaths. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying confessions in legal practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is a confession (iqrar) of a right. The ruling is that if the confessor immediately says 'in sha Allah' (connected), the confession is invalid and not binding.
The confession becomes batil (void).
2
Understand the condition for invalidity
The condition is that the phrase 'in sha Allah' must be uttered immediately after the confession (muttasilan). If it is disconnected (munfasilan), it is treated as a revocation and the confession becomes binding.
Connectivity is key.
3
Examine the dalil (proof)
The ruling is derived by analogy from a hadith about oaths: 'Whoever takes an oath and says in sha Allah, there is no breaking of the oath.' Just as 'in sha Allah' prevents an oath from taking effect, it also prevents a confession from being binding.
Hadith from Tirmidhi, no. 153.
4
Apply the exception
If 'in sha Allah' is said separately (e.g., after a pause), it is considered a retraction, and the original confession stands binding.
Disconnected statement = revocation.

Confession of Building and Courtyard: Non-Integral Parts
Ruling on separate confession of non-integral parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that excepting a non-integral part in a confession is valid, and distinguish integral from non-integral parts.

Core Idea
A confession separating building and courtyard is valid because the courtyard is not integral to the building.

In Hanafi law, when someone confesses ownership of a property but excepts a part for another, the validity depends on whether the excepted part is integral to the whole. This lesson explores a specific case: a man states the building is his and the courtyard is for someone else. The ruling turns on the courtyard's non-integral nature, which allows the exception to stand.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
The act is a confession (iqrar) of ownership. The confessor asserts that the building belongs to him and the courtyard belongs to another.
Confession is a form of evidence; it binds the confessor.
2
Determine Integrality of the Excepted Part
Examine whether the courtyard (arsa) is an integral part of the building. The source states it is not foundational; it is for additional benefit.
Contrast with a previous case (likely) where an integral part like a wall inside a house cannot be excepted.
3
Apply the General Principle
The principle: 'If he excepts something other than a foundational thing, the exception is valid.' Since the courtyard is non-integral, the exception is valid.
This principle is derived from the reasoning (talil) that non-integral parts can be separated in confessions.
4
State the Ruling
Thus, the building is for the confessor and the courtyard for the named person (confessee). The confession is sahih (valid) as stated.
No retraction is implied; the confessor has not withdrawn the courtyard confession by keeping the building.
5
Identify the Method (Talil)
The argument uses talil (causal reasoning): the cause of validity is the non-integral nature of the courtyard. This is a form of qiyas where the illa is identified as 'non-integrality'.
This shows how Hanafi fiqh uses rational analysis to extend rulings.

Confession to Movable Container and Contents
Ruling on confession of movable container and contents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on confession of movable contents and container, and apply the principle of inseparability to similar cases.

Core Idea
Confession to contents in a movable container includes the container.

When someone confesses to taking an item inside a container, does the confession cover the container as well? This lesson examines a clear Hanafi ruling based on the principle that if the container and its contents are together and movable, both are included. The key is the movability and physical inseparability at the time of taking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the confession scenario
Consider the example: a person confesses, 'I took dates in a basket.' The confession mentions the contents (dates) and implicitly the container (basket) because the dates were inside it at the time.
The container is not explicitly confessed but is implied by the context.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
According to the Hanafi school, the person must return both the dates and the basket. The confession is binding on both items.
This is the mufta bihi (operative) position.
3
Identify the underlying principle
The principle is that if the container (zarf) and the contents (mazruf) are together at the time of taking and are movable (can be transferred as a unit), then taking the contents necessarily involves taking the container.
Immovable containers (e.g., a fixed cupboard) are excluded.
4
Apply the principle to other movable containers
The same rule applies to any movable container such as a bag, purse, box, or envelope. If someone confesses to taking money in a purse, both must be returned.
Custom also plays a role: if the container is customarily considered separate, the ruling might differ.
5
Note the exception for immovable containers
If the container is immovable (e.g., a fixed safe), the confession to its contents does not include the container itself, because the container was not taken together.
This exception highlights the condition of movability.
