No One May Take Foundling from Finder
Ruling on the finder's right of possession over the foundling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a finder's possession of a foundling is protected and explain the proof from Umar's statement.

Core Idea
Once a foundling is taken by a finder, no other person may take him without the finder's consent.

This passage establishes a clear rule in Hanafi fiqh: the first person to pick up an abandoned child (foundling) gains an exclusive right to keep him. No one else may remove the child from the finder's possession. The ruling is supported by a statement of Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), which links the child's patronage (wala') to the finder, implying exclusive authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
Read the matn and commentary: the finder's possession is protected; another person cannot take the foundling.
2
Locate the Proof
The commentary cites Umar's statement: 'his patronage is for you' (wala'uhu laka). This is an athar (Companion's statement) used as evidence.
The proof is from Sunnah ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Analyze the Wajh al-Dalalah
The phrase 'laka' (for you) indicates exclusivity. Just as wala' is exclusive to the finder, so is the right of possession.
This is a dalili istidlal (textual implication).
4
Apply the Ruling
In practice, if a finder picks up a foundling, others (including relatives) cannot take the child without the finder's permission or a valid legal reason.
The ruling ensures stability and protects the finder's efforts.

Claim of Paternity for a Foundling: The Ruling and Its Basis
Ruling on accepting a paternity claim for a foundling with an oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on accepting a paternity claim for a foundling and explain why the claim is accepted with an oath despite overriding the finder's rights.

Core Idea
A paternity claim for a foundling is accepted with oath because the child's benefit outweighs the finder's right.

This passage addresses a unique scenario in the fiqh of foundlings (luqatah): what happens when someone steps forward to claim a foundling as his own child? The Hanafi school prioritizes the child's welfare and lineage, even if it means overriding the finder's established rights. But since no physical signs or witnesses are available to verify the claim, the claimant's word alone is insufficient—an oath is required to establish lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Recognize that this ruling applies when a person claims that a foundling (a child found abandoned) is his biological son. The finder has already established a right to care for the child, but the claimant asserts paternity.
The foundling is assumed to be free and Muslim unless signs indicate otherwise.
2
Extract the ruling
The text rules: 'فالقول قوله مع يمينه' — his statement is accepted with his oath. The claimant does not need witnesses or physical proof; an oath suffices to establish lineage (nasab).
This is a deviation from the normal requirement of two witnesses for paternity claims, due to the difficulty of proof and the child's interest.
3
Understand the underlying rationale
The commentary explains that although accepting the claim may override the finder's right (the finder loses custody or any claim for compensation), the benefit to the child in having a known father and lineage takes precedence. Child welfare (maslahah) is the primary consideration.
The Hanafi school often employs istihsan (juristic preference) to prioritize public interest over strict analogy.
4
Note the procedural requirement
Because there are no physical signs (e.g., resemblance) or witnesses to verify the claim, the claimant must take an oath. The oath serves as a substitute for external evidence.
If the claimant refuses to swear, his claim is rejected.
5
Consider the cross-madhhab perspective
Some other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) may require two male witnesses or one man and two women to establish paternity. The Hanafi position relies on the principle that lineage can be established by claim with oath when no other evidence exists, due to the child's need.
This difference stems from varying interpretations of the hadith 'Al-walad li al-firash' (the child belongs to the bed) and its application to foundlings.

Priority Based on Describing a Mark on the Foundling
Ruling on priority based on mark description

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that describing a bodily mark gives priority in paternity claims for a foundling and explain the qiyas (analogy) from the hadith on lost property.

Core Idea
Describing a mark is stronger evidence than a bare claim.

In a dispute over the paternity of a foundling, one claimant describes a distinctive mark on the child's body. The Hanafi school prioritizes this claimant because the mark provides concrete evidence. This ruling is derived by analogy from a hadith where describing a lost item's features entitles the finder to return it. Let's examine the legal basis and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the matn: if two claim a foundling and one describes a bodily mark, that claimant is more entitled. This priority establishes lineage (nasab).
The ruling is not about who speaks first, but who provides stronger evidence.
2
Locate the proof
The commentator cites the hadith of lost property from Bukhari. Recognize that this hadith is ahad, but accepted in Hanafi usul for such rulings.
The operative phrase is 'fain jaa ahad yukhbiruka biha' – if someone comes describing it, give it to him.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith shows that describing marks of a lost item makes one entitled to it. By qiyas, describing a mark on the foundling's body makes one entitled to paternity. The 'illa (effective cause) is the stronger evidence provided by the description.
Hanafi usul: qiyas must have a clear 'illa; here the 'illa is the ability to identify by marks.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
This ruling differs from schools that may require witnesses or qiyafa (physiognomy). The practical result is that a single description, if accurate, suffices for the Hanafi judge.
The mukhtalaf fih nature of this ruling – other madhahib have different evidentiary standards.
5
Consider the modern application
In contemporary cases, DNA testing could be considered an extension of 'describing marks' – in Hanafi law, scientific evidence can be analogized if it provides stronger proof.
The principle: evidence that uniquely identifies the child prevails.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A dhimmi's claim establishes a foundling's lineage, but the child remains Muslim due to the presumption of Islam in dar al-Islam.

This lesson examines a specific case in Hanafi fiqh: when a foundling is discovered in an Islamic city and a non-Muslim subject (dhimmi) claims the child as his son. The ruling balances two interests: establishing lineage to prevent illegitimacy and protecting the child by presuming Islam in a Muslim land. Understanding this ruling clarifies how the Hanafi school prioritises the child's welfare alongside legal lineage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Note the location (Muslim city/village) and the claimant (dhimmi). The child is a foundling of unknown parentage.
The location is critical — it triggers the presumption of Islam.
2
State the ruling on lineage
The dhimmi's claim establishes the child's lineage (nasab) to him. This prevents the child from being considered illegitimate (harami).
Lineage is established even though the claimant is a non-Muslim.
3
State the ruling on religion
Despite the lineage, the child is ruled to be Muslim. The default in dar al-Islam is Islam, and this is in the child's best interest.
The child's status as Muslim is not affected by the father's religion.
4
Identify the underlying reasoning
Two reasons: (1) necessity to establish lineage for the child's welfare; (2) predominant presumption (ghalabat al-zann) that the child is Muslim in a Muslim environment.
This reasoning is based on maslaha (public interest) and default rules.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
In other schools, the child might follow the father's religion. The Hanafi school prioritises the child's presumed Islam for his benefit.
This shows ikhtilaf driven by different prioritisation of principles.

Ruling on Announcement Period for Found Property
States ruling and proofs for announcement period based on value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ta'rif (announcement period) for luqatah based on value, identify the supporting hadiths, and explain the wajh al-dalalah indicating obligation.

Core Idea
Announcement period for found property scales with value: less than 10 dirhams → days, 10+ → one year.

Finding lost property is a common occurrence, but Islamic law imposes clear duties on the finder. Among the most critical obligations is ta'rif — announcing the find publicly to enable the owner to reclaim it. This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on how long ta'rif must last, which depends on the item's value, and the proof texts from the Sunnah that establish this graded system.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Locate the core ruling in the passage: found property (luqatah) requires ta'rif (announcement). The duration is based on value: less than 10 dirhams → a few days; 10 dirhams or more → one full year. If the owner appears, return it; otherwise, give it in charity.
The finder holds the item as a trust (amanah) until the owner appears.
2
Examine the first proof (small value)
The hadith of Ya'la ibn Murra: The Prophet said, 'Whoever picks up a small found item, a rope, a dirham, or the like, let him announce it for three days; if it is more than that, let him announce it for six days.' This establishes a shorter period for items of lesser value, though the Hanafi school interprets 'few days' as up to ten days, deriving the general principle.
The imperative 'فليعرفنه' indicates obligation (wujub) per Hanafi usul.
3
Examine the second proof (larger value)
The hadith of Zayd ibn Khalid al-Juhani: A bedouin asked about found property, and the Prophet said, 'Announce it for a year, then recognize its bag and its string.' This direct command (عرفها سنة) establishes the one-year period for valuable items (10 dirhams or more).
The Bukhari and Muslim version also states 'announce it for a year'.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
Both hadiths use imperative verbs ('فليعرفنه', 'عرفها'), which in Hanafi usul indicate obligation (fard or wajib) unless there is a contrary indication. Since ta'rif is not one of the primary acts of worship, it is classified as wajib, not fard. The duration is derived from the explicit wording.
The legal cause (illah) for the duration is the value-based likelihood of the owner searching.
5
Note the principle and applicability
The passage concludes with a guiding principle: the more valuable the property and the more likely the owner is to search, the longer the announcement period. This ta'lil (rationale) supports the ruling and can be extended to similar cases.
This principle is not a separate proof but a distillation of the Sunnah evidence.

Owner's Choice When Finder Gives Charity Before Owner Appears
States owner's option when charity was given before his arrival

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the owner's khiyar (option) when the finder has given charity and identify the dalil from the athar of Ali (ra).

Core Idea
Charity does not absolve the finder; the owner can still demand compensation.

This lesson examines a common scenario in luqatah: the finder, after announcing the lost item for a year, gives it in charity before the owner appears. The Hanafi school holds that the owner retains a choice: he may ratify the charity or claim compensation from the finder. The primary proof is the athar of Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra), which explicitly states the finder's liability if the owner is displeased.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the finder giving the found property in charity after a year of announcement. The ruling is that the owner has the option (khiyar) to either confirm the charity or demand compensation.
The charity itself is wajib after the year, but it does not bar the owner's right.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the athar of Ali (ra) in which he instructs a finder to give charity and states: 'If its owner comes and is pleased, he gets the reward; if he is not pleased, you guarantee it and you get the reward.'
This is a qawl sahabi, which is hujja in Hanafi usul when not contradicted.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit phrase 'if he is not pleased, you guarantee it' directly establishes the owner's right to demand compensation. It shows that the charity is conditional on the owner's consent.
The finder's disposal without permission creates liability.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some schools (e.g., Shafi'i) hold that charity given after the year absolves the finder completely. The Hanafi school requires the owner's consent for absolution.
The practical difference is that the owner may still seek compensation in the Hanafi view.

Owner's Option After Charity of Luqatah
Ruling and proof for finder's charity and owner's option

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the owner's options when the finder of a luqatah has already given it in charity, and explain the dalil from the actions of ʿAlī and ʿUmar.

Core Idea
The owner may confirm the charity or demand compensation; the finder is not free from liability.

This lesson addresses a key scenario in the law of luqatah: the finder has completed the required announcement period and given the lost item in charity, but then the true owner appears. What are the owner's rights? The text presents the Hanafi position with clear proofs from the conduct of the Companions, showing that charity does not extinguish the owner's claim entirely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling Scenario
Recognize that the case presupposes the finder has already given the luqatah in charity (after the required announcement). The owner now appears.
The charity is given on behalf of the owner with the intention of reward, but without explicit permission.
2
State the Owner's Options
The text says 'فهو بالخيار' (he has the choice). The owner may either confirm the charity (and the finder gets the reward) or demand compensation (ḍamān) from the finder for the value.
This is the muftā bihī position in the Hanafi school.
3

The hadith of ʿAlī with the man from Banu Ruwās: ʿAlī instructed the finder to give the purse in charity, and told the finder that if the owner is pleased, the finder gets the reward; if not, the finder must guarantee the value.
This establishes the owner's option directly.

The report from ʿUmar: after a year of announcing, the finder gives charity; when the owner comes, he is given the choice between the reward (accepting the charity) or taking his property (demanding compensation).
This reinforces the same principle.
5

Some scholars (e.g., Shāfiʿī) hold that charity after the year is obligatory and the owner has no choice. The Ḥanafī response is that the owner's consent is needed, as shown by the reports.
This is a legitimate ikhtilāf; the Ḥanafī view is supported by the explicit conditional wording in the proofs.

Permissibility of Picking Lost Camels: Hanafi vs Shafi'i/Maliki
Ruling on picking up sheep, cattle, and camels with proof and ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits picking up stray camels despite the Prophetic hadith, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i/Maliki position.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school permits picking up stray camels because the ruling changed with circumstances, based on the practice of Caliph 'Uthman.

This lesson examines a key ikhtilaf in the law of luqatah: the treatment of stray camels. While a well-known hadith appears to prohibit touching a lost camel, the Hanafi school follows the later practice of Caliph 'Uthman (رضي الله عنه) who permitted picking up, announcing, and even selling stray camels. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi jurisprudence accounts for changing social conditions through istihsan and the practice of the caliphs.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school rules that it is permissible to pick up stray camels (just like sheep and cows), and further, if the owner does not appear after announcement, the animal may be sold and the price kept for the owner.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the opponent and their proof
Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik hold that stray camels must not be touched. Their proof is the hadith of Zayd ibn Khalid where the Prophet's face changed and he rebuked the questioner, implying a clear prohibition.
The hadith explicitly mentions a camel and the Prophet's strong reaction.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that the Prophetic prohibition was applicable to a time of trust and integrity in society. When later (in the time of Caliph 'Uthman) people became less trustworthy, the ruling changed to permissibility to protect property. This is based on the practice of 'Uthman and istihsan (juristic preference).
The Hanafi school gives weight to the practice of the rightly-guided caliphs as a source of law.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is clear: Hanafis may pick up, care for, announce, and eventually sell a stray camel; Shafi'is and Malikis must leave it alone.
This affects the finder's rights and obligations.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi (operative for fatwa) because it follows the later practice of a rightly-guided caliph and addresses the reality of loss.
The tarjih authority is the text itself, which explicitly mentions the practice of 'Uthman as the basis for the fatwa.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Without judge's permission, expenses on found property are charity; with permission, they become a binding debt.

When you find lost property (luqata) and spend money on its upkeep, whether you can recover those expenses depends on one crucial factor: judicial authorization. The Hanafi school distinguishes between spending as a volunteer (mutabarra') and spending as a creditor. This lesson examines the ruling from the matn and its proof from the athar of Ibrahim and Abu Hanifa.

Volunteer means no legal claim for reimbursement.
3

The judge's general authority (wilaya 'amma) makes the expense binding on the owner. Without it, the expense remains a favor.
The commentary explicitly links wilaya 'amma to the creation of debt.

5
Apply the ruling
In practice, the finder must obtain permission from the judge or ruler before spending if he intends to claim reimbursement.
Otherwise, the expense is considered charity.

Judge's Procedure for Found Animals
Ruling on judge's procedure for found animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling for a judge when a found animal is brought to court, distinguishing between animals with and without benefit.

Core Idea
A judge must balance preservation of found property with minimal burden, either by hiring out or selling the animal.

When a found animal (luqata) is brought to court, the judge must act to preserve the owner's property while avoiding undue hardship on the finder. This lesson explains the Hanafi procedure: if the animal generates benefit, hire it out and use the earnings for upkeep; if not, sell it before costs exceed value.

Guided Path
1
Identify Presence of Benefit
Determine if the found animal provides a usable benefit (manfa'a), such as milk, wool, or labor. This is the key condition that triggers the first scenario.
Benefit must be regular and quantifiable, e.g., daily milk yield.
2
Apply First Scenario (Benefit Exists)
If benefit exists, the judge should hire out the animal and use the hire income to cover its expenses. Any surplus must be saved for the owner.
Example: A cow gives milk worth 3 dirhams daily and costs 2 dirhams for fodder. The judge arranges 2 dirhams for fodder, saving 1 dirham for the owner.
3
Apply Second Scenario (No Benefit or Cost Exceeds Value)
If the animal has no benefit or the cost of upkeep is so high that it will exceed the animal's value, the judge must order the animal to be sold. The sale proceeds are preserved for the owner.
The judge should not delay if daily expenses erode the value.
4
Recognize the Underlying Principle
Both scenarios are governed by the principle of choosing the most beneficial option ('mā kāna li'l-kulli akthara naf'an'). The judge must minimize loss and protect the owner's rights.
This is a manifestation of istiṣlāḥ (public interest) within the judge's authority.

Found Property in the Haram: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Establishes equal treatment of luqatah in Hal and Haram.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on found property in the Haram and identify the practical difference between them.

Core Idea
Found property in Mecca's Haram is treated like any other luqatah after one year of announcement, per the Hanafi school.

This lesson examines a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding found property (luqatah) inside the sacred precinct of Mecca (Haram). While both schools agree that announcement is required, they differ sharply on its duration and whether the finder may ever use the item. The practical implication affects anyone who finds a lost item in the Haram: can they benefit from it after a reasonable search, or must they wait indefinitely?

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify that the Hanafi school treats found property in the Haram the same as outside: after one year of public announcement (tashhir), the finder may use it, with liability to compensate the owner if they appear later.
This is based on the practice of Aisha (RA) and qiyas with luqatah outside Haram.
2
Identify the opposing Shafi'i view
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i holds that found property in the Haram must be announced perpetually until the owner comes; the finder may never use it.
The Shafi'i rely on the hadith 'لا يلتقط لقطتها الا معرف' and the prohibition on picking up a pilgrim's lost property.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
Analyze the athar of Aisha: she permitted using a found animal in the Haram after announcement. This shows that one year is sufficient.
The Hanafi interpret 'muarrif' as one who announces for a year, not perpetually.
4
Examine the Shafi'i proof
Consider the hadith of Ibn Abbas and the prohibition on pilgrim's lost property. The Shafi'i argue that 'muarrif' implies continuous announcement.
The Hanafi response distinguishes: the prohibition applies only to pilgrims' property, not general lost items.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response and practical difference
Understand that the Hanafi school prioritizes practical benefit for the finder while protecting the owner through compensation. The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi allows use after one year; Shafi'i forbids use indefinitely.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position, supported by Aisha's practice.

Claimant of Found Property: Witnesses or Description?
States that a claimant must bring witnesses to prove ownership of found property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school requires witnesses for a claimant of found property and apply the ruling to determine when a finder may hand over the item.

Core Idea
Description alone is not enough; the claimant must bring witnesses to prove ownership.

When someone claims a lost item, is a simple description sufficient, or must they provide proof? This lesson examines a key disagreement between the Hanafi school and the Shafi'i/Maliki schools. Understanding the Hanafi position protects the finder's trust and prevents fraudulent claims.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the core rule: a claimant must bring evidence (bainah) to prove ownership of found property; mere description is not sufficient for compulsion.
The finder holds the item as an amanah (trust).
2
Identify the opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Malik hold that accurate description alone obligates the finder to return the item.
Based on the hadith of Zayd ibn Khalid.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school cites general hadiths: 'البيّنة على من ادعى' (burden of proof on claimant) and 'لو يعطى الناس بدعواهم...' (if people were given on their claims, they would claim blood and property). These establish that claims without evidence are not accepted.
These are dalil naqli (textual proofs).
4
Analyze the opposing proof
The Shafi'i/Maliki proof is the hadith: 'اعرف وكاءها وعفاصها... فان جاء ربها فادها اليه' (recognize its string and container... if its owner comes, give it to him). This seems to imply description suffices.
The Hanafi response is that this hadith addresses the finder's voluntary action, not legal compulsion.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafi says the finder may ask for witnesses and is not compelled to give by description alone; Shafi'i says the finder must give upon accurate description.
Hanafi allows the finder discretion if satisfied.

The mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school is that the finder should ideally seek witnesses, but if he is comfortable with the description, he may hand it over, as the item is an amanah and he is not a judge.

Requiring Proof from Claimant of Found Property
Ruling that a claimant must provide evidence to receive found property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the necessity of evidence for a claimant of luqatah and explain the hadith proofs.

Core Idea
A claimant must produce evidence (bayyinah) to receive found property; mere description is insufficient.

When a person claims ownership of a found item, the finder cannot simply hand it over based on a description. Hanafi fiqh requires the claimant to provide formal evidence, typically two male witnesses, to protect the finder's liability and ensure rightful ownership. This lesson examines the ruling and its proofs from hadith.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling
Identify the precise ruling: the finder must not give the found property to a claimant until the claimant establishes bayyinah (evidence).
This is a wajib obligation on the finder.
2
Understand the underlying reasoning
The found property is an amanah (trust) with the finder. Handing it to a wrong claimant would breach that trust and make the finder liable.
The amanah nature is key to the ruling.
3
Cite the primary hadith proof
The hadith from Ibn Abbas: 'If people were given based on their claims, they would claim the blood and wealth of men, but the oath is upon the defendant.' This establishes that claims alone are insufficient.
The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear (qati' dalala).
4
Cite the secondary hadith proof
The hadith from Abu Huraira: 'Evidence is upon the claimant, and the oath is upon the denier, except in qasamah.' This directly applies to luqatah.
This general principle covers all claims, including found property.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Shafi'i and Maliki schools hold that a claimant's accurate description of the item suffices without formal evidence. Hanafi requires evidence even if the description matches.
The practical difference is the standard of proof in court.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Description of found property permits return but does not legally compel it; only evidence creates obligation.

When a person claims found property by describing its distinctive marks, a natural question arises: is the finder obliged to hand it over? The Hanafi school provides a nuanced answer. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its proof from hadith, clarifying the balance between trust and legal caution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is returning found property (luqatah) to a claimant who provides a correct description. The ruling in the Hanafi school is that it is permissible (mubah), not obligatory (wajib).
This is the core teaching of the matn.
2
Locate the dalil
The primary proof is the hadith narrated by Muslim: 'If someone comes and tells you of its number, its container, and its tie, then give it to him.' This hadith is ahad and zanni in transmission.
The hadith is quoted in the commentary.

Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'fa'atiha iyyahu' (give it to him) is interpreted as permissibility (ibahah) rather than obligation (wujub) because the description could be coincidental or guessed. Hanafi usul holds that an imperative from a zanni dalil is not binding.
This distinguishes Hanafi from Shafi'i interpretation.
4

The Shafi'i and Maliki schools view the same hadith as establishing obligation: the description alone suffices to compel return in court. The Hanafi school requires stronger evidence (bayyinah) for legal compulsion.
The disagreement stems from the usul principle of interpreting amr.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi (operative ruling) in the school. It prioritizes protecting the finder from fraudulent claims by not making return compulsory based on description alone.
This shows Hanafi caution in property matters.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A wealthy finder must give found property in charity — he may not consume it.

In the previous lesson, you learned that a needy finder may consume the found property after a year of announcement. This lesson addresses the opposite case: when the finder is wealthy. The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction based on the finder's financial status. The ruling is derived from the practice of the Companion Abdullah ibn Umar, who refused to permit a rich finder to consume the found property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition that changes the ruling
The key condition is the wealth of the finder. Wealth (ghina) is a legal condition that triggers a different ruling for luqatah. A poor finder may consume after one year; a rich finder may not.
Wealth is defined as having sufficient means or possessing nisab, making one ineligible for zakat.
2
State the ruling for the wealthy finder
The ruling is that it is not permissible (haram) for a rich finder to benefit from the found property. After one year of announcement, he must give it in charity to the poor.

4

Ibn Umar's refusal to give permission (لا آمرك) implies prohibition. The phrase 'if you had wished, you would not have taken it' suggests that the finder should have left it for a poor person. Thus, the rich must give it in charity.
This reasoning aligns with the Hanafi usul principle that a command (amr) does not necessarily imply obligation when coming from a Companion, but here the negation of permission indicates prohibition.

If a wealthy businessman finds a wallet with cash, after announcing for a year without success, he must donate the money to a verified charity. He cannot keep it even if he intends to return it later.
The ruling applies only after one full year of proper announcement (ta'rif).

Rich Finder and Luqatah: The Shafi'i Dispute
Hanafi ruling that a rich finder may not benefit from luqatah, with Shafi'i objection and Hanafi response

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a rich finder of luqatah and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the evidence cited.

Core Idea
A rich finder must give the lost item in charity after one year; he cannot benefit from it.

This passage presents a key inter-madhhab dispute in the law of luqatah (found property). The Hanafi school holds that a wealthy finder is prohibited from using the item even after a year of announcement, while the Shafi'i school permits it. The difference hinges on how each school interprets the hadith 'spend it' (fastanfiqha) and whether it applies universally or is restricted to the poor. Let's trace the arguments.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position and its evidence
Identify the explicit ruling from the matn: a rich finder (ghani) may not benefit from the luqatah. Note the evidence cited: the effect (athar) of Ibn Umar who did not command a rich finder to eat it.
2
Identify the opposing school and their proof
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the finder may use the luqatah after one year regardless of wealth. Their proof is the hadith of Zayd ibn Khalid where the Prophet said 'spend it' (fastanfiqha) without qualification.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds by restricting the hadith to a poor finder. The word 'spend' is often used for consumption, and other evidences (like Ibn Umar's effect) indicate that a rich finder should not benefit.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: a rich Hanafi must give the luqatah in charity; a rich Shafi'i may keep it after one year.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The Hanafi position is the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school. The matn concludes that for a poor finder there is no harm in benefiting.

Commentary
This is an inter-madhhab dispute. The Hanafi position: if the finder is rich, he may not benefit from the luqatah after one year of announcement; he must give it in charity. The proof includes the effect of Ibn Umar who forbade a rich finder from consuming it. Imam Shafi'i argues that the finder may use it regardless of wealth, citing the hadith of Zayd ibn Khalid where the Prophet instructed to 'spend it' (fastanfiqha) after one year if the owner does not appear. The Hanafi response, implied in the text, is that this hadith is interpreted to apply specifically to a poor finder, as evident from the context and other proofs. The ruling for a poor finder is given in (16): no harm in benefiting. Thus the Shafi'i hadith is harmonized by restricting it to the poor. The Hanafi position relies on the principle that wealthy individuals should not take from charity-like measures.

Poor Finder's Benefit from Luqatah: The Hanafi Concession
Ruling that a poor finder may use luqatah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a poor finder benefiting from luqatah and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
A poor finder may use luqatah after one year; a rich finder must not.

This lesson examines a specific concession in Hanafi luqatah law: the poor finder (faqir) may benefit from the found item after fulfilled announcement. While the general rule is to preserve the item for the owner, the Hanafi school allows the needy finder to use the item as his own, provided certain conditions are met. We will unpack the ruling and see how it resolves a key difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its condition
The act is the finder (multaqit) using the found item (luqatah) for personal benefit. The decisive condition is whether the finder is poor (faqir) or not.
Poverty here is defined by Hanafi standards: lack of nisab or being eligible for sadaqah.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
If the finder is poor, there is no prohibition (la ba's) – it is mubah. The finder may consume or use the item after one year of public announcement (ta'rif).
The ruling is conditional on both poverty and performance of ta'rif.
3
Recognize the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school permits any finder (rich or poor) to use the item after one year, citing the hadith 'fastanfiqha' (spend it). The Hanafi school restricts this permission to the poor.
The difference hinges on how the hadith is interpreted – Hanafis consider the context of poverty.
4
Understand the practical implication
A rich finder must continue to preserve the item or give it as charity (tasadduq) on the owner's behalf if the owner does not appear after one year.
The rich finder cannot personally benefit from the luqatah.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement: if the finder (multaqit) is poor (faqir), then there is no prohibition (la ba's) for him to benefit from the luqatah after the one-year announcement period. The commentary explains that the Shafi'i proof (the hadith of Zayd ibn Khalid with the instruction 'fastanfiqha' – spend it) is interpreted by the Hanafi school as applicable specifically to a poor finder. This harmonizes the Shafi'i evidence with the Hanafi position that the rich cannot use it. Thus, the poor finder has a concession to use the item as if it were his own, after proper announcement and with the intention of compensating the owner if he appears later (though that detail is not in this passage).

Giving Luqatah to Poor Relatives by a Rich Finder
Ruling permission for rich finder to give luqatah to poor relatives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a rich finder giving luqatah to his poor relatives and explain the underlying reasoning.

Core Idea
A rich finder may give luqatah as sadaqah to his poor relatives because it is not his own wealth.

In the previous lesson, we learned that a poor finder may consume the luqatah after one year. But what if the finder is rich? The text now extends this: a rich finder may give the luqatah as charity to his poor close relatives. This ruling uses the same principle—the luqatah belongs to the unknown owner, and charity on his behalf is valid. The commentary contrasts this with zakat, which cannot be given to one's own dependents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? The rich finder giving the luqatah as charity to specific relatives. The ruling is 'permissible' (yajuz).
The permission applies only after the one-year announcement period and the owner not appearing.
2
Identify the Conditions
The finder must be rich (ghani), and the recipient must be among the listed relatives: father, son, mother, or wife, and they must be poor (fuqara).
The list is exhaustive in the text; other relatives like uncle or cousin are not explicitly mentioned but analogized.
3
Understand the Reasoning
The luqatah is not the finder's property; it belongs to an unknown owner. Therefore, giving it in charity to one's own poor relatives is not like spending one's own wealth. It is akin to spending a stranger's wealth on needy family members, which is allowed.
This differs from zakat, which is one's own obligation and cannot benefit one's own dependents.
4
Apply the Ruling to Similar Cases
Consider if the finder is poor: he may consume it himself. If the finder is rich, he may give it to poor relatives. What about giving to a poor sibling? The commentary does not mention it, but by analogy, it may also be allowed if they are not dependent on the finder.
The key is that the recipient is not legally obliged to be supported by the finder.

Commentary
This is a ruling statement: it is permissible (yajuz) for a rich finder to give the luqatah as sadaqah to his own close relatives—father, son, mother, or wife—provided they are poor (fuqara). The commentary explains the reasoning: zakat cannot be given to one's own dependents (because they are generally supported by the giver), but luqatah is not the finder's own wealth; it is the property of an unknown owner. Since the finder is merely acting as a trustee and is commanded to give charity on behalf of the owner, it is valid to give to needy family members. The same logic extends from the previous ruling: if the poor finder can consume it himself, then directing it to poor relatives is even more meritorious. This ruling applies only after the one-year announcement period has passed and the owner has not appeared.

Key Terms
صدقہ (sadaqah): Charity given to the needy; here it is given on behalf of the lost item's owner as a substitute for returning it.
