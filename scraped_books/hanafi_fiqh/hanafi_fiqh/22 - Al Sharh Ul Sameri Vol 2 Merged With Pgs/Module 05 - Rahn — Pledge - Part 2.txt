Increase of the Pledge Belongs to the Pledgor
Ruling that offspring and other increases of the pledge go to the pledgor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the increase (namā') of a pledged item belongs to the pledgor and state that it is automatically included in the pledge.

Core Idea
The increase belongs to the pledgor but is automatically part of the pledge via the subordinate principle.

In this lesson, we examine a fundamental rule of pledges (rahn): any increase generated by the pledged property—such as offspring, milk, or fruit—remains the property of the pledgor (rāhin). However, because the increase is subordinate to the original item, it too becomes part of the pledge. This ruling is grounded in a prophetic hadith and the legal maxim that the subordinate follows the original.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
Identify the legal position: the increase of a pledged item belongs to the pledgor, not the mortgagee.
The increase is owned by the pledgor but is automatically pledged along with the original.
2
Understand the rationale
The increase arises from the pledgor's property, and he bears the expenses of maintaining it; hence he owns it.
This distinguishes ownership from possession—the mortgagee merely holds the item but does not own its benefits.
3
Examine the hadith evidence
The hadith 'له غنمه وعليه غرمه' (for him its benefit, on him its expense) proves the owner retains benefits and bears costs.
This hadith is cited from Abu Hurairah and recorded by al-Daraqutni and al-Bayhaqi.
4
Apply the subordinate principle
The increase is tābi‘ (subordinate) to the original; as a legal maxim, al-tābi‘ tābi‘. Thus when the original is pledged, the increase is also pledged.
This ensures the integrity of the pledge without depriving the pledgor of ownership.

Destruction of the Increase Without Compensation
Ruling statement on the loss of pledge increase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the perishing of the increase of a pledge and explain why the pledgor bears the loss without compensation.

Core Idea
The increase belongs to the pledgor; its loss is his alone, not the mortgagee's responsibility.

We have seen that the increase of a pledge (such as offspring or fruit) is owned by the pledgor, not the mortgagee. The question now is: what happens if that increase perishes while in the mortgagee's possession? Does the mortgagee owe compensation? This lesson clarifies that the loss falls entirely on the pledgor, with no claim against the mortgagee.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Understand the context: a pledge (rahn) with an increase (namā') that later perishes. Who bears the loss?
The increase is separate property owned by the pledgor.
2
State the ruling
The text states: 'فان هلک النماء هلک بغیر شیء' — if the increase perishes, it perishes without anything (no compensation).
This is a clear ruling: no compensation from the mortgagee.
3
Grasp the underlying principle
The increase is the pledgor's property. The mortgagee is merely a custodian. Loss of the pledgor's property falls on the pledgor, unless the mortgagee is negligent.
Negligence is not assumed here; the text implies natural perishing.
4
Apply the example
Example from the text: loan of nine pounds, goat pledged worth ten pounds, it gives birth to a kid worth five pounds. If the kid dies, the pledgor loses the kid's value; he cannot deduct from the debt or claim from the mortgagee.
The example illustrates the ruling clearly.

Ruling on Destruction of Original Pawn While Its Increase Remains
Ruling on debt calculation when pawn's original perishes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the debt reduction and redemption amount when the original pawn (asl) perishes but its increase (nama') survives, applying the Hanafi rule of proportional division based on values at possession and redemption.

Core Idea
When the original pawn is destroyed, only its proportional value cancels debt; the increase must be redeemed separately.

In the law of pawn (rahn), a special scenario arises when the original pawned item is destroyed but its increase (such as offspring or produce) remains. How does this affect the debt? The Hanafi scholars provide a precise calculation: the debt is divided proportionally between the original and the increase, based on their values at specific times. This lesson unpacks that ruling, explaining the distinction between the original as the primary security and the increase as a trust, and guides you through an example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components
Distinguish between the original pawn (asl) and its increase (nama'). The original is the primary security; the increase is subordinate, held as a trust (amana).
Example: a goat (asl) and its kid (nama').
2
Apportion the debt proportionally
Divide the total debt according to the value of the original at the time of possession (qabd) and the value of the increase at the time of redemption (fikak). This determines the share of debt attributable to each.
The original's value is fixed at possession; the increase's value is at redemption because it grows over time.
3
Apply the redemption rule
The share of debt corresponding to the original is cancelled (saaqit) from the total debt. The share of debt corresponding to the increase must be paid by the pledgor to redeem the increase.
This follows the principle: if the increase perishes, the debt remains; if the original perishes, only its portion is forgiven.

Permissibility of Increasing the Pawn (Ziyada fi al-Rahn)
Ruling on permissibility of increasing pawn with example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on increasing a pawn and explain the effect on debt coverage when the pawn perishes.

Core Idea
Increasing the pawn is permissible; the added item merges with the original and debt is proportionally covered by both.

In Hanafi fiqh, a pawn (rahn) is a contract of security. A common question is whether the pledgor can later add more items to the same pawn. This lesson clarifies that such an addition is valid and how it affects the debt in case of loss.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Note that the text clearly states permissibility: 'ويجوز الزيادة في الرهن'. This is the explicit ruling.
2
Analyze the example
The example: a loan of 9 pounds secured initially by a goat worth 10 pounds; later another goat worth 5 pounds is added for the same loan. This illustrates the addition.
The goats are valued in pounds; the total pawn value becomes 15 pounds.
3
Understand the merger
The original item is called 'asl' (principal) and the added item 'far'' (branch). They constitute a single pawn covering the same debt.
This merger means the debtor's obligation is secured by the combined value.
4
Apply the loss distribution rule
If the pawn perishes (e.g., both items), the debt is deducted proportionally from the total value of both items, not from one alone.
In the example, if both goats perish, the 9-pound debt is deducted from the combined 15-pound value, leaving 6 pounds to the pledgor.

Core Idea
Transgression makes the mortgagee liable like a usurper for the full value.

A mortgagee (murtahin) holds the collateral as a trust. If he destroys it without transgression, he owes only the debt amount. But if he intentionally wrongs the property, he becomes liable for its full market value. This lesson unpacks that rule and its qiyas-based proof.

The common illa is intentional transgression leading to destruction. Both the usurper and the mortgagee commit an unjust act that causes loss.

Since the mortgagee's transgression shares the same illa, the ruling (full-value liability) is transferred to the far' (mortgagee's transgression).

Without transgression, the collateral is an amanah; if destroyed by accident, the mortgagee owes only the debt, and the surplus (amanah) is not owed. Transgression removes this trust protection.

If your own property is destroyed in your hands, you owe nothing for it.

Core Idea

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Executor's right to sell collateral strengthens after mortgagor's death to ensure debt payment.

When a person dies with an outstanding debt secured by collateral, who has the right to sell that collateral to repay the lender? The Hanafi school provides a clear rule: the executor of the deceased mortgagor (rahin) is authorized to sell the pledged item and settle the debt. Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling

2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is not a specific verse or hadith quoted, but a logical a fortiori argument (bi darajah al-ūlā): if the executor had this right during the mortgagor's life, then after death—when the debt becomes an immediate obligation on the estate—the right is even more evident.
This is a form of qiyas jali (clear analogy) based on priority.
3

The underlying reason (illa) is the necessity to fulfill the debt. During life, the executor could sell with the owner's consent or for necessity. After death, the debt is a claim against the estate, making the sale even more necessary.
The strength of the need increases after death.
4

No intra-Hanafi disagreement is mentioned in this passage; the ruling is standard (zahir al-riwayah) and agreed upon.
Other schools may have different mechanisms, but the Hanafi position is clear.
5
Evaluate the practical implication
The ruling ensures that the mortgagee's right is protected without delay. The executor, if appointed, has direct authority. If no executor exists, the qadi appoints one. Heirs cannot prevent the sale.
This prioritizes the debt over inheritance distribution.

Reward for Returning a Pledged Runaway Slave
Ruling that the reward for returning a runaway pledged slave is payable by the pledgee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on who bears the reward for returning a runaway pledged slave and explain the legal basis in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
The pledgee pays the reward because he is responsible for safekeeping and benefits from the return.

This lesson examines a specific ruling in the chapter of rahn (pledge): when a pledged slave runs away and is returned, who must pay the reward? The Hanafi school places this obligation on the pledgee (murtahin), not the owner. Understanding the reasoning behind this ruling illuminates the broader principle that the one charged with safekeeping bears related costs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties involved
Determine who the rahin (owner/pledgor) and murtahin (pledgee/creditor) are in the rahn contract.
The slave is the collateral (marhun), held by the murtahin as security for a debt.
2
State the ruling clearly
The passage states: 'If the runaway slave is a pledge, then the reward is upon the murtahin.' Commit this ruling to memory.
The reward (ju'l) is a payment for returning the slave.
3
Explain the reasoning
Two reasons are given: (1) The murtahin is obligated to keep the slave safe (hifz), so return costs fall on him. (2) The murtahin benefits because his collateral is preserved.
This follows the principle that the one responsible for safekeeping bears expenses (al-ghurm bi al-ghunm).
4
Contrast with the owner's liability
Why not the owner? The owner's property is already tied up; he does not have direct possession. The murtahin's duty and benefit shift the cost.
If the slave were not pledged, the owner would pay, but the pledge modifies responsibility.

And if the runaway slave is a pledge (rahn), then the reward is upon the mortgagee (murtahin). [The murtahin, i.e., the one with whom the slave is pledged, is responsible for keeping the slave in safety. Therefore, the wage for returning the slave is upon the murtahin. The murtahin's wealth is tied up, and by returning the slave, his wealth is saved, so the wage for returning the slave is upon the murtahin. In short, the one upon whom safekeeping is obligatory, upon him is the wage.]
Source pages p. 429

Commentary
This passage deals with the case where a runaway slave is held as collateral (rahn) by a pledgee (murtahin). Since the pledgee is responsible for the safekeeping of the slave during the pledge period, the reward for returning the slave (if he runs away and is brought back) falls on the pledgee, not the owner (rahin). The reasoning is twofold: first, the pledgee is obligated to protect the slave; second, by having the slave returned, the pledgee benefits because his security (the slave) is preserved. Therefore, he bears the cost of the reward. This is a logical extension of the principle that the one responsible for safekeeping bears the cost of returning the property.

Key Terms
رهن (rahn): A pledge or collateral; a slave held as security for a debt.
مرتهن (murtahin): The person who receives the pledge (the creditor).

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi fiqh, when a pledged slave runs away, the reward for his return is wajib upon the pledgee (murtahin), not the owner, because the pledgee bears the duty of safekeeping and benefits from the return.
