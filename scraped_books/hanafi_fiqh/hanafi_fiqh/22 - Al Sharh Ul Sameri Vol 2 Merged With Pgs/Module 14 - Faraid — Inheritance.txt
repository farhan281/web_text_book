The Age of Puberty for Girls: Abu Hanifa vs. the Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on age of puberty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on the age of puberty for a girl without physical signs and explain the proof and practical difference.

Core Idea
For a girl without signs, Abu Hanifa sets puberty at 17; the Sahibayn at 15.

This passage from Kitab al-Hajr addresses a key intra-Hanafi dispute: what is the age of majority for a girl who shows no signs of puberty? The disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two foremost disciples has important practical consequences. We will examine their proofs and see how the hadith of Ibn Umar is interpreted differently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The issue is the age at which a girl without physical signs of puberty becomes legally adult.
2
State the two positions
Abu Hanifa: 17 years; Sahibayn: 15 years.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's dalil
They cite the hadith of Ibn Umar where the Prophet permitted him at 15, showing this is the age of majority.
4
Understand Abu Hanifa's reasoning
He considers that girls mature earlier, so one year less than the boy's age (18) gives 17.
Abu Hanifa's view on boys without signs is 18, though not in this passage.

Priority of Acknowledged Creditor Over Heirs in Death Illness
Ruling with proof from Quran and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that a debt acknowledged in death illness is given priority over heirs, and identify the conditions under which such acknowledgment may be subordinated or invalidated.

Core Idea
A debt acknowledged in death illness is paid before heirs, but if it harms prior creditors, the acknowledgment is subordinated.

The general principle of Islamic law is that debts are paid from the estate before inheritance. This passage addresses a specific scenario: when a person on their deathbed acknowledges a debt to someone. The author explains that such a creditor (muqarr lahu) is given priority over the heirs, but this is qualified by the concern of harming prior creditors. The ruling is derived from Quranic injunctions against causing harm (dirar) and Prophetic hadith emphasizing the same. The key is to balance the validity of the acknowledgment with protection of existing creditors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
The passage states: 'wa kana al-muqarr lahu awla min al-warathah' – the one for whom the debt is acknowledged is more entitled than the heirs. This establishes the priority of the acknowledged creditor over heirs in receiving payment from the estate.
This rests on the broader principle that debts are settled before inheritance (Quran 4:12).
2
Understand the exception: harm to prior creditors
The author introduces a concern: if the acknowledgment in death illness is made without specifying causes, it may be a ploy to favor the acknowledged creditor at the expense of prior creditors (from health or known causes during illness). In such cases, the acknowledged creditor is subordinated to prior creditors, meaning they are paid later.
The underlying legal cause (illa) is the potential harm (dirar) to prior creditors.
3
Examine the Quranic evidence
The passage cites Quran 4:12, which includes the phrase 'ghayra mudarr' (without harm). This verse is interpreted as prohibiting causing harm through wills and, by analogy, through acknowledgments of debt.
The verse shows that financial dispositions must not harm others.
4
Examine the hadith evidence
A hadith from Abu Hurairah is quoted: a person who causes harm in their will after sixty years of obedience will enter the Fire. This reinforces the prohibition of harm in all financial matters related to death, including acknowledgments.
The hadith underscores the severity of causing harm in such dispositions.
5
Apply the distinction: when is the acknowledgment valid?
The passage concludes that if there are no prior debts (from health or known causes), then no suspicion of harm exists, and the acknowledgment is valid; the creditor is paid before heirs. Conversely, if there is suspicion of harming prior creditors, the acknowledgment is invalid (batil).
This is a practical application of the maxim: 'Al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by their intentions).

Acknowledgment to an Heir: Void Unless Confirmed by Others
Ruling with proof: acknowledgment for an heir is void unless confirmed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that acknowledgment of debt to an heir during terminal illness is void unless confirmed by other heirs, and apply the condition of their consent to determine validity.

Core Idea
An heir cannot be favored via acknowledgment; other heirs must consent for it to be valid.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a dying person's acknowledgment of debt in favor of one of his heirs. The default ruling is that such an acknowledgment is void, based on prophetic hadith. However, there is a condition that makes it valid: the confirmation of the other heirs. Understanding this rule ensures fair distribution of the estate and prevents harm to the other heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrar) of debt by a terminally ill person in favor of one of his heirs. The default ruling is that it is void (batil).
2
State the condition for validity
The acknowledgment becomes valid if the other heirs confirm (tasdiq) it, i.e., they agree that the debt is genuine.
Confirmation must be from all other heirs? The text says 'remainder of heirs' (بقية الورثة), implying all except the one acknowledged for.
3
Cite the dalil
The hadith from Ibn Abbas and Ja'far ibn Muhammad: 'لا وصية لوارث ولا إقرار بدين' (No will for an heir and no acknowledgment of debt).
The hadith is ahad, so its thubut is zanni, but it is accepted in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Understand the underlying cause
The ruling prevents harm (darar) to the other heirs, as an acknowledgment could give the favored heir an extra share beyond his rightful inheritance.
This is similar to the prohibition of a will for an heir.
5
Note the exception
If the debt is established through known causes (e.g., evidence of a purchase), it can be enforced even without the heirs' consent, because the debt is independently proven.
The source mentions 'if the debt is known through common means (اسباب معروفہ)', i.e., witnesses or documentation.

Iqrar of a Sick Person for an Heir: Ruling and Proof
Ruling on iqrar of a dying person for an heir and its condition of consent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on iqrar made during terminal illness for an heir, identify the condition that makes it valid, and cite the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
Iqrar for an heir in terminal illness is invalid unless all other heirs consent to remove the harm.

Iqrar (declaration of debt) made during terminal illness (marad al-mawt) is suspect because it may favor one heir at the expense of others. The Hanafi school rules such an iqrar invalid (batil) by default, but allows it to become valid if all other heirs confirm the debt. This strikes a balance between honoring the deceased's declaration and protecting other heirs' rights. In this lesson, you will learn the ruling, its condition, and the proof from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and default ruling
The act is iqrar (acknowledgment of debt) made by a person in terminal illness in favor of an heir. The default ruling is batil (invalid).
The invalidity is due to potential harm (darar) to other heirs.
2
Identify the condition for validity
The iqrar becomes valid if all other heirs confirm (tasdiq) the iqrar, indicating they accept the debt.
Consent removes the presumption of harm.
3
Locate the primary dalil
Two hadiths: (1) Ibn Abbas: 'لا يجوز لوارث وصية الا ان يشاء الورثة' (a will for an heir is not permissible unless the heirs wish). (2) Ja'far ibn Muhammad: 'لا وصية لوارث ولا اقرار بدين' (no will for an heir and no iqrar of debt).
Both hadiths are narrated with zanni thubut but are relied upon.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The explicit prohibition indicates invalidity, but the exception 'unless the heirs wish' shows consent removes the prohibition. The second hadith equates iqrar with will, so the same rule applies.
The ruling is based on preventing harm.
5
Apply the ruling to a furu'
The passage gives an example: if the deceased had bought a bull with unpaid price, that debt is proven by external means and must be paid to the heir regardless.
This shows that if the debt is independently established, it is not subject to the iqrar rule.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of paternity is valid even in death illness if the child confirms and has no known lineage — lineage is an essential need.

In this ruling, we encounter a key principle of Hanafi family law: a man's acknowledgment of an unknown boy as his son, with the boy's confirmation, establishes lineage and inheritance rights. This is allowed even if the man is terminally ill, because lineage is considered a fundamental need (hājat aṣliyya). We will examine the conditions and effects of this legal act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is iqrar (acknowledgment) of a child as one's son. The ruling: lineage is established between the man and the child, and the child inherits from the man.
The ruling is valid even if the man is in marad al-mawt (death illness).
2
List the necessary conditions
Three conditions: (1) the child must be of an age that he could plausibly be born to the man (yuldhu mithluhu li-mithlihi); (2) the child must have no known lineage (laysa lahu nasab ma'ruf); (3) the child must confirm the acknowledgment (saddaqahu al-ghulam).
Confirmation is required if the child is of discerning age. For an infant, confirmation is not possible, but the ruling may still apply through other means.
3
Understand the exception for death illness
Normally, acts during marad al-mawt are restricted to one-third of the estate, but establishing lineage is an exception because it is an essential need, similar to marriage.
The child becomes a full heir, not a legatee, so the restriction does not apply.
4
Determine the legal consequences
Once conditions are met, lineage is established retroactively. The child inherits from the man and shares with other heirs as a natural son.
This also entitles the child to maintenance and other rights of legitimacy.

This ruling balances the validity of the acknowledgment with the protection of other heirs' rights.

Acknowledgment of brotherhood after father's death gives inheritance but not lineage.

In Hanafi fiqh, the principle that 'lineage cannot be imposed on another' (تحميل النسب على الغير) creates a unique ruling when a son acknowledges another as his brother after their father's death. The acknowledgment is valid for inheritance purposes but does not establish the brother's lineage from the deceased father. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its evidence from the hadith of the fallen man, and its practical application in inheritance distribution.

Guided Path

The act is acknowledging a brother after the father's death. The matn states two effects: (1) lineage from the father is not established (لم يثبت نسب أخيه منه), (2) but the acknowledged brother shares in the inheritance (ويشاركه في الميراث).
Note the two distinct legal consequences: nasab (lineage) and mīrāth (inheritance).
2
Locate the dalīl (evidence)
The author cites the hadith of a man who fell from a palm tree, leaving neither child nor close kin. The Prophet ﷺ ordered his inheritance to be given to a man from his village. This proves that when no legal heirs exist, the inheritance can be given to a deserving person via acknowledgment.
The hadith is a Sunnah āḥād; its thubūt is ẓannī, but its dalāla (indication) is qaṭī for this ruling.
3

The Prophet ﷺ did not establish lineage for the villager; he simply gave him the inheritance. Similarly, the acknowledger gives the inheritance without establishing lineage. The underlying principle is that lineage cannot be imposed on another (taḥmīl al-nasab ʻalā al-ghayr).
This analogy is based on the general rule that iqrār (acknowledgment) is binding on the acknowledger only.

The commentary clarifies that the acknowledged brother takes only half of the acknowledger's share, not from other siblings. This prevents injustice to other heirs. The acknowledgment is considered a transfer from the acknowledger's own right.
The phrase 'half of the acknowledger's share' (آدھا) is specific to the Hanafi position.

Core Idea
A debt in an estate cannot be transferred via khuruj; it can only be waived.

When an heir exits the estate (khuruj) and the estate includes a debt owed by a third party, the validity of the settlement hinges on how the debt is handled. The passage presents two scenarios: one where the remaining heirs take the debt and release the exiting heir (void), and another where they simply waive their claim against his share (valid). Dispute on Equal Urine Precedence in Hermaphrodite

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Urine quantity depends on opening width, not gender – Abu Hanifa rejects it; Sahibayn accept for practical determination.

In cases where a hermaphrodite urinates from both openings simultaneously with no temporal precedence, a dispute arises within the Hanafi school: should the volume of urine determine gender? Abu Hanifa argues that quantity is unreliable, while the Sahibayn adopt it as a practical measure. This lesson unpacks their reasoning and the school's tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
The case is a hermaphrodite (khuntha) who urinates from both the male and female openings at the same time, with no precedence (sabaq) in timing.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling
Abu Hanifa holds that quantity (kathrah) is not considered. The child remains indeterminate until another sign, such as beard growth or menses, appears.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the child is attributed to the opening from which more urine comes. If more from the penis, male; if more from the vulva, female.
4
Compare their proofs
Abu Hanifa's proof: Quantity depends on the width of the opening, a physical factor unrelated to gender. The Sahibayn's proof: In the absence of temporal precedence, quantity provides a practical means to determine gender, as the larger flow likely indicates the primary urinary organ.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of the Sahibayn, as it yields a determinate ruling.

Core Idea
After puberty, biological signs conclusively determine the gender of a hermaphrodite for all legal purposes.

This passage from the Hanafi text clarifies the post-puberty signs that settle the gender of a hermaphrodite (khuntha). Before puberty, the khuntha is treated as indeterminate, but once these signs appear, the legal identity is fixed, affecting inheritance, marriage, and other rulings. The signs are based on natural biological development and are considered definitive in the Hanafi school.

Inheritance of a Khuntha: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khuntha inheritance calculation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the three Hanafi positions on the inheritance of a khuntha when a father leaves a son and a khuntha, and EXPLAIN the difference in calculation methods between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
Three Hanafi calculations: Abu Hanifa treats khuntha as female; Sahibayn as intermediate; within Sahibayn, qiyas differs.

This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute on the inheritance of a khuntha (hermaphrodite). When a father dies leaving a son and a khuntha, Imam Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as female, giving the son 2/3 and the khuntha 1/3. The two disciples (Sahibayn) follow al-Sha'bi, giving the khuntha half of the male share plus half of the female share, placing the khuntha in between. However, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad disagree on how to apply this principle, leading to different share allocations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Determine the exact case: a father dies leaving only a son and a khuntha. Identify that the khuntha's ambiguous gender creates a dispute about sharing.
The khuntha is not conclusively male or female.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa says the khuntha is treated as female in inheritance unless proven otherwise. Thus, the estate is divided into 3 shares: son gets 2, khuntha gets 1.
Basis: default treatment as female.
3
State the Sahibayn's position (al-Sha'bi)
The Sahibayn follow al-Sha'bi: the khuntha gets half of what a son would get plus half of what a daughter would get. This places the khuntha between male and female.
This requires knowing how much a son and a daughter would receive separately.
4
Apply the Sahibayn's position in two different qiyas
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ on the basis of calculation. Abu Yusuf considers the scenario where only the khuntha inherits (as if no son, only khuntha), leading to a division of 7 shares (son 4, khuntha 3). Muhammad (it appears) considers both son and khuntha together, leading to a different division (likely 4 shares, son 2, khuntha 1 or similar, though the text is cut off).
The key difference is in the hypothetical (taqdir) used for qiyas.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical outcome: under Abu Hanifa, son gets 2/3, khuntha 1/3. Under Abu Yusuf's method, son gets 4/7, khuntha 3/7. Under Muhammad's method, the shares differ again.
The dispute affects actual distribution.

Three Hanafi Views on the Hermaphrodite's Share
Intra-Hanafi dispute on khuntha inheritance with three views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on a khuntha's share when surviving with a son, explain the qiyas behind each, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Three Hanafi imams give three different shares for a khuntha—disagreement on presumption and averaging method.

When a father dies leaving only a son and a khuntha (hermaphrodite), the Hanafi school does not have a single answer. Abu Hanifa treats the khuntha as female by default, while the two companions (Sahibayn) treat it as ambiguous and average male and female shares. Even among the Sahibayn, their qiyas methods yield different results. This lesson unpacks each position and its practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Heirs and the Core Issue
We have a son (male) and a khuntha (sex unknown). The issue: how to divide the estate when one heir's sex is ambiguous.
The khuntha's share depends on whether it is treated as male, female, or an average.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa presumes the khuntha is female unless male nature is proven. Therefore the son gets two shares, the khuntha one share (2:1 ratio).
This is the simplest method: treat as female.
3
State the Sahibayn's Common Principle
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad agree that the khuntha should receive half the share of a male and half the share of a female, but they differ in applying the qiyas.
Their principle comes from al-Sha'bi.
4
Distinguish Their Qiyas Methods
Abu Yusuf averages the shares assuming the khuntha is simultaneously male and female, giving the son 4/7 and the khuntha 3/7. Muhammad averages two separate scenarios (khuntha as male, then as female), giving the son 7/12 and the khuntha 5/12.
The difference stems from whether you average the fractions or the whole distributions.
5
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical outcome differs: son's share varies from 2/3 (Abu Hanifa) to 4/7 (Abu Yusuf) to 7/12 (Muhammad). The mufta bihi is typically Abu Hanifa's view unless male indicators are strong.
Later Hanafi fatwa favors the default female presumption.

Comparing two Hanafi inheritance calculations for a khuntha with a son

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state both Hanafi views on the khuntha's inheritance with a son, calculate the shares using each method, and explain the averaging principle underlying Imam Muhammad's approach.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school has two methods for khuntha inheritance — the earlier 7-share model and Imam Muhammad's 12-share averaging approach, both aiming to account for ambiguous gender.

Inheritance calculations become uniquely challenging when an heir's gender is ambiguous. The Hanafi tradition addresses this through the concept of the khuntha (hermaphrodite). This lesson examines two distinct Hanafi methods for dividing an estate when a son and a khuntha are the only heirs — one attributed to earlier authorities and another refined by Imam Muhammad. Understanding both views deepens your appreciation for how Hanafi jurists handle uncertainty in faraid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Heirs
There are two heirs: a son (definite male) and a khuntha (ambiguous gender). The son is always treated as male. The khuntha's gender is uncertain.
2
Understand the Earlier View
The text first states an earlier view: the estate is divided into 7 shares, with 4 for the son and 3 for the khuntha. This implies a simplified weighting, possibly treating the khuntha as predominantly female.
The reasoning is not elaborated in the text.
3
Understand Imam Muhammad's View
Imam Muhammad divides the estate into 12 shares: 7 for the son, 5 for the khuntha. This results from averaging two scenarios: treating the khuntha as a male (son gets 2/3, khuntha 1/3) and as a female (both get 1/2).
The averaging method is the hallmark of Hanafi khuntha inheritance.
4

If male: son 2/3, khuntha 1/3 → over 12 shares, son 8, khuntha 4. If female: both 1/2 → over 12, son 6, khuntha 6. Average: son (8+6)/2 = 7, khuntha (4+6)/2 = 5. This yields the 7:5 ratio.

Later Hanafi practice tends to prefer Imam Muhammad's method for its consistency.

Calculating the Share of a Khuntha in Inheritance
Intra-Hanafi dispute on calculating khuntha's inheritance share using percentage averaging.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the shares of a son and a khuntha (hermaphrodite) in inheritance using the averaging method and state the difference between Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad.

Core Idea
The khuntha's share is the average of hypothetical shares as male and female.

This passage from Al-Sharh al-Thumayri introduces a practical percentage-based method for calculating the inheritance share of a khuntha when a son is also present. It then presents a table comparing the results of Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, revealing a slight difference. We will work through the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
We have two heirs: a son and a khuntha. Because the khuntha's sex is ambiguous, we must consider two scenarios: one where the khuntha is assumed male, and one where assumed female. The actual share is the average of the shares in these two scenarios.
This method ensures the khuntha receives the minimum possible share (Hanafi principle).
2
Calculate shares in scenario 1 (khuntha as male)
If the khuntha is male, the heirs are two sons. The estate is divided equally: each gets 1/2, or 50%.
Son gets 50%, khuntha gets 50%.
3
Calculate shares in scenario 2 (khuntha as female)
If the khuntha is female, the heirs are a son and a daughter. The son gets twice the daughter's share: son gets 2/3 (66.66%), daughter (khuntha) gets 1/3 (33.33%).
These fractions are derived from Quran 4:11.
4
Average the shares for each heir
For the son: (50% + 66.66%) / 2 = 58.33%. For the khuntha: (50% + 33.33%) / 2 = 41.665% ≈ 41.67%.
These are the results according to Imam Muhammad.
5
Compare with Imam Abu Yusuf's results
Imam Abu Yusuf gives son 57.142% and khuntha 42.857%. The difference is 1.191% for each heir. The passage does not explain why, but it likely arises from a different rounding or averaging method.
The practical difference: in a 100 dirham estate, Imam Muhammad gives son ~58.33 dirhams, Imam Abu Yusuf gives ~57.14 dirhams.

Rulings on the Missing Person (Mafqud)
Stating the death ruling and inheritance rules for a missing person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for declaring the death of a missing person and apply the inheritance rules for heirs present at the time of the ruling.

Core Idea
A missing person is declared dead after 120 years from birth; only then is his estate divided among heirs alive at that moment.

The disappearance of a person creates uncertainty about life and death, affecting inheritance and marital status. Hanafi fiqh provides a clear framework: a judge declares death after 120 years from birth, or earlier if strong evidence surfaces. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and how inheritance is handled for the missing person and his heirs.

Guided Path

Locate the statement: after 120 years from birth, the judge rules death. Note that the ídda for the wife and division of wealth follow.
The 120-year period is based on maximum human lifespan.

The estate is divided among heirs present at the time of the death ruling. Those who died before do not inherit, because before the ruling the missing person is considered alive.
This prevents inheritance before the legal declaration.
3

The passage cites an athar from Qatadah where a death ruling and wealth division occur after four years when the wife raises the matter. Although the athar mentions four years, the Hanafi school uses 120 years as the default period.
The athar shows the principle that after death ruling, wealth is divided.
4

The missing person does not inherit from anyone who dies during his absence. He is treated as dead from the time of disappearance for inheritance purposes.
This symmetry ensures consistency: he does not benefit from estates of deceased relatives.
5

If a strong presumption (ghalabat al-zann) of death arises before 120 years, a judge may declare death earlier. This is based on istihsan.
The 120-year rule is the default, not an absolute requirement.
