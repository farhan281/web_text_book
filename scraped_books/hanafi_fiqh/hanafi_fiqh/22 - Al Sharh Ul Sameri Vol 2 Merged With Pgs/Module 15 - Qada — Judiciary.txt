Judge Pays Debt When Currency Matches
Ruling when bankrupt has same currency as debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when a bankrupt has the same currency as the debt and apply the hadith proof to justify the judge's direct payment without permission.

Core Idea
If the bankrupt has the same currency as the debt, the judge may pay without permission.

This lesson addresses a specific scenario in qada (judicial rulings): when a bankrupt person (muflis) has assets of the same currency type as the debt owed. The text provides a clear ruling that the judge can make the payment directly without seeking the bankrupt's consent. We will examine the proof from the Sunnah and understand the underlying rationale based on the fungible nature of dirhams.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Note the specific conditions: the bankrupt has dirhams, and the debt is also in dirhams. The ruling is that the judge pays the creditor without the bankrupt's permission.
The ruling applies only when the asset and debt are in the same currency.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites a hadith from Abu Huraira: 'Whoever finds his exact property with a bankrupt person has more right to it.' This is a proof from authentic Sunnah.
Hadith reference: Sahih al-Bukhari, no. 2402.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith gives the creditor priority over his property. Since dirhams are fungible (mithli), the creditor's right is considered attached to the bankrupt's dirhams of the same type. Therefore, the judge, as an authority, can enforce this right without the bankrupt's consent.
The same logic applies to dinars and other fungible currencies.

Imprisonment of Debtor Based on Type of Debt
Describes debts for which judge may imprison debtor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi classification of debts to determine when a judge may imprison a debtor.

Core Idea
A debtor can be imprisoned only for debts where he received property or voluntarily undertook the obligation.

The Hanafi judge has discretion to imprison a debtor who appears to have assets but refuses to pay. This lesson identifies which types of debts justify such imprisonment without additional evidence, based on the nature of the debt and a Prophetic precedent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of debts
Locate in the passage the two types of debts for which imprisonment is allowed: (1) compensation for property received, and (2) debts undertaken by contract. Note the examples given.
These categories share the characteristic that the debtor either had property in hand or voluntarily assumed the obligation, implying ability to pay.
2
Explain the reasoning
For each category, explain why the law presumes the debtor has assets. In the first category, the debtor actually received property; in the second, his voluntary contract suggests he had the means to promise payment.
This reasoning is implicit in the Hanafi position; the passage does not elaborate but relies on the principle that such debts indicate solvency.
3
Distinguish debts that do not permit imprisonment
Identify the debts listed as exceptions: compensation for usurpation (ghasb) and blood money (arsh). Note that for these, imprisonment requires witnesses proving the debtor has property.
These debts arise from wrongdoing, not from receipt of property or voluntary undertaking, so the presumption of assets is weaker.
4
Apply the hadith proof
Read the hadith cited: the Prophet imprisoned a man who freed a slave (a contractual debt) until his property was sold. Explain how this hadith supports imprisoning for contractual debts.
The hadith is from Sunan al-Bayhaqi and is ahad, making the ruling zanni but still authoritative.
5
Consider the role of witnesses
For debts outside the two categories, the passage requires witnesses to prove the debtor has property. Explain the significance: the burden of proof shifts to the creditor to show the debtor is not truly insolvent.
This protects the debtor from imprisonment based solely on the nature of the debt when there is no evidence of assets.

Imprisonment and Release of an Insolvent Debtor
Specifies imprisonment period and conditions for release

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi procedure for imprisoning an insolvent debtor and apply the conditions for release when insolvency is proven.

Core Idea
Imprisonment is for investigation, not punishment; release when insolvency is confirmed.

This passage addresses a common legal scenario: a debtor who cannot pay. The Hanafi school provides a procedure for the judge to imprison the debtor for a limited period to investigate his assets. If no property is discovered, or the debtor proves his insolvency, he must be released. The ruling is grounded in a Prophetic hadith that emphasizes taking what assets exist and then releasing the debtor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage describes the judge's authority to imprison a debtor. Determine that this is a mubah (permissible) discretionary action for investigation purposes, not a punitive measure.
2
Extract the duration and conditions
Note the specified imprisonment period: two to three months. Identify the condition: ongoing inquiry into the debtor's financial condition. Understand the two triggers for release: (1) no assets discovered after investigation, or (2) debtor establishes proof of insolvency via bayyinah.
Bayyinah here means clear evidence, typically two male witnesses.
3
Examine the dalil
Analyze the hadith from Abu Sa'id al-Khudri (Muslim). The Prophet ﷺ instructed creditors to take what they find and have no more claim, without ordering further imprisonment. This indicates that once insolvency is confirmed, imprisonment ceases.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted in practice.
4
Connect the proof to the ruling
Understand the wajh al-dalalah: The Prophet's silence about continuing imprisonment after taking assets implies that no further detention is allowed when the debtor has no remaining property. This supports the Hanafi position that imprisonment is only for investigation.

Ruling on Unspecified 'Dirhams' in Acknowledgment
States the ruling that 'على دراهم' implies at least three dirhams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the minimum number of dirhams implied by an unspecified plural acknowledgment (iqrār) and explain the linguistic basis in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the Arabic plural form of currency implies at least three units in an acknowledgment.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling when someone acknowledges a debt using the plural word 'dirhams' without specifying a number. The language itself provides the answer: the Arabic plural sets a minimum of three. Understanding this linguistically grounded ruling is essential for correct application in iqrār cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
The act is an acknowledgment (iqrār) of a debt using the plural noun 'dirhams' without specifying a quantity. Recognize that iqrār is a binding declaration in Hanafi law.
2
Apply the Arabic linguistic rule for the plural
In Arabic, the plural form (jamʿ) minimally indicates three. The word 'دراهم' is the plural of 'dirham', so it must refer to at least three dirhams unless the speaker clarifies otherwise.
Hanafis treat this linguistic rule as authoritative in legal interpretation.
3
Determine the ruling
The acknowledgment is valid for three dirhams. If the speaker intended more, he must specify; otherwise, three is incumbent upon him.
4
Consider exceptions
If the speaker adds 'more than that' (e.g., 'kathirah'), the ruling shifts to the specified amount. The plural form can include more, but the default is the minimum.

Minimum Amount in 'Kadha Kadha Dirham' Acknowledgment
States that 'كذا كذا درهما' is not believed for less than eleven dirhams

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the phrase 'kadha kadha dirham' in an acknowledgment and explain the linguistic reasoning for the minimum amount.

Core Idea
Saying 'kadha kadha dirham' without a conjunction implies eleven dirhams as the minimum.

When a person acknowledges a debt using the phrase 'kadha kadha dirham' (such such dirhams), what does it mean? The Hanafi school uses the structure of Arabic numerals to determine the minimum amount. This lesson explains that the minimum is eleven dirhams because the wording parallels the construction of the number eleven.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
Recognize that the act is an iqrar (acknowledgment) using the phrase 'kadha kadha dirham'. The ruling is that the acknowledgment is not believed for any amount less than eleven dirhams.
2
Understand the linguistic analogy
The phrase consists of two repetitions of 'kadha' without a conjunction (waw). This mirrors the Arabic number 'ahada ashara' (eleven), which also has two words without a conjunction.
In Arabic, numbers 11-19 are formed by two words without a waw.
3
Apply the analogy to determine the minimum
Since the pattern matches only numbers 11-19, the smallest number expressible is eleven. Therefore, the acknowledgment is valid only for amounts of eleven dirhams or more.
4
Note the consequence for lesser claims
If the claimant demands an amount less than eleven, the acknowledgment does not support the claim. The defendant is not believed for that lesser amount.
The ruling presupposes the acknowledgment is clear in its minimum; any claim below that is not considered acknowledged.

Ruling on 'Alayhi' or 'Qibali' as Acknowledgment of Debt
States that saying 'alā' or 'qibalī' constitutes acknowledgment of debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that saying 'alā' or 'qibalī' constitutes an acknowledgment of debt and apply the rule to similar expressions.

Core Idea
Saying 'upon me' or 'to me' imposes liability and is a binding acknowledgment of debt.

This lesson examines a key rule in Hanafi law of iqrār (acknowledgment). The words we use to acknowledge debt are not all equal; certain phrases carry legal force. Here we explore why 'alā' and 'qibalī' are treated as acknowledgments, backed by a tradition from the early period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is saying 'alā' (upon) or 'qibalī' (to me) in reference to another person. The ruling is that this constitutes an acknowledgment of debt (iqrār).
The ruling is from the matn 'qāla lahu ʿalā aw qibalī faqad aqqara bi-dayn'.
2
Identify the key terms
The terms 'alā' and 'qibalī' are the operative words. 'Alā' literally means 'on' and is used to impose liability on oneself. 'Qibalī' means 'to me' and similarly implies obligation.
These terms are distinct from neutral words like 'indī' (with me) which may indicate trust.
3
Understand the linguistic reasoning
In Arabic, 'alā' is a preposition that, when used in context of debt, indicates that the debt is upon the speaker. 'Qibalī' has a similar connotation. Therefore, the words themselves carry the legal effect of acknowledgment.
The commentator explains that these words 'impose something on oneself'.

Core Idea
In a shuf'ah claim, the judge first verifies the pre-emptor's ownership, then the sale, using evidence and oaths.

This lesson walks you through the Hanafi judicial procedure for handling a shuf'ah (pre-emption) claim. The procedure is derived from the general principle that the burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier. Understanding this process is crucial for anyone involved in property litigation or studying Islamic judicial procedure.

Guided Path

Step 1: Verify the pre-emptor's ownership
The judge first asks the defendant (buyer) whether he acknowledges the pre-emptor's ownership of the property that gives him the right to pre-empt. If the defendant admits, the case proceeds to the next stage. If not, the judge requires the pre-emptor to produce evidence of ownership.
This is the first of two sequential phases.
2
Step 2: Evidence and oath for ownership
If the pre-emptor cannot produce evidence (two male witnesses or equivalent), the judge asks the buyer to take an oath that he does not know the pre-emptor to be the owner. If the buyer refuses the oath, the pre-emptor's ownership is considered established.
The hadith 'al-bayyinatu 'alā man idda'ā' governs this step.
3

If the pre-emptor's ownership is established (by admission, evidence, or buyer's refusal to swear), the judge then asks the buyer whether he actually purchased the property.
The second phase begins.
4
Step 4: Evidence and oath for the sale
If the buyer denies the purchase, the judge asks the pre-emptor to produce evidence of the sale. If the pre-emptor cannot, the buyer is asked to take an oath that he did not purchase the property.
If the buyer refuses this oath, the pre-emptor's claim is confirmed and the judge grants shuf'ah.

Commentary
This passage explains the nature of sulh that occurs without admission, whether the defendant remains silent (sukut) or denies (inkar). In such cases, the defendant has not admitted the plaintiff's right, so if the case were to go to court, the defendant would be required to take an oath (yamin) denying the claim. By agreeing to a settlement, the defendant avoids taking that oath and ends the dispute. Therefore, the settlement amount paid (e.g., a cow) is considered a 'ransom' (fidyah) for the oath, not a payment of an admitted debt. For the plaintiff, accepting the settlement is akin to receiving compensation ('iwad) for his claim, even though the defendant has not admitted it. This highlights that the legal nature of sulh differs based on whether there is admission. In the absence of admission, the defendant does not become liable for the original claim; rather, he sacrifices some property to buy peace. The author implicitly notes that this type of sulh is also valid, as established earlier.

Key Terms
فدیہ (fidyah): A ransom; here, the property paid by the defendant to avoid the obligation of taking an oath.
قسم (yamin): Oath; in this context, the defendant's oath denying the claim.
معاوضہ (muʿāwaḍah): Compensation; the plaintiff receives the settlement property as substitute for his claim.
