Confession of a Horse in a Stable: Only the Movable Contents Bind
Ruling on confession of movable in immovable container

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when someone confesses to a movable item inside an immovable container, and apply the principle that the container is not included.

Core Idea
In a confession, immovable containers are not transferred; only the movable contents bind.

When someone confesses to a horse 'in a stable,' does he confess to both? The Hanafi school rules that only the horse is binding because the stable is immovable and cannot be transferred. This lesson unpacks the legal principle behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the confession components
The confession mentions two items: the horse (movable) and the stable (immovable). The horse is the content (mazruf), the stable is the container (zarf).
In this case, the container is a fixed structure.
2
Determine the nature of each item
The horse is movable (manqul), the stable is immovable (ghayr manqul). The container cannot be transferred independently.
If the container were also movable (e.g., a cart), the ruling would differ.
3
Apply the principle of separability
Since the container remains in place and is not part of the transfer, the confession only binds the movable contents. This is based on the maxim: 'when the container and contents are separable, only the contents are binding.'
The container is not included because it was not actually taken or transferred.
4
State the ruling
The confessor must return only the horse. The stable is not affected by the confession.
This ruling is consistent with the Hanafi position and is not disputed by other schools.

Confession of a Movable Container and Its Contents
Ruling on confession of movable container and contents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when someone confesses to usurping a movable container with its contents and explain the underlying principle.

Core Idea
Movable container and contents taken together are both binding in confession.

In Hanafi law, when someone confesses to taking a movable item (contents) that is inside another movable item (container), the confession covers both. This lesson examines the classic example: 'I usurped a cloth in a handkerchief.' The key principle is that container and contents form a single unit when transferred together, and the confessor must return both.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Confession
Read the statement: 'I usurped a cloth in a handkerchief.' Note the specific wording that links the cloth (contents) to the handkerchief (container).
The preposition 'في' (in) indicates the contents are inside the container.
2
Recognize the Hanafi Principle
The Hanafi school applies a general rule: if someone confesses to taking a movable container and its movable contents together, both are binding. This is derived from earlier cases like 'dates in a basket.'
The principle applies only to movable items; immovable property or separate confessions have different rulings.
3
Apply the Principle
Here, the handkerchief is a movable container and the cloth is its movable contents. Since the person said 'in a handkerchief,' both were taken as a unit. Therefore, the confession includes both items.
4
State the Ruling
The ruling is that the confessor is bound to return both the cloth and the handkerchief. He cannot return only one and claim the other was implied.
The Arabic 'لزماه جميعا' means 'both are binding.'

A confession to 'a cloth in ten cloths' binds one or eleven cloths depending on custom.

The dispute centres on whether the ten cloths can realistically serve as a container for one cloth. Custom ('urf) plays a decisive role: if custom regards the ten as a valid container, both are binding; otherwise only the one cloth is binding.

2

Abu Yusuf holds only one cloth is binding; Muhammad holds eleven cloths are binding (ten plus one).
3
Understand the reasoning based on custom
Abu Yusuf argues it is not customary to wrap one cloth in ten, so the ten are not a container. Muhammad argues that valuable cloths are often wrapped in multiple layers, so the ten can be a container.
The key difference is their assessment of 'urf.
4
Notice the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is the number of cloths the confessor must pay. This affects court judgments based on the custom of the trade.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a confessor produces a defective garment and explain why the term 'thawb' encompasses defective items.

Core Idea
A confession to usurping a 'garment' is fulfilled by a defective one, for the term is general and the defendant's oath stands.

When someone confesses to usurping a garment, does he have to return a sound one? The Hanafi school rules that if he brings a defective garment and swears it is the same, his word is accepted. This rests on the generality of the word 'thawb' and the evidentiary principle that the defendant's oath suffices when the plaintiff has no proof. This lesson unpacks the logic and its legal basis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Read the passage: a person confesses to usurping a garment, then produces a defective one, claiming it is the same. The Hanafi ruling: his word is accepted with oath.
The confessor is treated as a defendant (muddai 'alayh) regarding the identity of the garment.
2
Locate the linguistic principle
The passage states that 'thawb' is general ('amm), including both sound and defective garments. Therefore, admitting to a 'thawb' does not specify soundness.
General terms in Hanafi usul are binding until specified (takhsis).
3
Apply the evidentiary rule
Since the plaintiff has no bayyinah (evidence) that the garment should be sound, the rule 'al-yamin 'ala man ankara' applies: the defendant's oath is accepted.
The hadith 'al-bayyinah 'ala al-muddai wa al-yamin 'ala man ankara' is the proof.
4
Consider the opposition
Some schools might argue that a confession implies a sound garment unless otherwise stated. The Hanafi response: the general term suffices, and additionally the oath resolves the dispute.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate; the Hanafi position is mufta bihi.

The Ghayah Rule in Iqrar: Inclusion or Exclusion of the Endpoint
Intra-Hanafi dispute on endpoint inclusion in numerical iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on ghayah (endpoint) exclusion in numerical confessions (iqrar) and identify the practical difference between Abu Hanifa's position and that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, when confessing a range 'from X to Y,' the endpoint Y is excluded unless there is evidence of inclusion.

This lesson examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute over the interpretation of numerical ranges in legal confessions (iqrar). When someone says 'I owe from one to ten dirhams,' does he owe nine or ten? The answer depends on a subtle linguistic principle: whether the final number (ghayah) is included within the range. Imam Abu Hanifa held that the ghayah is excluded by default, a position that has become the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view in the school. The Sahibayn disagreed, arguing for inclusion. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply linguistic usul principles to concrete fiqh cases.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the dispute
Identify the confession scenario: 'I owe from one dirham to ten dirhams.' Then note the two positions: Abu Hanifa says nine dirhams owed; Sahibayn say ten dirhams owed.
The amount owed is the point of dispute.
2
Identify the key term: ghayah
The dispute revolves around the term ghayah (endpoint). Abu Hanifa treats it as excluded; Sahibayn treat it as included. Understand that ibtida' (beginning) is included by both parties.
Ghayah is the final number in the range.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's reasoning (talil)
Abu Hanifa analogizes to common speech: when someone says 'my age is from fifty to sixty,' sixty is not included. The intermediate numbers are included, but the endpoint is not. This is a linguistic custom (urf) that informs the legal ruling.
He applies the usul principle that al-ghayah la tadkhulu fi al-madhkoor (the endpoint is not included in the mentioned range).
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's reasoning and the thamarah al-khilaf
The Sahibayn consider the endpoint included, possibly based on the inclusive potential of the particle 'ila' (to) in legal contexts. The practical consequence (thamarah) is a difference of one dirham. Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., Al-Marghinani) adopted Abu Hanifa's view as mufta bihi.
The tarjih goes to Abu Hanifa because his view aligns with customary usage.

Conditional Confession: Iqrar bi-Shart for a Specified Slave
Ruling on conditional confession linked to delivery of a specified slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule on conditional confessions (iqrar bi-shart) and apply it to cases where a debtor ties a debt to delivery of a specified item.

Core Idea
A confession conditioned on an act (e.g., delivery) is not a retraction; the obligation arises only upon fulfillment of the condition.

In Hanafi fiqh, a confession (iqrar) that is linked to a condition — such as delivery of a specific item — does not create an immediate debt. The creditor must fulfill that condition to enforce the confession. This lesson explains that rule through the classic example of a slave purchased but not yet received, and clarifies why it is not considered a retraction of the confession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the confession and its condition
Locate the debtor's statement: he admits owing a thousand dirhams 'as the price of a slave I bought from him and did not take possession of.' The condition is that the slave has not been delivered.
The confession explicitly links the debt to the price of a specific undelivered slave.
2
Apply the rule for conditional confessions
The Hanafi rule: 'When a confession is made with a condition, the confession is executed upon fulfillment of the condition.' Here, the creditor must deliver the specified slave to become entitled to the thousand dirhams.
The condition is delivery; without it, the confession does not create a present obligation.
3
Contrast with unconditional confession
If the confession were unconditional (e.g., 'I owe a thousand dirhams'), the debtor could be compelled to pay immediately. In a conditional confession, the debtor cannot be forced to pay until the creditor fulfills the condition.
This protects the debtor from double liability: he already owes the slave to the creditor, so the price is contingent on receipt.
4
Determine the creditor's option
The court tells the creditor: 'If you wish, deliver the slave and take the thousand; otherwise, you have no claim against him.' The creditor has the power to activate the debt by delivering the slave.
The creditor is not compelled to deliver; he can choose to forfeit the debt entirely.
5
Derive the underlying principle
The ruling is built on the principle that an iqrar bi-shart is valid and suspends the obligation until the condition occurs. This is not a retraction of the confession but a conditional obligation.
This principle is widely accepted in the Hanafi school and is applied in various contractual contexts.

Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the phrase 'price of a slave' inherently implies a condition of exchange, even without specification. Thus, the thousand is only due when a slave is delivered. The confession is conditional.

The dispute has real impact on when the debt becomes enforceable.

The relied-upon position in the Hanafi school is that of Abu Hanifa. Al-Marghinani in Al-Hidayah prefers his view, as the lack of specification voids the sale, making the confession unconditional.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Iqrār for Price of Goods: Genuine vs. Counterfeit Dirhams
Intra-Hanafi dispute on iqrār for price of goods when quality is disputed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on iqrār for price of goods when quality is disputed and apply the ruling based on timing of specification.

Core Idea
In iqrār, the quality of dirhams owed depends on custom and whether the specification was immediate.

This lesson examines a common scenario: a person admits owing a debt for goods but claims the dirhams were counterfeit, while the creditor insists they were genuine. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement on whether the muqirr's claim is a valid clarification or a retraction. Understanding this dispute requires analyzing the role of custom ('urf) and the linguistic scope of the word 'dirham'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A muqirr says 'I owe you a thousand dirhams as the price of goods, and they are counterfeit.' The muqarr lahu says 'They are genuine.' No clear proof exists. Determine the ruling on what quality of dirhams is owed.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that the muqirr must pay genuine dirhams. His proof: custom ('urf) dictates that the price of goods is normally paid in genuine dirhams; therefore, the claim of counterfeit is a retraction, which is not accepted in iqrār.
The ruling is based on the default quality according to 'urf.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the word 'dirham' is general and includes both genuine and counterfeit. Thus, specifying counterfeit is a permissible clarification (takhsis) of the general term, not a retraction.
They add a condition: the specification must be uttered immediately (mawsulan).
4
Analyze the condition of connection
If the muqirr says 'counterfeit' connected with the iqrār, the Sahibayn accept it; if said later (mafsulan), it is considered a retraction and genuine dirhams are due.
The difference in timing changes the legal effect.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
Practical consequence: Under Abu Hanifa, genuine dirhams are always owed. Under Sahibayn, counterfeit dirhams are owed if specification was immediate; otherwise genuine. The relied-upon view in the school is often the Sahibayn's, but the dispute is recognized.
The issue is built on the linguistic principle: does 'dirham' include both types?

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition that makes an acknowledgment to a fetus valid and apply it to distinguish valid from invalid acknowledgments.

Core Idea
Acknowledgment to a fetus is valid only if the source is clarified as bequest or inheritance.

Imagine someone says, 'I owe this unborn child a thousand dirhams.' Is that statement legally binding? At first glance, it seems impossible because a fetus cannot trade or lend. But the Hanafi school shows that if the acknowledger clarifies that the debt comes from a bequest or inheritance, the acknowledgment becomes valid. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the proof from hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default interpretation
When someone says 'the fetus has a debt upon me', the apparent (mutabadir) meaning is that the fetus engaged in trade or lending. But a fetus cannot transact, so this interpretation makes the acknowledgment invalid.
The default ruling is invalidity due to impossibility.
2
Recognize the valid exceptions
The acknowledger can clarify that the debt is due to a bequest (وصية) to the fetus or inheritance (ميراث) from a deceased relative. These are possible because a fetus can own via inheritance or will.
Clarification removes the ambiguity.
3
Apply the condition of clarification
Without explicit clarification, the default impossible meaning is taken and the acknowledgment is void. With clarification, it becomes valid (sahih).
The burden is on the acknowledger to explain.
4
Examine the proof (dalil)
The passage cites a hadith about Safiyyah visiting the Prophet during i'tikaf. The incomplete hadith supports the principle that ambiguity requires clarification for validity.
Although the hadith is incomplete, the principle is derived from the general sunnah of clarifying ambiguous statements.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Acknowledgment of an object automatically includes its basic components.

When someone acknowledges ownership of an object for another person, what exactly is included? This lesson addresses that question through the example of a howdah (a covered litter). The Hanafi position is clear: the acknowledgment covers all basic components indispensable to the object's existence and use.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the acknowledgment (iqrar) of a howdah (حجلة). The ruling is that the acknowledgment includes both the wood (frame) and the covering (cloth).
The ruling is explicit: 'فله العيدان والكسوة' (for him are the wood and the covering).
2
Analyze the object's composition
The howdah is a composite object; it is not complete without both wood and cloth. The commentary states it cannot be made from wood alone nor cloth alone.
This establishes both as basic components (أجزاء أصلية).
3
Apply the principle of basic components
Basic components are automatically included in the acknowledgment of the whole object. The underlying principle is that what is essential for the object's existence follows the object's ruling.
The earlier hadith about body parts reinforces this: basic components and perpetually attached accessories are included.
4
Distinguish basic from accessory components
Not every part is automatically included. Only components that are fundamental to the object's identity are covered. Accessories or separable parts may require separate acknowledgment.
The text contrasts with items like the sheath (جفن) and strap (حمائل) for a sword, which are accessories.

Confessed debts in death illness are valid but subordinate to pre-existing debts from health or known causes.

When a person is in their death illness and confesses a debt, Hanafi fiqh balances the validity of the confession with protecting legitimate creditors. Iqrar of Paternity During Terminal Illness Supersedes Prior Iqrar
Ruling on iqrar during terminal illness with later paternity claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a later claim of paternity voids a prior iqrar for a stranger made during terminal illness, and identify the two conditions for establishing lineage.

Core Idea
A later claim of paternity retroactively makes the person an heir, voiding any prior iqrar.

This lesson tackles a specific scenario in Hanafi inheritance law. A person on their deathbed acknowledges a debt to a stranger, then later announces that the stranger is actually their son. The Hanafi school gives priority to establishing lineage, which retroactively changes the stranger's status to heir, thereby voiding the initial debt acknowledgment. We will examine the ruling, its proof, and the conditions required for lineage to be established.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial act and its ruling
The initial act is an iqrar (acknowledgment of debt) for a stranger (ajnabi) made during terminal illness. Normally, such an iqrar is valid if not for the later claim.
The iqrar is made in marad al-mawt (terminal illness), which has special rulings regarding heir rights.
2
Analyze the later claim of paternity
The declarant then asserts that the stranger is his son. This claim, if it meets conditions, establishes lineage (nasab).
Lineage is a foundational right in Shariah, given priority over financial claims.
3
Apply the retroactive effect of heirship
Once lineage is established, the child is considered an heir from birth (retroactively). Therefore, the iqrar now benefits an heir.
An established rule: iqrar for an heir during terminal illness is invalid (batil).
4
Conclude the outcome
The iqrar becomes void, and the child inherits as a son. The debt acknowledgment is nullified.
This protects the interests of other heirs and upholds the sanctity of lineage.
5
Check the conditions for establishing lineage
Two conditions: (1) the stranger must not already have a known lineage; (2) it must be biologically possible for the declarant to father such a child.
These conditions ensure the claim is plausible and not merely a device to circumvent inheritance rules.

Guided Path
1

Shuf'ah requires three sequential demands: (1) demand of notification (talab al-muwāsa'ah) upon first learning of the sale; (2) demand of establishment (talab al-ishhād) by obtaining witnesses to confirm the right; and (3) judicial demand (talab al-khusūmah) before a judge. His proof: the right is already established; delay is not equivalent to abandonment.
4

Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani argues that delaying more than one month without a valid excuse after testimony invalidates the shuf'ah. He sets 'one month' as the threshold between 'near period' and 'distant period', after which the buyer is harmed by uncertainty.
Muhammad's proof: to protect the buyer from indefinite waiting, a limit is necessary by analogy to other legal deadlines.

Although the text presents Abu Hanifa's view first, later Hanafi fatwa literature (such as al-Hidayah and Radd al-Muhtār) relies on Muhammad's position. The operative ruling is that the shuf'ah holder must seek judicial demand within one month of testimony; otherwise the right lapses.

(10) And when one owns real estate with a consideration that is property, pre-emption is established. When one owns property with a consideration that is property, pre-emption is established. The basic rule is that the buyer takes land in exchange for something that the pre-emptor can also give; for example, buying land in exchange for dirhams, dinars, rice, or wheat which the pre-emptor can also give. But if the buyer takes it in exchange for something that the pre-emptor cannot give, then how can pre-emption occur? For example, giving land as dower to a wife, then the wife's taking the land is in exchange for the vulva (bud'a), and the pre-emptor cannot give the vulva, so in such a case the pre-emptor has no right of pre-emption.

Commentary
Ruling (10) conditions pre-emption on the consideration being property (mal), i.e., something fungible that the pre-emptor can also offer. If the buyer acquires the property in exchange for a non-proprietary consideration like sexual relations (bud'a) in a dower, then pre-emption does not apply because the pre-emptor cannot offer the same consideration.

Key Takeaway
