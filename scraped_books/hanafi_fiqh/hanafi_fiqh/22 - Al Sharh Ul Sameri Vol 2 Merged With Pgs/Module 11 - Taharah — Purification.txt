Signs and Age of Puberty for Boys in Hanafi Fiqh
States signs of puberty for boys and the default age according to Abu Haneefah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three signs of male puberty according to the Hanafi school and apply the default age rule when signs are absent.

Core Idea
Puberty is established by physical signs; if absent, a boy is deemed pubertal at eighteen — the Hanafi default age.

This lesson examines the classical Hanafi ruling on when a male child reaches puberty (bulugh). The text from al-Sharh al-Sameri lists three definitive signs: nocturnal emission, ejaculation during intercourse, and causing pregnancy. In the absence of these signs, Imam Abu Hanifah set the maximum age at eighteen years. Understanding this ruling is essential for determining when religious obligations (taklif) become binding on a boy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three physical signs
The passage explicitly states that a boy reaches puberty through ihtilam (nocturnal emission), inzal (ejaculation), and ihbal (causing pregnancy). These indicate full reproductive capacity.
All three are equivalent in effect; any one of them establishes bulugh.
2
Understand the default age
If none of the signs occur, the boy is considered pubertal when he completes eighteen years of age. This is the position of Imam Abu Hanifah.
The age is a ruling of necessity to avoid indefinite postponement of obligations.
3
Examine the proofs
The hadith of Ali (no orphanhood after ihtilam) proves that ihtilam marks the end of childhood. The athar of Abu Hurairah states that the pen is lifted from a boy until he experiences ihtilam, and if not, until he is eighteen.
These ahādīth are used as evidence for both the signs and the maximum age.
4
Note the Hanafi position vs other schools
The Shafi'i school sets the age at 15 years for both genders, citing other evidence. The Hanafi school relies on the eighteen-year limit from the athar and the principle that minimal signs may not manifest.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within fiqh al-ikhilaf.

Puberty of the Female: Signs and Default Age
States signs of female puberty and Hanafi default age with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three signs of female puberty according to the Hanafi school and apply the default age of 17 when signs are absent.

Core Idea
For girls, puberty is established by menstruation, nocturnal emission, or pregnancy; otherwise age 17 completes it.

Puberty (bulugh) is the threshold for religious obligations (taklif). This passage specifies the biological signs that indicate a girl has reached adulthood, and provides a default age for cases where no signs appear. Understanding these rules is essential for determining when a girl becomes obligated to pray, fast, and observe other Islamic duties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three signs of female puberty
Read the passage to list the signs: hayd (menstruation), ihtilam (nocturnal emission), and hamil (pregnancy). Note that pregnancy presupposes prior menstruation.
These are the same signs for males except hamil.
2
Examine the proof for menstruation
The hadith of Aishah: 'Allah does not accept the prayer of a menstruating woman except with a head covering.' The word 'ha'id' (menstruating) is interpreted as an adult woman, showing that menstruation indicates adulthood.
This is a dalil naqli from ahad hadith.
3
Examine the proof for nocturnal emission and default age
The passage refers to an earlier hadith (of Ali) that there is no orphanhood after ihtilam. For the default age, the text states 17 years for girls, which is based on specific narrations.
The default age for boys is 18; the distinction is based on different hadith evidence.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Consider a 16-year-old girl with no signs. She is not yet balighah because she has not reached 17 and no signs appeared. If she menstruates at 16, she becomes balighah immediately.
Age is only a default when signs are absent.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school sets the default age at 15 for both genders. The Hanafi school distinguishes between genders based on different hadith evidence.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the Hanafi position is mufta bihi within the school.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three biological signs of bulugh for a girl according to Imam Abu Hanifa and explain how the hadith about the menstruating woman serves as evidence.

Core Idea
In the absence of biological signs, a girl reaches majority at seventeen years (Abu Hanifa).

This lesson examines the signs of puberty (bulugh) for a girl according to Imam Abu Hanifa. The passage presents three physical indicators and a default age. It also cites a hadith that links menstruation to legal obligation (prayer with covering). Understanding these signs is essential for determining when a girl becomes legally responsible (mukallaf).

Guided Path
1
Identify the three signs of bulugh
The passage lists menstruation (hayd), nocturnal emission (ihtilam), and pregnancy (hubl) as signs of puberty for a girl.
These signs indicate that a girl has reached reproductive maturity.
2
Determine the default age
If none of these signs appear, according to Imam Abu Hanifa, a girl becomes baligh when she completes seventeen years.
This age is specific to girls; boys have a different default age (not covered here).
3
Analyze the hadith
The hadith 'Allah does not accept the prayer of a menstruating woman without a head covering' is cited as evidence. The word 'ha'id' (menstruating woman) is used as a descriptor for an adult woman, implying that menstruation is a sign of adulthood.
This is an example of using the term's implication (dalala) to derive a ruling.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) hold that majority is reached at fifteen years for both boys and girls, regardless of signs.
This difference affects the default age when no signs appear.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position for fatwa is the view of the Sahibayn (fifteen years), but this lesson focuses on Abu Hanifa's personal view.
In practice, the fifteen-year rule is generally applied in the Hanafi school.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Age of Majority at Fifteen Years
Intra-Hanafi dispute on age of majority with hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the age of majority, cite the hadith evidence, and compare it with Abu Hanifa’s view.

Core Idea
In the absence of signs, puberty is fixed at fifteen years for both sexes according to the Sahibayn, based on the Prophet's practice.

This lesson presents the view of Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn) on the age of majority, an intra-Hanafi dispute with significant practical consequences. While Abu Hanifa set different ages (18 for boys, 17 for girls) in the absence of signs, the Sahibayn relied on a stronger hadith establishing fifteen years as the universal age. We will examine their proof and understand why later Hanafi scholarship preferred their position.

Guided Path
1
State the positions of both sides
Identify Abu Hanifa’s view (18/17) and the Sahibayn's view (15) regarding the age of majority when no signs appear.
2
Examine the Sahibayn's hadith evidence
Read the hadith of Ibn Umar carefully: the Prophet did not permit him to fight at fourteen but permitted him at fifteen. This directly indicates fifteen as the age of maturity for military service, a proxy for general majority.
The hadith is narrated by Nafi and was used by Caliph Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz as the legal standard.
3
Analyze the chain of transmission and authority
The hadith is a sahih ahad narration. Note that Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz, a recognized Caliph and mujtahid, implemented it as state policy, giving it additional weight.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa's view, a boy remains a minor until 18 if no signs, delaying his capacity for business, marriage, and criminal responsibility. Under Sahibayn's view, he becomes major at 15, with full legal capacity.
This has practical implications for contracts, testimony, and religious obligations like prayer and fasting.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., in Al-Hidayah and Al-Durr al-Mukhtar) preferred the Sahibayn's view because their hadith is more authentic and direct. Thus, the relied-upon position is that majority is reached at fifteen years in the absence of signs.

Acceptance of a Murahiq's Claim of Puberty
Ruling that the word of a minor close to puberty is accepted on his claim of majority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a murahiq's claim of puberty and apply it to a scenario where the puberty of a minor close to age is questioned.

Core Idea
A minor close to puberty who claims majority is believed — their word is law unless proven otherwise.

When a child is on the verge of puberty, it may be unclear whether they have actually reached it. The Hanafi school provides a clear procedural rule: the child's own statement is accepted. This lesson examines this ruling from the matn of al-Quduri and explains its basis in the principle of trustworthiness regarding one's own bodily state.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
Recognise that the rule applies to a boy or girl who is murahiq, i.e., close to the typical age of puberty.
Murahiq is defined as approaching puberty, not a child far below.
2
Determine the condition of applicability
The rule applies only when the matter of puberty is ambiguous — i.e., there are no clear external signs or the signs are unclear.
If puberty is clearly established by signs (e.g., menstruation, wet dream), the rule does not apply because there is no ambiguity.
3
Extract the operative ruling
The matn states 'fa al-qawlu qawlahuma' — their word is accepted. This means the statement of the child is taken as true in the absence of contrary evidence.
This is a procedural presumption in favor of the child's claim.
4
Note the legal consequence
Once the claim is accepted, the child is treated as an adult for all legal purposes: they become obligated for prayers, eligible for marriage, and liable for contracts.
This is the effect of the ruling.
5
Recall the supporting principle
The underlying reasoning is analogous to accepting a woman's statement about her own iddah or menstruation — a person is the best witness to their own condition.
This is based on the principle of 'al-amr al-mustatir' (matters that are concealed).

Definition and Permissibility of 'Ariyah
Definition of 'ariyah and its proof from Quran and Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'ariyah and cite the primary proofs (Quran and hadith) for its permissibility.

Core Idea
'Ariyah is ownership of usufruct without compensation; its permissibility is proven by Quran and the Prophet's practice.

This lesson introduces the concept of 'ariyah (borrowing) as defined by the Hanafi school. You will learn its precise legal definition—tamlik al-manafi' bi-ghayr 'iwad—and examine the evidence that establishes it as a permissible and commendable contract. The Quranic reference to 'al-ma'un' and the hadith of the Prophet's borrowing a horse provide the textual basis.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read the statement: 'al-ariyah ja'izah wa hiya tamlik al-manafi' bi-ghayr 'iwad'. Break down: tamlik (ownership), al-manafi' (usufruct), bi-ghayr 'iwad (without compensation). Note that ownership is of benefits, not the physical object.
The term 'tamlik' distinguishes 'ariyah from mere permission (ibahah).
2
Identify the Quranic proof
Locate the verse 'wa yamna'oon al-ma'un' (Surah al-Ma'un, 107:7). The word 'al-ma'un' is interpreted by the Companions as borrowed items like a bucket or pot. The verse condemns withholding such small favors, implying that giving them is commendable.
This is a primary textual proof for the permissibility of 'ariyah.
3
Examine the hadith proof
Read the hadith where the Prophet borrowed a horse from Abu Talhah during a panic and returned it. The Prophet's action confirms that borrowing is permissible. Note the terminology: musta'ir (borrower), mu'ir (lender).
The hadith also demonstrates the practice of returning the borrowed item after use.
4
Recognize the supporting athar
Consider the statement of 'Abdullah ibn Mas'ud that 'al-ma'un' during the Prophet's time referred to borrowing a bucket and a pot. This companion interpretation reinforces the Quranic proof.
This athar provides context for the Quranic verse.

Commentary
This passage begins the Book of 'Ariyah (Borrowing) by defining it. It states that 'ariyah is a permissible contract (ja'iz) and defines it as 'tamlik al-manafi' bi-ghayr 'iwad' — the transfer of ownership of usufruct without any compensation. The commentary then provides the evidence: a Quranic verse (Surah al-Ma'un, where 'al-ma'un' is interpreted as borrowed items) and a hadith where the Prophet borrowed a horse from Abu Talha. The Quranic proof is from the description of disbelievers who withhold even small favors like borrowing utensils. The hadith from Anas shows the Prophet's practice of borrowing a horse and returning it, confirming permissibility. The second proof is an athar from 'Abdullah ibn Mas'ud that during the Prophet's time, 'al-ma'un' was understood to refer to the borrowing of a bucket and a pot — common household items. Thus, the definition is supported by both revelation and practice.

تمليك المنافع (tamlik al-manafi'): Transfer of ownership of the benefits or usufruct of an asset, not the asset itself.
الماعون (al-ma'un): Common household items like bucket and pot that are lent for use; interpreted as borrowed items in the context of the Quranic verse.
