Hanafi Ruling on Waqf Ownership Transfer
States Hanafi ruling on waqf ownership with Quran and Hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on waqf ownership transfer and identify the two conditions that cause the waqif's ownership to cease.

Core Idea
Waqf ownership remains with the donor until a judge confirms it or the donor ties it to death.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational Hanafi position on waqf (endowment). While waqf is permissible and valid, the ownership of the donated property does not automatically leave the waqif's hands. We will explore the primary proofs from Quran and Sunnah and learn the precise conditions under which ownership transfers according to Imam Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Define waqf linguistically and legally
Understand the term 'waqf' as 'to withhold' and its legal meaning: dedicating usufruct while retaining the corpus.
2
Identify the Quranic and Hadith proofs
Locate the Quranic verse (5:106) as an 'ishara' (indication) and the Hadith of 'Umar (Bukhari, Muslim) as the direct evidence for waqf and its conditions.
The Hadith explicitly states the property cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited.
3
State the Hanafi ruling on ownership
According to Abu Hanifa, the waqif's ownership remains unless a judge (qadi) gives a verdict or the waqif suspends the waqf upon death.
This is unique to the Hanafi school; others differ.
4
Analyze the reasoning and practical effect
Understand why death or judge's verdict terminates ownership: death ends all ownership; a judge's ruling has binding legal effect.
Without these, the waqif can still revoke the waqf or sell the property (though sinful).

Book of Waqf. The meaning of waqf is to withhold. In waqf, land and property are withheld for the poor, therefore it is called withholding. The indication of waqf is in the verse of Allah: 'O you who believe, when death approaches one of you, at the time of bequest, [let there be] two just witnesses from among you...' (Surah al-Ma'idah 5:106). In this verse, there is an indication to making a will, and it also includes waqf, so this verse points to waqf. And in the hadith: from Ibn 'Umar that 'Umar ibn al-Khattab acquired land in Khaybar, so he came to the Prophet (SAW) seeking his advice about it. He said, 'O Messenger of Allah, I have acquired land in Khaybar; I have never acquired any property more precious to me than it. So what do you command me to do with it?' He said, 'If you wish, you may keep the original (land) and give it in charity.' He said, 'So 'Umar gave it in charity with the condition that it should not be sold, nor given as a gift, nor inherited, and he gave its proceeds in charity to the poor, the relatives, to freeing slaves, in the path of Allah, to the wayfarer, and to the guest. There is no sin on the one who administers it to eat from it in a reasonable manner and feed others without accumulating wealth.' (Bukhari, Book of Conditions, Chapter on Conditions in Waqf, and Muslim) From this hadith, the permissibility of waqf is established, and it also establishes the conditions with which waqf can be made. [1530] (1) The ownership of the waqif over the waqf does not cease according to Abu Hanifa (may Allah have mercy on him) unless the ruler gives a verdict regarding it, or he suspends it upon his death by saying, 'When I die, I have made my house a waqf for so-and-so.' Making a waqf is permissible according to Imam Abu Hanifa, and by making a waqf it becomes a waqf. However, since it is property, the ownership of the waqif will cease only when the ruler gives a verdict on the waqf, or he suspends the waqf upon death, saying, 'If I die, my house is a waqf for so-and-so.' Upon death, a person's ownership of all things ceases, so the waqif's ownership ceased upon death. Now since he has given it for the benefit of the mawquf 'alayh (beneficiary), the mawquf 'alayh becomes the owner instead of the heirs. (2) Similarly, by the verdict of a judge, anyone's ownership ceases. So ownership of the waqf property will also cease. (3) In this hadith there is an indication of this: from Sa'd ibn 'Ubadah, brother of Banu Sa'idah, his mother died while he was absent. He came to the Prophet (SAW) and said, 'O Messenger of Allah, my mother has died while I was absent. Will anything benefit her if I give charity on her behalf?' He said, 'O Messenger of Allah, I have acquired land in Khaybar; I have never acquired such good land. What do you command?' He said, 'If you wish, keep the original and give its proceeds in charity.' He said, 'So 'Umar gave it in charity in such a way that it would not be sold, not given as a gift, and not be inherited. And its proceeds are to be spent on the poor, relatives, freeing slaves, in the path of Allah, for the wayfarer, and for guests. There is no sin on the one who administers it to eat from it in a reasonable manner and to feed others without becoming wealthy.'

Commentary
This passage opens the Book of Waqf in Al-Sharh al-Thamiri. It begins by defining waqf linguistically as 'to withhold' and states that the legal validation of waqf is derived from both the Quran and the Sunnah. The author cites Quran 5:106, which discusses testimony at the time of death and bequest, as an indirect indication (ishara) for waqf. The primary proof, however, is the well-known hadith of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab regarding his land in Khaybar, which the Prophet (SAW) instructed him to dedicate as a sadaqah while retaining the original property—this is the precedent for waqf. The hadith also lists the conditions of a valid waqf: that it cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited, and its proceeds are to be spent on charitable causes. The core ruling presented is the Hanafi position on ownership of the waqf property. According to Abu Hanifa, the ownership of the waqif does not immediately transfer to the beneficiary or to Allah. Instead, it remains with the waqif until one of two events occurs: (1) a judge (qadi) issues a verdict confirming the waqf, or (2) the waqif suspends the waqf upon his death (i.e., makes a will-based waqf). The reasoning is that waqf, being a transfer of ownership of the usufruct while retaining the corpus, does not fully remove the donor's ownership until the legal process is completed. The commentary elaborates that upon death, ownership naturally ends, so a death-contingent waqf takes effect automatically. Similarly, a judge's verdict can terminate ownership. The passage also briefly mentions the hadith of Sa'd ibn 'Ubadah concerning charity on behalf of the deceased as an indicator of the permissibility of such acts, though it is not directly about waqf. This lesson focuses on the foundational Hanafi ruling that waqf is valid but the ownership transfer is conditional—a unique stance that distinguishes the Hanafi school from others who consider ownership to transfer immediately upon declaration.

Key Terms
وقف (waqf): Linguistically means to withhold or detain. Legally, it means to dedicate the usufruct of a property for charitable purposes while retaining the corpus, with the ownership remaining with the donor until certain conditions are met according to Abu Hanifa.
واقف (waqif): The person who establishes the waqf.
موقوف عليه (mawquf 'alayh): The beneficiary of the waqf.

Definition and Evidence for Waqf
Introduces the chapter on Waqf with definition and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define waqf according to the Hanafi school, identify the Quranic indication and the hadith of Umar as primary evidence, and state the key conditions of a valid waqf.

Core Idea
Waqf is retaining the corpus and giving its usufruct in charity perpetually, validated by the hadith of Umar's land endowment.

This passage opens the chapter on waqf (endowment) with a clear definition and the foundational evidence from Quran and Sunnah. The author first explains the linguistic and technical meanings, then provides two proofs: a Quranic verse that indicates charitable dispositions and the well-known hadith of Umar ibn al-Khattab that directly establishes the ruling and conditions. Understanding this origin is essential for grasping all later waqf rules and their application.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read the linguistic and technical definitions. Linguistically, waqf means 'to prevent/hold back.' Technically, it means 'dedicating the corpus of a property and giving its usufruct in charity perpetually.' Note that the corpus is 'prevented' from being sold, gifted, or inherited.
2
Identify the Quranic ishara
The author cites Quran 5:106 about witnesses at the time of bequest. This is not a direct command for waqf but an 'indication' (ishara) that charitable dispositions (like waqf) are included in the general encouragement of bequests. Understand that this verse alone is not a binding proof for waqf's validity.
The hadith is the primary proof; the Quranic verse is supportive.
3
Analyse the hadith of Umar
Read the full hadith: Umar consulted the Prophet about a piece of land in Khaybar. The Prophet said: 'If you wish, you may retain the original (ḥabs al-aṣl) and give its produce in charity.' Umar then endowed it with specific conditions. Extract the ruling: waqf is permissible and recommended.
4
Extract the key conditions
From the hadith, note the conditions: (1) The original capital is retained (ḥabs al-aṣl). (2) It cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited. (3) Its proceeds are given for charitable purposes (poor, relatives, etc.). (4) The administrator may eat from it in a reasonable manner without becoming wealthy.
These conditions form the core of Hanafi waqf law.

When Does Ownership Leave the Endower? Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Waqf Transfer
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the moment of ownership transfer in waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three Hanafi positions on when ownership of waqf property transfers from the endower and identify the evidence for each.

Core Idea
Ownership of waqf property leaves the endower only by judge's ruling, death condition, utterance, or delivery—Hanafis differ on which.

In this passage, the commentator presents the classic intra-Hanafi disagreement on the precise moment when the endower's ownership of a waqf property ceases. While all agree that waqf itself is valid, they differ on the legal effect of mere declaration. This dispute has significant practical consequences for the endower's rights and the transfer of property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
List the views of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Note the key condition each requires for ownership to leave the endower.
2
Examine the evidence for each position
Find the dalil (proof) cited for each imam: Abu Hanifa uses hadith of Sa'd and athar of Umar; Abu Yusuf uses hadith of the mosque; Muhammad relies on practice of appointment and delivery.
Identify the type of proof: hadith, athar, or qiyas.
3
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider how each position affects the endower's ability to revoke or sell the property before ownership transfers. This shows the real-world impact of the legal difference.
4
Note the tarjih
Understand which view is considered the strongest (mufta bihi) and why later jurists sometimes prefer Abu Yusuf's view for ease of practice.
The mufta bihi is often Abu Hanifa's, but context matters.

Commentary
This passage presents the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding when ownership of an endowed property is transferred away from the endower. It is part of the commentary (sharh) on the matn. The three positions are: (1) Abu Hanifa: ownership remains until either the judge issues a ruling on the waqf or the endower makes it conditional upon his death (e.g., 'When I die, my house is waqf for so-and-so'). The rationale is that the judge's ruling is binding, and death automatically ends ownership. Proof includes the hadith of Sa'd ibn 'Ubadah who made his mother's sadaqah without a judge, and the athar of Umar writing his waqf. (2) Abu Yusuf: ownership is severed immediately upon uttering the words of waqf, based on the hadith of the mosque built by the Prophet (SAW) where the owners gifted the land without a judge. (3) Muhammad al-Shaybani: ownership leaves only when a trustee (wali) is appointed and the property is delivered to him, as seen in practical administration. The commentary explains each position with its supporting evidence.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on the book of Waqf presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding when the ownership of the endowed property leaves the waqif (donor). The passage type is an intra-Hanafi dispute. The text begins by stating the position of Abu Hanifa, which is found in the base matn: the waqf becomes effective either by a judge's ruling or by the waqif suspending it upon his death (e.g., saying 'when I die, I have endowed...'). Then the two disciples differ. Abu Yusuf holds that mere pronouncement ('I have endowed') suffices for the property to leave the waqif's ownership, regardless of a judge's ruling or suspension upon death. His evidence includes the hadith of Sa'd bin Ubada, where Sa'd made the Prophet a witness to his endowing a wall garden for his mother without obtaining a judge's ruling or suspending upon death. Also, the hadith about the Prophet building the mosque: the Banu Najjar gifted their land by mere verbal consent without any judge's ruling or suspension upon death, and ownership transferred by their utterance alone. Muhammad holds that ownership does not cease until the waqif appoints a guardian (wali) for the endowment and physically hands it over to him. His evidence is the same hadith of the mosque, where the Banu Najjar made the Prophet the guardian of the land and handed it over; only then did their ownership cease. The commentary clarifies that according to Muhammad, both appointment of a guardian and delivery are necessary. The footnote provides the full contexts of the ahadith. The passage thus lays out the three views without explicitly stating which is relied upon for fatwa, but the Hanafi school generally gives preference to Abu Yusuf's view regarding the validity of waqf by mere pronouncement.

Commentary
This passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of waqf (endowment) of shared property (musha'). The discussion opens with the position of Imam Muhammad: he rules that waqf of an undivided share is not permissible unless the property is first divided. His reasoning is grounded in the requirement that the beneficiary (mawquf 'alayh) must be given possession (qabd). Possession of an undivided share is incomplete, hence division is a prerequisite. He supports this with an analogy (qiyas) to hibah (gift) and sadaqah (charity), where delivery of possession requires prior division of joint property. He also cites the hadith of Abu Talha, who divided his garden among his relatives after declaring it a waqf. However, an exception is made for indivisible properties like bathhouses and water mills, where necessity permits waqf without division. In contrast, Imam Abu Yusuf holds that waqf is completed by declaration alone; delivery of possession is not a condition for validity. Consequently, waqf of shared property is permissible without division. His evidence includes the hadith of Umar (RA) who made a joint waqf, and the incident of Banu Najjar who jointly dedicated land for the Prophet's mosque in Madinah. Both were shared properties, yet the Prophet accepted them as valid waqf. Abu Yusuf further qualifies that while shared waqf is permissible, it is not valid if the property is partly kept by the owner and partly given to the mosque or cemetery, as this leads to disrespect (hawn) of the sacred purpose. Thus, both imams agree on this restriction. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is that delivery of possession is a condition for the completion of waqf, following Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Hanifa, except in the case of a waqf for a specific institution like a mosque, which is completed by mere declaration. This passage illustrates how the Hanafi school engages in rigorous textual and analogical reasoning to derive rulings.

Commentary
This passage addresses a critical condition for the validity of waqf: perpetuity (ta'bid). The discussion centers on whether a waqf must explicitly state that its ultimate destination is perpetual, i.e., for the poor and needy indefinitely. According to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad (referred to as 'al-tarafayn' – the two sides), a waqf is only complete when the founder makes its ultimate destination perpetual and non-terminating. They require an explicit stipulation that after any named beneficiaries, the property will go to the poor forever. They base this on the practice of Umar (RA) in his famous waqf, where he explicitly stated that after his daughter Hafsah and then the 'ahl al-ra'y', the proceeds would go to the poor, and the property would never be sold or inherited. This hadith serves as their primary proof. In contrast, Imam Abu Yusuf holds a more flexible view. He argues that even if the founder names a specific group of beneficiaries whose existence will eventually terminate (e.g., a particular family line), the waqf is still valid. Upon the extinction of those beneficiaries, the property automatically passes to the poor and needy in perpetuity, even if the founder did not explicitly state this. His reasoning is that the objective of waqf is to attain closeness to Allah (qurbah), and this objective can be achieved both by immediate endowment for the poor and by endowment for specific individuals; the perpetual nature is inherent. Thus, while all three imams agree that eventually the property must go to the poor (to prevent it from reverting to the founder's heirs, which would defeat the perpetuity), they differ on whether an explicit stipulation of that ultimate perpetual destination is a condition for the initial validity. The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is that for a waqf to be binding (lazim), it must be perpetual in its ultimate destination. If the founder does not specify a perpetual destination, the waqf is still valid according to Abu Yusuf but not according to the majority. In practice, most scholars follow the view of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, requiring explicit perpetuity.

جهة منقطعة (jihah muntaqi'ah): A destination that terminates, such as a specific family line that will eventually become extinct.
قربت (qurbah): An act that brings one closer to Allah; the spiritual objective of waqf.

The Hanafi Ruling on Waqf: Immovable vs Movable Property
Ruling with proof on waqf of immovable vs movable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on waqf of immovable and movable property, explain the proof based on the definition of waqf, and apply the ruling to new cases.

Core Idea
Waqf requires the corpus to remain; immovables preserve it, movables do not, so waqf of movables becomes sadaqah.

This lesson examines the foundational Hanafi position on what can be validly endowed as waqf. Imam Abu Hanifa distinguished between immovable property (such as land and buildings), which can be perpetually preserved, and movable property (such as money and animals), which is typically consumed through use. Understanding this ruling is essential for correctly classifying endowments in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Identify the two rulings: waqf of immovable property is valid; waqf of movable property is not valid but becomes sadaqah.
2
Locate the proof
The proof is a rational argument (qiyas jaliy) based on the definition of waqf: the corpus must remain, and the poor only benefit from the usufruct.
No scriptural text is cited; it is an aqli proof.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The 'illa (effective cause) is that waqf requires perpetuity of the corpus (baqa' al-ayn). Immovables naturally satisfy this; movables are consumed or transferred, so they cannot fulfill this condition.
The consequence is that the transfer becomes sadaqah, not waqf.
4
Note the underlying principle
The classification hinges on the nature of the property: if its usage consumes the corpus, it cannot be waqf. If the corpus remains intact, it can be waqf.
5
Apply to new cases
Given the ruling, test whether a book (movable but not consumed by reading) would be waqf or sadaqah under Abu Hanifa's rule.
Later Hanafi jurists would allow books, but Abu Hanifa's literal rule still excludes them.

Waqf of Land with Movable Accessories: Abu Yusuf's Position
Abu Yusuf's ruling on endowing land with movable accessories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Abu Yusuf's ruling on including movable items in a waqf of land and explain the proof based on the principle of taba'iyyah (subordination).

Core Idea
Movable items can be endowed as accessories to land, even though they cannot be endowed independently.

Imam Abu Yusuf addresses a practical scenario: a donor wants to endow a farm along with its oxen and slave laborers. While Abu Hanifa generally prohibited endowing movables, Abu Yusuf allows it when they are accessories to immovable property. This lesson unpacks his reasoning and the analogy to the sale of water rights.

1
Identify the ruling
Locate the specific ruling of Abu Yusuf: waqf of a farm (day'ah) with its oxen and plowmen (akah) is valid (ja'iz). Note that the plowmen are owned slaves.
The ruling is stated in the first sentence.
2
Understand the principal-accessory relationship
Recognize that the land is the principal (asl) and the movables are accessories (tabi'). Abu Yusuf's key principle: something invalid independently can become valid as a subsidiary.
The text explicitly says: 'some things are not valid originally but become valid as subsidiary.'
3
Analyze the qiyas (analogy)
Trace the analogy to the right of irrigation water (haqq al-shurb): cannot be sold alone but can be sold with the land. Similarly, movables cannot be endowed alone but can be with land.
The illa (effective cause) is taba'iyyah (subordination).
4
Contrast with Abu Hanifa's position
Note the intra-madhhab dispute: Abu Hanifa would not allow movables even as accessories. Muhammad al-Shaybani may allow by custom. Understand why the later school favors Abu Yusuf.
The mufta bihi is the permissibility of including accessories.

Waqf of Movable Property: Imam Muhammad's Expansive View
Imam Muhammad's evidence-based extension of waqf to movable property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi intra-madhhab dispute on waqf of movable property, identify Imam Muhammad's evidence, and apply his view to customary endowments.

Core Idea
Imam Muhammad permits waqf of horses, weapons, and customary items based on hadith and practice.

This passage presents the view of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the third major Hanafi authority, who takes an even broader position than Abu Yusuf. He explicitly permits the waqf of horses and weapons based on direct hadith evidence and extends the ruling to any item that has become customary among Muslims to endow. Understanding this intra-Hanafi dispute is crucial for correctly applying the madhhab's rulings on movable property endowments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi split on movable waqf
Recognize that Abu Hanifa restricts waqf primarily to immovable property (except by istihsan), Abu Yusuf allows movable as dependencies, and Muhammad allows broader categories when supported by evidence or custom.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
2
Examine Imam Muhammad's primary evidence
Analyze the hadith of Khalid ibn al-Walid (armor and weapons) as direct proof. Note the word 'ihtabasa' meaning waqf. Muhammad also cites the hadith of Ibn Abbas (camel) and the practice of al-Zuhri (dinars).
The evidence is from ahad hadith and ta'amul.
3
Understand the role of 'urf and ta'amul
Imam Muhammad uses customary practice (ta'amul) as a basis for permissibility. If the community commonly endows certain items (e.g., Qurans, axes, funeral biers), that practice itself establishes validity.
Hanafis accept 'urf as a secondary source.
4
Recognize the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference in rulings affects which movable items can be validly endowed. Under Muhammad's view, items like books, tools, and vehicles can be endowed independently, while under Abu Hanifa's strict view they cannot.
Later Hanafi practice adopts Muhammad's view as mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage presents the view of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, the third major Hanafi authority, who takes an even broader position than Abu Yusuf. He explicitly permits the waqf of horses (كراع) and weapons (سلاح) based on direct hadith evidence, specifically the hadith of Khalid ibn al-Walid who endowed his armor. Muhammad also extends the ruling to any item that has become customary (ta'amul) among Muslims to endow, such as Qur'ans, axes, and funeral biers. He further supports his view with the hadith of Ibn Abbas regarding a camel endowed for Allah's path, and the practice of early Muslims like al-Zuhri allowing endowment of currency (dinars and dirhams) where the profit is given in charity. This demonstrates Muhammad's reliance on 'urf (custom) and istihsan (juristic preference) to expand the scope of waqf beyond the strict ruling of Abu Hanifa.

تعامل (ta'amul): Customary practice of the Muslim community, considered a source of law in the Hanafi school.

Invalidity of Selling or Transferring a Valid Waqf
Ruling that a valid waqf cannot be sold or transferred

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the sale and transfer of a valid waqf, cite the primary evidence from the hadith of Umar, and explain the prohibition's scope.

Core Idea
A valid waqf is inalienable: it cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational principle of waqf: once an endowment is validly constituted, the property leaves private ownership forever. The text presents the ruling and the primary hadith evidence from the Prophet's instruction to Umar. You will learn why this rule is universally accepted in the Hanafi school and how it protects the perpetual nature of charitable endowments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
The text states that when waqf is valid, its sale and transfer of ownership are not permissible. Recognise that this is a categorical prohibition (haram).
2
Locate the primary dalil
The commentary references the hadith of Umar (رضي الله عنه) where the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) said: 'لا يباع ولا يوهب ولا يورث' (It is not sold, not gifted, and not inherited). This hadith is the core evidence.
The hadith is recorded in Bukhari and Muslim.
3
Analyse the wording of the dalil
Note the three prohibitions: sale (bay'), gift (hibah), and inheritance (irth). The negative command 'la' indicates a clear prohibition. The three verbs cover all standard modes of transferring ownership.
The prohibition is explicit and leaves no room for permissibility.
4
Understand the epistemological status
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is qat'i (explicit). In Hanafi usul, an ahad hadith with clear meaning is binding in practice. This ruling is not subject to override by istihsan.
The ruling is relied upon in Zahir al-Riwayah.
5
Note the consensus
There is no intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on this point; all Hanafi authorities agree on the inalienability of a valid waqf. The ruling is muttafaq alayh across madhhabs as well.
This consensus strengthens the ruling's authority.

Inalienability of Valid Waqf Property
Establishes the ruling that valid waqf cannot be sold or transferred, with supporting hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that valid waqf property cannot be sold or transferred and identify the hadith evidence that supports this prohibition.

Core Idea
A valid waqf cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited — it is a perpetual charity.

This passage establishes a fundamental principle of waqf: once an endowment is validly constituted, the property becomes inviolable. The author provides multiple hadith proofs to demonstrate that the Prophet (PBUH) prohibited the sale or transfer of endowed assets. Understanding this ruling is essential for correctly applying waqf law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What act is being discussed? It is the sale or transfer of property that has been validly made waqf. The ruling is that it is not permissible (haram).
2
Locate the primary dalil
The primary proof is the hadith of Umar's horse from Sahih al-Bukhari (no. 2775), where the Prophet said: 'لا تبتاعها ولا ترجعن في صدقتک' (Do not buy it and do not take back your charity).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's command 'لا تبتاعها' is a direct prohibition of purchase, and 'لا ترجعن في صدقتک' prohibits taking back the charity. This shows that waqf property cannot be sold or reclaimed.
The dalil is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is qat'i (clear).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The Shafi'i school allows sale of waqf property under certain conditions (e.g., when it is no longer beneficial). The Hanafi school maintains the prohibition based on the general evidence.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi because the explicit prohibition in the hadith is general and the principle of waqf perpetuity supports it.

Waqif's Reservation of Benefit and Supervision
Ruling with proofs on waqif's reservation of benefit/supervision

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad regarding the waqif's reservation of benefit or supervision, explain their respective proofs, and identify the practical consequence of the ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
A waqif may reserve the benefit or supervision of his waqf according to Abu Yusuf, but Muhammad prohibits it.

When a person establishes a waqf, does he retain any right to benefit from it or to manage it himself? The Hanafi school has a significant internal disagreement on this question. This lesson examines the ruling and the evidence presented by two great imams: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this ikhtilaf is essential for drafting valid waqf deeds and for appreciating the diversity within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Issue
The passage discusses two specific stipulations a waqif might make: (1) reserving the income (ghullah) of the waqf for himself, and (2) reserving the supervision (wilayah) for himself. State clearly what is being disputed.
The issue is not the validity of waqf itself, but the validity of these two conditions.
2
State the Two Positions
According to Abu Yusuf, both stipulations are permissible (jā'iz). According to Muhammad, both are impermissible (ghayr jā'iz). Note that both are Hanafi imams; this is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's Evidence
Abu Yusuf relies on a hadith where the Prophet allowed the donor of a sacrificial camel to ride it, and another hadith about buying a well and using it like other Muslims. He also cites reports (athar) that many Companions managed their own endowments. Understand how these imply permissibility.
The key concept: the waqif may benefit if the property is not transferred entirely out of his ownership in a way that bars any use.
4
Analyze Muhammad's Evidence
Muhammad argues that after waqf, the property belongs to Allah, so the waqif cannot stipulate personal benefit. He cites the hadith prohibiting taking back a sadaqah (charity), equating waqf to sadaqah. He also holds that supervision must be given to another guardian.
Muhammad emphasizes the complete transfer of ownership (tamlīk) to Allah, which severs the waqif's connection.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
The practical difference is whether the waqif can retain control and benefit. Following Abu Yusuf, a waqif can stipulate to take income and be the supervisor. Following Muhammad, he must completely divest himself of both. This affects how waqf deeds are drafted.
The passage does not specify which view is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa). Later Hanafi works may give preference, but here both are presented as legitimate.

Conditions for Mosque Waqf: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf
Ruling on when a builder's ownership of a mosque ceases, with dispute between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions required by Abu Hanifa for the cessation of ownership when building a mosque and compare them with Abu Yusuf's position, and apply the ruling to analogous public utilities.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa requires physical separation and public prayer to complete a mosque waqf; Abu Yusuf deems mere declaration sufficient.

The act of building a mosque raises a key legal question: when does the builder's ownership end? In this passage, we encounter a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his distinguished student Imam Abu Yusuf. Their disagreement centers on whether a formal act of entrustment is required or whether verbal declaration alone suffices. Understanding this difference illuminates the broader Hanafi theory of waqf formation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed ruling
The act is building a mosque on one's own land. The disputed ruling is the moment when the builder's ownership ceases and the property becomes a waqf for the public.
Both imams agree that a mosque is a valid waqf, but differ on its completion.
2
State Abu Hanifa's conditions
Abu Hanifa requires three steps: (1) physically separating the property from one's ownership (e.g., by designating a door and a way), (2) granting permission for people to pray, and (3) at least one person actually praying. Only then does ownership transfer.
The reasoning is that waqf requires entrustment (taslim) to a guardian; since a mosque has no specific guardian, the act of prayer serves as acceptance.
3
State Abu Yusuf's alternative
Abu Yusuf holds that merely saying 'I have made it a mosque' (verbal declaration) is sufficient to complete the waqf and remove ownership immediately.
He considers entrustment unnecessary for mosques because they are inherently for public benefit.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the builder retains ownership until someone prays; he could theoretically revoke the declaration before prayer. Under Abu Yusuf, ownership ceases immediately upon declaration, and revocation is impossible.
This difference affects the validity of any subsequent sale or disposition by the builder.
5
Extend to analogous cases
The passage mentions similar public utilities: water fountains (saqaya), caravanserais (khan), hotels (funduq), and cemeteries. The same dispute likely applies to them, though the text does not elaborate.
In practice, the rule for each depends on whether they are treated like mosques or require additional conditions.
