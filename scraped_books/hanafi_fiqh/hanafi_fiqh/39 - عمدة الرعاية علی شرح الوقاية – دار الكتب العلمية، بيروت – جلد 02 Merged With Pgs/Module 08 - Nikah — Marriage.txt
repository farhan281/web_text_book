Intentions in Vow Formulas: Nadhr vs Yameen
Analyzes six scenarios of intention in vow utterances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a vow utterance (e.g., 'Allah 'alayya') into a binding vow, an oath, or both, based on the speaker's intention, and state the corresponding obligations (qada' and/or kaffarah).

Core Idea
The same vow formula can create a vow, an oath, or both — it all depends on the speaker's intention.

When someone says 'Allah 'alayya an asuma' (Upon me is Allah's obligation to fast), what legal effect does it have? The answer depends entirely on what the speaker intended. This passage from a Hanafi commentary unpacks six possible intentions, showing how each yields a different combination of a binding vow (nadhara) and/or an oath (yameen). Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying the rules of vows and oaths in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance and its literal meaning
The vow formula 'Allah 'alayya' literally places an obligation on oneself. Recognize that the default ruling is a vow unless the intention overrides it.
The literal wording governs when no intention is present.
2
Determine the speaker's intention
Classify the intention into one of six categories: no intention, vow only, vow with negation of oath, oath only with negation of vow, both, oath only without negation.
Intention is inferred from the speaker's statement or context.
3
Apply the ruling based on the intention
If it is only a vow, the person must fast or make it up (qada') if broken; if it is an oath, expiation (kaffarah) is required upon breaking; if both, both obligations apply.
The ruling differs if the vowed act is sinful (e.g., fasting on Eid): the vow is still binding but qada' is needed.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf holds that when both intentions are present, only the vow applies; when only the oath is intended, only the oath applies. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) hold that both apply in both cases.
The dispute centers on whether one can intend both literal and figurative meanings simultaneously.
5
State the practical consequence (thamarah)
Under the majority view, breaking the fast on Eid requires both qada' and kaffarah; under Abu Yusuf, only one obligation applies.
The mufta bihi is the majority position.

ثم إن لم ينو شيئاً، أو نوى النذر لا غير، أو نوى النذر ونوى أن لا يكون يميناً، كان نذراً فقط وأفطر هذه الأيام، وقضاها، ولا عهدة إن صامها: فرقوا بين النذر والشروع في هذه الأيام، فلا يلزم بالشروع؛ لأنه معصية، ويلزم بالنذر إذ لا معصية في النذر. ثم إن لم ينو شيئاً، أو نوى النذر لا غير، أو نوى النذر ونوى أن لا يكون يميناً، كان نذراً فقط. وإن نوى اليمين ونوى أن لا يكون نذراً كان يميناً، وعليه كفارة يمين إن أفطر. وإن نواهما أو نوى اليمين (من غير أن ينفي النذر) كان نذراً ويميناً. وعند أبي يوسف له نذر في الأول، ويمين في الثاني. المراد بالأول ما إذا نواهما، وبالثاني ما إذا نوى اليمين. الأولى: أن لا ينوي بهذا الكلام شيئا، وحينئذ هو نذر يجب وفاؤه عملا بالصيغة، فإن مثل هذا اللفظ موضوع للنذر، فيفيده لو حلفه إذا تكلم به من غير احتياج إلى النية. الثانية: أن ينوي النذر فقط من غير إثبات غيره أو نفيه، وحينئذ أيضاً هو نذر لتأكيد ما وضع له الكلام بالعزيمة. الثالثة: أن ينوي النذر مع نفي محتمله وهو اليمين، فيكون نذراً بالطريق الأولى لتأكد موجب الصيغة بالقصد، مع نفي ما يحتمله. الرابعة: أن ينوي بهذا الكلام اليمين مع نفي أن يكون نذراً، وحينئذ يكون يميناً؛ لأنه نذر بصيغة يمين بموجبه، فإن وضعه لإيجاب المباح على نفسه، وهو معنى النذر، ويلزمه تحريم ضدّه على نفسه، وتحريم المباح على نفسه هو معنى اليمين؛ كقوله: والله لا أفعل كذا، أو لا أترك كذا، فإذا نفى المعنى الموضوع له ونوى ما هو لازمه حكم به، ويكون من قبيل إطلاق الملزوم على اللازم. الخامسة: أن ينوي النذر واليمين كليهما. السادسة: أن ينوي اليمين فقط من غير أن ينفي النذر أو يثبته، وفي هاتين الصورتين يكون نذراً ويميناً، ويحكم بأحكامها. ويجب عليه بره كسائر الأيمان؛ فإن حنث لا قضاء، بل الكفارة. والكفارة تحرير رقبة، أو إطعام عشرة مساكين، أو كسوتهم. وعند أبي يوسف له نذر في الأول، ويمين في الثاني؛ لأنه موضوع له فيحمل عليه لا محالة عند اقترانه بالنية، ويصير قصد اليمين لغواً؛ لعدم جواز إرادة المعنى الحقيقي والمجازي معاً.
Then if he did not intend anything, or intended only a vow, or intended a vow and intended that it not be an oath, it is only a vow, and he breaks the fast on these days, and makes them up, and there is no obligation upon him if he fasted them. They distinguished between a vow and commencement in these days: commencement is not binding because it is disobedience, but a vow is binding because there is no disobedience in the vow. Then if he did not intend anything, or intended only a vow, or intended a vow and intended that it not be an oath, it is only a vow. And if he intended an oath and intended that it not be a vow, it is an oath, and upon him is the expiation of an oath if he breaks the fast. And if he intended both, or intended the oath (without negating the vow), it is both a vow and an oath. According to Abu Yusuf, for him there is a vow in the first case and an oath in the second case. What is meant by the first is when he intended both, and by the second is when he intended the oath. The first scenario: that he intends nothing by this utterance, and then it is a vow which must be fulfilled acting upon the literal wording, for such a word is placed for a vow, so it conveys the vow if he utters it without need for intention. The second: that he intends only the vow without affirming or negating anything else, and then it is also a vow to emphasize what the word is placed for by determination. The third: that he intends the vow while negating its possibility, which is the oath, so it is a vow a fortiori because the force of the wording is emphasized by the intention along with negating what it could imply. The fourth: that he intends by this utterance the oath while negating that it be a vow, and then it is an oath because it is a vow expressed as an oath by its requirement; for its placement is to make permissible things obligatory on oneself, which is the meaning of a vow, and it necessarily makes the opposite forbidden on oneself, and forbidding permissible things on oneself is the meaning of an oath, like his saying: 'By Allah, I will not do such and such' or 'I will not leave such and such.' So if he negates the meaning for which the word is placed and intends what is necessarily concomitant with it, judgment is given accordingly, and it falls under the category of using the implied meaning for the necessary concomitant. The fifth: that he intends both the vow and the oath. The sixth: that he intends only the oath without negating or affirming the vow. In these two scenarios, it is both vow and oath, and the rulings of both apply. And it is obligatory for him to fulfill his oath like other oaths; if he breaks it, there is no making up, only expiation. The expiation is freeing a slave, or feeding ten poor persons, or clothing them. According to Abu Yusuf, for him there is a vow in the first case and an oath in the second case, because it is placed for that meaning so it is necessarily interpreted accordingly when accompanied by intention, and the intention of an oath becomes null because it is not permissible to intend both the literal and figurative meanings together.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed expansion by the commentator on the rulings of vow formulas (nadhara) when uttered with various intentions. The core discussion revolves around the words 'Allah 'alayya an asuma' (Upon me is Allah's obligation to fast...) and whether it creates a binding vow, an oath, or both depending on the speaker's intention. The author first distinguishes between a vow (nadhara) and mere commencement of fasting on prohibited days: commencement is not binding because it involves sin, while a vow is binding since the vow itself is not sinful. Then, six scenarios are enumerated: (1) no intention: the literal wording makes it a vow needing fulfillment; (2) intention of vow only: still a vow; (3) intention of vow while negating oath: a fortiori a vow; (4) intention of oath while negating vow: it becomes an oath, and if broken (e.g., by fasting on a prohibited day), expiation (kafarah) is required; (5) intending both: both vow and oath apply; (6) intending only oath without negating vow: both apply. The commentator notes that for cases (5) and (6), both rulings apply: if the person breaks the fast, he must both make up the day (for the vow) and pay expiation (for the oath). An intra-Hanafi dispute is mentioned: Abu Yusuf holds that in the first instance (intending both), it is only a vow because the word is literally for a vow and the oath intention becomes null—one cannot simultaneously intend literal and figurative meanings. In the second instance (intending oath only), it is only an oath. The relied-upon position, however, is that both apply. The passage grounds these rulings in the principles of language and intention (niyyah), emphasizing that vow formulas are legally treated according to their subject matter and the speaker's aim.

Key Terms
نذر (nadhara): A vow: an obligation one places upon oneself to perform an act of worship or good deed, usually by saying 'Allah 'alayya an asuma' (Upon me is Allah's obligation to fast).
يمين (yameen): An oath: swearing by Allah to do or not do something, which entails expiation if broken.
كفارة (kafarah): Expiation: the penalty for breaking a valid oath, which is freeing a slave, feeding ten poor persons, or clothing them.
