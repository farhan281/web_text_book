Definition and Conditions of the Martyr (Shahid)
Defines the martyr with conditions and intra-Hanafi disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a shahid, enumerate each condition (tahir, baligh, hadidah, zulman, la yajib bihi mal) with its legal effect, and distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on disputed issues.

Core Idea
A shahid is a pure, adult Muslim killed unjustly by an iron weapon without blood money obligation — but the weapon and janabah issues are disputed among Hanafi masters.

In this lesson, we analyze the legal definition of a shahid (martyr) as presented in the Hanafi text 'Umdat al-Ri'aya. The definition includes several precise conditions that determine whether a slain Muslim receives the special burial rulings of martyrdom (no washing, burial in clothes). We will unpack each condition and also examine two important intra-Hanafi disagreements on whether killing by a heavy object qualifies and whether a martyr in a state of janabah should be washed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal definition
Read the matn: 'He is every pure, adult person killed by an iron weapon unjustly, and no financial obligation is incurred by it, or he is found dead wounded in the battlefield.' This is the core definition. Note the five explicit conditions: tahara, bulugh, hadidah, zulm, and absence of financial obligation due from the killing.
The phrase 'or found dead wounded' covers cases where the cause is inferred.
2
Analyze each condition
For each condition, understand its legal effect and what it excludes. 'Tahir' excludes those who died in a state of major impurity (junub, haid, nifas). 'Baligh' excludes children and (by extension) the insane. 'Hadidah' restricts to iron weapons (or sharp equivalents) according to Abu Hanifa. 'Zulman' excludes lawful executions (hadd, qisas). 'La yajib bihi mal' excludes killings that require blood money (diyah) as the primary obligation.
The commentary adds that 'tahir' can also mean pure from disbelief, thus excluding non-Muslims.
3
Understand the legal effect of shahada
The key effect is that the shahid is not washed and is buried in his clothes. This is a dispensation (rukhsah) specific to the shahid. Note that this is separate from the martyr's reward in the afterlife; the focus here is on the worldly rulings (ahkam al-dunya).
The two types of shahid: one in the afterlife only (e.g., plague victim) and one in both this world and the next.
4
Examine intra-Hanafi disputes
Two disputes are highlighted: (1) The weapon condition: Abu Hanifa restricts to iron (hadidah), while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) extend to any weapon that causes death with intent, thus committing qisas. (2) The purity condition: Abu Hanifa holds that a junub martyr must be washed because the obligation of ghusl from janabah persists; Sahibayn hold that the obligation falls with death and martyrdom does not create a new ghusl. Both disputes affect the definition and its application.
The hadith of Hanzalah is cited as evidence for Abu Hanifa's position on janabah.
5
Apply the definition to cases
Given a scenario, determine whether the person is a shahid by checking all conditions. For example, a father killing his son with a sword unjustly: the father owes blood money (since he is exempt from qisas), but the son is still a shahid because the money is not due 'by this very killing' but due to a textual exception. This shows the nuance of the financial condition.
The commentary clarifies that the financial obligation must be directly tied to the killing, not an incidental consequence.

The Chapter of the Martyr. [1] He is every pure, adult person killed by an iron weapon unjustly, and no financial obligation is incurred by it, or he is found dead wounded in the battlefield. 'Pure' excludes one upon whom ghusl is obligatory (like the junub, menstruating woman, and woman in postpartum bleeding). 'Adult' excludes the child. [1] His saying: The Chapter of the Martyr; it is fa'il in the sense of maf'ul from shahada or shuhud, and it is named such because he is testified for Paradise, or the angels of mercy witness him, or it is fa'il in the sense of fa'il because he is alive with his Lord, so he is a witness as in al-Durr al-Mukhtar and others. Then the martyr is of two types: a martyr only in the ruling of the afterlife like the one who dies of plague, stomach disease, and the like — I have discussed this in al-Taliq al-Mumajjad on Muwatta' Muhammad; and a martyr in the ruling of this world as well: that he is not washed and is buried in his clothes, and the author defined him by his statement: 'He is every pure.' [2] His saying: adult; better is 'accountable' to exclude both the insane and the child, and better to add the condition of Muslim, for the disbeliever is not a martyr even if killed in the battlefield, except if it is said: what is meant by 'pure' is pure from ritual impurity and from polytheistic beliefs. (3) His saying: wounded; what is meant by it is that he has a sign of killing, even if it is not a wound like strangulation or fracture of a limb; if there is no trace at all he is not a martyr because the apparent is that he died of severe fear, as in al-Fath. [4] His saying: excludes one upon whom ghusl is obligatory; then he is not a martyr so he is washed according to him (Abu Hanifa), and according to them (the two disciples): he is not washed because what was obligatory due to janabah has fallen due to death, and the second (ghusl for shahada) is not obligatory for martyrdom. And 'by an iron weapon' excludes killing by a heavy object. And 'unjustly' excludes execution by hadd or qisas. And 'no financial obligation is incurred by it' excludes killing for which financial obligation is due, and the intent is that the financial obligation is due by the very killing; for if the father kills his son unjustly with an iron weapon, the son is a martyr because even if financial obligation is due, it is not due by this very killing. And he (Abu Hanifa) has that martyrdom prevents the obligation of washing the deceased, not lifting what was obligatory before it, and it is supported by what is narrated: 'Hanzalah was martyred in the Battle of Uhud while he was junub, so the angels washed him' — narrated by al-Tabarani in his Mu'jam, Ibn Hibban, and al-Hakim. [1] His saying: excludes killing by a heavy object; it is maf'ul from tathqil, i.e., any heavy object with which killing usually occurs like a large stone, and similar to it is called shibh al-amd, and by it qisas is not obligatory but financial compensation; indeed qisas is obligatory only in intentional killing, which is when he intentionally strikes him with a weapon like a sword and what runs in its course in separating parts, like pointed wood or stone, and that is what is meant by 'iron weapon'; this is all according to him (Abu Hanifa), and according to them (the two disciples) in all cases qisas is obligatory, so the definition of the martyr is not restricted to an iron weapon according to them. [2] His saying: excludes execution by hadd; so whoever is killed in the hadd of fornication or is killed by qisas in exchange for a man is not a martyr, so he is washed and shrouded. [3) His saying: excludes killing... etc.; like killing with a small stone and similar to it that does not usually kill, and like accidental killing where he shoots at game and hits a person and kills him; in these cases the obligatory payment is blood money not qisas, and its detail is in al-Fara'id al-Sirajiyyah (2) and its commentary al-Sharif (3) and others. [4 His saying: then it is not due by this very killing; because killing unjustly with an iron weapon intentionally is originally obligated by qisas, but it falls due to a text indicating that a father is not put to death for his son as an honor to him, so financial compensation is obligated to avoid the blood of the slain being wasted entirely.

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya is a commentary on the chapter of the martyr (shahid) from al-Wiqaya. The author first presents the matn (base text) defining a shahid as every pure, adult person killed by an iron weapon unjustly without financial obligation, or found dead wounded on the battlefield. The commentary then unpacks each condition.

'Pure' (tahir) excludes anyone for whom ghusl is obligatory: the junub (person in major impurity), menstruating woman, and woman in postpartum bleeding. The commentary notes that a person in a state of janabah is not considered a martyr if killed in battle according to Imam Abu Hanifa (he must be washed), but according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), such a person is still a martyr and is not washed, because the obligation of ghusl fell with death and martyrdom does not impose a new ghusl. Abu Hanifa's view is supported by the hadith of Hanzalah, who was martyred junub and washed by angels, indicating that the prior obligation of ghusl remains.

'Adult' (baligh) excludes children and the insane; the commentary suggests that 'accountable' (mukallaf) would be more precise, and that 'Muslim' should be added, since a disbeliever killed in battle is not a martyr. The term 'pure' can be interpreted to include both ritual purity and creedal purity.

'Wounded' (jarihan) means having a sign of fatal injury, even if not a wound (e.g., strangulation or broken limb). If no trace is found, he is presumed to have died of fear and is not a martyr.

'By an iron weapon' (hadidah) excludes killing by a heavy object (mithqal), which is considered shibh al-amd (quasi-intentional) and does not entail qisas but financial compensation. According to Abu Hanifa, the definition is restricted to iron weapons; according to the two disciples, the definition is not so restricted, and any weapon (including heavy objects) that causes death with intent entails qisas.

'Unjustly' (zulman) excludes lawful execution by hadd (e.g., stoning for adultery) or qisas (retributive justice). Such persons are washed and shrouded normally.

'No financial obligation is incurred by it' excludes cases where the killer owes blood money due to the killing, such as accidental killing or killing with a small stone that does not usually kill. However, if the killer is the father, he is not liable for qisas but must pay compensation; this does not negate the martyrdom of the son, because the financial obligation is not a direct result of the killing (since the father is exempt from qisas by text).

The commentary also clarifies that 'by an iron weapon' refers to any sharp or pointed instrument that cuts or separates parts, like a sword, spear, or sharp wood/stone. The overall definition is analyzed with intra-Hanafi disagreements clearly noted.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahid): One who is killed unjustly in battle by an iron weapon (according to Abu Hanifa) or by any weapon (according to the disciples) without any financial obligation, and is not washed but buried in his clothes.
طاهر (tahir): Free from major ritual impurity (janabah, hayd, nifas) at the time of death.
بالغ (baligh): Adult; one who has reached puberty. The commentary suggests 'mukallaf' to include sanity.
حديدة (hadidah): An iron weapon or any sharp instrument that cuts or separates body parts, like a sword, knife, or sharp wood/stone.
ظلماً (zulman): Without legal justification; excludes execution by hadd (e.g., stoning) or qisas (retribution).

Key Takeaway
The legal definition of a shahid in Hanafi fiqh is precise: he must be a pure, adult Muslim killed unjustly by an iron weapon without blood money direct from the killing, and the definition admits intra-Hanafi disagreements on the weapon type and the effect of janabah.
