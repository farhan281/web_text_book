Sunnahs of Tawaf al-Qudum: Istilam, Idtiba', and Starting Point
Commentary on sunnahs of tawaf al-qudum for the afaqi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi sunnahs of tawaf al-qudum, explain the steps of istilam for the Black Stone, and identify the correct starting point and idtiba' position.

Core Idea
The Black Stone is honoured through touch if possible; if not, facing and reciting takbir is sunnah.

Tawaf al-qudum is the arrival circumambulation for the non-Meccan pilgrim. The Hanafi school teaches that this tawaf is a confirmed sunnah (sunnah muakkadah), not an obligation. In this lesson, we examine the proper method of performing it, especially the etiquette of touching the Black Stone and the recommended posture called idtiba'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The passage opens by describing the actions related to the Black Stone during tawaf. Note that the text uses the word 'sunn' indicating that these actions are sunnah muakkadah.
The ruling is sunnah, not wajib.
2
Understand the hierarchy of istilam
The author lists three levels: (1) kiss the stone if able without harming, (2) touch with an object like a stick, (3) face the stone and recite takbir, tahlil, etc. The hadith of Umar supports this.
The imperative in the hadith is interpreted as recommendation, not obligation.
3
Identify the sunnah starting point and idtiba'
The passage explains that the pilgrim should begin from the Black Stone, with the Ka'bah on his left, and adopt idtiba'—placing the upper garment under the right armpit.
Idtiba' is sunnah for men from start to end of tawaf.
4
Recognize special cases for mutamatti' and qarin
The text states that the mutamatti' and qarin are excused from tawaf al-qudum because their umrah tawaf suffices. The qarin may also perform it after umrah tawaf if he wishes.
This shows the flexibility of the sunnah.
5
Note the place of multazam
The area between the Black Stone and the door is called al-multazam, where supplication is answered. It is recommended to cling there after tawaf.
This is an additional sunnah.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of Al-Wiqayah detailing the sunnahs of tawaf al-qudum, specifically the actions related to the Black Stone and the manner of beginning the tawaf. The author explains the rulings for istilam (touching) the Black Stone: if one can reach it without harming others, he should touch it with his hand or kiss it; if unable, he may touch it with an object like a stick; if unable to do either, he should face it, raise his hands, and recite takbir, tahlil, tahmid, and salawat. This is based on the hadith where the Prophet told Umar not to crowd people. The hadith also supports touching with a stick. The commentary then clarifies that tawaf al-qudum is sunnah for the afaqi (someone entering Mecca from outside), and that the mutamatti' and qarin are exempted as their umrah tawaf suffices. However, the qarin may also perform tawaf al-qudum after the umrah tawaf. The sunnah manner of starting tawaf is to begin from the Black Stone, with the Ka'bah on one's left, and to adopt idtiba' (placing the rida' under the right armpit, leaving the right shoulder exposed). This idtiba' is observed throughout the tawaf and is dropped after finishing. The area between the Black Stone and the door is called al-multazam, where it is recommended to cling and supplicate. The entire ruling is based on Prophetic practice.

Key Terms
طواف القدوم (tawaf al-qudum): The tawaf performed upon arriving in Mecca, sunnah for the afaqi (non-Meccan).
افاقي (afaqi): A person who comes to Mecca from outside the miqat boundaries; a non-resident of Mecca.
اضطباع (idtiba'): To place the rida' (upper garment of ihram) under the right armpit, exposing the right shoulder, and throw the ends over the left shoulder.
ملتزم (multazam): The area between the Black Stone and the door of the Ka'bah; recommended for supplication after tawaf.

Ruling on Praying Facing the Hatim Alone
Explains that facing only the Hatim in prayer is invalid despite it being part of the Kaaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a definitive text (Quran) cannot be fulfilled by a probable proof (khabar al-wahid) and thereby determine the validity of facing the Hatim alone in prayer.

Core Idea
A definitive obligation cannot be fulfilled by a probable proof; prayer facing the Hatim alone is invalid.

This passage addresses a subtle issue: the Hatim is technically part of the Kaaba, but the obligation to face the Kaaba is from the Quran. The Hanafi school rules that facing only the Hatim invalidates the prayer because the evidence for the Hatim's inclusion is only a solitary hadith, which is insufficient to offset a definitive command. The commentator clarifies that this ruling is built on precaution (ihtiyat).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The act is praying while facing only the Hatim (the semicircular wall adjacent to the Kaaba). The ruling is that the prayer is invalid (batil).
2
Locate the textual evidence
Two sources are involved: the Quran (Surah al-Baqarah 2:144) commands facing the Masjid al-Haram (the Kaaba), and a solitary hadith indicates that the Hatim is part of the Kaaba.
The Quranic verse is the primary dalil for the obligation.
3
Classify the sources by certainty
The Quran is qat'i al-thubut (definitive transmission and meaning). The solitary hadith is zanni al-thubut (probable).
This classification is a key usul principle.
4
Apply the usul principle
A fard established by a definitive text cannot be fulfilled by relying on a probable evidence. Since the obligation is from the Quran, and the Hatim's status is from a zanni hadith, facing only the Hatim does not fulfill the obligation.
The commentator uses the term ihtiyat to indicate this precautionary approach.
5
Understand the role of ihtiyat
Ihtiyat here means the ruling is built on caution to ensure the obligation is met. It does not mean that facing the Hatim is permissible as a precaution; rather, the precaution demands avoiding it.
The commentator explicitly distinguishes these two meanings.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn stating that if a person prays facing only the Hatim (the semicircular wall adjacent to the Kaaba), their prayer is invalid. The commentator explains that the obligation of facing the Kaaba in prayer is established by the definitive text of the Quran (Surah al-Baqarah 2:144, 149, 150). The fact that the Hatim is part of the Kaaba is proven only by solitary (ahad) hadith, which provide probable (zanni) certainty. Since a definitive obligation cannot be fulfilled by a probable proof, the prayer is not valid. The commentator clarifies that the term 'ihtiyat' (precaution) here means that the ruling is built on caution: one should not rely on a solitary hadith to offset a Quranic obligation. However, if someone does face the Hatim alone knowingly, the prayer is invalid; it is not that it is permissible as a precaution. The precaution is to avoid facing the Hatim alone and to ensure that one is facing a part of the actual Kaaba.

Key Terms
احتياط (ihtiyat): Precaution: a principle of avoiding doubt by choosing the safer option; here it means the ruling of non-validity is built on caution to ensure the obligation is fulfilled.
خبر الواحد (khabar al-wahid): Solitary report; a hadith transmitted by one or few narrators, not reaching tawatur, providing probable (zanni) knowledge.

Raml in Tawaf and Facing the Hatim: Two Hanafi Rulings
Rulings with proofs on raml and facing the Hatim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on raml in tawaf and explain why salah facing only the Hatim is invalid, using the distinction between qat'i and zanni evidence.

Core Idea
Raml is sunnah; facing only the Hatim is invalid because zanni proof cannot fulfill a qat'i obligation.

This passage from a Hanafi fiqh text addresses two distinct rulings: the sunnah of raml (quick walking) in the first three rounds of tawaf, and a crucial condition for the validity of salah — the obligation to face the Kaaba itself, not just the Hatim. Both rulings are supported by proofs from the Quran and Sunnah, and their interaction illustrates the Hanafi principle that certain obligations require certain knowledge, not merely probable evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Locate the two main rules stated: (1) raml is performed in the first three rounds only; (2) salah facing only the Hatim is invalid.
2
Examine the proof for raml
The passage tells the story of the Umrah al-Qada' and the continuation in the Farewell Hajj. Understand that raml became sunnah despite the original reason ceasing.
The key dalil is the Prophet's action in the Farewell Hajj, which is a solitary report (ahad).
3
Examine the proof for the Hatim ruling
The obligation to face the Kaaba is from the Quran (2:144) which is qat'i. The Hatim being part of the Kaaba is from ahad hadith, which is zanni.
Hanafi usul: zanni cannot fulfill a qat'i obligation; hence precaution requires facing the actual Kaaba.
4
Understand the qat'i/zanni distinction
This distinction is critical. Certain obligations (like qibla direction) require certain knowledge. A solitary report gives only probability, not certainty, so it cannot substitute.
This is a recurring principle in Hanafi fiqh: zanni dalil cannot override qat'i dalil.
5
Note the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The Hanafi school requires avoiding Hatim alone when praying. Other schools may consider it valid. Know the mufta bihi for Hanafis.
This ensures the student applies the ruling correctly in practice.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the Hanafi school regarding raml (quick walking) in tawaf and the issue of facing the Hatim during prayer. The author first states the sunnah of raml: it is performed in the first three rounds of tawaf, between the Black Stone and the Black Stone. Then he addresses an important condition for the validity of salah: facing the Kaaba. If a person prays facing only the Hatim (the semi-circular wall adjacent to the Kaaba) and not any part of the Kaaba itself, the salah is invalid. The reason is that the obligation to face the Kaaba is established by the categorical text of the Quran (facing the Masjid al-Haram). That the Hatim is part of the Kaaba is established only by ahad (solitary) reports, which are zanni (probable) in transmission. Since the obligation is certain (qat'i), it cannot be fulfilled by something merely probable. Therefore, as a matter of precaution, one must ensure that the prayer is directed towards the Kaaba itself, not solely the Hatim. In tawaf, the precaution is to walk behind the Hatim (i.e., not cut through it). The passage then explains the wisdom behind raml: it was originally prescribed during the Umrah of Fulfillment (7 AH) when the Prophet ﷺ and his companions showed physical strength to the polytheists who had claimed they were weakened by the fever of Madinah. Despite the original cause being gone, raml remains a sunnah muakkadah because the Prophet ﷺ continued it during the Farewell Hajj. The Hanafi school holds that raml is sunnah for men in the first three rounds of tawaf al-qudum (arrival tawaf) and tawaf al-ziyarah (visit), but not in other tawafs like tawaf al-wada' (farewell).

Key Terms
الرمل (ar-raml): Quick walking with short steps and shaking the shoulders, similar to a wrestler, performed in the first three rounds of tawaf for men.
الحطيم (al-Hatim): The semi-circular area adjacent to the Kaaba; Hanafi: it is considered part of the Kaaba by ahad hadith but not to the degree of certainty, so salah facing only it is invalid.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof explaining the sunnah of ramal (quick walking with shoulder movements) during tawaf. The author first defines ramal: walking quickly while shaking the shoulders like a warrior entering battle, performed with idtiba' (wearing the ihram cloth under the right armpit). The reason for its prescription was to demonstrate physical strength to the disbelievers of Mecca. The historical context is given: in the 6th year of hijra, the Prophet and his companions were blocked from entering Mecca for Umrah, and a treaty was made at Hudaybiyyah. The next year they performed the 'Umrat al-Qada', during which the disbelievers, observing from the mountains, said the Muslims were weakened by Medinan fever. To refute this, the Prophet ordered ramal in the first three circuits of tawaf. The hadith confirming this is cited from the authentic collections. The author then addresses an important legal principle: even though the specific cause (the disbelievers' taunt) no longer exists, the ruling of ramal remains a sunnah. This is because the cause (sabab) that led to the legislation is not a condition for its continuation; rather, it was the occasion for its initial prescription. Once prescribed, it becomes a ritual act. To illustrate, the author compares it to the Friday ghusl: originally prescribed because companions came in sweaty work clothes causing odor, but even after conditions changed, the ghusl remained a sunnah. Thus ramal is a sunnah in tawaf regardless of the original reason.

السَّبَب (al-sabab): The occasion or event that led to the legislation of a ruling, not the effective cause ('illa).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof explaining the sunnah of ramal (quick walking with shoulder movements) during tawaf. The author first defines ramal: walking quickly while shaking the shoulders like a warrior entering battle, performed with idtiba' (wearing the ihram cloth under the right armpit). The reason for its prescription was to demonstrate physical strength to the disbelievers of Mecca. The historical context is given: in the 6th year of hijra, the Prophet and his companions were blocked from entering Mecca for Umrah, and a treaty was made at Hudaybiyyah. The next year they performed the 'Umrat al-Qada', during which the disbelievers, observing from the mountains, said the Muslims were weakened by Medinan fever. To refute this, the Prophet ordered ramal in the first three circuits of tawaf. The hadith confirming this is cited from the authentic collections. The author then addresses an important legal principle: even though the specific cause (the disbelievers' taunt) no longer exists, the ruling of ramal remains a sunnah. This is because the cause (sabab) that led to the legislation is not a condition for its continuation; rather, it was the occasion for its initial prescription. Once prescribed, it becomes a ritual act. To illustrate, the author compares it to the Friday ghusl: originally prescribed because companions came in sweaty work clothes causing odor, but even after conditions changed, the ghusl remained a sunnah. Thus ramal is a sunnah in tawaf regardless of the original reason.

الاضْطِبَاع (al-idtiba'): Wearing the ihram cloth under the right armpit, leaving the right shoulder bare.
السَّبَب (al-sabab): The occasion or event that led to the legislation of a ruling, not the effective cause ('illa).

When is 'Asr Prayer Valid Before Its Time?
Explains exception for 'Asr before its time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which 'Asr prayer is valid before its time in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
'Asr before its time is invalid unless prayed with Dhuhr in congregation while in ihram.

This short passage from al-Wiqayah addresses a key exception to the rule that prayers must be performed within their prescribed times. For a pilgrim in ihram, the Hanafi school permits combining Dhuhr and 'Asr at the time of Dhuhr, but only if both prayers are offered in congregation. If even one condition is missed, 'Asr must be delayed until its own time. Understanding this exception helps clarify the school's approach to combining prayers and the importance of congregation in the journey of Hajj.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule and the exception
First, note the default principle: every prayer must be performed within its time. Then, identify the single exception presented: 'Asr may be performed before its time.
The exception is not for Dhuhr; Dhuhr is already in its time when combined.
2
List the two conditions for the exception
From the text, extract the two required conditions: (1) the person must be in ihram (state of consecration for Hajj/Umrah), and (2) both Dhuhr and 'Asr must be prayed together in congregation.
The Arabic uses 'الجماعة' (congregation) and 'الإحرام' (ihram).
3
Check the consequence of missing a condition
If either condition is absent—i.e., the pilgrim prays alone, or is not in ihram, or misses only one of the two prayers in congregation—then the 'Asr prayer before its time becomes invalid. It must then be offered in its proper time.
The text explicitly says 'لا يجوز العصر' (the 'Asr is not permissible) without these conditions.
4
Understand the rationale
The Hanafi school allows this exception as a concession for the pilgrim during Hajj, recognizing the difficulty of praying separately while engaged in the rites. Congregation is emphasized as a necessary element to maintain the collective nature of the ritual.
This is a specific rukhsa (concession) tied to the unique situation of ihram and congregation.

Rulings of Rami al-Jimar on the Days of Tashreeq
Commentary on rami rulings for the days of Tashreeq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on stoning the jamarat during the days of Tashreeq, including the order, standing, timing, and the consequence of leaving a wajib.

Core Idea
Stoning the jamarat is wajib; order matters; stand after first two, not after the third.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah explains the detailed rulings of rami al-jimar on the days of Tashreeq. The commentary clarifies the wajib elements, sunnah practices, and timing, based on hadith evidence from Ibn Abbas and Ibn Umar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Rami al-jimar on the days of Tashreeq is wajib in the Hanafi school. Leaving it requires a dam (sacrifice). The act is stoning three jamarat with seven pebbles each.
Wajib not fard because the dalil is zanni.
2
List the prescribed order
The order is: first the jamrah nearest Masjid al-Khayf (al-sughra), then the middle (al-wusta), then Jamrat al-Aqaba (al-kubra). This order is also wajib.
3
Distinguish standing after stoning
After stoning the first two jamarat, one stands for du'a facing qibla with raised hands. After Jamrat al-Aqaba, one does not stand. Standing is sunnah muakkadah.
After the third, one moves away immediately.
4
Understand timing and permissions
Stoning begins after noon (zawal) on the 11th and 12th. On the 13th, stoning before noon is permissible but makruh tanzihi; after noon is sunna. The time on the 13th is from fajr to sunset.
Stoning on the day of Nahr (10th) is different.
5
Analyze the nafr (departure) ruling
Nafr al-awwal (12th) is allowed after stoning. Nafr al-thani (13th) is allowed before dawn; if dawn enters while at Mina, one must stay and stone before leaving.
Leaving before fajr means no stoning required for that day.

The Hanafi Position on Qiran: Superiority and Procedure
Exposition of Qiran's superiority and its ritual procedure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that Qiran is afdal (better) than other forms of Hajj, explain the proof based on the Prophet's practice, and describe the correct procedure for a qarin pilgrim.

Core Idea
Qiran (combining Hajj and Umrah) is the superior form of Hajj in the Hanafi school.

This lesson examines the ruling on Qiran (combining Hajj and Umrah) from the Hanafi school. The passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies that Qiran is absolutely better than Tamattu' and Ifrad, based on the Prophet's practice in the Farewell Hajj. We will explore the definition, the evidence, and the practical procedure that follows from this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on Qiran
The text explicitly states 'al-qiran afdalu mutlaqan' – Qiran is absolutely better than Tamattu' and Ifrad.
This is the mufta bihi (operative) position in the Hanafi school.
2
Define Qiran technically
Qiran is defined as raising the voice with talbiyah for both Hajj and Umrah from the miqat together. The linguistic meaning is 'combining two things'.
This distinguishes it from Tamattu' (separate ihrams) and Ifrad (Hajj only).
3
Locate the proof (dalil)
The commentator cites authentic hadith in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, especially the narrations of Ibn 'Umar and 'A'ishah, indicating the Prophet (ﷺ) performed Qiran in the Farewell Hajj.
This is a dalil naqli with qati' thubut but zanni dalalah regarding preference.

Qiran (and Tamattu') are only valid for those coming from outside Makkah (afaqi). The miqat is the boundary for entering ihram with both intentions.
A Makkan resident cannot perform Qiran; he must perform Ifrad.

After the two rak'ahs of ihram, the qarin says a supplication (mustahsan, not obligatory), then performs one tawaf and one sa'y without hair removal, and remains in ihram until after Hajj. Performing two tawafs and two sa'ys is makruh.
The du'a is not from sunnah but recommended by jurists to align heart and tongue.

Penalties for Shaving Body Hair and Cutting Nails in Ihram
Commentary on matn about shaving body hair and cutting nails.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for incurring a penalty when shaving body hair during ihram, and apply the principle of tadakhul to determine the number of penalties for nail-cutting across multiple sittings.

Core Idea
Shaving entire areas like armpits triggers a dam; cutting nails in multiple sittings may merge into one penalty or require separate penalties.

This lesson examines two types of offenses during Hajj: shaving hair from specific body parts (armpits, pubic area, neck, etc.) and cutting nails. The commentary clarifies that unlike the head, these areas require shaving the entire part to incur the penalty. Furthermore, the school has a dispute on whether multiple nail-cutting sessions merge into a single penalty or require separate penalties. Understanding these details ensures the pilgrim knows exactly when a dam is owed.

Unlike the head and beard, these areas require shaving the entire area; shaving only part does not trigger the penalty because custom does not involve partial shaving here.

Cutting nails of all fingers and toes in one sitting requires one dam. Cutting only one hand or one foot also requires a dam, treating the quarter as equivalent to the whole.

Imam Muhammad holds that multiple nail-cutting sessions merge (tadakhul) into one dam unless expiation is paid between them. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that each sitting requires its own dam.
The dispute is based on different interpretations of the principle of tadakhul.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on offenses (jinaayat) in Hajj. It covers three types of violations: shaving hair from specific body parts, cutting nails, and performing Tawaf in a state of janabah (major impurity).

Tawaf in Janabah: Penalty for Major Impurity
Commentary on matn about Tawaf in janabah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that performing any Tawaf (obligatory, wajib, or nafl) in a state of janabah requires a dam, and explain the reasoning based on the strength of the act.

Core Idea
Any Tawaf performed in janabah requires a dam because Tawaf is a strong act of worship that becomes binding once started.

This lesson focuses on the offense of performing Tawaf while in a state of major impurity (janabah). The commentary explains that obligatory Tawaf (Tawaf al-Ifadah) is the strongest, but even sunnah and nafl Tawafs like Tawaf al-Qudum become obligatory upon commencement. Performing them in janabah is a severe violation requiring a blood sacrifice. Understanding this ensures the pilgrim maintains ritual purity during Tawaf.

Penalties for Hunting and Cutting Plants in the Haram
Commentary on penalties for harming Haram resources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings on when compensation (qimah) is wajib for harming game, milking, or cutting vegetation in the Haram, and apply the distinctions (e.g., dry vs. green, rotten vs. fresh egg, loss of escape ability).

Core Idea
Any harm to non-owned, non-planted game or vegetation in the Haram requires compensation, even from non-muhrim.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position on penalties for violating the sanctity of the Haram by harming its wildlife or plants. The commentary from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies that the obligation of compensation (qimah) applies broadly, regardless of whether the offender is in ihram. We will trace the specific cases: plucking feathers, cutting legs, breaking eggs, milking, and cutting grass/trees, along with the key exceptions (dry wood, rotten eggs).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, list the four categories of prohibited acts in the Haram: hunting game, milking, cutting grass, cutting trees. For each, state that compensation (qimah) is wajib, provided the item is not owned and not planted by anyone.
The cutter or milker may be muhrim or non-muhrim; both are liable.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof cited is the hadith about not picking up lost items in the Haram except for a public announcer, with the exception of idhkhir grass. This establishes that the Haram has special restrictions, implying that any taking is prohibited unless exempted.
The exception for idhkhir shows that cutting dry or needed vegetation is allowed; otherwise, it is forbidden.
3
Trace the sub-rulings
The commentary expands on specific harms: plucking feathers or breaking legs that disable escape → value of the whole game; breaking an egg → value of the egg (unless rotten); milking → value of the milk; cutting green grass/trees → value of the cut; cutting dry wood → no liability.
The key principle: if the act prevents the game from escaping, it is as if the whole game is lost.
4
Note the conditions and exceptions
The vegetation must be non-owned and non-planted. Dry wood (jaff) is exempt. Eggs must be fresh (not rotten) to carry liability. If a chick dies from a cause other than the breaking, no liability for the egg.
The liability for eggs is for the potential to become game, not for the egg itself.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah on the matn of al-Wiqayah, dealing with penalties (jinaayat) in the Hajj. The author explains that the matn lists four types of violations in the Haram: hunting game, milking it, cutting grass, and cutting trees, with the conditions that these are not owned and not planted by anyone. The commentary clarifies each term: 'sayed al-Haram' excludes game outside the Haram which is permissible for non-muhrim; 'halab' (milking) requires compensation regardless of the milker's state, but if a non-muhrim milks game from outside the Haram, no penalty; 'qat' hasheesh' and 'shajar' include both dry and green vegetation, and the cutter may be muhrim or not. The value (qimah) is the due compensation. The commentary then details specific cases: plucking feathers or breaking legs that prevent flight or escape incurs the full value of the game; breaking an egg incurs its value; if a dead chick emerges, the chick's value is due; slaughtering by a non-muhrim requires the game's value. The hadith about not picking up lost items except for a public announcer, and the exception for idhkhir, is cited as proof. The exception 'except what is dried' (jaff) means dry wood is not liable because it is firewood. The commentary also explains that removing the instrument of prevention (e.g., feathers, legs) is like destroying the whole animal. For eggs, the value is based on the possibility of becoming game; rotten eggs have no value. If a chick dies from a cause other than the breaking, no liability for the egg. These rulings are based on narrations from Ibn Abbas and Umar, and are found in the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
صيد الحرم (sayd al-haram): Game animals and birds that are within the boundaries of the Haram (Mecca and its sanctuary).
حلبه (halabuhu): Milking the game; the act of extracting milk from an animal that is game in the Haram.
حشيش (hashish): Grass or herbage, both dry and wet, that grows in the Haram. The jurists use it generally for all vegetation.
شجر (shajar): Trees that are standing and growing; when dry they are firewood.
قيمة (qimah): Compensation or value that must be paid for damaging game or vegetation in the Haram.
