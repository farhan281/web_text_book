Purity at End of Prayer Time: Only the Current Prayer
Hanafi ruling with Shafi'i dispute on purity at end of prayer time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a woman becomes pure from menstruation during the time of Asr or Isha, and explain why she does not pray the preceding prayer, contrary to al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Purity at end of a prayer time obligates only that prayer, not the previous one.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: a woman's menstruation ends during the time of Asr. According to the Hanafi school, she only prays Asr. But Imam al-Shafi'i holds that she must also pray Zuhr because he considers the times of Zuhr and Asr as shared. How do the schools differ, and what is the evidence? Let us examine the passage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling
Read the first part: a woman who becomes pure (from menstruation) during the time of Asr prays only Asr, and similarly for Isha. Note that the wording 'صلتهما فقط' means she prays 'them both'? Actually 'them' refers to Asr and Isha? No, it means she prays that prayer only. The text says 'صلتهما فقط' - 'prays them only' meaning the prayer of that time.
The Arabic 'صلتهما' is feminine dual referring to the two prayers? Actually it's 'salatuhuma' meaning her two prayers? The passage is clear: she prays the current prayer only.
2
State the Shafi'i position
Al-Shafi'i holds that she must also pray the preceding prayer (Zuhr if pure in Asr, Maghrib if pure in Isha). His basis is that the times of Zuhr and Asr are considered one continuous time, and similarly Maghrib and Isha, as evidenced by the permission to join prayers during travel.
The text says 'فإن وقت الظهر والعصر عنده كوقت واحد'.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school does not accept this analogy. They maintain that the times are distinct; the permission to join is a concession (rukhsah) for travel, not a merger of times. Therefore, purity at the end of Asr only obligates Asr, not Zuhr.
The commentary mentions that the hadiths of joining are used by Shafi'i, but the Hanafi response distinguishes between concession and default rule.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the Hanafi school, a woman purified during Asr prays only Asr; under Shafi'i, she must also make up Zuhr. Similarly for Isha and Maghrib.
This affects actual worship and makeup obligations.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi ruling with a dispute against al-Shafi'i and Zufar. It consists of two parts. First: The Hanafi position that a woman who becomes pure (from menstruation) during the time of Asr prays only Asr, and if during Isha, only Isha. Al-Shafi'i holds that she must also pray the preceding prayer (Zuhr or Maghrib) because he considers the times of Zuhr/Asr and Maghrib/Isha as shared times, allowing travel-joining. The Hanafi response implicitly distinguishes between a shared time (for rain/travel) and distinct times for prayer. Second: If a person becomes obligated (by puberty, Islam, or purity) at the very end of a prayer time, such that only the takbir remains, they must make up that prayer later. Zufar says since performance wasn't possible, no makeup is required. The Hanafi response argues that one could theoretically perform in that instant (by breaking habit). However, a woman who menstruates at the end of the time is not obligated to make up that prayer, meaning it is waived due to the impediment having persisted through the beginning of the time. This is contrary to al-Shafi'i. The proof for al-Shafi'i's view on shared times is the hadiths on joining prayers, but the Hanafi school does not accept that as evidence for time-sharing outside travel.

Becoming Obligated at the End of Time: Makeup Required
Hanafi ruling on qada obligation for those who become eligible at the very end of prayer time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that a person who becomes eligible for prayer (by puberty, Islam, or purity from menstruation) at the very end of the prayer time must make up that prayer, and distinguish this from the case of menstruation at the end of time.

Core Idea
If only the takbir remains of prayer time, performance is possible, so qada is wajib.

What if a boy reaches puberty or a disbeliever accepts Islam right at the end of a prayer time, with only enough time for the opening takbir? The Hanafi school says they must make up that prayer later. But Imam Zufar disagreed, arguing that performance was impossible. Similarly, a woman who menstruates at the end of the time is not required to make up. Let's explore the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling for those who become eligible at the very end
The text says: 'ومن هو أهل فرض في آخر وقته يقضيه' - whoever is eligible for an obligation at the end of its time makes it up. This includes a disbeliever who accepts Islam, a boy who reaches puberty, or a woman who becomes pure from menstruation at the end of the time, as long as there is time for the opening takbir.
The minimum considered is the takbir (tahrimah).
2
Understand the dispute with Zufar
Imam Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) held that if only the takbir remains, the person cannot validly perform the prayer, so no obligation to make up exists. The Hanafi majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) respond that it is theoretically possible to perform the takbir in that instant (even if unusual), so the obligation remains and qada is required.
The text says 'خلافاً لزفر فإنه يقول لم يجب الأداء لعدم سعة الوقت فلم يجب القضاء'. The Hanafi response: 'أن السعة ممكن بخرق العادة' - capacity is possible by breaking the norm.
3
Contrast with the case of menstruation at the end
A woman who starts menstruating at the end of the prayer time is NOT required to make up that prayer. This is because she was not obligated at the beginning of the time (the impediment existed from the start? Actually the impediment occurs at the end; but the ruling is that menstruation at the end waives the obligation entirely, contrary to al-Shafi'i.
The text says 'ومن حاضت في آخر الوقت لا يجب عليها قضاء صلاة ذلك الوقت خلافاً للشافعي'.
4
Analyze the difference between the cases
The key difference: when a person becomes eligible (by puberty/Islam/purity) at the end, they were not exempt from prayer before (they were simply not obligated). At the moment they become eligible, they can theoretically perform the takbir, so the prayer becomes due. But a menstruating woman was already prevented by hayd; the onset of hayd at the end confirms that she was never fully obligated, and the prayer is waived.
This distinction is based on the status of hayd as a legal impediment (mani') that completely removes obligation, unlike simple lack of eligibility.

Erasing the Head: The Hanafi Method to Remove the Prohibition of Images
Commentary on the ruling for erasing the head of an image

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on erasing the head of an image and explain the required method to effectively remove the prohibition.

Core Idea
To remove the prohibition of an image, the head must be completely erased—not merely cut.

The prohibition of images of animate beings is well-established. But what if the image already exists? The Hanafi school provides a practical solution: erasing the head. This lesson examines the textual proof from the hadith of Jibril and the precise method required to render the image permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling and the action that can remove it
Images of animate beings are originally disliked (makruh tahrimi or haram). The text addresses how to remove this dislike: by erasing the head (muhi).
The ruling after erasure becomes permissible (mubah).
2
Locate the dalil: the hadith of Jibril
The commentator cites the hadith from Sunan al-Nasa'i where Jibril refused to enter due to a curtain with images, and the Prophet (ﷺ) was advised to cut off the heads or make them into cushions.
This hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning is clear (qati al-dalalah) for the Hanafis.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The command 'faqta'u ru'usaha' (cut off their heads) indicates that removing the head removes the prohibition. The Hanafi understanding is that 'cut' here implies complete erasure, not just a superficial cut.
4
Apply the method: what constitutes effective erasure?
Simple cutting with a thread is insufficient because thick necks may not be fully severed. Effective erasure requires covering the entire head (e.g., with a thread placed around the whole head), painting over it, washing it, or similar.
The goal is to make the head unrecognizable as part of a living being.
5
Note the limits: cutting limbs is insufficient
Cutting off hands and feet does not remove the dislike, because a person may survive with limbs cut off. This analogy shows that only removing the head (the vital part) removes the prohibition.
This is based on qiyas: a living being can lose limbs but not the head.

The Ruling on Urinating Over a Prayer Area
Explaining the ruling of actions on a roof over a non-masjid prayer area

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on actions like urinating on a roof above a non-masjid prayer area and explain why it does not have the sanctity of a true mosque.

Core Idea
A designated prayer area without waqf is not a masjid — so actions prohibited in a masjid are not disliked there.

This lesson clarifies a common misunderstanding: any place used for prayer does not automatically acquire the sanctity of a mosque. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a legally endowed masjid and a private musalla. The commentary from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains that actions like urinating on the roof above a musalla are permissible because the place lacks the full legal status of a mosque.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling in the matn
The base text mentions 'urinating on the roof of a house that contains a mosque'. The commentator clarifies the ruling: it is not disliked.
The term 'mosque' here is reinterpreted.
2
Understand the commentator's expansion
The commentator explains that 'masjid' in this context means a place prepared for supererogatory prayers, with a mihrab and cleaned, not a legally endowed mosque.
This is a key distinction.
3
Analyse the proof: why saleability indicates lack of sanctity
The commentator argues that since the place can be sold with the house, it is not a permanent, waqf-based mosque, so it lacks the full sanctity that would prohibit these acts.
The reasoning is based on legal effects (saleability).
4
Apply the distinction to other cases
Consider other acts like intercourse or defecation on the roof; they are also not disliked because the legal status is the same.
The ruling applies to any act that would be impermissible in a true mosque.

Washing the Found Dead and the Martyrdom Dispute
Commentary expanding on washing conditions and dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for washing a found dead body and distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on martyrdom.

Core Idea
A found dead is washed unless killed by a sharp weapon in a non-qasama place; heavy stick triggers intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

The washing of a person found dead with an unknown killer involves nuanced conditions. This lesson examines the base ruling, then the commentator's expansion, clarifying how location and weapon type affect the ruling, and the internal Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples on the heavy stick case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and its conditions
From the matn: a person found dead in a town with unknown killer is washed regardless of weapon type (iron, large stick, small stick) because diyah and qasama are obligatory.
This is the default ruling from earlier sources like al-Dhakhira.
2
Understand the commentator's refinement by location
The commentator distinguishes: if found in a place where qasama is obligatory (house, locality), the above ruling applies; if in a place without qasama (road, mosque), the ruling changes based on weapon.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute for the heavy stick
When found in a non-qasama place and killed with a heavy stick (large stick): Abu Hanifa says wash (not a shahid), while the Sahibayn say do not wash (he is a shahid).
The dispute stems from classifying killing with a heavy stick as quasi-intentional (shibh al-amd) vs intentional (amd).
4
Note the agreement for the small stick
If killed with a small stick (not typically lethal), all agree he is washed—the killing itself mandates diyah, so ignorance of killer does not make him shahid.
5
Apply the rule to other categories (children, etc.)
Children, menstruating women, etc., are always washed even if killed by iron, because they fall outside the textual exemption for shahid.
This confirms that martyrdom status is restricted to specific criteria.

Second, the found dead is washed, but with a critical condition: the ruling depends on whether the location is one where qasama is obligatory (like a house or locality) or not (like a public road or mosque). If in a place where qasama is not obligatory, the person is not washed if killed with iron (since he is a shahid), but there is disagreement (ikhtilaf) between Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) on the heavy stick case: Abu Hanifa says wash, the two say don't wash. For a light stick, all three agree he is washed. The reason: the killing itself necessitates blood money, so the fact that the killer is unknown (obstacle to payment) does not make him a shahid.

Key Terms
شهيد (shahid): A person killed unjustly with a sharp weapon (hadidah) who is not washed and is buried in his blood-stained clothes.
قسامة (qasama): Fifty oaths taken by the people of a locality where a dead body is found, to exonerate themselves; if taken, they pay blood money.
دية (diyah): Blood money: 100 camels or equivalent monetary compensation for wrongful killing.
حديدة (hadidah): An iron weapon or instrument that has a sharp edge, e.g., sword, knife, sharp stone.
عصا كبير (asa kabir): A large stick or heavy blunt object that typically causes death when used.
