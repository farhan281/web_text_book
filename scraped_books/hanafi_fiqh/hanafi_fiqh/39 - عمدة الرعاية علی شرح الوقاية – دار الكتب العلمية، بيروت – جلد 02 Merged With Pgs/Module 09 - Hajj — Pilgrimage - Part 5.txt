This passage contains two distinct rulings about the hired person (ma'mur) in hajj. Hajj on Behalf of Deceased: Where to Start After the Hired Person Dies
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the location for fulfilling a hajj bequest after the hired person's death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on completing hajj for a deceased hirer when the hired person dies on the way, explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the starting point, and apply the one-third rule using an example.

Core Idea
If the hired hajj performer dies, the hirer's hajj is performed from his home using one-third of the remaining estate.

This passage tackles a specific problem in executing a bequest for hajj (wasiyyat al-hajj): what happens when the person hired to perform the hajj dies en route? The Hanafi school is divided between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions. We will unpack the ruling, the reasoning, and the practical calculation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
A deceased person bequeaths that hajj be performed on his behalf. The executor hires someone and gives him funds. The hired person dies before completing the journey. How should the bequest be fulfilled?
The hired person is called al-ma'mur or al-na'ib.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Ruling and Reasoning
Abu Hanifa rules that the hajj must be performed from the hirer's home town, using one-third of whatever funds remain after the initial allocation. The reasoning: the portion of the journey that was completed is considered void (batal) in worldly legal terms, so the obligation reverts to the original place.
The 'void' principle applies to the contract of hire, not to the religious obligation.
3
State the Opposing View of the Two Companions
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that the hajj should be performed from the place where the hired person died, because the journey has not been entirely void; the portion travelled still has some legal effect.
This disagreement is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; the mufta bihi is Abu Hanifa's view.
4
Apply the Ruling with the Example
Take the given example: a man leaves 4,000 dirhams. He bequeaths hajj at a cost of 1,000 dirhams. The executor gives 1,000 to a hired person. The hired person dies (or the money is stolen). From the remaining 3,000 dirhams, one-third (i.e., 1,000 dirhams) is taken again to perform the hajj from the hirer's home. This process repeats if lost again.
The fraction one-third (thuluth) is used because the hajj is treated as a bequest (wasiyyah), which is limited to one-third of the estate.

Responsibility for Dam al-Qiran and Dam al-Jinaya in Hajj by Deputy
Defining who bears the blood sacrifices in Hajj by deputy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on who bears dam al-qiran and dam al-jinaya when Hajj is performed by a deputy, and explain the reasoning (ta'lil) provided by the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
The deputy bears the blood sacrifices for his own violations and thanksgiving; the principal bears only unavoidable prevention (ihsar).

When a principal (amir) appoints a deputy (na'ib) to perform Hajj on his behalf, questions arise about who is liable for blood sacrifices necessitated by violations or performing the combined rites (qiran). This passage clarifies that the deputy is responsible for dam al-qiran and dam al-jinaya, while dam al-ihsar remains on the principal. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) present a clear ta'lil based on causation and direct benefit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the ruling
The passage addresses two types of blood sacrifices in Hajj by a deputy: dam al-qiran (thanksgiving for combining Hajj and Umrah) and dam al-jinaya (compensation for violations). The ruling is that both are upon the deputy.
The text says 'by agreement' (ittifaqan).
2
Locate the reasoning
The Sahibayn provide the ta'lil: dam al-jinaya is caused by the deputy's own action (violation), and dam al-qiran is a thanksgiving for the deputy's own performance of both rites.
The causation is key: the deputy's act triggers the obligation.
3
Contrast with dam al-ihsar
Note that this reasoning does not apply to dam al-ihsar, which remains on the principal because ihsar is a prevention that affects the principal's obligation.
Dam al-ihsar is not caused by the deputy's action; it compensates for the principal's inability to complete Hajj.
4
Confirm agreement
The text states that this is by agreement among Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, so no intra-Hanafi dispute on this point.
The distinction from ihsar is highlighted as a point of contrast.

Effect of Intercourse Before and After Wuquf
Ruling on a deputy's intercourse before and after wuquf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for a deputy who has intercourse before and after wuquf, and explain the consequences of invalidity, expenses, and required Hajj.

Core Idea
Intercourse before wuquf invalidates the deputy's Hajj; after wuquf it does not.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi ruling on a deputy (na'ib) who has intercourse during Hajj. The timing of this act relative to the standing at Arafat (wuquf) determines whether the Hajj is invalidated or not. The passage clarifies that intercourse before wuquf is a major violation that voids the Hajj, while intercourse after wuquf does not affect its validity. Understanding this distinction is crucial for both deputies and those who appoint them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note that the ruling depends on whether intercourse occurs before or after standing at Arafat (wuquf). The passage explicitly contrasts these two timings.
Before wuquf: severe consequences. After wuquf: no invalidation.
2
State the ruling for intercourse before wuquf
The deputy's Hajj becomes invalid (fasid) and does not count for the principal. He must repay all expenses incurred from his own wealth and perform two Hajjs: one to make up the invalidated one, and another for the principal.
This is a harsh penalty to deter violations.
3
State the ruling for intercourse after wuquf
The Hajj is not invalidated; it remains valid for the principal. The passage does not mention a penalty, but standard Hanafi rulings require a dam (blood sacrifice) for intercourse during ihram even after wuquf.
The ruling is lenient because the essential pillar (wuquf) is complete.
4
Explain the rationale
The difference hinges on wuquf being the essential rukn of Hajj. Before it, the Hajj is still fragile; a major violation destroys its validity. After it, the Hajj is firmly established and not undone by later violations.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that once a rukn is fulfilled, the act is fundamentally sound.

The Rejection of Testimony about Arafat Standing After Its Time
Explains the ruling rejecting testimony about Arafat standing after the proper time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi fiqh rejects testimony about Arafat standing after the time and apply the principle of istihsan to override qiyas in such cases.

Core Idea
Testimony that would invalidate Hajj after the fact is rejected to prevent fitna and because rectification is impossible.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from al-Hidayah: when a group testifies that the people of Arafat stood after the proper time, their testimony is not accepted. The text presents an analogy (qiyas) that would invalidate the standing, but then overrides it with istihsan (juristic preference) due to public interest and hardship. Understanding this ruling reveals how Hanafi jurists balance textual analogy with practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and ruling
The subject is testimony (shahada) about the standing at Arafat occurring after the legal time (e.g., after dawn of the 9th or on the 10th). The ruling is that such testimony is not accepted (la tuqbal).
The ruling is specific to testimony that would imply invalidity of the Hajj after completion.
2
Understand the primary reason
The text states two reasons: impossibility of rectification (tadarak) and prevention of fitna (strife). Since the Hajj is already complete, accepting the testimony would cause social discord without any benefit.
Fitna here refers to confusion and conflict among the Muslim community.

The author presents a qiyas: if people stood on the Day of Nahr (10th) and claimed it was their Arafah standing, by analogy it should not suffice (la yujzi'ahum), just as standing on the Day of Tarwiyah (8th) does not count. The common illa (effective cause) is that the standing is specific to a time and place.
This qiyas is based on the principle that an act of worship must be performed at its prescribed time.

The Hanafi school overrides the qiyas with istihsan (juristic preference). Istihsan here is based on public interest (maslaha) and necessity (darura).

Core Idea
Testimony after wuquf time is rejected to prevent fitna; before time is accepted because rectification remains possible.

In this passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya, we examine a nuanced ruling on testimonies about the timing of standing at Arafat. We will walk through the reasoning and its practical implications for judges and the Hajj ritual.

Guided Path
1

The passage presents two distinct cases: testimony given after the time of wuquf (e.g., stating they stood on the day of Nahr instead of Arafa) and testimony given before the time (e.g., stating they stood on the day of Tarwiya). Note the rulings differ.

The baseline analogy (qiyas) would treat both cases identically: if people stood on a day and later claimed it was the correct day, their testimony should not suffice, akin to standing on Tarwiya being invalid. But istihsan breaks this analogy.

The ruling is that after-time testimony is not accepted, before-time testimony is accepted. The key factor is whether rectification (tadarruk) is possible. If not, to avoid fitna, the testimony is rejected. The judge's role is to preserve the peaceful completion of Hajj.

Ruling on Standing at Arafah on the Wrong Day
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mistaken timing in Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two Hanafi proofs regarding the validity of Hajj when standing at Arafah on the day of Tarwiyah by mistake, and apply the appropriate ruling based on the timing of discovery.

Core Idea
Two proofs yield opposite rulings: impossibility of remedy makes Hajj valid; no parallel for early performance makes it invalid.

This passage from a commentary on al-Hidayah addresses a practical problem: a community stands at Arafah on the 8th of Dhul-Hijjah thinking it is the 9th. After discovery, the ruling depends on whether the error is known in time to remedy. Two proofs are considered, leading to different outcomes. Understanding this dispute is crucial for a mufti or student of fiqh when dealing with mistaken timing in Hajj.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario
Identify the key elements: people stand at Arafah on the day of Tarwiyah (8th) mistakenly believing it is the 9th (day of Arafah). The error is discovered after the fact.
The scenario assumes they acted based on a mistaken sighting of the crescent or miscalculation.
2
Identify the two proofs from al-Hidayah
First proof: If remedy is impossible (i.e., the time for standing has passed), the Hajj is considered valid because the obligation has been discharged and cannot be repeated. Second proof: No act of worship can be performed before its prescribed time; therefore the Hajj is invalid because it was performed before its time.
The 'first proof' and 'second proof' are referred to in the commentary but not quoted verbatim.
3

An objector might cite exceptions like paying sadaqat al-fitr before its time or offering Asr prayer at Arafah before its time. The response is that these exceptions are themselves contrary to qiyas and cannot be used as analogies.
This defense strengthens the second proof but does not override the first.
4
Determine the practical consequence
If the error is discovered after the time for remedy, the mufti must choose between the two proofs. The passage does not decisively choose one, but the first proof (validity) may be preferred due to the difficulty of requiring repetition.
The mufta bihi is not settled; the scholar must weigh the proofs.

Vowing to Walk for Hajj: The Obligation to Walk Until Tawaf
Ruling on vowing to walk for Hajj

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on fulfilling a vow to walk for Hajj and identify the point at which riding becomes permissible.

Core Idea
A vow to walk for Hajj obligates walking only until the obligatory circumambulation; thereafter, riding is permitted.

Fulfilling a vow (nadhr) is a serious obligation in Islam. However, the scope of a vow is defined by its wording. This lesson examines a specific case: one who vows to perform Hajj on foot. The text from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies exactly how much walking is required and when riding becomes permissible. Understanding this helps you avoid common errors in interpreting vows.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
First, recognize that the act is a vow (nadhr) to perform Hajj while walking. The ruling derived is that walking becomes wajib (obligatory) due to the vow, not fard.
The obligation arises from the vow itself, not from the inherent nature of Hajj.
2
Determine the extent of walking
The text explicitly states 'until he performs the obligatory circumambulation' (Tawaf al-Fard, i.e., Tawaf al-Ziyarah). This is the limit beyond which the vow no longer applies.
After Tawaf al-Ziyarah, the pilgrim may ride for the remaining rituals like sa'ee and stoning.
3
Understand the basis in usul
The principle is that a vow must be fulfilled according to its wording (al-nadhr bi al-mashyak yujibu al-mashy). The word 'mashyan' (walking) is interpreted literally.
Hanafi usul holds that such a vow is binding and not subject to modification without necessity.
4
Identify what is not required
The vow does not require walking for the entire Hajj; once the essential circumambulation is done, the condition is satisfied. Riding after that does not violate the vow.
This distinction prevents overburdening the one who made the vow.
5
Apply to a similar scenario
Consider someone who vows to walk to the mosque every Friday. They must walk each time; if they ride on one Friday, they have violated the vow and may owe kaffarah (expiation).
The limit in that case would be the entire journey each time, not just a part.

Buyer's Authority to Release a Slave-Girl in Ihram
Ruling on buying a slave-girl in ihram

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on a buyer's authority to release a slave-girl from ihram and identify the condition that makes her ihram valid.

Core Idea
A buyer may release a slave-girl from ihram by cutting hair/nails or intercourse, but only if her ihram was with the owner's permission; otherwise it is invalid.

The state of ihram imposes restrictions, but certain acts can lift it. When a slave-girl in ihram is sold, the buyer inherits the authority to release her. This lesson unpacks the conditions and methods, highlighting the importance of the owner's permission for a valid ihram.

Guided Path
1
Establish the validity of the slave's ihram
First, check whether the slave-girl entered ihram with the permission of her owner. The text stresses that 'with permission' ('bi al-idhn') is attached to 'muhrimah' (in ihram). Without permission, her ihram is not considered legally.
This is a unique Hanafi condition: a slave's ihram without master's consent is invalid ab initio.
2
Identify the buyer's legal standing
The buyer, through the purchase, takes the place of the seller (al-mushtari qa'im maqam al-ba'i'). Therefore, whatever the seller could do (release the slave from ihram), the buyer can also do.
This is a form of substitution (qiyam maqam) common in sales of persons with legal states.
3
List the methods of release
The buyer may release her by: (1) cutting her hair, (2) clipping her nails, or (3) having intercourse with her. All three methods are effective in removing ihram.
These are the same methods that a husband or owner can use to release a free woman in ihram.
4
Rank the methods by merit
The text states that releasing by cutting hair or nails is 'awla' (more meritorious) than releasing by intercourse. This is because it achieves the purpose (making her permissible for intercourse) without the act itself, which is considered superior.
The order of preference is: cutting hair/nails first, then intercourse if desired.
5
Apply to an invalid scenario
If the slave-girl entered ihram without the owner's permission, her ihram is invalid (la i'tibara lahu). Therefore, she is not actually in ihram, and no release is needed; the buyer may have intercourse immediately without any action.
This is an important distinction: absence of valid ihram means no restrictions apply.
