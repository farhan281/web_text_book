Giving Zakah to a Student of Knowledge Even if Wealthy
Ruling on permissibility of giving zakah to a seeker of knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on giving zakah to a wealthy student of knowledge and identify the proof and rationale for this permissibility.

Core Idea
A student of knowledge may receive zakah even if wealthy because his devotion to learning prevents him from earning.

This lesson examines a special concession in the Hanafi school regarding zakah distribution. While the general rule prohibits giving zakah to someone who owns a nisab, an exception is made for those who have dedicated themselves to seeking sacred knowledge. The passage presents the ruling, its evidence, and the reasoning that supports this unique position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? Giving zakah to a student of knowledge. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) even if the student owns a nisab.
This is a special concession, not a general permission for all wealthy recipients.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is a hadith: 'It is permissible to give zakah to a student of knowledge, even if he has expenses for forty years.' This is cited from al-Mabsut and other sources.
The hadith is from al-Tabarani and is considered acceptable by the Hanafi school.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The hadith explicitly states permissibility. The rationale (ta'lil) is that the student has devoted himself to learning and is unable to earn, creating a need for support.
The need is not for wealth but for time to study.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) require the recipient to be poor and do not allow giving zakah to a wealthy person. The Hanafi school makes an exception for students, warriors, and those cut off from wealth.
The Hanafi position is a minority view within the broader madhhab landscape.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The mufta bihi (relied-upon) position is that it is permissible. Later authorities like Ibn Abidin affirm this based on al-Mabsut and the analogy with the zakah agent.
The ruling is supported by both textual proof and legal reasoning (qiyas).

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the ruling on the categories of recipients of zakah. The matn (original text) states that it is preferable to give to the most needy. The commentator explains that 'more needy' means those who are more righteous, more abstinent, or more beneficial for the Muslims. He then includes the category of 'student of knowledge' and provides a detailed investigation from later Hanafi scholars. The key ruling discussed is the permissibility of giving zakah to a student of knowledge even if he owns a nisab (minimum wealth threshold). This is based on a hadith narrated by al-Tabarani, and strengthened by the rationale that the student has devoted his time to learning, making him unable to earn. The author cites Ibn Abidin's research, which traces this ruling to al-Mabsut (a foundational text of the Hanafi school, part of Zahir al-Riwayah). The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that it is permissible, provided the student is genuinely occupied with seeking knowledge and is in need of support.

Key Terms
طالب العلم (talib al-'ilm): A person who is actively engaged in seeking sacred knowledge (shar'i 'ilm), having devoted himself to learning and teaching.
زكاة (zakah): The obligatory alms paid on wealth that reaches the nisab, one of the pillars of Islam.
نصاب (nisab): The minimum amount of wealth that makes zakah obligatory. For gold: 20 mithqal; for silver: 200 dirhams.

The Measure of Grains in Zakat al-Fitr
Ruling with proof on grain measures

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligatory measures for zakat al-fitr for wheat, its derivatives, raisins, dates, and barley, and explain the evidence from Companion practice.

Core Idea
Wheat and its derivatives require half a sa'; dates and barley require a full sa' — based on practice of the Companions.

This lesson examines the specific quantities prescribed for zakat al-fitr from various grains. The matn of al-Wiqayah states clear measures: half a sa' for wheat, its flour and sawiq, and for raisins; a full sa' for dates and barley. Although some hadith are weak, the practice of the major Companions establishes these rulings. We will explore the evidence and the intra-Hanafi difference regarding raisins.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
First, determine what act is being ruled upon (zakat al-fitr) and its level of obligation (wajib). Then focus on the specific measures being assigned.
Zakat al-fitr is wajib, not fard; this passage gives the quantities.
2
List the Grains and Their Measures
From the matn: wheat (burr), its flour (daqeeq), its sawiq (sawiq), and raisins (zabeeb) require half a sa'. Dates (tamr) and barley (sha'ir) require a full sa'.
The commentary clarifies that flour and sawiq of wheat follow wheat's ruling.
3
Examine the Evidence
The half-sa' for wheat is supported by the practice of prominent Companions (Umar, Abu Bakr, etc.) recorded by al-Tahawi, al-Bayhaqi. The full sa' for dates/barley is from sahih hadith (Bukhari, Muslim).
Although some marfu' hadith for the half-sa' are weak, the collective practice of the Companions provides sufficient proof.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf for Raisins
Hasan ibn Ziyad narrated from Abu Hanifa that raisins require a full sa' (like dates). Some later scholars (al-Bahnasi, al-Burhan) preferred this. However, the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the matn is half a sa'.
Some fatwa consider both quantity and value for raisins.
5
Apply to Practice
When giving zakat al-fitr, use the correct measure: half a sa' of wheat, flour, sawiq, or raisins; one sa' of dates or barley. The sa' is about 2.5 kg for wheat? Actually Hanafi sa' = 8 ratls ≈ 3.26 kg, but for wheat the weight varies.
The specific weight equivalences are given in other lessons; here focus on the volume measure.

Definition of the Sa' Measure for Zakat al-Fitr
Defining the standard sa' measure for zakat al-fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of the sa', identify the grains used to standardize it, and explain the rationale behind choosing those grains.

Core Idea
The Hanafi sa' is a volume measure standardized by eight ratls of mung beans or lentils due to their uniform grain size.

The sa' is a fundamental measure for zakat al-fitr, but its precise definition matters. The Hanafi school anchors the sa' to a specific volume that holds eight ratls of mung beans or lentils. This lesson unpacks why these grains are chosen and how the definition ensures consistency across different food types.

Guided Path
1
State the basic definition
The sa' is originally a volume measure that holds eight ratls of a substance. Identify the key elements: 'kil' (measure) and 'thamaniyat artal' (eight ratls).
The definition appears at the start of the passage: 'الصَّاعُ : كيلُ يسعُ فيه ثمانية أرطال'.
2
Identify the standard grains
The passage specifies that the eight ratls are measured using mung beans (majj or mash) or lentils. Note that these grains are used as a reference standard, not as the actual food given.
The text says: 'فقدّر بثمانية أرطال من المج: وهو الماش، أو من العدس'.
3
Understand the rationale
Mung beans and lentils have minimal variation in grain size and density (لقلة التَّفاوت بين حباتهما عظماً وصغراً، وتخلخلاً واكتنازاً). This consistency ensures that the same volume always corresponds to the same weight, making them reliable for calibration.
Other grains like wheat and barley vary greatly, so they would give inconsistent volume-to-weight ratios.
4
Follow the empirical evidence
The author recounts an experiment: he weighed mung beans, good quality wheat, and barley, and found mung beans heaviest. Therefore, a container that holds eight ratls of mung beans will hold less than eight ratls of wheat. This proves that the sa' must be defined by the uniform grain, not by any grain.
This rational argument (dalīl ‘aqlī) shows why wheat cannot be used as a standard.
5
Apply the definition
When paying zakat al-fitr, the food must be measured using a container that holds eight ratls of mung beans (or lentils). That volume is one sa'. The actual food (e.g., wheat, dates) is then filled to that same volume, not weighed.
This ensures the correct amount is given regardless of the food's density.

The Sa' Measure: Weight or Volume? The Hanafi Dispute on Zakat al-Fitr
Defining the sa' measure and resolving the Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the weight-based and volume-based views for half a sa' of wheat in zakat al-fitr and state the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Volume (kayl) is the standard for the sa' in zakat al-fitr, not weight—even within the Hanafi school, the relied-upon position confirms this.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya defines the sa' measure used for Sadaqat al-Fitr and addresses a key internal dispute among the Hanafi imams: should half a sa' of wheat be measured by weight or by volume? The resolution hinges on the nature of the sa' as a standardized volume container, calibrated using grains with consistent density. Understanding this dispute clarifies the correct method for fulfilling the wajib of zakat al-fitr.

Guided Path
1
Define the sa' as a volume measure
Identify that the sa' is a container holding exactly 8 ratls, and that it is calibrated using mung beans or lentils because their volume-weight ratio is consistent.
The text emphasizes that other grains vary too much in density.
2
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
State that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad differed on whether half a sa' of wheat should be given by weight (4 ratls) or by volume (kayl).
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's view aligned here? Actually only Muhammad's is mentioned; Abu Yusuf not mentioned.
3
Follow the proof for each position
Examine why Abu Hanifa allowed weight: presumably convenience and common practice. Examine why Muhammad insisted on volume: the sa' is originally a volume measure, and weight can vary with grain density.
The text provides an experiment: mung bean is heavier than wheat, so a mung-bean-calibrated sa' holds less wheat by weight.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response and tarjih
The commentator and later authorities (Fath al-Qadir, al-Hidayah) conclude that volume (kayl) is the criterion, not weight.
The mufta bihi is Imam Muhammad's view.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If someone gives 4 ratls of wheat by weight, according to Abu Hanifa it suffices; according to Muhammad it may not if the volume is less than half a sa'.
The practical consequence affects the validity of the zakat payment.

The Standard Sa' for Sadaqat al-Fitr: Volume or Weight?
Defining the standard sa' and its weight equivalents with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of the Iraqi sa' according to the Hanafi school, identify the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding weight vs volume, and explain why the sa' is standardized using mung beans.

Core Idea
The Hanafi sa' is a volume measure defined by eight ratls of mung beans, not by weight of wheat.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies the standard measurement for the sa' used in Sadaqat al-Fitr. While it might seem straightforward to pay by weight, the early Hanafi scholars realized that grains vary in density, so they fixed the sa' as a volume measure using mung beans or lentils as a reference. We'll explore the reasoning, the internal difference between Abu Hanifa and his student Muhammad, and how this compares to the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
The sa' is eight ratls by weight of mung beans or lentils, making it a volume measure. This is the standard for the Iraqi sa'.
The measure is by volume, not by weight of the grain being given.
2
Understand the rationale
Grains differ in weight per volume. Mung beans and lentils have equal weight and volume, providing a stable reference.
Wheat, barley, etc. vary; using them to define the measure would cause inconsistency.
3
Analyse the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa allows half a sa' of wheat by weight (four ratls), while Muhammad insists on volume, even if weight differs.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is Muhammad's volume-based definition.
4
Distinguish Iraqi vs Hijazi sa'
The Hanafi school uses the Iraqi sa' (8 ratls); the Shafi'i uses the Hijazi sa' (5 1/3 ratls).
This leads to different actual amounts given.
5
Apply the proper measurement
For Sadaqat al-Fitr, half a sa' of wheat is standard, but the measure must be taken by volume using the Iraqi sa' container.
Simply weighing 4 ratls of wheat is not sufficient if the wheat is light and does not fill half the sa' volume.

The Obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr: Conditions and Nisab
States who must pay Sadaqat al-Fitr and defines the Nisab that triggers the obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr and distinguish its Nisab from that of Zakat al-Mal.

Core Idea
Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib on any free Muslim with surplus Nisab, even if that wealth does not grow.

In this passage, the commentator explains the ruling from al-Wiqaya concerning Sadaqat al-Fitr. Unlike Zakat al-Mal, which requires the wealth to be productive (nama') and a full year to pass (hawl), Sadaqat al-Fitr is triggered simply by possessing a surplus Nisab above one's basic needs. This lesson will walk you through the conditions and the key distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is Sadaqat al-Fitr. The ruling is wajib (obligatory) upon a free Muslim who possesses a Nisab of Zakat, even if that wealth does not grow.
2
Identify the conditions for obligation
Conditions: being free, being Muslim, and possessing a Nisab of surplus wealth above basic needs (al-haajah al-asliyyah). The passage emphasizes that growth (nama') is not a condition.
3
Locate the dalil (proof)
The commentator cites the hadith: 'لا صدقة إلا عن ظهر غنى' (No charity except from surplus wealth), narrated by Ahmad. This hadith establishes that charity is only due from wealth that exceeds one's needs.
4
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof indicates the ruling)
The operative phrase 'عن ظهر غنى' means 'from surplus wealth'. 'Ghina' (wealth) according to Sharia is defined as possessing a Nisab surplus over basic needs. This surplus triggers the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr even if the wealth is not productive.
5
Distinguish from Zakat al-Mal
Zakat al-Mal requires both nama' (growth) and hawl (passing of one lunar year). For Sadaqat al-Fitr, neither is required; only possession of surplus Nisab at the time of obligation (e.g., sunset of the last day of Ramadan) is necessary.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of al-Wiqaya concerning the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. It first states that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib (obligatory) upon every free Muslim who possesses a Nisab of Zakat, even if that wealth does not grow (i.e., it is not subject to annual increase). Additionally, such a person becomes prohibited from receiving Zakat or other charities meant for the poor. The commentator explains that the Nisab referred to is a surplus amount above one's basic needs (al-haajah al-asliyyah). If the surplus wealth is from gold, silver, pasturing livestock, or trade goods, then Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib even before a year passes (unlike Zakat al-Mal, which requires a full year). If the surplus comes from other types of property (e.g., a house not used for residence or trade, but of high value that reaches the Nisab), then Sadaqat al-Fitr is also wajib even though Zakat upon such a house is not wajib. This Nisab is called Nisab of prohibition (Nisab hurman), because it prohibits the person from receiving Zakat. The key difference from Zakat is that growth (namaa) is not a condition for Sadaqat al-Fitr's Nisab, while it is a condition for Zakat. The commentary details the linguistic point of 'yamnu' (from namā) and explains the three restrictions for a house (not for residence, not for trade) to trigger the obligation. The proof is the hadith 'la sadaqata illa 'an zahri ghina' (no charity except from surplus wealth), where 'ghina' (wealth) is defined as possessing a Nisab above needs. Finally, the phrase 'for himself' indicates that the obligation is personal and not conditional upon having dependents or an excuse.

Key Terms
نصاب (nisab): A specific minimum amount of wealth (e.g., 200 dirhams or 20 mithqal of gold) that triggers the obligation of Zakat or Sadaqat al-Fitr; here it is the surplus above basic needs.
نماء (nama'): Growth or increase; in Zakat it means the wealth is potentially productive (through trade, livestock reproduction, etc.). For Sadaqat al-Fitr, growth is not required.
حول (hawl): The passage of a lunar year; for Zakat al-Mal, the Nisab must be possessed for a full year. For Sadaqat al-Fitr, no year is required, only possession at the time of obligation.
ظهر غنى (zahr ghina): Surplus wealth; the hadith 'la sadaqata illa 'an zahr ghina' means no charity is due except from wealth that exceeds one's basic needs.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the obligation of Sadaqat al-Fitr. The commentary (sharh) expands on the base text (matn).

The matn states that Sadaqat al-Fitr is wajib upon every free Muslim who possesses the nisab of zakat, even if that wealth has not increased or completed a year. The same nisab makes it prohibited for that person to receive zakat or other sadaqat intended for the poor.

Who Must You Pay Zakat al-Fitr For?
Commentary on Zakat al-Fitr obligations for dependents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the categories of dependents for whom Zakat al-Fitr is obligatory according to the Hanafi school and explain the rationale based on the hadith and the concept of guardianship (wilaya).

Core Idea
Zakat al-Fitr is wajib on the head of household for every dependent under his maintenance, including owned slaves regardless of religion, but not for those over whom he lacks complete guardianship.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies the scope of Zakat al-Fitr obligations. The original matn lists those for whom the household head must pay—his poor child, his owned servant, even if that servant is a mudabbir, umm walad, or disbeliever—and those for whom he does not pay, such as his wife and adult child. The commentary expands each term and provides the evidential basis from the hadith of Ibn 'Umar, then addresses an intra-Hanafi disagreement on jointly-owned slaves. Understanding this helps you apply the ruling precisely, avoiding common errors like including the fetus or excluding a non-Muslim dependent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act under discussion is Zakat al-Fitr. Determine the ruling category: is it fard, wajib, sunnah? The hadith uses the word 'farada', indicating obligation, but because the evidence is zanni al-dalala, the Hanafi school classifies it as wajib, not fard.
The distinction between fard and wajib affects the strength of the obligation and consequences of denial.
2
Locate the primary evidence (dalil)
The hadith of Ibn 'Umar is the central proof: 'The Messenger of Allah made Zakat al-Fitr obligatory: a sa' of barley or a sa' of dates upon every free person or slave, male or female, from the Muslims.' This is reported by the six major hadith compilers.
A second narration from al-Daraqutni adds: '...from those whom you support.' This highlights the condition of maintenance.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (how the proof yields the ruling)
The imperative phrase 'farada' indicates a binding command. The phrase 'on every free or slave' generalizes the obligation to all who are under one's authority. The additional narration 'from those whom you support' specifies that the cause (sabab) is the relationship of maintenance and guardianship.
This is why the obligation extends to owned slaves even if they are non-Muslims, but not to a hired servant or a fetus.
4
Apply the principles to each category
Using the sabab of maintenance and wilaya, the commentary explains each exception: the child must be poor (otherwise the child pays from his own wealth), the servant must be owned (not hired), the slave must be under full ownership (not a mukatib or runaway), and the wife and adult child are excluded because guardianship is limited.
The key is complete guardianship and financial responsibility.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on jointly-owned slaves
According to Abu Hanifa, if two partners jointly own a slave, neither partner has complete guardianship because the slave cannot be physically divided, so Zakat al-Fitr is not obligatory on either. The two companions (Sahibayn) hold that effective guardianship exists and the obligation is shared proportionally.
The relied-upon position for fatwa is Abu Hanifa's view, as recorded in al-Hidayah and al-Binaya.

Core Idea
Fitrah is wajib for every dependent under your authority, even a non-Muslim slave, but not for those beyond your full control.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah clarifies the scope of sadaqat al-fitr. It establishes that the obligation is tied to being the head of a household responsible for maintenance and having effective ownership. The text then lists several categories of people for whom fitrah is not due, each with a specific legal reasoning. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly fulfilling this wajib obligation.

Guided Path

Identify the act and its ruling
Sadaqat al-fitr is wajib (obligatory) upon every free Muslim who possesses wealth above basic needs. The passage specifies for whom this obligation must be fulfilled: every dependent under one's care.

2

The author cites the hadith of Abu Hurayrah narrated by al-Tahawi, which states that the Prophet would give fitrah for every young child he supported, including free and slave, even a Christian debtor.

The passage lists exceptions: wife (her wealth, not husband's), adult sane child, wealthy child, mukatab slave, trade slave, and absconding/lost/usurped slave. Each is based on deficiency of maintenance, authority, or effective ownership.

The Shafi'i school obligates fitrah for a wife and minor child regardless of wealth, while the Hanafi school does not for a wife (she pays from her own wealth) or for a wealthy child. The Hanafi position is based on the principle that the obligation follows effective maintenance and authority.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) affects who actually pays in family scenarios.

Core Idea
Zakat al-Fitr can be paid early, and it's recommended to do so, but if delayed it remains obligatory.

Many Muslims assume Zakat al-Fitr must be paid only on the day of Eid. The Hanafi school, however, permits and even recommends early payment, based on a clear analogy with zakat on wealth and the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him). This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and a minor difference among later Hanafi authorities about how early one may pay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is paying Zakat al-Fitr before the day of Eid. The ruling is that it is permissible (ja'iz) and recommended (mandub).
The base text says 'ولو قدمت جاز' (if given early, it is permissible) and 'وندب تعجيلها' (its early payment is recommended).
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is qiyas (analogy) with zakat on wealth. Just as zakat on wealth may be paid before the year completes because the cause (nisab) exists, so too Zakat al-Fitr may be paid before dawn because the cause (nisab) exists.
A hadith is also cited for the recommendation: the Prophet used to give it before the Eid prayer.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The operative cause (illa) is the existence of nisab, which is the cause of obligation. The condition of dawn is only for performance, not for obligation. Hence, payment before the condition is valid.
Hanafi usul distinguishes shart (condition) from sabab (cause).
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Some later Hanafi works (al-Bahr, al-Jawhara) restrict early payment to after the start of Ramadan. The majority view (al-Hidayah, etc.) allows it even before Ramadan.
This is an intra-Hanafi legitimate difference.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The majority view is authenticated in multiple authoritative works and is the mufta bihi (operative position for fatwa).
The commentator notes that al-Hidayah, al-Bazzaziyya, al-Kafi, and al-Tabyin authenticate the absolute permissibility.

Commentary
This passage discusses the permissibility and recommendation of paying Zakat al-Fitr before the day of Eid. The base text states that paying early is permissible, with no distinction between different times (i.e., any time before Eid is allowed), and it is recommended to pay early. However, if one delays it past Eid, the obligation does not drop; it must still be paid. The commentary explains the rationale: the time of dawn is a condition (shart) for the obligation to perform the payment, but the cause (sabab) of the obligation is owning the nisab (minimum wealth). Since early payment is allowed before the condition is met (as long as the cause exists), it is analogous to paying zakat on wealth before the year completes. The commentator notes a difference of opinion: the preferred view in many Hanafi works (like al-Hidayah) allows payment even before Ramadan, but some later authorities (like in al-Bahr and al-Jawhara) restrict it to after Ramadan begins. The recommendation is based on the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) who used to give zakat al-fitr before the Eid prayer and instructed to 'keep them from begging on this day.' This hadith is from al-Hakim. Finally, the obligation is not dropped by delay because the benefit is for the poor, and the act of worship is rational, so it must be performed eventually.

Key Terms
تقدم (taqdim): Giving zakat al-fitr before its time (before dawn of Eid).
تعجيل (ta'jil): Expediting or hastening the payment.
شرط الأداء (shart al-ada'): Condition for performing the payment; here it is the arrival of dawn on Eid day.

The Validity and Recommendation of Early Payment of Zakat al-Fitr
Commentary on early payment, recommendation, and validity of Zakat al-Fitr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on early payment of Zakat al-Fitr and apply the analogy to zakat on wealth to justify its validity.

Core Idea
Early payment of Zakat al-Fitr is valid and recommended at any time, because time is a condition, not the cause of obligation.

Many assume Zakat al-Fitr must be paid on the eve or day of Eid, but the Hanafi school allows early payment. This passage from the commentary on al-Wiqāyah clarifies the ruling, the evidence, and an internal disagreement. Let's explore the legal reasoning behind this flexibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The matn states that if Zakat al-Fitr is paid early, it is valid (jāz). The basis is the analogy to zakat on wealth, where payment before the year cycle is also valid.
Early payment means before the day of Eid.
2
Distinguish the cause of obligation from the condition of performance
The time (Eid) is a condition (shart) for the obligation to pay, while the cause (sabab) is owning the nisab. Since the cause exists at the time of early payment (ownership of nisab), one may pay before the condition is fulfilled.
This is a key usul principle: conditions do not block early performance when the cause is present.
3
Apply the legal analogy to zakat on wealth
Just as annual zakat can be paid before the completion of the lunar year if one owns the nisab, Zakat al-Fitr can be paid before Eid if one owns the nisab. The time is only a condition, not an unalterable deadline.
This qiyas is jaliyy (clear) and is the primary evidence.
4
Recognize the recommended timing and its proof
Despite the validity of early payment at any time, the Prophet (ﷺ) used to pay before the Eid prayer, instructing to 'make them independent of begging on this day'. Thus, it is recommended (mustahabb) to pay early, ideally before the prayer.
The hadith 'Agnūhum 'an al-ṭawāf fī hādhā al-yawm' is cited.
5
Note the intra-madhhab disagreement and the relied-upon position
Some Hanafi scholars (like those in al-Bahr and al-Jawhara) restrict early payment to after Ramadan, but the majority (al-Hidayah, al-Bazaziyyah, etc.) allow it unconditionally. The fatwa is on the majority view.
The minority view is a legitimate ikhtilaf but not adopted for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn of al-Wiqāyah regarding Sadaqat al-Fitr (Zakat al-Fitr). The commentator explains each part of the base text.

First, the matn states that if Zakat al-Fitr is paid early (before Eid), it is valid (jāz) without any condition of a specific interval between the time of payment and the due time. It is recommended (nudība) to hasten the payment, and if it is delayed past Eid, it does not fall off but remains a debt.

The commentator clarifies that 'if it is paid early' means the person pays it before the day of Eid, provided they own the nisāb (minimum wealth) at the time of payment. The validity is because the time (Eid day) is only a condition for the obligation to pay, while the actual cause of obligation is the nisāb on the day of Eid. Since the cause (niṢāb) exists at the time of payment (if the person already owns it), it is permissible to pay before the condition (time) is fulfilled. This is analogous to paying the annual zakat on wealth before the completion of the lunar year: one may pay early once one has the nisāb, because the year is a condition, not the cause.

As for 'without a separation', the commentator explains that there is no distinction between times: if one pays before Ramadan, it is valid. This is the authenticated position in major Hanafi works such as al-Hidāyah, al-Bazāziyyah, al-Kāfī, and al-Tabyīn. However, a different view from 'al-Bahr' (following al-Jawharah from al-Zāhiriyyah) holds that it is not permissible to pay before Ramadan. The first view is the relied-upon position for fatwa.

Regarding the recommendation (nudīb), the commentator cites the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him): he used to give Zakat al-Fitr before going out to the Eid prayer, instructing his Companions to make the poor independent of begging on that day. This hadith is narrated by al-Hākim in his work on hadith sciences, and similar narrations exist in the main hadith collections.

Finally, the commentator explains that if Zakat al-Fitr is delayed past Eid, it does not become void; rather, it must still be paid. The reason is that the intent of worship in this obligation is rational—it is meant to fulfill the need of the poor and save them from having to beg on the day of Eid. Therefore, the time for payment is not strictly limited; it remains an obligation until it is discharged.

Key Terms
تقديم (taqdīm): Paying Zakat al-Fitr before its due time (the day of Eid), which is valid as long as the payer owns the nisab at the time of payment.
تعجيل (taʿjīl): Hastening the payment of Zakat al-Fitr to before the Eid prayer, which is recommended (mustahabb) based on the Prophet's practice.
فصل (faṣl): The interval or separation between times; 'bila fasl' means there is no condition of a specific interval between the time of payment and the due time, so early payment is valid at any time.
