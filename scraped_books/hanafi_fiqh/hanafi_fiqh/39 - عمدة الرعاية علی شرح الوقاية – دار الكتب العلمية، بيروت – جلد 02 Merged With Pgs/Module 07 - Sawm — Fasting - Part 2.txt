Prohibited Days for Nafl Fast: Eid and Tashriq
Ruling on prohibited days for nafl fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five prohibited days for nafl fast and explain why starting a fast on them does not obligate completion or qada.

Core Idea
Starting a fast on prohibited days is haram; no need to complete or make up.

The matn lists five days on which it is prohibited to begin a voluntary fast: Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the three days after it (Tashriq). If someone starts a nafl fast on these days, the act is invalid from the start. Consequently, there is no obligation to complete it nor to make it up if broken. This lesson examines the ruling and its underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five prohibited days
List the days: Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the three days following Eid al-Adha (11th, 12th, 13th of Dhul Hijjah). These are called al-ayyam al-munhiyyah.
The prohibition applies to voluntary fasting only; obligatory fasts (e.g., missed Ramadan) are not valid on these days either.
2
State the ruling for starting a fast on these days
It is haram to begin a voluntary fast on these days. If one does start, the fast is invalid (fasid) and does not become binding.
This is because the act is inherently prohibited.
3
Explain the consequence of invalidity
Since the fast is invalid from the start, there is no requirement to complete it nor to make it up (qada). The person should break the fast immediately.
This contrasts with a valid nafl fast that is broken without excuse, where qada may be required (see next lesson).

Breaking a Nafl Fast: The Hanafi Dispute and Hospitality Excuse
Hanafi dispute on breaking nafl fast and hospitality excuse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi riwayat on breaking a nafl fast without excuse and apply the conditions for the hospitality excuse.

Core Idea
Breaking a nafl fast without excuse is prohibited (zahir al-riwaya); hospitality excuse allowed before noon.

This passage presents a key Hanafi dispute: once a voluntary fast is started, can it be broken without an excuse? The matn records two narrations: one (zahir al-riwaya) prohibits it, comparing it to invalidating a deed; another (from al-Muntaqa) permits it because qada substitutes. The commentary favors the first. It also details the excuse of hospitality: before midday, if the host would be offended, one may break and make up; after midday, only to avoid parental disobedience.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two riwayat on breaking without excuse
Riwāyah ūlā: Not allowed without excuse (invalidating the act). Riwāyah thāniyah: Allowed because qada can substitute. The phrase 'in one narration' refers to the first, which is zahir al-riwaya.
The second riwaya is recorded in al-Muntaqa but is considered weaker.
2
Analyze the proof for each riwaya
First riwaya: general principle that one should not invalidate good deeds. Second riwaya: hadith 'The voluntary faster is commander of himself' and qada as replacement.
The hadith is cited but its chain is considered weak (ikhtilaf fi sanadihi).
3
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (zahir al-riwaya) is that breaking without a valid excuse is not permissible. Qada is required if broken with an excuse.
This is the view preferred by al-Haskafi and others.
4
Understand the hospitality excuse
Before midday: if the host would be offended by the guest not eating, the guest may break and make up. After midday: breaking is not allowed unless it involves disobedience to parents.
The host's satisfaction is considered; if he is not offended, breaking is not permissible.

Obligation to Abstain When an Excuse Ends During Ramadan
Ruling on four categories when excuse ends during Ramadan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for each of the four categories (child reaching puberty, new Muslim, menstruating woman, traveler) and distinguish which ones must make up the day.

Core Idea
When an excuse ends during Ramadan, abstain for the rest of the day; but a new Muslim and newly pubescent child do not make up that day.

This lesson addresses a common question during Ramadan: what must a person do when their excuse for not fasting ends during the daytime? The Hanafi school rules that all such individuals must abstain from eating, drinking, etc. for the remainder of the day out of respect for the sanctity of Ramadan. However, whether they must make up that day (qada') depends on whether they were under an obligation to fast before the excuse ended.

Guided Path
1

The difference is that the first two were not obligated to fast at the beginning of the day, so no qada' is required.
4
Analyze the proof
The text quotes hadiths about breaking fast out of respect for a host, supporting the principle that certain situations override the fast but require qada'. Here, the sanctity of Ramadan overrides the need for a full valid fast.
The hadiths are not directly about these categories but demonstrate that the sanctity of the moment can create an obligation.
5
Apply the rule
In practice, if any such event occurs during daylight, the person must immediately stop eating/drinking and continue abstaining until sunset. The first two need not make up that day; the latter two must make it up later.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Ruling on Imsaak and Qadaa for Those Who Become Eligible During the Day
Ruling with proof for imsaak and qadaa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on imsaak and qadaa for a child reaching puberty, a disbeliever accepting Islam, a menstruating woman becoming pure, and a traveler arriving during a Ramadan day, and explain the rationale based on ahliyyah.

Core Idea
Imsaak is wajib in Ramadan for respect, but qadaa is not required if one lacked capacity at dawn.

Ramadan fasting is an obligation that begins at dawn. But what happens when a person becomes eligible to fast mid-day? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling: the sanctity of Ramadan requires restraint (imsaak) for the rest of the day, but make-up (qadaa) is not required if the person lacked legal capacity (ahliyyah) at the start. This lesson unpacks that ruling, its evidence, and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the individuals and their rulings
List the four categories: child reaching puberty, disbeliever accepting Islam, menstruating woman becoming pure, traveler arriving. For each, note the ruling on imsaak and qadaa as stated in the text.
2
Analyze the basis for distinguishing the first two
The text says the child and convert do not make up their day, even if they ate after intention. Understand why: they lacked ahliyyah at dawn, so no obligation was established.
Contrast with the traveler and menstruating woman who had capacity at dawn.
3
Examine the evidence (dalil)
The text cites hadith about breaking a voluntary fast for hospitality and the Ashura command to imsaak. See how these proofs support the principle of respecting the sanctity of the day without requiring qadaa for those who were not originally obligated.
4
Understand the concept of non-divisibility of the day
The commentary explains that a single day's fast cannot be partially obligatory: if the first part was not obligatory due to lack of capacity, the rest cannot become obligatory. This is key to the ruling for the child.
For the convert, the ruling is even stricter: his fast is not counted at all.
5
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
Consider a child who reaches puberty at 10 AM, intends to fast, then eats. According to the ruling, he must imsaak for the rest of the day but does not need to make up that day. Confirm with the text.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the chapter on nullifiers of fasting, explained with proof. The base text states that when certain individuals become eligible to fast during the day in Ramadan (a child reaching puberty, a disbeliever accepting Islam, a menstruating woman becoming pure, a traveler arriving home), they must restrain from eating, drinking, etc. for the remainder of the day out of respect for Ramadan. However, the first two (the child and the convert) do not have to make up that day, even if they ate after making an intention. The commentator clarifies that this is because they lacked the capacity (ahliyyah) for fasting at the beginning of the day, so performance (ada') was not obligatory, hence no make-up (qada') is required. The child, even if he reaches puberty before noon, his fast if he intends and does not eat is considered voluntary, not obligatory, because obligation cannot be partial for a single day. The convert's fasting does not count at all because he was not eligible at the start. In contrast, the traveler and menstruating woman are eligible (mukallaf) at the start, so they must make up the day if they break their fast, and if a traveler arrives after intending to break, his fast is valid if he intends anew, but in Ramadan it becomes obligatory to complete the day. The commentary cites hadith evidence for the permissibility of breaking a voluntary fast for a host, and for the command to restrain for the rest of the day on Ashura. The commentator also explains that the qadaa is not required because ada' was not wajib initially.

Key Terms
إمساك (imsaak): Refraining from eating, drinking, and other nullifiers of fast for the remainder of the day.
قضاء (qadaa'): Making up a missed obligatory fast on a different day.
أهلية (ahliyyah): Legal capacity to perform acts of worship; a child before puberty and a disbeliever lack ahliyyah for fasting.

Validity of Traveler's Fast After Intending to Break and Returning
Ruling with proof from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the validity of a fast for a traveler who intended to break, then returned and intended before midday.

Core Idea
A traveler's fast is valid if he intends before midday, and in Ramadan it is obligatory.

This lesson addresses a common scenario: a traveler begins the day intending to break his fast, then returns home and decides to fast. Is his fast valid? The Hanafi school, relying on the hadith of Ashura, rules that the fast is valid if the intention is made before the middle of the day. In Ramadan, it becomes obligatory to complete.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The act is: a traveler intends to break fast, then returns and intends to fast during the valid time (before midday). Ruling: the fast is valid (sahih), and in Ramadan it is obligatory (wajib).
The ruling distinguishes between non-Ramadan and Ramadan: valid vs. obligatory.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites the hadith of Ashura: the Prophet commanded those who had eaten to abstain from the remainder of the day, indicating that intention can be made mid-day.
This is a hadith ahad, so its thubut is zanni, but its dalala is considered qati (clear) for the Hanafi school.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The command to abstain after eating shows that a fast can be started after the day has begun, by intention. This supports the traveler's case: his intention after returning is effective if made before midday.
The key is that the fast does not require intention from the previous night; intention before midday suffices.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Some other madhhabs may require intention from the night for a valid fast. The Hanafi position allows flexibility, which is practically significant for travelers and those who become eligible later in the day.
The practical consequence: a traveler can salvage the fast upon return, rather than being forced to make it up.

No Qada' for Child Reaching Puberty or Disbeliever Converting During the Day
Ruling with explanation (ta'lil)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a child who reaches puberty or a disbeliever who accepts Islam during the day is not required to make up that day's fast.

Core Idea
Fasting obligation depends on eligibility at dawn; those who become eligible later owe no qada'.

A child may reach puberty or a disbeliever may convert to Islam in the middle of a day in Ramadan. Do they have to make up that day's fast? The Hanafi school rules that they do not, because the obligation to fast that day was never established without eligibility at the start of the day. This lesson unpacks the concept of ahliyyah and its role in the obligation of qada'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subjects and the ruling
Subjects: a child who reaches puberty (baligh) during the day, and a disbeliever who accepts Islam (aslama) during the day. Ruling: there is no qada' (make-up) for the fast of that day.
The ruling applies even if they acquire eligibility before midday.
2
Understand the reasoning: lack of ahliyyah at start of day
The obligation of fasting for a particular day is determined at the beginning of the day (true dawn). Since the child/disbeliever lacked eligibility at that moment, the fast was never obligatory on them for that day, so no qada' is due.
Ahliyyah requires being Muslim and of age (baligh).
3
Apply the principle to the child who reaches puberty before midday
Even if the child reaches puberty before midday, he may intend to fast and his fast will be counted as voluntary (nafl), not obligatory. He does not have to make it up if he breaks it, because it was never wajib.
The fast of a single day cannot be partially obligatory; it either is obligatory from dawn or not.
4
Distinguish between child and disbeliever
The child is eligible to perform a valid voluntary fast, so if he intends, his fast is valid as nafl. The disbeliever, however, is not eligible for any worship before Islam, so even if he converts and intends, his fast is not counted at all.
The difference is due to the legal capacity to perform acts of worship: child has partial eligibility (ahliyyat al-ada), disbeliever does not.

Rulings on Travel, Unconsciousness, and Insanity During Ramadan Fast
Ruling with rational proofs for three fasting scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rulings for a resident who travels during a fast, a person who becomes unconscious, and a person who becomes insane, and explain the underlying rationale (shubha, intention, removal of legal responsibility).

Core Idea
Once a fast is validly started, travel does not permit breaking it, and kaffarah is waived due to shubha; unconsciousness requires qada' except the day of recovery; total insanity throughout Ramadan removes all obligation.

This lesson examines three specific scenarios that can affect the validity and obligation of a Ramadan fast. Using rational proofs (dalil 'aqli), the Hanafi school derives clear rulings for each case. Understanding these rulings helps you distinguish between conditions that invalidate a fast and those that merely affect obligation or expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage addresses three distinct cases: (1) a resident who starts a fast and then travels during the day; (2) a person who becomes unconscious during Ramadan; (3) a person who becomes insane for the entire month. Read the Arabic and translation to locate each scenario.
2
Extract the ruling for the traveling resident
The ruling states that the resident must complete the fast and that if he breaks it, there is no kaffarah. The reasoning given is that travel does not permit breaking a fast already started; it only permits not starting. The absence of kaffarah is due to shubha (doubt) created by the existence of a valid excuse.
Note the principle: al-umur bi-maqasidiha – intention matters. The fast was started with intention to fast as a resident; travel later does not retroactively change that intention.
3
Extract the ruling for the unconscious person
The passage says he must make up all days he was unconscious except the day on which he regained consciousness (or if recovery occurred during the night). The rationale is that unconsciousness is like illness; it prevents intention, so those days are missed. The exception is the day of recovery because he likely had intention at the start of that day (before unconsciousness).
The commentary clarifies that if he knows he intended the first day, it is valid; if he knows he did not intend, it is invalid. The default is that he intended if unconsciousness began after dawn.
4
Extract the ruling for the insane person
If a person becomes insane before sunset of the first night and remains insane the entire Ramadan, he does not have to make up any fasts. This is because insanity removes legal responsibility (taklif). However, if he was conscious at the beginning of a night and then became insane, he makes up all days except that day (consensus).
The phrase 'junn kullahu' means total insanity throughout the month. Partial insanity may require qada' for days before onset.
5
Identify the proof type for each ruling
All three rulings are based on rational proofs (dalil 'aqli). The first uses the principle that travel does not permit breaking an already started fast (derived from the fact that the license to break fast is for not starting, not for stopping). The second analogizes unconsciousness to illness (qiyas) because both prevent intention. The third uses the principle that insanity removes legal responsibility (raf' al-qalam).
These proofs are consistent with Hanafi usul: they rely on the purpose (maqsad) of the rulings and the effective cause ('illa).

Commentary
This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'ayah is an expanded commentary on a ruling from al-Hidayah regarding a person who utters a formula that could be either a vow (nadhr) or an oath (yamin), specifically in the context of fasting (the chapter on fasting). The author presents six scenarios based on the speaker's intention when saying something like 'I will fast for the sake of Allah' or similar. The core ruling: The default, when no intention is made, is that such a formula is a vow (nadhr), obligating fulfillment. If broken, one must make up the fast (qada') but no expiation. However, if the speaker intends an oath (yamin) alone and negates the vow, it becomes an oath only, requiring expiation (kaffarah) upon breaking, but no makeup. If both are intended or the oath is intended without negating the vow, it is both a vow and an oath, so both qada' and kaffarah are due. The author then notes an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf holds that when both are intended (first case of the two), it is only a vow, not an oath; and when only the oath is intended without negating the vow (second case), it is only an oath, not a vow. Abu Hanifa (and the relied-upon position) says both are combined. The explanation elaborates on the relationship between vow and oath: a vow obligates a permissible act, which entails prohibition of its opposite, and prohibition of the lawful is the essence of an oath. This is supported by the Quranic verse (Surah al-Tahrim 66:1-2) where the Prophet's prohibition of Mariya was called an oath. Thus, the vow (the cause) implies the oath (the effect). When the speaker negates the vow and intends only the oath, the wording is taken metaphorically as an oath.

كفارة يمين (kaffarat yamin): Expiation for breaking an oath: freeing a slave, feeding ten poor people, or clothing them. If unable, fasting three days.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Intending Nadhr and Yamin Together
Intra-Hanafi dispute on combining intentions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf regarding the combination of nadhr (vow) and yamin (oath) intentions in one utterance, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
A single utterance can carry both a vow and an oath if intended, per Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, despite the objection of combining literal and metaphorical meanings.

When someone says 'Allah 'alayya...' (upon me is Allah's obligation) without specifying nadhr or yamin, the intention becomes crucial. This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya presents six possible intention scenarios and then delves into a key intra-Hanafi dispute: can the same utterance simultaneously activate the rulings of both a vow (nadhr) and an oath (yamin)? The answer depends on how we interpret the linguistic relationship between the two.

Guided Path
1
Understand the six intention categories
The author first lists six scenarios: (1) no intention, (2) intends both, (3) intends vow without negating oath, (4) intends vow while negating oath, (5) intends oath without negating vow, (6) intends oath while negating vow. This classification sets the stage.
These categories show that intention (niyyah) can vary, and the rulings depend on what is intended.
2
Recognize the relationship between nadhr and yamin
The text explains that a vow (nadhr) obligates a permissible act, which implies the prohibition of its opposite. Prohibiting the permissible is the essence of an oath (yamin). This is a necessary implication (dalalah iltizamiyyah), making yamin a metaphorical meaning for the literal nadhr wording.
The Quranic verse (Surah at-Tahrim, 1-2) is cited as evidence that prohibiting the lawful is considered a yamin.
3
Identify the objection: combining haqiqa and majaz
If yamin is a metaphorical meaning, then when someone intends both, the utterance would carry both the literal (haqiqa) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings. This combination is generally deemed impermissible in Hanafi usul.
This is the crux of the dispute: can one intent legitimately trigger both linguistic layers?
4
Compare the positions of the Hanafi imams
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that since yamin is only a necessary implication, not an intended metaphorical meaning, there is no actual combination. Therefore, both rulings apply. Abu Yusuf insists that the utterance cannot carry both, so only the literal meaning (the vow) applies.
The practical difference: if both apply, the person must fulfill the vow and, if the implied oath is broken, pay kaffarah. Abu Yusuf says only the vow applies.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the relationship between nadhr (vow) and yamin (oath) and whether one can intend both simultaneously when uttering a phrase like 'Allah 'alayya...'. The author first lists six possible cases of intention when someone makes such an utterance: (1) intending nothing, (2) intending both, (3) intending vow without negating oath, (4) intending vow while negating oath, (5) intending oath without negating vow, (6) intending oath while negating vow. This classification sets up the discussion. The commentary then cites al-Hidayah, which treats 'yamin' as a metaphorical meaning (majaz) for the phrase. The relationship between nadhr and yamin is explained: a vow obligates something permissible, which implies prohibition of its opposite—and the prohibition of what is permissible is the essence of a yamin. This is supported by the Quranic verse 'Why do you prohibit what Allah has made lawful for you?' (Surah at-Tahrim, 1), where the Prophet's prohibition of a lawful thing (honey or a slave-girl) was called a yamin. The commentators explain that this is a necessary indication (dalalah iltizamiyyah). However, on page 533, an objection arises: if yamin is a metaphorical meaning, then when someone utters a formula that is literally a nadhr and intends both nadhr and yamin, it would require combining the literal (haqiqa) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings in one utterance, which is generally impermissible. To avoid this, some Hanafi usul works assert that yamin is not a metaphorical meaning. The dispute among the Hanafi imams is then highlighted: Abu Yusuf argues that when someone says 'Allah 'alayya...' and intends both nadhr and yamin, or just yamin, the expression should be interpreted as only one of them (presumably the literal nadhr), because combining haqiqa and majaz is invalid. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, however, uphold that both meanings can be intended and both rulings apply, seeing no necessary contradiction. The commentary further discusses that the denial of yamin as majaz is not about its inherent nature but about whether it is actually intended by the wording; the wording itself is placed for nadhr, and yamin is only implied. This passage exemplifies the Hanafi school's careful attention to intention (niyyah) and the semantic relationship between legal terms.

Key Terms
النذر (al-nadhr): A vow: obligating upon oneself an act that the Lawgiver has not made obligatory.
اليمين (al-yamin): An oath: specifically, the prohibition of a permissible thing, often expressed as a statement like 'by Allah' or contextually.
المجاز (al-majaz): Metaphorical meaning: using a word for a related meaning other than its original, literal sense due to a relationship (here, necessary implication).
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): Literal meaning: the original, conventional meaning of a word (e.g., the word 'nadhr' meaning a vow).
تحلة أيمانكم (tahlilatu aymanikum): The dissolution or nullification of oaths, specifically by paying expiation (kaffarah) as mentioned in Surat al-Ma'idah.

Invalidators of I'tikaf: Intercourse and Sexual Acts
Ruling with proof from Quran on i'tikaf invalidators

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the acts that invalidate i'tikaf according to the Hanafi school, explain the Quranic proof (2:187), and distinguish between invalidating and sinful acts.

Core Idea
Intercourse and any sexual act leading to emission invalidates i'tikaf; other acts are sinful but do not break it.

This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies what exactly breaks i'tikaf. The Quran forbids sexual relations during i'tikaf, but the Hanafi school distinguishes between acts that directly invalidate and those that are merely prohibited. Understanding this distinction is crucial for a mu'takif to preserve his devotion without unnecessary repetition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on invalidators
List the acts that invalidate i'tikaf: intercourse (even forgetfully), and any sexual act (kissing, touching, thighing) only if emission occurs. Note that emission due to thought or looking does not invalidate.
The key nuance: emission must be caused by direct physical contact, not by internal reflection.
2
Locate the Quranic proof
The author cites Quran 2:187: 'Do not have relations with them while you are in seclusion in the mosques.' Identify the operative phrase (la tubashiruhunna) and note that it is a general prohibition.
The verse uses the verb 'bashara' which implies sexual contact, not necessarily intercourse.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the verse prove invalidation? The Hanafi usul principle: a prohibition (nahy) in acts of worship indicates that the act corrupts the worship. Since sexual contact is prohibited, engaging in it nullifies the i'tikaf. For acts without emission, they are also prohibited but the nullification only occurs when emission happens, because only then is the act akin to intercourse.
This is a zanni (speculative) inference, hence some disagreement with Shafi'is.
4
Note the ikhtilaf with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that any intentional sexual touch invalidates i'tikaf regardless of emission, because the prohibition in the verse covers all such acts. The Hanafi school responds that the prohibition is for the sake of invalidating only when the act reaches completion (emission).
The practical difference: a Hanafi mu'takif who kisses without emission retains a valid i'tikaf, albeit sinful.

Woman's I'tikaf: Home or Mosque?
Ruling on the recommended place for a woman's i'tikaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the recommended place for a woman's i'tikaf and state the permissible alternative with its evidence.

Core Idea
A woman's i'tikaf is best in her home prayer area; a mosque with a tent is permissible, as the Prophet's wives did.

This passage addresses the location of i'tikaf for women. While men must observe i'tikaf in a mosque, women are recommended to do so in a designated prayer place in their homes to maintain modesty and avoid fitnah. However, if they wish to join the congregational mosque, they may do so with a tent, based on the practice of the Prophet's wives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the recommended place
The author states: 'The woman observes i'tikaf in her house.' The commentary clarifies that it is recommended (yustahabb) for her to i'tikaf in the mosque of her house (i.e., the place she uses for prayer).
This is a recommendation, not an obligation.
2
Understand the reasoning
The reason given is that it is 'farther from temptation' (ab'adu al-fitna) and her condition is based on concealment (mabnaha 'ala al-sitr). Islamic rulings for women emphasize modesty and avoiding unnecessary mixing.
This reflects a general principle of sadd al-dhara'i' (blocking means to evil).
3
Note the permissible alternative
If she observes i'tikaf in a congregational mosque in a tent pitched for her, there is no harm. The proof is that the Prophet's wives did this during his lifetime, as recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari.
The tent provides privacy, so the objective of concealment is still achieved.

Rulings on Vowed I'tikaf: Inclusion of Nights and Consecutiveness
Ruling on vowed i'tikaf: nights and consecutiveness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on whether nights are automatically included in a vowed i'tikaf for days, and explain the consequence of specifying intention for days only.

Core Idea
When you vow i'tikaf for days, the nights are included consecutively — unless you intend otherwise.

A vow (nathr) makes a voluntary act obligatory. When someone vows i'tikaf for a certain number of days, a question arises: are the nights between those days automatically required? This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya clarifies the Hanafi stance: nights are included consecutively by default, but the vow's intention can restrict to daytime only. Understanding this helps avoid confusion in fulfilling one's vow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its obligation
The act is a vowed i'tikaf (nathr al-i'tikaf). The vow makes it wajib (obligatory). Determine what exactly the person vowed: days only, or days with nights?
The passage distinguishes between the default rule and the effect of explicit intention.
2
Apply the default rule for nights
If the person vows i'tikaf for 'days' without any qualification, the nights are automatically included. For example, vowing three days means three full days and three nights, observed consecutively. For two days, only one night (the night between them) is included.
Consecutiveness (walā’) is required even if not stipulated.
3
Consider the exception of explicit intention
If the person specifically intends only the daytime (niyyat al-nahār), that intention is valid. The nights are not required because the vow is interpreted according to the intention.
The phrase 'He intended what is real' indicates that the essence of i'tikaf is daytime seclusion; nights are adjunct.
4
Summarize the practical ruling
Default: nights included consecutively. If one intends only days, only days are obligatory. The mufta bihi (operative ruling) is the default, but the exception is accepted when intention is clear.
This shows Hanafi flexibility in vows: the intention of the vow-giver matters.
