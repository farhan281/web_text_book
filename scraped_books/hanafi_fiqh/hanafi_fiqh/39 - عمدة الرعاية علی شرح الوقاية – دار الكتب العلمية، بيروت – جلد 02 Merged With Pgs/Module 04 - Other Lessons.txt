The Sunnah of Directing the Dying Towards the Qibla
Ruling with proof on directing the dying

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for directing a dying person towards the qibla, identify the evidence, and explain why it is classified as sunnah rather than wajib.

Core Idea
Facing the qibla at death is a sunnah of the Prophet’s fitra, not an obligation.

When death approaches, the Prophet’s guidance encourages turning the dying person to face the Ka‘ba, lying on his right side. This practice, rooted in the fitra (natural religion), is a sunnah that reflects reverence for the qibla even at the final moment. In this lesson, we examine the ruling and its proof from the hadith of al-Bara’ ibn Ma‘rur and other narrations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon and its hukm level. Here, it is directing the muhtadhar (dying person) to face the qibla on his right side, and the ruling is sunnah (recommended).
The act is performed by those attending the dying person, not by the dying person himself.
2
Locate the dalil (evidence)
Identify the primary evidence cited: the hadith of al-Bara’ ibn Ma‘rur (al-Bayhaqi) where the Prophet approved his wish, and the hadith counting disrespect to the Ka‘ba as a major sin (Abu Dawud, al-Nasa’i, al-Hakim).
Both hadith are ahad (single-transmitter), hence zanni in transmission.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah (indication of the proof)
Explain how the proof supports the ruling. The Prophet’s approval (‘He was right in accordance with the fitra’) shows the act is commendable but does not command it. The second hadith emphasizes the qibla’s sanctity, implying care even for the dead, but not obligation.
The lack of an imperative verb or explicit command keeps the ruling at sunnah, not wajib.
4
Note the practical application
Describe how this is done: in regions where the Ka‘ba is to the west, the dying person’s head is placed north, feet south, and he lies on his right side. This follows the general legal principle of preferring the right (tayammun).
The right side is chosen because the right is generally favored in Islamic law, as seen in wudu, eating, etc.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on funerals (Bab al-Jana'iz) with a ruling on the preferred positioning of a dying person. It is a ruling with proof (sunnah). The author states that it is sunnah for the muhtadhir (one who is dying) to be turned to face the qibla, lying on his right side. The commentator first explains the term 'al-jana'iz': it can refer to the deceased (with fatha) or the bier (with kasra). Then he explains 'muhtadhir' as one who is near death, derived from the passive form meaning death has attended him. The proof for turning to the qibla is from the hadith of al-Bara' ibn Ma'rur, where the Prophet approved his wish to be turned towards the qibla, saying he had acted in accordance with the fitra (natural religion). Another hadith counts 'making the Sacred House permissible' as a major sin, emphasizing its sanctity as qibla for both living and dead. The commentator then details the practical application: in regions where the Ka'ba is to the west, the dying person's head should point north and feet south, and he should lie on his right side because of the general legal principle that prefers the right hand. This ruling is considered a sunnah, not a fard wajib, based on the hadith evidence.

The Sunnah Method of Washing the Deceased
Sequential steps of washing the deceased with proof from hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi sunnah method of washing the deceased, including the sequence of positions and the number of washes, and identify the proof from the hadith of Umm Atiyyah.

Core Idea
The deceased is washed three times, starting on the right side, with the first two washes with sidr and the third with water and camphor.

This lesson details the method of washing the deceased (ghusl al-mayyit) according to the Hanafi school. The passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya explains the sequential steps: first placing the deceased on the left side, then on the right, then sitting him up to press the belly, and finally a third wash on the left side. The proof is the hadith of Umm Atiyyah. Understanding this method is essential for those performing the ritual washing, as it is a communal obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is being ruled upon? (Washing the deceased). The hukm level of the specific method is sunnah muakkadah. The overall washing is fard kifayah.
The method itself is not obligatory but following it brings reward.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith of Umm Atiyyah states the number and substances: two washes with sidr, one with water and camphor. This is the primary proof.
The hadith is narrated by Abu Dawud and is ahad (zanni thubut).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative form of the Prophet's instruction to Umm Atiyyah indicates that the sunnah method includes three washes with the described substances. The sequence is derived from her practice.
The Hanafi school interprets the imperative as indicating sunnah, not wujub, due to the zanni nature of the hadith.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
There is no substantial disagreement within the Hanafi school on this method. The Shafi'i school also uses three washes but prefers all three with sidr and camphor.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is minimal; both schools agree on three washes.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon practice (mufta bihi) is exactly as described in the passage: first two washes with sidr, third with camphor; the sequence of positions ensures washing begins on the right.
This is confirmed by al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah and Ibn al-Humam in Fath al-Qadir.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the hadith of Umm Atiyyah on the method of washing the deceased. The matn states that the deceased is washed by first placing him on his left side and pouring water until it reaches the other side (the back), then on his right side similarly, then sitting him up leaning and gently pressing his belly. The commentator explains that the purpose of starting on the left is to ensure that the actual washing begins on the right side, avoiding starting on the left if one were to lay on the right first. This is because the right is more honored. The commentator also clarifies that the three washes are all similar, but some recommend the first with plain water, second with boiled sidr leaves, and third with camphor. Al-Fath prefers the first two with sidr based on the hadith, which also indicates sitting up and pressing the belly as part of the sunnah. The third wash is performed by laying the deceased on his left side after the sitting. This method ensures the sunnah number of washes.

Key Terms
التخت (al-tukht): The platform or the opposite side of the body; specifically here, the area under the body being washed, i.e., the back or the other side.
الاضجاع (al-ijda'): Laying the deceased on one side for washing.
التيمن (al-tayammun): Starting with the right side in the washing process.

Rulings After Washing: Impurity, Drying, and Grooming Prohibition
Ruling and dispute on post-wash impurity and grooming

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on impurity emerging after washing, apply the principle that ghusl is not repeated, and explain why cutting nails and combing hair are prohibitively disliked, evaluating the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Ritual purity ends with death: impurity after washing is cleaned, not nullified; grooming the deceased is prohibitively disliked.

After washing the deceased, several practical questions arise: What if impurity emerges? Must the ghusl be repeated? Should we dry the body? And is it allowed to cut nails or comb hair? This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya answers these with clear Hanafi rulings and also presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school on grooming. Understanding these points ensures that we handle the deceased with proper respect and legal precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
List the specific actions mentioned: washing impurity without repeating ghusl, drying the body, cutting nails, combing hair, and placing hanut. For each, note the ruling (cleaning, sunnah, makruh tahrimi, etc.).
Note that cutting nails and combing hair are ruled makruh tahrimi (prohibitively disliked).
2
Locate the Hanafi proof for grooming prohibition
The passage cites the hadith of Aisha (رضي الله عنها) who rebuked women for combing the deceased: 'Why do you sit with your deceased?' This is the primary dalil for the Hanafi position.
The hadith is narrated by Abd al-Razzaq and others. Its meaning is that beautification after death is inappropriate.
3
Identify the opposing proof
Imam al-Shafi'i relies on the hadith of Umm Atiyyah who, when washing the Prophet's daughter, combed her hair into three braids. This suggests it is recommended.
This hadith is in Bukhari and Muslim, so it is authentic.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school interprets the Umm Atiyyah hadith as specific to that situation (perhaps to prevent tangling) or abrogated. The general principle is that the deceased should not be treated as alive for adornment.
Also, the action may have been for practical necessity, not beautification.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Hanafis avoid all grooming (no cutting nails, combing hair, shaving pubic hair). Shafi'is consider it recommended. The mufta bihi (operative position) is the Hanafi view.
This difference affects funeral practice, especially when family members request grooming.

Commentary
This passage contains rulings after the washing of the deceased and an inter-madhhab dispute. First, if any impurity (such as urine or stool) emerges after washing, it is cleaned, but the ghusl is not repeated. The commentator explains this is because the nullification of wudu or ghusl by such impurities applies only during life, not after death. This is a fundamental principle: the rulings of ritual purity cease with death. The body is then dried with a cloth to prevent the shroud from becoming wet. Second, it is prohibitively disliked (makruh tahrimi) to cut the deceased's nails or comb the hair. This includes any action performed for adornment in life, such as cutting nails, combing head hair or beard, and shaving pubic hair. The Hanafi proof is the hadith of Aisha, who rebuked women combing the deceased's hair. The commentator notes that this is a 'ikhtilaf' with al-Shafi'i, who considers such actions recommended based on the hadith of Umm Atiyyah, who combed the Prophet's daughter's hair into three braids. The practical consequence: Hanafis avoid grooming, while Shafi'is do it. The relied-upon Hanafi position is prohibition. Finally, hanut (a perfumed powder) is placed on the head and beard as part of the preparation.

Key Terms
حنوط (hanut): A perfumed aromatic powder (e.g. camphor) applied to the head and beard of the deceased.
يكره تحريما (yukrahu tahriman): Prohibitively disliked; a ruling between makruh tanzih and haram, meaning one should avoid it and it is sinful.

Explains rulings for each burial action: wajib, sunnah, makruh, and bid'ah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the wajib, sunnah, and makruh rulings for each action during burial, and distinguish the specific rule for covering a woman's grave.

Core Idea
Facing the deceased toward the qibla is wajib; other actions are sunnah or makruh, and covering the grave after burial is bid'ah.

When burying a Muslim, several actions are prescribed. Some are obligatory, others recommended, and some disliked. This lesson walks through each action mentioned in the classical Hanafi text, clarifying the ruling and rationale behind them. Understanding these details ensures the burial is performed according to sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
For each action—saying Bismillah, facing qibla, untying knot, leveling, covering the grave, and using bricks/wood—determine the hukm level (wajib, sunnah, makruh, or bid'ah).
The text explicitly marks 'facing qibla' as wajib and 'baked bricks' as makruh.
2
Locate the textual basis
Find the specific phrase in the Arabic that indicates the ruling. For wajib, the text says 'إلى القبلة أي وجوباً'. For makruh, 'ويكره الآجر'. For sunnah, the absence of explicit obligation suggests recommendation.
Covering the grave after burial is explicitly labeled 'بدعة' (bid'ah).
3
Understand the rationale
Each ruling has a reason. Untying the knot helps the deceased sit for questioning. Leveling prevents soil from falling. Covering a woman's grave is for modesty. Bricks are disliked for resembling permanent construction and carrying the effect of fire.
Rationale is drawn from the commentary included in the source.
4
Distinguish between genders
Note which actions apply only to women: covering with a cloth (tasjiyah) and using a covered bier. Men do not need these.
This distinction is explicit in the text and commentary.
5
Identify the innovation
The text clearly states that covering the grave after burial is an innovation (bid'ah) for any deceased. This is separate from the recommended covering during burial for women.
Do not confuse the two practices.

The Shape of the Grave: Tasnīm vs Tasṭīḥ
Presents the dispute between Hanafi and Shafi'i on grave shape

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the shape of the grave (tasnīm) and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position, citing the proofs and the Hanafi response to the opposing dalil.

Core Idea
The Hanafi sunnah is to raise the grave like a camel’s hump; the Shafi’i flattening is based on a hadith that Hanafis interpret as removing excess.

This passage presents a classic inter-madhhab dispute on a practical matter of burial rites. The Hanafi school holds that the grave should be raised in a hump-like shape (tasnīm), while the Shafi'i school holds it should be flattened (tasṭīḥ). Understanding how each school derives its ruling from the same sources reveals key differences in interpretive methodology.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The matn explicitly says 'ويسنم القبر' – the grave is made musannam. This is the Hanafi ruling: raising the grave like a camel hump is sunnah.
Note the term 'musannam' – derived from 'sunnam' (hump).
2
Identify the opposing Shafi'i view
The commentary explains that tasṭīḥ (leveling the grave with the ground) is the sunnah for Imam al-Shafi'i, based on the hadith of 'Ali.
Tasṭīḥ means making it square and level without raising.
3
Examine the Shafi'i dalil
The hadith: 'The Messenger of Allah sent me that I should not leave a raised grave without leveling it.' The key word is 'raised' (مشرف).
This hadith is in Sunan al-Tirmidhi and is accepted by both schools.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
Hanafis interpret 'raised' as excessive raising beyond the recommended amount. They argue that the Prophet's own grave was musannam, as reported by Companions and recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari.
The Hanafi response uses the principle that the Prophet's own practice clarifies ambiguous commands.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis raise the grave about a handspan in a hump shape; Shafi'is make it level with the ground.
Many graveyards show this difference – look for the shape.

The Sunnah of Making the Grave Hump-Shaped (Musannam)
Hanafi ruling with proof and inter-madhhab rebuttal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on grave shape, explain the Shafi'i counter-argument, and apply the Hanafi response to the hadith of 'Ali.

Core Idea
The Prophet's grave was hump-shaped; this established practice overrides a general command to level graves.

A common question in funeral rites is the correct shape of the grave: should it be raised like a camel's hump or flattened? This passage from 'Umdat al-Ri'aya presents the Hanafi ruling that the grave must be musannam (hump-shaped) and rejects flattening (tasṭīḥ). The commentator engages the Shafi'i school's proof – the famous hadith of 'Ali – and explains how the Hanafis interpret it consistently with the sunnah demonstrated by the Prophet's own grave.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling in the matn: 'the grave is made hump-shaped and not flattened.' Note the hukm level: sunnah muakkadah.
The matn itself is a ruling statement; the commentary provides the proof and rebuttal.
2
Identify the Shafi'i view and its proof
The Shafi'i school holds that flattening (tasṭīḥ) is sunnah, based on the hadith of 'Ali: the Prophet ordered him to level every elevated grave. Recognise this as a legitimate ikhtilaf.
The hadith is recorded by al-Tirmidhi and is ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis interpret the hadith as removing only excessive elevation beyond the recommended hump, not complete flattening. Understand the principle: when a general command conflicts with established practice (the Prophet's own grave), the practice is prioritised.
This is a form of takhsis (specification) of the 'amm (general) command.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis raise the grave about a handspan (shibr) in a hump shape; Shafi'is make it level with the ground. This affects how graves are constructed in communities following each school.
The thamarah is the actual physical shape of the grave.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is relied upon because it is based on the mutawatir (mass-transmitted) report of the Prophet's grave shape, which is stronger than a single ahad report.
Al-Marghinani and the later Hanafi masters uphold tasnīm as mufta bihi.

Definition of a Shahid (Martyr) in Hanafi Law
Definition and conditions for a shahid not washed and buried in his clothes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of a shahid, list its conditions, and distinguish the two types of shahid (worldly and Hereafter).

Core Idea
A shahid in this world is a pure adult Muslim killed unjustly by iron, not washed, buried in clothes.

The term 'shahid' is often used loosely, but Hanafi law sets precise conditions for the special burial rites. This lesson unpacks the definition from the classical texts, including an important internal disagreement about those in a state of major impurity.

The Iron Weapon Condition for Martyrdom: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Martyrdom depends on no wealth due; iron ensures qisas, heavy object triggers diyah according to Abu Hanifa.

This passage examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about the conditions for martyrdom when a Muslim is killed unjustly by another Muslim or a dhimmi. Abu Hanifa requires the weapon to be iron; the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) do not. The disagreement hinges on whether killing by a heavy object obligates blood money (diyah) or retaliation (qisas), which directly affects the martyr's status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the issue
Note that the dispute is within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa vs. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn). The issue is whether a Muslim killed by a heavy object (e.g., stone, club) counts as a martyr.
Abu Hanifa requires iron weapon; Sahibayn do not.
2
Trace Abu Hanifa's reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that killing by a heavy object (muthqal) obligates diyah, not qisas. Since wealth is due, the victim cannot be a martyr (whose hallmark is no financial liability). Thus, only iron weapons (which mandate qisas) qualify.
Key: diyah negates martyrdom.
3
Trace the Sahibayn's reasoning
The Sahibayn hold that killing by a heavy object also obligates qisas, not diyah. Since no wealth is due, the condition for martyrdom is satisfied regardless of the weapon material.
They do not require iron; any instrument that causes death unjustly suffices.
4
Determine the practical consequence
According to Abu Hanifa, a victim killed by a stone must be washed (ghusl) and shrouded, and funeral prayer is performed. According to the Sahibayn, the victim is a true martyr and is buried without washing, like battlefield martyrs.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah) preferred the Sahibayn's view, making it the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Most fatwa collections adopt the Sahibayn's leniency.

Core Idea
The martyr is buried in his usual garment; extra items like fur coats and weapons are removed.

When a Muslim martyr dies on the battlefield, his burial differs from that of an ordinary deceased. The Hanafi school teaches that he is shrouded in the clothes he died in, but not all of them. This lesson clarifies which items are removed and which remain, based on the classical text.

Identify the ruling — what is removed?
The passage states 'inuz'a anhu ghayru thawbihi' — from him is removed other than his garment. Thus the baseline is that his garment (thawb) is kept.

Define 'garment specific to the dead'
The commentator explains that this refers to items not normally part of a shroud, such as a fur coat, padded vest, cap, weapons, and boots.
These items are removed because they are not typical burial attire.

List the removed items

The phrase 'yuzadu wa yunqasu' means that if the remaining clothing is less than the sunnah shroud (three sheets), extra cloth is added; if more, excess is removed to achieve the standard.
The goal is to ensure proper covering without extravagance.

Consider a martyr wearing a jacket, shirt, and trousers. The jacket is like a fur coat (outer garment) and is removed; the shirt and trousers are kept as his shroud. If needed, an extra sheet may be added to cover him fully.
This demonstrates the method of identification and adjustment.

Combining Reality and Metaphor in Vows and Oaths
Expands on combining literal and metaphorical meanings in vow/oath utterances.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on combining literal (haqiqi) and metaphorical (majazi) meanings in vow utterances and apply the distinction between religious and judicial validity.

Core Idea
A vow's literal obligation stands by utterance alone; its status as an oath requires intention.

This passage from the super-commentary 'Imdad al-Ri'ayah' delves into the intricate interplay between literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings in the utterances of vows (nadhr) and oaths (yamin). The commentator refines the position that while combining both meanings in intention is generally impermissible, in the case of vows the literal meaning is established solely by the utterance, independent of intention, while the metaphorical meaning of being an oath depends on the speaker's intention. This analysis clarifies the Hanafi stance on the enforceability of such utterances in religious (dinan) versus judicial (qada) contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings
Distinguish between the literal meaning (haqiqi) of a vow as an obligation that must be fulfilled, and the metaphorical meaning (majazi) of being an oath confirming or emphasizing something. The text refers to 'nadhr' as the reality and 'yamin' as the metaphor.
In Hanafi usul, haqiqah is primary; majaz requires a qarinah (indication).
2
Understand the role of utterance vs intention
The literal obligation of the vow is established by the mere utterance (sigha) of the vow, regardless of the speaker's intention. However, for the vow to be considered an oath (yamin) in the legal sense, the speaker must have intended it as such. Intent does not affect the literal meaning.
This is a key difference from other constructs like divorce and emancipation, where intention is irrelevant for the literal meaning.
3
Apply the distinction to validity
In religious matters (dinan), a person is believed if he claims he did not intend the literal vow but only the metaphorical oath. In judicial matters (qada), the literal meaning is enforced regardless of his claim, because the utterance establishes the obligation. This differs from divorce and emancipation, where the literal meaning is always enforced in both dinan and qada.
Thus, if someone utters 'I vow to give sadaqah' but claims he meant it as an oath, he is not bound to give sadaqah dinan (before Allah) but is obliged in court.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed expansion by the commentator on the principle of combining literal (haqiqi) and metaphorical (majazi) meanings in utterances, specifically concerning nadhr (vows) and yamin (oaths). The base text had stated that combining them is not permissible in intention, but here there is an exception: the literal meaning of the vow is established by the mere utterance (sigha) regardless of intention, while the metaphorical meaning of being an oath requires specific intention. The commentator then refutes a previous answer that claimed otherwise, presenting a rebuttal: we concede that the oath is a metaphorical meaning, but in constructs (insha'at), both meanings can be intended—the real by the utterance alone, the metaphorical by intention. If one intends only the oath, it works; if one intends both, the real is still there. A doubt is raised: since intention does not affect the real meaning, combining does not occur. The commentator concludes that in all cases, the real meaning is by the word, so no combining in intention is needed. Then a further point: if someone intends the metaphorical meaning (being an oath) and negates the real meaning (vow), he is believed in his claim before Allah (dinan) but not in a court of law (qada), because for divorce and emancipation, the real meaning is always enforced regardless of what one claims.

Key Terms
حقيقة (haqiqah): Literal, real meaning of an utterance as originally intended by the lawgiver.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical or figurative meaning; here specifically the meaning of being an oath (yamin) derived from the utterance of a vow.
نذر (nadhr): A vow made by saying 'I vow to do X' which obligates the vowed act upon utterance.
يمين (yamin): An oath sworn by Allah or a vow considered as an oath; here the metaphorical meaning of the vow utterance.

Core Idea
I'tikaf: a confirmed Sunnah requiring fasting, a congregational mosque, and intention – its minimum duration is disputed among the Hanafi imams.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition and ruling of i'tikaf (spiritual seclusion in the mosque). The author begins by clarifying that i'tikaf is a highly recommended Sunnah (sunnah mu'akkadah), which means it is established by the Prophet's consistent practice, especially in the last ten days of Ramadan. The linguistic meaning of i'tikaf is 'restraint' or 'prevention,' and legally it is defined as staying in a mosque with the intention of worship, while fasting. The commentary notes that fasting is a condition for i'tikaf, supported by the hadith 'No i'tikaf except with fasting' and the practice of the Companions. There is a difference of opinion on the ruling: some say it is merely recommended (mustahabb), but the preferred view is that it is sunnah mu'akkadah in Ramadan and recommended at other times. It becomes obligatory if one vows to do it or commences it. In Ramadan, it is a communal Sunnah, not an individual one. The author also discusses the minimum duration: the relied-upon view (from Abū Ḥanīfah) is that it is a full day, while Muḥammad al-Shaybānī holds that any duration suffices, the minimum being an hour, based on leniency in supererogatory acts. However, the mufta bihi position is that the minimum is a day, except in the case of a vow. Regarding the mosque, the passage explains that i'tikaf must be in a congregational mosque (jāmi')—one with an imam and muezzin where the five daily prayers are performed. Abū Ḥanīfah is reported to require that the five prayers be actually performed there, while his two disciples consider any mosque valid, a view preferred by later scholars. Finally, the passage lists prohibitions for the mu'takif: not visiting the sick, not attending funerals, not touching or having intimacy with one's spouse, and not leaving the mosque except for necessities. These are based on the Sunnah as reported by ʿĀ'ishah.

The Hatim and the Reconstructions of the Kaaba
Historical expansion on Kaaba construction linked to the Hatim hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the legal status of the Hatim in Hanafi fiqh and explain why it was excluded from the Kaaba building by Quraysh.

Core Idea
The Hatim is part of the original Kaaba; tawaf must be performed outside it.

This lesson traces the building history of the Kaaba from the angels to the Prophet's time, focusing on the famous hadith where the Prophet expresses his wish to reintegrate the Hatim. Understanding this history clarifies the ruling that the Hatim is part of the Kaaba, affecting tawaf and prayer placement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its fiqh implication
Locate the hadith quoted from Aisha. The Prophet states that the Hatim is part of the House (min al-bayt). This is the primary proof that the Hatim is an integral part of the Kaaba. In Hanafi fiqh, this means tawaf must be outside the Hatim, and prayer inside it may be valid but is disliked without being part of the Kaaba.
The hadith is reported in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim.
2
Trace the construction changes from Ibrahim to Quraysh
From the textual account, Ibrahim built the Kaaba on foundations, with two doors level with the ground. Quraysh, before the Prophet's prophethood, rebuilt it but ran short of funds, so they shortened the walls, raised the door, and excluded the Hatim area (six cubits). This change led to the Hatim being outside the building.
The exclusion was due to financial constraints, not design.
3
Follow the historical restorations by Ibn al-Zubayr and al-Hajjaj
After the Prophet, the Kaaba remained unchanged until Ibn al-Zubayr rebuilt it on Ibrahim's foundations, incorporating the Hatim and adding two doors. Later, al-Hajjaj demolished that and restored the Quraysh design, which remains to this day.
The commentator notes the political context of these changes.
4
Apply the fiqh implication to current practice
Since the Hatim is part of the Kaaba, tawaf must be performed outside it. If one passes through the Hatim during tawaf, the circuit is invalid and must be repeated. Similarly, praying inside the Hatim is considered praying inside the Kaaba by some, but the Hanafi view allows it though it is disliked (makruh) if one deliberately enters without necessity.

Commentary
This passage is a historical introduction from the commentary on al-Wiqayah, placed in the chapter on Ihram. It begins by citing the famous hadith from Aisha (رضي الله عنها) in which the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) expresses his wish to rebuild the Kaaba on the foundations of Ibrahim (عليه السلام) and reintegrate the Hatim. The commentator then traces the history of the Kaaba's construction: first by the angels, then by Adam, then by Ibrahim with two doors level with the ground. He explains that Quraysh, before the Prophet's prophethood, rebuilt the Kaaba but due to lack of funds, they shortened its walls, raised the door, and excluded the Hatim area. After the Prophet's time, the Rashidun caliphs did not change it. Later, Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr rebuilt it according to Ibrahim's foundations, incorporating the Hatim and making two doors. However, after his death, al-Hajjaj demolished that construction and restored it to the Quraysh design. The passage ends with a brief chronology of the caliphs and Umayyad rulers.

Key Terms
الخطيم (al-Khatim): The curved semi-circular wall opposite the corner of the Kaaba; it is considered part of the original Kaaba structure according to the hadith.
النَّفَقَة (al-nafaqa): Funds, expenditure; in the context of the Kaaba construction, the funds available to Quraysh were insufficient to complete the full building.
