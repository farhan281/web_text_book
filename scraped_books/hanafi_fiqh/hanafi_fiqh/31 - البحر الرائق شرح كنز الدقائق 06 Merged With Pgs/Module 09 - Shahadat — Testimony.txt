The Binding Effect of Judicial Decrees: Emancipation, Marriage, Lineage, and the Dispute over Waqf
Analysis of judicial decree binding on public

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish which judicial decrees are binding on all people (kafah) in Hanafi fiqh and identify the correct view regarding waqf decrees.

Core Idea
A qadi's decree binds all only in freedom, marriage, lineage, and wala—not in waqf.

The authority of a qadi's judgment is not uniform across all matters. This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq clarifies that while some decrees have universal effect, others, like waqf, allow subsequent claims. Understanding this distinction is crucial for the mufti and qadi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The qadi has general authority; his judgment is executed regarding all people. But this is not absolute—only certain matters carry universal binding effect.
The text initially states the general principle from al-Fusul.
2
List matters with universal binding effect
The passage specifies that decrees on freedom (emancipation), marriage, lineage, and wala are judgments over all people. No subsequent contradictory claim is heard.
These are considered to affect the status of a person, thus binding on everyone.
3
Analyze the waqf dispute
Some Hanafi scholars (e.g., Shams al-A'immah al-Halwani) analogize waqf to emancipation, saying a waqf decree is binding on all. Others (al-Imadi, al-Fakih al-Badriyyah) hold it is not binding on all, and a later ownership claim is heard.
The relied-upon view (al-sahih) is that waqf decrees are not on kafah.
4
Recognize the practical consequence
If a qadi declares a property waqf, someone can later claim ownership and the claim is admissible. This contrasts with emancipation, where a later ownership claim is not heard.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf – the practical difference.
5
Understand agency as an exception
The passage ends with an example from Mu'in al-Hukkam: a judgment establishing agency (wakalah) is also binding on all because it establishes a cause affecting dealings with that agent.
This shows that not only status matters but also certain rights can have universal effect.

Commentary
This passage continues the chapter on Istihqaq, focusing on the transitivity (ta'addi) of a qadi's judgment. The author explains that a judge's decree has general authority and is executed regarding all people, but this is limited to specific matters. The commentator clarifies that the transitivity intended here is not that all judgments by bayyinah are binding on everyone, but only those in emancipation (freedom), marriage, lineage, and wala. For these, once the qadi rules, no subsequent contradictory claim is heard. For waqf, there is a dispute: some scholars (like Shams al-A'immah al-Halwani) hold that a judgment of waqf is also binding on all, similar to emancipation, because waqf once established is not easily invalidated. However, al-Imadi in al-Fusul and others correct this, stating it is not a general judgment, and a later claim of ownership is admissible. The commentator then reconciles the views, noting that the relied-upon position is that for freedom (including emancipation and original freedom), marriage, lineage, and wala, the judgment is universally binding; for waqf, it is not. An exception is when the emancipation is conditional on a date, then it binds from that date. A final example from Mu'in al-Hukkam shows that a judgment of agency (wakalah) is also binding on all, because it establishes a cause against the agent that affects all who deal with him.

Key Terms
قضاء (qada): Judicial decree of a qadi; a binding decision that resolves disputes.
ولاء (wala): Clientage; the relationship of patronage between a freed slave and his former master, which carries inheritance rights.
عتق (itq): Emancipation of a slave; the act of freeing a slave.
وقف (waqf): Endowment; the dedication of property for charitable purposes, where ownership is suspended and the usufruct is for beneficiaries.

Key Takeaway
A qadi's decree is universally binding only in matters of personal status (emancipation, marriage, lineage, wala); for waqf, the decree is not binding on all, and ownership claims can still be heard.
