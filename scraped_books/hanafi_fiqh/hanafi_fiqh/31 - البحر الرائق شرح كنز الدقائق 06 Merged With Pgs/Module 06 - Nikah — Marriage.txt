Marriage of a Slavegirl in a Fasid Sale: Effect of Rescission
Resolves an apparent contradiction about whether marriage of a slavegirl is voided by rescission of the fasid sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the validity of marriage contracted before vs after delivery in a fasid sale and state the resulting Hanafi ruling.

Core Idea
Marriage of a slavegirl after delivery in a fasid sale remains valid even if the sale is later rescinded.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq resolves an apparent conflict between two Hanafi authorities, al-Walwaliji and al-Siraj. The key is timing: whether the marriage was contracted before or after the buyer took delivery (qabd) of the slavegirl. Understanding this distinction clarifies when rescission of a fasid sale affects a subsequent marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting statements
Locate al-Walwaliji's statement that rescission voids the marriage, and al-Siraj's that the marriage stands. Note the apparent contradiction.
The conflict arises because both statements are cited without context.
2
Determine the key distinction
Examine the timing of the marriage relative to delivery (qabd). Al-Walwaliji's statement applies to marriage before delivery; al-Siraj's applies after delivery.
Qabd in a fasid sale confers ownership (milk) to the buyer.
3
Apply the principle of fasid sale effects
After delivery, a fasid sale is treated like a valid sale in many legal effects. Ownership is established, so the buyer's authority to marry is not retroactively voided by rescission.
This is based on istihsan; the dalil is the maxim that fasid sale after qabd is like valid sale.
4
State the resulting Hanafi ruling
If marriage is contracted after qabd, rescission does not annul the marriage. If before qabd, rescission voids the marriage from the start.
The ruling resolves the contradiction: both statements are correct in their respective contexts.

Hibah of Mahr with Conditions: 'Iwad vs Non-‘Iwad
Intra-Hanafi dispute on conditional hibah of mahr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conditions that constitute a proper return ('iwad) and those that do not in a hibah of mahr, and state the Hanafi ruling for each scenario.

Core Idea
A condition that is a return makes the hibah conditional; a condition that is not a return either invalidates the gift or is void, leaving the gift valid.

When a wife gifts her mahr to her husband with a future condition, the validity of the gift depends on whether that condition is a proper 'iwad (exchange) or merely a prohibition. The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on this issue, with scholars like Abu Bakr al-Iskaf and Muhammad ibn Muqatil offering different analyses. This lesson will guide you through parsing the key distinction and applying it to various scenarios from the classical text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting positions
First, locate the two main positions: al-Iskaf/al-Saffar argue that a gift conditioned on 'not oppressing' is a suspended gift (ta'liq) and thus invalid; Ibn Muqatil argues that the gift fails if the condition is violated because consent was conditional.
Use the madhhab_dispute_map as a reference.
2
Understand the 'iwad distinction
The author (Mawlana) distinguishes between conditions that are a return (like providing Hajj expenses or cutting cloth) and those that are not (like refraining from oppression). Only conditions that are a proper 'iwad make the hibah conditional.
An 'iwad is something of value given in exchange; leaving oppression is not a valid 'iwad.
3
Apply the distinction to the oppression scenario
Since 'not oppressing' is not an 'iwad, the hibah is either valid immediately (if phrased as 'on condition') or invalid as a suspension (if phrased as 'if you do not'). Under the author's view, the hibah is valid and unconditional, so the mahr does not return even if oppression occurs.
Contrast with the Hajj case where the condition is an 'iwad.
4
Analyze the disciplinary hitting exception
If the husband hits for legitimate discipline (right), it is not 'oppression', so the condition is not violated. This shows that the condition's scope must be precisely defined.
Hitting without right would be oppression.
5
Synthesize the practical thamarah
The practical consequence: if the condition is a return, the mahr returns upon failure; if not, the mahr does not return. The mufta bihi is that for non-'iwad conditions, the hibah stands unconditionally.
This ruling affects financial outcomes in divorce.

Commentary
This passage from al-Khaniyyah presents a detailed intra-Hanafi dispute about the validity of a wife's hibah of her mahr conditioned on certain actions by her husband. The key distinction is whether the condition constitutes a proper 'iwad (exchange/return) or not. If the condition is something that can be considered a return (like providing Hajj expenses or cutting garments), then the hibah is conditional upon the fulfillment of that return; if the return is not provided, the hibah fails and the mahr remains due. If the condition is merely a prohibition (like not to oppress), it is not a valid return, so the hibah is either invalid as a suspended gift (according to al-Iskaf and al-Saffar) or valid as an immediate gift with a condition that does not affect its validity (according to the analogy with the divorce cases). The author (Mawlana) distinguishes between the Hajj case (where the condition is a return) and the oppression case (where it is not). Also, when the husband's action is a right (like disciplinary hitting), it is not oppression, so the condition is not violated. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that hibah can be made conditional upon a return ('iwad), and if the return is not fulfilled, the hibah is rescinded. However, if the condition is not a return, the hibah is either suspended and invalid (if considered ta'liq) or valid and unconditional (if considered ta'liq bi'l-qabul). The fatwa in many of these cases is that the mahr remains due if the condition of a return is not met.

Conditional Gift of Mahr: Ta'liq vs. Acceptance
Ruling on conditional gift of mahr with proof by qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a void conditional gift of mahr (ta'liq al-hibah bi al-shart) and a valid conditional gift (ta'liq al-hibah bi al-qabul), and apply the Hanafi ruling to similar scenarios.

Core Idea
A gift conditioned on the contract itself is void; conditioned on acceptance is valid.

A wife may wish to gift her mahr to her husband but attach a condition she deems protective. However, how that condition is phrased determines whether the gift is legally valid. This lesson unpacks a classical distinction: a condition that suspends the gift versus a condition that qualifies the acceptance. You will learn the Hanafi rationale and a parallel from divorce law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the source passage and identify the two different wordings used by the wife. The first: 'My mahr is yours if you do not oppress me.' The second: 'I gift you my mahr on condition that you do not oppress me.'
The difference lies in the phrasing: 'if' vs 'on condition that' and the structure of the sentence.
2
Determine the ruling for each
According to Abu Bakr al-Iskaf and Abu al-Qasim al-Saffar, the first gift is void (fasid) because it is a ta'liq (suspension) of the gift by a condition. The second gift is valid (sahih) because the condition is attached to the acceptance (qabul) itself.
The key is whether the condition is part of the offer-acceptance mechanism or an external suspensive condition.
3
Understand the legal reason (illah)
In Hanafi law, a gift (hibah) requires immediate transfer of ownership. Suspending it on a future condition (ta'liq) is invalid because it delays the transfer and creates uncertainty (gharar). However, attaching a condition to the acceptance does not delay the gift; it becomes a conditional acceptance, which is valid.
The condition 'that you do not oppress me' is a stipulation that the husband must fulfill. If he fails, the wife may have a legal claim, but the gift remains executed.
4
Examine the qiyas analogy
The passage provides a qiyas from divorce: if a man says 'You are divorced if you enter the house', divorce does not occur until entry (ta'liq). If he says 'You are divorced upon your entering the house' and she accepts, divorce occurs immediately upon acceptance. This mirrors the gift scenarios.
The analogy shows the same principle: a condition attached to the inception of the contract (acceptance) is valid; a condition that suspends the contract is void.
5
Apply the ruling to new cases
Consider a wife who says: 'I give you my mahr provided you build me a room.' Here the condition is part of the offer? Actually 'provided' is ambiguous. But if she says 'I give you my mahr if you build a room', that is void ta'liq. If she says 'I give you my mahr on condition that you build a room', and he accepts, it is valid.
The principle is not limited to mahr but extends to all gifts (hibah) in Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary

A conditional gift of mahr returns when the condition is breached, but a condition to refrain from oppression is not a financial consideration.

This lesson examines a nuanced issue in Hanafi family law: when a wife gifts her mahr to her husband on condition that he does not oppress her, what happens if he later oppresses her? This refines the analogy.

A condition attached to acceptance does not void the gift unless the condition itself has a time limit that is breached.

A wife may gift her mahr to her husband on the condition that he keep her without divorcing her. The ruling hinges on whether a specific time for the condition is set. Marriage with Suspensive Condition: The Rule and Its Exception
Intra-Hanafi ruling on ta'liq in nikah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between invalid ta'liq (suspension) and valid tanziz (immediate condition) in Hanafi marriage contracts and apply the exception when the concerned person consents in the session.

Core Idea
Marriage cannot be suspended on a future condition, but a condition that is already true is immediate and valid.

The Hanafi school generally holds that marriage must be immediate and unconditional like a sale, but unlike a sale, ta'liq (suspension) invalidates the contract. However, Abu Yusuf introduced an exception when the condition involves the consent of a present party, such as the father. This lesson unpacks this nuance and clarifies when a supposedly 'conditional' marriage is actually valid because the condition is already fulfilled or given immediately.

Guided Path
1
State the general Hanafi rule on ta'liq in nikah
Identify the principle: marriage does not accept suspension on a future condition (e.g., 'if my father agrees'). The contract must be tanziz (immediate).
This is the default position of Abu Hanifa and the relied-upon view.
2
Explain the exception from Abu Yusuf
If the condition is about a person present in the session (e.g., father) and that person consents immediately, the marriage is valid by istihsan.
This is found in al-Zahiriyyah and accepted in later fatwa works.
3
Differentiate ta'liq from tanziz with ka'in
When the condition refers to an existing fact (e.g., 'if I have no husband'), and that fact is true, the statement is treated as tanziz (immediate), not ta'liq.
The key is whether the condition is 'ka'in' (already existing/fulfilled) or 'mustaqbal' (future/unfulfilled).
4
Analyze the examples given in the text
The man who said 'if I have no husband' and she indeed had none – valid. The father who said 'if I have not married her' – valid if true. Contrast with the absent father case – invalid.
The presence of the father in the session makes the consent effectively immediate, even if phrased conditionally.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The praktical difference: in the absent-father scenario, the marriage is invalid and must be renewed unconditionally. In the present-father scenario, it is valid from the outset.
The mufta bihi is the general rule of invalidity for pure future conditions, but the exception applies when the condition is immediately fulfillable.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi discussion on the validity of marriage contracts that include suspensive conditions. The general rule, cited from al-Khaniyah, is that marriage does not accept suspension (ta'liq). Therefore, if someone says 'I marry you if my father approves' and the woman accepts, the marriage is invalid because it is conditional on a future event. However, a ruling from al-Zahiriyyah, attributed to Abu Yusuf, provides an exception: if the father is present in the same session and consents immediately, the marriage is valid by istihsan. The text further illustrates with an example where the condition is actually an existing reality (ka'in) making the statement immediate (tanziz) rather than suspensive. For instance, if a woman says 'If I have no husband, I marry you' and it is true that she has no husband, the marriage is valid because the condition is already fulfilled. Similarly, if the father says 'I marry you my daughter if I have not already married her' and he has not, the marriage is valid. This distinction between pure ta'liq (future conditional) and tanziz (existing condition) is crucial in Hanafi nikah jurisprudence.

Key Terms
تعليق (ta'liq): Suspending a contract on a future condition whose occurrence is uncertain.
تنجيز (tanziz): Making a contract immediate and unconditional, or with a condition that is already fulfilled.
كائن (ka'in): Existing, already true. Used to describe a condition that is already fulfilled at the time of the contract.
