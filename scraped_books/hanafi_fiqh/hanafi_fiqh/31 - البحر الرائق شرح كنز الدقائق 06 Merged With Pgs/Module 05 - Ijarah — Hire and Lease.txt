Ownership of Water in Cisterns in Rented Houses: The Principle of Ihraz
Fatwa determining water ownership in rental cisterns

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that water collected in storage structures (cisterns, jars) is owned by the owner through ihraz, and apply this rule to determine water ownership in rental disputes.

Core Idea
Water in a cistern made for storage is owned; the tenant only gets what the landlord permits.

When you rent a house with a water cistern, does the rainwater already in it belong to you? The Hanafi school distinguishes between natural water sources and man-made storage. This lesson examines the concept of ihraz and why stored water remains the property of the cistern owner.

Guided Path
1
Identify the property
What is a cistern (sahrij)? A structure built to collect and store rainwater.
In the text, cisterns are described as 'prepared to collect rainwater'.
2
Understand ihraz
Ihraz is the act of taking possession of mubah (ownerless) water by collecting it in a container.
Mubah means permissible/ownerless; through ihraz it becomes owned.
3
Apply the analogy
Cisterns are like jars – both are for storage; water in them is owned through ihraz.
The commentator says cisterns are 'bi-manzilat al-ahabab' (like jars).
4
Contrast with natural sources
Rivers, wells without storage structures are not for ihraz, so their water remains mubah.
The text mentions 'anhar al-mamluka, al-abar, al-hiyad' as comparisons.
5
Derive the ruling
In a rental, the lessor owns the stored water; lessee needs permission.
The fatwa states: 'laysa lil-mustajir illa ma abahu al-mujir'.

The cisterns that are placed to store (ihraz) water in houses: there is no doubt that their water becomes owned by their owners, in the status of jars and vessels. So ponder. The case that was raised to me from Jerusalem: if someone rents a house for dwelling in its rooms, and in the house there is a cistern prepared to collect rainwater, and there is water in it before the lease, is this water the property of the lessor, and the lessee has only what the lessor permits? Commentary
This is a fatwa-like ruling from the commentator regarding water in cisterns (saharij) built for rainwater collection in rented houses. The principle is that mubah (ownerless) water becomes owned through acquisition (ihraz), i.e., placing it in a container. Cisterns are made specifically for storage, analogous to jars and tanks. Thus the water in them is owned by the owner of the cistern (the lessor). When a house is rented, the tenant does not automatically own the water already present unless the lessor explicitly gives permission. The commentator compares to owned rivers, wells, and basins: those are not made for storage, so their water remains mubah. But cisterns are for storage, so owned. This is a practical ruling for rental disputes, following Hanafi principles of ownership through acquisition.

Key Terms
الإحراز (al-ihraz): The act of taking possession of a mubah (ownerless) thing by placing it in one's control, making it owned.
الصهريج (al-sahrij): A cistern, a tank built to collect and store rainwater.

Invalid Extraneous Conditions in Ijarah
Lists invalid extraneous conditions in ijarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify extraneous conditions that invalidate an ijarah contract in Hanafi law.

Core Idea
An ijarah contract is void if a condition imposes an extra obligation on the lessee for the lessor's benefit.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq lists specific conditions that ruin the validity of a lease: requiring the lessee to give a loan or gift, or suspending the lease on a contingent event. Understanding these forbidden conditions is essential for drafting valid lease agreements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition in the contract
Find the specific term that imposes an additional obligation on the lessee beyond paying rent and using the property.
For example, 'on condition that you lend me money' or 'if Zaid comes'.
2
Determine if the condition benefits the lessor
Check whether the condition provides a direct or indirect benefit to the lessor without additional consideration.
A loan or gift benefits the lessor monetarily; a contingent event may benefit him by providing flexibility.
3
Check if the condition is extraneous to the usufruct exchange
The essence of ijarah is exchange of usufruct for rent. A condition that goes beyond this scope (like a loan) is extraneous.
Extraneous conditions are those not necessary for the lease itself.
4
Conclude the ruling
If the condition is extraneous and benefits the lessor, the contract becomes fasid (invalid). The lessee must pay fair rent (ujrat mithl).
The Hanafi position is strict: such conditions nullify the lease.

قوله : (والإجارة) أي كان أجر داره على أن يقرضه المستأجر أو يهدي إليه أو إن قدم زيد ; كذا ذكره العيني. ومن صورها : استأجر حانوتاً احترق كل شهر بكذا على أن يعمره ويحتسب ما أنفقه من الأجرة لأن شرط العمارة على المستأجر يفسد العقد فعليه أجر المثل وله ما أنفقه وأجر مثل قيامه عليه، واشتراط تطيين الدار ومرمتها أو تعليق الباب عليها أو إدخال جذع في سقفها على المستأجر مفسد للعقد، وكذا اشتراط كري النهر أو حفر بئر فيها أو أن يسرقنها، وكذا على أن يردها مكروبة هكذا أطلقه في الكافي وفصل خواهر زاده فإن شرطه في المدة فسدت وبعد انقضائها لا والصحيح أن شرطه في المدة فسدت وإلا فإن قال أجرتك بكذا بأن تكربها بعد انقضاء المدة فتردها على مكروبة فلا تفسد وإن قال على أن تكربها بعدها فهي فاسدة؛ الكل من فتاوى الولوالجية. ويستثنى من إطلاق قولهم لا يصح تعليقها بالشرط ما صرحوا به في الإجارات لو قال لغاصب داره فرغها وإلا فأجر كل شهر كذا فسكت ولم يفرغها وجب المسمى مع أنه تعليق بعدم التفريع.
His saying: (And leasing) that is, if he rented his house on condition that the lessee give him a loan or give him a gift, or if Zaid comes; thus al-Ayni mentioned it. Among its forms: He rented a shop for each month for such amount on condition that he repair it and deduct his expenses from the rent, because the condition of repair by the lessee invalidates the contract; so he owes the fair rent, and he gets back his expenses and the fair wage for his work. And stipulating plastering the house and its maintenance, or hanging the door, or inserting a beam in its ceiling on the lessee invalidates the contract. Likewise stipulating dredging the river, or digging a well in it, or that he steal it, and likewise that he return it manured (makruba). Thus it is stated absolutely in al-Kafi, but Kharazada detailed: if he stipulates it during the term, it invalidates; after the term ends, it does not. The correct view is that stipulating it during the term invalidates. Otherwise, if he says, 'I rent it to you for such by your manuring it after the term ends and return it manured,' it does not invalidate; but if he says 'on condition that you manure it after it,' it is invalid. All from Fatawa al-Walwalijiyya. And an exception from the absolute statement that it is not valid to suspend it by a condition is what they explicitly stated in leasing: if he says to the usurper of his house, 'Vacate it, otherwise the rent is such per month,' and he remains silent and does not vacate, the named rent becomes obligatory, even though it is a suspension on non-vacation.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on conditional leasing (ijara) in Hanafi law, citing al-Ayni, al-Kafi, and Fatawa al-Walwalijiyya. It begins by listing invalid conditions: requiring the lessee to give a loan or gift to the lessor, or condition on a future event (if Zaid comes). These conditions are extraneous to the lease and benefit the lessor improperly, thus invalidating the contract. The text then gives detailed examples involving maintenance: if a shop is rented on condition that the lessee repair it and deduct costs from rent, the lease is invalid because the lessee is not obliged to repair; instead, he must pay fair rent and claim his expenses and labor as compensation. Similarly, any condition imposing structural work (plastering, door hanging, beam insertion, dredging, well digging) on the lessee invalidates the lease. A special case is manuring (kurab): if stipulated during the lease term, it invalidates; if stipulated to be done after the term ends, the ruling depends on wording. If phrased as 'by your manuring it after the term' (بأن تكر بها), it is valid; if phrased as 'on condition that you manure it after' (على أن تكربها بعد), it is invalid. Finally, an exception: if a lessor tells a usurper, 'Vacate or pay rent per month,' and the usurper remains silent, the named rent is due even though it is a conditional suspension. This exception shows that in certain contexts, conditional leases can be valid.

Key Terms
إجارة (ijara): Leasing or hiring, a contract for usufruct in exchange for rent.
فساد (fasād): Invalidity due to a corrupting condition; the contract is not void ab initio but requires rectification (paying fair rent).

Maintenance Conditions and the Manuring Distinction in Ijarah
Details maintenance conditions, manuring distinction, and an exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between permissible and impermissible conditions regarding maintenance and manuring in ijarah, including the effect of wording according to Kharazada.

Core Idea
Conditions requiring the lessee to maintain the property during the term invalidate the lease, but after the term, the wording determines validity.

This passage continues with detailed examples of conditions related to maintenance and repairs. The general rule is that imposing physical work on the lessee during the lease term corrupts the contract. However, there is a subtle distinction for manuring (kurab) and an exception for conditional suspension when dealing with a usurper.

Guided Path
1
Classify the type of condition
Determine whether the condition involves maintenance during the lease term or after it ends. Conditions during the term are generally invalid.
Examples: plastering, door hanging, beam insertion, dredging, well digging.
2
Apply the general rule
If the condition requires the lessee to perform work on the property during the lease, the contract is fasid. The lessee still owes fair rent and can claim expenses.
The reasoning: such work is not part of the usufruct exchange.
3
Analyze the manuring case
For manuring (kurab), Kharazada distinguishes: if stipulated during the term, invalid; after the term, the wording matters: 'by your manuring' (bi-an takribaha) is valid, but 'on condition that you manure' (ala an takribaha) is invalid.
The correct view (sahih) is that stipulating during the term invalidates.
4
Identify the exception
The exception: when a lessor says to a usurper 'Vacate or pay rent,' and the usurper stays silent, the named rent becomes due even though it is a conditional suspension.
This is allowed because it protects the lessor's right and doesn't impose an extraneous obligation.
