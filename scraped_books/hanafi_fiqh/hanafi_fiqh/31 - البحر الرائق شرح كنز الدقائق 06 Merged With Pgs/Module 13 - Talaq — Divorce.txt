Revocable Divorce (Raj'ah) Cannot Be Conditional
Intra-Hanafi dispute on void conditions in raj'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether a void condition invalidates revocable divorce (raj'ah) and distinguish it from the position that it does, citing the key differences between raj'ah and marriage.

Core Idea
Raj'ah cannot be conditional, but a void condition does not invalidate it—unlike marriage.

In this passage, the commentator of al-Bahr al-Raiq critically refines an earlier view on revocable divorce (raj'ah). While it is agreed that raj'ah cannot be made conditional (ta'liq bi al-shart), there is a dispute whether a void condition (shart fasid) invalidates the raj'ah itself. The commentator firmly holds that it does not, distinguishing raj'ah from marriage in several rulings and citing multiple Hanafi sources.

Guided Path
1
State the agreed-upon rule for raj'ah
Identify that all Hanafi scholars agree raj'ah cannot be conditional (cannot be ta'liq bi al-shart). This is the starting point.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
Al-Ayni and some transmitted views hold that raj'ah is also invalidated by a void condition, arguing by analogy to marriage (both are a form of ownership).
The opposing view uses qiyas: marriage invalidated by void condition → raj'ah should be too.
3
Analyze the commentator's critique
The commentator rejects the analogy, pointing out that marriage itself is not invalidated by a void condition, and that raj'ah differs from marriage in multiple rulings (no witnesses, no financial compensation, etc.).
The commentator also cites many sources that only mention the first rule (cannot be conditional) without mentioning invalidation.
4
Examine the two-rule framework
The matn's phrase 'what is invalidated by a void condition and cannot be conditional' is actually two separate rules. Raj'ah falls only under the second rule (cannot be conditional), not the first.
This distinction is key to resolving the dispute.
5
Determine the practical consequence
If a man says 'I take you back on condition that you lend me money', the raj'ah is valid but the condition is void (mufta bihi). The opposing view would declare the raj'ah invalid.
The thamarah al-khilaf affects the status of the marriage: if invalid, the couple must remarry; if valid, they are still married.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding revocable divorce (raj'ah). The matn states that raj'ah cannot be conditional. Ayni argued that it is also invalidated by a void condition, likening it to marriage. The commentator strongly rejects this, citing multiple sources that only state it cannot be conditional but do not mention invalidation by a condition. The discussion includes references from al-Zahiriyya, al-Jawhara, al-Bada'i', al-Tatar Khaniyya, and others. The commentator argues that raj'ah differs from marriage in several rulings (e.g., no witnesses required, no financial compensation, possibility of taking back an enslaved woman), so it does not necessarily follow that it is invalidated by a void condition. He cites al-Nahr and al-Shurnubulaliyya. The passage ends by distinguishing two separate rules: (1) what is invalidated by a void condition, and (2) what cannot be conditional. Raj'ah belongs only to the second rule; its invalidation by a void condition is not established according to the commentator.

Key Terms
رجعة (raj'ah): Revocable divorce return; the husband's right to take back his wife during the waiting period without a new marriage contract.
تعليق بالشرط (ta'liq bi al-shart): Making a legal act contingent upon a future condition; here applied to raj'ah.

The Effect of a Corrupt Condition on Ruj'ah: An Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Critique of the view that ruj'ah is invalidated by a corrupt condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions within the Hanafi school on whether a corrupt condition invalidates ruj'ah, explain the analogical reasoning using jest, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A corrupt condition does not invalidate ruj'ah because ruj'ah is valid under jest, and acts valid under jest resist corruption.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq critiques al-Ayni's view that ruj'ah is invalidated by a corrupt condition. The commentator argues that ruj'ah, like marriage, is valid under jest, and according to usul al-fiqh, acts that remain valid under jest are not invalidated by corrupt conditions. This lesson examines the two positions within the Hanafi school and the reasoning behind each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Recognize the two conflicting views within the Hanafi school: the author of Kanz al-Daqa'iq (and al-Ayni) holds that ruj'ah is invalidated by a corrupt condition, while the commentator (Ibn Nujaym) argues that it is not invalidated, though it cannot be suspended.
Both views are from Hanafi authorities; the dispute is about the correct analogy.
2
Understand the reasoning with jest
The commentator uses the principle that an act valid under jest is not invalidated by a corrupt condition. Since al-Bada'i' states ruj'ah is valid under jest, it follows that corrupt conditions do not invalidate it.
This principle is a well-known usul al-fiqh rule.
3
Evaluate the proofs of each side
The author's proof emphasizes ruj'ah as a continuation of marriage, akin to a transaction. The commentator counters by citing specific texts and the jest analogy. Evaluate the strength of each proof.
Note that the author's view may rely on a broader analogy, while the commentator uses a more specific rule.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: if a husband attaches a corrupt condition to ruj'ah, is the ruj'ah itself invalid or just the condition? The answer determines the marital status.
This difference affects whether the couple can continue the marriage after the 'iddah.

Distinguishing Iqrar from Insha' in Talaq and 'Itaq
Distinction between iqrar and insha in talaq and 'itaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between iqrar (acknowledgment) and insha' (creation) of talaq and 'itaq, and state the Hanafi ruling when each occurs under coercion.

Core Idea
Iqrar is weaker than insha' — coercion invalidates iqrar but not insha'.

In Hanafi law, a person may acknowledge a prior divorce (iqrar) or create a new divorce (insha'). These two acts have different legal effects, especially when performed under duress. This lesson explores the distinction and its practical consequences, drawing on the passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq.

Guided Path
1
Define iqrar and insha'
Clarify that iqrar is an acknowledgment of a pre-existing legal fact (e.g., 'I confirm that I divorced her yesterday'), while insha' is the creation of a new legal effect (e.g., 'I divorce you now').
In Arabic, iqrar is a form of testimony; insha' is a direct operative act.
2

According to the transmitted ruling in Al-Bahr al-Raiq, if a person is coerced into insha' of talaq or 'itaq, it takes effect. But if coerced into iqrar of the same, it does not take effect.
This difference shows that iqrar has lower binding force, akin to testimony, which is affected by duress.
3
Apply to conditional statements
The passage considers whether conditional iqrar (e.g., 'If I enter the house, I acknowledge her divorce') functions like conditional insha'. The ruling implies they may differ because of the coercion distinction.
One must check whether the statement is an acknowledgment or a creation when conditions are attached.
