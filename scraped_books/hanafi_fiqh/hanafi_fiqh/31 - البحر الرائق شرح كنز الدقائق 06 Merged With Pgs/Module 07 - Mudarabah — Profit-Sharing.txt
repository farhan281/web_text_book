Harmonizing the Rulings on Murabahah in Mudarabah
Resolving contradictory rulings on Murabahah in Mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the different scenarios in which a mudarib sells to the capital owner and correctly calculate the Murabahah price.

Core Idea
The Murabahah price includes the mudarib's profit share only when the sale price exceeds the capital cost.

In this passage, the author resolves a confusion about seemingly contradictory rulings on Murabahah in a mudarabah context. By distinguishing scenarios where the sale price equals the capital (no profit) and where it exceeds the capital (profit), he shows consistency. This lesson guides you through the key scenarios and the correct calculation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of scenarios
The first type (from al-Muhit) involves no excess: the sale price equals the cost, so the Murabahah price is simply the cost. The third and fourth types involve a profit: the sale price exceeds the cost, and the mudarib's profit share must be added.
The confusion arises from mixing these scenarios.
2
Understand the example with profit
The mudarib has capital of ten with a half share of profit. He buys a cloth for ten and sells it to the capital owner for fifteen. This sale generates a profit of five (15-10). Since the mudarib's share is half, he is entitled to 2.5.
The sale price of fifteen is the price to the capital owner, which includes the mudarib's share already? Actually, the sale to capital owner is a separate transaction.
3
Calculate the Murabahah price
When selling Murabahah, the mudarib must add his profit share to the cost. Cost = 10. Profit share = half of 5 = 2.5. Murabahah price = 10 + 2.5 = 12.5.
This matches the text: 'he sells it Murabahah for twelve and a half'.
4
Apply the principle to the no-profit scenario
If the sale price equaled the cost (e.g., sold for ten), there is no profit, so the Murabahah price would be ten. The ruling is consistent across all types.
Thus, the apparent contradiction is resolved.

Commentary
This passage clarifies an apparent contradiction in the rulings about Murabahah when a mudarib sells an item to the capital owner. The confusion arises because there are multiple scenarios. In the first scenario (from al-Muhit), there is no profit in the transaction because the sale price equals the cost. In the third and fourth scenarios (discussed in the chapter of Murabahah), there is a profit share for the mudarib. The author explains that what he said in the Mudarabah chapter is consistent: he gave the example of a mudarib who has ten with a half-ownership, buys a cloth for ten, sells it to the capital owner for fifteen, and then sells it Murabahah for twelve and a half (the cost plus half the profit). This is a different scenario from the one in al-Muhit. Thus, there is no contradiction, and this is the correct response to al-Zayla'i's criticism.

Void Conditions in Mudarabah: The Contract Remains Valid
Examples of void conditions in mudarabah and their effect on validity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a void condition does not invalidate a mudarabah contract, and explain the distinction between conditions that are ignored and those that would invalidate the contract.

Core Idea
A void condition does not invalidate mudarabah; the contract stands while the condition is ignored.

In Islamic finance, mudarabah is a profit-sharing partnership where capital and labor are combined. Often, parties attach conditions to the contract, some of which may be void (fasid). This lesson examines the Hanafi position that such conditions do not nullify the mudarabah itself, drawing from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq and later fatwa collections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the attached condition
Read the example given: 'I give you mudarabah with one thousand for half the profit if so-and-so wills or if Zayd comes.' Recognise that the condition ('if so-and-so wills') suspends the contract's effect.
Suspension on a future event is a condition.
2
Understand the ruling from al-Bazzaziyya
The passage states: 'And in al-Bazzaziyya: it (mudarabah) is not invalidated by a void condition.' This is the core ruling – the contract is sahih (valid), while the condition is considered null and void.
Void condition: contravenes the nature of the contract.
3
Compare with the gift case from al-Walwalijiyya
A wife gives a house to her husband on condition he does not divorce her. If he divorces before a stipulated time, the gift is void (because he breached). If he fulfills, it is valid. This shows that conditions can affect the validity of a gift if they are not merely void but integral to the transaction.
Gift (hibah) with a condition of retention – condition may be valid if time-bound.
4
Apply the principle to other contracts (bequest)
The text briefly mentions bequest (إيصاء) with a condition to marry the testator's daughter. Such a condition does not invalidate the bequest either, reinforcing the rule that void conditions in contracts are ignored.
Consistency across different contract types.

Commentary
This passage covers conditions in mudarabah (profit-sharing). The commentator first notes that the matn phrase 'والمضاربة' is accompanied by an example: 'I give you mudarabah with one thousand for half the profit if so-and-so wills or if Zayd comes.' This is a valid suspension of the contract on a condition. The commentator criticizes al-'Ayni for giving a poor example. Then it quotes from al-Bazzaziyya that mudarabah is not invalidated by a void condition. A supporting case from al-Walwalijiyya: a wife gives a gift to her husband on condition that he does not divorce her; if he divorces, the gift is void if she set a time; otherwise it is valid if he fulfills. This illustrates how conditions affect gifts. Finally, a second mention of bequest (الإيصاء) giving an example: 'I appoint you executor on condition that you marry my daughter' – this condition does not invalidate the bequest. So in both mudarabah and bequest, void conditions do not necessarily void the core contract.

Key Terms
المضاربة (al-mudarabah): A profit-sharing partnership where one party provides capital and the other provides labor.
الهبة (al-hibah): A gift; a contract of donation.
الشرط الفاسد (al-shart al-fasid): A void condition that does not invalidate the contract in Hanafi law unless it contradicts the contract's nature.
