Conditions in Transfer of Debt (Hawalah)
Rulings on conditions in hawalah and their effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conditions that render a hawalah contract void and those that are void but leave the contract valid, based on Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Not all conditions in hawalah are void; some invalidate the contract, while others are void but the contract remains binding.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the effect of conditions in hawalah. It addresses a common misunderstanding that all conditions in hawalah are simply void but the contract stands. The text shows that some conditions, such as a condition preventing the creditor from returning to the transferor, are void but do not affect the contract's validity. However, other conditions, like requiring payment from a source the transferee cannot guarantee, can invalidate the entire hawalah. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying hawalah in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Locate the specific condition being placed on the hawalah. For example, a condition that the creditor cannot return to the transferor if the transferee defaults.
The text uses 'بشرط أن لا ترجع علي عند التواء'.
2
Determine the ruling on the condition
Is the condition valid or void? Does it affect the contract's validity? The text states that the condition not to return is void but the hawalah is valid.
The condition is void because it contradicts the nature of hawalah, which allows recourse upon default.
3
Recognize valid conditions
Some conditions are permissible, such as suspending hawalah on a future event or stipulating an option (khiyar) for the creditor. These do not invalidate the contract.
The text mentions 'ويصح تعليقها بالشرط، ومنه اشتراط الخيار للمحتال وهو جائز'.
4
Identify invalidating conditions
Conditions that make fulfillment impossible or uncertain can void the entire hawalah. Example: requiring payment from the sale price of the transferor's house, which is not under the transferee's control.
The transferee must be able to fulfill the obligation; otherwise the contract is fasid (invalid).
5
Reconcile with the author's absolute statement
The author's general statement that hawalah is not invalidated by conditions must be qualified. Al-Bazzaziyyah provides exceptions that limit the absolute rule.
This is a refinement (takhsis) of the general principle.

Commentary
This passage discusses conditions in hawalah (transfer of debt). The author gives an example from al-Ayni: the transferor says, 'I transfer your debt to so-and-so on condition that you do not return to me if he refuses to pay.' The ruling is that the transfer is valid but the condition is void; the creditor can still return to the transferor if the transferee defaults. Suspending hawalah on a condition is valid, and stipulating an option for the creditor (e.g., to cancel the transfer) is also valid. However, some conditions can invalidate hawalah entirely. For example, if the transfer stipulates that the transferee must pay from the sale price of the transferor's house, this invalidates the hawalah because the transferee cannot guarantee that sale. But if the transferee undertakes to pay from the sale of his own house, the hawalah is valid (though he cannot be forced to sell). Similarly, if acceptance was conditional on payment at harvest time, the transferee cannot be forced to pay before that time. These nuances show that the author's absolute statement that hawalah is never invalidated by conditions is not entirely accurate.

The Nature of Hawalah: Transfer of Debt or Right to Claim?
Defines hawalah and examines internal Hanafi dispute on transfer of debt vs. right to claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi views on Hawalah and identify the practical consequence of each in at least two scenarios.

Core Idea
Hawalah transfers either the debt itself or only the claim — a difference with real legal effects.

Hawalah is a contract that transfers a debt from one person's liability to another. While Hanafi scholars agree on its validity, they differ on what exactly is transferred: does the debt itself leave the original debtor, or only the right to claim it? This lesson unpacks the internal Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, examining their proofs and the practical consequences that follow from each view.

Guided Path
1

State the legal definition of hawalah and identify the three parties: transferor (muḥīl), creditor (muḥtāl), and transferee (muḥāl ʿalayhi).
Ensure you understand the basic structure before diving into the dispute.
2
Identify the two views on transfer
State the positions of Abu Yusuf (transfers debt and claim) and Muhammad (transfers only claim).
Both views are within the Hanafi school; the dispute is about legal effect, not validity.
3
Analyze the proofs for each view
For Abu Yusuf: if creditor releases the transferee, it's valid; releasing transferor is invalid. For Muhammad: if transferor pays after hawalah, he is forced to accept; also analogies with kafalah.
Note how each proof relies on consequences that would follow if the debt had or had not left the transferor's liability.
4
Examine the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider two scenarios: (1) pledgor hawalah of mortgagee's debt — can he retake pledge? (2) creditor releases transferor after hawalah — is it valid?
These scenarios show the real-world impact of the dispute.
5
Determine the preferred position (mufta bihi)
According to Fath al-Qadir, the corrected view is that hawalah effects complete discharge (bara'a), i.e., transfer of the debt itself.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa today.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq defines the contract of Hawalah (transfer of debt) and presents an internal Hanafi dispute regarding its legal nature. The passage begins by stating the definition: Hawalah is the transfer of debt from one person's liability (dhimma) to another's, valid only for debts, not for specific physical assets (ayn), and requires the consent of both the creditor (muhtal) and the debtor being transferred to (muhtal 'alayh). The author then discusses the linguistic origins of the term, citing classical lexicons. The core of the passage is the second section on the Shari'ah meaning: although scholars agree that Hawalah involves transfer (naql), they differ on what is transferred. One view, attributed to Imam Abu Yusuf, holds that both the right to claim (mutalaba) and the debt itself are transferred. The other view, attributed to Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, holds that only the right to claim is transferred, not the debt itself. Each view is supported by consensus (ijma') inferred from practical rulings. The text elaborates on the proofs. For Abu Yusuf's view: if the creditor releases or gifts the debt to the transferee, it is valid; if he does so to the transferor, it is not. This indicates the debt has left the transferor's liability. For Muhammad's view: if the transferor pays the debt after the transfer before the transferee, he is forced to accept (not a volunteer), showing the debt was still in his liability; also releasing the transferee does not revert the debt, but gifting does—similar to a surety (kafalah) where the original debt remains on the principal. The preferred position in the school (al-mu'tamad) according to Fath al-Qadir is that Hawalah effects complete discharge (bara'a) from the debt, meaning the debt itself transfers. The passage concludes by highlighting two practical consequences (fā'idah al-ikhtilāf): (1) If a pledgor (rahin) makes a Hawalah of the mortgagee's (murtahin) debt to a third party, then according to Abu Yusuf the pledgor can retake the pledge, but according to Muhammad he cannot. (2) If the creditor releases the transferor after the Hawalah, it is invalid according to Abu Yusuf (since the debt has left his liability) but valid according to Muhammad. Some later investigators (al-muhaqqiqun) argue that the disagreement is not genuine; rather, Muhammad considered different aspects: in some rulings he treated Hawalah as deferment (ta'jil) and in others as discharge, depending on whether the literal meaning of transfer or the underlying meaning of deferment is prioritized. Core Idea
Hawala transfers the debt itself (al-bara'ah min al-dayn) — the relied-upon Hanafi view.

This lesson unpacks an important intra-Hanafi dispute: when a debt is transferred from one debtor to another, does the original debtor become completely free of the debt, or does the creditor only gain the right to demand payment from the new debtor? The answer affects the validity of subsequent releases, gifts, and recourses. We will examine the evidence from the rulings on ibra' (release) and hibah (gift) of the debt, and see how the later Hanafi masters settled the issue.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Identify the two views within the Hanafi school: (1) hawala transfers the debt itself (the muhil is freed), and (2) hawala only transfers the right to demand payment (the muhil remains indebted).
The first view is attributed to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf; the second to Muhammad al-Shaybani.
2
Examine the evidence for debt transfer from ibra' and hibah rulings
The passage presents a key argument: if the creditor releases or gifts the debt to the muhal 'alayh (transferee), it is valid; but if he does so to the muhil (original debtor), it is invalid. This only makes sense if the debt has moved to the muhal 'alayh's liability.
3

The opposing view relies on consensus that if the muhil pays after hawala, he is not a volunteer and must be accepted — suggesting the debt remains on him. Also, ibra' of the muhil is invalid even under the transfer view, but the opponent argues it would be valid if the debt had truly left him.
The author notes this 'consensus' is not unanimous, and that the compelled payment can be explained as protection for the creditor.
4

The author cites al-Bada'i' which presents the rulings but does not prefer one view. However, Fath al-Qadir (Ibn al-Humam) declares that the corrected madhhab position is that hawala causes freedom from the debt (al-bara'ah min al-dayn). This is now the mufta bihi.
The tarjih is based on the harmony of all rulings with this view.

The dispute affects two main issues: (1) whether the muhil can reclaim a mortgage given for the debt after hawala, and (2) whether ibra' of the muhil is valid. Under the transfer view, ibra' of the muhil is invalid and the mortgage is released. Under the claim-only view, ibra' would be valid and the mortgage remains.

The Three Types of Hawala: Binding, Permissible, Invalid
Describes three types of hawala based on conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the three types of hawala (binding, permissible, invalid) according to the Hanafi school and state the legal effect of each.

Core Idea
A hawala is binding if the transferee accepts unconditionally; from his own assets it's optional; from the transferor's it's invalid.

Hawala is a transfer of debt. Not all transfers are immediately binding; the outcome depends on how the payment is conditioned. This lesson will help you identify which conditions create a binding obligation, which leave it optional, and which render the contract invalid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
List the three categories: laazima (binding), ja'iza (permissible), fasida (invalid). Understand that the distinction turns on the source of payment and the certainty of availability.
2
Analyse the binding type
The binding hawala occurs when the creditor transfers the debt to a person who accepts, whether the transfer is conditional or unconditional. The transferee is immediately obliged to pay.
Acceptance by the transferee makes it binding regardless of conditions.

Analyse the permissible type
If the condition is that the transferee pays from his own assets (e.g., the price of his house or his slave), the transferee is not compelled to pay before those assets are realised. It is a non-binding promise.
The transferee may pay voluntarily but cannot be forced.
4
Analyse the invalid type
If the condition requires the transferee to pay from the transferor's assets (e.g., price of the transferor's house), the hawala is invalid because the transferee cannot sell the transferor's property without authorisation.
The condition makes fullfilment impossible for the transferee.

Core Idea

This lesson examines two key issues in hawala (transfer of debt). First, why the debt must be known for the transfer to be valid. Second, what happens when the parties do not specify whether the debt is immediate or deferred. The analysis uses an analogy to kafala (suretyship) and then highlights a critical distinction between the two contracts.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents two interrelated rulings on hawala (transfer of debt). First, hawala is invalid if the debt being transferred is unknown (majhul). Consent of the Muhtal Alayh in Hawala: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on consent condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the relied-upon Hanafi position on the requirement of the muhtal alayh's consent in hawala and explain the evidence and tarjih that support it.

Core Idea
The muhtal alayh's consent is required for hawala validity in all cases, per the relied-upon Hanafi madhhab.

This lesson addresses a debated point within the Hanafi school: whether the consent of the person on whom a hawala is made (muhtal alayh) is a necessary condition for the contract's validity. You will encounter two conflicting reports—one requiring consent, another likening hawala to agency—and see how the later authorities resolved the issue. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to distinguish between authoritative and weak positions within the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the disputed condition
Recognize that hawala is a transfer of debt from a muhil (debtor) to a muhtal alayh (third party). The disputed condition is whether the muhtal alayh must consent to this transfer for the hawala to be valid.
The condition is called rida al-muhtal alayh.
2
Contrast the two opposing views within the Hanafi school
One view (reported in al-Mujtaba) requires consent and declares hawala without consent impermissible. Another view (reported in Shurut al-Zahiriyya) says consent is not a condition, drawing an analogy to appointing an agent to collect a debt.
3
Examine the commentator's reconciliation
The commentator ('I say') interprets the claimed consensus in Shurut al-Zahiriyya as limited to cases where the transferred debt matches a debt the muhtal alayh owes to the muhil. This reconciles the apparent contradiction.
The commentator is Ibn Nujaym in al-Bahr al-Ra'iq.
4
Identify the relied-upon position and its rationale
The relied-upon madhhab (al-madhhab al-mu'tamad) requires consent in all cases, regardless of whether the muhtal alayh owes a debt or the amounts match. The analogy to agency is rejected because hawala transfers liability, not merely the right to collect.
5
Understand the practical consequence of the dispute
Under the relied-upon view, a hawala executed without the muhtal alayh's consent is invalid, and the original debts remain with the original parties. Under the opposing view, it would be valid in certain circumstances.
This shows the importance of tarjih (weighing) in Hanafi fiqh.

Acceptance of Muhtal Alayh's Claim in Muhil's Absence
Ruling on admissibility of muhtal alayh's claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between objectionable (mustankar) and non-objectionable claims to determine whether the muhtal alayh's claim is accepted in the absence of the muhil.

Core Idea
Claim to nullify a valid debt must be plausible; inherently improbable claims are rejected even with proof.

In hawala, once the transfer is validly concluded with the consent of the muhtal alayh, the original debtor (muhil) may be absent. The question arises: can the muhtal alayh raise claims that would absolve the muhil and free himself? Hanafi law applies a filter: the claim must not be 'mustankar'—inherently objectionable or contradictory to apparent facts. This lesson explores the principle and its application through examples from al-Bazzaziyya and Furuk al-Karabisi.

Guided Path
1
Identify the procedural context
Recognize that the ruling applies after a valid hawala with the muhtal alayh's consent, when the muhil is absent. The muhtal alayh is asserting a claim to discharge the muhil.
The muhil's absence is key; if present, the claim could be addressed directly.
2
Classify the claim as objectionable or non-objectionable
Determine whether the claim is 'mustankar'—objectionable and improbable. Claims that contradict the apparent validity of the underlying obligation (e.g., the debt was for an illicit transaction) are mustankar. Plausible claims (e.g., payment, release) are not.
The standard is objective: what would an average person consider inherently improbable?
3
Apply the rule to specific examples
From the text: claiming the debt was for wine price or that the marriage was invalid is mustankar → claim rejected. Claiming ibra', payment, or sale with possession is not mustankar → claim accepted.
The distinction is independent of the availability of evidence; even if the muhtal alayh brings proof, an objectionable claim is not heard.

The Muhil's Release from Debt in Hawala: Effect and Limits
Definition and refinement of the muhil's release in hawala, with consequences for retention rights and special cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the muhil's release in hawala, distinguish it from the Shafi'i view, and apply the consequences to rights of retention and special cases.

Core Idea
In Hanafi hawala, the muhil is temporarily freed from the debt itself, not just the claim, and loses rights of retention.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the phrase 'برئ المحيل بالقبول من الدين' and defends the Hanafi position that the muhil is freed from the debt itself, not merely the claim. It clarifies the temporary nature of this release (bara'a muwaqqata) and derives practical consequences for sales, pledges, dower, and the case of a mukatib. It also addresses a contrary transmission from al-Ziyadat and the distinction between debt and claim in the context of surety.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
Recognize that the Hanafi school holds that the muhil is freed from the debt itself (min al-dayn) upon acceptance, not just from the claim (al-mutalaba). This is a point of ikhtilaf with the Shafi'i school.
The commentator explicitly refutes those who say it is only freedom from the claim.
2
Understand the temporary nature
The release is temporary (muwaqqata) because if the hawala fails (e.g., the muhtal 'alayh cannot pay), the debt returns to the muhil. This preserves the possibility of the original obligation reviving.
This is contrasted with the permanent release in a true transfer of ownership.
3
Apply to three retention scenarios
The text gives three examples: seller's right to retain goods, pledgee's right to retain pledge, and wife's right to retain herself (habs nafs) for dower. In each, if the muhil transfers the debt, the right to retain is lost because the muhil is no longer indebted.
The opposite cases (when the seller/pledgee are the recipients of the transfer) are governed by the transmission from al-Ziyadat.
4
Note the contrary transmission from al-Ziyadat
According to one transmission in al-Ziyadat, when the seller or pledgee makes the hawala, their right of retention falls; but if they are the ones to whom transfer is made, their right remains because the muhtal 'alayh stands in their place.
This intra-madhhab variant shows that the effect on retention depends on who initiated the hawala.
5
Consider special cases: mukatib and surety
For a mukatib (slave under kitaba), if he transfers his master's debt to another, he is freed because his liability is cleared; if the master transfers to him, he is not freed until he pays. For a surety (kafil), the muhil's release from debt does not apply because the surety has no original debt; he is only freed from the claim.
The distinction between debt and claim is crucial in understanding the surety's position.

The Order of Hawalahs and Their Validity
Ruling on sequential hawalahs from two creditors to two debtors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling for two sequential hawalahs and apply the condition regarding which hawalah is performed first.

Core Idea
If the hawalah to the surety comes first, both are valid; if the hawalah to the principal comes first, the second is invalid.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a nuanced scenario in hawalah (transfer of debt). It demonstrates how the sequence of transfers affects their validity, highlighting the importance of order in Islamic financial transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and debts
The creditor owes 1000 dirhams to each of two men. He makes two restricted hawalahs: one to the surety (kafil) and one to the principal (asil) for separate debts.
Each hawalah is muqayyada (restricted), tied to a specific debt.
2
Recognize the two scenarios
The hawalahs can occur sequentially (in either order) or simultaneously. This step sets up the conditional ruling.
Simultaneous: both valid. Sequential: depends on order.
3
Analyze the first sequence: surety first
If the creditor first transfers to the surety, both hawalahs are valid. The first is obvious; the second is valid because delaying the claim from the surety does not affect the principal.
The delaying effect (takhir) of the first hawalah does not invalidate the second.
4
Analyze the second sequence: principal first
If the creditor first transfers to the principal, the second hawalah to the surety is invalid. This is because once the claim is transferred to the principal, the claim against the surety is released.
The invalidity is due to the loss of the claim against the surety.
5
Conclude the rule
The ruling is: sequential hawalahs are valid only if the transfer to the surety precedes the transfer to the principal. Otherwise, the second is fasid (corrupt).
This rule applies to restricted hawalahs.

Definition of Tawa and Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Judicial Bankruptcy
Defines tawa and presents intra-Hanafi dispute on judicial bankruptcy as tawa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the terminological definition of tawa (financial ruin) in hawalah and explain why Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn disagree on whether judicial bankruptcy constitutes tawa.

Core Idea
Tawa is only death or denial with oath; Abu Hanifa rejects judge's bankruptcy as tawa.

In hawalah, the creditor can only revert to the transferor if the transferee's debt is lost through tawa (financial ruin). This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq defines tawa both linguistically and terminologically, and then presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that a judge's declaration of bankruptcy does not constitute tawa, while his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that it does. Understanding this difference is crucial for determining when a creditor can revert a hawalah debt.

If a judge declares the transferee bankrupt, according to Abu Hanifa the creditor cannot revert to the transferor; according to the Sahibayn, he can.
The practical consequence is the right of recourse against the transferor.

Effect of Surety and Pledge on Reversion After Tawa
Rulings on reversion of debt in hawalah upon transferee's insolvency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the effect of a surety from that of a voluntary pledge on reversion of debt in hawalah, and apply the ruling to given scenarios.

Core Idea
A surety prevents reversion of debt after tawa; a voluntary pledge does not.

In hawalah (transfer of debt), if the transferee (new debtor) dies insolvent (tawa), the debt may revert to the transferor (original debtor). However, the presence of a surety (kafil) or a voluntary pledge (rahn) changes the outcome. This lesson examines the nuanced rulings from the Hanafi school, distinguishing between personal guarantee and collateral.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the security
In a hawalah transaction, identify the transferor (original debtor), transferee (new debtor), and creditor. Determine whether a surety (kafil) was provided by the transferee or a voluntary pledge (rahn) by a third party.
A surety is a personal guarantee; a pledge is an asset given as collateral.
2
Apply the rule for surety
If there is a surety at the time of hawalah, and the transferee dies insolvent, the debt does NOT revert to the transferor. This holds regardless of whether the surety was with the transferee's order or not, and whether the surety is immediate or deferred.
Al-Bazzaziyya confirms this rule; Al-Ziyadat and Al-Khulasa discuss the definition of insolvency but agree that surety itself does not prevent reversion.
3
Apply the rule for voluntary pledge
If there is no surety but a third party voluntarily gives a pledge (rahn), and the transferee dies insolvent, the debt DOES revert to the transferor. The pledge does not block reversion.
The pledge is only collateral; it does not guarantee the debt in the same way as a surety.
4
Consider special cases
If the creditor releases the surety after the transferee's death, reversion becomes possible. If the transferor authorized the sale of the pledge and sale occurred but price not received before tawa, the hawalah is void and the sale price belongs to the pledgor.
These details refine the main rule.
5
Note the evidence rule
If there is a dispute over whether the transferee left an estate, the claimant's (creditor's) statement is accepted upon oath, because they claim the return of the debt based on tawa.
This procedural rule protects the creditor.

Ruling on hawala conditioned on a deposit and effect of gift or destruction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a gift by the transferee in a hawala conditioned on a deposit and explain the reasoning behind its validity.

Core Idea
A transferee can gift the deposit he hasn't yet owned because he has the right to own it.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in hawala where the transfer is conditioned on a particular deposit. The text discusses whether the transferee (muhtal) can validly gift that deposit to the debtor (muhal 'alayh). At first glance, this seems problematic since the transferee does not yet own the deposit. However, the Hanafi school resolves this by invoking the transferee's right to take ownership, which gives him the capacity to transfer ownership. Additionally, the lesson distinguishes this case from hawala conditioned on a usurped item (maghsūb), where the consequences differ.

The hawala is 'muqayyadah bil-ʿayn al-wadiʿah' – conditioned on a specific deposit. The transferee (muhtal) gifts that deposit to the debtor (muhal ʿalayh).
The deposit is wadiʿah, meaning a trust not owned by the debtor but held by him.
2
Recognize the issue
The apparent problem: the transferee does not own the deposit, so how can he legally gift it? Ownership is a prerequisite for a valid gift.
Gift (hiba) in Hanafi fiqh requires the donor to be the owner of the item.

Understand the objection
The text states 'how can he own it?' – meaning how can the transferee give ownership to the debtor when he himself does not own it yet.
This is a hypothetical objection, not a real disagreement between schools.
4

The answer: because the transferee has the right (haqq) to take ownership of that deposit (by virtue of the hawala), he also has the right to transfer ownership to another. The right is treated as equivalent to ownership for this purpose.
This is a Hanafi legal extension: the right to own is sufficient to transfer.

The text notes that the ruling is specific to wadiʿah. For a hawala conditioned on a usurped item (maghsūb), destruction does not void the hawala; the debtor remains liable for a substitute (mithl or value). Here, the liability is transferred to a substitute, whereas for a deposit the liability is tied to the specific item.
This distinction prevents the analogy from being applied universally.

The Effect of Debt Extinction on Hawala: Valid vs. Void Transfers
Ruling and distinction between debt falling after obligation vs. non-existence at time of hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between hawala that remains valid despite later debt extinction and hawala that is void ab initio, and apply the distinction to cases like mukatab manumission and sale rescission.

Core Idea
A hawala is valid if the debt existed at the time of transfer; later extinction does not void it, but non-existence from the outset renders it void.

In this passage, the author of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a critical nuance in hawala (transfer of debt). He distinguishes between cases where the underlying debt falls after the hawala was validly concluded and cases where the debt never existed at the time of transfer. This distinction determines whether the hawala remains valid or is void. Understanding this difference is essential for correctly applying hawala rulings in practice.

Hawala is a transfer of debt from one party (transferor) to another (transferee). The ruling varies: if the debt existed at the time of transfer, the hawala is valid (sahih); if the debt never existed, it is void (batil).
2

The proof is the principle that the existence of the debt at the time of transfer is a condition for validity. The text states 'عندنا' (in our school) indicating the Hanafi reliance on this principle.
No explicit Quran/sunnah citation is given; the reasoning is based on the nature of hawala as a transfer of an existing right.

Trace the wajh al-dalalah
If the debt was present when the hawala was concluded, the transfer is valid. Subsequent extinction (e.g., manumission, death of slave, return by option) does not retroactively invalidate the contract because the right existed at the time of transfer.
The key is timing: existence at contract formation, not later.

Zufar al-Hudhayl held that if the underlying debt is extinguished, the hawala becomes void because there is nothing left to transfer. The Hanafi majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) disagreed.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute; Zufar's view is a minority.

The text uses 'عندنا' to assert the majority position, and it is the mufta bihi. The practical result (thamarah al-khilaf) is that the transferee can still recover from the transferor if the debt later falls.
The tarjih is based on the principle that a valid contract is not undone by a later event.

Hawala for Orphans by Guardians
First sub-ruling on hawala with orphans

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on hawala on behalf of an orphan and identify the condition of wealthiness and the difference of opinion when the transferee is equally wealthy.

Core Idea
Guardian may accept a hawala only if the transferee is wealthier; if equal, disagreement.

This lesson covers the first of several important branches on hawala. It addresses the permissibility of a guardian (father or executor) accepting a transfer of an orphan's debt to a wealthier person, and the difference of opinion when the transferee's wealth is equal. Understanding this ruling protects the orphan's interest and highlights the Hanafi emphasis on benefit (nazar).

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties involved
The guardian (father or executor) is accepting a hawala on behalf of an orphan. The debt is owed to the orphan by the original debtor. The transferee is the new debtor.
The guardian's action is bound by the condition of benefit (shart al-nazar).
2
Check the wealth condition
The ruling states that acceptance is permissible only if the transferee is more solvent (amla') than the original debtor. If equally wealthy, there are two opinions.
This condition ensures that the orphan does not suffer a loss.
3
Recognize the difference of opinion
When the transferee's solvency is equal, the text says 'there are two opinions'. These are ijtihad-based differences within the Hanafi school.
The mufta bihi position is the one that gives the orphan the most security.
4
Apply to the trustee (mutawalli)
The same ruling applies to a waqf trustee (mutawalli) accepting hawala on behalf of the waqf, as stated later.
The trustee also must act in the waqf's interest.

Deferred Hawala on Inherited Debts
Second sub-ruling on deferred hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a deferred hawala on an orphan's inherited debt is invalid, and distinguish it from a debt contracted by the guardian.

Core Idea
Deferring a hawala is like a temporary release, which is invalid.

This lesson addresses the second important branch: accepting a deferred hawala on an orphan's inherited debt. The Hanafi school treats a deferred hawala as a temporary release (ibra' muwaqqat), which is invalid. However, if the debt was created by the guardian themselves (e.g., for the orphan's benefit), deferment is allowed by the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).

1
Identify the type of debt

Ibra' must be immediate and unconditional.

The Sahibayn allow it because the guardian had the authority to contract the debt with terms.
4
Note the difference of opinion
There is ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school on contracted debt: Sahibayn allow deferred hawala, while (probably) Abu Hanifa does not.
The mufta bihi is the view of the Sahibayn, as it is more facilitative.

Hawala in Illness and on Deposit
Third and fourth sub-rulings on hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rulings on hawala when the transferor falls ill and when the subject of hawala is a deposit, and distinguish the treatment of creditors.

Core Idea
Payment after hawala in illness is distributed among all creditors; a deposit subject to hawala is not part of the transferor's estate.

This lesson covers two more branches: hawala when the transferor falls ill, and hawala on a deposit. If the transferee pays after the transferor's illness, the money is treated as part of the estate and shared among all creditors. However, if the hawala was on a deposit, the creditors cannot touch it because the deposit is not owned by the transferor.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario of illness
The transferor falls ill after the hawala but before the transferee pays. The transferee then pays the debt to the creditor (the original creditor who accepted the hawala). Now, the amount paid is considered part of the transferor's estate because the payment was made while he was ill (a period akin to death illness).
The money is taken from the transferee and distributed among all creditors of the transferor pro rata.
2
Apply the rule for deposit
If the hawala is on a deposit (i.e., the deposit is treated as a debt owed by the depositee to the depositor), the creditors of the transferor cannot claim from the transferee because the deposit is not part of the transferor's general assets.
The deposit is a trust, not a debt.
3
Identify the outcome
In illness, the transferee pays but then can later recover from the transferor's estate, joining other creditors. In deposit, the transferee is safe from other creditors.
The transferee in the deposit case has priority because the deposit is specific.

Multiple Transfers and Conditional Hawala
Fifth and sixth sub-rulings on hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on hawala upon hawala (multiple transfers) and on a conditional guarantee involving hawala.

Core Idea
Hawala upon hawala is valid and discharges the first transferee; a conditional guarantee with hawala depends on acceptance.

The final two branches deal with multiple transfers and conditional hawala. If the transferee (first transferee) transfers the debt to a second person, the first is freed and the debt becomes on the second. Also, if someone guarantees a debt with the condition that he will make a hawala, the guarantee stands unless the hawala is accepted.

Guided Path
1
Understand hawala upon hawala
The first transferee (muḥāl ʿalayh) transfers the debt to a third party. This is valid. The first transferee is discharged, and the debt shifts to the third party.
This is like a chain of transfers, similar to a guarantor guaranteeing another guarantor in kafala.
2
Understand conditional guarantee with hawala
A person says: 'I guarantee what X owes you on condition that I transfer you to Y' and the plaintiff agrees. If he actually makes the hawala to Y and Y accepts, the guarantee ends. If Y refuses, the guarantor remains liable.
The condition is part of the promise, but the actual release depends on the completion of the hawala.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This lesson examines two crucial branches of hawala concerning orphans. First, the guardian must seek a more solvent assignee to protect the orphan's interest. The ta' is open in both; it is a Persianized word. Some explained it as: a letter from the owner of money to his agent to pay money as a loan to secure safety from road danger; as in al-Misbah. Commentary
This passage provides several rulings. First, in a void hawala, the payer can claim from either the assignee (who received the money) or the assignor, whichever is convenient. This applies also to the case of a hired worker being sold and then reclaimed. Second, the text defines suftaja (a type of credit instrument used to avoid danger in travel) and states it is disliked (makruh) because it is a loan that benefits the lender (by securing safety). However, if the benefit is not stipulated, some say there is no harm. The Bazzaziyyah prefers the permissibility of gifts and better repayment without stipulation, but if the extra weight is significant, it is not allowed. Third, a comment clarifies that restricted hawala (for a specific debt) may be valid, but unrestricted hawala (for a general right) is invalid because hawala is only for debts. The author notes a possible exception for rights to spoils under the imam's control, but he is hesitant about it.

The Ruling and Definition of Suftaja
Definition and ruling of suftaja

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define suftaja, state its default ruling (disliked), and identify the condition that changes the ruling to permissible.

Core Idea
Suftaja is disliked because it is a loan that benefits the lender, but if the benefit is not stipulated, it is permissible.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq defines suftaja, a financial instrument used to secure safety in travel, and discusses its ruling in the Hanafi school. The default position is dislike (makruh), but a crucial distinction is made based on whether the safety benefit is stipulated as a condition. The later Hanafi scholars prefer the view that if the benefit is not stipulated, it is permissible.

Guided Path
1
Define suftaja linguistically and technically
Understand that suftaja is a Persianized term; technically, it is a letter from a money owner to his agent to pay a loan to someone in another town to secure safety from road danger.
Check the dictionaries al-Misbah and al-Qamus for definition.
2
Identify the default ruling
The text states 'وكره السفاتج' - suftaja is disliked (makruh). The reason is that it is a loan that brings benefit (safety) to the lender, which is prohibited as riba.
The hadith 'كل قرض جر منفعة فهو ربا' is the basis.
3
Note the condition for permissibility
Some Hanafi scholars say: if the benefit (safety) is not stipulated as a condition, then there is no harm (لا بأس به). This is the second opinion.
4
Understand the tarjih
Al-Bazzaziyyah in Kitab al-Sarf indicates preference for the second opinion (permissibility if not stipulated). This is the mufta bihi position.
The passage also discusses related rulings on gifts and repayment, but the focus is on suftaja.

Scope of Hawala: Debts Only vs Rights
Expansion on the condition that hawala is for debts only

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the condition that hawala is restricted to debts and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on its validity for rights such as spoils.

Core Idea
Hawala is only valid for debts, not for rights; the case of spoils under the imam is disputed.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq expands on a comment in Kanz al-Daqaiq that a restricted hawala (for a specific debt) should be valid. The commentator clarifies that unrestricted hawala is invalid because hawala is based on transfer of debt. A dispute arises regarding whether hawala can be used for rights (huquq), such as the right to spoils under the imam. The author of al-Bahr hesitates on this point.

Guided Path
1
Understand the base text
The base text (Kanz) says 'ينبغي أن تكون صحيحة' (it should be valid) referring to a restricted hawala (muqayyada).
Restricted means the hawala is for a specific known debt.
2
Note the commentary on unrestricted hawala
The commentator explains that unrestricted hawala is definitely invalid because Hanafi scholars explicitly state hawala is restricted to debts (اختصاصها بالديون).
Debts (duyun) are transferable; rights (huquq) are not.
3
Identify the dispute on rights
Al-Jawhara states hawala is not valid for rights. However, al-Bahr suggests it might be valid for the right of booty (ghanima) collected under the imam from one of the fighters.
The author of al-Bahr (Ibn Nujaym) expresses hesitation ('وعندي فيه تردد').
4
Derive the operative ruling
The majority view (al-Jawhara, al-Nahr) is that hawala is only for debts. The spoil case is an exception that requires further reflection, but it is not the relied-upon position.
The mufta bihi is that hawala for rights is invalid.

Hawala is exclusively for debts; rights are not assignable by hawala, except possibly spoils under the imam, which is disputed.

The Ruling on Al-Safatij (Loan for Security)
Explains the ruling on al-safatij and the principle of prohibited benefit in loans.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on al-safatij and explain the difference between stipulated and unstipulated benefit in loans.

Core Idea
A loan that secures the lender's money is makruh if the benefit is stipulated; otherwise it is permissible.

In this lesson, we examine the ruling on al-safatij, a loan arrangement used to protect money during travel. The core issue is the prohibition of a loan that draws benefit (قرض جر منفعة). We will explore the Hanafi position, the evidence, and the important distinction between stipulated and unstipulated benefits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is al-safatij? According to the text, it is a loan where the lender benefits from security during travel. The ruling in the matn is 'كره السفاتج' (he disliked al-safatij).
The term is derived from Persian, and defined in lexicons.
2
Locate the dalil
The prohibition is based on the hadith 'النهي عن قرض جر منفعة' (prohibition of a loan that draws benefit).
This hadith is zanni al-thubut, but its implication is clear.
3
Note the ikhtilaf
Some Hanafi scholars (from al-Bazzaziyya) say that if the benefit is not stipulated, it is permissible.
This is an intra-Hanafi difference based on the interpretation of the hadith.
4
Determine the mufta bihi
According to the tarjih in al-Bazzaziyya, the permissibility without stipulation is the preponderant view.
This view is preferred by later fatwa authorities.
5
Apply the principle to related cases
The text extends to gifts, repayment of better quality, etc., using the same principle: unstipulated benefits are permissible.
Examples include accepting a gift from a debtor or repaying with a higher quality item.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the ruling of al-safatij, which is a loan arrangement used to secure money during travel. The author first defines the term linguistically, quoting from authoritative lexicons. The core issue is that this loan benefits the lender by securing his money, which falls under the prohibition of a loan that draws a benefit (نهي عن قرض جر منفعة). The Hanafi ruling is that such a loan is makruh (disliked) if the benefit is stipulated. However, if the benefit is not stipulated and occurs incidentally, there is disagreement. The text then cites al-Bazzaziyya which gives preponderance to the permissibility without stipulation. The passage also extends this principle to gifts, repayment of better quality, and excess in weight or measure, where the general rule is that benefit to the lender is not allowed if stipulated or if it is excessive. The discussion highlights the Hanafi concern for avoiding riba-like benefits in loan transactions.

Key Terms
السفاتج (al-safatij): Plural of siftijah, a loan given to an agent to secure money against travel risks.
سفتجة (siftijah): The singular form, defined as a document for a loan that provides security.
قرض جر منفعة (qard jarra manfa'ah): A loan that brings benefit to the lender, which is prohibited.
