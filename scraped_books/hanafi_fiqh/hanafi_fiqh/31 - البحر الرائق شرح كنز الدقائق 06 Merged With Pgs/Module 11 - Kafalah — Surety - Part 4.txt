Purchase with Guarantee of Recourse: When to Sue the Guarantor
Ruling on buyer's recourse against guarantor in case of claim of ownership

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when a buyer with a guarantee of recourse can take the guarantor after a claim of ownership, and distinguish this scenario from a claim of freedom.

Core Idea
The buyer cannot sue the guarantor until he first obtains a judgment against the seller for the price.

In commercial transactions, a guarantee of recourse (kafalat al-dirk) is common to protect the buyer if a third party claims ownership. This lesson examines a key procedural rule: the buyer's right to recourse against the guarantor is conditional on first obtaining a judgment against the seller. Understanding this condition prevents premature lawsuits and clarifies the legal hierarchy of liability in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transaction and the guarantee
A buyer purchases a slave-girl (or any item) with a third party guaranteeing recourse (dirk) – i.e., if the item is claimed, the guarantor will compensate the buyer.
The guarantee is a form of kafalah, valid in the Hanafi school.
2
State the claim scenario
A third party successfully claims ownership of the slave-girl (istihqaq) through evidence or court judgment.
This is a claim of ownership (istihqaq naqil), not a claim of freedom.
3
Apply the Hanafi ruling
According to the apparent transmission (zahir al-riwayah), the buyer cannot take the guarantor until judgment is given in his favor for the price against the seller.
The claim alone does not rescind the sale; the buyer must first establish the seller's liability.
4
Distinguish from claim of freedom
If the slave-girl is judged to be free (istihqaq mubtil), the sale is nullified immediately due to lack of subject matter, and the buyer can directly sue both the seller and the guarantor.
The difference lies in whether the claim invalidates the sale itself.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi difference
Imam Abu Yusuf held that the buyer could take the guarantor first, without suing the seller. However, the relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is the apparent transmission: the seller must be sued first.
The text explicitly rejects Abu Yusuf's view, saying 'خلافاً لما عن أبي يوسف'.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the ruling: if someone buys a slave-girl with a guarantee of recourse (dirk) from a guarantor, and then a third party claims the slave-girl successfully, the buyer cannot take the guarantor until he obtains a judgment against the seller for the price. Mutual Guarantee: No Recourse Between Guarantors
Ruling statement on joint guarantee

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mutual guarantee (kafalat al-rajulayn) and explain that the paying guarantor has no right of recourse against the co-guarantor.

Core Idea
In mutual guarantee, the paying guarantor forfeits any claim for reimbursement from his partner.

This lesson addresses a specific case in the chapter of kafalah: two men each owe a debt and each guarantees the other. The Hanafi school rules that if one of them pays the entire debt, he cannot claim reimbursement from his partner. We will examine the textual basis for this ruling, noting that the source passage is incomplete and may contain further details on exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
Two men each owe a debt (e.g., jointly or separately) and each guarantees the other's obligation. This creates a mutual guarantee where each is both debtor and guarantor.
The debt is against both, and each is a kafil for the other.
2
State the Ruling
If one of them pays the debt, he cannot claim reimbursement from his partner. The text says: 'فما أداه أحدهما لم يرجع به على شريكه'.
Memorize the key phrase: 'lam yarji' bihi 'ala sharikihi'.
3
Understand the Logic
Since each is a guarantor, the payment is considered as fulfilling one's own obligation as a guarantor, not as a loan to the partner. Hence no right of recourse.
This differs from a case where one person pays on behalf of another without being a guarantor.

The passage ends with 'فإن زاد' (then if he increases...), indicating there are additional rules when the payment exceeds the debt or the share. These are not covered here.

Ruling on Mutual Guarantee Between Two Debtors
Explains the ruling for mutual guarantors who are also debtors

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for mutual guarantee between two debtors, explain the reasoning based on the dual role of principal and guarantor, and apply the rule to scenarios where debts differ in type or cause.

Core Idea
Each debtor is principal for half and guarantor for the other half; recovery only for the excess over half.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling in Hanafi fiqh regarding kafalah when two individuals each owe a debt and each guarantees the other's debt. The commentary from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains why the paying debtor can only recover from his partner if he pays more than half of the total amount. Understanding this rule requires appreciating the dual legal personality of each party as both principal debtor (asil) and guarantor (kafil).

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize that we have two individuals, each with a debt, and each guarantees the other's debt. Both are simultaneously principals (asil) for their own debt and guarantors (kafil) for the other's debt.
This is a mutual guarantee, not a one-sided guarantee.
2
State the ruling
If one of them pays, he cannot recover anything from the other unless he pays more than half of the combined debt. If he pays more than half, he can recover the excess.
Example: If each owes 100 and one pays 150, he recovers 50.
3
Understand the reasoning
Each debtor is considered a principal for half (his own debt) and a guarantor for the other half. Payment is first applied to his own half, so the first half paid covers his own obligation. Only the excess over half is paid as a guarantor, giving him a right of recourse.
The dual role prevents conflict: the debt as principal is stronger than the claim as guarantor.
4
Consider the role of circularity
If recovery were allowed for the half paid on behalf of the partner, the partner could then recover from the payer as his deputy, leading to an infinite loop. The ruling avoids this circularity (daur).
Circularity is a rational basis for the rule.
5
Apply the nuances
If the debts differ in description (e.g., one immediate, one deferred) or cause (e.g., loan vs. sale price), the paying debtor can specify which debt he is paying, and recovery follows accordingly. The ruling assumes equal debts otherwise.
Intention matters when the debts are of different types.

Ruling on Two Guarantors Who Also Guarantee Each Other
Explains a specific kafalah scenario with mutual guarantees.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two options for recourse when two guarantors mutually guarantee each other, and identify the two conditions that must be met for the ruling to apply.

Core Idea
When two guarantors also guarantee each other, the paying guarantor recovers half from his partner or the whole from the principal.

In this passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, the author examines a nuanced scenario in kafalah: two individuals guarantee a common debtor and also mutually guarantee each other's guarantee. The ruling establishes a symmetrical right of recourse. The author also expands the matn by clarifying two essential conditions that must be present for this ruling to apply.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the scenario
There are three parties: the principal debtor (makful 'anhu) and two guarantors (kafil). Each guarantor guarantees the principal's debt and also guarantees his fellow guarantor's liability.
This is a second scenario in the chapter; the first scenario had only mutual guarantees between two debtors, not guarantors.
2
State the two available options for the paying guarantor
If one guarantor (e.g., Zayd) pays the entire debt, he has two choices: (1) recover half from his co-guarantor (Amr), or (2) recover the full amount from the principal debtor (Bakr).
He cannot recover the full amount from the co-guarantor because liability is shared equally.
3
Explain the reasoning behind the half recovery from the co-guarantor
Because both are guarantors for the principal and also guarantee each other, the payment is considered to fall equally on them. There is no preference for one over the other, so recovery from the partner is limited to half.
This does not lead to circularity because the half recovery maintains equality; each guarantor ultimately bears half the burden.
4
Recognize the two conditions (restrictions) omitted by the matn
First condition: each guarantor must guarantee the entire debt of the principal successively; if each guarantees only half initially, the ruling reverts to a different scenario. Second condition: the mutual guarantee between the two must be for the entire debt; if only for half, again the earlier ruling applies.
These conditions ensure that the mutual liability is complete and symmetric.
5
Apply the ruling to determine when it applies
If both conditions are present, the paying guarantor has the two options. If either condition is missing, the scenario is treated as the first case where each guarantor is only liable for half and recourse is limited accordingly.
Understanding these conditions prevents misapplication of the ruling.

Commentary
This passage explains a second scenario in the chapter on kafalah. Here, two people (Zayd and Amr) act as guarantors for a third person (Bakr) who owes a debt. In addition, each of these two guarantors also guarantees the other (i.e., Zayd guarantees Amr's liability, and Amr guarantees Zayd's). The ruling is that if Zayd pays the entire debt to the creditor, he has two options: he can recover half of the amount from his co-guarantor Amr, or he can recover the entire amount from the principal Bakr. The reasoning is that because both are guarantors for the principal and also for each other, the payment is considered to be shared between them equally; there is no preference for one over the other in terms of guarantee liability. Recovering half from Amr does not lead to circularity because the half recovered by Zayd is balanced by the fact that Amr also has a claim against Zayd in a similar way, resulting in equality. After Zayd recovers from Amr, both then recover from the principal Bakr. Alternatively, Zayd can skip Amr and recover the whole from Bakr if the guarantee was by Bakr's command. The commentary notes that the matn omitted two important conditions: (1) The two guarantees must be successive and cover the entire debt; if each guarantees only half initially and then they guarantee each other, it reverts to the first ruling (each debtor guarantees the other) and recovery is only for excess over half. (2) The mutual guarantee between the two must be for the entire amount; if it is only for half, again the first ruling applies. This ensures that the mutual guarantee is complete and symmetric.

Kafala of Two Mutual Sureties – Apportionment and Recourse
Basic ruling on mutual suretyship of two persons

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of mutual suretyship to calculate the recourse amount when two sureties are each surety for the other.

Core Idea
In mutual suretyship, each surety is principal in half and surety in half, ensuring no circular recourse.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the core ruling when two sureties guarantee a debt and also guarantee each other. Understanding this apportionment is critical for correctly calculating how much a paying surety can recover from his co-surety and from the principal debtor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the suretyship structure
Note that two sureties (K1 and K2) each guarantee the entire debt of the principal (P), and each also guarantees his fellow surety. This creates a dual role: each is a principal debtor for half the debt and a surety for the other half.
The wording 'كل واحد منهما في النصف أصيل وفي النصف الآخر كفيل' establishes the half-and-half apportionment.
2
Analyze the payment allocation
When one surety pays any amount, the payment is treated as: up to half of the total debt comes from his own liability as principal (as a debtor), and any amount beyond half comes from his suretyship on behalf of his co-surety.
Payment is divided because neither role (principal vs surety) is intrinsically stronger; otherwise circularity (دور) would result.
3
Apply the recourse rules
The paying surety can recover the excess over half (i.e., the portion paid as surety) from his co-surety. He cannot recover the half he paid as principal from the co-surety, as that would be recovering from himself indirectly. Then both sureties can recover from the principal debtor.
The text says: 'رجع بنصفه زاد على النصف رجع بالزيادة' – he recovers half (of what he paid) up to half the debt? Actually careful: He recovers half of what he paid if he paid exactly the full debt? The text says he recovers half of what he paid? Wait: 'رجع بنصفه' means he recovers half of what he paid? The translation: 'he recovers half of it' – ambiguous. But according to the reasoning: each is principal in half, so if he pays the whole debt, half is on his own account, half on suretyship. So he can recover the half that was paid as surety from his co-surety. So he recovers half the total debt from co-surety.
4
Understand the prevention of circularity
If payment were not apportioned and the paying surety could recover the entire amount from the co-surety, then the co-surety could in turn recover the same amount from the paying surety because the paying surety is also a surety for the co-surety. This circularity (دور) is why the ruling apportions payment.
The text explicitly mentions: 'لأن أداء نائبه كأدائه فيؤدي إلى الدور' – the payment of his deputy is like his own, leading to circularity.

Since mufawada includes mutual agency and suretyship, each partner is a surety for the other even after separation, so the creditor may claim the entire debt from whichever partner he wishes. This does not apply to an ‘inan (limited) partnership, where there is no such suretyship. The passage also notes that if there are three debtors and they become sureties successively, each is liable for the full debt. The authorities cited are al-Sarakhsi, al-Marghinani, and al-Tamartashi.

Key Terms
المفاوضان (al-mufawidan): Two partners in a mufawada (unlimited partnership) where each is agent and surety for the other.
شريك العنان (sharik al-‘inan): A partner in an ‘inan (limited) partnership, where each contributes capital and shares profit, but there is no mutual suretyship.

Mutual Suretyship in Joint Kitaba
Ruling on mutual suretyship in joint kitaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on mutual suretyship in a joint kitaba contract and apply the rule of recovery when one slave pays.

Core Idea
In joint kitaba with mutual suretyship, each slave pays as principal and surety; recovery is half.

When a master contracts with two slaves under a single kitaba agreement and they mutually guarantee each other, the law treats each slave as both a principal debtor for his own share and a surety for his companion. This lesson explains the valid structure by istihsan and outlines the recourse rights after payment or partial emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contract type
Recognize that this is a joint kitaba (كتابة واحدة) with explicit mutual suretyship (كفل كل عن صاحبه). This is permissible by istihsan.
Without suretyship, each slave is only liable for his own share.
2
Determine the legal nature of each slave
Each slave is treated as an asil (principal) regarding the obligation of the entire sum on him, and a kafil (surety) regarding the obligation on his companion. Their freedom is suspended on full payment.
This dual role is the core of the ruling.
3
Apply the rule of recourse
If one slave pays the entire kitaba amount, he recovers half from his co-slave because they are equal. Full recourse would violate equality.
The payment is considered as paying both shares.
4
Handle partial emancipation by the master
If the master frees one slave, he can claim the unfreed slave's share from either the freed slave (as surety) or the unfreed one (as principal). Recovery differs based on whom he collects from.
The freed slave can recover from the unfreed if paid, but not vice versa.
5
Understand the rationale (ta'lil)
The kitaba amount is in exchange for the slaves' persons. The suretyship is a stratagem to facilitate payment. When emancipation occurs, the need for suretyship diminishes, but the liability adjusts accordingly.
Istihsan permits this arrangement despite potential qiyas restrictions.

Guarantee for a Slave's Non-Collectible Debt
Ruling on guarantee for a slave's non-collectible debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a guarantee for a slave's debt that is not collectible from the slave is immediately binding on the guarantor, and distinguish it from deferred debts.

Core Idea
A guarantee for a slave's unenforceable debt is immediately binding on the guarantor because the debt is valid in principle.

Slaves often incur debts that cannot be collected from them directly due to their master's ownership and their lack of assets. This lesson explains that if someone guarantees such a debt, the guarantee is immediate (حال) on the guarantor, even though the slave cannot be compelled to pay. The rationale is that the debt's cause exists, but enforcement is blocked by the slave's insolvency; the guarantor is not insolvent, so he must pay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the debt type
The debt is one that cannot be collected from the slave in the present, either because the master denies liability, the slave is interdicted, or the debt arises from actions without the master's permission.
Examples include: slave confesses to destroying property (master denies), loan to slave without permission, sale to interdicted slave, etc.
2
Understand the slave's liability
The slave is liable in principle (the debt exists), but he cannot be forced to pay because his assets belong to the master and the master has not consented to attachment.
The slave is effectively insolvent (معسر) due to the master's ownership.
3
Apply the guarantee ruling
If a person guarantees such a debt without specifying deferment, the guarantee is immediately due (حال) on the guarantor. The guarantor's solvency means he must pay immediately.
This is analogous to guaranteeing for an absent or insolvent debtor.
4
Distinguish from deferred debts
If the underlying debt is deferred (مؤجل) because of a deferment period, the guarantee is also deferred accordingly.
The ruling only applies when the debt itself is immediate but uncollectible from the slave.
5
Apply the consequence
The guarantor, upon paying, may have recourse against the slave if the slave later acquires assets (e.g., after freedom), but that is a separate matter.
The immediate nature of the guarantee ensures the creditor is not left unpaid.

Joint Kitabah with Mutual Kafalah: Ruling and Istihsan Reasoning
Ruling on joint kitabah with mutual kafalah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on joint kitabah with mutual kafalah and explain the istihsan reasoning that overrides qiyas.

Core Idea
Joint kitabah with mutual guarantee is valid by istihsan: each slave is principal for half and guarantor for half.

This passage from Bahr al-Ra'iq explains a distinctive Hanafi ruling: when two slaves are contracted together for manumission by payment (kitabah) and each guarantees the other, if one pays the full amount, he can recover half from his partner. The ruling is based on istihsan (juristic preference), which sets aside strict qiyas that would make each solely liable for his own portion. We will unpack the reasoning and the conditions required for this ruling to apply.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a joint kitabah of two slaves with explicit mutual kafalah. The ruling is that if one pays, he recovers half from the other. The contract is valid (sahih) by istihsan.
The key condition is the explicit guarantee clause.
2
Understand the default qiyas
By strict analogy (qiyas), each slave should be solely liable for his own portion. The kitabah payment is a contract, and each person is only responsible for his own debt. Without the guarantee, each pays his share and becomes free.
3
Apply the istihsan reasoning
Istihsan here recharacterizes each slave as principal for the full amount to the master, but a guarantor for the other. This allows mutual recourse. The ruling achieves equality between the slaves.
The master's condition of mutual payment is honoured.
4
Distinguish the scenario without explicit kafalah
If the master contracts them jointly but without mutual guarantee, each owes only his own half and becomes free upon paying it. If the master stipulates that both must pay the full amount, neither is free until the total is paid.
The master's explicit stipulation must be observed.
5
Examine the case of manumission before payment
If the master frees one slave before payment, he may collect the unfreed slave's share from either the freed slave (as guarantor) or the unfreed slave (as principal). The freed slave can recover from the unfreed, but not vice versa.
The freed slave paid on the other's behalf by the master's order.

Guarantee for a Slave's Deferred Debt is Current
Ruling that guarantee for a slave's deferred debt is current for the guarantor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a guarantee for a slave's deferred debt and explain why the guarantee is considered a current (hāl) obligation for the guarantor.

Core Idea
A guarantee for a slave's post-emancipation debt is immediately due from the guarantor, even though the slave cannot be sued until freed.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on a specific scenario in kafalah: when a person guarantees a debt owed by a slave that will only become collectible after the slave's emancipation. The classical Hanafi texts, including Al-Bahr al-Raiq, rule that such a guarantee is a current (hāl) debt upon the guarantor. This creates a distinct legal consequence: the guarantor must pay immediately, and then he has recourse against the slave after emancipation. We will explore the reasoning behind this ruling and a key intra-Hanafi dispute about the basis for the guarantor's recourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is guaranteeing (kafalah) a debt that is deferred until the slave's emancipation. The ruling: it is a current (hāl) debt on the guarantor.
This is established in Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Understand the basis for the ruling
The debt is current because the cause (sabab) exists – the slave incurred the debt – and the guarantor accepts liability. The slave's insolvency (because everything he owns belongs to his master) does not affect the guarantor.
Analogous to a guarantee for an absent or bankrupt person.
3
Distinguish from a deferred debt
Adeferred debt (mu'ajjal) is delayed by a contractual term; here the delay is due to the slave's inability to pay, not a term. Hence the guarantee is not deferred.
If the debt were genuinely deferred by agreement, the guarantee would also be deferred.
4
Examine the dispute on recourse
There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: whose order (the slave's or the master's) is considered for the guarantor's recourse? The relied-upon position is that the master's order is the basis because recourse is ultimately against the master.
This is discussed in Fath al-Qadir and Al-Bada'i'.
5
Apply the ruling to a concrete case
If someone guarantees a slave's debt for consuming property without permission, the guarantor must pay now, then can have recourse against the slave after emancipation.
The master's order is required for the guarantor's recourse.

Core Idea
A master and his slave cannot owe each other a debt, so kafalah between them yields no right of recourse.

Islamic commercial law includes the contract of kafalah (suretyship), where one person guarantees another's obligation. A special case arises when the guarantor is a slave and the principal is his master, or vice versa. The Effect of Deferment and Death in Hawalah vs. Kafalah
Comparative analysis of hawalah and kafalah regarding deferment and death

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the effect of the transferee's death on the deferment in hawalah and distinguish it from the case of kafalah.

Core Idea
In hawalah, the death of the transferee makes the debt due on the transferor; in kafalah, the surety's death does not accelerate the principal's debt.

This lesson examines a critical distinction in Hanafi muʿāmalāt between two similar contracts: ḥawālah (transfer of debt) and kafālah (suretyship). While both involve a third party assuming responsibility for a debt, they differ fundamentally in how deferment (ajal) and death affect the obligations. We will analyze the key ruling from al-Baḥr al-Rāʾiq and understand the reasoning behind this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contracts and their core features
Begin by recalling the definitions of ḥawālah (transfer of debt) and kafālah (suretyship). In ḥawālah, the original debtor (asl) is fully discharged; in kafālah, the surety is an auxiliary. This difference is crucial.
The passage contrasts them specifically regarding deferment and death.
2
Understand the role of deferment (ajal)
When a debt is transferred in ḥawālah, any deferment (ajal) attached to the original debt transfers to the transferee. The deferment is for the benefit of the transferee, not the transferor. In kafālah, the deferment remains a condition for the principal unless explicitly attributed to the surety.
The text states: 'In kafālah, if the surety guarantees a debt and the creditor defers the debt without attributing the deferment to the surety, the deferment becomes a condition for the principal.'
3
Analyze the effect of death: ḥawālah
If the transferee (muḥtal ʿalayh) dies before the term, the debt returns to the transferor (muḥīl) as immediately due. The reasoning is that the deferment was for the benefit of the transferee, and with his death, the need for deferment ceases.
The passage explicitly states this ruling and contrasts it with kafālah.
4
Analyze the effect of death: kafālah
If the surety (kafīl) dies, the debt on the principal remains deferred (if it was deferred). The surety's death does not affect the principal's deferment because the surety is not the primary debtor; the principal remains liable with the original term.
This is the opposite of ḥawālah, where the transferor becomes liable immediately.
5
Apply the distinction to a concrete scenario
Consider a case where A owes B a due debt, and A transfers it to C for one year. C dies before the year ends. Under ḥawālah, B can demand payment from A immediately. Under kafālah, if C were a surety, B would have to wait until the term ends to claim from A.
This practical difference underscores the distinct legal natures of the two contracts.

If the transferee (muhal 'alayh) dies before the expiration of the term, the debt returns to the transferor (muhil) as due. There is a difference between hawalah and kafalah: In kafalah, if the surety guarantees a debt and the creditor defers the debt without attributing the deferment to the surety, the deferment becomes a condition for the principal, so even if the surety dies, the debt on the principal remains deferred. In hawalah, whenever the deferment is attached to the debt and not attributed to the transferee, it does not become a condition for the principal, so even if the transferee dies insolvent, the debt does not revert to the principal as due. Commentary
This lengthy passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq provides a detailed comparison between hawalah and kafalah, particularly regarding the effect of deferment (ajal) and the death of the parties. It begins by stating a ruling: if the debt is due (hal) from the original debtor, and he transfers it to someone for a one-year term, the hawalah is valid. However, if the transferee (muhal 'alayh) dies before the term expires, the debt returns to the transferor (muhil) as immediately due. This contrasts with kafalah, where if the surety dies, the debt on the principal remains deferred. The reason is that in hawalah, the transferor is released from the debt entirely (the debt is 'turned over'), while in kafalah, the surety is only an auxiliary. The passage then cites a ruling from al-Mujtaba which states that hawalah without the consent of the transferee is invalid according to one view, but valid like agency according to another. It also cites al-Zahiriyyah that the consent of the transferee is not a condition by consensus (when the debt is similar). The remainder of the passage, from al-Dhakhirah, elaborates on the distinctions: if the debt is due, it remains due; if deferred, it remains deferred. The passage discusses scenarios such as the death of the original debtor (the debt does not become due on the transferee) versus the death of the transferee (the debt becomes due on the original debtor). The key principle is that the hawalah transfers the debt with its attributes; the original debtor is completely discharged. Therefore, the deferment is for the benefit of the transferee, and if he dies, the need for deferment ceases, so the debt becomes due on the original debtor who is responsible if the transferee's estate is insufficient.

Key Terms
أجل (ajal): The term or deferment of a debt; in hawalah, it is transferred from the original debtor to the transferee.
المحتال عليه (al-muhtal 'alayh): The person upon whom the debt is transferred (the new debtor).
الكفالة (kafalah): Suretyship; a contract where one person guarantees the debt of another.

In ḥawālah, the death of the transferee makes the debt due on the transferor; in kafālah, the surety's death does not accelerate the principal's debt.
