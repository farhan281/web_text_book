Excusing Contradiction in Claims of Freedom, Lineage, and Divorce
Explaining that contradiction in legal claims is excused in three matters due to their obscurity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that contradiction is excused in claims of freedom, lineage, and divorce, and explain the rationale of concealment (al-khafa').

Core Idea
Three hidden matters: freedom, lineage, and divorce — contradiction in claims is excused because only Allah and the individuals know the truth.

In legal claims, a person's contradictory statements typically undermine their case. However, the Hanafi school recognizes an exception for three matters that are inherently hidden from public knowledge: a person's freedom, their lineage, and divorce. This lesson unpacks the principle, its rationale, and its application through detailed examples from classical Hanafi texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule about contradiction
First, understand that in most legal claims, contradiction (al-tanaqud) invalidates a claim because it undermines credibility. The passage notes that the apparent (al-zahir) suffices for defense but not for entitlement.
The linguistic and logical definitions of contradiction are provided for context.
2
Recognize the three exceptions
The passage explicitly states that contradiction is excused in matters of freedom, lineage, and divorce (الحرية والنسب والطلاق). These are the core exceptions.
Freedom includes both original freedom and emancipation.
3
Understand the rationale: concealment (al-khafa')
These three matters are based on concealment: lineage depends on conception (private), divorce is known only to the husband, and freedom is known only to the master. Because the truth is hidden, contradictory claims are tolerated.
The passage says: 'لأن مبناها على الخفاء' (their basis is on concealment).
4
Apply the rule to the examples
Work through the examples: a female slave confesses to slavery then later claims freedom with evidence (accepted); a seller of a slave later claims the slave is his son (voids sale); a wife obtains khul' then proves prior triple divorce (reclaims compensation).
The principle extends beyond these three to any matter based on concealment.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq comments on a line from Kanz al-Daqa'iq that states contradiction is excused in three matters: freedom, lineage, and divorce. The passage begins with a general discussion of the principle of 'al-zahir' (the apparent) in legal claims: the apparent evidence suffices for defense but not for establishing a right. The commentary then defines 'contradiction' (al-tanaqud) linguistically and logically, noting that the jurists use the linguistic meaning. The key point is that in claims of freedom, lineage, and divorce, the law tolerates contradictory statements because these matters are inherently hidden. For freedom: the state of a slave is known only to the master; for lineage: conception and birth are private; for divorce: the husband alone knows his intention. The passage provides detailed examples: a female slave who confesses to slavery then claims freedom after sale, her evidence is accepted by istihsan. A slave who remains silent during sale is deemed to have confessed to slavery, but if he later proves freedom, contradiction does not bar his claim. For lineage: if a seller of a slave later claims the slave is his son, the claim is heard and the sale voided, because lineage is based on concealment. However, claiming brotherhood for inheritance is only accepted if a property right is involved, not for mere lineage, since it implicates the father. For divorce: a woman who obtained khul' can later prove a prior triple divorce and reclaim the compensation. The passage concludes that the principle is broader: any matter based on concealment excuses contradiction, as illustrated by a guarantor's claim.

Key Terms
التناقض (al-tanaqud): Contradiction: two statements that negate each other. In fiqh, it refers to a claimant making two inconsistent claims about the same matter.
الحرية (al-hurriyya): Freedom: the state of being free as opposed to a slave. The passage includes both original freedom (from birth) and acquired freedom (by emancipation).
النسب (al-nasab): Lineage: the relationship of paternity or kinship. In this context, it refers to claims about being a child or parent.
الطلاق (al-talaq): Divorce: the dissolution of marriage by the husband's pronouncement. The passage focuses on the wife's claim of divorce.
الخفاء (al-khafa'): Concealment/obscurity: the state of being hidden from public knowledge, which justifies excusing contradiction.
