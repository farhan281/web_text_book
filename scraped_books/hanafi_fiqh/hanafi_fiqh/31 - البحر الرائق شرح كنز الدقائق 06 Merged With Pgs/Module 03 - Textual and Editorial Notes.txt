Correcting the Number of Exceptions in Khiyar al-Ayb
Textual correction of a numbering error

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct number of exceptions to the general rule of khiyar al-ayb and explain the copyist error found in some manuscripts.

Core Idea
Ten exceptions only — three important notes follow, not exceptions.

In the Hanafi chapter on khiyar al-ayb, the author discusses cases where the buyer's right to choose is limited. Some manuscripts erroneously listed fifteen exceptions due to a numbering mistake. This lesson clarifies the correct structure by distinguishing between the ten actual exceptions and the three important notes that follow them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule of khiyar al-ayb
Recall that in khiyar al-ayb, the buyer generally has the option to keep the defective item for the full price or return it. The exceptions are cases where this choice is restricted.
This rule is the baseline against which exceptions are counted.
2
Locate the copyist error
Examine the text: some manuscript copies mistakenly labeled the first note after the tenth exception as 'eleventh', continuing to 'fifteenth'. This created the impression of fifteen exceptions.
The error arises from misclassifying the three important notes as exceptions.
3
Apply the correction
Understand that the passages following the tenth exception are not exceptions but separate important notes. Therefore, the correct count of exceptions remains ten.
This correction is supported by the majority of manuscripts and by al-Ramli's commentary.

His statement: (Eleventh) He said in al-Muhit: In some copies, the expression about it is with 'tenth', so he mentioned 'tenth' twice. And after this tenth, he mentioned eleventh, twelfth up to fifteenth coming in the notes. And apparent from al-Ramli's statement that his copy was like that, and it is a mistake of the scribe because the discussion is about the exceptional cases that deviate from their general application of giving the choice between keeping the defective item for the full price or returning it, and the five coming cases are not that. That is what you know about mentioning 'tenth' twice. So the correct thing is to mention them after the tenth of the important notes, as in this copy, which conforms to most copies.

Commentary
This passage is a textual note (critical_refinement) in which the commentator corrects a copyist error found in some manuscripts. It addresses a common mistake where scribes labeled the first exception after the tenth as 'eleventh', and then continued numbering up to fifteenth, thinking there were fifteen exceptions. In reality, after the tenth exception listed in the main text, the remaining points are three 'important notes' (tanbihat muhimma), not further exceptions. The correct numbering is: ten exceptions followed by the three important notes. This correction ensures clarity in the structure of the chapter on khiyar al-ayb.

Definitions of Sijil and Hujja
Defines two types of judicial documents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-sijil and al-hujja and explain their respective roles in Hanafi judicial practice.

Core Idea
The sijil is the court's internal record; the hujja is the certified copy given to the litigant.

In Hanafi courts, documentation is crucial for preserving rights. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies two key terms: al-sijil (the original witness record) and al-hujja (the certified copy). Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping how Hanafi fiqh structures judicial proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two documents
The passage defines two types of judicial documents: al-sijil and al-hujja. Note their names and that they are distinct.
2
Analyze the characteristics of al-sijil
Al-sijil is what the two witnesses write about the incident. It remains with the judge and does not bear the judge's signature.
It is the internal record.
3
Analyze the characteristics of al-hujja
Al-hujja is copied from the sijil. It has the judge's mark at the top and the witnesses' signatures at the bottom, and is given to the litigant.
It is the external proof.
4
Recognize the role of 'urf
The passage states that the custom ('urf) has standardized these definitions. This shows how Hanafi practice relies on customary usage.

Source Text

أو وفي ما كتب فيه البيع والرهن والإقرار وغيرها والحجة والوثيقة متناولان الثلاثة ا هـ . العرف الآن السجل ما كتبه الشاهدان في الواقعة وبقي عند القاضي وليس عليه خط القاضي، والحجة ما نقل من السجل من الواقعة وعليه علامة القاضي أعلاه وخط الشاهدين أسفله وأعطي للخصم.
Or regarding what is written about sale, pledge, acknowledgment, etc., and 'al-hujja' and 'al-wathīqa' encompass the three (types). The custom now: 'al-sijil' is what the two witnesses write about the incident and remains with the judge, without the judge's signature; 'al-hujja' is what is copied from the sijil about the incident, with the judge's mark at the top and the witnesses' signatures at the bottom, and is given to the litigant.
Source pages p. 463

Commentary
This passage clarifies the distinction between two types of judicial documents used in the Hanafi court: al-sijil and al-hujja. The custom (urf) has standardized their use. The sijil is the original record written by the witnesses, kept by the judge, and it does not bear the judge's signature. The hujja is a copy made from the sijil, certified with the judge's mark at the top and the witnesses' signatures at the bottom, and it is handed over to the litigant as proof. This distinction ensures proper record-keeping and authentication.

Key Terms
السجل (al-sijil): The original document written by witnesses recording an incident, kept by the judge without the judge's signature.
الحجة (al-hujja): An official copy of the sijil, bearing the judge's mark and witnesses' signatures, given to the litigant as proof.
