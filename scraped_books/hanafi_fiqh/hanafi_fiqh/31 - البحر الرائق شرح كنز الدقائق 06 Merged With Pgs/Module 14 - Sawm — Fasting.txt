Conditional I'tikaf is Invalid
Ruling that conditional i'tikaf is invalid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on conditional i'tikaf and explain why it is invalid.

Core Idea
A conditional i'tikaf is invalid because worship requires an unconditional intention.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies that attaching a condition to i'tikaf, whether by a specific condition or by the phrase 'if Allah wills', renders the i'tikaf invalid. Understanding this principle is crucial for correct intention in acts of worship. The text also addresses a potential confusion about vows and distinguishes between conditioning the obligation and conditioning the commencement of the act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the core ruling: conditional i'tikaf is invalid (bāṭil) and not binding on the person.
2
Understand 'Condition' in Context
Recognize that any condition attached to the intention—whether explicit ('on condition that I do not fast') or implicit ('if Allah wills')—invalidates the i'tikaf.
3
Review the Examples
Read the examples from al-Hawāshī al-‘Azmiyyah: conditions like not fasting, touching one's wife, or leaving the mosque whenever one wishes all make i'tikaf invalid.
4
Distinguish Conditional Vow from Conditional Act
Understand that the objection about vows is based on a misunderstanding: the condition here is on the commencement (shurū‘) of i'tikaf, not on its obligation. A conditional vow is also invalid because it introduces uncertainty.
5
Apply to New Scenarios
Use the principle: any statement that makes the intention of i'tikaf dependent on a future event or condition nullifies the act.

And among what indicates the establishment of the issue of i'tikaf is what is in al-Fusul al-Imadiyyah where he said: Conditioning i'tikaf upon a condition is not valid and does not bind him; thus mentioned in Sawm al-Asl. Al-Asl is from the works of Imam Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on him. And in al-Hawashi al-Azimiyyah: The invalidity of i'tikaf by condition, such that he says 'I intend to observe i'tikaf for ten days for His sake on condition that I do not fast or that I touch my wife during i'tikaf or that I leave it whenever I wish, for a need or without need' then the i'tikaf is invalid. And conditioning it by condition, such that he says 'I intend to observe i'tikaf for ten days if Allah wills.' This is what the author of al-Nahr mentioned first from some contemporaries, and it is objected to by some of them regarding making i'tikaf obligatory. And it may be answered by saying: If he vows to observe i'tikaf for a month, then enters the mosque and says 'I intend the vowed i'tikaf if Allah wills,' then he has obligated i'tikaf conditionally, so it is not valid. So the intended meaning is not to condition its obligation by the vow, but rather to condition the commencement (shuru') in it. So there is no error in their speech at all; rather the error is in understanding their intent. And since the invalidity of conditioning it by condition is established, its mention in this context is correct.

Commentary
This passage discusses the invalidity of conditional i'tikaf (seclusion in the mosque). It quotes al-Fusul al-Imadiyyah stating that conditioning i'tikaf is invalid and not binding. Further, from al-Hawashi al-Azimiyyah, examples are given: if one intends i'tikaf on condition of not fasting or touching one's wife, it is invalid. Even saying 'I intend i'tikaf for ten days if Allah wills' is invalid. The author then addresses a potential objection: some contemporaries said that making i'tikaf obligatory by vow is valid, but the response clarifies that the vow itself is conditional and thus invalid. The error lies in misunderstanding: the conditioning is on the commencement of the i'tikaf, not on the obligation itself. Since the principle is established, it is correct to mention i'tikaf in this context of conditional invalidity.

Key Terms
الاعتكاف (al-i'tikaf): Seclusion in the mosque for worship, often with fasting.
التعليق بالشرط (al-ta'liq bi al-shart): Attaching a condition to the validity of an act, which in worship renders it invalid if the act requires an absolute intention.
