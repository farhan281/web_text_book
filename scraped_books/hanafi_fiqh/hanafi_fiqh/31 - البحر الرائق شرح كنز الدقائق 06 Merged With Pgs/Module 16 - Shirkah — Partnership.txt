Conditions in Partnership and Mudarabah
Explaining rulings on conditions invalidating partnership and mudarabah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a condition that voids a contract and a condition that is merely void, applying the Hanafi principle to partnership and mudarabah contracts.

Core Idea
A void condition does not automatically void the contract; only conditions that sever the essence of the contract make it fasid.

In Hanafi law, contracts are generally upheld even if they contain invalid conditions, provided the condition does not strike at the contract's core. This lesson examines the specific rulings for partnership (shirkah) and mudarabah, where conditions about profit, land, or expenses are evaluated. Understanding this distinction is crucial for drafting valid contracts and recognizing which stipulations are enforceable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contracts and the condition
Note the contracts listed (shirkah, mudarabah, etc.) and the specific condition being evaluated. For example, a condition that guarantees a fixed profit to one partner.
The condition is about the distribution of profit or additional obligations.
2
Determine if the condition severs the essence
Assess whether the condition alters the fundamental nature of the contract. For shirkah, profit/loss sharing is essential; for mudarabah, profit sharing based on work is essential.
A condition that guarantees a fixed profit severs the partnership's essence.
3
Apply the ruling: void condition vs. void contract
If the condition severs the essence, the contract becomes fasid (void). If the condition is merely invalid but does not affect the core, the condition is void but the contract remains sahih (valid).
The Hanafi position distinguishes between the two outcomes.
4
Consider the evidence and cross-madhhab view
The evidence from al-Dhakhirah (qiyas) shows that a void condition does not attach to the contract, leaving it as a mere promise. Other schools may consider any invalid condition to void the contract, but Hanafis differentiate.
Understand the basis for the Hanafi ruling and its practical implications.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq comments on the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, addressing the effect of conditions in various contracts. The author begins by listing several contracts (partnership, mudarabah, etc.) and then gives specific examples for each. For partnership: if a condition is stipulated that severs the partnership (e.g., guaranteeing a fixed profit to one partner), the partnership is void, not because the condition is invalid per se, but because it destroys the essence of partnership (which requires shared profit and loss). For mudarabah: the author gives three scenarios. First, if the capital owner stipulates that the mudarib must provide land or a house, the condition is void but the mudarabah remains valid. Second, if the mudarib stipulates that the capital owner give him land or a house, the entire contract becomes void because the mudarib is making his work and the house rent part of the profit sharing, which is not allowed. Third, if the mudarib stipulates that expenses during travel are on him, the condition is void and the mudarabah is valid (expenses default to the capital owner). The author then mentions an evidence from al-Dhakhirah, with a comment from al-Nahr clarifying that a void condition does not attach to the contract, leaving it as a mere unenforceable promise.

Key Terms
شركة (shirkah): A partnership contract where two or more contribute capital and share profit/loss.
مضاربة (mudarabah): A contract where one party provides capital and the other provides labor, sharing profit.
