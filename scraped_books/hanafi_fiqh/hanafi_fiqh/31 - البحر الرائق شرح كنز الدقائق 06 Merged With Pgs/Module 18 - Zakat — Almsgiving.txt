The Majority Principle for Mixed Coins (Maghshush)
Explains the rule of majority (ghalib) in determining whether a coin is treated as pure silver/gold or as alloy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle 'al-hukm li al-ghalib' to determine the legal status of mixed coins in exchange (sarf) and state the conditions for validity.

Core Idea
The ruling follows the majority: predominant pure metal = pure coin; predominant alloy = base coin.

This lesson examines a practical case from classical Hanafi fiqh: how to treat coins that contain a mixture of precious metal and base alloy. The determining factor is the predominant component. We will see how the principle 'al-hukm li al-ghalib' applies, the reasoning behind it, and the conditions for valid exchange.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Distinguish between coins where the majority is silver/gold (ghalib al-fidda/ghalib al-dhahab) and coins where the majority is alloy (ghalib al-ghish). Note the different rulings for each in exchange.
The text explicitly contrasts 'ghalib al-fidda wa al-dhahab' with 'ghalib al-ghish'.
2
Examine the legal reasoning (ta'lil)
Understand why the subordinate component is ignored in one case and considered in the other: pure metal can be recovered by melting, so it remains legally significant; alloy burns away, so it is disregarded.
The key distinction: existence 'hissan wa hukman' (in reality and legally) vs. mere temporary presence.
3
Analyze the qiyas (analogy) with olives and oil
The author compares the situation to olives: when pressed, oil is the valuable part and the solid residue is waste. Similarly, in a coin the pure metal is the valuable part and the alloy is waste if subordinate.
The illa (effective cause) is the possibility of extraction and the enduring value of the pure substance.
4
Work through a hypothetical scenario
Suppose you have a coin with 40% silver and 60% copper. Apply the rule: since alloy is predominant, it is not treated as a silver coin but as a commodity. However, due to the presence of silver, immediate exchange is still required. If exchanging for pure silver, match the pure silver weight.
Use the olive-oil example to see how the pure metal is the focus.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the ruling on coins that contain an alloy (ghish) of base metal mixed with silver or gold. The key principle is that the legal status of a coin is determined by its predominant component (ghalib). If the majority is silver or gold, the coin is treated as pure silver or gold for the purposes of sarf (exchange) and other rules: it must be sold for an equal weight if exchanged for the same type, and hand-to-hand delivery is required. If the majority is alloy, the coin is treated as a commodity like copper, and it can be sold with disparity in weight and count, but still requires immediate exchange because it contains some silver/gold (making it a partial sarf). The author distinguishes between the two cases by noting that the subordinate silver/gold in a coin can be recovered by melting, so it remains legally significant (e.g., for zakat nisab), whereas the subordinate alloy burns and perishes, so it is ignored. The passage also addresses a potential objection about why the alloy itself requires hand-to-hand delivery: it is not because the alloy is valuable, but because it cannot be separated from the silver/gold that must be exchanged immediately. The example of olives and oil is used as an analogy: just as when olives are pressed, the oil is considered the valuable part, similarly, the pure metal is the valuable part in a coin.

Key Terms
الغالب (al-ghalib): The predominant component in a mixture; the majority by weight or value that determines the legal ruling.
الغش (al-ghish): Alloy or base metal (like copper) mixed into silver or gold coins to make them harder; it is considered worthless in legal terms when subordinate.
المغشوش (al-maghshush): Coins that have been mixed with alloy; impure coins.
