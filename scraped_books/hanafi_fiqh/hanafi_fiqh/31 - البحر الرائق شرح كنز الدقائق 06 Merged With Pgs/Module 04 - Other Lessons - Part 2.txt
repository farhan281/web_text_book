Invalidity of Kafalah by a Proxy or Mudarib for the Principal
Ruling on invalidity of kafalah by proxy/mudarib for principal concerning sale price

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that kafalah by a proxy (wakil) or mudarib for the principal concerning the sale price is invalid in the Hanafi school, and explain the underlying reasoning regarding the capacity of receipt.

Core Idea
A proxy or mudarib cannot guarantee the sale price for the principal because they themselves are the original recipients of the price.

In Islamic law, suretyship (kafalah) is a contract where one party guarantees the debt of another. However, when a proxy (wakil) or mudarib guarantees the sale price for the principal in a sale, the Hanafi school deems it invalid. This lesson unpacks the reasoning: the proxy and mudarib are considered the original parties in receiving the price, so they would be guaranteeing their own right. We will also explore exceptions and the cross-madhhab difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is kafalah (guarantee) by a proxy (wakil) or mudarib for the principal (muwakkil or rabb al-mal) concerning the sale price. The ruling is batil (invalid).
2
Understand the core reasoning
The reason is that the proxy and mudarib have the right to receive the price in their own capacity (jthat al-asa) in the sale, not as mere agents. Thus, they would be guaranteeing their own right, which is invalid.
This is why the right to receive is not extinguished by the death or dismissal of the principal.
3
Explore the qualifications
The ruling is restricted to a wakil (proxy) as opposed to a rasul (messenger). A messenger's guarantee is valid. Also, it is restricted to the price, not the mahr in marriage agency where the proxy is a mediator.
The text also mentions that if the seller appoints a separate person to receive the price, that person's guarantee is valid.
4
Compare analogous cases
The executor (wasi) and administrator of a waqf (mutawalli) are treated like the proxy and mudarib, so their kafalah for the buyer is also invalid.
This extends the ruling to similar fiduciary positions.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
The Shafi'i school may allow the kafalah if the proxy is a pure agent. The Hanafi school distinguishes based on capacity of receipt, leading to a different ruling.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that a Hanafi judge would not enforce the guarantee, while a Shafi'i judge would.

Commentary
This passage explains the ruling that the kafalah (suretyship) of a proxy (wakil) for his principal (muwakkil) concerning the price in a sale is invalid. Similarly, the kafalah of a mudarib for the owner of the capital is invalid. A branch (far') is mentioned by the commentator, which will be discussed separately.

مضارب (mudarib): The managing partner in a mudaraba contract, who uses the capital of the owner (rabb al-mal) to trade, and in this context, sells goods.

Invalidity of Kafalah in a Single Transaction Sale
Ruling on kafalah invalidly in joint single sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on a partner's kafalah for his partner in a single joint sale and identify the two reasons for its invalidity.

Core Idea
A partner cannot guarantee his partner's share of the price in a single transaction because it guarantees his own share and divides a debt before receipt.

This passage addresses a specific scenario: two partners sell a jointly owned item in one contract. Can one partner act as surety (kafil) for the other's share of the price? The Hanafi ruling is clear: it is invalid. The reasoning touches on two core principles: one cannot be a guarantor for oneself, and one cannot divide a debt before it is collected. This lesson unpacks these reasons and the crucial condition that invalidity only applies when the sale is one transaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a partner becoming a surety (kafalah) for his partner's share of the price when they sell a jointly owned item. The ruling is that this kafalah is invalid (batil).
The text says 'وبطل كفالة الشريك لشريكه' – the kafalah becomes void.
2
Understand the first reason: self-guarantee
Every part of the price paid by the buyer or the surety is joint between them because the sale was one transaction. Thus the surety would be guaranteeing his own share, which is impossible.
The text: 'لأنه حصته ضامناً لنفسه' – he guarantees his own share.
3
Understand the second reason: dividing debt before receipt
By guaranteeing only one partner's share, they are effectively dividing the joint debt (the price) before receiving it, which is prohibited in Hanafi law.
The text: 'ولأنه يؤدي إلى قسمة الدين قبل قبضه' – leads to dividing the debt before receipt.
4
Note the condition and exception
The invalidity is specifically when the sale is one 'صفقة واحدة' (single transaction). If they sell in two separate transactions (صفقتين) by each naming a price for his share, then the kafalah is valid.
The text restricts by 'قيد بقوله صفقة واحدة' – the condition is the single transaction.

Intra-Madhhab Dispute on Separate Transactions and Extension to Joint Debt
Intra-madhhab dispute on separate transactions and extension to joint debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the views of Abu Hanifa and the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) on when a partner's kafalah is valid in separate transactions, and apply the same principle of invalidity to joint debts.

Core Idea
For separate transactions, the two accept price allocation, but Abu Hanifa requires repetition of 'I sold'; the same ruling applies to any joint debt.

The previous lesson established that kafalah in a single transaction is invalid. But what if the partners sell in two separate transactions? The passage reveals an intra-madhhab difference: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that simply detailing each share's price suffices to make the transactions distinct, while Abu Hanifa insists on repeating the word 'bought' (i.e., two offers). The author then suggests that the same ruling applies more broadly to any joint debt, citing examples from al-Khaniyya. This lesson examines these nuances.

Guided Path
1
Recall the exception for separate transactions
If the partners sell in two transactions (صفقتين), e.g., by each naming a price for his share, then a partner's guarantee for the other's share is valid because each share is distinct.
The text: 'لو باعاه صفقتين بأن سمى كل واحد منهما لنصيبه ثمناً ضمان أحدهما نصيب الآخر لامتياز نصيب كل منهما'.
2
Identify the intra-madhhab difference
According to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), detailing the price is enough. According to Abu Hanifa, there must be a repetition of the word 'bought' (i.e., two separate offers).
The text: 'وذكروا في البيوع أن هذا قولهما، وأما قول أبي حنيفة فلا بد من تكرار لفظ بعت'.
3
Understand the extension to joint debt
The author prefers to state the ruling broadly: for any joint debt, one of the creditors cannot guarantee the other's share. This is supported by examples from al-Khaniyya.
The examples: two men having a joint debt on a man, or two sons inheriting a debt.
4
Note the voluntary payment exception
While kafalah (guarantee) is invalid, voluntarily paying the other's share (تبرع) is permissible.
The text: 'ولو تبرع أحدهما بأداء نصيب صاحبه من الدين كان جائزاً'.

Master Guarantee of Freed Slave's Debt
Ruling on invalidity of a master's guarantee of his freed slave's debt due to self-interest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a master's guarantee of his freed slave's debt is invalid and identify the underlying principle of suspicion (tuhma) that prevents self-serving transactions in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
A master cannot guarantee his freed slave's debt because he is suspected of acting to absolve his own liability.

This lesson examines a specific branch (far') concerning the validity of a master's guarantee of his freed slave's debt. The commentator (sharh) presents a scenario where a master frees an indebted slave, becoming liable for the slave's value, and then attempts to guarantee the slave's debt. The ruling highlights a key Hanafi principle: transactions that appear self-serving are invalid due to the suspicion (tuhma) that the master is seeking his own benefit over that of the creditors. Understanding this principle helps clarify many similar cases in mu'amalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and obligations
Recognize the master, the freed slave, and the creditors. Note that upon freeing, the master becomes liable for the slave's value (qima) to the creditors.
The slave remains liable for the original debt.
2
Understand the master's liability
The master must pay the slave's value to the creditors. This is a separate obligation from the slave's debt.
This liability arises because the slave's debt takes priority over the master's asset.
3
Analyze the guarantee transaction
The master guarantees the slave's debt (not the value). This would make him responsible for paying the original debt.
If the master pays the debt, he may later claim that his liability for the value is reduced or extinguished.
4
Apply the principle of tuhma (suspicion)
The master is suspected of acting to absolve himself of his own liability for the slave's value. Any transaction that appears self-serving in such a context is invalid.
Tuhma prevents conflicts of interest and protects creditors.
5
Confirm the ruling
The guarantee is invalid (batil). The rationale is purely the suspicion of self-interest.
This ruling is specific to the master; a stranger could validly guarantee the debt.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition and conditions of hawala (transfer of debt) from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq. The author begins by clarifying that hawala is a transfer of the right to claim a debt, not a transfer of the debt itself from one liability to another. This is because if the debt were fully transferred, many legal consequences would follow that do not actually occur, e.g., the debt would not return to the original debtor if the transferee becomes insolvent, but in fact it does. The commentator lists ten objections to the definition that hawala is a transfer of debt, then answers each using rational arguments and precedents from the Hanafi school. The essence of the response is that hawala transfers only the right to demand payment, not the debt itself; the original debtor remains secondarily liable if the transferee cannot pay. This is why the original debtor can be compelled to pay later, why the debt is divided among his creditors after his death, etc. The commentary then moves to the conditions for each party: the transferor must be sane and of age (though a minor's transfer is valid if approved by guardian), consent is required, etc. For the transferee, sanity and puberty are needed for enforceability, and consent and the session are conditions according to the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), while Abu Yusuf alone considers the session a condition for enforceability only. The transferee must also be sane and of age to accept; a minor's acceptance is invalid. Special cases are discussed, such as a minor merchant being treated as an adult, and a slave's transfer being valid. The passage concludes with nuanced rulings about the right of retention and the distinction between gift and absolution. Throughout, the commentator adheres strictly to Hanafi positions, citing authorities like al-Dhakhira and al-Ustrushani.

The Nature and Definition of Hawala
Defining hawala and distinguishing it from other legal concepts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of hawala and distinguish it from agency, hibah, and ibra'.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers the debt and releases the original debtor; the muhil acts for his own release, not as an agent.

Hawala is a unique contract in Islamic finance that allows the transfer of debt. This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies its nature by comparing it to related concepts like agency and relinquishment. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying hawala in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal category
Hawala is a contract that transfers a debt from one debtor to another. Identify the ruling: it is valid (sahih) and binding.
The passage establishes that hawala is not prohibited simply because a single debt might appear to have two claimants.
2
Distinguish hawala from agency
The muhil (original debtor) cannot act as an agent for the muhtal (creditor) in receiving the debt because the muhil acts for his own benefit—to obtain permanent release.
This is a key difference: in agency, the agent acts for the principal; in hawala, the muhil acts for himself.
3
Distinguish hibah from ibra'
Ibra' (release) is a pure relinquishment (isqat) and is irrevocable, while hibah (gift) is a cause of ownership and can be revoked before possession.
The passage states that ibra' is permanent; hibah can be rescinded because the debt is not completely dropped.
4
Analyze successive hawala
If a debtor transfers the same debt to two different persons successively, the second transfer nullifies the first, and the first transferee (muhal) is released.
This is from al-Dhakhira: the second hawala is a nullification of the first.
5
Apply the rule to the seller's right of retention
Unlike the seller who retains goods until payment, if the seller is the muhil, his right of retention invalidates because his claim drops—similar to a mortgagee transferring the debt.
This shows that hawala affects ancillary rights.

Commentary
This passage from Sharh Kanz al-Daqa'iq defines the nature of hawala. It begins by establishing that in hawala, there is a debt owed to the muhil (the original creditor) and also a debt owed to the muhtal (the new creditor) from the same debtor. The author clarifies that the prohibition is against having two claimants for one debt, but here we have one claimant for two debts from two perspectives. The muhil cannot act as agent for the muhtal because the muhil is acting for his own benefit to achieve a permanent release. The author then distinguishes between hibah (gift) and ibra' (release): ibra' is a pure relinquishment and cannot be revoked, while hibah creates ownership and is subject to revocation (faskh) because the debt is not completely dropped. A quote from al-Dhakhira illustrates that if a debtor transfers to two different persons successively, the second transfer nullifies the first, releasing the first transferee. Finally, the author discusses the right of retention (habus) of a seller: unlike the seller, if the muhil is the seller, his right of retention is invalidated because his claim is dropped, analogous to the mortgagee transferring his creditor to the mortgagor.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-hawala): A transfer of debt from one debtor to another, whereby the creditor's claim is transferred to the new debtor.
الإبراء (al-ibra'): A legal act of relinquishing a debt, which is irrevocable.
المحيل (al-muhil): The original debtor who transfers the debt to another person.

Types and Evidence of Hawalah
Classifies hawalah into three types with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three types of hawalah and explain the Hanafi interpretation of the hadith evidence as permissibility (ibāḥah).

Core Idea
The hadith on hawalah commands permissibility, not obligation—Hanafi fiqh upholds the binding transfer of debt.

This passage from al-Baḥr al-Rāʾiq presents the classification of hawalah (debt transfer) into three types, provides the key hadith evidence, and clarifies essential conditions. Understanding this foundation is crucial for applying hawalah correctly in financial transactions. We will walk through the types, the proof, and the Hanafi position on the legal status of the command.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of hawalah
List the three types: binding (lāzimah), temporary (jāʾizah), and invalid (fāsidah). Note the conditions that distinguish each: binding requires acceptance and a fixed debt; temporary is conditional on a future event; invalid involves uncertainty (e.g., tied to sale of property).
The binding type can be absolute or specified.
2
Extract the hadith evidence
Cite the hadith: 'The procrastination of the rich is injustice, and if one of you is referred to a solvent person, let him follow.' Note that it is narrated by the six major hadith collectors.
The key operative phrase is 'falyatbaʿ' (let him follow).
3
Analyze the Hanafi interpretation of the imperative
The Hanafi school interprets the imperative as permissibility (ibāḥah), not obligation. Contrast with Imam Aḥmad who held it is obligatory. The Hanafi position is based on consensus and the principle that commands default to permissibility unless evidence indicates obligation.
The text states: 'wa-l-ḥaqqu al-ẓāhiru annahu amru ibāḥah'.
4
Note the requirement of a prior debt
Hawalah is valid only for a debt (dayn), not for specific assets (ʿayn). The transferee must owe a debt to the transferor; otherwise, the transfer is a mere agency (wakālah), not a hawalah.
This condition ensures that hawalah involves a legal transfer of obligation.
5
Understand the practical implications
The thamarah al-khilāf (practical difference) between Hanafi and Aḥmad is whether accepting a hawalah referral is obligatory. Hanafis hold it is merely permissible, giving the creditor flexibility.
The consensus (ijmāʿ) on permissibility is a strong evidence for the Hanafi view.

Conditions of Hawalah: Consent and Acceptance
States conditions for validity of hawalah: consent, acceptance, and dispute over consent of muhil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for the validity of a hawalah contract and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the consent of the muhil.

Core Idea
The consent of the muhil is not a condition for hawalah's validity, only for recourse.

The validity of a hawalah (debt transfer) contract rests on the consent of the creditor (muhal) and the debtor being transferred to (muhal 'alayh). This consent must be expressed as acceptance in the same session as the offer. However, a point of debate among Hanafi scholars is whether the consent of the original debtor (muhil) is also required. The passage resolves this by distinguishing between validity and the right of recourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic condition for validity
The passage states that hawalah requires consent (rida) of both the muhal (creditor) and the muhal 'alayh (the person to whom the debt is transferred). This consent is interpreted as acceptance (qabul) in the session of offer (majlis al-ijab). Identify these two parties and the nature of their consent.
Consent here is a condition for validity; coercion invalidates the contract.
2
Analyze the exception for absent parties
The passage cites a ruling from al-Bazzaziyyah: if the muhil transfers the debt to an absent muhal 'alayh, the hawalah is valid upon that person's later acceptance. However, if the muhal (creditor) is absent, the hawalah is not valid unless someone accepts on his behalf. Note the asymmetry: the muhal 'alayh can accept later, but the muhal's acceptance must be immediate or by proxy.
This parallels the rule in kafalah (suretyship).
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on muhil's consent
According to Imam Muhammad in al-Ziyadat, the consent of the muhil (the original debtor who initiates the transfer) is not a condition for validity. Al-Quduri, however, made it a condition. The author of the passage resolves this by stating that al-Quduri's condition is only for the purpose of the muhal 'alayh having recourse (ruju') against the muhil, not for the contract's validity itself.
Thus, the two positions do not actually conflict; they address different legal effects.
4
Derive the practical consequence
If a hawalah is performed without the muhil's consent, the contract is valid, but the muhal 'alayh cannot claim reimbursement from the muhil unless the muhil had a debt on him. This means the muhal 'alayh bears the risk of non-payment by the original debtor if the muhil did not consent.
This protects the muhil from being forced into a hawalah against his will, while still allowing the transfer to proceed.

Definition of Tawa: No Recourse Except Upon Loss
Definition of tawa from the matn and its explanation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of tawa in the Hanafi school and identify the specific circumstances under which a transferee may have recourse against the transferor.

Core Idea
Tawa is the loss of the debt through denial with oath or death insolvent – only then can the transferee revert.

This passage establishes the foundational ruling of hawalah: once the transferor transfers the debt and the transferee accepts, the transferor is free from liability. The only exception is when the debt is 'lost' (tawa). Understanding what constitutes tawa is crucial, as it determines whether the transferee can reclaim the debt from the transferor.

1
Identify the core ruling
Locate the phrase 'لا يرجع المحتال على المحيل إلا بالتوى'. This is the pivot of the passage – the transferee cannot revert except upon tawa.

Commentary
This passage is from Al-Bahr al-Raiq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq, and it discusses the ruling on hawalah (transfer of debt). The author begins by stating the core ruling from the matn: the transferor (المحيل) is freed from the debt upon the acceptance of the transferee (المحتال), and the transferee cannot have recourse against the transferor except upon 'tawa' (loss). The author then explains 'tawa' as the transferee upon (المحتال عليه) denying the hawalah and swearing without evidence, or dying insolvent. The commentary then delves into various details: the necessity of acceptance, the requirement of possession (qabd) in certain cases, the validity of hawalah in different scenarios, and the procedure when there is a dispute about whether hawalah occurred.

The passage then addresses the main point of dispute: the definition of tawa and whether a judicial declaration of insolvency (taflis) qualifies as tawa. Abu Hanifa holds that taflis is not tawa because the debt remains in the dhimmah and inability to collect does not give recourse, analogous to the case of the transferee upon being absent. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that taflis is tawa because the qadi's order cuts off the transferee's ability to pursue the debt, similar to denial or death. The author also discusses the effect of a surety (kafalah) on the definition of insolvency: according to al-Ziyadat, the existence of a surety prevents the debtor from being considered insolvent, while al-Khulasa says it does not. The Passage also covers a case from Muhammad's al-Ziyadat involving multiple consecutive hawalahs on a principal debtor and a surety, ruling that if the transfer to the surety occurs first, both are valid; if the transfer to the principal occurs first, the subsequent transfer to the surety is invalid. Finally, the author discusses the condition that the transferor cannot be the secondary transferee upon (المحتال عليه ثانياً) for the tawa exception to apply, and clarifies the linguistic and idiomatic meanings of tawa.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Is Judicial Declaration of Insolvency (Taflis) Tawa?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on taflis as tawa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two companions regarding whether a judicial declaration of insolvency (taflis) constitutes tawa, and evaluate the reasoning on both sides.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: taflis is not tawa; Sahibayn: it is – the debt's existence in dhimmah vs practical inability to collect.

This passage presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute on the scope of tawa. The context is the general rule that the transferee cannot revert except upon tawa. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) expand the definition to include a judicial declaration of insolvency (taflis), while Abu Hanifa restricts it to the two original cases. Understanding this dispute illuminates the Hanafi methodology of balancing strict textualism with practical necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed element
Find the phrase 'ولو فلسه الحاكم بعد ما حبسه لا يكون توى عند أبي حنيفة وقالا : هو توى'. This is the crux: does a qadi's declaration of insolvency count as tawa?
2
Analyze the Sahibayn's proof
They argue that taflis is tawa because it creates an inability to collect (عجز عن الأخذ), similar to denial or death. The qadi's order cuts off the transferee's access.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa argues that the debt remains in the dhimmah (ذمة) and inability to collect does not automatically give recourse. He uses the analogy of absence: if the transferee upon is absent, no recourse. Therefore, taflis is not tawa.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if the qadi declares the transferee upon insolvent, according to Abu Hanifa the transferee cannot revert, but according to the Sahibayn he can.
5
Place the dispute in context
Note that the author does not give a tarjih (preference). This indicates that both views are relied upon in different contexts.

Commentary
This passage is from Al-Bahr al-Raiq, a commentary on Kanz al-Daqaiq, and it discusses the ruling on hawalah (transfer of debt). The author begins by stating the core ruling from the matn: the transferor (المحيل) is freed from the debt upon the acceptance of the transferee (المحتال), and the transferee cannot have recourse against the transferor except upon 'tawa' (loss). The author then explains 'tawa' as the transferee upon (المحتال عليه) denying the hawalah and swearing without evidence, or dying insolvent. The commentary then delves into various details: the necessity of acceptance, the requirement of possession (qabd) in certain cases, the validity of hawalah in different scenarios, and the procedure when there is a dispute about whether hawalah occurred.

The passage then addresses the main point of dispute: the definition of tawa and whether a judicial declaration of insolvency (taflis) qualifies as tawa. Abu Hanifa holds that taflis is not tawa because the debt remains in the dhimmah and inability to collect does not give recourse, analogous to the case of the transferee upon being absent. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that taflis is tawa because the qadi's order cuts off the transferee's ability to pursue the debt, similar to denial or death. The author also discusses the effect of a surety (kafalah) on the definition of insolvency: according to al-Ziyadat, the existence of a surety prevents the debtor from being considered insolvent, while al-Khulasa says it does not. The Passage also covers a case from Muhammad's al-Ziyadat involving multiple consecutive hawalahs on a principal debtor and a surety, ruling that if the transfer to the surety occurs first, both are valid; if the transfer to the principal occurs first, the subsequent transfer to the surety is invalid. Finally, the author discusses the condition that the transferor cannot be the secondary transferee upon (المحتال عليه ثانياً) for the tawa exception to apply, and clarifies the linguistic and idiomatic meanings of tawa.

التفليس (al-taflis): Judicial declaration of insolvency; the qadi orders that the debtor is bankrupt.
الذمة (al-dhimmah): The legal personality or capacity of a person to be liable for debts; the debt exists in the dhimmah even if collection is difficult.

Amount Recoverable by Transferee: Like the Debt, Not the Payment
Ruling on the amount the transferee can recover from the transferor after paying the debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the transferee recovers mithl al-dayn and apply it to different payment scenarios.

Core Idea
The transferee recovers the debt itself, not the payment method.

In hawalah (transfer of debt), when the transferee pays the creditor, he seeks reimbursement from the transferor. The question arises: does he recover exactly what he paid, or the original debt? This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position: the transferee's right is based on the debt, so he recovers the like of the debt (mithl al-dayn) in type and quantity, regardless of how he paid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Recognise that the text states the transferee recovers 'mithl al-dayn' (the like of the debt). This is the core ruling.
2
Understand the rationale
The reason is that the transferee pays by the transferor's order, so the transferor owes the debt amount, not the specific payment. The burden of proof is on the denier (transferor).
3
Examine the examples
Work through each example: paying in different currency, goods, counterfeit coins, or settlement. In each case, the recovery is based on the original debt unless a settlement for less with a different type occurs.
4
Contrast with the ma'mur case
Compare with the person ordered to pay (ma'mur bi qada' al-dayn): he recovers what he actually paid, except if he paid better quality or different type. This distinction is important.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Use the ruling to determine recovery in a novel situation, e.g., if the transferee pays with a cheque that bounces, or pays before the due date.

The Dispute: Is Hawalah an Agency?
Ruling on the transferor's claim of agency vs. hawalah, with objections and responses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when the transferor claims he only appointed an agent, and explain why the transferor's statement is accepted with his oath.

Core Idea
The word 'hawalah' can mean agency figuratively; thus the transferor's claim is accepted with oath unless clear guaranty language is used.

In hawalah, the transferor (muhil) transfers a debt from himself to the transferee (muhal 'alayh). But what if he later claims he only meant to appoint the transferee as an agent to collect the debt, not to transfer liability? This dispute raises the key distinction between hawalah and wakalah. The Hanafi position, based on linguistic probability and the burden of proof, gives the transferor the benefit of the doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting claims
The transferor says he transferred for collection (agency); the transferee says it was a transfer of debt (hawalah). Who is the plaintiff and who is the defendant?
The transferee claims a debt, so he is the mudda'i (claimant).
2
Analyze the linguistic evidence
The word 'hawalah' is used in Arabic for both a true transfer of debt and figuratively for agency. This ambiguity allows the transferor to argue that he meant agency.
Majaz (figurative usage) is recognized in Hanafi usul.
3
Apply the burden of proof principle
Since the transferee claims a debt against the transferor, he is the claimant. The transferor denies the debt, so his statement is accepted with an oath.
Al-amm yufidu al-wujub? Not directly; rather, the principle that the denier's statement is accepted.
4
Consider the objection and Hanafi response
Objection: If hawalah is like agency, why can't the transferor revoke it? Response: Hawalah once concluded is a transfer of liability; the possibility of it being agency does not allow revocation because the act is definitive.
The Hanafis prioritize the definitive effect of the contract over mere probability.
5
Note the exception for clear guaranty language
If the transferor says 'adman 'anni' (be my guarantor), that language clarifies it is a guaranty, not agency, and the transferor's claim of agency is not accepted.
The text explicitly excludes this case from the general rule.

Ruling on hawalah with a deposit and classification into absolute and restricted.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the validity of hawalah on a deposit and distinguish between absolute and restricted hawalah, stating the rulings for each.

Core Idea
Hawalah on a deposit is valid as a restricted hawalah; absolute hawalah does not fully transfer the debt but gives recourse to the transferee.

The text moves to a key application: can a transferor transfer a debt using a deposit held by someone else? Yes, it is valid. This introduces the distinction between absolute and restricted hawalah. Understanding this classification is essential for correctly applying hawalah in various circumstances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on hawalah with a deposit
The text states that if a transferor transfers his property held as a deposit by Zayd, it is valid; if the deposit perishes, the debtor is discharged.
This is a practical example of restricted hawalah.
2
Classify the types of hawalah
Restricted (muqayyada) and absolute (mutlaqa). Restricted: specifies the debt or item the transferee owes. Absolute: no specification.
Restricted hawalah makes the transferee an agent; absolute makes him a volunteer.
3
Understand the agent vs volunteer distinction
In restricted hawalah, the transferee is an agent for payment; in absolute, he volunteers to pay.
This affects the rights of recourse.
4
Apply the rulings of absolute hawalah
For absolute: the transferor's right does not cease; the transferee can recourse against the transferor after payment if he paid with the transferor's consent. Deferred debts remain deferred; death of transferor does not accelerate, but death of transferee does.
This is a key difference from restricted, where the right is fully transferred.

Commentary
This passage covers several rulings. First, it discusses a case from Al-Bazzaziyya: a buyer transfers the price to someone, and a third party volunteers to pay the price; the transferee has no recourse against the buyer unless he volunteered on behalf of the transferee. Then it quotes Al-Khulasah and Al-Ziyadat regarding a guarantor when the transferee dies bankrupt, noting a discrepancy: according to Al-Ziyadat, taking a guarantor does not cause the debt to return to the transferor; but the commentator notes that the ruling about recourse from the guarantor is due to the release of the guarantor, not a general rule. Next, the passage addresses the dispute when the transferor claims the transfer was only an agency (wakalah), not a true hawalah. The Hanafi position is that the transferor's statement is accepted with his oath because the word 'hawalah' can be used figuratively for agency. However, an objection is considered: if the transferor could revoke agency, why can't he revoke hawalah? The answer is that hawalah, once valid, is treated as a transfer of liability, not a revocable agency. An exception is made if the transferor says 'be a guarantor for me' (adman 'anni), which clearly establishes a guaranty, not agency. Two narrations are given regarding an absent transferee: one accepts the transferor's claim of agency, the other does not; the text does not decide. Finally, the passage explains hawalah on a deposit (wadi'ah): it is valid as a restricted hawalah; the transferor is an agent for payment. It distinguishes absolute hawalah, where the transferor's right does not cease and the transferee has recourse.

Core Idea
In restricted hawalah, the muheel forfeits the claim; in unrestricted, he retains it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the crucial difference between two types of hawalah (transfer of debt). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rulings of debt transfer in Hanafi muamalat.

الحوالة المطلقة (al-hawalah al-mutlaqah): A transfer where the muheel does not tie the payment to a specific debt or asset of the muhal 'alayh; rather, the muhal 'alayh is assigned to pay from his general assets.

A mufti must be qualified, avoid carelessness, and may receive a stipend but never a direct fee.

This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq outlines the conditions and etiquettes of a mufti in the Hanafi school. It addresses who may give fatwa, what states to avoid, and the complex rulings on payment. Understanding these rules is essential for anyone involved in issuing or seeking legal rulings.

This ensures the integrity of fatwa.

Fatwa Procedure: Local Usage and Preference Among Opinions
Rules on fatwa according to local terminology and preference between scholarly opinions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi fatwa methodology when local terminology or multiple scholarly opinions exist, identifying which opinion to prefer based on the hierarchy given.

Core Idea
Fatwa must respect local usage; when opinions conflict, prefer the later, the preferred by the imam, or the majority's view.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq outlines the mufti's duty to understand local conventions before issuing fatwas on wording-related matters. It then provides a clear hierarchy for choosing between multiple opinions (qawlayn) or legal faces (wajhayn), ensuring that the mufti acts on a well-grounded reliance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mufti's first duty regarding local terminology
The mufti must know the local istilah relevant to the fatwa. Without it, he cannot give fatwa on wording-related issues.
Local custom (urf) is a recognized source in Hanafi fiqh.
2
Determine the hierarchy for two sayings (qawlayn)
First: later opinion (muta'akhkhar) if known; second: the opinion preferred by the imam (here al-Shafi'i, but in Hanafi it would be Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf/Muhammad); third: research the principles; fourth: take from transmitters. If no preference, stop.
The 'later opinion' is given precedence because it reflects the imam's final view.
3
Apply the same hierarchy to two faces (wajhayn) with a caveat
Same ruling as qawlayn, but the later opinion is only considered if both faces come from the same scholar.
Wajhayn often arise from later Hanafi jurists' ijtihad.
4
When scholars differ on the arjah (more preponderant)
If the mufti is not qualified for tarjih, he relies on the majority and the most knowledgeable. Otherwise he stops.
This ensures that fatwa is based on the strongest evidence.
5
Note the special case of the Shafi'i jadid
The new opinion of al-Shafi'i is generally followed except in about thirty issues. This illustrates the principle that newer opinions take precedence.
In Hanafi, later opinions of Abu Hanifa are similarly treated.

Etiquette of Writing a Fatwa Response (Adab al-Ifta')
Instructions on how to write and respond to fatwa queries in writing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rules for writing a fatwa response, identify what is disliked, and apply them to a written query scenario.

Core Idea
A mufti must answer only the questions asked, in order, with clarity and conciseness, avoiding vague or extraneous statements.

This lesson draws from the Hanafi tradition on the proper etiquette (adab) for a mufti when responding to written fatwa queries. These guidelines ensure that the answer is clear, relevant, and respectful to the questioner and the ruler. They also protect the mufti from issuing ambiguous or incomplete guidance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the document and its contents
Recognize that the question is presented in a paper (ruq‘a). If there are multiple questions, note them in order.
The mufti must rely solely on what is written, not on his own knowledge of the person.
2
List the order and placement rules
Answers should be arranged in the same order as the questions. The response is written on the upper left side of the paper, not before the basmala.
The left side is the usual placement in classical fatwa papers. Avoid placing the answer before the basmala.
3
Note what is disliked and why
It is disliked to simply say 'there are two sayings' without explanation, and to give an unqualified statement where detail is needed. Such practices are not beneficial and may cause confusion.
The mufti should tailor the answer to the question’s level of detail.
4
Recognize the beginning and ending formulas
Start with 'Alhamdulillah' and conclude with 'Wallahu A‘lam'. It is allowed to say 'indana' (according to us) if the ruling is specific to the Hanafi school.
The formula 'indana' reinforces school affiliation without disrespecting other opinions.
5
Understand the allowances for the ruler and conciseness
If the question involves the ruler, it is permissible to supplicate for him with phrases like 'may Allah guide him' or 'may Allah strengthen his authority'. It is disliked to say 'may Allah prolong his life'. The mufti should keep the answer brief yet clear.
These supplications follow the Hanafi tradition of respect for authority without excessive flattery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling regarding the statement of a deposed judge and apply it to different causes of imprisonment (debt, qisas, zina, theft, slander).

Core Idea
A deposed judge's word is not law unless the possessor confirms – each prisoner's case demands a distinct response.

In Islamic judicial procedure, a judge's authority terminates upon dismissal. However, situations arise where the new judge must deal with cases left by his predecessor. This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies that the deposed judge's statements are generally not relied upon, except when the current possessor of property acknowledges delivery. The text then applies this principle to five categories of prisoners, showing how the new judge should proceed.

Guided Path
1
State the default rule
The statement of a deposed judge is not acted upon in judicial matters after his dismissal. Identify this as the base ruling.
This applies to all statements unless an exception is established.
2
Identify the exception
If the possessor (dhu al-yad) acknowledges that the deposed judge delivered the property to him, then the statement is accepted regarding that property. This is the sole exception mentioned here.
The exception is limited to property matters, not to general testimony.
3
Analyze the imprisonment categories
The source lists five causes: debt, qisas, hadd for zina, theft, and slander. For each, the new judge must apply specific rules regarding acceptance of acknowledgement, imposition of hadd, and release conditions.
These cases illustrate how the general rule is applied differently based on the cause of imprisonment.
4
Distinguish between types
For debt: the right is already confirmed. For qisas: retaliation is carried out but release for limb requires surety. For zina: the prior confession is ignored; the prisoner must confess anew. For theft: confession leads to cutting of the hand and release with surety; evidence without confession requires fresh proceedings. For slander: hadd is imposed only if the victim confirms; release with surety.
The key is that the new judge does not rely on the deposed judge's findings but acts on independent evidence.
5
Synthesize the pattern
The general rule is that the deposed judge's statement is not accepted, but in each imprisonment scenario, the new judge uses his own judicial discretion based on the evidence before him. The pattern emphasizes the independence of the new judge while acknowledging the factual reality of the imprisonment.
This ensures justice and prevents abuse from the previous judge's potential bias.

The Deposed Judge's Statement: Deposits, Endowments, and the Five Cases
Rules for deposits, endowments, and the five cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain when the statement of a deposed judge is accepted regarding deposits and endowment yields, and derive the ruling for each of the five scenarios involving the possessor's acknowledgement.

Core Idea
In deposits and endowments, the possessor's word weighs more than the deposed judge's – five scenarios determine who keeps the property.

Having covered the general rule and prisoners, this part of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq focuses on property disputes involving deposits (ودائع) and endowment yields (غلات الوقف). The new judge accepts the possessor's acknowledgement or evidence from others, but not the mere word of the deposed judge. The text then details five specific scenarios that delineate when the deposed judge's statement is accepted alongside the possessor's. These scenarios are crucial for deciding ownership in complex situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of acceptance
The new judge works with evidence or acknowledgement in deposits and endowment yields. The key is that the acknowledgement must come from the possessor (ذو اليد). His admission is accepted as proof.
This is an exception to the general rule: here, the possessor's word can substitute for formal evidence.
2
Distinguish between deposit/yields and original endowment
The rule applies only to the yields of an endowment, not to the original endowment itself. If the heirs deny the endowment and the deposed judge claims it, his statement is not accepted; the property is divided as inheritance.
This limitation prevents the deposed judge from unilaterally declaring someone's property as waqf.
3
Learn the five scenarios
The text lists five possible interactions between the possessor's acknowledgement and the deposed judge's statement. Each has a different outcome. The first three involve conflicting acknowledgements; the fourth involves ignorance; the fifth involves mutual confirmation.
These scenarios cover the main possibilities that arise in practice.
4
Analyze each scenario
First: possessor acknowledges to another and to the judge → property to first, guarantor for second. Second: possessor denies delivery → judge's word rejected. Third: possessor acknowledges delivery but then claims for another → second claim rejected. Fourth: possessor acknowledges delivery but doesn't know owner → judge's word accepted. Fifth: both agree on recipient → property delivered to that person.
Note that the deposed judge's statement is accepted in four out of five scenarios, but only when coupled with the possessor's acknowledgement.
5
Synthesize the pattern
The deposed judge's statement is never accepted alone. It becomes effective only when the possessor's acknowledgement confirms it, or when the possessor is uncertain and defers to the judge. The general rule is preserved: the deposed judge has no independent authority.
This ensures that the new judge protects rights while maintaining the principle of judicial independence.
