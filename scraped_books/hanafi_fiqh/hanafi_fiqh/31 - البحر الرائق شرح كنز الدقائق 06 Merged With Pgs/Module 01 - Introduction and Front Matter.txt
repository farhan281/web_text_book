Introduction to al-Bahr al-Ra'iq: Authors and Text Hierarchy
Front matter identifying the book and authors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three layers of al-Bahr al-Ra'iq (matn, sharh, hashiya) and state the name and death date of each author.

Core Idea
Hanafi fiqh study rests on a three‑layer hierarchy: matn, sharh, hashiya.

Before diving into the fiqh rulings, it is crucial to know the text you are studying. This front matter introduces al-Bahr al-Ra'iq, one of the most important Hanafi fiqh commentaries. You will learn the structure: the core text (matn) by al-Nasafi, its commentary (sharh) by Ibn Nujaym, and the supercommentary (hashiya) by Ibn Abidin. Understanding this hierarchy helps you navigate the text and appreciate the scholarly tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn and its author
Find the name of the matn (core text) and its author. The matn is Kanz al-Daqa'iq by al-Nasafi (d. 710 AH). Notice that the matn is placed at the top of each page.
Al-Nasafi's text is a concise summary of Hanafi fiqh.
2
Identify the sharh and its author
The commentary on the matn is al-Bahr al-Ra'iq by Ibn Nujaym (d. 970 AH). His text is placed directly below the matn on the page.
Ibn Nujaym's commentary is known for thorough treatment of issues.
3
Identify the hashiya and its author
The supercommentary is Minhah al-Khaliq by Ibn Abidin (d. 1252 AH). His notes appear at the bottom of each page.
Ibn Abidin is a late Hanafi authority; his hashiya refines the commentary.
4
Recognize the page layout
The editor placed the matn at the top, the sharh below it, and the hashiya at the bottom. This layout allows you to see all three layers at once.
This physical arrangement mirrors the scholarly hierarchy.
5
Note the publisher and edition
This is the sixth volume, published by Dar al-Kutub al-Ilmiyyah, Beirut, first edition 1418 AH/1997 CE. The text was verified by Zakariyya Umayrat.
Knowing the edition helps when citing the text.

Linguistic Meanings of Qada
Linguistic definition of al-qada'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the eight linguistic meanings of al-qada' and cite a Quranic example for at least five of them.

Core Idea
Al-qada' means judgment, completion, destruction, performance, going, making, and more.

Before diving into the Hanafi legal definition of al-qada' (judicial judgment), we must first understand its rich linguistic range. The classical lexicons report that this single word carries no fewer than eight distinct meanings, all rooted in the Arabic language. This linguistic foundation is crucial because the legal term retains some of these nuances — for example, a judge's verdict 'completes' a dispute and 'determines' rights.

Guided Path
1
Trace the etymology
Note that qada' comes from qaday, with the ya' converted to hamza due to the preceding alif. Recognize that the word is used with madd (elongation).
The plural is aqdiyah.
2
Identify the core meanings
List the eight meanings given in the passage: hukm (judgment), faragh (completion), halak (destruction), ada' (performance), inha' (finishing), mady (going), sun' (making), taqdir (determining).
Memorize these in order.
3
Match each meaning to a Quranic example
For each meaning, locate the verse cited in the text: 17:23 (judgment), 17:4 & 15:66 (performance/finishing), 41:12 (creation/determining), etc.
This anchors the meaning in revelation.

The first (chapter) concerning its meaning linguistically: it is with elongation (al-madd) like kisa' or aksiya. In al-Misbah: it is the masdar of 'I judged between the two litigants' and 'I ruled against them.' In al-Sihah: al-qada' is judgment, its origin is qaday because it is from qadaytu, except that when the ya' comes after the alif, it is changed to a hamza. The plural is aqdiyah. Qada means he judged, and from it is His statement 'And your Lord has decreed that you worship none but Him' [17:23]. It may also mean completion: you say 'I completed my need' and 'he struck him and qada upon him' meaning he killed him, as if he finished him off. And it is said 'qadi' meaning killer, and 'qada nahbahu' meaning he died. It may also mean performance and finishing: you say 'I paid my debt' and from it is His statement 'And We decreed to the Children of Israel in the Book' [17:4] and His statement 'And We decreed to him that matter' [15:66] meaning We finished it to him and conveyed it. Al-Farra' said regarding His statement 'Then acquire to' [10:71] meaning go to, as it is said 'so-and-so qada' meaning he died and passed away. It may also mean making and determining. Abu Dhu'ayb said: 'And upon them are two coats of mail, which Dawud qadahu (made) or the maker of the wide coats followed.' It is said qadahu meaning he made it and determined it. From it is His statement 'So He qadahunna (completed them) as seven heavens in two days' [41:12]. From it is al-qada' wal-qadar. It is said 'istaqda fulan' meaning he made himself a judge. In summary, it is used linguistically to mean judgment, completion, destruction, performance, finishing, going, making, and determining. In al-Qamus: al-qada', whether elongated or shortened, is judgment: he judged against him, he judges a judgment and a case, and it is also the noun, etc.

Commentary
This passage is a linguistic definition of the word 'qada' (القضاء). The author begins by citing multiple classical lexicons. He first quotes al-Misbah, which states that qada is a verbal noun meaning to judge between litigants. Then from al-Sihah, he explains its etymology: the original form was qaday, but the ya' was converted to a hamza due to the preceding alif. The plural is aqdiyah. The author then lists several semantic fields: (1) judgment, exemplified by Quran 17:23; (2) completion, as in finishing a need; (3) killing or destruction, as in 'qada upon him' meaning killed; (4) death, as in 'qada nahbahu'; (5) performance or payment, as in paying a debt, with Quran 17:4 and 15:66; (6) going or proceeding, from al-Farra's interpretation of Quran 10:71; (7) making or creating, as in Abu Dhu'ayb's poetry and Quran 41:12, which relates to qada and qadar. The summary lists eight meanings: hukm (judgment), faragh (completion), halak (destruction), ada' (performance), inha' (finishing), mady (going), sun' (making), and taqdir (determining). The Qamus confirms that qada can be pronounced with lengthening or shortening and means judgment.

Key Terms
القضاء (al-qada'): Linguistically, it means judgment, completion, death, performance, going, and making/determining as detailed in the passage.
