Invalidity of a Kafir's Waqf of a Quran
States the ruling and reasoning for a kafir's waqf of a Quran.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a kafir's waqf of a Quran and explain the underlying reasoning that invalidates it.

Core Idea
A kafir's waqf of a Quran is invalid because it is not an act of worship for them.

This brief passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq addresses a specific case: what if a non-Muslim tries to endow a copy of the Quran as waqf? The author explains that such a waqf is not valid, and the kafir is compelled to sell the Quran. This ruling is based on the principle that waqf is an act of religious piety (qurbah), and that which is not considered piety by the waqif cannot be validly endowed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is a non-Muslim (kafir) establishing a waqf (endowment) of a copy of the Quran. The ruling is that this waqf is invalid, and the kafir is compelled by the court to sell the Quran.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The proof is the reasoning given in the commentary: the waqf is invalid because it is not an act of worship (qurbah) for the kafir, and there is a fear that he may desecrate the Quran if he retains ownership.
The dalil here is a rational argument (istihsan) based on preventing harm.
3
Analyze the 'illa (effective cause)
The effective cause ('illa) for the invalidity is that waqf requires the act to be a qurbah (act of piety) according to the waqif's religion. For a Muslim, waqf of the Quran is a qurbah; for a kafir, it is not, so the essential condition is missing.
This is a condition of validity, not a pillar.
4
Consider the consequence
Because the waqf is invalid, the kafir retains ownership of the Quran. However, to prevent potential desecration (e.g., burning), the judge compels him to sell it to a Muslim.
The sale is forced, not voluntary.

Commentary
This brief passage from the commentary addresses the ruling on a non-Muslim (kafir) establishing a waqf of a copy of the Quran. The matn states that the author has not seen a ruling on this issue. The commentary from al-Nahr, citing al-Siraj, explains that a kafir is compelled to sell the Quran because there is a risk that he may desecrate it. The reasoning indicates that waqf is an act of religious piety, and since the kafir does not consider it a pious act, his waqf is not valid. Things that are validly made waqf are not feared to be destroyed in forbidden ways, but the Quran is at risk in the hands of a kafir, hence the sale is enforced.

Conditional Waqf is Void Because Waqf Requires Immediacy
Rulings on conditional waqf with examples and comparison to nadhr.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that conditional waqf is void and explain the rationale that distinguishes it from conditional nadhr.

Core Idea
A waqf must be immediate; conditional waqf is batil because it involves risk and is not sworn by.

The Hanafi school holds that a waqf cannot be made contingent on a future event. This lesson examines the ruling from al-Bahr al-Raiq and other sources, the reasoning behind it, and the contrast with vows (nadhr), which can be conditional. We will also consider a minority narration that allows conditional waqf but note that the relied-upon position is its voidness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on conditional waqf
From the source, determine the hukm: a waqf that is mu'allaq (suspended on a condition) is batil (void). The text explicitly says 'لا يصح تعليقه بالشرط' and 'يكون الوقف باطلاً'.
The ruling is uniform across Hanafi texts despite a weak narration.
2
Understand the condition of tanjiz
Waqf requires tanjiz (immediacy). The passage states that waqf must be 'منجزاً غير معلق'. Without tanjiz, the waqf is not formed.
Tanjiz is a condition (shart) for the validity of waqf.
3
Analyze the ta'lil (reason)
The text gives the reason: 'لأنه ليس مما يحلف به أيضاً فلا يصح تعليقه بالشرط'. Waqf is not something that can be sworn by (yahlaf bih), unlike nadhr. Conditional waqf involves risk (khatar) which waqf cannot tolerate.
This is a key distinction between waqf and nadhr.
4
Apply the ruling to examples
Work through the examples given: saying 'If my son arrives' or 'If I recover from illness' as waqf is void. But if said as nadhr, it is valid upon condition.
The same expression can have different rulings depending on the intent (waqf vs nadhr).
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The passage mentions 'في رواية' that there are two narrations on the validity of conditional waqf. The relied-upon view (zahir al-riwayah) is that it is void.
The mufta bihi is the view that conditional waqf is batil.

And in Fath: the book of waqf, and its condition is that it be immediate not conditional; if he says, 'If my son arrives, then my house is sadaqah endowed to the poor,' then his son arrives, it does not become a waqf. And in al-Is'af: If he says, 'When tomorrow comes' or 'When the beginning of the month comes,' or he says, 'If I speak to so-and-so,' or 'If I marry so-and-so,' and similar, 'then this land of mine is sadaqah endowed,' the waqf is void because it is a ta'liq, and waqf does not tolerate attachment by risk because it is not something sworn by, unlike nadhr because it tolerates attachment and is sworn by. So if he says, 'If I recover from this illness, then my land is sadaqah endowed,' he is obliged to give it in sadaqah upon the condition. And if he says, 'Sadaqah endowed if you wish or love or approve or desire,' it is void. End.

Commentary
This passage addresses conditional waqf. The Hanafi position is that a waqf cannot be made conditional (mu'allaq) because waqf is an immediate, irrevocable transfer. If someone says 'I endow my house if X arrives,' it is not a valid waqf. This is because waqf is not an act that can be sworn upon (like a vow). However, a nadhr (vow) can be conditional, as in 'If I recover, I will give sadaqah.' The difference is that nadhr can be linked to a condition, but waqf cannot. The text also notes a minority narration allowing it, but the relied-upon view is that conditional waqf is void.

Key Terms
تنجيز (tanjiz): Immediacy; a condition for validity of waqf - it must be executed now, not dependent on a future event.
نذر (nadhr): A vow to perform an act of worship or charity; can be conditional, unlike waqf.

Waqf is Not Invalidated by Fasid Conditions: The Hanafi Principle
Refutation of the claim that waqf is invalidated by a fasid condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a fasid condition does not invalidate a waqf, and identify the exception when the condition negates the essence of the donation.

Core Idea
A fasid condition does not void a waqf unless it contradicts the very nature of the donation.

A common misunderstanding is that any invalid condition (shart fasid) automatically nullifies a legal act. In Hanafi fiqh, however, the effect of a fasid condition depends on the nature of the contract. This lesson unpacks a critical refinement: for donations (tabarru'at) like waqf, a fasid condition generally leaves the donation intact. Only when the condition strikes at the heart of the donation—such as requiring that ownership never transfer—does the entire act become void.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Hanafi Principle for Donations
The passage states that in tabarru'at (donations), a fasid condition does not invalidate the act. Confirm this principle from the text: the author cites it as an 'asl' (original rule).
This principle applies to hibah, waqf, and similar gratuitous transfers.
2
Apply to Waqf
Waqf is classified as a donation. Therefore, the default ruling is that a fasid condition does not invalidate the waqf. Note Qadikhan's explicit statement to this effect.
Contrast with contracts of exchange (mu'awadat) where a fasid condition often voids the contract.
3
Recognize the Exception
The exception arises when the condition negates the very essence of the donation, such as stipulating that the donor retains ownership or that the property remains in his possession without substitution. In such cases, the donation never truly occurred, so it is void.
This is not a true 'invalidation by condition' but rather a failure of the donation to exist.
4
Compare with Hibah Example
The passage uses hibah as an analogy: 'I give you this house on condition that it does not leave my ownership' is void because it contradicts the essence of gift. Similarly, a waqf with a condition that defeats permanence or irrevocability would be void. Reflect on the boundary.
The author ends with 'ta'ammul' (reflect) to invite deeper consideration of subtle cases.

Commentary
This passage discusses whether a fasid (invalid) condition invalidates a waqf. The author notes that the original principle is that donations (tabarru'at) are not invalidated by fasid conditions. Waqf is a donation, so a fasid condition should not invalidate it. Qadikhan explicitly states this. However, an exception arises if the condition negates the very essence of the donation, such as stipulating that ownership does not transfer (like in hibah with a condition that the donor retains ownership). In such a case, the donation is void because it never truly occurred. The author advises reflection (ta'ammul).

Key Terms
شرط فاسد (shart fasid): A condition that is legally invalid (fasid) but does not necessarily void the contract except in certain cases.
تبَرُّع (tabarru'): A gratuitous act or donation, such as hibah, waqf, or sadaqah.
