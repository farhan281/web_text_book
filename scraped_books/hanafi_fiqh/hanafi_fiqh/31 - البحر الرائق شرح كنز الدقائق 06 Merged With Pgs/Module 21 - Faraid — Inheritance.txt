Death of the Muheel: Voiding the Hawalah in the Hanafi School
Dispute over death's effect on hawalah validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that the death of the muheel voids the hawalah before payment and evaluate Zufar's opposing view.

Core Idea
A hawalah is a temporary concession; the muheel's death ends the need, so it reverts.

In this lesson, we examine a debated issue in Hanafi fiqh: what happens to a debt transfer (hawalah) when the original creditor (muheel) dies before the new creditor (muhal) receives payment? The majority Hanafi position states that the hawalah is voided, returning the claim to the muheel's estate, where the muhal becomes an ordinary creditor. However, Zufar disagreed, arguing that the transfer remains valid. We will explore the proofs, the rationale, and the practical consequences of this difference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the relied-upon ruling: the hawalah is voided by the muheel's death before payment. The asset or debt becomes part of the muheel's estate, distributed among all creditors proportionally.
This is the mufta bihi position, supported by al-Hidayah and al-Hawi al-Zahidi.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Note that Zufar (a Hanafi scholar) held that the hawalah remains valid after death, meaning the muhal is entitled to the specific debt or asset from the muhal 'alayh even after the muheel's death.
Zufar's view is not the relied-upon position but represents a valid ijtihad within the Hanafi school.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Although the text does not detail Zufar's evidence, logically he likely argued that the hawalah is a binding contract once concluded, and death does not inherently invalidate it.
The absence of explicit proof suggests Zufar's argument may be based on general contract validity.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds by emphasizing the nature of hawalah as a concession (rukhsah) for necessity. Transferring a debt from someone not liable is normally invalid; it was allowed only due to need. Death removes the presumed need, so the concession lapses.
This reasoning is explicitly stated in al-Hawi al-Zahidi: 'it was allowed for necessity, and by death it falls.'
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under the Hanafi position, the muhal shares equally with other creditors; under Zufar's view, the muhal would have priority to the specific asset. This affects estate distribution and creditor rights.
This difference is critical in bankruptcy and inheritance scenarios.

Commentary
This long passage addresses a critical issue: what happens to a hawalah (debt transfer) if the muheel (the original creditor who makes the transfer) dies before the muhal (the new creditor) receives payment? The author explains that in a restricted hawalah (muqayyadah), the debt or asset transferred is considered part of the muheel's estate and is distributed among all his creditors (including the muhal) in proportion to their shares. The muhal does not own the debt because transferring a debt from someone not liable is invalid; rather, the hawalah creates a debt in the muhal 'alayh's liability while the muheel's debt remains. After death, the muhal 'alayh's debt reverts to the muheel's estate. The author then compares to pledge (rahn) where the pledgee has a right of recovery. He discusses the concept of discharge (bara'ah): one meaning is the nullification of the hawalah. The passage also discusses the case where the hawalah was tied to a deposit (wadi'ah): if the depositor claims the deposit is lost, the hawalah is voided. If the muhal 'alayh pays from his own wealth, he is a volunteer by qiyas but not by istihsan (Hanafi preference). Then the author quotes al-Bazzaziyya which seems to say that regardless of whether the hawalah is restricted or unrestricted, the muhal is like other creditors in the muheel's estate, implying that the death voids the hawalah. This is supported by al-Hawi al-Zahidi, who states that the hawalah is voided by death because it was a transfer of debt from someone not liable, permitted only for need; with death the need ends. Zufar disagreed. The passage continues with details: if the hawalah was restricted to a specific deposit and the muhal gifts it to the muhal 'alayh, the gift is valid because the muhal has the right to own it. Hawalah with a usurped item is not voided by its destruction because an alternative (value) exists. Finally, if the hawalah was tied to a debt that is later extinguished (e.g., by return of a sold item due to defect), it is not voided if the debt fell after the hawalah, but if the debt never existed, it is voided.

Key Terms
البراءة (al-bara'ah): Discharge; here it means the nullification of the hawalah or discharge from the claim.
المقيدة (al-muqayyadah): Restricted hawalah where the transfer is tied to a specific debt or asset of the muhal 'alayh owed to the muheel.
الوديعة (al-wadi'ah): Deposit; property left for safekeeping. If the hawalah is tied to a deposit and the depositor says it is lost, the hawalah is voided.
المحيل (al-muheel): The original creditor who initiates the transfer of debt (hawalah).
المحتال (al-muhal): The new creditor who receives the right to collect the debt.
المحال عليه (al-muhal 'alayh): The debtor who owes the debt that is transferred.

Division of Inheritance Without Evidence: Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Ruling and intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on division of inheritance with absent heirs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on dividing inheritance with absent or minor heirs, and apply the Hanafi principle that a judge's division is a judgment requiring evidence.

Core Idea
The judge's division of inheritance is a judgment (qaḍā'), not mere administration — evidence is required.

When heirs are present but some are absent or minors, can the judge divide the estate based solely on the acknowledgment of the present heirs? Abu Hanifa demands evidence (bayyinah) because he considers the division a binding judgment that affects absent parties. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad allow division on acknowledgment, viewing it as a form of partition. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the decisive conclusion from al-Aṣl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Heirs come to the judge requesting division. Among them is an absent or minor heir. The estate is real estate. The judge must decide based on the present heirs' claim alone.
2
State the positions
Abu Hanifa: do not divide without evidence. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad: divide based on acknowledgment.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Analyse the reasoning (ʿillah)
Abu Hanifa's reason: the judge's division is a judgment (qaḍā'), and a judgment requires a valid claim supported by evidence. The Sahibayn apparently view the division as a non-judicial partition.
The distinction hinges on the legal nature of qismat al-qāḍī.
4
Trace the authority
The author cites Muḥammad's own statement in al-Aṣl: 'the division of the judge is a judgment from him.' This is given as decisive proof that Abu Hanifa's view is correct.
This is a rare case where the Sahibayn's own text supports Abu Hanifa.
5
Determine the muftā bihī
The author declares the statement from al-Aṣl cuts off all doubt, so Abu Hanifa's position is the relied-upon one.
Authority: al-Asl is the core of Zahir al-Riwayah.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about whether a judge's division of inheritance among present heirs, when there are absent or minor heirs, is a judgment that requires evidence. The author cites that many scholars—including the authors of al-Tajnīs, al-Tatimmah, al-Dhakhīrah, al-Muhīt, Imam al-Sarakhsī, and Qāḍīkhān—transmitted that the action of a judge is a judgment. Abū Ḥanīfah's reasoning is that the division of the judge is a judgment (qaḍā’), and a judgment requires a valid claim and evidence; it cannot be based solely on the acknowledgment of the present heirs because that would prejudice the absent or minor. The author concludes that the statement from al-Aṣl decisively settles the matter: since division is a judgment, it must meet the conditions of judgment, including evidence. This illustrates the principle that a judge's action in dividing property is a judgment (ḥukm), not mere administration.

Specifying Siblings and 'Awl in Inheritance Fatwas
Instructions for mufti on answering inheritance questions about siblings and 'awl.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the precise specifications a Hanafi mufti must include when answering about brothers in inheritance, and identify when and how to mention 'awl.

Core Idea
In inheritance fatwa, the mufti must specify full, paternal, or maternal siblings and note any 'awl reduction.

When asked about inheritance shares for brothers, a mufti cannot give a generic answer. The Hanafi school requires precision: specify whether the brothers are full, paternal half, or maternal half. If the total prescribed shares exceed the estate ('awl), the mufti must mention that the eighth share is reduced. This passage from Al-Bahr al-Raiq details the mufti's procedural duties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of brothers
When asked about 'brothers', immediately determine whether they are full brothers (from both parents), paternal half-brothers, or maternal half-brothers. Each category has different inheritance shares.
Full brothers inherit as residuary heirs; paternal half-brothers also as residuary but are excluded by full brothers; maternal half-brothers inherit as Quranic heirs with fixed shares.
2
Check for 'awl
Calculate the total of all prescribed shares. If they exceed the estate (e.g., shares sum to more than 1), apply 'awl proportional reduction. The mufti must state, for example, 'the eighth is 'a'il (deficient)' to indicate the reduction.
In Hanafi school, 'awl is accepted and shares are reduced proportionally.
3
Clarify exclusion of heirs
If any heir is excluded in one scenario but inherits in another (e.g., a son excludes full brothers), the mufti must clarify this conditional exclusion in the answer.
This ensures the fatwa is clear and applicable to the asker's specific family structure.
4
Write the fatwa with proper notation
After deriving the correct distribution, write it down. If the mufti is confident it is correct, he may add a note like 'sahih' (correct) or similar. He may also abbreviate the answer if appropriate.
The fatwa should be concise yet comprehensive.
5
Investigate the asker's situation if unclear
If the mufti does not know the family composition (e.g., whether there are full brothers), he must inquire. If still unclear, he can ask the questioner to rewrite the question more clearly. If that is difficult, he may answer orally instead of in writing.
This protects against issuing an incorrect fatwa based on incomplete information.

العول (al-'awl): Proportional reduction of shares when the total prescribed shares exceed the inheritance fund.
الثمن عائلاً (al-thumnu 'ā'il): The share of one-eighth is reduced due to 'awl.
ابن الأبوين (ibn al-abawayn): Full brother (from both parents).
أخ لأب (akh li-ab): Paternal half-brother (same father, different mother).
أخ لأم (akh li-umm): Maternal half-brother (same mother, different father).
