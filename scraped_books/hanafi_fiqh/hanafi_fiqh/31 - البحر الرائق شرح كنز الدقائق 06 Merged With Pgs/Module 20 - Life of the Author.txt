Types of Hawala: Restricted and Unrestricted, and Rulings on Death of Transferor
Differentiating between restricted and unrestricted hawala and their legal effects, including death of transferor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between restricted and unrestricted hawala and state the ruling on the death of the transferor in a restricted hawala.

Core Idea
Upon the transferor's death, a restricted hawala is voided and the debt is shared among all creditors.

Hawala is a transfer of a debt from one person to another. This lesson explores two types: restricted (muqayyadah) and unrestricted (mutlaqah). A key issue arises when the transferor dies before the transferee collects; the relied-upon Hanafi position is that the hawala is voided, and the debt becomes part of the transferor's estate, distributed among all creditors equally. Understanding this distinction prevents unfair priority claims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of hawala
Read the passage to find the definitions of restricted and unrestricted hawala. Note the key difference: in restricted, the transferor specifies a particular debt or asset; in unrestricted, he does not.
Restricted: agent (wakil). Unrestricted: volunteer (mutabarri').
2
Distinguish the legal effect on the transferor's right
Determine who can claim from the muhaal `alayh after the hawala. In restricted, the transferor cannot claim; in unrestricted, he can until payment is made.
This is due to the attachment of the transferee's right in restricted.
3
Analyse the ruling on death of transferor
Focus on the section discussing death before collection. The relied-upon position (from al-Hawi al-Zahidi) states that the hawala is voided. Understand the reasoning: hawala is an exceptional transfer, permitted only for need; death ends the need.
Zufar disagrees, holding the hawala remains valid.
4
Apply the ruling to a scenario
Take a concrete example: A transfers a debt to B via restricted hawala, then A dies before B collects. State that the debt is part of A's estate and B is an ordinary creditor.
Contrast with unrestricted: if the hawala is unrestricted, the death does not affect the transferor's right because he remains liable.

Restricted is that he restricts it to a debt owed to him, or a deposit, or an asset in his hand as a deposit, or usurped property, etc. Unrestricted is that he sends it without restriction and does not restrict it to any of these; whether he has a debt against the muhaal `alayh or has an asset with him or not, such that he accepts it as a volunteer. Commentary
This passage explains the two types of hawala: restricted and unrestricted, and their different rulings. In a restricted hawala, the transferor restricts the transaction to a specific debt or asset owed to him, and the transferee acts as an agent for payment. In an unrestricted hawala, the transferor does not restrict, and the transferee is a volunteer. The key distinction is that in a restricted hawala, the transferor's right is transferred to the transferee, and the transferor cannot later claim from the muhaal `alayh. In an unrestricted hawala, the transferor retains the right to claim. A major discussion concerns the effect of the transferor's death before the transferee collects. According to the relied-upon position, the hawala is voided, and the debt becomes part of the transferor's estate, divided among all his creditors equally. This is because the hawala was a transfer of debt from someone not liable, which is not inherently permissible but was allowed only for need. Upon death, the need ends. Zufar disagrees, holding that the hawala remains valid. The passage also discusses the case where the deposit perishes: the hawala is voided because the depositor is considered an agent. The text includes various narrations and opinions, illustrating the internal Hanafi differences.

Key Terms
مطلقة (mutlaqah): Unrestricted hawala: a transfer without specifying a particular debt or asset; the transferee acts as a volunteer.
مقيدة (muqayyadah): Restricted hawala: a transfer restricted to a specific debt or asset; the transferee acts as an agent for payment.
متبرع (mutabarri'): A volunteer who pays without having a debt; here, the transferee in unrestricted hawala who pays voluntarily.
غرماء (ghuramaa'): Creditors of the transferor's estate.
