Release of the Insolvent Debtor: General Rule and Exceptions
Commentary on the ruling to release a debtor with no apparent assets

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on releasing an insolvent debtor and identify the three exceptions where a surety is required.

Core Idea
The judge must release an insolvent debtor without surety, except to protect orphan property, an absent creditor, or waqf property.

This lesson examines the judge's duty when a debtor imprisoned for debt is proven insolvent. A Qur'anic command mandates release, but three exceptional cases require a surety to safeguard vulnerable parties. Understanding this rule and its exceptions prevents unjust detention while protecting rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
The passage states that if the debtor's insolvency is established, the judge must release him. The proof is the Qur'anic verse (2:280) granting respite until ease, making continued imprisonment oppression.
2
Note the general rule: no surety required
The apparent meaning of the matn is that release is without surety. The commentary emphasizes this is the default.
3
Learn the three exceptions requiring a surety
Three cases are exempted: (1) when the debt involves orphan's property (to protect minors), (2) when the creditor is absent, (3) when the debt is owed to a waqf. In these, the judge must take a surety before releasing.
These exceptions are based on the need to secure the absent or vulnerable creditor's right.
4
Understand the procedure for release
The creditor may release voluntarily, but if the debtor wants a formal ruling to prevent re-imprisonment, the judge must issue it. Release without formal evidence may allow the creditor to re-imprison if wealth later appears.
The debtor's consent is relevant when the creditor releases before the judge's ruling.
5
Apply the rule of re-imprisonment
Once released for insolvency, the debtor cannot be re-imprisoned for the same or another debt until the creditor proves he has become wealthy (e.g., by testimony).
This protects the debtor from repeated detention.

Commentary

If the creditor releases him without formal evidence of bankruptcy, he may re-imprison him later. The phrase 'release him' also implies that once released for bankruptcy, he cannot be re-imprisoned for the same or another debt until the creditor proves he has become wealthy.

Judge's Ruling on Debtors with Hidden Assets or Wealthy Imprisoned Debtors
Expansion of rulings on debtor imprisonment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a judge may not imprison a debtor and identify the rulings on re-imprisonment after release without evidence and acceptance of insolvency evidence before imprisonment.

Core Idea
A judge does not imprison a debtor known to have hidden assets or a wealthy imprisoned debtor – inability to pay must be genuine.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq clarifies the qadi's discretion in imprisoning debtors. It addresses three key scenarios: hidden assets, claims on imprisoned wealthy persons, and the effect of releasing a debtor without formal evidence. Understanding these nuances prevents unjust imprisonment and ensures that imprisonment is reserved for those who can pay but refuse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling on hidden assets and wealthy imprisoned debtor
Read the initial statement: 'If he knows that he has hidden wealth or a detained wealthy debtor, the judge does not imprison him.' Recognize that this is a prohibition on imprisonment based on the debtor's potential ability to pay.
The debtor must actually be insolvent after considering these resources.
2
Understand the condition for not imprisoning
The debtor is considered ma'sir (insolvent) only if he has no accessible wealth other than these hidden assets or the claim on a wealthy imprisoned person. The judge must know of these resources to withhold imprisonment.
Knowledge (علم) is key – the judge must be certain or have strong indication.
3
Analyze the ruling on re-imprisonment after release without evidence
The passage addresses a situation where a judge releases a debtor without formal evidence (bila bayyinah). If litigation had occurred, the creditor may re-imprison him; otherwise, the judge may not re-imprison because the release was based on his own ijtihad.
This protects the debtor from arbitrary re-arrest.
4
Distinguish between litigation and non-litigation scenarios
If there was a court session (khusumat), the judge's release is provisional and can be reversed. If no litigation took place, the release is final and binding on the judge.
The source cites al-Bazzaziyya for support.
5
Apply the rule on accepting evidence of insolvency before imprisonment
If the debtor appears poor (poverty is apparent), the judge should act urgently and accept evidence of insolvency to release him. If his situation is doubtful, evidence is still accepted but may take time.
This prevents unnecessary imprisonment of the genuinely poor.

Dispute on Preventing Creditor from Following Debtor
Intra-Hanafi dispute on mulazamah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on mulazamah and distinguish the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, citing the mufta bihi and its practical implications.

Core Idea
The judge does not prevent the creditor from following the debtor, according to Abu Hanifa.

In this passage, the author addresses an intra-Hanafi dispute: should the judge prevent a creditor from physically following an insolvent debtor to pressure payment? The matn states the judge does not interpose, which is Abu Hanifa's view. The Sahibayn argue for prevention based on the divine command to grant respite. This lesson unpacks the proofs, the practical consequences, and the relied-upon position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling in the matn: the judge does not interpose between the debtor and his creditors. This is Abu Hanifa's view. Note that the two disciples (Sahibayn) oppose it.
The matn phrase 'wa lam yahul baynahu wa bayna ghuramaa'ih' indicates non-prevention.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that the judge should prevent the creditor from following the debtor. Their proof: the debtor is given a divine respite (nazirah) which is stronger than a human deferment, and following contradicts that respite.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
The Sahibayn argue that since Allah commands respite (Quran 2:280), the creditor cannot pressure the debtor. The divine command supersedes any private right to pursue.
The text states 'li-kawnihi munazziran bi-anzar Allah ta'ala'.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa responds that the respite is conditional on inability to pay. Following is a means to ascertain ability and prevent the debtor from hiding assets. The debt is due (hal), unlike deferred debt, so pursuing is not a violation.
Imam uses a qiyas distinction: the analogy with deferred debt fails because here the debt is due.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
Practical difference: In Imam's view, the creditor may continue to follow the debtor within reasonable limits; in Sahibayn's view, the judge prevents such pursuit entirely. The relied-upon position is Imam's view, as stated in Anfa' al-Wasa'il and al-Muhit.
Tarjih authority: Al-Marghinani and later texts affirm Imam's view as the correct one.

Commentary

Testimony of Insolvency and Imprisonment of the Destitute
Explaining conditions for accepting testimony of insolvency and judge's ruling on imprisonment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on testimony of insolvency, explain why the fuller wording is preferred, and apply the ruling that a destitute debtor with no surety is not imprisoned.

Core Idea
Testimony of insolvency is positive, not negative; a destitute debtor is not imprisoned.

This passage from Bahr al-Raiq examines two views within the Hanafi school on how witnesses should testify to a debtor's insolvency. While al-Khassaf accepts a simple statement, al-Saffar requires a detailed investigation and explicit denial of wealth. We also learn the procedural rule: a truly destitute debtor with no surety is not imprisoned.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions on testimony
First, note al-Khassaf's view that witnesses say 'he is poor, we do not know of any wealth or asset that removes him from poverty.' Then, note al-Saffar's requirement that witnesses say 'he is bankrupt, destitute, we do not know of any wealth except his clothing and night garment, and we investigated secretly and publicly.'
Both are Hanafi scholars; the difference is about the level of detail.
2
Understand the concern about testimony of negation
Al-Saffar's added detail avoids the prohibition of testifying to a negative fact (shahadat 'ala al-nafy). The author of Anfa' al-Wasa'il clarifies that insolvency after wealth is a new event (hadith), so testimony to it is positive.
Testimony of pure negation is generally impermissible.
3
Trace the resolution
Anfa' al-Wasa'il endorses al-Saffar's wording as not being negation, and al-Sighnaqi confirms. The fuller wording is thus preferred (tarjih) to avoid any appearance of testifying to a negative.
The preferred view is the more detailed one.
4
Apply the ruling on imprisonment
From al-Nawazil: if a debtor is utterly destitute (no wealth, no one to guarantee him), the judge does not imprison him. The creditor may choose to follow him or let him go.
This is a separate rule, not in dispute.

Core Idea
Mulazamah is the creditor's right to follow the debtor to prevent concealment; Abu Hanifa allows it, Sahibayn restrict it.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq presents the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the permissibility of the judge preventing mulazamah. Acceptance of Insolvency Evidence Before Imprisonment
Explains the dispute on insolvency evidence before imprisonment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi majority ruling on accepting insolvency evidence before imprisonment, explain the minority view from Muhammad, and apply the judge's discretion based on the debtor's character.

Core Idea
Evidence of insolvency is a negative that requires support; imprisonment provides that support.

This lesson examines a procedural dispute within the Hanafi school: when can a debtor present evidence of his insolvency? The majority holds it is only accepted after imprisonment, but a minority view from Imam Muhammad allows it earlier. We will also see how the judge's assessment of the debtor's character plays a role.

Guided Path
1
Identify the majority rule
The majority of Hanafi scholars (عامة المشايخ) rule that evidence of insolvency is rejected before imprisonment because it is evidence of a negation (nafi). Imprisonment serves as a supporting factor, after which such evidence is accepted as a precaution (ihtiyat), not as an obligation.
This is the position in al-Hidayah and al-Nihayah.
2
Recognize the minority view
Imam Muhammad is reported to accept evidence of insolvency even before imprisonment. Later scholars like Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn al-Fadl and Nasir ibn Yahya gave fatwa according to this view.
This is a legitimate intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Consider the judge's discretion
Al-Khaniyyah adds that the judge may consider the debtor's character. If the debtor is rude (waqih), his evidence is not accepted before imprisonment; if gentle (layyin), it is accepted.
This nuance is analogous to al-Khassaf's ruling on determining imprisonment duration based on the debtor's generosity.
4
Apply the ruling
Under the majority view, the debtor remains imprisoned until he can provide evidence of insolvency after imprisonment. Under Muhammad's view, he may be released earlier if evidence is accepted. The judge's discretion further refines the application.
The majority view is correct (asahh) and relied upon (mufta bihi).

Wealth is a temporal attribute (arid) that can be observed and proven through witnesses. Insolvency is a negation (nafi), and evidence is for proving existence, so the positive proof (wealth) is given priority.
The analogy is to evidence of release (ibra') taking precedence over evidence of lending (iqrad).
3

Just as evidence of release (a positive event) is preferred over evidence of lending (a positive but older event) in case of conflict, evidence of wealth is preferred over insolvency. This analogy is mentioned in al-Bazzaziyya.
The common illa (effective cause) is that both involve a positive proof over a negative.

Fath al-Qadir states an exception: if the debtor claims he became insolvent after being wealthy and brings evidence of that new event, then that evidence should be given priority because it contains knowledge of a new event (ilm bi-hadith). However, the commentator (al-Bahr al-Ra'iq) questions this because wealth could also arise anew, and the rule of priority is general.
The exception is not universally accepted; the majority holds that wealth evidence always prevails.

Intra-Hanafi dispute on insolvency evidence and qiyas on solvency preference.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Negative evidence needs corroboration; solvency is a current condition so its evidence beats insolvency evidence.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the Hanafi rules for evidence of insolvency and solvency in debt cases. Evidence of Solvency: Accepted Without Specifying Amount
Explains the ruling on evidence of solvency and addresses an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that evidence of solvency is accepted without specifying the amount of property, and distinguish it from evidence of ownership of a specific share.

Core Idea
Evidence of solvency proves ability to pay, not specific ownership; thus it need not quantify property.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains a key ruling of Imam Muhammad regarding evidence of solvency. When a debtor claims insolvency, the creditor may produce evidence that the debtor has means to pay. The text resolves an objection: why is this evidence accepted without detailing the amount? The answer lies in the nature of the evidence – it proves the debtor's capacity to pay, which implicitly includes ownership of at least the debt amount. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and contrasts it with the case of a preemption share.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What is the ruling on evidence of solvency from the matn? The text states that evidence of solvency takes precedence after a claim of insolvency and is accepted even without specifying the amount of property.
The ruling is that the evidence is accepted without quantification.
2
Locate the proof
What proof or reasoning is given in the commentary? The commentators cite istihsan (juristic preference) and the purpose of the evidence: to continue imprisonment, not to prove specific ownership.
The proof is based on the objective of the legal procedure.
3
Analyze the objection
How does the objection from the preemption case arise? In al-'Inayah, it is asked: if evidence of solvency is accepted without specifying the amount, why is evidence of a share in property not accepted without specifying the share?
The objection points to an apparent inconsistency.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
How does the text distinguish the two cases? The witness to solvency testifies to the ability to pay, which necessarily requires ownership of the debt amount, so the amount is implied. In the preemption case, the witness testifies to a share without any known quantity, so it is not accepted.
The distinction is between testimony about ability (which includes quantum) and testimony about an unspecified part.
5
Apply the ruling
When is evidence of solvency accepted? It is accepted when witnesses do not specify the exact property or amount. If they specify an amount, it becomes a claim of specific ownership and is not accepted.
This principle is used in Hanafi courts for enforcing debts.

Imprisonment for Wife's Maintenance and Conflict of Evidences
Explains the ruling on imprisonment for wife's maintenance and the conflict between evidence of solvency and insolvency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between current and past maintenance for the purpose of imprisonment, and apply the rule that evidence of insolvency after solvency is accepted as proof of a new event.

Core Idea
A husband is imprisoned only for current maintenance, not past, and evidence of new insolvency overrides prior solvency evidence.

This passage covers two interconnected issues. First, the rule that a husband is imprisoned for his wife's maintenance only if it is current; past due maintenance does not warrant imprisonment because it is not a substitute for property. Second, a discussion on the conflict between evidence of solvency and evidence of insolvency: if a debtor was proven wealthy but later claims insolvency with evidence, that evidence is accepted because it proves a new event (loss of wealth). Al-Ramli's comments refine this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on imprisonment for maintenance
What does the matn say? 'And a man is imprisoned for his wife's maintenance.' The commentary restricts this to current maintenance, not past.
Past maintenance is a personal duty, not a property debt.
2
Understand the distinction between current and past maintenance
Why is there no imprisonment for past maintenance? Because it is not a substitute for property (badal mal) nor is it binding by contract. It is a personal obligation that lapses with time.
This distinguishes nafaqa from a loan.
3
Apply the condition of the wife proving solvency
If the husband claims poverty, he is not imprisoned unless the wife proves his solvency. The burden shifts to her.
This is to prevent false claims of poverty.
4
Analyze the conflict of evidence discussion
When evidence of solvency and evidence of insolvency conflict, which takes precedence? Generally, solvency evidence is preferred because it includes additional knowledge (previous solvency). However, if the debtor produces evidence of a new insolvency after a period of solvency, that evidence is accepted because it proves a new event.
The exception ensures that true changes in circumstance are recognized.
5
Apply the conflict rule
In practice, if a debtor was proven wealthy and later claims poverty with new evidence, the judge should accept the new evidence and release the debtor from imprisonment.
This is supported by al-Ramli's position.

No Imprisonment of a Parent for a Child's Debt and the Guarantor Exception
Explains ruling with qiyas and fatwa on guarantor exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a parent is not imprisoned for a child's debt, explain the qiyas with qisas, and apply the exception when the parent is a guarantor.

Core Idea
A parent is never imprisoned for a child's debt; but if a guarantor, imprisonment is allowed.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq explains the matn statement 'لا في دين ولده' – a parent is not imprisoned for a child's debt. The reasoning is based on a qiyas to the rule that a parent is not subject to qisas for killing his child, because the parent does not deserve punishment due to the child. The commentary extends this to all ascendants and clarifies that even a wealthy parent is not imprisoned, though the judge may sell the parent's property to satisfy the debt. The passage ends with a fatwa from al-Ramli confirming that if the father is a guarantor for his son's debt, he can be imprisoned because the debt is now his own obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and its Scope
The matn states that a parent (أصل) is not imprisoned for the debt of a child (فرع). This includes fathers, mothers, grandfathers, and grandmothers, except for maintenance obligations as noted in al-Muhit.
The scope covers all ascendants.
2
Understand the Proof (Qiyas)
The ruling is proven by qiyas to the rule that a parent is not killed for killing his child (no qisas). The common illa is that the parent does not deserve punishment due to the child. This qiyas is jaliy (clear analogy).
The hadith 'A parent is not killed for his child' is the basis.
3
Apply the Ruling to a Wealthy Parent
Even if the parent is wealthy and refuses to pay the child's debt, the parent is not imprisoned. Instead, the judge sells the parent's property to discharge the debt, as with any debtor who refuses.
This distinguishes the parent from other debtors who may be imprisoned for non-payment.
4
Recognize the Exception for Guarantors
The ruling does not apply when the parent is a guarantor (كفيل) for the child's debt. In that case, the debt is legally the parent's own obligation, so the parent can be imprisoned just as any guarantor can.
Al-Ramli's fatwa confirms this, rejecting a contrary opinion attributed to al-Qahistani.
5
List the Seven Categories
The passage enumerates seven types of people who are not imprisoned for debts. The first is the parent for the child's debt. Others include the master for the authorized slave's debt, the slave for the master's debt, etc. These illustrate the general principle.
The full list is given in al-Fawaid al-Fiqhiyyah.

The Six Exceptions: When Imprisonment for Debt is Permitted
Listing six exceptional cases of permissible imprisonment for debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the six exceptional cases where a debtor may be imprisoned in Hanafi law, and apply the conditions that make imprisonment permissible or prohibited.

Core Idea
A minor is imprisoned only for discipline with guardian's refusal; the 'aqila only if they have no stipend.

The general rule in Hanafi fiqh is that a debtor should not be imprisoned merely for being in debt. However, when a debtor deliberately obstructs the creditor's right or engages in transgression, the judge may permit imprisonment in certain exceptional cases. This passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq enumerates six such exceptions, with special focus on two: the minor (sabi) who has a consumption debt, and the 'aqila (tribal guarantors) who owe blood money. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for a qadi or mufti when adjudicating debt cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
Recognize that the default ruling in Hanafi fiqh is that imprisonment for debt is not permitted. The judge may not imprison a debtor simply for inability to pay; it is only allowed in specific exceptional circumstances listed in the passage.
This rule is based on the principle of not punishing someone for inability.
2
Analyze the Sixth Exception: The Minor's Consumption Debt
The passage details the ruling for a minor with a consumption debt. Note the two subcases: (a) if the minor has no father or guardian, the judge may sell his property to pay the debt, but cannot imprison him; (b) if the minor has a father or guardian who refuses to pay from the minor's property, the guardian may be imprisoned. The minor himself is imprisoned only for discipline (ta'dib), not punishment, and only if he deliberately commits a transgression.
The minor's imprisonment is disciplinary, not punitive; accidental acts do not warrant it.
3
Analyze the Seventh Exception: The 'Aqila's Stipend
The 'aqila (group responsible for blood money) are treated differently based on whether they receive a government stipend (ata'). If they have a stipend, they cannot be imprisoned for non-payment of diyah or arsh, because the stipend is designated for such obligations. If they have no stipend and are able to pay but refuse, they may be imprisoned.
The presence of a stipend signals that the state has already provided for the obligation, so imprisonment is unnecessary.
4
Note the Two Additional Exceptions
The passage adds two previously mentioned cases: (a) if the judge knows the debtor has absent property (mal gha'ib), he does not imprison him but arranges sale of the property; (b) if the debtor is detained but is financially able, he is not imprisoned. These two, together with the earlier detailed exceptions, total six (or nine, if counting subcases).
These exceptions protect the debtor from double hardship.
5
Synthesize the Underlying Principle
The common thread across all exceptions is that imprisonment is a last resort, used only to enforce rights or to discipline deliberate wrongdoing. The judge must assess the circumstances and choose the least harmful method of enforcement. The key is balancing the creditor's right with the debtor's dignity.
The fatwa (operative ruling) is as stated in the passage, with the author's endorsement.

Imprisonment for Refusal to Pay Maintenance: Conditions and Property Sale Dispute
Explaining the exception of maintenance non-payment as a cause for imprisonment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions that must be met before a debtor can be imprisoned for refusing to pay maintenance, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether the judge may sell the debtor's property without his consent.

Core Idea
Imprisonment for non-payment of maintenance requires a court order and subsequent refusal; the judge may sell the debtor's property per the Sahibayn's view, which is the operative fatwa.

This passage from al-Bahr al-Raiq expands on a key exception to the general rule that debtors are not imprisoned for non-payment. The exception applies to maintenance (nafaqah) for close relatives and spouses, where the law seeks to prevent imminent harm. The author explains the conditions for imprisonment and then addresses an intra-Hanafi disagreement: can the judge sell the debtor's movable and immovable property to satisfy the debt? Understanding this dispute is crucial for applying the ruling in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its general ruling
The act is imprisonment of a debtor who refuses to pay maintenance. The ruling is that it is permissible, even obligatory, when the conditions are met to prevent harm to the dependent.
2
List the conditions for imprisonment
From the text: the judge must first order maintenance (fard al-nafaqah), the debtor must refuse to pay after a day has passed, and the dependent must be entitled to maintenance (ancestors, descendants, spouse). Also, the intention of refusal to cause harm is considered.
The condition of a day's delay is derived from Fath al-Qadir.
3
Understand the rationale (talil)
The rationale is that refusal to spend on a dependent is a means of destroying them, so imprisonment is to ward off that harm. It is analogous to killing in self-defense.
The author uses qiyas: just as one may kill in self-defense, one may imprison to prevent harm.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on selling property
Abu Hanifa holds that the debtor's property is not sold without his consent; the Sahibayn hold that it is sold. The fatwa is on the Sahibayn's view.
The text states: 'وعندهما يباع وعليه الفتوى'.
5
Determine the practical consequence of the dispute
Under Abu Hanifa, if the debtor refuses to sell, he is imprisoned to compel him. Under the Sahibayn, the judge sells directly, so imprisonment may be avoided. The mufta bihi is direct sale.
This affects whether imprisonment is necessary or avoidable.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the exception in the matn: a debtor is imprisoned if he refuses to pay maintenance (nafaqah) to his dependents. The author explains that this includes ancestors (parents, grandparents) and descendants (children, grandchildren) and spouses. The rationale is that refusal to spend is tantamount to causing harm or destruction to the dependent, so imprisonment is permissible to prevent that harm. The restriction in al-Siraj al-Wahhaj that the child must be small (saghir) and poor (faqir) is discussed; it is argued that this is not a restrictive condition but rather an example, so if an adult descendant is poor and disabled, the same ruling applies. The definition of 'refusal' (imtina') is clarified: it is not merely non-payment after the court's order; rather, the judge must first order payment, and if the person does not comply by the next day, then he may be imprisoned. The text also discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Hanifa, the debtor's assets (movable and immovable) are not sold without his consent to satisfy the debt; his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that they are sold. The fatwa is on the view of the companions (yubaa' wa 'alayhi al-fatwa). The passage ends with a discussion on a problematic fatwa from Qari' al-Hidayah regarding the husband who is not a man of means but the judge knows he harms by not spending; the commentator notes that the judge can order maintenance directly, so there is no need to wait for the husband to set an amount.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance, including food, clothing, and shelter, obligatory for certain relatives and spouses.
امتناع (imtina'): Refusal to comply with a court order for payment after it has been issued and a day has passed.
فرض النفقة (fard al-nafaqah): The court's determination and order of a specific amount of maintenance.
زوجة (zawjah): Wife entitled to maintenance from her husband.
