Qada for Maintenance Paid in Installments
Ruling on whether fixing maintenance by month constitutes a single judgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a judgment fixing periodic maintenance is considered a single judgment in Hanafi fiqh and apply the rule to scenarios involving ibra' (discharge).

Core Idea
A judgment for periodic maintenance is one judgment covering all installments as long as the cause (marriage) continues.

When a judge fixes maintenance for a wife at a daily or monthly rate, does that create a separate judgment for each day or month, or a single overarching judgment? The Hanafi school answers that it is one judgment for all periods as long as the marriage bond remains and no new obstacle arises. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical implications, especially when the wife discharges the husband from future maintenance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core question
Is a judgment for periodic maintenance one or many?
The passage explicitly raises this question: 'هل يكون قضاء بواحد أو بالكل؟'
2
State the Hanafi answer
It is one judgment as long as the cause (marriage) continues and no preventer arises.
Author says: 'هو قضاء بالجميع ما دامت في عصمته ولم يمنع مانع'
3
Examine the evidence from al-Khizana
Analyze the example: if wife discharges from all future maintenance, only the first month is cleared.
This shows the judgment is a single entity; discharge of future only affects the nearest installment.
4
Analyze the implication
Understand that ibra' of future maintenance does not cancel the entire obligation, only the immediate next period.
The passage distinguishes between discharge of past and discharge of future.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Given a fixed monthly maintenance and a discharge from all future, what is the effect?
Practice using the rule in a different context.

قضاء بجميعها فإذا فرض لها نفقة كل يوم أو كل شهر هل يكون قضاء بواحد أو بالكل؟ قلت : هو قضاء بالجميع ما دامت في عصمته ولم يمنع مانع بدليل ما في الخزانة : فرض كل شهر عشرة دراهم فأبرأت من نفقتها أبداً برئ من نفقة الشهر الأول، فإذا مضى أشهر فأبرأته من نفقة ما مضى وما يستقبل برئ مما مضى ومن شهر مما يستقبل؛ وتمامه فيها.
Judgment for all of them. So if he fixes for her maintenance for each day or each month, is it one judgment or for all? I said: It is judgment for all as long as she is in his bond and no preventer prevents. Evidence is what is in al-Khizana: He fixed each month ten dirhams, then she discharged him from her maintenance forever; he is free from the maintenance of the first month. Then if months pass and she discharges him from the past and future maintenance, he is free from what passed and from one month of the future; and its completion is in it.

Commentary
This is a ruling passage from al-Bahr al-Ra'iq on the nature of a judgment fixing periodic maintenance. The question is whether a judge's order for daily or monthly maintenance is a single judgment covering all installments or a separate judgment for each. The author answers that it is a single judgment covering all periods as long as the marriage continues and no obstacle arises. The evidence from al-Khizana: if a judge fixes monthly maintenance at ten dirhams, and the wife later discharges the husband from all future maintenance, the husband is free only of the first month's obligation. But if she discharges him for past and future, he is free of past and one future month. This shows the original judgment was one for all.

Key Terms
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Maintenance owed by husband to wife, including food, clothing, and shelter.
الإبراء (al-ibra'): Discharge or acquittal of a debt or obligation by the creditor.

Imprisonment for Refusing Child Maintenance
Explains the exception where a parent is imprisoned for refusing to provide maintenance for a child.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on imprisoning a parent for refusing child maintenance, explain the qiyas-based proof, and distinguish the operative condition regarding the child's age and poverty.

Core Idea
Refusal to maintain a child is akin to destroying them, so imprisonment is justified to prevent harm.

In Hanafi law, a parent is generally not imprisoned for a child's debt or failure to provide maintenance. However, there is an important exception: when a parent refuses to provide nafaqah (daily maintenance) for a child, the judge may imprison the parent as a coercive measure. This lesson examines the reasoning behind this exception, the proof from qiyas, and a key intra-madhhab difference about whether the child must be a minor and poor or can also include an adult old poor child.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The act is the parent's refusal to pay nafaqah for a child. The ruling: the parent is imprisoned (by judge) to compel payment. This is an exception to the general rule that parents are not imprisoned for maintenance debts.
The imprisonment is a coercive measure, not a penal punishment.
2
Locate the Dalil
The proof is qiyas: refusal to maintain is like an attempt to destroy the child. The text analogizes to the permission to kill in self-defense (daf'). Just as killing is allowed to repel a direct attack, imprisonment is allowed to repel starvation.
The main text says: 'Do you not see that he has the right to kill him in defense of himself?'
3
Trace the Wajh al-Dalalah
The analogy works because maintenance is a daily need (hajat al-waqt); refusal to provide it aims to destroy the child (by starvation). Imprisonment removes the means of refusal and compels payment, thereby repelling harm. The effective cause (illa) is the immediate threat to life.
The qiyas is based on the principle of preventing harm (daf' al-darar).
4
Note the Intra-Madhhab Ikhtilaf
Al-Siraj al-Wahhaj restricts the exception to a child who is a minor and poor. Al-Minh suggests this is not a restrictive condition; it can also apply to an adult old poor child who cannot earn.
The mufta bihi position is that both are included, but the majority follow the restriction to minor poor.
5
Derive the Practical Consequence (Benefit)
If the parent has property, the judge must order its sale before resorting to imprisonment. Imprisonment is only for when the parent has no property or refuses to sell.
This is from Ibn Wahab, mentioned as a 'good benefit'.

His statement: (except if he refrains from spending on him, then he is imprisoned, because it is for the need of the time and he aims to destroy him by refusal, so he is imprisoned to repel destruction from him. Do you not see that he has the right to kill him in defense of himself? And likewise is the ruling of grandparents, even if they are high, because in refraining from spending is an effort to destroy them. And he restricted in al-Siraj al-Wahhaj the child to minor and poor.) He said in al-Minh: And apparently it is not a restrictive condition excluding the adult old poor. And from this it is taken that the judge nor his deputy may [sell] his land nor his property, because if he had it, he would have ordered sale before imprisonment. Ibn Wahab said: and it is a good benefit.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling-with-proof from the sharh. It addresses an exception to the general rule that a parent is not imprisoned for a child's debt. When the parent refuses to pay nafaqah (maintenance) for a minor child, the parent is imprisoned. The reasoning is that maintenance is a daily need, and refusal to provide it is akin to intending the child's destruction, so imprisonment is used to prevent harm. The author draws an analogy: just as a person can kill in self-defense, a parent's refusal to maintain a child is like attacking the child's life, so imprisonment is justified. This ruling extends to all ascending relatives (grandparents). The author notes a qualification in al-Siraj al-Wahhaj that the child must be a minor and poor. However, al-Minh suggests this is not exclusive; it might also apply to an adult old poor child. The passage concludes with a benefit: if the parent has property, the judge should order its sale before imprisonment, so imprisonment is only when the parent has no property.

Key Terms
نفقة (nafaqah): Maintenance (food, clothing, shelter) for a dependent.
حاجة الوقت (hajat al-waqt): An immediate need that cannot be delayed.

Key Terms from Source Passage

Key Takeaway
A parent may be imprisoned for refusing to pay maintenance for a child because the refusal is akin to destroying the child, and imprisonment is a necessary coercive measure to protect the child's life.

Imprisonment for Refusal to Pay Maintenance: Conditions and Qiyas
Explains when a husband is imprisoned for refusing maintenance after judge's order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a husband is imprisoned for refusing to pay maintenance and explain the qiyas argument from the division of nights.

Core Idea
Imprisonment for non-payment requires a prior judicial order and a clear refusal after obligation, not mere delay.

This lesson from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq explains the precise circumstances that justify imprisoning a husband who refuses to provide maintenance. The text clarifies that the judge must first fix the amount and give a day's respite; only then can refusal lead to imprisonment. The author also critiques an alternative view and draws an analogy with the division of nights among co-wives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its conditions
The general ruling is that he who refuses maintenance for ascendants, descendants, or wife is imprisoned. But conditions apply: the judge must fix the maintenance amount and a day must pass. The refusal is established on the second day.
The passage from Fath al-Qadir specifies the second day from fixation.
2
Locate the proof
The text cites Fath al-Qadir and draws an analogy with the chapter on division of nights among co-wives.
The analogy is a qiyas jaliy.
3
Analyze the qiyas argument
The asl is the husband's refusal to divide nights fairly after a judicial order. The far is refusal to pay maintenance after judicial fixation. The illa is refusal after a judicial order causing harm to the wife.
The same pattern of order, then punishment after refusal.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi critique
The author finds problematic Qari al-Hidaya's view that imprisonment can be for refusal to fix maintenance. The author argues that the judge fixes it anyway, so imprisonment should be for refusal to pay after fixation.
This critique sharpens the understanding of the correct trigger.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that imprisonment is for refusal to pay after judicial fixation, not for refusal to fix. This is the view of the majority of Hanafi authorities.
Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq resolves the conflict.

Source Text

وَفِيهِ تَأَمُّلٌ لَا يَخْفَىٰ وَالحَاصِلُ أَنَّهُ إِذَا امْتَنَعَ مِنَ الإِنْفَاقِ عَلَى أَصْلِهِ وَإِنْ عَلَا وَفَرْعِهِ وَإِنْ سَفَلَ وَعَلَى زَوْجَتِهِ يُحْبَسُ. وَفِي فَتْحِ القَدِيرِ: وَيَتَحَقَّقُ الامْتِنَاعُ بِأَنْ تُقَدِّمَهُ فِي اليَوْمِ الثَّانِي مِنْ يَوْمِ فَرْضِ النَّفَقَةِ، وَإِنْ كَانَ مِقْدَارُ النَّفَقَةِ قَلِيلًا كَالدَّانِقِ إِذَا رَأَى القَاضِي ذَلِكَ، فَأَمَّا بِمُجَرَّدِ فَرْضِهَا لَوْ طَلَبَتْ حَبْسَهُ لَمْ يَحْبِسْهُ لِأَنَّ العُقُوبَةَ تَسْتَحِقُّ بِالظُّلْمِ وَهُوَ بِالمَنْعِ بَعْدَ الوُجُوبِ وَلَمْ يَتَحَقَّقْ. فَهَذَا يَقْتَضِي أَنَّهُ إِذَا لَمْ يَفْرِضْ لَهَا وَلَمْ يُنْفِقِ الزَّوْجُ عَلَيْهَا فِي يَوْمٍ يَنْبَغِي إِذَا قَدَّمَتْهُ فِي اليَوْمِ الثَّانِي أَنْ يَأْمُرَهُ بِالإِنْفَاقِ فَإِنْ رَجَعَ فَلَمْ يُنْفِقْ أَوْجَعَهُ عُقُوبَةً، وَإِنْ كَانَتِ النَّفَقَةُ سَقَطَتْ بَعْدَ الوُجُوبِ فَإِنَّهُ ظَالِمٌ لَهَا وَهُوَ قِيَاسُ مَا أَسْلَفْنَاهُ فِي بَابِ القَسْمِ مِنْ قَوْلِهِمْ إِذَا لَمْ يَقْسِمْ لَهَا فَرَفَعَتْهُ يَأْمُرُهُ بِالقَسْمِ وَعَدَمِ الجَوْرِ، فَإِنْ ذَهَبَ وَلَمْ يَقْسِمْ فَرَفَعَتْهُ أَوْجَعَهُ عُقُوبَةً، وَإِنْ كَانَ مَا ذَهَبَ لَهَا مِنَ الحَقِّ لَا يُقْضَى وَيَحْصُلُ بِذَلِكَ ضَرَرٌ كَبِيرٌ. اهـ. وَفِي فَتَاوَىٰ قَارِئِ الهِدَايَةِ: إِذَا لَمْ يَكُنِ الزَّوْجُ صَاحِبَ مَائِدَةٍ وَعَلِمَ القَاضِي أَنَّهُ يُضَارُّهَا فِي الإِنْفَاقِ فَرَضَ نَفَقَتَهَا عَلَيْهِ دَرَاهِمَ بِقَدْرِ حَالِهِمَا، وَإِذَا امْتَنَعَ مِنْ أَنْ يَفْرِضَ شَيْئًا حُبِسَ حَتَّىٰ يَفْرِضَ. اهـ. وَهُوَ مُشْكِلٌ لِأَنَّ القَاضِي يَفْرِضُ إِذَا امْتَنَعَ فَلَا حَاجَةَ إِلَىٰ فَرْضِ الزَّوْجِ لِيُحْبَسَ إِذَا امْتَنَعَ وَاللَّهُ أَعْلَمُ.
And in it is a consideration that is not hidden. The conclusion is that if he refrains from spending on his ascendant (father/mother) even if high, and on his descendant even if low, and on his wife, he is imprisoned. In Fath al-Qadir: The refusal is realized by her bringing him forward on the second day from the day the maintenance was fixed, even if the amount of maintenance is small like a daniq, when the judge sees that. As for merely fixing it, if she requested his imprisonment he would not imprison him, because punishment is deserved by oppression, and oppression is by prevention after obligation, and that has not been realized. So this implies that if he did not fix (maintenance) for her and the husband did not spend on her on a day, it is befitting that if she brings him forward on the second day he should order him to spend; then if he returns and does not spend, he should punish him. And if the maintenance had fallen after obligation, then he is an oppressor to her. This is analogous to what we previously stated in the chapter of division: when he does not divide for her and she raises the matter to him, he orders him to divide and not to be unjust; then if he goes away and does not divide, and she raises the matter again, he punishes him, even through what has passed of her right is not adjudicated and thereby great harm occurs. End. And in the Fatawa of Qari' al-Hidaya: If the husband is not a man of wealth and the judge knows that he harms her in spending, he fixes her maintenance in dirhams according to their condition. And if he refuses to fix anything, he is imprisoned until he fixes. End. And it is problematic because the judge fixes if he refuses, so there is no need for the husband's fixing so that he may be imprisoned if he refuses. And Allah knows best.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq discusses the judicial remedy of imprisonment for a husband (or a relative) who refuses to pay maintenance after it has been made obligatory by the court. The author first states the general ruling: if a person refuses to spend on his ascendants (parents, grandparents) or descendants (children, grandchildren) or his wife, he is imprisoned. This is a coercive measure to force compliance. Then, from Fath al-Qadir, the author details the procedure: the refusal is only established after the judge has fixed the maintenance amount and the husband had a day to comply. If on the second day the wife brings the matter to the judge, he orders the husband to spend. If the husband still refuses, the judge may then punish/imprison him. The case is compared to the division of nights among co-wives: initially the judge orders fairness, and only after refusal does punishment come. The passage then quotes Fatawa Qari' al-Hidaya, which suggests that if the husband is wealthy, the judge may fix maintenance in dirhams, and if the husband refuses to fix anything himself, he is imprisoned. But the author of Al-Bahr al-Ra'iq finds this problematic because the judge already fixes maintenance when the husband refuses, so there is no need for the husband to fix anything; the imprisonment should be for refusal to pay, not for refusal to fix. The overall lesson is that imprisonment for non-payment of maintenance requires prior judicial fixation and a clear refusal after obligation.

Key Terms
النفقة (al-nafaqah): Maintenance: food, clothing, and shelter that a husband must provide for his wife, or a person for his parents and children.
الحبس (al-habs): Imprisonment as a penal measure to compel performance of an obligation, here payment of maintenance.
الامتناع (al-imtina'): Refusal to pay maintenance after it has been made obligatory by the judge.
