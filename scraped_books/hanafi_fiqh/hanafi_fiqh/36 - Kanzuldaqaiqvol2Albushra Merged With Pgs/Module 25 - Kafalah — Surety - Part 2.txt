Dual Liability in Guarantee: With and Without Principal's Order
Ruling on guarantee with and without order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the liability of a guarantor based on whether the guarantee was undertaken with or without the principal's order, and state the judgment in each case.

Core Idea
Guarantee with the principal's order binds both; without order, only the guarantor is liable.

When a creditor proves that a guarantor acted on the principal's order, the guarantee is treated as an acknowledgment by the principal, making both liable. Without such order, only the guarantor's promise holds him liable. Understanding this distinction is crucial in commercial transactions where guarantees are common.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: (1) guarantee with the principal's order, and (2) guarantee without his order. State each clearly.
2
Determine the legal effect for each
With order: judgment against both guarantor and principal. Without order: judgment against guarantor only. Understand that the order is akin to the principal's acknowledgment of the debt.
The principal's order is considered an admission of the debt (إقرار), binding him.
3
Analyze the reasoning (ta'lil)
Why does order extend liability? Because the principal's command to guarantee implies his awareness and consent to the debt, making him jointly liable.
This is based on the principle that a person is bound by his own orders (المأمور به ينسب إلى الآمر).
4
Apply to a hypothetical scenario
A creditor sues a guarantor and an absent debtor. He produces a written guarantee signed by the debtor asking the guarantor to stand surety. How does the court rule? Apply the rule from the text.
The court would rule against both if the order is proven.

Commentary
This passage states the ruling when a creditor brings evidence that the present person is a guarantor for an absent debtor by the debtor's order. In that case, judgment is passed against both the guarantor (present) and the absent principal, because the order by the principal is considered an acknowledgment of the debt. If the guarantee was without the principal's order, judgment is only against the guarantor. The passage then discusses 'kafalah bil-darak' (guarantee of recourse), which is a guarantee by the seller to the buyer against eviction. When someone guarantees that the seller's title is valid, if the buyer later faces eviction, the guarantor is liable. This guarantee is considered an acknowledgment by the guarantor that the seller had valid title, so any later claim against the sold property (e.g., ownership or pre-emption) is not heard. This is a form of esoppel.

Key Terms
كفالة (kafālah): A contract of guarantee, where a third party (guarantor) assumes liability for a debt or obligation of another (principal).
أمر (amr): The order or command of the principal directing the guarantor to guarantee the debt.

Kafalah bil-Darak: Guarantee of Recourse as Estoppel
Ruling on kafalah bil-darak as estoppel

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a guarantee of recourse (kafalah bil-darak) prevents the guarantor from later claiming ownership or other rights in the sold property.

Core Idea
Guarantee of recourse is an acknowledgment of the seller's ownership, estopping the guarantor from later contradictory claims.

When a person guarantees the seller's title (kafalah bil-darak), it is not merely a promise of indemnity; it also functions as an acknowledgment (iqrar) that the seller owned the property at the time of sale. This acknowledgment bars the guarantor from later asserting ownership, pre-emption (shuf'a), or lease (ijara) rights. Understanding this estoppel effect is critical in property transactions.

Guided Path
1
Define kafalah bil-darak
Learn the term: it is a guarantee against eviction (istikhlāṣ). The guarantor promises to pay the price if the sold property is reclaimed by a rightful owner.
The word 'darak' means loss or injury; here it refers to the buyer's loss of the property.
2
Understand the legal effect: taslim (delivery/acknowledgment)
The text says 'kafālatuhu bil-darak taslīm'. Taslīm means acknowledgment. The guarantee is treated as an admission that the seller had valid title at the time of sale.
Commentators explain taslīm as iqrar (acknowledgment).
3
List the claims that become inadmissible
The text mentions: claiming ownership, claiming shuf'a (pre-emption), and claiming ijara (lease). All such claims are not heard because they contradict the acknowledgment.
The rationale: the guarantor cannot later deny what he already affirmed.
4
Derive the principle of estoppel
From this we derive the Hanafi principle that a person is bound by his own acknowledgments (al-iqrār yulzim). The guarantee creates an irrevocable admission.
This is similar to the maxim: 'من أقر بشيء لا ينفك عنه' (one who acknowledges something cannot escape it).

Suretyship with and without the Principal's Order: Liability and Recourse
Ruling with proof on suretyship scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between suretyship with and without the principal's order, state the resulting liability, and explain the surety's right to recourse.

Core Idea
Suretyship with order is a compensated guarantee; without order, a pure donation.

In this passage from Kanz al-Daqā'iq, the author presents the rule on suretyship (kafālah) and its consequences for liability and recourse. The key factor is whether the surety acted with the principal's order or not. This distinction determines who can be sued and whether the surety can seek reimbursement. The text also discusses a stratagem to establish debt against an absent principal and resolves an intra-Hanafi dispute with Imam Zufar regarding recourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: suretyship with the principal's order (bi-amrihi) and without order (bi-lā amrihi). Note the different rulings on liability and evidence.
2
Analyze the liability outcome
With order, judgment is against both the surety and the principal (even if absent). Without order, judgment is only against the surety. Understand why the principal's presence matters.
The principal's order is considered an admission of the debt.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
Suretyship with order is a donation at the start (the surety undertakes voluntarily) but becomes a compensation at the end (he can recover). Without order, it is a pure donation with no right to recourse.
This explains the difference in judgment against the absent principal.
4
Examine the ḥīlah (stratagem)
The text describes a legal trick: a creditor colludes with a third party to claim suretyship against an absent debtor, then releases the surety, leaving the debt against the absent principal. This allows judgment against an absent person indirectly.
This is an example of legal ingenuity within Hanafi law.
5
Resolve Zufar's dissent
Imam Zufar argues that if the principal denies the debt, the surety cannot recover because the payment is voluntary. The Hanafi response: the principal's order is a legal admission, so his denial is disregarded (mukadhdhab shar‘an).
The preferred (muftā bihī) position is that the surety can recover.

If he proves that he has a claim on Zayd and that this person is a surety for him with his order, judgment is given against both; and if without his order, judgment is given only against the surety. So when he claims suretyship with order, the extension of judgment against the absent is necessary for it; otherwise it would not be compensation; and because the principal's order to the surety is an admission of the debt, so he becomes effectively judged against necessarily. And if the suretyship is without his order, it is not necessary for its validity to extend to the absent; because its validity depends on the existence of the debt in the surety's belief, so it does not extend to another. In suretyship with order, the surety recovers what he paid from the principal. Zufar said: He does not recover; because when he (the principal) denied, he has wronged in his belief, so another should not be wronged for him. We say: He has been disbelieved legally, so what was in his belief is nullified.

Suretyship for Defect as Admission
Ruling on suretyship for defect as an admission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that suretyship for defect (ḍamān al-darak) constitutes an admission (taslīm) and explain why it bars the surety from later claiming ownership or other rights in the sold property.

Core Idea
Guaranteeing the defect admits the seller's ownership — you cannot later claim the item.

When a person acts as a surety for defect in a sale, they guarantee the purchase price in case the sold item is reclaimed by a rightful owner. This act has a powerful legal consequence: it is treated as an admission that the seller owned the property at the time of sale. Therefore, the surety cannot later bring a claim of ownership, pre-emption, or lease against the property. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this ruling and its practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its legal context
The act is kafālah bi al-darak — acting as surety for the defect in a sale. The context is a sale where the surety guarantees the buyer against any future claim on the property.
This is a common practice in property sales.
2
Recognise the ruling: it is an admission (taslīm)
The passage states that 'his suretyship for defect is an admission.' This means the surety's action is legally considered an acknowledgment that the seller had valid ownership at the time of sale.
Admission precludes later contradictory claims.
3
Understand the consequence: claims are barred
Because suretyship is an admission, the surety cannot later claim the property for himself, nor can he assert pre-emption (shuf‘ah) or lease. Any such claim is inadmissible in court.
The purpose is to bind the sale and protect the buyer.
4
Analyse the reasoning (illah)
The textual reasoning: the surety's act of guaranteeing the defect implies he acknowledges the seller's ownership at the time of sale. The illah (effective cause) is that the suretyship would be meaningless if the surety himself had a competing claim.
This is a form of legal implication (dalālah).
5
Apply the ruling to a new scenario
Consider: if Bakr acts as surety for a house sale between Zayd and 'Amr, and later Bakr claims the house was mortgaged to him, his claim is not heard because suretyship admits the seller's ownership.
The ruling is categorical: any claim by the surety is barred.

Writing and Sealing Testimony: Not an Admission Unless Express
Ruling on testimony and seal as possible admission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when writing and sealing a testimony on a deed constitutes an admission of ownership and when it does not, and apply the conditions that trigger admission.

Core Idea
Writing testimony on a deed is not admission — only specific words making the sale binding turn it into one.

A person may write and seal a testimony on a purchase deed to record the event, but this act alone does not mean they acknowledge the seller's ownership. The Hanafi ruling carefully distinguishes between merely witnessing and confirming a binding sale. The exact wording used in the testimony determines whether it becomes an admission that bars later claims. This lesson explores the nuances of this ruling and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act: writing and sealing testimony on a sale document
The act is the witness writing his testimony (shahādah) on a purchase deed (sakk) and sealing it. The question is whether this constitutes an admission (taslīm) of the seller's ownership.
This was a common practice to authenticate documents.
2
State the default ruling: it is NOT an admission
The passage clearly states: 'his testimony and his seal do not [constitute acknowledgment].' The default is that the witness can later claim the property for himself.
Reason: the testimony may have been written for preservation or to later examine the sale.
3
Identify the exception: when it becomes an admission
The passage gives two scenarios where testimony does constitute admission: (a) if the wording expressly states that the seller owned the property and sold it finally, e.g., 'he sold it while owning it' or 'a final binding sale'; (b) if the witness testifies before the judge and judgment is passed (or even if not passed, according to one view).
In those cases, the witness cannot later claim ownership.
4
Understand the reason for the distinction
The default writing is ambiguous — it may be to preserve the event or to allow for ratification. Only when the wording removes ambiguity and affirms the binding nature of the sale does it become an admission.
This is based on the principle that acts are interpreted according to their apparent meaning (ẓāhir).
5
Apply the ruling to a real scenario
If a witness writes 'I witnessed that Zayd sold the house to 'Amr' and seals it, later claiming the house is his, the claim is admissible. But if he writes 'Zayd sold the house while owning it and the sale is final' and seals, his claim is barred.
The key is the wording that indicates the sale was binding.

Core Idea
In guarantee, the guarantor's word on deferral is taken because he acknowledges a right after the term, not an immediate demand.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq examines a subtle dispute in suretyship (kafalah). The Hanafi school distinguishes this from a direct debtor's claim of deferral, where the creditor's word is taken. Core Idea

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq: two men each owe a debt and each stands surety for the other. What happens when one pays? The Hanafi majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) counter that the suretyship occurred while the party was still a slave; no debt was established between them at that time.
Analogy: ratification of an unauthorized kafalah does not create recourse.
4
Distinguish the pawn analogy
In pawn, the debt was established when the slave was free (at the time of pawning), whereas here the debt arises from the suretyship contract itself, which occurred during slavery.
The time of establishing the debt is the critical difference.
5
Derive the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the majority view, neither master nor former slave can claim reimbursement after paying the other's debt post-emancipation. Under Zufar, each would have recourse.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning the consequences of kafalah when the parties are slaves. The matn states two rulings: (1) If a person becomes surety for the person of a slave (i.e., guarantees the slave's presence), and the slave dies, the surety is discharged because the principal obligation is extinguished by death, just as if the principal were free. (2) If a slave, with his master's permission, becomes surety for his master's debt, then the slave is freed and pays the debt, or if the master becomes surety for his slave's debt and pays after the slave is freed, neither has recourse against the other. The commentary clarifies conditions: the slave must have no debts exceeding his value at the time of suretyship; otherwise, it is invalid as it harms creditors. Zufar argues that after freedom, the former slave should have recourse because the impediment of slavery is gone. The Hanafi position (madhhab) holds that the suretyship was contracted while the slave was property, and the obligation did not create a debt between them; thus it cannot become a debt later. An analogy is drawn with ratification after unauthorized suretyship, which does not create recourse. The distinction from the case of a freed pawned slave is that the debt in pawn was established when the slave was free, whereas here it was established during slavery.

Key Terms
كفالة بالنفس (kafalah bi al-nafs): Suretyship for the person of the debtor, guaranteeing his presence (not his debt).
رجوع (ruju'): The right of the surety to claim reimbursement from the principal after paying the debt.
دين مستغرق (dayn mustaghriq): A debt that equals or exceeds the value of the slave's property (i.e., the slave's market value).
