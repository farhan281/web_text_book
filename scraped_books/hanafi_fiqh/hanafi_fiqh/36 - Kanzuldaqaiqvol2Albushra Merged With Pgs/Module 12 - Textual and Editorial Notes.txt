Second Type of Oaths: Actions on Specific Objects
Defining the second type of oath and its rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of oaths in Hanafi fiqh and apply the ruling that the preposition 'lam' in oaths indicates ownership of the object, not agency.

Core Idea
In an oath on a specific object, the 'lam' indicates ownership, not agency—the oath is broken if you touch what belongs to the other person.

In Hanafi fiqh, oaths are categorized based on what they are attached to: actions specific to the person, or actions tied to a specific object. Understanding this distinction is critical for determining when an oath is broken. This lesson unpacks the second type, where the action is linked to a specific object belonging to the person sworn by via the preposition 'lam'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Types
First, recall that oaths are divided into two types: those involving actions specific to the person (e.g., 'I strike you') and those involving actions on specific objects (e.g., 'I eat food for you'). The key is the preposition 'lam'.
The first type was covered earlier.
2
Recognize the Role of 'Lam'
In the second type, the 'lam' indicates ownership of the object by the person sworn by, not that the action is performed for their benefit. For example, in 'If I eat food for you,' the food belongs to you.
This is the core distinction.
3
Apply to Examples
Work through the examples given: striking a slave belonging to the person, eating their food, drinking their water, entering their house, selling their garment. In each case, the oath is broken if the action is done, regardless of permission.
The ownership of the 'ayn (specific object) is what matters.
4
Note the Consequence
The legal effect is that the oath is broken by the mere act of performing the action on the owned object, irrespective of whether the owner commanded it. This contrasts with the first type where the action is directed at the person.
Thus, the ruling hinges on ownership.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and elaboration of the second type of oaths. The first type involved actions specific to the person (like 'I strike you'), whereas the second type involves actions attached to a specific object through the preposition 'lam' ('for'). The key distinction is that 'lam' in 'I eat food for you' refers to the ownership of the food (i.e., it is your food), not to the action being performed for your benefit. The commentator 'Ayni explains that this applies to striking, eating, drinking, and any specific object. The 'lam' indicates that the object belongs to the person sworn by, so the oath is broken regardless of whether that person commanded the action. For example, if someone says 'If I enter for you' and the house is yours (or even if not owned but under your control), he breaks the oath.

Key Terms
القسم الثاني (al-qism al-thani): The second type of oath where the action is not specific to the person but attached to a specific object via the preposition 'lam'.
العين (al-'ayn): A specific, tangible object like a garment or house, as opposed to an action or benefit.
ملك العين (milk al-'ayn): Ownership of the specific object, as opposed to ownership of the action (milk al-fi'l).

Intention in Oaths: When the Swearer's Interpretation is Accepted
Ruling on accepting the swearer's interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that a swearer is believed only if his interpretation imposes a stringency on himself, and apply it to distinguish between interpretations that are accepted and those that are rejected.

Core Idea
A swearer is believed only when his interpretation makes the oath harder for himself, not easier.

In Hanafi fiqh, when someone makes an oath and later claims a specific intention that differs from the apparent wording, the critical question is whether this interpretation benefits him or harms him. This lesson unpacks the key principle: the swearer is believed only when his interpretation imposes a stringency (tashdid) on himself, not when it brings leniency (takhfif). The passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, with commentary from al-'Ayni, clarifies this rule and explores two distinct interpretations of its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Recognise that the passage addresses a dispute over the interpretation of an oath: the swearer claims a specific intent that differs from the apparent meaning.
This is a procedural issue in oath adjudication, not a primary ibadah ruling.
2
State the core rule
The rule is that the swearer is believed (tasdiq) only when his interpretation operates against his own interest (fima 'alayhi) – i.e., it makes the oath more burdensome or more likely to be broken.
The Arabic phrase "صدق فيما عليه" uses 'alayhi (against him) to indicate stringency.
3
Distinguish between tashdid and takhfif
Understand that if the interpretation benefits the swearer (takhfif), it is not accepted because he would be claiming a meaning that avoids breach of the oath.
The benefit in question is avoiding the penalty or consequence of breaking the oath.
4
Analyse the two interpretations of the author's words
The passage gives two possible readings: (1) relating to the distinction between first and second types of oaths (direct vs. indirect?); (2) relating to breaking the oath by direct action vs. by commanding another. Both uphold the same core principle.
Both interpretations are valid and cited in al-Kifayah and al-Hidayah respectively.
5
Apply the rule to examples
Practice with concrete scenarios: e.g., 'If I sell a garment for you, my slave is free' – if the swearer says 'I intended (sell) a garment owned by you', that is a stringency (he cannot sell it) so he is believed. If he later sells a garment not owned by the other but by command, and claims he intended the second type, that is a benefit (he avoids breach), so he is not believed.

And if he intends something else, etc. Know that in this speech there is some ambiguity. It possibly means: if he intends the second type instead of the first, as when he said: 'If I sell a garment for you, then my slave is free,' and he says: 'I intended (if I sell) a garment belonging to you (i.e., owned by him),' he is believed because he imposes a stringency on himself. That is the meaning of the author's statement: 'he is believed regarding what is against him.' For the word 'against' is used in the case of stringency. But he is not believed regarding what is in his favor, as when he sold a garment not owned by the one sworn by but by his command, then said: 'I intended the second type,' he is not believed because he claims no breach of oath, which benefits him. It is also possible that this speech refers to the beginning of this chapter: that in a case where he breaks the oath by direct action, not by command, if he says: 'I intended also not to command someone else,' he is believed because it is a stringency. The first interpretation is indicated in al-Kifayah, and the second in al-Hidayah. (Mustakhlas) His own (statement): 'he is believed regarding what is against him' means in a situation where there is stringency for himself, not in a situation where there is leniency. ('Ayni)

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on a person's interpretation of his oath. When someone makes an oath and later claims that he intended a different meaning than the apparent one, the general principle is that he is believed if his interpretation imposes a stringency (tashdid) on himself, i.e., it makes the oath more difficult to fulfill or more likely to be broken. He is not believed if the interpretation would benefit him (i.e., make the oath easier to avoid or less binding). The text gives two possible interpretations of this rule, one involving the distinction between the first and second types of oaths (as in the previous lesson), and another involving whether the oath is broken by direct action or by commanding another. Both interpretations agree on the core principle: belief is granted only when it is against the swearer's own interest.

Key Terms
تفسير (tafsir): Interpretation of the meaning of an oath by the swearer; a claim about what he intended by his words.
تشدید (tashdid): Stringency or hardship imposed on oneself by an interpretation that makes the oath more difficult to fulfill or more likely to be broken.
تخفیف (takhfif): Leniency or benefit obtained by an interpretation that makes the oath easier to avoid or less binding.
