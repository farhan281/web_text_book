Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Freedom Shares in Repeated Ambiguous Emancipation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on repeated ambiguous emancipation with terminal illness calculations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the freedom shares for each slave (thābit, khārij, dākhil) in a repeated ambiguous emancipation scenario and distinguish between the positions of the Shaykhayn and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Core Idea
The dākhil's freedom share depends on whether the impediment of prior emancipation transfers to him.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a complex case: a master with three slaves makes two ambiguous emancipation statements, one after the other, and dies without clarification. The ruling determines how much of each slave is freed, with an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa/Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani regarding the dākhil (the slave who entered after the first statement). The lesson walks through the logic of the Shaykhayn, Muhammad’s counterargument, and the application when the master is terminally ill, requiring proportional reduction of the bequest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three slaves and the sequence of events
Label the three slaves: thābit (present for both declarations), khārij (present for first, exited before second), dākhil (entered after first, present only for second). The master says 'one of you is free' twice, then dies without clarifying.
The thābit is the one against whom both statements were repeated; this is by consensus.
2
Determine the freedom shares of thābit and khārij
The first declaration is divided between thābit and khārij, each getting half (1/2 each). The second declaration is divided between thābit and dākhil. For thābit, half of the second declaration (1/4) overlaps with his already freed half, so that overlapping portion is void (laghā). Thus thābit gets 1/2 + 1/4 = 3/4. Khārij gets only 1/2 from the first declaration (the second does not involve him).
Thābit's total is 3/4: 1/2 from first, 1/4 from second (the remaining 1/4 of the second is void).
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute for the dākhil
Muhammad argues that the second declaration is divided equally between thābit and dākhil, so each gets 1/4 (since thābit effectively receives only 1/4 due to the impediment). The Shaykhayn argue that the impediment (thābit's prior half) is specific to thābit; dākhil has no impediment, so he receives the full half from the second declaration.
The thamarah al-khilaf: dākhil freed by 1/2 (Shaykhayn) vs 1/4 (Muhammad).
4
Apply the terminal illness limitation
If the master is terminally ill and the emancipation exceeds one-third of the estate (since it is a bequest), the freedom shares are proportionally reduced. The total freedom fractions (thābit 3/4, khārij 1/2, dākhil 1/2 or 1/4) sum to 7/4 (Shaykhayn) or 6/4 (Muhammad). This is scaled to fit within 1/3 of the estate using an 'awl-like method.
The calculation uses a base of 4 shares for the healthy case, then adjusts for the illness case.
5
Calculate the final distribution with sa'yah
For Shaykhayn: total freedom shares = 7, with base 4, then set 1/3 of estate = 7 shares, so estate = 21 shares, each slave valued at 7 shares. Freed portion: thābit 3 (works off 4), khārij 2 (works off 5), dākhil 2 (works off 5). For Muhammad: total freedom shares =6, estate=18, each slave=6. Freed: thābit 3 (works off 3), khārij 2 (works off 4), dākhil 1 (works off 5). The partially freed slaves must perform sa'yah to pay the heirs the remaining value.
Sa'yah is work to earn the unfreed portion; it is required when freedom exceeds the one-third bequest limit.

Commentary
This passage is an expanded commentary on the chapter of partial emancipation in Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It addresses a specific scenario: a master has three slaves, two enter, he says 'one of you is free', then one of those two exits, a third enters, and he repeats 'one of you is free', then dies without clarifying. The ruling determines how much of each slave becomes free. The slave who was present for both declarations (thābit) is freed by 3/4: 1/2 from the first declaration and 1/4 from the second (the other 1/4 of the second declaration is void because it overlaps with the first). The slave who exited (khārij) is freed by 1/2 from the first declaration (since there is no later interference). The slave who entered (dākhil) is freed by 1/2 according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, but only 1/4 according to Muhammad. The Shaykhayn argue that the dākhil has no prior emancipation, so the full 1/2 from the second declaration applies to him. Muhammad draws an analogy with the thābit, where the second declaration only gave 1/4 due to the impediment of prior emancipation. The Shaykhayn respond that the impediment is specific to the thābit and does not transfer to the dākhil. The passage then addresses the case where this occurs in the master's terminal illness and the bequest (the emancipation) is limited to one-third of the estate. Using an 'awl-like method (proportional reduction from inheritance), the shares of freedom and sa'yah (work to pay off remaining value) are calculated. The final part discusses what happens if a slave dies before clarification, with different outcomes depending on which slave dies.

Key Terms
ثابت (thābit): The slave who remains present for both declarations (the constant one).
خارج (khārij): The slave who was present for the first declaration but exited before the second.
داخل (dākhil): The slave who entered after the first declaration and was present only for the second.
إيجاب (ījāb): A declaration or utterance that creates an obligation; here, the statements of emancipation ('one of you is free').
سعاية (sa'yah): The work performed by a partially freed slave to earn money to pay off the remaining portion of his value to the master's heirs.

Ruling on Both Co-Owners Claiming the Child: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Inter-madhhab dispute: Hanafi vs Shafi'i on co-ownership of a child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when both co-owners claim the child of a jointly-owned slave-girl and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the Hanafi proofs.

Core Idea
A child can be legally attributed to both co-owners for inheritance and custody, even though biological paternity is singular.

In this lesson, we examine a unique case from Hanafi fiqh: when two co-owners of a slave-girl both claim her child as their own. The Hanafi school rules that the child's lineage is established from both, with specific legal and financial consequences. We will also explore the opposing view of Imam al-Shafi'i, who insists on referring the matter to a lineage expert (qa'if). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to compare proofs and appreciate the Hanafi methodology.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the exact ruling: the child's lineage is established from both co-owners, she becomes their umm walad, each owes half the dower, and the child inherits from both as a full son. Note that the co-owners then offset their mutual claims (taqasu).
This is the mufta bihi position; it is based on the practice of Umar, Ali, Ibn Abbas, and Zayd ibn Thabit.
2
Identify the opponent's view
Al-Shafi'i argues that the matter must be referred to a qa'if (an expert in lineage through resemblance). His main proofs: the Prophet's pleasure at the qa'if's statement about Usama, and that lineage is indivisible.
The qa'if's judgment is based on physical resemblance, which the Hanafis consider unreliable.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the Shafi'i reasoning: a child cannot have two biological fathers; lineage is indivisible like marriage; the Prophet was pleased by the qa'if's word, indicating it is a valid proof.
They also argue that establishing lineage from two persons is impossible.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that the Prophet's pleasure was to silence the polytheists' taunts, not to establish a legal precedent. The qa'if's statement is a report of a situation, not a binding proof. Moreover, relying on the qa'if involves speculation (rajan bi al-ghayb) and may lead to false accusations (qadhf).
The Hanafi proof is stronger because it is based on the practice of the Companions, which occurred in their presence without disapproval.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafi allows the child to inherit from both co-owners and receive maintenance from both; Shafi'i attributes the child to one father only, as determined by the qa'if. This affects inheritance shares and the mother's status.
The Hanafi position also explains that while lineage itself is indivisible, its attendant rulings (inheritance, custody) are divisible.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab dispute about the case where both co-owners claim the child of a jointly-owned slave-girl. The Hanafi position is that the child's lineage is established from both, she becomes their umm walad, each owes half the dower, and the child inherits from each as a full son; the two co-owners then offset their mutual claims. The opposing view of al-Shafi'i is to refer the matter to a qa'if (a lineage expert who judges by resemblance), arguing that a child cannot have two fathers and that lineage is indivisible. The Hanafi school counters with several proofs: a letter from Umar to Shurayh commanding that both be considered fathers; this was accepted by the Companions and is the position of Ali, Ibn Abbas, and Zayd ibn Thabit. They argue that resorting to a qa'if is tantamount to casting into the unseen and involves accusing chaste women (qadhf). The pleasure of the Prophet regarding the qa'if in the case of Usama was to silence the polytheists, not to establish it as a legal proof; it was a report of a situation, not a binding ruling. The Hanafis also explain that while lineage itself is indivisible, its attendant rulings (like inheritance, maintenance) are divisible, so the child can inherit from both and receive maintenance from both, while the non-divisible aspects (like the right to give in marriage) are held completely by each. The passage then details the practicalities of the offset (taqasu) and the calculation of the dower, noting that the dower is owed in proportion to each co-owner's share in the slave-girl, while inheritance is equal due to the indivisibility of lineage.

Key Terms
القافة (al-qāfa): The science of identifying lineage through physical resemblance; a qa'if is an expert who determines parentage by looking at features.
التقاص (al-taqāṣ): Offset; mutual cancellation of debts between the two co-owners: each one's liability for half the value and half the dower is set off against the other.

Ruling and proof for two co-owners claiming paternity of a child

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when two co-owners both claim paternity of a child from their jointly owned slave-girl, and explain the Hanafi evidence and rebuttal against the Shafi'i reliance on the qa'if.

Core Idea
When two co-owners jointly claim a child, Hanafi law establishes lineage from both without need for a physiognomist.

This lesson addresses a unique scenario in classical Hanafi fiqh: two partners jointly own a slave-girl, and both claim that the child she bears is theirs. The Hanafi school rules that the child's lineage is established from both partners, with full inheritance and compensation. This ruling is supported by a Companion precedent and a critical analysis of the Shafi'i argument based on the qa'if (physiognomist). You will learn the substantive ruling, its dalil, and the inter-madhhab debate.

Guided Path
1

2
Locate the Hanafi dalil

3
Understand the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i school argues that lineage cannot be from two people, so a qa'if (physiognomist) must decide. Note their proof: the Prophet's approval of a qa'if in Usama's case.

Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi rebuttal: the Prophet's approval was to rebut polytheists, not as legal proof. The qa'if's statement is conjecture, not binding evidence. The letter of 'Umar had sahabi consensus.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
In practice, Hanafis establish lineage from both with full inheritance; Shafi'is would refer to a qa'if, potentially ruling for only one father.

Inheritance of the Apostate's Child from a Kitabi Slave-Girl
Rulings on paternity, freedom, and inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios for inheritance of the child of an apostate from a slave-girl and explain which scenario excludes inheritance and why.

Core Idea
A child born to an apostate's Kitabi slave-girl does not inherit if born six months or more after apostasy.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq and its commentary deals with a specific case: a man apostatises and later claims a child born to his Kitabi slave-girl. The rulings cover the child's freedom, lineage, and inheritance. The key issue is how the child's religion is determined, which in turn affects inheritance. Let's walk through the four possible scenarios and the legal reasoning behind each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling
The text states that the child is free, paternity is established via istilad, but the child does NOT inherit the apostate father. First note this default.
2
Introduce the exception for a Muslim mother
The text adds 'if she was Muslim, the son inherits.' This shows that the mother's religion changes the ruling. Pause to understand why religion matters.
The child's religion follows the parent with stronger Islamic status: the apostate father is closer to Islam than a Kitabiyya, but a Muslim mother is stronger than the apostate father.
3
Enumerate the four scenarios
Based on two variables (mother's religion: Kitabiyya vs Muslim; time of birth: less than 6 months vs 6+ months after apostasy), list all four combinations. The commentary does this explicitly.
1. Kitabiyya, 6+ months → no inheritance. 2. Kitabiyya, <6 months → inherits. 3. Muslim, 6+ months → inherits. 4. Muslim, <6 months → inherits.
4
Analyse the reasoning (talil) for each scenario
Scenario 1: child follows apostate father in religion (apostate), and apostates cannot inherit. Scenarios 2-4: child is Muslim (either conceived before apostasy or following Muslim mother), so inherits. Confirm the logic.
The crucial distinction is whether the child's existence as a Muslim can be established before or after apostasy.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The commentary mentions that according to Abu Yusuf and Hasan, the child must be an heir at the time of apostasy, which could exclude scenario 4. According to Muhammad, the critical time is death/judgment, which includes scenario 4. The relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is the latter.
This difference only affects scenario 4 (Muslim mother, <6 months); under the weaker narration it would not inherit.

Inheritance of the Child of an Apostate: The Four Scenarios
Ruling on inheritance of child born to an apostate mother

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios for a child's inheritance from an apostate father and identify which scenario yields non-inheritance according to the relied-upon Hanafi position.

Core Idea
A Muslim son inherits from his apostate father in three of four scenarios, depending on mother's religion and birth timing.

The inheritance rights of a child born to an apostate father depend on nuanced conditions: the mother's religion (Muslim or Kitabiyya) and whether the child was born before or after six months from the apostasy. This lesson unpacks the four possible scenarios, the general Hanafi rule, and the intra-school disagreement among Abu Hanifa and his disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties
Determine the apostate father, the child, and the mother's status (Muslim, Kitabiyya, free, or slave). The text assumes the mother is a slave woman.
The child is free and his lineage is established.
2
Check the timing of birth
Ascertain whether the child was born before or after six months from the date of the father's apostasy. This determines the child's legal status at the time of apostasy.
Six months is the minimum period of pregnancy in Hanafi fiqh.

A Muslim heir inherits from an apostate father. Since the child follows the mother's religion, if the mother is Muslim, the child is Muslim and inherits in all timing scenarios.

If the mother is Kitabiyya and the child is born after six months from apostasy, the child does not inherit according to Abu Hanifa. Note that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad differ, considering the child an heir at the time of death.

Inheritance When a Killer Claims Justification (Ta'wil) – Hanafi Dispute
Resolves Hanafi dispute on inheritance when killer claims justification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on inheritance when a killer claims ta'wil, distinguish Abu Yusuf's view, and apply the rule to scenarios involving just and rebel killers.

Core Idea
Justified killing does not prevent inheritance; only prohibited killing does.

In Islamic law, killing generally deprives the killer of inheritance from the victim. But what if the killer claims he acted on a valid interpretation (ta'wil) of the Quran, even if that interpretation is corrupt? The Hanafi school is divided: the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) hold that such killing is considered justified and thus does not block inheritance, while Abu Yusuf disagrees. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind this dispute and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage covers two cases: a just person killing a rebel, and a rebel killing a just person. In both, the killer claims to be on the truth.
The first case (just kills rebel) is unanimous.
2
Analyze the consensus case
When a just person kills a rebel, the just inherits by consensus because the rebel's sanctity is nullified by rebellion. The killing is permissible, so no penalty attaches.
This sets the baseline: justified killing does not cause deprivation.
3
Examine the disputed case: rebel kills just
Here, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say the rebel inherits because his corrupt interpretation (ta'wil fásid), backed by mutual strength (man'ah), removes liability. Thus, the killing is treated as justified for inheritance purposes.
The key concept is that ta'wil fásid with man'ah is like a valid interpretation in liability matters.
4
Understand Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf argues the killing is without right (ghayr haqq) because the rebel is sinful, so inheritance is blocked. He is joined by Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad (one narration).
The disagreement hinges on whether the claim of being on truth makes the killing bi haqq.
5
Apply the mufta bihi position
The text favors Abu Hanifa and Muhammad. The operative ruling for fatwa is that the rebel inherits if he claims truth and has man'ah. If he admits falsehood, he does not inherit by consensus.
This is the practical takeaway for muftis.

Inheritance Distribution with a Missing Heir
Explaining inheritance rules when an heir is missing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the inheritance shares for each heir when one heir is missing, applying the Hanafi rule of giving the lesser of two possible shares and holding the remainder.

Core Idea
Each non-excluded heir receives the minimum of the two scenarios; the difference is held for the missing person.

When an heir is missing, we cannot know his fate. The Hanafi school solves this by calculating two scenarios: one assuming the missing person is alive, and one assuming he is dead. Each living heir receives the amount they are certain to get (the lower of the two shares), and the difference is suspended until the missing person's status is resolved. This lesson walks you through the method with two classic examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the missing heir and the two scenarios
Determine who is missing. Then define two cases: (A) missing person is alive at the time of the decedent's death, (B) missing person died before the decedent. All calculations are done under both assumptions.
The missing person does not inherit from relatives who die during his absence unless his life at that time is confirmed.
2
List all other heirs and apply hajb under each scenario
Identify all living heirs. Under scenario A (missing alive), check if the missing person excludes any heir entirely (hajb hirman) or reduces their share (hajb nuqsan). Under scenario B (missing dead), hajb by the missing person does not apply. Record the status.
In the first example, the missing son excludes the son's son entirely if alive. In the second, the missing brother does not exclude the full sister but reduces her share.
3
Calculate shares in both scenarios
For each scenario, determine the base number (asl al-mas'alah) of the inheritance. Assign Quranic fixed shares to eligible heirs and the remaining balance to 'asabah. If the shares exceed the base, apply 'awl (increase the base). Record the share of each heir in each scenario.
In the second example, scenario B (missing dead) requires 'awl from 6 to 8 because the husband's half (3/6) and the full sister's half (3/6) and mother's one-third (2/6) sum to 8/6.
4
Determine the minimum certain share for each heir
Compare each heir's share in scenario A and scenario B. The heir is given the smaller of the two amounts immediately. This is the amount they are certain to receive regardless of the missing person's status.
In the first example, the two daughters receive half (the lesser of half and two-thirds). In the second example, the husband receives half (lesser of half and one-third), the mother receives one-sixth (lesser of one-sixth and one-fourth), the full sister receives one-ninth (lesser of one-ninth and three-eighths).
5
Hold the remainder and reconcile mathematical differences
Subtract the total given immediately from the estate. The remainder is held for the missing person. If the two scenarios have different base numbers or fractional shares, adjust by finding a common multiple (tashih) to ensure the shares are integers. This remainder is returned to the other heirs only if the missing person is later declared dead.
The text mentions the rule for tashih: if the two base numbers are compatible (have a common factor), multiply the concordance; if incompatible, multiply each by the other.

Inheritance of a Missing Person: The Lesser Share Rule
General ruling and example of missing person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the shares of heirs when a missing person is involved, applying the Hanafi rule of giving the lesser certain share and stopping the remainder.

Core Idea
Heirs receive the minimum certain share; the rest is stopped until the missing person's status is resolved.

When an heir is missing (mafqud), his life or death is uncertain. This uncertainty affects the inheritance of all other heirs. The Hanafi school resolves this by giving each heir the lesser of the two possible shares — the share they would receive if the missing person were alive versus dead — and stopping the remainder until the missing person's status is known. In this lesson, we unpack the rule and apply it to a concrete case involving daughters, a missing son, and a grandson.

Guided Path
1
Identify the heirs and the missing person
List all living relatives and note which one is missing (mafqud). In the example: two daughters, a missing son, and a deceased son's son (grandson). The grandson is alive but may be excluded.
The missing son is the key; his status affects others' shares.
2
Determine the two possible scenarios
Consider two scenarios: (A) missing son alive at the time of death, (B) missing son dead at that time. Calculate shares for each scenario.
The missing son is presumed alive regarding his own property but dead regarding others' claims?
3
Calculate shares in each scenario
Scenario A (son alive): Daughters get 1/2 (as two daughters with a son get 1/2 as fard), son gets the rest as asabah. Scenario B (son dead): Daughters get 2/3 (as two daughters with no son), and the grandson would get the remaining 1/3 as asabah.
The share of the daughters differs: 1/2 vs 2/3. The grandson gets nothing in A (excluded by son) and 1/3 in B.
4
Apply the rule: give the lesser certain share
For each heir whose share varies between scenarios, give the minimum that is certain. For the daughters, the lesser of 1/2 and 2/3 is 1/2. The grandson gets nothing because in Scenario A he gets nothing (excluded), and in Scenario B he gets 1/3 but entitlement is uncertain, so he receives the lesser (zero).
This follows the principle that certainty (yaqin) prevails over doubt.
5
Stop the remainder for the missing person
The remaining portion after giving the lesser shares is stopped (waqf) for the missing person. In the example, after giving 1/2 to daughters, the other 1/2 is stopped for the missing son.
The stopped portion is held until the missing person's status is resolved by a court judgment.

Inheritance Rules for a Fetus (Haml) in Hanafi Fiqh
Analogy between missing person and fetus in inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for stopping the share of a fetus in inheritance and identify the mufta bihi among the three imams.

Core Idea
The fetus's potential heirship requires stopping part of the estate; the amount stopped depends on whether it excludes or reduces others.

In the previous lesson, we learned about stopping the estate for a missing person (mafqud). The same principle applies to a fetus (haml) of a deceased man. However, the amount to be stopped varies depending on whether the fetus, if born alive, would exclude or merely reduce other heirs. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rules, including a well-known intra-madhhab dispute among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of heirs relative to the fetus
Heirs affected by a potential fetus fall into three types: (1) those whose share is unchanged regardless of the fetus, (2) those who are totally excluded if the fetus is a son, and (3) those whose share changes (either partial exclusion or reduction).
This step classifies the heirs to determine the stopping rule.
2
Apply the rule for heirs who are not excluded or reduced
If an heir's share does not change due to the fetus (e.g., a son among other sons), that heir receives his full share immediately. The fetus's share is stopped based on the number of possible children.
E.g., if there are two sons and a fetus, the two sons get their shares, but a portion is stopped for the fetus.
3
Examine the non-exclusion scenario: two sons and a fetus
When the deceased leaves two sons and a pregnant wife, the fetus does not exclude any heir. The amount to stop is disputed: Abu Hanifa says stop 2/3 (as if the fetus is a son), Muhammad says stop 1/2 (as if the fetus could be a single daughter), and the mufta bihi is Abu Yusuf's opinion: stop 1/3.
The thamarah al-khilaf: the amount kept in abeyance differs.
4
Apply rules for total exclusion and partial exclusion/reduction
If the fetus would totally exclude certain heirs (e.g., brothers, uncles if a son is born), the entire estate is stopped. If partial exclusion (e.g., brothers with grandfather), the grandfather gets 1/6 and the rest is stopped. If reduction (e.g., husband or wife), they get the lesser of their two possible shares.
The same logic as the missing person: give the minimum possible share to those whose share could be reduced.
5
Summarize the parallel to the missing person (mafqud)
The final summary states: an heir unaffected by the fetus gets full share; an heir excluded entirely gets nothing; an heir whose share changes gets the lesser of the two possible shares. This mirrors the rule for a missing person.
This generalization is the key takeaway.

Faraid of the Missing Person and Comparison with Pregnancy
Faraid calculation with suspense and pregnancy comparison

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the interim distribution of an estate when an heir is missing or unborn, using the Hanafi method of two scenarios and suspense holding.

Core Idea
Each heir gets the minimum possible share; the rest is held until the missing or unborn heir's status is known.

When an heir is missing (mafqud) or unborn (haml), we cannot simply distribute the entire estate. Instead, we calculate what each heir would receive under the best and worst cases for the missing heir, give them the smaller share, and hold the difference. This lesson walks you through the arithmetic and the legal reasoning behind this suspense mechanism, including an intra-Hanafi disagreement on how much to hold for a pregnancy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two assumptions
For a missing person, we consider two scenarios: (1) the missing person is alive, and (2) the missing person is dead. List the heirs under each scenario.
In the example: husband, mother, brother (missing), sister. Under death, brother is gone.
2
Calculate shares under each assumption
For each scenario, compute the standard faraid shares. Apply awl if the sum of shares exceeds the base.
Alive: base 6 becomes 18 after correcting for uneven division. Dead: base 6 awl to 8.
3
Find a common denominator and determine suspense
Use the concordance method (muwafaqa or mubayana) to combine the two scenarios. Multiply each heir's share in each scenario to an overall common denominator. The smaller amount is given immediately; the difference is held.
Example uses 72 as common denominator for 18 and 8.
4
Apply the same principle to pregnancy
For an unborn child, similar reasoning applies. Determine if the child is a hijib (excluder) or not. Use the minimum share for each heir based on possible outcomes.
The child's sex is unknown, so we assume worst-case for other heirs.

This is a distinct legal mechanism from testimony.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on disputes in testimony (bab al-ikhtilaf fi al-shahada) discusses rules for establishing ownership through testimony on possession (yad), especially in inheritance cases. The author explains exceptions where testimony on possession suffices without further mention of 'jar' (i.e., that the deceased died owning it as inheritance). If witnesses testify that property was in the deceased's hand at death, it is accepted regardless of whether that hand was ownership or trust (amana), because trusts convert to ownership upon death if the deceased dies unknown, due to guarantee. Similarly, testifying that property was in the hand of the deceased's depositary, borrower, lessee, pledgee, or usurper suffices because establishing the hand of his representative is like establishing his own hand in meaning. The passage also discusses a contrasting case: if witnesses testify that a living person had possession of an object a month ago but not currently, it is rejected according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the two parties) because the type of hand is ambiguous. Abu Yusuf accepts it, analogizing to testimony of ownership. The text further mentions that if the defendant acknowledges ownership, or witnesses testify that he acknowledged it, the property is given to the plaintiff. The ruling is based on consensus for the excepted cases and on istihsan for the general principle.

Handing Over Deposit or Debt on Inheritance Claim
Ruling on claimant asserting ownership of deposit via purchase or inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a claimant asserts ownership of a deposit or debt via inheritance or bequest, and identify the conditions under which the depositee or debtor must hand over.

Core Idea
A depositee must not hand over the deposit on a claim of inheritance unless the claimant asserts no other heir exists; the debtor may pay upon confirmation.

This lesson addresses a practical scenario: someone claims that the depositor died and left the deposit (or debt) to them as inheritance or bequest. The Hanafi school imposes a crucial condition before the depositee or debtor can safely hand over the property. Understanding this condition prevents injustice to other potential heirs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claimant's assertion
The claimant states that the depositor or creditor died and left the deposit or debt as inheritance (or bequest) to him. The depositee or debtor must evaluate this claim.
2
Determine the depositee's default obligation
For a deposit, the depositee is a trustee (amin). He must not hand over based solely on the claimant's statement; he must be cautious because other heirs may exist.
3
Apply the condition for safe handover of deposit
The depositee may only hand over if the claimant explicitly says that the deceased left no other heir. For a bequest, the legatee is treated similarly, but the claimant must also state that there is no heir.
4
Distinguish the debt scenario
For a debt, if the debtor confirms the claimant's statement (that the creditor died and left the debt as inheritance), he is ordered to pay. No additional condition about other heirs is required.
5
Recognize the exception for lack of knowledge
If the depositee or debtor denies the death of the original owner or says 'I don't know', they must not hand over unless the claimant produces evidence (bayyinah).

Agency for Inheritance or Bequest of Deposit: Conditions for Payment
Ruling on agency claims involving inheritance or bequest of a deposit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a claim of inheritance of a deposit and identify the conditions under which the depositary must pay.

Core Idea
A depositary's confirmation of an inheritance claim does not oblige payment; it is an invalid acknowledgment of another's property.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a scenario where a claimant alleges that the depositor has died and left the deposit as their inheritance or bequest. Even if the depositary confirms the claim, payment is not ordered. The reasoning is rooted in the principle that acknowledging another's property is invalid. The lesson unpacks this ruling and its conditions, including the need for evidence when the depositary denies or is uncertain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim scenario
The claimant asserts that the depositor died and left the deposit as inheritance (or bequest) to him, and there is no other heir.
The claim involves ownership transfer after death.
2
Note the depositary's confirmation
Even if the depositary confirms the claim (i.e., agrees that the depositor died and the claimant is heir), the depositary is not ordered to pay.
Confirmation alone is insufficient.
3
Understand the legal reason (talil)
The prohibition is because the depositary's confirmation constitutes acknowledgment of another's property (i.e., property not yet proven to belong to the claimant).
Acknowledgment of another's property is invalid in Hanafi law.
4
Recognize the exception when evidence is required
If the depositary denies the death or pleads ignorance, the claimant must produce evidence (bayyinah) before the depositary can be compelled to hand over the deposit.
The condition 'and he confirms him' is a necessary condition for the ruling; without it, evidence is mandatory.
