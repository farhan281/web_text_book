Consecutive Fasting in Kaffarah: The Hanafi Position
Dispute on consecutive fasting in kaffarah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school requires consecutive fasting and contrast the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school requires consecutive fasting because the Quranic reading is treated as a well-known hadith.

When unable to free, feed, or clothe, one must fast three days. The Hanafis insist on consecutiveness, citing the reading of Ibn Mas'ud and Ubayy. The Shafi'is allow non-consecutive fasting based on the absolute text. This lesson examines the evidence and the Hanafi usul principle of augmenting the Quran with khabar mashhur.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
What is the act? Fasting three days as expiation when unable to do other options. The Hanafi ruling: must be consecutive (mutatabi'at).
2
Locate the Hanafi Proof
The Hanafis rely on the Quranic reading of Ibn Mas'ud and Ubayy: 'fast three consecutive days'. This reading is classified as khabar mashhur, which can augment the Quran.
Hanafi usul: khabar mashhur is binding and can add a condition to a general command.
3
Understand the Opposing School's Proof
Imam al-Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad argue that consecutiveness is not required because Quran 2:196 is absolute and does not mention 'consecutive'.
They accept only the transmitted mushaf text.
4
Analyse the Hanafi Response
The reading is as reliable as a well-known hadith; it specifies the general command. This is a key usul principle: khass can specify amm.
The Hanafis treat this reading as a hujja even though not in the mushaf.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence
Hanafi: non-consecutive fasting invalid, must repeat. Shafi'i: valid. This directly affects validity of expiation.
Thamarah al-khilaf: difference in validity.

Timing of Kaffarah: Can Expiation Precede the Oath-Breaking?
Dispute on timing of expiation relative to oath-breaking.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that expiation cannot be offered before breaking an oath and contrast the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Expiation covers a violation; no violation exists before the oath is broken, so prior expiation is invalid.

The second dispute in the passage addresses whether expiation (kaffarah) can be performed before actually breaking the oath. The Hanafis argue that expiation is to cover a violation (jinaya), which only occurs upon breaking. The Shafi'is permit prior expiation by wealth, reasoning that the oath itself is the cause. This lesson examines the proofs and the linguistic reinterpretation of a key hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
Can a person give expiation (freeing, feeding, or clothing) before breaking his oath? The Hanafis say no; the Shafi'is say yes (for wealth-based expiation).
The dispute is about the cause (sabab) and condition (shart) of expiation.
2
State the Hanafi Proof
Expiation is to cover a violation (jinaya). No violation occurs before the oath is broken. Therefore, prior expiation is futile.
Rational argument: the oath is meant for fulfillment (birr), not violation.
3
Understand the Opposing School's Proof
The Shafi'is cite a hadith: 'If you swear an oath, then expiate your oath, then do what is better.' The word 'then' (thumma) indicates order, implying expiation before doing the better act (which is breaking? Actually, the hadith suggests expiating first then doing the better act, which is breaking the oath if it is better).
They also argue that the oath itself is the cause (sabab) of expiation, and breaking is just a condition.
4
Analyse the Hanafi Response
The Hanafis reinterpret the hadith: 'thumma' here is used in the sense of 'wa' (and), not to indicate order. Thus, the hadith does not mandate prior expiation. Also, the Hanafis categorize the oath as a preventer (mani‘) of expiation, not a cause.
The Hanafi usul principle: al-umur bi-maqasidiha — actions are judged by intentions; the oath is to prevent violation, so it cannot be a cause.
5
Determine the Practical Consequence
Hanafi: if someone expiates before breaking, the expiation is invalid and must be repeated after breaking. Shafi'i: prior expiation by wealth is valid and the expiation is fulfilled.
This difference affects whether the expiation stands.

Oath Using Dahr and Abad: Lifetime Default
Determining default duration for oaths using dahr and abad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for oaths using definite 'al-dahr' or 'al-abad' and explain the evidence and differences with the Shafi'i and Maliki schools.

Core Idea
A Hanafi oath using 'al-dahr' or 'al-abad' covers the oath-taker's entire lifetime.

When someone swears using the phrases 'al-dahr' or 'al-abad' with the definite article, what duration is intended if they have no specific intention? The Hanafi school answers: their entire lifetime. This ruling is based on linguistic usage and a Prophetic hadith. Other schools disagree, leading to important practical differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Note that the terms 'al-dahr' and 'al-abad' appear with the definite article (al-). The Hanafi position hinges on this grammatical feature, which indicates the whole of the class – meaning the whole lifetime.
The definite article in Arabic often implies generality, but here it is interpreted as the entire lifespan.
2
State the Hanafi default ruling
The ruling is that the oath is valid for the oath-taker's entire lifetime. If the action occurs at any time during the remaining life, the oath is violated and expiation (kaffarah) becomes wajib.
This default applies only if the oath-taker had no specific intention.
3
Examine the evidence (dalil)
The text mentions two evidences: (a) a poetic line using 'al-dahr' in the sense of passing time with sin remaining; (b) the hadith 'Whoever fasts al-abad, he has no fast', understood to mean fasting one's whole life is prohibited, hence 'al-abad' means the entire lifetime.
The hadith is ahad, so the ruling is based on zanni evidence, but it is sufficient for an oath interpretation.
4
Understand the linguistic reasoning
With the definite article, 'dahr' and 'abad' indicate the whole of the relevant span. For a human being, that span is the lifetime. Indefinite forms would be ambiguous, but the definite form removes ambiguity.
The Hanafi usul principle: al-amm yufidu al-istiqraq (the general term indicates complete inclusion) applies here.
5
Compare with opposing schools
Imam al-Shafi'i and Ahmad consider a minimum of one hour; Imam Malik considers one year. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is visible when an oath-taker acts after a month: Hanafi counts it as a violation, Shafi'i does not.
The disagreement is legitimate (mukhtalaf fih) and each school has its own evidence.

Oath Not to Fast: When Does Fasting Break It?
Ruling on oath not to fast: effect of wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between unqualified and qualified oaths not to fast and apply the Hanafi ruling on when hanith (oath-breaking) occurs.

Core Idea
An unqualified oath not to fast is broken by any fasting; a qualified oath requires a full day's fast.

This passage addresses a common scenario: a person swears not to fast, but then fasts for a short time. The ruling hinges on the wording of the oath. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a general prohibition and one that specifies 'a fast' or 'a day'. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining when an oath is broken and expiation becomes necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Oath Formulations
The passage presents two types: an unqualified oath (e.g., 'I will not fast') and a qualified oath (e.g., 'I will not fast a fast' or 'I will not fast a day').
The Arabic wording determines the legal effect.
2
Determine the Ruling for Each
For an unqualified oath, any fasting, even for an hour, constitutes hanith (breaking the oath). For a qualified oath, only a full day's fast from dawn to sunset triggers hanith.
The key is the presence or absence of a qualifying word like 'day' or the masdar form.
3
Analyze the Legal Reasoning (Talil)
The word 'sawm' in an unqualified context includes partial abstinence. When the masdar 'sawman' or the word 'yawman' (day) is used, it implies the complete act – a full day's fast from dawn to sunset.
This is based on linguistic and legal interpretation.
4
Apply to New Scenarios
Practice with similar oaths, such as 'I will not eat' versus 'I will not eat a meal', to reinforce the principle of qualification.
The same rule applies to other acts in oaths.

Oath Not to Fast: Effect of Partial Fasting
Ruling on breaking an oath not to fast by fasting only part of a day

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on when an oath not to fast is broken by partial fasting and distinguish between different oath wordings.

Core Idea
The wording of the oath determines whether partial fasting breaks it.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on oaths related to fasting. The key is the precise wording: a general 'I will not fast' is broken by any amount of fasting, while specifying 'a fast' or 'a day' requires a complete day's fast. We will explore the linguistic and legal reasoning behind this distinction.

Identify the general ruling

This is the default rule based on the common meaning of 'fasting'.
2
Note the qualification for specific wording
If the oath is 'لا أصوم صوماً' (I will not fast a fast) or 'لا أصوم يوماً' (I will not fast a day), only a complete fast from dawn to sunset breaks the oath.
The wording changes the scope of the oath.
3
Analyze the linguistic basis
The masdar 'صوماً' implies a complete act, and 'يوماً' explicitly specifies a full day. Hence, partial fasting does not fulfill the oath.
This shows the importance of precise wording in legal oaths.
