Determining a Foundling's Religion: Four Scenarios and Hanafi Narrations
Explains default Islam of foundling and four scenarios with Hanafi narration differences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling on a foundling's religion and explain the differences among the four Hanafi narrations when the finder and location conflict.

Core Idea
A foundling's religion is Islam by default; but when finder and place disagree, Hanafi narrations offer four different criteria.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq establishes the baseline: a foundling (laqit) is considered Muslim unless found in a dhimmi territory. However, when the finder's religion and the location's predominant religion conflict, the Hanafi school contains multiple narrations. We will examine these four narrations and understand the legal reasoning behind each.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default ruling
The base matn states: 'He is Muslim if he is not found in the locality of the people of dhimmah.' So the default is Islam, with an exception for dhimmi territory.
2
Distinguish the four scenarios
The commentary lists four combinations of finder (Muslim/disbeliever) and location (Muslim/disbeliever). The first two are clear; the last two are disputed.
3
Understand the clear cases
Scenario 1: Muslim finds in Muslim place → foundling is Muslim. Scenario 2: disbeliever finds in disbeliever place → foundling is disbeliever. These are unambiguous.
4
Analyze the disputed cases
Scenarios 3 and 4 (misaligned finder and place) have four narrations: (a) place prevails, (b) finder prevails, (c) whichever brings Islam prevails, (d) clothing determines. Each narration is based on a different legal consideration.
These narrations come from different sources: al-Mabsut, Muhammad ibn Sama'ah, etc.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that the foundling's religion may be ruled Muslim, disbeliever, or dhimmi depending on which narration is followed. The principle 'Islam prevails and is not prevailed over' suggests preference for the narration that results in Islam.

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents the ruling on the religion of a foundling. The base text states that the foundling is considered Muslim by default unless found in dhimmi territory. The commentary expands by presenting four scenarios based on who finds the foundling and where. The first two scenarios are clear: a Muslim finder in Muslim territory yields a Muslim foundling, and a disbeliever finder in disbeliever territory yields a disbeliever foundling. The third and fourth scenarios—where the finder and territory do not match—are subject to multiple narrations within the Hanafi school. These narrations differ on whether the criterion is the location, the finder, whichever brings Islam, or the foundling's clothing. One narration gives precedence to whichever factor would result in Islam, because Islam is more beneficial and 'Islam prevails and is not prevailed over.' Another narration looks at the foundling's attire. The commentator notes that this is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, and the practical ruling may depend on the preferred narration. The passage does not explicitly state which narration is mufta bihi, but the principle of giving the foundling the benefit of the doubt for Islam is consistent with the Hanafi school's orientation.

Property Found with the Foundling Belongs to Him
Rules that any property found with the foundling is his, based on apparent possession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on property found with a foundling and explain the reasoning based on apparent possession (al-zahir).

Core Idea
Property found on a foundling is his by virtue of apparent possession, rebutting the finder's claim.

This passage addresses a classic fiqh scenario: a foundling (al-laqit) is discovered with money or goods physically attached to him. Does the finder have any claim? The Hanafi school rules decisively that the property belongs to the foundling, relying on the principle of al-zahir (apparent evidence). We will unpack the reasoning, examine an objection, and note an intra-madhhab difference on spending from that property.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and ruling
Read the source: property found physically attached to the foundling (tied on, under, or on his mount) belongs to the foundling, not the finder.
The property is considered part of the foundling's apparent possession.
2
Analyze the proof based on al-zahir
The proof is that the foundling is a free person capable of ownership and the property is in his hand (possession). The apparent possession repels claims of others.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on presumption and capacity.
3
Consider the objection and response
An objection is raised: al-zahir only works for defense (daf‘), not to establish entitlement (istihqaq). The response: the property is in the foundling's possession and he is ahl al-milk, so the claim of another is repelled; ownership follows from continuous possession.
The defense becomes a basis for ownership due to the foundling's capacity.
4
Note the intra-madhhab difference on spending
Some Hanafis require the finder to obtain a judge's order before spending the property on the foundling (comparing it to lost property). Others permit spending without an order because the property is apparently the foundling's.
This difference does not affect the main ruling that the property belongs to the foundling.

Commentary
This passage addresses the status of property found with a foundling. The ruling is simple: any money or goods found physically with the foundling (tied on him, under him, or on his mount) belong to the foundling, not the finder. The reasoning is based on apparent possession (al-zahir). The commentary addresses an objection: some argue that apparent possession only serves to defend against claims, not to establish ownership. The reponse distinguishes: the apparent possession here repels claims by others (defense), and since the foundling is free and capable of ownership, and the property is in his hand, it is presumed to be his. The intention is that the one who placed the money did so for the foundling's expenses. There is a difference of opinion on whether the finder needs court permission to spend from it: some require a judge's order because it is like lost property, others say no because it is apparently the foundling's property. The conclusion reaffirms that the property is for the foundling.

Limitations on the Finder's Authority Over the Foundling
Lists what the finder (multaqit) may and may not do regarding the foundling's person and property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state which actions the finder (multaqit) may and may not perform on behalf of a foundling, and explain the basis for each restriction.

Core Idea
The multaqit may only benefit and train the foundling; he holds no guardianship for marriage, sale, or lease.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies the boundaries of the finder's authority. The base text lists four actions, but only two are permitted. The commentary explains why: the finder lacks the necessary guardianship (wilayah) for marriage and property disposal. Understanding these limits is crucial to protect the foundling's rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four actions mentioned in the base text.
The text states: 'لا يصح للملتقط عليه نكاح وبيع وإجارة ويسلمه في حرفة، ويقبض هبته.' This lists marriage (nikah), sale (bay'), lease (ijarah), handing over to a craft (taslim fi hirfah), and accepting gifts (qabd hibah). Note: the last two are permitted.
2
Separate the actions into permitted and prohibited.
The text explicitly says marriage, sale, and lease are not valid. Handing over to a craft and accepting gifts are allowed. The commentary confirms each.
3
Analyze the reason for each prohibition.
Marriage: no guardianship (wilayah) because the multaqit is not a relative, owner, or sultan. Sale: only father and grandfather have authority over property. Lease: the correct view (al-asahh) prohibits it, similar to sale.
4
Analyze the justification for each permitted action.
Apprenticing in a craft is part of upbringing, discipline, and refinement (taqwim, ta'dib, tahdhib). It is pure benefit (naf' mahd) and prevents idleness. Accepting gifts is also pure benefit; the child owns the gift if rational, and the mother or her guardian can accept.

Ruling on Converting Road into Mosque or Vice Versa
Permissibility of converting between road and mosque under necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which converting a road into a mosque or vice versa is permissible, and identify who may pass through a mosque that has a public passage.

Core Idea
A road may become mosque or vice versa if no harm and necessity, based on custom and Companions' precedent.

This lesson examines a specific ruling from Kanz al-Daqaiq regarding the conversion of public roads into mosque space and vice versa. While such conversion might seem problematic due to ownership and sanctity, the Hanafi school permits it under conditions of necessity and no harm, based on customary practice and the precedent of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dual ruling
The matn states that making part of a road into a mosque is valid, and likewise the reverse (making part of a mosque into a road). Recognize that both directions are permissible, provided the condition of no harm is met.
2
Understand the conditions
The permissibility is conditional: (1) the conversion must not harm the road or public, (2) it must be based on necessity (e.g., overcrowding with no alternative), and (3) local custom (ta'aruf) may be considered.
Harm includes obstructing passage or causing inconvenience.
3
Examine the exceptions on usage
Once a mosque has a public passage, anyone may pass through it, even a disbeliever, except those in major ritual impurity (junub, menstruating woman, woman with postpartum bleeding). Additionally, animals are not allowed to enter.
The prohibition of junub is based on Quran 4:43.
4
Study the Companions' precedent (dalil)
The commentary cites the Companions' action when expanding the Sacred Mosque: they forcibly purchased adjacent land with compensation. This demonstrates that taking land for a mosque with compensation is permissible, analogous to taking from a public road in necessity.
The Hadith is narrated with zanni thubut and dalala.
5
Recognize the Hanafi reliance on necessity and custom
The Hanafi school allows conversion when necessary, using the principle that public properties can compensate each other. Custom (ta'aruf) of cities where passages are made through mosques also supports the ruling.
Other schools may restrict more strictly, but Hanafi relies on istihsan and public benefit.

Salam in Munqati' Goods: Hanafi Position and Inter-Madhhab Dispute
Ruling on salam in goods that become unavailable at delivery time

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on salam in munqati' goods, identify the three scenarios of availability, and explain the Hanafi proof from hadith against the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Salam is invalid if goods are unavailable at any time from contract to delivery; continuous availability is required.

This lesson examines the Hanafi position on salam (forward sale) when the subject matter is not continuously available in the market. While other schools allow salam as long as goods are available at delivery, the Hanafi school requires continuous availability from contract to delivery, based on a hadith of Ibn 'Umar. We will explore the scenarios, the evidence, and the intra-Hanafi dispute on what happens if goods become unavailable after maturity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios of munqati'
The passage lists three situations: goods unavailable only at contract, unavailable only in the period between contract and delivery, and available at both ends but unavailable in between. List each clearly.
These scenarios are described in the opening sentences.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for each scenario
For all three scenarios, the Hanafi school rules salam as invalid (batil) because the condition of continuous availability is broken.
The matn says 'لا يجوز السلم في المنقطع عن أيدي الناس مطلقاً' – absolutely not permissible.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali schools consider salam valid in the latter two scenarios (available at delivery or at both ends) because ability to deliver at the time of obligation suffices.
They do not require continuous availability before delivery.
4
Examine the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi evidence is the hadith of Ibn 'Umar forbidding the sale of fruits before ripeness. The Prophet's question 'by what right do you take your brother's wealth?' indicates that if delivery becomes impossible, the sale is invalid.
This is a dalil naqli directly quoted in the text.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on post-maturity unavailability
If goods become unavailable after the delivery date, the majority (including Abu Hanifa) give the buyer an option to rescind or wait. Zufar says the contract becomes void immediately, analogous to perishing before possession. The text prefers the option view.
The commentary distinguishes this from perishing because the unavailability is temporary.

Salam in Fish: Fresh vs Salted, Weight vs Count
Rulings on salam in fish with fresh/salted and weight/count distinctions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid salam in fish and distinguish the rulings for fresh vs salted fish and small vs large fish.

Core Idea
Salam in salted fish is always valid by weight; fresh fish requires guaranteed availability.

This lesson examines the Hanafi rulings on salam (forward sale) in fish. The key distinction is between fresh fish, which is seasonally unavailable in cold climates, and salted fish, which is preserved and always available. Additionally, the method of measuring (weight vs count) and the size of the fish (small vs large) affect validity. Understanding these conditions prevents invalid contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that salam in fish is a type of forward sale. The ruling depends on the type of fish (fresh/salted), method of specification (weight/count), and size (small/large).
The base ruling: salam in fish of a different type is invalid; same type is valid by weight but not by count.
2
Analyze the condition of availability
For fresh fish, the contract is valid only if the fish is available at the time of delivery. Seasonal unavailability (e.g., winter) invalidates unless the location never experiences unavailability (e.g., Egypt) or the term avoids the unavailable season.
Salted fish is always considered available, so availability is not a concern.
3
Distinguish by method of measurement
Count is invalid for any fish due to variation (tafāwut). Weight is valid. For small fish, both weight and measure (kayl) are valid; for large fish, only weight is valid.
Small fish are uniform enough for measure; large fish vary in size, so only weight works.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa prohibits salam in large fish that become unavailable, analogizing to meat. Muhammad allows fresh fish if not in winter. The correct view (al-ṣaḥīḥ) follows the general principle: small fish by weight/measure, large fish by weight.
This ikhtilaf affects only large fresh fish.
5
Apply the rulings to practical scenarios
Use the conditions to evaluate any salam contract for fish: specify type, ensure availability (fresh) or automatic (salted), and use weight (large) or weight/measure (small).
The muftā bihī position: small fish valid by weight or measure; large fish valid by weight only.

Salam in Meat: The Hanafi Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Intra-Hanafi dispute on salam in meat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the two companions on salam in meat, explain the reasoning behind each, and identify the operative fatwa.

Core Idea
Salam in meat is valid if properly described, according to the operative Hanafi position.

Salam (forward sale) in meat raises a key question: can a commodity that varies in fatness, bone content, and seasonal quality be sufficiently specified? The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement between Abu Hanifa, who rules it absolutely invalid, and his two students Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, who permit it if all relevant attributes are described. This lesson unpacks the reasoning on both sides and clarifies the relied-upon ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Read the source text and note: Abu Hanifa says salam in meat is invalid absolutely; the two companions say it is valid if type, kind, age, part, and weight are specified.
The dispute is intra-Hanafi.
2
Analyze the proofs
For Sahibayn: meat is a weighed item (mawzun) with precise description, like fat. For Abu Hanifa: meat varies in fatness seasonally, contains unknown bones, and involves haggling and hiding of bones—uncertainties not removed by specification.
Both sides use analogy: Sahibayn analogize to weighed items, Abu Hanifa to animals.
3
Examine the deboned meat argument
The text discusses makhlu' al-'azm (deboned meat). Even if bones are removed, the ruling remains invalid according to Abu Hanifa because the other causes (seasonal variation, etc.) still exist. The ruling is not negated by removal of one cause.
This shows the reasoning is based on multiple causes.
4
Identify the operative fatwa
The commentator explicitly states: 'wa al-fatwā 'alā qawl al-ṣāḥibayn' – the fatwa is on the statement of the two companions. Thus, practically, salam in meat is valid if properly described.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn view.
5
Apply to bird meat
Note the special case: salam in bird meat is valid by consensus if the part is specified, because bones are not considered problematic.
This exception is based on custom.

Salam Tied to a Specific Source Is Invalid
Ruling on salam when goods are specified to a particular location or tree

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on salam when goods are specified to a particular village or tree and distinguish between invalid specification and permissible quality description.

Core Idea
Salam tied to a specific source is batil due to risk of failure; but describing quality is allowed.

This lesson examines a key invalidity condition in salam (forward sale) contracts. When the subject matter is specified to a particular location or tree, the contract becomes void because delivery may become impossible. The Hanafi school bases this on a hadith warning against taking wealth unjustly if the crop fails. However, if the specification is merely a description of quality, such as 'Syrian wheat,' the salam remains valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is salam (forward sale) of wheat from a specific village or dates from a specific palm tree. The ruling is batil (invalid) because delivery depends on a source that may fail.
2
Understand the reason
The reason given is the possibility that an affliction (e.g., blight, drought) may destroy the crop, making delivery impossible and leading to unjust taking of wealth.
3
Examine the dalil
The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Do you see if Allah prevents the fruit, by what right does any of you take his brother's wealth?' This hadith, though about selling fruits before they ripen, is applied analogically to salam on specific sources.
The hadith is from Sahih al-Bukhari (Ibn 'Abbas).
4
Apply the exception
If the attribution to a village is merely to describe quality (e.g., 'high-quality wheat like that of village X'), not to require delivery from that exact place, the salam is valid because there is no risk of unavailability.
Example: 'Syrian wheat' is a quality description, not a geographic restriction.
5
Distinguish the two cases
Invalid: explicit tie to a specific source (e.g., 'wheat from this village'). Valid: general description of quality (e.g., 'wheat of Syrian type'). The key is whether delivery from that specific source is required.

Core Idea
The commissioner has khiyar al-ru'yah; the manufacturer does not—this is the correct Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq's chapter on salam (salam) deals with two issues: the khiyar al-ru'yah (option of inspection) for the commissioner (mustasni') in an istisna' contract, and the effect of stipulating a term (ajal) on the nature of the contract. The author begins by stating that the commissioner has khiyar al-ru'yah upon seeing the manufactured item; he may accept or reject it. This is because he bought something unseen, unlike salam where the subject matter is a debt in the dhimmah. The manufacturer has no such option, as he sold something unseen. The commentary notes that this is the correct position (al-asahh) based on treating istisna' as a sale, not a promise. However, a narration from Abu Hanifa gives the manufacturer an option due to potential harm. Abu Yusuf denies option for both parties, arguing the manufacturer already destroyed material to produce the item. The relied-upon position is that only the commissioner has the option. The passage then addresses the manufacturer's right to sell the item before the commissioner sees it: he may do so because the item is not yet specified. Once the commissioner sees and approves it, the manufacturer loses that right. The core of the lesson is the effect of setting a term (ajal) in istisna'. According to Abu Hanifa, if a term is stipulated in an istisna' transaction where common practice exists (like making khuffs), it becomes a salam contract, with all its stricter conditions (prepayment, full specification, etc.). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) disagree, maintaining it remains istisna', as the two contracts are fundamentally different. Abu Hanifa's argument relies on the principle that validating the contract as salam is preferable because salam is based on prophetic sunnah, while istisna' is merely customary. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that if it is salam, the seller must receive full payment upfront and the buyer has no khiyar al-ru'yah. The passage concludes with detailed rules: if the term is for respite (istimhal), it leans toward salam; if for haste (isti'jal), it remains istisna'. The great scholar al-Hinduwani differentiates based on who proposed the term—if the commissioner, it's for haste; if the manufacturer, for respite. This intra-madhhab disagreement is significant for practical transactions.

Key Terms
السلم (al-salam): A sale where the price is paid immediately for a deferred delivery of a specified commodity (a type of forward contract).
الاستصناع (al-istisna'): A contract to manufacture a specific item, typically with deferred payment and no immediate price.
الأجل (al-ajal): The term or deadline set for delivery of the manufactured item.

Suspending I'tikaf by Condition: Intra-Hanafi Correction
Intra-Hanafi dispute on suspension of i'tikaf by condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on suspending i'tikaf by a condition and distinguish between the weak narration and the relied-upon majority position.

Core Idea
A vowed i'tikaf can be suspended by a condition, contrary to a weak narration that denies it.

This passage addresses a common question: can a person make their i'tikaf dependent on a condition (e.g., 'if Zaid returns')? The text initially says no, because i'tikaf is not like a sworn matter. However, the commentator immediately corrects this, pointing out a contradiction with the established consensus on vowed i'tikaf. Understanding this correction reveals how Hanafi scholars critically assess transmitted rulings and prioritize the majority view over weaker narrations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the condition
The act is i'tikaf (spiritual retreat). The condition is a suspended clause like 'if Zaid comes' attached to a vow to perform i'tikaf.
The condition makes the obligation contingent on a future event.
2
State the initial ruling from the text
The text claims i'tikaf cannot be suspended because it is 'not something that can be sworn by' (laysa mimmā yuḥlafu bihī).
This implies i'tikaf is analogous to acts that cannot be conditional, like pure acts of worship.
3
Identify the contradiction with consensus
The commentator notes that this contradicts the unanimous validity of suspending a vowed (mandhūr) i'tikaf. If a vowed i'tikaf can be conditional, then i'tikaf in general must allow suspension.
Consensus (ijmāʿ) is a strong proof that overrides a weak narration.
4
Analyze the correction by al-Bahr
Al-Bahr (a later Hanafi authority) declares this the third and worst error in the list of things that cannot be suspended, because numerous explicit texts (ṣarāʾiḥ) support suspension. He explains the initial statement is based on a weak narration (riwāyah), while the majority view accepts suspension.
This shows the critical refinement process within the madhhab.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) is that i'tikaf can be suspended by a condition, especially when it is a vowed i'tikaf. The weak narration is not operative for fatwa.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that a conditional i'tikaf is valid and must be fulfilled if the condition occurs.

Partial Payment in Sarf and the Buyer's Option on a Silver Vessel
Ruling on partial payment and claim for a silver vessel in sarf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on partial payment in sarf for a silver vessel and identify when the buyer has the option to rescind due to a claim.

Core Idea
In sarf, partial payment validates only the paid portion; the buyer gains no option if he caused the partnership.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in sarf: selling a silver vessel with only part of the price paid before separation. The Hanafi school distinguishes between the paid and unpaid portions, and also addresses the buyer's rights if a third party later claims part of the vessel. Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to the condition of hand-to-hand delivery and the nature of the item sold.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Recognize that the act is a sale of a silver vessel in a sarf transaction where only part of the price is paid. The ruling: the sale is valid for the portion paid and void for the unpaid portion.
This applies only if the parties separate after partial payment; if they separate after full payment, the whole is valid.
2
Understand the consequence of partial payment
Because sarf requires hand-to-hand delivery, the unpaid portion lacks this condition, hence fasid. The invalidity does not spread to the whole.
The vessel becomes jointly owned (musharakah) between buyer and seller in proportion to payment.
3
Analyze the buyer's option when a third party claims part of the vessel
If part of the vessel is claimed after sale and before separation, the buyer has the option to take the remainder at its share or return the sale. This is because partnership (shirkah) is considered a defect for a composite item like a vessel.
The option arises only if the claim appears before the transaction is complete (before separation).
4
Distinguish when the buyer caused the partnership
If the buyer caused the partnership by not paying fully before separation, he has no option because he agreed to the defect by delaying payment.
The text contrasts this with the case where the partnership existed at the time of contract (due to the seller's ownership), which gives the buyer an option.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Sarf (Currency Exchange) in Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents several rulings, with commentary from al-'Ayni, al-Fath, and others. It begins by discussing the sale of a silver vessel with partial payment and separation. The ruling is that the sale is valid for the portion of the price actually received before separation, and void for the unpaid portion. This is because in sarf, hand-to-hand delivery is required; if only part is received, only that part is valid. The vessel becomes jointly owned between seller and buyer in proportion to the payment. When part of the vessel is later claimed by a third party, the buyer has an option: he can take the remainder at its proportionate share of the price or cancel the sale. This is because partial ownership (shirkah) is considered a defect in a composite item like a vessel, as partition reduces its value. In contrast, if a buyer causes the partnership by not paying fully before separation, he has no option because he agreed to the defect. The passage then compares this with a lump of pure silver (nqrah). For a lump of silver, if part is claimed after delivery, the buyer has no option; he just takes the remainder proportionally. Because pure silver is homogeneous, partition does not harm it. But if the claim occurs before delivery, the buyer has the option, as the transaction was not completed. The second part of the passage deals with exchanging mixed types: selling two dirhams and one dinar for one dirham and two dinars, and selling a load of wheat and barley for double the opposite. Zufar, al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad (in one narration) argue this is invalid because the contract compares total with total, leading to unspecified apportionment across types, which is a change. The Hanafi school, however, validates it by istihsan. They argue that the contract intends absolute exchange, not specific partitioning. By interpreting it as each type being exchanged for its opposite, the contract becomes valid. This is supported by consensus when explicitly stated. The principle is that in riba transactions, the apportionment must be made in a way that validates the contract, which is done by pairing like with like in quantity and different types against each other, as illustrated by the examples.

Key Terms
صرف (sarf): Exchange of gold, silver, or currency; requires hand-to-hand delivery before separation.
إناء فضة (ina' fiddah): A silver vessel; considered a composite item (muttafiq) whose partition may be harmful.
استحقاق (istihqaq): A claim of ownership by a third party on part of the sold item.
خيار (khiyar): Option to rescind the sale due to defect or loss of part.

Lump of Silver vs. Vessel: No Option for Homogeneous Items
Ruling on claim after delivery for a pure silver lump

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the buyer has no option for a claimed portion of a pure silver lump after delivery, and distinguish this from the vessel case.

Core Idea
Partition of a homogeneous silver lump causes no harm, so partnership is not a defect and no option arises.

This lesson contrasts the earlier ruling on a silver vessel with that of a pure silver lump (nqrah). While both involve partial payment and a claim, the buyer's right to rescind depends on whether the item is composite or homogeneous. The Hanafi school uses this distinction to define when partnership constitutes a defect, providing a clear criterion based on the nature of the item.

Guided Path
1
Identify the item and the ruling
The item is a lump of pure silver (nqrah). The ruling: if part of it is claimed after delivery, the buyer takes the remainder proportionally with no option to rescind.
This assumes the claim occurs after the buyer has taken possession and they have separated.
2
Understand why there is no option
Partnership in a homogeneous item like a silver lump is not considered a defect because partition does not reduce its value. The lump can be divided without harm.
The text explicitly says: 'الشركة في النقرة لا تعد عيبا لأن التشقيص لا يضرها'.
3
Contrast with the vessel case
A silver vessel is a manufactured, composite item. Partitioning it damages its functionality and value, so partnership is a defect giving the buyer an option.
The key difference: harm caused by partition.
4
Note the exception for claims before delivery
If the claim occurs before delivery (before the buyer takes full possession), the buyer does have an option even for silver lumps, because the transaction is not complete.
This follows the general principle that the sale is not complete until delivery, so any defect before completion gives an option.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Sarf (Currency Exchange) in Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents several rulings, with commentary from al-'Ayni, al-Fath, and others. It begins by discussing the sale of a silver vessel with partial payment and separation. The ruling is that the sale is valid for the portion of the price actually received before separation, and void for the unpaid portion. This is because in sarf, hand-to-hand delivery is required; if only part is received, only that part is valid. The vessel becomes jointly owned between seller and buyer in proportion to the payment. When part of the vessel is later claimed by a third party, the buyer has an option: he can take the remainder at its proportionate share of the price or cancel the sale. This is because partial ownership (shirkah) is considered a defect in a composite item like a vessel, as partition reduces its value. In contrast, if a buyer causes the partnership by not paying fully before separation, he has no option because he agreed to the defect. The passage then compares this with a lump of pure silver (nqrah). For a lump of silver, if part is claimed after delivery, the buyer has no option; he just takes the remainder proportionally. Because pure silver is homogeneous, partition does not harm it. But if the claim occurs before delivery, the buyer has the option, as the transaction was not completed. The second part of the passage deals with exchanging mixed types: selling two dirhams and one dinar for one dirham and two dinars, and selling a load of wheat and barley for double the opposite. Zufar, al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad (in one narration) argue this is invalid because the contract compares total with total, leading to unspecified apportionment across types, which is a change. The Hanafi school, however, validates it by istihsan. They argue that the contract intends absolute exchange, not specific partitioning. By interpreting it as each type being exchanged for its opposite, the contract becomes valid. This is supported by consensus when explicitly stated. The principle is that in riba transactions, the apportionment must be made in a way that validates the contract, which is done by pairing like with like in quantity and different types against each other, as illustrated by the examples.

استحقاق (istihqaq): A claim of ownership by a third party on part of the sold item.

Mixed Currency Exchange: Hanafi Validation by Istihsan
Inter-madhhab dispute over mixed currency exchange in sarf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on exchanging mixed types (e.g., dirhams and dinar) and explain the istihsan-based proof against the Shafi'i/Zufar objection.

Core Idea
Exchanging two dirhams and one dinar for one dirham and two dinars is valid by interpreting each type against its counterpart.

This lesson confronts a direct inter-madhhab dispute over the validity of exchanging mixed currency bundles. The Hanafi school, relying on istihsan, holds that such contracts are valid by construing each type as exchanged for the opposite type. The opposing school, including Imam Zufar within the Hanafi tradition, argues that total-for-total comparison necessitates a common split, which changes the contract. The Hanafi response refines the nature of the contract and the legitimacy of changing description without changing essence.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that selling two dirhams and one dinar for one dirham and two dinars is valid. Also, selling a load of wheat and a load of barley for double the opposite (e.g., two loads of each) is valid.
This is the majority view of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad.
2
Identify the opposing view
Zufar (a Hanafi companion), al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad (in one narration) say this contract is invalid. They argue that total-for-total comparison implies a common undivided portion, which is changed when applied to different types.
The opposition is based on the principle that changing the contract's nature to validate it is impermissible.
3
Understand the Hanafi proof (istihsan)
The contract intends absolute exchange, not specific partitioning. When literal total-for-total is impossible due to riba (like with like), the contract is interpreted in the most valid manner: each type is paired with its opposite. This is a change in description, not in the essence of exchange.
The Hanafi rely on the maxim: 'The valid is legislated in its essence and description; the invalid only in its essence.'
4
Examine the counter-argument and response
The opponents say such interpretation is a change that invalidates. The Hanafi respond that the essence of the contract (transfer of ownership in exchange for ownership) remains intact; only the description of how the exchange is apportioned changes. This is permissible because the default is validity.
5
Note the consensus when explicitly stated
If at the time of sale the parties explicitly say that each type is exchanged for the opposite type, it is valid by consensus. This supports the Hanafi interpretation that the implicit contract is also valid.

Commentary
This passage from the Book of Sarf (Currency Exchange) in Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents several rulings, with commentary from al-'Ayni, al-Fath, and others. It begins by discussing the sale of a silver vessel with partial payment and separation. The ruling is that the sale is valid for the portion of the price actually received before separation, and void for the unpaid portion. This is because in sarf, hand-to-hand delivery is required; if only part is received, only that part is valid. The vessel becomes jointly owned between seller and buyer in proportion to the payment. When part of the vessel is later claimed by a third party, the buyer has an option: he can take the remainder at its proportionate share of the price or cancel the sale. This is because partial ownership (shirkah) is considered a defect in a composite item like a vessel, as partition reduces its value. In contrast, if a buyer causes the partnership by not paying fully before separation, he has no option because he agreed to the defect. The passage then compares this with a lump of pure silver (nqrah). For a lump of silver, if part is claimed after delivery, the buyer has no option; he just takes the remainder proportionally. Because pure silver is homogeneous, partition does not harm it. But if the claim occurs before delivery, the buyer has the option, as the transaction was not completed. The second part of the passage deals with exchanging mixed types: selling two dirhams and one dinar for one dirham and two dinars, and selling a load of wheat and barley for double the opposite. Zufar, al-Shafi'i, and Ahmad (in one narration) argue this is invalid because the contract compares total with total, leading to unspecified apportionment across types, which is a change. The Hanafi school, however, validates it by istihsan. They argue that the contract intends absolute exchange, not specific partitioning. By interpreting it as each type being exchanged for its opposite, the contract becomes valid. This is supported by consensus when explicitly stated. The principle is that in riba transactions, the apportionment must be made in a way that validates the contract, which is done by pairing like with like in quantity and different types against each other, as illustrated by the examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling for sarf of adulterated coins and apply the condition of i'tibar (dominant consideration) to determine permissibility of disparity.

Core Idea
In sarf, the dominant metal determines the ruling — if adulterant dominates, disparity is allowed; if not, it's prohibited.

This lesson unpacks a key ruling from the chapter of sarf (currency exchange) in Kanz al-Daqa'iq. You will learn how Hanafi fiqh handles coins mixed with base metal. The principle of i'tibar (consideration of the dominant substance) is central, and the conditions of hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) and equality for same-genre exchange are examined. By the end, you will be able to apply this framework to real-world coin exchange scenarios.

This illustrates the stricter ruling when no element dominates.

In all sarf transactions, hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) in the session is required to avoid riba.

Vowing to Give All Property: What Must Be Given and What May Be Kept
Ruling with proof on nadhr and wealth categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the types of wealth that must be given in charity when one vows to give all property, and explain the Hanafi rule that allows keeping basic sustenance.

Core Idea
A vow to give all property binds only for zakatable wealth; you may keep enough to live on.

A person may make a vow (nadhr) to give all his property in charity. Does this mean he must give away everything, including his home, furniture, and land? The Hanafi school, drawing on Quranic principles of charity, provides a nuanced answer: the vow is binding, but the obligation is limited to types of wealth that are subject to zakat. Moreover, the person may retain enough for his own sustenance and that of his dependents. In this lesson, we will examine the ruling, its proof, and the practical application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is the act being discussed? A person vows to give all his property in charity. The Hanafi position: this is a binding obligation (wajib), but it does not require giving everything. Instead, the person must give charity from certain types of wealth.
The ruling is based on istihsan and Quranic verses about charity.
2
Locate the dalil
What proofs are cited? Two Quranic verses are quoted: 'And in their wealth is a known right' (70:24) and 'Take from their wealth a charity' (9:103). The 'known right' implies a specific portion, not all wealth. The command 'take from their wealth' indicates taking from some, not all.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How do these verses prove the ruling? The phrase 'known right' shows that charity is due only on a specified amount, not the entire wealth. The phrase 'from their wealth' shows that charity is taken from certain types of wealth, just as zakat is taken from specific categories.
4
Identify the exemption for sustenance
Even if a person's entire wealth falls under the categories to be given, the law allows him to keep enough for his own sustenance and that of his dependents. The later scholars specified amounts based on the person's occupation: a day's food for an artisan, a month's for a landlord, a year's for a farmer, and for a merchant, enough to generate further wealth.
5
Note the ikhtilaf with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that a conditional vow (e.g., 'if I recover, I will give all my wealth') is an oath; breaking it requires expiation. The Hanafi school distinguishes this as a binding vow of charity, not an oath.
