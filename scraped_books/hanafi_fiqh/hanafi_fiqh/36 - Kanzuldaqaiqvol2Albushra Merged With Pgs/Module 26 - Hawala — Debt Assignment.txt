Definition and Validity of Hawala
Defines hawala, provides proofs, and states conditions for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of hawala, identify its conditions for validity, and explain the proof for its permissibility from hadith and reason.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers debt only; consent of creditor and assignee is required.

In this passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, al-Nasafi defines hawala (assignment of debt) and lays down its essential conditions. He then provides textual and rational proof for its validity and notes the inter-madhhab dispute over the assignee's consent. Understanding these foundations is crucial for correctly applying this contract in practice.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Hawala is a transfer of debt from one person's liability (dhimma) to another's. Note that it applies only to debts (dayn), not to physical property ('ayn).
This definition is standard in the Hanafi school.
2
Identify the conditions
The debt must be known (ma'loom), the contract must be for a debt, and the consent of both the creditor (muhtal) and the assignee (muhtal 'alayh) is required. The debtor's (muhil's) consent is not necessary.
The condition of known debt excludes unknown or disputed amounts.
3
Examine the proofs
The author cites the hadith: 'Procrastination by the wealthy is injustice, and if one of you is referred to a wealthy person, let him accept.' The imperative indicates permissibility. Rational proof: the assignee is able to pay, so it is permissible.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut and zanni al-dalala, but sufficient for permissibility.
4
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute
Shafi'i and Ahmad hold that the assignee's consent is not required; Malik requires it only if the assignee is an enemy. The Hanafi school requires it because no one can be compelled to undertake a debt.
The thamarah al-khilaf: without consent, the hawala is void in Hanafi but valid in Shafi'i.
5
Understand the tarjih
The author follows the position that consent of the assignee is required, and the debtor's consent is not needed. This is the mufta bihi position.
Some sources like al-Quduri mention debtor consent, but the preferred view is otherwise.

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq introduces the Book of Hawala (Assignment of Debt). The text first defines hawala as 'the transfer of debt from one liability to another' and states that it is valid only for debts (dayn), not for specific physical property ('ayn). It requires the consent of two parties: the creditor (muhtal) and the assignee (muhtal 'alayh). The commentary then provides evidence for its validity: a hadith from Abu Dawud where the Prophet commands following the referral to a wealthy person, and rational proof that the assignee is able to fulfill the debt. The text explains that hawala is only for known debts, not unknown ones, and not for non-monetary rights. There is a discussion on the necessity of the assignee's consent, noting that the Hanafi school requires it, while al-Shafi'i and Ahmad do not, and Malik requires it only if the assignee is an enemy. The consent of the debtor (muhil) is not required according to the preferred view, though some sources mention it. The passage concludes by reaffirming that hawala is not valid for physical property, which requires physical transfer.

Key Terms
الحوالة (al-ḥawālah): Transfer of a debt from one person's liability to another's.
المحيل (al-muḥīl): The original debtor who transfers his debt to another (the assignee).
المحتال (al-muḥtāl): The creditor who accepts the transfer (also called muḥāl).
المحتال عليه (al-muḥtāl ʿalayh): The person who accepts the debt and becomes the new debtor (assignee).

Discharge of the Muhil: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Debt vs. Claim
Intra-Hanafi dispute on nature of discharge in hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the discharge of the muhil and apply the practical differences in the two scenarios of release and pledge.

Core Idea
The muhil's discharge from the claim does not automatically discharge him from the debt.

Upon acceptance of hawala, the muhil is discharged from the debt according to the majority of Hanafi scholars. However, two leading disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differed on the scope of this discharge. Abu Yusuf held that the muhil is discharged from both the debt and the right to demand, while Muhammad held that he is discharged only from the right to demand, leaving the debt intact. This seemingly subtle difference has concrete implications in subsequent transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
Read the passage and state Abu Yusuf's and Muhammad's views on discharge. Note the exact wording: Abu Yusuf says 'discharged from the debt and the right to demand'; Muhammad says 'discharged from the right to demand only'.
The difference hinges on whether the debt itself is transferred (Abu Yusuf) or only the claim (Muhammad).
2
Understand the thamarah (practical consequence)
The passage gives two scenarios: (a) if the creditor later releases the muhil from the debt, and (b) if a pledgor assigns the creditor's debt. For each, the ruling differs based on the view adopted.
These scenarios are not exhaustive but illustrate the real-world impact.
3
Apply to the release scenario
If the creditor releases the muhil from the debt, Abu Yusuf says it is invalid (since the debt is already discharged), while Muhammad says it is valid (since the debt remained). Determine which view leads to the creditor being able to still claim.
Abu Yusuf's view is that the muhil is already free; release has no subject.
4
Apply to the pledge scenario
If a pledgor assigns the creditor's debt and later the assignee defaults, Abu Yusuf allows the pledgor to reclaim the pledge (as if the debt is gone), while Muhammad does not (as the debt remains). Distinguish the rulings.
This shows that the scope of discharge affects ancillary rights like pledge reclamation.

Commentary
This passage discusses the effect of hawala on the original debtor (muhil). Upon acceptance by both the creditor and the assignee, the muhil is discharged from the debt. However, there is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad on the nature of this discharge: Abu Yusuf holds that he is discharged from both the debt and the right to demand payment, while Muhammad holds that he is discharged only from the right to demand, the debt remaining in his liability. The practical consequence is shown in two scenarios: (1) if the creditor later releases the muhil from the debt, Abu Yusuf says it is invalid (since the debt is already gone), while Muhammad says it is valid; (2) in a pledge scenario, the pledgor's right to reclaim the pledge differs. The passage then addresses the exception of tawa (loss of right): the creditor can only revert to the muhil if his right is lost through the assignee's denial with oath or death insolvent. A disagreement with al-Shafi'i is noted: Shafi'i holds that the creditor cannot revert even in case of tawa, as the debt is permanently transferred. The Hanafi response is that the creditor implicitly consented on condition of payment, so if the condition fails, the right returns. The passage also details the procedures for claiming tawa and mentions a third form added by the two companions (court declaration of insolvency), while Abu Hanifa did not accept this as tawa.

Key Terms
التوى (al-tawā): Loss or destruction of the creditor's right, specifically by the assignee's denial with oath or death insolvent.
الموت مفلساً (al-mawt muflisan): Death in a state of insolvency, without leaving assets to pay debts.
جحد الحوالة (jahd al-ḥawāla): Denial of the hawala by the assignee, often accompanied by a sworn oath.

Tawa (Loss) and Recourse: The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Inter-madhhab dispute on reversion in case of loss of right

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tawa in Hanafi law and explain why the creditor can revert to the muhil in case of tawa, and why the Shafi'i school disagrees.

Core Idea
The creditor's recourse is limited to cases of tawa—loss of right through the assignee's denial or insolvency.

Hawala transfers the debt, but what happens if the assignee fails to pay or denies the debt? The Hanafi school recognizes an exception called tawa (loss), where the creditor's right is lost due to the assignee's denial with oath or death insolvent. In such cases, the creditor can revert to the original debtor. The Shafi'i and Hanbali schools, however, hold that the transfer is permanent and no reversion is allowed. This lesson examines the definition of tawa, the proofs of both sides, and the practical implication.

Guided Path
1
Define tawa
Read the passage to identify the forms of tawa: (1) the assignee denies the hawala and swears, (2) the assignee dies insolvent without leaving a guarantor. Note that the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) add a third form: the judge declares the assignee bankrupt during his lifetime.
The third form is based on their view that insolvency is established by court decree.
2
Identify the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi argument is that the creditor consented to the hawala only on condition of payment from the assignee. This condition is inferred from the situation (qarinat al-hal). Since the condition failed (tawa), the right returns to the muhil.
This is a form of istihsan based on implicit condition.
3
Identify the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i and Hanbali schools argue that the debt has been permanently transferred from the muhil to the assignee, analogous to release (ibra'). Once transferred, it cannot return.
They consider hawala as a complete transfer of liability.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Hanafi scholars distinguish hawala from release: release is a gift (tabarru'), while hawala is a transfer with an implied condition. Therefore, the analogy to release is not valid.
The response uses linguistic and usul principles to differentiate.
5
Apply the rule
Given a scenario, determine whether tawa has occurred and decide if the creditor can revert. Consider also the procedure for proving tawa (e.g., creditor's claim in assignee's absence).
The passage provides rules for when the creditor's claim is accepted.

Successive Assignments Cancel the First
Ruling on sequential hawala of same debt to two assignees

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on sequential hawala assignments and explain why the second cancels the first, including the exception for tawa (loss).

Core Idea
A single debt cannot be assigned twice: the second hawala cancels the first.

This lesson examines a specific scenario in hawala (assignment of debt) where a debtor assigns the same debt to two different assignees consecutively. The Hanafi school resolves this by treating the second assignment as an automatic cancellation of the first, ensuring clarity in obligations. However, an exception arises if the second assignment leads to loss (tawa) for the creditor, in which case the original debtor remains liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
Recognize that the scenario involves sequential hawala: a debtor first assigns a debt to one person, then assigns the same debt to another person, and both accept.
The debt must be the same in both assignments.
2
State the ruling
According to the text, the second assignment cancels the first, and the first assignee is discharged. The debt is now owed by the second assignee.
This is the general rule without tawa.
3
Explain the legal logic
A single debt cannot be owed to two different persons as assignees. Once the second assignment is accepted, it supersedes the first.
The creditor's right transfers to the second assignee.
4
Note the exception
If the second assignment leads to tawa (loss of the right), Abu Hanifa holds that the original debtor may be reverted to. This is an exception to the general rule.
Tawa means the creditor loses his right due to the second assignment.

The Core Ruling of Hawala: Transferor Freed, Reversion Only in Talaf
States the fundamental ruling of hawala and defines the exception of talaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the fundamental ruling of hawala and identify the two specific causes of talaf that allow the creditor to revert to the transferor.

Core Idea
Hawala transfers the debt completely, but the creditor keeps a conditional right of reversion if the debt is lost through denial with oath or death insolvent.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational ruling of hawala (transfer of debt) from the Hanafi school. The matn states that the transferor is freed upon acceptance, and the creditor may only revert to him in case of talaf—a specific legal loss. We will unpack the two forms of talaf and understand why the Hanafi school permits reversion while other schools do not.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is hawala (transfer of debt). The ruling is that the transferor (muhil) is freed from the debt by acceptance of the hawala by the creditor and transferee.
Acceptance is a condition for the transfer to be valid.
2
Locate the exception
The exception to the transferor's freedom is talaf (loss), which allows the creditor to revert and claim the debt from the transferor.
3
Define talaf
The text defines talaf as two specific events: (1) the transferee denies the hawala and swears an oath, or (2) the transferee dies insolvent.
These are the only two causes according to Abu Hanifa.
4
Understand the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi school argues that the creditor's consent to the transfer is conditional on receipt of payment. This condition is implied by custom (dilalat al-hal). If the condition fails due to talaf, the creditor's right returns to the transferor.
This distinguishes hawala from ibra' (release), where no such condition exists.
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Shafi'i and Hanbali schools hold that the transferor is freed absolutely and reversion is never allowed, even in talaf. They analogize hawala to ibra'.
The practical difference: in Shafi'i, a creditor cannot revert if the transferee denies; in Hanafi, he can.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the basic ruling of hawala (transfer of debt). The matn states that the transferor (muhil) is freed from the debt by the acceptance of the hawala by both the creditor (muhtal) and the transferee (muhtal 'alayh). The creditor has no right to revert to the transferor except in case of talaf, which is loss of the right. The text then discusses sub-issues: if the transferee later demands from the transferor what was transferred, and the transferor claims it was for a debt owed to him, the transferor must guarantee the debt. If there is disagreement between the transferor and creditor about the nature of the hawala (whether it was a transfer for a debt or a collection agency), the word is that of the transferor with his oath. The ruling that a hawala on a deposit (wadi'a) is valid is also stated, because the transferor has the ability to deliver. The commentary then details the two types of talaf and adds a third type according to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): bankruptcy declared by a judge. This difference stems from whether insolvency is established by court ruling or by actual state. The text emphasizes that talaf allows the creditor to revert to the transferor only when the hawala is still in effect; if the hawala is rescinded, reversion is possible without talaf.

The Scope of Talaf and Intra-Hanafi Differences on Bankruptcy
Defines the additional third cause of talaf according to Sahibayn and discusses sub-rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the third cause of talaf (bankruptcy by court ruling) and apply the rulings related to guarantor and dispute over insolvency.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa limits talaf to two causes; the Sahibayn add bankruptcy declared by a judge, illustrating how judicial declaration can affect contractual rights.

This lesson continues the discussion on hawala by examining the detailed conditions of talaf. The text presents additional scenarios: when the transferee leaves a guarantor, when the creditor claims in the transferee's absence, and a third type of talaf introduced by the two companions Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. We will explore the basis of this difference and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Review the two talaf causes
Recall that talaf has two causes: denial with oath and death insolvent. These are from Abu Hanifa.
2
Analyze the guarantor sub-rule
If the transferee dies insolvent but leaves a guarantor (kafil), the debt does not return to the transferor's liability because the creditor can claim from the guarantor.
This is regardless of whether the guarantor guaranteed with or without permission.
3
Examine the dispute on insolvency
If the creditor and transferor disagree on whether the transferee died insolvent, the word is that of the creditor (claimant) with an oath, because the original state is hardship (i.e., the creditor's claim that the debtor was insolvent is given weight).
This is based on legal preference for the one who asserts a fact that is contrary to the apparent state.
4

The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) add a third cause of talaf: when a judge declares the transferee bankrupt during his lifetime.
This addition is based on their view that insolvency is established by court ruling, whereas Abu Hanifa holds that insolvency is a factual state that does not require a judicial declaration.
5

The difference stems from the epistemological weight given to judicial rulings. For Sahibayn, the court's declaration is a form of evidence that transforms the actual state; for Abu Hanifa, it merely confirms but does not change the reality.
This affects whether the creditor can revert to the transferor when the transferee is bankrupt by court order.

The Transferor's Word in Disputes about Hawala
Rulings on disputes in hawala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the transferor's word is taken with oath when the transferee claims the hawala was for a debt, and explain why acceptance of hawala is not an admission of debt.

Core Idea
In a dispute over the purpose of hawala, the transferor's word is taken because acceptance of hawala does not imply debt.

Hawala involves transferring a debt to a third party. Sometimes disputes arise about the nature of the transfer: was it to pay a debt the transferor owed, or to collect a debt owed to him? The Hanafi school provides clear rules, prioritizing the transferor's word in such conflicts, based on the general principle that the denier's word is taken.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Dispute
The passage presents two scenarios: (1) The transferee demands payment from the transferor after the hawala, claiming the transfer was to settle a debt. (2) The transferor claims the hawala was an agency to collect a debt, while the creditor claims it was for a debt owed by the transferor. Recognize the parties and their claims.
In both, the transferor (muhil) denies owing a debt to the transferee (muhtal 'alayh) or denies that the hawala was for a debt.
2
Apply the Principle of Burden of Proof
The hadith 'al-bayyina 'ala man idda'a wa al-yamin 'ala man ankar' is invoked. The transferor is the denier, so his word is taken with an oath. The transferee is the claimant and must provide evidence. If the transferor admits the hawala was for a debt, he guarantees it.
Acceptance of hawala itself is not an admission of debt because hawala can also be used as agency. The transferor's oath resolves the ambiguity.
3
Note the Linguistic Basis
The word 'hawala' can be used metaphorically to mean agency (wakalah). Therefore, the transferor may have intended agency. Since there is a possibility, his interpretation is given weight, but he must still swear an oath due to the apparent contradiction.
This shows the importance of linguistic analysis in Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage covers several independent rulings. First, it discusses a scenario where the transferee (muhtal 'alayh) demands payment from the transferor (muhil) after the hawala, claiming that the transfer was to pay a debt owed by the transferor to the transferee. If the transferor admits this, he must guarantee the debt. But if the transferor claims that the hawala was for a different reason (e.g., a debt the transferee owes him), then the word is that of the transferor with his oath, because he is the denier and the principle is 'al-bayyina 'ala man idda'a wa al-yamin 'ala man ankar' (the burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier). Acceptance of hawala is not an admission of debt, as it could be for agency. Second, the passage validates a hawala where the transferor transfers something he has as a deposit (wadi'a) with the transferee. The reason given is that the transferor is more able to deliver the item, so it is permissible. Third, the passage provides a taxonomy of hawala: muqayyada (restricted) and mutlaqa (unrestricted). Muqayyada is further subdivided: (a) restricted to a specific guaranteed item (like usurped property) or a trust (deposit), (b) restricted to a debt owed by the transferee. Mutlaqa is unrestricted and can be immediate or deferred. Finally, the passage discusses the effect of destruction of the subject matter. If a restricted hawala is on a deposit and the deposit perishes, the transferee is freed because he was only obliged to deliver from that specific deposit. However, if the hawala is on a usurped item (ghasb), the transferee is not freed by its destruction because the value (qima) is a substitute; loss to a substitute is not true loss. But if the usurped item is reclaimed by evidence (i.e., the owner proves ownership and takes it), then it becomes like a deposit, and the transferee is freed if it perishes after that.

Key Terms
المقيدة (al-muqayyadah): Restricted hawala tied to a specific asset or debt.
المطلقة (al-muṭlaqah): Unrestricted hawala not tied to any specific item or debt.
الوديعة (al-wadīʿah): Deposit, a trust held by the transferee.

The Ruling of Suftajah and Its Conditional Variation
Explaining the ruling of suftajah and its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on suftajah and apply the distinction between stipulated and unstipulated benefit.

Core Idea
A loan that benefits the lender beyond repayment is problematic; stipulating the benefit makes it haram.

The term 'suftajah' refers to a loan where the lender gives money to a traveler to avoid the risk of loss, expecting repayment in a safe location. This arrangement raises the issue of loans that bring a benefit to the lender. The Hanafi school, guided by the hadith prohibiting such loans, provides a nuanced ruling that depends on whether the benefit was explicitly stipulated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its base ruling
The matn states that suftajah is 'makruh' (disliked). Identify that this is a loan arrangement where the lender benefits by avoiding travel risk.
The ruling is not absolute; it varies with conditions.
2
Locate the dalil
The text cites the hadith: 'The Prophet ﷺ prohibited a loan that brings a benefit.' This is the primary proof.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut).
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The prohibition (nahy) in Hanafi usul indicates tahrim (prohibition). The benefit (naf') in this context is the removal of road danger.
The benefit is considered usurious because it is extra beyond repayment.
4
Identify the condition that changes the ruling
The commentary clarifies: if the benefit is stipulated as a condition in the loan, it becomes haram and the loan is fasid. If not stipulated, it remains permissible, though the suftajah of merchants (implicit benefit) is makruh.
The key is stipulation (shart).
5
Apply the Hanafi tarjih
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position, per Al-Fatawa al-Sughra and Al-Waqi'at, is that conditional suftajah is prohibited, unconditional is permissible, and trade suftajah is makruh.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq on the book of Hawalah (transfer of debt). It first states a previous ruling: if the deposit (the subject of the debt) perishes, the transferor is free of liability. Then it addresses the issue of suftajah, which is a loan arrangement where the lender sends money through a borrower to a safe destination to avoid the risk of travel. The matn says suftajah is makruh (disliked), but the commentary clarifies that the ruling depends on whether the benefit (removal of risk) was stipulated as a condition. If stipulated, the loan is haram and fasid; if not stipulated, it is permissible. The suftajah of merchants (where the benefit is implicit) is disliked due to the hadith prohibiting a loan that brings benefit. The hadith 'The Prophet ﷺ prohibited a loan that brings a benefit' is cited as proof. The essential principle: any loan that confers a benefit on the lender beyond repayment is problematic, but the severity depends on stipulation.
