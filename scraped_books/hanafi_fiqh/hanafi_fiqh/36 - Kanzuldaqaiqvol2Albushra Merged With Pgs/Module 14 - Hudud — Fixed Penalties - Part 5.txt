Core Idea
For highway robbery with property theft: cut the right hand and left foot, only if nisab is reached.

Highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) endangers public safety and is punished by a severe hadd. When the robber takes property without killing, the Hanafi school prescribes amputation of the right hand and left foot from opposite sides. However, this punishment is conditional upon the stolen property reaching the nisab (minimum value of 10 dirhams per robber). The Maliki school differs, applying the hadd for any amount. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Killing by a highway robber is a hadd executed by the imam; pardon by heirs is invalid.

In this lesson, we examine the third scenario of highway robbery (qati' al-tariq): when the robber kills but does not take property. The Hanafi school holds that the punishment is execution as a hadd (fixed penalty), not as qisas (retribution). This distinction has significant practical consequences, especially regarding the possibility of pardon by the victim's heirs. We will explore the reasoning and the proof from hadith.

Crucify alive for three days then stab — deterrence outweighs mutilation concern.

This lesson examines the hudud punishment for highway robbery (hirabah) when the robber kills and takes property. The Hanafi school has a detailed procedure for crucifixion: the criminal is kept alive on the cross for three days, then stabbed to death. Guided Path

Hadd Falls When Some Highway Robbers Are Minors, Insane, or Close Relatives
Ruling on highway robbery when participants include minors, insane, or relatives.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that all highway robbers are spared from hadd when any participant is a minor, insane, or close relative of the victim, and distinguish the differing view of Abu Yusuf and the 'three' concerning sane direct actors.

Core Idea
If any highway robber is a minor, insane, or close relative, all are spared from hadd — the crime is one defective whole.

This lesson continues the chapter on highway robbery (qatʿ al-ṭarīq). The previous two cases dealt with conditions where hadd applies. Now we examine a scenario where the presence of a non-culpable participant (minor, insane, or close relative) prevents the hadd penalty from being inflicted on any of the robbers. The reasoning hinges on the unity of the crime: if part of the cause is defective, the entire ruling falls.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its exceptions
The passage states that when some of the highway robbers are minors, insane, or close relatives of the victim, none of them receive the hadd penalty. Note that this is the third case in the series.
The hadd penalty for highway robbery includes execution or crucifixion.
2
Understand the rationale: a single defective offense
The text explains that the crime is one single offense (jinaaya waahida qaa'ima bil-kull). The presence of a non-culpable participant makes the cause of the punishment defective (ba'd al-'illa), so the hadd does not apply to anyone.
This is a qiyās khafī (hidden analogy) based on the unity of the criminal act.
3
Contrast with Abu Yusuf's view
Imam Abu Yusuf holds that if the sane participants directly killed the victim, they alone are punished with hadd. The child or insane person's involvement does not shield them. If the child or insane person did the killing, then the sane ones are not punished.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilāf.
4
Apply the ruling to women and mutes
The text includes that women are generally subject to the same ruling (they can be highway robbers and hadd applies), though some say they are not. The mute is treated like a child (i.e., not mukallaf), but Abu Yusuf disagrees.
Women are considered capable of highway robbery despite physical limitations.
5
Determine the consequence when hadd falls
When hadd is not applied, the matter goes to the guardians (awliya') of the victim. They may either execute the sane killers in retaliation (qisās) or pardon them, because the victim's personal right (ḥaqq al-ʿabd) remains.
This shifts the punishment from a fixed divinely ordained penalty to a victim's right.

Learning Objective

This lesson covers two interconnected aspects of the law of highway robbery (qat' al-tariq). First, when the prescribed hadd punishment is not carried out, the victim's family gains the right to demand retaliation or grant pardon. Second, the crime itself must be proven either by the robber's confession or by the testimony of two reliable male witnesses. Understanding these rules is essential for distinguishing between hadd and private claims.

The guardian may either execute the robber as qisas (if the killing was intentional with an iron tool) or pardon him. The right belongs to the victim's heir.
The text specifies that qisas is only applicable if the killing was 'amd (intentional) with a hadid (iron tool).
3
Identify the methods of proof for highway robbery
The text states that the crime is established by a single confession (iqrar) or by the testimony of two men (shahadat rajulayn) who witnessed the act of cutting the road or the confession to it.
Two witnesses are required, not one, and they must have witnessed either the act or the confession.

The Hanafi school is strict: one confession is enough, but for testimony, two male eyewitnesses are needed.

Single strangling: Abu Hanifah says diyah, Sahibayn say qisas; repeated strangling: killed by siyasah.

This lesson dives into the specific case of death by strangulation. The Hanafi school has an internal disagreement between Abu Hanifah and his two main disciples on whether a single act of strangling warrants retaliation (qisas) or only blood money (diyah). Siyasah is discretionary punishment for public order, not a fixed hadd.

This passage from Bab Qat' al-Tariq presents a series of rulings on highway robbery (qat' al-tariq) and related matters. The text begins by stating that if the prescribed hadd punishment is not carried out against the highway robber in the situations previously mentioned, the matter is left to the guardians (awliya) of the victim. Core Idea
The dhimmi's covenant is not broken by jizyah refusal, zina with a Muslim, killing a Muslim, or private insult to the Prophet.

The dhimmi enjoys protection under the Islamic state, but the limits of that protection are debated. This lesson examines four specific acts that, according to the Hanafi school, do not automatically terminate the dhimma. Commentary
This passage covers two distinct rulings. First, the diya (blood money) for accidental killing committed by an apostate. Core Idea

For both sorcerer and zindiq, the operative ruling is execution without accepting repentance. Invalidity of Conditional Discharge and Kafala for Specific Obligations
Rulings on conditional discharge and invalid suretyship for specific obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three cases where kafala is invalid in Hanafi fiqh, explain the reasoning for each, and distinguish the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on kafala for the entity of a sold object.

Core Idea
Kafala is invalid when it involves a conditional discharge, a corporal punishment, or an entity not personally guaranteed.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq examines three specific invalidities in the contract of surety (kafala). The first relates to conditional discharge, the second to suretyship for hudud and qisas, and the third to suretyship for the entity of property that is not personally guaranteed. Underlying all three is the Hanafi principle that kafala is only valid for obligations where performance can be delegated (niyaba) and for property that is guaranteed by itself (dhaman bi nafsihi). We will explore each case and the reasoning behind the rulings, including the difference with the Shafi'i school on the last point.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three invalid types
The text explicitly states three invalidities: conditional discharge (ta'liq al-bara'a), kafala for hadd and qisas, and kafala for the entity of a sold object (mabi') and a pledged object (marhun).
Conditional discharge is not a kafala itself but a condition attached to discharge, making it invalid.
2
Understand the reason for each invalidity
For conditional discharge: it contains the meaning of ownership (tamlik), which cannot be suspended on a condition. For hadd/qisas: these punishments cannot be executed by proxy because their purpose is deterrence specific to the offender. For mabi'/marhun: before delivery, these are trust (amana) items and are guaranteed by another (e.g., the price), not by themselves.
The reasoning for conditional discharge also applies to discharge from debt, but there it is valid because it is pure tamlik.
3
Analyze the internal dispute on conditional discharge
Some Hanafi scholars hold that conditional discharge can be valid if the condition is customary. However, the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is its invalidity because it resembles tamlik, which does not accept conditionality. Al-Diraya interprets the text as referring to a condition that is not customary.
The intra-Hanafi difference does not change the main ruling in the matn.
4
Distinguish kafala for the person vs. the punishment
Kafala for the hadd or qisas itself is invalid, but kafala for the person upon whom the punishment is due is valid. This is because delivering the person is a delegable obligation, whereas inflicting the punishment is not.
This distinction applies only to corporal punishments; for financial debts, both the debt and the debtor's person can be guaranteed.
5
Compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i views on entity kafala
The Hanafi school holds that kafala for the entity of a sold object before delivery is invalid because it is not personally guaranteed. The Shafi'i school considers it valid because the object is valuable property. The practical consequence is liability: if the object perishes, the Shafi'i surety pays its value; the Hanafi surety is not liable for the entity.
This is the key inter-madhhab ikhtilaf in the passage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling on executing a hadd punishment upon a judge's order and explain why the executor bears no liability.

Core Idea
Executing a judge's hadd order is permissible; executor bears no liability because obeying authority is obligatory.

This lesson examines a key ruling from Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding the execution of hadd punishments. When a just and knowledgeable judge issues a verdict and commands a qualified individual to carry it out, that individual may do so without bearing any liability. The ruling clarifies the legal basis and conditions, drawing on the principle of obedience to those in authority. Understanding this ruling is essential for grasping the Hanafi framework of judicial authority and delegation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
Determine what act is being ruled upon (execution of hadd punishment) and the hukm level (ja'iz/permissible).
2
Identify conditions for the order
Note the conditions for the judge (just and knowledgeable) and the executor (skilled in the punishment).
The text specifies 'each who is skilled in cutting and beating.'
3
Determine liability
Understand that the executor has no liability in this world or the Hereafter. If an unintentional mistake occurs, it falls on the judge (the one for whom the ruling was executed).
4
Understand the evidence
The evidence is the obligation to obey those in authority (ulā al-amr), as per the Quranic principle (4:59) and qiyas.
Text states: 'because obedience to those in authority is obligatory.'
5
Note the authority
The ruling is attributed to Imam al-Maturidi, indicating it is a sound position within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage expands on the matn from Kanz al-Daqa'iq regarding the execution of hadd punishments by a judge's order. The ruling states that if a just and knowledgeable judge (qadi 'adil 'alim) issues a verdict of stoning, amputation, or beating against a convicted person and commands a qualified individual to carry out the punishment, it is permissible (yasa'uhu fi'luhu) for that individual to execute the order. The executor bears no liability in this world or the Hereafter because obeying those in authority is obligatory. The commentary differentiates between the judge's verbal order and the actual execution: the judge's statement 'I have ruled this' constitutes a final verdict, and the executor's action is based on a valid command. It also notes that if the executor makes a mistake unintentionally, the liability falls on the one for whom the ruling was executed (the judge or the state), not the executor. This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that the judge's order is binding. The passage is attributed to Imam al-Maturidi.

Witness Requirements for Hudud, Qisas, and Women's Cases
Specifies number and gender of witnesses for zina, hudud, qisas, and women's matters

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the required number and gender of witnesses for zina, other hudud, qisas, and manslaughter, and explain the Hanafi intra-school dispute on midwife testimony for inheritance.

Core Idea
Four men for zina, two men for other hudud and qisas; women testify only where men cannot.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies the strict evidentiary requirements in serious crimes and gender-specific matters. While zina demands four male witnesses—a near-impossible number—other hudud require only two, reflecting different standards of proof. The text also addresses childbirth and women's defects, where female testimony is accepted due to necessity, leading to a key Hanafi internal debate on its effect on inheritance.

Commentary
This passage specifies the conditions for witnesses in various categories of cases. For zina (unlawful sexual intercourse), four male witnesses are required, as per Quran 4:15 and the consensus of the Companions that fewer than four subjects the accusers to the hadd of qadhf (false accusation). The requirement of four men effectively ensures concealment, as such a crime is rarely witnessed by four. For other hudud (qadhf, drinking, theft) and qisas (retaliation), two male witnesses are required, based on the established sunna that women's testimony is not accepted in hudud and qisas. For manslaughter (qatl khata'), the Quranic rule of 2:282 applies: if two men are not available, one man and two women may testify. For matters of childbirth, virginity, and defects of women that men cannot ordinarily see, the testimony of women (specifically midwives) is accepted. There is a disagreement within the Hanafi school regarding the effect of a midwife's testimony on inheritance: Imam Abu Hanifa holds it is accepted for lineage and prayer but not inheritance; the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) accept it for inheritance as well, provided the woman is upright. The passage also notes that defects that men can observe (like an extra finger) require male testimony.

Key Terms
الحدود (al-hudud): Fixed punishments for major crimes determined by Shariah (zina, qadhf, theft, drinking).
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation in kind for murder or injury, subject to strict evidentiary rules.
القابلة (al-qabilah): Midwife; her testimony is accepted in matters related to childbirth that men cannot witness.

Agency for Paying and Receiving Rights: The Hadd and Qisas Exception
Ruling on agency with exception for hadd and qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on agency for paying and receiving rights and identify the conditions under which agency for executing hudud and qisas is invalid.

Core Idea
Agency is valid for all rights except executing hudud and qisas in the principal's absence.

In Hanafi fiqh, the general rule is that any right that a person can fulfill personally can be delegated to an agent. This includes both paying (fulfilling) and receiving (collecting) rights. However, there is a critical exception: hudud (fixed punishments) and qisas (retaliation) cannot be carried out by an agent if the principal is absent. This lesson unpacks that exception and explains the reasoning behind it, including the key distinction between paying (proving) and receiving (executing) these penalties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Ruling
Read the opening statement that agency is valid for both paying (ifa') and receiving (istifa') rights. Understand that this covers all types of rights, including financial and penal, unless an exception is stated.
The term 'ifa' can also include proving (ithbat) a right.
2
Locate the Exception
Find the exception clause 'except in hadd and qisas' (illa fi hadd wa qisas). Determine whether it applies to both paying and receiving or to receiving only by following the commentators' reasoning.
The apparent reading is that the exception covers both, but some scholars restrict it to receiving.
3
Understand the 'Udul (Shift)
Analyze why the exception is applied specifically to receiving and not to paying. Paying here means handing over the offender for punishment, which is not something that can be delegated – it is the principal's own act. Receiving means executing the punishment, which is possible to delegate but restricted.
This is a technical point about the nature of the act.
4
Distinguish Presence from Absence
Note that the prohibition applies only when the principal is absent from the court. If the principal is present or has given a direct order, agency for receiving hadd/qisas is valid. This nuance is explained in Sharh al-Niqayah.
The condition of presence removes the repulsion by doubt.
5
Grasp the Rationale
Understand the underlying principle: hudud are repelled by doubts (tudra'u bi al-shubuhat). Because an agent's involvement introduces a doubt about the principal's intent and presence, the punishment cannot be carried out in absentia. This is a consensus among all schools.
This rationale applies to both hadd and qisas.

The conclusion summarizes that agency is valid for all rights either through litigation or by payment/receipt, except for executing hadd and qisas in the principal's absence. The basis for litigation is the precedent of 'Ali (RA) appointing 'Aqil as his agent, who was eloquent. The basis for payment/receipt is the general principle: whatever one can do oneself, one can appoint an agent for. The exception for hadd and qisas is because these punishments are repelled by doubts (tudra'u bi al-shubuhat) by consensus, so they cannot be executed by a representative in the principal's absence.

Agency in Hadd and Qisas: Exception for Execution
Explains exception for hadd and qisas in agency for collection of rights.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that agency for executing (istifa') hadd and qisas is invalid when the principal is absent, and explain the reason based on the principle that hadd punishments are averted by doubts.

Core Idea
Agency to execute hadd/qisas is invalid when principal is absent because shubha of pardon exists.

This passage addresses an important exception in the law of agency (wakala). While agency is generally valid for collecting rights and even for proving claims, when it comes to executing punishments like hadd and qisas, the agent cannot act in the principal's absence. The Hanafi school holds that hadd punishments are averted by doubts, and this principle governs here. The key is the probability of pardon, which creates a doubt preventing execution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Agency is valid for fulfilling and collecting rights. This includes delivering what is owed (ifa') and receiving what is due (istifa').
The source text begins by stating this general permissibility.
2
Locate the exception
The exception is 'except in hadd and qisas'. The commentators clarify this exception pertains specifically to collection (istifa'), not proof.
The reason for this specification is that 'fulfilling' in this context means delivering the criminal, which is not delegable anyway.
3
Determine the condition for the exception to apply
The exception applies when the principal is absent. If the principal is present or gives explicit orders, execution by agent is permissible.
The text says: 'la yajuza istifa'uhuma in gha'iba al-muwakkil' – not permissible if principal absent.
4
Understand the reasoning
Hadd punishments are averted by doubts (tundaru'u bil-shubhat). In the principal's absence, there is a probability that he might have pardoned, since pardon is recommended (mandub). This probability constitutes a doubt (shubha) that prevents execution.
This is a well-known Hanafi principle applied in many areas.
5

Proving vs. Executing Hadd/Qisas: Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Explains the intra-Hanafi dispute on agency for proving hadd and qisas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on the validity of agency for proving (ithbat) hadd and qisas, and identify the relied-upon position.

Core Idea
Agency for proving hadd/qisas is valid per Abu Hanifa, but Abu Yusuf rejects it; the relied-upon view is permissibility.

While the execution of hadd and qisas cannot be delegated to an agent when the principal is absent, the issue of proving these punishments is more nuanced. Abu Hanifa holds that an agent may present evidence to establish the hadd or qisas, so that the principal can later execute it. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that if execution is not delegable, proving should also not be delegable. The middle position of Muhammad is inconsistent, and his confession as agent is not accepted. This lesson unpacks this intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and its practical implications.

The text reports this ikhtilaf explicitly.

Abu Hanifa: proving is separate from executing; the doubt only affects execution. Abu Yusuf: proving leads to execution, so if execution cannot be delegated, proving cannot either.
Abu Yusuf draws an analogy (qiyas) from execution to proof.

If the principal is absent, an agent can bring evidence to establish the crime under Abu Hanifa, enabling the principal to execute later. Under Abu Yusuf, the principal must do everything in person.
The difference is significant for those unable to attend court.

The same ikhtilaf applies when the accused appoints an agent to answer charges: Abu Hanifa allows, Abu Yusuf disallows.
This shows the consistency of each imam's position.

Abu Hanifa's view that agency for proving hadd and qisas is valid is the relied-upon position in the Hanafi school, distinguishing proof from execution.

Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Appointing an Agent for Hudud and Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether an agent can be appointed to establish qisas, hadd, etc.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the difference between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf on appointing an agent to establish hadd and qisas, and apply the rule to a new scenario.

Core Idea
Agency for establishing hudud is valid even if execution is not delegated; proof and execution are separate stages.

In Hanafi law, agency (wakala) is generally permitted for acts that can be delegated. However, when it comes to criminal penalties (hudud, qisas), a dispute arises: can an agent be appointed merely to prove the offense, while the principal executes the penalty? This passage presents the views of the three founders of the school: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and the inconsistent narrations from Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding this khilaf reveals deeper principles about the nature of hudud as rights of Allah and the separation of proof (ithbat) from execution (istifa).

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What exactly is being ruled upon? Appointing an agent to establish (ithbat) hudud and qisas. According to Abu Hanifa, it is permissible. Note that execution remains with the principal.
The text begins with 'yaguzu' indicating the relied-upon permissibility.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Abu Yusuf holds that it is not permissible. His reasoning: if execution cannot be delegated, then establishment, being a step toward execution, also cannot be delegated.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute; both imams are within the same school.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The text does not give an explicit rebuttal, but the inclusion of Abu Hanifa's view as the opening 'yaguzu' suggests it is the preferred position. The underlying rationale is that ithbat is a preparatory act distinct from istifa.
Muhammad's position is mudtarib (inconsistent) and not relied upon.
4
Extend to the accused's defense
The same khilaf applies to the accused appointing an agent to reply (jawah). Abu Hanifa allows; Abu Yusuf disallows. Also, the agent's admission is not binding due to shubha.
The principle of shubha (doubt) is central to hudud cases.
5
Determine the thamarah and implication for ta'zir
Practical difference: under Abu Hanifa, a plaintiff can hire a lawyer to file a criminal case; under Abu Yusuf, he must appear personally. The exception of hadd and qisas implies that ta'zir (discretionary punishment) can be delegated for both ithbat and istifa.
Ta'zir is less severe and has fewer shubha restrictions.

Commentary
This passage discusses an intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the validity of appointing an agent to establish (prove) certain penal claims such as qisas, hadd for false accusation (qadhf), and theft. Abu Hanifa permits such appointment: the agent can bring evidence, and once the right is established, the principal may execute the penalty. Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that if the agent cannot execute the penalty, he also cannot establish it. Muhammad's position is inconsistent (mudtarib), meaning different transmissions from him. The same difference applies when the accused (the one on whom hadd is due) appoints an agent to reply (to defend) – Abu Hanifa allows it, Abu Yusuf does not. Additionally, the agent's admission (iqrar) against the accused is not accepted due to doubt (shubha) that the accused did not authorize admission. Finally, the text notes that by exception, limiting the rule to hadd and qisas implies that appointment to establish ta'zir (discretionary punishment) is valid, as ta'zir does not have the same gravity of shubha.

Key Terms
إثبات (ithbāt): Establishing or proving a legal claim, such as by presenting evidence in court.
حد القذف (ḥadd al-qadhf): The fixed punishment for false accusation of unchastity.
مضطرب (muḍṭarib): Inconsistent or contradictory; used here to describe conflicting narrations from Imam Muhammad.
