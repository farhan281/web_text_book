Manumission for a Consideration: Third-Party Payment and Marriage Condition
Explains ruling when third party offers payment for slave-girl's manumission with marriage condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a third party promises payment for manumission under a condition and distinguish between binding and non-binding conditions.

Core Idea
A condition of payment from a third party in manumission is not binding unless it implies a purchase.

This lesson examines a specific transaction where a third party offers to pay for a slave-girl's freedom on condition that she marries him. The Hanafi school distinguishes between manumission and divorce (khul') regarding third-party liability. We will explore the default ruling and the effect of adding the phrase 'for me'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its conditions
The act is manumission by the master at the request of a third party who promises payment of 1000 dirhams on condition that the master marries the slave-girl to the third party.
Consider who is making the promise (third party) and who is performing the manumission (master).
2
Determine the binding nature of the condition
In Hanafi law, manumission is an act of piety; stipulating a payment from a third party is invalid. Thus, the condition is void, the manumission takes effect, and the third party owes nothing.
The master still receives the reward from Allah, not the payment.
3
Contrast with khul' (divorce for compensation)
In khul', a third party can validly guarantee the compensation, and if the husband divorces, the third party owes the amount. The difference arises because in khul', the third party stands in place of the wife.
This contrast highlights that manumission cannot be made contingent on payment from an outsider.
4
Analyze the addition of 'for me' (عني)
If the third party says 'Free her for me for 1000 on condition you marry her to me', the word 'for me' implies a purchase of the slave-girl by legal implication (iqtida'). Hence, the payment is split between the value of the slave-girl and her dower.
Implication makes the unspoken (purchase) part of the spoken contract to clarify it.
5
Apply the division method
Divide the 1000 dirhams between the slave-girl's market value and her proper dower. The third party owes the share corresponding to the value (since he receives ownership) and does not owe the dower share (since the marriage may not occur).
Example: if value is 2000 and dower 1000, the 1000 is split 2/3 (666.66) for value and 1/3 (333.33) for dower; the dower share is dropped if marriage fails.

Commentary
This passage discusses the chapter on manumission for a consideration. It presents two scenarios: first, if a third party instructs a master to free a slave-girl in exchange for one thousand dirhams, on condition that the master marries her to the third party. If the master frees her but she refuses to marry, the slave-girl becomes free and the third party owes nothing. This is because in Hanafi law, stipulating a payment from a third party in manumission is invalid; the manumission takes effect but the condition is void. The text contrasts this with the case of khul' (divorce for compensation), where a third party can validly guarantee payment. Second, if the third party adds the word 'for me' (عني), the thousand is divided between the slave-girl's value and her dower of the like. This is because saying 'for me' implies a purchase of the slave-girl, so the payment is apportioned. The method of division is explained: add the value and the dower, then allocate the thousand accordingly. The commentary illustrates with examples and emphasizes that the payment only applies to what is actually received.

Rulings of Umm Walad and Proof of Lineage
Defines istilad, outlines rights/restrictions of umm walad, and discusses lineage proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a slave woman becomes umm walad and apply the Hanafi rule that lineage requires the master’s acknowledgment for the first child.

Core Idea
The umm walad cannot be sold; her child’s lineage requires the master’s acknowledgment for the first child.

This lesson examines the rulings of istilad (having a child from a slave woman) and the special status of umm walad. The passage defines the two ways she acquires this status and the legal consequences: she cannot be sold, her earnings belong to the master, and lineage of her children follows specific rules. We will walk through the Hanafi proofs and the dispute with other schools regarding lineage establishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two ways istilad occurs
Determine from the text the two scenarios that make a slave woman an umm walad: the master claims the child of his slave woman, or a husband comes to own his wife who is a slave woman and who gave birth to him.
The second scenario invalidates the marriage, and she becomes umm walad without a claim.
2
State the ruling on selling umm walad
The umm walad cannot be sold or transferred to another owner (lam tumlik). This is based on the hadith from Ibn Abbas and the story of Mariya al-Qibtiyya.
This ruling is a proof against the Zahiris who permitted her sale.
3
Explain the lineage rule for the first child
Lineage of the first child is not established unless the master explicitly acknowledges the child as his. Once acknowledged, the mother becomes a farash (legal bed).
Subsequent children are automatically attributed to the master without further acknowledgment.
4
Compare the Hanafi proof for lineage with the Shafi'i view
Hanafi: she does not become farash by intercourse alone; if she did, she would owe idda upon separation, which she does not. Shafi'i: intercourse itself is more conducive to lineage than marriage contract, so acknowledging intercourse should suffice.
The Hanafi proof relies on the absence of idda to deny farash status.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Shafi'i, if the master acknowledges intercourse, the child is attributed to him even if he does not acknowledge the child. Under Hanafi, acknowledgment of the child is required; acknowledgment of intercourse alone is insufficient.
This difference has direct implications for inheritance and custody.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq explains the rulings of istilad (having a child from a slave woman) and the status of umm walad (mother of the child). The author first defines istilad linguistically and legally, then states the two ways it occurs: the master's claim of the child, or a husband owning his wife who is a slave and has borne him a child. The slave woman who bears her master's child is called umm walad, a term of predominant usage. The text then addresses the key ruling: the umm walad cannot be sold or transferred to another owner (lam tumlik), based on hadith from Ibn Abbas and the story of Mariya al-Qibtiyya. This is a proof against the Zahiris who permitted her sale. She can be cohabited, employed, rented, and married (after istibra) because the master's ownership remains. The master retains her earnings and other benefits. Regarding lineage: the first child's lineage is only established if the master acknowledges it. Once he acknowledges the first child, she becomes a farāsh (like a wife), and subsequent children are automatically attributed to him without need for acknowledgment. The passage then engages in a dispute with al-Shafi'i (and Malik and Ahmad) who hold that lineage is established by the mere fact of intercourse, even if he withdrew. The Hanafi argument is that she does not have the status of farāsh by intercourse alone, because if she did, she would owe a waiting period (idda) when her farāsh ends. Since she does not owe idda, lineage is only from acknowledgment. Finally, the passage notes that if the master acknowledges a pregnancy and the child is born after six months (minimum gestation), the lineage is established; if after more than six months, it is not. Regarding anal intercourse, the child is attributed according to Malik and Ahmad, and weakly by Shafi'i.

Key Terms
استيلاد (istilad): Legally, having a child from one's slave woman, which changes her status to umm walad.
أم ولد (umm walad): A slave woman who bears a child to her master, acquiring special rights and restrictions.
فراش (farash): The legal status of a woman (wife or slave) whose bed legally attributes children to the man; here used for the slave woman after the master acknowledges the first child.

Core Idea
The umm walad of a kafir does not become free instantly upon Islam but must earn her freedom through sa'aya.

When a non-Muslim master's umm walad converts to Islam, her status changes: she can no longer remain in his ownership, but the master's property rights must be respected. The Hanafi school balances these interests by requiring her to work to pay off her value. Imam Zufar, however, argued for immediate freedom with a debt. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind the main position and the practical differences.

Guided Path
1

Umm walad and mudabbara have different values: one-third vs two-thirds of her slave value.
2

The main position: she is not freed immediately; she must perform sa'aya (work to earn her value). For the umm walad, the amount is one-third of her value; for the mudabbara, two-thirds. She is like a mukatiba (contract slave) except she is not returned to slavery if she fails to pay.

The practical consequence: under the main view, she remains a slave until she completes sa'aya; under Zufar's view, she is free immediately but owes a debt. The practical difference: Hanafi: she becomes umm walad and is freed upon the master's death. Shafi'i: she remains a slave and does not become umm walad.

Joint Ownership: Claiming Child and Financial Liability
Ruling on joint slave-girl: one partner claims child, she becomes umm walad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling when a partner claims a child from a jointly-owned slave-girl, including the financial obligations and the intra-Hanafi difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
If one partner claims a child from a jointly-owned slave-girl, lineage is established, she becomes umm walad, and the claimant must pay half her value and half her dower, but not the child's value.

This passage deals with a common scenario in classical property law: a slave-girl owned by two partners gives birth. One partner claims the child. The Hanafi school provides a detailed ruling on lineage, umm walad status, and financial compensation. Additionally, there is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether she becomes all umm walad immediately or only the claiming partner's share. Understanding this ruling clarifies the principle that lineage is indivisible and that umm walad status follows accordingly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
A slave-girl is owned jointly by two partners. She gives birth. One partner claims the child. What happens?
2
State the ruling on lineage
Lineage is established from the claiming partner because he has ownership in half the slave-girl, so his claim is valid in that half. Then, because lineage is indivisible, it is established for the whole child.
The text says: 'if a slave-girl is between two partners and she gives birth, and one of them claims the child, his lineage is established from him... because lineage is indivisible.'
3
State the ruling on umm walad status
The slave-girl becomes umm walad. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), she becomes all umm walad immediately because istilad (motherhood of a child) is indivisible. According to Abu Hanifa, only the claiming partner's share becomes umm walad; he then acquires the partner's share by paying half her value.
The mufta bihi is the view of the Sahibayn: she becomes all umm walad.
4
Explain the financial liability
The claimant must pay half the value of the slave-girl (assessed at the time of conception) to his partner, and half her dower (mahr al-mithl for intercourse with a slave-girl). He does NOT pay the child's value, because the child was conceived free from his part.
The value is assessed at conception because umm walad status relates to that time.
5
Note the rationale for not paying the child's value
The child is considered free from the father's side because lineage is traced to the time of conception. Since the father had ownership in half the slave-girl at conception, the child is born on his ownership and thus free.
'The claimant is not liable for the child's value, because the child was conceived as originally free, since lineage is traced back to the time of conception, and the guarantee is required at that time.'

Oath on Wife's Spinning: Custom vs Ownership
Intra-Hanafi dispute on oath and hadi obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) regarding an oath on a wife's spinning and apply the Hanafi principle of custom (urf) to derive the ruling.

Core Idea
Custom (urf) binds the husband's oath: wife's spinning is presumed from his cotton.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute concerning an oath that triggers a hadi (sacrificial animal sent to Mecca) when the wife wears clothing made from her own spinning. While at first glance the issue may seem about ownership of raw materials, the debate reveals deeper Hanafi principles about the role of custom (urf) in defining property rights and the scope of vows. The disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hinges on whether a wife's spinning, as a customary act, establishes the husband's ownership for the purpose of an oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its condition
The husband swears: 'If you wear from your spinning, it is a hadi.' The condition is the wife wearing a garment made from yarn she has spun. Determine what triggers the obligation.
The hadi is a form of charity in Mecca, similar to the hadi of Hajj.
2
State the point of dispute
The core disagreement: must the cotton be owned by the husband at the time of the oath for the hadi to become due? Abu Hanifa says no; the two companions say yes unless the cotton was already his property.
This is based on the principle that a vow is only valid over something owned or attributable to ownership.
3
Present Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa relies on custom (urf): customarily, a wife spins from her husband's cotton. Additionally, if she spins without permission, the garment belongs to the husband, showing her spinning is a cause of ownership.
This is an argument from custom and analogy (qiyas).
4
Present the two companions' position and proof
The companions argue that the cotton must be owned at the time of the oath because wearing or spinning does not prove ownership (one can wear borrowed items; the wife may spin her own cotton).
They require a direct link to ownership at the moment the oath is made.
5
Derive the practical difference and note consensus
Under Abu Hanifa, the hadi is due even if the cotton was bought after the oath; under the companions, it is not due until ownership at the time of oath is proven. If the cotton was already owned at the time of the oath, there is consensus that the hadi is due.
This thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) directly affects whether the husband must sacrifice an animal.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a Muslim son's duty toward his polytheist father in battle and explain the self-defense exception.

Core Idea
A Muslim son must not kill his polytheist father; he steps aside for another, but self-defense permits it.

This lesson addresses a sensitive battlefield scenario: a Muslim son faces his own polytheist father. The Hanafi school sets a clear default — the son must not be the one to kill his father, but must protect the community and himself. The passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq and its commentary explains the reasoning and extends the ruling to other relatives. You will learn the precise obligations and exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary act and ruling
Recognize that the act is a Muslim son encountering his polytheist father in battle. The default hukm is prohibition of initiating killing (wajib to abstain).
The text says 'فليمتنع عن قتله' — he must refrain from killing him.
2
Understand the alternative actions
If possible, the son should leave the father for another Muslim to kill. If no one else, he must detain the father rather than let him return to fight.
Detaining is a lesser harm than killing.
3
Analyze the self-defense exception
If the father attacks the son and the son cannot repel him except by killing, then killing is permitted. The text uses a qiyas: if a Muslim father may be killed in self-defense, a disbelieving father is even more deserving.
This is based on the principle of daf' (repelling harm).
4
Compare with other relatives
The ruling extends: killing a disbelieving brother, uncle, or son is not disliked (except a baghi brother who is Muslim). Each has a basis in the absence of 'ismah or obligation to preserve.
A baghi (rebel) Muslim cannot be killed because he retains inviolability.
5

Shafi'is generally permit killing but consider it makruh. The Hanafi position is stricter in default but allows self-defense more explicitly.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf in fiqh.

Core Idea
The missing person is legally alive for his own rights but dead for others' – a guardian preserves his wealth and takes his acknowledged debts.

The Hanafi school defines the missing person as one whose location, life, and death are unknown. Because he cannot manage his own affairs, the judge appoints a guardian to protect his rights. A missing person's wife and lineal ascendants/descendants are maintained without court order; others are not, as their maintenance requires a ruling in absentia.

After appointing a guardian, the judge must ensure that the dependents of the missing person are supported. The Hanafi school sets a clear criterion: only those relatives who would receive maintenance without a court order when the person is present are entitled during his absence. This prevents the need for a ruling against the absent person.

Guided Path
1
List the categories of relatives mentioned
The text specifies 'relative by birth' (walad) including parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren; and the wife. Non-lineal relatives like siblings and uncles are excluded.
Walad here means lineal descent, not just offspring.
2
Understand the governing principle
Maintenance of ascendants, descendants, and the wife does not require a court ruling when the person is present. Therefore, it can be provided during absence without a ruling.
For non-lineal relatives, maintenance depends on a court ruling because it is need-based and contestable.
3
Apply the principle to absence
Since maintenance for lineal relatives and wife is not conditional on a court order in presence, it can be given from the missing person's wealth during absence.
The guardian may spend without seeking a judgment.
4
Identify the exception for non-lineal relatives
Maintenance for siblings, uncles, etc., requires a qadi's order when the person is present. Since such an order cannot be made in absentia, they do not receive maintenance.
The text explicitly says 'li-anna nafqatahum tatawaqqaf 'ala al-qada'.

Book of the Missing Person: concerning the explanation of its rulings. He is an absent one whose location, life, and death are unknown. So the judge appoints someone to take his right, preserve his wealth, manage it, and spend from it on his relative by birth and his family who are seeking him and whose news has been cut off and trace hidden, so by effort they may reach the goal, perhaps the meeting is delayed until the Day of Summoning. The appointment of a guardian for his wealth is for his benefit. His statement 'who takes his right' means he collects his produce and the debt that the debtor acknowledged, because it is a matter of preservation; so he may litigate for a debt established by contract, but not for a debt not acknowledged, nor for a share in property in another's hand, because he is not an owner nor his deputy, but only an agent for collection from the judge, and he does not own litigation by agreement due to its implying a ruling against an absent person. The known dispute among companions is regarding one whom the owner appointed for collection of a debt: according to Abu Hanifa he owns litigation, according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) he does not. He preserves his wealth from people after collection and manages it: there is no liability for loss because if preservation in form is impossible, it is achieved in meaning. He spends from it on his relative by birth, i.e., on his relatives by lineage like parents, grandparents, great-grandparents, children, grandchildren. And his statement 'and his wife' means he spends on his wife as well, because maintenance of ascendants, descendants, and wife does not depend on a court ruling; so it is assistance for them. Therefore if they obtain his wealth they take it without a ruling, unlike non-lineal relatives like brother, uncle, maternal uncle; they are not spent on because their maintenance depends on a court ruling, which is against an absent person and not permissible. The principle is that whoever deserves maintenance from his wealth while present without a court ruling also deserves it while absent, and whoever does not deserve it without a ruling while present does not deserve it by court ruling while absent, because maintenance then becomes obligatory by ruling and ruling against an absent person is prevented. And he does not separate them, i.e., the judge does not separate between the missing person and his wife absolutely. Malik said: after four years, the judge separates them, she observes the 'idda of death, then marries if she wishes. Thus did 'Umar judge in the case of the wife of the man whom the jinn had taken, and sufficient a precedent. And because the missing person prevented her right by absence, so the judge separates after the period by analogy with ila' and impotence. Our evidence: the Prophet's statement regarding the wife of the missing person: 'She is his wife until evidence comes to her,' and 'Ali said: 'She is a woman tested; let her be patient until death or divorce becomes clear,' which is meant by the evidence in the marfu' hadith. And because marriage is established with certainty, and absence does not necessitate separation, and death is possible, so marriage is not removed by doubt. It is confirmed that 'Umar returned to 'Ali's opinion. The analogy with ila' is incorrect because it was immediate divorce made deferred by law, so it necessitated separation. Likewise the analogy with impotence, because absence may end with return, while impotence, if innate, does not change. Al-Shafi'i in one opinion and Ahmad in one narration said similarly to Malik. And he ruled his death after ninety years, i.e., the death of the missing person. Ahmad and al-Shafi'i also said this in one opinion. The opinions differed on specifying the period: Abu Yusuf estimated one hundred years, al-Hasan narrated from Abu Hanifa that he estimated one hundred twenty years, and in the apparent narration it is estimated by the death of his peers from his town. The chosen view is that it is delegated to the imam's opinion because it varies by country and nature. Some said it is delegated to the judge's opinion; whenever he sees maslaha he rules his death. Some said the estimation of seventy is better due to the hadith 'The ages of my ummah are between sixty and seventy.' The later scholars of our school said it is sixty out of kindness to people and to prevent harm. Al-Qahustani said: if one gives fatwa by Malik's view in a case of necessity, there is no harm. And she observes 'idda: i.e., when the judge rules his death, his wife observes the 'idda of death from the time of the ruling.

Commentary
This passage is from the chapter on the missing person (mafqud) in Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentary. It begins by defining the missing person as one whose location is unknown and whose life or death is uncertain. The Hanafi ruling is that such a person is considered alive with respect to his own rights: his wife may not remarry, his property is not divided, and his contracts remain valid. However, he is considered dead regarding the rights of others: he does not inherit from anyone who died during his absence unless his life at the time of the death is known. The judge appoints a guardian to take his rights, preserve his wealth, manage it, and spend on his relatives. The guardian may collect debts and litigate for debts that are acknowledged, but not for unacknowledged debts or property in another's hands, as this would involve ruling in absentia. Maintenance is provided to lineal relatives (parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren) and the wife, as these do not require a court ruling when the person is present. Non-lineal relatives like brothers and uncles are not maintained because their maintenance would require a ruling against the absent person, which is impermissible. The passage then addresses the wife's status: the Hanafi school holds that the judge does not separate the couple under any circumstances, based on the hadith 'She is his wife until evidence comes to her' and the practice of 'Ali. This is opposed by Malik, who, following a ruling of 'Umar, would separate after four years by analogy with ila' and impotence. The Hanafis respond that ila' is a deferred divorce, not comparable, and impotence is irreversible while absence may end. The dispute extends to the duration after which a missing person is presumed dead. The Hanafi school includes several opinions: Abu Hanifa (in a narration) said 120 years, Abu Yusuf said 100, the apparent position of the school is upon the death of peers, and the later scholars preferred 60 years based on the general lifespan. The judge's ruling is required for the wife to begin the 'idda of death. The passage concludes that if necessary, one may follow Malik's view of four years to prevent hardship.

The Missing Person's Wife: Status and Presumption of Death
Marital status and presumption of death of the missing person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the wife of a missing person remains married indefinitely, compare it with the Maliki view, and identify the opinions on the duration for presuming death.

Core Idea
The missing person's wife must remain patient until death or divorce is certain; no separation by analogy with ila' or impotence.

The marital status of the missing person's wife is one of the most pressing issues. The Hanafi school relies on the hadith 'She is his wife until evidence comes to her' and the saying of 'Ali. This lesson explores the dispute with the Maliki school, the rebuttal of analogies, and the various opinions on the time after which a missing person is presumed dead.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on separation
The judge does not separate the missing person and his wife under any circumstances. She remains married until definite news of death or divorce.
This is absolute; even after many years.
2
Cite the primary evidence
The Prophet said: 'She is his wife until evidence comes to her.' 'Ali said: 'Let her be patient until death or divorce becomes clear.'
These are dalil naqli (textual proofs).
3
Explain the opposing view (Maliki)
Malik holds that after four years of absence, the judge separates them, and the wife observes 'idda of death and may remarry. This is based on 'Umar's ruling in the case of the man taken by jinn and analogy with ila' and impotence.
Ila' is a vow of abstinence; after four months, the judge forces divorce.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The analogy with ila' is flawed because ila' is a deferred divorce, not mere absence. The analogy with impotence is flawed because impotence is irreversible, while absence may end. Also, 'Umar later retracted and accepted 'Ali's view.
The principle: marriage is certain and cannot be removed by doubt.
5
Summarize the dispute on the duration for presuming death
Opinions vary: 90 years (some Shafi'i, Hanbali), 120 years (Abu Hanifa narration), 100 years (Abu Yusuf), death of peers (zahir riwaya), 70 years (based on hadith), 60 years (later Hanafi scholars). The preferred view is delegated to the judge's discretion (al-mukhtar).
The later school adopted 60 years for convenience.

Ruling on the Missing Person's Wife: Maintenance and Non-Separation
Explaining maintenance and non-separation rulings for a missing person's wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on maintenance and judicial separation for a missing person's wife and evaluate the opposing Maliki argument.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school does not separate a missing person's wife, even after many years, because marriage is certain and does not dissolve by doubt.

This lesson addresses a critical area of family law: the missing person (mafqud). When a husband disappears, his wife and children face both financial hardship and marital uncertainty. The Hanafi school provides clear rulings that aim to protect the wife's rights without presuming death or divorce. We will explore the two key Hanafi rulings — maintenance without a court order and the prohibition of judicial separation — and contrast them with the Maliki view. The passage also establishes a general principle: those entitled to maintenance in the husband's presence without a court order retain that right in his absence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two Hanafi rulings
Read the opening lines of the passage. The text states that the judge does not separate the missing person from his wife, and that maintenance (nafaqah) for the wife and children is due without a court order.
These two rulings are the foundation of the Hanafi position.
2
Understand the underlying principle
The passage explains a general rule: whoever is entitled to maintenance in the husband's presence without a judge's order (wife, children, parents) is entitled in his absence. Others (siblings, uncles) require a court order, which cannot be issued against an absent person.
This principle avoids hardship for dependents.
3
State the Maliki opposing position
Imam Malik held that after four years of absence, the judge should separate the wife, she observes 'iddat al-wafat (four months and ten days), and then may remarry. His evidence includes a precedent from 'Umar and analogy to ila' and 'innah.
The Maliki view is also attributed to al-Shafi'i in one opinion and Ahmad in one narration.
4
Examine the Hanafi proofs
The Hanafi school cites a marfu‘ hadith: 'She is his wife until clear proof comes to her,' and the saying of ‘Ali: 'She is a woman who has been tested, so let her be patient until death or divorce becomes evident.' Marriage is established with certainty and does not vanish due to doubt. Additionally, ‘Umar himself retracted his judgment after hearing ‘Ali’s view.
These proofs override analogical reasoning.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Maliki analogy
The Hanafi rebuttal distinguishes ila': it is an immediate divorce deferred by the Lawgiver, thus necessitating separation. ‘Innah (impotence) is a permanent condition, whereas absence can end with return. Therefore, the analogies are unsound.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that under Hanafi law, the wife waits indefinitely; under Maliki, she may remarry after four years.

Discussing the time after which a missing person is ruled dead and the 'iddah of his wife

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the different Hanafi positions on the time to presume the death of a missing person, identify the mufta bihi (operative) view, and explain the beginning of the widow's 'iddah.

Core Idea
A missing person is not presumed dead by a fixed number alone; Hanafi law leaves the timing to judicial discretion, with sixty years from birth as the fatwa view.

Determining when a missing person is legally dead affects his wife's ability to remarry. The Hanafi school offers multiple views, from a fixed timespan to the death of contemporaries. This passage explores the ikhtilaf and identifies the operative ruling, emphasizing that a judge's ruling is required before the wife begins her 'iddah.

List the positions: Abu Yusuf (100 years), a narration from Abu Hanifa (120 years), the Zahir al-Riwayah (death of contemporaries), and the later scholars (60 years from birth). This ensures certainty in the start of the waiting period.

The Agent as Mere Messenger in Marriage, Khula, and Compromise
Ruling on agency as mere messenger in marriage, khula, and compromise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contracts in which the agent is a safir mahd and explain the legal consequences of this classification.

Core Idea
In marriage, khula, and sulh, the agent is a messenger; rights attach to the principal, not the agent.

This lesson explores a key distinction in Hanafi agency law: the concept of safir mahd (mere messenger). In certain contracts—marriage, khula, and compromise—the agent's role is purely conveyancing. Understanding this affects who receives the dower, who can demand payment, and how set-off works. We will examine the ruling, its proof, and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contracts
List the contracts mentioned where the agent is considered a safir mahd: nikah (marriage), khula (divorce by redemption), and sulh (compromise) for intentional killing and for denial of a claim.
These are contracts involving relinquishment (isqat).
2
Understand the Legal Proof (Talil)
The text argues that in these contracts the ruling (hukm) cannot be separated from the cause (sabab) because the cause is an isqat (relinquishment). If separated, the ruling would dissipate. Hence the agent must be a mere messenger so that the ruling coincides with the cause.
This is contrasted with sale, where the ruling can be separated (e.g., sale with option).
3
Apply the Rule to Dower and Compensation
The agent in marriage cannot receive the dower; the agent in khula cannot receive the compensation. Rights attach to the principal (wife in nikah, husband in khula). The agent may guarantee the dower but not receive it.
4
Analyze the Buyer's Right to Refuse Payment
When the agent sells, the buyer may refuse to pay the price to the principal directly, as the principal is a stranger to the contract. Payment to the principal discharges the buyer, but the agent cannot demand a second payment.
Set-off rules differ depending on who the buyer owes.
5
Note the Intra-Madhhab Dispute
Abu Yusuf disagrees with the majority on the agent's liability when paying the principal's debt with the principal's property. The majority holds the agent is liable; Abu Yusuf holds he is not. The text adopts the majority view.
Contrast with cases of orphan's property and currency exchange.

Commentary
This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq, with commentary from Ayni and others, presents the ruling on a slave being appointed as an agent (wakil) to buy himself from his master. The text opens with a hypothetical: if someone gives a slave one thousand dirhams and says 'buy yourself from your master,' and the slave tells the master 'sell me to so-and-so' (naming the principal), then the slave becomes the property of the principal. If the slave simply says 'sell me' without specifying, he becomes free. The commentary explains that the price is of the same type (dirhams or dinars) as what the slave was given. The core legal discussion analyzes three scenarios regarding how the slave attributes the contract: (1) if he attributes it to his principal by saying 'sell me to so-and-so,' the contract and the slave belong to the principal, because the slave is a valid agent for another in purchasing himself, being a stranger to his own monetary value (which belongs to the master). The analogy is drawn to a depositary purchasing the deposited item. The price is in the slave's liability, and he recovers it from the principal. (2) If the slave leaves the contract general by saying only 'sell me,' the slave becomes free because the general wording admits both possibilities (for the principal or for himself), and it cannot be determined as compliance with the agency. The price remains in the slave's liability. The objection that a slave is under interdiction is answered: the interdiction is lifted here because the contract was made with the master's permission. (3) If the purchase is for the principal, the slave's acceptance (qabul) is required because it is a sale; one person cannot be both offeror and acceptor. But if the purchase is for the slave himself (emancipation by purchase), the master's offer suffices and the slave's acceptance is not needed, because in emancipation the same person can undertake both sides, as in marriage. The passage uses analogical reasoning (qiyas) and istihsan extensively, comparing the slave agent to a depositary and emphasizing the unique nature of emancipation.

إعتاق (i'taq): Emancipation; freeing a slave, considered a contract where one person can be both offeror and acceptor.

When One Joint Agent May Act Alone in Divorce, Manumission, Return of Deposit, and Debt Payment
Rulings on when one joint agent may act alone in divorce, manumission, returning deposit, and paying debt.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four cases where one joint agent may act alone and explain the underlying rationale: does the act require opinion (ra'y) or not?

Core Idea
A joint agent may act alone when the act does not require opinion — returning, paying, or releasing without compensation.

In Hanafi law, two agents appointed jointly generally must act together. However, there are exceptions. The key criterion is whether the act requires opinion (ra'y) or is purely mechanical. This lesson explores those exceptions: divorce and manumission without compensation, returning a deposit, and paying a debt. Understanding these exceptions is crucial for correctly structuring agency contracts.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule for joint agents.
Two agents appointed together usually must act jointly; one acting alone may be invalid or cause liability.
2
List the exceptions mentioned in the matn.
The matn lists divorce without compensation, manumission without compensation, returning a deposit, and paying a debt. These are acts that do not require opinion.
3
Apply the condition 'without compensation' and 'immediate' for divorce/manumission.
If divorce or manumission involves consideration (like khul') or is conditional/delegated (tafwid), joint action is required because deliberation is needed.
The phrase 'without compensation' (bilā badal) applies to both divorce and manumission.
4
Distinguish between returning and receiving.
Returning a deposit does not require opinion; one agent may return it. But receiving a deposit involves preservation and joint action is better; if one receives alone, he becomes liable.
The same distinction applies to debt: paying (returning) vs. receiving requires joint action.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of exceptions to the general rule that two joint agents must act together. The matn states: 'And divorce and manumission without compensation, and returning a deposit, and paying a debt.' The commentator explains each. For divorce and manumission, the key condition is that they be without compensation (i.e., no financial consideration) and immediate (munajjaz) with a specified woman or slave. If the principal says 'Divorce her if you wish' or 'Her affair is in your hands', one agent cannot act alone because it requires opinion and deliberation. Similarly, if the divorce is unspecified, joint action is needed. However, if the principal says 'Divorce her' or 'Free him' (immediate, specified), one agent may act alone. For returning a deposit, the crucial point is that returning does not require opinion, while receiving (qabd) does require joint action because the principal's interest in preservation is better served by two. Thus, if two agents are appointed to receive a deposit, they must both be present; if one receives alone, he becomes liable. But if they are appointed to return a deposit, one may do it alone. The same applies to returning a loaned item, usurped item, or defective sale item. For paying a debt, it is similar to returning a deposit: no opinion needed, so one agent may pay alone. However, receiving a debt requires joint action like receiving a deposit. The commentator also notes that if an agent for paying debt uses the principal's money for himself and then pays with his own money, he is a volunteer and may seek reimbursement? Actually, the text says he becomes a mutabarri' (volunteer), meaning he cannot claim reimbursement from the principal for that payment.

طلاق بلا بدل (ṭalāq bilā badal): Divorce without any financial consideration (i.e., not in exchange for khul'). One agent may act alone.
رد وديعة (radd wadīʿah): Returning a deposit to its owner; this does not require joint action.
رأي (ra'y): Opinion or deliberation; acts requiring ra'y need joint action because they involve judgment (e.g., setting price, accepting conditions).
