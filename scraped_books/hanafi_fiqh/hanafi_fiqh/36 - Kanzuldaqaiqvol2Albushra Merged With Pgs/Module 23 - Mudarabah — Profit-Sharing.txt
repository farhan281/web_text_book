Murabahah by Mudarib Selling to Rab al-Mal
Ruling on murabahah when the mudarib sells to the capital owner

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate the correct murabahah base price when a mudarib sells the purchased item to the capital owner, and explain the Hanafi justification based on milk al-yad.

Core Idea
Murabahah cost after mudarib's sale to capital owner excludes the capital owner's pre-existing profit share.

In a mudarabah, the mudarib may sell the purchased item to the capital owner (rabb al-mal). How should the capital owner then sell it in murabahah? This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq clarifies that the cost basis must exclude the profit share that already belonged to the capital owner. It also refutes Zufar's objection by invoking the concept of milk al-yad (right of possession and disposal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The mudarib buys a garment for 10 dirhams and sells it to the capital owner for 15 dirhams. The capital owner now wants to do murabahah.
2
Calculate the pre-existing profit share
The profit is 5 dirhams. Half of that (2.5 dirhams) belonged to the capital owner as his share in the mudarabah, so it never left his ownership.
This half is deducted from the price.
3
Determine the adjusted cost basis
The amount that actually left the capital owner's ownership is: the original cost paid by mudarib (10 dirhams) plus the mudarib's profit share (2.5 dirhams) = 12.5 dirhams.
Thus murabahah is based on 12.5, not 15.
4
Understand Zufar's objection
Zufar argued the sale is invalid because it is selling property to its owner (the capital owner already partially owned the profit).
5
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response: the contract confers milk al-yad (right of possession and disposal) even if not full ownership (milk al-raqabah). Validity of sale follows benefit, not absolute ownership, as seen in mukatab contracts.
There is a tashbih (semblance) of nullity regarding half the profit, so the second sale is partially ignored.

ولو كان مضارباً يبيع مرابحة رب المال باثني عشر ونصف. يبيعه مرابحة باثني عشر ونصف؛ لأن نصف الربح، وهو درهمان ونصف سلم لرب المال، ولم يخرج عن ملكه فيحط عن الثمن، فتبقى اثنا عشر ونصف خارجة عن ملكه، عشرة منها دفعها المضارب إلى بائعه ودرهمان ونصف نصيب المضارب من الذي دفع إليه رب المال بحكم أنه الثمن، فتم ما خرج عن ملكه في تحصيل هذا الثوب اثنا عشر ونصف، فيرابح عليها. وقال زفر : لا يجوز هذا البيع من رب المال؛ لأنه بيع ماله بمال، قلنا: يستفيد كل واحد منهما بهذا العقد ملك اليد والتصرف، وإن كان لا يستفيد ملك الرقبة، فكان صحيحاً؛ لإفادته ملك اليد والتصرف، ولا يلزم من جواز البيع إفادة ملك الرقبة، ألا ترى أن المكاتب تجوز تصرفاته ولا تفيد ملك الرقبة، فعلم أن البيع يتبع الفائدة لا الملك عينا؛ مع هذا فيه شبهة العدم؛ لأن المضارب وكيل عنه في البيع الأول من وجه فاعتبر البيع الثاني عدما في حق نصف الربح. (فتح، زيلعي ونصف لأن نصف الربح ملك رب المال، وكذا حكم العكس. (ط، عيني)
And if a mudarib sells, the capital owner (rabb al-mal) does murabahah at twelve and a half. He sells it in murabahah at twelve and a half: because half of the profit—which is two and a half dirhams—is surrendered to the capital owner and did not leave his ownership, so it is deducted from the price. Thus twelve and a half remains as having left his ownership: ten dirhams of which the mudarib paid to his seller, and two and a half dirhams are the mudarib's share from what the capital owner paid to him by virtue of being the price. So what left his ownership in acquiring this garment is twelve and a half, and he does murabahah on that. Zufar said: This sale from the capital owner is not permissible because it is selling his property for property. We say: Each one benefits from this contract ownership of possession and disposal (milk al-yad wa al-tasarruf), though he does not benefit ownership of the substance (milk al-raqabah), so it is valid because it confers milk al-yad wa al-tasarruf. Validity of sale does not require ownership of the substance; do you not see that the mukatab's transactions are valid even though they do not confer ownership of the substance? Thus it is known that the sale follows the benefit, not absolute ownership. Nevertheless, there is a semblance of non-existence because the mudarib is an agent on his (the capital owner's) behalf in the first sale in some respect, so the second sale is considered non-existent regarding half the profit. (Fath, Zayla'i) 'and two and a half because half the profit belongs to the capital owner.' The same rule applies in reverse. (T, Ayni).

Commentary
This passage deals with a mudarabah scenario: the mudarib (investment manager) buys a garment for ten dirhams and sells it to the capital owner (rabb al-mal) for fifteen dirhams. When the capital owner then wishes to resell the garment in murabahah, he must base his cost on twelve and a half dirhams, not fifteen. The reasoning is that half the profit (2.5 dirhams) already belonged to the capital owner as his share of the mudarabah profit, so that amount never left his ownership. Therefore, the actual cost that left his ownership is the original ten dirhams plus the mudarib's share of the profit (2.5 dirhams), totalling 12.5 dirhams. The text also refutes Zufar, who argued that this sale is invalid because it is selling property to the owner himself. The Hanafi response is that the sale gives both parties the right of possession and disposal (milk al-yad wa al-tasarruf), even if not ownership of the substance (milk al-raqabah). The example of the mukatab (a slave who pays for his freedom) shows that valid contracts can exist without full ownership transfer. Still, there is a semblance of nullity regarding the half-profit, so the second sale is partially ignored.

Key Terms
مضارب (mudarib): The manager of capital in a mudarabah (silent partnership) contract.
رب المال (rabb al-mal): The capital provider in a mudarabah.
ملك اليد (milk al-yad): Right of possession and disposal without full ownership of substance.
