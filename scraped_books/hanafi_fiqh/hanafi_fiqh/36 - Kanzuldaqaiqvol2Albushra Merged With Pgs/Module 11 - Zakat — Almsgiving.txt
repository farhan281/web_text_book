Three Kaffarah Options and the Clothing Dispute
Explaining the three options for oath expiation and conditions of clothing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three obligatory alternatives for kaffarah and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on the minimum garment requirement.

Core Idea
Kaffarah for oaths has three equal alternatives; clothing must cover most of the body according to the preferred Hanafi view.

When a valid oath is broken, kaffarah (expiation) becomes wajib. The Quran and Sunnah provide three options: freeing a slave, feeding ten poor people, or clothing them. This lesson focuses on the clothing option and a subtle but important disagreement within the Hanafi school about what constitutes adequate clothing. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply rulings accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three obligatory alternatives
Read the Arabic text and its translation. Note that the word 'or' (أو) indicates choice, not sequence. The three alternatives are: freeing a slave, feeding ten poor people, and clothing them.
The text lists them in brief; the full details of each option are elaborated elsewhere in Kanz al-Daqa'iq.
2
Focus on the clothing condition
The text states: 'ما يستر عامة البدن' (what covers the majority of the body). This is the condition for the garment. Understand that this is the minimum according to al-Shaykhan (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf).
The phrase 'عامة البدن' is key—it means 'most of the body', not just the 'awra.
3
Recognize the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani disagreed: he considered any garment valid for prayer (even trousers) sufficient. His reasoning: any garment that legally covers the body for prayer is enough for kaffarah.
This is a legitimate difference of interpretation within the school.
4
Determine the mufta bihi and its basis
The commentators (Fath al-Qadir, 'Ayni) prefer the view of al-Shaykhan. They argue that 'clothing' in custom implies covering most of the body, not just the minimal prayer dress. This is the relied-upon position for fatwa.
Note the additional nuance: for women, a headcover is required because her head is 'awra.

Crucifixion and Non-Liability in Highway Robbery Hadd
Ruling on crucifixion and property liability after hadd for highway robbery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the method and duration of crucifixion for the highway robber and explain why no property liability remains after the hadd execution.

Core Idea
The highway robber is crucified three days for public deterrence; after hadd, neither his estate nor family owes compensation.

This passage from the commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses two key rulings for the punishment of the highway robber (muharib). First, how crucifixion (salb) is to be carried out: three days on the cross, then release to the family for burial, with no funeral prayer. Second, the effect of the hadd on civil liability: once the robber is executed, his obligation to compensate for destroyed property is extinguished. These rulings reflect the Hanafi emphasis on finality of punishment and deterrence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings in the passage
The passage covers (1) the procedure of crucifixion and (2) the non-liability for property after hadd. Pinpoint the Arabic phrases that state each ruling.
Look for 'يترك مصلوباً ثلاثة أيام' and 'لم يضمن'.
2
Extract the conditions for crucifixion
The text specifies duration (three days), release to family, and prohibition of funeral prayer. List these as the Hanafi conditions for implementing this punishment.
Contrast with Malik's view that duration is left to the imam's discretion.
3
Analyse the non-liability rule
The text states that after execution, no guarantee is taken from his estate for destroyed property. This is analogous to petty theft (sariqa sughra). Understand the rationale: the hadd expiates both sin and civil liability.
If the stolen property is still in his possession, it must be returned—liability for value is removed only.
4
Apply the analogy
The text explicitly compares to petty theft. Ask: Why does the same principle apply? Because in both cases, the hadd punishment fully substitutes for financial liability.
The illa (effective cause) is the completion of the prescribed punishment.

Division of Khums and Abrogation of the Prophet's Share
Explains division of khums and abrogation of Prophet's share

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the division of khums into three categories and explain why the Prophet's share and safi are abrogated.

Core Idea
The khums is divided among orphans, the poor, and wayfarers; the Prophet's share lapsed with his death.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies the Hanafi interpretation of the khums verse. The author explains that the khums is not divided into six or five shares as some claimed, but only into three. Moreover, the Prophet's share and his right to choose a safi were abrogated by his death because the ruling was tied to the attribute of prophethood.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi recipients of khums
From the text, the khums is divided among three categories: orphans, the poor, and wayfarers. The rich among the relatives have no share. Note that this is the Hanafi position.
The verse (al-Anfal:41) mentions five categories but Hanafis interpret them as three.
2
Contrast with opposing views
Abu al-Aliyah divides into six shares (including one for Allah for Kaaba/mosques). Ahmad divides into five shares (Prophet's share for public works). The author refutes these.
The mention of 'Allah' in the verse is for blessing (tabarruk), not a separate share.
3
Understand the proof for abrogation
The ruling regarding the Prophet's share is attached to the derivative 'rasul' (messenger). In usul al-fiqh, a ruling attached to a derivative indicates that its cause is the attribute from which it is derived. Since prophethood ended with the Prophet, the share lapses.
This is a key usul principle: al-hukm yudafu ila al-mushtaqq yadullu ala al-illah.
4
Apply the proof to the safi
Just as the Prophet's share abrogates, so does the safi (his right to choose an item from spoils). After him, the Imam cannot take a safi for himself.
This is agreed upon (mujma' alayh) according to the text.
5
Evaluate the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis give no separate share for Allah or the Prophet; the entire khums goes to the three categories. Others allocate portions to specific causes like repairing mosques or funding soldiers.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi due to the strength of the proof.

Classification of Conquered Lands: Kharaj and Ushr
Ruling on conquered lands tax classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for lands conquered by force, identify the exception of Makkah, and explain the proof from ijma.

Core Idea
Lands conquered by force become kharaji unless consensus exempts them, like Makkah.

This lesson examines the foundational Hanafi rule for classifying conquered lands as either kharaj (land tax) or ushr (tithe). The passage establishes that lands taken by force and left with their owners are kharaji, with the notable exception of Makkah, which remains ushri by consensus of the Companions. Understanding this classification is essential for applying land tax laws correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The matn states: whatever is conquered by force and its people are left upon it is kharaj land. Note that the condition is that the land is not divided, not necessarily that the people are left.
2
Recognize the exception
Makkah is an exception: it was conquered by force but remains ushri. The Prophet did not impose kharaj there.
This exception is based on ijma.
3
Understand the evidence
The proof for Makkah's status and for kharaj rates is the consensus (ijma) of the Companions.
Ijma is a source of law.

Revival of Dead Land: Proximity vs. Irrigation Source
Ruling on revived dead land tax classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Yusuf's proximity criterion and Muhammad's water source criterion for the tax classification of revived dead land, and apply the correct ruling.

Core Idea
The tax of revived dead land depends on either proximity to existing lands or the source of irrigation; Muhammad's view is mufta bihi.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rules for reviving dead land (ihya al-mawat). The passage presents two criteria: physical proximity to existing kharaj or ushr lands (Abu Yusuf), and the source of irrigation (Muhammad). Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for correctly classifying newly cultivated lands.

Guided Path
1
Define mawat
Mawat is uncultivated, unowned land, distant from inhabited areas (or lacking any water source according to Muhammad).
2
Identify the two criteria
Abu Yusuf considers proximity to existing kharaj/ushr lands. Muhammad considers the water source: if revived by ownerless water (rain, springs, great rivers), it is ushri; if by man-made canals, it is kharaji.
3
Apply Abu Yusuf's criterion
If revived land is closer to kharaj land, it is kharaji; if closer to ushr land, it is ushri; if equidistant, it is ushri.
4
Apply Muhammad's criterion
If revived by rainwater, springs, or rivers that no one owns, it is ushri. If by dug canals (like king's canal), it is kharaji.
Test of irrigation source is more reliable as it directly relates to productivity.
5
Note the mufta bihi
Later Hanafi authorities prefer Muhammad's view (water source criterion).

Fixed Rates of Kharaj per Jarib for Different Crops
Fixed kharaj rates with proof from Umar's ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi fixed kharaj rates per jarib for grain, vegetables, vines, and connected date palms, explain the proof from Umar's ijma, and distinguish between kharaj wazifa and muqasama.

Core Idea
Umar's settlement fixed kharaj per jarib: grain 1 sa'+1 dirham, vegetables 5 dirhams, vines/palms 10 dirhams — binding by ijma.

Kharaj is a land tax on non-Muslim owned agricultural land. The Hanafi school follows the rates established by Caliph Umar (RA) through a survey of Iraq, which achieved consensus among the Companions. This lesson unpacks those fixed rates, the definition of a jarib, and the two types of kharaj: wazifa (fixed) and muqasama (crop-share).

Guided Path
1
Identify the crops and their kharaj rates
From the matn, list the four categories: grain (wheat/barley), vegetables (radat), vines, and connected date palms. Note the amount due per jarib for each.
Grain: 1 sa' + 1 dirham; Vegetables: 5 dirhams; Vines: 10 dirhams; Connected palms: 10 dirhams.
2
Distinguish kharaj wazifa from kharaj muqasama
Kharaj wazifa is a fixed annual amount regardless of cultivation; it does not repeat with multiple harvests. Kharaj muqasama is a share of the produce (like ushr) and repeats with each harvest. This passage deals with wazifa.
Maximum in muqasama is half the produce.
3
Understand the jarib definition
A jarib is 60 cubits x 60 cubits by the Kisra cubit (approx. 1,592 m²). In the Sawad of Iraq this standard is used; elsewhere custom prevails.
4
Trace the proof: Umar's survey and ijma
Umar sent Uthman ibn Hanif and Hudhayfa to survey Iraq. They measured 36 million jaribs and imposed these rates in the presence of the Companions without objection, forming a binding ijma.
This ijma is the primary dalil for the rates.
5
Note cross-madhhab ikhtilaf and the Hanafi tarjih
Shafi'i differs on amounts (wheat 4 dirhams, barley 2, vegetables 6, palms/vines 8). Malik holds no fixed amount but capacity-based. Hanafi holds to Umar's ijma as mufta bihi.
For items not covered by Umar, capacity is used with a maximum of half the produce.

Commentary
This passage is a set of rulings with proofs on the obligation of kharaj in three scenarios and the prohibition of combining 'ushr and kharaj. The matn states: kharaj is due (1) if the owner neglects the land intentionally, (2) if the owner becomes Muslim, (3) if a Muslim buys kharaj land. Additionally, no 'ushr is levied on the produce of kharaj land because the two cannot be combined. The commentary elaborates: neglect means intentional non-cultivation; if prevented by another, no kharaj due. If the owner is unable, the imam may arrange sharecropping. For conversion, kharaj continues because it has aspects of both maintenance and punishment—maintenance continues, but punishment is not newly imposed. For purchase, the practice of the Companions (Ibn Mas'ud, Hasan, Shurayh) is cited, and a hadith misinterpreted by dissenters is explained. The prohibition of combining 'ushr and kharaj is based on the Prophet's statement and practical consensus. Al-Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad allow combination. This also extends to zakat on trade land: if land is for trade, Hanafi says only 'ushr or kharaj applies, not zakat; others say zakat also applies.

Key Terms
تعطيل (ta‘ṭīl): Intentionally leaving land uncultivated without excuse, resulting in kharaj still being due.

عشر ('ushr): Tithe (10% or 5%) on the produce of land that is 'ushri land, i.e., land owned by Muslims from the time of Islam without conquest.
خراج (kharāj): Land tax imposed on kharaj land, paid by the owner regardless of religion.

Kharaj Obligation in Three Cases: Neglect, Conversion, Purchase by a Muslim
Ruling on when kharaj land is neglected, owner converts, or Muslim buys it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three scenarios in which kharaj remains obligatory on kharaj land, explain the ruling for each, and distinguish between fixed and proportional kharaj in cases of neglect.

Core Idea
Kharaj is wajib on kharaj land even if the owner neglects, converts, or sells to a Muslim — it is a tied right.

The Hanafi school holds that kharaj (land tax) is a perpetual obligation on kharaj lands, regardless of changes in ownership or the owner's faith. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentary (likely 'Inayah or Fath al-Qadir) details three key scenarios: intentional neglect of cultivation, conversion of the owner to Islam, and purchase of the land by a Muslim. Understanding these exceptions helps clarify the nature of kharaj as a hybrid of maintenance (mu'nah) and punitive (uqubah) elements, and how the school balances rights of the treasury with practical realities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three cases from the matn
The base text (matn) states: 'If its owner neglects it, or embraces Islam, or a Muslim buys kharaj land, the kharaj is obligatory.' Isolate each case: ta'til (neglect), islam (conversion), and shira' (purchase by a Muslim).
The matn is concise; the commentary expands each.
2
Analyze the neglect case: fixed vs proportional
The commentary explains that for fixed kharaj (muwazzaf), neglect does not cancel the obligation, but for proportional kharaj (muqasamah), neglect results in no tax. Also, if the owner is physically unable (ajz), the imam can arrange sharecropping to collect kharaj from the owner's share.
Key distinction: fixed kharaj is tied to land; proportional is tied to produce.
3
Understand the conversion case: maintenance and punishment
The commentary says kharaj has two aspects: maintenance (mu'nah) which continues after conversion, and punishment ('uqubah) which is not initiated on a Muslim. Since kharaj is already established before Islam, the maintenance aspect remains, so the tax continues.
This dual nature is unique to Hanafi fiqh.
4
Examine the purchase case: permissibility and apportionment
The commentary cites Companions' practice to refute those who consider buying kharaj land makruh. It explains that the hadith about 'humiliation' refers to abandoning jihad, not to kharaj. Finally, it gives a rule: if the year's farming season remains after purchase, the buyer pays kharaj; otherwise the seller pays.
This shows practical ijtihad in balancing rights.

Ruling that 'Ushr and Kharaj are Not Combined
Ruling with inter-madhhab dispute and extension

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that 'ushr and kharaj are not combined on the same land, cite the two proofs (hadith and practical ijmā'), and distinguish the Hanafi ruling from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
A Muslim's land bears either 'ushr or kharaj, not both — proven by the Prophet's hadith and the consensus of rulers.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi ruling: a single piece of land cannot be subject to both 'ushr (tithe) and kharaj (land tax). The evidence is a direct prophetic hadith and the unbroken practice of all imams, just and unjust. We will also contrast the Shafi'i view and see how the principle extends to zakat on trade goods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Consider a Muslim buying kharaj land from a dhimmi, or a dhimmi buying 'ushr land from a Muslim. The Hanafi ruling: the new owner does not have to pay both taxes; only the land's original tax applies ('ushr for 'ushr land, kharaj for kharaj land). The two taxes are never combined.
The text says 'فلا يجب العشر والخراج' meaning neither is added to the other; each land retains its single liability.
2
Examine the first proof: the hadith
The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'لا يجتمع عشر وخراج في أرض مسلم' (No 'ushr and kharaj combine in the land of a Muslim). This explicitly prohibits the combination on Muslim-owned land.
The hadith is a clear textual evidence (dalīl naqlī) that the Hanafis treat as decisive.
3
Examine the second proof: practical ijmā'
None of the Muslim rulers, whether just or unjust, ever imposed both taxes on the same land. This consistent practice over centuries amounts to a practical consensus (ijmā' 'amalī) that reinforces the hadith.
The Hanafis consider the sustained practice of the imams as a binding proof.
4
Understand the opposing view and its rebuttal
Imam al-Shafi'i (and Malik, Ahmad) argue that the two are different rights in essence (one is zakat, the other is land tax), place, cause, and expenditure, so they can coexist. The Hanafi response: the hadith and consensus override this qiyās; textual evidence takes precedence over analogical reasoning.
This is a legitimate ikhtilāf based on different weights given to text vs. reasoning.
5
Extend the ruling to zakat on trade
The same principle applies when land is bought for trade: Hanafis say only the land tax ('ushr or kharaj) is due, not zakat al-tijārah. The opposing schools require both the land tax and zakat on trade.
This practical consequence (thamarah al-khilāf) shows the real impact of the disagreement.

Ruling on Saying 'My Wealth is for the Poor'
Ruling on a charity declaration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a charity declaration covers only zakatable assets, and distinguish the Hanafi position from Zufar's strict analogy and the bequest analogy.

Core Idea
A charity declaration binds only to zakatable assets — not all wealth — by custom and istihsan.

When someone makes a sweeping declaration like 'my wealth is charity for the poor,' does it obligate every possession or only those subject to zakat? The Hanafi school restricts the obligation to zakatable assets (cash, livestock, trade goods) based on common usage and istihsan. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its proof, and the key disagreements within and outside the school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the Hanafi ruling
The act is a binding charity declaration. The Hanafi ruling: it obligates only zakatable assets (mal al-zakah), not all property. The hukm level is wajib.
The statement is not a bequest; it is a direct charity declaration.
2
Understand the dalil (proof) for the Hanafi position
The Hanafi rely on 'urf (custom) and istihsan. The word 'mal' (wealth) in such contexts is customarily understood to refer to the type of property on which zakat is due. This restricts the literal generality.
Zufar's strict qiyas (literal interpretation) is overridden by istihsan.
3
Examine the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf (Zufar's view)
Zufar holds that the literal meaning of 'mal' includes all property, so one must give all. This is the pure qiyas. The Hanafi response: the context of charity associates it with zakatable property, not all possessions.
This is a classic case of istihsan overriding qiyas.
4
Examine the inter-madhhab ikhtilaf
Al-Sha'bi says nothing is required. Malik and Ahmad analogize to a bequest, limiting the obligation to one-third. The Hanafi reject both, as the declaration is not a bequest but a direct charity obligation.
The Maliki position treats it like a wasiyyah, which is limited to one-third.
5
Determine the mufta bihi (operative ruling)
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi view: only zakatable assets must be given. This is based on the preference of istihsan over qiyas and the common understanding of such declarations.
The text's author prefers the Hanafi position.
