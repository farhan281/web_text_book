Ushri Lands: Classification and Evidence
Defines and proves which lands are subject to ushr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of ushri lands according to the Hanafi school and explain the legal evidence (dalil) from the practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the caliphs.

Core Idea
Arab lands and lands of Muslims are ushri because kharaj is not taken from Muslims—ushr is lighter and includes worship.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq opens the chapter on ushr, kharaj, and jizyah. It defines the three categories of lands that are subject to ushr: the lands of the Arabs, lands whose inhabitants embrace Islam voluntarily, and lands conquered by force and distributed among the fighters. The ruling is supported by the continuous practice of the Prophet ﷺ and the rightly guided caliphs (sunna fi'liyya), along with a rational explanation based on the nature of kharaj and ushr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of ushri lands
From the text, list the three types: (1) Arab lands, (2) lands whose people embraced Islam, (3) lands conquered by force and divided among the spoilers.
The commentary defines Arab lands geographically: from al-‘Udhayb to the far Yemen in length, and from the sands of Yabrin to the Syrian border in width.
2
Understand the primary dalil
The text states: 'the Prophet and the rightly guided caliphs did not take kharaj from the Arabs.' This is a sunna fi‘liyya (practical precedent) that serves as proof. Also, kharaj is like fay' and cannot be taken from Arabs just as jizyah cannot be taken from them.
This dalil is zanni al-thubut (continuous practice but not explicitly legislated in a verse).
3
Grasp the rationale behind ushr for Muslim lands
For lands whose owners are Muslims, ushr is more suitable because it has an element of worship (similar to zakat) and is lighter since it only applies to the produce, not the land itself. Kharaj, on the other hand, is a punishment for unbelief and is not appropriate for Muslims.
The rationale is based on istihsan (juristic preference) even though the primary dalil is the practice.
4
Note the commentary's expansion
The commentary (Fath al-Qadir) elaborates on the definition of Arab lands and explains why kharaj is not established on Arab persons: kharaj is like fay', which is taken from non-Muslims as a penalty for unbelief, but Arabs were never allowed to remain in unbelief.
This expansion clarifies the distinction between ushr and kharaj.

Commentary
This passage opens the chapter on ushr, kharaj, and jizyah, defining each term. It then presents the ruling that Arab lands, as well as lands whose inhabitants become Muslim or are conquered by force and distributed among the fighters, are ushri lands (subject to ushr tax). The proof is the practice of the Prophet and the caliphs. The rationale is that kharaj is a punishment for unbelief, but Arabs were not allowed to remain in unbelief, so kharaj is not taken from them. For Muslims, ushr is lighter and carries a sense of worship. The commentary elaborates on the definition of Arab lands and the reasoning.

Key Terms
عشرية (ushriyya): Lands subject to ushr (a 10% or 5% tax on produce).
خراج (kharaj): A land tax imposed on non-Muslims or conquered lands not divided.
فيء (fay'): Booty taken from non-Muslims without fighting, or land that comes into Muslim possession without military expedition; here, refers to kharaj as a type of fay'.

Key Takeaway
