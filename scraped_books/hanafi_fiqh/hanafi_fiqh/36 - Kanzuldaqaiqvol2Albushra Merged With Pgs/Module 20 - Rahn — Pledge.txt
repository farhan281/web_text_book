Who Pays the Reward for a Runaway Slave with Encumbrances?
Ruling on ju'l when the runaway is mortgaged or has other claims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the reward liability for returning a runaway slave in various overlapping rights (pawn, debt, crime, gift, minor, usurpation) and apply the condition that the reward is on the beneficiary of the return.

Core Idea
The reward for returning a runaway slave is paid by the one whose right is preserved by the return.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a set of rulings on who must pay the reward (ju'l) when a runaway slave is returned, but the slave is subject to multiple claims: pawn, debt, crime compensation, gift, minor ownership, or usurpation. The guiding principle is that the reward is due from the person who benefits from the return, i.e., whose right is enforced or revived. The passage details the division when the slave's value covers only part of the mortgage, and the liability of the owner or third parties in other scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Principle
The reward is on the person whose right is revived by the return of the slave. This is the underlying rationale for all subcases.
The 'right' can be a pawn debt, an ownership claim, or a right to receive the slave as gift or compensation.
2
Analyze the Pawned Slave Case
If the slave's value is equal to or less than the debt, the entire reward is on the mortgagee (murtahin). If the value exceeds the debt, the reward is split: the portion equal to the debt is on the mortgagee, and the excess portion is on the pawner.
The mortgagee benefits by securing his debt; the pawner benefits by recovering the surplus equity.
3
Examine Cases with Other Claims
For a slave who is in debt (owner owes money), the owner pays the reward if he chooses to pay the debts; otherwise the slave is sold and the reward taken from the sale price. For a slave who committed a crime, the reward is on the owner if he ransoms the slave, otherwise on the guardian of the victim. For a gifted slave, the donee pays. For a minor's slave, the reward is from the minor's property. For a usurped slave, the usurper pays.
In each case, the reward liability falls on the party whose interest is advanced by the return.
4
Apply the Principle to Each Scenario
Verify that the assigned payer in each scenario is indeed the one who benefits from the return. For example, the usurper benefits by regaining possession wrongfully, so he pays.
This consistent logic helps remember the rules without memorizing each case separately.

And the reward for a pawned slave is upon the mortgagee: meaning, if the pawned slave runs away and the taker returns him during the pawner's lifetime or after his death, the reward is upon the mortgagee if the entire slave is guarantied (i.e., its value equals the debt or less), because the taker revived his debt by the return. If part of the slave is a trust and part is guarantied (i.e., its value exceeds the debt), then the reward for the guarantied portion is upon the mortgagee, and the reward for the trust portion is upon the pawner. If the slave is in debt (i.e., the owner owes money), then the reward for return is upon the owner if he chooses to pay the debts; otherwise, the slave is sold, and the returner takes his reward from its price, and the remainder goes to the creditors. If the slave has committed a crime and the owner chooses to ransom him, the reward is upon him; otherwise, upon the guardian of the crime victim. If the slave is gifted, the reward is upon the donee. The reward for the slave of a minor is from his property. And the reward for a usurped slave is upon the usurper.

Commentary
This passage provides a comprehensive set of rulings for cases where the runaway slave is subject to various rights: pawn (rahn), debt (dayn), crime (jinayah), gift (hibah), minor ownership, or usurpation (ghasb). The general principle is that the reward (ju'l) is paid by the person whose right (or benefit) is preserved by the return. For a pawned slave, if the slave's value equals or is less than the pawned debt, the entire reward is on the mortgagee (murtahin) because the return secures his debt. If the value exceeds the debt, the slave is partly trust (amanah) to the pawner and partly guarantied to the mortgagee; reward is divided proportionally. If the owner is in debt to others and does not pay, the slave is sold, and the returner takes reward from the sale proceeds. For a slave who committed a crime, the reward is on the owner if he ransoms the slave; otherwise on the guardian of the victim (since the slave's value covers the crime). For a gifted slave, the donee pays. For a minor's slave, the reward is from the minor's property. For a usurped slave, the usurper pays because the return benefits him by restoring his possession.

Key Terms
مرتمن (murtahin): The mortgagee (creditor who holds the pawn)
غاصب (ghasib): Usurper; one who wrongfully takes property

Agent's Liability for Lost Pledge or Guarantor
Ruling on agent taking pledge or guarantor and loss thereof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's liability when a pledge or guarantor taken for the sale price is lost without his negligence.

Core Idea
An agent is not liable for the loss of a pledge or guarantor if he acted without negligence.

This lesson addresses a common scenario in agency (wakalah): an agent sells goods on behalf of the principal and takes a pledge or guarantor for the price. What happens if the pledge is lost or the guarantor becomes insolvent? The Hanafi school provides a clear ruling that protects the agent from liability, provided he has not been negligent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context and action
The scenario involves an agent authorized to sell a slave. After sale, he takes a pledge (rahn) or guarantor (kafil) for the price. Understand that this is part of the agent's duty to collect the price.
The agent is acting within his authority.
2
State the ruling on liability
If the pledge is lost in the agent's hand without his fault, or if the guarantor dies insolvent, the agent is not liable for the loss. The text says 'fatawa against him' meaning the legal opinion absolves him.
The ruling is based on the agent not being negligent.
3
Identify the exception
The agent becomes liable if he is negligent, e.g., mishandles the pledge or fails to take proper care. The text implies that without negligence, there is no guarantee (daman).
Negligence would shift liability.
