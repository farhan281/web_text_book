Definition and Conditions of Rebels (Bughat)
Definition and conditions of rebellion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of bughat and identify the three conditions (Muslim, overpower, having a faction) that establish their legal status.

Core Idea
Rebels are Muslims who gather and overpower a town; their status depends on forming a powerful faction.

The passage opens the chapter by defining who qualifies as a rebel. It distinguishes rebels from dhimmis and highway robbers, and clarifies that the legal status of rebellion only applies when they have dominated a locality and assembled as a force.

Guided Path
1
Define the term bughat
State the Hanafi definition: Muslims who leave the obedience of the rightful Imam and overpower a town with a corrupt interpretation.
The term is from 'baghy' meaning transgression.
2
Identify the three conditions
1) They must be Muslims (dhimmis are excluded); 2) They must have overpowered a town (galabu); 3) They must have a faction (fi'ah) to return to.
Overpowering (taghallub) means domination, not just temporary control.
3
Explain the role of fi'ah
The existence of a faction (fi'ah) changes how they are treated in battle: if they have a faction, the Imam may finish off their wounded and pursue the fleeing; without a faction, they are treated more leniently.
If they have no faction, they are like mere lawbreakers, not a persistent threat.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the chapter on rebels (bughat) from Kanz al-Daqaiq, primarily based on the commentaries of al-Ayni and Fath al-Qadir. It begins by defining who qualifies as a rebel: Muslims who leave the obedience of the rightful Imam and overpower a town. The text emphasizes that this ruling applies only to Muslims; if dhimmis become dominant, they are treated as combatants (ahl al-harb). The condition 'overpower' (ghalabu) is crucial: the legal status of rebellion does not apply unless they have gathered and become a formidable group. Next, the passage discusses the Imam's duty to call the rebels to repentance, citing the example of Ali (RA) sending Ibn Abbas to the Khawarij at Harura. This call is not obligatory but is recommended as it may bring about a peaceful resolution. The text then details the responses the rebels might give and how the Imam should react. If they claim the Imam is oppressive, he must abandon oppression. If they insist they are right and claim authority, he may fight them, and the public must assist him because they have become like apostates or combatants who have been called to Islam. The method of fighting is the same as against combatants: every permissible weapon may be used. The passage then addresses specific rulings: finishing off the wounded, pursuing the fleeing, taking captives, and dealing with their property. The key distinction is based on whether the rebels have a safe haven or faction (fi'ah). If they have a faction, they remain a threat, so the Imam may finish off the wounded and pursue the fleeing; otherwise, he does not. This is a point of contention with the Shafi'i school, which holds that fighting should only be initiated when the rebels attack first. The Hanafi position, supported by the general command in Quran 49:9 and practical considerations, is that the Imam may initiate war when they gather for battle. The text also notes a narration from Abu Hanifa recommending staying away from fitna, but clarifies that this applies when there is no rightful Imam calling for jihad against rebels. The passage concludes with rulings on not taking offspring captive and holding property until repentance, with consensus on returning property if they repent.

Key Terms
بغاة (bughāt): Muslims who rebel against the rightful Imam with a corrupt interpretation, believing they are right.
فئة (fi'ah): A faction or group to which the rebels can return and from which they derive strength.
تغلب (taghallub): Overpowering or domination of a town.

The Imam's Response: Invitation to Repentance and Initiation of War
Procedural rules: invitation to repentance and initiation of war.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the necessity of calling rebels to repentance and the permissibility of initiating war, and contrast it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
The Imam must call rebels to repentance before fighting, but may initiate war when they gather; the Quranic command is absolute.

The passage now moves from definition to procedure. It discusses the Imam's duty to invite rebels to return to obedience, using the example of Ali (RA) and the Khawarij. It then addresses the key dispute: can the Imam start fighting before the rebels attack? The Hanafi school says yes, based on the general command in the Quran, while al-Shafi'i says no.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Imam's initial action
The Imam should call the rebels to repentance and debate them, as Ali did with the Khawarij. This invitation is not wajib but recommended.
The goal is to resolve the conflict peacefully.
2
Analyze the Hanafi–Shafi'i dispute on initiating war
Hanafi: The Imam may attack when rebels gather for battle, based on the absolute command of Quran 49:9. Shafi'i: He must wait until they attack first, because they are Muslims.
Hanafi distinguishes between rebels and disbelievers: rebels are fought due to their rebellion, not disbelief.
3
Evaluate the Hanafi proof
The imperative 'faqātilū' in the verse indicates obligation (wujūb) and is general, not conditioned on the rebels striking first. Also, practical necessity: delay may make it impossible to repel them.
The Hanafi principle 'al-amr yufīdu al-wujūb' is key.
4
Address the apparent contradiction with Abu Hanifa's statement
Abu Hanifa reportedly said to stay home during fitna. The commentators explain this applies when there is no rightful Imam calling for battle, not when a legitimate Imam leads the fight.
Thus, the ruling is consistent: obey the Imam when he is justified.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the chapter on rebels (bughat) from Kanz al-Daqaiq, primarily based on the commentaries of al-Ayni and Fath al-Qadir. It begins by defining who qualifies as a rebel: Muslims who leave the obedience of the rightful Imam and overpower a town. The text emphasizes that this ruling applies only to Muslims; if dhimmis become dominant, they are treated as combatants (ahl al-harb). The condition 'overpower' (ghalabu) is crucial: the legal status of rebellion does not apply unless they have gathered and become a formidable group. Next, the passage discusses the Imam's duty to call the rebels to repentance, citing the example of Ali (RA) sending Ibn Abbas to the Khawarij at Harura. This call is not obligatory but is recommended as it may bring about a peaceful resolution. The text then details the responses the rebels might give and how the Imam should react. If they claim the Imam is oppressive, he must abandon oppression. If they insist they are right and claim authority, he may fight them, and the public must assist him because they have become like apostates or combatants who have been called to Islam. The method of fighting is the same as against combatants: every permissible weapon may be used. The passage then addresses specific rulings: finishing off the wounded, pursuing the fleeing, taking captives, and dealing with their property. The key distinction is based on whether the rebels have a safe haven or faction (fi'ah). If they have a faction, they remain a threat, so the Imam may finish off the wounded and pursue the fleeing; otherwise, he does not. This is a point of contention with the Shafi'i school, which holds that fighting should only be initiated when the rebels attack first. The Hanafi position, supported by the general command in Quran 49:9 and practical considerations, is that the Imam may initiate war when they gather for battle. The text also notes a narration from Abu Hanifa recommending staying away from fitna, but clarifies that this applies when there is no rightful Imam calling for jihad against rebels. The passage concludes with rulings on not taking offspring captive and holding property until repentance, with consensus on returning property if they repent.

فئة (fi'ah): Faction to which rebels return.
إطلاق (iṭlāq): Absolute, unqualified command in the Quran.

Battlefield Rulings: Wounded, Fugitives, Captives, and Property
Specific battlefield rulings for bughat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that rebels with a faction may have their wounded finished off and fleeing pursued, while those without a faction are protected, and state the prohibition on enslaving offspring and the treatment of property.

Core Idea
Rebels with a faction are a persistent threat; those without are treated leniently; their property is held, not distributed.

The passage now details specific rules for the battlefield, based on whether the rebels have a faction (fi'ah). If they have a safe haven, they remain dangerous, so the Imam may finish off their wounded and pursue fugitives. If they have no faction, they are not a lasting threat, so leniency is shown. The passage also prohibits taking offspring captive and states that property is held until repentance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key variable: existence of a faction (fi'ah)
The ruling on wounded and fleeing depends on whether the rebels have a group to return to. If yes, they remain a threat; if no, they are effectively defeated.
The faction gives them potential to regroup.
2
Apply the rule for wounded and fugitives
If they have a faction: finish off wounded, pursue fleeing. If no faction: do not do so, by consensus.
The Hanafi school differs from the three (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, al-Shafi'i) who say never finish off or pursue because they are Muslims.
3
State the rule for captives and offspring
Captives may be killed if the rebels have a faction (but imprisonment is better); offspring are never taken captive (based on Ali's practice).
The prohibition on enslaving offspring shows they are not treated as kuffar.
4
Explain the treatment of property
Property of rebels is held (not divided as booty) until they repent; if they repent, it is returned to them by consensus.
This distinguishes bughat from disbelievers: their property is not ghanimah.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the chapter on rebels (bughat) from Kanz al-Daqaiq, primarily based on the commentaries of al-Ayni and Fath al-Qadir. It begins by defining who qualifies as a rebel: Muslims who leave the obedience of the rightful Imam and overpower a town. The text emphasizes that this ruling applies only to Muslims; if dhimmis become dominant, they are treated as combatants (ahl al-harb). The condition 'overpower' (ghalabu) is crucial: the legal status of rebellion does not apply unless they have gathered and become a formidable group. Next, the passage discusses the Imam's duty to call the rebels to repentance, citing the example of Ali (RA) sending Ibn Abbas to the Khawarij at Harura. This call is not obligatory but is recommended as it may bring about a peaceful resolution. The text then details the responses the rebels might give and how the Imam should react. If they claim the Imam is oppressive, he must abandon oppression. If they insist they are right and claim authority, he may fight them, and the public must assist him because they have become like apostates or combatants who have been called to Islam. The method of fighting is the same as against combatants: every permissible weapon may be used. The passage then addresses specific rulings: finishing off the wounded, pursuing the fleeing, taking captives, and dealing with their property. The key distinction is based on whether the rebels have a safe haven or faction (fi'ah). If they have a faction, they remain a threat, so the Imam may finish off the wounded and pursue the fleeing; otherwise, he does not. This is a point of contention with the Shafi'i school, which holds that fighting should only be initiated when the rebels attack first. The Hanafi position, supported by the general command in Quran 49:9 and practical considerations, is that the Imam may initiate war when they gather for battle. The text also notes a narration from Abu Hanifa recommending staying away from fitna, but clarifies that this applies when there is no rightful Imam calling for jihad against rebels. The passage concludes with rulings on not taking offspring captive and holding property until repentance, with consensus on returning property if they repent.

مولي (mūlī): The one who flees (fugitive).
