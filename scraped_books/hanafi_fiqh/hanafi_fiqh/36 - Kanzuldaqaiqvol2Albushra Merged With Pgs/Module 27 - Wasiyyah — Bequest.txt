Executor Unaware of Will vs Agent Unaware of Agency
Intra-Hanafi dispute on executor's knowledge condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference in ruling between an executor and an agent when both are unaware of their appointment, and identify the Hanafi position in the intra-madhhab dispute.

Core Idea
An executor becomes effective automatically even without knowledge, unlike an agent, because executorship is a deputyship after death akin to inheritance.

This lesson unpacks an important intra-Hanafi dispute about whether an executor (waṣī) must know of his appointment before his actions on the estate become valid. The passage distinguishes the executor from an agent (wakīl), who requires knowledge. Understanding this difference clarifies that executorship carries legal effects that are automatic after death, while agency is a living arrangement with no element of compulsion.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling held by Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani: an executor's sale of estate property before knowing of the will is valid. The executor becomes effective immediately upon appointment, even without knowledge.
This is the mufta bihi position from Zahir al-Riwayah.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Abu Yusuf disagrees: he analogizes the executor to an agent, requiring knowledge for validity. For him, the sale is invalid until the executor learns of his appointment.
Abu Yusuf is the opposing voice within the Hanafi school.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Abu Yusuf's proof is that both executorship and agency are forms of representation (inābah). Since an agent's action is invalid without knowledge, the same should apply to an executor.
He relies on qiyas: analogy between executor and agent.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad respond that executorship is a deputyship (istikhlāf) that takes effect after the principal's authority ends, akin to inheritance. Knowledge is not a condition because the executor acts in place of the deceased, not the living principal.
The key distinction: agency operates during life, executorship after death.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: If the executor sells estate property before learning of the will, the sale is valid according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, but invalid according to Abu Yusuf. The mufta bihi is the position of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad.
This difference affects the legal status of transactions conducted by an uninformed executor.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute about the status of an executor who does not know he has been appointed. The matn states that such a person becomes an executor even without knowledge, unlike an agent. The commentary explains that the executor's sale of estate property before knowledge is valid. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad hold this view, while Abu Yusuf analogizes it to agency, requiring knowledge. The difference is that executorship is a deputyship after death, while agency is during life. The passage also covers the case where someone informs a person about his appointment as agent: even a single informant suffices if the informant is of any status, as there is no compulsion in agency. The key distinction is that executorship carries legal consequences that affect the deceased's estate, while agency is a living arrangement with no element of compulsion.

Key Terms
وصي (waṣī): A person appointed to execute a will and manage the estate after death.
وكيل (wakīl): A person authorized to act on behalf of another during the principal's lifetime.
استخلاف (istikhlāf): Deputyship; appointment of a successor to act after the principal's authority ceases.
