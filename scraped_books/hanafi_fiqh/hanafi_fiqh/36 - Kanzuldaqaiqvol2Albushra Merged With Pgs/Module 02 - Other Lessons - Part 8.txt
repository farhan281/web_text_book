Why a Guarantor Cannot Be an Agent to Collect the Debt
Ruling with talil on invalidity of guarantor as agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school invalidates appointing a guarantor as an agent to collect the guaranteed debt and distinguish between agency and tamlik.

Core Idea
A guarantor cannot be an agent because he acts for himself, destroying the rukn of agency.

In Hanafi fiqh, agency (wakalah) requires the agent to act for the principal's benefit. This lesson examines a specific case: a creditor appointing the guarantor to collect the debt. At first glance, this seems efficient, but the Hanafi jurists identify a critical flaw: the guarantor acts for himself (freeing his own liability), not for the creditor. This violates the very essence of agency. The lesson walks through the textual reasoning, including a qiyas distinction with a seemingly similar valid case: appointing the debtor himself to discharge the debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Scenario
Read the passage example: Zayd creditor, Amr debtor, Bakr guarantor. Zayd appoints Bakr to collect. The ruling is that the agency is invalid (batil). Note the explicit conclusion: 'Bakr is never an agent.'
The invalidity is immediate; no possibility of ratification.
2
Understand the Rukn of Agency
The essential pillar (rukn) of agency is that the agent works for another. In this scenario, Bakr works for himself because collecting frees him from his guarantee liability. Since he acts for himself, the rukn is absent, making the agency void.
The text uses the phrase 'فانعدم الركن' (so the rukn is absent).
3
Distinguish from the Debtor Case (Qiyas)
A potential objection: if a creditor appoints the debtor to discharge himself, it is valid even though the debtor acts for himself. The text distinguishes this as tamlik (transfer of ownership), not agency. In tamlik, the principal transfers the right to act; the agent then acts on his own behalf. This is analogous to a husband telling his wife 'divorce yourself,' which is a tamlik of divorce, not an agency.
The key difference: agency requires acting for another; tamlik allows acting for oneself. The guarantor's case is pure agency, so it fails.
4
Apply the Consequences
If the invalid agent collects money and it perishes, it does not come out of the creditor's property but remains the debtor's responsibility. Also, later discharge of the guarantor does not validate the agency retroactively, similar to a guarantee for an absent person that is invalid even if later approved.
This reinforces that the initial appointment was void ab initio.

Commentary
This passage explains the matn's statement 'و بطل توكيل الكفيل بمال' (and the appointment of a guarantor for money is invalid). The commentator gives a concrete example: Zayd is a creditor, Amr is a debtor, Bakr is the guarantor (kafil) for that debt. If Zayd appoints Bakr to collect the debt from Amr, the agency is invalid because Bakr, as guarantor, would be acting for his own benefit: collecting the debt frees him from his guarantor liability. The rukn of agency (working for another) is absent. A possible objection is raised: if a creditor appoints a debtor to discharge himself, that is valid even though the debtor acts for himself. The commentator distinguishes: that is not agency but tamlik (transfer of ownership), similar to a husband telling his wife 'divorce yourself', which is a tamlik of the right of divorce. Thus, it is not agency. Consequences: if the invalid agent collects the money and it is destroyed, it does not come out of the creditor's property but remains the debtor's responsibility. Also, subsequent discharge of the guarantor does not validate the agency retroactively, just as a guarantee for an absent person is invalid even if later approved. The restriction to 'bimal' (relating to property) is noted: it is valid to appoint a guarantor as an agent for litigation concerning the person (nafs) because one person can handle both the guarantee and the agency for litigation.

Key Terms
الكفيل (al-kafil): Guarantor or surety; one who guarantees the debt of another.
التمليك (al-tamlik): Transfer of ownership; in this context, it is a legal construct distinct from agency, where the principal transfers the right to act to the agent, making the agent act on his own behalf.

Debtor's Compulsion to Pay Agent of Absent Principal: Confession, Denial, and Recovery
Rulings on debtor's compulsion and recovery when agency is denied.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a debtor is compelled to pay a claimant of agency for an absent principal and apply the rules for recovery when the principal denies.

Core Idea
The debtor's own confession can compel payment to a self-proclaimed agent, but the debtor bears the risk if the absent principal later denies.

This lesson examines the Hanafi ruling on a debtor who confirms a person claiming to be the agent of an absent creditor. The initial compulsion to pay is based on the principle of confession, but subsequent denial by the absent principal triggers complex recovery rules. Understanding these rules protects the debtor from double payment without remedy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
What is the act being ruled upon? It is the debtor's payment to a person who claims to be the agent of an absent principal. The ruling: the debtor is compelled to pay if he confirms the agency claim.
The compulsion is only according to Abu Hanifa; the three (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, al-Shafi'i) disagree.
2
Locate the dalil
The proof is the debtor's own confession (iqrar). The text states 'because he confessed against himself' (li-annahu aqqara 'ala nafsihi).
This is a rational proof (dalil 'aqli) based on the maxim that one's confession is binding.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
How does the confession prove compulsion? By confirming the agent, the debtor admits the agent's right to receive payment. Since debts are paid by equivalents, the debtor becomes bound to pay his property to the agent.
The confession overrides any later claim of prior payment.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
The three (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, al-Shafi'i) hold that the debtor is not compelled to pay. Their reasoning: the absent principal may deny, making the payment unsafe.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under their view, the debtor may refuse until the principal confirms.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The matn (Kanz al-Daqaiq) and commentators (Ayni, Fath) uphold Abu Hanifa's view. The debtor is compelled because his confession takes effect immediately.
The risk of future denial is borne by the debtor, who can later seek recovery from the agent under conditions.

Commentary
This extensive passage from the commentary (by Ayni and Fath al-Qadir) explains the matn statement: 'And whoever claims that he is the agent of an absent person to collect his debt, and the debtor confirms him, he is ordered to pay it to him.' The author then discusses the consequences. First, if the debtor confirms the claimant as agent, he is compelled to pay. The reason is that by confirming, he confesses that the claimant has a right to receive the debt, and one's confession is binding. Even if the debtor later claims to have already paid the principal, he is not believed because the confession overrides. However, the text notes that 'according to the three' (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and al-Shafi'i) the debtor is not compelled to pay; only Abu Hanifa holds the order. Then the passage discusses what happens when the absent principal appears. If he confirms the agency, the matter ends and the debtor is free. If he denies the agency, the debtor must pay the principal a second time, and can then recover from the agent whatever he paid, provided the money is still in the agent's hand (or its substitute if consumed). This is because the debtor's purpose was to clear his liability, which failed due to the denial. If the money is lost in the agent's hand, the debtor cannot recover unless he made the agent a guarantor (daman) at the time of payment. The text explains two forms of 'damanahu' (making a guarantor): either the debtor makes the agent a guarantor, or the agent voluntarily guarantees. This guarantee is valid because it is attached to a future cause. Additionally, if the debtor did not confirm the agent at all (silence or denial), he can recover from the agent because he paid on hope of approval, which failed. The debtor cannot recover the paid money in any of the four scenarios until the absent principal appears, because the matter is pending his confirmation or denial.

Key Terms
الغريم (al-gharim): Debtor; the person who owes the debt.
الضمان (al-daman): Guarantee or suretyship; in this context, making the agent a guarantor for potential future repayment.

Principal Confirms Agency After Payment
Ruling on debtor's liability after principal confirms agent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the debtor's liability when the absent principal confirms the agent's authority after payment, and identify the key condition that discharges the debtor.

Core Idea
A principal's subsequent confirmation validates the agent's past receipt and discharges the debtor.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a practical scenario in agency law: a debtor pays an agent who claims authority from an absent principal. Later, the principal appears and confirms the agent's authority. Is the debtor free from the debt? The Hanafi school rules that the debtor is discharged, because the principal's confirmation retroactively legitimizes the payment. This lesson explains the rationale and the conditions for this discharge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the parties and the transaction
Recognize the three parties: the debtor (gharim), the agent (wakil), and the absent principal (gha'ib). The debtor pays money to the agent based on the agent's claim of authority.
The agency is in litigation and receipt (al-wakala bi al-khusuma wa al-qabd).
2
Observe the default ruling without confirmation
If the principal does not confirm, the debtor may have to pay again to the principal and then recover from the agent. This is the rule when the principal denies the agency.
This is elaborated in the broader text, but our focus is on confirmation.
3
Locate the critical event: confirmation (tasdiq)
The principal appears and 'confirms' the agent (saddaqa), i.e., affirms that he had indeed authorized the agent.
The verb sadaqa implies believing or ratifying.
4
Derive the legal effect of confirmation
Confirmation makes the agency valid retroactively (from the time of payment). Therefore, the debtor's payment is valid and the debt is discharged.
The commentary states: 'then there is nothing upon the debtor'.
5
Note the Hanafi position on ratification
Hanafi accepts subsequent confirmation as sufficient; some other schools may require prior explicit authorization. This is an ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi position is based on the principle that agency can be established by ratification (ijaza).

Money Lost in Agent's Hands: No Recourse Unless Guarantee
Ruling on loss of agency money with guarantee exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling when money paid to an agent is lost and identify the condition under which the debtor can recover from the agent.

Core Idea
Without a guarantee, the debtor bears the loss; the agent is not liable for accidental loss.

In agency (wakalah), the agent holds money as a trust (amanah). If the money is lost without negligence, the agent is not liable. But what if the debtor fears the principal will demand payment again? The Hanafi school provides an exception when the debtor makes the agent a guarantor at the time of payment. This lesson examines that ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
Identify the legal scenario: the agent (wakil) receives money from the debtor (gharim) to pay the principal (talib). The money is lost before recovery. The default ruling is that the debtor has no recourse against the agent.
2
Recognize the exception
The debtor may recover from the agent only if the debtor explicitly made the agent a guarantor (daman) at the time of payment. This is an exception to the general rule that the agent is not liable for loss.
The guarantee must be attached to the cause of obligation (i.e., the future possibility of the principal reclaiming the debt).
3

The word can be read with tashdid or takhfif, both leading to a valid guarantee. With tashdid: the debtor makes the agent a guarantor. With takhfif: the agent promises to guarantee if the principal returns.
Both constructions show that the guarantee is conditioned on a future event (the principal's second claim).
4
Understand the validity condition
The guarantee is valid because it is attached to the cause of obligation (sabab al-wujud), analogous to saying 'Whatever so-and-so usurps from you is upon me.' This is permissible as a conditional guarantee in Hanafi law.
The guarantee cannot be for what the agent already holds (as it is a trust), nor can it be made before the principal makes a claim.

Core Idea
An agent can demand an oath from debtor who admits debt but denies agency, even without proof of agency — Sahibayn's view is mufta bihi.

This lesson examines an intra-Hanafi dispute about procedural rights in agency. The matn states that when a debtor acknowledges the debt but denies the agent's authority, the agent may demand an oath. Abu Hanifa dissents, arguing the agent lacks standing as a litigant without evidence. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists balance procedural formality with substantive truth-seeking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The core question: does the agent have the right to administer an oath to the debtor who admits the debt but denies the agency? This hinges on whether the agent qualifies as a litigant (khaṣm).

The text says: 'the agent has the right to make the debtor swear...' This is the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The agent can demand an oath, and if the debtor refuses (nukūl), judgment is given against him.

Abu Hanifa holds that the agent cannot administer the oath because the right to do so depends on being a proven litigant. Since the agency is denied and not proven, the agent lacks standing.

For the Sahibayn: the agent's claim is not frivolous because the debtor admits the debt, giving the agent a colorable right. The oath is a means to discover the truth. For Abu Hanifa: without proof of agency, the agent is a stranger; the oath is a serious matter requiring clear standing.
Both views are valid inside the Hanafi school; the dispute is about when a person qualifies as a khaṣm.

Under the Sahibayn: if the debtor swears, he does not pay; if he refuses, he pays. Under Abu Hanifa: the agent cannot even demand the oath, so the debtor's denial of agency alone would suffice to avoid paying the agent.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view; the agent may administer the oath.

Agency for Deposit vs. Debt: Confirmation Does Not Compel Payment
Ruling on agency for deposit vs. debt with proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between agency for a deposit and agency for a debt, and explain why the depositary is not ordered to pay while the debtor is ordered to pay upon confirming the agent.

Core Idea
Acknowledging another's property (deposit) is invalid; acknowledging one's own debt binds the debtor to pay.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a key ruling from the chapter on agency in litigation and collection. The Hanafi school draws a sharp distinction between an agent collecting a deposit (wadi'a) and an agent collecting a debt (dayn). The depositary who confirms the agent is not compelled to hand over the deposit, while the debtor who confirms the agent must pay. The reason lies in the nature of the property: a deposit is a specific asset belonging to another, whereas a debt is a liability on the debtor himself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, identify the two cases presented: a claimant says he is an agent to collect a deposit, and the depositary confirms; second, a claimant says he is an agent to collect a debt, and the debtor confirms.
The deposit is a specific property ('ayn), the debt is an obligation in the dhimmah.
2
State the ruling for each scenario
For the deposit: the depositary is not ordered to pay. For the debt: the debtor is ordered to pay.
The passage states 'مَا يُؤْمَرُ بِالدَّفْعِ إِلَيْهِ' for deposit and 'يُؤْمَرُ بِالدَّفْعِ إِلَيْهِ' for debt.
3
Identify the proof (dalil)
The ruling is based on a qiyas (analogical reasoning) derived from the nature of the property. The passage provides the 'illa: the deposit is مال الغير (another's property), while the debt is مال نفسه (one's own property).
The consensus (ijma') on the deposit case confirms its strength.
4
Explain why acknowledgment for another's property is invalid
An acknowledgment (iqrar) that affects another's specific property is invalid because it would harm the owner's right. The depositary's confirmation of the agent is an iqrar about the owner's property, so it is not binding and payment cannot be compelled.
In contrast, the debtor's acknowledgment is about his own liability, which is valid and binding.
5
Contrast with the debt case
For the debt, the iqrar is about the debtor's own property (his liability), so it is valid. Hence, the debtor is ordered to pay the agent.
A debt is not tied to a specific object; it is satisfied by any equivalent, so no harm to a third party's specific property.

Agent Spending His Own Money: Istihsan Over Qiyas
Ruling and istihsan reasoning for agent spending own money

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when an agent for spending uses his own money, explain the istihsan reasoning, and identify the conditions for the offset to apply.

Core Idea
An agent who spends his own money is not a volunteer—istihsan equates him to a purchaser, allowing offset.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents a key application of istihsan in Hanafi fiqh. It addresses a practical problem: when a principal gives an agent money to spend on his family but the agent uses his own cash. The seemingly logical ruling (qiyas) would treat the agent as a volunteer who must return the given funds. However, Hanafi jurists prefer a different ruling by istihsan, recognizing the agent as a purchaser on behalf of the principal, thus enabling a set-off. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the conditions under which this ruling applies.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
Identify the parties: principal gives ten dirhams to an agent to spend on the principal's family. The agent spends ten of his own dirhams instead.
The given dirhams are still in existence at the time of spending.
2
Identify the default ruling by qiyas
Qiyas would say the agent is a volunteer (mutabarri‘) because dirhams are specified in agency; using his own money violates the instruction, so he must return the given dirhams.
This is the 'qiyas jaliy' (clear analogy).
3
Explain the istihsan ruling
By istihsan, the agent is not a volunteer. The ten he spent offset the ten given, resulting in a set-off (taqāṣ). He does not need to return the given dirhams.
This is the mufta bihi (relied-upon) position.
4
Trace the istihsan reasoning
The agent for spending (wakīl bi-l-infāq) is considered an agent for purchase (wakīl bi-l-shirā’) because spending on the family implicitly involves buying necessities. An agent for purchase is allowed to pay from his own money and recover from the principal. The same logic applies here to avoid hardship.
The effective cause (illah) is the need to facilitate agency and remove hardship (nafy al-haraj).
5
Note the conditions
The offset applies only if: (1) the given dirhams were still in existence at the time of spending, and (2) the agent either attributed the spending to the given dirhams or spoke generally. If the given dirhams were consumed or the agent explicitly attributed the spending to his own money, he becomes a volunteer.
These conditions are derived from the exception clauses in the text.

Agency ends at death unless a third party's right makes it binding.

The text explicitly contrasts with agency for litigation or divorce, which are not binding and thus terminate upon death. Understand the difference: binding agency involves a third party's interest; non-binding agency is purely personal.

The rationale is that once a third party's right is attached (e.g., the mortgagee's right to sell the pledge), the agency becomes necessary to protect that right and cannot be undone by death or the principal's unilateral act.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probe covering the remaining causes of agency termination mentioned in the matn: (1) Dissolution of partnership: When partners separate (dissolve the partnership), the mutual agency implied in the partnership contract ends automatically, even without the agent's knowledge, because it is a constructive dismissal (azl hukmi). (2) Inability of a mukatab principal: If the principal is a mukatab (slave who contracted manumission through payment) and becomes unable to pay, reverting to slavery, his agency ends. Similarly, if the principal is a ma'dhun (slave permitted to trade) and is interdicted (hajr), the agency ends. However, this applies only to agents for contracts and litigation; an agent appointed simply to collect debts or receive deposits is not affected. (4) Principal's own action: If the principal himself performs the act for which he appointed an agent, the agency ends because the subject matter no longer exists.

Causes of Termination of Agency with Intra-Hanafi Disputes
Commentary on grounds that terminate agency with intra-Hanafi rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four main causes that terminate agency in Hanafi fiqh, explain the intra-Hanafi disputes regarding apostasy and al-mutbaq, and apply the ruling that agency terminates only when the principal's act prevents the agent from compliance.

Core Idea
Agency terminates by constructive death, partnership dissolution, incapacity, or principal's own act – each with Hanafi nuance.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq expands on the matn's list of causes that dismiss an agent. We will examine each ground, the conditions for termination, and the disputes among Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. Understanding these nuances prevents invalid transactions and clarifies when an agent's authority remains intact.

Guided Path
1
Define al-mutbaq and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute
Determine the period after which an apostate is legally dead, comparing Abu Yusuf's one month (fatwa) and Muhammad's one year (sahih). This period triggers termination of agency.
Qadi Khan attributes the month view to Abu Hanifa; another narration from Abu Yusuf says more than a day and night.
2
Analyze the effect of apostasy and lahaq on agency
Abu Hanifa holds agency is suspended until the judge declares lahaq, then void. The Sahibayn hold agency remains valid because the apostate's transactions are effective. Note the practical consequence: actions after lahaq are void under Abu Hanifa, valid under Sahibayn.
If the agent returns Muslim before lahaq, agency revives; after lahaq, it does not. If the agency was binding, it does not terminate by these events.
3
Examine constructive dismissal by partnership dissolution
When partners separate, implied agency between them ends automatically, even without the agent's knowledge. Also, an agent appointed by one partner is dismissed regarding the other unless both authorized the appointment.
This is 'azl hukmi (constructive dismissal); knowledge is not required.
4
Assess termination by principal's incapacity
If the principal is a mukatab who becomes unable to pay (returns to slavery) or a ma'dhun slave who is interdicted, agency for contracts and litigation terminates, but not for simple acts like collecting debts. The agent for debt collection remains.
The master's dismissal of the agent of a permitted slave does not terminate the agency.
5
Evaluate termination by principal's own action
The principal's own act terminates agency only if it prevents the agent from performing (e.g., selling and delivering the item). If the act does not prevent (e.g., one divorce without 'idda expiry), the agent can still act. Agency can revive if old ownership returns via a true rescission (e.g., return by option) but not via iqala or consent-based return.
Knowledge of the principal's act is not required for termination.

In the first case, the agent is prevented from compliance in what he was appointed, so the agency becomes void. It returns if his old ownership returns, as if he was an agent for sale, sold himself, then it was returned to him by option of condition or sight or due to a defective sale or option of defect by court order, the agency returns because his old ownership returned. But if it was returned by iqala or option of defect by consent, it does not return because it is a return that is not a rescission; it is a sale with respect to a third party, and the agent is the third of them, and agency is attached to the first ownership, and this is new ownership. (Ayni, Fath)

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the chapter 'Dismissal of the Agent' from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It expands on the matn's listing of causes that terminate agency. The first part defines the term 'al-mutbaq' (the complete period) which is the period after which an apostate is legally considered dead, triggering termination. There is an intra-Hanafi dispute: according to Abu Yusuf it is one month (based on analogy with fasting), and this is the fatwa; Qadi Khan attributes it to Abu Hanifa. Another narration from Abu Yusuf says more than a day and night (analogy with prayer). Imam Muhammad says it is a full year, which is considered correct for zakat. The author follows the order of the matn: first, the agent's apostasy and joining dar al-harb. According to Abu Hanifa, once the judge declares lahaq (joining), agency is void from that point; before that it is suspended. If the agent returns Muslim, it revives. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), the apostate's transactions are valid, so agency does not become void unless the agency was binding. There is further ikhtilaf on whether agency returns if the apostate returns as Muslim while being an agent (Muhammad says yes, Abu Yusuf no). Second, dissolution of partnership: if partners separate, the implied agency between them ends automatically (constructive dismissal), and any agent appointed by one partner is dismissed with respect to the other partner unless both authorized. Third, the principal's incapacity: if the principal is a mukatab who becomes unable to pay (returns to slavery) or a ma'dhun slave who is interdicted, the agency is terminated for contracts and litigation but not for simple acts like collecting debts. Fourth, the principal's own performance: if the principal performs the act himself in a way that prevents the agent from performing (e.g., selling and delivering), agency is void. If the principal's act does not prevent the agent (e.g., one divorce without 'idda expiration), the agent can still act. Agency can revive if the property returns to the principal via a rescission that is a true rescission (e.g., return due to option) but not if via iqala or consent-based return of defect, which is considered a new sale.

Key Terms
المطبق (al-muṭbaq): The complete period of time that legally renders an apostate as dead (e.g., one month or one year).
اللحوق (al-laḥq): The act of an apostate joining dar al-harb, which triggers legal death if confirmed by a judge.
الوكالة (al-wakālah): Agency: a contract where one person appoints another to act on his behalf in a permissible matter.
العزل الحكمي (al-ʿazl al-ḥukmī): Constructive dismissal: termination of agency without explicit revocation, due to a change in circumstances.

Termination of Agency by Principal's Inability
Explains when agency ends due to the principal's inability to perform

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the cause for termination of agency due to the principal's inability and distinguish between cases where agency terminates and where it does not.

Core Idea
Agency terminates automatically when the principal can no longer perform the act — not because of the agent's inability, but the principal's.

This passage from al-'Ayni's commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq explains a key principle in Hanafi agency law: the agent's authority is rooted in the principal's capacity to act. When the principal becomes legally incapable of performing the delegated act, the agency ends. We will examine the examples of divorce and sale to see how this rule applies and when agency can return.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the general rule: agency terminates when the principal is unable to perform the delegated act. The text gives examples: sale with delivery, manumission, etc.
The inability is legal (e.g., expiration of iddah), not physical.
2
Analyze the Divorce Example
If a principal appoints an agent to divorce his wife, and then the principal himself divorces her, the agent's authority depends on whether the principal can still divorce. If the iddah expires after a final divorce, the principal cannot divorce again, so the agent is discharged.
The iddah expiration creates a legal impossibility.
3
Distinguish the Second Scenario
If the principal divorces once and iddah has not expired, he can still divorce another time. Thus the agent retains authority for that remaining divorce.
Partial ability preserves partial agency.
4
Examine the Return of Agency
After the agent sells, if the property is returned to the principal by court-ordered rescission (khiyar 'ayb by qada), the old ownership returns and the agency revives. But if returned by mutual revocation (iqalah), it is a new sale, so no revival.
This shows that agency is tied to the specific ownership, not the person.
5
Apply the Principle
Test your understanding by changing facts: What if the principal becomes insane? That also terminates agency because he cannot legally act.
Insanity is another cause of inability.

Chapter on the Removal of the Agent. He (the principal) delegated him (the agent) if the matter is an act that renders the agent unable to comply, whether he knows it or not, such as sale, gift with delivery, manumission, tadbir, istilad, and kitabah. Similarly, if he appointed him to divorce his wife, and the principal divorced her three times or once, and her iddah expired, then even if he marries her later, the agent does not have the right to divorce her — because the principal's inability to effect the divorce is realized by the expiration of the iddah, and likewise the agent. Commentary
This passage from al-'Ayni's commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq explains the ruling on the termination of agency (in'izal al-wikalah) due to the principal's inability to perform the act delegated. The author first states the general rule: if the principal's own legal capacity to perform the act is lost (e.g., after divorce with expiration of iddah he can no longer effect a divorce on that particular bond), the agent is likewise discharged. Then he distinguishes two scenarios: (1) If the act itself becomes impossible for the principal, as when the iddah has expired, the agency terminates because the subject matter no longer exists. (2) If the principal can still perform a part of the act (e.g., one more divorce while iddah remains), the agency continues. The passage also addresses the return of agency: if the principal reacquires ownership through a court-order rescission (like khiyar al-'ayb by qada), the original ownership is considered to have returned, and the agency is restored. But if ownership returns through a mutual rescission (iqalah) or consensual option of defect, that is a new contract, not a return of old ownership, so the agency does not revive. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that agency attaches to a specific property or right, not to the person generally.
