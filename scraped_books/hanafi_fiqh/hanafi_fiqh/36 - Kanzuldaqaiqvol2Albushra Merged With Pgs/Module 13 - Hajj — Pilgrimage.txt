Oath to Walk to the House of Allah: Obligation and Compensation
Ruling on oath to walk and compensation for riding

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an oath to walk to the House of Allah, explain the role of istihsan in overriding qiyas, and determine the compensation required if one rides instead of walking.

Core Idea
An oath to walk to the House of Allah obligates Hajj or Umrah walking; riding requires compensation via istihsan.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a specific type of vow: when someone says 'Upon me is walking to the House of Allah.' The Hanafi school, using istihsan (juristic preference), interprets this as an obligation to perform Hajj or Umrah while walking, even though walking itself is not a distinct act of worship. The ruling is supported by a hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and customary understanding. Additionally, if the person rides instead of walking, they must slaughter a sheep as compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and ruling
The vow 'walking to the House of Allah' obligates the performance of Hajj or Umrah while walking. This is the primary ruling derived from custom (urf) and istihsan.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith of the sister of Utbah ibn Amir: the Prophet ordered her to ride and spill blood. This indicates that riding requires compensation. Also, the custom of people understanding such a vow as imposing pilgrimage.
Hadith: مرها أن تركب ولترق دماً
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The Prophet's imperative 'li-turq diman' (let her spill blood) indicates obligation (wujub) according to Hanafi usul (al-amr yufidu al-wujub). Thus, riding necessitates a dam.
4
Note the role of istihsan
Istihsan overrides qiyas (analogy). Qiyas would require nothing because walking is not a specific act of worship. But istihsan, based on hadith and custom, imposes the obligation.
Istihsan is a key Hanafi source.
5
Apply the compensation rule
If one rides all the way, he must slaughter a sheep. If he rides only part, he gives charity proportional to that part from the value of a sheep.
Compensation is wajib.

Commentary
This passage covers two interrelated rulings. First, if someone says 'Upon me is walking to the House of Allah' or 'to the Ka'bah,' it obligates him to perform Hajj or Umrah while walking. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference), which takes into account the custom (urf) that such phrasing intends to impose pilgrimage. The analogy (qiyas) would require nothing because walking itself is not a specific act of worship, but istihsan overrides this due to the hadith about the sister of Utbah ibn Amir. The text then details that the walking is from his house, not from the miqat, unless he entered ihram from home. If the person is in Makkah, he goes to Arafat on foot. If he wants to fulfill the vow with Umrah, he goes outside the Haram. Second, if the person rides instead of walking, he has an option: walking is better and fulfills the vow, but if he rides, he must slaughter a sheep (dam) as compensation because he performed a lesser form of the act. The hadith 'Order her to ride and let her spill blood' is cited. If he rides for only part of the journey, he gives charity equivalent to the portion ridden from the value of a sheep.

Key Terms
الاستحسان (al-istihsān): Juristic preference that overrides qiyas in favor of a more suitable ruling based on custom, necessity, or textual indication.
الدم (al-dam): Blood, used here to mean the sacrifice of a sheep as compensation for deficiency in fulfilling a vow.

Ruling on a vow to walk to Bayt Allah, with proof from hadith and istihsan.

Core Idea
Vowing to walk to the Kaaba obligates Hajj or Umrah, not just walking—custom determines the meaning.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a common vow: someone says 'Upon me is walking to the Kaaba.' At first glance, it might seem that only walking is required. However, the Hanafi school, using a hadith and custom, interprets this as a vow to perform the pilgrimage itself. We will explore the proof, the concept of istihsan, and the consequences if the vower rides instead.

Examine the primary proof: the hadith of Utbah's sister

Commentary
This passage from the commentary explains the ruling on a person who vows to walk to the House of Allah (Bayt Allah) or the Kaaba. The matn states that such a person must perform Hajj or Umrah while walking. The commentary clarifies that this obligation is established by istihsan (juristic preference) based on custom (ta'āruf) and a hadith, not by qiyas. By strict analogy (qiyas), only walking would be required, but since people customarily intend the pilgrimage acts when they say this, the vow is interpreted as a vow to perform Hajj or Umrah. The proof is the hadith of the sister of Utbah ibn Amir who vowed to walk to the Kaaba, and the Prophet ordered her to assume ihram for Hajj or Umrah. The commentary then details the practical application: the vower walks from his house on the preferred view, not from the miqat. If he assumes ihram from his house, walking from there is obligatory by agreement. If he is in Makkah, he assumes ihram from the Haram and walks to Arafat. If he wants to fulfill the vow by Umrah, he goes out to the Hill (al-Hill). The same ruling applies if he says 'upon me is walking to Makkah.' The passage then addresses the case where the vower rides instead of walking: the obligation of Hajj/Umrah remains, but since he was required to walk as a perfection, he must compensate by sacrificing a sheep (or giving charity proportional to part of the journey ridden). This is based on the Prophet's instruction to the woman who vowed to walk. The ruling is that riding the entire journey requires a sheep; riding part requires charity proportional to the part.

التعارف (al-ta'āruf): Customary usage; the common understanding of a phrase among people that gives it a legal meaning beyond its literal words.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference; departing from strict analogy (qiyas) in favor of a ruling that is more appropriate due to custom, necessity, or other considerations.

Vow to Walk to Haram, Safa, Marwa: Hanafi Position and Ikhtilaf
Ruling on vows to walk to sacred sites.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on vows to walk to sacred sites and explain why custom (ta'āruf) determines obligation.

Core Idea
Custom, not literal meaning, governs ambiguous vows; walking to Haram does not necessarily mean Hajj.

When someone vows to walk to a sacred site, does that obligate pilgrimage? The Hanafi school distinguishes between customary usage and literal meaning. This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq explores the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two companions, and how other schools rule differently. Understanding this helps clarify the role of 'urf (custom) in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the two views within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa's position (no obligation for all phrases) and the Sahibayn's view (obligation for Haram and Sacred Mosque, but not for Safa and Marwa).
The matn presents Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Name the opposing schools (Shafi'i, Hanbali, Maliki via Ashhab) and state that they obligate Hajj/Umrah for all these phrases.
Their basis is that visiting these sites is inherently linked to pilgrimage.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Understand the opponent's dalil: these phrases (walking to Haram, to Bayt Allah, to Safa and Marwa) are customarily understood as intending pilgrimage, so they are binding.
The Sahibayn's proof for Haram is qiyas: Haram includes the House, so mentioning Haram is like mentioning the House.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Examine Abu Hanifa's rebuttal: the literal meaning does not indicate Hajj/Umrah, and custom (ta'āruf) does not link these phrases to pilgrimage. Therefore, no obligation arises.
The Sahibayn agree on Safa and Marwa because those are separate from the House.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: According to the Hanafi mufta bihi, a person who makes such a vow need not perform Hajj/Umrah. According to the other schools, they must.
This affects whether compensation (kaffarah) is due for breaking the vow.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling when someone vows to walk to the Haram (the Sacred Precinct), the Sacred Mosque, or Safa and Marwa. According to Abu Hanifa, none of these phrases obligate Hajj or Umrah because there is no customary usage linking them to pilgrimage, and the literal meaning does not imply worship. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differ: they hold that 'walking to the Haram' or 'to the Sacred Mosque' does obligate Hajj or Umrah walking, because the Haram and the Sacred Mosque encompass the Kaaba, so mentioning them is like mentioning the Kaaba itself. However, they agree with Abu Hanifa that 'walking to Safa and Marwa' does not obligate anything because those are separate from the Kaaba. The passage also notes that other schools (Shafi'i, Hanbali, Maliki via Ashhab) obligate Hajj or Umrah for all these phrases. The Hanafi position is based on the principle that custom (ta'āruf) determines the meaning of such vows; without custom, the literal meaning (walking) is just walking, not pilgrimage.

Key Terms
الحرم (al-haram): The Sacred Precinct of Makkah, including the Kaaba and surrounding area.
غير متعارف (ghayr muta'āraf): Not customary; the phrase is not commonly used to mean pilgrimage.
