The Role of 'Yawma'idh' in Conditional Manumission
Rulings on conditional manumission with and without 'yawma'idh'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether a slave acquired after the oath is freed under a conditional manumission that includes the word 'yawma'idh' versus one that omits it, and explain why a fetus, mukatab, or jointly owned slave is not covered.

Core Idea
The word 'yawma'idh' links freedom to the day of the condition, covering later-acquired slaves; without it, only current slaves are included.

This lesson examines the key linguistic element 'yawma'idh' (that day) in conditional manumission oaths. The Hanafi school provides detailed rules on how the wording determines which slaves are freed upon fulfillment of the condition. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying these oaths in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
The passage presents two cases: with the word 'yawma'idh' and without it. Identify the difference in wording and its effect on which slaves are freed.
2
Analyze the linguistic rationale
The word 'yawma'idh' refers to the day of the condition (e.g., entry), not the day of the oath. Explain how the tanwin substitutes for a deleted sentence, making the condition time the reference point for ownership.
The tanwin on 'idh' compensates for a deleted clause, e.g., 'yawma idh dakhaltu'.
3
Trace the legal consequence
With 'yawma'idh', the slave is freed if owned on the condition day, even if acquired after the oath. Without it, the oath applies only to slaves owned at the time of speaking.
If no slave exists at the time of the oath, the oath becomes languid (laghw).
4
Apply the principle of implicit attribution
Even without explicit mention, the condition implies ownership at the condition time, as seen when the oath is made on another's slave – no freedom results because attribution to one's own ownership is missing.
5
Identify excluded categories
The term 'mamluk' does not include a fetus (owned as part of mother), a mukatab (slave under contract), or a jointly owned slave, because ownership is incomplete or not independent.
A fetus owned through a will is considered a mamluk only if specifically designated, but not under a general oath.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the chapter of swearing by manumission from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It opens by discussing the linguistic meaning of 'al-hilf' and explains that in this context it refers to suspending manumission on a condition. The first ruling states that if a person says, 'If I enter the house, every slave I possess on that day is free,' then a slave acquired after the oath is freed upon entry. The key is the word 'yawma'idh' (that day), which the commentary explains refers to the day of entry. Even if the person did not own a slave at the time of the oath, but purchases one later and then enters, that slave is freed. However, if the person says this to someone else's slave, it is not effective because there is no attribution to his own ownership. The second ruling covers when the word 'yawma'idh' is omitted. In that case, the oath applies only to slaves owned at the moment of speaking. A slave acquired later is not freed. The commentary also clarifies that the term 'mamluk' does not include a fetus held as part of a pregnant slave, because the fetus is not considered an independent slave. It also excludes a mukatab (slave in a contract of manumission) and a jointly owned slave, as their ownership is not complete. The third section deals with oaths specifying a future time like 'after tomorrow' or 'after my death'. The general principle is that such oaths only cover slaves owned at the time of the oath. However, regarding 'after my death', there is a dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that a slave acquired after the oath is also freed from the one-third of the estate, as this is considered a will (wasiyyah). Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that the wording is for the present and cannot cover future acquisitions. The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa, but with the condition that the manumission is from the one-third.

Key Terms
الحلف بالعتق (al-ḥilf bi al-'itq): Swearing by manumission; the act of suspending a slave's freedom on a condition, often used as an oath to enforce or prohibit an action.
يومئذ (yawma'idh): On that day; a compound word meaning 'the day of the condition's fulfillment' (e.g., the day of entry).
مملوك (mamlūk): A slave owned by a person; in legal terms, a human being over whom ownership is established.

Future-Timed Manumission and the Hanafi Dispute
Rulings on future-timed manumission oaths with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on oaths like 'every slave I possess is free after my death' and explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf regarding slaves acquired after the oath.

Core Idea
Oaths with 'after my death' are treated as wills, covering slaves owned at death; the dispute is over whether future acquisitions are included.

When a master declares that his slaves will be free after a future event (tomorrow or his death), the Hanafi school distinguishes between the timing of the condition and the ownership. The passage presents the general rule that only slaves owned at the time of the oath are covered, but introduces a crucial disagreement for oaths contingent on death.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
For oaths specifying a future time ('after tomorrow', 'after my death'), only slaves owned at the time of the oath are initially covered. The present tense 'amlikuhu' (I own) is taken literally.
2
Identify the exception for 'after my death'
Abu Hanifa holds that slaves acquired after the oath are also freed from the one-third of the estate because the wording is like a will (wasiyyah), which takes effect at death and covers whatever one owns at that moment.
Imam Muhammad agrees with Abu Hanifa.
3
Understand Abu Yusuf's objection
Abu Yusuf argues that the word 'amlikuhu' (I will own) is literally present tense, so it cannot refer to future acquisitions. Only slaves owned at the time of the oath are covered.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The text permits combining literal and metaphorical meanings because the causes are different: one part is an oath (for present slaves) and the other is a will (for future slaves). A single wording can serve dual purposes.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is Abu Hanifa's, with the restriction that manumission is from the one-third of the estate, as it is considered a will.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the chapter of swearing by manumission from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It opens by discussing the linguistic meaning of 'al-hilf' and explains that in this context it refers to suspending manumission on a condition. The first ruling states that if a person says, 'If I enter the house, every slave I possess on that day is free,' then a slave acquired after the oath is freed upon entry. The key is the word 'yawma'idh' (that day), which the commentary explains refers to the day of entry. Even if the person did not own a slave at the time of the oath, but purchases one later and then enters, that slave is freed. However, if the person says this to someone else's slave, it is not effective because there is no attribution to his own ownership. The second ruling covers when the word 'yawma'idh' is omitted. In that case, the oath applies only to slaves owned at the moment of speaking. A slave acquired later is not freed. The commentary also clarifies that the term 'mamluk' does not include a fetus held as part of a pregnant slave, because the fetus is not considered an independent slave. It also excludes a mukatab (slave in a contract of manumission) and a jointly owned slave, as their ownership is not complete. The third section deals with oaths specifying a future time like 'after tomorrow' or 'after my death'. The general principle is that such oaths only cover slaves owned at the time of the oath. However, regarding 'after my death', there is a dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that a slave acquired after the oath is also freed from the one-third of the estate, as this is considered a will (wasiyyah). Imam Abu Yusuf disagrees, arguing that the wording is for the present and cannot cover future acquisitions. The relied-upon position is that of Abu Hanifa, but with the condition that the manumission is from the one-third.

Key Terms
مدبر (mudabbar): A slave whose manumission is postponed until after the master's death; the slave becomes free upon the master's death from the one-third of the estate.
وصية (wasiyyah): A will or bequest; a legal disposition that takes effect after the testator's death and is limited to one-third of the estate.
أملكه (amlikuhu): I will own; a verb in the present tense but can indicate future ownership depending on context.

The Legal Effect of 'Kullu Hallin 'Alayya Haram': From Food to Divorce by Custom
Ruling on the phrase 'kullu hallin 'alayya haram' and its legal effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi fatwa on the phrase 'kullu hallin 'alayya haram' and explain how custom ('urf) overrides the literal meaning to produce divorce without intention.

Core Idea
When custom changes meaning, the fatwa changes: a general oath becomes divorce by prevalent usage.

This passage addresses a common legal statement: 'every permissible thing is forbidden for me.' In the Hanafi school, the literal rule restricts it to food and drink, but later scholars, observing widespread usage for divorce, gave a different fatwa. We will explore how this shift happened, what the operative ruling is today, and the subtle differences when similar phrases are used.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Literal Ruling (Zahir al-Riwayah)
According to the foundational texts, the phrase 'kullu hallin 'alayya haram' is treated as an oath abstaining from food and drink only. Breaking it requires expiation (kaffarah). This is the rule of the matn.
Imam Muhammad's Zahir al-Riwayah texts are the source of this literal view.
2
Recognize the Shift to the Fatwa
Later Hanafi masters (mutakhkhirin) observed that in common usage, this phrase is predominantly employed to effect divorce, not to swear off food. Therefore, they ruled that uttering it results in an irrevocable divorce (talaq ba’in) even without explicit intention.
The text explicitly says 'wal-fatwa 'ala anna tabayyanat imra'atahu bi-la niyya'.
3
Analyze the Reasoning: Istihsan and Custom
The shift is justified by istihsan (juristic preference) based on prevailing custom ('urf). The literal qiyas would demand immediate hanath for any permissible act, but custom restricts the meaning. The story of Abu Ja'far's daughter illustrates that women's non-use of the phrase for oaths proves its divorce connotation.
This is a classic example of takhsis al-‘amm bi al-‘urf (specifying a general text by custom).
4
Examine the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf on Similar Phrases
For phrases like 'halal Allah 'alayya haram' or 'halal al-muslimin 'alayya haram', there is disagreement: some scholars extend the same divorce ruling by custom; others require asking the speaker's intention. Learn both positions and the practical consequence.
The fatwa on 'kullu hallin' is clear; but for other formulations, caution may be needed.
5
Determine the Mufta Bihi and Its Application
The relied-upon position (mufta bihi) for 'kullu hallin 'alayya haram' is that it effects divorce without intention. If the speaker has no wife, it is an oath requiring expiation. For similar phrases, the mufta bihi is to ask the speaker’s intention if there is doubt.
Authority weight: fatawa mutakhkhirin. This shows how later Hanafi scholars adapt rulings to changing customs.

Oaths on Speaking to a Friend or Spouse: Pointing and Non-Pointing
Rulings on oaths using pointing to friends/spouses of others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between oaths with pointing and without pointing regarding friends/spouses of others, and apply the Hanafi ruling (Shaykhayn vs. Muhammad) to determine whether violation occurs after the relationship ends.

Core Idea
Pointing makes the oath about the person himself; without pointing, the motive may be the relation, so doubt prevents violation.

This lesson examines oaths that involve speaking to a friend or spouse of another person. The key distinction is whether the oath-taker pointed to that person (e.g., 'this friend of Zayd') or did not point (e.g., 'a friend of Zayd'). The Hanafi school has a consensus when pointing is used, but an internal dispute when pointing is omitted. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to analyze oaths and their conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
First, recognize that the passage treats two distinct cases: (1) oath with pointing (using 'this' or 'this') to a specific friend/spouse, and (2) oath without pointing (just 'friend of Zayd' or 'wife of Zayd'). In the first case, there is consensus. In the second, there is a dispute between the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
The presence of a demonstrative pronoun (هذا) is the key indicator of pointing.
2
Understand the consensus (pointing case)
When the oath includes pointing (e.g., 'I will not speak to this friend of Zayd'), if Zayd later ends the friendship or divorces, the oath-taker still breaks his oath by speaking to that person. This is by consensus of all Hanafi imams. The reason: a free person (friend/wife) is avoided for their own sake, not merely for their relation to Zayd. The addition 'of Zayd' is only for identification.
The text emphasizes 'al-ijmā'' (consensus) for this part.
3
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute (non-pointing case)
If the oath is without pointing (e.g., 'I will not speak to a friend of Zayd'), and then Zayd makes the friend an enemy or divorces the wife, the Shaykhayn rule that the oath-taker does NOT break his oath by speaking to them. Muhammad holds that he DOES break his oath. The Shaykhayn's argument: the omission of pointing and name indicates the oath might have been due to the relation, not the person. Doubt prevents violation. Muhammad argues: the objective is to avoid the person, and the addition is just for identification, so it is like the pointing case.
This is a pure madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school; the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) is that of the Shaykhayn.
4
Apply the rule for newly acquired relations
The passage also discusses when Zayd acquires a new friend or a new wife after the oath (without pointing). According to the Shaykhayn, the oath applies to the newly acquired person as well, so speaking to them would cause violation. Muhammad disagrees, saying the oath refers only to those existing at the time of the oath. The Shaykhayn's view is preferred.
This shows that without pointing, the oath is interpreted broadly ('every friend of Zayd') unless intent suggests otherwise.
5
Determine practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical difference between the Shaykhayn and Muhammad is seen in two cases: (a) after the relationship ends, the Shaykhayn say no violation, Muhammad says violation; (b) for newly acquired relations, the Shaykhayn say violation, Muhammad says no violation. The mufta bihi is the Shaykhayn's position.
Knowing the mufta bihi is essential for issuing fatwa. This is the operative ruling.

When Is the Last Slave Freed? Timing of Manumission in Oaths
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the timing of manumission in a conditional oath.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the intra-Hanafi dispute on the timing of manumission in the 'last slave' oath and explain the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) in both slave and marriage cases.

Core Idea
The dispute: freed from purchase (Abu Hanifa) or at death (Sahibayn)? The difference affects wealth and inheritance.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf about determining the exact moment a slave is freed under an oath like 'The last slave I own is free.' While all agree the last slave purchased before death is freed, they differ on whether freedom takes effect from the moment of purchase (retroactively) or only at death. This subtle distinction has major financial and legal consequences, affecting inheritance, dowry, and waiting periods in analogous marriage cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Common Ground
All agree that the last slave purchased before death is freed. The dispute is only about when that manumission takes effect.
The oath is conditional on the slave being the 'last' – the final one owned.
2
State the Two Positions
Abu Hanifa: freed from the time of purchase. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): freed at death.
This difference is the core of the ikhtilaf.
3
Understand the Reasoning
Abu Hanifa argues that 'last-ness' is determined at purchase; death merely reveals it. The Sahibayn argue that 'last-ness' is only confirmed at death because the possibility of another purchase remains during life.
Abu Hanifa sees death as a 'revealer' (mukashif); Sahibayn see it as the moment the condition is fulfilled.
4
Apply to the Slave Case – Thamarah
If purchased in health: Abu Hanifa says the slave counts from all wealth; Sahibayn say from one-third of the estate. If purchased in illness: both agree from one-third? Actually Sahibayn still say one-third, Abu Hanifa says from all if in health, but from one-third if in illness (since manumission in illness is from one-third). The passage states: 'at Abu Hanifa: from all property if bought in health; at the two: from one-third whether in health or illness.'
The practical difference is which part of the estate bears the cost.
5
Apply to the Marriage Case – Analogous Dispute
If a man says 'The last woman I marry is thrice divorced,' the same dispute applies: Abu Hanifa says divorce occurs at marriage; Sahibayn say at death. Consequences differ for inheritance, dowry, and waiting period.
This shows the principle is applied consistently across different legal domains.

The Rule of 'Or' and 'And' in Divorce Statements
Explaining the effect of 'or' and 'and' in divorce and analogous oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a husband says 'This wife is divorced or this wife, and this wife (third) is divorced' and apply the same rule to emancipation and admission.

Core Idea
When 'or' introduces two alternatives and 'and' connects a third, the last is fixed; the first two remain a choice.

Divorce statements often include particles like 'or' and 'and' that determine the legal outcome. This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq clarifies the Hanafi position: if a husband says to his wives 'You (first) are divorced or you (second) are divorced, and you (third) are divorced', the third wife is definitely divorced, while he retains the right to choose which of the first two is divorced. The same reasoning applies to freeing slaves and admitting debts. Understanding this rule helps you accurately apply the school's methodology to similar expressions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operative words
Locate the words 'or' (أو) between the first two wives and 'and' (و) attaching the third wife. The wording is: 'هذه طالق أو هذه وهذه طالق'.
The phrase 'وهذه' (and this) explicitly conjoins the third wife.
2
Determine the effect on each wife
The third wife is immediately divorced due to the conjunction 'and', which assigns the ruling to her. For the first two, the 'or' creates a choice; they are not divorced until the husband specifies one.
The husband may remain silent indefinitely; the divorce of the first two is contingent on his selection.
3
Understand the legal reasoning
The first statement uses 'or' to indicate one of two alternatives. The second statement, conjoined with 'and', applies the same ruling to a separate individual. Thus the third is bound, and the first two are left as an option.
The Hanafi principle: al-‘atf yashraku fī al-ḥukm – conjunction shares in the ruling.
4
Apply to analogous cases
If a master says to his slaves 'You (first) are free or you (second) are free, and you (third) are free', the third is freed, and the master chooses between the first two. Similarly, in an admission of debt: 'So-and-so has a thousand on me, or So-and-so and So-and-so' – the third gets half, and the other half is assigned at his choice.
These analogies show the principle's breadth across fiqh.
5
Note the condition regarding the predicate
The rule applies only if the second statement does NOT repeat the predicate (khabar). If the predicate is mentioned again, e.g., 'this is divorced or this, and these two are divorced', the result differs: the husband may choose between the two groups.
The absence of a predicate in the second statement is essential for the last wife to be fixed.

Ruling on 'or' and 'and' in Divorce, Manumission, and Acknowledgment
Explains the effect of 'or' and conjunction in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for utterances containing 'or' and 'and' to determine that the last mentioned is fixed and the first two are subject to choice.

Core Idea
When a speaker says 'A or B, and C', the last is fixed and the first two are subject to choice.

In Hanafi law, the precise wording of divorce, manumission, and acknowledgment declarations matters greatly. A statement like 'This woman is divorced or this woman, and this woman' produces a specific result: the last woman is immediately divorced, while the first two are left to the husband's choice. This lesson explains the grammatical and legal reasoning behind this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Parse the utterance into two parts
Identify the first part containing 'or' (أو) between two named individuals, and the second part joined by 'and' (الواو) introducing a third individual. For example: 'Zaynab is divorced or Amina is divorced, and Fatima is divorced.'
The first part ends before the conjunction 'and'.
2
Understand the function of 'or' (أو)
The word 'or' indicates a choice between the two items it connects. Neither is fixed; the speaker can select one of them to apply the ruling.
This is a key grammatical point in Arabic: أو is for taraddud (uncertainty) or تخيير (choice).
3
Understand the function of 'and' (الواو) in conjunction (عطف)
When 'and' is used to join a third individual to the previous phrase, it makes that individual part of the same judgment. Here, it attaches the third to the overall declaration, but because the first part had 'or', the third is not affected by the choice; it is directly included.
The conjunction is 'عطف' meaning sharing in the ruling of the first part, but limited to its own clause.
4
Apply the rule: last is fixed, first two are subject to choice
The third individual is immediately divorced/freed/obligated. The first two are not immediately affected; the speaker has the option (choice) to decide which one of them will receive the judgment.
If the speaker fails to choose during the relevant period, further rules apply (e.g., he must choose within the iddah or the marriage remains valid).
5
Extend the principle to analogous cases
The same reasoning applies to manumission of slaves ('This slave is free or this slave, and this slave') and acknowledgment of debt ('I owe Zaid a thousand or Amr and Bakr'). The last slave is freed; the last creditor gets half, while the speaker chooses between the first two.
This shows the rule is based on the linguistic structure, not the specific legal area.

Core Idea
A husband's general divorce oath includes the initiating wife; Abu Yusuf's implied exception is rejected by the majority Hanafis.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling with an inter-madhhab dispute regarding oaths not to strike one's wife. The core issue is whether actions like pulling hair, strangling, and biting constitute striking (ضرب). The Hanafi position, as stated by the commentator, is that these actions do break the oath because they cause pain (إيلام), which is the essence of striking. However, Imam al-Shafi'i holds that they do not break the oath, as they are not technically 'striking.' Some Hanafi scholars also initially adopted this view. A further nuance is introduced: if these acts are done in anger, they break the oath; if in jest or play, they do not, because jest does not constitute striking. The text then mentions that biting always breaks the oath, even if joking, according to 'Ayni. Finally, it is stated that the relied-upon view (mufta bihi) requires intention (qasd) for striking, similar to causing pain, so accidental harm in play does not break the oath. This highlights the Hanafi emphasis on the underlying substance (pain) over the literal wording, and the importance of context (anger vs. jest).

Conditional Revocation Void in Hanafi Law
Intra-Hanafi critique and refinement on conditional revocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that raj'a is invalidated by a void condition and distinguish the argument of al-Bahr from that of al-Nahr.

Core Idea
Raj'a is void with a void condition because it lacks witnesses and dower, unlike marriage.

This lesson examines a subtle dispute within the Hanafi school about the effect of attaching a void condition to the revocation of divorce (raj'a). The passage presents an objection from the author of al-Bahr and a refined response from the author of al-Nahr, clarifying why raj'a is more vulnerable than marriage to invalidation by conditions. Understanding this distinction deepens your grasp of how Hanafi jurists reason by analogy (qiyas) and draw boundaries between similar rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Determine the precise legal question: Can a void condition attached to raj'a invalidate the revocation? The text addresses this by presenting an objection and response.
2
State the objection of al-Bahr
The author of al-Bahr argues that raj'a is like marriage in that it is valid under duress and jest. Therefore, if raj'a were invalidated by a void condition, it would not be valid under jest, which contradicts the known rule.
Al-Bahr uses qiyas (analogy) to marriage.
3
Analyse the response of al-Nahr
Al-Nahr differentiates raj'a from marriage: marriage requires witnesses and financial consideration (dower), while raj'a does not. Hence, raj'a is akin to a simple declaration and is invalidated by a void condition.
The key illa (effective cause) distinguishing the two cases.
4
Understand the underlying principle
Raj'a is considered a continuation of ownership (istidamat al-milk). It must be treated like its beginning; just as an initial suspension of divorce is invalid, a final suspension is also invalid.
This principle supports the invalidation.
5
Apply the ruling
In practice, any conditional raj'a—whether explicitly conditioned on a loan ('I revoke you on condition you lend me money') or suspended on a future event ('if Zaid comes')—is void.
The mufta bihi position follows al-Nahr.
