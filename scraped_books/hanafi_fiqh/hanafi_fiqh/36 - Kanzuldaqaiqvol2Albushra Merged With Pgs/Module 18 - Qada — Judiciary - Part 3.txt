Debtor's Claim of Payment Does Not Excuse Payment to Agent
Ruling on forcing debtor to pay agent when debtor claims repayment to principal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a debtor who claims payment to the principal without proof must pay the agent, and identify the distinction regarding who may be sworn.

Core Idea
Implied acknowledgment of agency binds the debtor to pay the agent.

When a debtor claims he already paid the principal, he implicitly acknowledges both the debt and the agent's authority. The Hanafi school holds that this implicit acknowledgment is equivalent to explicit acknowledgment, forcing payment to the agent. However, the debtor cannot demand the agent swear; only the principal may be sworn. This lesson unpacks the proof and the minority view of Zufar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and the Act
The matn states: if the agent demands payment and the debtor claims he already paid the principal, the debtor is forced to pay the agent. Determine the hukm: wajib (enforced payment).
The ruling applies only when the debtor has no evidence of payment.
2
Analyze the Dalil: Implied Acknowledgment
The debtor's claim 'the owner took it' does not deny the agency but asserts payment. This implies acknowledgment of the debt and agency. In Hanafi usul, such an implication (dalalah) is legally effective.
This is a form of qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) from explicit acknowledgment.
3
Apply Qiyas from Explicit Acknowledgment
If the debtor explicitly admitted the agency, he would be forced to pay. Since his claim includes the same acknowledgment implicitly, the same ruling applies.
The illa is acknowledgment of the agency, whether explicit or implicit.
4
Note the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Swearing
Zufar held that the debtor can demand the agent be sworn. The majority (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) ruled that only the principal can be sworn because representation does not extend to oaths.
The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is that the agent is not sworn.
5
Distinguish Between Types of Agency
The agent for debt receipt is also an agent for litigation, so he can sue and respond. In contrast, an agent for leasing property who receives rent cannot decide on claims of payment until the principal appears.
This distinction prevents binding the absent principal without counsel.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding agency for debt receipt. The matn states: if a person appoints an agent to receive his money, and the debtor claims that the principal already took the money, the debtor is forced to pay the agent. The commentary explains the reasoning: the debtor's claim implies acknowledgment of both the debt and the agency, since he does not deny the agency but only claims payment, which is not proven by mere assertion. An analogy is made to explicit acknowledgment. Furthermore, the debtor cannot demand the agent swear; rather, the principal is sworn. The distinction is made between an agent for receiving money and an agent for renting property, where the latter, if the tenant claims payment to the absent principal, the matter is suspended until the principal appears. The passage also notes that if the debtor can prove payment with evidence, it is accepted. This ruling protects the agent's right to collect while allowing the debtor to later seek repayment from the principal if wrongfully paid.

Key Terms

غريم (Ghareem): Debtor: the person who owes a debt to the principal.
إيفاء (Ifa'): Payment or fulfillment of a debt; in this context, the claim that the debt was already paid to the principal.

Agency for Defect: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Swearing the Buyer
Intra-Hanafi dispute on procedural requirement for oath

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Abu Hanifa's and Sahibayn's positions on requiring the buyer's oath in a defect case and explain the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa says the judge's ruling is final; Sahibayn say it can be overturned, so the oath matters.

When a buyer is absent and his agent litigates a defect, the seller may claim the buyer was satisfied. The judge must decide whether to order return immediately or wait for the buyer's oath. This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute on this point, focusing on the nature of a judge's ruling: is it effective both apparently and substantively, or only apparently if based on error? We will see how this affects the procedure.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the issue
A buyer purchases a slave-girl, appoints an agent to litigate a defect, and then goes absent. The seller claims the buyer was satisfied with the defect. The judge must decide whether to order the return immediately or require the buyer's oath first. The issue is whether the judge's ruling on return is final or reversible.
The passage from the matn initially states the general rule that the judge does not return until the buyer is sworn.
2
Explain Abu Hanifa's position and his dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that once the judge rules for return, the ruling is effective both apparently (zahir) and substantively (batin). Therefore, it cannot be overturned, and swearing the buyer afterward is useless. In contrast, the debt case (where an agent collects a debt) involves no ruling, only an order to hand over money, so correction is possible.
Abu Hanifa distinguishes the defect case from the debt case by the presence of a judicial ruling.
3
Explain Sahibayn's position and their dalil
Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argue that a ruling made in error is effective only apparently. Thus, if it later becomes clear that the buyer was satisfied, the ruling can be overturned. Therefore, the judge must swear the buyer to ensure the ruling is correct before ordering return.
They treat the defect case and debt case alike because correction is possible in both.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf and the preferred view
The practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the agent can obtain a return without the buyer's presence; under Sahibayn, the buyer must be present and sworn, delaying the process. The commentary notes that the more correct position according to Abu Yusuf is to postpone in both cases and require the oath. Later Hanafi fatwa often adopts this cautious approach.
The mufta bihi is often the Sahibayn's view to avoid potential injustice.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding the procedure when a buyer's agent litigates a defect with the seller and the seller claims the buyer was satisfied with the defect. The matn initially states the rule that the judge does not return the slave-girl until the buyer himself swears he was not satisfied. The commentary then explains the difference in reasoning: Abu Hanifa argues that once the judge rules on the return, it is final (effective apparently and substantively), so swearing the buyer afterward is useless. In the debt case, however, there is no ruling but merely an order to hand over money, so if error appears, the money can be retrieved without overturning a ruling. The two companions (Sahibayn) hold that both cases are alike: correction is possible because a ruling made in error is only effective apparently and can be invalidated, so the buyer should be sworn before the return. According to Abu Yusuf's more correct position, the judge postpones in both cases and requires the buyer's oath. The practical consequence: Abu Hanifa allows the agent to obtain a ruling without the buyer's presence, while Sahibayn require the buyer to be present and swear.

رد (Radd): Return of the sold item (the slave-girl) to the seller due to a defect.
فسخ (Faskh): Rescission of the contract; in this context, the judge's ruling to rescind the sale due to defect.
