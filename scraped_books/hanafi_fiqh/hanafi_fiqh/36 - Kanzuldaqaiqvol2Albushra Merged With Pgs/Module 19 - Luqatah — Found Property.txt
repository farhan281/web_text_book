Rulings on the Foundling (Luqit): Limits of the Finder's Authority
States the finder's limited authority over a foundling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three actions prohibited to the finder (marriage, sale, hire) and the two actions permitted (handing to trade, accepting gifts), and explain the legal reasoning based on the foundling's independent legal personality.

Core Idea
The foundling is free and owns himself; the finder has no guardianship and cannot marry, sell, or hire him.

When a person finds an abandoned child (luqit), a natural instinct is to assume full responsibility. However, Hanafi fiqh carefully distinguishes between the finder's duty of care and legal guardianship. This lesson examines the precise limits of the finder's authority over the foundling's person and property, as stated in Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundling's legal status
The text begins 'فهو له' meaning the foundling owns himself and any property found with him. He is free (hurr) and capable of ownership (ahl al-milk).
This is the foundational principle from which the finder's limitations derive.
2
List the finder's prohibited actions
The text explicitly prohibits marriage (giving in marriage), sale, and hire. The commentary explains that these are invalid because the finder lacks wilaya (guardianship).
Wilaya comes only from kinship, ownership, or sultanic authority.
3
Understand the analogy of emancipation and its rebuttal
An objector argues: the finder saved the child's life, so he should have wilaya like an emancipator over a freed slave. The answer: a slave is legally dead (no ownership capacity), but the foundling is alive and an owner; the finder did not give life.
This illustrates the principle that iḥyāʾ (giving life) applies only to the legally dead.
4
Recognize the finder's permitted actions
The text permits handing the foundling to a trade (sina'a) as a form of education and discipline, and accepting gifts on his behalf as pure benefit.
These actions are purely beneficial and do not require wilaya.
5
Note the relied-upon opinion regarding hire
The commentary records that the finder cannot hire out the foundling (al-asahh), even if the mother can, because the finder lacks the lesser power of using the child's services without compensation.
This marks the muftā bihī position.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rulings of the foundling. It begins by stating the fundamental principle: the foundling (al-luqit) owns himself and is considered free, so any property found with him belongs to him. The finder (multaqit) has no authority to marry him off, sell his property, or hire him out. The commentary explains that the finder lacks guardianship (wilaya) because such authority comes only from kinship, ownership (like a slave master), or sultanic power; none of these apply to the finder. An objection is raised: since the finder saved the child's life through picking him up and raising him, should he not gain guardianship like an emancipator gains over his freed slave? The answer distinguishes that a slave is like a dead person in terms of ownership capacity, whereas the foundling is alive and capable of ownership; the finder has not given him life in reality or legally. Regarding sale, only a father or grandfather has complete discretion and compassion to sell a child's property; even a mother cannot sell, though she can marry him off. Therefore the finder cannot sell. Regarding hire (ijara), the finder cannot hire out the foundling because he does not have the lesser power of using his services without compensation, unlike the mother who can use the child's services freely and thus can hire him out. The relied-upon opinion (asahh) is that the finder cannot hire him out. However, the finder may hand the foundling over to a trade (sina'a) because it is a form of discipline and education, beneficial for the child. The finder may also accept gifts (hiba) on behalf of the Foundling, as this is purely beneficial. This is similar to the mother and guardian's ability to accept gifts for a minor.

Key Terms
اللقيط (al-luqīṭ): A foundling; a child abandoned and found by someone, legally considered free and capable of ownership.
الملتقط (al-multaqiṭ): The finder of the foundling; the person who picks up the abandoned child.
الولاية (al-wilāyah): Guardianship or authority over another's affairs; in this context, it refers to the right to marry, sell, or hire the foundling.

Rulings on Lost Property (Luqta) – Definition, Trust, and Requirement of Witnessing
Defines luqta, states its trust status, and discusses witnessing with proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on lost property, explain the conditions for liability, and apply the requirement of witnessing with its proof.

Core Idea
Lost property is a trust; witnessing is required to avoid liability, based on the Prophet's command.

Finding lost property is a common occurrence, but Islamic law prescribes specific rules to protect the rights of both the finder and the owner. This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq defines luqta, establishes it as a trust in the finder's hand, and discusses when it is commendable or obligatory to pick it up. It then focuses on the requirement of having witnesses, citing a hadith and addressing an intra-Hanafi dispute, and briefly touches on the obligation of announcement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
What is luqta? What is the default legal classification when taken with intention to return? It is a trust (amanah), so the finder is not liable unless he transgresses or refuses to return.
The passage contrasts this with taking it for oneself, which would be a guarantee.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith from Ahmad and Ibn Majah commands witnessing: 'Whoever finds a lost item, let him have just persons witness...'. This is the primary proof.
The hadith also includes instructions about preserving the item and returning it.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah
The imperative 'falyushhid' (let him have witnesses) indicates obligation (wujub) according to Hanafi usul. Thus, witnessing is a required legal act.
Hanafi principle: al-amr yufidu al-wujub.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Abu Yusuf and the other three imams (Shafi'i, Malik, Ahmad) do not require witnessing, comparing it to taking with the owner's permission. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad disagree.
This is a legitimate disagreement; the Hanafi mufta bihi is the view requiring witnessing.
5
Apply the ruling
If the finder cannot find witnesses at the time and fears the item will be lost, he is not liable by consensus. Otherwise, failing to witness without excuse makes him liable.
The announcement (ta'rif) is also required; the passage begins a discussion on its duration.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rulings of lost property (luqta). It begins by stating that whether found in the Haram of Mecca or elsewhere, a lost item is considered a trust (amanah) in the finder's hand, provided he takes it with the intention to return it to its owner. This conditional taking is authorized by the Lawgiver. The finder is not liable for the item unless he transgresses (like using it) or refuses to return it when the owner demands it. The text discusses the preferability of picking up lost property: the majority of jurists (including Hanafis) hold it as better to take it than leave it, because leaving it risks it falling into dishonest hands. Some, like the Mutazila, forbid it, and some early scholars preferred leaving it. The Hanafi position is that it is commendable (mandub) to take it if one feels safe; if one fears for oneself, one should not; but if one fears the item will be lost, it becomes obligatory (wajib). The text then discusses the requirement of having witnesses (ishhad) when taking the item, citing a hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him) instructing the finder to have just witnesses and preserve the item's description. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require witnessing, while Abu Yusuf and the other three Imams (Shafi'i, Malik, Ahmad, in one opinion) do not require it. If the finder cannot find witnesses at the time and fears for the item, he is not liable by consensus. The method of witnessing is to say, 'Whoever you see inquiring about a lost item, direct him to me.' The text then discusses the obligation of announcing (ta'rif) the lost item in markets, streets, and mosques for a period until the finder is reasonably sure the owner has given up. The duration varies, with a narration from Muhammad from Abu Hanifa that if the value is less than ten dirhams, announce for a few days; if ten dirhams or more, announce longer. The passage ends mid-sentence.

Key Terms
اللقطة (al-luqaṭa): Lost property; property found on the road whose owner is unknown and which is not permissible to simply take (unlike permissible things like water or grass).
الأمانة (al-amānah): Trust; the lost item is considered a trust in the finder's hand, meaning he is not liable for its loss unless he transgresses.
الإشهاد (al-ishhād): The act of having witnesses testify that the finder took the lost item with the intention to return it to its owner.

Rulings on Found Property (Luqata)
States rulings on picking up, witnessing, announcing, and charity of found property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on picking up found property, identify the role of witnessing and announcement, and apply the rules of liability upon charity after announcement.

Core Idea
Picking up found property is recommended unless loss is feared; it is a trust, not a guaranty.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq covers the Hanafi rules for found property (luqata). It addresses the recommended act of picking it up, the requirement of witnessing and announcement, and what to do if the owner does not appear. The ruling is supported by a hadith and distinguishes between Hanafi, Shafi'i, and other positions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition and initial ruling
Identify the legal definition of luqata and the default ruling on picking it up (mandub). Note when it becomes wajib (fear of loss).
The text says 'yandubu raf' al-luqata wa la yajib' and 'idha khafa diya'aha yajib'.
2
Identify the proof from hadith
Locate the hadith quoted about witnessing. Understand that the imperative 'falyushhid' is taken as evidence of obligation in the Hanafi usul.
Hadith from Ahmad and Ibn Majah.
3
Analyze intra-madhhab differences on witnessing
Note the disagreement between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad (witnessing required) and Abu Yusuf (not required). Understand the exception when witnessing is impossible.
Abu Yusuf analogizes to taking with owner's permission.
4
Understand announcement duration and charity
Review the different durations for announcement based on value (less than 10 dirhams, 10+, 200+, etc.). Learn that after announcement, the finder may give in charity or keep.
Various narrations from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad; gradations from Hasan ibn Ziyad.
5
Grasp the owner's options upon return
If the owner appears after charity, he may validate the charity (get reward) or hold the finder liable for the value. The finder's liability is like that of a person in dire necessity.
Charity does not remove liability; owner may also hold the poor person liable if the item perished.

The madhhab of the general jurists is that picking up is better than leaving because if left, it may reach a treacherous hand. It is said in Sharh al-Tahawi: if one finds a lost item, it is better to pick it up if safe; if not safe, do not pick. The gist: it is recommended (mandub) not obligatory; if fear of loss, it becomes obligatory. According to al-Shafi'i in one opinion: obligatory absolutely. From Ahmad and Malik: leaving is recommended, and it is a narration from us. It is a trust: in the finder's hand, so he is not liable except by transgression or refusal after claim. If he cannot bring witnesses, e.g., no one at the time of picking, and fears thieves, he is not liable by agreement. Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive ruling statement on the law of found property (luqata) in the Hanafi school. It begins by defining luqata as property found on the road whose owner is unknown and which is not permissible (i.e., not ownerless). The author then notes the difference of opinion: the Mu'tazila forbid picking it up entirely, some early tabi'un preferred leaving it, but the majority of jurists, including the Hanafis, hold that picking it up is better (afdal) because it preserves the property from being stolen. The ruling is further refined: it is recommended (mandub) but not obligatory unless there is fear of loss, in which case it becomes obligatory. This differs from Shafi'i (obligatory) and from Malik and Ahmad (recommended to leave). The finder takes it as a trust (amanah), so he is not liable for loss or damage unless he exceeds limits (ta'addi) or refuses to return it when the owner demands. The obligation to bring witnesses (ishhad) is supported by a hadith from Ahmad and Ibn Majah. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad require witnessing, while Abu Yusuf does not. If the finder cannot find witnesses, he is excused by consensus. The method of witnessing is to say publicly that he has found a lost item. Then comes the requirement of announcement (ta'rif): the finder must announce the item in public places for a period sufficient to make it unlikely the owner is still seeking. The duration varies according to value: for less than 10 dirhams a few days, for 10 dirhams or more a year, with further gradations reported from Hasan ibn Ziyad. After announcement, if the owner does not appear, the finder may give the item in charity or keep it. If he gives charity and the owner later appears, the owner has the option to validate the charity (thereby gaining reward) or to hold the finder liable for the value. The finder is liable even though he acted with legal permission, because the permission only removes sin, not liability, analogous to one in dire necessity taking another's food. If the item is still in the hands of the poor person, the owner takes it. The owner may also hold the poor person liable if the item perished. The poor person cannot seek reimbursement from the finder but can seek from the poor person.

Key Terms
لقطة (luqata): Found property: an item found on the road whose owner is not known specifically, and it is not ownerless.
أمانة (amanah): A trust: the status of property in the finder's hand, so he is not liable for loss/damage unless he is negligent.
تعريف (ta'rif): Announcement: the finder must publicly announce the found item in markets and mosques for a period until he is sure the owner no longer seeks it.

Rulings on Found Animals: The Hanafi Position on Picking Up Lost Livestock

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Picking up lost animals is recommended in the Hanafi school except in safe environments where the animal is self-sufficient.

This lesson examines a classical inter-madhhab dispute about whether one should pick up a found camel or other large animal. While the Maliki and Shafi'i schools argue that large animals should be left alone, relying on a prophetic hadith and the principle that taking another's property is prohibited, the Hanafi school holds that the ruling depends on the environment and whether the animal is at risk of loss. We will explore the proofs on both sides and understand the Hanafi reasoning based on istihsan and custom.

Malik and al-Shafi'i hold that leaving large animals in the desert is better, relying on the hadith about lost camels.

The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim.

The practical consequence: in safe environments, the Maliki/Shafi'i leave the animal; the Hanafi may still pick it up if fear of loss exists, but if the animal is truly safe, leaving it is also acceptable.
The ruling is contextual.

Volunteer or Debt? The Finder's Expenses
Core ruling on finder's legal status when spending on foundling/lost property

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when the finder's expenses are voluntary and when they become a debt, and state the consequence for recovery.

Core Idea
Without judge's permission the finder is a volunteer; with permission expenses become a recoverable debt.

When someone finds a lost child (foundling) or lost property, they may incur expenses. The Hanafi school distinguishes two scenarios based on whether the finder obtained the judge's permission. This rule protects the owner's liability while encouraging proper care of the found item.

Guided Path

These are the two legal possibilities for the finder's expenses.
2
Analyze the reason for volunteer status
The commentary explains that without permission the finder has no authority to obligate the owner, similar to paying another's debt without order.
The key principle: no authority to impose a debt.
3
Understand the effect of permission
With the judge's permission, the judge acts as deputy for the absent owner, so the expenses become a debt (dayn) on the owner or the foundling.
The judge's authority stems from his general guardianship over absent persons.
4
Apply to foundling vs. lost property
For a foundling, the debt is on him when he grows up; for lost property, on the owner. Without permission, the finder cannot recover from either.
The ruling differs based on who is ultimately responsible.

Lost Animals: Hiring, Selling, and Detention
Detailed rules for lost animals: hiring, selling, detention, and destruction effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school treats lost animals that can be hired vs. those that cannot, and the rules for detaining property and the effect of destruction.

Core Idea
Lost animals with benefit are hired; without benefit they are sold; the finder may detain until paid, and destruction before detention does not cancel the debt.

When a lost animal is found, the Hanafi school provides detailed rules to balance the owner's interest with the finder's expenses. The judge plays a central role in deciding whether to hire or sell the animal, and the finder has a right to detain the property as security for the debt of expenses. The effect of destruction on that debt depends on timing.

Guided Path
1
Classify the lost animal by benefit
Read the passage: if the animal has a benefit (e.g., can be hired as a mount), the judge hires it out for a period and spends from the hire. If it has no benefit (e.g., a sheep that is not rented), the judge may sell it after a waiting period.
The distinction is based on whether the animal can generate income to cover expenses without imposing a debt.
2
Understand the judge's actions
The judge hires the animal if it has benefit; otherwise, he sells it after defining a period. The sale proceeds replace the animal for the purpose of safekeeping.
This preserves the owner's property while covering necessary expenses.
3
Identify the finder's right of detention
Once expenses are authorized (with judge's permission), the finder may detain the property from the owner until the debt is paid.
This right is similar to a pledge (rahn).
4
Analyze the effect of destruction
If the property is destroyed before the finder detains it, the debt of expenses remains. If destroyed after detention, the debt falls (since the finder's security is lost, akin to a pledge).
The commentary compares to an agent who pays from his own money: destruction before detention does not extinguish the debt, but after detention it does.
5
Apply the special case of a runaway slave
A runaway slave (abq) is treated like lost property except he cannot be hired due to risk of escape.
This exception shows sensitivity to the nature of the property.

Reward for Returning a Runaway Slave (Ju'l of the Abiq)
Ruling on the reward for returning a runaway slave from a journey distance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the reward for returning a runaway slave from a journey distance and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Forty dirhams are obligatory for returning a runaway from a journey distance, even without stipulation.

This lesson examines a key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the reward (ju'l) for returning a runaway slave. The Hanafi school, relying on Companion practice and a prophetic report, fixes the reward at forty dirhams regardless of the slave's value. You will see why the Hanafis reject the analogy to volunteer services and how textual evidence overrides qiyas.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Clearly articulate the Hanafi ruling: the finder of a runaway slave brought from a journey distance (three days or more) is entitled to forty dirhams as a fixed reward, even if no reward was stipulated and even if the slave's value is less.
This is based on istihsan (juristic preference) overriding qiyas.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Shafi'i school holds that no reward is due without a prior agreement because the finder is a volunteer. They analogize the case to returning a stray animal.
The Shafi'i proof is purely analogical (qiyas).
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Examine the Shafi'i analogy: The finder voluntarily undertakes the task, so if no reward was promised, he is like any volunteer. Without contractual obligation, no compensation is owed.
The Shafi'i rely on the principle that services cannot be charged without stipulation.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond with specific textual evidence: the report that the Prophet ﷺ fixed the reward at forty dirhams, and the practice of the Companions who affirmed the reward even without prior agreement, though they differed on the amount. This textual evidence overrides general analogy.
The Hanafis also harmonize the Companion differences by applying the forty dirham amount to journey distance and lesser amounts to shorter distances.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Hanafi law, the finder can claim forty dirhams from the master in court even without a prior agreement. Under Shafi'i law, the finder gets nothing unless the master promised a reward beforehand.
This affects how finders of lost property are compensated in Islamic legal systems.

Spending on a runaway slave without judge's permission makes you a volunteer; with permission, the owner owes a debt.

This lesson explores a specific ruling in Hanafi fiqh: what happens when someone spends money on a runaway slave they have found. The text draws a direct analogy to found property (luqatah), establishing that the legal consequences depend critically on whether the finder sought and obtained the judge's permission before spending. Understanding this ruling requires careful attention to the conditions that transform a voluntary act into a recoverable debt.

The analogy is based on the common element of being lost and needing care.

The text splits the ruling into two cases: if the returner (radd) spends without the judge's permission, he is a volunteer (mutabarri') and cannot claim reimbursement. If he spends with the judge's permission, the expense becomes a debt (dayn) on the master (mawla).
The key variable is i'dhn al-hakim (judge's permission).
3
Understand the judge's authority
The judge has authority (wilayah) over the runaway slave and can imprison the master for the debt when the master appears. This is because the judge acts as a guardian for the absent owner.
Imprisonment is a coercive measure to enforce payment.

The passage adds that the judge may not hire out the slave (yu'ajjiruhu) to earn money to pay the expenses, due to the fear of a second escape (khawf al-ibaq thaniyan). This protects the slave's owner from further loss.
This exception prevents the slave from being used as a source of income while in custody.
5

Consider a found runaway slave today — the principle applies to a lost person or property that requires maintenance. The finder must act responsibly and seek judicial authorization to ensure reimbursement.
Modern analogies could include found animals or abandoned property, though specific rules differ.
